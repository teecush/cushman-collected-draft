Dr. Givings, New York physician circa 1880, turns on the newly installed electric light in his living room and wonders at the marvels Edison has wrought. His wife, Catherine, feels less jubilant about the invention; she misses the subtler, flickering illumination of candlelight. This isn't all she misses; shes a nursing mother whose milk is insufficient and who has to hire a wet-nurse to compensate. Shes frustrated in other ways, too, ways that she can't quite identify or at least can't quite describe. Meanwhile, in the next room, which is his consulting room, her husband treats the fashionable and predominantly female complaint of hysteria by applying to his patients another new electronic device, a vibrator. The patients react with shocked enjoyment, though they get over the shock part. Can I, one of them inquires, get one of these to use at home?

In the Next Room, subtitled the vibrator play, is the work of the much-lauded youngish American playwright Sarah Ruhl, and its being given its Canadian premiere at the Tarragon (in a co-production with the Royal Manitoba Theatre Centre). Its an engaging play, in an engaging production. But it doesn't really hang together. It plays as if the author had let her mind roam over a series of loosely associated ideas, and assumed they would cohere into a narrative. It has the structure of a story but the feel of a fantasia. It reaches, for example, a hopeful ending that's nice in itself but seems in context like wishful thinking.

Its also historically suspect, maybe not in scientific detail but in philosophical assumption. Dr. Givings is allowed to take credit for an improved device, a splendid Heath Robinson contraption in the current staging, called the Chattanooga Vibrator, ("Pardon me, boy, is this the Chattanooga Woo-Woo?"). It gets rapturous results from his principal patient, a Mrs. Daldry; and though the doctor likes to keep the two halves of his life and of his house separate, Mrs. Givings prevails on him to try it out on her. The two ladies compare mystified notes, and then describe their sensations to Elizabeth, the wetnurse, who happens to be black. She says the reactions sound similar to what the women would experience when having relations with their husbands. They dont know what she's talking about.

The implication is that Victorian women, or at least white middle-class Victorian women, had no idea there could be such a thing as a female orgasm. Which suggests that the decorous conventions of the 19th-century novel are an infallible index of what actually went on in the bedrooms of the time and that we should be congratulating ourselves on our enlightenment. It's as if the patronizing first act of Caryl Churchills Cloud Nine were to be played without its compensatory self-critical second. I doubt if this is what Ruhl actually intended, but she does seem to be going for the easy contemporary laugh.

Not that she cant be witty, both in dialogue and in situation. The web she spins also encompasses Leo, a young painter who speaks at rapturous and entertaining length about the sexual freedom he's encountered in Europe but is still inhibited at home; he becomes a rare male recipient of Dr. Givings' good vibrations. The doctors wife falls for him, prompting her husband to compare her to "that Mrs. Bovary." Leo, however, only has eyes for Elizabeth, whom he wishes to paint in the act of suckling. Elizabeth has recently buried her own child, but she has others, and her fecundity is implicitly contrasted with the barrenness of Mrs. Givings, who has but the one child, and Mrs. Daldry who has none at all, this probably being their neglectful husbands fault. They predictably develop a lesbian attraction, this giving them a theoretical kinship with Annie, the doctor's mature unmarried assistant. They also have a musical relationship, Mrs. D having a knack for inventing tunes and Mrs. G supplying the words. The patterning is ingenious, but it's never more than that since the characters remain puppets, smaller than life.

Richard Roses production is distinguished, as the text virtually dictates it must be, by David Boechlers boldly bisected set, full of elegantly exaggerated period detail, but it leaves the actors in sink-or-swim mode. David Storch's Dr. Givings is the most efficient swimmer; his character's name brings with it a panoply of double meanings - very giving in his work, very withholding in his personal life, well-meaning but blinkered in both - and Storchs performance is, as it should be, undeviatingly ambiguous. The doctors effect on his patients might be described as intermittently galvanic, and the same description might fit several of the performances.

If the play has a central character, it's the frustrated Catherine, gradually discovering the depths of that frustration; Trish Lindstrom has some startlingly powerful moments on the voyage, but she's less successful at making her situation seem, as is apparently also intended, funny. Melody A. Johnsons Mrs. Daldry, by contrast, is hilarious in her evocations of unexpected arousal (which happen to resemble closely a recent episode of Curb Your Enthusiasm) but even her powers of making eccentricity look normal are strained in her other scenes, maybe because shes meant to be your average Victorian and Johnson isn't your average anything. Jonathan Watton is in terrific control of Leo's first long speech, but rushes everything afterwards. Elizabeth Saunders is fine as Annie, unassailably patient with the patients. The palm goes, though, to Marci T. House who plays Elizabeth with a dignity and poise unmatched anywhere else in the cast. But then she's the only one who's given the chance.
