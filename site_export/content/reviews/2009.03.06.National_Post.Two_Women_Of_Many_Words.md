Last year at Passe Muraille, Anusree Roy gave us a one-woman play, Pyaasa, that showed her to be a good writer and an exceptional performer. The same qualities are on view in her new piece, Letters to My Grandma, but the result isn't as satisfying. There's nothing actually wrong with the piece itself, and there's much about its presentation that's perfect, but you're still left wondering at the end of its 50-minute length what it was all for.

Roy appears here in two roles. She's Malobee, who in her childhood emigrated with her parents from India to live in Toronto, and whom we meet in her early twenties when she's about to get married. And she's Malobee's beloved grandmother, whom the family left behind in India and with whom Malobee has constantly corresponded but

has never visited. Somehow, it never seemed to be the right time for the trip. Despite its title, the play contains as many letters from grandma as to her. Malobee sorts through a cache of correspondence on the day before her wedding, and it's this that sets off the reminiscences that make up the play.

It isn't all letters. There's an early funny scene in which we see Malobee's arrival in Toronto, dealing with airport administration on behalf of her family and going through the numerical intricacies of calling her grandmother via phone card. The old lady is now in a nursing home, and Malobee is much concerned -- it's a constant theme of the play -- that she's taking her medication properly. We hear grandmother herself dealing imperiously with her nurse. And, in the most obviously dramatic sequence, we flash back to her experiences as a semi-refugee in the chaos that overtook India at the end of the Second World War. We hear about the strains on her marriage, to a man who took off to work in Burma for years at a time. We gather that she is a survivor, and surmise that that's one of the things about her that her granddaughter loves.

Of the generation between them, we hear virtually nothing. And no real parallel emerges between the two people we do meet. The older woman's struggles in her birth-country dwarf those of the younger in her adopted one, though presumably we're meant to make some kind of connection between them. If Malobee (who I imagine is somewhat autobiographical) has undergone any great pressures in Canada, we don't hear about them. Her fiance is apparently British, but any tensions arriving from this are confined to the reactions of her grandmother, who carries bitter memories of the English but eventually admits that they may be all right in peacetime.

Pyaasa had more characters, more atmosphere and a bitterly ironic story. Letters to My Grandma is much slighter, more a two-headed lyric than a play. It does have an ear for detail, and Roy's acting, here as before, gets the best of two worlds: the formality of Eastern theatre and the naturalism of Western. She is very particular in delineating her roles, the grandmother's especially, while giving each a stark, stylized physical outline. She moves, and occasionally chants, with a grace that transcends prettiness. She executes thunderclap transformations from one role to the other, abetted by Thomas Morgan Jones' direction and, especially, David DeGrow's lighting. I don't know whether she's taken vows of theatrical solitude; personally, I'd like to see her follow Kristen Thomson's current example, and both act with and write for other performers. There's nothing aesthetically immoral about one-person shows, but a glut of them such as we are currently experiencing --mainly for economic reasons -- can leave an audience feeling starved. - Letters to my Grandma runs until March 8.
