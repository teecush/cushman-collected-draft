Spectator

Sorting out Broadway's traffic jam

Spectator

Saturday, May 31, 1997

I

RECENTLY spent a week in New York City collecting Broadway musicals: eight in six days. Seven of them had crammed their premieres into the same two-week period at the end of April.

This Gadarene rush was no coincidence. It was an attempt to rationalize an irrational, and very unhealthy, situation. Broadway -- musical Broadway, anyway -- has convinced itself of its dependence on the annual Tony awards. All the shows were opening as close as possible to the cutoff date for Tony nominations. The awards are presented tomorrow night. On Monday, if they abide by their own logic, the producers of these multimillion-dollar extravaganzas (not all of which look so extravagant) will decide whether to run with their prizes or cut their losses and take early retirement.

I have reported on five of the new openings: Titanic , The Life , Steel Pier , Jekyll and Hyde and the Livent revival of Candide . The remaining two, both small-scale, were devoted to settling old scores: That is, they served up old songs in new settings. The first, Play On ,inserted classic (and a few obscure) Duke Ellington numbers into an updated Twelfth Night ,set in a mythical Harlem. There was no good reason for this to work, but in a naive and casually entertaining way it did. Andrée de Shields, a monstrously relaxed eccentric hoofer, has received a Tony nomination as best supporting actor in a musical. He deserves to win; however, as the show, sadly, has already thrown in the towel, he probably won't.

Dream ,the other new oldie, still hangs on, but this parade of the songs of Johnny Mercer is one dumb show. Mercer was an unrivalled pop lyricist but not very theatrical, and the production misses whatever comic or dramatic clues the songs do provide. His more obscure material, for example, is dispatched by lining up the cast and having them chant the titles, phrases such as "Liquapep" and "He Didn't Have the Know-How, No How" I've often wondered what those songs were like and I'm still wondering. If there was ever a concept to Dream ,it died somewhere on the road.

So hand me the crystal ball. I haven't seen many of this year's Broadway plays, but I note with pleasure that the four contestants for best dramatic actor (Brian Bedford, Michael Gambon, Christopher Plummer, Antony Sher) are all graduates of the British and/or Canadian classical theatre. Up for best new musical are The Life , Steel Pier , Titanic and an oddball, Juan Darien ,billed as a "carnival mass," with lyrics in Latin, that came and went earlier in the season. It might just win (all right, in my dreams) as a rebuke to the more conventional shows, because although none of them have been embarrassing, none have been inspiring either. Sometimes -- often ,actually -- one show pulls away from the pack, and even if you don't much like it, even if it's Hello Dolly ,you still can see why. Whatever wins this year will do so by default.

The smart money is on The Life ,which is street-smart and has the best score (or at least the best collection of songs) and, in Lillias White's jaded hooker, an unbeatable entry for best supporting actress. (Debra Monk in Steel Pier is as good but doesn't get to light as many fireworks.) Of everything I saw, I think I had the best time at it. But I don't know, in retrospect, that it's better than Titanic ,which has the loftiest ambitions and, surprisingly often, comes close to realizing them.

However, it would have had to be a masterpiece to overcome its image problems. A musical about a sinking ship is -- how can I put this? -- a downer. It also sounds, as a stage subject, inherently ludicrous, and the awards that tend to reflect popular sentiment -- this is their importance, however distasteful -- probably will reflect this one as well. Steel Pier is dreary, but some people are rooting for it. And having a score by John Kander and Fred Ebb may net it a sympathy vote.

That's not as rude as it sounds. Twenty years ago, Kander and Ebb wrote the score for Chicago . It lost to A Chorus Line ,as did everything, but its revival has been the unconditional hit of this season. (It's had time to establish itself as such; it opened well ahead of the rush.) Much as I enjoyed Candide ,I have no doubt that Chicago will win best revival and every other Tony for which it's eligible: some that it throughly deserves (best actress -- Bebe Neuwirth is sensational) and some (best direction) that it possibly doesn't. Chicago has won all kinds of dubious plaudits: for being refreshingly minimalist (it's a slightly blown-up concert version), for having been ahead of its time (actually, A Chorus Line would win now as it did then) and for being prophetic (though celebrity murder-trials are nothing new in the United States and Chicago's much-vaunted parallels to the Simpson trial are virtually non-existent). The truth is that, with the death of creator Bob Fosse, the show shed its load of self-hating obsession. It is now less brilliant but more palatable: another fun musical about corruption.

It will look great on the awards show. That's another producer's prayer: Even if you don't win, you have a terrific shop window. A few years ago, Maury Yeston's Grand Hotel lost to Cy Coleman's City of Angels ,but Grand Hotel staged such a great number that everybody turned off his or her TV set thinking it had won. Both composers are represented this year -- Coleman with The Life ,Yeston with Titanic -- and I'm sure they have not forgotten. I wonder if they'll show anything from Jekyll and Hyde , directed by Robin Phillips.It was snubbed for best musical, but rules can be bent. Did I ever tell you about the duet that Robert Cuccioli, in the leading roles, sings with himself? As Jekyll, he stands up straight and sings at the ceiling; as Hyde, he crouches and sings at the floor; he just can't look himself in the eye. Don't laugh too hard; I wouldn't be surprised if Jekyll and Hyde were the only one of this year's new bunch still open when next year's awards come round. It's in a pop-opera style that people seem to like, and it has a title with which they connect. (So does Titanic ,but in the wrong way.) It's all about image.

It's a sad truth: from Kiss Me Kate to Rent , shows that take the prize don't really need it. Shows that really need it aren't saved by it, even if they get it.

The 51st annual Tony Awards, with talk-show star Rosie O'Donnell as host, are presented Sunday evening, with the televised program starting at 9 p.m. (ET) on CBS and selected Canadian channels.
