THEATRE REVIEW

Botticelli in the Fire /Sunday in Sodom

Up until last week I had never seen a play by Jordan Tannahill: this despite his being probably the hottest young playwright in Toronto. My fault, and also my misfortune: Tannahill, as Sandro Botticelli said of Leonardo da Vinci, is the real deal.

Well, all right, Botticelli probably didn't say that about Leonardo, at least not in those words. But he does say it in Botticelli in the Fire, the first in a double bill of plays by Tannahill, presented by Canadian Stage. Leonardo figures here as Botticellis apprentice, much admired by his master for his speed and dexterity in mixing paints, and also as his lover. Botticelli himself, riding high as Florences master painter of the late 15th century, is at work on his masterpiece The Birth of Venus.

In Tannahill's telling, the model for this painting was Clarice, wife of Lorenzo de Medici, Duke of Florence and source of all power and patronage. She's also Botticellis mistress, he being omnivorously omnisexual. This is a dangerous game for the painter to be playing, a point driven graphically home by a scene in which he and Lorenzo meet up for a round of squash; the ruler scores effortlessly while the artist gets winded.

This, you will have noticed, is a play that goes in wholeheartedly for anachronisms, verbal and physical. The whole-heartedness is the point, the style and the attraction. It isnt, as has been known to happen, just a matter of throwing in the odd self-conscious bit of modern slang. In one of the sharpest and most startling scenes, we find ourselves in a TV studio where Savonarola, the famous fanatical friar, is being interviewed, stating his case with all the apparent reasonableness of any contemporary puritan pundit offered a platform for his platform. Savonarola is out to purge Florence of its vices, which include painting and sex.

This of course makes Botticelli doubly if not triply vulnerable, and he agrees to throw his own works into the flames. Orthodox history holds that he had, at least temporarily, got religion. The play holds that he was acting out of a mixture of fear and cunning. He even comes up with the phrase Bonfire of the Vanities which Savonarola, after mulling it over, pronounces a good one. He does manage to salvage the Venus painting, by working it over in the manner of a Renaissance Jackson Pollock. How it got it back to its pristine state, for posterity's delectation, isn't explained, but doubtless Leonardo helped. Though on second thought, given the way the play develops, he probably didnt.

Botticelli acts, sparingly, as his own narrator, telling us his story in our terms, which means through a hand mike. He even gets to make the introductory cellphone announcement: an awkwardly ingratiating beginning that threatens the worst. But the play recovers, as does Salvatore Antonios performance, a masterful trip through bravado, panic and shame.

Christopher Morris gives a brilliant account of Lorenzo, switching in a second from boyishly bland to ducally dangerous, and keeping us in constant suspense as to which way he will jump. He's been cuckolded by the hired help, and their catand-mouse scenes together are the play's finest, for wit and suspense and general resonance. Theyre finer still for being shared with the Clarice of Nicola Correia-Damude, red-hot in passion and white-hot in contempt; these Medicis have an amusingly modern marriage. Stephen Jackman-Torkoff s Leonardo is also excellent, though his relationship with Botticelli, presented as crucial and consuming a lot of dialogue, is dramatically underdeveloped. It encompasses both betrayal and sacrifice, but they never seem more than words. Alon Nashman is unexpectedly mild as the firebrand Savanorola, though this could be another piece of mischievous revisionism.

Completing the cast, and doing it well, is Valerie Buhagiar as Botticell's mom, who recalls what he was like as a boy - i.e. very self-willed - and encourages him to keep on being it. Hers is the smallest role, but she's compensated in the evenings second and shorter play Sunday in Sodom, which she easily dominates despite being glued to one spot for the duration. This is because she has been turned into a pillar of salt; the Sodom Sunday thats being commemorated is the last and most famous day in that city's history, cheekily updated.

The play might easily have been called Lot's Wife or The Wifes Lot. Or, considering her fate and the plays use of it, The Saline Solution. She has to cope with everything, though at least she now has a name, Edith. She tells us that the day had been rough even before the apocalypse, what with Lot spending all his time watching the war news on TV and nephew Isaac phoning up, understandably traumatized by father Abraham's near-miss attempt to turn him into a ritual sacrifice.

When the angels arrive, they are American-sounding soldiers called Chris and Derek. While Edith stands and narrates, centre stage, the other characters, played by the same actors we met earlier, pop up around her and pop down again, as in a cartoon strip. Tannahill's writing remains lively but, shorn of its religious underpinnings and with nothing substantial replacing them, the story seems arbitrary, overloaded but unanchored. The Botticelli story has been creatively reimagined; this one, despite being set in a recognizably modern war zone with the Lot family as refugees, feels jokey rather than witty. One good joke, implicit in the juxtaposition of the two plays, is that there's no mention of sodomy in this Sodom. Savonarola might have given the place a pass.

Buhagiar's Edith starts her testimony, and wins our allegiance, by suggesting possible reasons, ignored by Scripture, for her fatal turnaround: Had she, for example, left the stove on? The real reason turns out to be equally human and considerably more poignant but, as with Sandro and Leonardo, stated rather than dramatized. Reservations aside, this is an impressive showcase of an evening, and not just for its author. Matjash Mrozweski directed the first play and Estelle Shook the second; both are graduates of the CanStage/York University training program and both have delivered fine productions, pictorially striking and emotionally acute.

Botticelli in the Fire/Sunday in Sodom runs through May 15 at the Berkeley Street Theatre in Toronto.
