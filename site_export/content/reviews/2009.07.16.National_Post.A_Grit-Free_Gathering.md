Perhaps the key line in Michel Tremblay's Albertine in Five Times is "I wish I'd never been like you." It's more than a figure of speech, being as how it's addressed by Albertine at age 50 to Albertine at age 40. These two share the stage with Albertines at 30, 60 and 70. Just to spell it out, we are beholding, simultaneously, the same Montreal woman at five stages of her life, from 1942 to 1982, the last date doing duty for the present since '82 is just two years before the play was first produced. Another way of putting it is that Albertine at 70, newly settled in a retirement home, is looking back at, and interrogating, her younger selves.

It's been a see-saw kind of existence. Roughly speaking, 50 and 70 have reached plateaux of acceptance and even happiness, while 30, 40 and 60 are locked into various flavours of bitterness and rage. The causes include lifelong poverty, a smothering mother, a husband who quickly absented himself and a mentally handicapped son and a daughter, Therese, who made an even less fortunate marriage choice than her mother and went downhill from there. Albertine calls her a whore, and seems to have been doing so ever since the girl was 11 and allowed herself to be approached by a grown man -- the very man, it turns out, whom she married 10 years later. Albertine (at 30) had been so enraged by her daughter's earlier behaviour with this man that she beat her, uncontrollably; the memory of this has left her consumed with guilt, reinforced by Therese's early, sordid and probably violent death.

When not blaming Therese, Albertine (at least in most of her incarnations) blames men, of whom she seems to have encountered a small and possibly unrepresentative sample. That at least is the opinion of her sister Madeleine, the play's one remaining character, who keeps talking about her own happy marriage. Albertine resents this, possibly because Madeleine's husband was a former beau of her own. That isn't mentioned in this play, but it was revealed in the later-written prequel Past Perfect, which presents Albertine at 20 as a raging and unrepentant narcissist. There's rather more to be said for the five-fold Albertine we meet here, including occasional signs of a sense of humour, but not as much as the play itself seems to think. It may have Canadianclassic status, but it's built on an unsustainable gimmick.

It pretends that we are totally distinct people at different stages of our lives. In fact, other than physically, most people don't change; that's usually their problem. Marking the distinctions off in decades makes them seem especially arbitrary. Even if we grant that the idea is intended as a conceit, a way of juxtaposing different chapters of a person's biography, it still presents a huge casting problem, one that the Shaw Festival's revival hasn't solved. We have to believe that the five actresses are playing aspects of the same person. I didn't. I couldn't see Patricia Hamilton, who plays the oldest Albertine, as the sum of the other four parts.

The lady that Hamilton presents is too much the serene gentlewoman to have demons, or even to have come from the same background as the others. It's especially hard to see how she got to where she is from where Albertine at 60 was; her we meet hunched over her resentments and sustained by tranquilizers, but despite being handed the surefire appeal of misanthropy, Wendy Thatcher's performance obstinately refuses to come into focus.

As the two youngest points of the pentagon, Marla McLean and Jenny L. Wright are mostly planted upstage and fade accordingly into the background; McLean, too, seems surprisingly un-leathery for someone who's seen the troubles she knows. Mary Haney commands the most attention as the middle Albertine, who has found a precarious independence working in a restaurant, whose patrons come for miles to sample her tomato sandwiches; her ode to her own job satisfaction is as mouth-watering as the sandwiches but it still comes over as a set-piece.

Not a monologue, not a conventional drama, the play combines the disadvantages of both forms; we never get right inside Albertine's head, or right outside it either. The play suffers from a sense of entitlement, not so much the character's as the author's. Tremblay had written about Albertine before and would again, and there is a sense that we had better care about her because she's a talismanic cultural figure. It's reinforced in this production, which offers little evidence of the Tremblay grit we keep hearing about; any demotic power has vanished in Linda Gaboriau's translation. Its literary air especially afflicts Nicola Correia-Damude, who, as Madeleine must have one of the least rewarding roles ever written; it's trying enough playing confidante to one heroine, but Correia-Damude has five bending her ear while complaining of her eternal sweetness. The production adds its own layers of gentility with Teresa Przybylski's wispy sets and especially with Marc Desormeaux's enervating and omnipresent incidental music that casts a sentimental glaze over anything. Though in a play that ends with all five Albertines reaching for, or at least towards, the moon (because "she's alone too") sentimentality must be hard to avoid. - Albertine in Five Times is in repertory through Oct. 10.
