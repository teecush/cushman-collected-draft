THE VIC

Theatre Passe Muraille

This is a listless production, crammed with earnest but unfocused performances, of a first play that isn't ready. It neatly sums up a lot of what's wrong with the Toronto theatre. (The not-for-profit Toronto theatre, anyway; the commercial sector has other problems.)

The Vic, we are informed rather late, is TV cop-drama shorthand for victim; and Leanna Brodie's play is a study in victimhood, in how it gets invited and might be avoided. Set nominally in Winnipeg and scenically in limbo, it deploys its characters, all of whom are female, in four separate groups.

In one, a recent and severely damaged refugee from a cult is tended by her sister, an overseas aid worker currently in a state of burnout, and their well-meaning but baffled middle-class mother. In another, a PhD student, deeply depressed by the realization that her thesis is neither wonderful nor terrible but merely "adequate," enrages and is enraged by her girlfriend, an uneducated recovering alcoholic with a violent streak. In a third, a young, gifted and black filmmaker, who doesn't want to spend her career either trading on or apologizing for her race, signs on as assistant to an idolized and apparently world-famous director, and is sadly disillusioned. The fourth group consists of one person: an adolescent girl who keeps a diary on videotape.

There are vestigial links between some individual members of these set-ups, but essentially we are being told four separate stories in rotation, and sorting them out proves a hard task for everyone involved, not least the audience.

There is an intermittently recurrent framing device: Four of the characters are searching for a fifth, who is missing and feared dead. That the person they're looking for proves not to be the one you -- or at least I -- thought she was is either a cunningly devised red herring or just one more confusion: I'm afraid I vote for the latter. When we discover the truth of the situation, it's obviously meant to throw all we have previously learned about the characters into sharp and illuminating relief. But it doesn't, because in fact we haven't learned much about them; the author's chosen structure hasn't given her much time to tell us.

All of the characters are there to illustrate a theme: A couple of them are aggressors, most are at least potential victims, some are both. Some resist their fate, some collude in it, a few seem capable of escaping it altogether. But this is just theory. In practice, they are simply rushed onstage and told to confront one another. There is little feeling for any of the niceties, the half- tones, the hesitations that actually make up human behaviour. At one point a mother, who is anything but callous and who knows her daughter is in a fragile state, walks on and tells her one of her former associates has been found murdered, as unceremoniously as if she were reading the ten o'clock news. The actors are virtually compelled to go for the jugular, but with no solid ground from which to launch their attacks. So they come off awkward.

Waneta Storms, a gifted actress who has been turning up this year in consistently rocky circumstances, is the only one here to occupy her space onstage with any sense of either strength or repose; though, as the cultist living largely in a world of her own, she may have an advantage over the others. (She has a solo scene that seems to have come from some other play, but it is still the highlight of the evening.) Marjorie Chan as the solitary diarist is given the tenderest writing, and Moynan King as the reformed drunk the fiercest; and they both have their moments. Ann Holloway's turn as the swaggering sacred monster of the movies is embarrassingly overdone. But then she is isn't given any context.

That is partly the fault of the writing, but some blame must go to David Oiye, whose direction is atrocious. Steve Lucas has designed an all-purpose steps-and-ramp setting, with a withered tree- branch overhanging, that seems to have been left over from a run- down production of a Shakespearean history. Meant to accommodate everything, it suits nothing. Trying to treat the various levels as if they were hotel beds or domestic furnishings or even part of a credible outdoors, the actors get nowhere; and neither the lighting nor the staging contributes any atmosphere.

It's no accident that this year's best new plays -- Hysteria, The Weir, Elizabeth Rex -- have all been written in sustained scenes; I can't be the only person to be sick of watching plays with episodic television construction limping across inhospitable stages. (People sometimes claim the Elizabethans wrote this way. They didn't. And neither did Brecht, whatever else may be held against him.) But if a play is going to use this method, then a director has to find some kind of rhythm, and some way of capping the scenes. It isn't enough to dim the lights on one part of the stage, bring them up on another, and leave the performers to find their own way home in the gaps. (Are they meant to be in or out of character at these points? They look sheepishly undecided.) It may also make them bashful about starting again; the lack of flow and definition within the scenes reflects what goes on between them.

Anyone can make a mistake, but this play, according to the program, has been in preparation for three years and in workshop for nearly two. Surely, during that time, somebody might have noticed that its method was unworkable. Or has the workshopping of a play become an end in itself? On a related note: Could we put an end to the sickly, patronizing and increasingly common practice of letting the actors applaud the audience at the curtain. It usually looks desperate; and it's always presumptuous.

Clapping is our job. If we feel like it.
