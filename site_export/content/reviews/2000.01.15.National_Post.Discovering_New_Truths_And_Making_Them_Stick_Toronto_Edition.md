THE YOKO ONO PROJECT

Theatre Passe Muraille

THEATRE

Against all augury, The Yoko Ono Project turns out to be a terrific show. Few people can have looked forward with enthusiasm to a celebration of one of the most reviled figures of recent times, a woman widely believed to have taken the worlds most overrated songwriter and made him worse. (Well, it did coincide with their marriage.)

Nobody liked her own music much either, or her art, or her theories. She was generally thought to be posturing and untalented. Her greatest sin, though, was to be Asian; any Western woman who became the second Mrs Lennon would have been granted a far easier ride. That at least is the position taken by the creators of this entertainment -- a troupe with the engaging name of Loud Mouth Asian Babes -- and they make it stick. They proceed on the ancient principle that the best form of defence is attack, but rather than charging head-on they bob and weave. If their show were not so witty, poignant, deft and agile, you would say that it was uncomfortably persuasive. As it is, it just persuades.

For a start, it looks great. The set (designed by Vikki Anderson) features a dazzling black-and-white chequered floor, on which stand panels of frosted screens. Dotted around are a large shiny black box, a short white monolith and a couple of black pillars. (These last aren't strictly part of this show -- they hold the theatre's roof up. But they blend in very well.) These objects are mostly Ono artworks, or reproductions thereof, and they make a very good case for their creator. Regardless of how they might look in a gallery, they are fine occupants of a stage.

There is a cast of six and all of them -- the four women anyway - - are in a sense playing Yoko. The one doing it most directly, though, is a musician, Lee Pui Ming, and our first sight of her seems to confirm our worst fears. She rushes through the auditorium, arms outstretched and flashing an arent-you-glad-to-see-me smile. After that, though, she retires to a gallery to do her own thing, and the actresses take over. There is, surprisingly, a story. It concerns three Asian-Canadian girls, the brother of one of them, and the European boy-friend of another. We see them in and around an exhibition: the Yoko Ono Project. This overlaps with the Ono exhibit at which she first met John Lennon: an encounter thats re-enacted for us, with the actor, Bruce Beaton, doing a first-rate Liverpudlian.

Two issues are on these characters' minds: one amusing, one not. One is the supposed preference of Asian women for going out with European men: a propensity of which Yoko herself of course provided a spectacular example, and a tendency that drives the brother (Glenn Marc Silot) crazy. Mischievously, the ladies conduct a poll of the Asian women in the audience. (Results on the first night seemed to confirm the theory.)

The other matter is the abuse hurled at Asian women by men -- especially North American men -- who have decided they hate Ono and who can't tell the others apart. The anecdotal evidence here is apparently drawn from the personal experience of Jean Yoon, who wrote the show and who has staged it (brilliantly) with Marion de Vries. The performers, though, easily make the words their own. One of them, Keira Loughran, is going to be a star; she has an enchantingly light touch and a fast, warm rapport with her colleagues and with the audience. (When her lover asks her to move in with him because "youre small and you wont take up much room" she details all the physical and psychological space that she will occupy, concluding I'm small, but I'm not microscopic. Its not the deepest writing but it tells.) Balancing her sharp joyousness is the wary, heavy-set sadness of Denise Fujiwara (a dancer making her acting debut) as a woman who has suffered more than her share of insult and has hardened because of it. At one of the shows climaxes she retreats into Ono's magic box and emerges strengthened if not transformed. M. J. Kang has less definition than the other two but is unexpectedly moving towards the end.

By dramatising their own experience -- and doing it blithefully and forcefully without self-pity -- they gain our respect for their unseen patron. "Don't diss Yoko" they say at the end, with awkward (and unncessary) explictiness, but their point is made. It's plain that she's been vilified over the years in ways that were not merely offensive but irrelevant. That does not in itself make her a genius, even if the show would like to think so, but in fact the issue is sidestepped. They make a better case for her visual creations than for her music: a lot of it is played but mostly while other more interesting things are going on. Some prejudices remain intact. The production itself, though, has a future.
