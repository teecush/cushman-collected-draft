THEATRE REVIEW

Fela! Canon Theatre, Toronto

Here is a production with a large cast but only one real character; at its heart, in fact, a one-man show. It's an audience-pleasing, even audiencebaiting, piece of work that can also land hard political punches. It's been a success on Broadway and at Britain's National Theatre, in that order, which is the reverse of the usual one.

It's a vibrant show - visually, choreographically and especially musically - that can lurch into quite lengthy periods of boredom. It has a story to tell, which it seems passionate about but of which it also sometimes loses track. Fela!, in fact, is one paradoxical entertainment.

Fela is Fela Kuti, the Nigerian musician and activist, and the show is his biography. Or maybe that should be autobiography. We are supposedly in Kuti's Lagos club The Shrine, on what we are told is its last night. He's about to leave the country, following a murderous raid by the government. (At least I think that's the sequence of events. It can be confusing.) Kuti is played by Sahr Ngaujah, who is extraordinary; he sings, dances, delivers long sharp-edged stand-up comedy routines with perfect timing and seamless command, and plays the saxophone; it's conceivable that he's only miming the last accomplishment, but I don't want to think so.

He tells us the story of his life, growing up in a middleclass family with an inspiring and politically fervent mother. Already immersed in music, he goes to study in London in the late 1950s; the British jazz and blues names he drops at this point (Tubby Hayes, Joe Harriott, Cyril Davies) reveals it is the rare bio-musical that has bothered to get its facts right; there's also a five-second clip of Frank Sinatra, from whom Kuti seems to have de-rived some of his cool, that's a more effective tribute than the whole of Fly Away with Me.

He begins to develop his own style, which he describes as a mixture of jazz and highlife; it's an intoxicating sound that gets the evening off to a great start. There's a lengthy onstage overture, before the lights even go down and the actors assemble, with captivating work from trumpet and, as I guess you'd expect, drums. It's a tribute to the production, and especially to Ngaujah, that they can keep that momentum going when the play proper begins.

Returning to Africa, Kuti gets both funkier (both enraged and inspired by a local James Brown imitator) and more traditional, forging the style that becomes Afrobeat. It lends itself to the jokes and rants that become his spe-

cialty and that sound increasingly like rap, only musical. He also visits the U.S. where he meets Sandra, his second wife. They do some reciprocal consciousness-raising; she teaches him about Black Power, he teaches her about the abuse of it, in a country that's exchanged the shackles of colonialism for those of corrupt generals and international oil. Raging against these entities is to consume much of the second half of his life and of the show.

Sandra appears as a character, with a couple of scenes, rudimentary ones. This is a breach of the show's prevailing convention, and not a very happy one. The script, written by Jim Lewis and Bill T. Jones, mostly lets Fela tell his own tale, with sung and danced support from a 20-strong ensemble. Jones, whose choreography was the best thing about Spring Awakening, scores again here with hard-and-fast dances that fuse traditional African with very contemporary American.

As author-director, though, he lets some of the narrative slip through his fingers. We experience the music (nearly all the score is authentic), we trust the show's evocation of Kuti as a performer, but we only hear in general terms what he was for and against. We do discover that he later took 12 wives at once, something of which the show wants us both to disapprove and to be amused by, but we never discover what Sandra thought about this or even if she were still around. We note that Kuti does tend to bully people, audience included, though he claims it's for their own good.

There are some powerful evocations of police brutality; one comic-grotesque, another overwhelmingly brusque and factual, when there's an allout raid on Kuti's compound. (He seems almost to have set up a state within the state.) This includes the murder of his mother, thrown from a second-storey window. Up to now she's been a figure more talked about than seen, unless you count her everpresent picture hanging upstage left.

Now Kuti undertakes an extremely protracted journey into the spirit world, to seek her counsel; should he stay or should he go? She tells him in what, despite Melanie Marshall's searing singing of it, is unmistakably a Broadwayinspirational number, the show's only song to be written by someone other than the protagonist.

The protagonist's later life is only relayed in surtitles at the curtain call; and even they don't mention the possibility that his death was AIDSrelated. Still, this is a show far out of the norm, in music, setting (Marina Draghici's design features a catwalk and presumably traditional abstract icons), real feelings, and a genuine star performance.
