THUNDERSTICK

Theatre Passe Muraille

- - -

I was fairly thunderstruck by Thunderstick. All its constituent parts work together: The writing rambles, the acting totally lacks conviction, and the direction is limp. For those who prize consistency in a work of art, it's something of a jackpot.

The title is apparently old Hollywoods idea of the American Indian name for a rifle. I owe this information to the author, Ken Williams, who mentions it in his program note but not, I think, in his play. (Its possible he did and I missed it. The proceedings were numb and left me number.)

Anyway, he gives us two contemporary Native Canadians whose weapons are the pen, or whatever you write with these days, and the camera. In other words they are journalists, working out of Ottawa, and they have been assigned by their masters to cover some complicated political shenanigans. They are also cousins who have not set eyes on one another for some time and are not too crazy about the prospect now.

The reporter is called Jacob, and the photographer, returned to Canada from an extended tour of the world's worst places, is called Isaac. We begin in Jacob's bathroom, watching him in the toils of a major hangover. (I cant help feeling that this reinforces, rather than mocks, a stereotype.) Eventually he throws up.

There is a toilet bowl on stage throughout the first act; sometimes it has a visible cistern, which means were at Jacob's place, and sometimes it doesn't, which means were in jail. Jacob and Isaac have been sent there after Jacob vomited again, this time over the Prime Ministers shoes. (The PM was giving a press conference at the time.)

Eventually they are turned loose, and set off in pursuit of their story. So, helplessly, do we.

Unfortunately, though the characters seem to have some idea what they're looking for, we don't. I said the intrigues were complicated; actually, theyre impenetrable. Theres something about a murder, and something else about a missing lesbian minister, and terrorists come into it, and so -- near the end, in time-honoured fashion -- do the Mounties.

It's possible the author doesn't care very much whether we understand or not, but I think this is rash of him. One of the theatres few natural laws is that an audience, given the merest whiff of a narrative, will do their level best to follow it; when they are unable to, they get frustrated and start to howl like beasts in the wilderness.

The second act is, in fact, set in the wilderness, or at least in Muskoka. The stage is now bare of sanitation, but it does have our two heroes car, which has broken down. This forces them to talk to one another -- about their childhoods and about their respective marital pasts, Jacob's being checkered and Isaac's tragic.

Jacob momentarily wonders how his ancestors would feel if they could see him now, lost in the open and scared of wolves -- a wry line that unexpectedly made me laugh. (It was the high point of the evening.) For a moment, there is the suggestion of a mood, but the actors prove unable to sustain it.

Here begins the tricky business of apportioning guilt. Of course, one can never really know; the author always gets the lions share of the blame, and its true that the storytelling here is confused, the comedy clumsy, the characterization less than rudimentary, and the theme indecipherable.

But though the actors may have been demoralized by the material, they seem, in turn, to have dragged it lower than it needs to go; these things tend to spiral. Ian Ferguson sometimes manages a coarse, blubbery energy as Jacob; Tim Hill as Isaac is simply lame.

The production looks as if the director, Paul Thompson, threw his hands amiably in the air and left text and performers to their own devices. The action happens on a pair of adjacent platforms, divided by a wriggly fault line. Sometimes the staging exploits this gap; sometimes it doesn't.

At the height of the drama, the players mimic a flight through the woods by running around the stage in a series of zig-zags; it looks like panic-by-numbers, and is unrelated to anything else in the show.

Somehow they get their scoop, whatever it is, and are rewarded. Jacob is given a job at the National Post (assistant features editor, in fact); Isaac turns one down. I'm not sure which of these is meant to constitute a happy ending.
