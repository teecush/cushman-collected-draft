Most of Another Home Invasion consists of Nicola Lipman sitting in a chair and addressing the audience. That's a lot more compelling than you might think.

The play was written by Joan Macleod, who seems to do her best work in monologues. She wrote The Shape of a Girl, a fine portrait of a teenager that was partly a response to the murder of Reena Virk. This play, which may be even better, takes us to the opposite demographic extreme. Lipman plays Jean, a woman of about 80 who is now the caregiver for her husband, Alec, who recently suffered a stroke. They live in the same house in their beloved North Vancouver that they've occupied for upwards of 50 years, but they are now awaiting relocation to another kind of home. The place they have set their hearts on is the nearby Kiwanis facility, which has one floor that takes couples. What fuels the play, giving it both passion and suspense, is Jean's and the audience's growing fear that this will not be allowed to happen.

Author and actress may do even better at summoning up unseen characters than Kristen Thomson in I Claudia. Actually, this play has a Claudia of its own, a lady from social services who monitors the couple's state of co-existence and (unequal) co-dependence. Claudia is fussy and bossy, but she has her uses; she persuades Jean to enrol in Aquafit classes, something that Jean at first resists and then embraces as the highlight of her existence. She won't hear of missing one, sometimes to the chagrin of her daughter, Bethie, who has to come over and hold the fort at home in her absence, and who operates according to a whole different body clock. (Jean's idea of a civilized dinnertime is something like 5:30 p. m.; Bethie likes to treat dinner as more of a midnight snack). Alec is vividly depicted as a man who once had the fortitude to conquer alcoholism, but is now in a condition that he cannot overcome. Jean has sons living in Toronto and Calgary, but her accessible family is completed by Bethie's teenage daughter, Amber, who comes over to do the vacuuming; for this Amber gets minimum wage and does, as Jean tartly remarks, minimum work. She apparently concentrates on upstairs, an area which, as Jean also tells us, nobody uses anymore.

One day, a man arrives on the doorstep, makes aimless conversation and, at one point, grabs Jean's arm when she seems in danger of falling. He seems, to Jean anyway, to be harmless if mysterious, but he's a crystal-meth addict and his visit has repercussions. He seems to be somewhat shoehorned into the play, but has the effect of speeding up what already seems an inexorable slide toward disaster. Alec's deteriorating condition is already putting the couple's planned future in jeopardy; now, an unexpected rally of strength hastens the process. Jean's home, and her hopes, are being invaded from all sides: by the system, by an intruder, by mortality. She rages, but she doesn't whine.

The play's great quality is its mixture of strength and gentleness. Jean describes her daily rounds, and her larger issues, economically, unsparingly and with an overridingly tart humour. These qualities are brilliantly reflected in Lipman's performance: mainly sedentary, like the life it's depicting, and so taking on extraordinary force on the occasions when she forces herself out of her chair. She can have a mean mouth: almost literally, given the firm, sardonic set of this actress's jaw. She only relaxes it at the end of the play, and the effect is like a burst of sunlight. On the most immediate level, the play's ending is a grim act of acceptance. But there is something about this character and this performance that dismisses defeat. Her horizons are limited, her battles are all personal, but she can still reflect on the deafening noise that might be made if all the women in her situation were to raise their voices in unison. This is as close as the play gets to the explicit raising of issues, and it justifies itself on every level. Richard Rose's direction coaxes the writing and the performance sympathetically along. For staging, he gives us the solitary figure surrounded by presumably symbolic autumn leaves.

The song Autumn Leaves actually plays on the soundtrack that precedes and follows the performance, along with such other relevant tunes as I'll See You in My Dreams and, most poignantly jaunty at the end, We Just Couldn't Say Goodbye. Maybe these were just chosen at random as old music to suit old people. But that would be quite a coincidence. - Another Home Invasion runs through April 19 at the Tarragon Theatre.
