Idon't know why One Wooden Shoe, a theatre company new to me, is called One Wooden Shoe, but its members seem to me a nice bunch of people with, collectively, an inquiring mind and a talent to amuse. That, anyway, is the impression left by their show Dedicated to the Revolutions, whose title is likewise something of a curveball. The revolutions it's dedicated to, six of them, arent of the barricaded variety. They are, in one way or another, scientific. The performers list them for us, vocally and by writing them on a chart: the Gutenberg, the Copernican, the Newtonian, the Industrial, the Darwinian, the Nuclear and the Information.

The Newtonian gets star billing. When we enter the theatre, five of the cast are playing a game involving the piling of toy bricks one on top of another until the tabletop tower theyve erected topples over, enabling its constituent parts to demonstrate their obedience to the law of gravity. The players stand around the table taking or awaiting their turn and behaving with the kind of studied casualness that people, especially when they're professional performers, always exhibit when trying to act as if they don't know people are looking at them. Now seems as good a time as any to introduce them. They are Frank Cox OConnell, Chad Dembski, Aimee Dawn Robinson, Erin Shields and Jacob Zimmer, who nicely rounds off the alphabetical list by being the prime mover of both the company and the show. He looks and behaves like an amiable don. OConnell, who has the most active stage presence, is more your livewire high school teacher. Robinson, who has a different but equal kind of authority, emerges as a compelling lecturer and natural mistress of ceremonies. Shields, also known as an accomplished playwright (her If We Were Birds was the hit of last year's Summerworks and is scheduled for next year at Tarragon) comes on here like an untidy but gifted student. Dembski is an imp.

Evan Webber, the young actor left over, is at the side of the stage performing his own experiment. He's testing the efficacy of Mr. Gutenbergs invention by writing out the Bible by hand while a printer (in the cause of a level playing field theyve chosen a model created in the same year that Webber himself was) does its own thing with the same material. They both plunge synchronously into Genesis, the mechanical scribe getting as far as the story of Lot (spicy stuff for a machine) while the human one has barely progressed beyond "in the beginning." In fairness, he has taken time off to include a drawing, though it doesn't seem to compare with the figures in a monk's illuminated manu-script. Game, set and match to Gutenberg, Id say. And Newton hasnt done too badly either.

Dedicated to the Revolutions is the kind of show after which people put the fear of God into me by saying, I can't wait to see what you're going to say about this one, to which I generally reply, "Neither can I." That doesnt mean its a bad show, rather that it isn't amenable to the orthodox rules. It certainly isn't a play, or even a revue; its more of a lecture-investigation with theatrical and musical illustrations. The performers dont have to act (which doesn't mean they can't) but they do have to command our attention. This they accomplish; theyre actually rather heavy on the charm. (They admit, freely and repeatedly, that theyre no more scientists than we are, though they've probably done more research more recently.) In terms of theatrical invention the production reaches its peak early in a sequence on distribution networks, which involves the depiction of different methods of interpersonal communication -- one to one, one to two, one to many -- through an assortment of tin cans and cables. They're at their foggiest when they get to Darwin, and try to distinguish between evolution and natural selection. The end of the world is also prophesied, though its comfortingly consigned to billions of generations in the future: How many billions is illustrated by having Shields engaged, like T. S. Eliots Macavity, in "complicated long-division sums."

At two points in the evening, the audience is allowed to participate -- though we to choose from a list of pre-selected questions to which the cast presumably have pre-rehearsed answers. (This doesn't stop them from going into huddles before replying.) Everyone's favourite, perhaps because it looks so funny just on paper, is: Be-fore Newton, how did things fall? The answer of course is, exactly the same way as they have since, but elucidating this point involves the actor concerned in a suspicious number of "you know"s -- about three a sentence, I calculated, though unlike Shields I didnt have a blackboard to help me.

Ive hinted that, despite its title, the show isn't political in any obvious sense. But protest, or at least mordant doubt, does slip in. There's a reference to the transformative effects of the Industrial Revolution on the owners and the children who worked for them. Mutually Assured Destruction (theres a nostalgic phrase) gets compared, under the Information heading, to "the belief that someone will rip you off" -- which, were told, most people actually don't. (Don't believe it, I mean, not dont rip you off, but I guess it works both ways.) At the end the group both expresses its doubts about, and pays tribute to Progress with an unexpectedly haunting tune whose refrain is "nothing ever wants to change but it does." Its both a celebration and a challenge. The show is casual to a fault, sometimes beyond but, as Galileo more or less said, I guess it works.
