NIGHTSONGS FACE TO THE WALL

DuMaurier Theatre Centre

- - -

World Stage Preview is a short international season designed as a taster for the full-blown festival planned for next year. It has kicked off, imposingly, with a double bill from the Royal Court Theatre in London, both its components directed by Katie Mitchell, who was responsible for the memorable production of The Oresteia that came here two years ago.

Mitchell has always been known as a stylistic puritan, and with this program she has professedly gone into understater's overdrive. She has turned her back on such decadent frivolities as designed scenery and computerized lighting and sound effects. Her stage is furnished only by found objects. Question: Were they brought over from Britain or did the rules dictate that they be found anew in Canada?

Nightsongs, the first of the evenings plays and much the longest (90 minutes to its companions 18), takes place in a flat occupied by a young couple and their baby: The production scavengers have furnished it with a sofa and a pram, but not much else.

An interview inserted in the program associates all this economy with recent developments in the Danish cinema and the English novel. (On the first of these, I pass. On the second, all I can say is that my own impression of the current English novel, formed by such exuberantly panoramic writers as Jonathan Coe and Zadie Smith, is obviously out-of-date, but I like it that way.) In fact, the production style looks to me like Royal Court minimalism of a kind that has been in place for at least three decades. On its home ground, I used to regard it with grudging respect. Meeting it here I find the grudge lifting and the respect increased.

Nightsongs is by an apparently highly regarded Norwegian playwright, Jon Fosse. This may seem to reinforce the Scandinavian connection, but in the text as we hear it, nothing, nowhere and nobody seems specifically Norwegian; it sounds, in Gregory Motton's spare and demotic translation, as if it's going on in London.

It depicts a relationship that is miserable. The man hasnt left the house in ages. He spends his time lying on the sofa reading; he also writes manuscripts that are invariably rejected. His parents pay an embarrassed visit; the first, apparently, since the baby was born. The woman, craving release, goes out for the evening -- with a girlfriend, she says, though her partner seems disinclined to believe her. She does, indeed, eventually reappear with another man, named Mike (hes the only character who has a name, at least one that we ever learn). She is moving out to live with him.

Not all of the production's scenic elements are as ruthlessly realistic as the propaganda would have us believe. A rope-ladder contraption suggests, but does not resemble, a window. There are clock faces built into either side of the stage, and a large wristwatch hovering in space over the middle of it, like an unsmiling Cheshire Cat. These timepieces are reinforced by the recurring sound of a speaking clock, as heard on the British telephone system. All this is to emphasize that the scenes we witness, though not the intervals between them, are being played out in real time.

The principal duo nag, complain, feel sorry for themselves but never actually quarrel, largely because the man refuses to put up a fight. It's maddening, but it is not boring. The relationship is very real, especially in the way it sags without breaking. The mans reiterated assertion, whined rather than stated, that he thought the two of them were good together, has its own truth; certainly one can see how it would be true, and necessary, for him.

Maybe it is this fated interdependence that has caused Fosse to be described, or so we are told, as a 21st-century Beckett. I can see no other resemblance, though the actors accents occasionally make him sound like Pinter, while the grandparents' desultory attempts at polite conversation seem initially like early Ionesco: incongruous attempts to ridicule the banality of everyday speech. It turns out, though, there's a perfectly sober reason for them to talk as they do, and that its connected to the withdrawn behaviour of their son.

Not that his mate proves a paragon of decisiveness either. When it comes time to make a choice, she is racked by doubts. "I dont want things to happen" she says, and it seems to be a comment on the play.

Then we find out that things do happen, shockingly. A play about stasis turns out to be a declaration of its opposite; and very dramatic, considering. Paul Higgins as the man makes a surprising positive out of a collection of negatives; Sophie Okonedo is trenchant in a low-key way, maybe too low-key, even for a small space.

The afterpiece, Face to the Wall, is by Martin Crimp, who is prominent among younger English playwrights (and also among younger English translators). Here he employs a device that I suppose is daring but, in fact, turns out to be theatrically suicidal. A middle- aged man, fleshy and florid, appears to be pitching a movie to a couple of young Americans: a story about a killer mowing down four children. In full, eager spate he suddenly stops and asks for a prompt; this is supplied, he goes vigorously on, and then dries again, and again. We, of course, are uncertain whether the actor is really forgetting his lines, or whether this is a device. The longer it goes on, the likelier it seems to be the latter, especially since Peter Wight's performance is, between pauses, strikingly forceful and assured.

But it's never quite certain, and anyway the damage has been done; were too horrified by the spectacle (chronic actors amnesia has the fascination of a car-wreck) to have any shock left over for what he's saying. He starts to verbally brutalize the prompter, and I suppose this is meant to key in to the brutality he's narrating and, apparently, enjoying. He then sings a blues song about a murderous postman.

Real-life child massacres haunt us, and are obviously meant to; but in that context the tricksy little play seems a) tasteless and b) pointless: not a satire on media exploitation but the thing itself. In this instance, Katie Mitchell's direction seems minimal rather than minimalist -- less an artistic choice than a helpless bow to the inevitable.
