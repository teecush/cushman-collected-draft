HEDDA GABLER

Buddies in Bad Times

12 Alexander St. - 416-975-8555

In Judith Thompson's adaptation, directed by Ross Manson, Ibsen's 1890 play has its first half advanced to the beginning of the 20th century and its second to now. In the world of Kinko's, we are asked to believe that Hedda is destroying the only copy of Tesman's manuscript. The play's famous last line is cut in favour of a new dying aria for a bleeding heroine. Yanna McIntosh's Hedda (Dora- nominated) mainly projects a majestic resentment, monotonous and rather forced. Closes tomorrow.

JOURNEY'S END

Court House Theatre - Niagara-on-the-Lake - 1-800-511-7429

A jolly good show. R.C. Sherriff's classic play about the trenches of the First World War focuses almost entirely on the English officer class, but transcends those cozy limitations. It shows how desperately these men, mostly very young, need the memory of home comforts and home beliefs, when what lies ahead is the near- certainty of injury or death. Christopher Newton's production, relaxed and amused in its first half, gets very tense in its second. In repertory through Oct. 8.

HAPPY END

Royal George Theatre - Niagara-on-the-Lake - 1-800-511-7429

Tadeusz Bradecki's revived production of this Brecht-Weill musical, set in Brecht's favourite mythical Chicago, is given a stylish visual bite, with every move precisely plotted. The songs are some of Weill's best but the book, drawn-out and laborious, is almost contemptuously slight. Cast changes are up and down, with Glynis Ranney's Hallelujah Lil (she sings the great Surabaya Johnny) one of the ups. In repertory through Oct. 28.

THE TEMPEST

Festival Theatre - Stratford

1-800-567-1600

Verse acting at its highest stage of development. At 85, and playing Prospero for the fourth time, William Hutt can still surprise us. From anger to gentleness and back again, reversals -- constantly repeated and immaculately executed -- underpin Hutt's whole account of a man who takes a play's length to determine whether the rarer action is in virtue or in vengeance. The pause before he bows to the inevitable is going to be legendary. In repertory through Oct. 28.

HELLO, DOLLY!

Festival Theatre - Stratford

1-800-567-1600

Lucy Peacock doesn't find herself in Dolly until she reaches her big first-act closer, Before the Parade Passes By. At last the actress has something to act. Most of Dolly, though, is shtick, for which she isn't equipped. The show itself is an honest and successful attempt to make audiences feel happy; the production, though prosaic, has humour, colour and good dancing. Peter Donaldson's skinflint widower is the best and wittiest performance. In repertory through Nov. 6.
