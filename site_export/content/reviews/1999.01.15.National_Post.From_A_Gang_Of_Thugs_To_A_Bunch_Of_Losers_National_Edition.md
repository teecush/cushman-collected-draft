STEEL KISS/GULAG

Buddies in Bad Times Theatre, Toronto

Robin Fulford wrote Steel Kiss 12 years ago as a response to the murder by four drunk youths of a gay man in a Toronto park. The play depicts a similar event, and focuses on the perpetrators rather than the victim. Last year's crucifixion of Matthew Shepard in Wyoming gave the play a renewed topicality, which its author would probably have been happy to forgo.

Buddies in Bad Times has now revived it, in repertory with Gulag, a sequel written nine years later that follows the killers into prison and out on to parole. The advance word on Steel Kiss was about the authors courage in presenting four homophobic homicides as human beings rather than monsters.

I wonder how else you could write a play about them? Delivering on good intentions is, of course, another thing. Steel Kiss brings its characters into the light but doesnt bring them to life.

It uses a stage full of devices. The actors playing the boys also perform all the other roles. They appear as family members, yackety- yacking their kids from behind a set of freak masks. They are preachers, filling the air with righteous prejudice (the boys don't look much like church-goers but we get the point), and they are high- school teachers, boring their contemptuous charges to death.

They also play the man who will be killed and his new-found lover. At least I assume he's new-found; the chronology is murky. Anyway it doesnt matter. It may seem heartless to say, but this element is sentimental and irrelevant. The crime would be just as appalling, whatever the state of the victims love life.

The role-sharing has another function. It points up the kinship between the prey, who may just have discovered his sexual identity, and the predators, who seem unsure of theirs and are furiously compensating. Their bull sessions, which take up most of the play, include much mocking of gay stereotypes, and they play the roles with great enthusiasm.

Other times they are taunting one another, and tormenting themselves, about the girls they haven't had and the booze they can't get enough of. Between scenes the stage darkens, and they thrash about in vengeful frustration to the sounds of an especially ugly rock score. (The production seems to be suggesting a new legal strategy: Heavy metal made me do it.)

Its a matter of time before they find a scapegoat, and the threat of violence -- implicit from the first moment -- is all that keeps the play alive. The writing is base-level competent, and three of the actors -- Shaun Benson, Paul Dunn, Gil Garratt -- go vigorously through the motions. The fourth, Jamie Robinson, does the same, only with a higher degree of fluency, authority, and sometimes even wit. The play gets us from "what a gang of thugs" to "what a bunch of losers," but that isn't much of an advance.

Gulag starts better and ends worse. We meet the characters at the end of their jail term and note how the experience has destroyed their previous pecking order. The class bully is no longer in command, and though he makes regular attempts to reassert his authority, it's gone forever.

Out on the street, they try to reassemble their lives. They might be any young men with dead-end prospects, except they are occasionally haunted by their past. (Whether they are most affected by what they have done or by being caught is something the author sensibly leaves unresolved --the characters wouldnt know either.)

They try to mend fences, or build new ones, with the women in their lives: mothers, sisters, girlfriends. A new cast-member, Stephanie Jones, plays all these roles, and does fairly well, though theres something temporary about her performance. She seems preoccupied at the end of each vignette by the thought that she has to rush off and change into someone else. But all these scenes are scrappily written, acted, and staged; and the attempt to play them in a kind of hopeful counterpoint doesn't add much.

Eventually one of the guys gets engaged, and the four convene to celebrate. A long party-scene ensues. It is obviously crucial, and it may conceivably be wonderful. I couldn't say since the director, Sarah Stanley, has placed the whole thing in the corner of the stage farthest from where I happened to be sitting. Anyway, the old acting- gay games resurface, and this time they turn serious: blackout, followed by lights up on one of the quartet recalling a forgiving homosexual experience in prison.

Its the end of the play, and its plainly meant to be an epiphany. But it can't carry the weight. The audience is left stranded, wondering if it's time to applaud. (To be fair, the previous transitions from ensemble to solo have generally been the strongest parts of both productions.)

Talking of sloppiness, where does the author get off appropriating for his title the symbol of an oppressive system far worse than anything his characters are likely to experience? Aren't their problems real enough without this inflation? All his sentiments are correct, but they don't make a play. Let alone two.
