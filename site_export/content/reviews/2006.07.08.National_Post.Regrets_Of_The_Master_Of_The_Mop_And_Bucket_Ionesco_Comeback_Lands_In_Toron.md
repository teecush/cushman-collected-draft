If the term "theatre of the absurd" accounts for anything, it accounts for Eugene Ionesco, whose plays, farce with a dusting of nihilist philosophy, came violently into fashion in the 1950s, slipped quietly out of it in the '60s and have been creeping back again over the past decade. The centrepiece of this renaissance has been The Chairs, which premiered in Paris in 1952 when it managed to bewilder even the French, was revived there in triumph in 1956 before taking off to conquer the world. In 1997, it was a surprise success all over again in London and New York. I'm not sure that it's his best play, but it makes an excellent calling card, functioning as it does on so many interlocking levels.

It's the play about the old couple, each of them 90-plus, who live on top of a very high building -- conceivably a lighthouse, certainly overlooking some kind of waterway -- where the Old Man is caretaker. He has a message to give to the world; and he and the Old Woman, a faithful helpmate, summon an Orator, whom we see, to deliver it, and a large audience of celebrities and others to listen. We don't see them, but -- an image that has made the play's fortune -- we do see the chairs they sit upon: 56 of them in the new Soulpepper production, all dragged and arranged on stage by the tireless couple themselves. You can see why these parts are generally cast far younger than they're written.

The Old Man has regrets and thwarted ambitions, as who has not, and he has of course had plenty of time, and plenty of space, to accumulate and brood upon them. As both he and his wife say, repeatedly, he could have been a grand master, a chess master, a master of the revels, a master builder, a master don as opposed to just a mastodon (these are all my own suggestions, but I think they're in the spirit of the text), or at least master in his own house; instead he's just -- these are his own words -- "master of the mop and bucket."

All these reiterations are the kind of thing that made Ionesco famous, demonstrations of the meaninglessness of language, or at least of its ability to deaden our responses, and of the supposed inability of human communication actually to communicate. It's an unproved case, though everybody lapped it up in, roughly, the years between existentialism and Vietnam. Prone to redundancy and sloppy thinking though we may all be, this isn't really the way people talk, and the more virtuoso Ionesco's puns and patterns become, the more tiresome become their pretensions to comment on either the social system or the human condition. Just as wordplay, it's fun if over-extended, though the new translation by Martin Crimp, the current go-to guy in England for translating anything, strikes me as less flowing and felicitous than the traditional one it's replaced. I think this is what's known as putting a Crimp in the style.

However: This is also a play about a marriage, and here the repetition does have a point. It underpins a deeply observed portrait of fretfulness and consolation, a mutually supportive, mutually exploitative relationship that's long been on automatic pilot. As the Old Man's confidence grows, and as his imaginary audience swells (it includes an Emperor), he moves, with absolute logic, from regret to resentment, and from there to outright paranoia. He spills out accusations, very persuasive-sounding ones, against all the people who blocked his advancement. His wife's contribution is to jolly him up while urging him to make the most of his last opportunity. It's hers, too; when company calls, she flirts like there was no tomorrow (a reasonable assumption in the circumstances), while maintaining a pose of outraged virtue.

This is where Jim Warren's production comes into its own. Its great virtue is that nobody seems to have told it that the play is Absurd. Oliver Dennis and Kristen Thomson play the couple absolutely for real, he dapper and hopeful and bitter, she bustling and buxom, if sagging. Just as Waiting for Godot always works best in productions that take the metaphysics for granted and concentrate on the interaction of two guys at the side of a road, so this one flowers as a day in the lives, that happens to sum up all of the days that have gone before. Of course, most marriages don't last this long, at least not with both partners sentient, but this is what it would be like if they did.

The two actors are also whizzes at the farcical mechanics, which gets us to yet another level. The accumulation of the chairs, shoved on stage at breakneck speed through the multiple doors of Sue LePage's admirable set, is a theatrical treat in itself, quite apart from what it may actually mean. As to that, there are two interpretations. The most popular holds that the guests are a delusion, comparable to the Old Man's conviction that he has the key to all humanity's problems, if only humanity would listen.

But this does seem to be laying things on with a trowel, and it's equally possible to believe that the guests are really there and that the chairs are only theatrical shorthand for their presence. Certainly the way Thomson chats them up, or hawks programs, had me believing, as did the perfect timing of their imaginary entrances, each prolonged (those doors again) until you could swear you actually see them. And if the spectators are real, then the failure of the imposing Orator (Kevin Bundy) actually to orate, is all the more poignant, while the self-immolation of the couple who dive through separate windows -- portholes, really -- into the depths far below, is all the more logical. They have prepared for their moment and, whether they truly anticipate its being a triumph or a disaster, they have nothing left to live for. The resultant muted splash, craftily delayed, is a highlight of Richard Feren's sound score, a vital asset from the first foghorn to the last hubbub.

I have a personal theory, quite unsupported, that the murky waters below used to be the Seine. This is, among other things, an end-of-the-world play, or at least an end-of-French-civilization play, unfolding in a barren room comparable to the hecatomb setting of Samuel Beckett's Endgame. So we have yet a fourth level, a blend of the political and the apocalyptic. (We need an Ionesco word for that. Apoliticalyptic, perhaps.) It doesn't hold too much water, holy or otherwise, since Beckett writes theatrical poetry, while Ionesco deals in whimsy. This production, featuring two outstandingly likeable actors, probably accentuates that; Ionesco has been called "an antic poet of despair," but the antics here outpace the desperation. That, though, is a reasonable choice. It plays to the interpreters' strengths but it also, whatever he himself may have wanted to believe, does honour to the author's.
