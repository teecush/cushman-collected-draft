The import of Porter MUSIC"Cole Porter, who was born 100 years ago tomorrow, was an impeccable craftsman in words and music, an elegant, priviledged patrician who made sophistication commercial. But there was more to him than that

Saturday, June 08, 1991

ROBERT CUSHMAN

BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail THERE is a bouncing, but little-known, Cole Porter song called It Ain't Etiquette, hidden away in the score for his 1939 musical DuBarry Was a Lady and written as a vehicle for that show's star comedian, Bert Lahr. It begins: "Missus Emily Post Who they tell me is most Reliable For a helluva sum Wrote a book that's become My Biable."

It goes on to counsel us: "And if only you'll look In that etiquette book Of dear Emily's You can co-habitate With America's great Femilies."

That's still pretty funny, especially if you imagine Lahr gnong- gnonging out the trick rhymes in the Cowardly Lion voice that he had patented to immortality the same year.

What is impressive, though, is the way in which the rhymes reinforce the jokes; and what is really impressive is the way in which, without any apparent forcing, the jokes connect, so that an attitude and a thought- line go through the whole passage.

Porter is famous for list-songs like You're the Top, spectacular accumulations of one-liners, but what really distinguishes him from all other U.S. lyricists is his ability to write in paragraphs. Begin the Beguine, the longest popular song ever written, became a hit through its throbbing exoticism, but it would fall apart if Porter had not managed, through 108 virtuoso bars, to tell a coherent story.

It was a story that the public wanted to hear, of love found and lost in a tropical paradise. That was a standard theme in tin-pan alley.

But Porter, Broadway's favourite rich boy, gave it a flavour of his own. He made it a sophisticated traveller's tale, which was fair enough since he was known to be a sophisticated traveller. People expected him to entertain lavishly and to go on world cruises, though many of them would have been perturbed had they known what some of Porter's cruising entailed.

He was a life-long homosexual, sheltered from scrutiny by wealth, both inherited and earned, and by a happily platonic marriage that incidentally brought him a third fortune.

Of all the American Golden Age songwriters (and they are an extraordinary parade; just when you think you have them all down, you realize that you have forgotten Kurt Weill or Duke Ellington) only two had high public profiles, personalities that the public saw reflected in their work. There was George Gershwin, buoyant and aggressive, the archetypal poor boy made good; and there was Porter, the Yale man, elegant and patrician and all-around WASPish.

Gershwin must have been easy to identify with; Porter's popularity - at its height during the Depression - is less expected. People should have resented him, but in fact they cheered him on. They didn't feel part of his world, but they didn't feel excluded from it either. They were let in, so to speak, on the ground floor.

Porter made sophistication commercial. There is a universal masochistic appetite for songs of lost love, but he put a new spin on it.

"So goodbye, dear, and amen Here's hoping we meet now and then It was great fun But it was Just One Of Those Things."

Wouldn't we all like, in romantic extremis, to be capable of that wit and that courtliness? Porter makes it seem like everybody's birthright.

Indeed, he perfected the art of the gracious farewell. During the Second World War, when relationships faced challenges far deadlier than erotic caprice, he wrote what is arguably his finest song, Ev'ry Time We Say Goodbye, in which parting is seen as an inescapable event, one that recurs if you're lucky. The serene logic of its melody is so convincing that the song's most famous moment of cleverness - when the words speak of "the change from major to minor" and the music follows suit - registers as an unself-conscious emotional peak. The feeling is universal, but it's still conveyed in Porter's own fastidious language, complete with reference to "the gods above me, who must be in the know."

The gods often came in handy for Porter. In the early I Loved Him (But He Didn't Love Me), "they sit in crowds on exclusive clouds and laugh at our love affairs."

That was the brittle jazz-baby Porter, known mainly to the small fraction of his total audience that attended his Broadway shows. They heard the famous ballads in a different context, surrounded by comedy songs satirically depicting a heterosexual world in which every woman seems to be a nymphomaniac and every man either a stud or a dud.

Or there were songs in which Porter seemed to be amusing himself and his friends, actual or vicarious, with patter that catalogued the Manhattan party-givers among whom his own life was lived. These songs were as much celebration as lampoon.

He reserved his bile, expressed with acidic restraint, for social unfortunates: for Mrs. Lowsborough-Goodby, too gauche to know how to organize a weekend, or for Mister and Missus Fitch, the nouveaux riches, strongly reminiscent of the Veneerings in Dickens' Our Mutual Friend, who were taken up by society, and then promptly disowned when they lost all in the Crash.

The middle America that took Porter to its heart might have expelled him, had it heard some of these songs.

In fact, for all his privilege, he was doubly an outsider: not just for his gayness, but also because, in his heavily Jewish professional milieu, he was not Jewish. He told Richard Rodgers that he had discovered that the secret of songwriting success was "to write Jewish," by which he meant he would specialize in minor-key laments. (The Slavonic melancholy of I Love Paris prompted Alec Wilder to dub it I Love Russia.) Similarly, his boy-girl love songs were riper and more urgent - sometimes, in their mixture of the taut and the torrid, exaggeratedly so - than anyone else's.

It's tempting to see him as a Joe Orton figure, playing a prolonged joke on the straight world by providing the soundtrack for its mating rituals, starting with his first big hit Let's Do It, Let's Fall in Love, which, when you boil it down, amounts to a chilling catalogue of animal compulsion. ("Moths in your rugs do it"What's the use of moth-balls?") But if it is a joke, it's one that most of us will happily go along with.

Nobody, even in this centenary year, has called Porter a warm man or even a very nice one. Socially gracious, yes, and unquestionably brave: in 1937, a horse threw him and crushed both his legs. His doctors wanted to amputate, his wife and mother forbade it, and the resultant agony was ended only by his death in 1964.

But he rarely complained and went on producing good work, including his most acclaimed score, Kiss Me Kate. His stylistic range was wide and his craftsmanship in words and music almost always impeccable, but there was more to him than that. He had a sensibility, an individual view of the world, that was implicit in every word and note. If the word had not become so suspect where songwriting is concerned, I would call him a poet.
