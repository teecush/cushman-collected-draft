WHO'S AFRAID OF VIRGINIA WOOLF?

Avon Theatre, Stratford, Ont.

- - -

STRATFORD, Ont. - Edward Albee's Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf?, as you doubtless remember, is about a middle-aged couple who are trapped with one another, and who for the duration of one drink- sodden late night and early morning virtually compel a younger couple to climb into their hellhole with them. Why have George, minor professor of history at a New England college, and his wifem Martha, daughter of the university president, stayed together all these years? For reasons of economic and emotional interdependence, and because, in some of the more twisted of the myriad ways available, they love one another. They sure know how to laugh together, mainly at other people, and we're always told that that's important. Why do the younger pair, Nick and Honey, a biology professor and his wife, stay in their hosts' living room and watch them tearing one another apart, while absorbing a considerable amount of the fallout themselves? This is a question that commentators have more frequently asked, but the remarkably fine revival at Stratford supplies an answer: Apart from being too drunk to move, they are having too good a time to leave.

The dynamics of George and Martha (named after America's first First Couple, for reasons that have little to do with anything beyond the author's playfulness) are fascinating, and they vary from production to production. In the 1962 original, Uta Hagen gave an exhilarating powerhouse account of Martha; she was a destroyer, literally and nautically, and the rest of the play had to get out of her way. Martha Henry's Stratford performance is equally aggressive, but she doesn't carry the same guns, and we are less inclined to see the play through her eyes. Peter Donaldson's George, the very soul and image of a cankered academic and a performance of consummate technical authority, is initially the more sympathetic.

For a start, in a 3 1/2-hour play that holds us mostly with its humour, he has the most elegant tirades and the snappiest one- liners. To his guests, he is aggressively patronizing; to his wife he is defensively patronizing, and he does have a lot to put up with. She humiliates him verbally in company, taunting him with his professional failures. And having cuckolded him verbally, she proceeds to do so physically, at least to the extent of Nick's alcohol-diminished capacity. (Nick's wife is immobilized on the bathroom floor at the time). However, George does play along with all this, even orchestrates it; he degrades Martha by letting her degrade herself and then pretending not to care about it. He is the cleverer of the two, and by so much the crueller.

As it happens, Henry's Martha in her swaggering, sashaying mode is rather forced; alone in the cast, she doesn't seem to be acting in her own voice but in something put on for the occasion. She is at her best (as she was, rather differently, in Long Day's Journey into Night) when the character is furthest from conventional lucidity -- most memorable when she sits silent on the floor, her mouth working convulsively in the intervals when she doesn't have a glass at it.

Sean Arbuckle and Claire Jullien are perfect as Nick and Honey, she, with a wide-eyed Mia Farrow touch, sinking steadily into girlish denial as the horrors mount around her. It isn't that the younger couple see in the older a warning of what they might become; it's that they are confronted with a mocking mirror of what they already are, except that they aren't even in love. The four play together, under William Carden's direction, with awe-inspiring precision and flexibility. The play, as always happens in great ensemble performances, grows stronger by the moment.

Stronger but not necessarily better. The conscious stages into which George and Martha, especially George, divide their games -- "Get the Guest," "Hump the Hostess" -- always seem to me like devices for the playwright rather than credible crutches for the playwright. So, too, with the most notorious crutch of all: their shared pretence that they have a child, a grown son. (They have an imaginary kid; Honey supposedly has hysterical pregnancies, though the truth, revealed by the author with old-time melodramatic relish, turns out to be even more hysterical.)

In some productions the child has been presented as Martha's genuine fantasy, one that George has gone along with but that he finally punctures as a kind of desperate therapy. In this one it's an acknowledged ploy that they can both use as a weapon: telling people he's a bad father, she's a bad mother. Martha plays the game best, so George kills the kid, so to speak, as an act of revenge that doesn't preclude a note of shared pity at the end. (It's like the finale of Look Back in Anger, and as ambiguously sentimental.)

Either way, it feels too cut-and-dried, as if reality and illusion were separable compartments rather than shifting mental states. It also occasions lyrical arias that I confess I did not, that late in the evening, want to hear. The play's length is justifiable; it needs to be relentless. Oddly, what slows it down is a major change in tone.

The standard argument against such criticisms is that Albee was not writing a realistic play at all but an absurdist parable. But this won't wash. Whatever may be true of his other plays (most of which I find deadly dull) this one has real, rooted characters in a real room with real walls and a real time-scheme. It even has a traditional Ibsenite structure, in which some things are left obscure but most questions are answered, including some we never asked. It also, now, has nostalgic appeal: It pinpoints the liberal Kennedy moment -- before Oswald, before Watergate, and above all before campus unrest; these academics had to make their own entertainment.

On top of this it has such mild pretentions as George's liberal attacks on what he takes to be Nick's heartless new scientific order or the fact that he happens to be reading Spengler's Decline of the West. This is all too scrappy to constitute a satirical comment on the American dream (title of an earlier Albee play), though George's speeches work brilliantly as part of his ongoing stand-up act. He has to sound off about something, just to keep the party and his own adrenaline going. No audience is ever going to take the play as anything other than a high-powered naturalistic play with a few odd bits sticking out. That, apart from the jokes, is why it plays so well; that is how these actors triumphantly play it.
