THEATRE REVIEW

The Summoned, Tarragon Theatre

Years and years ago I saw a play by a writer new to me, Fabrizio Filippo, entitled Waiting for Lewis. It was presented by Theatre Direct, a young people's company, but it struck me as being better worth taking seriously than many plays intended for adults. Filippo was an actor as well as a playwright, and he disappeared from Toronto to make his fortune in film and television. But hes now back as both author and lead performer of a new play The Summoned. This one is presented by the Tarragon, a theatre for grownups. But for much of its length, it seems a less-mature play than its predecessor. Or maybe it just needs an audience of especially tech-savvy kids.

Its premise is traditional enough. The characters who have been summoned in The Summoned are gathered for that age-old theatrical ceremony, the Reading of the Will. The deceased is an Internet-security mogul, fabulously rich of course, named Khan. Or maybe Kahn: since he does not, for obvious reasons, appear on stage and is therefore unlisted in the program, either spelling may be correct. But I ll go with Khan, for its megalomaniac associations.

The gathering takes place at an unglamorous Toronto airport hotel, owned and run by Annie, an old flame of Khan's who was also, we are told, Wozniak to his Steve Jobs; so he used and discarded her on two levels simultaneously, which may make him super-Jobs. The place has come in useful over the years as a rendezvous for Khan's more reclusive clients - think Edward Snowden or Salman Rushdie - but Annie has otherwise retired from the game, morally as well as emotionally disillusioned. Helping her run the place is a young man named Aldous; he is Annies son and, we assume, Khans as well. He may even have been named in ironic tribute to Khan's brave new world, the full extent of whose braveness and newness is to be posthumously revealed.

Aldous acts as our narrator, or our geek-lecturer. Filippo plays the role himself, and he comes over as precociously, inhumanly glib. There turn out to be good dramaturgical reasons for this but we have to wait a long time to discover them, and in the meantime we're stuck with him. It may be a tribute to Filippos performance that he comes off so insufferable, but his being the author makes the play seem insufferable, too.

The other characters are hardly better company. Annie is the only one granted anything approaching feelings, but Maggie Huculak has an uphill battle conveying them. Then theres Gary (John Bourgeois), a high-level ex-associate who can't decide whether he

loved or hated Khan but can't stop breaking into tears either way. Laura, a lawyer, has the reverse reflex; her shtick is non-stop hysterical laughter, always a difficult effect for an actor to bring off but one that Kelli Fox manages without embarrassing either us or herself. From another part of Khans life, and of Aldouss, there's Isla (Rachel Cairns) who comes on as ditzy and then back-pedals like crazy, or perhaps that should be like sane. Keeping them all more or less in line is Quentin, the security man's security man, barking messages on an apparently limitless succession of cellphones, each one of which he proceeds to break in two; Tony Nappo, alone in the cast, makes a personality out of a running gag.

Sets (Jason Hand), costumes (Charlotte Dean), and of course video (Kurt Firla) are all high-class, and for live action we get some instant replays of the groups previous relationships. The flash and the flashbacks are slickly, speedily and even on occasion wittily handled in Richard Roses production. But the characters remain cardboard, so the story stays uninvolving. Until, that is, we get to the unfolding of Khan's last will and testament, or rather to its theatrical codicil.

Aldous is detached from his companions, to play a scene with a disembodied robotic voice whose identity it would be unethical to disclose. Enough to say that it introduces Aldous to horizons previously undreamed of, while presenting him - and us - with some pretty, and genuinely perplexing, moral problems. The teasing pre-recorded voice, by the way, belongs to Alon Nashman, currently Toronto's most ubiquitous actor; you may hear him here and both see and hear him in the Jordan Tannahill double bill that opened almost simultaneously at CanStage. This scene galvanizes the play, and the energy sustains through our heros return to thrash things out with his companions. Were presented with a choice between the pain of mortality and the no-horizon promises of technology. The play turns out to be about something, and it proves in retrospect to have been cunningly constructed. For a moment, it almost feels worthwhile.

The Summoned runs through May 29 at the Tarragon Theatre.
