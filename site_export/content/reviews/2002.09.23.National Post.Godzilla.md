GODZILLA

Factory Theatre Studio, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - The new Toronto theatre season gets off to an appalling start with the English-language premiere of Godzilla. This play, like the movie franchise that begot it, comes from Japan, where it has achieved what can only be described as a monster success. Even the most popular Japanese plays usually run, we are told, for a mere two or three weeks, but this one has kept coming back on an annual basis -- like Anne of Green Gables over here, only not quite as sophisticated.

The play concerns a love affair between the celebrated 50-metre- long, 100-ton fire breather and earth leveller and an idealistic maiden of conventional human proportions whose name is Yayoi. That is the whole story. It is also the whole joke, and you could write most of it yourself. You know that Yayoi's family are going to object to their putative son-in-law, and so they do, at what feels like enormous length. (The play is actually quite short, but it seems interminable.) The author, Yashuiko Ohashi, does, to be fair to him, add a little twist: He proceeds to show us Godzilla's relatives voicing their own reservations. Theirs, though, are more easily and generously overcome; I guess they can afford to be big about these things.

Godzilla himself, whatever misdemeanours may be strewn across his past, is presented to us here as a gauche, well-meaning creature who tries very hard to watch where he puts his feet. A parable about tolerance and prejudice comes with the territory. It's so obviously built-in, in fact, that the author has no need to spell it out -- not that that stops him.

Here I have to confess my ignorance. Before seeing this play, I knew little of Godzilla beyond the name; in fact, I really could not have told him and King Kong apart. I had no idea that Godzilla was conceived by a Japanese film-maker in the 1950s as a sort of psychic compensation for what his country had endured at the end of the Second World War. I never knew either that Hollywood had added to America's imperialist record by appropriating the character but deleting the social comment. I learned this not from the play, which depends on but never mentions it, but from the press package; these days, though, I try never to read the package until after I've seen the play. (And of course the non-professional audience never gets to read it at all.) So, for example, I had no idea that Ultraman, the name bestowed on the heroine's vengeful former suitor, was himself a sci-fi icon from Japanese television. This, obviously, makes me a less than ideal audience for the play: a frustrated non-cultist at a cult entertainment. Still, my indifference to the genre never stopped me enjoying Little Shop of Horrors or The Rocky Horror Show, so I refuse to shoulder all the blame.

Godzilla is directed by Jim Millan for his very own Crow's Theatre company. I used to have a lot of respect for this director and his troupe, since my first encounter with their work was Brad Fraser's Unidentified Human Remains and the True Nature of Love, a sparky play excellently performed. Since then, though, my admiration has cooled, in the face of a line of indifferent scripts, messily produced. Last year's Hedwig and the Angry Inch was a nadir, but Godzilla lowers the ante still further, with a static production full of squawking actors, and with an ending as feebly staged as any I remember. It comes with the usual junk manifesto about plays that "question authority, social norms and accepted truths about history and our times." Accepted by whom? Most of Crow's self-selecting audiences are going to agree with the shows before they even start. This isn't a terrible thing in itself -- there are only so many opinions in the world, after all -- but it hardly seems like something to brag about. (It does seem to work, though. Reputations in Toronto not-for-profit theatre, once gained, are very hard to lose. And one way to gain them is by telling everyone, very loud, how revolutionary you are.)

Anyway, it hardly seems to apply to Godzilla, a thin slice of camp whose only gesture toward social comment is the suggestion that Japanese families have become very like American ones, or at least like American ones as depicted on TV. In other words, Japan, like the rest of the world, has watched too many sitcoms and has internalized its values. This joke, though, is short-circuited by the casting; all but two of the actors are Western, and there is no particular kick in seeing North Americans acting like North Americans. In other words, what we have is colour-blind (or at least ethnically blind) casting, working in the reverse of its usual direction but just as unpersuasively. Not that the two Japanese actors add very much; Jean Yoon's performance as the heroine's grandmother who once had a thing for a tadpole is as baffling as her character, and of course their presence means that the Westernization doesn't even amount to a consistent style.

The only tolerable performances come from those few actors who are allowed, now and then, to cool it. Melinda Deines' Yayoi is indeed more than tolerable; she's consistently delicate and sensible, and her opening speech (the play's best), describing the necessarily platonic nature of her love, promises better, wittier things than actually arrive.

Godzilla himself is played with pleasant aw-shucksiness by John O'Callaghan; he is portrayed as of human size, on the sensible grounds that attempts at spectacle would be self-defeating. (They would also be harder, but nobody mentions that.) He shares an agreeable guys-together pre-nup scene with his future father-in- law, played by Keith Knight. I took a special dislike to our Cinderella's jealous younger sisters, but it's probably not their fault.

On a more cheerful note: Daryl Cloran, one of our most promising young directors, has completed a year's internship at Soulpepper, with a workshop production of Edward Albee's The Play about the Baby. No more likeable than its author's other plays, it is still one of his wittiest and most riveting; and this production -- impeccably acted by Juan Chioran, Kate Lynch, Christopher Morris and Sarah Wilson -- was exemplary in its attention to pace and rhythm. It was given two matinee performances last Friday and Saturday. Soulpepper, or somebody, should bring it back.
