EL PASO

Factory Theatre, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - Michael Miller, the author of El Paso, writes excellent dialogue, and he comes out stinging. His opening scene shows Vivian, a black woman with terminal cancer, locked in bitter verbal combat with Junior, her neglectful and chronically unfaithful husband of 30 years. Junior once had a successful military career and may even have had a shot at becoming a general, but he is now in insurance.They live in Washington, D.C., but Vivian's hometown is El Paso, Tex., and she longs to go back there. In the next scene we take the trip for her, and almost immediately the play starts to unravel.

We are transported not only in time but in space. The date now seems to be shortly after Vivians marriage. Papa Spiller, her grandfather, already fears for her safety and tells his daughter Ida Lee, Vivians mother, to take his shotgun and go straighten Junior out. Ida Lee, however, has other concerns, mostly the succession of men in her life. She is a schoolteacher and both resentful and neglectful of her two daughters; the other, known as Tiny, works as a janitor in her mother's classroom, a fact her mother never lets her forget. To make things even more complicated, Papa Spiller, at some questionable point in the time scheme, gets ghostly visitations from a boy called Marcus, the child of Vivian and Junior (and therefore Papa Spiller's great-grandson), killed in an accident when his father should have been looking out for him. Vivian and Junior also have a daughter, Juanita, who, in what you might call the plays present-tense action, is struggling to get some belated professional qualifications; her relationship with her mother is edgy. (For her father she has, like nearly everyone else in the play, nothing but contempt.) It takes the audience the better part of the evening to get all this straight, and Philip Akins direction does nothing to lift the confusion. Nor does it help that several members of the cast are prone to fits of the mumbles.

This is a shame, because there are some powerful themes at work here. Papa Spiller's own parents were slaves; nevertheless, he has risen to be the owner of the biggest house in El Paso. But he and his family discover that there is more than one kind of enchainment, and that they are perfectly capable of manufacturing their own. ("Your house," Vivian yells at Junior, "was the hell of slavery, in blackface.") The middle generation grows up through the era of Martin Luther King and has to balance violence against forgiveness. There is also intermittent but inconclusive stress on disputed property, from houses to chinaware. I guess if the play's about anything, its about the tyranny of history and about the futility of blaming what you have become on what has been done to you. We get to hear a lot of recrimination; most of the characters are virtuosi of it. The effect is sometimes bracing, but cumulatively wearying. Its not enough that characters in the present should be dredging up the past; when we travel into the past, we find that they're still at it.

It also lacks focus. Miller has not written a Chekhovian ensemble piece in which all the characters contribute equally to a fluid pattern. He's writing more like Ibsen, telling a traumatic story backwards. But Ibsen had the sense to tell only one such story at a time, and -- since his generation had mercifully never heard of flashbacks -- he had to work hard at his exposition.

Watching El Paso, you keep wondering who the central character is meant to be. On the face of, its Vivian; we begin with her, shes in the direst straits, and the characters cluster around her sickbed. But it's Ida Lee, her monstrously selfish mother, who dominates the climactic scene with a violent transformation that is neither properly prepared nor credibly written. The protagonist might even be Tiny, the warmest and most tolerant figure in the piece (though certainly no pushover) and the one who, both figuratively and literally, travels easiest between El Paso and Washington, then and now.

When he writes a group scene, Miller seems to have problems keeping all his characters in play; the people in the middle are lively, but the others are reduced to implausibly mute spectatorhood. The production, which leaves them sitting around gaping, makes it worse. The contrast is a metaphor for the performances at large; most of them have their moments, but nobody is able to carry a character all the way through.

Kim Roberts, sharp-tongued and warm-natured, comes closest as the ironically named Tiny (I assume it's ironic, as the actress is anything but). Jeff Jones Papa is a fine figure of a sad, angry patriarch and he has a nice line in sarcasm, but psychologically he remains in his own corner, intoning. Satori Shakoor manages Vivians anger effectively, but can do little with her more passive moments. David Collins as Junior is hardly allowed to be more than a one- dimensional villain; every so often, the script tosses him a bone, in the shape of one or two grievances of his own, but it never develops them, though you can sense the actor straining at the chance. (It's not that Junior's cruelty isn't credible -- sadly, it is -- but that it has nowhere to go.)

Vivian spends much of the play locked in her bedroom; she and Junior berate one another through an invisible wall, like Pyramus and Thisbe having a succession of bad days. This is a cute enough idea, but it leaves the actors delivering tirades in no specific direction -- and, I would guess, with no specific direction, either.

Lili Francks has some rather shallow fun with the flirty side of Ida Lee. She cannot cope with the bitter old woman she turns into, but then she seems, like most of the play, to be written moment to moment, with no connecting line. Rothaford Gray is sweet as a suitor who marries her (or rather, whom she marries) and who swiftly repents.

The company in general has more energy than finesse, and the production lets them squander it; script and performance expend so much emotion on the small moments that they have none left for the big. I imagine that several strands of inexperience have become tangled here. The play may be about mothers and daughters, men and women, past and present, North and South, reality and dream. It cannot in reason be about them all.
