Britain is about to be invaded by Tim Hortons. Or, to quote an article in the Financial Post, "Canada's biggest coffee chain is venturing into the land of tea and crumpets."

Somebody's obviously been watching too much Downton Abbey. I grew up in England, but most of the crumpets I've eaten have been bought in Canadian supermarkets. There's also no such thing in England as an English muffin.

Tea, of course, is another matter. They do indeed drink a lot of it in Britain. It's probably why it took them so long to get out of India. But here's a funny thing: the best tea I've had in Canada has been at Tim Hortons. It's the only chain where they boil the water hot enough. It's also the only place where the lady behind the counter (it's usually a lady serving at Tims) will ask you whether you want milk and sugar, and then put it in for you, rather than waving you over to an untidy assortment of cartons and containers at the far end of the room. It's the next best thing to being served tea by your mother. In fact, anybody pouring tea in England, regardless of gender, will ask, "Shall I be mother?" They don't say that at Timmys but the thought is there.

I should add that in mentioning sugar I was paying craven homage to convention. George Orwell, in his classic essay on How to Make a Cup of Tea, said that if you put sugar in tea, you weren't drinking tea, you were drinking sugar. He went on to advise sugar-drinkers to try drinking their tea without it for a few days, promising that, if they did, they would never go back. He was right. Orwell, of course, never crossed the Atlantic. If he had, he would have found people doing even more abominable things to tea than putting sugar in it. He would have found them topping it up with cream. Still, this practice seems to be dying out in Canada, if not in the United States. That's progress.

Yes, there is such a thing in Britain as cream tea, but the cream comes separately. You put it on scones, preferably with jam. It's delicious, though I wouldn't advise having it too often.

Now, I don't know how successful Tim Hortons will be in marketing its tea to British drinkers. (I wish I could avoid mentioning coals and Newcastle, but the best I can do is parentheses.) I realize that it probably isn't what the company has in mind, and it certainly isn't what it's best known for at home. Maybe it can bring something new to the coffee market. When I first came to Canada, I didn't know what a double-double was, and I was shocked when I found out. Actually, I still am.

Britons drink a lot of coffee as well, and they don't seem to mind if it's supplied by foreigners. Starbucks is big there. Still, in terms of the number of outlets, it comes in a distant second. First place belongs to a chain that's homegrown: Costa Coffee, named not, as you might expect, because its beans come from Costa Rica, but because it was founded by two immigrant brothers from Italy named Bruno and Sergio Costa. Sergio subsequently bought out Bruno - maybe there's a movie there - and the whole operation was later bought by Whitbread, the giant British-based conglomerate that started with beer. This year Costa made a deal with Shell Canada, so its coffee may be coming soon to a service-station near you.

Perhaps the traffic can also go the other way. Perhaps. But Tims isn't Starbucks. Starbucks has a North American glamour about it. Tims is more about homeliness than glamour - it has built its reputation on being quintessentially Canadian - and that may not be exportable. Still, it does have an extra weapon, what businesspeople call a "unique selling proposition:" it has food.

Nobody goes to any of those other chains to eat. They know about doughnuts in the U.K., of course. They may even know maple doughnuts. But there's no place where doughnuts have the same cachet as they do at Tims, and they could have exotic appeal over there.

The British have notoriously sweet teeth. Though I have to say that some of Tims' recently added flavours are altogether too exotic. They're so smothered in toppings, you can't even find the hole.

But it's not just the doughnuts. My wife and I had a gourmet Canadian friend, a businessman and world traveller, whose favourite dining place was London's justly celebrated Connaught Hotel. But he was equally passionate about the chili at Tim Hortons. So am I. During the few years when Tims was allied with Wendy's, I developed a gourmet combo of my own: I would buy a chili at Tims, then go to the adjoining Wendy's and buy one of their excellent baked potatoes with sour cream and chives (eat your heart out, McDonald's) and apply the cream to the chili. Now, with the link to Wendy's broken, maybe Tims could add potatoes and sour cream to their own menu. They could even forget about the potatoes. The British might lap it up.

They might even be able to persuade the great British public to roll up the rim. But I doubt that they'll ever be able to sell them on Timbits.
