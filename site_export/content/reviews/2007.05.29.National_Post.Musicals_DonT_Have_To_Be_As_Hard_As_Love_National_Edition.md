MACK AND MABEL

Festival Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont.

- - -

Im in the camp with those, including the composer-lyricist himself, whose favourite Jerry Herman score is Mack and Mabel.

Mack is Mack Sennett, the pioneering maestro of early silent film comedy, and Mabel is Mabel Normand, his favourite leading lady and the love, the show insists, of his life. (And he of hers, but stuff happens.)

The show flopped when it was done on Broadway in 1974, but its tunes have kept it alive, and just listening to them all flow past in the overture to the Shaw Festivals production makes you understand why.

We get invigorating foretastes of the commanding and pulsating Movies Were Movies, the pugnacious Big Time, the poignant I Won't Send Roses, the enchanting Look What Happened to Mabel and much else. There are so many goodies in this medley that it can afford to hold the show's closing number, called I Promised You a Happy Ending. It isnt a particularly good song, but its crucial to understanding a) what the musical is trying to do and b) why, with all its attractive qualities, it doesn't work.

The show is a first-person narrative with flashbacks. Mack, adrift in a horrible new world where actors talk, starts the proceedings by singing at us that "movies were movies when during the titles you knew / You'd get a happy ending.

By the end of the evening, Mack is fantasizing of just such a fadeout for himself and Mabel, even while acknowledging that there was no reunion and that Mabel really wound up an unemployable drug addict caught up in a murder scandal. At least, thats how things played out in the shows original book, written by the late Michael Stewart, and many people blamed the production's failure on the implied downer of its finale.

The script has since been reworked (by Francine Pascal, Stewart's sister) so that there really is a happy ending. Mack and Mabel get to make one more picture together and, for all we are told, live happily ever after. This feels false, because it is false, although not so much false to history as false to the shows own purpose.

Mack and Mabel tells two stories: the story of silent comedy and the story of a love affair. For a time, the two tales get along together. Its lovely when Mabel, a deli waitress, comes to Mack's New York lot to collect payment on a sandwich and gets caught up in the action, revealing herself to be a great natural comedienne. It's neat, and musically and lyrically delightful, when she first sees herself on the screen, and understands just what has happened to the former queen of corned beef and salami." Its a thrill when the whole Sennett operation hits the big time and sets off for California, full of beans and schadenfreude: "This time, exults an unusually frank lyric in Big Time, we wont say 'those lucky bastards' / this time those lucky bastards are us."

The romance gets off to a bittersweet start when Mack, a workaholic, admits that he isnt the flower-sending type and Mabel ruefully accepts him. When Mabel muses later that there are things time doesnt heal, it's an admission worth the price of admission.

But, even in the original unsweetened book, there are fatal flaws. The love songs may engage the characters' emotions and ours, but the love story doesnt. Its a formulaic affair, with predictable complications arising from Mack frustrating Mabel's ambitions of becoming a dramatic artiste. The story of the Sennett studio also becomes formulaic, and here it's the fault of the score, or of what the score is asked to do. Mack outlines his philosophy (I Want To Make the World Laugh). He invents the Sennett Bathing Beauties (Hundreds of Girls). He invents the Keystone Kops (Hit Em on the Head). The numbers are cheery and repetitive, both individually and as a group, and they dont get us anywhere.

Molly Smith's production, the last item in the Shaw's impressive opening hand, is slick and spectacular, on sets by William Schmuck that are skeletal, mobile and appropriately filmic. But it cant conceal the fact that the show itself is largely static, however energetic the scenery and the performers might be.

A show that failed with Robert Preston and Bernadette Peters is unlikely to succeed with anyone else, but Niagaras two leads sing their numbers and play their characters very well.

Benedict Campbell, rhythmically barking his songs, is as driven and driving as Mack should be and more sympathetic than might be expected. Glynis Ranney is likable, too, and sings beautifully, but makes no visible or audible connection between Mabel as Brooklyn gamine and Mabel as Hollywood goddess.

Gabrielle Jones belts admirably as a showgirl-of-all-trades, the only other character to get anything approaching a solo, though her Tap Your Troubles Away number is ruined by generic choreography that realizes too late that it's meant to be caustic commentary.

This production makes the half-heartedness work for it, giving the performers shy solo lines and letting the whole thing build. At its best Mack and Mabel is exhilarating. But its best doesn't happen often enough. - Mack and Mabel is in repertory through Oct. 28. For tickets, call 1-800-511-SHAW or visit www.shawfest.com
