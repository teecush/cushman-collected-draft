Canons to the left and right of him THE WESTERN CANON: The Books and School of the Ages

BY HAROLD BLOOM Harcourt Brace, 578 pages, $39.95

Saturday, December 24, 1994

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Review by ROBERT CUSHMAN WHY do we read great books? Quite often because we have to; we are taking or teaching a course, or we feel some kind of cultural compulsion, or we are just driven readers.

Harold Bloom is one of those. A voluminous critic, 40 years on the English faculty at Yale, he seems to have read everything. The Western Canon -an intimidating, uneven, repetitive and magnificent book - is his attempt to order and to crystallize the tradition of European and American literature. Bloom does not deny that there are other canons, but what concerns him is the nature and the probable fate of this one.

Shakespeare - whom he places, uncontroversially, at the very centre of the canon - is also his main exception: the only one exempt from what Bloom calls "the anxiety of influence," which is his pet theory. As I understand it, which is probably imperfectly, major writers are affected by previous major writers, and do battle with the influence in their own works. (But are they conscious of this? That's what bugs me.) How good a fight (an "agon") they put up determines whether they themselves become canonical. Shakespeare influences everyone, but himself owes nothing to anyone except Chaucer; the Wife of Bath, in a splendidly gender-free gesture, begat Falstaff.

So authors rather than readers or critics, in Bloom's view, do the selecting; and the canon is self-perpetuating and elitist beyond the wildest nightmares of conspiracy theorists. It's a pleasing idea, but not entirely buyable; laypersons do have a vote, if only at the sifting stage. Bloom leans heavily upon his theory to shape his story; so Endgame, rather than the more obvious Waiting for Godot, becomes Samuel Beckett's canonical work, on account of its Shakespearean connections. Beckett is the latest author to whom Bloom gives detailed treatment; the earliest, since he doesn't deal with the Greeks, is Dante. More momentously he spotlights Bleak House, which may indeed be Dickens's masterpiece, while omitting Pickwick Papers, an equally great work in its own way and an obvious descendant of Don Quixote which has earlier inspired one of his best chapters. On the other hand, Bleak House does look forward to Kafka. . . you see how the game goes.

There are 26 authors on Bloom's short list, ranging from Tolstoy to Emily Dickinson, Dr. Johnson (the most human of critics and rightly Bloom's own favourite) to Freud, who gets in as a lousy theorist but a great essayist. There is a yawning gap where Dostoevsky should be, and I would love to read Bloom on Jean Jacques Rousseau; but obviously omissions are unavoidable.

A greater problem is that his theories cannot unify those he does include; the book is a collection of discrete essays, dotted with idees fixes. One is his insistence on the "uncanniness" of canonical works, "their ability to make you feel strange at home" - a description I find too Blooming flowery to be helpful. Then there are Bloom's beefs: his running battle with the enemies of the canon, and with its false friends.

The latter are the conservative moralists, the do-gooders of criticism whom Bloom has fought, he tells us, all his career and who profess to find in classic texts a validation of their own beliefs. I would call them the status-quoters. On the other side, but equally sanctimonious, are the assorted Marxists, feminists, deconstructionists and multiculturalists who denounce the canon for its exclusivity and whom Bloom dubs the School of Resentment. (A provocative label: what many of them seem to resent is that others can write and they can't.) Against both factions Bloom proclaims that books have no moral and little political value; they must be enjoyed for themselves. I'm with that, but I cannot help remarking that the Resenters are not always as resentful or as monolithically silly as he makes out, and that even sensitive readers make moral judgments (as Bloom's own commentaries bear witness), even when both the text and its author seem to be nasty pieces of work.

Bloom offers his canon as a labour-saving device, to stop us wasting our limited time on the wrong books, but he also writes as though his own choice were (except by Resenters) unassailable. It isn't. Milton's reputation has been up and down and over and out as often as Frank Sinatra in That's Life. Both Bloom and I know that Ibsen's Peer Gynt is the greatest play since Shakespeare, and one of the funniest, but a surprising number of people disagree. Walter Scott was once ranked ahead of all other novelists. I expect to go to my grave with The Faerie Queen and Finnegans Wake still unconquered, and probably unattempted.

As crotchety as he is defiant, Bloom constantly prophesies a new Dark Age with serious readers an endangered species even - or especially - in the universities, where Literature will become as small and specialized a faculty as Classics. This may not, he implies, be all bad; it will get rid of the fashionable charlatans. English as an academic subject is a recent growth anyway, regarded by the older disciplines as an intolerably soft option. (That generations of undergraduates have thought the same may be its best chance for survival.) If it goes as quickly as it came, novels and poems will still, for those who fancy them, remain.

Plays are a special case, and a fortunate one. "The theatre," Bloom remarks, "has its own traditions and its own continuity" or, to put it another way, it needs product. Hamlet gets staged a lot more often than War and Peace gets read, and it has to stand up and justify itself on each occasion. So do its interpreters; they have to co-exist with their subject, which makes even the most wayward actor and director more responsible than most critics. Bloom says that Shakespeare's supremacy "has little to do with drama as such" (in other words, he's a different animal from Ibsen or Moliere) but the physical persistence of the plays is the best possible argument for the canon.

A further case is presented in Bloom's appendix: a compendious list of the great and the good from the ancients until now. Up until the last century the list obviously aims to be definitive, and there is only one omission I would question: Dumas pere. (The Musketeer novels aren't canonical? D'Artagnan isn't a great soliloquist? Even Shaw knew better than that.) The modern section is confessedly more speculative, racing tips rather than classified results. I'm fairly aghast that the Thomas Mann canon doesn't include Buddenbrooks, and I wonder what Bloom has against the post-war English novel, with no bets placed on Anthony Powell, Angus Wilson, Kingsley Amis or Salman Rushdie. He also omits his own country's line of New York humourists, from Thurber to Catch-22, though he seems a funny man himself. I imagine him played by Zero Mostel. He writes fat. Robert Cushman is still trying to catch up on his reading.
