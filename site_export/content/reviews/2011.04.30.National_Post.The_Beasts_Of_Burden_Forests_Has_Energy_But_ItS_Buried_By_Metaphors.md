Critics making their way out of Wajdi Mouawad's Forests were presented with a family tree of the characters' tangled relationships over two continents and several generations. We were told that being given this information earlier would have spoiled the surprise. I find it hard to take seriously a play whose ultimate effect depends on genealogical suspense, but that may not matter as the author takes it seriously enough for both of us.

So, what's the big idea? It seems to be about memory, or perhaps historical awareness. The play, a companion but not a sequel to Mouawad's highly praised Scorched, begins in Montreal with Aimee, an expectant mother, declaring that she doesn't know or care much about the Berlin Wall, which has recently fallen. "Our lives," she says, "must be pretty boring to care so much about other people's happiness!"Pretty soon she sees the error of her ways. She's diagnosed with brain cancer, with a bizarre genetic complication that means if her child lives she herself will shortly die. She decides against an abortion because that would mean adding to the number of women killed in the Montreal massacre. As it reaches back in time, and into Aimee's ancestry, the play also brings in the French resistance, the Holocaust and the Nazi connections of Alsace industrialists whose 19th-century employment practises had already verged on slave labour. (They supplied the transport to the death camps; for once the trains that roar past the Tarragon have a macabre effectiveness.) I criticized Scorched for its historical vagueness, but I guess I was wrong. Forests uses real events, whose horror is already of mythical proportions, as fodder for a rickety myth of its own. It involves, among other things, the idea that a known resistance fighter might have been spared by the Nazis, just because they didn't know she was Jewish. Halfway through the three-hour play I felt angry. By the end I just felt numb.

The first scene invokes an actual Greek myth, that of the House of Atreus whose members killed and even ate one another. The events of Forests, though, don't bear much relation to it except that, as someone once said of a Jacobean tragedy, the incest is sustained throughout. The play also makes even heavier use than Scorched of the "twin" metaphor. Interrogating her family past is Aimee's 16-year-old daughter Loup, who's been sent on one of Mouawad's maternally mandated treasure hunts. She isn't happy about it, but then she isn't happy about anything; she takes teenage resentment to new exhibitionist levels. Escorting her is a polite paleontologist whom she calls a "pantologist," this, like the malapropisms in Scorched, representing the play's ration of humour. Loup's unstoppable discoveries lead her, quite arbitrarily, to enlightenment, or at least to the right to deliver a cracker-barrel epilogue. She isn't the only person here with an animal name; the Alsatian branch, revolting against their inhuman inheritance, founded a zoo, in the play's only actual forest, and one of their progeny is known as Gerard the Boy Giraffe. Maybe it sounds better in French.

In fact, once past the first couple of scenes, hardly anybody in the play utters a believable word. Instead they go in for fake poetry. "She's as beautiful as the horizon," says a father of his newborn daughter, really straining for simile. A man rhapsodizes over his beloved's "you-ness." (Shame, it sounds as if he's calling her Eunice.) Or they go in for shameless exposition: "Don't forget," a son reminds his father, "that I am your company veterinarian." It can't all be the fault of the translation.

You can certainly give Mouawad points for energy and ingenuity, and the same goes for Richard Rose's production, which careers through the undergrowth at a tremendous lick. But it leaves in its wake some wretched acting, some of it from normally excellent actors. Vivien Endicott-Douglas is half an exception; she plays Loup's abrasiveness way beyond the hilt, but her calmer moments shine, if only in comparison. As her confidant, R.H. Thomson brings a pleasing ease to a long but sketchy role; the play pretends that it's established a relationship between the two of them but it hasn't. Jan Alexandra Smith delivers Aimee's opening speech very well; as her husband Alon Nashman, granted the only recognizable human emotions in sight, takes full sym-pathetic advantage of them. Terry Tweed plays a scarifying grandmother, while Matthew Edison does a remarkable job of riding the play's rhetoric rather than, like too many of his colleagues, drowning in it. If you want a play that really puts a human face on atrocity, or a whole gallery of faces, go see Our Class. If you want a play whose characters are the partners, not the puppets, of its metaphor, try The Cosmonaut's Last Message to the Woman He Once Loved in the Former Soviet Union.
