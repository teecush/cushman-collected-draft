Second in a week-long series

Ihad the luck to grow up on the BBC. One of the best things about Britain is that it has managed, despite constant political sniping, to maintain a publicly funded nationwide broadcasting service that doesn't have to be supported by advertising and that has made an enormous contribution to the cultural life of its country. I owe a lot of my education to it. We have a comparable thing in Canada. It's called CBC Radio. We don't at the moment have it on television: at least, not to the extent that we could.

Some of the reasons for this are political. Public broadcasting always gets hammered by governments, who believe that if the thing is going to exist at all it had damn well better function as their propaganda arm. This, by the way, has nothing to do with the supposed "liberal bias" of the CBC. One thing that comically unites left and right is the entrenched belief of both that there is some monolithic force called "the media" that is out to get them. The fallback position of both is that, in its news coverage at least, "the media" should be impartial and objective. Actually, for as long as broadcasting is conducted by human beings, complete objectivity cannot exist and would be boring if it did. The most we can hope for is conscientiousness, and CBC news does pretty well on that score.

As for what is known as "creative" programming: The CBC's job here is to encourage talent, and if artistic talent declares itself as left of centre -- well, maybe that's something to think about. It certainly isn't a merely fashionable or transient phenomenon. The Victorian novelists we now revere were savagely critical of "Victorian values." Whatever kind of show we're talking about, fact or fiction: A public broadcasting organization that didn't offend or annoy people wouldn't be doing its job. It's one of the definitions of democracy.

A friend in the business has advised me not to give in to nostalgia. Still, I want to indulge my own long enough to recall that at one time the world was quite envious of Canadian television, and especially of Canadian television drama. The BBC in the 1950s ran whole seasons of it. In the early '60s when one of the British commercial companies embarked on a series of edgy new plays -- they called it Armchair Theatre, which I guess dates it in two ways -- they hired a Canadian, Sydney Newman, to run it. (One of his beneficiaries was an emerging playwright called Harold Pinter.) This

was a case of a commercial station consciously competing with the standards set by the subsidized. A public broadcaster's job, like that of any public cultural organization, is to get stuff on that wouldn't get on otherwise. And to be pleased when it gets picked up or imitated by someone else.

I don't think that this ideal relationship has been changed by the advent of the multi-channel universe, though it has been refined by it. There will always be overlap, and there should be, but the profusion of niche stations should have relieved CBC TV of any lingering obligation to be all things to all people. But it's hamstrung by commercialism, and of course by commercials. Leave aside their irritant factor, or the extent to which advertisers may influence programming. It affects tone. Listen in Toronto to CBC Radio 1, through Metro Morning, The Current, Q, As It Happens (you have to bypass The Point, which is pointless), and through all the varieties of voice you get a consistency of quality -- urbane, caustic, inquiring -- that you seldom encounter on television.

The mindset of the ads also seeps into management psychology. It means the frenetic, and foredoomed, attempt to appeal to a phantasmal "popular" audience -- and not just for the sake of ratings; I think there's an existential fear of being thought uncool. (It's also, in a clear case of cross-media contagion, draining the soul from commercial-free Radio 2, the corporation music station.) It underlies the proud purchasing of senescent American game-shows. It accounts, especially when you add in the concomitant grovelling to a generalized "youth," for George Stroumboulopoulos getting his Hour every night rather

than a more reasonable half-an-hour a week. And it may explain why, when the CBC decided for the first time in years to do Shakespeare on television, it opted for a cut-to-the-bone Othello starring an actor from Little Mosque on the Prairie with no theatrical experience, classical or otherwise. It had its merits, but in a country boasting at least three major classical companies, each filled to bursting with first-rate players who've spent their lives doing Shakespeare, it hardly seems the best way of showing off our riches to the world. Or, more importantly, to one another. Why not an ongoing scheme to preserve and adapt Canada's best theatre productions (as with the Stratford Festival's Caesar and Cleopatra, that it was left to CTV to screen), alongside the fostering of new TV plays by current writers?

I say this in hope as well as frustration, since I can't bring myself to give up on CBC's once-proud drama tradition, and because there have in fact been encouraging, if limited, signs. There was the artistic triumph of The Englishman's Boy -- though we have yet to see or hear of a comparable successor. That show was something of a throwback in being a single play (in two parts, but still). The Single Play, whose demise used to be much lamented, has effectively been replaced by something more exciting and unique to television: the long-form narrative serial as pioneered in Britain ( Pennies from Heaven, The Singing Detective) and brought to fruition on American cable. CBC had a go at this with George Walker's funny and unsettling This Is Wonderland -- even if it was axed after two seasons. Since then there's been The Border, whose American sale everybody is very jazzed about but which in fact represents a retreat to the formulaic model of a self-contained episode -- meaning essentially the same episode -- every week. There's a better form out there, one that explores and doesn't manipulate, and CBC ought to be pursuing it.

You can't will such things into being. But you can provide the soil for them, and a public broadcasting system is ideally equipped to do so. Or it should be. But it can't, if it operates as a faux-commercial network. A subscriber-supported system isn't the answer either, if PBS is any guide; it's principally a buyer of foreign goods, and it seems to equate "culture" with anything in crinolines. (And pledge-breaks are even more annoying than ads.) Cable has proved a more creative alternative, but it's fragile; HBO's golden era may already be over. Like most of our cultural institutions, CBC is undersubsidized. Like them, it shouldn't be: not just because they create jobs or attract the attention of foreigners, though they do, but because they're good things in themselves. Like them it needs to be funded to a high standard, and then held to it.
