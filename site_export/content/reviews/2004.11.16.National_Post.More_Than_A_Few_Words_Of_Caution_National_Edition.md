CANADA HOUSE

Theatre Passe Muraille Backspace, Toronto

- - -

When a play is titled Canada House and is largely set in a bar of that name, and when it also concerns the activities of a company called Dominion Inc. whose logo is boldly emblazoned on the back wall of the set, it's reasonable to assume that symbolism is being committed.

So, in intention at least, is prophecy. J. Karol Korczynski's play takes place far enough in the future for one of its few amusing lines to be a reference to the Stockwell Day Memorial Golf Tournament, which, to judge from the script's overall tone of voice, is not very far at all. Canada House is one of those allegories about what's in store for us if we don't watch out. Dominion Inc. -- a neatly sinister pun -- is the project of a bold young entrepreneur with aggressive ideas for the mixing and marketing of non-medicinal drugs.

For putting these schemes into practice, he expects and receives respect, in the shape of rewards and awards, from a think-tank known as the Social Market Foundation. He is also into grinding the faces of the poor, big time. For his next trick, he plans to introduce Social Robotics, by which he means reducing your employees to a state where they will do anything you tell them to, the more immoral the better.

I can believe this. What I can't credit is the story that goes with it, at least not as it's told here. Probably for economic reasons, we only meet three characters, slim pickings for a dystopia. There is ruthless Ray, the aspiring dope king, who lives his life as a revenge for the day strikers occupied his father's factory and whose one exploitable weakness is his dependence on kinky phone sex.

There is Sally, a gnarled and feisty ex-everything, who services him in this regard and thus has a hold on him, but who also, by extreme coincidence, becomes one of his wage slaves, except she doesn't get wages. Commuting between them, as an old associate of Sally's and a new employee of Ray's, is Louis, a walking shrugging cliche from Quebec. They all talk a lot, so much so that we are near the end of a long first act before anything remotely gripping happens. I know it's a highly unfair comparison, but I kept remembering the operatic version of The Handmaid's Tale, a not dissimilar cautionary tale that was no more subtle but at least looked good and moved fast.

Canada House itself is a last-chance saloon where the regulars express themselves through karaoke and the occasional comic monologue. Atmospherically, this is quite convincing. The author, according to his bio, has been there, so he can't be accused of vicarious slumming. Dramatically, though, he dawdles, wallowing in local colour while leaving big holes in both the narrative foreground and the political background. He manages, against all odds, to end on an optimistic note, but that too seems self- indulgent, the kind of rallying cry best left to the audience's own response. Throughout the play, speeches -- hopeful, caustic or despairing -- are delivered to extra unseen characters who remain, at least in this production, obstinately unbelievable. The case is not much better with the ones we do see.

The director is Graham Cozzubbo, whose work earlier this year on The Ibsen Project I greatly admired, but who cannot make this piece function. The stage at Passe Muraille Backspace always looks at first glance dauntingly cramped, but it has in the past proved surprisingly flexible. Cozzubbo and his designer, Lee Wildgen, have made one huge scenic miscalculation that starts by being irritating and ends by robbing the play's climactic scene of any effect it might have had.

The actors flounder, each in a different way. Wendy Thatcher, perhaps in reaction to the constrained gentlewoman roles that generally come her way at the Shaw Festival, plays Sally's outspokenness not as the woman's natural mode but as if she were trying to impress someone. As Ray, Brian Marler deals entertainingly with his and the play's best moment, a creepy parody of an Oscar acceptance speech; everything else he plays with an embarrassing lack of conviction. As Louis, Daniel Kash seems to be staking everything on his accent and losing. The sense of the role, and hence of much of the play, drowns in the sound. The Canada House Collective, presenting the show, has plans regarding political theatre that sound virtuous, if not especially original. One can only wish them, and us, better luck next time.
