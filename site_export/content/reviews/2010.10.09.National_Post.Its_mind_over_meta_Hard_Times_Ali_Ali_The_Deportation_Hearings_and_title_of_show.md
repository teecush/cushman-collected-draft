The puppets are taking over. They're everywhere. They'll soon be pulling all our strings. There are, though, no strings on Hard Times, the newest and most ambitious show from Puppetmongers. The dolls in their adaptation of the Dickens novel are, in a departure for this troupe, glove puppets. They are supplemented by other devices; there is much use of silhouette and shadow-play, and the three human performers also appear in their own persons, full length and generally wearing masks. Much has had to be cut of course, even from Dickens' shortest and severest novel, but Chris Earle's adaptation and direction manage to present a remarkably complete account of its setting, ethos and principal characters.

Some of these characters are passed, vocally as well as physically, from one performer to another, and even from one convention to another. The puppetmongers themselves, David and Ann Holmes, make up two-thirds of the cast, and as actors they have their limitations. David's range seems confined to geniality, though this comes in very handy for the role of Mr. Sleary, the circus manager (here deprived of his lisp) who represents the forces of generosity and imagination that Dickens opposes to the pitiless aridity of utilitarian philosophy and the downright inhumanity of the Manchester mills and factories that are its practical expression. Ann seems to have no range at all; her delivery is, perhaps appropriately, wooden, and on the first night she often seemed uncertain of her lines. Non-acting is preferable, though, to bad acting; and the neutrality of her performance actually pays off in the central role of school superintendent Gradgrind, arch-advocate of Fact and arch-enemy of Fiction, whose principles stunt the emotional growth of his own children and who becomes a tragic figure because of it. An uninflected rendition of a studiedly dispassionate man balances out and is moving. Anand Rajaram, who has plenty of acting and comedy credits in his resume, overdoes the role of Gradgrind's crony Bounderby, the fraudulently self-made mill owner, who emerges as a mouthful of north-country vowels with hardly a consonant to be heard. But he compensates with brilliant portrayals of James Harthouse, the languid cad who finds himself in Parliament without the faintest notion why, and of Stephen Blackpool, the honest illiterate mill-hand who finds everything "a muddle" and who is indeed crushed between millstones: between his mates, who shun him when he refuses to join a strike, and Bounderby, who fires him anyway. Stephen's tangled love life has been sternly excised, and he emerges here as a genuine working-class hero, rather than the cardboard martyr he is in the book.

All these characters are among the walking masked. The younger Gradgrinds and Sissy Jupe, the circus orphan taken in as the Gradgrind maid and eventual moral conscience, are handpuppets who amusingly sprout extra inches before our eyes as they enter adolescence. Puppet as she is, Louisa, married lovelessly off to Bounderby, becomes a searching study in emotional blight; you feel she's lucky to get out of the story alive. Bounderby's genteel housekeeper Mrs. Sparsit sadistically imagines Louisa falling down a staircase of disillusion, and the image is here brought before us as a shadow-play upon a screen. The same device is briefly used to evoke a parliamentary meeting: one of the few moments at which Earle overplays the story's obvious, and devastating, contemporary relevance. Another is the provision of Mr. Sleary with-- rather too appropriately -- a ventriloquist's dummy to voice the subversive sentiments that Dickens himself inconveniently left unspoken. His method, a far more effective and imaginative one, was to let his enemies condemn themselves out of their own mouths, as in Bounderby's definition of his workers' ambition to be paid a decent wage as a desire to ride in a coach-and-four and be fed on turtle soup. (He dismisses Stephen -- in two senses -- as "another of the turtle-soupers.") It's also odd that Earle has the novel's subdivisions -- Part One: Sowing; Part Two, Reaping -- inscribed upon the stage but leaves out Part Three: Garnering, especially as the harvesting process is so powerfully presented. Dickens' central and recurrent insight, the equation of philistinism and oppression, comes over beautifully. The show takes time to get going, but once it's established its terms, it works wonderfully. And Earle, with this and the current Second City revue, has two of the best shows in Toronto to his credit.

Dickens could have taught some moderns a few things about political satire. Ali and Ali, who gave us a terrific show about reactions to terrorism some seasons ago, return with Ali and Ali: The Deportation Hearings, in which our two Middle Eastern comedians undergo the process described in the title. It's a dogged performance that carries on as if there has to be something funny here somewhere but never locates it. Since the Alis are interrupted in the course of presenting their own self-confessedly awful show about a President Osbama, there's a lot of meta-joking, the highlight being the anguished comment of Paul Sun-Hyung Lee ( "our added Mongolian") that they should have hired a fight-captain. He deserves better material. So really do the Alis themselves, Guillermo Verdecchia and Marcus Youssef, though sympathy is limited by the fact that they wrote the show with Camyar Chai (and Verdecchia directed it). Completing the cast as an investigating Mountie is Anita Majumdar, uncomfortably out of her element. The show gets good when it gets serious, but this happens very briefly and very near the end.

[title of show] is what two musical-theatre creators and their two actor-friends entered on a festival application form when asked for the title of their show. The finished piece depicts the evolution of a musical from those festival beginnings to the Vineyard Theatre, off-Broadway, to the Lyceum Theatre, on it. This is the exact trajectory pursued by the show about the show; it's all, again, very meta, and it's fun to imagine the frantic re-writing and re-existing that must have gone on as art and life panted to keep up with one another. There's fun, too, in the four performances of Angelwalk Theatre's Toronto production and in Tim French's neat direction. But without the original creators playing themselves, and with an uninvolving book and an undistinguished score, the show seems like pointless camp: narcissism at one remove.
