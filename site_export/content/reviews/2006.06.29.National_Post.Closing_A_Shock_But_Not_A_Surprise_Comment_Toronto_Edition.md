Kevin Wallace, the British producer of The Lord of the Rings, blames the show's early Toronto demise on the critics. He may well be right. As he says, if youre being asked to shell out $120 for a theatre ticket, you need the affirmation." In this case the Toronto critics, or most of them, were decidedly non-affirmative.

Personally, I can plead not guilty. I liked the show. But Ive been in the opposite position too often to deny my colleagues their right to disagree. Nor do I think it's true that critics from outside Canada were uniformly more enthusiastic than the locals: The New York Times was vitriolic, and as for the London critics --well, it depends on who you read: The Times was enthusiastic, The Daily Telegraph the reverse. The Guardian was favourable, but I wrote that one so it probably doesn't count.

There were, I think, enough positive responses to have persuaded anyone who read all the reviews and judiciously compared them, that this was a show worth taking a chance on. But of course such people dont exist. Most rely either on the one paper they read or, more crucially, on a hunch, a general, undocumented sense that a particular show has been praised by everyone.

There are shows that prosper hugely no matter what the critics may have said. Some recent examples in Toronto are Beauty and the Beast, The Lion King and Mamma Mia. These are all shows for which the audience went into the theatre humming the tunes. The Lion King was an imaginative production, while the other two shows were deplorable, but it really didnt matter. Audiences went because they wanted more of the same.

The Lord of the Rings didn't have that advantage. It may have looked pre-sold; it was based on a hugely successful book that had been turned into three hugely successful movies. But those movies weren't musicals, so the show lacked a crucial element of built-in reassurance.

If people had trouble believing in the possibility of staged Tolkien, they had even more trouble crediting the idea of staged Tolkien with songs. What they did know about the book and the films were that they were long, and this made them nervous.

John Karastamatis, director of communications for Mirvish Productions, which presents the show in Toronto, said people would phone the box office asking if it were true the show lasted nine hours. The first preview actually lasted five; by the time of the official first night it was down to three and a half, but psychologically the damage had been done.

The closing in September is a shock but not a surprise. The shock is partly due to the announcements coming just two days after Torontos Dora awards, for which The Lord of the Rings had been nominated in a record 15 categories and won seven. Now, the Doras don't have anything like the commercial clout of the Tonys. Still, one doesn't envision the producers meeting the morning after and saying, "All those prizes; well have to close.

Obviously, the decision must have been taken earlier, and there have been mutterings of the shows departure for months, practically since it opened. In fact, according to Karastamatis, it hasn't been losing money. But neither, playing at 70% or 80% of capacity rather than the hoped-for 90%, has it been making enough to arouse confidence that it could run happily through the summer when audiences tend to dwindle. The final blow, ironically, was the announcement of the show's London opening next May at the Theatre Royal, Drury Lane. The elaborate Toronto sets and machinery are being shipped to Britain, for extensive reworking. This will give the Canadian investors, who include the province of Ontario, a stake in the London production and even a chance to recoup their investment.

What does the shows Toronto failure mean to the Canadian theatre? Artistically, its sad and a blow to morale. We are losing an imaginative and adventurous production, which audiences, when they actually saw it, enjoyed. For the Mirvishes (who are the commercial theatre in Toronto), it must mean confirmation of the lesson already taught by the disappointing showings of The Producers and Hairspray: the days of the guaranteed hit musical with an open- ended run are over.

The pattern now will be to bring in touring productions for limited periods and bring them back if they take off. This is already happening with Wicked, and they must be hoping that it will happen again with Spamalot. This in turn means that the days in which Toronto was a city that Americans would visit just to see shows are gone. SARS and 9/11 put a dent in that, and the rise in the Canadian dollar and the increased tourist-friendliness of New York have reinforced it. The theatre may be an adjunct of our tourist industry, I doubt if it can ever be the basis of it.

To the other theatres and theatre companies in the city -- Soulpepper, CanStage, Tarragon and right on down the line -- it will make no practical difference at all. They're structured, rightly, to play to locals.

To most Canadian theatres outside Toronto, it will mean even less. The exceptions perhaps are the Stratford and Shaw festivals, which are dependent on tourists, especially Americans. But according to Antoni Cimilino, Stratford's executive director, people who are devoted to classical theatre, come every year, and may take in a whole week of plays.

But the end of The Lord of the Rings does cast what Tolkien's characters would call a shadow. It's psychological. As Cimilino says, We needed this to be a success, to shine a spotlight on our theatre."
