THEATRE REVIEW THE BOY FRIEND

Thursday, June 01, 1995

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Stratford, Ont. -- Written by Sandy Wilson Directed by Brian Macdonald Starring Patty Jamieson, Danny Austin, Eric Donkin, Karen K. Edissi and Cara Hunter At the Stratford Festival until Oct. 28 Rating: The Boy Friend a limited masterpiece Reviewed BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to the Globe and Mail Stratford, Ont. ACKNOWLEDGED masterpiece of the British musical though it is, The Boy Friend has yet to be seen at Britain's National Theatre. This may be because the current director of that institution, the estimable Richard Eyre (whose own acting career virtually ended with a role in the show) has a block about it. He has dismissed it as insipid nostalgia fodder for remote outposts of the Empire. He may not, of course, have had the Stratford Festival directly in mind.

I can call The Boy Friend a masterpiece without blushing, but I have to admit that it is a masterpiece of a peculiarly limited kind. In the mid- 1950s, Sandy Wilson came up with book, music and lyrics for what he called "a new musical comedy of the 1920s." He faithfully observed every convention. He set his story on the French Riviera, where rich, sweet young ladies at finishing school pretend to be typists in order to romance handsome young delivery boys who turn out to be aristocrats in disguise. Meanwhile, more madcap flappers disport themselves with young American millionaires, not in disguise. The action regularly stops for wistfully virginal ballads or exuberant dances, cued in with breathtaking (and frankly delicious) artlessness.

Compared with actual musicals of the twenties, The Boy Friend is a very gentle show, with no set-piece comedy routines and the merest wisp of a mistaken-identity plot. Wilson's songs are stylistically spot-on; his rhymes have just the right fanatical ease ("psychic/I kick") and the music, however derivative in theory, sparkles in its own right: the love- duets, I Could Be Happy with You and A Room in Bloomsbury, are at least as good as the Vincent Youmans and Noel Coward numbers that inspired them, and the title number is a worthy partner for Rodgers and Hart's Girl Friend.

The show, and especially its score, has tremendous conviction; as if Wilson truly believed that this was the only way to write musicals. (None of his later shows, in different styles, achieved anything like the same success, though he wrote an interestingly pungent thirties sequel to The Boy Friend called Divorce Me, Darling. Some enterprising theatre - hey, what about the Stratford Festival? - should revive them in tandem.) The Boy Friend isn't nostalgia, though most productions of it are. It isn't parody; it may not even be pastiche. It's a distillation, that separates out one of its mythical period's salient qualities: its innocence. What makes the show more durable than its role models is that, with all its silliness, it makes you care about heroine Polly Browne and her all too suitable suitor. Even if you smile at the style, you should believe in the people.

The balance, of course, is devilishly difficult to strike. Brian Macdonald's production cannot resist letting everybody pout, posture and preen too much; while it practically signals its willingness to do anything for a laugh by having one of its women played (not very well) by a man. More damagingly, it has a Polly (Patty Jamieson) whose talents are more soubretish than ingenuous, matched with a boy friend (Ian Simpson) who is the height of nondescript. Eric Donkin and Douglas Chamberlain do reliable senior-comic work; Donkin, the most restrained person on stage, looks woefully hilarious when kitted-out as a reluctant sultan at a fancy- dress ball, and Chamberlain shakes a mean elderly leg when paired with a flapper called Dulcie (inspired name) in the show's most infallible number, It's Never Too Late To Fall In Love (boop-a-doop, boop-a-doop, boop-a-doop). Choreographic verve carries the show through; Macdonald is a whiz at sneaking extra characters on stage so that a small ensemble becomes a big one without you noticing; and in a first-act Charleston and a thoroughly abandoned finale, the stage explodes.
