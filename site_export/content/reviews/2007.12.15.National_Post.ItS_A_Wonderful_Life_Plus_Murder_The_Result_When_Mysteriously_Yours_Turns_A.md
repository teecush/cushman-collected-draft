The conventions governing the interactive murder mysteries at the Mysteriously Yours dinner theatre are as rigid as those for Japanese Kabuki or Greek Old Comedy. You start with a source, some popular movie that's taken on iconic significance. Thus the current show, It's a Wonderful Death!, is inspired, as hardly needs saying, by the 1946 Frank Capra-James Stewart picture of almost the same name that television and American sentimentality have turned into a Christmas classic. You assemble its characters, or variations on them, in a confined space. You isolate one of them -- usually some old geezer with more money and less charity than is good for him -- and establish that each of the others has reason to wish him dead. Then you kill him. He will be played by Jean Daigle, director, co-writer and star; his early disposal in one role makes it possible for him to return in another -- generally a bulky cop with a Newfie accent - - and investigate his own demise. He will interrogate the other actors, and also members of the audience who have been summarily issued with temporary identities and even the occasional spoken line. He will have us all vote on the murderers identity, and at the end he will say which of us got it right. I never do.

Sometimes it works, sometimes it doesnt. It has a lot to do with the fecundity of the material that the authors have chosen to lampoon. ("Parody" is too flattering a word, plunder too harsh.) If your target is The Godfather, you can expand it to draw on the whole tradition of gangster movies, and the audience's awareness of it. But It's a Wonderful Life isnt a genre picture in that sense. Its a one-off, a moralizing fantasy: Instead of mobsters, it features angels; in place of a godfather, God. George Bailey, a.k.a. Jimmy Stewart, is in despair at his failure to protect his savings-and-loan clients from the depredations of the local Mr. Big, but a heavenly emissary saves him by showing him--in a reverse-Scrooge effect -- how much worse his world would have been had he never existed. It's an alternate-universe movie.

One result of this is that the characters, whichever plane of reality they may currently be inhabiting, are shadowy. The nefarious Mr. Potter, George's wife, Mary, his fighter-pilot brother Harry, their feckless Uncle Billy, Violet the local tramp -- none of them are substantial enough to make fun of. A good joke has to have its basis in some kind of reality. Anyway thats the best excuse I can offer for the flatness of the script here. The authors -- Brian Caws, Brian Smith and Daigle -- even fail, for the first time in my experience, to offer solid reasons to suspect every one of the characters. Its one thing not to know who did it; it's another not to care.

I never thought I'd say such a thing about a Mysteriously Yours production, but this show may be the victim of its own sophistication. It keeps the motif of God sending down an angel to take care of good ol George. This deity doesnt run to a burning bush, but he does speak -- in much the best effect of the evening -- from within a suddenly illuminated chandelier. (And his voice, not too surprisingly, is the voice of Daigle.) This seraph, recruited from somewhere far in the East (that is, beyond Ottawa),manifests himself as the investigating cop -- not, I would say, an easy fit and one that the show has no idea what to do with. Still, it makes the attempt and even goes metaphysical on us in the process. We must, we are told, find a motivation that will fit our suspect in both worlds. This fairly made my head spin, and I gave up on any serious attempt to spot the culprit. When the time came to hand in our ballots, I didn't exactly spoil mine, but I did vote for Mr. Potter as the author of his own demise; he seemed to have by far the best reason. I was wrong, of course, and my conclusion (in which, incidentally, I was not alone) was roundly scorned by Daigle at the last judgment. I have to say, though, that when the truth was revealed, it struck me as cheating, even by the elastic standards previously established around here. Maybe the real truth is that It's a Wonderful Life was never meant to be a murder story, or even the farfetched excuse for one; its too, well, bloodless. Maybe they were confusing it with Its a Wonderful World, a Stewart vehicle of the previous decade, that really was a comedy-thriller. Though, since it isn't a famous title, probably not.

What compensations can I list? The menu, though I wish I'd ordered the steak. A couple of musical numbers for Brigitte Solem as Violet; shes flat -- her voice anyway -- but I think thats the point. She welcomes George's brother back from the war with I'm Just Wild about Harry, fitted out with wonderfully excruciating, new lyrics. "Those Axis powers/They fought for hours." Daigle, who as that "scurvy little spider" looks and sounds like James Gandolfini playing Winston Churchill, and who, in his subsequent police persona, manages as ever to make bullying the audience seem positively endearing. We know its for our own good. I wonder, as Ive done before, whether this superb comedian might be lured away from his present, obviously profitable activity and persuaded to act in, say, Moliere. I wonder similarly whether the whole Mysteriously caboodle might be persuaded to expand its horizons and try some other format. Or at least if it might acknowledge that its performance space actually includes a stage and that it might utilize it from time to time instead of leaving the cast to weave ceaselessly between the customers' tables.
