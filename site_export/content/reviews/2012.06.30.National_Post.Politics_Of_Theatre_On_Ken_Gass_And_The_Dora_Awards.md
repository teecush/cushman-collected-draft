Just over a week ago, the board of Toronto's Factory Theatre caused a sensation by announcing that they had fired their long-serving artistic director, Ken Gass. Gass's service has been exceptionally long, since he's had the job twice. He actually started the Factory way back in 1970 and, 15 years ago, returned to run it again, sinking some of his own money into it to save it from a threatened closure. This record obviously places him in a strong moral and artistic position. It's strengthened by the fact that the issues between him and the board are cloudy. Gass hasn't said much, and what the board has said sounds petty.

About all that seems agreed is that Gass wanted major structural renovations to the place that would, among other things, make it more wheel chair accessible. The board, while agreeing in principle, decided that Gass's proposals were too expensive. On Wednesday, they issued a statement citing unspecified "irreconcilable differences" and saying that "this wonderful institution - is in great shape, and we are moving ahead with our new season and plans to renovate our building." This, of course, is the kind of thing that always gets said in such circumstances, and it hardly addresses the difficulty of finding a new artistic director at short notice or of how that director will organize the next season, at even shorter notice and in a climate of inevitable suspicion. George F. Walker, Factory's senior playwright, has already, as a gesture of solidarity with Gass, announced the withdrawal of a scheduled new play of his own

The Factory building could certainly use a facelift. Still, it seems to me that, given the way theatres and other organizations are governed and financed, the board was within its rights - was, in fact, doing its job - in deciding what it could or could not afford. It also seems that the right thing in these circumstances would have been to state this position to Gass and to give him the option of accepting it or not. If he couldn't accept it, he would have had to resign. This would still have caused bad blood, but nothing like the amount that's been caused by simply ousting him. It might also have made for a relatively peaceable transition period. The present furore is reminiscent, if on a smaller scale, of the scandal that rocked the Stratford Festival in 1980 (and that's being rather clumsily memorialized in the current Festival production of The Pirates of Penzance), when the incoming artistic directors unofficially dubbed the Gang of Four were summarily dismissed because the board thought - wrongly as it turned out - that they could secure somebody more famous. The Factory board, though, don't even appear to have a glamorous replacement up their sleeve.

Gass was offered a severance package and the title of artistic director emeritus. It's hard not to agree with his description of the latter proposal as "a ludicrous token position." It's the kind of gesture that committees make when they're feeling embarrassed. If there are artistic, as opposed to architectural, disagreements between Gass and the board, then neither side is coming clean about them. The Factory's track record under Gass has been up and down, but I wouldn't expect that to be different under any conceivable successor. The theatre has built its identity on doing Canadian, and only Canadian, plays - mainly new ones. There are not, and are unlikely ever to be, enough of those to make up seasons composed entirely of winners.

Of course, you never know. The late John Hirsch, in 1981, did manage to save Stratford's bacon when all had thought it irretrievably lost. And who, a year or two ago, would have guessed that the Toronto house to emerge most triumphant at this season's Doras would be Theatre Passe Muraille? TPM housed and coproduced Ride the Cyclone, which took the award for best touring production (and would undoubtedly have taken more if the Doras' rules weren't so arcane), and Crash, which won in four categories, including two of the sexiest: best play and best female performance. Both of those statuettes went to the same lady, actress and playwright Pamela Mala Sinha. Since she was also in the cast of The Penelopiad, which won best ensemble, she this year received two-and-one thirteenth Doras. This probably isn't a record, but it's certainly noteworthy.

And there was I, absolutely confident that Kim's Convenience, a triumph of feelgood theatre, would win for best play and best male performance. It lost out in the latter to Nigel Shawn Williams in Topdog/Underdog; I suspect that I discounted Williams and his equally meritorious co-star Kevin Hanchard because I'd never registered that show, an import from the Shaw Festival, as a Toronto production. It was indeed co-produced by the city's own Obsidian Theatre, which also had a year, as it was responsible along with the musical specialists Acting Up Theatre, for Caroline or Change, which cleaned up on a scale that even I hadn't anticipated.

The Dora show itself was one of the better ones with Eric Peterson, in the home stretch, qualifying for an award himself as best scene-stealing presenter. Maybe next year they should make him the host. After all, he can even sing; though perhaps not as well as Thom Allison, who presided this year in glittering style, though his presumably satirical opening number might have been even more devastating if the sound system had let us hear any of the words.

While I'm on a mea culpa kick: I've been justifiably assailed on the National Post website for misattributing songs and sketches that I said were missing from Stratford's You're a Good Man, Charlie Brown. Actually they're from another show entirely, or almost entirely: a show called Snoopy!!! that was a follow-up to the original Clark Gesner show, but in the way of sequels, never caught on the same extent. It is, though, at least as good, and has an excellent score by the underrated Larry Grossman: not that I want to give anybody any ideas. Anyway, my bad: I'm currently in sackcloth, and have sent out for some ashes.
