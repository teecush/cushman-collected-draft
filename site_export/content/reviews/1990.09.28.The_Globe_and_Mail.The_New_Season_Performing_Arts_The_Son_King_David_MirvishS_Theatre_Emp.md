THE NEW SEASON PERFORMING ARTS THE SON KING David Mirvishs theatre empire, stretching from Toronto across the continent and to London, is based on the principle of talk softly and carry a big hit

Friday, September 28, 1990

ROBERT CUSHMAN

By ROBERT CUSHMAN There is a theatrical producer in New York of whom it has been said thateverybody hates him at first sight, because it saves time. David Mirvish has theopposite problem. Everybody likes this gentle, soft-spoken man as soon as theymeet him; I have yet to encounter anyone who has had cause to change thisopinion. Nor does it help his credibility as Canada's major theatrical powerfigure that as his hairline recedes he gets to look increasingly like his evenquieter father the self-made, self-satirizing tycoon, Honest Ed. Still, there must be iron in there somewhere as well as a lot of disposableincome. Both will be needed this fall, as, after more than a year of housing thehit musical Les Miserables, Mirvish and his venue, The Royal Alexandra Theatre,have returned to limited runs and subscription audiences. Meanwhile the worldaround them has, at least superficially, changed. Toronto now has a commercial theatre sector.

Anyway it has commercial theatres.There, sharing the same block on Yonge between Dundas and Queen, are GarthDrabinsky at the Pantages and Marlene Smith with the upstairs-downstairscombination of The Elgin and Winter Garden. Toronto may not be the hub of worldtheatre but it can certainly claim to be the world capital of theatrerenovation. Even The Alex's plush comforts pale beside the gleam of the Pantagesand the magnificence of The Elgin and Winter Garden. Never mind the product.Feel the foyers. In fact, Toronto still lacks a commercial theatre as the term is understood inLondon and New York. There it means putting a show on, and hoping it will runforever. That is the scene that The Royal Alex got into with its long fit of theMiserables; its how Drabinsky opened the Pantages with The Phantom of theOpera, whose cast has been signed up for a second years contract. But inToronto these megamusicals, which invariably begin their existence somewhereelse, are the only products with that kind of life expectancy. No ordinarymusical will do the trick, and no play either. This fact is what marks Torontoas a provincial theatre city. There's something depressing about the inevitability of the behemothproductions. Granted, they are enormous risks at source but once successfullylaunched, they hardly have to prove themselves. The one-to-one relationship ofan audience to a show, the true lifeblood of the theatre, disappears, andworldwide marketing takes over. They say of these musicals that the show is thestar" it would be more realistic to say that the hype is. David Mirvish doesnot have the Canadian rights to Miss Saigon, the new musical from the creatorsof Les Mis. However, he was able, when I met him, to offer me coffee in a MissSaigon cup a memento of the London production. That said a lot. Garth Drabinsky does have the rights to the most recent Andrew Lloyd Webbermusical, Aspects of Love, but, as he is not about to close The Phantom, no houseof his own to put it in. Since he wanted the province to lease him The Elgin andWinter Garden in the first place, he would have to be a saint not to be castingcovetous eyes down the block. It is, after all, a law of nature that no LloydWebber show shall go homeless; Drabinsky will solve his problem somehow.Meanwhile The Elgin and Winter Garden, under Marlene Smith, steer a middlecourse, lacking either sure- fire marathon attractions or a year's worth of thekind of guaranteed mixed bill that makes up a subscription season. Living handto mouth is perilous, and Smith sometimes finds herself wondering whetherToronto has a big enough audience to sustain all the theatre that is currentlybeing thrown at it. So, back into this competitive local arena comes David Mirvish, who for thepast few years has been in charge of an international theatre operation: Ed hasgradually bowed out. "I ask my fathers opinion but I make the decisions. Ithink people go into this business to be pretty independent. This September hisindependent organization, Mirvish Productions, has six separate shows on theboards or in rehearsal in Toronto, London and at various stops on the NorthAmerican road. That includes both coproductions and plays specially mounted byMirvish himself for his own faithful Toronto audience. One hopes it is faithful, not only for Mirvishs sake but for Toronto's. It isimportant that at the heart of the city there should be an established theatrewith a changing and varied bill. The Royal Alex matters, even to those theatreworkers and theatre fans who would never go to it. It defines the middlebrowground. Changes," says David Mirvish, "come out of the times, and even anaudience that's behind the times is affected by them. His father firmly advocated the subscription system; when he bought LondonsOld Vic, he claimed it would be that theatres salvation. But Londoners with 50shows to choose from on a given night declined to commit themselves to onetheatre months in advance. Subscription presupposes an audience attracted bybrand names and security, and the idea may be outmoded. Now, even in Toronto, Edis having doubts. David says, My father sometimes envisions a theatre withoutsubscribers: a theatre with open-ended runs." But for this season David is sticking to the package deal. An engaging mixtureof idealism and caution, he sounds shy but certain. "I was one of the audience,and I wanted to be better entertained. I see the theatre as something thatexists for a brief period of time, and while it lasts I want to be absorbed byit." But this prophet of evanescence and nostalgia is well aware of the need fora show to stay around if it is to pay for itself and for others lessfortunate. For weeks now there has been a large glossy photo of Albert Finney outside TheAlex. Finney, coming to this theatre near you in his latest London vehicle,Another Time, is the one marquee name The Alex's season can boast, and naturallyhe is being plugged. (Subscription audiences have not quite cottoned to the ideaof the play being the star. They think the star is the star.) At the other endof the scale, and the tail end of the season, is Dry Lips Oughta Move toKapuskasing, the Native play that scooped the 1989 Dora Awards. Putting thisplay on his bill has earned Mirvish unprecedentedly warm words from the leadersof Toronto's nonprofit theatres, who dont go around being as nice aboutDrabinsky or Smith. Thats unfair: Both moguls have in their time promoted plays from the fringefor commercial runs, as is common practice in London and New York. ButDrabinsky's transfer of George F. Walker's Love and Anger to the Bluma AppelTheatre met only limited success; perhaps the ideas time has not yet come inToronto. For The Phantom, Drabinsky draws in audiences from hundreds of miles,in all directions; its difficult to imagine the tourists flocking to Walker. For now, Toronto has two different theatre worlds The Phantom audience isnot being siphoned off from Theatre Passe Muraille. But these worlds do have tocoexist, and people do move from one to another. Bill Glassco, until the end ofSeptember the interim artistic direetor of the Canadian Stage Company, directsat The Royal Alex this season a Broadway hit, The Heidi Chronicles, whichMirvish is coproducing with the nonprofit Manitoba Theatre Centre. Meanwhile,Mirvish sends Les Mis on tour (in Montreal it will be retranslated into French such is the cultural unity of show biz) and brings it back for a limited seasonin 1991. The Royal Alex is the Toronto theatre scene's nearest approach to abroad church. It might, in an odd way, be regarded as our national theatre. Itcannot do everything but it can do more than most. Dry Lips oughta move to KingStreet. It could become a habit.
