MURDER ON THE LIMELIGHT EXPRESS

Limelight Dinner Theatre, Toronto

Mysteriously Yours, Mystery Dinner Theatre has been operating in Toronto for upwards of a dozen years now, playing in odd taverns and working the corporate circuit, doing no harm to anyone. They have now put down roots at the Limelight Dinner Theatre with a show called Murder on the Limelight Express, which -- obviously with that title -- is a spoof tailored to the locale.

I'm not sure that it's reviewable, at least not by any normal criteria, but it is some fun. It's what you call an experience: You go in and you eat.

The Limelight is a large, split-level room that, like so many in the real world, would be a very pleasant restaurant if it weren't for the food. Still, if you drink enough, you should be able to get into an appropriately receptive mood. You may even believe that you are in the dining car of a good old-fashioned European train, hurtling across the country in nostalgic luxury.

Actors come among you to encourage you in this delusion, dressed as collectors and inspectors. They chat you up and maybe ask you what you do for a living.

It's difficult to isolate the beginning of the play proper. There is a teaser in which a shot rings out and someone slumps over his table. We are then harangued by an implausibly slim young man claiming to be Hercule Poirot, Agatha Christie's famously pudgy Belgian detective, here to solve the case. He is speedily put in his place by the real Poirot, who rebukes him for the feebleness of his imposture.

This new Hercule is played by Jean Daigle, a founding father of the troupe, and his efforts are, well, Herculean. He directed the show and shares an author credit (with "Brian Caws, Andrew Welch & the Cast"). He speaks his lines Poirot-fashion (i.e. he is crisp and bossy and always refers to himself in the third person) and he governs the plot, the audience and his fellow actors with impeccable aplomb.

The train stops, because a man lies dead on the track. He is the rich owner of the railway and, naturally, the express is crowded with people just bursting with motives for doing him in. There are discarded mistresses, disgruntled employees and disinherited offspring -- he's been dissing everybody. The characters of course are not just stock but caricatures of stock. This makes them better and funnier company than the usual denizens of a whodunnit, but it does make it difficult to take seriously the question of what it was they've done.

I'm appallingly dense at picking up clues at the best of times. On this occasion it didn't even dawn on me that I was meant to be doing so until halfway through. I got a considerable jolt when I realized that the audience was going to be polled on the identity of the murderer. Those who were right would be rewarded and those who were wrong would be held up to public ridicule. I should have been among the latter, since I fingered the detective himself; not that I had any evidence, it's just that I'd longed for years to see that Brussels know-it-all given a taste of his own medicine.

Others in the audience apparently shared my feelings and, as had been threatened, their names were named. I was spared; maybe the PR department had blown my cover. Anyway, the real solution seemed very ingenious, as far as I could follow it, and I congratulate those who got it right.

The actors make extensive use of the audience, many of whom are drafted to play small parts, some of them involving lines. The best of them were amazingly good and should be retained for the run. You want appearance and reality, life and art, you got it.
