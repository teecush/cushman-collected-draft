HEART OF A DOG

Artword Theatre, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - A dogs life is much like a mans -- nasty, brutish and short -- only more so. That is as much of a message as I can glean from Heart of a Dog, a show in which presentational flair considerably outdistances clarity of intent.

The piece has a complicated history, and comes at us from a number of cultural and linguistic removes. It started out in 1925 as a novel by Mikhail Bulgakov, the Russian writer whose affection for the old order (see his play The White Guard) and skepticism toward the new made him a special pet of that tolerant old sentimentalist Joseph Stalin. Bulgakov's work got banned, this story included, but he himself was protected; he was even given a theatre to run, to keep him out of trouble.

Anne Nenarokoff, a Canadian writer, turned the Russian book into a French play, which was presented last month by Theatre francais de Toronto. This, in turn, has been translated into English by Nenarokoff and her husband, John Van Burek, and the production has been remounted by the same director with most of the same cast.

We are presented with Professor Philip Philipovitch, a doctor practising in Russia in the early post-revolutionary period, whose specialty is rejuvenation by transplant and whose waiting room is regularly crowded with those who can afford his fees. Expanding his patient base, he inserts the sexual organs and pituitary gland of a dead young man into a living dog. It works.

The dog gets up on its hind legs, puts on a suit of clothes and talks, though less decorously than the doctor, his assistant and his nurse would wish. (What we hear of his conversation sounds innocuous enough, but things have moved on since the '20s.) His bathroom habits are distressing, and he takes to sneaking into the nurses quarters. And he gets into bad company, chiefly that of a housing apparatchik intent on expelling the doctor from an apartment suite of counter-revolutionary spaciousness. But then he was an ill-bred dog in the first place.

My opening citation of Thomas Hobbes was glib, like everybody elses, and requires some modification. The celebrated Hobbesian estimation of human life referred to man's fate and behaviour if not put on a leash by authority. Sharik, the former canine, is plenty leashed; he has, indeed, two kinds of authority to contend with: that of the medics who made him and that of the state.

The two are not working in concert. The bureaucrat's suspicions are fully justified; the professor may claim that he was trying, under laboratory conditions, to create "a good Socialist citizen, but his truer feelings seem reflected in his reference to this farce that calls itself a revolution."

He thinks that he, like his author, has friends in high places, but he is brutally disabused by the end. (His nurse, scenting danger, has already attempted a getaway.) So the overreaching scientist comes off almost as a freedom fighter. This may be intended as complexity, but it registers as confusion; we are never sure what is being satirized.

Sharik, meanwhile, takes his place, alongside the heroes of such more-or-less contemporary Russian plays as Mayakovskys The Bed Bug and Erdmans The Suicide, in a gallery of unregenerates making their scruffy individual protests against the brave new world.

He differs from them, though, in apparently wanting to join it; his protest lies not in what he does but in what he incorrigibly is. On two legs or four, he's a walking objection to the idea of human perfectibility, but the play doesn't seem to like him any more than it likes anybody else.

This may not be the case in the novel, which I havent read but which I gather begins with a monologue for the mutt. This might be enough to put us in his corner, or his kennel, but the stage version isnt having any. It keeps everybody at paw's length.

Satires on Practically Everything sound fine in theory, but they get very wearing in practice. As narrative, the play unrolls slowly and, despite its clinical setting and even more clinical air, muddily.

Jean-Stephane Roy is obviously a director to watch, and what he does here is very watchable. It is easier, though, to admire than to enjoy. It is impressively balletic in its deployment both of the performers and of Rudy Braun's sets. The doors are especially graceful.

The actors sometimes wear masks and sometimes exaggerated makeup (though in todays theatre, any makeup looks exaggerated). This gives a distancing effect, presumably intended and conceivably enhanced by the fact that some of the performers are playing in their second language. The effects of this probably arent intentional, but I certainly found the thought processes of Patricia Marceau's nurse hard to follow.

There are vigorous commedia turns by Eric Goulem as the junior medic, and by Martin Albert as the housing man, though breathing new life into this familiar caricature of a slimy functionary is pretty much impossible.

As the dog, Rafal Sokolowski gives, perhaps logically, about the most human performance of the evening. He never sentimentalizes and always seems, at least metaphorically, to be sniffing at his acquaintances' trousers.

He is rivalled for fullness of effect only by William Webster's chief organ grinder, whose eyes have a demonic glitter especially impressive for having to transmit itself from beneath a pair of ferociously bushy eyebrows. At a moment of especial stress he declares that his hair is standing on end -- and so it does, great grey tufts of it. He is, though, rather likeable, as vainglorious scientists go; having the secret police on your tail tends to rank you among the good guys, even if you are in the Frankenstein business.

Besides, there's a cultural thing going on here; Philip Philipovitch would like everybody else to be addressed by name and patronymic like himself, and has a hard time calling anyone Comrade. What audience that has been warmed by Chekhov and chilled by Orwell could fail to sympathize?
