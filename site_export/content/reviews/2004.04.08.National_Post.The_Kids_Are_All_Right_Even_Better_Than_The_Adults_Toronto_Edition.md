NUMBER ONE AND JAMIE

Lorraine Kimsa Theatre for Young People

Alone among Toronto theatres, the former YPT seems to be on a roll. Where the other houses are, at best, treading water, this one is actually improving.

It even seems to be developing a style, one characterized by narrative drive and geometric neatness. These qualities, which were evident in this season's revival of New Canadian Kid, are even more forcefully present in the new and more ambitious, Number One and Jamie. It is, in fact, over-ambitious but thats a good fault, certainly at this address.

Usually, when a childrens theatre mounts a two-act two-hour production, it's of something borrowed from the adult repertoire. Paula Wing's play, by contrast, is custom-made. Jamie is a 10-year old whose problem, like most peoples, is fear.

His parents, currently together, have repeatedly split up in the past, which is enough in itself to make him insecure. His mother doesnt help by constantly worrying about him, though not about the things he worries about. (We don't meet his father; he works long hours and probably wouldn't have the time to put in a personal appearance.) Predictably but still amusingly, Jamie is embarrassed rather than reassured when his mother insists on collecting him from school.

Besides the terrors that really haunt him reside at home; he has a paper route in his own apartment building; fearful of what may lie in wait behind doors or around corners, he counts every one of his early-morning steps.

He lives in special fear of the unseen woman in Apartment One, whom he calls the Gorgon. When he does meet her, she turns out to be the wheelchair-bound landlady. She doesnt mind being called a cripple; "There are," she says, "worse things than insensitivity." Its a refrain with her that there are always worse things; a woman with a sharp and salty tongue, she listens in on her tenants' conversations, both live and telephonic, and is convinced that people are out to get her. She is, in fact, as scared as Jamie, though more entertainingly aggressive about it. She is especially afraid of a blue car parked outside, and part cajoles, part threatens, Jamie into trailing it. Spy and spymistress communicate first by walkie-talkie, later -- at Jamie's insistence and to Number Ones disgust -- by cellphone.

The chase is complicated by Jamies encounters with another lodger, an elderly Hungarian who qualifies, by current theatrical usage, as a second-generation refugee; he escaped, that is, not from the Nazis but from the Communists.

The riddles are only partly solved by the end; frustrating at one level, this is reasonable at another and a considerable compliment to young audiences. What Jamie has wandered in to is the world of adult sorrows, a world in which terrors persist and admit no easy solutions.

A less fruitful complication is Jamie's relationship with his friend of his own age, a boy whom he calls Risky Phil, because he's so risk-averse. Phil is as nervous as Jamie, but his response is to retreat into elaborate technological fantasies. The author is getting at something here but its not clear what it is.

Micheline Chevriers production unfolds in pools and strips of light, admirably designed by Christopher Dennis. Around the twilit edges of Deeter Schurig's ghostly set there lurk and skulk menacing anonymous figures in trench coats and fedoras. It isn't clear why the nightmares of a 21st-century child should be haunted by figures from 1940s film noir (maybe Jamie has been allowed to stay up too late watching television), but they are effective, if ultimately overused. We get into Jamies mind; we understand why, when his flat is burgled, he -- for complicated but childishly logical reasons -- should feel guilty about it.

The relationship between the plays two titular figures is not as central as the author presumably wants it to be; too many other elements jostle for our attention.

Still both Number One and Jamie are admirably played. Robin Craig makes a splendidly witty and fretful spider at the centre of the web; Keir Gilchrist copes very engagingly with what may be the longest role ever written for a pre-teen. (He is the play's narrator as well as its protagonist.)

As his contemporary, Demetrios Matthews has more trouble projecting, and their scenes together are murky. Deborah Drakeford is frazzled and funny as Jamie's mom, Harve Sokoloff capable but predictable in a role that may not permit of anything more.

It remains one of the best plays seen in Toronto this season; more compelling than most of the supposedly grown-up plays we have recently been offered and at least as intelligent.
