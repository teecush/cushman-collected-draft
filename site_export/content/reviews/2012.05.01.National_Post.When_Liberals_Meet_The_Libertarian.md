THEATRE REVIEW

You Can't Take It with You Young Centre, Toronto

As dysfunctional families go, the one in You Can't Take It with You is remarkably functional. They may have their disagreements with the outside world, but among themselves the Vanderhof-Sycamore clan - Mr. Vanderhof 's daughter married a Mr. Sycamore, and the generations and their many associates all live together in a cozy New York house within whistling distance of Columbia University - never exchange a cross word. This probably makes them unique among theatrical families. It would certainly make them unusual among real ones.

That isn't a problem while you're actually watching the play, certainly not in as good a production as Soulpepper's, which finds the company at its warm-hearted, quickwitted best. In 1936, Moss Hart and George S. Kaufman, the joint gold standard-bearers of interwar Broadway, wrote what may be the prototypical screwball comedy, a handsomely structured celebration of principled fecklessness.

Grandpa Vanderhof retired from (unspecified) business years ago, having apparently made enough of a pile to subsist ever after in nonconforming comfort; he occupies himself in attending college commencement ceremonies and comparing their merits. His daughter Penny writes un-performable plays because a typewriter was once delivered to the house by mistake. (I've seen plays that could have been written for worse reasons.) Her husband Paul spends most of his time in the basement, making fireworks with the help of Mr. de Pinna, who once came to make a delivery and never left. Their elder daughter Essie makes candies, and takes ballet lessons from a wannabe Diaghilev who genially confides that, as a dancer, "she stinks." And so it goes.

The first external threat comes from the IRS, who have discovered that grandpa owes them big-time and are unimpressed by his objections to how they might spend his money. A second, more serious threat arises when younger granddaughter Alice falls for the son of her prosperously proper boss, and he for her. Not that anybody in either family objects to young love - the Sycamores, as you might expect, go positively gaga over it - but Alice fears that her prospective in-laws, Mr. and Mrs. Kirby, may object to her family's eccentricities; and her fears, when the two sides have an unscheduled collision, prove justified.

The play is directed by Joseph Ziegler, and it fits beautifully with his unrivalled instinct both as director (Harvey) and actor (The Time of Your Life, A Christmas Carol) for shrewd, joyous benevolence. For the presiding Vanderhof he has Eric Peterson, who commands the scene even before uttering a word, standing on a step, his eyes darting around like those of the pet snakes he keeps in an onstage tank. (Yes, it's that kind of play.) All the same, Ziegler's greatest triumph is to structure the story around Alice, the one character with an emotional stake both in the house and outside it. Krystin Pellerin, who seems more and more to have greatness in her, is instantly, unpretentiously radiant; we not only see but feel the play through her eyes. She's every youngster, both loving her family and embarrassed by them, only more so. The courtship scene she shares with Gregory Prest is a delight, gauche and detailed and surprisingly deep; you're happy for both of them.

Nancy Palk, capping a rather extraordinary run of performances, turns up again as a very good Penny from whom kindness and eccentricity flow unforced. Derek Boyes is admirably gentle as her husband, as is Mike Ross as Essie's husband, who likes to print things and who plays the xylophone, which is about the only thing I've never seen Ross play before.

Essie herself is Patricia Fagan, delightfully and continually en pointe; Diego Matamoros plays her self-appointed inspiration with a rich Russian voice and even richer Russian hair; and Maria Vacratsis is low-key but hilarious as his emigre compatriot, a blintz-dispensing former grand duchess whom the authors craftily introduce to pep up the play in its closing stages.

The play's politics, as far as they can be pinned down, are an odd mixture of liberal and libertarian; there's a depression on, and a war looming, but they're hardly mentioned, though at some level the play knows about them. It's a knowledge that was to be brought to the surface a few years later in the aforementioned The Time of Your Life, whose portrayal of the wealthy drop-out is more apparently fantastical but also more painful.

It's wryly appropriate, too, that for a few days You Can't Take It with You should have co-existed in Toronto with Clybourne Park, which effectively dynamites the whole American family-comedy genre, especially those descendants of Kaufman and Hart who have gone heavy on their progenitors' whimsy and much lighter on the wit. But the original stands up irresistibly. A final word of commendation for Christina Poddubiuk's set and especially for her costumes which, for the women in particular, are beautiful or funny or, quite frequently, both.

You Can't Take It with You runs in repertory until June 20. For tickets, call 416-866-8666 or visit soulpepper.ca.
