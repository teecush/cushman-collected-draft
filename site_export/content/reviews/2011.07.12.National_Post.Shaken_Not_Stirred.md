THEATRE REVIEW

ON THE ROCKS

Court House Theatre,

Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont.

The central character of George Bernard Shaws On the Rocks (1933) is Sir Arthur Chavender, the Liberal head of a British coalition government. Outside 10 Downing St. -and, we gather, throughout the country and indeed the world -huge numbers of the unemployed are out protesting. Well, it's 1933.

All Sir Arthur has to offer them, or anyone else, are affable bromidic speeches about the virtues of the family. In himself, he's a compassionate man and a conscientious worker. So its hardly surprising that hes rundown and depressed. At the urging of his wife, he takes himself off to a sanatorium, run by a mysterious lady who promises him that after two weeks of meditation "your dead mind will come to life." Prompted by a recent meeting with a delegation from London's povertystricken Isle of Cats (a facetious variant of the actual Isle of Dogs) he asks his secretary to pack him all the books you can find by a revolutionary German Jew named Harry Marks. She puts him right about the name.

That's Shaws Act 1. In his Act 2, the regenerated PM attempts to put through a program of wholesale nationalization. He gets initial support from surprising places: the police, the navy, the city and the landed aristocracy all decide theres something in it for them and are prepared to overlook as impracticable the parts that might work for the benefit of anybody else. Chavender only meets implacable opposition from the Conservative leader Sir Dexter Rightside and, extremes meeting, from the very proletarians who inspired him in the first place. They balk at the ideas of compulsory public service ("Slavery," moans Sir Dexter. Call it by its proper name) and a ban on strikes.

For what is only the play's second appearance at the Shaw Festival, and taking advantage of the expiration of Shavian copyright, Michael Healey has prepared a new adaptation. His boldest stroke is to reverse the order of the two acts. The advantage of this is to confront us at the outset with some of the juiciest issues and most explosive characters. The disadvantage is that what was once exposition is now back story, giving us information that at this point of the evening we are not too interested in hearing. More importantly, it removes Chavender himself from his place at the centre of the play; he's now just one voice among many. This is compounded by the casting of Peter Krantz, who has some nice winsome moments of hope and disappointment, but lacks the joviality and the sheer force of personality to convince as one of Shaws compulsive orator-politicians.

Hes also been deprived of what might have been his finest moment: Sir Arthur's acknowledgment that "I'm not the man for the job . and I shall hate the man who will carry it through for his cruelty and the desolation he will bring." This must be part of an attempt on Healeys part to save his deceased collaborators face: to clear him of any sympathy for fascism in the very year of Hitler's accession to power. (The left was attacking Shaw for this long before it occurred to the right to do so.) Healey's Sir Arthur explicitly declines to take Mein Kampf away with him on holiday, though Shaw was ahead of his adaptor in satirizing Sir Dexters threat to put his supporters on the streets in Union Jack shirts. But the suspicion still lingers, notably in the speeches of old Hipney, the veteran working-class delegate who has come to think democracy a sham and says "you cant frighten me with a word like dictator." Some critics have claimed that Shaw meant him as devil's advocate rather than author's mouthpiece but, certainly in Guy Bannermans gently persuasive performance, it doesnt play that way.

As Shavian pastiche, Healey's version is first-rate; you can't tell where one author leaves off and the other starts. It works both ways; a startlingly unShavian word like sexy turns out to be totally authentic. Some jokes have been reassigned, notably one about "the nationalization of women." (Neither Shaw nor Healey explains how thats supposed to work.)

As noted, the new text treats us immediately to some exuberant cartoon characters with strong political underpinnings, nearly all of them, under Joseph Zieglers direction, excellently acted. Steven Sutcliffe's hysterical (in both senses) Dexter is admirably counterpointed by Norman Browning's huffy First Lord of the Admiralty, rosyfaced and rosy-voiced.

Cherissa Richards Singhalese businesswoman lights up the stage when a racial epithet sets her off on a splendidly indignant tirade. Marla McLean, as one alderwoman Aloysia Brollikins, signally fails to do the same with her long speech, but is neat in her less demanding stretches. She and Martin Happer, as a sulky aristocratic revolutionary, form half the plays love interest, as they pair off with the junior Chavenders. There's supposed to be an ironic contrast here between Sir Arthur's paeans to the family and the mess his own is in; but Shaw didnt make it work and Healey cant do it for him.

The original play starts with the police threatening to break the heads of protesters and ends with a description of what sounds like kettling. Its main concerns are an intractable economic crisis and the thwarting of reform by special interests. It all seems, when you read it, blazingly topical. It has some of the same effect onstage but not as much, because the action, what there is of it, doesn't so much carry the ideas as buckle under them. That's true of both versions, though on consideration I prefer the original, ugly warts and all. The rewrite gives the final moment to Chavenders secretary. It doesnt mean much because, even though she's played by Mary Haney, we don't know anything about her. ? On the Rocks runs until Oct. 8. For tickets, call 1-800-511-7429 or visit shawfest.com.
