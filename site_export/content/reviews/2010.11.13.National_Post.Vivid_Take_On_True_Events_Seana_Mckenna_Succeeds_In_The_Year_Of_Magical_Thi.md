Seana McKenna is wonderful. That's almost a given. Her solo performance in The Year of Magical Thinking is a super-intelligent, beautifully controlled account of a woman who prided herself on both her intelligence and her control, and who lost the latter but not the former when confronted by events that, for sanity's sake, she would previously have thought unthinkable. There are very few moments of emotional breakdown in McKenna's performance. When they do occur, they are, of course, all the more lacerating.

Which is all very well, but the play is Joan Didion's adaptation of her own book about an appalling period in her life. First, her married daughter, Quintana, went into a mysterious coma. Then her husband, John Gregory Dunne, like her a writer, suffered a massive heart attack while sitting right there at the dinner table, and died soon after being admitted to hospital. Then her daughter, who had come out of her coma, was stricken again; and she, too, died in hospital. This last event happened after the writing of the book -- just before its publication, in fact--so the play can't strictly be described as a dramatization of the non-dramatic text. And, in fact, it doesn't play like one, but for other reasons, it's more like a very animated recitation. The material hasn't been transformed or reimagined.

The title refers to a coping strategy. Didion finds herself, after her husband's death, imagining that he has simply gone away: to Europe, where the couple spent many happy times. (We're told about them in some detail.) Quintana, in the period between her recovery and her relapse, indulged -- by coincidence, not collusion -- in the same fantasy. "Magical thinking" Didion tells us, "requires thinking 'if.' " It sounds very much like the bargaining stage of the classical grieving progression.

It's a progression that the protagonist has undergone by the time she sets foot on the stage. That's a constant problem with monologues, especially those with a biographical source. It's very hard to make anything external happen, so the action has to take place inside the character. She has to discover something about herself while we watch. In this case, as in many others, the discovery has already happened. She simply tells us about it.

She does it vividly. I've only read extracts from the book, but taken with what I've read of her journalism, I can readily believe it's an elegant, accomplished piece of work, mordantly witty, spare but unsparing. It's a New Yorker level of writing, but different from what would usually appear in that magazine in that the writer is reporting on her own tragedies rather than somebody else's. It's very civilized, though in this instance part of its effect is to show us how thin a protection civilization can be. Fate can strike as mercilessly in Manhattan or Malibu -- Didion's two main points of reference -- as anywhere else. (Though I have to say that the famous names that constitute the author's social circle have a more distancing effect than may have been intended.) McKenna's brains and taste are a close match for those of the text. Maybe too close: some friction, rather than duplication, might actually be welcome. On the carefully chosen occasions on which she unleashes her feral tragedienne's strength, she does bring us closer to the experience of desolation than we might get to by simply reading it for ourselves. These moments, though, are necessarily few, and McKenna is both too sensitive and too technically skilled to overuse them.

Maybe her most powerful moment of all is her first. After holding a long pause (possibly longer on the first night, as she had to wait for the passing of one of the trains that regularly disturb the tensest moments at the Tarragon), she tells us she is about to describe something we are all going to go through. If we don't want to believe it, well, neither did she. We feel disturbed, as intended; we also feel vaguely guilty. I don't think there's any pretence that what we are about to hear will make us feel better in our own turn. The highest, and the most shocking comic moment comes when Didion demands that the TV be turned off in her comatose daughter's hospital room, so that she may not learn of her father's death by hearing it on CNN. She returns to find a sign that reads "Keep TV Off: Dad Dead." Or did she, she wonders, dream that? That is a scene worth sharing with a live audience; it's always best to laugh in company, however ruefully. Michael Shamata's direction and John Ferguson's dark design give the evening some discreet visual interest. But the evening remains a demonstration, not a drama.
