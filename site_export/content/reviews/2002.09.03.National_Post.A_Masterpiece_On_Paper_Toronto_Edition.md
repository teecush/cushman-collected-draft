THE MAIDS

du Maurier Theatre Centre, Toronto

I can never consider the dramatic work of Jean Genet without remembering Kenneth Tynan's description of his play The Blacks as belonging to "that large category of avant-garde plays that sound more like masterpieces in description than in performance." The category, as far as I am concerned, is large enough to accommodate all of Genet's plays, including The Maids, which has been newly presented by Soulpepper.

The Maids is more bearable than The Blacks -- not least because it's much shorter -- but it has only one idea, which it keeps reiterating. This is that servants resent their employers while secretly identifying with them. Strindberg said the same thing in Miss Julie, Soulpepper's other recent opening, but he managed to say other things as well, and in about the same length of time.

Like Miss Julie, The Maids, first produced in Paris in 1947, has three characters. There are Solange and Claire, young-middle-aged domestics who are also sisters, and there is their mistress, known only as Madame. In the first section of the play, the cat is away and the mice are behaving accordingly. Claire dresses up as Madame and torments and patronizes Solange, whom she addresses as Claire. This is partly the girls' satiric comment on the fact that Madame always gets their names mixed up, but it's also a device for further titillating the audience. Who knows what incestuous lesbian identity swapping may be simmering beneath the surface? There is also some plot going on; by means of forged letters, the maids have framed Madame's lover on a charge of theft, and he apparently stands a chance of being sent to Devil's Island.

Genet's admirers tend to chatter enthusiastically about "ceremonial" and to claim that his plays bypass all the tiresome clutter of bourgeois drama and take us down to the roots of theatre, back to the Greeks and even beyond. That sounds all right until you encounter the plays themselves and discover that they're all talk. (The best Genet production I've ever seen was an English staging of his quasi-political play The Balcony that treated it frankly as dialectic, like Shaw without the jokes.) The dialogue may succeed in being perfumed and poisonous -- the fans tend to invoke the magic phrase "black mass" -- but that doesn't make it any less static. Some productions of The Maids up the ante by employing an all-male cast; Genet himself would apparently have approved, even though (or conceivably because) it adds another level of useless artifice.

Diana Leblanc's production, with real women, is the best I have seen; and though that isn't actually saying much, the back- handedness of the compliment is a reflection on the other versions, not on this one. Indeed, for the first third of the evening, I really thought they were going to get away with it. Nancy Palk as Solange and Martha Burns as Claire act out their initial masquerade with great emotional bravura and inventively stylish staging from behind a gauze. Or rather from between two gauzes, since the audience is seated on both sides of the stage. When the outside world is about to intrude, the curtains are whisked away and half the house finds itself confronting the other half. This is a striking moment , but it doesn't lead anywhere. For a moment, the audience may feel implicated in the charade, but the feeling wears off.

A phone rings, and we learn that Monsieur has got bail. Madame arrives and, having received the good news, sails off again, though not before the maids have tried to poison her. They prove, though, even more ineffectual at executing this scheme than they were at their previous one. The narrative tension, anyway, is not exactly overpowering; storytelling was far from being Genet's strongest suit, and he's at his worst when he stops playing games. In a sense, of course, he never stops playing them. The play's picture of domestic service has less to do with conditions in France in the 1940s, or at any other time, than with the author's fantasies. The Maids was inspired by, if not based on, an actual murder case, and awareness of this fact makes the play seem chilly in the wrong way: frivolous without being funny.

The exigencies of the plot sometimes force the actresses to behave as if they were in a detective story: They try for some realistic tension, though "the lamp will betray everything" is not a credible line, even in a melodrama. (Martin Crimp's translation is nevertheless a great improvement on the previous official version. This gives the production an advantage over Miss Julie, which is hobbled by a stilted text to which nobody in the program owns up.) Palk and Burns are a well-contrasted pair, the former towering over the latter but emotionally subservient to her. Burns makes especially bold choices, sometimes achieving an intensity that's not only white-faced but white-voiced. She seems, unfortunately, to have only one rhythm and one register, but the monotony is less damaging here than it would be in a more colloquial piece. Palk plays a worm that turns, or perhaps a jelly that sets; she has some memorable moments of stupefied helplessness, and she launches full-sail into a final vengeful tirade, though she cannot raise it from melodrama to the perverse tragedy its author seems to have intended. Both actresses go to heroic lengths, but they too seem in the end to be playing games. Maybe they're too nice.

Genet specified that the maids be between 30 and 35, and that's more or less credible here. He also said Madame was 25; no production that I have seen has taken him remotely at his word, though the sisters' jealousy might have a lot more edge if their tormentor were played as a patronizing youngster. This production goes to the opposite extreme by casting veteran Charmion King; she gives a flossily efficient performance but one that proceeds inexorably on its own tram lines, which only periodically intersect with those of the play. The production seems to be getting back on its own track when Claire, like one rising from the grave, dons Madame's dress -- or slips into her slip -- one last time; but this scene too peters out in verbal gestures.

The pundits used to label Genet as a theatrical absurdist, along with Beckett and Ionesco; this just shows how lazy pundits can be, since he had neither the starkness of the one nor the craziness of the other. He was rather the heir to the rhetorical tradition of the French classics, long after it died of inertia. As a homosexual who went repeatedly to jail (as a thief, like Monsieur, except he was guilty) he has enjoyed a sort of outsider's cachet; the Parisian intellectuals, led by Jean-Paul Sartre, adopted him as if he were a species of literary rough trade. English playwright Joe Orton -- also gay and with a record -- was to get the same kind of attention in the '60s, but his plays, riotously entertaining and far more seriously subversive, knock Genet's out of the ballpark.
