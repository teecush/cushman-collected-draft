BEAVER

Factory Studio Theatre, Toronto

There are now three plays in Toronto whose starting-points are a family and a funeral. This, the latest, arrives a few weeks after The Memory of Water and just one night after the joyous Sibs. It too is largely concerned with sibs, and, as in The Memory of Water, they are three grown-up sisters. This time, one of the sisterhood doubles as the corpse, but -- again as in The Memory -- the deceased makes spectral appearances in the action. The person she appears to is her daughter -- 12 at the beginning of the play, 17 at the end -- whose name is Beatrice, and whose nickname gives the play its title.

So the play is about her; that, at least, is what the author seems to be insisting, both in the title and in the text. But in fact there is no central character and no real unifying theme. Beaver, which lasts three hours with the special-occasion luxury of two intermissions, is a meandering mess.

The play, in its present form, has no business on a professional stage. Its author, however, has.

She is Claudia Dey, whose first full-length piece this is, and she has been entranced by the way people talk in the snow-clad fastness of Northern Ontario. I have to take her word for it that conversation up there is as luxuriant and as incisive as she makes it out to be, but I'm happy to do so: at least as happy as when listening to Sean O'Casey's Dubliners whose lyricism seems self- conscious by comparison. The trouble is that having come up with a style, she has not found a story or characters to hang it on.

Things do of course happen, and to people. The place is Timmins, the date of commencement 1988. Beaver's mother, Rose, committed suicide, perhaps out of frustration at unfulfilled dreams or from the pain of living with her alcoholic husband, Silo. Her two aunts are Nora, nervous and all-round inhibited, and Shira who, not inhibited at all, is a noted local prostitute specializing in domination.

The family circle is rounded off by their mother, Edna, a saltily foul-mouthed old lady who, like nearly everyone else on stage, drinks a lot. Their first, improbable, action is to lose Beaver in the graveyard and return home, in the depths of winter, oblivious. Nora has persuaded social services to place the girl in her care: this over the objections of her father -- who loves her, whatever else -- and the jealous disgust of Shira. Beaver isn't happy and makes the first of several attempts to leave town. Eventually, though, she gives in. These manoeuvrings, interspersed with much rambling reminiscence, take up the play's first two acts. There is then a five-year gap, at the end of which we find Beaver about to get married (in a motel) to someone we never meet for reasons that are barely explored. Her relatives are still striking the same attitudes, except that the once-puritanical Nora now seems willing to swear and swig with the best. The end is predictably unpredictable.

A few scenes have tension and rhythm, notably one in which Silo (the best-drawn character in the piece, and richly played by Glen Cairns) clings desperately to his daughter, trying to be cunning, while Nora waits with a kind of embarrassed implacability. There are two characters from outside the family: an anecdotalizing neighbour whose quirk is that she wants to set up as an independent sovereign state, and a drinking buddy of Silo's. The former is well-enough played (by Tracy Wright) and the latter excellently played (by Lorne Cardinal) but the play would be better off without them. They merely add to the volume of self-reflexive talk, and slow the proceedings down further. They're already dragging dangerously, partly because of the amount of alcohol that goes down; you may think that your mind is racing when you're drunk, but from an outside viewpoint you're at a crawl. Sarah Stanley's droopily-paced production doesn't help. The actors in the communal scenes strike no sparks off one another though individually -- Charmion King gritty and commanding as Edna, Jan Derbyshire quivering as Nora, Linda Prystawska pluming and bridling as Shira -- they do their prescribed thing efficiently. Waneta Storms is wasted as the late Rose about whom everybody talks reverentially, but who never, either as a memory or a ghost, displays a single positive characteristic.

The same lack of definition more crucially afflicts Beaver, whose development is meant to be the spine of the play. It simply isn't there. Simone Rosenberg does strikingly well as the young girl forging a precocious personal and sexual identity, but she can't bridge the gap to the mystery bride of the last act, though by now she's playing closer to her own age. There is some kind of message here about working out your own salvation, and it seems to embrace the other characters as well, but it remains fuzzy and sentimental. The play suffers from the conviction that if you talk around something enough, then it's happened.

It also carries the curse of the shifting location. Most of it occurs in tucked-away corners of the stage; the centre goes almost entirely uninhabited, which gets very frustrating. It represents the impassable outdoors, and is piled high with blankets, which seem to be awaiting a dramatization of The Princess and the Pea. The stage, in Troy Hansen's design, points out into the audience, whose seats have been arranged in a receptive inverted V-shape. This is an excellent focusing device, and in the last act, when the weather has broken and we gaze on an inviting waterscape, we do finally get something worth looking at. For the previous two hours, though, we have watched actors bunched together in poky rooms whose barrenness suggests not so much the poverty of the characters are the non- arrival of the scene-painters. Bleak can be bracing; drab is just depressing.
