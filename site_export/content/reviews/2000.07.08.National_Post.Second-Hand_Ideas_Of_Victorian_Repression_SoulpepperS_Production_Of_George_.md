The Mill on the Floss continues to July 27 at the Premiere Dance Theatre, Harbourfront Centre. Tickets are $25 to $46 and are available at the Harbourfront box office or by calling (416) 973- 4000.

It may seem like quibbling but I wish the Soulpepper company had worked up its own dramatization of The Mill on the Floss rather than buying one from England ready-made. As it is, the company isn't staging a novel by George Eliot but a play by Helen Edmundson. Eliot is a name worthy to stand on this year's Soulpepper bill alongside Shakespeare, Moliere, Chekhov and Pinter. Edmundson is not, and though her adaptation is technically ingenious, it is also misguided. The novel is not the joyless, pentecostal affair she seems to think it is.

The play was first presented by Shared Experience, a British company that goes back to the 1970s, at which time it presented extraordinary examples of pure actor-based theatre; it used literary texts (from Dickens to the Arabian Nights) but mined them directly, without the benefit of a playwright. Its more recent work is still accomplished, but is much less exciting. It's from the company's later, more conventional, period that this version of The Mill derives.

Robin Phillips's Soulpepper production has improved immeasurably since its flustered debut at this year's World Stage season. Staged on a low versatile platform dressed only by a couple of screens, it moves with a sober grace that can rise to a dreamlike magic, especially when boats are involved. The excellence of the direction, though, points out the weaknesses of the script. The climactic flood is far more impressive than it was, though it still leaves a feeling of, "is that all?" But then so, in a more exalted way, does the book.

The flooding of the river Floss is the author's method of disposing of her two central characters, Maggie Tulliver and her brother Tom. They are the children of the mill, or more prosaically of the miller. We have seen their father lose his property, through hot-tempered litigation, and his life, through hot temper. We have seen Tom subjected to a private education that bears no relation to his talents, and then wrenched from it and from his dreams of a comfortable life as a gentleman on horseback, to take on the backbreaking task of repaying the family debts. We have seen Maggie, far the more sensitive and imaginative of the two, denied the schooling she deserves -- because she's a girl -- and growing up lonely and vulnerable. The two of them love one another, resent one another, depend on one another.

The novel's genius lies in the many-sidedness of its characters, who can be mean or ridiculous one moment, noble or generous the next. The play takes this very literally in the case of Maggie, whose role gets divided among three actresses, according to the character's spiritual development, which roughly corresponds with her chronological age. Torri Higginson is shiny-eyed, the bookish, romantic Maggie, a bit like Alice in Wonderland, always ready to lecture her elders or tell them stories. Julia Arkos is the religious Maggie, the most irritating of the trio, who, when trouble strikes, reads Thomas a Kempis's The Imitation of Christ, and sets about imitating the imitation. (The novel makes clear, as the play doesn't, that she has got it wrong, using self-abnegation as a form of self-indulgence.) Finally and commandingly -- if with rather more glow than is credible in the circumstances -- there is Brenda Robins as the grown young woman, faced with new temptations in the form of two rival suitors: an introverted hunchback and a self-satisfied young man-about-the-country.

All three Maggies are on stage simultaneously -- though in practice their interaction breaks down into Maggie Three weighing the attractions of a new dress or a new man while Maggie One tells her to go for it and Maggie Two adjures her to repent. In Phillips's production, these episodes are perplexing: beautifully staged but ludicrous. The math is wrong: Three one-dimensional characters do not add up to one three-dimensional one.

The treatment both diminishes Maggie -- she becomes a victim -- and unfairly privileges her. The story as written is Tom's as well as hers, but his more prosaic struggles are kept largely offstage. Still, with no alter egoes around, Stephen Ouimette can at least fuse what complexities are left him into a powerful whole; from first to last he is the same cub -- self-centred, patronizing, hurtfully well-intentioned.

The minor figures, meanwhile, are diminished. Eliot's Mrs. Tulliver is a great, almost Dickensian, comic creation, cluckingly devoted to her linen, her china and her family, especially her son, then surprising us with her loyalty to her daughter. Roberta Maxwell plays her as a sour-lipped woman who goes through the evening resenting everybody, especially her husband; her remark that he likes his tea early is practically a plea for liberation. She then doubles as Maggie's flaxen-haired cousin Lucy, light to her dark, who here emerges as another mother-figure. Both these performances are fine in themselves; they're just wrong, and I persist in believing that if the actors had gone back to the text, they might have been right.

As it is, they seem to be acting out somebody's second-hand ideas about Victorian repression, rather than the far richer, more- involved critique provided by Eliot herself. An incompetent teacher becomes a cane-carrying sadist; a level-headed rector seems, for an alarming moment, to have lecherous designs on Maggie. This is of a piece with Edmundson's dramatization of War and Peace, in which the pious Princess Mary was presented as a hysteric; it's pointlessly cheap.

Stephen Guest, Maggie's more persistent seducer, also comes off shallower than he should (he does actually love her) but within those limits Tom McCamus's performance is excellent, as is Steven Sutcliffe's as his disadvantaged rival. Sutcliffe also doubles, this time round, as one of Tom and Maggie's aunts; at one point the exigencies of costume-change force him to put in an amusingly unexplained belated appearance at a family gathering. The real multiple triumph, however, is that of Jack Wetherall, who gives delicate life to at least five characters.

Few of these actors have worked for Soulpepper before, though some are slated to reappear later in the season. A couple of weeks ago, John Neville's Krapp's Last Tape magically became a Soulpepper production, though it was exactly the same show as had been seen earlier under another banner at World Stage. This year will be a crucial one; by the end of it we should have a better idea whether Soulpepper is to be an identifiable company or -- what may be just as valuable -- a general classical-theatre resource.
