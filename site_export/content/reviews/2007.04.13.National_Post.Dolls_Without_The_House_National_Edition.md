THEATRE REVIEW

JOHN GABRIEL BORKMAN

Young Centre for the Performing Arts, Toronto

- - -

After Soulpepper's recent production of Ibsen's The Wild Duck, it's a shock to find the company going into reverse gear with its new production of John Gabriel Borkman, a ghastly piece of museum- theatre that thinks it's being experimental.

The show is actually a co-production with the fringe group Theatre Columbus; it combines their aesthetic with some of Soulpepper's resources. Ibsen's last great play, his last but one of any kind, takes to what should be thrilling extremes his technique of advancing the present through exposing the past. J. G. Borkman himself is a financier whose empire crashed when he was caught embezzling on a huge scale. He did five years in jail, and has served another eight of more or less voluntary imprisonment on the top floor of his own house. He only sees one visitor, his old clerk Foldal; the two men humour one another's illusions, which, in Foldal's simple opinion, is what friendship is about. Down below sits Gunhild, Borkman's massively embittered wife, who is visited by Ella, her twin sister and Borkman's former love, sacrificed long ago to his ambition. Both women also want vindication, and both cling to it in the form of Borkman's son Erhart. He, however, wants no part of either of their plans, having attached his own to the notoriously worldly lady who lives down the street.

The play as written has a cast of eight, of whom five are simultaneously on stage for the big confrontation scene. In Leah Cherniak's production it's performed by only three actors; they carry dolls, each representing a single character, so that we'll know which they're meant to be at any given moment. Sometimes an actor will hold two dolls and have a conversation with herself, looking like a Punch and Judy man. In the last scene the dolls are dropped altogether; this may mean that Borkman and the sisters, out in the snow after years of being cooped up, are finally revealing their real selves. Or it could mean that, with only the three principal characters present, we no longer need mechanical aids. The convention never comes clear.

The dolls and doubling concept (read: gimmick) prevents the actors from engaging with the text, the characters, and one another. It saves them, in fact, the trouble of acting. The results are predictably depressing.

This, of course, is something that Ibsen is always suspected of being but doesn't have to be. If his plays are to transcend their superficial gloom, they have to be presented with passion and wit, two qualities for which this production substitutes melodramatic posing and an occasional laugh at the play rather than with it. Borkman is probably deluded and unquestionably selfish; his predicament may be cruelly comic, but he sees himself as tragic, and he needs to be played on that scale. Casting Michael Simpson, an actor good at playing endearing elders, is downright weird; the character becomes merely pettish and the play pointless, though Simpson does play young Erhart as effectively as a middle-aged character man is ever likely to do. The one role for which he would be perfectly suited is that of old Foldal, but this has gone, in the evening's only instance of crossgender casting, to Nancy Palk. She plays it well, though less well than she plays the Borkmans' perpetually surly maid. Her major role though is Gunhild. This, uniquely in this show, is a role in which she might very well be cast in more conventional circumstances, but she muffs it here; the woman's ice-cold anger comes off as mere spitefulness. Martha Ross (who, with Cherniak, is Theatre Columbus' guiding spirit) is a soft- centred Ella: competent but bland. The director doesn't seem to have much interest in actually guiding actors through a scene. In their long establishing duet, the two actresses recite their lines, shouting when they sense that some violent emotion might be called for. It has all the shape and force of a first read-through.

The visual interest here is confined to the opening moment when we glimpse the actors, and the dolls, through a scrim. The poster looks far better than the play; it depicts not dolls but marionettes, with broken strings. If there's a joke there, it hasn't landed on stage. Borkman is a play we hardly ever see, and one that Soulpepper might have cast superbly from its own ranks; there are at least two potential, though widely different, Borkmen among the company's regulars. To do it like this is an opportunity criminally wasted. - Until April 21. Call 416-866-8666.
