THEATRE REVIEW

Have You Seen Zandile?

Burton Auditorium and

Equity Showcase Theatre

The African Theatre Ensemble, who produced Have You Seen Zandile?, say that Gcina Mhlophes play is an attempt to escape the South African tradition of apartheid drama with its persistent rhetoric of victimhood. Thats a praiseworthy aim.

All the same, it's hard to believe while watching the play, which is based on the author's own childhood, that any black South Africans could have lived through the apartheid years as untouched by the system as these characters appear to be. As a contribution to Black History month, it seems surprisingly unhistorical.

Its a flimsy piece in other ways, too. Its ostensible theme is the power of storytelling. Zandile is a Zulu girl -- eight at the start of the play, 18 at the end -- whose parents have separated and who lives with her much-loved and much-loving grandmother. Granny, or Gogo, is an inspiring storyteller whose imaginative powers rub off on Zandile.

Their happy life together is interrupted when Zandiles mother appears (in a white car, a point that's stressed but not elucidated) to reclaim or, more precisely, to kidnap her. Zandile spends her teenage years in a maternal environment more repressive and protective than she has been accustomed to; she misses her grandmother desperately, but still makes friends and does well at school.

Finally, through an amazing coincidence involving her best friend's boyfriend being related to her own family, she is able to find out where her Gogo lives. What happens in the last scene should probably not be revealed, but it doesnt come as much of a surprise.

We are in effect watching pages from a scrapbook, assembled on the thinnest biographical premise. Zandile invents an imaginary friend; thats one scene. She talks to flowers, and drills them as if they were a glee club; that's another.

Her Gogo, having lost her, wanders through the auditorium desperately asking the spectators "have you seen Zandile?"; but if that, as per the title, is meant to be the emotional heart of the play, it stops beating very quickly. And so to adolescent confidences between Zandile and her more earthbound friend Lindiwe, which seem to be there mostly to fill in the time before the final curtain.

I saw the play at the Workman Theatre on Queen Street, where it wasn't much helped by flat direction (from Bunmi Oyinsan) and even flatter lighting. It is helped, somewhat, by accompanying drums and, a lot, by its actors. dbi. young portrays the growth from childhood to young womanhood with unfettered grace and unabashed immediacy. Though, there are times, especially when she and Lindiwe discuss menstruation and "sleeping with boys," when one feels that she has played this role before, in her own play blood.klaat, and with better material.

Joke Silva, a star actress from Nigeria (via the U.K.), doubles - - unrecognizably -- as both grandmother and mother; shes especially fine as the latter, when granted a humanizing monologue about her own musical aspirations and how they were blighted by marriage. (Which, to be fair, is one up to the power of storytelling.) Olivia Duodu rounds out the cast, cheerfully if without her colleagues' distinction, as the girlfriend with the fortunate connections.

- Have You Seen Zandile? is at the Burton Auditorium at York University until Feb. 8 at 9:30 a.m. and at Equity Showcase Theatre Feb. 7-8 at 2:30 p.m. and 8 p.m., and Feb. 9 at 8 p.m.
