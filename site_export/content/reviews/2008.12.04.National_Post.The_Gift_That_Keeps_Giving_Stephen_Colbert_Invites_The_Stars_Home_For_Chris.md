Some time ago, Stephen Colbert had Tony Bennett as a guest on The Colbert Report and came close to blowing his cover. (His own cover, not Tonys.) Bennett is an obvious liberal, and Colbert duly pretended to bait him with his faux Bill O'Reilly shtick, something on the lines of "why do you have to do all these charity tours to poor countries?" But his heart obviously wasn't in it, and it was a relief when he gave it up and let the singer sing. And then, all pretence of opposition was thrown aside when, halfway through Bennetts rendition of They All Laughed, Colbert, obviously thrilled and having the time of his life, joined in. Lo and behold, he could sing. Rather well. Very nearly very well.

It was obvious, from his days as a reporter on The Daily Show, that Colbert was a first-rate actor-comedian with an intellectual top-dressing. On The Report hes had to confine himself to a single persona, but the quick-thinking virtuosity with which he's sustained it, through interviews as well as monologues, has been amazing. (There has been the odd slippage. In the run-up to the U. S. elections he seemed to be getting really angry underneath it all. So did Jon Stewart on his, but Stewart's format allows for it.) So perhaps it shouldnt have been a surprise when he came out as what used to be called an all-round entertainer.

Thats how he appears on A Colbert Christmas: The Greatest Gift of All. He sings, he dances -- or at least throws himself around in a rhythmic kind of way. (He doesn't lip-synch so well, though.) But this time he does it while staying in character. The Colbert of the Report has gone beyond being a mere parody of a bullying right-wing TV host. Come to think of it, he never has bullied; what he does is wheedle, belligerently. The Colbert-figure transcends politics. What he presents, with unflagging panache and almost unflagging precision, is a delirium of self absorption, narcissism on speed. He is, something that might once have seemed impossibly oxymoronic, a manic Jack Benny.

Like Benny, Colbert is vain, greedy and a coward, and so shameless about it, he's lovable. The format of his Christmas show -- which, in a typically Colbertian pre-emptive strike, has hit the airwaves and hence the DVD stores a full three weeks early -- is not the greatest, but it will serve. Colbert wants to get to the studio to record his TV special, but he cant leave his seasonal cabin in the woods

because theres a bear outside, and it might eat him. (The bear does eat a celebrity in the course of the show, but has to disgorge him. The celebrity is grateful. Not being digested by a bear was his Christmas wish.) Thankfully Elvis Costello, who has always looked and sounded somewhat Dickensian, is holding the fort and he sends progress reports on what seems like a cut-rate production. (Goats standing in for reindeer. And, more bemusingly, for mice.) But if Colbert can't get there to see the stars, they can come to see him.

Or maybe he dreams that they do. "I'm so high," says Willie Nelson, youre hallucinating me. ("Youre really high," Colbert agrees, harmonizing along there, very nicely.) Nelson, the favourite country singer of people who don't like country music -- I'm speaking personally here -- appears in miniaturized form as Fourth Wise Man in a nativity scene, offering a herb that smells more sweetly than either frankincense or myrrh. Colbert also greets "legendary country singer Toby Keith," who offers musical support to his War on the War on Christmas. (Separate church and state, some liberal said / I think its time we separated him from his head. I hope he knows hes being satirical.) And there is "legendary R & B singer John Legend," according to whom R & B stands for roots and berries and who has a suggestive joke about nutmeg; indie folk deity Feist, as an angel holding your prayers in strict sequence; and -- in a final glittering display of verbal front-loading-- "ageless icon of generosity and joy Santa Claus." The nicest turn of all, though, is done by Jon Stewart, dropping in from the cabin half an hour before yours -- the one where you used to live, and engaging his ex-employee in a duet about Hanukkah, a subject of which Colbert knows proudly nothing. Stewart has a shy falsetto of a singing-voice, much unlike the full-throated Colbert. But they go well together.

Colbert begins the proceedings with a rousing Christmas-song-to-end-them-all, including a line celebrating "a freshly hobbled Tiny Tim." But, for all the barbs, this is not so much an anti-Christmas special as a Christmas anti-special. It ends with Colbert and Costello duetting what must be the best seasonal song in years, which I can best sum up as tolerantly skeptical. ("There are worse things to believe in" is its refrain, and it lists some of them.)The lyric-writing throughout the show is first-rate. I'm not advocating Colbert doing regular musical interludes on The Report --it would spoil his image. But I'd like to see him some time in a revival of The Music Man. Who better to play a soft-centred egomaniac? - A Colbert Christmas: The Greatest Gift of All airs tonight on The Comedy Network at 10 p. m.
