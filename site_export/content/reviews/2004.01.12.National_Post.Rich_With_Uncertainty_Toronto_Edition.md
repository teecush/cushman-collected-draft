COPENHAGEN

Winter Garden Theatre, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - Few theatregoers, I imagine, actually thrill in advance to the idea of a dramatized debate on nuclear physics, especially one in which two of the three participants are genius scientists who left to themselves would probably be conversing in equations. But in Copenhagen, which reaches us six years after its London premiere, Michael Frayn brings it off, using the technicalities as both the springboard and the metaphorical armoury for an examination of motive and morality.

In 1941 Werner Heisenberg, probably the most brilliant German physicist of his time, visited his old friend and teacher Niels Bohr, Danish, half-Jewish, and living -- so far more or less unmolested -- in his occupied country's capital. Nobody knows what they said to one another, and their own subsequent testimonies are contradictory and inconclusive. It is, however, a safe bet that they talked about atomic weapons and about the prospects of either Germany or the Allies developing them. Heisenberg, who after all initiated the meeting, may have been asking Bohr for information, either political or theoretical, or he may have been trying to issue a warning, or he may have been requesting a kind of absolution from a man his fellow scientists called "the Pope": an implicit instruction not to help Hitler build a bomb. This last is the interpretation the play seems to find most interesting and, in the end, most persuasive.

It is also the most controversial. Heisenberg may not have been a card-carrying Nazi, but he remained in Germany and worked for the government. It is easy to think the worst of him. Frayn is unusual among playwrights in that he likes to believe the best of people, but he is also a comic writer, sometimes a satirist, and no pushover. Here, working like a scientist himself, he tests and retests the meeting, playing it out three times with different results. He also harks back and forth to Heisenberg's other visits to Copenhagen: in 1924, as Bohr's eager student and -- very quickly - - his favourite, and in 1947, when the two men met again, irreconcilable in post-war bitterness.

In a way, the play takes place in the present. The three characters -- Bohr, Heisenberg and Bohr's wife, Margrethe -- are all dead, and they review their lives, and especially this one incident, from some kind of limbo. This means they can be quite shameless about reminding one another, for our benefit, of what they already know. But then, how else are you going to spend eternity? Together or apart, the trio trade memories, excuses, recriminations and mathematical formulae as if there were no tomorrow, as for them of course there isn't. And as, Margrethe grimly suggests, there may be for none of us, thanks to "this wonderful machine" her husband, a thoroughly nice man, made possible with his inspired discoveries in the 1920s.

Heisenberg says there are two things for which he is remembered: his visit to Bohr and -- his own great discovery -- the uncertainty principle. This concerns the movement of particles, but the play uses it as a bridge -- a facile but effective one -- to get at the uncertainty of human motive. Not only can we never be sure what other people think or want, neither can they. Heisenberg unquestionably impeded, probably prevented, the development of the German bomb by leading his superiors to believe it couldn't be done. He may have misled them, for reasons of conscience, or he may have been telling what he thought to be the truth because, as the Bohrs point out -- he gently, she caustically -- he hadn't done the sums. (Of course it didn't help that all the people who might have put him right were Jewish.)

Anyway, he spent the rest of the war working on uranium 235 in a cave (literally) with no hope of applying it practically -- i.e., destructively -- and in imminent danger of blowing himself up: pure research, pure happiness. Meanwhile Bohr, that nice man, having shared in the great evacuation of the Danish Jews to Sweden, proceeded to the United States and worked, in a small but supportive way, on the device that devastated Hiroshima.

Frayn was a journalist and then a novelist before turning to the theatre, and he continues to keep all three options open. He is not, I think, a natural playwright but he has turned out better than many who are. He sets himself huge technical problems -- reinventing full- scale farce in Noises Off, full-scale debate in Copenhagen -- and solves them. The range seems effortless; a whole act of Noises Off is virtually silent while this play is nothing but talk. The chat is sometimes overwhelming, the characters dispensing information, technological or historical, faster than we can keep up with it. But most of the time Frayn's skill at weaving his tapestry is remarkable. Apart from the crucial meeting, the recurring threads, each reinforcing the others, include the memory of Bohr's son, drowned in a boating accident with his father helpless to save him; Heisenberg seeing his country brought to ruin by war, twice; his internment in England, where his kindly hosts bugged him as ruthlessly as the Gestapo might have done; the irony of good, or indeed bad, intentions. And time has been kind to the play if cruel to everything else. Anything that touches on lethal weapons and who might have the right to use them, or on the politics of occupation, strikes deeper now than it did in 1998.

Diana Leblanc's production, played with minimal furniture against a set of translucent screens, imposes some needless visual and musical flourishes. The lighting, though, is superb and both the placing and the orchestration of the actors first-rate. The performances themselves are even better than those in the admirable British original. As Margrethe, the judge and observer, Martha Henry is as watchable as she is watchful. As Bohr, Michael Ball finds a new use for his familiar bumbling-academic persona; Ball always seems to be padding inquisitively through his lines and the historical Bohr, endlessly modifying his utterances, must have done much the same thing. Bohr's strongest term of disapprobation is "interesting," and Ball wields the word like a self-sharpening knife.

Ultimately, though, the play is about Heisenberg, and it's Jim Mezon's performance that dominates, even when he sits with his back toward us, so that we focus on his bald head and protruding ears, a big man who might be asking for forgiveness or demanding justification; probably he thinks they're the same thing. Vocally he may hit the note of anguished perplexity too hard and too often, but he communicates an enormous amount through it. He is a man, or at least a consciousness, never at rest. Even though the play may settle for a sympathetic view of him, you sense that he himself could never settle -- in life or after it.
