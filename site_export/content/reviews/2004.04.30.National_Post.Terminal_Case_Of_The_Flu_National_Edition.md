UNITY (1918)

Theatre Passe Muraille, Toronto

- - -

I wondered, while watching Unity (1918), whether all Canadian drama used to be like this. Rumour suggests that it did; that once upon a time, not so long ago, everybody was writing and producing wordy and worthy historical dramas with multiple plotlines and irritating poetic streaks, plays that implicitly ordered their audiences to pay attention because a) these things really happened and b) they happened here. How did people stand it?

The thing that happened in this case was an epidemic of influenza that took millions of lives just as the First World War was ending, and that had an especially devastating effect on Canada. Plays in this genre tend not to be set in big cities (though they're often performed there; this one started out in Vancouver) and Kevin Kerrs piece takes place in Unity, Sask., a real place with a symbolically useful name.

The characters, though, are fictitious. They include a trio of teenage girls -- one shy, one saucy, one sensitive. The sensitive one keeps a diary, which I think is fair warning. The saucy one is enthusiastically convinced that the world is about to end, and would like to get some fun in before it does, preferably with a nondescript young man popularly referred to as the Preacher. Most of his contemporaries are of course at the front, and the girls, especially the shy one who has a special attachment, are eagerly awaiting their return. When they do come back, those we meet tend to be sick or blind or married.

We are probably the better for being reminded that 1918 saw a forgotten apocalpyse on the heels of the well-remembered one. We dont get much else out of it, though. The play is two years old, so it cannot be accused of cashing in on SARS; now, of course, the sight of a whole population going about its business in surgical masks does offer a familiar jolt, but there's no particular merit in that. Nor is the mere fact of loss going to affect us much, unless we have some insight into the people who suffer it. Here they are so many marionettes, all awaiting their turn to die or be mourned.

There is one sweetly personal moment when the diarist gets to meet the local hero for whom she has been darning and mailing socks. There's also the piercing image of one plague victim, condemned by the laws of quarantine to shuttle back and forth by train without ever being allowed to get off. I dont know whether or not this is drawn from life, but I did get the feeling that a documentary approach to what actually happened would be more compelling than the evening of free association were offered here. It's an unfocused fantasia on the subjects of sex (frustrated) and death (achieved), in which the flu is only intermittently present.

Some variations on the theme are, so to speak, left over from the war. Others find expression through the character of a young Icelandic woman who has inherited the family gravedigging business (there must be limericks about this) and is generally resented. (She's played black. Colour-blind casting is all very well, but I dont see why it should preclude some attempt at an appropriate accent.) Like nearly everybody else, she makes flowery speeches. The showpiece one is given to the blind soldier. It turns out that he lost his sight not, as we first thought, in a gas attack but by gazing between the thighs of a prostitute the night before. His former story makes a lot more sense, on every level.

Chris Abrahams production frames the play handsomely without doing much for its insides; it may even have obstructed them. Actors are sculpturally disposed across a wide, wide-open set, designed by John Thompson and dominated by what I took at first to be a couple of tall crosses (emblems of mortality) but which are, more prosaically, telegraph poles. A slatted road runs across the front of the stage and then proceeds to climb up what would otherwise be the proscenium; a few intrepid actors attempt to climb after it, or on it, but not very often. There are not, after all, many situations that cry out to be played at an angle of 45 degrees.

It is all superbly lit by Andrea Lundy, whose dawn comes up, twice, like visual thunder. The director has not, however, managed to infuse much energy into the performances; the holes at the top seem to have let the air out, and the actors -- some of whom have good track records -- are indistinct and anonymous. A couple of biddies who serve as the town's communication system are especially badly served, stuck in a trench at the back of the stage and having to compete with the sound of their own machinery.

At the end the cast join in a hymn to Canada. They sing it well - - it's not the national anthem -- but it's as if the play had suddenly remembered its own title. And a dull season gets, at its last gasp, even duller.
