I nSecurity (CBC), whose title is its best joke, starts with an Ottawa-based team of agents debating whether the code name of their most intrepid member is Goldilocks or Mama Bear, and finally giving up on the idea altogether. That just about sets the tone. They then fail to rescue her from her current daredevil assignment.

These operatives--there are six of them--belong to a mercifully fictitious National Intelligence and Security Agency (NISA) and judging from the first two episodes, the only reason any of them are still alive and employed is that their opponents are even more insecure and unintelligent than they are. The idea, I guess, is to satirize the equally implausible inhabitants of the 24 universe: those supermen and women, goodies and baddies alike, whose martial skills are only equalled by their deductive prowess, and whose computers never malfunction. We'd all like to take them down a peg, and the idea might make a good half-hour spoof. I question whether its going to make 13 of them.

Leading the pack, if only by virtue of being marginally more competent than her fellows, is Alex(Natalie Lisinska), a blonde spitfire who can really kick ass, and quite frequently kicks the right one. Claude (Remy Girard, from numerous Denys Arcand movies) also has some authority, chiefly as a lethargic, roly-poly whipper-up of souffles, to be consumed during stake-outs. Some of his colleagues feel that such haute cuisine lowers their status as tough guys and would prefer to order in pizza; but they greedily consume Claudes creations nonetheless. Then we have Peter (William deVry), top guy on the official food chain, and the obligatory self-appointed office Lothario; Jojo (Grace Lynn Kung) who's brusque and Asian; N'udu (Richard Yearwood) whos violent and African; and Burt (Matthew MacFadzean) who really, really fancies himself.

The show is officially described as character-driven comedy, a claim that rests somewhere between ambitious and delusional. Episode 1 has the gang trying to bust a uranium-smugglingoutfit(Saskatchewan to Tehran); the big giggle is that, when Alex gets captured, the doctor assigned to torture her is someone she knew in high school, and a wimp to boot (which Alex pretty much does). In Episode 2, which is marginally better, they infiltrate a Russian group who have stolen the plans to Canadas incredibly secret nuclear submarine, but whose efficiency is weakened by their propensity for having sex on the job: with Alex or Peter or one another. NISA's own secret weapon is that our clean half-dozen always seem to operate in three groups of two apiece, so that there's generally one couple left to ride to the rescue of the others. Well, that and dumb luck, in the shape of dumb opponents. Thats the problem.

Even the wildest crime-spoofs generally incorporate some real threat. So far, InSecurity has none.

Episodes (TMN) has more going for it in that, rather than satirizing a fantasy genre, it satirizes the making of TV itself; so it has more connection with real life, if only with a very rarefied section of it. Its starting point is the process by which smart British comedy shows are bought by American companies, rewritten, re-shot and destroyed: as in Fawlty Towers, Coupling, The Office -- well, perhaps not The Office.

So: in what is, fittingly, a U.K.-U. S. co-production, we have Sean Lincoln (Stephen Mangan) and his wife Beverly (Tamsin Greig), authors of a BAFTA-award winning show called Lymans Boys, set in an exclusive academy, which attracts the fervent admiration of Merc Lepidus (John Pankow), even though, it transpires, he hasn't seen it. Someone who has says it reminds him of The History Boys, a charge Sean and Beverly hotly deny, even though their principal character, who's also the schools principal, is supposedly played by an actor who strongly resembles The History Boys Richard Griffiths: not surprisingly since Griffiths himself acts the actor.

The high point of Episode 1 of Episodes is Griffiths' U.S. audition for a role he thought he'd made his own, and his philosophical acceptance of Mercs discovery that "hes -- I can't quite put my finger on it -- too British." So they call in Matt Le-Blanc -- the real Matt LeBlanc -- who, as is obvious to everybody including himself, is totally wrong for the role. At least he is until the authors are part charmed, part blackmailed, into re-conceiving the character as an all-American hockey coach. (New title: Pucks.)

There are plenty of things to object to here. Hollywood philistinism is a barn-door of a target, already riddled with bullets, and Merc's grossness is, well, gross; he greets his new British subjects with a different fake-British accent every week, and it makes your skin crawl. Sean and Beverly in Beverly Hills arent in themselves all that magnetic, though Greigs sharpness (she's like a cut-rate Emma Thompson) registers more strongly than Mangan's tousled amiability. Theres a tiresome running gag in which theyre never recognized by the guard at their luxury compound, who seems to work a 24-hour shift. However, the voice that says "front door ajar" every time they open it did crack me up, and our sightings of the studio's head of comedy are hilarious; she's the most unsmiling woman you saw in your life, and thoroughly credible.

Above all, and perhaps surprisingly, theres LeBlanc himself, whos become the Gen X Dean Martin: a master of amiable self-satire. There's some texture both to the writing and acting of his role; he alternates between boorish and sensible. He seems, all told, to be the only member of the Friends ensemble currently doing honest work. ( Friends' David Crane is one of Episodes' creators.) Both these shows try hard to seem laid-back. Episodes shows that relaxation is easier with a real subject to lay back on.

- InSecurity premieres Jan. 4 at 8:30 p.m. on CBC. Episodes premieres Jan. 10 at 8 p.m. on Movie Central and 9 p.m. on The Movie Network.
