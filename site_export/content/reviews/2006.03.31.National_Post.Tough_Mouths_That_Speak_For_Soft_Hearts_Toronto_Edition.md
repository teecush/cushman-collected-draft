ESCAPE FROM HAPPINESS

Factory Theatre

- - -

In 1992, when the Factory Theatre first staged it, George F. Walker's Escape from Happiness was in three acts and took three hours. It has now slimmed down to two of each. Not having seen the original production I cannot say if the compression is responsible for the plays disjointed feel, as if its constituent parts arent quite fitting together. It could be bred in the bone; this being Walker, it could even be intentional. Like most of his plays it is funny, a bit suspenseful, and generally more fun than many other people's. It also has two separate plots that don't cohere, even though most of the characters are involved in both of them.

There are 10 people in the cast, which was unusual 14 years ago and is phenomenal now. The gender-balance rivals Noahs Ark for strictness, and the women are without exception the stronger. Nora, in whose east-end Toronto house we are, is the eccentric mother of Elizabeth, who is a lawyer; Mary Ann, who is a flake; and Gail, who is married to Junior. Junior starts the play with a bang by being found lying on the floor with a chair rammed into his chest, having been rather severely beaten up. Gail wants to call the police, but Nora advises against it. She herself was married to a policeman whose name was Tom and who once burned down the family home. A man exactly resembling Tom now lives upstairs; Nora claims that he isnt her husband but nobody believes her and, no spoiling, we aren't expected to either. We also meet the two guys who did it to Junior and who it is assumed were involved with him in some kind of drug deal, and two cops who come to investigate. One of the latter is female with fancy college training, the other, old-school male, was once Tom's partner, though the play makes less of that than you might expect. Maybe their relationship was a casualty of cutting.

Theres the family story and theres the crime story, and they're united by emotional volatility if by nothing else. The question why do you say outrageous things? receives the answer "to bring the truth into focus, and to get things rolling." Which could be Walker copping, you should pardon the expression, to his own method. Nora is trying to hold her family together, though that's neither as clear nor as central as it might be. (It doesnt help that the other plot requires her to spend long periods off-stage. In custody, if you must know.) Shes sharp-tongued and somewhat saintly; non- family members cower when she talks to them, especially when she's doing it for their own good. She's played by Clare Coulter, whom I havent seen on a stage in years and who makes, apart from some vocal blurring, a triumphant return; Nora lives by her own logic, and Coulter never deviates from it for a moment.

None of the other ladies in the cast has quite the same degree of unforced confidence. As Gail, Rebecca Auerbach starts surly, and then has to spend the rest of the evening backtracking, which she does quite successfully. Mary Ann has left her husband, and puts implicit trust in a shrink who has told her to be authentic; she does this by telling her sister Elizabeth, so repeatedly that it becomes a running gag, not to be ashamed of being a lesbian. (Elizabeth isnt. Ashamed, I mean.) It must be difficult not to play Mary Ann as a comic turn and Catherine Fitch, though certainly spirited, doesn't bring it off. No offence, but I found myself fantasizing about having this role played by Mary Lynn Rajskub, the wonderfully tactless Chloe of 24.

And maybe Kiefer Sutherland could play Elizabeth who, if not quite in the Jack Bauer class, does have a habit of taking the law into her own hands --ironically, as she's on a personal crusade against police brutality. Shes also very tough on her own father. In both cases shes right but self-righteous. Cast, as frequently before, in a killer-dog role, Irene Poole again gives a tornado of a performance, the kind you root for even while feeling that at certain moments the lady's pushing it. If Poole prompts that response occasionally, Nola Augustson's college cop, over the top without a springboard, gets it all the time.

The gentlemen are notably quieter, though they all turn out to have interesting things to say for themselves. The two crooks, a father-and-son act, are far more eloquent threatened than threatening; Oliver Becker positively blossoms under duress, while Jamie Robinson goes electric in what looks like a nothing role and suddenly isnt. Junior is an underwritten role, but Brandon McGibbon gives him a personality, sort of rabbity. R. D. Reid makes, at the very last minute, a thought-provoking case for the policeman and his unhappy lot. Ken Gass, the Factorys artistic director, directs artistically (apart from one very forced grouping near the beginning); Layne Coleman, his opposite number at Theatre Passe Muraille, has nipped around the block to play Tom, with great shamefaced authority. He isn't exactly a moral centre, more of a litmus-test; his family have to figure out how to respond to him. Pending better times, which may come, this usually involves making him soup.

Tom and Junior have some kind of scam of their own going, which I couldn't follow. This is an example of an overall problem. There is plenty of life on stage, but its hard to believe in what happens off it, either now or in the past. The title refers to a theory that Nora and her family have spent their lives purposely making themselves miserable. Quite conceivably they have, but it just sounds like something people say. The play is supposed to be unified by the idea that its a rough world out there, and getting rougher, so the best thing to do is to be nice to our relatives. Walker's plays have a tough mouth and a soft heart, and the combination worked better across the sprawling canvas of This Is Wonderland, a series that might have gone on forever (though it sadly didn't) and therefore didnt need a shape. A play does; and I still think that Walkers best is his Turgenev adaptation, Nothing Sacred, which came with one built in.
