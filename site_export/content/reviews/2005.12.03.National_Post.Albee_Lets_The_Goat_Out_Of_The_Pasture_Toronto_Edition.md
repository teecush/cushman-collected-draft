It is rare these days for a play to sport a double title, especially one whose second half asks a question that its first has already answered. Such is the case with The Goat or Who is Sylvia?, Edward Albee's latest. I give nothing away in revealing that Sylvia is a goat, indeed the goat, the new love object of a man who, up until shortly before the play begins, has been perfectly happy with his wife.

A more perplexing question is, why is Sylvia? Why, that is, did Americas senior practising playwright choose to build a play around this particular compulsion? He is clearly not concerned to examine bestiality for its own sake, an omission for which most people will be grateful. Maybe he was just challenging himself to write an entertaining play on an unlikely subject, in which case he passes his own test with flying colours -- this is possibly his wittiest play. Most likely he is pursuing his ongoing project of anatomizing the American marriage and how it copes with shock. Though if he wanted to shock us, hes too late. The audience with whom I saw the play seemed composed mostly of senior citizens, and they lapped it up, especially the ladies.

Martin, the goat-fancier, is 50 years old and an acclaimed architect. (If world drama has taught us anything, it is that successful master builders are peculiarly prone to bizarre infatuations and tragic falls.) He is devoted to his wife, Stevie, and she to him. But when Martin, on a trip to the country, comes face to face with Sylvia, he is smitten, and embarks on a full- blown affair. When Stevie finds out she is, of course, devastated.

Albee has long moved on from the clangorous marital warfare of Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf? to something subtler. Here, as in his previous Play About the Baby, he gives us a textbook happy marriage, one moreover that prides itself on its tolerance. The couple's teenage son, Billy, is gay, and theyve coped with that all right. (Well, sort of.) Indeed Billy has been boasting to his friends about what an exceptional advertisement for wedlock his folks are. But apparently there are limits, and Martin crosses them. Billy thinks so, and so does Stevie, who coats her fury in a savage humour that takes us back to the earlier Albee. Martin, by contrast, is the kind of man who cant get angry. He can be aloof and ironic, especially when people misuse words in his presence, and he stews in his own compound of helpless guilt and injured innocence. When he is roused to defend himself, in the play's most reverberant sequence, it's on the universal subject of involuntary sexual arousal. He is not, at this point, talking about goats.

For a play that has been eagerly awaited since its New York premiere in 2002, The Goat has received a strangely muted Toronto staging. Micheline Chevriers production, at CanStages secondary address, has two things going for it. One is the set, designed by Judith Bowden. Its clean lines do the hero's profession proud. Its slatted walls have an appropriate air of barn door, and its imposing central arch might be the frame for a Greek tragedy, which is what, at its bloody finish, the play surprisingly but logically becomes. Not to give anything away, but tragedy may have had its origins in ritual sacrifice, and the word itself means "goat-song."

The other asset, a vital one considering he's on stage throughout, is R.H. Thomsons Martin, a delicate, worried portrayal of the cerebral brought up against the visceral. Its high time that Paul Dunn progressed from playing teenagers, but his Billy is accurately self-absorbed and accurately lost. John Jarvis, though, is lumpen as the family friend who lets the cat out of the bag, or the goat out of the pasture. And though Gina Wilkinson seems to have the right tragicomic idea about Stevie, she hammers that idea into the ground. Her touch is as heavy as Thomson's is light.

According to John Van Burek, Canada's champion of 18th-century comedy, the plays of Carlo Goldoni fall into three periods: Early, when Goldoni was in thrall to the commedia dellarte, its stock characters and plots; Late, when he had developed his own far more realistic approach; and Intermediate, which is a bit of both. The Amorous Servant, which Van Burek has translated, very well, and directed, less surely (and in which he also acts, quite creditably) is Intermediate, but I suspect it might be more profitably treated as if it were Late. It concerns Florindo, a young man thrown out of his fathers house through the wiles of his stepmother, and Corallina, a maidservant who looks after him, lives with him (platonically) and falls in love with him (probably). He certainly falls for her, at least for a time. She, though, tells him that he would be far better off with a girl of his own station, and fixes him up with one, while she stakes out a male coeval for herself. The scene in which Corellina and Florindo make their positions known represents an extraordinary clashing of gears, between one school of drama and another, one political era and another. Tradition, social and theatrical, wins out, but not without regrets, on stage and certainly in the auditorium.

This, the play's central scene, suffers from being staged somewhat off-centre, a miscalculation emphasized by the geometric precision of Michael Gianfrancesco's charming set. The productions first act is altogether stretched very thin, with too much strenuous clowning draining the blood from a story that, though not remarkably complicated, becomes hard to latch on to. The characters arent necessarily played to commedia type. Dov Mickelson's Arlecchino, for example, is his own kind of buffoon, a sweet amalgam of Chico and Harpo Marx. But he has some coarse colleagues, and the show doesn't take hold until after the intermission.

Its only at this point that the two best performers are allowed a scene together. They are Christine Brubaker, a heartwarmingly practical Corellina, and Jerry Franken, who clowns as much as anyone, but with authority and economy, as the hoodwinked dad whom she calls mi padrone. The translation retains many Italian phrases and they work beautifully, making the play seem just strange enough; some of the actors even do accents. This duet sets the plays denouement in motion. Goldoni, an unassuming master craftsman, gathers all his threads together, and the process is very satisfying. This is the play's English-language premiere; watch for other countries to follow.
