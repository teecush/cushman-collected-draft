Toronto has a new cabaret venue, the Flying Beaver Pubaret, which as its name might suggest, is in the back room - a long and surprisingly elegant one - of a pub (488 Parliament St., at Carlton). This past Wednesday night, singer John Alcorn took command of its stage for an evening of Cole Porter songs. It's the first in a weekly series of Songbook programs (next week, Jerome Kern; after that, Berlin and Gershwin) that Alcorn has planned for as long as the traffic will allow. Judging by the packed house he attracted this week, that could be quite some time.

I labelled Alcorn as a singer, but in fact he does, and is known for, other musical things as well. "I think of myself," he said when I spoke to him the evening before the Porter gig, "as a singer/composer." He does a lot of theatre work, and is especially known for providing the scores for the virtuoso puppet shows of his partner, Ronnie Burkett: "When I'm working on one of Ronnie's shows, I submerge myself in it for a few months." But when he re-emerges he gets back to singing.

Given these dual talents it's logical that he should have started as a singersongwriter, on the approved model from the 1970s. The emphasis changed when a journalist friend gave him a copy of Alec Wilder's book American Popular Song: The Great Innovators 1900-1950. Wilder, a composer and lyricist himself, codified and analyzed the work of his peers and predecessors in a way that had never been attempted before. First published in 1972, it was a triumphant bucking of the tide. It may have been the single greatest factor in the rediscovery and reassessment of what's become known as the Great American Songbook. Tony Bennett refers to it, every chance he gets, as "the Bible," and it has by now inspired two further generations listeners and performers. For Alcorn, it was an education but also, in a way, a re-education. "It goes back to when I was a kid. My mother's father was in a dance band; my dad was a big Sinatra fan. And there were also a lot of original Broadway cast albums in the house."

Alcorn was born in Toronto, but his family subsequently moved to New Brunswick and then Nova Scotia. But in the middle of this Canadian childhood, "when I was 12 or 13, we moved to New Hampshire for a couple of years." And there, in a flea market, he came across an old Victrola, going cheap. He bought it, and found that it came with "a stack of old 78s," including Ella Fitzgerald's original hit recording of A-Tisket, A-Tasket. In terms of musical and verbal sophistication that's a fairly far cry from Cole Porter, but it unquestionably swung.

Jump a few decades and find that, in Alcorn's words, "as the Baby Boom generation grows older, our tastes tend to grow more sophisticated. There's a different level of creativity going on in the music of Porter, Harold Arlen and the others, both in lyrics and music. It's not a question of what's better or what's worse, it's more in qualities of emphasis. For Bob Dylan and Leonard Cohen, they were more focused on words. But they didn't have great compositional chops. And," he added hurriedly, "they didn't need them."

When he started out, Alcorn accompanied himself on piano but he gave that up some years ago. At the Flying Beaver, he doesn't have keyboard accompaniment at all. The main reason for this is that the stage is only big enough for a small electric piano, which he understandably dislikes. He has two sidemen: Steve Wallace, bass, and Reg Schwager, guitar. Wallace persuaded him that Schwager could play as richly and supportively as any pianist. He's turned out to be right.

This was amply demonstrated at Wednesday's show, which began with three familiar songs - You're the Top, I Get a Kick Out of You, Anything Goes - all done uptempo. After the show was over, I asked Alcorn if he thought of himself as a jazz singer. He answered that he'd started to, once he'd begun winning jazz polls, and that he was certainly happiest when singing with jazz musicians. I remarked that he did daring things with rhythm (that's especially true when he comes in after one of Schwager's solos), and he said that those choices were dictated by how he wanted to phrase the lyric: "I learned a long time ago that I can't sell just anything; I need to have a personal commitment to what I'm singing about." Frankly, I didn't get that from those first three songs - the sense was there, but not the peculiar semi-jaded, semi-ardent Porter style - but I rapidly changed my mind.

The next song up was the obscurest selection of the night, the lovely rueful ballad After You, Who, and it was done delicately, with an affecting deep note on the crucial vulnerable word "care." Even better was What Is This Thing Called Love?, done much slower than usual. This song has a seldom-sung introductory verse, that's almost a song in itself. In fact, it has two, and Alcorn is the only singer I've ever known do both of them. The second, in particular, takes the song into far more despairing territory than we might have suspected, and Alcorn struck just the right balance of quizzical cut-glass introspection and baffled desolation. The inevitable Night and Day was rejuvenated by a surging vocal, and even more by some fine melodic re-invention from Schwager. I've Got You Under My Skin (which is really Night and Day's twin) was given a treatment that avoided all foredoomed comparisons with Sinatra's Swingin' Lovers version by opting for a slow, incantatory, almost unearthly treatment that brought out the song's driven hopelessness. That's a constant note with Porter; I Concentrate on You, which was Alcorn's closer, may be the only serene unshadowed love song he ever wrote, and even that has an obsessive note about it, if only in the title.

There were lighter treats as well: Too Darn Hot, in which Wallace's bass made a thoroughly persuasive substitute for the "full drum kit" that Alcorn said he originally envisaged; Let's Do It, all verse-and-five-choruses of it, with Alcorn providing especially amusing visual illustration of the old sloths who hang down from twigs while doing it; and, as an encore, Miss Otis Regrets, which managed to be both funny and mordant. "It all felt," Alcorn said to me afterwards, "very much like an opening night." I think it was a good one.
