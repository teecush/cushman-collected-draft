The George Brown College Theatre School is taking us back to the 1950s: the early '50s, before the drama was upended by Beckett and John Osborne and the other cats. It's an instructive journey, and an entertaining one.

The school's graduating class is presenting, in repertory, Clerambard, by the virtually forgotten French author Marcel Ayme, and Cock-a-Doodle Dandy, one of the later and lesser-known works of the Irish maestro Sean O'Casey. The two plays have a lot in common. They're both serious comedies. They're both fantasies, of a literary bent. They both have sex on the brain, the French one more earthily and imaginatively than the Irish. They're both pastorals: the generic term for satiric works with settings somewhere between the rural and the mythical. They are both attacks on the Catholic church, Ayme's a light-fingered insider's job, O'Casey's the virulent outpouring of a Protestant-turned-atheist, for whom the religious hierarchy represented everything he most hated about his native land.

The title character of Clerambard is a deeply impoverished provincial nobleman, working the fingers of his wife, son and mother-in-law to the bone in order to hang on to his title and his chateau. His favourite off-duty pastime is strangling cats and dogs. One day he has a vision of St. Francis of Assissi, together with the revelation that one of his canine victims is mysteriously still alive.

This causes a 180-degree turn in his behaviour, like Timon of Athens in reverse; he switches from aggressive misanthropy to self-abasing benevolence. He vows poverty, no great stretch really, and tries to marry his son off to the local prostitute: again no big deal since the repressed youth has been fantasizing about her since he was 12. It doesn't, though, sit too well with the womenfolk who, in an unconscious echo of Gilbert and Sullivan, want him to marry the ugliest of a rich attorney's ugly daughters; the lawyer himself is one of the working-girl's regular clients, with the expected farcical results, though with unexpected side effects. (The legal family seem, in the George Brown production, to come from the famous French province of Alabama. It sort of works.)

The count discovers that the resurrected dog was a mistake but, in a brilliant twist, this only confirms him in his new course: When you know you're right, who needs miracles? The entire cast gets one anyway; they all have a heavenly vision, the only one who can't see it being the parish priest. His excuse is that he's lost his glasses, but we know better. He's the symbol of blinkered conformity, answering only to his bishop and, through him, to respectable society.

The O'Casey play also features an other-worldly visitation, in this case from a fertility symbol in the shape of an enormous cock. (As a revered English critic wrote when the play was done in London, I trust I make myself clear.)

The nicer people in the community make much of the bird, the nastier ones excoriate him as an emissary of flesh and the devil, and a few characters are stuck in the mushy middle. Chief among these are a typical O'Casey pair of timorous yarn-spinning drinking buddies, one of whom, unusually, has a job, while the other, name of Michael Mathruan, has a house, in front of which the play happens. Both fellas have moments of rooster-induced generosity, but slip back into their old ways of cheating one another while exploiting and abusing their womenfolk.

In the latter pursuit, they're much encouraged by one Father Domineer (O'Casey isn't being subtle), who regards dancing as an abomination and females as domestically necessary evils. Michael's wife, daughter and even his Irish equivalent of a saucy French maid abandon him for cursed heathen climes, such as England-- which is where O'Casey himself had been since circa 1930. When he lived in Ireland, he wrote great realistic plays about his own Dublin; after he fled, he wrote embittered fables about imaginary villages. When the priest is defied by a local labourer, he literally strikes the man dead, and gets away with it. Things can't have been that bad, even though we're learning that in some respects they were worse.

These student performances of stylistically alien texts are, as you'd expect, uneven but they're never boring. Gina Wilkinson's Ayme production has the advantage in speed, Todd Hammond's O'Casey in tone and rhythm, though the cock himself doesn't make the choreographic impact he probably should.

Clerambard has a great opening tableau, the count's family slaving away at their sewing machines. Later, the actors tend to hug rather than use the furniture; they fare better when the stage is fairly clear, as it also is throughout the O'Casey. There are 16 actors, all of them in both plays.

They are generally most comfortable, playing people their own age. In Clerambard Jonathan Brass's itchy-twitchy Vicomte is outstanding, closely followed by Kate Ross's warmly pragmatic sex worker. As the comte de Clerambard, Alex Dault is forgivably taxed by a role that really needs Christopher Plummer, but he does very well in Ireland as the domineering Domineer.

The age barrier seems to count for less in Cock-a-Doodle Dandy, and Andy Trithardt as Michael bursts wittily and authoritatively through it. There's one vital element these plays and productions share; they remind us of the lost theatrical art of confident storytelling. I really wanted to know what happened next.

- Cock-a-Doodle Dandy continues to April 22, and Clerambard to April 23 at the Young Centre for the Performing Arts. For tickets, call 416-866-8666.
