THE TEMPEST

Festival Theatre, Stratford, Ont.

- - -

At the age of 85, and playing Prospero for the fourth time, William Hutt can still surprise us. In what he insists will be his last stage performance, Hutt takes the old magician of The Tempest to new extremes. We first see him with his hands shaking as he lifts his staff to start the storm that will shipwreck his enemies. Our next sight of him, and our first hearing, comes when his daughter Miranda pleads with him to break the spell. For a moment his anger, still on the boil and fused with pride in his own accomplishments, seems directed at her. Then it dissolves into gentleness, suddenly and completely.

These reversals, constantly repeated and immaculately executed, underpin Hutts whole account of a man who takes a plays length to determine whether the rarer action is in virtue or in vengeance. He's probing Prospero's emotions and the colours of his words at the same time. There are speeches in his long tale to Miranda of how they came to the island that struck me as though I had never heard them before. He relives the love he had felt for his usurping brother Antonio, as well as the pain of his betrayal.

He goes to the furthest edge with our revels now are ended, that prophecy of doom deceptively packaged as a nostalgic farewell. Its prompted by Prosperos remembrance of a second plot against his life, and by his possibly guilty rage at Caliban, the exploited "demi-devil" responsible for it. Hutt has spoken in interviews of his desire to take this speech to its nihilistic limits, and he does. I think he takes it too far (does Prospero really feel only contempt for "the cloud-capped towers" whose dissolution he predicts?) but that's a matter of interpretation, not execution. It's thrilling to hear him ride the wave of his passion and to see it crash clear into the next scene, to break only when Ariel, who isnt human, challenges him to remember that he himself is. The pause before he bows to the inevitable, which is mercy, is going to be legendary.

He is also from time to time very funny, especially when wryly surveying the instant love affair of Miranda and Ferdinand. The mood is catching; in Richard Monettes production the youthful absurdity of this pair is played up (by Adrienne Gould and Jean-Michel LeGal) far more than usual. They can be delightful, she especially, but they can hardly have Hutt's capacity to switch moods, to go the brink of farce and then step back from it. Once set on their course they stay there.

Most of the actors make strong initial impressions they fail to reinforce. The courtiers in the wreck are clearly distinguishable, which is uncommon, and when they come ashore it looks as if Monette will be able to make their plottings as compelling as he did in his production with Hutt six years ago. It's not to be; the scenes are still less boring than in most productions, but only Gonzalo (Bernard Hopkins) strikes any fresh sparks with his vision of an island Utopia. The toffs costumes may be authentically Jacobean, but they look constraining. And their varying degrees of guilt and repentance, which are crucial, are here routine.

Prosperos relations with his servants are also rudimentary. Ariel (equally well played and sung by Jacob James, with strange Struwwelpeter fingernails and stranger poses) is faithful with a touch of impatience, and that's that. Nor do you have to believe that the play is a commentary on colonialism to want there to be more going on between Prospero and Caliban; Stephen Ouimette is surprisingly subdued, though he grumbles and rhapsodizes as required. Brian Tree, a commanding and properly nasty Stephano, dominates the clown scenes. Steven Sutcliffe's Trinculo has one nice touch: a ventriloquist himself, he gets very spooked when Ariel ventriloquizes him.

The productions one lapse from basic coherence is the betrothal masque, with the text cut and the dancing prolonged, considering the choreography, beyond reason. The best that can be said is that it makes Prosperos loss of temper very understandable, though since it's staged for exit applause, that connection gets obscured. After that, though, it's plain sailing, with Hutt at his peak in the speech of renunciation -- which, incidentally, struck me as being far more about the character than about the actor. Maybe I used up my responses on the latter score in 1999, when we all thought he was retiring; which isnt to say I wont be crying buckets if they let me in to his farewell performance in October. Until then, concentrate on experiencing verse acting at what is currently its highest stage of development.
