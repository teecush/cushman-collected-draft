THEATRE REVIEW LION IN THE STREETS

Monday, June 04, 1990

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Written and directed by Judith Thompson Starring Tracy Wright, Jane Spidell and Ann Holloway At the du Maurier Theatre Centre in Toronto Exploring a limitless domain of human misery By ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Judith Thompsons Lion in the Streets, which kicked off the World Stage season at Harbourfront, is a daisy-chain drama. This is an uncommon species in which an opening scene between A and B is followed by one between B and C, and so on, as far down the alphabet as the author cares to go. It is a point of honor with most practitioners to come back to A for the final eposode.

Actually the only other practitioner who comes to mind is Arthur Schnitzler whose La Ronde goes on a progress through the bedrooms of old Vienna, beginning and ending with a prostitute. This single-minded concern with copulation gives unity to a form that can use it, having denied itself such conventional crutches as character and narrative.

Thompson also concentrates on a city; her play belongs in a thriving line, being the latest vision of Toronto-as-urban-inferno.

Thompson's subject is human misery, a limitless domain that she has to fence in a bit. So she varies the pattern by keeping her initial character on stage throughout, a generally silent witness to everybody else's suffering. This all-seeing person is Isobel (Tracy Wright), a Portuguese girl who begins the play being taunted by neighborhood kids and then turning on them with scary ferocity.

She is rescued by a do-good middle-class mother, subsequently seen being tauntingly abandoned by her husband in the presence of a roomful of their friends.

One of these friends then takes the stage at a meeting of concerned parents, denouncing a nursery teacher for dosing her charges with sugar. The teacher, feeling distinctly battered, meets in a restaurant with a friend who turns out to have bone cancer. The waiter from the restaurant drops in at a cathedral for confession (this is a very Catholic play) and encounters a piece of his past - and so it goes.

Isobel shadows all these people, hoping, she says, that each will provide her with a home, and being progressively disillusioned.

In Act Two her function changes. She no longer mopes, she warns. She tells some well-heeled Torontonians who are watching their off-stage offspring cavorting in the park there is a lion at large. Cant they hear it roaring? (We can, but they dont have our privileged access to the theatre soundtrack.) The lion, it becomes clear, represents the destructive qualities in everybody. Up till now violence in the play, though ubiquitous, has been purely psychological.

From here on in, respectable people take to beating up on one another, not always convincingly. It all culminates in a cemetery where a child- murderer confesses to his adopted mother. He then has a vision of his victim, who turns out to be Isobel. A verbal reconstruction of the crime brings, in some mysterious way, peace. Some kind of atonement has been made. Isobel, having taken the sins of the city - if not of the world - on her shoulders, ends the play standing aloft, arms outstretched, looking crucified.

This rhymes with the first-act finale, which has found the entire cast boogeying on down, around the cathedral altar. But Isobel's redemptive powers do not extend to her own function in the action. As a device she is kitsch, and as a dramatic presence she is very irritating. Whatever the theory, in practise her antics come between us and the other people, cueing us in to be moved, amused or horrified by them.

This underlines the extreme inequality in the writing. On the whole, the starker it is, the better it is. The mother-and-son confrontation in the graveyard is calmly horrific with a murderous pulse beating in the dialogue. The sugar meeting (program description) gets sympathetic comedy out of the caregiver's defence and patronising comedy out of the parents prosecution. It is too easy to make a middle-class audience laugh at itself - there is a comedy of guilt in the auditorium as well - especially when parenthood or marriage are involved.

A husband who had found a younger woman might very well leave his wife as callously and abusively as the one in this play, but it stretches credibility to have him do it so publicly. Stylising the guests embarrassment recognizes the problem without solving it.

The author is also the director and reportedly has firm ideas about the way in which her lines should be delivered. So the wife's metronomic response - "I-can't-believe-I-am-hearing-these-words" - is presumably her idea of irony.

More perplexing is the bad radio acting, full of significant pauses before emotive words, that disfigures much of the evening and yet co- exists with playing as subtle and flowing as Stephen Ouimettes. He is entrusted with three varieties of gay guilt.

First, and amusingly, he is the waiter, once an altar-boy, whose past impulsively reasserts itself to get us into the cathedral. Then he is a journalists researcher forever haunted by a homosexual incident in his youth; the actor's command of first-person reminiscence is seamless. Finally he is the killer, convincing in his aggression and harrowing when he recalls its origin - continual sexual abuse by his stepfather.

In the economic and unsentimental evocation of childhood helplessness actor and author are at their best. Other performers - Maggie Huculak, Ann Holloway, Jane Spidell - can be on the ball in one scene and off it, exaggerated or inaudible, in another. Staging similarly varies from taut to drab. The set mainly consists of a rotating raised disc on the floor, and a stationary flat one on the wall.
