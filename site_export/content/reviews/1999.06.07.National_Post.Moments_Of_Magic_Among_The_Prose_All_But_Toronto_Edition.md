A Midsummer Night's Dream

Festival Theatre, Stratford, Ont.

Richard Monette's production of A Midsummer Night's Dream is, in one respect, quite recklessly experimental. It takes a play that is officially set in and around ancient Athens and dresses the characters as Athenians. This is unheard of. In most Dreams, shields, tunics and breastplates are reserved for the concluding Pyramus and Thisby scene in which the Athenian Working Men's Amateur Dramatic Association unintentionally burlesque the romantic antics of the main action. Here, though, all the courtly characters rush around in diaphanous frocks, even the women.

Well, they have to wear something; and one character is positively helped by his air of having just stepped off an antique vase. This is Theseus, legendary hero, urbane Earthly ruler, conqueror and fiancee of the Amazon queen Hippolyta. Jonathan Goad, a promising if currently over-emphatic young actor, makes his skeptical observations on the habits of lunatics, lovers and poets sound reasonably fresh, and also gets the proceedings off to a swinging start by seeming genuinely eager to bed his betrothed.

She seems less keen, a now-standard interpretation that has no warrant whatever in the text, and as usual condemns the actress to misread her one speech of the opening scene and then to stand around looking grumpy. There is supposed to be a contrast between the festive Theseus and Hippolyta, and the quartet of squabbling mismatched young lovers who immediately break up the party. It registers here only at half strength.

The pressure further decreases when the four kids take to the woods. Here they spend most of their time falling down and bumping into trees. (The trees are graciously and uncomplainingly played by the pillars of the Festival stage's chaste and versatile permanent set.) This isn't funny, not because it's slapstick but because it's slapstick executed by characters we don't care about.

The boys are anonymous, which isn't new; so are the girls, which is. Michelle Giroux as the masochistic Helena, who likes to call herself her lover's spaniel, has a fetchingly literal habit of going down on all fours, but she is no more successful than her peers at bringing the lines to life.

The long quarrel scene, always a hazard, has no shape. There are a few gestures towards fashionable darkness, but the production wants mainly to be light, if not downright pretty. There are dancing fireflies, twinkling stars, a charmingly artificial crescent moon, and a sudden attack of dry ice moving in from an easterly direction to engulf the entire stage, but it all seems like the gratuitous decoration of a void.

There are also, in time-honoured Stratford tradition, a lot of actors. Puck, who prides himself on his prowess as a practical joker, cannot so much as mislead a mortal without enlisting the entire corps de ballet of fairyland to help him out. He then has the cheek to go to Oberon and take all the credit.

Juan Chioran brings some authority to the fairy boss-man, but having brought it he never lets go of it; he chants everything, as if it were a condition of high immortal rank never to be caught addressing a conversational word to anyone. Seana McKenna's Titania is far more flexible and far more agreeable, the only person on stage who seems consistently comfortable speaking verse.

Otherwise, the best people in this production talk prose. These, of course, are the play-acting mechanicals. They, too, are dressed Greek (and they do their final dance to kebab-house music) but underneath they are still plainly their author's neighbours.

Brian Bedford's Bottom, far more detailed than his last Stratford performance of the role, is for once the senior man of the troupe, a village oracle whose assurance will withstand any challenge, human or otherwise. He doesn't have to jockey for position, he simply and delightfully takes it. He also wears an ass' head with style.

Steven Sutcliffe's Peter Quince, by contrast, is young and earnest: a self-taught intellectual, perhaps, who interrupts rehearsals to demonstrate some highly professional acting exercises. (The gods know where he picked them up. Maybe he's been hanging out with Sophocles at Epidaurus.)

Their play scene is not as effective a travesty of the main action as I had hoped, but it is acceptably hilarious on its own account. Starveling has a comic stammer, which is a bad sign, but Snout (Keith Dinicol) is a truly sweet and lovely Wall, and proud of it.

Best of all is the spectacle of Bottom stepping out of character to explain to Theseus, in Bedford's friendliest Lancashire, some subtle points about the action that the Duke has missed. Here the actor's natural rhythms fuse with those of the character and the result is magic. But the whole play should be.
