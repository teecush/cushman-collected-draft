Last weekend I caught the final performance of the Shaw Festival's When the Rain Stops Falling, directed by Peter Hinton, and enjoyed it even more than I had on the first night. This Australian fable of catastrophe and (possible) redemption, followed across two continents and four generations in terms both human and meteorological, has been staged with Hinton's signature qualities of imagination and sensitivity, and also with his ability to draw from his actors work that's both committed and combustible. At the curtain call some of the cast seemed close to tears, as if this were one show they didn't want to let get away.

The director himself wasn't there. He's in Fogo Island, "an incredible place" (his words) in the northwest corner of Newfoundland, working on the plays of Henrik Ibsen, which somehow seems an ideal match of material and locale. As he pointed out to me in a telephone conversation, with the single exception of A Doll's House, all of Ibsen's plays are set in rugged coastal communities. It is, he says, "an amazing place to investigate Ibsen."

Hinton, a gentle and softspoken man who was once an actor and is still a playwright, is artistic director of the English company at the National Arts Centre in Ottawa, and the Ibsen project is the fifth incarnation of an NAC institution known as "The Ark": a kind of annual three-week retreat in which a group of actors "research a period of theatre history or an author."

Previous arks have been devoted to Shakespeare and Greek tragedy. Part of the idea, according to Hinton, is to "put together a company that's half students from the National Theatre School and half professional actors. It makes for a kind of informal mentorship. It also reminds the professionals that all of us are students."

They research, they read and work on the plays, and they hear lectures. "We've had historians, scholars, novelists. This year we've a philosopher telling us about Hegel, Kierkegaard, Nietzsche."

There's a company of 35, one of whom is actually Norwegian. Discussing the problems of translating Norway's national playwright, Hinton remarked that "Ibsen wrote plays in a language that's no longer spoken, a Danish-Norwegian hybrid. Even in Norway nowadays they have to be translated" - which I confess was news to me.

"We'rereading14 plays,"Hinton says. That's starting with the two great verse-dramas, Brand and Peer Gynt, and moving on, as Ibsen himself purposefully did, to the more familiar prose plays, a 12-play progression that the dramatist, according to Hinton, saw as a complete cycle. They do indeed mark a steady progression, beginning in the social drama of Pillars of Society and ending in the near-mysticism of When We Dead Awaken.

All this activity culminates this Friday in what's described as "the summit" or, more jovially, as "a presentation of a bit of what we've encountered." There'll be scenes and lectures. And then what? How does it affect what happens next in Ottawa? It doesn't apparently have to be anything obvious: "We're not necessarily planning to do An Enemy of the People next year," Hinton says. Still, "it has influenced our programming"; the Shakespeare project led to an NAC production of Macbeth, the Brecht to Mother Courage, the medieval one to "our cycle of morality plays," and "the Greek work fed into Saint Carmen of the Main," the early Michel Tremblay play on a Greek model, which the NAC visited in Toronto last year.

Hinton also mentions his superb NAC/Soulpepper production of The Way of the World, though I can't quite see which ark that restoration comedy escaped from. Anyway, he sees the effects of these projects as being somewhat less tangible. "The ark has fed into the NAC work in a very profound way. Here there's less pressure, it's more about the process," he says. "It's a great way to get to know our new artists."

All the company members currently in Fogo will reunite in Ottawa. "We're employing 34 actors this season, so it will be large," he says. And permanent? "About 20 of them will be there for the entire season." And, befitting a National Arts Centre, "it's national because our actors come from all across the country."

It's hard not to see a fanciful link between Hinton's current and most recent projects: When the rain stops falling, it's good to have had an ark. (And Ibsen, challenging the moral complacencies of his own time, says that his aim was "to torpedo the ark.") The connection, though, as you might expect, goes deeper than wordplay.

Hinton's own major work as a playwright is the Victorian trilogy The Swanne, a comic epic staged over three years at the Stratford Festival, and as a director he exhibits and responds to a combination of delicacy and strength. When Jackie Maxwell at the Shaw brought him the Rain play he responded to its "range of ideas, and the scope of its dramatic form. It bridges the gap between the tragic, poetic, sacred and the very real world."

And of course there's the landscape and the weather. Andrew Bovell, the author, describes his play as "a fog that gradually clears," which is how Hinton's production works and what relates it, not just to other wild classics, but to us.

"There are Australian and Canadian similarities: in the relationship to the land, and the colonial ties to England," Hinton says. "And the humour, in a funny way. An Australian woman I know told me 'the Australian sensibility is sarcastic.' "

From Niagara to Fogo to Ottawa, by imaginative way of the fjords and the Outback, may not be so impossible a journey after all. And it's hard to think of anyone better qualified than Hinton to pilot the ark.
