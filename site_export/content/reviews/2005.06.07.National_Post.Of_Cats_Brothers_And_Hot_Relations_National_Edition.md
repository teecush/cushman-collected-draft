CAT ON A HOT TIN ROOF

Avon Theatre, Stratford

THE BROTHERS KARAMAZOV

Tom Patterson Theatre, Stratford

- - -

Tennessee Williams' Cat on a Hot Tin Roof, superbly revived at Stratford, is a three-act play unlike any other. Its action is continuous -- the first line of each new act is a repeat of its predecessors last -- and maybe some hardy director will one day pick up on that and play it straight through without intermissions. It might then shake down into a seamless whole, a single rushing stream on which the lights keep shifting. As it stands, and has often been pointed out, it keeps changing shape and focus while maintaining a ferocious hold on its story.

Act One is a duet between Brick, the crippled, alcoholic football- hero son of the Southern millionaire Big Daddy, and his wife Maggie, self-surnamed the Cat. Or rather its a solo for Maggie, who is desperate that she and Brick should have a child so that they can grab a major share of the family fortune, with sullen interjections from Brick, who wants as little as possible to do with Maggie, especially sexually, and is drinking to forget. Act Two brings Brick up against Big Daddy himself, whose birthday it is. This act is more truly a double, for though the father does most of the talking, he does goad his son out of his cage. Brick's shame is that a relationship with his best friend Skipper, that may or may not have been homosexual, led Skipper to kill himself. Big Daddy has terminal stomach cancer but thinks he's been reprieved; Bricks revenge is to tell him the truth -- an unaccustomed thing in these latitudes or indeed, as the play repeatedly tells us, anywhere. Act Three is an embattled ensemble for the whole family, including Bricks lawyer- brother Gooper and his wife Mae, who have five kids with a sixth on the way. If there's a central figure here it's Big Mama, who, having spent two acts in volubly happy denial, has to cope with the facts and, after taking time out to melt into a piteous puddle, does so.

What binds all this thrillingly together, in the writing and in Richard Monettes direction, is character-conflict and atmosphere. The scene throughout is Brick and Maggies bedroom, where they get no privacy. Lorenzo Savoini's set is a series of tall shuttered window frames, open to a darkling sky, with a balcony outside that, we assume, runs right around the house. Here Gooper and Mae's brood - - the no-neck monsters as their aunt Maggie unfondly calls them -- run wild; they also erupt into the room itself. Interruption at tense moments is a residential hazard in this house since Big Mama, whether from hospitality or paranoia, forbids locked doors. Its a brilliant theatrical device, which emerges here as a series of perfectly timed explosions. The culmination, one of the great grotesque scenes in modern drama, is the kids unctuous rendition of Happy Birthday, with the parents who coached them looking on: a real all-American moment.

Monette's estimate of Williams in general, and this play in particular, is higher than mine; I don't feel that were in the presence of Greek tragedy, modern style. Id call it high-grade melodrama -- a form I like -- with a human face. But the idea of playing the longer, lyrically-charged speeches downstage and out- front works very well; each major character gets to reflect or suffer in solitude before returning, newly enlivened to the fray. (Fray, in this play, is just another word for life.) Both Cynthia Dale as Maggie and David Snelgrove as Brick seem freed up by their Southern accents. Hers sometimes courts caricature, but she projects a very fetching mix of daintiness and sensuality, aiming her body like an arrow at her husband, and faltering only when she reaches the peak of rage that provokes him into throwing his crutch at her. He, alternately brooding and taunting, gives his best performance to date.

The gap in the electric fence is James Blendick's Big Daddy, a perfectly intelligent reading of the lines minus the crude elemental force for which they cry out. (Williams thought the character his best-written, and he was probably right.) Without that power, the old man's arraignments of "mendacity" -- the plays last best chance of establishing a universal theme -- seem like special pleading. Its not the actor's fault, though, that Williams' decision to bring him back in the last act seems more mistaken than ever; he would be more powerful, as first envisaged, as an off-stage howl of agony. Lally Cadeaus Big Mama, dolled-up and smothering, is perfect; you see just where she is coming from and just why her husband cant stand her. Brigit Wilson is a properly screeching Mae, and Thom Marriott a superbly deceptive Gooper; at first just a set of dentures in a suit, he comes into his professional kingdom at that infallible theatrical stand-by, the squabble over the will.

Fathers-and-Sons, the engine that drives the second and finest of Cat's acts, is the whole motor of The Brothers Karamazov, where at least three of the four title characters have some motive, personal or philosophical, for offing their dad. I have a hunch, unsupported by research, that Dostoevsky's novels rank second only to Dickens in frequency of dramatization, mainly because each of them is, among other things, a murder story. This is the aspect that Jason Sherman has seized upon in an ingenious adaptation that gradually reveals itself as a continuous trial-scene, with the evidence presented as a series of flashbacks and flashbacks-within-flashbacks. And even then there turns out to be a false bottom to the whole thing.

There is also a sporting, though probably foredoomed, attempt to do justice to the books ideas along with its story. Briefly: Dmitry, the prime suspect in the murder of Fyodor Karamazov, is trying to find his own tortured way between the belief-systems of his older and younger brothers. Dmitry is admirably played by Jonathan Goad, who gets a drunk scene that summons all the quicksilver self-hatred of the Dostoveyskyan hero. He is obviously the most dramatically viable character, unless you count the father himself, red-bloodedly rendered by Scott Wentworth as the Big Daddy of St. Petersburg, coarse but vital. Ivan the agonized atheist (Shane Carty) and Alyosha the monk-in-training (Peter van Gestel) are merely mouthpieces, though Ivan's famous fable of the Grand Inquisitor, his case against official Christianity, is at least given a chance, and a deftly-staged one. But you feel, as earlier with the stinking corpse of Father Zossima, that it's included because its too famous to leave out.

The fourth brother, the bastard Smerdyakov, is also the narrator; hes played by Ron Kennell, in sneering mode, and is maybe one device too many. The women, apart from a dependably fastidious and dependably mysterious Michelle Giroux, are woeful. It's a mystery how an actress as good as Dixie Seatle could end up so thoroughly patronizing the character she plays, while Dana Green's Grushenka, the whore desired by two generations of Karamazovs, seems inspired by the legend that this was a role once coveted by Marilyn Monroe.

These lapses apart, Richard Roses production is first-rate, an endlessly resourceful re-shuffling of chairs and tables to create instant new locales, peopled by an ensemble who start out resembling a church congregation, then morph into courtroom spectators and participants, and are always specific and always involved. Shermans ear is liable to go tin (somebody says "uber-bad"); still, this is probably as good a three-hour version as anyone could come up with. But it can't shake -- probably no version could -- the feeling of a book hanging in the wings and denying the play any chance of autonomous life.
