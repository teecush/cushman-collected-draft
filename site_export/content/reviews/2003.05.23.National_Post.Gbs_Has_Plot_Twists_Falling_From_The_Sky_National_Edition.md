MISALLIANCE

Festival Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont.

- - -

Nobody ever accused Bernard Shaw of being self-effacing. He liked to write himself into his own plays, not only through his ideas, which can be blessedly hard to pin down as both he and his characters keep contradicting themselves, but through his perceived public personality. The central character (i.e., principal chatterbox) of Misalliance is John Tarleton, Edwardian underwear millionaire, self-made and self-taught, with a literary reference to fit every occasion. "Read Darwin," he will say when the conversation takes an appropriate turn, or "Read Chesterton." When the topic that arises is the Superman, Tarleton's recommendation is "Read Whatsisname." And he doesn't mean Nietzsche.

A bust of Whatsisname, a.k.a. GBS, and another of Ibsen, Whatsisname's playwrighting idol, adorn the set for Neil Munro's production of Misalliance, which opens this year's Shaw Festival. (It's also the first production of the new Jackie Maxwell regime, though in style, quality and personnel it's indistinguishable from the old Christopher Newton one. I'm not complaining.)

There's a moment in Shaw's text at which an incensed young man lashes out and smashes the nearest available object. In this production, that object is the Shavian head. "I never liked it much," says the next arrival, surveying the debris.

The director makes the author's presence felt in other ways as well. He begins with a film clip of Shaw, delightedly presenting his right "profeel" (his pronunciation) to the camera, before turning round and, with equal satisfaction, showing off his left. The message seems to be that you never know where to have him but he's always in control.

The play itself, as already noted, is pretty self-referential to begin with, not only in its incidental little jokes but in the larger joke that constitutes its very nature. It's subtitled A Debate in One Sitting (though an intermission is permitted), and it's like a parody of the popular idea of Shavian drama: all talk, no action.

Actually, Misalliance has plenty of action, much of it bizarre. What it doesn't have is plot. As for the chat, Hypatia Tarleton, daughter of the underwear magnate, complains repeatedly that her family and friends do nothing, but of course in doing so she's merely adding to the volume. (Pun intended. To put it another way: She can talk. And does.)

The title seems to derive from Hypatia's insecure engagement to an overbred young runt named Bentley Summerhays, but the last thing anyone cares about, either on stage or in the auditorium, is whether this match will go through. Hypatia prays for adventure to fall from the skies, and so it does when a plane crash-lands onto her father's grounds, piloted by an old friend of her fiance, and with a Polish lady acrobat as passenger.

This leads to amorous complications, but you could hardly say the play is about them. Nor, despite the protestations of its preface, is it about the relationships of parents and children; that's merely a subject that gets vigorously discussed from time to time. In the end it seems to be about itself, about its cheekiness in bending our ears, and its success in not boring us while doing so.

And, yes, it's probably about a privileged section of English society that is neither as secure nor as virtuous as it would like to believe. In that respect, it's a light-hearted preview of Heartbreak House, a more sombre Shavian talkfest that ends with an impromptu air raid, a more sinister attack from the skies. Munro pushes the resemblance as far as it will go, and probably farther; he begins with the sound of an aerial takeoff and ends with the characters huddled together in anticipation of another descent.

It's a prickly, enjoyable production that falters only when the director lets his own sense of humour, which is heavy-handed, get in the way of his author's; and a uniformly expert team of actors ensures this doesn't happen very often. Only two performers cause misgivings, and they, I suspect, are obeying orders.

One of them is Pete Treadwell as the would-be assassin of Tarleton, who long ago done his mother wrong. This character, a downtrodden but volubly politicized cashier, emerges from a portable Turkish bath brandishing a revolver (which earns him the name Gunner) and proclaiming, "I am the son of Lucinda Titmus!" Later, confronted by evidence of immorality in the Sussex countryside, he utters the dumbfounding prophecy, "Rome fell! Babylon fell! Hindhead's turn will come!"

It must be very difficult not to make him funny, and no other actor I have seen has tried, but Treadwell, who plays him as a genuine skulking menace, brings it off. The idea presumably is to make his subsequent description of the wage slave's appalling lot hit home, and to make his bullying at the hands of his betters seem despicable. Both these things happen, but they could have happened anyway, and been even more effective for the change in tone.

The opposite fault -- pressing too hard for laughs -- afflicts Sharry Flett's Mrs. Tarleton in her exaggerated efforts to make the Polish lady, who speaks the language perfectly, understand English. Mrs. Tarleton is in some respects a blinkered woman, but she doesn't deserve to have this kind of stupidity attached to her; indeed, she emerges later on as the wisest and most charitable person in the play, and when Flett is allowed to take this opportunity she does, beautifully.

Nobody is right, nobody is wrong, everyone is fallible and therefore funny, everybody has a case. That's the key to Shaw, and this company knows exactly how to turn it. Lorne Kennedy is a bantamweight but thoroughly persuasive Tarleton, spryly phrased and with solid Northern roots, and Michael Ball gives the wittiest performances of the evening as Lord Summerhays, ex-colonial governor, sinking into old age with every ironic nerve twitching.

Of the youngers: Laurie Paton plays the acrobat with suitably perfect poise, finding juice in every syllable; Mike Shara, beefily cast against his usual type, wins an excellent hearing for the proudly philistine Tarleton junior who would have infuriated his author had anyone else created him; David Leyshon snivels gently as Bentley; Graeme Somerville makes an ambiguously clean young Englishman out of the aeronaut, who has a better build-up than he has a role; and Jane Perry's pivotal Hypatia deserves the last word.

That indeed is what the text gives her, but she doesn't get it here. The production substitutes a final clip of Shaw himself, smiling, with infuriating assurance, as if to say, "There! I knew best." He didn't always, but in this instance he did. It seems a shame to contradict him.
