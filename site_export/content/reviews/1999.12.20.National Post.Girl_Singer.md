Two years ago I went to see Rosemary Clooney, performing what was until recently her annual New York cabaret season at Rainbow and Stars, on the top floor of Rockefeller Centre. (Its still there, but no longer a cabaret room.)

Her opening song in that show was a brisk, no-nonsense version of Get Me to the Church on Time. It was captivatingly joyous, and it brought a beaming smile to every face in the house. Everybody knew that Clooney, then aged 69, was about to get married to Dante DiPaolo, her companion of 20 years and the man largely responsible for steering her through the most gratifying comeback in popular music. At the end of the set she brought him on to sing, in Italian and very sweetly, Come Back to Sorrento.

It occurred to me, in my cheerfully tearful state, that I would not have sat still for this from any other performer. I wouldnt have felt especially interested in any other singers' marriage plans, and I certainly wouldn't have felt happy for them as I did for her.

But feeling happy for Clooney is a legitimate payback for pleasure received. She can make you laugh at a comedy song or sigh at a ballad, but so can other singers; her ability to move you with happiness is unique. It doesnt usually operate on so unabashedly personal a level; generally the joy she communicates is in the music itself. She is the best living interpreter of the great American songbook.

There are three factors in play at a Clooney performance: her musicianship, her presentation and her personal history.

Her professional life divides into three periods, all of them illuminated in her new autobiography, Girl Singer (Doubleday, $34.95), written -- very well -- with Joan Barthel. For most of the 1950s -- her own 20s -- she was Americas top female singer, her success sparked by Come On-a My House, a monster hit that she had to be forced to record. (I didn't," she says in the book, "have any appreciation for the song's slightly tongue-in-cheek, self-parodic edge. These days she introduces it as a folk-song, written by an Armenian, sung by a girl from Kentucky, so of course we did it Italian.")

In the 60s her career slipped and eventually crashed: The pressures included the ascendancy of rock n' roll, a traumatic marriage to Jose Ferrer, the assassination of Robert Kennedy, which she personally witnessed, and pills. "And so it came to pass that in the summer of 1968, Rosemary Clooney, girl singer, age 40, was locked up in the psychiatric unit of the hospital for which, not so long before, she'd smiled and broken ground.

Discharged, and broke with five children, she had to start over, rebuilding not only her career but, as it turned out, her entire vocal personality. The turning point came in 1977 when she recorded an album for a small Californian label, Concord Jazz; it proved to be the first in an annual series that is still continuing.

One of the songs on it was Hey There, from The Pajama Game, a big hit for her in the 50s. She had done it then as a straight, plaintive theatre ballad; now she swung it, with a wry edge.

It remains a constant in her act (so youll remember which one I was," she says when introducing it), but though she plays with nostalgia she doesn't play on it. When I heard that '77 LP I suspected within minutes that she was going to be my favourite singer, but sentiment had little to do with it; she was only a vaguely remembered voice from my childhood and I knew nothing of her breakdown. Unlike Judy Garland or Billie Holiday, she has not turned her scars into a style. Holiday sounded bruised; Clooney, with her deep and round and richly-contoured voice, sounds bruised and healed, which I heretically find more interesting.

Humour has a lot to do with it; she herself once identified it as a salient quality of her singing, but it comes between the songs as well. Her patter is the best I have ever heard; gossipy and anecdotal and apparently spontaneous. The last has to be an illusion; I have now heard three times the routine about the grandchild who provoked her wrath with an unflattering imitation of her act ("the kids out of the will!) but it cracks me up every time.

Shes even been known to play the critic, remarking that White Christmas (her best-known film) is not a movie you'd want to risk seeing at the wrong time of the year, or that I'll Be Seeing You has a weak verse but a strong chorus; I disagree with the latter assessment but Im impressed that she should bother to make it.

She sounds both shrewd and generous, quizzical and self-mocking, emotional but unsentimental, and it is these qualities that light up her singing, along with an expert and irresistible rhythmic sense. She denies that she is a jazz singer (she doesnt improvise) but if you accept the definition of The New Yorker's Whitney Balliett, that a jazz singer makes everything she sings swing, then Clooney -- the post-'74 Clooney -- sings quintessential jazz. She finds the pulse in anything that has one (leaving out the occasional folk or soft- rock number that couldnt swing if you hung it), urging even the most lachrymose ballad along, stripping it of self-pity, and making it more rather than less affecting in the process. On the latest of her 24 Concord CDs, at long last, she is teamed with the Count Basie orchestra, the liveliest of ghost-bands, and delivers a deliciously rueful Guess Ill Hang My Tears Out to Dry and a wonderfully poignant Willow Weep for Me.

In the first half of her Concord period, the material is organized by composer, anthologizing songs by Arlen, Mercer, Berlin and Clooney's Beverly Hills neighbour of many years, Ira Gershwin. She was accompanied, superbly, by small groups of mainly young musicians; the vocal-instrumental interplay was the finest since the heyday of Holiday.

More recently she has been backed by big bands, and gone punch for punch with them (the Basie set is the apogee of this), and chosen her songs according to loosely autobiographical themes: an approach that courts self-indulgence but has usually -- not invariably -- avoided it. Discs have been devoted to songs evoking New York, to capsule summaries of her career (including a hilariously uninhibited revisiting of her old hit, Mambo Italiano, in tribute to her husband, who is both Italian and a dancer) and to her professional and personal association with the master arranger, Nelson Riddle.

My own favourite, For the Duration, collects songs of the Second World War, and presents an aching, mature refraction of their original innocence; it includes a particularly fine account of These Foolish Things in which Clooney's voice gently smiles on the line about the song that Crosby sings," Bing having been her friend, mentor and duet-partner.

Once you start listing songs from these discs, there is no end. They function among other things as a living library of the standard repertoire, with the singer mining the songs that she grew up on for wit and meaning. I think of a transfixingly confidential One for My Baby, accompanied only by Canadian guitarist Ed Bickert and the sole version to challenge Sinatra; a tough I Dont Want to Walk Without You, Baby, full of decisive consonants; an understated Autumn in New York, the yearning shrugged off but still potent.

She does especially well by Cole Porter, sliding in and out of his trademarked iceberg elegance; her Miss Otis Regrets is a comic tour de force, very clipped and businesslike before becoming deliciously sanguinary, while her Dont Fence Me In takes a sophisticate's throwaway parody of a cowboy song and turns it into an expression of real feeling, while still relishing the fruity absurdity of a phrase like "straddle my old saddle.

Perhaps her finest quality, apart from her intelligence, is her economy, though the two of course go together; at 70 she may sometimes meet a note she doesn't like, but she never injures or inflates a word. She is the only singer, apart from their composer, who can handle the songs of Dave Frishberg, jazz's reigning melancholy clown. The others all embroider them to death; her wistful Do You Miss New York? and rollicking I Want to Be a Sideman are sublime.

The word everybody uses for her is warm"; after hearing her sing, or after reading her book, you feel that you have met a real woman and that you would like to meet her again.
