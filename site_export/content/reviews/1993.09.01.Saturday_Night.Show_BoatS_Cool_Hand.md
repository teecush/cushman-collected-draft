Harold Prince is known for making musicals that, within the narrow world of the theatre, make history. He also makes musicals that are made of history. I first became aware of this in 1968 when I saw the London production of Cabaret, the show that Prince himself rates as the real beginning of his style as a director. Probably everyone who saw the show remembers the opening: the uneasy silence in which the audience gazed at its own slightly distorted reflection in a huge on - stage mirror, then the roll of drums as the master of ceremonies proceeded to welcome us - in three languages - to the nightclubs of 1930s Berlin and topromise us a temporary escape from our troubles. "In here everything is beautiful... the girls are beautiful... even the orchestra is beautiful." The beauty was grotesquely rouged, the music was a sweet cacophony, but it was undeniably alluring: alluring and menacing.

As the evening progressed, the menace began to outweigh the allure. In the world outside the club, Hitler was coming closer to power, and an engagement scene in the second act -- a Jewish man, an Aryan woman -- was disrupted by a brick hurled through the window; I reflected at the time that, with pogroms the current in - subject for musicals, that single stone was scarier than all the carefully choreographed furniture - smashing in Fiddler on the Roof. The scene was followed by one of the shows periodic returns to the nightclub, where the routines had been getting progressively seedier. The number at this point was the famous one in which Joel Greys epicene MC danced romantically with a large, sad - faced lady gorilla and sang of how people misunderstood their relationship, building sweetly to the punch line: If you could see her through my eyes, she wouldn't look Jewish at all.

I was blown away. Nazi anti - Semitism had crept up on the audience, as insidiously as it must have done in the Germany the show depicted. It is rare enough for a straight play to capture the movement of history. It was, as far as I knew, unprecedented in a musical (though with its battery of production devices -- above all its in - built talent for self - parody - the musical starts with every advantage). And though many people -- librettist, composers, choreographer, designer -- had contributed to the effect, it was obvious that the show's trajectory had been plotted and piloted by one man. If programme credits meant anything, that man was the director, Hal Prince. (As Harold he seems to exist only in programmes. In every other context, including the title page of his autobiography, he is Hal. It must be the most constantly and affectionately employed diminutive in all of show business.) "If its a Hal Prince show he is the Muscle," wrote William Goldman in a show - by - show analysis of Broadway, The Season, and if you like Cabaret, praise him, but if you dont, blame him. I liked it.

After seeing the show -- it was the second night of the London run -- I ate with friends, and poured out my enthusiasm. When I got to "she wouldn't look Jewish at all," a frost descended. There was a young American lady in the party, and she had found the line gratuitously offensive. (Of course I'm Jewish myself, she said, smiling defensively and pugnaciously at the same time. "So am I," I said, probably looking much the same.) The song, she told me, had ended differently in New York, and she strongly implied that its present state must be a sop to English anti - Semitism.

Actually, I discovered, the show had previewed on Broadway with the incendiary line in place, but had changed it after objections from Jewish pressure groups deaf to arguments about dramatic context. Prince, producer then as well as director, regards his capitulation as a professional defeat -- one he emerged determined not to suffer again. (Fred Ebb, lyricist of Cabaret, said sorrowfully at the time, Ive never known you to pander before.) The affair of course prefigures the storm over Princes Toronto production of Show Boat, a point not lost on Prince himself. "Here," he said, we go again.

Prince says of Show Boat that, despite a "transcendent score," I've never seen it work entirely. Which might seem to give comfort to the show's detractors until you realize that hes talking not of the musicals racial attitudes but of its structure. Based on a sprawling novel, Oscar Hammerstein's script loses focus in its second half, largely deserting the showboat itself and leapfrogging all over the States, something Prince blames partly on the demands of the show's original 1927 producer, Florenz Ziegfeld. Prince has never directed a full - dress musical revival before, unless you count Cabaret, to which he returned twenty years after his original production, and Candide, a wholesale re - inventing, with a heavily adapted score and an entirely new text, of a show that had flopped first time round. He has directed operas, though, specializing in Puccini: "Id never seen a Butterfly or Fanciulla that I liked, so I did them; Ive seen any number of wonderful Bobemes so why compete? I want to see if I can make Show Boat work for me."

If the show's size is a problem, it's also an attraction. It has a time span of forty years -- the 1880s to the 1920s -- and Princes design - scheme is based on the passage of time -- from gaslight to automobile -- in line with his belief that "the most effective social commentary is to illuminate one period through another." Cabaret -- in its creators minds, if not in those of audiences -- was as much concerned with the 1960s and civil rights as with the thirties and Nazi Germany; and the passage of time has been a Prince preoccupation in such later, and diverse, works as Follies and Pacific Overtures. Apparently, though, historical does not, in this instance, mean "political"; and on this matter Prince sounds exasperated. This isn't a political musical. It's an absolutely romantic, beautiful story about naive, good - hearted mummers on a show - boat. Still, that boat is being designed not by Maria Bjornson, who did Princes visually ravishing Pbantom of the Opera, but by Eugene Lee, his collaborator on the overwhelmingly threatening Sweeney Todd; to judge from the set - model, this is not going to be a prettified Mississippi.

There is an imbalance in the show as written, though not one that any of the protesters have pointed out. The theme of racial oppression is subordinate to the main love story, but theatrically it bulks very large, inspiring some of the most powerful writing: "Ol Man River" - - not explicitly a protest song but certainly a song about suffering and the one part of the show that everybody knows -- and the miscegenation episode in which Julie, the mulatto actress, is thrown off the showboat for the crime of being married to a white man. This, despite Prince's insistence that Show Boat is "not a musical of 'Strange Fruit [the celebrated Billie Holiday song about a lynching]," has to be taken seriously.

Of course Im taking it seriously. I have respect for these characters; I had respect before people started making any noise. What does Joe [the stevedore who sings Ol' Man River] actually do? No previous production has made it clear what his job is; I'm going to. Theres a difference between history and stereotyping. Butif they say that you cant use a sepia photograph of a black man carrying a bale on his back, then that's denying history. Nobody is going to question my integrity." (The italics are his, if it's possible to talk in italics.)

All the same, the controversy exists, and it hasnt gone away. Is it going to weigh on him in rehearsal?

Not one whit. The show will offend no - one. That Im darned sure of. People misunderstand. They misunderstood Cabaret. But they got over it quite quickly."

Nor was Cabaret even the first time. The New York Board of Rabbis, Prince says, "harassed me over Fiddler on the Roof." This sounds improbable, but apparently they objected to the rabbi in the show being depicted as a marginally comic character. Like Cabaret, and indeed like Show Boat, Fiddler is a show that plays off the epic against the domestic. Prince produced but did not direct it; although his directing career was under way when the show was conceived, he was always insistent that nobody except Jerome Robbins should stage it. There's a paradox here: Robbins -- an icy, dictatorial figure who withdrew from musicals in favour of ballet where he could have even more complete control -- nevertheless came up with warm, moving shows: Fiddler or West Side Story (and a string of totally light - hearted musicals before them). Prince -- confident, courteous, and gregarious -- has the reputation of doing cool shows: a label that he acknowledges and ascribes to his own sense of what excites him in the theatre: "theatrics... tension... conflict."

These are not the qualities traditionally associated with musicals though there is no reason why they shouldn't be. But Princes position in musicals is an anomalous one. He has now produced twenty of them and directed twenty (that doesnt make forty shows; there is a considerable overlap), and he has won more awards than anyone else in the field. To many people, he personifies the musical. But he says it's a form that he slipped into and that he "didn't like musicals as a kid." He was born in 1928 into a New York middle - class family, and grew up in the thirties and forties seeing a lot of plays. His idols were such as Orson Welles and Eva Le Gallienne who worked in classical theatre, and its easy to see how Welless scope and ambition have affected his own work. prince did see musicals as well in those days; he seems to have seen everything. He still talks excitedly about having caught the first major revival of Porgy and Bess in 1942; but a more typical show of the period was Rodgers and Hart's Too Many Girls (1939), about a football - mad college where the girl students wore beanies for as long as they remained virgins, and the principal narrative interest was in seeing which were still wearing them by the end of the evening. Those silly old shows with the swell songs are, for Prince, dead beyond recall, and he seems mystified that so many people should regard them with affection and even want to revive them. But then he has also confessed that "there are pitfalls in my preferences, and they tend to take the fun out of an evening." He seems on firm ground, though, in his bewilderment at silly new shows that don't even have swell songs.

Still, it was through the world of Too Many Girls that Prince came to work in musicals. That show, like many more of its era, was directed by the legendary George Abbott, and it was to Abbotts office that Prince, an ambitious theatre - struck twenty - year - old, naturally gravitated. Abbott hired him for his brightness and enthusiasm; Prince worked as a stage manager on Abbott musicals and came to admire the complex craft involved. From Abbott and later from Robbins, he found that "I could use the form to do what I wanted" -- which wasnt necessarily what they had wanted. And gradually he was seduced: I've done straight plays, but without music I feel like I'm only doing half a show. Or: a rehearsal room without a piano in it has to be kidding.

In 1953 Prince and an older colleague, Robert Griffith, were stage - managing Abbotts production of a Leonard Bernstein musical, Wonderful Town. They were looking for a property to produce, and they found it in a novel, Seven and a Half Cents, by Richard Bissell. Their employer became their employee, Abbott co - writing and directing the musical version that they called The Pajama Game. He also helped them raise the money, though this apolitical showmans typically blunt announcement that "this is a musical about a strike in a pajama factory" scared away more backers than it attracted. It was Prince, the socially conscious, who charmed the investors by describing it as Romeo and Juliet in the Midwest. The show was a hit and Prince became famous as, in Goldman's words, "the last of the boy wonder producers."

So he can claim, I've been doing political musicals since 1954 -- a claim that seems a little excessive until you look up a standard history of the musical that describes this harmlessly enjoyable show as "noisy, coarse - grained and leftish," three traits apparently equally objectionable. It makes you realize how much Prince has been up against when he has tried to buck the Broadway mentality -- or, more precisely, the Broadway - fanciers mentality.

Do you, I asked him recently, ever feel out of place doing musicals?"

No. Why should I?"

Well, to be blunt, you and your work often seem a lot more intelligent than most of whats around.

Yeah. That's certainly blunt. I suppose I do feel like a fish out of water sometimes. I feel out of place when a jolly, mindless musical is overly welcomed. Unless it's stylish. I loved Me and My Girl. And Ill take Guys and Dolls and How To Succeed every time.

To this list of stylish comedy musicals I imagine he would add A Funny Thing Happened on the Way to the Forum, which he produced but didnt direct, and On the Twentieth Century, an updated comic opera about sublimely selfish theatricals, which he directed but didn't produce. Somewhere in the same category -- though it's romantic rather than comic escapism -- I would place The Phantom of the Opera, far and away his greatest commercial success. (Prince likes success; when we spoke he delighted in pointing out that Phantom was still the highest - grossing show on Broadway.) Surprisingly weak on story, Phantom is a gorgeous feat of visual orchestration. A sneering review in The Globe and Mail loftily declared that spectacle can be bought in. Of course it can; so can good actors. But, once bought, they have to be used. (Princes name, amazingly, was omitted from the Globe reviews of both Phantom and Kiss of the Spider Woman. Nobody is obliged to like either show, but to ignore the director of two productions to which the director was so obviously crucial is professionally irresponsible. If you hate them, blame him.)

The Harold S. Prince of early days -- the middle initial presumably there to balance that of his partner, Robert E. Griffith -- appears in photographs as an eager, glossy - haired young executive, fairly recently out of college. Robert Morse once played him that way in a musical roman a clef called Say, Darling. Prince, unamused at the time, later acknowledged that it was a funny portrayal, adding that Morse -- who now, decades later, is his Capn Andy in Show Boat -- "still looks like me. It is I who don't." And indeed young Harold Prince -- the S. was dropped when Griffith died in the early 1960s -- is almost unrecognizable in the Hal Prince of today: white - bearded, spectacles permanently elevated on his shining dome (he must sometimes wear them on his nose, but nobody can remember seeing them there), an appealing mixture of handyman and sage. His energy is proverbial and, though much of his artistic frame of reference is European, his enthusiasm is engagingly all - American: nobody else can get away as he does with describing something as swell or addressing any gathering of two or more people as "gang." Searching for something to say against him, I can come up half - heartedly with a story I once heard of his storming out of a rehearsal of one of his less - successful productions exclaiming, I don't care what you do, only make it better; and even that might be taken as a demonstration of faith in his assistants. An English actor who loves working with him confirms his impatience at rehearsals -- "He has the lowest boredom threshold of anyone I know" -- and it seems generally agreed that detailed work with actors is not his strongest suit.

I prefer sweeping, dramatic grand theatre. Small, delicate character studies are not my forte. I dont think my experience with musicals has fitted me to direct Ibsen and Chekhov. I do think its fitted me to direct Shakespeare. [He never has, though.] I don't talk for two weeks about motivation; I wouldn't know how to spend two weeks that way. I point the actors at the end result and lead them to it; the more experienced and inventive they are, the less I have to do, the more I serve as editor.

On his first major directing assignment, an atypical musical (it might even qualify as small and delicate") called She Loves Me (1963), the actors asked a lot of awkward questions. Nowadays its easier, as he acknowledges: "I bring a whole lot of experience and reputation with me. Theyre not going to argue with me.

From She Loves Me (Bock and Harnick) to last year's Kiss of the Spider Woman (Kander and Ebb) Prince has worked with nearly all of the last great generation of Broadway composers, all like him now in their sixties: not the last talented generation but the last whom economics permitted to make a fulfilling career in the theatre. From 1970 to 1981 he worked consistently with the best known of them all.

When Steve Sondheim and I were working together, people thought we were taking the fun out of musicals. Sure we were -- for anyone who wants a steady diet of Hello, Dolly! and Mame. The six - show collaboration of Sondheim, composer - lyricist, and Prince, producer - director, is unique in theatrical history. They had known one another since 1949 -- they met at the opening night of South Pacific, one having come with Rodgers and the other with Hammerstein -- but it is unlikely that when they finally embarked on their first full collaboration they envisaged a decade's worth of joint ventures. But their talents turned out to be complementary: Sondheim digging into human character -- and especially human frailty -- in a manner unprecedented in musicals, Prince conjuring up the broad social context. (Sondheim once summed up Princes talent as the "ability to handle unconventional material in terms of truly popular theatre.") Even when their preoccupations might seem to conflict, they managed to coexist. Sondheim, the prime mover in this case, conceived the musical of Sweeney Todd (1979) as a serial - killing melodrama expanded into a tragedy of obsession; Prince -- who says I cant afford to be interested in revenge -- saw it as a parable of social alienation in the industrial age and set the whole thing in a giant factory. In strictest terms the production was too big for the play; and yet -- with archetypal performances from Len Cariou and Angela Lansbury that got the best of both worlds -- the human passions survived, and the combination looms in memory as an enormous thing. (The National Theatre in London has just staged a fine, smaller production, but it doesn't have the same impact.) Prince and Sondheim always knew how to end a show, and the closing moment of this one -- a contemptuous slam of a great door by the two leading characters, both locked in their own disgust -- was blackly witty and brilliantly powerful.

And so, in a different key, was the augustly sorrowful narrator who bade us "welcome to Japan" while a Broadway chorus, made up of Asian - Americans, raved on behind him. This was the close of their previous show, PacificOvertures (1976), which presented the forcible opening - up of Japan to European and American interests in the nineteenth century as a No or Kabuki play gradually infiltrated by the tones and techniques of the American musical. Only Prince would have wanted to turn that subject into a musical, and probably only he would have come up with that theatrical metaphor. Sondheim, who "couldn't have been less interested in politics," had to be talked into the project and then fell in love with it; Prince said, He may not come in political but he goes out political. Obviously he relished the opportunity to cauterize political opportunism, East and West (the show is remarkably even - handed); he also explored the human consequences, so that a musical chronicle with little conventional characterization was nevertheless suffused with emotion.

The production, not surprisingly, lost its entire investment; and Prince himself had to put up the money to bring it into New York. It virtually ended his career as an independent producer, though I suspect that he often regards this as a blessing in disguise. Ambitious as it was, the show was never pretentious. It seemed the logical way to tell the story. And this has been the great strength of Prince and Sondheim; they go where their intelligence leads them. Or, as Prince puts it, "I put on the stage what I would like to see myself."

This includes a lot of machinery. I sat with Prince at a technical rehearsal for the New York opening of Kiss of the Spider Woman and marvelled at his apparent command of every element of sound, setting, lighting, music, and performance; also at his good humour under pressure. He kept reverting, as he invariably does these days, to the impoverishment of Broadway; to the lack of opportunities for experiment or for developing new talent; to the prohibitive sky - high economics; to the lack of community spirit in a threatened and diminishing theatrical population; to the impossibility, in short, of a career such as his ever getting started today. He pointed to a rehearsal pianist talented enough, he said, to be the next Gershwin or Sondheim, but who would give him the break?

Well, I said, maybe you could."

The gleam in his eye suggested that he very well might.

Hey," he said sharply, turning back to the stage, "look at that." One of his favourite numbers was in progress; he has turned into an ideal father - figure, but he still seemed boyishly happy in his work.

There is more to Kiss than computerized cues. Part of the thrill of it is the expertise with which it has been manoeuvred; part is the skill in storytelling; part is the moral passion, which feels genuine; a larger part still is the music, which has real romantic appeal; and the largest part is the human element. Brent Carver's performance is about as good as I have seen in a musical. Though all three of the stars won Tonys, and the show itself was voted best musical, Prince lost out as best director. He has won as many prizes as anybody needs so one hardly needs to cry for him (yes, he directed Evita as well), but it was odd. He drove the show. And it was a swell ride.
