(From the National Post, Thursday, June 29, 2000) Reporting on the Doras two days ago, I re-christened prize-winning director Daniel Brooks as Richard. Sorry. *****

Last night, at the Pantages Theatre, Toronto's Dora Mavor Moore Awards came of age. Well, anyway, they celebrated their 21st birthday.

They are, like most awards, weird. They are administered by the Toronto Theatre Alliance, and it seems that, once upon a time, just about everybody in the business (or, as they insist on calling themselves, the Community) got to vote, rather like with the Oscars. This apparently led to abuses, and in the last few years, it has been changed.

The awards are divided into four categories: General Theatre (big and medium shows), Independent Theatre (small shows -- and independent of what, for God's sake?), Theatre for Young Audiences, and Dance. Each is appraised by a separate jury of 10, compromising both participants and onlookers. Juries even include some critics, though none whom I know personally. (I'm not suggesting that's necessarily a bad thing.)

These jurors nominate five people for each prize, and then at a later date they choose a winner from among them. Since it's the same people voting at each stage, it's hard to see why they don't cut straight to the chase instead of teasing everybody. But it presumably helps in the publicity build-up. The envelope please -- and try not to drop it. (People do drop things at the Doras. I suppose it's endearing.)

The 1999-2000 season in Toronto was a strange one. Most of it was dull, but there was a small embarrassment of riches at the top. The most starved category this year was New Play, General: There was no predestined winner on the order of last year's The Drawer Boy. Ronnie Burkett's Street of Blood (a puppet show) and Morris Panych's The Overcoat (a mime) would have been worthy contenders this year, but both were excluded, presumably on technical grounds. If it was on grounds of quality, we're really in trouble. The best conventionally written play, and an unusually rich text, was Florence Gibson's Belle. Thankfully, it was nominated; depressingly, it lost. The joint winners were Linda Griffiths' Alien Creature, the latest instalment in Toronto's ongoing romance with the vaguely literary one-person show, and For the Pleasure of Seeing Her Again, a sub-standard Michel Tremblay.

Both Griffiths and Tremblay are, of course, long-standing OK names in Canadian theatre. To be fair, the equally revered George F. Walker and Judith Thomson failed this year to make the cut -- though Thomson's Perfect Pie should probably have been there.

Outstanding New Musical (an even murkier category) was the Ann- Marie MacDonald/Allen Cole show Anything That Moves, as good a choice as any. Things improve, markedly, when we get to the area of production.

Last year, notoriously, the voters shut out the Soulpepper company. This year it did rather better, if not quite well enough. Any one of Soulpepper's five 1999 shows would have been a credible nominee, or indeed winner, for best direction, but the two that actually got on the list -- Richard Brooks' Endgame and Laszlo Marton's Platonov -- probably deserved it the most. In the event, both won: Endgame for best production and Platonov for best direction. How anyone who wasn't at rehearsals is supposed to tell the difference is beyond me, but it spreads the glory around a bit. The directors' category was in fact the most agonizing, since two non-Soulpepper nominations -- Brooks' Faust and Panych's The Overcoat -- were equally deserving. (The fifth directorial entrant, Sarah Stanley's Beaver, must have been either a joke or a gesture of sympathy for being saddled with an unworkable play -- though, considering what was excluded, it's not funny.)

Another crowded category was that for best lead female in a play. The odd thing here is that none of the best available performances (Fiona Reid in The Beauty Queen of Leenane, Kristin Thomson in A Streetcar Named Desire, Nancy Palk in Perfect Pie) was even nominated. Of those who got the nod, only Yanna McIntosh in Belle was in that league. The actual winner was Nora McLellan in Music for Contortionist, another one-woman show, though I suppose you couldn't call it literary.

The men fared better. Soulpepper's Diego Matamoros got, deservedly, to run against himself: He won for Endgame and lost for The Play's the Thing. (The wrong way around, I think, but congrats anyway.)

The other nominees -- Oliver Becker (Beauty Queen), Donald Sutherland (Enigma Variations) and Burkett -- were worthy competition, though no more than Albert Schultz (Platonov), Peter Donaldson (Our Town) or Randy Hughson (Faust) would have been.

The insane policy of lumping opera and musicals together resulted, this year, in the older form not getting a thing. (Not even an opera house). A surprise, though not necessarily a mistake, was the judges' ranking of Sam Mendes' Cabaret, an overheated account of a good show, above Julie Taymor's The Lion King, an inspired salvage job on a poor one. (Taymor did get the costume award. Denying her that could have caused riots in the streets.) Norbert Leo Butz's show-off turn as the emcee in Cabaret was a disappointingly obvious choice for best musical male. Graham Bickley's straight-arrow hero in The Pajama Game (somebody has to do it, and he did it well) was far better -- as indeed were both Joely Fisher and the magnificent Alma Cuervo among the Cabaret ladies. I've no qualms, though, about the actual winner of the musical female award, Paula Wolfson in Shaking the Foundations. She was a turn-on.

Outstanding Performance in a Featured Role, Play or Musical, Male or Female (how do you choose?) went to Jonathan Wilson, one of the comics in The Lion King. He was all right, but when you consider they might have given it to Robyn Stevan in Platonov or Waneta Storms for her lovely work in Faust ...

Down among the independents, there were uncontroversial prizes for Building Jerusalem and Chekhov's Shorts. And, at the opposite extreme of prosperity, there were deserved special awards for David Mirvish, the producer, and Joan Chalmers, the patroness extraordinaire.

Theatre Direct, consistently first-rate by any standards, took the Young Audiences Prize for its production of The General.

The most disgracefully neglected show (in any category): Sibs, a sparkling two-hander , which may have committed the crime of being too entertaining. The Toronto Theatre Alliance seems to be a solemn as well as a sentimental bunch.
