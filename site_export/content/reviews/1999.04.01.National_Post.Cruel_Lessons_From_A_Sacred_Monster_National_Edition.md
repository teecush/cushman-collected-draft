MASTER CLASS

The Royal Alexandra Theatre

There are two plays going on in Master Class. One of them can be very entertaining and the other is at least informative. They have the same central character, and they do, in a way, complement one another. But they do not add up to a satisfying whole.

In 1971, with her operatic career virtually over, Maria Callas agreed to give a series of public classes to aspiring singers at the Juilliard School in New York. These sessions provide the frame, and much of the detail, for Terrence McNally's script. It takes place in a bare, spacious cream-coloured room with imposing double doors in its back wall and a splendid cupola ceiling -- a great set (Michael McGarty is the designer) that, in its combined suggestion of gymnasium and church, pays appropriate tribute to the artists strenuous devotion to her art. This is certainly what the Callas of the play preaches, and it seems fair to assume that its what she has practised.

Elizabeth Ashley, playing the diva, enters -- but naturally -- through the double doors and proceeds to greet and harangue the assembly, played by us. The house lights are up, which is unsettling. (Are we meant to feel unsettled? If so, why? And are we meant to join in, to offer responses when the lady seems to be buttonholing us? Some people did. I rather wished they hadn't.) Callas finds it unsettling as well and complains, and after some humorous delay her complaints take effect. Everybody relaxes -- the audience, Callas, and the actress playing her, who up to this point has been noticeably edgy, seeking authority rather than embodying it.

Art, says McNally's Callas very wisely, is "making people think that for that precise moment in time there is only one way, one voice -- yours."

Unencumbered by any particular memories of the original, I could accept Ashleys Callas, for most of her moments, as the real thing. She is an imposing performer, with a sulphurous voice, a handsome presence, and an air of lived-in outrageousness. She speaks in the measured tones of someone who takes herself absolutely at her own valuation. Her timing, as opposed to her delivery, is very fast; and the meat of the show comes when she has a chance at cut-and-thrust.

Three victims, I mean students, present themselves for her evaluation -- one male, two female. The man gets a noticeably easier ride than the women. Each of the three delivers an aria; each is stopped after barely a note has been sung, interrogated, lectured, and criticized. It isnt their voices that's in question; it's their interpretation, their commitment, and even their appearance. A look, she says, is all-important: "If you dont have a look, get one, as soon as possible."

Sophie, the first contestant, has the wrong look. She is a plump girl in a pink flowered dress, and the divine one, perhaps remembering her own early weight problems, fairly dismembers her. Sophie, however, is possessed of a placid good humour and, though somewhat distressed, is not destroyed. In our eyes -- though not perhaps in Callas -- she certainly gives as good as she gets. And Frances Limoncelli's amused performance is absolutely perfect.

Tony (Alexander Kariotis) is a golden-voiced stud who wants to be Mario Lanza. Sharon (Melissa LaFrance), having perhaps taken to heart the earlier comments on a look, presents herself in a long, slinky number, purple and backless, that evokes an equally withering response. I would love to say that she also sings a long, slinky number, but she actually offers the letter aria from Verdi's Macbeth, which is neither. She proves Callas most formidable antagonist, being good but not in the way Callas would like. She, too, is very well played, by LaFrance.

Ashley apart, all the performers in Leonard Foglias production come from music rather than theatre, but their acting can hardly be faulted. In this respect, they're living up to Callas' own proclaimed standards. This even goes for the onstage pianist, whom Jeff Lewis endows with the courteous sufferance that has been the badge of the accompanist tribe since the beginning of musical time.

While Sophie sings, and while Sharon sings, the stage darkens and Callas thoughts go back to her own past -- to her early struggles, to her rejection and her triumphs, to her marriage, and in particular to her liaison with Aristotle Onassis -- to return in her mind from master class to mistress class. These monologues allow the star to stretch out (Ashleys earth-toned evocation of Onassis is terrific) and they provide some clues about the evolution of the sacred monster we have been observing. But they are an essentially crude device that lands the play in an indeterminate zone between full biography and licensed impressionism.

Back in the present tense, Sharon lashes out at Callas for trying to turn her students into carbon copies of herself. Callas responds with another statement, a little weary this time, of her own artistic beliefs: an avowal of the essential selflessness of egomania. Both are probably right. But by this point Ashley has run out of new notes to strike, and so has the play. It has held out against being just a solo turn, and against sentimentality about the sacred mission of the artist, but in the end it succumbs. You don't have to be a genius to portray a genius, unless genius happens as here to be your essential subject.
