THEATRE REVIEW

42nd Street

Festival Theatre, Stratford

When David Merrick, also known as Broadways Abominable Showman, purchased the stage rights to the 1933 Warner Bros. movie musical 42nd Street, he didnt stop there.

There are only four real songs in the movie so, to eke them out, Merrick also bought up the entire catalogue of numbers that Harry Warren, the composer, and Al Dubin, the lyricist, wrote for Warner Bros. in the '30s. One of the most famous of these was Lullaby of Broadway, which is from Gold Diggers of 1935. It became, in many ways, the defining number of the new stage show. (Well, new in 1980, when Merrick produced it.) It sums up the appeal of the show. It also indicates what's wrong with it.

The song happens when Peggy Sawyer, the eager and naive but super-talented young chorine from Allentown, has to be wooed back into the musical Pretty Lady to take over from the star Dorothy Brock, who has broken her leg. Peggys sensitive soul has already been badly bruised by the rehearsal process and shes about to catch the train home, so Julian Marsh, the tough and tyrannical but (we assume) also super-talented director, descends on the railroad station to persuade her to change her mind, closely followed by the entire company. They beg her to consider their jobs, their hard work and - most of all - the theatre itself. Lullaby of Broadway is the medium through which they deliver their message.

It is, as it has always been, an irresistible toe-tapper of a tune with a highly evocative lyric. Except that, if you actually listen to that lyric, what it evokes has nothing to do with what the performers are supposedly trying to say. The song is a down-to-earth celebration of New York's underside: subways, taxis, laterising good-time-girls and their sugar-daddies, "the daffodils who entertain at Angelo's and Maxies." In the Gold Diggers movie, it climaxes in a suicide. About the one feature of Broadway it doesnt mention is the theatre.

Well, I guess nobody does listen to lyrics. The calculation is that we will just hear the word Broadway and melt. The same goes for Julian's pre-song invocation of "musical comedy - the most marvellous words in the English language." Nobody really thinks that, and a musical that feels the need to proclaim it obviously doesn't. This one is saying, Look at all these outdated sentiments we dont believe any longer; arent they sweet and ridiculous? The word for that is camp.

I should add that in Gary Griffin's Stratford production of Lullaby of Broadway is very well staged: better, in fact, than I remember it being in Gower Champion's 1980 original. Griffins version, choreographed by Alex Sanchez, is less clangorous, more playful, more seductive, especially in the way Sean Arbuckle as Marsh moves around Peggy on her bench before the reinforcements arrive. The scene finds Arbuckle at his best; elsewhere hes damagingly miscast, ineradicably gentle, neither driven nor driving enough.

However many pills he pops, he can't convince us that Pretty Lady is his all-important last chance. This, admittedly, is something on which the show itself goes easy, and why it's a betrayal of the film on which its based. The book (by Michael Stewart and Mike Bramble) does throw in the odd reference to breadlines, and the interpolated numbers include the sardonic Were in the Money, but the Depression becomes a faraway fantasy. In the movie, within its backstage limits, it looks and feels real.

All that said, I had quite a good time. This very proscenium show takes surprisingly well to the open stage, letting the audience peer down into the centre of the production numbers; and Griffin, a master of musical storytelling, moves it smoothly along. There are some neat scenic jokes, plus a few involving Michael Barber's invigorating onstage band; indeed the production's own shafts of self-mockery are defter than those of the script, which often seems to be hedging its bets between hand-on-heart and tongue-in-cheek.

Cynthia Dale as Dorothy gets the stylistic balance, along with the modulation from spoiled diva to sympathetic professional, exactly right; as she sings it, About a Quarter to Nine is the best number in the show, and not just because its the simplest. Steve Ross and C. David Johnson do more for her rival suitors than the roles probably deserve. Jennifer Rider-Shaw has an appealing pudgy earnestness as Peggy and justifies the storys claims for her singing and, especially, her dancing, while Kyle Blair is relaxed dynamite as the juvenile lead who takes her under his wing and into his heart.

The chorus, not all of whom pass strict muster as "kids," come in an unusual variety of shapes and sizes, but not of colours; of course a musical company in 1933 wouldn't have been integrated, but this show doesn't seem to be going in much for historical realism. Anyway, as a tap extravaganza, it doesnt measure up to My One and Only a few seasons back. Geoffrey Tyler (oil) and Gabrielle Jones (vinegar) add flavour as Pretty Ladys writers, reminding us how much TV's Smash must owe to 42nd Street; Peggy is really a Karen without an Ivy. It's even harder here than in Smash to imagine what plot the show-within-a-show could possibly have, but its nice that the authors get to perform Shuffle Off to Buffalo, the number thats gets closest to the surreal suggestiveness of the Busby Berkeley original.

Berkeley's more elaborately kaleidoscopic routines of course could never be recreated in a theatre; which of course raises the question, why try? James Magruder's program note tells us that the 50-year delay in moving 42nd Street from screen to stage "isnt so mysterious." It isnt mysterious at all; in 1980 people weren't doing that kind of thing. It's only in later years that the theatre has become dependent on pointless imitations of things that were done perfectly the first time around and that still exist. Its a depressing and ultimately disastrous trend.

At least 42nd Street can claim to have been ahead of the curve; it wasnt quite the first, but it's still probably the best.
