THE PROOF

duMaurier Theatre Centre

- - -

You've heard about the terrifying telegram: the one that read simply "ignore previous telegram" when there had been no previous telegram. The two linked productions, brought to World Stage by the Belgian company De Onderneming, are something like that, though from a critic's point of view maybe even scarier. In this case, there has been a previous telegram but, memory being what it is, it's impossible to ignore. You're confused already.

Let me try to explain. The Notebook, which I reviewed on Thursday, and its sequel The Proof, are based on a trilogy of novels by Agota Kristof, who is Hungarian and not, as I previously and carelessly assumed, Polish. The Notebook presents the experiences of twin boys scrambling for survival in an unnamed but guessable Central European country during the Second World War; it takes its title from a journal supposed to have been kept by the pair of them. At the end of the play, after the country has passed from Nazi occupation to Communist dictatorship, one of the brothers, now aged fifteen, escapes across the border. The Proof tells what happens to him afterwards. And almost the first thing we learn is that nearly everything that's in the notebook (and therefore in The Notebook) was a lie. For a start, the two brothers we thought we saw were only one, or at least one and a fantasy companion. These means that nearly everything I wrote in my review was a lie, too. I fell right in.

The Notebook was about not letting anything take you by surprise; it was linear and Brechtian, complete with captions. (Many of these on the first night failed to appear. I'm not sure whether this was an accident, or the production's own jokey little tip-off as to what was in store.) The Proof, by contrast, is dark-cornered and full of surprises; they are relentless, disorienting and finally very moving. It's impossible to discuss either play without giving stuff away, and if you plan to see them, which I strongly recommend, you might do better not to read this review until afterwards. Bravo, the Internet.

In The Notebook, none of the characters were named. In The Proof, nearly all of them are. We discover that the boy who crossed the border was called Lucas; the brother whom everybody tells him he imagined was named Claus. We start by getting a look at Lucas's life before he escaped; one seedier, though less primitive, then the story that was told before. He was more engaged with the community around him, but essentially even lonelier. (Before, he said that the woman under whose roof he lived, now dead, was his grandmother. She wasn't.)

Twenty-five years later, with international tensions reduced, Lucas returns to his homeland. He says he is looking for his brother Claus. What's more, though again nobody will believe it, he finds him. The two brothers had never shared an evacuee existence; Lucas had made that up to assuage an unbearable loneliness. They had been separated at four years old, when their parents' marriage broke bloodily up. (I doubt if papa and mama did their twin sons any favours by giving them anagrammatic names,either). Claus's life, though more comfortable than Lucas's, has been every bit as unhappy; maybe more so.

Reviewing The Notebook, I tried hard to see the twins' plight as emblematic of the fate of children in times of war. I was wasting my time. War obviously doesn't help, but it turns out to be only the backdrop to an agony that could happen anytime, anywhere. Nor do I take much stock in the idea that the boys are symbols of a divided Europe, even if it's what the author herself thinks. As surely as Oedipus, Claus and Lucas are pursued through their lives by childhood trauma, aggravated by adult cruelty and folly, their own included. (The young orphaned Lucas in an institution is a very nasty piece of work; Claus is a coward.) It may not quite be tragedy, but it's as close to it as our age is likely to get, and it's presented with unflinching, and ungloating, honesty. Claus believes Lucas's story, but he cannot bear to admit it, and the last part of the story shows us why.

In the end, despite the false trails and changes of front (gleefully aggravated here by having a company of four cover all the parts), it all adds up. Well, nearly all; there are still some puzzles and ambiguities, including the minor one of why we're constantly told that Lucas plays the harmonica, when we actually see him playing the accordion.But I don't believe that the play's ultimate goal is mystification, and I wouldn't have much time for it if it were. The story that emerges by the end is the one we're meant to believe. Anyway, I'm sticking to it.

Underrunning the narrative is the idea of storytelling itself, as a device for validating a life. As somebody says, rather extravagantly, "He who writes nothing is lost/He has merely passed through life without leaving a trace." And in Eastern Europe, whose soul so long resided in banned books, that may not be altogether a joke. Lucas writes his notebook, though he apparently pieced it together very quickly and long after the events it purports to depict. Claus puts his experiences into poems which are described, unsurprisingly, as "definitely not amusing" but powerful, and are published semi-anonymously. He writes them under the unsympathetic eye of his mother, who keeps comparing him unflatteringly to his long-lost brother. And this theme must account for an early, grimly funny digression about a would-be writer who falls apart when his loving but overpowering sister actually gives the opportunity to write . (He's also a smoker who would give it up except that, being an alcoholic, he doesn't have the will power.) There's a strong whiff of incest in all the story-lines, as indeed there was in The Notebook, though it seems less taken for granted in town than in the country

As in The Notebook, the actors have made their own adaptation, and perform, superbly, under their own joint direction. The mood though has changed. The Notebook was light, a perverse pastoral; The Proof is heavier, more detailed, more citified. The same actors play the brothers, Gunther Lesage as lanky Lucas and Robby Cleiren as chubby Claus, but where the previous style was about denying feeling, this is about showing it; even repression here is a kind of demonstration. Ryszard Turbiasz supplies a balancing slyness, and can be very funny. Carly Wijs exchanges the flirtatious Scots of her earlier roles for sardonic London. Your last chance of catching both plays is today, and you should take it. They're wonderful.
