THEATRE REVIEW DIONYSUS

Tuesday, September 19, 1995

Robert Cushman

Based on The Bacchae by Euripides Directed by Tadashi Suzuki Starring the Suzuki Company of Toga At Harbourfront Centre in Toronto until Thursday Rating: Drearily predictable world-view mangles Euripides play Reviewed by Robert Cushman I HAD some trouble understanding this Japanese adaptation of The Bacchae, and not just because I don't speak Japanese. Euripides's tragedy, perhaps the starkest ever written, stages the conflict between Dionysus, god of wine and the raging unconscious, and Pentheus, the young and uptight King of Thebes. Dionysus's cult has invaded Thebes, and Pentheus wants to stamp it out. He imprisons its prophet: actually the god himself in disguise. The prophet escapes. He then persuades Pentheus to dress in drag to spy on the female Dionysiacs cavorting in the hills. The women discover and dismember him. Their leader is Pentheus's own mother.

There is nothing just about this; Dionysus may represent impulses in humanity that it represses at its own peril but he is also avenging a slight against his family. The Bacchae, like all Greek tragedies, only more so, is about the unfairness of life, raised to a cosmic level. There may be a suggestion that wisdom lies in some psychic compromise between the sensualist and the censor, but the play gives little indication that such a safety-zone actually exists.

The most obvious departure in Tadashi Suzuki's Dionysus is that it leaves out Dionysus. He makes no personal appearance, and his lines have been appropriated by the chorus: six sacerdotal gentlemen in pointed hats that made them look like bishops and to a friend in the audience like Venetian Doges. She shouldn't have said that: "Git along, little Doges", I found myself muttering as the sextet closed in for the kill, conceivably intoning the Japanese for "that'll learn ya."

Suzuki's view of the legend, as expounded in his program notes, is that "Dionysus never existed as such; rather (there was) a group with a communal need for unity and the will to spiritually influence the masses." Instead of the Euripidean conflict between a god and a man who between them speak for all of us (and who forge an eerily sympathetic bond), we get a struggle between two axiomatically corrupt political establishments. Suzuki says he has used Euripides "to stage my world view." That is his right but his world-view seems, especially when compared with the original, drearily predictable.

He has added another layer: a second chorus of moaning men in wheelchairs, like emissaries from the world of Samuel Beckett. Their leader is attended by a shambling figure with lank flaxen hair, an undead ringer for Lucky from Waiting for Godot. They are identified in the program as "believers of the Farewell Cult" though what that cult might be, we are not told. But their chief does speak (in English) of the desirability and the impossibilty of escaping history. They are not part of the story; they are an awful comment on its awfulness.

The execution is better than the conception. On a stage furnished only with seven uncomfortable-looking yellow chairs the actors move and speak with iron control. A trio of Maenads appear in red-and-white robes that trail enticingly along the floor. It's what a Westerner expects experimental Japanese theatre to look and sound like: savage, disciplined and sardonic. (By the same token I expect a well-drilled kick-line in a Broadway musical.) But only one scene stirred me, and it may be a comment on me that it was the only scene in the main action to be done in English. Agave, Pentheus's mother, discovers what she has done to her son, and banishes herself from the city. It is the most extreme of situations, appallingly difficult to act. The actress, Ellen Lauren, is Australian but she sounds like a Japanese speaking impeccable English rhetoric. Her words burn with implacable fire, as if sculpted out of sulphur. The chorus reply to her in their own language but at the same temperature. They meet the scene at its own daunting level. They made me wish Suzuki had staged The Bacchae. Dionysus is being presented as part of Harbourfront Centre's Today's Japan, an exposition of contemporary culture.
