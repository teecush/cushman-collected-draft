Where The Producers is concerned, I may be part of the backlash against the backlash. I got caught up, just from reading about it, in all the excitement surrounding the show's smash opening in New York in 2001, and yearned to see it there immediately. When it became clear this wasn't going to happen, I began to develop a skepticism bordering on hostility, a feeling much enhanced by the production's clean sweep of the Tony Awards. The number that was performed at the Tonys didn't look too hot on the small screen, and anyway there had to be something wrong with a show that won all those prizes.

Besides, Mel Brooks wasn't a real composer and this had to be an ersatz score. I lost interest in catching up with the show once its original cast had departed. I knew it would be done in Toronto sooner or later, and if I was going to see replacements, I decided I would wait for them to come to me. (I think, as things have turned out, I was right about this.) I also virtuously refrained from listening to the CD, in the belief that when you do hear a score in the theatre for the first time, you ought to hear it fresh. (I was right about that, too.) Anyway, it took the better part of three years for The Producers to get here, and that made it seem like the day before yesterday's news. I didn't expect it to be bad, exactly, but I didn't expect it to be wonderful either.

In the theatre as elsewhere, low expectations frequently turn out to be justified, but there are times. ...

I was delighted, by the sheer fun and confidence of the thing, from the moment the curtain went up; actually, from before the curtain went up, since this show has an energizing overture. When did you last hear an overture at a musical? When, come to that, did you last see a curtain? By mid-point of the second act, I had surrendered. (Just as well, since it's at this point that the show, its plot line diverging from that of the original movie, takes something of a dive.)

Brooks' music may not be on the highest level, even as pastiche, but it has sparkle and versatility. It sounds right in every style it attempts, and at each point of the story that it serves. Maybe at this point it takes an inspired amateur to write a traditional showbiz score and do it with love; the professionals, hacks and geniuses alike, have moved on to other things. The score and the show have got under my skin. The thought of them, the sound of them (I have now played the CD) make me smile. I went back last week for a second look and, away from first-night pressures, enjoyed it even more. In the leading role of Max Bialystock, the understudy was on. I can't claim it was a star-is-born occasion: Steve Ross, who normally -- and excellently -- plays half-a-dozen minor roles, sings better than Sean Cullen, for whom he stood in, but is no great comedian. Actually, there are running gags in the script that neither he nor Cullen has brought off. But this is obviously one of those shows that work whoever's in them.

Not that I would want to be deprived of Michael Therriault -- his frame, as in a Hirschfeld drawing, seemingly reduced to arms, legs and top hat -- going through his I Wanna Be a Producer number, accompanied by a bevy of chorus girls, every one of whom, including the Bette Midler look-alike on the end, is taller than he is. The actor's joy in executing Susan Stroman's choreography fuses with the character's at bursting out of his shell. So contagious is the expression of this Leo Bloom's desire to be a producer "with a hit show on Broadway" that one loses sight momentarily of the fact that what he and his partner Max are actually shooting for is the mother of all Broadway flops.

Which brings me to a flaw in The Producers -- a tiny flaw, perhaps, and insignificant in the great scheme of things, but someone has to point it out. The story, in its stage version, doesn't make sense.

It did in the film, sort of. It all boils down to what they do to Springtime for Hitler, the title song of the certified worst play in the world. Now it's unclear, in both versions, whether Franz Liebkind, the play's pigeon-raising Nazi author, actually planned his masterpiece as a musical or whether that's just something that is done to it by the terminally camp director Roger de Bris. We never learn either what was in the piece apart from its famous title number, or just where in the evening that number was supposed to occur. In the movie it seems to be the first-half closer; in the show it's apparently both the finale and the first appearance of the leading character, which suggests Liebkind was indeed quite the master of dramatic construction.

Anyway: In the movie, the theatre audience sits through the number in exactly the kind of frozen horror on which Max and Leo are banking. Then on comes LSD, the actor cast as Adolf, who, stoned, departs from the text and calls everyone "baby." This incenses Franz ("the fuhrer never said baby!"), and disarms both the audience and the critics, who decide the whole thing is a delightful spoof. You may think them rather easily amused, but at least the film is trying for some kind of cause and effect.

There is no LSD in the show. Franz himself is cast as Hitler, having won the role with his knockout rendition of what remains my favourite number, Haben Sie Gehort Das Deutsche Band. Franz apparently is Herr Broadway; he must have spent his refugee time in New York sneaking into the balcony of every musical in town. "That's our Hitler," shouts Max, understandably on one level, perplexingly on another; didn't he want someone bad? We'll never know, since Fritz has a mishap on the first night, and is replaced by Roger the director, who stops the show. This time, a star is born. (Boy, in Juan Chioran's performance, is he ever.) But Roger's performance is absolutely of a piece with Roger's production, and we can only assume the audience loves both. So Max and Leo are defeated, not by fate, but by their own incompetence at assembling even the elements of a flop. Which, after all the buildup, is less interesting.

Maybe the show is making a savage comment on the tastes of Broadway audiences; though in fact circa 1959, which is when it's set, hit musicals tended to be good. (Some of them were even funny, in a way that The Producers itself is the first show in years to revive.) More likely, Brooks and his colleagues are trying to have their kuchen and eat it. The rest of the show bears this out. Max, Leo and Franz go to jail, and put on a show there, Prisoners of Love. In the movie this was just another scam. On stage, it's the prelude to another Broadway hit, starring, and presumably directed by, the same Roger de Bris. Logically it would be just as bad as Springtime for Hitler (which presumably is still running, though with God-knows-who in the lead) and it would bomb; that kind of freak lightning doesn't strike twice. But at this point all the show cares about is sending us away happy.

And it does. There are a few gestures toward post-Chicago cynicism ("It ain't no myst'ry,/If it's politics or hist'ry./The thing you gotta know is,/Ev'rything is show biz.") but the stage Producers, unlike its cinematic forebear, is a feel-good entertainment. Whatever shock value there was in the idea of singing and dancing Nazis has long since departed. And maybe there's a kind of justice in that. There's a famous line attributed over the years to a variety of Broadway savants who were going through tryout hell: "If Hitler's alive, I hope he's out of town with a musical." Now here he is, in town in a musical -- a figure of fun in an especially Jewish example of a form that was virtually invented by Jews. It can hardly be a complete revenge, but it's a sweet one.
