THEATRE REVIEW PINOCCHIO

Thursday, March 14, 1991

ROBERT CUSHMAN

BY JEROME COOPERSMITH, MARY RODGERS AND SHELDON HARNICK Directed by Carl Harms Starring Bil Baird's Marionettes At the Bayview Playhouse, Toronto His nose still grows when he lies and contracts when he tells the truth, but in this marionette version at Toronto's Bayview Playhouse, Pinocchio breaks convention by having no aspirations toward humanity Updated classic loses relevance BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail AFTER years of Muppet-gazing, it is rather a shock to be confronted by dolls which are visibly wired. But Bil Bairds Marionettes are pedigree puppets. Baird himself died in 1987 after more than 50 years of string-pulling; his son Peter S. Baird has been minding the store since then. The troupes version of Pinocchio has been in the repertoire since 1973.

I don't recall whether self-referential is a term we were tossing about much 18 years ago; if we weren't, this show was ahead of its time. For Pinocchio himself, of course, is a puppet, and even though he started in a book and hit the big time in a Disney cartoon, it was left to the Bairds to present him in his existential state. We actually see him transformed, at his own clamorous insistence, from a block of wood that might be raw material for a rolling pin into a toy boy, the immediate apple of his creators eye. That creator, the carver Gepetto, is played logically by a full-sized actor, decked out in a commedia mask in presumed homage to the storys Italian origins.

Other grown-up characters are played by other grown-up actors. I assumed that it was always the same grown-up actor, until two of them ganged up on my theory by appearing simultaneously. Gepetto is reduced to puppethood after being swallowed by a dolphin, and Pinocchio, on joining him in the interior, takes a corresponding reduction in size. This is sporting of Pinocchio and ingenious of the Bairds, but I still felt cheated. A basic convention had been broken: all the more basic in that, in this version, Pinocchio has no aspirations toward humanity. Each species is allowed its own pride. Block is beautiful.

Unfairly perhaps, one takes the best things for granted. Ten unseen hands bring the accoutrements of the carpenter's shop to quivering life. Pinocchio takes gradual shape before our eyes. There are comic interludes involving collapsing puppets felled by their master's sneeze, and a school of conversational fish. Pinocchios nose lengthens when he lies, and contracts when he tells the truth, and we dont give the mechanics a thought.

But we do notice - at least I did - that this crucial element is introduced rather late, and with no prior warning. The storytelling, in fact, is scrappy. Here television probably has done us in. We expect fluidity, and get small pauses between each tableau, pauses which the narrative is not strong enough to override. It is, after all, a reworking of the Everyman story, just one temptation after another, and hence very prone to monotony. Nor do the Baird bunch seem to have total faith in it. They don't wholeheartedly spoof it, as might be done on Sesame Street. They intermittently update it, like their Greenwich Village neighbour Joe Papp, knocking himself out to make Shakespeare relevant to the inner city.

Pinocchio, strictly for Toronto's benefit, is on his way to school on Eglinton Avenue. Waylaid by a streetwise Fox and Cat, he is busted by the cops and befriended by a social worker, standing in for the originals Blue Fairy. All these inventions have their funny moments, but their combined effect is anything but relevant. Its digressive. The least-guyed element - the relationship of father and son - is by far the most effective. Especially marginal is Pinocchio's conscience, a Hispanified cricket, who never stands a chance of influencing anyone.

He's the best singer though. The mini-score is the product of two hot Broadway talents, hot at least in 1973: Sheldon Harnick (Fiddler on the Roof and Fiorello!) and Mary Rodgers (Once Upon a Mattress, and daughter of Richard). They preserve the disciplines of the old school while flirting daintily with the rhythms of the new. The prerecorded vocals (also 1973?) make a bright but jarring contrast with the acting, which is altogether more homespun. The virtuoso puppet stuff comes in a post-story variety show which includes one of those dazzling abstract montages on a black background in which Central Europe meets Off-Broadway: no words, no strings. My own brood, having loved the Young People's Theatre's live- action Christmas Carol, which made far fewer concessions, were fitfully enchanted.
