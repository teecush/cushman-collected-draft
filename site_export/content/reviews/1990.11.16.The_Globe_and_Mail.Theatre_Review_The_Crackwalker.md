THEATRE REVIEW THE CRACKWALKER

Friday, November 16, 1990

ROBERT CUSHMAN

By Judith Thompson Directed by Urjo Kareda and Andy McKim At the Tarragon Theatre in Toronto The Crackwalker tells the bleak story of four people who dont have much going for them, but manages not to be depressing. A harrowing walk with the urban underclass BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail JUDITH Thompson is being doubly feted - practically festivalized - at Toronto's Tarragon Theatre. Her latest play, Lion in the Streets, is the current main-stage attraction, while the Extra Space has a revival of the piece with which she began her career 10 years ago.

The Crackwalker now seems a good play but a bad argument for progress. Life for the urban underclass, in whose Kingston, Ont., sector the play is set, has hardly improved since 1980; Thompson's four major characters have very little going for them, but none is actually homeless. A fifth figure seems more prophetic: an insensible, bloodsoaked silent messenger of doom, he crouches menacingly at the edge of the stage while the others get on with the action. His lurching and vomiting are unflinchingly realistic but his placement (Is he the Crackwalker? If not, who is?) is disconcertingly Symbolic.

But The Crackwalkers principals - the Kingston trio plus one - are mesmerizingly real. All are young. Theresa, plump and retarded, lives chiefly on doughnuts, and sells and gives sex fairly indiscriminately. (In this production, she sounds Irish.) Sandy, the friend on whose sofa she regularly crashes, is the most together of the bunch, except for her dependence on Joe, who abuses her and may have raped Theresa. Joe feels permanently trapped and is first seen stripping a stolen motorbike. Alan is Joes sidekick, helplessly anxious to please.

Alan takes on Theresa. He marries her and, against all expert advice, they have a baby. Both the marriage and the child are doomed and, on our awareness of this, Thompson builds a fearful arc of tension. I don't know that the gods of the drama require such a propitiatory sacrifice, but Thompson is the second modern playwright - Edward Bond with Saved was the first - to build a major debut play around an infanticide. (And both have gone on to write even starker, more ambitious and less convincing things.) The baby-killing scene in Saved was sickening. The one in The Crackwalker is even more painful, since we care for the parents, and believe that they care for the child and one another.

The Tarragon production, directed by Urjo Kareda and Andy McKim, is unsensational and unfussy. It finds room, like the writing, for a surprising amount of warmth and humor. The humor, actually, is almost of the Ben Jonson variety: set up a collection of monomanias and watch them collide. Sandy, being the most balanced, is the toughest assignment and Cheryl Swarts's performance does exude, in her hands-on-hips declarations of independence, the faint taint of slumming. She is a lot better when pitiable, in her scene of silent erotic surrender to Joe, or pitying, in a toughly compassionate monologue that ends the play. Each character has one of these soliloquies. They could be dangerous authorial intrusions, but they actually seem to come from within their speakers skins.

Joe is played straightforwardly by Ron White as an angry uncomprehending stud. His paranoia infects the admiring Alan who takes it, in Randy Hughsons performance, to awesomely grotesque extremes. Alan is haunted by his father's death from lung cancer and makes it a pretext for a virtuoso tirade against Sandy's taste for cigarettes. The same experience makes him distrustful of doctors and, since a social worker has told him that he is a good father, he takes it on himself to throw out the babys medicine. The childs fate is sealed by a volcanic outburst when Theresa, fearing another pregnancy, denies him sex. Alan grows credibly from a shambling incompetent to a death-dealing obsessive; to conceive this progression, and feed it into a coherent dramatic structure, is quite an achievement.

Alan may be crazy but he is not irresponsible. Theresa, lacking the power to connect, can step through life moment by moment, suffering sorely but never long. She bridles at being called a whore, but she is in fact a mental nymphomaniac: always ready for the next experience, which needn't be sexual (motherhood will do) but generally is. She even fantasizes herself as "the Madonna lady" - a phrase that of course has rather a different ring to it 10 years on. She is multifariously single- minded; you might even call her oxymoronic. These sudden dislocations make her funny, and Debra Kirshenbaum's performance, blowsy and befuddled, is richly comic - but never patronizing.

These people live with you. The play is harrowing without being depressing. And that is high achievement.
