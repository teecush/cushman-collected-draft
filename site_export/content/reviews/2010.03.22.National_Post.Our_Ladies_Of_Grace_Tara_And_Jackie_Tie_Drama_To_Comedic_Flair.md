The two best comedic series of recent years are back: back, to back, in fact. Actually I hate that word "comedic" -- I can't see what was wrong with good old unpretentious "comic" -- but it's clear that neither Nurse Jackie nor United States of Tara can be called a sitcom in the traditional sense. Tara is the closer of the two to the old model; it has the traditional family setting, even if the protagonist's multiple-personality disorder means that the family itself is far from traditional, but both shows break the mould in being about development rather than stasis. No episode of I Love Lucy or Seinfeld ever had to start with a recap of the story so far. Perhaps the decisive difference is that both Jackie and Tara have children; and the ineluctable fact of their growing up immediately imposes a new and urgent dimension.

There is, of course, repetition in these shows. Each set up its framework of character and situation at the outset, and has adhered to it. "Previously on ..." at the start of both new seasons means, in large part, going back to the very first episodes. Nurse Jackie, which gets off to the more bracing new beginning, finds its title character as capable as ever and even more strung out. "Why do all the things that are good for you make you feel so bad?" asks her gay orderly friend Thor during one of their recurrent meetings in the hospital chapel, to which Jackie replies "I hear you." The amount of pain, regret and acceptance mined by Edie Falco from those three little words is extraordinary.

What's haunting her most at the moment is the situation of Eddie, her ex-pharmacist/ ex-lover, dumped by both Jackie and the hospital but still very much around. He's taken to hanging out at the bar where Jackie's husband Kevin works, trying to experience her vicariously.

Kevin is, for me, one of the less convincing characters. I am, conversely, one of the few who finds the friendship between Jackie and the English Dr. O'Hara thoroughly believable, if only because Falco and Eve Best play so irresistibly together. Young nurse Zoey is coming along well too; next week's instalment finds her coping admirably with a local schizophrenic who calls himself God, because that's who he thinks he is, and who has to be coaxed through a crisis of self-disbelief.

Jackie's own religious beliefs are an impotant part of her personal mix; when the favourite

painting of the Virgin to which she gravitates in her off-moments is bruised by gunfire (this is an inner-city hospital) she calls out, with indignation but no particular surprise, "she's a mother." Her pill-popping is as compulsive as ever, mined for humour of various degrees of darkness.

Falco excels at presenting a woman who's personally vulnerable and professionally invincible. Tonight's episode shows her expertly manipulating an insurance company that's trying to default on covering a desperate patient. It gets you thinking that the best thing for American healthcare would be to put Jackie in charge of it. But she isn't infallible, and next week shows her, for once, coming off worst in one of her conflicts with the infuriating Dr. Cooper. His habit of hitting on her despite all discouragements is one tired device the show might be better without, but his larger combination of extreme arrogance with extreme insecurity can produce comic gold.

More deeply haunting is the sight of Grace, Jackie's anxiety-stricken little girl, offering to put her allowance towards the latest model smoke detector, because she's afraid of the house burning down. Speaking of houses: As a hospital series, Nurse Jackie has House beat right down the line, beginning with their protagonists' respective bedside manners. One is rude, the other just practical. Apart from being funny, Nurse Jackie may be the only believable medical series in the history of television.

Comparisons for Tara are harder to come by. At the moment, it seems in greater danger of becoming formulaic. Tara and Max's two children are each striking out on their own paths: daughter Kate gets a job with a collection agency, which seems to suit her acquisitive nature, while school-age son Marshall is struggling with his sexuality. That is, he's been adopted by the cafeteria gayble (gay table) whose presiding figure is an aggressive proselytiser, but is getting suspiciously comfortable with a girl who sits there too because she is, in her own description, gay-friendly. Go Marshall, whichever way you decide to go.

Tara's sister Charmaine remains more interesting for the actress who plays her (Rosemarie DeWitt) than for anything she actually does. The central situation has been put, to a certain extent, on hold, with Tara on her meds and, hence, off her alters. However, a suicide in the house next door gets to her, and her biker-dude persona takes possession of her body and also, in another sense, of the body of a neighbouring barmaid. There's a lot of sex on this show, one way or another. It's everyone's fantasy suburbia.

Well, maybe not everyone's. Alice, Tara's prim housweife identity, would have something to say about it and, I hope, soon will. The dialogue still zings, the style remains attractively cool, and Toni Collette is still irresistible.

These two sagas of conscientious, drug-dependent wives and mothers must rank as twin fables for our times. And cable is finally doing for comedy what it has previously done for drama. It's brought the two genres closer together, while still allowing each a separate identity.

- Season 2 of Nurse Jackie premieres tonight at 10 p.m.; Season 2 of United States of Tara premieres tonight at 10:30, both on The Movie Network.
