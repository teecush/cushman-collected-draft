FULLY COMMITTED

Winter Garden Theatre

I recently declared a bias against solo shows that are not funny. Fully Committed helps stoke my prejudice in the nicest way. It is a one-man play, it is very funny, and -- after a few doubtful moments - - it is totally beguiling.

The doubts come while the premise is being set up. Mark McKinney, ex-Kids in the Hall, comes on stage and the first thing he does is answer the phone. Having done so in his own voice, he shifts accent and position and starts playing the part of the caller, too. It seems impossibly arch, and you just wait for it to be over. Then there is another call, and he puts on another voice, and you realize that the whole play is going to be like that, and it becomes a convention rather than a gag, and not arch at all. You still wonder whether the author and the script can keep it up, but it soon becomes obvious that they can, and you go with it. In fact, you don't want them to stop.

McKinney plays Sam, a restaurant switchboard operator; he never says a word throughout the play that is not spoken on some phone line or other, external or internal. The text is very pure that way. It was written by Becky Mode, a New Yorker who has apparently logged time in the cafe trade, and it rings true. (That seems the appropriate phrase.)

Sam, to nobody's surprise, is an actor by vocation, and at least he isn't surviving between engagements by working among the waiters; though whether he ranks above or below them in the pecking order is an open question. He sits in a basement office, takes or refuses reservations and generally fends off the outside world.

He has a co-worker who today has failed to show up, so Sam has to take every call himself. Since this is a very fashionable, popular and expensive restaurant, he is generally juggling three or four calls at once; some callers are philosophical about being put on hold and others are not. Interestingly, the patient ones, as rendered here, are every bit as entertaining as the impatient.

Besides these, Sam has to deal with his employer, a sadistic prima donna of a chef who, also interestingly, seems to spend more time in his office or jetting off to celebrity junkets than he does in the kitchen; and with a temperamental Gallic maitre d'; and with various other more or less unhelpful colleagues.

In the few intervals he is allowed, Sam talks to his agent, or at least to his agent's secretary, about a part he may be up for; and to his widowed father in Indiana about the slim chances of the restaurant letting him come home for Christmas; and to a supercilious actor friend about the friend's success, which Sam clearly finds disgusting. Up above it is lunchtime; Sam, a virtual prisoner, gets very hungry -- a starving man amidst plenty.

The play has a classical sort of structure. Everything goes wrong for Sam initially; halfway through he descends into the mire (literally); and after that ritual cleansing, things start to look up. There is a hint that Sam has learned to play the world's game, but it looks more as if the world, through no intention of its own, has begun to play his.

I suppose there is some cheating here. We learn very little about Sam's personal qualities but we are on his side because he is the only person we see. The rest of the world consists of hostile voices trying to make his life miserable, which is how most of us see the rest of the world. And on this occasion we not only see it through his eyes, we hear it through his lips. No wonder we identify and take his small triumphs for our own. He even gets to tell the chef what he really thinks of his orange roughy. Stuff your Greek tragedies -- this is catharsis.

McKinney can make Sam little more than nice and, when occasion finally offers, quick-witted. That doesn't matter. We like him most because we admire his technical skill: the multiplicity of tones and shadings he comes up with and the precision with which he juggles them. They arrive, after all, in haphazard order, with no logical connections to smooth the transitions. (The first question actors always get asked is how they remember their lines. This time, even a spectator as jaded as I would like to know.)

McKinney is less adept at distinguishing the boys from the girls than Harold Pinter was last week when he read us his restaurant piece, Celebration. But maybe that's intentional; maybe that's New York. I'm still uncertain of the gender of Bryce, Naomi Campbell's assistant, who calls periodically to bark instructions about a table for 15 with a vegan menu and halogen lamps, while a gently complaining female senior citizen sounds like Alan Bennett. Never mind -- I enjoyed them both. Stephanie, the establishment's frazzled receptionist, does sound a very convincing transplanted English rose, with that London weariness that has taken over the whole culture, and I loved Mrs. Sebag, who is driven to hysteria by the news that her reservation has gone missing and has to stop yelling at the maid in order to scream upstairs to her husband for succour.

"Fully committed" is apparently upscale bistrospeak for "all booked up" -- which is what happened to the play when it was first done off-Broadway. After a long diet of worthy plays in small rooms and soulless shows in big ones, it is a treat to encounter a commercial success that actually deserves to be one.
