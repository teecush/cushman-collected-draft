NAC's artistic director returns to Stratford with contrasting pieces

Peter Hinton used to think that the essence of theatre lay in acting, so he became an actor. Later he thought that it might reside in directing, so he started to direct. Later still he decided that the most important thing was writing, so he began to write plays. He has kept the latter two talents running in tandem, and they've established this soft-spoken man as one of the most provocative talents in the Canadian theatre, an Anglophone in an artist-showman mould we more usually associate with Quebec.

His playwrighting debut was maybe the most elaborate ever seen on a major Canadian stage: The Swanne, a fantastical-historical- allegorical trilogy about events leading up to the accession to the British throne of the young Queen Victoria, told largely from Hinton's highly individual version of Victoria's own point of view. It took him a decade to write. Quite apart from the research involved, it took him, he told me with a laugh, "10 years to learn how to write." It was staged over three years -- 2001 to 2004 -- at the Stratford Festival, and he directed it himself. "Writing," he says, "is about creating problems. Directing is about solving them." He had to solve mountainous problems of his own creation, and he did it spectacularly well. He returned to Stratford last year with a darkly imaginative production of the musical Into the Woods, and he's back again this season in both his capacities. He directs John Webster's The Duchess of Malfi (opening today), a Jacobean tragedy whose luxuriant excesses should suit him perfectly, and he also stages a contrastingly modest piece of his own, Fanny Kemble, a solo piece, starring Domini Blythe, about a pioneering 19th-century actress.

And if that's not enough, he's also the newly appointed artistic director of the English theatre company at the National Arts Centre in Ottawa, with a self-imposed mission to take the "national" part of that title seriously -- not in the sense of doing every kind of play, or being all things to all people, but of, in his words, "encouraging artists across the country, from British Columbia to Halifax." He wants to attach Canadian writers, including First- Nations writers, to his companies; make co-productions with other companies "an absolute boon;" and give a big central space to "Canadian theatre groups that are lauded around the world but are relegated here to 200- or 300-seat houses." His first season, starting this fall, will consist, almost unprecedentedly for a major residential theatre, entirely of new Canadian work. In his second he's focusing on the classics. And working towards that, he has a workshop program planned for the end of this year exploring the neglected plays of Shakespeare's predecessors, contemporaries and successors. Which brings us back to The Duchess of Malfi.

A baroque play, I suggested, using the term rather loosely to suggest something unruly and extravagant, but Hinton went along with it. "I love the Jacobean theatre. It's high theatre -- high horror - - that still demands great emotional truth from the actors. We've talked a lot in rehearsal about not going either over the top or under the top: about how to keep that razor-balance." As Jacobean plays go, The Duchess ("an incredible play," Hinton says, as directors do) isn't all that neglected. It's been done at Stratford once before, in 1971, in a legendary production by the late Jean Gascon. It was, Hinton notes, "the first play performed by Shakespeare's own company after his death," and they did it not in the open-air Globe but at the roofed-in Blackfriars -- a theatre "with 400 or 500 seats, roughly the same as the Tom Patterson," which is where the new Stratford production is happening. And Webster took full advantage of his setting and of its technology; some of the greatest and most terrible scenes happen in the dark as, in a metaphysical sense, does the whole play. Hinton has a cast of old Stratford hands, headed by Lucy Peacock (Richard Monette, Stratford's artistic director, has long thought of the play as a possible vehicle for her) as the widowed Italian duchess who incurs the wrath of her brothers, a duke and a cardinal, by marrying beneath her, and is tortured -- more mentally than physically -- and eventually murdered for it. "It's wonderful," Hinton says, "to look at the play with actors who know their Shakespeare. They keep saying, 'This is just like Shakespeare,' then they run up against something that's quite unlike him.

"In Shakespeare, when you get a couplet at the end of a scene, it sums up and reinforces what has gone before. In Webster, the couplet actually increases the complexity of the scene." The style is unpredictable: "Characters jump from verse to prose, with no reference to their social status. The play is full of ambiguities." We sympathize with the Duchess, obviously, but Hinton also links her to the Jacobean genre of the "lusty widow," women sexually experienced and financially independent, regarded by authors and audiences with as much nervousness as admiration. They're rebels. ("The Duchess," Hinton notes, "has huge issues with God.") The character we most identify with is the nominal villain, ex-galley- slave Bosola, the Cardinal's hit man and fall guy who kills the Duchess and spends the rest of the play trying to avenge her. "Webster has the ability to make us like people who are so evil." Even Ferdinand, the Duchess's ducal brother. whose sadism can only be motivated by incestuous passion, "is in such pain, you feel for him." The mystery is in the characterization rather than the narrative. "So often in a new play a playwright will want to withhold a great secret. Webster lays it all out immediately." Hence the galloping excitement with which these plays begin, and which the best of them maintain.

In that sense they connect with Hinton's own teeming achievement in The Swanne, though he says -- suprisingly, but persuasively -- that his own play has "a more gentle heart; it's more Dickensian." Webster presents us with a cruel world that's morally in free fall. It feels -- as people have been saying for 400 years -- very modern.
