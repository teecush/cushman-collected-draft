robertcushman@lycos.com

Matt Dusk and David Mahogany cast themselves in the moulds of legends

Bono and The Edge, of U2, wrote a song called Two Shots of Happy, One Shot of Sad that they intended for Frank Sinatra. He died without recording it, which, from the point of view of his legacy may be just as well. Not that he didn't, from time to time, record far worse things, but this one is so obsequiously tailored to his perceived image that any performance of it he might have given would have sounded like self-parody. And though that is not a trap he always avoided, he usually did.

The song has now fallen into the hands of the 25-year-old Canadian singer Matt Dusk, the latest performer to cast himself in the Sinatra mould and one whose tone and angle-of-attack do, at moments, sound surprisingly like the original.

It's the virtual title track of his first CD Two Shots (Decca, a sign of the copycat times that a newbie would-be crooner should get a major-label contract first time out) and the singer does indeed get two shots at it, one at each end of the record; though, as usual in these circumstances, the vocal is identical on both mixes. The singer's backing groups include the Toronto Symphony and the Royal Philharmonic; and he claims, at a frighteningly young age, to have found his spiritual home in Las Vegas.

The title song's title idea isn't bad, but it isn't sustained. The lyric, grabbing at one pre-packaged image after another, comes to sound like a Frank Sinatra free-association test: "I'm just a singer, some say a sinner/ Rolling the dice, not always a winner"; "We were walking together down a dead-end street/Mixing the bitter with the sweet." The worst of it is that the authors obviously thought they were being sophisticated. Most of the new songs on the disc (from a number of different teams, though the name Robin Smith crops up on many of them) are the same, only worse. None of them have any inner logic. Something called Cold as Ice, aimed at some presumably faithless lady, is a catalogue of random reproaches. Lonely Road, whose title alone sounds warning-bells, is foolhardy enough to end with a moaning coda about "the long, long road." Other songs tell us that "when love is young, the air is sweet/forbidden fruit is all we eat" and that "life goes on like a carousel."

Some of Dusk's phrasing on Miracle, one of the more rhythmic items, would certainly have raised his idol's eyebrows: "Remember the bolt that came out of -- breath -- the blue." Nobody is going to die of suspense waiting for the end of that thought. He sings it twice, with no variation. For somebody who is being touted as a jazz singer -- or indeed any kind of singer -- he doesn't seem very inventive.

He does have a voice, and some technique; and if you want imitation-Sinatra, you do get someone who seems to have gone to the source, rather than to Jack Jones or Bobby Darin. It seems, understandably, to be the insouciant Sinatra of the '50s and early '60s who has captured his imagination, but the material on which he tries to recapture this approach is more like the turgid stuff to which the master surrendered in the '70s and '80s. The great lyricists took their inspiration from their own sensibilities and the common speech around them; these take it from other songs.

The sad fact is that love songs in the classic American tradition can no longer be written. The older writers still trying it end up sounding like hacks and the younger ones like hicks. This doesn't mean that the existing repertoire has to be retired, though of course anyone singing it faces daunting comparisons. Dusk does two songs that might be called standards: Fly Me to the Moon, hardly the cream of the Sinatra crop and sounding here like the Basie version with its teeth drawn, still appears a masterpiece of coherence in this company; the Beatles' Please Please Me (with "girl" changed to "love," I assume for political correctness) is sung and arranged with ludicrous solemnity, and slowed down to what must be meant as a sensual pace, though in fact it sounds nothing like as sexy as the cheerfully lustful original.

Dusk himself is part-author of two of the songs. One, The Theme from Loaded Gun, is the worst thing on the disc, a James Bond knock- off ("he plays the game, like a moth that finds love in a flame") that hasn't the wit to be a spoof. The other, a song called Five, is the best, an original idea that is followed through and isn't chained to an antiquated structure.

If Dusk sounds at times like a boy sent to do a man's job (though less so than his peers, Peter Cincotti and Michael Buble) the American Kevin Mahogany sounds man enough to overpower any song or setting you could throw at him. A string of excellent albums on big labels had led me to believe he was a singer who could sing anything. So indeed he is; but there are some things that are more suited to him than others. When he appeared last month at Top of the Senator he offered a tribute to the late Johnny Hartman, the gentlest of all accredited jazz singers -- almost a crooner -- but one authoritative enough to be John Coltrane's performer of choice on one of the best vocal albums ever made. Clint Eastwood used his recordings on the soundtrack of The Bridges of Madison County, which brought him a posthumous fame greater than any he had known in his life, and made rather odd Mahogany's introductory claim that he was now "in some ways ignored."

Stranger still, certainly in live performance, was the sight of him singing Hartman's songs -- nearly all ballads -- in, for the most part, Hartman's manner. He is a mountain of a man, with a barrel chest from which issues a magnificently rich, resonant and flexible voice. He is in perfect control of it and had no trouble scaling it down. But he looked like a man who should be singing the blues, urban style (well, he is from Kansas City) and it was both a thrill and a relief when he actually did.

The Hartman program is preserved, recorded live, on To: Johnny Hartman, the first release on his own label, Mahogany Music. He does Fools Rush In, Body and Soul, In the Wee Small Hours of the Morning, and does them well, reproducing Hartman's delicate, purposeful placement of words and his way of electrifying an unexpected word; his air of being polite but no sucker. But while Hartman made you feel that this was the way to sing these songs, with Mahogany it only seems like a way. Cutting back on the dynamics robs his voice of its identity. You are spared the visual incongruity of the live experience, but the disc becomes monotonous. The faster numbers are all bunched at the end: a Nature Boy in which subtle rhythmic phrasing helps, blessedly, to take your mind off the words; a fun Kiss and Run; and two Ellington-Strayhorn songs that suit Mahogany far better than they did his model, Satin Doll and an exciting scat- filled Take the A Train.

A companion disc, Big Band, finds the singer in far more congenial surroundings even though the Frank Mantooth Jazz Orchestra -- the big band principally in evidence -- sounds, like so many now, more like a sleek screaming machine than a living organism. One song, Moonlight in Vermont, turns up on both albums; though it is, on the face of it, better suited to the quiet ruminative approach, the extrovert version, though it robs us of Mahogany's evocative emphasis on "Icy finger-waves," is far more invigorating.

Other songs Mahogany refurbishes are One for My Baby, the one with which Matt Dusk's Lonely Road rashly courts comparison, and that he makes not just haunting but ghostly, and the melodramatic I'll Never Stop Loving You, on which he's disarmingly smoky. Two roller-coasting blues, Centrepiece and In the Evening, confirm him as Joe Williams' natural heir. He doesn't have Joe's majestic smoothness, but maybe that will only come with Joe's silver hairs. Both those tunes, incidentally, were even more exciting when he did them at the Senator with just a trio; a big band helps set a context, but he doesn't need it to swing.

As if to prove it, the last and best cut on the Big Band disc, a light-footed Don't Get Around Much Any More, is done with just piano. Compared with his previous discs, these two are curtly- produced reach-me-down affairs, but I know of no better male singer working this territory.
