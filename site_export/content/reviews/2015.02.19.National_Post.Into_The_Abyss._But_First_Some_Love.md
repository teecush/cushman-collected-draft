Well into the 20th century, the Victorians Robert Browning and Elizabeth Barrett Browning were cited in the textbooks as the only major English poets to have been married to one another. Their ownership of the marital Parnassus wasn't to be challenged until the union of Ted Hughes and Sylvia Plath.

But while the Hughes-Plath marriage was notoriously stormy, and eventually tragic, the Brownings relationship was popularly regarded as an idyllic meeting of minds and talents: celebrated in such things as the play and movie The Barretts of Wimpole Street and the musical Robert and Elizabeth. Now here, to shatter the idyll, comes Florence Gibson Mac-Donald with her play How Do I Love Thee? Her Robert and Elizabeth could give Ted and Sylvia a good, or bad, run for their money.

The title comes from Mrs. Brownings most famous poem; Elizabeth gets to count the ways quite late in the play, and the recitation, passionately done, is all the more moving for coming at a point where what might seem to be uppermost in both their minds is How Do I Hate Thee? Their early encounters have indeed been idyllic, conducted initially by letter. Robert's attention is drawn to something nice that Elizabeth has written about his work, and soon he's at her door and all over her sofa.

Although shes self-cast as a shut-in and invalid, her dashing young idol has her out and about in London, and even persuades her to elope with him to his beloved Italy. MacDonalds poets speak, for the most part, in her own rich prose, which is both worthy of them and gratifying for us.

Once they've set up house in Florence, it all falls apart: the relationship and, I'm afraid, the play. There are two subsidiary characters. One is John Kenyon, the couples mutual friend who brings them together; left behind in London, he necessarily fades out. Whereas he seems to love the pair mainly for their poems, Wilson, Elizabeths maid, loves the poems - Elizabeth's anyway - because it's her mistress, whom she calls "mum," whos written them.

The first, very telling, moments of the play find Elizabeth rhapsodizing on a swing; then Wilson creeps up behind her and administers what we subsequently discover to be a cocktail of laudanum, morphine and opium.

Wilson believes the drugs necessary to Elizabeths writing; so, even more damagingly, does Elizabeth herself; and, most distressingly, so apparently does the play. Its second act becomes the chronicle of Elizabeth's addiction, and Robert's increasingly angry and desperate attempts to wean of her off it (and to stop the apothecarys bills from bankrupting them). Cold turkey proves disastrous, and the lady wont keep to a regimen of gradual withdrawal. Two of the most monotonous things in the world are drug addiction and marital quarrels; both just keep repeating themselves, and so does the play.

One taunt that Elizabeth keeps throwing at Robert is that she's the one who's writing all the successful verse. This is outrageously onesided, but the play seems to agree with her; its notable that two of her major works, Sonnets from the Portuguese and Aurora Leigh, get mentioned by name, but none of his. The idea seems to be to make her a feminist heroine at his expense. (But dont trust me; I'd back Ted Hughes against Sylvia Plath.) Lurking somewhere in the background is Elizabeth's controlling monster of a father; and its frustrating that, though we often hear about him, we never meet him.

Ken Gasss production, for his Canadian Rep Theatre, is well manoeuvred and well acted. Irene Poole, whose intensity in other roles has sometimes seemed a bit much, gives a wonderfully uninhibited but still splendidly controlled performance as Elizabeth, as unbridled in joyous awakening as in rage and pain; her evocation of orgasm-through-verse is delicious.

Matthew Edison's Robert grows from fuzzy romantic symbol to passionate maturity. David Schurmann is an agreeable John, while Nora McLellan's Wilson gives a triumphant demonstration of the many possibilities of keeping mum.

Abyss is a translation by Maria Milisavljevic, of her own award-winning 2013 play. Its a three-hander, set in the authors native Germany, whose unnamed narrator has a former lover and current roommate called Vlado, a traumatized exile from the former Yugoslavia, and a sister called Sophia.

Vlado's present girlfriend disappears, and the play tells of the trio's increasingly desperate attempts to find her, despite getting minimal help from the police (Sophia) or disdaining it altogether (Vlado).

As an atmospheric thriller, it pretty much succeeds, despite getting murky towards the end. But it also seems meant as a metaphor for the traumatic aftermath of the Balkans war, and here it takes far too much for granted, not helped by verbal mannerism (Vlado keeps being referred to as "the one who finds") and gratuitous sub-plots (Vlado is jealous of the narrator's new boyfriend, played by the same actor).

It's given a very taut production by Richard Rose, the three actors whirling and writhing while holding and relinquishing hands. Cara Pifko is a most lucid and sympathetic storyteller; Gord Rand is magnetically hollow-eyed and hollow-souled; and Sarah Sherman copes in a businesslike fashion with a role that requires her to interrupt the action periodically with instructions on how to kill and skin a rabbit or, by sinister extension, a person.

How Do I Love Thee? runs through Feb. 22 at the Berkeley Street Theatre Upstairs in Toronto; Abyss runs through March 15 at the Tarragon Extraspace in Toronto.
