LONDON ASSURANCE

Avon Theatre, Stratford

- - -

Designers dont usually take curtain-calls, but the Stratford first night of London Assurance presented a special case. Desmond Heeley, one of Stratford's (and London's and New Yorks) finest, was celebrating his 75th birthday. He had also done a bang-up job on this particular show, an early Victorian piece in the grand English tradition of comedy of manners. It moves from the house of a metropolitan swell to a rambling country estate, glimpsed both inside and out. Heeley evokes both locations with his characteristic blend of the sumptuous and the airy. His costumes, similarly, range from the comfortably homely to the eye-poppingly fantastic. It was good to have a chance to applaud him.

Unfortunately, there really isnt too much else to applaud. But - - keeping for a moment to the good stuff, and still on the subject of curtain-calls -- Brian Bedford, who both directs and plays the leading role, seems already to be taking one at his very first appearance. That is not a criticism. The character he plays, Sir Harcourt Courtly -- an ageing buck left over from the age of Beau Brummell -- has lived his life on the assumption that his mere physical being is God's gift to men, and especially to women. To preen and to pose are as natural to him as breathing. Probably more natural, given the tightness of the stays into which he must lace himself to preserve his figure as he advances in age. That's not even to mention the weight of the rouge, the powder and the monstrous black wig he has to carry, or the waistcoats that include a plum-coloured creation whose fruitiness you can practically taste.

Dion Boucicault, the young playwright who had a first-time hit with this play in 1841 and went on to many others in a variety of veins, puts Sir Harcourt through some time-honoured hoops. Basically, he takes the mans urban self-satisfaction (or London assurance) and exposes it to shocks. Its bad enough that, wooing an 18-year old for her money, he has to journey into the wilds; once there, he is confronted by his own son, who insists so emphatically that he is somebody else that his father is compelled to believe him. Bedford plays gorgeous variations on a single theme of nose- wrinkling incredulity. His technical virtuosity is, as usual, kept strictly within the parameters set by the play. He also plays the man's decency; unlike some of his Restoration ancestors, Sir Harcourt is vain but not vicious. When made a fool of, by himself or others, he knows how to climb down. He ends the play with a heartfelt assertion of the true nature of a gentleman and does it, for all his previous absurdity, beautifully. Has anybody ever thought of casting Bedford as Falstaff?

If the character goes, in the end, for less than he should, that's because Bedford the director hasnt provided Bedford the actor with much of a context. Apart from the star, the designs and a handful of sterling supports, this is a sad affair in which the disciplined self-awareness of the central performance has notably failed to trickle down to the majority of the unwashed. A variety of servants, tricksters and pretenders are misplayed by actors who all but wink at the audience while going through some laborious routines. The idea may be to acknowledge that they are actors playing stock roles in a stock play, but conscious artifice needs far more rigour than this and it sells this particular play short. To take the worst -- because most prominent -- example: Sean Arbuckle, the excellent Worcester of Henry IV, gets no handle on Dazzle, a sponger from nowhere, who cascades through the play on sheer self-confidence until someone at the end speaks for us all by asking "who is Mr. Dazzle?" The moment is a send-up in itself, a comment on all the unaccountable dandies who populate period comedies. Boucicault was a shrewd operator, a man who here plundered all his predecessors back to Shakespeare (and was to be quarried in turn by The Importance of Being Earnest) while creating something warm but unsentimental of his own. It seems here like a bad play, a mere collection of familiar intrigues; a shock, too, for anyone old and lucky enough to have seen the crack Royal Shakespeare Company production (it once played Toronto) that rediscovered it and to which Bedford himself plays generous tribute in the program.

The consolation prizes include a pleasant pair of lovers: Sara Topham as one of the plays real inspirations, a bespectacled bookworm who thinks she has no illusions about romance; and Adam O'Byrne as her sheepish suitor, whose multiple identities confuse even him. More emphatically --much more emphatically -- Seana McKenna is all top-hole and tally-ho as Lady Gay Spanker, a fox- hunting beauty who arouses Sir Harcourt's wayward passions even while the sheer outdoorsiness of her personality perplexes him to death. Even better, indeed with a touch of comic poetry as he ties himself up in verbal and emotional knots, is Brian Tree as her husband, Dolly, whom she treats as an adored household pet and who, after a brief fling at self-assertiveness, relapses contentedly into subservience. What do you know? A happy marriage.
