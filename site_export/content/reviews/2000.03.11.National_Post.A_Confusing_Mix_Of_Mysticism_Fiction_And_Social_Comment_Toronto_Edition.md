THE CLAIRVOYANT

Studio Theatre

We are in what is described as a large coastal city in the year 2038, a time and place apparently characterized by broken-down social structures and terrible architecture. The stage is occupied by half a dozen surly obelisks which, over the course of the evening, are shuffled around by sad-looking extras to create the play's various scenes.

The first location is a seaside cafe through which there mysteriously wanders an alluring blond lady. At your first dazzled glance, she appears to be about seven feet tall, is cradling a glass of champagne and is largely naked. When she reappears later she is fully clothed and remains so for the rest of the evening, even -- according to some pattern of behaviour reversal that will doubtless prove characteristic of this new century -- while having sex.

She is still, in the person of the actress Thea Gill, the most beautiful woman I have seen on a stage in months. The management may quote me on that.

I cant offer them much else in the way of advertising help. The Clairvoyant is the first offering of something called the North American Drama Alliance. Its the work of a Canadian, Larry Fineberg, but it has already been staged at a small theatre in Los Angeles, where it won a prize. This is fair enough; the play's unholy mix of mysticism, spy fiction, and apocalyptic social comment is the kind of thing that might well pass for profundity in the hinterlands of Hollywood.

Its title character, called Timon presumably in homage to Shakespeare's misanthrope, once had a thriving career as a psychic but has been driven into near-destitution, along with most of the rest of the world, by what everybody knowingly calls the crash. A courturiere friend advises him to join a cut-price fortune-telling operation since, as she approximately puts it, the future is just a commodity like any other. She also gives him a room above her store. Timon has come to dislike his gift of what he calls "reading people." But he needs money, and he soon attracts the attention of some very highly-placed human tea-leaves.

One of them is Goddard Bernstein (an inspired choice of name, I confess), who heads a corporation: the biggest of about 10 that between them control the planet. Another attraction of this brave polluted new world is something called ocean cancer, which has people nostalgic for the days when there were just ordinary cancer and AIDS.

As prophecy, all this is by no means unlikely, but it doesnt in itself make a play. Fineberg tries to structure his around the moral contortions of his hero who has never come to life in the first place and is anyway at the mercy of an increasingly frenzied plot. Goddard seems to be making billions from an extremely sinister social-engineering scheme; he is your standard amoral mastermind, with occasional glimmerings of a better self.

He is also ageing unnaturally fast; and his ambitious second-in- command who is very slimy (I think thats the intention; it's certainly the effect) wants to find out how long he has left. He wants Timon to find out and tell him, which would be a major breach of the soothsayer's code.

Timon is further compromised when, in strict accordance with fictional convention, he falls in love with the villains girl; she, the gorgeous blonde aforesaid, also falls for him, in what looks like a major piece of wish-fulfillment.

I suppose theres something cute about setting a fortune- teller's story actually in the future. (I was reminded of a deathless exchange from A Funny Thing Happened on the Way to the Forum: You want a soothsayer? "How did you know that?" "I'd be a fine soothsayer if I didn't.") The play occasionally captures the atmosphere of big business at its craziest. Mostly, though, it has all the self-importance and technical awkwardness of our local theatres of good works, plus an unctuousness all its own.

The actors grab for the nearest stock characterization and hang on to it like grim death; Hugo Dann is a ruffled Timon whose best moment comes when he lets out with an unnervingly-timed scream. Brian Richmond's production is mostly the victim of its own scene- changes; he seems to be trying for abstraction but caves in whenever he needs a prop. So people sit on plinths and drink from real glasses, which makes them look doubly uncomfortable.
