I was in London when Sarah Kane's play Blasted opened there in 1995, but felt too squeamish to go see it. It was denounced by most reviewers as being pointlessly, even pornographically, violent; praised, notably by other playwrights, as an imaginative portrayal of the way things actually are. As generally happens when the critical morality cops come out against a play (Ibsen's Ghosts, Edward Bond's Saved) history has ruled against them.

I still wouldn't blame anyone who couldn't face seeing it, and there were times when I averted my own eyes. But the play, finally receiving its Englishlanguage Canadian premiere in a superb production at Buddies in Bad Times, is a remarkable experience.

It starts with sexualized aggression on a small scale that's both psychologically and geographically specific; then explodes--literally--into global atrocity that comes, apparently, from nowhere. Ian, a middle-aged journalist, has brought Cate, a much younger woman from London, to a hotel room in his home town of Leeds. They have had a previous relationship, though how bright that old flame actually burned is open to question. Ian is drinking and smoking himself to death. Cate has sporadic fits; Ian, at least once, calls her "a retard," which is a fair example of the way he usually talks, especially on the subject of immigrants and other foreigners.

Ian of course wants to get Cate into bed, and that would seem indeed to be part of the deal. But she's resistant; and though what eventually happens may not technically qualify as rape, it leaves her bruised and resentful. Ian, by the way, is no monster; every advance is balanced by a shamed retreat, and he spends more time comforting Cate than threatening her. The acting of this duo, with all their shifts and gradations of feeling, is beyond praise. I couldn't quite believe in David Ferry's accent as native to Wales (where Ian was born) or Yorkshire (where he lives) but the man's belligerence and his hopelessness are there before us as living facts. So is the unreachableness of Michelle Monteith's Cate, at once full-blooded and detached ; the way she jumps up and down on an unwontedly luxurious bed is a perfect, joyous foil to the horrors to come.

They come with the irruption into the literally blasted room of an alien soldier (Dylan Smith, powerful if excessively indistinct) who announces that the town has been taken over, war brought home. It comes home to Ian in the form of anal rape, blinding, starvation and eventual cannibalism: scenes that are graphic, but not gloated over. Cate, who has mysteriously escaped, returns carrying a baby that she wants to take care of but has no idea how to. The British director, Dominic Dromgoole, an admirer of Kane's, has described her as a fine writer rather than a natural playwright. I'd agree, if it weren't for the initial scenes between Ian and Cate--the bulk of the play -- which are models of economy, of creating a relationship through hints and actions.

Where the play falls down is in its explicit attempts to tie its parts together. The soldier accuses Ian, who files tabloid stories about sex crimes abroad so long as they've happened to British holiday-makers, of ignoring far worse, more distant things. This must have seemed flimsy then, and it's flimsier now, when atrocities of all kinds get extensive coverage. What works better is the suggestion that Ian with Cate, and the soldier with Ian, are both avenging wrongs done to them: an endless cycle of reprisal.

Everything in Brendan Healy's production is brilliantly timed and brilliantly staged. He starts the evening with a pointless piece of audience-baiting that has us entering the theatre through the back door and in groups, and ends with Gene Kelly's recording of Singin' in the Rain: a monstrously heavy-handed attempt at irony, and A Clockwork Orange did it already. In between though, his direction is perfect: detailed, unhurried, ruthless and compassionate. Julie Fox's set, a white box that's the essence of every soullessly pristine hotel bedroom, is enclosed by a descending shutter that slams open and shut in a series of short, sharp shocks. If that isn't enough to scare the life out of you, Richard Feren's sound and Kimberly Purtell's lighting are there to finish the job.
