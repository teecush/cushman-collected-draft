"I feel," says Chita Rivera, "like a kid, looking at a toy she had all those years ago." Actually, it's quite a grown-up toy -- Chicago, the most cynical of musicals -- and she's playing with it, or in it, after leaving it alone for nearly 25 years.

What she is doing is unusual, possibly unique. An actor may go from playing Hamlet in his youth to Hamlet's wicked uncle Claudius in his middle years, and then may age still further into Polonius. It's common form for a grande dame to revisit the Chekhov plays in which she once scored as an ingenue.

That's in plays. In musicals, an actor may sometimes spin out a career by continuing to exploit a single famous role (Yul Brynner in The King and I, Carol Channing in My Fair Lady), but few shows offer more than one star role per gender.

Chicago is an exception: the story of two ladies of the 1920s, each of whom has bumped off the unfaithful man in her life and is now in prison awaiting -- or avoiding -- punishment. You might call them First and Second Murderesses; and which ranks as first and which as second is apt to depend on who the performers are.

In an ideal world, the two would be equal. This actually happened in the original 1975 production, when Rivera played Velma Kelly, the more hardened of the pair, opposite Gwen Verdon as the debutante killer Roxie Hart. The two top singer-dancer-actresses of the American musical, with Jerry Orbach, one of its best leading men, thrown in as well: It was one of the best package deals Broadway has ever offered. Directed, choreographed, and masterminded by Bob Fosse, the show ran a respectable two years but was overshadowed by the giant success of A Chorus Line, which opened the same season.

In 1996, the show came back in a revival that started out as a concert performance and was promoted to Broadway with its scale kept purposefully small. It tore the place up, and is still running. It has sent out touring companies, one of which played in Toronto last year. Now we have another; and it brings back Rivera, this time in the other role, that of Roxie.

"Both," muses Rivera, asked about the difference between the two characters "are killers. They're both desperate. Roxie starts out desperate; then Velma becomes desperate because Roxie is taking over." Roxie is the new kid in the jail, stealing Velma's thunder; and since Velma is a vaudeville performer in formal life, she feels the loss of limelight keenly.

The criminal-as-star is one of the main things the show is about. Roxie has a monologue ("wonderful to do," says Rivera) about having always wanted to be famous, and about the deficiencies of her kind but unglamorous husband. She's more obviously an acting role than Velma, who is "more physical -- she has chairs to deal with." (She picks them up and uses them as dancing partners.) This perhaps makes Roxie a more amenable role than Velma for a mature performer, but this is not a line of questioning that gets an interviewer very far. "I don't" says the lady firmly "talk about that kind of thing."

Her privilege: though it's impossible not to recall that she was in the original production of West Side Story, 42 years ago, scoring a major success (the greatest in what was primarily an ensemble show) as Anita, an updated and far sexier version of Juliet's nurse. Like Chicago, the show was thought in its time to be shocking: "People stormed out. They hadn't heard such language before."

Half wisecracker, half firecracker, the role led to her starring assignment in Bye Bye Birdie, an endearing if lopsided show (half of it was Rivera, and half was the story, and the two rarely touched) that confirmed her as a musical-comedy treasure. She worked steadily in the diminishing Broadway of the next 30 years, and was often the best thing in some dubious shows. The quality of her work earned her regular Tony nominations; the shows sank her chance of actually winning. This happened five times. She finally won in 1984 for The Rink: an especially noteworthy achievement, since this was another of those rare two-star shows, and Rivera was playing Liza Minnelli's mother. (I don't, of course, mean Judy Garland.)

As best-actress-in-a-musical awards go, this was especially well deserved, since Rivera really did act, truthfully and uncompromisingly, through and between the numbers. Perhaps it's the West Side Story connection that has tagged her forever as primarily a dancer, but in fact she is -- like Verdon and Minnelli -- one of those rare triple threats. Fred Ebb, lyricist of Chicago, The Rink and Kiss of the Spider Woman -- the recent show that found her at the top of her game -- once described her as one of the best singers he knew, and the echo of her voice clings distinctively to every number she has ever done.

That's one thing she has in common with Verdon, another who danced so enticingly that her singing was underrated. (Their performing lives crossed before Chicago. In the national company of Sweet Charity, Rivera played the title role that Verdon had created on Broadway. Neither got the role in the film -- it went to Shirley MacLaine -- but Rivera was in it as heroine's best friend. She had some great scenes, and led the Big Spender number unforgettably.) Their styles and personalities were complementary: Verdon melting and Rivera rasping, Verdon the gamine and Rivera the soubrette.

With Verdon retired, Rivera is incontestably the American musical's First Trouper. It will be interesting to see her crossing into her old colleague's territory, and seeing how the actress and the role adapt to one another.

As Rivera says, as everyone has said, the show -- written in the '70s, set in the '20s -- is topical today ("People only have to open the newspapers, turn on the news; they live with this kind of celebrity") but she adds refreshingly that it always was: "I thought at the time -- oh, please, this is nothing new. Look at history."
