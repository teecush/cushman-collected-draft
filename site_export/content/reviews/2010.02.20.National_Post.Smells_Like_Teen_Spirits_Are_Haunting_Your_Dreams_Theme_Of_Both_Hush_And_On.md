We have a show entitled Once and for all we're gonna tell you who we are so shut up and listen. We also have another show called Hush, which is a much shorter way of saying the same thing. Both focus on characters who are under voting age.

Rosa Laborde's Hush seems to have taken its title from lullabies. Its first line is "I dreamt about you last night." Its second is "people used to think that dreams are a second world and that's what led to the invention of a soul and ghosts and gods." The play seems to inhabit that second world; it presents itself as a series of interlocking dreams.

The dreamers are Harlem, a dentist (his name derives from Dutch ancestry, not from blackness), and his 12-year-old motherless daughter, Lily. They are half the cast. The others are Andre, Harlem's colleague (who is black), and Talia, a lady with whom Harlem appears to be having a relationship. Andre, though perfectly amiable in his everyday life, doubles in Lily's nightmares as a sacrificial priest intent on slaughtering her favourite teddy bear. Lily claims that she never dreams, but Harlem knows that everyone does; her nightmares naturally become his as well. Lily sorely misses the mother she never knew, and acts up at home and at school. The only person she seems able to connect with or confide in is Andre. Harlem ceaselessly intellectualizes (he's the one who advances the "second world" theory) and it puts up barriers between them. Though a convinced carnivore himself, at one meal, he tries the experiment of feeding Lily tofu, which only arouses her disgust. Here he seems to be reflecting the vegetarian influence of Talia, who also comments unfavourably on his keeping a jar of his baby teeth beside his bed. She leaves elegantly phrased messages on Harlem's answering machine, asking him why he hasn't called.

Elegant phrasing is a feature of Laborde's writing here; her people communicate in pithy, electrically charged sentences. A rejoinder at the end of one scene frequently doubles as a punning introduction to the next: a device that seems witty at some points and a mere nervous tic at others. We're kept guessing about what's dream, what's real, what's present, what's past. There's a tantalizing hole in the play's pattern of relationships; exactly half-way through it gets filled in. When the play loses its mystery, it also -- a not uncommon occurrence -- loosens its hold. We aren't meant to take the whole play as a dream(or if we are, I lose interest), but there's one plot development that on any realistic level is, to employ the play's own brand of imagery, almost impossible to swallow. The father-daughter relationship at the heart of the play is well observed and/or imagined, but it comes with a lot of fancy baggage. Richard Rose's production is first-rate, not an ounce of flab upon it, scenic or emotional. The acting, yet again, is super. Graeme Somerville's performance lands him in the front rank; his Harlem coats a furrowed brow and mind with a smiling gloss that may be of immense professional help. Vivien Endicott-Douglas has all the pain and fight and resilience of contemporary pre-adolescence. Conrad Coates radiates reasoned, quizzical concern as Andre, and Tara Rosling would haunt anybody's dreams. Laborde's previous play, Leo, was full of promise and, in style and technique if not in substance, her new one delivers. I was impressed, but I never cared.

"Dad," says Lily at the end, "we've entered the teen years. Could be rocky." She must have seen Once and for all..., which has been brought to World Stage from Belgium by Ontoerend Goed & Kopergietery & Richard Jordan Productions Ltd., credits almost as long as the title. It has been assembled and ferociously ordered by its director Alexander Devriendt from the improvisations of its 13 performers, aged 14 to 18, on the theme of teenage rebellion. Or just teenagedness. What comes across is that adolescence -- a word of which they're fond on the rare occasions when they get to use words -- is a state in which you alternate between having fun and getting mad at everyone, including yourself. Just what it's always been in fact: All that changes is the soundtrack and maybe the access to drugs. One of the cast as good as admits it. After announcing to her (invisible) parents her intention of disobeying everything they tell her, she says, "Everything has been done before; but not by me, not now." True enough, but she doesn't admit the corollary, which is that for us to care about something so commonplace happening to her, we would have to know something about her, and we don't. Or about any of them; they display more individuality in their program bios than on stage. As cliches go, though, this one is forcefully presented; the group alternately burn up the place with energy or cool it with their sullen bewilderment when the energy subsides. It's mainly a series of party scenes with recurring images, the most prominent being a couple heading together into the erotic privacy of a black garbage bag. Like Hush it presents itself as a dream, but a much noisier one. It runs about an hour; Hush goes about an hour and a half.
