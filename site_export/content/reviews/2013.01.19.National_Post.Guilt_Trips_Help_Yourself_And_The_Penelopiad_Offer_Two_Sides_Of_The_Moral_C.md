Two years ago, the award for best new play in the Toronto Fringe Festival was given to Kim's Convenience, which went on to glory. Last year, the prize was won by a play called Help Yourself, which is unlikely to achieve the same wide success, partly because it isn't as sunny and partly because it isn't as good. It is, though, worth checking out; as can now be done courtesy of a re-mount at the Red Sandcastle, an intimate makeover of a theatre on Queen West, which is easy to miss but very welcoming once found.

Help Yourself is indeed about self-help, or about somebody who sells it. He's Donny, a high-living (in a condo), fast-talking young man who charges a cool $5,000 an hour to clients who want to find the courage to do something forbidden. He's consulted by Ted, an older man, or at least a man with less hair and worse posture, who wants to work up the nerve to kill his wife, whom he's convinced is cheating on him. Ted has some moral qualms about this, as well as the apparently less troubling fear that he might be caught. Also he's never fired, or even held, a gun. Donny undertakes to free him from his misgivings, especially the ethical ones.

His technique is aggressive brainwashing, involving press-ups, forced marches around the room, and making Ted take copious draughts of hooch at an ungodly hour of the morning. "Ungodly" is indeed the word, since the more cerebral part of Donny's technique includes philosophical disquisitions on the irrelevance of conventional morality, as mainly derived from religion. He also confuses Ted with citations from Hume, Kant and Machiavelli. (Neither man completed college, but while Ted flunked it, Donny dropped out, believing - obviously rightly - that he could make more money following his own star. Ted, on the other hand, is a security-guard in a jewellery store. This turns out to be a plot point.) Donny's method is to be fast, loud and in-your-face, often literally. Ted frequently threatens to leave, sometimes even does so, but always comes back.

Interspersed with these moments from a consultation are scenes from Donny's private life: visits, presented mostly in reverse chronological order, from his girlfriend, a fairly recent acquisition who would like him to find a nicer line of work, though she does appreciate the perks of this one. The eventual convergence of the play's two narratives is predictable from a long way off: From so far in fact that, by the time it's actually fulfilled, we've forgotten about predicting it, so it still has an element of surprise, even of shock. It also grows in the mind; some behaviour (Ted's especially) that seems inexplicable when it happens proves in retrospect to have been both logical and clever. This is the second time in a couple of weeks that I've had occasion to recall The Public Eye, a 50year-old one-act play by Peter Shaffer. I doubt that the creators of Help Yourself, a group called Theatre Brouhaha, have seen or even heard of it but, take it from me, there's a structural resemblance, though the Shaffer play is much gentler.

The principal creator here, as writer and director, is Kat Sandler, who contributes a program note about ours being a society in which everything is permitted, and of which a personality and profession like Donny's are the natural products. This strikes me as specious generalization, but it does produce some effective drama, rather like Theatre Brouhaha's previous brouhaha LoveSex Money, though that one was more varied in its attack. The problem with this one is that Donny's abrasiveness takes over the play itself; his humiliation of Ted is all it has to offer, and though the process is never boring, it's grating to sit through. This is probably accentuated by the performance as Donny of Daniel Pagett (who was also involved in the writing); he seems to be enjoying himself more and holding himself back less than is strictly necessary. As, respectively, his punching-bag and sparring-partner, Tim Walker and Tosha Doiron are thoroughly credible

Nightwood Theatre's The Penelopiad, Margaret Atwood's re-imagining of the Odysseus story from the point of view of the wandering hero's famously patient wife, is back for a well deserved second season. It isn't quite a play, more a feat of theatrical storytelling and illustration: What is meant to be its binding element - Penelope's guilt over the fate of her faithful maids, brutally executed by her returning husband - never really registers as a dramatic fact, however much she talks about it, and however much they continue to haunt her in the afterlife from which she now addresses us.

Their concerns have to share our attention with Penelope's bruised and amused recollection of her entire life story from neglected royal childhood to semi-happy, semi-aggrieved and highly resourceful royal wifehood. (Her travails include what she might, if she didn't happen to be in Hades herself at the time, describe as the mother-in-law from Hell.) It's written with understated passion and superbly sustained wit, and these elements are preserved and amplified in Kelly Thornton's production, distinguished by Monica Dottor's choreography, Denyse Karn's spare, versatile set and tasteful, graceful costumes, delightful choral singing and dancing, and a wonderful corps of 13 actresses, several of them of actual or potential star quality, and all but one of them playing maids (and just about everybody else).

The exception of course is Megan Follows, who must have to deliver half the text and, without a hint of strain, conveys every jest, thought and cry of pain within it. I can hardly call Follows our most underrated actress - everybody knows how good she is - but she may be our most under-used. I've hardly ever, for example, seen her play Shakespeare (she was Juliet at Stratford, opposite the festival's current director, but that was before my Canadian time) though she'd seem to be made for it, with a command of text and mood, and a fastidious unforced charm, second to none. She heads a remarkable team (the few newcomers including a spirited Telemachus from Neema Bickersteth) but this is her show. We don't just see the story through her eyes; we feel it.
