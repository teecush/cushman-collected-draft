THEATRE REVIEWS MASADA

Tuesday, April 23, 1991

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Toronto ONT -- WRITTEN BY ARTHUR MILNER Directed by Simon Malbogat Starring Gary Reineke At the Theatre Centre, Toronto GOD AND THE BIG BLUE CHAIR WRITTEN BY RAYMOND STOREY, EDWARD ROY AND THE COMPANY Directed by Edward Roy At the Passe Muraille Backspace, Toronto Gary Reinekes refreshing one-man show about Israeli history is neither academic nor didactic, though it is informative Giving the devil his due BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Toronto MASADA, a Mixed Company production at the Theatre Centre in Toronto, is a breezy, biting conspectus of Jewish and Israeli history from Abraham to Shamir. The title refers to the Zealots' last stand; it was at Masada that fanatical Judean rebels chose suicide rather than surrender to legions of the Roman Empire.

Masada is also a one-man show. It takes the form of a lecture, supposedly delivered by a visiting Israeli historian to a Jewish audience somewhere in Canada, a young audience that has to be reminded that Moshe Dayan was the one with the patch. It is not, however, either academic or didactic, though it is informative.

Solo performances are often remorselessly dreary, but when they work they can be refreshing. It is a relief not to have to struggle through furlongs of exposition, or listen to actors trying to make conversation out of some playwright's tin-eared meanderings. A man speaking directly to us or himself can talk as he likes. And if he wants to make us laugh he can set about it directly, without involving third parties. As does Gary Reineke, the actor here, when he dryly remarks that if God had asked him to pick out a promised land he would have chosen France.

However, Moses plumped for what was then called Canaan, and by that ancestral choice our speaker stands. Or, perhaps more accurately, flies; he is a far-out hawk. The message he has come to deliver is: "Sure the Palestinians deserve a state. If they can get it." What Israel has it holds, not just the occupied territories but eventually - and here the actors eyes perceptibly glitter - Jordan as well.

And here, of course, if not before, shudders run down our liberal spines, as we sit with hackles ascendant. These are not opinions that can be expected to go unchallenged in an alternative theatre. And if there is nobody else on stage to challenge them, as of course there isnt, then it's up to the audience. Indeed, if there were no tension between the speaker and the house, there would not be a play here at all, merely a weird vanity showcase. But plainly playwright Arthur Milner has not gone to the expense of hiring an actor in order to spout his own beliefs. We are expected to ponder the title, and to reflect that unrestrained zealotry can be the path to self-destruction.

Our lecturer is in fact an anti-mouthpiece, not so much the devil's advocate as the devil himself. So you will be pleased to learn, especially if you dig proverbs, that he is given his due. He is rather convincing, which may be to Milners disadvantage as a polemicist, but does him credit as a playwright. It is actually refreshing to hear a Zionist argument that makes minimal reference to the Holocaust, one that sees the Jewish state as the reward of struggle rather than the worlds pat on the back for sufferings rendered. The speaker doesn't flinch from endorsing Irgun terrorism, and he doesn't whine about the PLO employing the same tactics. This is the world we live in, and here at least there is no politicians hypocrisy about it. Reinekes performance, balancing the demands of narrative with those of character, the pedagogue with the fanatic, is excellent.

Where is God in all this? Nowhere much, except in a mood-setting reference to Abraham's willingness to sacrifice his son. But a character in Topological Theatre's God and the Big Blue Chair does see God sitting on, indeed, a big blue chair, and dozens of similar furnishings hang from the ceiling over the acting area at Passe Muraille, perhaps to encourage others who would like to be similarly privileged. As the lights go up we see a selection of them, apparently fighting over a chair. That is, each of them is gripping onto it with one hand, while keeping the other arm balletically outstretched. It looks like the kind of thing that gives religious drama, and group-improvised drama, and drama itself, a bad name.

And so it proves. Half-a-dozen stories of people seeking faith are intercut and occasionally interwoven, though with five actors in multiple roles it is difficult to keep track. There are some passages of anecdotal, or even anthropological, interest: a mother-goddess priestess draws thoughtful parallels between her own cult and that of the Virgin Mary. Some, including a scene depicting a young woman searching for her hospitalized grandfather, are pointlessly feeble. And even the better bits suggest a mechanical searching of received scenarios to pad out a prearranged theme. Here is another gay couple blighted by AIDS. Here is another yuppie hitting the skids among hookers and winos.

Debra Kirshenbaum rises above her material as an Alabama housewife, pregnant in middle age, who goes looking for herself on the road and mysteriously winds up in Canada. This at least allows her to meet the rest of the cast, who have been striving and suffering here all along. Toward the end, moments of exaltation abound, though probably not for the audience. Some performances are anonymously competent; some others aren't.
