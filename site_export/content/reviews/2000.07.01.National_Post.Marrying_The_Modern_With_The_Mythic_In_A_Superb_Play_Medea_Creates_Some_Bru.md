Medea runs in repertoire at the Tom Patterson Theatre in Stratford until Oct. 1. Tickets are $60.50 to $67.50 and can be ordered by calling the box office at 1 (800) 567-1600.

The infinitely quotable American theatre critic Walter Kerr once devoted a column to bemoaning the prevalence in modern drama of genteel dinner-party scenes, comparing them to the more robust culinary conventions of earlier theatres. "The Greeks," he said, "when they ate at all, ate their children: a procedure with some dramatic point to it."

It's a habit, as we saw earlier this week in the Stratford production of Titus Andronicus, that the Greeks bequeathed to the Elizabethans, or at least to the Elizabethan idea of what went on in ancient Rome. There are, however, worse things than cannibalism.

The night after Titus, Stratford took us back to Greek tragedy itself: to Euripides' Medea, a play that cannot compete with Shakespeare's in sheer volume of horrors but far surpasses it in intensity. The child-eating mother in Titus is the victim of a trick; she hasn't actually slaughtered and dressed the meat herself. Medea doesn't eat her own young sons or cause anyone else to do so. She does, though, kill them: on purpose. She does it to get back at the husband who is deserting her for a younger woman. It seems very modern.

In Greek myth, Medea was the Asian sorceress whose arts helped the heroic Jason to win the famous Golden Fleece; her services to him included the drowning of her own brother who was pursuing their escaping ship, the Argo. Euripides, never a great one for heroes, shows us this couple 10 years on, back in Jason's home city of Corinth. Given the chance to marry the daughter of Creon, the Corinthian king, Jason jumps at it; and Creon, acting presumably on his new son-in-law's advice, orders Medea to leave town. The whole action of the play is Medea's revenge. First, she gives Jason's bride (whom we never meet) and her father (who does admittedly seem insufferable) betrothal gifts: magic robes that burn and flay when they touch the skin, condemning their wearers to an agonizing death. Then, fearful that she may have let her ex off too lightly, she sacrifices their boys -- having first ensured that she herself has an escape route to Athens.

The play is performed in a free, and 50-year-old, adaptation by the American poet Robinson Jeffers (who, incidentally, gets far bigger program billing than the original playwright). Adaptation here turns out to mean a certain amount of softening; Medea is allowed more elaborate maternal qualms than I remember from other versions. It also means outright trivialization; Euripides is known for taking his mythic characters down a few pegs, but I can't believe that Jason's response when granted a peek at the fatal bridal shower is adequately rendered by the words "Medea, you shouldn't ... it's too much."

Still, this is a play that can use some help, or perhaps more accurately, a play whose audiences can use some help. It's an immensely powerful myth, with a ring of contemporary truth to it; but its heroine is not, to put it banally, very likeable -- not just because of the monstrosity of her actions but because her entire role is a litany of grievances. This makes the play itself difficult to warm to; and if Jeffers' text works too obviously hard at making it accessible, the work is usually justified.

Miles Potter's production approaches the play's two levels very cunningly. We begin with the sound of an off-stage lament in (presumably) the original Greek, and a brief glimpse of the company in masks: a brief gesture to the traditional tragic plateau we are trying to reach. The lights go down, to come up on modern actors, still in ancient robes but using their own faces and their own voices. We are now, give or take a few smashing special effects, in their hands, and, above all, in those of the leading actress. Seana McKenna's Medea is, almost unwaveringly, superb.

She is sulphurous and coruscating; a believable sorceress and a believably wronged woman, who at once thrives on her wrongs and is lacerated by them. She also has a whiplash wit, which she perhaps employs too freely; but then the translation encourages her. One still doesn't warm to Medea (and her adoption as a feminist icon has never seemed to me to do the women's movement much credit), but her play held me as it never has before. The scene in which she swaps recriminations with Scott Wentworth's Jason, each endlessly resourceful at blaming the other for the fate of their marriage, is bruisingly recognizable. The actor cannot always match her on her own level (he is more apt than she to fall back on stock anger or stock irony) but most of the time he is very fine, and he is never afraid to act contemptible.

"I believed I was alone," says Medea early on, proceeding to complain that "nothing is ever private in a Greek city." This is a bit like complaining that you didn't expect the Spanish Inquisition; you're never alone in a Greek tragedy, because the Chorus has always got there before you: listening, commenting, shuddering at the way you go on. There are three chorines here, drawn from three generations of Stratford leading ladies: Patricia Collins, Kate Trotter (both powerful) and Michelle Giroux (who seems out of her depth). Two kings appear; Robert Benson's Corinthian resident intones like a man who thinks he's in a Greek tragedy, but John Dolan's Athenian visitor is pleasantly doddery. Rita Howell's nurse matures impressively from glum nanny to messenger of death. She ushers us into the play's last phase, haunted here by the authentic sense of implacable doom; the action is both itself, and more than itself.

There is seldom any hopeful restoration of order at the end of a Greek tragedy, as there is with the Elizabethans -- perhaps because, with all the violence offstage, nobody has to supervise the removal of the bodies. For a modern actor to find within himself the final desolation of the bereaved Jason must be almost impossible; Wentworth goes honourably through the motions, but the production rescues him. There is a thunderclap; the lights again go down and come up, to reveal him again with a mask clapped to his face. We return again to the conventions for which the play was written; past and present, ritual and reality, join hands. Kenneth Tynan, my other favourite 20th-century critic, once said that he rarely emerged from a production of Medea feeling that he knew several marriages exactly like that. I think this production might have changed his mind.
