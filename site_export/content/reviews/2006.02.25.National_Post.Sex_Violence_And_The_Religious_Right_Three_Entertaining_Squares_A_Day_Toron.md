robertcushman@lycos.com

Curtains are coming back. That doesn't mean theyre coming down, with a satisfying whoosh, like in the good old days. The drapes that have been hung downstage, first at the Young Centre, for Soulpeppers The Government Inspector, and now at the Passe Muraille Backspace, for Three Squares a Day, are frail, temporary things that have to move sideways to reveal or conceal the riches within. Still, they're a start. For decades now it's been taken for granted that audiences, when they arrive in a theatre, will be gratified to see the set there waiting for them. Its supposed to liberate them from bourgeois notions of concealment and titillation.

Ive never quite bought this. Seems to me that if a set isn't good, then you don't want to spend any more time looking at it than is absolutely necessary. And if it is good, then it will seem even better with the element of surprise.

Enough with the heavy irony. The curtains in the two new shows arent there just to be decorative or nostalgic. They are trying to tell us something. In the case of The Government Inspector its that the show we're going to watch is a consciously artificial celebration of the theatre itself. In the case of Three Squares a Day it's that the show were going to watch is a peep show. This latest offering from veteran playwright Tom Walmsley begins with Patricia Fagan, delectable in a flaming-red negligee, entering to push the curtains aside. What lies behind, designed by Kelly Wolf, is a life-size inhabited etching of a small-town Canadian kitchen. It oozes cartooned conventionality. The program, with an unusual degree of up-frontness, has already alerted us to what the style and the targets are to be. A play about the religious right, it promises. Sex. Violence.

So then. We are in the house of Morgan, who runs a security company that specializes in strike-breaking, and his wife, Candace. They are evangelical Christians, very much into God, breakfast cereals and excruciating snack foods. They read, often aloud, a newspaper that looked, from where I was sitting, exactly like the National Post but that sounds like a cross between a local gossip sheet and a supermarket tabloid. It features stories about how fast Hell is filling up.

Morgan gives a roof and a job to his older brother, Thomas James, who used to be a drug-taking, free-loving leftie but has taken a partial step toward his brothers good graces by converting -- like Walmsley himself -- to Catholicism. But he's one of your progressive social-justice Catholics. He translates right-wing economic theory as "the poor have too much money and the rich don't have enough, which he thinks is the opposite of Christian. By contrast, Morgans idea of a political position is to contemplate the terrorist armaments that may be aimed at the United States and to say, Its a disgrace those weapons aren't aimed at Canada."

Candace seems, on the surface, to be ready for debate. When Thomas James comes up with the de rigueur references to camels and needles, she goes so far as to tell him, with sweet pity in her voice, that he does not understand. This is as close to a meeting, or even a battle, of minds as the play gets. As dialectic, it's a frost.

A related but deeper flaw is that Morgan and Candace are hypocrites. Their favourite social activity is to attend "Arabian Nights, for couples only, involving wine, nudity and whatever else. (If it cant make me pregnant," Candace says, "it isnt sexual relations.) We also learn that Morgan has been getting it on with every available young woman in his catchment area. I know that fundamentalist ministers are continually getting caught out in real- life scandals, and last Sunday's New York Times said that the Bible Belt has the highest divorce rate in America. But a clash of values is more interesting dramatically if the values are sincerely held. And besides, the religious right is far more frightening if we assume it means what it says.

So we have to fall back on the sex and violence, which, fortunately, are good. One sibling beats the other up, and the scene is very well staged; tactfully, too. The comic mainspring is that Candace is hot for Thomas James and progressively less coy about it. Morgan, who is sterile, wants his brother to father Candace's child. What he has in mind is artificial insemination via a turkey baster. This, by Candaces definition quoted above, would of course count as sexual relations, maybe with a pun on relations," but anyway it isnt what she has in mind. TJ, despite some mental twistings-and- turnings, agrees with her. The play resolves into a neat satirical sex farce, lampooning the striving and decorous middle class. It's like the German classic The Underpants, seen recently in Toronto and at the Shaw Festival. Walmsley isn't as good as Carl Sternheim at keeping his eye on the object, but he does pretty well.

- - -

Kate Lynch has given the play a very witty production. Jordan Pettle, an actor who exudes amiable doubt, is an unlikely choice for the ironclad Morgan, but he makes his charm work for him, presenting a bully so sure of his own righteousness and so adept at double- think that he hardly has to work at it. He also looks, as the plot requires him to be, easy to deceive. David Jansen is a powerful, easy, ambiguous presence as TJ: ambiguous because the dramatic weight of his Catholicism remains a secret between the playwright and God. But the most perfect of the three squares is Fagans Candace; theres a new comic boldness in her playing, a perfect matching of means to ends. She gets both the shimmying temptress bit and the mental solipsism that makes it possible. Like Candace, she invents her own outrageous conventions and makes them work for her.

- - -

Kurt Weill's 1941 Broadway musical Lady in the Dark also made its own rules. Two years before Oklahoma! it proposed a new equality between book and score. Moss Hart's voluminous script is about the psychoanalysis of a fashionable magazine editor baffled by her life. Weills chameleonic, echoing score (the best-known song is My Ship; the best is This Is New) and Ira Gershwins tricky lyrics depict her dreams. The show's account of, and faith in, analysis is simplistic and its conclusions dubious (the heroine needs only to find the right man), but there is a haunting, ambitious quality to it. It has never had a major American revival, partly because the central role, created by Gertrude Lawrence, is a terror. Donna Murphy might have the best shot today at the necessary mixture of brittleness and warmth. Last weekend's production by the Toronto Operetta Theatre (at the Jane Mallett) was a non-starter in almost every respect.

Some of the performances, in Guillermo Silva-Marins production, were so inept one didnt know whether to be embarrassed by them or for them. Not only could most of the cast not speak or move, they couldn't sing, at least not in the sense of making themselves heard over the orchestra: that includes the leading lady, Elizabeth Beeler. The only bright spot was Thom Allison, the one recruit from the regular musical theatre, who got regulation camp laughs as an art editor and did coruscating song-dance-and-patter work in the musical bits, especially as a judge at a trial conceived, Chicago- style but years earlier, as a circus.

- Three Squares a Day runs through March 12 at Theatre Passe Muraille Backspace, 416-504-7529.
