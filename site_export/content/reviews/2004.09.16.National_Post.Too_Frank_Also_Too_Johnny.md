FRANKIE & JOHNNY IN THE CLAIR-DE-LUNE

Jane Mallet Theatre, Toronto

- - -

Frankie and Johnny are sweethearts. At least they are on the night we meet them in Terrence McNally's play. Previously they have been co-workers in a New York greasy spoon, she a waitress, he a short-order cook. At the end of their first date, they go back to Frankie's apartment and into her bed. And, oh lordy, how they can love.

I suppose it's to the play's credit that it's explicit about the physical nature of human relationships, especially the messier ones. Both Frankie and Johnny are in their 40s and neither of them is a conventionally glamorous figure; there is plenty of material here about sagging flesh and flagging energy. But if the laughter this arouses is to be sympathetic rather than queasy, we need both to believe in the characters and to feel for them. McNally, one of the most successful of American playwrights, writes realistically about their bodies, but when dealing with their minds he is apt to get mushy. It's a very Broadway thing -- the desire to write romantically without being sentimental -- and it's likely to trip up. Nobody holds it against McNally that he's slick -- it's quite an achievement to keep us interested in two characters and one situation for two whole acts -- but he can also be glib. And he's inadequately served by the newly fledged BirdLand Theatre, whose second flight this is. The production gets the rhythms only fitfully correct, and neither of its performances comes fully alive.

The dynamic is that Johnny wants to hold on to Frankie while Frankie has her doubts. Both of them have been bruised in the past, both by family and partners, and these are the differing ways in which they react; he's a battered optimist, she's a wry pessimist. Both, needless to say, have shattered dreams; she wanted to be an actress, he didn't want to be a cook. And then there is the coincidence of their names, which the play brazenly, and I suppose charmingly, exploits by having Johnny call in to a classical music DJ who flatly refuses to believe it, but who anyway plays for them what he announces simply as "the most beautiful music ever written." It's Debussy's Clair de Lune; we know it, they don't. It goes with the actual moonlight that's streaming full-glow outside; they tell us so, and we believe them.

There can be no quarrel with the play's atmospherics; we believe that this is New York, and we can smell the food in the cramped apartment. Not all the cultural references are musical. Some are literary, and they work less well. Johnny, an autodidact, has a Complete Shakespeare in his locker; he's working his way slowly through it, or so he tells us, and he keeps quoting from it. But the book might just as well be a Dictionary of Quotations, because somehow all the lines he comes up with are the famous ones. And it's at points like this that McNally's writing becomes dangerously coy.

The play was first staged in 1987, and it was revived on Broadway a few years ago with Edie Falco, alive in every nerve end, playing Frankie. Falco's resilient spirit and crinkled, world-weary smile are difficult images to erase from any role, and Zorana Kydd who plays it here (also BirdLand's Artistic Producer) is ill-equipped to compete. She gets the outline of the character well enough, especially in its resentful aspects, but she seems to have only two modes of performance; she's either exclamatory, which can be wearing, or passive, which can be dull. Peter Van Wart's Johnny is competent but raucous; his dreams of a perfectly imperfect love never achieve lift-off. Rod Ceballos' direction lacks any sense of thrust-and-parry, not only between the characters but between the play's various levels of wit; these, being stage New Yorkers, are always making jokes, and the production seems uncertain of when they mean it and when they don't. The play reaches its predestined, qualifiedly happy ending with nothing much seeming to have happened, inside the characters or out.
