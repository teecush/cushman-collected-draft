BETHANY'S GATE

Theatre Centre, Toronto

The most influential theatrical practitioners in Toronto may be the clowning duo known as Mump and Smoot. Their own appearances may be infrequent, but they are always turning up, as directors or coaches, in the credits for other peoples productions. Or their names may just be listed among the acknowledgments in the program, as is the case with Bethanys Gate, a show with unmistakable symptoms of Mumps and Smootiness.

Bethany's Gate is a short, rather sweet lampoon of classical ballet. All the performers wear shiny red noses, an unusual occurrence in ballet but very much in accordance with the technique of Pochinko clowning, which is the brand espoused and disseminated by M&S. The noses carry with them a couple of built-in advantages, both fully exploited here: They make the performers look slightly funny, no matter what they're doing; and they function as a uniform without destroying anybodys individuality. If anything, they accentuate it.

The story concerns a princess who is the victim of a magic spell, a swain who hopelessly falls for her, and a corps of animals. The ending is happy, if you discount the fact that the principal characters are dead. It is, in fact, what philistines, myself included, will recognize as basic ballet. Whetstone Productions, the group presenting the show, seems to be composed of performers who are not primarily dancers, at least not in this idiom, so the piece has an unfortunate tendency to look less like a parody than like the real thing done badly. This is not, however, catastrophic.

Bethanys Gate has been advertised as "a ballet within a play." I don't think the thing the ballet is within can truthfully be described as a play, but it does involve a lot of talk, much of which is amusing. The eight actors, or whatever they are, purport to be members-in-exile of the National Ballet of Gorgontrevich. We are never told precisely where Gorgontrevich is, but one would guess it to be somewhere in the East End of Central Europe. I deduce this from its former inhabitants' accents, which are ripely generic, and from the slide show of their homeland with which they favour us early on. This suggests that Gorgontrevich is one of those picturesque states whose main industry had better be tourism. Otherwise, its in trouble. Not that it isnt anyway, what with the revolution that has driven out the dancers (monarchists to a person) and with the terrible memory of what its citizens tremblingly refer to as the Great Schism.

Most of this valuable knowledge is relayed to us by the troupe's artistic director, one Nicholas Denouement, who also dances the role of the hero, for which he seems frankly a little long in the tooth. He begins the evening in fairly standard fashion by forbidding us to use cameras (unless, he notes while adopting a becoming pose, we use them to take pictures of him) and goes on to tell us the history of Bethany's Gate, the Gorgontrevich National Ballets national ballet. It seems the original choreography (1793) was lost for a couple of centuries and has only recently been rediscovered, though what we see might easily have been assembled from guesswork by any reasonably competent dance teacher at an elementary school.

Nicholas is played, in a wittily captured vein of pained egomania, by David Tomlinson, though you have to dig deep in the program to disinter this information. Meagan OShea appears as Madame Zola, who in turn appears as the bewitched and bewitching Bethany. The madame seems -- one feels free to mention this now that the girth of opera singers has become a matter of open debate -- somewhat plumper than one expects of a prima ballerina. Indeed, the relationship of Nicholas and Zola -- he evasive, she adoring -- is strongly reminiscent of that of Kermit the Frog and Miss Piggy.

This brings us to the dance itself, in which the creatures surrounding the unfortunate Bethany (and her gate) are all, of course, offering red noses to a blue lady. This raises a question: Do the appendages belong to the characters in the ballet or to the dancers portraying them or to the actors portraying them? And then again: Are the lumpen movements of the chorus actually a choreographic comment on the assembled beasts of field and forest (indistinguishable on stage, if not in the cast list) or are they just bad luck?

Not that there aren't some pleasing performances going on here. I liked the coyly flirtatious Mina (Diana Kolpak) and the shy but intense Patina (Kathleen LeRoux), and was greatly struck by Eeejit (Hadrian Abbot) who is both surly and burly and looks like Rick Salutin in tights. But, frankly, if Whetstone Productions really expect us to see any interaction between the characters in the ballet and those of the Gorgonetrevichers themselves (or do we, with special reference to the name Zola, call them Gorgons for short?) then Whetstone Productions is fooling itself. Both levels are far too sketchy to permit that kind of carry-on.

There is some musical pleasure. The original Gorgontrevich score must have gone the way of the original Gorgontrevich steps, but we are treated instead to Bernstein and Borodin -- the overture to Candide and dance music from Prince Igor. The former is always a delight to hear, and those in the know can sing along to it, as its principal melody is that of the song Oh Happy We. You can also, if you had the same kind of warped musical upbringing I did, annoy your neighbours by singing the Borodin, as it's the tune that turned up in Kismet as Not Since Nineveh.

Though the show lasts only an hour, it does have an intermission. For this, however, we stay in the auditorium -- which, considering the available lobby space at Theatre Centre, is a good idea.

As our hosts put it, we bring intermission to you. They also bring trays of doughnuts and bottled water that we are allowed, indeed encouraged, to buy. The doughnuts are a dollar apiece; "dollars to doughnuts" used to signify long odds but the economy has changed.

More intrepid patrons can buy kisses from Mina or have their faces sculpted in cheese (a traditional Gorgon art form) by Patina. We have here an exemplary combination of hospitality with entrepreneurship, one that our more established theatres might well emulate. I look forward to being served by company members at CanStage (wine and cheese) or the Shaw Festival (tea and crumpets).

Bethanys Gate is directed by Kolpak and Tomlinson, and written by them "with the company." Original music is by James Fisher, who has CDs for sale though not during the intermission, and choreography by OShea. Lighting is credited on one slip of paper to Sharon DiGenova and on another, far more plausibly, to Oskar Gongonzovich.
