RESTITUTION: AN IRISH-CANADIAN RHAPSODY

Factory Theatre, Toronto

A series of misadventures kept me from the opening of Restitution, but I could hardly avoid noticing a marked chilliness in the reactions of those colleagues who were present. Having caught up, I have to say I found the play less of a trial than did the other reviewers, and less long as well. They were complaining that the show had robbed them of three hours of their lives; on the night of my visit, it clocked in at a mere two hours twenty.

It remains a rum piece, and by its close a vexatious one. I also have no idea what kind of play it is trying to be. Consider this: The date is 1882, the place the Ottawa valley. There are two brothers, Stephen and Seamus, whom their late father brought to Canada from Ireland when they were both small boys. Now grown up (physically anyway) and with kids of their own, they enjoy a fraternal relationship which would be like that of Cain and Abel, except that both of them are Cain. Seamus begins the proceedings by dealing a fatal knock on the head to Stephens son James for the crime of touching Seamuss favourite horse.

James is an accomplished harpist who was all set to go to Toronto to study at the Academy of Music. When Stephen's wife hears of her loss she says what any Irish mother would say if her musically gifted boy had been snatched up to heaven: He's forgot his harp. I suppose thats a funny line, though I cannot imagine what the right tone would be for delivering it; in this production it comes out of nowhere.

I dont think it's appropriate to compare Michael O'Briens play to those of Martin McDonagh. Its true that O'Brien's characters tend, like McDonaghs, to be violent, vindictive and Irish, and that they say "feck" a lot, though not as often as McDonaghs do. But McDonagh's plays (pre-eminently The Beauty Queen of Leenane) are tightly plotted and well motivated. O'Brien merely tells a tale, which is his right, but doesnt it ramble.

I confess to bearing him a grudge for a politically correct version of Oliver Twist that he committed for the Young Peoples Theatre a few years back, but I also confess that he can write. His lines and speeches have rhythm. But his scenes don't, either in themselves or when placed against one another, and so neither does his play.

Stephen's wife commits suicide, obliging him, once the priest gets to hear about it, to dig her body up and remove it from consecrated ground. Seamus, though he does feel bad about some of this, seems to think he can pass it all off with a few apologies, though he later throws in some cash as well.

This turns out to be mistaken generosity; Stephen promptly hires three local nondescripts to hunt Seamus down and crucify him. He spends the rest of the money on moving to Toronto, in order to pursue a career as an author, which seems a bit arbitrary.

Eight years pass, and we find ourselves in the big city, still never encountering a soul who isnt from the ould counthry. This second act, as cheerfully bloody as its predecessor, might be Torontos answer to Gangs of New York, though, alas, without the spectacle.

Stephen writes and even sells idyllic stories about his beloved brother's untimely death, though the bottom falls out of this market when Seamus reappears. (Crucifixion is obviously not what it once was: I mean, we're not talking resurrection here.)

The feud is on again, despite the best efforts of Seamuss daughter, who was always a reconciler, and has now got her to a nunnery. This gives OBrien, at his lowest ebb, the cue for a few jokes about the habits that nuns get into under their habits.

Meanwhile, in the opposite corner, there is the ghost of Stephen's father (Seamus's too, of course, but he only appears to Stephen), a mountainous apparition who carries a chain. I initially took this, since the action takes place around Christmas, to be a jovial reference on the spirits part to Jacob Marley, but he turns out to have more personal reasons.

He was a mean old man, to put it mildly, who now claims credit for having, among other things, caused the great Irish famine, and he has terrorized his sons into being terrors themselves. All ends comparatively happily, for those still alive. One of them, probably speaking for the author, blames all the suffering on religion, or maybe its just Catholicism.

And sometimes the characters feel their pain like pain, and sometimes they don't; it seems to depend on whether the playwright, at any given moment, is after a laugh or a shudder. I can best describe the style as brutal whimsy. It's seldom actually dull, but if theres a way of making it theatrically coherent, Sarah Stanleys production hasn't found it.

The Factory here adds to its record of mounting the unsightliest shows in Toronto. Most of Carolyn M. Smith's set consists of a raised tilted disc; it might be an impressive ground for action, except that the cast are seldom allowed to act on it. Most of the time, they are huddled in corners at the sides. This is all right for the more soothing scenes, of which there are a couple, but most of the play is confrontation, and the actors have no room in which to square off. They look and sound unruddered. According to the program, the director is currently teaching at the National Theatre School -- not, I hope, on the uses of theatrical space.

Richard McMillan is an actor fully capable of either full- blooded melodrama or comically understated angst. As Stephen he seems to be switching at random between the two. As Seamus, John Dolan varies between a grizzled cackle and a grizzled moan.

Kelli Fox, in a variety of roles, comes off best, except when, for economy's sake, she dons a moustache and flat cap and tries to pass as a male desperado. Lisa Norton has only one role, but her character is so inconsistent that she too seems to be playing several. She does each of them well but cannot connect them.

As the dead da, Jefferson Mappin gets the play off to a tremendous start with a fine anecdote (misleadingly fine, as it turns out) about a bear. His barnstorming later gets monotonous, but he may not have any choice. The company is completed by Alon Nashman, a fine actor whom God probably never intended to play an Irishman, and certainly not, as here, half a dozen of them.
