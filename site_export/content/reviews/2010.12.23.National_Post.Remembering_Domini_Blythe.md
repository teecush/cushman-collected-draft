In 1984, visiting Canada from England, I caught a Stratford Festival production of Molieres Tartuffe, directed by John Hirsch. It was splendid all around, but in one scene it touched greatness: the attempted seduction by Brian Bedford's Puritan hypocrite of Elmire, the married woman into whose house he had insinuated himself. She was played by Domini Blythe.

I had seen spectacular things at Stratford on previous trips, but this was in a different class of intimacy and concentration; never before had I known the Festival theatre, with its epic stage and auditorium, to be so transfixed by a domestic encounter. Elmire is playing a double game in this scene, leading her tempter on so that her husband, concealed under the table, will see him for what he is. Blythe, in her late thirties at the time, blended the eroticism, the uncertainty and the underlying purpose with a superb sense of comic tension. Her duet with Bedford (Douglas Campbell, in furious hiding, making a third) was, in terms of contact and concentration, the most breathtaking acting I had seen at Stratford up to that time and nothing has surpassed it since.

Domini Blythe, who died last week of throat cancer at the age of 63, was one of the most loved actresses in the Canadian theatre. To meet her was to be enchanted by the beautiful face and honeyed voice that adorned her acting. To get to know her was to be knocked out all over again, by both her toughness and her gentleness: by her honesty, her forthrightness, her sense of fun, and her ability to make whoever she was talking to feel like the most important person in the world. Peter Hinton, who wrote and directed the one-woman play Fanny Kemble that was her last Stratford appearance, speaks of her genuine curiosity about other people. She cared so much about the young people in the company, about passing on her knowledge and expertise.

She was born into a British theatrical family, and she began her career there. She was in the original London cast of the famous erotic revue Oh! Calcutta!; I must have seen her in it, though I regret that I have no specific memories. She must have made an impression, though, on Kenneth Tynan, who created the show; in one of his posthumously published letters he wrote that she was one of only two actors in the cast whose performance sustained over the run. The other was a Canadian: Richard Monette, in fact, who, when he returned to Canada, and ultimately to Stratford, brought her with him. For several years, they lived together. I have a fond memory of Richard, years after they had split up and he was running the Festival, standing on the Stratford sidewalk during the intermission of Private Lives, and telling Domini how much the tempestuous relationship in the play reminded him of their own. They remained devoted to one another, and fiercely loyal.

She joined the Stratford company in 1976.The following year she had her first major role there, and one of her few failures: an oddly passionless Miss Julie (a role and a play that have defeated many people). In 1979 she was Rosaline, the dark lady of Love's Labours Lost. Martha Henry, who played the Princess of France, recalls that, "Robin would get all the rest of us into the house to watch Domini and then try to get us to do that!' I could indeed see what 'that was but couldnt for the life of me do it. She had the ability to join the word to the thought

absolutely seamlessly so that the two things were one -- so that there was never a schism in her characters -- they were always absolutely authentic."

In the early '80s she returned to Britain for a couple of years, acting with the Royal Shakespeare Company and more than holding her own. I remember her as an indignant Elizabeth to Alan Howard's Richard III. Even better was her performance of the title role in the Jacobean rarity The Maid's Tragedy. Back at the Canadian Stratford, she moved gracefully and wittily into character roles, playing a deliciously amused lady-in-waiting in Henry V, an imperious Maria in Twelfth Night and a primly provocative Prism in The Importance of Being Earnest. Her solo Fanny Kemble was a long-cherished project; according to Hinton, "she loved the rigour of a woman who fought for her political and literary and social and professional life."

She was forging a new career in Montreal, where she lived with her husband, when illness struck. Her brother Ben, who came from London to be with her in her last days, says that she faced death with peace and acceptance and even with happiness. All the same, losing one who still had so much to give seems cruel. She was lovely.
