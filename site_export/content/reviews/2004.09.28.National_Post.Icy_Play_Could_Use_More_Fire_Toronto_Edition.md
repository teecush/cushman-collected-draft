SNOWMAN

Buddies in Bad Times Theatre, Toronto

- - -

Greg MacArthur is currently functioning in Toronto as both actor and playwright. He's one third of the cast of pppeeeaaaccceee, (a title I am getting quite tired of typing) but, though theres nothing wrong with that performance, he is shown off to better advantage as author of Snowman. Its an unsatisfying but resoundingly talented piece, the most interesting new play I remember seeing at Buddies in Bad Times, whose new season it opens.

It's set in the far and frozen north, in a small township whose inhabitants seem to be marked by their loneliness, their transience, or both. Here is a guy named Denver and his partner Marjorie; some years ago, after existing on the fringes of petty crime, they pointed their noses toward the north and followed them. This is where they ended up.

Denver survives by renting videos, stolen from his previous employers down south. (You'd think his stock and clientele would by now have have exhausted one another, but he does in fact talk about expanding.) Marjorie doesnt do much of anything; shes drugged-out and depleted.

Their closest friend in town is Jude, a soulfully beautiful young man (Denver reckons he'd be a sensation in town, though here he passes unnoticed) who woke up one morning to find that his parents had walked out on him. Jude first wandered into Denver's store asking to rent some gay porn, of which Denver has a good supply, all of it German. As Jude doesnt have a TV or VCR of his own, Denver lets him use his and Marjories living room. Jude is a good supplier of coke, which, Denver notes, is easier to obtain in these latitudes than feta cheese: much easier.

It's only Denver who really takes to Jude. Marjorie doesn't much like him or, as far as we can make out, anyone else. Jude in turn becomes fixated on Denver. Dialogue scenes between these people are brief and rudimentary; they tell us most of their stories in monologues, which are both the plays greatest strength and its greatest weakness.

Hovering on the edge of the action are a bunch of vaguely threatening Nintendo-hungry teenagers. Theyre vital to the atmosphere and, as it turns out, to the story, but we never actually see them.

There is a fourth player whom the author cunningly holds in reserve, sending her out on the ice comparatively late in the game. This is Kim, a government archeologist, who has arrived from the big city to investigate a discovery of Jude's. He has found a human body, male, perfectly preserved in ice for what may be centuries.

Denver gets really attached to this Snowman, who also seems to take on a symbolic significance for everyone else in the play; he represents something they want or lack or resent. Kim adds to the emotional mix by developing the hots for Denver; indeed, she fancied him, just from his name, even before she met him. She is a past- mistress, however, at keeping an emotional distance.

Monologue seems a fitting mode of expression for all these separated souls, and the ones MacArthur has written vividly evoke both the physical and psychological landscape. In their last-gasp lyricism, the speeches feel very Canadian (and not just because of the climate) but they have a distinctive voice, sawn-off and weather- beaten. Denver's tone is especially compelling; hes an urban animal who is yet quite at home in the tundra, a born exile.

The narrative technique helpfully absolves the director from the necessity of actually staging some very tricky episodes in which frozen human limbs get tossed about, to macabre or farcical effect. But of course this avoidance of whats generally identified as drama becomes a problem. The characters may not be able to connect but we would like to see them try, rather than just hearing about it. The piece comes off as a dramatized novella rather than a play; and, short though it is, it becomes tedious.

David Oiye's production is lucid and sympathetic. David Fraser has designed a glistening set as well as lighting that's both sensitive and sensible. The acting is excellent, with Eric Goulems glowering, stoical Denver keeping the centre firm, and Paul Dunn (another talented actor-playwright, though presently visible in only one of his guises) radiantly quivering as Jude. As Marjorie, drab and dangerous, Philippa Domville effects a pitch-perfect transformation from the brittle, upscale ladies she usually plays; Marjorie is an ice-maiden too, of course, but Domville has found a new battered voice to go with the curlers and the housecoat make a lot of difference. Kim is more recognizably icy. Veronika Hurnik gets both the metropolitan sheen (by local standards, anyway) and the inner chill.

All the characters, in the famous Thoreau phrase, live lives of quiet desperation, and the play shows that just getting out of the rat race is no solution; even just below the Arctic, both life and despair go on. The play extends hope to its characters and then withdraws it, while leaving us in no doubt that they will survive. It isnt the most enlivening conclusion, and the play sputters out. But I have great hopes for the playwright.
