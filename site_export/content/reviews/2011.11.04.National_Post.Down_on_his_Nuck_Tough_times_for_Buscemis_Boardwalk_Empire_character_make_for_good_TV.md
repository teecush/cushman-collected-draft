Acorner was turned in the first season of Boardwalk Empire when we were introduced to Richard Harrow. Harrow is an ex-soldier who had half his face blown off in the First World War. He now wears a half-mask and is naturally shy about it, especially around women. He must also have lost half his vocal cords, and speaks in a gentle croak, which makes him, in Jack Huston's performance, very sympathetic.

We're now into the second season of the acclaimed HBO drama, with Richard the devoted henchman of Jimmy (Michael Pitt), the young man who would be boss of Atlantic City and who once, in Chicago, helped him to get laid. Two weeks ago Richard almost committed suicide - on Remembrance Day, poignantly enough - but, to every viewer's relief, was distracted when a dog ran off with his mask. Richard is also an expert sharpshooter, and in that same episode he reasserted his loyalty to Jimmy by tracking down a pompous big shot who had disrespected him and, wait for it, scalping him. In a way this was poetic justice since the victim (played incidentally by the distinguished Canadian actor Richard Easton) liked to boast about how he had helped massacre the Sioux.

Harrow's two faces are emblematic of Boardwalk Empire, a show that has improved immeasurably now that the grunt work of establishing the characters and filling us in on the Prohibition era has been completed. Most people in it are ambiguous, as all good dramatic characters are. Most complicated of all is Nucky Thompson, the elegantly corrupt city treasurer who, in all senses, runs the show.

Steve Buscemi, slight and precise, wasn't obvious casting for this more or less historical character, as was admitted from the outset by the series' creators; obvious casting, they said, would have been James Gandolfini, though we must be glad for everybody's sake, including Gandolfini's, that it didn't come to pass; the echoes of The Sopranos, in a show from the same creative stable, would have been too deafening.

Buscemi doesn't have Gandolfini's heft, or his pudgy intimations of a lingering vulnerability, and Nucky, perhaps because he's tied to his reallife origins, doesn't have the complexity of Tony, who remains the definitive TV antihero. But the producers also prophesied that by the end of the first season we would be unable to imagine anyone else but Buscemi in the role, and he's proved them right. In a way he's Tony in reverse. We were always surprised by Tony's better impulses; we're always shocked by Nucky's worst ones. Because of his wit and his intelligence, and what seems to be a genuine reluctance to hurt more people than is absolutely necessary, we forgive him a lot.

When we first met Nucky he was on top, which naturally means that his position has since been challenged at every turn. Last Sunday's episode found him facing three separate challenges. His plan for evading charges of electionrigging began to crumble; Jimmy made his strongest bid yet to take over Nucky's bootlegging empire; and, though Nucky doesn't know it yet, his Irish live-in love Margaret is beginning to let her thoughts stray in the direction of a young hit man from the same old country. She revealed this under seal of the confessional, a motif that does seem like a retread from The Sopranos; despite Kelly Macdonald's vibrant performance, her relationship with her priest hardly compares to Carmela's with Father Paul.

However the religious angle does connect with the character of Agent Van Alden (the riveting Michael Shannon), the fanatical prohibition agent undone by his own uptightness. He and Nucky, who hardly ever meet, are emerging as the show's opposite poles, and the most recent episode was built on the contrast. In a show marked by its pointed transitions we kept switching between them, with Van Alden impaled on a few dilemmas of his own.

There was the delirious subordinate whom he feared was about to reveal his guiltiest secret, and there was the secret herself: Lucy the exshowgirl, his one moral lapse who also happens to be part of Nucky's more colourful past. Last week, in one of the more wrenching scenes of a violent show, we practically watched her giving birth - all by herself, as she later proudly announced to Van Alden. He still can't catch a break; he'd planned, he says, to give the baby to his own childless wife who, when she arrived unexpectedly at the bedside, was not amused.

Paz de la Huerta's Lucy, a discouragingly whiny character in the first season, has become both interesting and moving. Confined for her pregnancy she was visited by the great Eddie Cantor (a fine imitation, incidentally), who told of a Broadway show that she yearned to try out for, though it hardly needed Van Alden to point out that her condition made this unlikely. (Her incredulity at his ignorance about the theatre, and especially of what a script might be, was among the show's most delectable moments.) The musical in question was called A Dangerous Maid, which, apart from being a neat handle for Lucy herself, is the title of an actual show, an early effort by the Gershwins. It closed out of town, which suggests that Lucy was well out of it. But it's a pleasing example of how thoroughly Boardwalk Empire does its homework.

This also shows, of course, in its parade of famous gangsters. We have vivid portrayals of Al Capone (typically humanized by having a disadvantaged son) and Lucky Luciano, both still underlings on the way up; and it's always a pleasure to meet Michael Stuhlbarg's Arnold Rothstein, who beats even Nucky in the fastidiousness department. Waxey Gordon has just put in an appearance, and Dutch Schultz can surely not be far behind.

One problem the show does face, common to all drama series with ambitions, is that of where it thinks it's going. They never know how long they have. The Wire is the only one to announce a five-year arc and then stick to it, with gratifying results. The Sopranos got extended, and distended. Deadwood, by contrast, is an example of a masterpiece cut off in its prime, leaving its devotees permanently frustrated. It's hard to tell whether Boardwalk Empire has a shape in mind. Its texture is rich enough for us to hope that it has. Nucky's historical prototype ended up in jail. Will time and the networks allow that to happen?
