All of the characters in The Importance of Being Earnest are convinced that they are right. They may sometimes be checked by circumstance, but as far as they're concerned that's circumstance's fault. They go straight on in pursuit of their aims, be those aims marriage or money or muffins. This universal single-mindedness is what makes Oscar Wilde's comedy a farce: a farce of manners, one of a kind. It seems to be an extremely difficult quality for contemporary actors, bred on hesitation and ambiguity, to encompass. That impression is strongly reinforced by the new production from Soulpepper, which is shockingly below this company's standards.

The play was first staged in 1895, which also turned out to be the year of Wilde's downfall. Ben Barnes, the director here, has set it in the 1920s. This enables Patricia Fagan, whose Gwendolen is much the best performance, to look very fetching in a cloche hat. Otherwise the updating is at best irrelevant and at worst ridiculous. And before you accuse me of being an old reactionary who only wants to see Shakespeare done in doublet and hose: Few of Shakespeare's plays are tied to a specific time and place, and none of them to his own; they were born anachronistic. Plays that depict solid, identifiable societies -- which includes the majority of English comedies -- can be successfully transported, but only if the new setting connects in some way with the old.

Wilde's play depends on its characters' acceptance of rigid social and sexual conventions; the younger ones may find small ways of bending the rules, but they are still unquestioning parts of the system. Just 30 years later, this had changed; a world war helped. Youth rebelled and didn't care who knew it; anything went. To see the difference dramatically, look at Wilde's plays and look at Noel Coward's, with their flirtatious flappers. Wilde's young ladies are predatory but they are also demure, and the contrast is a large part of the humour. Samantha Espie's Cecily looks as if she would fit very nicely into a production of Hay Fever; she is hopelessly at sea in Earnest.

In fact, arguing this production's conception is almost beside the point, since the execution is so sloppy. John Gielgud once said that Earnest has almost too many funny lines. Well, that isn't a problem here. Even the wretched Shaw Festival production a couple of years ago got more laughs than this one does. It's tone-deaf; or perhaps I should say rhythm-deaf. Actors take breaths in the middle of lines, or let their endings droop in the dust, or come down heavily on the wrong words, or address their remarks to the wrong people or to no people at all. The cumulative effect of this is to make the play all but meaningless.

It's a structure of language (another thing that differentiates it from other farces), and poised, ornate language at that (another thing that makes it look out of place in the '20s, when casualness was all). It isn't just a collection of detachable epigrams; that aspect of the play can degenerate into a kind of nervous tic, but there isn't nearly as much of it as people think. The ceaseless verbal paradoxes create a world, an absurd one in which a tale of imaginary invalids and of babies left in handbags at railway stations becomes perfectly logical, but also a confining one in which the attempts to subvert the proprieties only testify to their overriding power. It's the world by which Wilde himself was shortly to be destroyed.

Obviously there's more to a successful production of Earnest than just getting the words right. They have to be turned into modes of communication between the actors, and this production does at least seem to have that in mind, especially in the scenes between Kevin Bundy's Jack Worthing and Damien Atkins' Algernon. Bundy, who usually plays losers, is happiest in the moments of frustration, of which Jack has more than any other character (i.e. he's the hero). Assurance is more of a stretch cfor him, but he shows signs of getting there, and in a better-orchestrated production he might actually arrive. Atkins' willowy Algy has the right attitude, as a young man obsessed with finding an appropriate verbal and physical pose, but he's the worst gabbler of the bunch. Fagan's Gwendolen, wielding pertness and preciousness as erotic weapons, is absolutely on the button in the first act, and though she loses focus later on she is, definitely, her mother's ruthless daughter.

As for mama herself, Nancy Palk's Lady Bracknell has the blessed abil- ity to deliver a long sentence without choking on it, and her censoriousness is beyond reproach. But she can't link it to a sense of unquestioned and unquestioning privilege, though she brought the trick off very handily in last year's Olympia. Like everyone else in this show she sounds, at some level, apologetic. (And her costumes don't let her take full advantage of her height; they seem to be showing off, rather than showing her off.) Nor do the other veterans supply as much as one would hope. The attraction between Canon Chasuble and Miss Prism is virtually non-existent. Oliver Dennis's canon does supply a nice sense of pedantic self-satisfaction; Brenda Robins seems, to judge from her wig and her teeth, to have taken Lady B's description of Prism as "a female of repellent aspect, remotely connected with education" excessively to heart. This, I think, is unnecessarily self-denying of her.

David Storch plays both the valet and the butler, and it says something that the production's most joyful moment comes when he peels off his moustache to change from one servant into the other. Maybe Barnes wants to destroy the play. That would explain Peter Hartwell's set, which wishes the same drab wallpaper on two separate rooms and, most bizarrely, a garden. It also incorporates two long, sliding library ladders that people keep climbing whatever the locale, which I suppose may count as a unifying device. Other props include a handbag too small to hold either a baby or a three-volume novel, and a cake stand that would seem ungenerous in 1895, 1929 or, if you go to the right hotel, 2006.

Random bursts of stylization that fall short of actual style (a recurrent problem at Soulpepper) include having Cecily, who should be talking to Algernon at the time, pick up a tennis racquet to hit imaginary balls while sound effects check in from the wings, and having Jack conduct his final search for the handbag onstage rather than off, thereby littering the floor with debris over which the actors have to pick their way to take their curtain calls. This supersedes what would otherwise have been the coup de grace: having the entire cast join in Jack's concluding line about having discovered the vital importance of being earnest. It isn't about them; it's about him. For the 100th time: There's no point in making fun of a farce. It got there first. Not getting this play right may be a misfortune; it happens to a lot of people. Getting it so insistently wrong looks like carelessness. Or that's the most optimistic interpretation.

CORIOLANUS

Festival Theatre

Stratford - 1-800-567-1600

Colm Feore's Coriolanus is outstandingly lucid and intelligent, his classical chops in excellent shape. He has two separate body languages for a hero who combines intolerable arrogance with agonizing modesty. But the most powerful moment in Antoni Cimolino's production comes from Martha Henry, shaking uncontrollably after destroying the son with whom she has experienced a shared joy in mutual dependence. In rep through Sept. 23.

THE GLASS MENAGERIE

Avon Theatre

Stratford - 1-800-567-1600

In Miles Potter's production, this is the hardest-edged, least wispy memory-play imaginable. Something is lost, but what remains is hypnotically gripping, every moment charged. Seana McKenna's performance is startlingly devoid of nostalgia, but you never catch her playing against the text. Sara Topham is a moving Laura, Matthew MacFadzean a perfect Gentleman Caller, and Steven Sutcliffe a delicate and witty autobiographical stand-in for Tennessee Williams. In rep through Oct. 22.

LONDON ASSURANCE

Avon Theatre

Stratford - 1-800-567-1600

Veteran designer Desmond Heeley has done a bang-up job on the town and country settings of this vintage Victorian comedy. Brian Bedford as an ageing buck plays gorgeous variations on a theme of nose-wrinkling incredulity. As a director, though, he hasn't provided himself with much of a context; with a few notable exceptions (especially Brian Tree), the star's discipline hasn't percolated down to the rest of the cast. The play seems weak, though earlier productions have shown it isn't. In rep through Oct. 21.

THE DUCHESS

OF MALFI

Tom Patterson Theatre

Stratford - 1-800-567-1600

Certain scenes in Peter Hinton's production are so dark you can't see your severed hand in front of your face. It's a magnificently atmospheric realization of both the sights and sounds of John Webster's Jacobean masterpiece. The performances, apart from Scott Wentworth's malcontent murderer Bosola, are disappointing, but for once that doesn't matter. The production creates a world, and never lets go. In rep through Sept. 23.

OLIVER!

Festival Theatre

Stratford - 1-800-567-1600

Donna Feore directs a stage-filling production of Lionel Bart's epic crowd-pleaser of a musical, one that even manages in its opening scenes, and sometimes later, to sneak in some of the nightmare atmosphere of Dickens' original non-stop chronicle of crime and child abuse. Bart, whose genius was instinctive if short- lived, spoke showbiz like a native, resulting in a self-believing score in which even a rank bad song like As Long As He Needs Me sort of works. Colm Feore revels in Bart's comical Fagin; Tyler Pearse's Oliver is splendidly acted and sung; Bruce Dow and Mary Ellen Mahoney are perfectly matched workhouse tyrants. In rep through Oct. 29.

HENRY IV, PART ONE

Tom Patterson Theatre

Stratford - 1-800-567-1600

Richard Monette's production understands that the prose and verse parts of this great play are equally comic and equally serious. Adam O'Byrne's gangling impetuous Hotspur is an especial revelation, witty in himself and the cause of wit in others (to quote Part Two). Scott Wentworth's sonorous King is both calculating and devout, two faces sporting one guilty conscience. Falstaff and Hal are less exciting, but there are many vivid minor performances and some brilliant strokes of staging. In rep through Sept. 24.

ARMS AND THE MAN

Festival Theatre

Niagara-on-the-Lake

1-800-511-7429

Jackie Maxwell's production of Shaw's anti-romantic comedy is cute as a whole row of buttons; this has its charms but ends up selling the play short. Peter Hutt and Patrick Galligan give entertaining performances, but the exaggerated Balkan sets and costumes dominate the evening. In rep through Oct. 29.

THE HEIRESS

Royal George Theatre

Niagara-on-the-Lake

1-800-511-7429

Tara Rosling gives an extraordinary performance, rising to tragedy as Henry James' shy, plain heroine. Mike Shara, smooth and gauche at the same time, is the suitor who tantalizingly may not be faking it. Taut direction by Joseph Ziegler, superb design by Christina Poddubiuk.In rep through Oct. 7.

HIGH SOCIETY

Festival Theatre

Niagara-on-the-Lake

1-800-511-7429

The show of the movie musical of the play (that was also a movie). It never worked, and this busy production is particularly coarse. The augmented Cole Porter score is made up not of songs the characters just have to sing but of songs it's not quite impossible they might; it's dehydrating. Dan Chameroy and the sets are good. In rep through Nov. 19.
