How's this for a pedigree? The Black Rider, subtitled The Casting of the Magic Bullets and billed as a rock opera, is credited to Tom Waits, Robert Wilson andWilliamS. Burroughs. Their individual responsibilities are not disclosed, but the natural assumption is that Waits wrote the songs, Burroughs wrote the dialogue and Wilson, America's all-purpose theatrical visionary, came up with the concept and the original staging. The first English-language production was mounted in Edmonton in 1998 by a group called November Theatre, who have been touring and refining it ever since. It's their version, in what is announced as its final form, that has now been brought to Toronto.

I thought it 10% extraordinary and 90% tedious going on unbearable. To deal with the worst first: The piece derives (yet more pedigree) from a German folk tale that previously inspired, among other things, the non-rock opera Der Freischutz. It tells the story of a servant whose prowess as a marksman (he aimed at a stag to which was tied a wounded man, successfully shooting the stag while sparing the man) won him the title of Royal Huntsman. This post was passed through his descendants, until a daughter threatened to disgrace the family name by marrying a clerk; to prove himself a worthy shot, her beloved made, in effect, a deal with the devil, purchasing magic bullets with a fatal clause in the agreement.

This potentially haunting tale is obscured in the telling, partly by being narrated out of chronological order, and partly by being festooned with song, dance and movement that is sometimes effective and is sometimes just full of itself. The whole show is bent on proving how sinister it is. It comes with a frame, and not a terribly original one: The story is presented to us by a travelling fairground troupe so menacingly grotesque in themselves that their subject matter has a hard time measuring up. It's a freak show before it even gets started. Once under way, it takes a strenuously pseudo-Gothic view of people and events that is sometimes funny and more often merely mean.

Wilson is a venerated prophet of non-verbal theatre, so it's ironic that his ideas and his creations generally sound more interesting when he or someone else is describing them -- in words, of course -- than when they're actually experienced. Nothing gets more monotonous more quickly than the theatre of image, especially when the images are slow to change. The Black Rider, inescapably linear even if the lines are tangled, is far from being an extreme example of the genre; it has a lot of words, but they aren't all clear. Many of them, of course, occur in the songs. Waits has provided a jangling, commentative score that seems to take in everything except rock: There's operetta, folk and even some jaunty early jazz. The lyrics are foggy, doing little to further the action and not much to explain it. In short, they aren't very dramatic; which is disappointing since Waits, when he isn't writing for the theatre, is one of the few contemporary songwriters who might be able to. The show is all affekt (the German term seems appropriate), a matter of striking attitudes at the expense of narrative. Kevin Corey does have a nerd-ish appeal as the clerk, but in general you don't care enough about the characters even to wish them ill.

Now for the good stuff. Some of the attitudes, under Ron Jenkins' direction, are very impressive. Marie Nychka, the choreographer, has staged a wild ball. Much of the singing is muggy, but Michael Scholar Jr., as the diabolic tempter named Peg Leg, cuts through the haze with a ringing solo that warms up the theatre while chilling the blood. Rachael Johnston is marvellous as the heroine, especially when sharing a duet with her intended, he hiding under her skirts, she -- we have to assume -- standing on his shoulders. (This makes hers a literally towering performance.) Finally, within sight of the end, there's a slow-motion death-fall, hilarious and horrific at once, that comes as close as anything I've seen to making manifest the inexorable. It should invade the dreams of everyone who sees it. - The Black Rider runs until Nov. 16. Call 416-531-1827 for ticket and information.
