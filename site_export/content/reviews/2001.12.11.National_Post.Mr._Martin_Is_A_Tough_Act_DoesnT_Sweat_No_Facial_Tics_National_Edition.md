Robert Cushman is a theatre critic for the National Post

THE PAUL MARTIN BUDGET SHOW

As a performer, Paul Martin is a critic's nightmare, because he doesn't have any mannerisms. He has no facial tics, he doesn't wave his arms in public, and though, in the course of his 65-minute budget speech he took the occasional sip of water, he never once mopped his brow. He also reads a script lucidly; unlike, say, the average radio newsreader in this illiterate age, he never emphasizes the wrong word in a sentence. He did read very fast -- too fast and too unemotionally for his words ever to pass as normal conversation - - but then he had a lot to cram in.

Confidence is obviously one of the things they teach you in politicians' school, but it must take colossal nerve to get up as Finance Minister in what everybody knows is a recession, and still act as if the good times are here. Well, maybe there were a few problems but nothing that Canadians couldn't lick. On and on the promises and predictions rolled, with never a hint that some of them might just fail to come true.

Now, considered simply as entertainment, budget speeches are boring. But history this year gave Mr. Martin something to talk about besides money. The first half of his speech was devoted to the "human tragedy" and its aftermath. I'm not sure that there's such a thing as an inhuman tragedy and the briskness of Martin's delivery told against him anyway. It was just another phrase, and not a very inspired one.

Anyway, tragedy as a concept is not a vote-catcher, so Mr. Martin presented recent events not as a disaster but as an opportunity.

It was an opportunity to feel good about our boys fighting on distant shores. It was an opportunity to strengthen our bonds with the United States -- and here. Canada's economy was inherently strong, and $23-billion were going into health care, there were going to be more tax cuts and no deficit. All of which was very good news, and you could hardly blame the minister's supporters for shouting "bravo" when they heard it. I, however, being professionally cursed with the tragic sense, felt the opposition parties were falling down on the job. Obviously they didn't want to spoil Mr Martin's big day but were there no skeptics, left or right, who felt moved to shout out "how?" This show lacked conflict.

In fact I heard no angry murmurs from the floor until Mr. Martin announced his plan for a $12 security charge to be added to every airfare; that really got them going. It came at the end of what may have been the emotional centre of the whole speech: the strategy for airplane security. Here I felt Mr Martin blew his top too early; the wildest cheers from his own side came on his first proposal, the one about having armed undercover cops on Canadian aircraft. If this was his strongest point, he should surely have saved it for the big finish. But big finishes are not perhaps his strong suit. He ended a paragraph on terrorists by saying, with machine-gun insistence, that we had to find out "who-they-are-and-what-they-plan-to-do." It sounded obvious.

Still, you can never be sure what will get the crowd going. A rallying cry about "education and innovation" (it even rhymes) woke only tepid applause. On the other hand, the phrase "facilitate cross- border trade in all parts of the country", brought the house down. As the act moved into its second half -- becoming a laundry-list like ordinary Budget speeches -- the mechanics of applause became more evident.

Then, when the clapping became frenzied, he got louder and more emphatic, and that excited them and they clapped even harder. At the end he even permitted himself the occasional gesture: the raising of a hand, the merest hint of an outstretched arm.

But he never followed through. As befitted a finance minister, restraint ruled.
