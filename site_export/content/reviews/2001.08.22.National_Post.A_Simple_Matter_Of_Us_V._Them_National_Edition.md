THE RETURN OF THE PRODIGAL

Court House Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont.

This years Shaw Festival has been a downbeat affair, with the best productions -- Picnic, Laura, Love from a Stranger -- lavished on decidedly minor plays. The last production of the season, though, redeems nearly everything: The aptly named Return of the Prodigal introduces us to a brilliant and almost-forgotten playwright in a performance that shimmers, provokes and finally hits greatness.

St. John Hankin, the author, was a contemporary and colleague of Shaw and Granville Barker, and this play, an icily powerful social comedy, was performed alongside theirs in the advanced London theatre of 1905.

Set in the English county of Gloucestershire, a pleasant sort of buffer state between the West country and the Midlands, it concerns the Jacksons, a nouveau-riche family whose head is a self-made mill owner about to make still more of himself by standing for Parliament in the Conservative interest. His elder son, Henry, is also his business partner. Mrs. Jackson and daughter Violet sit and sew and receive company and generally acquiesce. The visitors are mostly drawn from the local gentry, who despise the Jacksons for being in trade but are prepared to overlook it for the sake of their money. Lady Faringford, the neighbourhood she-dragon, hopes to marry her daughter to Henry and is impatient with the girl's slowness at getting a proposal out of him.

The Jacksons have another son, Eustace, a ne'er-do-well who, after failing in an impressive variety of careers at home, was packed off in time-honoured fashion to Australia, with (ps)1,000 on which to make something of himself. He failed there, too: As he points out -- he can certainly talk with engaging fluency if he can do nothing else -- failure or success in one place will usually be duplicated in another.

He comes home and carefully collapses on the family threshold, feigning illness and exhaustion. Once the first flush of concern has passed, his father and brother start wondering what to do with him. He has an answer: They can support him, in return for his continued absence, since his continued presence would embarrass them and ruin their political prospects.

They do not, of course, take kindly to this blackmail; though perhaps even more disturbing to them is the philosophy with which Eustace backs it up. It is not, he points out, his fault that he was born without energy or willpower; given the choice, he would far rather be rich than poor. And even leaving the money out of it, success feels a lot nicer than failure. By what right do the successful punish the unsuccessful for the crime of getting less out of life than they do? Radical stuff then, this may hit even harder now. It strikes at the root of just about everything. It is the more powerful for being couched in a tone of insouciant irony -- the real thing, not our sickly modern counterfeit -- that never once raises its voice or lowers its guard. The play does at one point raise the spectre of a love interest, but Eustace and his author proceed to trample it blithely underfoot.

The values that Eustace observes under his parents roof are rampant, too, of course, in the world outside. Lady Faringford begins the play by envying Mrs. Jackson her new electric light because "lamps are so troublesome -- the servants are always setting themselves on fire with them -- and she maintains that same compassionate social tone throughout. She tells of a tenant who died of pneumonia through living in a damp cottage (so ridiculous ... as if all cottages were not damp") and of his widow who was promptly evicted for non-payment of rent, both events arousing the unaccountable indignation of Mr. Jacksons electoral opponent.

Eustace grasps the dilemma immediately and can see its consequences for those unfortunates who don't even have his advantages of a quick tongue and a rich father: "The strong must have the good cottages in order that they may grow stronger. And the weak must have the bad cottages in order that they may die off. Survival of the fittest, you know. Sounds quite topical, doesn't it?

The characters divide then, into the Jacksons and the rest, and the rest are the harder to bring off. So at least it seems in Christopher Newtons production, in which the minor nobility and their hangers-on are played as caricatures -- an effect that is especially disorienting in the early scenes, when we are still trying to sort out who everybody is.

Lady Faringford, as you may have observed, bears in her merciless snobbishness a marked resemblance to Oscar Wildes Lady Bracknell. The difference is that this lady's epigrams have perceptible consequences in the real world -- and that people occasionally answer her back. If Lady Bracknell were to say, I don't like this pernicious modern jargon about shopkeepers and gentlefolk being much the same -- theres far too much truth in it to be agreeable," the action would simply roll on to the next joke. When Lady Faringford says it, her daughter (prettily played by Susie Burnett) takes her up on it, and her mama has candidly to explain that social distinctions have to be ruthlessly maintained because without them people like themselves -- they have, after all, no real money -- would starve. Lady B knows these things but blots them out; Lady F admits them but carries on regardless. This makes her a fascinating study in selfish single-mindedness, but it imposes considerable strain on the actress, and Brigitte Robinson bends beneath it. Lacking both social and intellectual style, she seems to be dissociating herself from the character, as if fearful that she might be suspected of holding such horrible views herself.

Still, it is the family who are the heart of the play and certainly of this production. All five of them act superbly -- and interact even better. Ben Carlsons Eustace, stretched out in a hammock, with a straw boater perched rakishly on his outstretched foot, is a joy, perfect in both his impudence and his self-hatred. Patricia Hamilton, once she has disentangled herself from her guests, brings Mrs. Jackson to full life as a kind, unheeding woman, tirelessly capable of believing the best. Kelli Fox, having sat meekly on the sidelines for three acts, takes searing advantage of the fourth, in which she tells how much more hopeless, even than Eustace's, her prospects are.

The action finally resolves into a triangular contest between father and sons; their final scene, a battle in which ideas and emotions run on parallel lines, sends one of those new-fangled electric currents coursing through the theatre. Bernard Behrens is Jackson pere, trying to stand on his dignity but uncertain where it lies, his peppery temper compromised by the knowledge that he ought to feel affection for his black-sheep son even if he doesn't.

And nothing is finer than the relationship between Carlsons Eustace and Blair Williams smug, clever Henry; they don't like one another, they don't trust one another, but at some deep fraternal level they understand one another, and the knowledge brings them into occasional surprised alliance.

The Jackson house, as economically designed by William Schmuck, is creamily elegant, with beautifully etched rolling-hill vistas in the background.

Politically and psychologically, Hankins play seems to cut deeper than most of Shaws, though maybe its unfamiliarity has something to do with that. It unfolds with an undeviating logic, but spares us the accustomed Shavian sensation that the author knows all the answers and that all we have to do to be saved is listen. Hankin just keeps asking the questions; that's a cliche of a critic's compliment, I know, but this time it's true.

He himself had only one answer: There is much talk of suicide in the play, with drowning invariably the chosen method, and that is how Hankin, at the age of 39, ended his own life, a major loss to English drama. (He is the second drowned dramatist -- Frank Vosper, of Love from a Stranger, was the first -- to be premiered at Niagara in two days. If this is policy, I don't think it has a future.)

Hankin wrote at least two other plays -- The Cassilis Engagement and The Charity That Began at Home -- that are worth reviving, and one hopes that the Shaw may be embarking on a Hankin cycle to match its tour through the works of Granville Barker. If that leads to Christopher Newton returning as a guest director after he vacates the festival hot seat next year, then so much the happier.
