DETECTIVE STORY

Festival Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont.

- - -

NIAGARA-ON-THE-LAKE, Ont. - This is the Shaw Festival at its very best: the stylish reclamation of an imperfect but exciting play by a stageful of actors with skill and humanity pouring out of their ears. Sidney Kingsley's Detective Story, premiered in New York in 1949, is one of the last of the American verismo dramas that peaked in the '20s with The Front Page, and only died out when large-cast plays on Broadway became commercially impossible. Plays like these need to be crammed to bursting point with people; they depict buzzing workplaces that were thought to be seedily glamorous even in their own time, and now have period flavour going for them as well. This is the energy of life one step removed from the streets, transmuted into the energy of the theatre.

Detective Story depicts four hours (theatrically telescoped into three) in a Manhattan police station. Cops, suspects and witnesses come and go in profusion, but basically four cases are unfolding, ranging from a petty shoplifting to a death on an abortionist's table.

At the centre of the web sits Jim McLeod, a proudly incorruptible detective whose virtue is in danger of becoming its own disease. He has seen those he considers guilty go free, too many times, and is at least theoretically capable of taking the law into his own hands. One of his cases is a young man, a war hero for whatever that's worth four years on, who has stolen from his boss's till so as to impress his girl; he is transparently unlikely to reoffend, and even his wronged employer seems willing to drop the charge, but McLeod refuses to co-operate. The abortion case enrages him even more, and when the fake doctor seems about to cheat justice, McLeod beats him up. It then transpires that McLeod's loved wife -- the only softening influence in his life -- once had dealings with the man, and the revelation sends Jim, who is as Pentecostal about sex as about everything else, even further over the edge. Just about everyone in the cast -- his partner, the lieutenant who dislikes him but values his integrity, a cop-worshipping reporter who confesses that he'd be hanging around the station even if he didn't have the job, a velvet-tongued and possibly venal attorney -- lectures him on the dangers of his intransigence; and still he goes on.

They're right, of course; a cop who holds himself above the law is the devil, and nothing that McLeod says about the holes in the system is likely to make us feel any different. To that extent, the play is as unbalanced as its protagonist, if in the opposite direction. Nor, despite its air of outspokenness (and for a Broadway play of its time, it does talk very freely about abortion and, briefly, homosexuality), does it grapple seriously with the issues it raises. It never, for example, suggests that one answer to the scandal of illegal abortions might be to change the law. Kingsley's characters talk tough (another of his plays, Dead End, improbably provided a launching pad, via its movie version, to those B-feature comic favourites, the Bowery Boys) but his fictitious 21st Precinct seems remarkably clean; not one of its policemen is shown to be on the take. McLeod, you feel, has no kick coming about the company he keeps.

He isn't big enough to make a tragic hero; the reasons we're given for his behaviour -- chiefly, his revulsion at his father's abuse of his mother -- are Broadway-glib. In fact, his scenes may be the weakest in the play; as often in this kind of genre piece -- even The Front Page, which draws on farce and melodrama to create something that transcends both -- the milieu is more compelling than the story. The ingredients have changed -- there isn't much farce now, just the occasional hard-bitten laugh. There is still melodrama, and it's expertly thrilling -- but what continues to hold us is the sight of men at work, under a pressure that's second nature to them, and the vast spectacle of human behaviour. It's at least the illusion of realism; and apparently the original production was praised for its authenticity, by critics who had mostly been crime reporters themselves. (These days, we don't get the training.)

And here, Neil Munro's production displays a cunning that amounts to brilliance. It places action and performance of absolute precision within a ghostly surround. The furnishings on Cameron Porteous's set are of weather-beaten solidity but the walls are outline sketches; you'd call them blueprints except that they're in black and white. There are mouth-watering snatches of orchestral jazz -- Ellington or Miles Davis -- to introduce each act, but very sparing use of music thereafter.

The atmospherics (like those in Munro's superb production of The Petrified Forest a few seasons ago) are their own reward; they also promote style and discipline in the acting. Everybody's performance here is full to the brim, but nobody over-emotes; which is remarkable. McLeod's scenes, in particular, could lend themselves to Oscar-baiting emoting (there was a movie, and they probably did); Peter Krantz's performance is a triumph of buttoned-down restraint, a tour de force in reverse, and it's matched by Jane Perry's in the even more perilous role of the wife. Jeff Meadows as the embarrassed embezzler and Fiona Byrne as his hometown sweetheart play an equally perilous duet with comparable tact, though with a few more false notes.

There is a magical moment, halfway through the play, when William Vickers as the reporter, momentarily forgotten at the side of the stage, bursts back into our consciousness like a one-man Greek chorus, adjuring McLeod to "humble yourself, sweetheart, humble yourself." Presumably Jewish (McLeod calls him "Yossel," he calls McLeod "Seamus," which could be racial or professional or both), he moves through the play with sad, chirpy dignity, looking, with a piece of paper stuck in his hatband, like the Mad Hatter.

I'm tempted to call this the best of the supporting performances, but I could equally well say it of Neil Barclay's lawyer, as orotund as rotund, and developing a surprising moral weight to match his physical; or of Jim Mezon, grey and authoritative as the lieutenant. Really, the only way to do justice to this superbly orchestrated production is to put the whole cast in the lineup: Sarah Orenstein's shoplifter-for-a-day, who says "emotions are bad for me" and who rolls her eyes in shock when the personable young cop she has ruled out for being married is heard flirting with a client less particular; Robert Benson's paternal detective and Norman Browning's impassive one; Lorne Kennedy's silky abortionist; George Dawson's especially slimy crook; Simon Bradbury's visiting gangster from Sopranos territory; Al Kozlik's dubious philanthropist; Goldie Semple's fur-laden false witness; Jennifer Phipps, whose deranged complainant, reporting the atom bombs being manufactured in the next apartment, sets the tone for a day in the life; right down to Richard Farrell and Jillian Cook as a prosperous pair of robbery victims, barely visible but still -- like everyone else on stage -- alive and fully breathing.
