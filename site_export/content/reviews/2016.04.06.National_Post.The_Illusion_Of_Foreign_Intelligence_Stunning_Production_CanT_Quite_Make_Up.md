You could hardly wish for a more exciting opening scene than the one that kicks off Chimerica, by the British playwright Lucy Kirkwood. It's 1989, and we are in a hotel room in Beijing, overlooking Tiananmen Square. It's the last gasp of the protests, crushed by the Chinese government troops. In the room is Joe Schofield, a rookie American photojournalist on his first major assignment. His editor is on the line from New York, beseeching him to get the hell out, and hes about to, but his attention is caught by a scene below: a man carrying shopping-bags standing in front of an oncoming tank, which doesnt mow him down. Joe takes the famous Tank Man photo. And then the police burst in.

Presumably, Joe (a fictional character) escapes unharmed and unbanned, since we now flash forward twenty-three years to find him flying back to Beijing on another story. The play will continue to alternate between past and present, and also between China and America: hence its portmanteau title, which also invokes the word "chimera," meaning illusion.

Chris Abraham's production - by Canadian Stage out of the Manitoba Theatre Centre - follows its thrilling prologue with equally resourceful handling of the constant, hectic transitions; Judith Bowden's rotating set, Deco Dawsons video design, Michael Waltons lighting and Thomas Ryder Payne's sound are all top-notch. They work seamlessly together, to create the impression that something really momentous is happening. In the broadest strokes, something is. The play contrasts a China, making huge economic strides while remaining repressive, with an America - well, I'm not sure what its saying about America, other that its in a state of uncertainty while being in economic thrall to a country it used to despise. The U.S. scenes all take place in the election year of 2012.

However, it's in human detail that the play falls apart. The story hinges on the friendship between Joe and his Chinese contact Zhang Lin, who as a student was one of the Tiananmen rebels and is now an English teacher, living alone and widowed; his wife was one of those massacred and her memory, physically evoked, still haunts him. Lin leads Joe to believe that the Tank Man, to whose image Joe owes his professional success, may be alive and possibly well and probably living in New York. This in turn leads to a succession of the kind of scenes in which a self-anointed sleuth goes from one seedy location to another, asking questions, proffering cash, and being sent somewhere else.

These scenes, and the ones in newspaper offices, are presented in the selfconscious tough-guy mode common to plays about journalists, especially when a British writer is trying to sound American. Joe himself is a puppet, there to push the story along; Evan Buliung does this with commendable urgency, adding a nervous sensitivity beyond what's furnished in the writing. Joe doesnt really have a character; he has issues, but these feel pasted on - hes egotistical, doesn't want children, but does want an English marketing consultant, who seems to be there because this is a play and there has to be a love-interest. Laura Condolln plays this functional character with skilled conscientiousness, and makes the most of the play's best American scene, in which she lets down her professional guard and warns her corporate clients against reducing the population of China to power-point stereotypes.

The scenes in China support her case; they can be repetitive, but theyre subtler and more affecting than the western ones. Zhang Lin has sunk into post-activist torpor, but he rouses himself to post a quickly-deleted email about Beijings levels of smog. It's enough; he's hauled in for correction and confession. Paul Sun-Hyung Lees performance is alight with mournful irony; his sad eyes, as he remembers and imagines his lost wife, are a spectacle to rival any of the productions technical wonders. All this performance has in common with the one he gave in Kim's Convenience is its sheer presence. He has a fine foil here in Richard Lee as Lin's practical businessman brother Zhang Wei, who has made philosophical peace with the regime and wonders why Lin cannot do the same.

Otherwise, the cliches in the writing infect the supporting performances, of which several are mediocre and a couple downright dreadful. The play does come together, somewhat, in its last scene. Joe, semipenitent after ruining several lives, is having an exhibition of his work. Among the visitors is Zhang Weis son Benny (Norman Yeung), a Harvard student with no desire to return home. He seems to be getting the best of both worlds, or maybe the worst. He brings a message from his uncle Lin, one bearing a revelation about the Tank Man, whose picture is, of course, the exhibitions centrepiece. It's the second revelation we've heard; the first was a legitimate shocker, genuinely thoughtprovoking. This new one is very neat, and momentarily effective. And then you start wondering why Zhang Lin kept it a secret all this time and realize its just because if he hadnt, there wouldn't be a play.

Chimerica runs until April 17 at the Bluma Appel Theatre in Toronto.
