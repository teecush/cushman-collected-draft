Harold Pinter's Nobel Prize for literature is only the third time in the past 40 years that the award has gone to a playwright. The Italian Dario Fo won in 1997, and then you have to go back to 1969, when the recipient was Pinters own hero, Samuel Beckett, who in any case is nearly as important for his novels. Its cheering, just as a matter of genre chauvinism, that drama has been invited back to the grown-ups' table. And it's justified because Pinter, like Beckett, occupies a theatrical space that is wholly his own. If ever the phrase "legend in his own lifetime" were justified, its here.

It happened, too, amazingly early in that lifetime. He was 28 when The Birthday Party was pretty much mocked off the stage, inspiring all the same a groundswell of support that ensured a responsive hearing for The Caretaker two years later. That play (which, most fortuitously, Soulpepper is reviving next year as part of its inaugural season in the Distillerys Young Centre) had a cultural impact still unmatched by any subsequent English play. Suddenly there was this thing called Pinter: tense, witty, enigmatic and amazingly confident in its placing of words and moods and actions and people. And so it has continued.

Scenically, his plays divide into the rough (everything up to The Homecoming in 1964) and the smooth (everything since). The former, infused with the idioms and the terrors of his London Jewish boyhood, are the more obviously resonant, but the latter find a comparable music, clipped rather than flowing, in the theoretically petrified rhythms of the English middle class. However cloaked the emotions, the language is pellucid and the jokes among the funniest in modern drama. And he has a gift denied to most of his contemporaries and successors; he creates great characters. A wise director once suggested that Davies, the tramp in The Caretaker, may be the last role created by a British playwright that future actors will yearn to play, though on the strength of William Hutt's Toronto performance, I'd be inclined to add Spooner, the poetaster of No Mans Land. Pinters plays keep passing the revival test; if they aren't classics, nobody's are.

- - -

One of Pinters more unfortunate, but short-lived, effects was the creation by other writers of mechanical "comedies of menace" written in imitation Cockney. Far more important has been the arrival in his wake of a whole generation of seriously comic playwrights, boulevardiers with muscle. One of the best of them is Alan Bennett, whose Habeas Corpus, a big success in London in 1973, has just received its Toronto premiere at CanStage. There are two mysteries about this. One is why its taken so long. The other is why it's happening now. It looks and sounds a long way from home.

Habeas Corpus is a comedy from the '70s thats set in the 60s, though from this distance of time and space the distinction may be difficult to discern. The scene is Hove, an especially respectable adjunct to the seaside resort of Brighton, which itself has always seemed respectable enough. Beneath the prim exteriors of course, lewd passions can rage, and they do here.

Our central character is Dr. Arthur Wicksteed, a physician with a consuming non-professional interest in women's bodies, notably excepting that of his wife, Muriel. She, long weary of being ignored, thinks longingly of a diminutive former admirer, now president of the British Medical Association and, naturally, in town for a meeting. The Wicksteeds have a spotty and miserable son whose name, Dennis, his father can never remember, this being one of the play's less happy running gags. There is also the doctors unfulfilled sister, Constance, who is persuaded that her problems would be solved if she had bigger breasts, and who sends away for a pair of false ones. These duly arrive, as shortly afterward does a man whose job it is to fit them. He encounters the wrong lady, and she, with pleasing symmetry, is run into by the wrong gentleman. This leads to double-entendres and the promise of worse.

Meanwhile -- there has to be a meanwhile -- the Wicksteeds, father and son, each have an assignation with the same visiting sex bomb; of the two she prefers Dennis, since she believes he is mortally ill and will therefore be the ideal official father for the baby she happens to be carrying. And further meanwhile there is the local vicar, a bucktoothed clown in whom lustful fantasies cohabit with a virtuous desire for Constance, who doesnt want to be virtuously desired, and there is the unmarried mother-to-be's own mother, a battleaxe recently returned from the colonies and shocked by everything she sees.

If all this sounds farcical, that's because it is. Bennett himself apparently calls it a farce without doors. But hes only the author; what would he know? The fact is that the entire first act depends on an assortment of characters all with nefarious and conflicting designs on the same small piece of living space, and this space had better be demarcated by something, even if it isnt doors. Otherwise the play isn't funny, and if it isn't that, it cant aspire to be anything else, such as deep. Morris Panychs production is unrelentingly open-plan, with no concessions to domestic or any other kind of realism. Ken MacDonald's set is a gleamingly clinical framework, forbiddingly adorned with X-rays, including one of a foot in a high-heeled shoe.

One gets the point. Humankind is a bag of bones, destined for the grave, and entitled to as much sensual gratification as possible before it arrives there. "This," say at least four people in the course of the evening, in varying shades of delight and dismay, "must be what they mean by the Permissive Society." There's a nostalgic phrase for you. The whole piece is nostalgic, in that it keeps echoing what other British playwrights of the time had already done better, notably the late Joe Orton, whose What the Butler Saw beats Bennett hollow in using brilliant farce to present a satiric indictment of British morality. Orton, however, had a throughgoing heartlessness that few could equal (though many have tried, including Pinter). Bennett is nicer and sadder, but though this play seems to celebrate and mourn in about equal measure, its final mood is one of oddly spinsterish distaste.

The actual dialogue jokes are less than Bennetts best, and anyway, good or indifferent, they involve references and idioms that current Canadian actors and audiences are likely to find foreign. Fiona Reid, who can time any line to its best advantage, is the most at home as Muriel; and Joseph Ziegler, playing his second cynical doctor in a row, is a bearded elf, caught between a twinkle and a leer. The most emphatic treat, though, is Melody A. Johnsons Constance, as demanding as she is dolorous until her moment of expansion. As her clerical admirer, David Storch is upstaged by his own set of falsies (the dental ones); he is funnier in his other, quieter role as an aspiring suicide whose efforts nobody notices. Cyrus Lane does all right as Dennis, and so does Tara Rosling as the vamp, though she has to work at it. The others are at sea; Astrid Van Wieren seems to be channelling a character, a premature Maggie Thatcher, whom she knows must exist but can't quite reach. Sheila McCarthy, as an omniscient choral housekeeper, has a more sophisticated version of the same problem -- she's constantly on the edge of the right style. But she's saddled with an annoying and unnecessary role: Why should we need someone to tell us this is a cartoon?
