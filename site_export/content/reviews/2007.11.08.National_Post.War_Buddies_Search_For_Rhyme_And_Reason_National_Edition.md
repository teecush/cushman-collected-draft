THEATRE REVIEW

NO GOOD REASON

Young Centre for the Performing Arts

---

There are two characters in Stephen Baetz's No Good Reason, both of them shell-shocked soldiers from the First World War. We meet them on the terrace of a military hospital in Kent that is dedicated to the recuperation of only the Canadian troops. One of them, Will, is an officer who is compulsively re-living the horrors of active service. In the first couple of brief scenes, we watch him hurl himself, wordlessly and alarmingly, around the stage, while his companion Sam, who is wheelchair-bound but seems better adjusted, watches with concern.

The play proceeds on the premise that a play about the horrors of one war will automatically get us thinking about those of all wars. To an extent that's true, but those thoughts aren't going to be either deep or disturbing unless the play itself is. We need to care about the characters in their own right before we can make any useful comparisons.

These two boys never come to life. The script keeps hopping from one aspect of their situation to another, never alighting long enough to illuminate anything. They act as mutual
therapists:Will gets Sam out of his chair, Sam jollies Will out of some of his terrors. There are bits about their girls back
home: Will has a fiancee whom, in his shattered state, he's afraid to contact; Sam speaks of a love who is plainly a fantasy. There are flashbacks to the traumas of the trenches. There are references to a tyrannical doctor who's trying to make Will forget when Sam knows that his only hope is to remember. It all gets to seem like filler. Even the characters' doubts about what it was all for -- the aspect that's meant to remind us of Iraq and Afghanistan-- are perfunctory.

The familiar "you be your girlfriend and I'll be you" routine (nothing physical; it's about help in writing a letter home) yields a couple of lines that are funny in a better sense. It turns out that one of the pair may not even be Canadian. This crops up late in the play, but for some reason the outcome is given away in the publicity.

There's a well-staged fight, but for the most part Leslie O'Dell's production can make the play nothing but dull. Christopher Stanton, once past his early contortions, gives a decent account of Will, but Mark McGrinder's Sam is done in by a continuous vocal smirk, which is intended to convey ambiguous cheerfulness; his attempts at bolstering his companion's morale sound positively nannyish. Perhaps, if a playwright wants to get us thinking about modern warfare, he should choose a different conflict. It's true that 1914-18 was the entrance to the chamber of horrors in which we still live, but dramatically and otherwise it's been done -- to use a rather too appropriate phrase -- to death. There have been innumerable literary and dramatic treatments of this war, and it's hard to add anything new. At any rate, it hasn't happened here. It's cruel, but true, that the best comment on this play is its title. - Performances run through Nov. 10. For tickets, call 416-866-8666.
