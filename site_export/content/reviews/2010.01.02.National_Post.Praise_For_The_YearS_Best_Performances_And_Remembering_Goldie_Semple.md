Goldie Semple, whose death darkened the end of the year, went out on a high professional note. In Brief Encounters, the first of the Shaw Festival's trio of Noel Coward triple bills, she unveiled her own triptych of comic vignettes, culminating in a gloriously unbuttoned drunk in Hands across the Sea. Ive been asking some of her peers about her. Lucy Peacock, who names Semple as a mentor (they joined the main Stratford company together in 1987, though Semple was eight years the older) confirmed that she loved being wicked on stage ... she had a sparkle and a wit," and says that her Cleopatra, which I never saw, was "fantastic. ...I remember her in this huge play on this seemingly huge stage, and absolutely filling it -- the complexity she had and the full-stage commitment.

Complexity," expressed through grace and wit, is what distinguishes the two Semple performances, both at Shaw, that I most treasure: her uneasily retired actress in The Royal Family and her uneasily scheming mother in The Cassilis Engagement, both triumphant explorations of mixed motives. In the latter play, she was a fastidious society woman descending to unimaginable depths to prevent her son from making a declasse marriage; snobbery, shame, and ruthless determination performed an intricate dance through the evening. In The Royal Family, she had a matchless comic scene on the telephone, her words proclaiming her intention to give up stardom for domesticity, her body language revealing, hilariously, the precise opposite. Martha Henry, who directed that production, says, "You couldnt shirk in the same rehearsal hall as Goldie. She wanted to be very clear about exactly what I was asking of her -- and why -- but once you explained what you wanted, she bent over backwards to give it to you, and always went further than you could ever have imagined. She would take a piece of direction and (sometimes, literally) fly with it. Her immense sense of humour and fun always caught me by surprise-- you never really expected this beautiful, glamorous woman to be so wildly funny.

There were times, speaking for myself, when her star quality seemed to be operating at some distance from the character she was playing (Coward's Easy Virtue) or when (as with the pile-driving protagonist of The Matchmaker) she was simply miscast. Against that, I recall her being very moving as the bereaved Constance in a Stratford King John, and both moving and enchanting as Desiree in last year's Shaw production of A Little Night Music, in which her singing of Send in the Clowns seems, of course, even more touching in retrospect than it did at the time. Jennifer Phipps, who acted with her at both festivals, recalls how, when she herself was suffering from damaged hips that were cutting me off from the rest of the world, Goldie brought the community back to me. ... She was a very good community person" -- a description echoed, almost exactly, by everyone I spoke to -- and applied to her life both inside and outside the theatre, where her kindness and concern were legendary. All, too, were unanimous that, having fought cancer for years, she seemed indestructible, making her death an even greater shock.

Two of the actresses who talked to me about Semple were involved in the theatrical event of the departed year that touched me most deeply, Stratfords Three Sisters, directed by Henry and with Peacock heading a superbly unified ensemble. Continuing further, a brief catalogue of some of the years other outstanding acting. I named Geraint Wyn Davies' Bottom last week as the year's best Shakespeare; hed already been a fine Duncan and Julius Caesar, a set of performances that make his return to Canadian theatre the most satisfying of recent comebacks. Runner-up Shakespearean was Gord Rands abbreviated Hamlet; this unclassifiable actor was also good in Goodness. The Hamlet production had many provocative things, not least Eric Peterson's Polonius, vocally limited but still transfixingly nasty. This was Peterson's year, mainly at Soulpepper where he flew solo in Billy Bishop Goes to War (another triumphant return) and was part of another fine ensemble in Glengarry Glen Ross. Melody Johnson had a wild comedy time: She directed a good Second City revue; she co-wrote Mimi, the years best new musical; and as an actress she was dottily dignified and superbly funny in 7 Stories. Joseph Ziegler brought off an even rarer trifecta by working with all three major classical companies: acting at Stratford, directing at Shaw, and doing both at Soulpepper --all good stuff, with his performance in Parfumerie taking the palm. Nancy Palk surpassed herself in Whos Afraid of Virginia Woolf, a show that was faultlessly acted throughout with Tim Campbell being absolutely perfect. Other great teams were those in Stratford's Importance of Being Earnest and BirdLand's Trial of Judas Iscariot, with Ted Dykstra and an irresistibly squealing Morris Panych, among many others, joining Diego Matamoross smooth and terrifying Satan. Matamoros was neither smooth nor terrifying in Travesties, merely enchanting. Hardee Lineham in Blackbird was disturbingly good. Erica Peck in The Boys in the Photograph and Chilina Kennedy in West Side Story gave fine musical and dramatic performances. Jim Mezon broke new and pungent ground in A Moon for the Misbegotten, and Benedict Campbell gave a new but convincing take on Archie Rice in The Entertainer with sublime assistance from Corinne Koslo. Fiona Reid gave two magical performances in Rock n' Roll and Shaun Smyth a very good one. In a year of one-person plays, Nicola Lipman was virtuoso in Another Home Invasion (a play that I should have listed last week among the year's best). Michael Hanrahan and Oliver Dennis were magisterial in an uneven Loot, and Dennis went on to delight us in Parfumerie in a rare romantic lead. The list is incomplete. But in a year that deprived us of Neil Munro and Douglas Campbell as well as Goldie Semple and with the loss of William Hutt and Richard Monette still fresh in memory, it's consoling and, in its way, inspiring.
