Kevin Spacey, beginning his second year as artistic director of the Old Vic, is playing Richard II, and everybody is very relieved. Spacey offers something new in Richards; not the traditional mellifluous martyr, nor the more recent mordant ironist exploiting his own humiliation, but an arrogant playboy monarch who never dreamed that power could have limitations.

Spacey is one Hollywood star whose vanity projects justify themselves. It may seem a leap from playing Bobby Darin (in Beyond the Sea) to playing Richard but the two characters, as he presents them, share a lust for the limelight and a certain doomed cockiness. They also demand that the actor master new skills, and Spacey swings Shakespearean verse as confidently as he swung Mack the Knife.

He appears in a modern-dress production by Trevor Nunn, whose themes are surveillance and publicity. Everybody is on show, and most of the characters collaborate with the process. John of Gaunt delivers his famous lament for England -- "this royal throne of kings" -- directly to a news team. After his death, the footage is cut up and replayed, to the specifications of whoever wishes to profit from it. Gaunt's son, the usurper Bolingbroke (Ben Miles), inherits his father's skills and refines them; everything he says and does is calculated for public consumption.

Spacey's Richard also plays to the cameras. His problem is that he does it without calculation and enjoys it too much. He barely troubles to conceal his boredom with his squabbling barons. Only once they're banished is he able to stretch out at ease -- apparently in his favourite nightclub -- though even here he is as likely to snap at his cronies as to indulge them. He has authority; his decision to go in person to the Irish wars seems, for once, to be more than a mischievous whim. His return, to find himself virtually deposed by Bolingbroke, gives full vent to his talents for angry self-pity. I have never heard "what must the king do now?" delivered with such savagery. Richard never stops talking but it's not until his last scene in prison that he has a soliloquy, or -- in this production's terms -- that he has no camera to play to. The ex- king's driven self-discovery, however, is the one point at which Spacey's performance falls down. It's more a matter of equipment than of understanding; he just doesn't have the notes in his voice with which to move us. The episode, though, in which he's visited by his loyal groom is unusually touching.

It's the kind of human detail at which Nunn, the best Shakespearean director there is, habitually excels. He both burrows into the text and builds a world around it. Modern dress can sometimes be a too-easy option, but it can also help supply a rationale for every scene, and that's what happens here. The strange semi-farcical interlude of the York family conspiracy has never made as much political or psychological sense as it does in this production, though I thought the Duchess's arriving at Bolingbroke's court in a motorcycle helmet was a bit much -- surely she could have taken a taxi.

Visiting London's theatres from Toronto currently inspires a sense of deja vu. They have, of course, the same musical franchises ours do, plus a couple of incipient ones such as Mary Poppins and The Woman in White, while revivals of Journey's End and You Never Can Tell make the West End look like a mini Shaw Festival.

And then there's Schiller. Next Tuesday in Toronto the Goethe- Institut has a video presentation on "Rediscovering Friedrich Schiller as a political playwright." Schiller, once practically a synonym for Neglected European Dramatist, is now hot even among insular anglophones. Canada led the way, with Soulpepper's inaugural production of Don Carlos. The same play was a West End hit last season and it's now been succeeded by Mary Stuart, in a production by Phyllida Lloyd that was acclaimed at the intimate Donmar Warehouse but looks punishingly bleak at the full-sized Apollo Theatre. The translation is an awkward compromise between ancient and modern, and the costuming is similarly split. Or rather it's segregated, along gender lines; women in period frocks are oppressed by men in modern suits. The contrast helps us to tell the gents apart, but -- despite the efforts of two fine actresses, Harriet Walter and Janet McTeer -- it does little for the ladies.

Kristin Scott Thomas is magnificent in an even less familiar classic, Luigi Pirandello's As You Desire Me, as a sleazy-nightclub singer who may be somebody's long-lost wife. Or, this being Pirandello, may not. Bob Hoskins plays her brutish protector and seems uncertain why he's doing it. The supposedly unpredictable play is predictable. We know we're going to be told that identity is unstable and unknowable and we sit there waiting for the penny, or the lire, to drop.

And much the same is true of the season's new dramatic hit, Heroes. Written by a young French playwright, Gerald Sibleyras, it has been translated by Tom Stoppard, whose jokes are expectedly good in a, for him, unexpected mode, i.e. they aren't quotable out of context. A bewitchingly verdant set (by Robert Jones) frames three enchanting actors: Richard Griffiths, John Hurt and -- especially good -- Ken Stott. They play a trio of old soldiers plotting their escape from a First World War veterans' home. It's a given that they won't succeed, or even seriously try. We watch them squabbling and reconciling and generally acting bittersweet on their way to the play's inevitable dying fall. It's been packaged like that more interesting French three-hander, Art, and I expect it to arrive at the Royal Alex -- or, if a post-Broadway tour falls through, at CanStage -- around 2007.
