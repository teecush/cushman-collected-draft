White Mice runs to Oct. 15. For ticket information, contact the Theatre Passe Muraille box office at (416) 504-7529.

Darren O'Donnell, who wrote and directed White Mice, is also half of its cast. He and his colleague, Bruce Hunter, play just what the title says they play, and they establish this through a highly effective piece of theatrical shorthand. Each appears in a snazzy ensemble of shirts and jeans, with the unexpected accessory of a long, twirlable tail. In O'Donnells previous piece, Boxhead, the actors spent the entire show with their heads encased in boxes. Actors with tails turn out to be more fun to watch than actors in boxes, and a lot easier to listen to. Besides, what they say is remarkably straightforward. The subject is racism. The play is about how white mice -- white Canadian mice -- treat black and brown mice. We have people acting like rodents who act like people.

It takes one minute to get the idea, and another 10 or 15 to stop resenting it. The actors are acting brilliantly, but in this kind of show we take that for granted. Hunter plays Douglas, a stay-at-home mouse, and ODonnell is his brother, Robert, who goes out into the world and keeps returning with ever-more-disturbing news. The dialogue is a sustained crosstalk act, full of fast patter and dialogue that almost overlaps, and the performers' phrasing and timing are impeccable. Oh, they're fur and whiskers, but theyre funny.

We havent had a sustained anthropomorphic satire in these parts for some time, not since a certain record-breaking English musical last made the rounds. While Cats is away, White Mice will play. The comparison is instructive, since a very irritating thing about Cats is its redundancy; if the cats were all meant to be human types anyway, why spend so much energy on dressing them up?

White Mice initially irritated me in the same way; if they want to attack racism in Canada, then why not do it head-on, instead of resorting to coy metaphor? But the strategy justifies itself. It isn't a matter of sparing our feelings. The show doesn't; its as didactic as any piece of theatre youre ever likely to see. It's a way of making the didactic theatrical, of having a style.

It's a very self-conscious style, the joke thoroughly shared with the audience. At one point, Robert says "white people" instead of white mice, and then begins frantically backtracking. Douglas wonders aloud why anyone would ever want to play to an audience of white mice, and then gets similarly flustered. (When the lights went up, I conducted a spot check; the Passe Muraille audience was composed predominantly of white mice, but not exclusively.) The duo begin by talking, fairly whimsically, about "cap-ap-ap-italistic prop-pop-aganda" (I hope I counted the syllables right) and about drinking cappuccino weenies (I can hardly bear to type that) on College Street. Bay Street and Rosedale are also good for automatic laughs, even at a time when the script is getting fairly sanguinary.

Increasingly, Robert confronts Douglas with discomfiting facts and figures about how the white mice in Toronto, in Canada, in North America, in the Western world, treat mice of other complexions. Douglas keeps protesting that it isnt that bad, but as the debate progresses he is left with fewer and fewer legs to stand on. (Two fewer than the average mouse, for a start.) Robert quotes Amnesty International and, less expectedly, Adolf Hitler. It seems that in Mein Kampf, Hitler claimed to have modelled his racial policies on the American settlers treatment of their indigenous population. Having advanced this thesis, Robert ratchets it up and challenges Douglas to deny that he is living in the realized embodiment of Hitler's dream. Douglas can't. Its as easy to pick holes in Roberts arguments as in a piece of cheese.

"Hitler's dream" consisted of ridding the world of Jews, something that in North America has noticeably not happened. Even if you want to play the game (a dangerously frivolous one) of equating one oppressed people with another, the fact remains that if Hitler had not been defeated in war he would have killed every Jew in Europe. The slaughter of the American Indians was ineradicably shameful, but it was not as thoroughgoing and it stopped. Exploitation is not the same as murder or as (another of Robert's buzzwords) slavery; slavery consists of employing people without paying them, and of literally buying and selling them. If "the white race" -- according to Robert, a cultural construct defined by its enslavement of other races -- only came into existence around 1800, how does he explain the existence of slavery, usually conducted on racial lines, for most of recorded history? Is symphonic music any less, or less worthy, an example of white culture than jazz is of black? (Assuming, as I dont, that a work of art has to be rated as an example of something.)

The play is not a balanced contest; Robert wins every round. Its true that he turns out to have his own agenda (it has to do with his love life) but this is less than the twist it promises to be. Douglas doesn't press him on it very hard and Douglas ends up converted, a liberal-turned-firebrand, and hysterically asks Robert what he should do. (Robert can't tell him.) We, who have identified with Douglas all the way, are left writhing with him. Because, even if the play overstates its points, it makes them, unanswerably; there may not be genocide or slavery out there, but there is the next worst thing. And it makes them by a legitimate manipulation of its own medium.

The play ends with the two mice settling down to the giant piece of cheese that has stood temptingly on their table throughout. They are like the protagonists of Wallace Shawns monologue, The Fever, who could find no moral justification for his own privileged existence but went on with it anyway. ODonnell, like Shawn, has stated the problem by writing a play, directing it, co-designing it (with Naomi Campbell) and, finally, by acting in it -- which, of course, is not quite the same thing as acting on it. Maybe it is; I wonder whether he thinks so. White Mice is a production of Mammalian Diving Reflex; the mouse tails are by Lori Hickling, and they are, like everything else about the presentation, excellent. -
