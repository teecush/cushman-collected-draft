At the start of Scorched, Wajdi Mouawad's play about memory, kinship and genocide, 10 of Toronto's finest troop on stage and fix the audience with their best impassive stares. There is one among them who, though looking initially no less baleful than the others, stands out as the only cast member wearing a suit and tie. This is Alon Nashman who plays, delightfully, a Quebec lawyer with a talent for mangling the language that goes beyond mere malapropism. Life," he tells his clients, "is not a bowl of berries, especially it seems when youre caught between the devil and the Blue Danube."

Linda Gaboriau, who translated the play from French, has been most creative with these addled aphorisms. Of course, they eventually begin, like all running gags, to limp a little, and there is the question of what theyre doing here at all. The play is anything but a comedy, and "comic relief" in a sombre context can become a mechanical and even demeaning device. One can, though, make a case for the relevance of this one. The play is partly about language, about its power to recall and record events and maybe to redeem them. Some of it concerns the attempts of a Taliban-like regime to eliminate reading and writing, especially among women.

There are things in Scorched I respect, but I didn't much like it. It's another Quebec play (Mouawad is Lebanese-Canadian) in which abstraction trumps action. Thats not to say that it doesnt have lots of story. Like Mouawad's Tideline, it's a quest play concerned with honouring the dead. A male and female twin come to the lawyers office to hear the will of his old friend, their mother. She came to Canada a devastated woman from a devastated Middle Eastern country; unnamed of course, according to the annoying belief that one can get at the particular from the general rather than the other way about. She has left her children letters to deliver to their father, whom they never knew, and their brother, of whom theyve never heard. So the daughter, and later the son, set off for the old country, where they discover one horrific piece of history after another: reprisals and counter- reprisals for an original offence nobody can now remember. And wherever they go, the siblings keep running into David Fox whose recurring appearances as a series of reminiscent elders, each more doddering than the last, become another running gag.

In counterpoint, we watch the story of the mother, Nawal, herself. She is played at three stages of her life by three different actresses, though a single player (Valerie Buhagiar, who wavers between nagging and genuinely fierce) plays a friend who's with her two-thirds of the way. Janick Hebert is appealing and later troubling as the teenaged Nawal whose newborn boy is torn from her by order of a malevolent mother. Kelli Fox is the fortyish Nawal who's become literate and is politicized when she finds herself in the middle of an atrocity. She has a set-piece speech describing it, and its a sign of whats wrong with the play that even Fox, who can do grief and anger better than anyone, can't stop it from sounding like a set piece, though it's supposed to be a spontaneous reaction. Conversely, Nicola Lipman gives a shattering account of Nawal in her sixties, testifying at the trial of a torturer, with a speech that actually is a prepared statement.

The things the play tells us (or, more accurately, reminds us of) are genuinely terrible. The dramatic test is whether they are any more terrible for being told us by these people in this story, and apart from Lipmans speech, I dont think they are. The play stakes an awful lot on metaphor. The initially battling siblings, for example, reflect the country from which they came. (And even, come to think of it, the one they're in now. She's a mathematician. Hes a boxer. Whats that supposed to add?) Internecine warfare occasions a particularly brutal case of incest, and the whole play can be taken as a scrambled riff on the Oedipus theme, down to the abandoned child rescued on a mountainside. It's also about mother- daughter hatred, and that's one theme too many, with a posthumous reconciliation that smacks of fairy dust.

The twins are excellently played by Sophie Goulet and Sergio Di Zio who, doubling as the young Nawals lover, more than confirms the promise he showed in Leo. Theres one more actor, Alex Poch- Goldin, who's held in reserve till near the end and turns out to be a devastating secret weapon. Nearly everyone in Richard Rose's production indulges at some point in unfocused rhetorical acting but they're a fine crew in a mostly swift and adroit staging. There's a scene in which characters from one time frame run round the perimeter of the stage while characters from another conduct a conversation in the middle. Like much else, it starts out impressive and ends up obscure.
