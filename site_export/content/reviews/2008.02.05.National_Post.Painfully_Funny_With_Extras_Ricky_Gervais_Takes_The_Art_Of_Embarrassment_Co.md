Our hero, trying to impress a deeply religious girl and her priest, goes on about what a good place Heaven must be. "It's gonna be brilliant up there", he enthuses; then, trying to be more specific, he adds "it'll be like Ibiza."

This is the humour of self-embarrassment, carried to such extremes and with such conviction, that it can be genuinely painful. Ricky Gervais is back.

He shares his thoughts on the hereafter in the first episode of his new -- well, officially new to Canada-- series Extras. This also featured a running gag about cerebral palsy. He--or, to be pedantic, Andy Millman, the character he plays -- meets a woman who suffers from it. "Don't worry," she says, as if to ease his obvious discomfort. "Oh, good," he replied, this in the circumstances being the best he can come up with in the line of polite chit-chat.

Extras arrives on the Comedy Network a few years after being seen in the U.K. and the U.S., not to mention worldwide availability on DVD. It's still a treat for anyone who likes the comedy of the excruciating. Gervais' character, a mixture of the fawning and the incompetent, is close kin to the one he created as David Brent in the original version of The Office, with the crucial difference that this time he isn't anybody's boss and so is in no position to hurt anyone except himself -- at which he is a virtuoso. In The Office, Gervais' character was at the top of a small heap; Extras finds his new character at the bottom of a big one. Andy is a fortyish actor, a late beginner in the profession but keen. Having yet to be given a single line to speak in any medium, he is working as what the world in general calls an extra and what he himself calls a background artist. ("Just for the pocket money," as he says, convincing nobody, including himself.) He has a friend, Maggie, who's also in the background business, and with roughly the same number of social skills. He is also cursed with an agent whose attributes include "having trouble with his cellphone -- and technology generally." Stephen Merchant, Gervais' co-writer, plays him with an air of listless self-deprecation that could be either charming or alarming, depending on whether or not you're his client.

So what Andy actually does is hang around movie sets in uncomfortable and unbecoming costumes, waiting to be called and hoping to make contacts. And he does meet some real-life stars. In the first episode, it was Kate Winslet, uttering friendly obscenities that sounded amusing because a) she was Kate Winslet, and b) she was dressed as a nun, for a film that seemed to be The Sound of Music with realistic Nazis. (As she said herself, the best way to get an Oscar is to do a Holocaust picture.) Last week, Andy met Ben Stiller, taking a flight at directing ("I'm one of the world's most successful comedy actors, but what does that mean?"). He, too, was making an ostentatiously humanitarian picture, which didn't stop him throwing a very nasty, and very convincing, tantrum when crossed -- by Andy, as it happened. This was tough, since Andy had spent the entire show trying to ingratiate himself, feigning enthusiasm over classics like The Seven Samurai: "I love all the number pictures."

Gervais can almost make you believe that Andy believes that he means it. That's because, though his character is pushy, his delivery isn't. He's quite the understater --even working from a script, he sounds improvised.

It's the verite aspect that makes the show work, enhanced of course by the celebs sending themselves sportingly up. (One assumes they know what they're doing. Though sometimes -- especially with Stiller -- one hopes that they don't.) The Stiller episode was notably well-constructed, with Andy the architect of his own misfortune; the Winslet episode not so much. But the persona Gervais has constructed is fascinating. He's a misanthropic sycophant, a self-defeating combination that's common enough in real life but under-exploited in fiction.

There's an obvious danger of repetition. However, there have been two seasons of Extras, and the Comedy Network has cannily jammed them together. In the first season, Andy tells us that he's written a TV series. (Shades of George Costanza, of whom Andy may be the unsanitized version-- if you can imagine that.) We assume this is another of his hopeless projects, but in the second season it gets written, and even made. And then the suits -- this may be the BBC, but they're still suits --move in and start to change it. This is a more fertile situation. Plus, these shows have A-list guests of the acting calibre of Ian McKellen (who does the self-satirizing classical actor bit better than anyone) and Patrick Stewart. It also has Gervais reacting to the accusing headline "TV bully kicks dwarf in face" with the observation that "there's no such thing as bad publicity."

It's tempting to call Gervais' comedy of masochistic cruelty typically English, but its most notable TV antecedents are American: The Larry Sanders Show and, later, Curb Your Enthusiasm. Both of those, though, are set in a world of real celebrity (as well as, like Extras, featuring real celebrities). Extras, however, is seedier. It's unlikely ever to be sweetened into a vehicle for Steve Carell.

- Extras airs Tuesdays at 10 p.m. on The Comedy Network.
