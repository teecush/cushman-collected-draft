COUNTERFEIT SECRETS

Artword Theatre, Toronto

It has taken the better part of 300 years but the plays of Pierre Carlet de Marivaux seem finally to be happening in English. We know this because one of them, The Triumph of Love, has become a film, playing in Toronto even as I type.

Some of us were able to steal a march on the movies last year, when the same play was staged here by John Van Bureks Pleiades Theatre company. That production was the central panel of a triptych. It had been preceded the year before by the same author's The Game of Love and Chance, and Van Burek's troupe has now completed the trinity with Counterfeit Secrets, which is his translation of Les Fausses Confidences. The play may well be the best of the three, but the production -- though intelligent and well worth seeing -- is the least successful.

The play is a knife-edged courtship comedy in which -- as suggested by the title -- just about every character is being doubly duplicitous. Some of them are never less to be trusted than when they are telling the truth.

Dorante, the son of a lawyer, infiltrates himself into the household of Araminte, a wealthy young widow with whom he has fallen in love. His agent in this scheme is Dubois, once his valet and now in the service of Araminte herself. He lets her know of his former masters passion (pretending hes doing so without Dorante's knowledge), embroiders matters by saying he's gone crazy with it, and waits for the tender-hearted Araminte to fall in return. Which she does, almost immediately; but there are, of course, complications.

Dorante, at the urging of a managing uncle, pretends love to Marton, Aramintes female companion; and she, too, is only too happy to reciprocate. Araminte is engaged in a lawsuit with a neighbouring count, and the prevailing wisdom is that this could best be settled if the two of them got married.

As Aramintes steward and legal advisor, Dorante is in an excellent position to frustrate this -- another test of his already compromised integrity. Araminte herself is highly uncertain about whether to follow her heart, and begins developing her own schemes and keeping her own secrets. There is a trap door beneath nearly everything anybody says.

Van Burek has given it a fittingly geometric staging, on a set (by Andjelija Djuric) that looks like an unused clinical ward into which somebody has mysteriously introduced an elegantly old- fashioned desk and chair. It's ages before anybody gets to sit down on the chair, comfortable though it looks; for the most part they have to deposit themselves on cruel high-backed contraptions that are moved and removed as needed. The walls are mobile, too; the actors push them around, often while talking.

Much of this struck me as wasted labour; the stage seldom looks much different when they've finished, and there are no crucial changes of locale in the play anyway. The main point seems to be to allow an occasional central position to a door with a small square window in it, through which the plays scheming servants can favour us with smug or menacing glances. These are effective, and they pay off especially well at the end, but Im not sure they're worth the effort.

The director has a firm grasp on the play, and his steel-and- gossamer translation is excellent.

Where he falls down is in his casting, or at least in one central piece of it. Arsinee Khanjian plays Araminte -- and seems piteously out of her depth. This is a play where language is a weapon -- one that needs to be wielded with absolute precision. Khanjian can muster neither the concentration nor the attack; losing the will to listen to her, we start missing out on bits of the plot.

Though the characters have secrets from one another, they don't have them from us; but its often hard to tell what Araminte is after. Her accent doesnt help, but it's probably sheer theatrical inexperience that is the main culprit. She looks as unhappy as she sounds -- frumpy, really, though neither she nor the other actresses are well-served by their costumes. (Julie LeGal, a Marton of more enthusiasm than finesse, looks half-dressed.) Araminte ends up as a puddle of hard-used innocence, which is surely sentimental; the lady may tell fewer fibs than anyone else on stage, but she has her own agenda and pursues it.

The best performance comes from William Webster as the uncle, who can turn on a dime from geniality to venom and who alone plays with the unfaltering pace and precision the text demands. Dawn Greenhalgh is amusing as Araminte's social-climbing mother, aghast that anyone should stand in the way of her becoming a counts mother-in-law.

Andrew Pifko is pleasant as Dorante, though without fully exploring the characters various levels of sincerity and deceit. Xuan Fraser is smoothly sinister as his servant, manager and -- the production strongly suggests -- lover. (The idea behind this particular beaux' stratagem seems to be that the two boys will live happily together off the widow's money. I doubt that even Marivaux meant to be that cynical.)

The modernish dress again leaves the traditional role of Harlequin (Thomas Hauff) out on a limb; with no spangled costume to hide inside, the actor is enforced to turn a clever idiot into a realistic character, and that is an impossibly tall order. He should exist on a different level from everyone else on stage; here, he's a schemer like the rest, and that is one more than the play needs.
