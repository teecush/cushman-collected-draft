MONTY PYTHON'S SPAMALOT

Canon Theatre, Toronto

- - -

Some people chop trees; they're lumberjacks and they're all right. The Monty Python boys, who taught us this and much else, are intellectual lumberjacks; they chop logic. It's an old English tradition, but one that the Pythons took to new heights with their sequences of insanely intense argument, their erudition worn at dangerously rakish angles. Some of these sublimely funny dialogues light up Monty Python's Spamalot, the stage version of Monty Python and the Holy Grail. Many of them, indeed, are taken from the movie word for word. There is, pretty near the beginning, the one in which a couple of sentinels debate, from opposite apertures high up in a medieval keep, the respective wing capacities of the European and the African swallow for carrying coconuts from the Caribbean to King Arthur's Britain, where the natives use them to provide sound effects for horses' hooves. Quite soon after that, there's the one in which an oppressed peasant named Dennis, questions, from an orthodox Marxist perspective, the right of Arthur to rule over him or anyone else. ("It's always class with you, isn't it?" sniffs Dennis' mother for whom, plainly, Dennis is not Che Guevara, just a very naughty boy.) Dennis is especially scornful of the king's claim to derive his legitimacy from the Lady in the Lake or, as he puts it, "some tart in a pond" holding a sword. Even if the sword is called Excalibur.

There are also passages that, though they don't derive from the film, are fully worthy of it. After the voice of God (played by the voice of John Cleese; shrewd casting, that) has commanded Arthur and his team to seek the Holy Grail, the knights fall to questioning the omniscience of a Deity who can't keep track of his own tableware. That hits the authentic, wildly literal note. Which, since founding Python Eric Idle wrote it, is no surprise. Idle is responsible for the show's book, lyrics and, in collaboration with John Du Prez, music. Monty Python's Spamalot is a musical -- a spoof musical. In a way, that's a logical progression, since the movie was a spoof epic. But there is a difference. There were not many mock-Arthurian pictures before Holy Grail. (Or since, if it comes to that.) Just about every musical we see now is a parody musical, to the extent that there is little in the form left to make fun of; if song-and- dance shows are self-evidently ridiculous, why go on writing them?

As with The Producers (which used parody but wasn't one), the stand-out number in Spamalot is a carry-over: Always Look on the Bright Side of Life, which isn't quite as funny when the people singing it aren't being crucified, but is still irresistibly jaunty. I Am Not Dead Yet is cute, sung by a man refusing to be loaded onto a plague cart, and The Song That Goes Like This is a hilarious take- off of an Andrew Lloyd Webber ballad, even if its authors protest it isn't, which in turn makes you wonder why the staging incorporates a collapsing chandelier. But most of the numbers seem like intrusions, even though the writers and the director, Mike Nichols, have tried to make the idea of "being in a musical" part of the concept. It culminates in something called You Won't Succeed on Broadway, which is about musicals being mainly done by Jews. Well, Jews used to write most of them (not so much any more) but they've never mono- polized the performance of them, nor would most audiences care one way or the other; and though I salute Idle for rhyming "Gentile" with "percentile" this number doesn't so much stop the show as stop it in its tracks. It drives its one joke into the ground.

That's another thing. The great strength of the Python TV shows, carried over with remarkable success into the movies, was their speed. When an idea was exhausted, they moved unceremoniously on to the next. A musical is ceremonious, almost by definition. And though the stage cast do a lot of doubling, they can hardly be expected to recover the do-it-yourself exhilaration of half-a-dozen writer- performers doing all the parts. Nor do the present touring company put much of an individual stamp on their roles. (The starry Broadway originals may well have done.) Michael Siberry's Arthur is closely patterned on Graham Chapman's baffled authority in the movie, only with geniality standing in for ineffability. His bluffness kept reminding me of Ross Petty, who might be ideal Canadian casting; and indeed the show, which includes audience participation and a singalong, not to mention mock-medieval decor, does sometimes suggest a superior Petty panto (as opposed to Wicked, which was like an inferior one.) The various knights are hard to tell apart but Jeff Dumas, strongly resembling Baldrick from Blackadder, is delightful as Patsy the put-upon squire who wields the coconuts that, in a nice touch, also come in useful for providing tap-dance noises.

This is not a show with great opportunities for women, a point repeatedly made by the only one present, Pia Glenn, whose Lady in the Lake is a vociferously frustrated diva fashioned after Eartha Kitt. Again one wishes that the show would stop making jokes about musicals and get back to making jokes about Arthur and his knights, especially as those jokes are very good ones. When this show is funny, it's explosively so; and it finishes better than the movie whose ending, it must be confessed, was a cop-out. (It actually involved cops.) There is what the show's author might characterize as a rather high nudge nudge, wink wink quotient; it's hard not to wince when an audience of devotees starts applauding before a favourite scene starts; comedy is supposed to be about surprise. On the other hand, only an arrant churl could resist an opening fish- slapping dance, especially as it turns out to be sublimely irrelevant. Some of the show is the purest Python, even if it isn't the full Monty.
