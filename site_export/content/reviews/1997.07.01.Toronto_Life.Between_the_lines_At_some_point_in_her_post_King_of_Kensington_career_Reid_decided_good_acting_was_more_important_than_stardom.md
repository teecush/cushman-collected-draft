Between the lines

At some point in her post -- King of Kensington career, Fiona Reid decided good acting was more important than stardom. The money's not so hot, but who's complaining? By Robert Cushman

I HAVE THIS indelible memory of Fiona Reid in Noel Coward's Fallen Angels. It was in 1992, for the Canadian Stage Company at the St. Lawrence Centre. She played a fashionable 1920s wife awaiting the arrival of an ex - lover through a mist composed of equal parts lust, fear and alcohol. She created a character of wild gentility, pixieish in a cloche hat and flashing a smile crammed with eager, nervous teeth. In an outrageous piece of physical comedy, Reid's character, infuriated by a constantly ringing phone, stuffed the offending instrument up her dress and then tried to act unconcerned when surprised by the maid.

Fiona Reid, by her own admission, is associated with Coward heroines, and with what she describes as "witty, British, sardonic" things in general. She told me this over lunch at the Four Seasons, while gearing up to move with her family for a summer at Niagara - on - the - Lake. She's appearing there at this year's Shaw Festival, playing Madame Arkadina, the self - made theatrical grande dame who is the queen - pin of Chekhov's The Seagull. To balance that play, she will also appear in one almost totally unknown, The Secret Life, by the genius of the English Edwardian theatre, Harley Granville Barker. After that, she's back in Toronto to play in Edward Albee's A Delicate Balance, a Canadian Stage coproduction that will open here in January and then move on to Edmonton. This regular alternation between Niagara and the St. Lawrence Centre -- with the occasional television movie of the week -- has been the shape of her career for several years now, and it has brought her a stream of posh parts in high - gloss plays. But the financial rewards are slightly less glossy. "I must," she muses, "be the only person to have earned around $30,000 last year [an unusually bad one] and have a serious number of points at Holt Renfrew. I like to be generous, I like to enjoy life. If you have some notoriety, you have to act as if you have money. I spend lots."

There's a paradox here, in a career that's rife with them, and it says a lot about the Canadian theatre. Reid, now forty - five, has for years been one of Canada's leading actresses, and she seems to be getting better all the time. But her greatest period in terms of notoriety and earning power was twenty years ago when she landed the female lead, opposite Al Waxman, in the most successful of all Canadian sitcoms, King of Kensington. Influenced by the hit American series All in the Family (itself a clone of the British Till Death Us Do Part), King of Kensington was still innovative for Canada as a sympathetic comedy with a blue - collar setting. Reid played Kathy, the thirty - seven - year - old wife with a tender social conscience. (The age bit was a portent of her acting future: "I've usually played characters older than myself -- pressing into the next hormonal stage.") Rather sniffy about the show while she was doing it (she thought it "kitschy"), she now wishes that she'd been more appreciative of its groundbreaking qualities: "When I meet people who are first - or second - generation immigrants, who were teenagers at the time, I discover it spoke to them as no other show did."

She had already done TV comedy with John Candy and Dan Aykroyd, and though back then she may have looked and sounded like a dizzy debutante, those professional jokers weren't fooled. They ribbed her about her intensity. "Oh, Fi," Candy used to say when they were doing a show called Coming Up Rosie, "you wanna do a bit of Chekhov?" As it happened, she did, and during TV's seasonal layoffs she would play classics -- including Chekhov -- in regional theatres. After three years of King of Kensington, she got out, turning down a proposed series tailored strictly to her to be called simply Fiona.

She still retains the look, the style, the achievement and -- at least among the cognoscenti -- the reputation of a star. "I'm not," says Reid, "at all what I appear. I appear to have some degree of class, but it's a huge joke."

In fact, one of the things she's best at on stage is pointing up the absurdities, the insecurities, and through them the dignity, of people who might appear to have it made. She's been called a patrician actress, but it might be more accurate to say that she gets cast as middle - class characters with variously moneyed lifestyles. They could of course be British, but they could also be American, like the heroines of Terrence McNally's Lips Together, Teeth Apart and John Guare's brilliant Six Degrees of Separation, both of whom she has played for Canadian Stage. The Guare character -- a rich, vulnerable and exquisitely dressed woman, half - wise, half - foolish -- elicited one of her finest performances and won her her second Dora. Claire, her role in the forthcoming A Delicate Balance, is the prize member of a family of blue - chip Yankee neurotics.

She's good with words; or, as she puts it herself, "I've always had a sense of the music." Her first Dora had been for Fallen Angels, in which she had a line about passion -- "a base uncouth thing lurking within us" -- making it sound hilariously obscene, while also suggesting a woman rebuking an embarrassingly unruly poodle. Later, she remarked of her old flame's behaviour: "It's probably an elfin joke. He was always being elfin." Many actresses, faced with the delectable adjective "elfin," would have stressed it in the first sentence. Reid, by contrast, set it off by placing the weight on the noun "joke." This meant that "elfin" still had some life left in it when it came round again; it also established the speaker as a woman who would say such a thing without thinking about it. "You can't," she says, expressing her own distaste for acting that signals the audience when to laugh, "give away what reaction you might want." You can still of course get the reaction: the phone business earned "the longest, loudest laughter I have ever received."

Christopher Newton, director of the Shaw Festival and one of Reid's closest friends and colleagues, says, "I think the thing that happened to her was that she decided good acting was more important than stardom. It happened here at Shaw in the mid - eighties. That and having two kids." He also says that for the plays they do at Shaw, "it's quite useful if someone has an English mother and father. Fiona's parents are very nice British, middle - class people."

HER FATHER, Grainger Reid, is Scots, a doctor and a former officer in the Royal Army Medical Corps, which he quit with the rank of colonel. He worked for many years for the Ontario Ministry of Health and, his daughter says, "was viewed, and indeed carried on, as the ex - colonel." In fact, he grew up in very modest circumstances, "and to imagine how he got to the University of Edinburgh at age seventeen by dint of his brains puts a different light on the image I and my friends always had of him."

He married Becky Sharpe, an army nurse whose own childhood, according to her daughter, "always seemed charmed. Born in Tokyo, educated in Switzerland, she is the daughter of a Church of England missionary." The couple met just after the Normandy invasion, and their relationship developed during the liberation of Europe. Their medical duties involved them in the most traumatic episodes of that campaign. They were among the troops who saw the notorious concentration camp of Bergen - Belsen (it was the first camp that the British army entered, so all its horrors were fresh), and they conducted their courtship there. Her father never talked about it, though he was obviously deeply affected by it. Her mother used to speak in awed tones of the suffering they witnessed and especially of the extraordinary graciousness of the victims. "She said it taught her how lucky one was to be alive. After the war she had a bout of polio; she was in an iron lung, and was supposed to die, but said she couldn't because of her kids. She never complained." Fiona's parents followed the army to India at the time of partition; they married in Calcutta (while the riots were in progress) and honeymooned in Darjeeling.

Fiona was their third and last child, and her upbringing was even more geographically varied than her mother's. She was born in Britain and lived there between the ages of five and nine. "When I go back, I try to pretend I'm British; it works for about three weeks. You're British, so I slip into it when I talk to you, but if you were Canadian, I'd have an irreproachable Canadian accent." Other parts of her early childhood were spent in Germany, Africa and the United States. She wasn't terribly happy in the U.S. ("I wasn't friendly enough, my teeth weren't white enough, I came from a British girls' boarding school, wearing National Health Service glasses"). The family came to Canada in 1964 when she was thirteen, and she began to thrive, though not, initially, as an actress. She went to Lawrence Park Collegiate and couldn't get cast in a school play. Still, she went on to study drama at McGill, where she was ambitious but "not sure what I was ambitious about."

She seems to have been carrying a heavy load of paternal ambition. "I was the one he deemed to be intelligent -- he wanted me to be a doctor." In the post - Kensington years, she spent a couple of not too happy seasons at Stratford. This is another period of her life that she wishes she had enjoyed more; it was, after all, the kind of work she left television to pursue. "I didn't know how to play the game. I wasn't a very likable person then. I felt really exposed because of the TV success. I must have come over as a terribly insecure snob." She pauses. "What a dreadful combination."

One of the plays she appeared in at Stratford was The Comedy of Errors. The morning after it opened, her father called to ask how it had gone. When she said it had been all right, he responded, rattling his newspaper, "It says here you were awful." Having absorbed his high standards, she says, "I never enjoyed my success in my twenties; I never thought it was good enough."

Two years ago, her father had a stroke, and he has become increasingly immobile. Reid has begun to spend time putting his recollections on tape. "I had the feeling one had to get a complete document of his life. I never had the feeling my parents were normal -- I used to brag about that. We fought a great deal and there was a lot of humour in that. He swore, so I did. I'd stalk out of the room and he'd follow me. There was much shouting. But in middle age I'm coming to know who my parents really are. There's something heroic about both of them. My mother was never given much credit for being intelligent, but she is; her true personality was obscured over the years by the 'dutiful wife and mother' aspect. She sounds proper, so she gets misunderstood, but she's very funny. What makes her amusing? It's that she doesn't know she is."

Her mother's legacy can filter through into her performances in various and subtle ways. In 1995, in a Shaw Festival production of Coward's Cavalcade, she played Jane Marryot, the nearest thing to a full - length character in what is less a play than a huge pageant. On victory night, like a good patriotic English lady, Jane is out in the crowded streets of London, waving a flag. But she has lost a son in the war, so while everybody else is going crazy with joy, she's going crazy with grief. It's a wordless scene that Coward, always the showman, knew would be desperately affecting with the right actress. Reid made it the most human moment in the show.

Christopher Newton directed her in Cavalcade. He will also be appearing with her this year in The Secret Life. Their collaboration started back in 1984, when they played the lovers, Elyot and Amanda, in Coward's Private Lives, and led to what Reid calls "a very special relationship. It probably comes from being thrown into that play, kissing together on the sofa while not knowing each other. We still fight. One night I started telling him how to run his theatre. He looked at me coldly and said, 'Back right off.' Nobody had ever said that to me before. It made me realize I ought to be less confrontational. I spent the earlier part of my career vacillating between a ridiculous amount of confrontation and blind obedience.

I'm constantly discovering, contrary to what people may say about me, that acting is more about generosity than anything else." Newton points to her "increasing generosity of spirit" and adds that "she's very willing to have a go at strange stuff."

It doesn't always work. In 1994 she appeared for Canadian Stage in a much - feted South American play, Death and the Maiden, portraying a torture victim avenging herself on her chief persecutor. It should have been a fine part for her -- a cultivated woman in extremis -- but this time her brittleness went into overdrive, and the character, intended to be fanatical, came across as plain crazy. There is a kind of acting sometimes described by fans, critics and performers themselves as "terribly brave" and by skeptics as "over the top and unconvincing," and this looked like an example of it. (It turns out she didn't like it much, either. She and the director did not get on.)

THE MAIN stabilizing influence in her life has been her husband. She met MacCowan Thomas, now proprietor of a Toronto scenic workshop, in 1973 when they were both working at a summer theatre in Muskoka, and they married in 1977. "My husband has a terrific sense of calm; I instantly glommed onto that. He always loved working with wood, which represents everything I couldn't do, something methodical and painstaking." What do they have in common? "We both have a very good sense of humour, we both have champagne tastes with beer incomes, and we're terrific parents. Well, anyway, he is." They have an eleven - year - old son, Alec ("A bit like I was when I was young, he's easily impressed by fame -- he dreams of being in the NBA"), and a nine - year - old daughter, Julia ("She just reads and lives theatre, she dances and plays the violin").

This at least sets her apart from the lady she plays in The Seagull; Madame Arkadina was clearly an awful parent, and has the mixed - up, suicidal son to prove it. All the same, Newton says cryptically that "Arkadina's a bit Fi - like; Fiona knows what it means to be a leading actress who's gone out to the provinces. It seems to sum up where she's got to. Now is a good time in her life to play Arkadina."

Reid also acknowledges the fragility of early ambitions: "I used to think, if you're good, you'll always make movies; now I have a good laugh at myself. TV and film auditions are a modelling contest anyway. A lot about getting that work is acting as if you don't care; I'm getting better at that." What about balancing acting and motherhood? "Well, I never had to say, 'Will I work full time or not at all?' You're relentlessly part - time in the theatre whether you like it or not. It's been pretty hand - to - mouth for the last twenty years."

After which she pulls herself up. "I hope I don't sound like I'm complaining all the time. Perhaps you could add, 'She then gives a perky laugh.'"

She then gives a perky laugh.
