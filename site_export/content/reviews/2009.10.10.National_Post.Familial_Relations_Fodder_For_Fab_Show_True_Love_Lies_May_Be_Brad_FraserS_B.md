The new Toronto theatre season has got off to a remarkably good start all around. There's a delightful small musical, Mimi, and a flawed but affecting big one, The Boys in the Photograph. There are three productions at Soulpepper: one great, one good, one ... interesting. There's Stoppard at CanStage. And there's True Love Lies, a new comedy by Brad Fraser that may be his best play yet.

Its title may remind you of the more unwieldy one with which its author first grabbed attention, Unidentified Human Remains and the True Nature of Love, and the two plays have characters in common. David McMillan, raffish protagonist of the earlier piece, is now a restaurateur. His former lover, a respectable citizen named Kane, is now married to a woman called Carolyn. They have a media business plus two children. Daughter Madison, 21, is aggressively sexually active, and aggressive about most other things as well; her younger brother, Royce, is more or less the opposite, spending hours looking at porn on the computer but defining himself, through a desperation that colours his entire existence, as asexual. Madison applies, all unknowing, for a job at David's eatery, and the interview goes swimmingly until he realizes who her father is. She returns home for a row around the family dinner table, in the course of which Kane's past gets revealed. The younger generation is appalled, if not by the relationship then by having it come so close to home ( "it's bad enough having a father who's creative and artistic, without discovering he's gay as well"). Carolyn, troubled but intrigued, takes the emancipated course of a woman faced with a possible rival; she has Kane invite David over for dinner.

If you find this last development hard to accept, you're in good company; David himself can hardly believe it and has grave misgivings about accepting. He does, though; and the gathering, despite everybody's best intentions except perhaps Royce's, is a social disaster. It does result, however, in Madison, through some polite blackmail, getting the job chez David, a position from which she can realize her transparent ambition of throwing herself at him. Royce has similar intentions, though not for the reasons you might think. When he and David have their confrontation it's shocking, but it isn't played for shock value: a mark of maturity that's characteristic of the play as a whole. Meanwhile, Carolyn, initially a willing partner in a marriage based (as the play caustically, but not cruelly, implies most are) on "insecurity, co-dependency and fear," sets about tentatively liberating himself. David, who has his own personal and financial problems lurking somewhere in the background, finds himself in the unsought role of family counsellor: a function that he discharges, in David W. Keeley's performance, with a magnetic mixture of weariness, tolerance and grace.

The play, which unfolds with admirable logic as well as being line-byline funny, has been given a very zippy production by its author. The action moves back and forth between eating places; and Britta Gerecke's functional single set makes all the scenes seem at home, the restaurant doubling easily and without confusion as family kitchen and dining room. Writing and performance probe the pain and, even more, the doubt, beneath the bright surface, without ever becoming self-consciously dark. Ashley Wright's Kane has the harassed look of a sitcom dad with blood-pressure problems, but his nervous reaching out to family and former partner is still believable. So are the well-meaning discomforts of Julie Stewart's Rose, the unnerving assurance of Susanna Fournier's Madison, and the terrors, both given and received, of Andrew Craig's Royce. The last word of the title is interestingly ambiguous; is it a noun or a verb? The latter would be the more interesting, with the play itself providing a rider: Love, it is suggested, does lie, sometimes and justifiably; but it doesn't have to do it all the time. The closing scene combines the attractions of inevitability and a question mark -- the neatest of the play's neat tricks.

Maintaining my unaccustomed Pollyanna mode I have to report that the new Second City revue Shut Up and Show us Your Tweets is one of this troupe's best, though not the best. Its attractions include a sketch about a married couple, separated by her travelling for her job, who try some self-conscious phone sex but keep getting sidetracked by memories of domesticity; a man's frantic call to a sadistically unhelpful computer help-line, proving that old jokes can be the best, especially if they tap familiar agonies; and a step-by-step historical retrospective on the ancient theme of "there goes the neighbourhood," allowing the cast to show off their prowess at accents and ethnic walks. The superb Darryl Hinds introduces this as "white people doing stereotypes" but does not participate himself; his personalized air of incredulous detachment, paraphrasable as "lord, what fools these Caucasians be" is given free and delightful rein elsewhere. Stabs at political satire feel dutiful, as usual; though there is a number depicting an American health-care town-hall meeting that spirals into a riotous identity of its own, each protester's absurdity feeding on the one before. Reid Janisse's patience as the man on the platform is ennobling to behold. Rob Baker, another in the cast of six, is older than the average Second Citizen, or maybe just looks it. Sandy Jobin-Bevans directs, smoothly; the designer, as always, here (and most of the time, everywhere else), is Camellia Koo. The show's first half is better than its second, but that's forgivable.
