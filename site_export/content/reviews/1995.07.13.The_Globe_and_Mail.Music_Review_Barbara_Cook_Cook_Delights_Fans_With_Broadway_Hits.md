MUSIC REVIEW BARBARA COOK Cook delights fans with Broadway hits

Thursday, July 13, 1995

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Toronto ONT -- BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Toronto ONE of Barbara Cook's favourite lines, referring to her tenure as Broadway's favourite ingenue of the fifties and sixties, is that she never knew at the time that she was part of some Golden Age of musical comedy. She is not a nostalgia-monger, but her cabaret and concert act is based on our awareness of who she was and what she stood for. Performer and audience unite in contemplating where she's come from, and where she thinks she's going.

She rations her greatest hits severely. One pleasant chorus of 'Till There Was You (from The Music Man) is buttressed by the two longest- serving crowd-pleasers in her repertoire. One is Losing My Mind, the breakdown song that she sang in the celebrated concert revival of Follies. The other, Cook's tour de force, is Ice Cream, the ecstatic monologue of romantic discovery (finding her mind, perhaps) that she introduced in She Loves Me. Cook is now a voluminous woman with shimmering gowns, but when she sings this piece she creates a witty and precisely distanced cameo of rushing girlish excitement, culminating in a celebrated and intoxicating high B. It would bring down the hardest-hearted house.

That's it for history. There are none of her jewels from Flahooley, The Gay Life, The Grass Harp or even Candide, though she does tell a long anecdote about auditioning for Leonard Bernstein, and terrifying him with the top notes in Madam Butterfly. Turning to now, she does a couple of countrified plaints by Amanda McBroom that she rates more highly than I do, though I grant them the occasional piercing line.

After a roguish reference to Duke Ellington and the boys, she sashays into I'm Beginning to See the Light, to my complete disbelief. Her rhythm is perfectly fine, an exemplary Broadway lilt, but she is not a jazz singer and her paraphrases, extensions and elisions sound as if they have been written down and learned by rote. This is not to say that Ellington should be off limits to her, merely that she should find her own way into him, rather than trying to turn herself into a finger-snapper. I also, in the face of roomsful of cheering devotees, am appalled by her slow, show- boating one-long-climax version of Carolina in the Morning: because it has nothing to do with the song. Cook is an actress; to be true to herself, she has to find the truth in her material.

So Ice Cream works wonderfully and so does Arthur Schwartz's Make the Man Love Me, a lovely song simply sung, and another instance of innocence reached through experience. Losing My Mind fails (this is another minority report) because, emote though she may, she remains a very together lady. She cannot move me to sorrow, though she frequently moves me to delight. She does so in The Surrey with the Fringe on Top - it's the best thing in the show - where, though clearly not the cowboy envisioned by Rodgers and Hammerstein, she creates a new person with a delightful ironic kinship to the original. The voice is still a wonder, and the showmanship is impeccable.
