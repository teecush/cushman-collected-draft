Cinderella in Muddy York

Tarragon Extra Space

The brother-and-sister team of Ann and David Powell, known as Puppet mongers, has been presenting Cinderella in Muddy York, for audiences aged five and up, since 1990. They and their mostly stringless dolls retell the old story by setting it in 1834, the year the town of York changed its name to Toronto. The heroine is now the daughter of an absentee father whose second wife has come over from England -- with her own two daughters, of course -- and who proceeds to take her disenchantment with Upper Canada out on poor Ella, whose cinder-grey dress inspires her familiar sobriquet.

This is more motivation than the trio of intruding harpies customarily gets, and indeed the domestic relations in this 50- minute production are sketched both more subtly and more fully than is customary in your average family spectacular. This Stepmother doesn't know shes being cruel; she merely suffers from a severe case of entitlement. She calls Cinderella "dear in sweetly reasonable tones even while ordering her to prepare cups of herbal tea that will be sympathetic to her allergies. As for Cinders step- siblings, the show is far too modern to call them the Ugly Sisters but it's still hard to think of them any other way. They are named Zelda and Imelda; one of them looks like Miss Piggy and the other has a figure like a tent-pole and a face like a hatchet. They are allowed a measure of farcical poignance when they go galumphing at the ball. When they get to try on the Slipper of Destiny, one of them has a foot that's too big to fit, the other has a foot thats too small.

The ball is thrown to celebrate the citys re-branding, and the guest list includes an array of starchy couples destined to donate their names to major thoroughfares. (Mr. and Mrs. Henry Eglinton Mr. and Mrs. Donald Valley"). The celebration is also a chance for the Lieutenant Governor of Ontario to marry off his son, a shy and studious young fellow named Mr. Charming Prince. As in the original story, the ball is a three-night affair, and it's only at the last midnight that the heroine almost forgets to leave. As in no other version that I know, she says -- while leaving the room and shedding her shoe -- what her name is. This is very practical of her, but it leaves young Charming looking distinctly twittish. Armed with this vital information, why doesn't he just send a flunkey from door to muddy door asking for a young lady by the name of Ella? They could always fall back on the footwear routine in the event of contesting claims.

This particular Ella shows traces of post-Beauty and the Beast Disney feminism; she wonders why she cant be a doctor or lawyer and she has premonitory doubts about the idea of getting married. When Mr. Prince pops the question, however, she accepts immediately. The show, directed by Sue Miner, is sweet but skimpy. The Canadian historical setting is about the limit of the scripts inventiveness. As for staging, the Powells bring off one pocket-sized coup in the transformation of pumpkin into coach. They manipulate their puppets in full view of the audience, and also share the stage with them in narrating and even in acting roles. They are unlikely to give Ronnie Burkett any sleepless nights, whether in point of technical wizardry, richness of detail or intensity of performance. Ann's stage-presence is modest, in both the good and bad senses; David's is plain overbearing. They take bows at the end of the show. The puppets don't.

- Cinderella at Muddy York runs until Jan. 1 at the Tarragon Theatre. 416-531-1827
