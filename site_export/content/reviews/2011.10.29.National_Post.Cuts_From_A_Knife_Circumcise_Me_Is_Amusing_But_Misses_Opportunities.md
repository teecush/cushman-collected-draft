Bearing a title that may well be the least enticing come-on in the history of show business, Circumcise Me, presented by the Harold Green Jewish Theatre, turns out not to be the kosher alternative to either Puppetry of the Penis or The Vagina Monologues. It isn't as frivolous as the first or as partisan as the second. Its a moderately likeable monologue in which a writerperformer called Yisrael Campbell tells us of his conversion from Catholicism to Judaism, a journey that I would guess is more frequently taken in the opposite direction.

Obviously Yisrael is not the name the protagonist was given at birth. He was originally called Christopher, a name that an Israeli rabbi told him - quite late on in the conversion process, apparently - it might be tactful to change. He grew up in Philadelphia where, he tells us, he became an alcoholic at the age of nine. Theres an implication it was partly to purge these early excesses that he turned to a different religion than the one in which he was raised. (His mother, he says, was briefly a nun. His aunt became and remained one. She was also a mental patient. Apparently there are mental institutions especially for nuns. Fascinating.) It's a major shortcoming of the show, though, that the author never delves very deeply into his own motives or mental processes. He tells us that his interest in Judaism was first sparked when a girlfriend gave him a copy of Exodus: the Leon Uris book, not the Old Testament one. He makes a joke of having been so influenced by a not-good novel that became a worse movie (starring Paul Newman, he says, as if this somehow compounded the crime) but he never offers a more sustained explanation.

In fact, it seems he underwent conversion in three stages, moving from Reform to Conservative to Orthodox, and undergoing two adult circumcisions in the process (that's on top of a non-religious one he received as an infant but that unhappily wasnt considered sufficient) and two ritual baths, one of them in deluxe circumstances in California, the other less sybaritically in Tel Aviv. He now lives full time in Israel, having originally committed to a couple of years there to qualify for full orthodoxy. Having attained it, he has gone, to use a most inappropriate metaphor, the whole hog, and appears before us clad in what he himself calls (having, he proudly notes, spent just two days in Toronto) the full Bathurst-and-Lawrence. It looks good on him, and he sounds good in it.

What he doesnt tell us is how, either in America or in Israel, he supported himself. You have to consult his program bio to discover he is a trained actor and comedian. The acting part shows, in his confidence and authority onstage, and I'm sure the stand-up experience helps there, too. This is despite the fact he isn't all that funny, though its a saving grace that he doesnt carry on as if he thinks he is. He does have one great line that, despite overwhelming temptation, I will refrain from quoting; I'll merely say that it has to do with handicapped parking permits and the healing qualities of the sidewalks of Jerusalem. I suspect, too, that he somehow inherited a Jewish cast of humour from his lapsed Catholic dad who, when declining to attend his son's first conversion, said hed wait 13 years and come to the bar mitzvah. The program also tells us Campbell belongs to "a group of comedians bringing awareness to the Palestinian-Israeli conflict and seeking peace through comedy." Id like to have heard something about that in the show, which all but skates over the complexities of the situation. It does include an account of how it feels in the aftermath of a bombing to wonder how many of your friends may have died: powerful in itself, but not very well connected to the main theme. Rather more blatantly dragged in is a protracted account of how his life was instantly changed when he became a father. You don't have to be Jewish or Catholic or both or neither to feel that; you just have to be human. This is, by its author's own now inaccurate description, "an off-Broadway show," with an American director, Sam Gold, whose listed credits are very interesting; his production here is barebones but hardly needs to be anything else

At the opposite extreme: The most visually and conceptually exciting theatre in Toronto has been happening, not for the first time, at the Canadian Opera Company. Its production of Gluck's Iphigenia in Tauris, in its compression and concentration, cut to the merciless heart of concentration and compression. Its Rigoletto was set in a plush 19thcentury men's club, brilliantly created by the great Canadian designer Michael Levine. The program notes claimed that this was a comment on the patriarchal society in which the story is set, but it was selling itself short; what we saw was a privileged group ganging up on all outsiders, including the crippled clowning protagonist; which is what the Verdi work is all about. (The singing in both productions was great, too, but you expect that.) ? Circumcise Me runs through Nov. 6 at the Jane Mallett Theatre.
