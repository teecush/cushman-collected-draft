THE ALCHEMIST

Festival Theatre, Stratford, Ont.

There are few better plays in the English language than Ben Jonsons The Alchemist, and fewer still that are harder to perform. Its problem is that it moves at two different speeds simultaneously. Its action is that of a quick-witted farce of roguery and deception. Its dialogue is a heady concoction of street slang and pseudo- scientific jargon, dense enough in its day and rendered even more daunting by the passage of 400 years.

Most productions turn into exhausting romps or enervating poetry recitals. (The Alchemist, most unusually for an Elizabethan or Jacobean comedy, is written entirely in verse.) Douglas Campbells Stratford production has the melancholy distinction of falling into both traps, though it is more consistent in the second. It crawls through the text without illuminating it, occasionally throwing in an arbitrary gag. As a farce, it lacks urgency: as a picture of a society, it lacks any kind of reality.

Jonson wanted both. He set the play in his own London, malevolent and malodorous, teeming with trickery and plagued by plague. A citizen, fearful of infection, has quit town and left his house in the care of his butler, who promptly invites a con man and a prostitute onto the premises.

Alchemy is their game -- the promise to turn all metals into gold by means of the mythical Philosopher's Stone -- with a bit of fortune-telling and prostitution on the side. Over a couple of hours, stage time more or less mirroring real time, we watch them entertaining half a dozen different clients, adopting different personae to swindle each one, and therefore under considerable pressure to keep them from colliding. Finally, the master returns and the syndicate, always fragile, breaks up.

The play, teeming as it is with local colour, works best in the trappings of its own time. Failing that, it needs to be set in some other recognizable world. Campbell's production, designed by John Colm Leberg, recklessly mixes periods, presumably on the grounds that greed and gullibility are eternal. So they are; but in order to draw general conclusions we have to believe in specific examples.

From time to time, this show brings on a writhing chorus in bodysuits to provide a spooky atmosphere. Just who are they meant to be? They cant be on the alchemists payroll -- he's too cheap and too secretive to have one. So they're real spirits? But that would mean he does have magical powers, which means the play is rubbish. Or are they just there to give the tourists, supernaturally unsated by The Tempest and A Midsummer Nights Dream and even Dracula, more of their moneys worth? Either it's an incredibly dumb decision or an incredibly insulting one.

The production's one merit is that it brings out the rivalry of the conspirators. The play starts with a quarrel between Face, the butler, (Benedict Campbell) and Subtle, the mystagogue, (Keith Dinicol) that is here taken far too ponderously but that does prepare the ground for some increasingly nasty needling later on.

But there is no joy in the intrigues that make up the bulk of the play, and no suspense. The one successful production of this play I have seen was directed by Trevor Nunn, with Ian McKellen brilliant as Face. Nunns version worked by making life as difficult for the tricksters as possible; for each encounter they had to re-arrange the furniture and don new disguises, and they had to do it at breakneck speed, increasingly so as events threatened to spiral out of control.

Here, the actors slip unconcernedly into their various get-ups, and then proceed to let the costumes play them.

As Dol Common, the resident temptress (a famous but underwritten role), Diane DAquila has more presence than either of her gentlemen friends, and most of the dupes manage to strike a spark or two. James Blendick as Sir Epicure Mammon, the visionary voluptuary, has some grandiloquence if not comic grandeur; his skeptical friend Surly (Bradford Farwell) initially takes the stage with more authority than anyone else, but loses it when he goes into disguise as a Spanish grandee.

The two comic Puritans, wonderfully written satires on greasy fundamentalist hypocrisy, are unprecedentedly unfunny. Michael Therriault as Abel Drugger, the shy tobacconist, is pretty good, and Thom Benson as an aspiring tough boy from the country, reasonably equipped with a kilt and a burr, even better.

The returning householder is Bernard Hopkins who is saddled, so to speak, with an entrance on a pantomime horse but manages in his sterner moments to rise above it. We also get Keystone Kops and a transvestite priest.

It is to weep.

- In repertory until Oct. 30.
