BIGGER THAN JESUS

Factory Theatre, Toronto

- - -

Rick Miller is best known as author and performer of MacHomer, in which he showed that he could recite the text of Macbeth in the voices, all the voices, of the characters from The Simpsons. This was clever, since he was doing, live and solo, what it takes more than half-a-dozen unseen actors to accomplish on TV. It was also pointless, partly because he was merely imitating the characterizations that others had created, and partly because his extended party-trick cast no light, satirical or otherwise, on either Springfield or Shakespeare.

Miller's new show, Bigger Than Jesus, is better than MacHomer, far better, and also much more ambitious. It's a seriously comic inquiry into Christianity, and especially into the reputation and historical effects of its founder. Parts of it are brilliant and other parts silly. Mathematically the silly parts predominate, but the brilliant parts are the ones you'll remember.

Miller appears before us as a black-clad young man whose self- effacing vocal manner co-exists with absolute physical and technical command. He is, he tells us, 33 and a lapsed Catholic, two highly significant circumstances. The second of them prompts him to structure his show in the form of a Mass, a unifying concept that, from this non-Catholic perspective, fails to unify. I just had to grab at the different sections as they passed.

One of the best of them comes at the beginning. Miller gives us a slick, fast, witty lecture on the Gospels as history. His conclusion is that, in that light, they don't amount to much, having been written down centuries after the events of which they profess to be eyewitness reports. He comments on the threat that dissenting Jewish sects posed to the Roman government of Judea. He explains, grimly and dispassionately, the physical details of crucifixion. He outlines the paradoxes of a revolutionary preacher having been adopted as a symbol of political and religious repression, of a charitable activist being used to promote intolerance, of a Jew becoming the arch-pretext for anti-Semitism. None of this, of course, is new, but Miller, stating rather than hammering, makes it urgent.

Much of what follows is, unhappily, not so much a development of the argument as a series of whimsical re-statements. There is a long sermonizing turn by a black-sounding preacher who has the fervour of a fundamentalist but the humour of a humanist; that may sound like a stimulating combination but it plays like self-indulgence, Miller back in funny-voice mode. There's a flight on something called Air Jesus. There are frequent returns to the MacHomer fallacy that pop- culture references are automatically hilarious: The four evangelists are compared to the Beatles (Mark is Ringo, Matthew is George, Luke is Paul, John is John) and, in a re-enactment of the Last Supper with toy-figures, H. Simpson himself reappears as Judas. In sequences like these, Bigger Than Jesus is smaller than Jesus Christ Superstar.

It always looks good; the show is presented by Necessary Angel, which seems appropriate, and is the company's first production by its new director, Daniel Brooks, who is also credited, with similarly solemn overtones, as co-creator. This, following Cul-de- Sac, is the second occasion this season on which, through timing and technology, Brooks has taken a one-man show to a higher plane. There is especially striking use of instant video, affording us overhead views of Miller in crucified pose, this nice inquiring young man turned into a bewildered martyr.

The show's second great sequence does indeed come when Miller himself turns into Jesus; or rather into a cross -- pun not intended but welcomed -- between Jesus and a baffled comedian facing a tough house. He delivers, very well, some of the most challenging sentences in the New Testament, ending with "love one another." He pauses. "Any questions?" he asks hopefully. He tries a few more quips. All of them die. He still goes on standing there. This says something, something both moving and disturbing, though I'd hesitate to say exactly what. The show also demonstrates, unexpectedly though not too surprisingly, that Miller can sing.
