The title Wicked is an understatement. This musical, a hit on Broadway last season, is diabolical. One thing that can be said for it, though, is that it has a nerve. That's what it takes to begin a show that's meant to be a sort of parallel text to The Wizard of Oz with a song proclaiming, just like the first big number in the classic movie, that "the wicked witch is dead."

Which old witch? Well, not the one you thought. The musical begins close to the end of the familiar story, with the death of the Wicked Witch of the West -- as opposed to her sister, the Wicked Witch of the East, whose flattening by a stray house from Kansas set the original revenge plot in motion. Through the magic of flashback, we now get to see that plot from a new angle. Glinda the Good Witch, who seems to make a career out of hanging around near the corpses of rival sorceresses, volunteers to tell us the truth about the newly deceased, whose name, this is news, was Elphaba. It seems Elphaba wasn't really old, nor -- more important -- was she really wicked. Those were just malicious rumours spread by the kind of people who write stories and believed by the kind of people who read them.

There is, of course, a fallacy here. There is no "truth" about fictitious characters, waiting to be uncovered. The only reality the Oz characters have is the one bestowed upon them by their original author, L. Frank Baum. There is nothing to stop subsequent scribes from using his fictions to create their own alternate universe. But there are certain rules that need to be followed if the exercise is to be any fun.

Wicked trades on our knowledge of the basic story, especially in its MGM incarnation. Its book (by Winnie Holtzman, from a novel by Gregory Maguire) gets a few chuckles from the sidelong glances it takes at the adventures of Dorothy (who never actually appears), the Wizard, the Tin Man et al., just as Tom Stoppard's Rosencrantz and Guildenstern Are Dead gets huge laughs from the exactness with which it dovetails with Hamlet. Wicked, however, after yapping at its source's heels for most of the evening, runs away from it when the going gets rough. Narrative consistency goes by the board. I defy anyone to make sense of what happens at the end of this show to the Witch and the Scarecrow.

Elphaba here isn't a villain; she's an abused minority of one, being the only person, even in Oz, with its Emerald City, who is green. She owes her complexion to a dalliance of her mother's with a mysterious stranger who passed through town bearing a talismanic bottle of something that looks like mouthwash.

It seems nobody in this society is born a witch; you have to learn it. Elphaba and her crippled sister, Nessarose, go off to the local equivalent of Hogwarts (someone must even now be at work on Harry Potter: The Musical, though maybe they're still trying to get the rights), where another student is the fair, spoiled and crushingly popular Glinda, who progresses from persecuting Elphaba to feeling sorry for her.

Elphaba, the spunky waif, comes over as the Liza Minnelli character (even, piquantly enough, the Judy Garland character), especially as performed here, very well, by Stephanie J. Block, fresh from playing Liza on Broadway in the unrelated Boy From Oz. Kendra Kassebaum, despite having the right spun-sugar voice and looks, gets less out of the co-starring role of Glinda, which is unfortunate, as Glinda has the more to offer. Her attitudes and actions oscillate, while Elphaba simply goes from being misunderstood to being even more misunderstood. Even when she accepts the supernatural identity everybody is trying to force on her (in a strenuously unmemorable Act One finale titled Defying Gravity), it doesn't lead to her doing anything even vaguely malevolent. Anyone looking for the moral complexity of Into the Woods ("witches can be right / giants can be good / you decide what's right / you decide what's good") is going to be disappointed.

It's not easy being green, but it has its compensations. Elphaba can make an environmental statement without even opening her mouth. She also stands up for the animals, who are being prevented from teaching in schools (Timothy Britten Parker is both charming and touching as a learned goat who becomes a learned scapegoat) and even from talking. Oz is turning into a police state, and Elphaba wants to stop it. The show makes a great parade of being against stereotypes, but it's sacrificed a lively original to a virtuous stereotype of its own. It may act brave, but it doesn't even have the guts to deliver the unhappy ending its own narrative logic clearly demands. Earnest and preachy, it's one of the new-style humourless musicals, only with a few jokes thrown in. The tone is pop-Oprah. Or is that a tautology?

This brings us to Stephen Schwartz's music and lyrics. Schwartz used to write tinkly pop scores (Godspell, Pippin) relieved by the occasional really nice tune. He has progressed to writing bombastic pop scores (Children of Eden, this one) also relieved by the occasional really nice tune. The prize here is I'm Not That Girl, a sweet addition to Broadway's treasury of plain-Jane laments, sung by Elphaba when she believes the cutest boy in school fancies Glinda rather than her. (She's wrong, of course.) The girls' closing duet, For Good, also has appeal. It's surprisingly quiet, for one thing, and its key line -- "who can say if I've been changed for the better -- I have been changed for good" -- if not the wittiest pun you've ever heard, is still neat and makes a point. But the other emotional numbers, especially Elphaba's various epiphanies, simply scream the place down (they also bring the house down, but, given today's debased tastes in musicals, they would), while most of the humorous ones are very self-conscious. A word about trick-rhymes: They work only if a) they're part of an overall playful style; b) they reinforce metre and sense; c) the author can keep them coming, unforced. Schwartz incorporates them as and when they occur to him, then stops. He and his librettist also go in, a lot, for words like "rejoicify" and "disgustified." I don't know what Yip Harburg, who wrote the lyrics for The Wizard of Oz and for whom the invention and up-ending of words were the brilliant expression of a puckish imagination, would have said about these. But I can imagine, and it wouldn't be pretty.

I doubt he would have approved of this show's take on the Cowardly Lion, a caged cub who seems a very poor exchange for Bert Lahr rhyming "dinosaurus" with "king of the faurus." Or of its Munchkins, who are remarkably large. Or indeed of the Wizard himself -- the show is at pains to prove he's a charlatan, but we knew that coming in. The actor, David Garrison, spends the evening frantically searching for something to play. Joe Mantello's production also looks terrible, with heavy costumes and an even heavier set. The renowned Eugene Lee has designed a set of doors that regularly slide apart to reveal what appear to be the interstices of a giant clock. It takes up a lot of space, that being the only excuse I can think of for Wayne Cilento's constricted choreography, which rivals his work on Aida for atrocity. The whole thing resembles a Ross Petty panto, with a slightly larger budget and considerably less wit. Come back, Bat Boy, all is forgiven.

David Macfarlane's Fishwrap takes us into the lair of a freelance journalist who seems, at first glance, to be living in Beckettian squalor, as if this one-man play were Krapp's Last Laptop. It turns out he has the renovators in. Also, he has lost his regular arts column and his bicycle. So far, so autobiographical; we have learned as much from interviews with the author in various papers, including, quixotically, the one that fired him. Vogel, his onstage alter ego, has also contracted a tropical disease and been deserted by his wife, and these things -- as revealed in those same interviews -- are fictitious. Much of the play is a duet for Vogel and his alter ego, an older, harder-bitten (though not, in his case, by African insects) journo called Teddy. So much I learned later from consulting the printed script. In the piece itself Teddy's name isn't mentioned, and in fact I never even guessed there were two of them. I merely thought that the actor, John Jarvis, was talking in funny voices.

The gripes about the newspaper business are the play's most amusing things, and not just, I fancy, to professionals. Too many of them have been given away in advance, but there are also lines about the habits of builders that had me laughing hard. I was moved, too, by the moment of realization concerning the absent Mrs. Vogel, and intrigued by Mr. Vogel's apparent inability to distinguish his feelings for her from his feelings for his bike.

In fact, the play and Jarvis's performance, as acrobatic physically as vocally, kept me happy until the last stretch when both turn mawkish. Vogel recalls, at lyrical length, his first job, a paper route that included, talk about wheels turning full circle, an old journalist. We are meant to be touched, and I wasn't.
