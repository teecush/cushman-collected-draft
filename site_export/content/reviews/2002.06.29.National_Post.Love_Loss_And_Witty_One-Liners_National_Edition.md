THE PLAY GOES ON by Neil Simon

Touchstone/Simon & Schuster $21.50

- - -

One of my favourite moments in a Neil Simon play comes in The Gingerbread Lady, when someone asks the protagonist, "Can I say something? and she snaps back, Only if it makes me laugh." This heroine, a compulsive wisecracker herself, seems to have released something in Simon. He seems to feel here the same carnival freedom he does when he writes books for musicals. "Yes says the hero of They're Playing Our Song, one such work, as he reports the breakdown of his car to the American Automobile Association, I'm a member; I even go to the meetings."

Much of the time, though, Simons jokes are fettered by the demands of psychological realism and the well-made play, often dealing with ostensibly painful subjects. This means the plays are frequently not as funny as one feels they might be -- the one- liners, though they usually make you laugh at the time, are oddly unquotable -- and also not as serious as they might be. This is not to accuse Simon of pandering. Like any decent writer, he writes up to his own limits. It has been his fortune -- in all senses -- that his limits have generally coincided with those of his audience.

Like the plays, this volume of memoirs, Simons second, goes down easily -- remarkably easily, given that it begins with the death of his beloved first wife and takes in two subsequent marriages that ended in divorce. There are plenty of good times recorded as well. At the point at which the book begins, Simon -- with Barefoot in the Park, The Odd Couple and Plaza Suite under his belt -- was already the most prosperous and popular playwright in the history of Broadway. This book chronicles the writing of more hit plays, and such lines-round-the-block movies as The Goodbye Girl. It also sees this quintessential New Yorker move, somewhat reluctantly, to California, and find that -- some of the time, at least -- he likes it. His relationships begin well -- the lovers' meeting is cute -- almost as if they were in a play or a movie, and naturally one cheers them on. There are also endearing moments of contact with children, step-children and grandchildren. All the same, it's largely a chronicle of loss and loneliness. Its told with an admirable lack of self-pity, but it speaks freely of pain, though it hardly ever makes us feel it.

Simon writes consummately for a live audience; he knows instinctively and precisely how to play with its reactions. (Many of his films, lacking that vital current between stage and auditorium, are dead by comparison.) His dialogue needs actors to bring it alive. The dialogue in this book, of course, does not have that advantage; and besides, the presence of dialogue is, in itself, questionable. It immediately -- unless you grant the writer the gift of total recall -- turns memoir into fiction. Which, of course, it may well be, but Simon does not seem to have any such deconstructive method in mind. He is merely trying to liven up his story the best way he knows how. You end up accepting the convention, but at a cost.

In part, the book is the autobiography of an autobiographer. Simon, in the period it covers, had increasingly taken to basing his plays on his own earlier life. "Writing, he says, is not always an act of creation. Sometimes ... its like a poison that inhabits your being, and the only way to get rid of it is to have the pen press deeply and quickly on the empty pages.... Those writers who go to the deepest and darkest of agonies are the ones who have lived there as well.... I wish I could go deeper, further, but then again I think as I do. I do make the effort if not the entire journey." That remarkably echoes the way many of us respond to Simon's plays: We think they're good and wish they were better.

The play he is introducing at this point is Chapter Two, about a novelist whose second marriage is threatened by his inability to forget his first. It contains a long speech in which the woman tells the man exactly what she thinks about the way in which he is underrating her and taking her for granted. It is, we are told, based very closely on a diatribe directed at Simon in similar circumstances by his own second wife, the actress Marsha Mason. In life, apparently, he thought it magnificent. In the play, I find it, Im afraid, stagy. But there you go. It helped the Simon-Mason marriage, but did not, in the end, save it. (Though Mason did play her own role, so to speak, in the movie version.) Their fictional equivalents -- in classic storm-weathering Broadway fashion -- do stay together; at least the audience is given no reason to believe they wont. The book tries to go more deeply into the failure of that marriage, and of Simon's subsequent one to a magazine writer, Diane Landers, but in neither case do we really find out why things fell apart.

Chapter Two was not, at the time, widely publicized as being autobiographical. Several of Simon's later plays were. There was the Depression idyll Brighton Beach Memoirs (maybe his best play, a seamless fantasia), drawn from his own adolescence, and its two sequels -- Biloxi Blues, derived fom his military service, and Broadway Bound, whose title suggests that its about the beginning of his writing career, but which is really about his mother, a martyr to housework and to life generally, sustained by the memory of the one night in her youth when she danced with George Raft. In real life, it had been George Burns, but Simon thought a Latin lover would sound more romantic than a Jewish comic. The story as told in the book has a charming coda, when the octogenarian Burns, appearing for Simon in the film version of The Sunshine Boys, is introduced to Mrs. Simon and recognizes her instantly.)

Simon is very interesting on how the plays depart from their historical moorings and why. This is especially helpful when he gets to Lost in Yonkers, which seems to be about the same characters as the trilogy but cannot be factually reconciled with it, almost as if it were going on in a parallel universe. He also, in confessedly self-indulgent style, uses the plays as pretexts to examine parts of his life that, incidentally, long predate the period the book is supposed to be dealing with. (So Biloxi Blues occasions reminiscences about his enforced stint as editor, and sole writer, of the official Army Air Force newspaper: "I was a tough editor .... I wanted to fire me but that didnt seem practical.")

The best bits, though, are the backstage stories about the triumphs and traumas of getting plays on. Simon says, more or less, that it's only when he's writing that he really knows who he is, and I suspect he knows it best when he's writing his plays, turning fact artfully into fiction rather than trying laboriously to turn fiction back into fact, as he is doing here. Which is as it should be: What interests us about a playwright is his plays.
