10 DAYS ON EARTH

Berkeley Street Theatre, Toronto

Ronnie Burketts marionettes can move you in ways unavailable to actors of mere flesh and blood. In his new piece, 10 Days on Earth, he shows us a man named Darrell who has a chronological age of about 50 and a mental age of about 10. All his life he has lived with, and been cared for by, his single mother. One night she dies in her bed. Darrell simply thinks that, for whatever reason, she doesnt want to get up. So, for the 10 days denoted in the title, he goes about his business, making himself sandwiches, taking the bus to and from his job at a shoe-shine stand, talking to his few friends and preparing for Halloween.

Since Darrell behaves like a child we react to him as powerfully as if he actually were one. And because he's a puppet, we don't feel manipulated; he does that for us, so to speak, and its all in plain view with Burkett, the master of all puppet-masters, standing at the top of the stage, pulling all the characters strings. When Darrell, small, neat and dignified in his suit and bow-tie, returns home each evening and calls out to his non-appearing mom, the effect is heartbreaking and, because it's been pared down to essentials, not at all mawkish.

At least it works that way the first couple of times. After that, of course, the effect begins to wear off; I didn't actually resent the repetitions, as theyre so clearly part of the package, but I began to wish for something more. Burkett -- who is, as usual, his own writer, director, designer and supplier of all the voices, including some nifty singing -- is at pains to supply it, but he isnt always successful. He has spoken of the play as being a celebration of maternal love, a description calculated to elicit an incredulous blink, since Ivy, Darrell's mother, only appears briefly and in flashbacks. But at second blush, it's true. We feel the quality of her love through the effects of its forcible removal; and the more you think about it, the more overwhelming it seems. In the one extended encounter between mother and son, she catches him masturbating and counsels him to do it behind closed doors, like -- as she all but spells out -- she does. It sounds embarrassing, if not grotesque, but in fact it says something serious about the needs of both of them, and does so seriously and -- if its the word I want -- touchingly.

Theres also a powerfully suggestive scene in which Ivy, on her one night out a week, re-encounters Darrell's father (and namesake) who refuses to go home with her. But a prologue that sets up the whole situation is confusing, and some of the supplementary material feels like padding. One of Darrell's friends is a homeless man called Lloyd, this being a pun on Lord; he thinks hes God -- literally -- and he despises his creations, except for Darrell, who shares his lunch with him. Lloyd has two angry tirades; the first one makes a point, the second just repeats it.

Darrell also has a girlfriend named Patsy, with whom his scenes are shorter and more effective. But most of his feelings are directed towards a storybook character, a smart red-coated hound called Honeydog with whom he identifies and whose adventures are, at regular intervals, acted out. Its characters are on wires thicker and more visible than those of the humans in the main plot; we have, in fact, a puppet show within a puppet show, which may be a first. These inset scenes give us a taste of Burketts technical virtuosity, especially when a homing pigeon collapses inwards and turns into a multi-coloured balloon. These interludes may be visually striking but they're verbally dull; the introduction of a sheep called Blanche DuBaa comes off as a pale imitation of Sesame Street ("Meryl Sheep, a sheep's sheep" -- is she still around?). Whats worse, they have no discernible relevance to the main action. Honeydog and his protege-companion, a duck named Little Burp, may invert the mother-son relationship of Ivy and Darrell, but that doesnt get us far.

The play's final gesture is sad but hopeful; there are people who will look out for Darrell but they'll never replace what hes lost. Its nice but unsatisfying. Ever since the superb Street of Blood, Burkett seems to have been looking for a story spacious enough to match his talents; this isn't it, and his decision to stay aloft of his characters, rather than moving among them as in previous shows, seems to me regrettable. Left to operate on their own scale, his creations seem, paradoxically, not less like puppets but more. The show completes a season of CanStage productions that have, without exception, been disappointing or worse. It doesn't break the jinx, but it puts a dent in it.
