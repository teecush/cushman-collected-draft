A CHORUS OF DISAPPROVAL

Premiere Dance Theatre, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - For sheer volume of output, Alan Ayckbourn long ago outran comparison with any other contemporary playwright. Unfairly, he is as talented as he is prolific. To find a match for him you would probably have to go back to the 19th-century novel, and especially to Balzac. Ayckbourn, too, though without himself slapping that label on it, has been assembling an ongoing Human Comedy, an amused, appalled depiction of a venal society at odds with itself: in his case, England from the late 1960s until now. With the exception of Bedroom Farce (a minor classic), the roster contains no outright masterpieces, but the canvas entire is an extraordinary achievement; once you've seen one or two sections of it, you want to collect them all.

Several of them are set in Pendon, an imaginary town whose location has never been disclosed but which it is tempting to identify with Scarborough, the North Eastern spa that has been Ayckbourn's base for years and where he runs a theatre-in-the-round where his own and many other plays get tried out. (Quite apart from his career as a dramatist, his record as a director, facilitator and encourager of other people's talents has been exemplary.) A Chorus of Disapproval introduces us to the Pendon Amateur Operatic Society (PAOS), who are engaged in putting on The Beggar's Opera. That link to a more canonical classic may well have been the cue for this new production by Soulpepper (they themselves are giving concert performances of The Beggar's Opera this summer), and the fact that the play was produced in London by the British National Theatre probably didn't hurt its chances either. Anyway, it occasions one of Soulpepper's best productions to date.

Ayckbourn likes playing games with time, and this show starts with a curtain call. PAOS are at the end of their first night, and the director steps forward to thank everybody, with especial reference to Guy, who stepped in at the last moment to play the leading role of Captain Macheath. Everybody applauds, then the curtain falls and Guy, turning to his fellow actors, finds himself the object of dirty looks all round. The rest of the play shows how he got there.

A Chorus of Disapproval isn't really a backstage play. It's less about a dramatic society than about a real society whose members -- some complacent, some insecure, and many mysteriously both -- are jockeying for position in rehearsal, just as they do in their everyday lives. The success or failure is not -- for us or for most of them -- a consuming issue. Backstage plays tend to be about professionals; I'm probably forgetting something, but I think this may be the first major play about amateur actors since A Midsummer Night's Dream.

The London production was nearly 20 years ago, and my memories of it are hazy, but Albert Schultz's production seems to have pushed the play more in the direction of a theatrical in-joke than did Ayckbourn's own. As before, songs from The Beggar's Opera itself are used to comment on the characters in the main story. A ballad about greedy lawyers reflects on the director, who in private life is a hopeless rather than actively crooked solicitor. The familiar "how happy could I be with either" follows -- though, in typical Ayckbourn fashion, it doesn't directly follow -- a scene showing Guy being fought over by two female admirers.

Soulpepper, though, go further and offer us whole routines. I confess to a slight queasiness at seeing skilled professional actors making fun of amateurs (as opposed to making fun of less skilled professionals, as in Noises Off) but the burlesques are so hilarious that I gave up worrying. There is also, reflecting Soulpepper's own status as established town favourites, a certain amount of self- satire. Nancy Palk wafts weightily about as a heavily pearled senior member, vocally insecure but financially impregnable, who -- in contrast to some of this actress' previous great ladies -- wants everything of Guy except his body. A speech recalling PAOS' disastrous production of The Sound of Music is all the funnier for being delivered by C. David Johnson, last year's Captain von Trapp at Stratford.

Schultz's production is a bravura success on every level. Cunningly and imaginatively staged, it also keeps the tricky balance between characters. This is a play with two male leads. There is Guy, who indeed is just a guy: a blank page on whom his new acquaintances can write whatever pleases them. Two married women -- one of whom has been having a lot of indiscriminate sex and the other very little sex of any kind -- throw themselves at him. He has a low-level job with a big company, and this leads three men (and their wives) to seek his help in a spot of insider trading. He makes no promises to anyone, but everyone assumes he has; meanwhile, he progresses from one role in The Beggar's Opera to another, ending up with the lead. David Storch makes him gently unassuming, and -- to his frustration as well as everyone else's -- as unknowable at the end as at the start. Or probably more.

This clears the space for Dafydd, the director, whom Ted Dykstra plays, entrancingly, as a Celtic mole with an enquiring stoop. He approaches the show with a professional commitment that he realizes to be somewhat ludicrous, given that his real job -- and his marriage -- are in tatters. A virtuoso scene shows him directing a lighting rehearsal while his wife and Guy, trying for a tryst, move in and out of the pools of illumination he insists on creating. As his spouse, Susan Coyne trumps her fine Hermione in The Winter's Tale -- a woman suspected by her husband and championed by everyone else -- with a beautifully detailed portrait of her exact opposite. Sharpness is her note: She is sharply frustrated, sharply angry and sharply in love.

Among the supports, Oliver Dennis gives great joy as an especially incompetent player, tiptoeing through his own zone of terror, with Denise Fergusson as the wife who protects him as well as she dares. I feel, as I always have, that the swinging couple (C. David Johnson and Nicole Underhay) are done with excessively broad strokes, and that the rich bore (Jim Warren) who decides that Guy is Scots and will not be told otherwise, can begin to bore us. But there is not a performance that is less than true or less than funny.

When he first emerged, Ayckbourn was routinely compared to Neil Simon -- on the grounds that they were both comic and both successful -- but the comparison has long ceased to make sense. Simon writes wisecracks, often very good ones; Ayckbourn comes up with a quotable joke. The funniest in this play is entirely a matter of situation, and it involves the person who hears it in depths of humiliation that Simon, as far as I know, has never plumbed; Ayckbourn being Ayckbourn, they are not prolonged.

Simon gives his characters little social or political context, and is therefore compelled, when he drops the clown's mask, to get mushy. Ayckbourn, the writer, hasn't a sentimental bone in his corpus. He can show suffering (and also embarrassment, a milder form of pain that is sometimes indistinguishable from it) and he never begs our pity for it. He can, sometimes though not in this play, be moving, but generally he is as unknowable as Guy himself, which can be frustrating. After all these years, he still confounds everybody's chief preconception about a commercial playwright: He tells no lies.
