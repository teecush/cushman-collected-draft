SALT-WATER MOON

Young Centre for the Performing Arts, Toronto

---

Soulpepper sems to be working its way through David French's Mercer cycle, a set of plays that chugs through mid-20th-century Canadian history by following the fortunes of a Newfoundland family who, like the author's own, moved to Toronto. The company had a big success last year with Leaving Home, the first-written play of the series; it now jumps ahead to the third, Salt-Water Moon. But it also jumps back, since Salt-Water Moon shows the small-town courtship of the young couple whom we met in Leaving Home as fractious parents, marooned in the metropolis.

Jacob Mercer has been away from home for a spell -- in Toronto, in fact -- and he comes back to win Mary Snow, with whom he had once spent the night and whom he believes still loves him. So, indeed, she does -- otherwise there'd be no other plays in the cycle -- but there is the small matter that in Jacob's absence Mary has got engaged to someone else, a solid young citizen with prospects. Mary is "in service;" Jacob arrives at her employers' door, on a balmy night in 1925 when everyone else in town is off at a funeral, staking everything on his own charm.

Here he has a lot in common with the play, the clue to which is its beguiling title. Whatever painful baggage the two characters and their unseen community may be presumed to be carrying, this is a lightweight mood-piece. The mood is beautifully realized in Ted Dykstra's production, which has the enhancements of Victoria Wallace's set (practically archetypal in its porch, garden and picket fence), Leigh Ann Vardy's melancholy seductive lighting and a haunting sound-scape with the concluding bonus of a recorded song from Melanie Doane. Most of all it has the Jacob of Jeff Lillico, a sharp-edged and sharp-witted scapegrace with heartsick vulnerability at his core, ready to tell any number of lies in the service of what he genuinely feels. Shakespeare in All's Well That Ends Well has a character describe our lives as "a mingled yarn, good and bad together," yarn meaning wool. Jacob's life is a series of mingled yarns, the kind you tell, true and false together. Lillico spins them with enchanting speed and control, his exhilarating expertise even extending to some momentary spasms of song and dance. He's had the practice. This actor has specialized in musicals at the Shaw, modern classics at Soulpepper and is about to embark on a full round of Shakespeare at Stratford. This is playing the system in a very good way. And if he doesn't gather a Dora for his present performance, I am fully prepared to be hornswoggled.

The play's dynamic is that Jacob's newly citified confidence meets Mary's home-brewed suspicions, partly rooted in her status as an orphan, with a younger sister whom she is determined to rescue from a brutal-sounding institution: hence her marriage plans. Krystin Pellerin makes these revelations very affecting. She plays Mary's stony anger, though, at the expense of conveying any dormant affection for Jacob; so the pre-ordained happy ending, though it still gladdens the heart, seems arbitrary. It isn't easy to imagine either of these youngsters growing into the middle-aged figures who we saw portrayed, only last year, by Kenneth Welsh and Diane D'Aquila. Pellerin does bring an authentic Newfie sound to the play; with Lillico's accent you have to believe very strongly in the transformative powers of a year spent in Ontario.

Salt-Water Moon is a slighter play than Leaving Home, but it's also more likeable and has fewer loose ends. Its principal weakness is that though we hear, evocatively, about the decline of the local fisheries and, less evocatively, about the shadowing trauma of the recent war, neither theme seems to have entered into the blood and being of the couple we actually see. It's background, part of the "this is our country, look at it" aesthetic that underlies most of French's plays, not to mention others of the time, such as David Fennario's Balconville. It was probably a necessary phase for Canadian drama to pass through, but it's difficult to imagine the plays being taken as seriously anywhere else. But here is here, and the plays have cut a distinctive new panel in Soulpepper's tapestry. A company whose repertory roams the world needs to come home now and then. - Salt-Water Moon runs until Jan. 31. Call 416-866-8666 for more information.
