Ken Gass' Canadian Rep Theatre - known in some quarters as the Factory-in-exile - begins operations with Pacamambo, a play by the Lebanese-Quebecois playwright Wajdi Mouawad that was originally designed for young audiences and should probably be approached with that in mind.

It begins with a contest in shouting: an infallible way of grabbing attention in the theatre (and outside it, for that matter) but one calculated to produce more heat than light. A psychiatrist is interrogating a young girl named Julie about some very peculiar behaviour; she had been found in a the basement of her grandmother's house, keeping watch over the grandmother's body, death having occurred 19 days previously. Julie initially refuses to offer any explanation, delivering her denials in Amy Keating's performance with a feral intensity that's very powerful in itself but doesn't have much to do with what she shows us later. The shrink, played by Karen Robinson, seems herself to have been driven round the bend, or at least to the end of her tether; she responds to Julie by yelling right back at her, an approach both ethically and dramatically dubious. You feel like sitting her down in front of Peter Shaffer's Equus, in which a psychiatrist dealt gently and patiently with an equally obstreperous child patient and got results.

Still, Julie does eventually open up, and flashes us back to the scene of the trauma. Her grandmother, called Marie Marie, had died when Julie was visiting, death being depicted here as a visit from The Moon, and causing grandma's bed to rise in the air, leaving her clinging to its now vertical frame: a strong image, though the sequence might benefit from some more balletically elaborate staging. Julie, whose only companion is now a talking dog named Growl, cannot accept the loss; she is torn between caring for the corpse and trying to resuscitate it, the latter process including emptying all the bottles of perfume that granny had stashed in her bottom drawer. Above all, she remembers what Marie Marie had told her about a land called Pacamambo, "the land of universal empathy" where people respond to one another as people, without regard to colour, which apparently is not the way they behave at Julie's school. These speeches seem preachy and simplistic, even for a young audience's play: perhaps especially for a young audience's play, though given the tensions in both halves of the author's own dual background, they have a certain theoretical sting.

It's unclear whether Julie wants to give her grandmother a nice sendoff to Pacamambo or actually to join her there; either way, she later disarmingly admits that she knows the place isn't real. It's one of the play's best moments, and keys also into the great strength of Keating's performance, which is to find the practicality in whimsy. Whimsy is here in plenty; you only have to look at the characters' names. Growl the dog admits, not quite so disarmingly, to her own inherent improbability, and is anyway delightfully played by Michelle Polak. Kyra Harper's Marie Marie is wise and endearing, even from beyond the grave. Robinson redeems her psychiatric excesses when she re-appears as Death herself, svelte and implacable though perplexed by the particular circumstances; she arrives to announce that though in the fullness of time she will indeed get Julie (and presumably her medium-sized dog too) that event is a long way off and right now she should get over herself. It works, which may be the point of the doubling. "I wish you" says Death in parting "a long life." It's the best moment in the best scene of the play. Whether it's the best way to acclimatize children to the idea that nobody goes on forever I'm not sure, but it resonates for grown-ups.

The whole play is an effective fable, far preferable to Mouawad's more grandiose attempts to forge his own mythology out of real-world ethic and political conflicts. For an adult evening out, though, and for all the virtues of the acting, it's thin.
