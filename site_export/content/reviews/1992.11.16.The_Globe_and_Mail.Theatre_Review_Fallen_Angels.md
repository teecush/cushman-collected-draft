THEATRE REVIEW FALLEN ANGELS

Monday, November 16, 1992

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Toronto ONT -- WRITTEN BY NOEL COWARD Directed by Marti Maraden Starring Rosemary Dunsmore and Fiona Reid At the Bluma Appel Theatre Toronto Rating: Delightfully decadent in its day, Noel Coward's Fallen Angels is played today for the comic effect that has always existed in the text New production revives discarded Coward BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Toronto WHERE I come from, revivals of Noel Coward comedies are a feature of the landscape or, for a critic, an occupational hazard. The danger part is that the West End of London long ago decided that its favourite son, the boy-genius who persuades them that nothing has changed and supper is still being served at the Savoy Grill after the show, wrote only four plays worth bothering with: Hay Fever, Private Lives, Blithe Spirit and Present Laughter. Others of course are tried from time to time, but they are generally found wanting and replaced with a shudder. In my day, one of these discards was Fallen Angels; some distinguished players performed it conscientiously, but conscience cannot make Cowards of us all.

So Marti Maraden's sharp and funny production is a bit of a revelation, hauling the play right up into the pantheon. In its day, which was 1925, Fallen Angels was a shocker: ironic considering its author's crusty attitude toward the playwrights of the 1950s and 1960s, but that kind of mutation is one of time's favourite tricks. Fallen Angels presents two society ladies who (a) had sex with the same man before marrying two other men; (b) are eager to do it again when the old flame flickers back into their lives, seven years on; and (c) while awaiting his arrival, get very very drunk and very very jealous.

The CSC program prattles about this being "delightfully decadent" but grown-ups in the audience are unlikely to be so easily thrilled. All that can tickle us now is the contrast between the heroines' unctuous primness before temptation and their lubricious abandon after it. This production plays both moods to their respective hilts; even more important, it keeps them in constant tension. Every line and every moment is mined for comic effect but hardly anything is imposed. It was all there in the text, just waiting.

The play begins, most reassuringly, with breakfast: the kind you hardly get any more, on stage or off. Fiona Reid's Julia, addressing her stodgy spouse across the marmalade, remarks that "Muriel Fenwick is divorcing her husband." Her acerbic delivery, light and inventive, is offset whenever she smiles by a display of eager, nervous teeth. When she says of her lover, "it's probably all an elfin joke - he always was being elfin," she reaffirms Coward's skill with an adjective, mainly by not stressing it. Describing passion as "a base, uncouth thing lurking within us," she makes it sound hilariously obscene, while also suggesting a woman rebuking an embarrassingly unruly poodle. Apart from her intoning a phrase from Blues in the Night, 15 years before its time, I cannot fault her.

The play's middle section finds Julia and Jane, her friend and rival, indulging (definitely the right word) in a champagne-and-oyster supper while awaiting their fate. The things that Julia does with a telephone receiver will surprise you; they might even have surprised Coward.

Rosemary Dunsmore's Jane provides fingertip counterpoint in their scenes together, though on her own she's all thumbs. The husbands are wretched roles; and Richard Binsley, an elfin joke in his own right, is horribly miscast as one of them. There is naturally a maid, whose name is Jasmine but who is addressed as Saunders because her employer can never remember the name Myfanwy, if you follow me. Hazel Desbarats plays her with awesome assurance, that being what the character is about.

The fallen angels expect a lot of their nemesis. I didn't; but Nicu Branzea makes him a very suave dieu hors de la machine. (For an English matron temptation has to come in French.) At the end he helps them hoodwink their husbands. The traditional lesson of comedy - that happiness consists of being well deceived - has seldom been so conclusively taught.

Fallen Angels runs until Dec. 5.
