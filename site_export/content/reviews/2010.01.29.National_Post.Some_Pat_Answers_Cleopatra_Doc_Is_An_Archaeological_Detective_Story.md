It seems that Bernard Shaw, in his Caesar and Cleopatra, didn't tell the whole truth about Caesar and Cleopatra. Well, we guessed that up to a point; few can have believed that the real Caesar was the sexless superman and cryptopacifist of the playwright's self-identifying dreams. It now appears, though, that GBS even got the facts of his star couple's first meeting wrong. In the play, it's not until they are already well acquainted that Cleopatra has herself delivered to Caesar rolled up in a carpet. In history, it's how she got in to see him in the first place. Least that's what Plutarch says.

It's also what Cleopatra: Portrait of a Killer says. This BBC documentary is part archeological detective story, part bargain-basement classic epic. Its starting-point is the discovery, 80 years ago, of a buried body believed to be that of Cleopatra's sister Arsinoe, described in the narration as "the only member of Cleopatra's family we have the remains of." The burial-site was in Ephesus, in what is now Turkey; and the reason Arsinoe ended up so far from her Egyptian home is that, after her sister had become queen courtesy of the invading Romans, she had been banished there. Initially, she had been taken to Rome as part of Caesar's triumph but, unusually for such captives, her life had been spared. Her two brothers, both named Ptolemy and both in succession married to her, hadn't been so lucky; their sister-bride didn't want any rivals in the family. Either Arsinoe benefited from Caesar's famous clemency or (this program suggests) sacrificing a frightened teenage girl would have looked bad even to a Roman crowd. So long as Caesar remained alive, Arsinoe was safe. After his assassination, and especially after Cleopatra hooked up with the less scrupulous Mark Antony, she was dead meat.

All this is history, and it's unclear why this TV show is presenting it as some kind of shocking revelation. The precise details of Arsinoe's murder may be unclear, but nobody can doubt that she would have been disposed of like this, or that Cleopatra would have been behind it. We've always known she was ruthless. Nor are her marriages to her brothers anything remarkable; these incestuous unions were standard practice among the Pharaohs. The show wants to destroy a "romantic illusion" that no one actually has. Eager to dish what it seems to believe is fresh dirt, it suffers from a bad case of split focus. It wants to be about Cleopatra, but the only "portrait" it paints is that of her sister.

The only mystery surrounds the body itself: whether it's that of Arsinoe or of some other woman buried in Ephesus around the same time. The tomb's decoration, based on the Pharos lighthouse in Alexandria that was one of the ancient world's seven wonders, suggests that the corpse must have been Egyptian. An Austrian archeologist and an English historian join forces to identify the skull and bones more precisely, and they build a conclusive case that they are indeed those of Arsinoe. They even manage a forensic reconstruction of her face: the closest thing to a snapshot of Cleo herself that we are ever likely to behold. They also manage to establish that Arsinoe, and hence Cleopatra herself, were of part-African descent; though even here they've been anticipated by the professional theatre, which has been casting black actresses as Cleopatra (in both Shaw and Shakespeare) for years now. The show also seems dubious that mere scholarship will hold its audience. It's punctuated by melodramatic music, to (literally) drum up suspense. A standard mid-Atlantic narrating voice (belonging to Colin Stinton, an American actor based in London) makes portentous promises about "the killler, the victim, and why she died:" cue percussive music and commercial break.

The principals are regularly seen in ruminative close-up; at other times guys in tunics and breastplates march across the screen, pretending to be the Roman army. Nobody, of course ever says anything, though Arsinoe at the point of death is allowed a scream, which may have necessitated the striking of a separate contract. This undramatic "dramatization" looks, as always, ludicrous. The bits that don't look like rejects from Rome look like inserts from Indiana Jones, but at least there's some reason for them. People digging may not be the most telegenic of subjects, but they are what the program is meant to be about.

- Cleopatra: Portrait of a Killer premieres Sunday at 9 p.m. ET on Discovery Channel.
