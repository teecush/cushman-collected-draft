Sometimes there can be truth in advertising. The Exquisite Hour does run for an hour, or something just over, and it is exquisite, or something just under. "Exquisite" is pitching it a bit high, but it's certainly charming. Its also cunning.

Its the work of Stewart Lemoine, a successful and prolific playwright from Edmonton whose work is hardly known here. The production is by an ad hoc collective of predominantly expat Edmontonians calling themselves the Theatre Department. The director is Ron Pederson, best known (to me, anyway) as one of the guiding lights of the group of improvizational parodists who call themselves the National Theatre of the World. The actors are the up-and-coming Daniela Vlaskalic and the very-much-arrived Ted Dysktra.

We begin with Dykstra in his backyard, getting busy with a watering can. He plays Zachary Teale, who is in his late thirties and has a managerial position in a department store. Into his flower-rimmed garden comes Vlaskalic in a flower-rimmed hat. Wearing it she looks middle-aged but she sheds about half her seeming years when she takes it off. She announces herself as a Mrs. Darimont; maybe I should have mentioned that the play is set in 1962, before the '60s became the Sixties.

Mrs. Darimont is here to change what Mr. Teale admits to be his fairly sheltered life. She wants to open him up to the world of knowledge; in other words, as Zachary shrewdly and rapidly realizes, she's in the encyclopedia business. She has, naturally, brought along a specimen volume, one simply - and, if my memory serves, atypically - labelled with a single letter, in this case H. Mrs. D suggests, more or less, that dropping salient facts on alphabetically allied subjects might improve Zacharys chances with women, which have hitherto been non-existent. All she asks is an hour of his time.

Zach tells, rather touchingly and very amusingly, of his married sisters repeated failed attempts to fix him up with eligible young women over dinner, primarily due to her own habit of hogging all the talk at the table. It may seem implausible that he should open up so quickly to a total stranger on so delicate a subject but the conversation is being lubricated with lemonade, which Zach likes to lace with bourbon. It's a Teale family tradition, apparently. There's free lemonade available in the Factory Studio lobby; Id say it was to get us into the spirit of the thing except that the spirits come extra.

Up to this point the piece is, at best, superior dinner theatre. Its also a role-reversed version of the Lanford Wilson two-hander Talley's Folly in which a man sets himself to win the affections of a woman in a bucolic setting and in a stated period of real time.

I don't think Im giving anything away in saying that its plain early on what Mrs. Darimont's game is and it isn't just selling encyclopedias; I found myself counting the minutes before Zach would nerve himself to enquire as to the whereabouts and precise earthly status of Mr. Darimont. What kicks the play into a higher gear is when the two of them start play-acting. They act out situations into which encyclopedic knowledge of the letter H could actually be introduced. This may not be terribly likely but, as written and acted here, its delightful. Its also, I suspect, catnip to Pedersen as director; he errs in underlining the fantasy with lighting changes and music cues but in all other respects his touch, and that of his actors, is featherlight.

Vlaskalic takes time to reach that state. She seems at first to be offering a caricature of a busybody, wellmeaning but overbearing. Once the games begin, though, she's transformed as well as transforming. A trial conversation about St. Hubert (dedicated hunter, converted to an ascetic life after encountering a miraculous stag with a blazing forehead) somehow becomes a re-enactment of the legendary meeting in which she is so enchantingly demure and flirtatious that one shares the disappointment of both parties when reminded that she's meant to be the antlered kind of venison rather than a deer, a female deer. She tops even this as a dental patient whose waiting-room agonies Zach attempts to soothe with fascinating facts about the Hohenstaufens (ruling family of medieval Swabia, distinguished by the frequency with which its members were named Frederick or Agnes).

Dykstra is superb from beginning to end, a lovely portrait of shy wonderment in which perfect comic timing is a vehicle for truth. Both he and the play rise to surprising heights when he contemplates the 60 minutes he has just enjoyed, so fleeting and so precious. It makes him want to return to the book to look up the word Hour. He might also, if he dared, look up Happiness.

The setting, spare but pretty, is by Marzena Cegys. Meghan Hubley is aid de camp, which I mention because its the only other credit in the program and its one I've never seen before. I'm sure shes very good at it.

Talking of things fleeting and precious but possibly renewable: The show is only here for a short time (till tomorrow, in fact) and it deserves to resurface. The word that rises to ones mind at the end of it is "sweet"; the sound that rises to the audience's lips - I actually heard it - is what a songwriter once called a soft satisfied sigh.

The Exquisite Hour closes April 29 at the Factory Studio. Call 416-504-9971 or visit factorytheatre.ca for ticket information.
