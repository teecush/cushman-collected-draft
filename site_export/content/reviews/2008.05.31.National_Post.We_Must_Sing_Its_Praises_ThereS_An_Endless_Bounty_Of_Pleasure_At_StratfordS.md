Its always a kick to find that an admired actor can sing as well. Jonathan Goad, starring in and as The Music Man at the Stratford Festival, certainly can -- like a bird on certain notes. He also has nimble feet, roguish comic timing and oceans of shy charm.

But there's trouble, right here in River City, which you can take to mean either the Iowa town where the show is set or Stratford itself; and it makes this great musical -- a maverick posing as a conformist, like its hero -- slightly less than the total tonic it could be. The clue is in that word shy. Professor Harold Hill, the fake vendor of 76 trombones (and assorted other instruments), is a charmer all right, but his charm is of the shameless, barnstorming variety. He imposes himself on the River Citizens by sheer fast-moving, crisp-talking energy. Ya Got Trouble, which epitomizes the process, here becomes the chorus's number rather than his. Or maybe its just that I cant get Robert Preston's powerhouse inflections out of my head.

Now let's, as Harold himself might say if it suited him, shoot straight pool: Goad is not just streets but whole highways ahead of the last man to play this part at Stratford. His playing of Hills more sensitive side -- his immediate empathy with the disadvantaged, for example -- couldnt be bettered, and it extends to his love-scenes with Marian Paroo, the book-loving music teacher (or music-loving librarian). Harold is meant to be an experienced sexual as well as commercial con-man, whereas the heart that Goad wears on his sleeve is very evidently real; he doesn't really need the salvation she brings him. Still, there's genuine magic between them. Leah Osters acting is occasionally stiff, but she has the square root of Marian in her, and her singing is radiant, especially in her near-aria My White Knight. The highlight of the evening is Marian the Librarian, the odd-metered number in which Harolds incorrigible wooing leads to a mass outbreak of soft shoe all over the reading room. In Michael Lichtefeld's choreography, this includes a marvellous in-house parody of Romeo and Juliet. But his work, and the company's execution of it, is top-notch throughout, from the invigorating gymnasium-floor hop of Shipoopi (this show and West Side Story opened the same season, a great one for dances at the gym) to the displays of interpretive dancing presided over by Fiona Reid as the mayors wife. Its worth the trip to hear her say, "Ladies, let us attend to our Grecian urns" (or words to that effect).

Actually, the list of pleasures in Susan Schulman's production is endless. There's the show itself, for a start: a first-time-out one-of-a-kind masterpiece, whose librettist-lyricist-composer Meredith Willson, though a richly experienced musician, had never written a musical before and never wrote a good one again. It cant have hurt that for this one he had Frank Loesser as a mentor; rumour credits Loesser with having ghosted My White Knight and rumour is probably right. He encouraged Willson to mine his turn-of-the-century childhood, in just such a proud and sheltered Iowa township as the shows, and encouraged him to match a story that's funny and makes sense to a most out-of-the-ordinary score: one that takes in marches, ballads, barbershop, irresistible patter-songs and the extraordinary word-jazz opening in which kibitzing salesmen provide both the exposition and the sound of the train they're travelling on.

The Avon stage cant match the vistas of the shows previous airing at the Festival Theatre. Philosophically I approve of the decision to restrict musicals to the proscenium house, but I have to confess that The Music Man was one of two shows -- Fiddler on the Roof, also about a town, was the other -- that justified the epic look. Still, Patrick Clark's sets bring striped-suit ice-cream-parlour America to delectable life, and the population is by and large terrific. It includes such surprise musical casting as Sara Topham, doing some earnestly bedraggled hoofing, and Michelle Fisk, wonderfully warm, sharp and tuneful as the heroine's mother. Those more accustomed to this kind of thing include Eddie Glen, an expert sidekick; a formidable colla voce quartet (Laird Mackintosh, Shawn Wright, Jonathan Monro, Marcus Nance); and a malapropping Mayor (Lee MacDougall) who, like the hero he opposes, is delightful but a little short on threat. There are also some more-than-bearable children, headed by Christopher Van Hagen as the lisping boy who learns to sing Gary, Indiana. He is key to the redemptive ending, which comes a bit sudden in this production but casts a glow anyway. The Music Man beat West Side Story at the 1958 Tonys. Historians usually reckon this to be an injustice, but I'm not so sure. - The Music Man is in repertory through Nov. 1 at the Avon Theatre, Stratford. Call 1-800-567-1600 for more information.
