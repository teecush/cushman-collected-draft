The reopening attraction at the Sony Centre (formerly the Hummingbird, pre-formerly the O'Keefe, and frankly not looking startlingly different after its latest makeover) climaxes in the most exhilarating trampoline act you have ever seen. One of the wildest things about it is the fact that the trampoline itself is sunken, and hence out of sight. So there are all these bodies bouncing high in the air, apparently from nowhere. It's like whatever the opposite may be of free fall.

And I do mean to say "all these bodies:" There are 17 named performers in iD, a production from the Montealbased Cirque Eloize, and -- though it seems like more -- there are six of them on the trampoline or, as the program calls it, Trampowall. And there's another thing: The set for this production, designed by Robert Massicotte, is a cityscape, whose walls--especially in this number -- are lavishly washed with video projections. When the human projectiles grab onto the wall in passing, as they often do, it's as if they're seeking support from something insubstantial. It really looks death-defying.

Unlike some other cirques one might mention, Cirque Eloize -- in this show at least--doesn't offer much in the way of a storyline. It's more like a tone-poem, in which urban alienation gets conquered, repeatedly, by the power of positive acrobatics. A number will typically start with one performer grabbing aggressively at another, both looking as if they're ready to rumble. But before you know it they're in harmony, though with a competitive edge still remaining. The more traditional acts tend to be the less exciting. The juggling can't really compare with what the du Soleil bunch are offering a few blocks up Yonge Street, and even the famed lady (Josieanne Levasseur) with, or in, a hoop -- virtually the show's logo--isn't all that hot. The show's signature performer may be its trick cyclist (Thibaut Philippe), whose bike is vertical at least as often and it's horizontal, and who has a nice line in leaping it from ledge to ledge. When confined to street-level, he specializes in jumping over prone bodies, including one belonging to an intrepid member of the audience (who, to the best of my belief, was not a plant).

This is a dance show as much as it's a circus, though the really thrilling aspect of Jeannot Painchaud's production is the way in which the two forms blend. That and the sense of place, especially when the scene is set down in the docks. Rival groups confront one another though, as already noted, the confrontations tend to be short-lived; togetherness keeps breaking through. It's probably impossible to evoke any kind of youth-gang warfare onstage without waking echoes of West Side Story, and this show duly wakes them. When the dancing breaks out, though, it's very different. The choreographic style is hip hop, raised to a higher power. It seems spotty to begin with, but it gradually takes over. An environment that initially seems something either to oppose or to conquer becomes something to accept, celebrate and feel part of. The Trampowall, which seems to engage the entire company even if it factually doesn't, seals the deal. The evening starts out tepid and ends up supercharged.
