Elliot is a theoretical physicist and Carmen a musician. So they are both deeply and professionally concerned with time. They are two of the three speaking characters in Hannah Moscovitch's new play, Infinity. The third is a young woman called Sarah Jean, whose role consists mostly of monologues, and who initially seems to exist in a different dramatic frame, and time-frame, from the others. It turns out that she's their daughter; that information is withheld for some time in the play, but it's freely available in the program notes, and the play wouldn't make much sense without it. This is a case where not giving something away would be a spoiler.

I tried not to keep repeating the word "time" in the above paragraph and failed miserably. This may prove Moscovitch's apparent point that time is both a slippery concept and an inescapable one. Elliot believes that it does not exist, at least as commonly conceived; and he devotes his life, starting with his PhD thesis, to proving it.

He and Carmen meet, in the play's first scene, at a postgraduate bash, a meeting that turns into a seduction in what must, even by academic standards, count as record time. (That word again.) Their relationship continues, and Sarah Jean is the result. They get married, and though they probably have a few happy moments, the play isn't concerned to show them. What we witness are quarrels and threats of separation. And those are the good times, at least compared with what is to come. It's no wonder, though no explicit connection is made, that Sarah Jean grows up with a jaundiced view of love and sex, one confirmed by her own later experiences, about which she tells us in graphic detail. When she goes into academe, her chosen field is mathematics, which could mean that she's splitting the difference between her parents' specialties or that she has rebelled against both of them by choosing the most intellectual and least emotional pursuit available.

The parents' rows are vividly written and powerfully performed, as are the daughter's reminiscences. Ross Manson directs, and the play is a co-production between his Volcano Theatre and the Tarragon. The other credits include Lee Smolin as "consulting physicist", and he's quoted in the program as follows: "What is time? ... Everything we think, feel, or do reminds us of its existence." True enough: but dramatically something that applies to everything might as well apply to nothing. We get a couple of fairly effective domestic stories tied together with a piece of string theory, and the string frays.

Paul Braunstein plays Elliot as a man whose lack of social graces is its own grace, and its own teddybear charm. The best scene is sparked not by his ideas but by his insistence on devoting himself to them even after he's won his doctorate. Carmen feels shut out and victimized by his single-mindedness, though it would seem likely that, as a composer, she would be as fanatically devoted to her own concerns as he is to his. Carmen is in fact severely underwritten; vocally, Amy Rutherford is compelled to strike a single recurrent note of angry grievance; physically, she's subtler. A significant part of her role has been contracted out. I said there were three speaking characters, but there is a fourth performer: a violinist, Andrea Tyniec, who wanders onstage to play what we must take to be Carmen's music, actually composed by Njo Kong Kie. The play's best single line is about music being "a sculpture in time" but the image is never pursued.

Haley McGee's Sarah Jean is a compelling study of young-adult anger, grown out of childhood belligerence. In her parents' story she figures as an eight-year-old, surprisingly ignorant of the facts of death. It's death that pokes a hole in Elliot's theories and converts him to what might be called the common-sense view of time, or at least the one that we rely on, if only by default, to get us through our days. After those days, there's only the infinity of the play's title, which might be either the negation of time or the ultimate confirmation of it. But these are themes that the play merely picks up and puts down again. It doesn't embody them.

Manson's production does its best to suggest higher things, with a set, designed by Teresa Przybylski, of black whorls on a white background, and choreography, by Kate Alton, that's an extended sequence of hand-jive, presumably meant to suggest the characters trapped in their own obsessive patterns. Like most of Moscovitch's plays, this one is gripping from moment to moment; unlike her best ones, it doesn't make those moments cohere.

Infinity runs through May 3 at the Tarragon Theatre.
