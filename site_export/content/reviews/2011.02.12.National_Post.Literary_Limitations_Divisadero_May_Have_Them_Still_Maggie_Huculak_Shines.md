The title Divisadero ostensibly derives from a street in San Francisco that, in the current theatre version, is mentioned just once. The show's official title is Divisadero: a Performance, a description both apt and honest. Michael Ondaatje's novel has been adapted for the stage -- there it is, or bits of it, up there -- but it hasn't been dramatized. The result is not, in any traditional sense, a play. But it is a performance, quite an impressive one, and it's also a set of performances: some of them excellent and one of them exceptional.

That would be Maggie Huculak, who stands at a microphone and talks to us in the person of a mature woman named Anna, telling what happened in her youth; and does it so carefully and yet so fluently that, when she slips back into her past persona, you never hear or feel the joins. Anna grew up motherless on a farm in California along with two adopted siblings, Claire and Coop. When she was 16, she and Coop became lovers, which made Claire jealous (partly due to Coop's endearing habit of mixing up the girls' names) and their dad, when he found the two lovebirds together, furious. Coop, after being savagely beaten, fled. Anna, at the first opportunity, took off, too, and never went home.

I confess: I've read about the novel, but I've never read it. I can see the merits of Ondaatje's meticulously parcelled prose, but I can't get through it. (And on The English Patient, which I did get through -- in both media -- I'm with Elaine.) I therefore cannot say how much of the script, which Ondaatje worked up in rehearsal with the actors and director, comes directly off the printed page and how much, if any, is fresh. It all sounds of a piece, and though the accumulation of detail might have been expected to die in the theatre, it actually works well. It certainly does when spoken by Huculak, who makes the cadences her own and is only rarely self-indulgent with them. Tall and still, she's superb both as actress and storyteller.

She carries most of the weight in the first part of the first act, though some of it is shouldered by Liane Balaban, whose Claire is more effortful but effective. (Her strongest work comes later.) Just when all the highly wrought lyricism threatens to become too much, Daniel Brooks's Necessary Angel production invigoratingly changes pace. Lights brighten, mics disappear, a vestigial supply of furniture gets shifted, and Tom McCamus rolls on, wry and rambunctious, as a gambler ( "born in Rochester -- for my sins") to tell us how he used to hang out at Lake Tahoe with a gang of cronies including the Dauphin (so named because he was once caught reading a French novel) and Coop. Coop, we learn, had matured into a champion card-shark who had to leave town--again -- after outsmarting an unforgiving evangelical. McCamus, unlike his narrating predecessors, gets to move around while talking, and his intricate depictions of an endless series of poker games are virtuoso feats of delivery, not to mention of memory. It's a juicy turn, flanked by two other performances from the same actor in sterner, plainer mode. We've already seen him, briefly, as the vengeful father by whom Coop was beaten up; we will next see him, again briefly, as a Nevada operator by whom Coop is also beaten up, worse.

If this were a play, Coop would be its protagonist, since he's the one who does things and to whom things happen. As it is, he does the least talking but is still the centre of everybody else's attention. And here's a problem, because he's played by Justin John Rutledge; and if Balaban, a film and TV actress, has never acted on stage before, Rutledge has never acted anywhere before, and it shows. He isn't a disgrace, he's intelligent, he has a pleasant presence, but it's presence without definition. His Coop seems too nice a guy to get into the trouble he gets into. It's funny, because he's the reason the show exists. Ondaatje saw him in concert, and thought his singing and song-writing would be a perfect match for the book if ever it were put on stage. Oddly, the singing and strumming Rutledge actually does in the show isn't particularly helpful. It contributes a vague sense of Western place but nothing more. Indeed, the most electrifying musical sequence in the show is a song that Rutledge didn't write and doesn't sing; it's London Calling, given a despairing, discordant (though with a song by the Clash, how can you tell?) near-suicidal rendition by Amy Rutherford as a woman sent to lure Coop to his doom and hating herself for it. She's good and biting when she talks, too.

The play this staged Divisadero most resembles is probably Brian Friel's Faith Healer; but though that, too, is a series of monologues, they're speeches that offer us divergent views of a situation. So there's a kind of conflict there. Here it's just someone talking when someone else leaves off, a sequence of soliloquies. That makes Brooks, a past master at staging one-man shows, its ideal director; his inventiveness here is as stylish and as fiercely disciplined as usual; John Thompson has designed another of his spare platforms and Andrea Lundy does the lighting. I've read enough crib-notes to know that the book has an entire second half, set in Europe, that's omitted here. All that remains -- I guess -- is Anna's final announcement that she became a writer, as if that were somehow the point of the whole thing. Huculak can make it sound like a benison, but even she can't avoid the unspoken response "so what?" With all that she, McCamus, Rutherford and the others can do for them, none of the characters here are more than the sum of their words.
