THE CLEAN HOUSE

Bluma Appel Theatre, Toronto

Sarah Ruhl's The Clean House is a North American play that has gone warmly South. Its Toronto production seems misguidedly intent on cooling it down again. The author, who is thirtyish and much- lauded, has said that her initial idea came from an overheard remark at a party; a doctor was complaining that her maid, a Brazilian, refused to clean her house. "I did not," she said "go to medical school in order to clean my own house."

That line has gone straight into the play where it's spoken by Lane, a physician who does indeed have a Brazilian maid called Mathilde. Mathilde used to be depressed and her pills have left her with an antipathy towards cleaning anybody's house. What she really wants to be is a comedienne; she comes from a funny family, and both of her parents pretty much died laughing.

The play's unstressed point is that this is the best way to go; and who would argue? Well, Lane and her sister Virginia ("like the state", though the name may have another significance) both would, at least to begin with. They are crazy about having spotless surroundings, though while Lane wants someone else to do the work, Virginia likes to do it herself. And since she's through with cleaning her own place at approximately 3:12 every day (well, she says "approximately," though to anyone else it sounds unnervingly precise), she has more than enough time to take care of Lane's, which is a great blessing for Mathilde.

Both sisters are married; we never meet Virginia's partner -- she doesn't seem to see much of him herself -- but Lane's husband Charles is a player. He's a surgeon who falls in love with, and leaves his wife for, a breast-cancer patient named Ana.

Ana laughs a lot; she also knows how to cry despite coming from a country, Argentina, where crying is said to be officially discouraged.

"They fall in love," says a subtitle, as surgeon and patient do just that. Only here there are no titles. I only know that they existed in previous stagings from reading American reviews supplied to me by Canadian Stage: A generous gesture, since those reviews point to a purposeful craziness -- the ethnic quotient makes the term "magical realism" impossible to avoid -- that Alisa Palmer's production either damps down or misses entirely.

Charles and Ana declare their passion, mid-mastectomy, in operatic duet. Ana's half of this is ringingly performed by Mary Ann McDonald, an accomplished singer who also gives a joyous, and eventually moving, spoken performance.

Joseph Ziegler plays Charles, and though he acts charmingly, his singing is muted. The song is accompanied by a dance, and this too lacks something in abandon. When Mathilde "imagines" her parents, we see them in mimed flashbacks; these are preserved, but in a nervous hole-and-corner fashion. Judith Bowden's chilly set is more attuned to the play's title than to its spirit, though Kevin Lamotte's lighting is, by its own lights, superb.

Mathilde is on a quest for the perfect joke, and eventually she finds it and tells it, though tactfully not out loud. In its first half, the play's devices may seem cute or cloying, but they justify themselves in the second, which is a generous testament to all kinds of love, including euthanasia.

It is also quirkily funny; Charles' announcement that he is shacking up with Ana according to Jewish law, despite the fact he isn't Jewish, occasions as well-built a comic scene as I have recently encountered. Later, with Charles off in Alaska chopping down yew trees with prophylactic properties, the four women draw unexpectedly together by sharing a bowl of chocolate ice cream: a lovely image.

Seana McKenna and Fiona Reid play the sisters, but it's a partnership that provides less than promised; they're both mistresses of timing, so their performances dovetail nicely, but they don't strike any fresh sparks off one another. Both are in familiar modes: Reid fluffy and neurotic, McKenna steely on top and jellied underneath, with a glance that could shame any basilisk and a touching forgiveness at the end.

The play's heart, though -- and the evening's salvation -- isn't really with them, it's with the two women from South America: Ana, and Mathilde, the latter of whom has two households understandably vying for her non-domestic services and whom Nicola Correia-Damude plays boldly and delightfully.

She starts the play by telling a joke in Portuguese. I suspect that non-Portuguese-speakers may not be missing much, but her body- language suggests that it's hilarious. - The Clean House is at the Bluma Appel Theatre until March 8. For more information call 416- 368-3110.
