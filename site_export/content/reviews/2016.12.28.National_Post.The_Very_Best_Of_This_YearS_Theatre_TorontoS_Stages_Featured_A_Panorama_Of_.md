Will Eno, a true original among American playwrights, was in Toronto recently for the closing performance at the Tarragon of The Realistic Joneses, his most successful play to date. We had a phone conversation during which he said, I hope there's something in it that promotes the view that kindness is necessary, crucial, a great thing.

I can't say that those sentiments, true and humane though they are, were what has stayed with me from a witty and unsettling play about two couples suffering from the same illness and the same surname; a play whose last words are "I dont think anything good is going to happen with us, but what are you going to do?" But I am happy to report Enos enthusiasm for the acting, the design and the overall impact of Richard Rose's production (It was coherent in a deep way - made the funny stuff funny and the sad stuff sad.).

There will be more Eno on our stages next year when the Shaw Festival presents his Middletown, which he insists is not a response to Thornton Wilder's Our Town. It is set in a smallish town, not unlike Carlyle, Mass. ("a library, a fire station, one store - a lonely place in some ways") where Eno himself, now 51, grew up. "I was trying to create a little place that represented a larger place. We tend to look for meaning in our birth, which we cant remember, and our death, which we wont be able to respond to. Mathematically, we are usually in the middle of things."

Meanwhile, The Realistic Joneses certainly makes my list of the best theatrical things to happen here in 2016. And since it opened near the end of the year, I propose to list my other choices in - roughly - reverse chronological order.

COME FROM AWAY

A certifiable no-seats-available hit. A musical about the welcome extended by the citizens of Gander, N.L., to the airplane-loads of unwitting refugees from 9/11, it proved to be more suspenseful and less sentimental than anyone could have hoped. Simply, fluently and imaginatively staged, it was one of two outstanding new Canadian musicals, the other being James Smith's Chasse-Galerie, brought in from the fringe to the Distillery. Also note that another Canadian musical, Ride the Cyclone, has made it to New York's Public Theatre, though both the Public and The New York Times insist on describing it as coming from Chicago. (The NY production originated there but the show didnt.) And a friend remarks that, even in Come from Away which has Canadian writers and an American production team, though Newfoundland is frequently mentioned, Canada never is.

MASTER HAROLD ... AND THE BOYS

This came from the Shaw Festival, but I caught it at the Toronto Arts Centre. Athol Fugards reminiscence of early apartheid was brought to heartbreaking life by director Philip Akin and actors Allan Louis, Andre Sills, and James Daly whose subsequent singing-dancingclowning appearance in the Sleeping Beauty panto proclaims him this year's Mr Versatility.

THE UNENDING

Aaron Willis' and Julie Teppermans site-specific Convergence Theatre offered a terrific triple bill that included Becketts Play, in an inventive staging by Willis that beat out the concurrent one at CanadianStage, and a penetrating new play by Tepperman with a tremendous performance by Mayko Nguyen.

FATHER COMES HOME FROM THE WARS (PARTS I, II, III)

It was a great year for new and old American plays. Suzan-Lori Parks' sardonic epic about the Civil War from the slaves' perspective - or perspectives - was a breath-taker, as directed by Weyni Mengesha. It was a fine ensemble showing, with possible personal-best performances from Dion Johnstone and Oliver Dennis. We now await the next parts. Arthur Millers neglected Incident at Vichy proved both tense and intense, offering insights on demagogy that seemed pertinent at the beginning of the year, and downright frightening at the end. Alan Dilworths production was another ensemble achievement, culminating in a soul-shaking duet between Diego Matamoros and Stuart Hughes. These two shows were beacons in an otherwise middling Soulpepper season - along, that is, with Damien Atkins' emergence, through revelatory supporting performances in The Heidi Chronicles and A Doll's House and a star turn in Hosanna, as Actor of the Toronto Year.

ALL MY SONS, ANOTHER MILLER

Given a probing, pitch-perfect production by Martha Henry, Joseph Ziegler, Lucy Peacock and Tim Campbell were great as the central family; Michael Blake gave a breakthrough performance (reinforcing his admirable Macduff) as the outsider who shatters their illusions. This was Stratfords prize production this year, though The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe and Shakespeare in Love were populist choices that earned their artistic keep; Stephen Ouimette in The Hypochondriac confirmed his position as farceur supreme; and A Little Night Music triumphed over inexplicable set-designs by virtue of the piece itself and the performances of Ben Carlson, making you laugh, and Yanna McIntosh, making you cry.

ENGAGED

The highlight of the Shaw Festival was Morris Panychs production of this bracingly cynical comedy by W. S. Gilbert-without-Sullivan. Gray Powell's susceptible skinflint, turning on an endless succession of dimes, was a serious contender for performance of the year. Other Shaw goodies: outgoing director Jackie Maxwell's farewell duo of Uncle Vanya and Sweeney Todd (a good year for festival Sondheim) with Benedict Campbell a rivetingly realistic Sweeney, down to earth and high on hate.

BOTTICELLI IN THE FIRE/SUNDAY IN SODOM

Jordan Tannahills Botticelli was the best new Canadian play of the year; Sunday, its double-bill partner, wasnt but was still worthwhile. Tannahill offered himself serious competition, though, when Concord Floral finally reached Toronto, though the staging and the young performers were the real knockouts here.

KILLER JOE

American Tracy Letts' inexorably compelling first play (far better, and shorter, than his August: Osage County) was given a perfect production by Peter Pasyk, making inspired use of the Coal Mine Theatre space. Is this tiny house the most reliably exciting theatre in Toronto? That's a rhetorical question. Its other prize offerings included Instructions to Any Future Socialist Government Wishing to Abolish Christmas, which would have been the new Canadian play of the year, if it hadnt taken so many years to get here; Breathing Corpses, the only good new British play to arrive this twelvemonth; and the new Groundling Companys The Winter's Tale, top-flight Shakespeare in an intimate setting, such as Toronto hardly ever sees.
