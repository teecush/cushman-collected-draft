THE MACKENZIE/ PAPINEAU REBELLION

Cameron House

- - -

From 1985 to 1999, the company called VideoCabaret occupied itself telling the nation its own story in a 12-play comic epic entitled The History of the Village of the Small Huts. They are now telling us again, reviving the original cycle, with a few amendments born of hindsight.

Part Three, first seen in 1987, has returned; and though I missed it the first time around I judge, from its resemblance to other segments, that its vision is unaltered and its style undimmed. As usual, every character who sets foot on the tiny, black-swathed cartoon stage is either a fool or a scoundrel, except for some who are both.

As a philosophy of history (and not just Canadian history), this is extremely plausible. As drama, especially given the short-scene format that comes with the VidCab territory, it has its limitations; as one disgraceful episode succeeds another, the revelations of corruption and incompetence become not just predictable but interchangeable. However, The Mackenzie/Papineau Rebellion is more tightly focused than other bits of the cycle, and therefore less monotonous. It has a strong story -- two in fact, told in alternating vignettes -- and though it may not dramatize the participants' complexities, it does at least suggest that they may, somewhere off-stage, exist. As well, the dark elan of the presentation remains seductive.

Michael Hollingsworth, author and director of the project, has perfectly defined its virtues and limitations in the clinching sentence of his auteur's manifesto: "The only thing one knows for sure is that the new boss will be the same as the old boss." So we get a style that seems to advocate revolt and ends up mocking it.

We have William Lyon Mackenzie, firebrand journalist and first mayor of Toronto, leading his insurrection against the oligarchic Family Compact, while, at the same 1837 moment, Louis Joseph Papineau makes his bid to replace the rich English rulers of Quebec with some rich French ones.

As ever, I find myself naively astonished at the sheer efficiency of the presentation: at the unerring speed with which seven actors, sharing 34 roles, switch between voices, wigs and boldly complicated costumes; at the accuracy with which they hit their marks in the blackouts on a stage scaled to look as much like a TV screen as possible (an inch out of place and they'd ruin the lighting on which the show largely depends); at the confidence with which they embody the chosen style. There is not a moment of uncertainty in the show.

Hollingsworth's script shows the way here; it has a remarkably confident tone. It uses anachronism without being silly or smug; it sounds, in fact, timeless.

Eddie Glen plays Mackenzie as a one-man ginger group; his face, broad in its own right, is protectively encircled by a halo of bristling ginger hair, and his voice bristles, too; he measures out his rhetoric like a pharmacist dispensing brimstone, so that a comparatively mild denunciation like "primeval" emerges as a fundamentalist "prime evil." Especially taking is his march downtown at the head of his pitchforkwielding troops; starting tremulously at Yonge and Eglinton ("thank God it's downhill"), he gains in missionary zeal as he passes the fleshpots of Summerhill and Rosedale, only to meet a rude military awakening somewhere south of College.

A fine orator but a lousy general (and not above exploiting his solitary tongue-lolling apprentice), his disillusion, when both his hopes and his troops lie in ruins, is still treated with something suspiciously resembling sympathy.

No such charity, and considerably less time, is accorded to Ostap Soroka's popinjay Papineau, excoriated by just about everybody including his own massacred supporters, each identified simply as "Patriote," just as every one of Mackenzie's men is billed as "Rebel." (The best way of attracting sympathy in a Hollingsworth production is not to have a name.)

The ruling English and French get it, naturally, in their stiff, preening necks. The twin commanders of Upper and Lower Canada compete in their lip-curling contempt for "the mob"; while the British governor, Sir Francis Bondhead (a name that, if it hadn't existed, would have to have been invented, probably by Dickens or Ben Jonson) comes off like a declining Roman emperor: murderously weak and nourishing a suspicious, though apparently unrequited, affection for a tough tycoon called McNab. Janet Burke as Bondhead gives the showpiece performance of the evening; she also plays three other roles, all of them male. (The few women in the cast, most of them Mackenzie's or Papineau's mothers, are played by men.)

Greg Campbell plays an escalating assortment of oppressors, at their summit Bishop Strachan, an ambulant snarl whose face clenches and whose teeth contort at the scent of anything or anyone to the left of Genghis Khan; one thinks of such contemporary holy rollers as Pat Robertson or Ian Paisley.

Some intermediate henchmen and turncoats come and go with confusing frequency, so that the show becomes rather muddy as a history lesson, but there is generally some sharply etched new arrival to pick up the slack: Bruce Vavrina as an anglophile veteran of 1812, expressing his contempt of all trimmers and moderates with a ferocious grin; or Patrick Conner as Lord Durham, the Governor General, who arrives like a courtly deus ex machina to do, as my children's Civics textbook describes it, the right things for the wrong reasons. The upshot, as always in VideoCabaret's chronicles, is a mixture of scorn and sadness; scorn wins out, but this time sadness gives it an unusually close run.
