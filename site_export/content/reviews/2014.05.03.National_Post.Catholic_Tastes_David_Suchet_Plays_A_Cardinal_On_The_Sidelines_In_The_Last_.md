Plays about God move in mysterious, not to say frustrating, ways. Two of them are currently playing in Toronto.

The movement in The Last Confession is backwards and sideways. This play, a British success now commencing a world tour with an international Anglophone cast, concerns the brief papal reign of John Paul I (1978-1978), who suffered a fatal heart attack after only 33 days in office. By Vatican standards he was, or intended to be, a radical reformer, especially in the matters of birth control and financial corruption. This, together with the fact that no autopsy was permitted after his death, gave rise to the idea that he may have been murdered. These theories, neither proved nor disproved, are the underpinning of this first play by an American lawyer, Roger Crane.

John Paul, as Crane presents him, is an engaging and sympathetic figure, delightfully played by Richard O'Callaghan with a mixture of ingenuousness and determination that makes him seem at first an impossible Pope and then a formidable one. His is not, however, the star role.

That belongs to David Suchet as Cardinal Giovanni Benelli. (Both Suchet and O'Callaghan are reprising the roles they created in England,) Benelli is an accomplished ecclesiastical politician, with reforming and maybe papal ambitions of his own, who is largely responsible for securing John Pauls accession, but then leaves Rome in, apparently, a fit of pique at not being offered the key position of Secretary of State on sufficiently flattering terms. So he isnt around to support the new Pope during his battles with an implacably obstructionist Congress (sorry, Curia), something for which he never ceases to reproach himself. The last confession of the title is Benelli's own, a desperate one. He believes that in failing to help or save John Paul he effectively murdered him.

This is reminiscent of Salieri in Peter Shaffer's Amadeus, claiming responsibility for the death of Mozart in a testament that nobody believes (and that also has a spiritual dimension). The Last Confession also shares with Amadeus a cumbersome flashback structure and a clodhopping prose style. What it doesnt have is the fierce portrayal of

personal jealousy that makes Shaffers Salieri a compelling figure, and justifies his displacing his great contemporary as the focus of the drama. Benelli remains intractably on the sidelines. His only claim on our interest lies in his repeated protestations that he has lost his faith. But since we've never seen him in possession of it in the first place, that doesn't mean much.

You abandoned God? Benelli is asked, to which he replies "I think God abandoned me." Theres a lot of this automatic back-and-forth in the writing. It reaffirmed your belief in God?

"I began to believe in man." Or: Youre afraid you've lost God? "I'm afraid I might find him." It gets so you can predict the answer as soon as you hear the question. Cliched writing tends to breed cliched acting, and Jonathan Churchs production is full of it. Even Suchet, one of the best actors on the British stage (he was, among other things, my favourite Shylock) isnt immune; he has brought incisiveness, of both voice and gaze, to a fine art, and there's a commanding urgency about his performance, but he still succumbs to the play's own stock rhythms. He does, when heading a secondact murder investigation, share an invigorating shouting-match with Nigel Bennett, who plays an unrepentant but comparatively honourable reactionary. The various clerics can be hard to keep track of, and some of them are atrociously played, but Bernard Lloyd gives a fine, elegant performance as senior Cardinal. Donald Douglas is both touching and authoritative as the Pope-before-last, and David Ferry amusingly adapts his native Newfoundland accent in the service of an ambiguous Irish factotum. The identity of Benellis Last Confessor is revealed at the end of the play, and is one of those closing moments that makes you think the author should have gone back and started again. It raises central issues that the play has never begun to explore.

Something similar happens at the end of Sean Dixons A God in Need of Help, though somewhat more forgivably. In this concluding scene, and with some indebtedness to Euripides' The Bacchae (a play that has influenced nearly everything) the author, in a play set in 1606, raises the question of the persistence of paganism, into the era of the Reformation and Counter-Reformation. That must sound awfully remote, but the play makes its complicated historical background a lot more comprehensible than the far more recent one of The Last Confession. At least its people, though not devoid of stock characteristics, sound more or less like people. A giant painting of the Nativity is being bodily transported, by the four winners of a strong man contest, across the Alps from nominally Catholic but largely secular Venice to Prague, where its presence has been requested by the Holy Roman Emperor (who at this date was neither especially holy nor especially Roman). They're escorted, and bullied, by a mercenary military captain. In a German village theyre confronted by a group of the image-destroying extreme Protestants to whom we owe the term "iconoclast"; but both they and the painting are saved by an apparent miracle. The Virgin Mary walks bodily out of the picture, causing the villagers to fall to their knees and become Catholic. That, at least, is the story told by the picture-bearers when they return to Venice, where they are severally interrogated by a cynically nasty magistrate and a rather nice archbishop.

Their stories - told, yet again in flashback - vary, but not so much as to engender a Rashomon level of suspense. The last scene, featuring the youngest and unlikeliest of the four porters of the apocalypse, offers the most startling variation, but its so out of sync with the others as to make most of what has gone before seem irrelevant. Form and (eventual) subject seem at war with one another. Camellia Koo's set is dominated by a stunning reproduction of the painting itself (a Durer) with a detachable frame, which is brought forward in Richard Rose's production so that the various narratives can be enacted within it. The acting is unspectacularly decent, with Daniel Kash a standout as an ex-soldier who has seen it all.
