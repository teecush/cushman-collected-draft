Joy runs until tomorrow at the Joseph Workman Theatre, Queen Street West. Tickets are $15. Call the box office at (416) 583-4339.

Obviously there is irony intended, but the title Joy refers to a character rather than an emotion. The producers bill their show as "a musical about depression" and they use the word "depression" in the clinical sense. They are also, perhaps unintentionally, using the word "musical" in a clinical sense.

Joy is a production of the Workman Theatre Project, which operates out of the Centre for Addiction and Mental Health on Queen Street West, and which -- apart from operating theatrical training programs -- regularly mounts shows cast from a combination of its own members and Equity full-timers. The present show has a script by Maja Ardal, former director of the Toronto Young People's Theatre, and a score by the increasingly ubiquitous Joey Miller, already represented in town by Outrageous. As a social enterprise, it's admirable; as a theatre piece, it's worthy and well-performed, but flawed.

The eponymous Joy is a woman who apparently has everything to live for but cannot believe it; less and less can she believe it and the spiral seems unstoppable, except through chemical intervention, which brings its own problems. Inevitably, her story recalls the recent suicide of a Toronto woman who was a psychiatrist. This story is not quite as horrific -- there are, for one merciful thing, no children involved -- but the echoes, though involuntary (musicals don't get written and produced that quickly) are inescapable. They work to the show's advantage in sharpening our antennae, but they also raise our expectations. We want to be told things: There is information and there are insights that we accept we cannot have in real life, but that we instinctively demand in the theatre.

Ardal's script, unfortunately, stays on an anecdotal level. These things happen to Joy, and then these other things happen to Joy. There are hints that her marriage is sterile and unsympathetic, but the means chosen to demonstrate this are so predictable that we can't tell whether we're dealing with a failed relationship or simply uninspired writing.

Perhaps that's the job of the songs. But that's another problem. As staged by Patricia Vanstone -- in a plodding, opaque fashion -- Joy is only a musical because it has songs. (That's why I called the classification clinical.) Which raises another question: Why -- except that everything seems to be nowadays -- is this show a musical? Musicals don't have to be celebratory, but they have somehow to raise the emotional and imaginative stakes. It's hard enough to write a play about illness, mental or physical, and make it anything more than a display of symptoms. The only way music might help is to make the whole thing a kind of phantasmagoria; there are occasional hints of this in Joy but they're kept in check by everybody's honest desire to avoid the sensational. There are some nice things in Miller's score: an early ballad for the heroine that begins by sounding like Andrew Lloyd Webber's Unexpected Song (from Song & Dance), then strikes out on interesting paths of its own; a despairing cry from her largely uncomprehending mother. Much of the time, though, the songs are stuck with reiterating emotions we already know about or with delivering exposition, which is hard work.

Jayne Lewis in the title role does well; script and score between them give her something to bite on and bite she does. She acts astringently, sings immaculately, and is never for a moment self- pitying or sentimental. She runs the show. Mary Ann McDonald is similarly powerful as Joy's mother. Nine-year-old Adanya Dunn is precociously professional as a ghostly little girl; and Mike Lummis makes a delicate impact as a sympathetic fellow patient.

As a self-described product of "the mental health community," the piece tells us remarkably little about that community. I don't suggest, though, that such scenes should be musicalized. We need to re-think what musicals can and should do. The prevailing wisdom is that they make forbidding subjects "accessible." They may make them more superficially approachable, but they are likely to make the most important issues even harder to get at. -
