I dont know where the title of So You Think You Can Dance Canada? gets off being so mocking and derisive. This show proves Canada can dance, or at least the representatives of it who get to appear onscreen. Turning this show on is like tuning into the Olympics; the TV is full of people doing, very well, what most of us could hardly hope to do at all.

Mary Murphy was certainly impressed. One of the trio of judges on last week's opening round, she could hardly control her enthusiasm. Imported from the United States, she sounded as if Canada were a country she had only just discovered and could hardly wait to tell her friends about. "Some of the best dancers on the planet are here!" she squealed. Murphy's appearances on American TV have earned her the title Queen of Scream, a badge, to judge from the way its quoted in her biography, she wears with pride. But it really is more of a squeal than a scream, and its one of the most grating sounds to have emerged from my TV in recent months, rivalled only by Murphy's laugh, which, once started, goes on forever. The crazy thing is that when the former dancer quiets down and says something specific about the talent in front of her, it's on the mark. This despite the fact that neither she nor her two quieter colleagues -- who could hardly help being lower key but seemed equally judicious --had a bad word to say to, or about, anybody. A few of the initial parade of hopefuls had their hopes deliriously realized and were sent directly to the Heaven of the shows finals. A larger number were consigned to Purgatory, otherwise known as Choreography, where they are forced to execute steps other than the ones theyve dreamed up on their own. A very small number -- of those we were allowed to see, anyway -- were damned, meaning sent home and told not to come back. And even they were allowed to depart with encouraging words in their ears.

Compared to American Idol and its derivatives (not that AI isn't derivative itself ), this is a very kind show, low on embarrassment (unless you count Murphy's eruptions) and humiliation. Either the initial screening process had been ruthless at weeding out incompetence, or the producers were charitable about showing it to us. Or maybe having two left feet is a condition more easily acknowledged by the sufferer than being tone deaf. Anyway, everybody we got to see could actually do it. Nearly all the dancing on show was hip hop or something akin. From the casual viewers perspective, the more acrobatic or eccentric the gyrations were the better, since we only saw them in short spurts anyway and could hardly judge things like line or flow. (A lot of the dancing seemed, to this untrained eye, to bear little rhythmic relation to the music it was being done to. That may be one thing that Choreography is supposed to straighten out.) The show is both a celebration of, and made possible by, the return of dancing to the streets. At best, its the everyday made marvellous. One of my own favourite contestants (and, at 28, one of the oldest) was Karl, a scissor-legged performer, who said that dancing had saved him from a life with the gangs, and also that, I got my hip-hop influence from living in the street. I never knew what it was -- I was living it. Then there was Amanda, who appeared in tears because "my dad passed away -- I'm willing to bare my soul to express myself." She duly broke down at her audition, and was voluminously comforted by the judges.

I just wanna come up and give you a hug, Mary said. This is the kind of thing reality TV can't resist, and that one wishes it would. Anyway, Amanda was sent to Choreography -- "not just because your father died" -- but no further, which seemed fair. Honour and sympathy were both satisfied.

So this show belongs, with Triple Sensation and (I hate to admit) How Do You Solve a Problem Like Maria?, at the top end of the talent-show spectrum. It starts with a certain assumption of professionalism. Beyond that, it falls into all the cliches. We get the breathlessly inane narration (because theres nothing to say), the long line of aspirants shown being nervous before the audition and jubilant afterwards. We also get the inescapable fact that, since we see the auditionees for seconds at a time and in endless succession while the judges remain constant, its the latter whom the show ends up being about. But that may change as the concentration narrows. I wonder if, as the season progresses, anyone will get the chance or show the ability to dance slow. Even if the street style could produce an updated equivalent of the tap-dancing Astaire, could it also yield the Fred who danced with Ginger? Maybe that's a demand that will be made in later episodes, when the kids are left to someone else's devices. (Though I have heard it said, and it must be true, that there are far more good dancers than good choreographers.) Maybe the title will become more relevant, and more biting, as the weeks dance on. - The second episode of So You Think You Can Dance Canada airs tonight at 8 p. m. on CTV.
