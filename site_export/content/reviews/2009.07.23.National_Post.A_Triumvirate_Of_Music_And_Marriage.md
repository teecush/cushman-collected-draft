PLAY, ORCHESTRA, PLAY

Royal George Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

---

The umbrella title Play, Orchestra, Play shelters three of Noel Coward's Tonight at 8:30 oneacters, all with musical connections. The middle piece includes a character who torments her family by practising the piano, badly. The plays on either side of it are outright mini-musicals. The evening's principal connecting thread, however, is that all three playlets depict marriages, none of them happy.

The first couple up are the seedy British music-hall duo Red Peppers, the title of the play being also the name of their act: a song, dance and crosstalk routine that may, as the male Pepper proudly says, have been good enough for his father (and probably for his father before him), but is looking and sounding decidedly archaic in the brave new talkie-infested world of 1936. So the Peppers are reduced to playing the provinces, and nowhere near the top of the bill either.

In between musical performances, we spend time in the Peppers' dressing room listening to them bicker with one another, with the conductor (trouble over tempo), the manager (trouble over billing) and a senior actress with classical pretensions (trouble). The play is more about flavour than action, a nostalgic but mercilessly clear-eyed look at a dying theatrical world Coward himself had long since left behind. Christopher Newton's production imposes more shape on it than some I have seen, marking off very clearly the point at which our two troupers sink their differences and close ranks against the outside world. I had trouble buying Jay Turvey as a leathery veteran of the halls, but he and Patty Jamieson expertly calibrate their bitter rows and unspoken reconciliations, while Steven Sutcliffe offers an original view of the manager as an insolent sycophant, half toady, half toad.

The second play, Fumed Oak, takes Coward back to another world he had escaped but obviously still had nightmares about: that of stifling lower-middle-class respectability. Henry Gow, retail employee, sits meek and silent in his South London home where the first thing he hears every morning is his wife and her mother nagging one another. They nag, or ignore him too, and Mrs. Gow also nags their daughter (the amateur pianist), though her mother prefers to compete by buying the kid sweets. Finally Henry speaks, and once he's started he doesn't stop.

The play's entire second half is his tirade as he tells the assorted females what he thinks of them, how he was trapped into marriage and how he is now going to get out of it. Tears and threats are in vain, and when his mother-in-law insults him he slaps her, provoking an intake of breath from the auditorium but no noticeable protest. Sutcliffe, stewing behind his specs and then exploding through them, is a gloriously turning worm who has the audience spellbound. Jamieson, in a rare nonmusical role, is a thoroughly believable spouse; and Wendy Thatcher and Robin Evans Willis bring the oldest and youngest generations to horrific life. The play is snobbish, misogynist, one-sided and undoubtedly expresses deep, and deeply discreditable, impulses in its author. I loved it.

I was less transported by the third piece, Shadow Play, though it's potentially the most interesting of the trio and certainly the most advanced. In fact, it feels experimental even now. Victoria Gayforth, half of a troubled society marriage, comes home from seeing a musical comedy and dreams the nightmare of her own life, interspersed with memories of a happier past. The songs that invade her mind include two of Coward's best: Play, Orchestra, Play itself, frantically driven, and the truly haunting You Were There. This is Coward dealing not with worlds he'd left but with the one he now belonged to. And that, I think, is the trouble; he doesn't have to work at the background.

Victoria's problems are taken to be automatically interesting.

Despite all that Julie Martell does for her, she doesn't take on any individual life. And the staging, though expert in its own right, is too much. Newton and his designer, Cameron Porteous, make great use of projections; entertainingly in the first play and with bleak wit in the second. In the third, though, they go wild with them, providing a filmed backdrop for every scene. If someone mentions a Venetian honeymoon, we see gondolas. It's overkill; and has the perverse effect of damming up the play's fluidity rather than releasing it. Add everything up, though, and this is a fine and various evening. - Play, Orchestra, Play is in repertory through Oct. 31. Call 1-800-511-SHAW or visit shawfest.comfor tickets and information.
