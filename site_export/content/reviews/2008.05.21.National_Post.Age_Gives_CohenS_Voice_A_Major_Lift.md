"This," said the 74-year old maestro, "is the first time I've been on a stage in 14 years. Of course, back then I was just a crazy kid with a dream." Big laugh. Leonard Cohen is on a comeback tour, and he's kicked it off in the Maritimes: first stop Fredericton, second Halifax. In fact --however he may have looked, felt or sounded when he was a mere incipient sexagenarian -- his present age becomes him. Where once he had the hawk-like look of a youngish Al Pacino, he now has the foxy demeanor of an oldish Dustin Hoffman: dapper with a slight edge of disreputable. He looks sidelong at everything including, if such a thing is possible, himself. He seems especially surprised, though certainly not displeased, by his trio of female backup singers; "How," he appears to be wondering as, hat-in-hand, he watches them undulate, "could I have gotten this lucky, this late?"

He spends a lot of time clutching that fedora; nearly every moment, in fact, that he isn't actually singing. It gives him an air of humility ringed with confidence; it also summons the image of the wearily romantic chansonnier he must always have yearned to be: Charles Aznavour with a dust-and-ashes touch of Jacques Brel. Dust and ashes characterize his voice, which would be a growl if it had more body. It's a more attractive as well as a more compelling sound than the terror drone with which he started his recording career 40 years ago. It also sorts better with his material, which is downbeat even when the subject appears to be redemption.

Even his defining hit Suzanne sounds better coming from an old man than a young one; its fuzziness can at least be defended as the haze of memory. That song has always seemed to me to sum up the worst of the singer-songwriter school -- the kind of "poetic vision" that sees no further than the end of the next line. What does "she has touched your perfect body with her mind" mean? The man is just spraying adjectives around -- it's the kind of vagueness that a whole generation mistook for poetry, when it isn't even decent songwriting. The sole exception of the iconic singer-songwriter bunch is Randy Newman, who is always specific, even at his most oblique. But "specific" is what it should be about. Is there -- quite apart from the whiny tune, which even Cohen's mature voice can't disguise--any reason or relevance for the piled-up similes of Bird on a Wire?

Still, if there isn't reason, there is usually rhyme: one thing that Cohen's far better at than most of his peers. The comparatively recent That Don't Make It Junk has a very satisfying clunk. Hallelujah is a tour-deforce of rhyming, though it's a shame that Cohen now sings "do you" and "to you" rather than "do ya" and "to ya;" maybe he's trying for subtlety, but it's self-defeating. It weakens the song's alternation of sacred and profane. This mix is something that Cohen has gone in for ever since the gratuitous -- read: meretricious -- intrusion of Jesus in the middle of Suzanne. The imagery is usually Catholic -- odd, from a Jewish Buddhist --and it works far more powerfully in his political songs. At least, I assume they're political. First We Take Manhattan (Then We Take Berlin) seems to be an outpouring of all-purpose resentment; it could be the marching song of a crusading Fascist. Presumably it's meant more as a warning than an inspiration, though its power comes from putting you emotionally on the side of the devils. Even Democracy sounds as much threat as promise, even with its disarming reference -- maybe his best single line -- to the Sermon on the Mount "which I don't pretend to understand at all." Everybody Knows, one of my two favourite songs of his, is definitely warning, with an intoxicating touch of gloat. It's in this mode that Cohen's authority as writer and performer comes most satisfyingly together: here, and in my other favourite, Tower of Song, which brings all his recurring personae -- the lover, the outcast and the troubadour -- into a reasonably coherent whole, and sports a marvellously self-deprecating stanza about Hank Williams. Take along with these his best mordant-romantic songs, I'm Your Man and Ain't No Cure for Love.

He played a nearly three-hour show, with a half dozen built-in encores. The band moved beautifully between folk, pop, klezmer and-- with special credit to guitarist Bob Metzger --jazz. The backup ladies moved less beautifully -- they swayed their hips in a bored sort of way, as a part of the contract that they hadn't properly read -- but their harmonies and occasional solos were lovely; one of the trio is Sharon Robinson, Cohen's songwriting collaborator on Everybody Knows, among other things; one wonders how much of that song's tightness and straightforwardness are due to her. Cohen himself is in complete command, an impish, minor prophet vociferously honoured in his own country.
