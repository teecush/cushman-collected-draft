Its worth checking out Nellie McKay on YouTube, although she might not want you to. She describes the Internet as a bag full of poison and refuses to be on Facebook or Twitter. She says these things, as she seems to say most things, with an enchanting whispery earnestness, which is a tone that's prevalent in her music as well.

McKay is a New York-based singer-songwriter whose birthdate is a secret to Wikipedia but whom I would peg at about 30. She's about to visit Canada to play at the Ottawa, Toronto and Montreal Jazz Festivals. This, like much else about her, is intriguing, because she isnt commonly thought of as a jazz performer, though jazz certainly figures among the many styles and influences that permeate her work.

On one of those YouTube clips youll find her singing, to her own piano accompaniment, the track Sari, which seems to be a pun on the song's key word "sorry"; it's a pounding mock-apologia for just about everything, from the way she dresses to losing a Monty Python CD, whose conclusion - most of all, Im sorry for you, suggests she isnt really sorry at all.

Better still is a clip that starts with her getting up from the piano (whatever she's been doing there remains a secret), picking up a small guitar and going into a song called Mother of Pearl, which begins with what is probably her most famous line, "feminists don't have a sense of humour," on which she elaborates with the more inventive theyve got a tumour on their funny bone.

The point of the song is, of course, that feminists do have a sense of humour - certainly this one does - but it sounds slyer here than it does in its official recorded version. On the CD, a male chorus chimes in with the sexist, derisive comments the song is lampooning; live, she delivers them herself, between barely audible quotation marks, which is funnier and more devastating. She then goes into a song she didnt write, one that in fact dates back to 1928: the sweet, yearning If I Had You, whose avowal "I could cross the burning desert" she makes more touching and fervent than any other singer I have heard.

Between 2004 and 2010, McKay recorded five CDs, four of her own material and one tribute to Doris Day that finds her, apart from her singing, taking turns on seven different instruments. That's more than she actually admits to playing.

I fool around on about four, she tells me in the first of three phone conversations this week and last. (She has a hectic schedule, even without preparing for Canada, and sounded gentle but frazzled.) "The number goes up when we run out of money." At the jazz festivals, it'll be just her, the piano and whatever else she cares to play, which I suspect is preferable. She writes some headlong, wordy, free-associating lyrics; and on record, with a band, they can get lost in the mix.

I hope, she says, "therell be a few less words as I go on."

Of course, live (or on YouTube) you get the bonus of seeing as well as hearing McKay: blond and blue-ribboned, simultaneously demure and outspoken, fragile and steely, with a wide-eyed look that matches her wide-eyed voice (if there is such a thing).

Shes nervous about meeting jazz festival expectations, but at least it seems her repertoire will match up. This festival season, she'll be concentrating on the standards. If it was up to me, I'd sing them all the time. Theyre fresh, theyre exquisite; there's a reason people can't stop singing them, she says. "It makes you a little embarrassed to record your own songs after all those beautiful melodies."

McKay is also quite the musical scholar. On Normal as Blueberry Pie, the Doris Day album, she sometimes sounds more authentic than her model. Day, in her movies, sang 1920s songs in 40s and 50s arrangements; McKay has gone back to the originals. On Crazy Rhythm, her inspiration was Whispering Jack Smith, a pioneering crooner of whom few artists of her generation can even have heard.

Those '20s arrangements, they change, they do repeats, she says, before asking earnestly, "have you ever seen Second Chorus? I love it." It's a 1940 Fred Astaire movie from which comes a Johnny Mercer song with the deathless title (I Aint Hep to That Step But Ill) Dig It. Day sang it in her band-singer days, because it was current; McKay excavates the Astaire original, complete with an intro on which she trades jivey rhythmic spoken phrases with the boys in the band. It's my favourite thing on the CD.

McKay says she doesn't know where her own songs originate. It could be a melody, it could be the lyric, which neatly pre-empts the old question about which comes first. "I always say, I only wrote them. I dont know why Mother of Pearl is called that," she adds. Maybe it has something to do with Janis Joplin. Or maybe its a drug reference.

McKay also says that most of her songs aren't autobiographical. Asked to name one that could be, she chooses The Dog Song, a ragtime romper in honour of a pet that had been put down: "I don't care bout your hatin and your doubt/ And I don't care what the politicians spout/If you want a companion/Well, just go right to the pound/And find yourself a hound."

It figures, since McKay is a fervent spokesperson for People for the Ethical Treatment of Animals and, naturally, a vegetarian: another of the accusations misogynistically hurled by the redneck chorus of Mother of Pearl. This may explain why, of all the great pop vocalists, she chose to honour Day, whose principal postretirement profile has been as an animal rights activist. (McKay also has a biting - in every sense - song about eating burgers.) But in other respects, she's hard to pin down.

Like Randy Newman and Dave Frishberg, two writers McKay admires, shes oblique, a musical actor. Shes appeared on Broadway in The Threepenny Opera, and both her (divorced) parents are actors; her mother, Robin Pappas, some of whose work I saw and admired in Britain in the early '80s, is now her sometime producer and fulltime manager.

She has extraordinary taste, McKay says. "She adds the good stuff; I supply the drive." And, more prosaically, It's good to have someone you can trust; who can take care of all the stuff that has to be planned: visas, flights. In this business, nobody wants to be the facilitator. I was very naive when I started. Maybe I should have some media training. I can't seem to stop getting into trouble.

She says things like that in a whimsically self-deprecating tone few others could get away with, as when she talks of her own work. "I take credit for the bad songs. The good ones come from someplace else."

Nellie McKay plays the Ottawa Jazz Festival on June 29, the Toronto Jazz Festival on June 30 and the Montreal Jazz Festival on July 2.
