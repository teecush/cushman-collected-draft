BETHUNE IMAGINED

Factory Theatre, Toronto

---

Ken Gass' Bethune Imagined imagines the celebrated doctor and political activist in Montreal in 1936. He's considering going off to fight in the Spanish Civil War. In the meantime, he amuses himself by drinking heavily, railing at the medical establishment while touting his own surgical prowess, organizing a children's art workshop, and dallying with a troika of admiring women. It's the last activity that takes up most of the play's time, and supplies most of its interest.

All three ladies have their moment, hence the play does too. But they are moments without momentum. Norman Bethune, known to his female friends as Beth, does take off to join the anti-Franco loyalists at the end of the play, but he might equally well have done so at the beginning. It's hard to see that his amatory exercises have done anything to change his mind. Indeed, it's hard to get into his mind at all. He talks, but his passions, sexual and otherwise, seem interchangeable and indistinguishable. At one point he takes off into a piece of political oratory, for which he's spotlit like a bullfighter in an arena: an incongruous device in a play and production (the author's own) whose style is otherwise routine naturalism. This applies even to a later, and livelier, homage to Hispanic culture in which the characters extol the virtues of flamenco dancing while trying a bit of it.

First flame on stage is Marian Dale Scott, a talented painter with whom Beth had one night of passion -- consummated or not is hard to determine -- on the ocean, years ago; both of them wish at different times to reignite it but never, unfortunately,

at the same time. Marian is Norman's conscience, insofar as he has one. She is followed by Margaret Day, a 22-year-old political acolyte, who offers him her virginity, which he accepts. He gets her pregnant, and she asks him to perform the abortion; again he grants her wish. Marian shares Norman's artistic interests, but is indifferent to his politics; with Margaret it's the reverse. No wonder he needs both of them. Finally, meaning in the second act, we meet Frances Penney, the former Mrs. Bethune, who is over him but not through with him. (Or possibly the other way around.) She is a twinkling, practical soul, who is probably the only one who really understands him. This gives the play a surprising resemblance to Noel Coward's Present Laughter, and as in that play it's the former wife manages to get her ex packed and on board the boat he has to catch.

Advanced soul that he is, Norman has no problems with the three of them knowing about, and even meeting, one

another. Indeed Marian and Margaret, after a few understandably awkward moments (one of them has found him in bed with the other) get on like a house on fire, even collaborating on Norman's art-teaching project. When Frances arrives, they turn out to have a lot in common with her too: All find Norman both exasperating and irresistible, and are very happy to discuss it. It's a middle-aged man's dream; three attractive women arrive at his place in succession, and each of them either starts taking her clothes off or talks as if she might.

Some of the talk is entertaining, even informative, but the total effect is -- in more than one sense -- of vamping till ready. We are obviously meant to see Bethune as a force of nature to whom all must be forgiven, because he's on the right side politically and approaches everything in his life, if not with passion, then at least with gusto. Here this boils down to obstinate shamelessnes; if that's meant to amount to magnetism, the writing doesn't explain it and Ron White's energetic performance doesn't supply it. Marian has the most interesting trajectory -- to put it another way, we're never sure how she feels about Norman, and neither is she--and Irene Poole plays her intelligently, if sometimes excessively, on her nerves. Sascha Cole plays newbie Margaret with appropriate bluntness, and as Frances, Fiona Byrne brings onto the scene a welcome Scots practicality, and an even more welcome Scots mischief. But if Bethune has been imagined, the imagination seems to have been extremely selective. The play leaves you wanting to know more about him, not because it's made him fascinating but because it hasn't.
