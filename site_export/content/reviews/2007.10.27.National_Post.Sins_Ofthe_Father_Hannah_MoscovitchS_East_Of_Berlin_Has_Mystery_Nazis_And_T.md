Hannah Moscovitch is 29, so it's a bit early to go hunting for grand patterns in her work. Nevertheless, the four plays of hers I've seen, all of which I like a lot, do seem to have a common theme. That theme, an inherently dramatic one, is entrapment. Her new play, East of Berlin, presents this in classic form. Its hero might be under a Greek curse. He might also be under a Biblical curse. There's a note in the program that's labelled "The Sins of the Father," but even without that it would be impossible to sit through this piece's packed 90 minutes without that text coming to mind.

The father's sin in this play is a damnable one; he was a doctor at Auschwitz. Like so many Nazis, he fled to Paraguay after the war, with his wife and his young son, Rudi. The play is Rudi's monologue, interspersed with encounters with two other people. Rudi has grown up in ignorance of his father's past (he's now a real estate salesman) until a friend, a more precocious and inquisitive boy called Hermann with equivalent skeletons in his own family closet, clues him in. Rudi, now in his late teens, is naturally appalled. He confronts his father, who doesn't exactly deny the charges but doesn't admit much guilt either: He had no choice, he would have been punished had he refused, and so on. (According to Jonah Goldhagen's Hitler's Willing Executioners -- admittedly a tendentious book -- this isn't true.) His wife reveals that she urged him to stay, which seems all too plausible.

These parental encounters are narrated, not shown, but we do witness the crucial conversations with Hermann, which end in a seduction. Hermann is gay, Rudi isn't, but it's Rudi who makes the advances, primarily as a way of getting back at his old man. This isn't enough rebellion, though, so Rudi takes himself off to study in Berlin. He's financed by a grant from a Nazi foundation; I usually despise exclamation points, but !

Dropping out of medical school (the apple doesn't fall, etc.), he spends his time in the archives, researching his father's guilt. There he meets Sarah, a German-Jew-ish-American girl investigating her own family's history, i.e., the same subject from the opposite side. The two fall, axiomatically, in love. Moscovitch, who has reported on love in previous plays, here proves herself capable of presenting it: economically, delightfully, movingly.

Both are turned on by the differences between them; and for Rudi especially -- if he shocked his father with a gay fling, then how about a Jewish marriage? Naturally, he puts off telling Sarah the whole truth. When it comes out, he's faced with an impossible choice: of loyalties, of betrayals. And it isn't his fault, any more than it was Hamlet's or Oedipus's. Less, really. But that never let anybody off the hook. We're always told that plays should ask us questions. Usually the questions are rigged. But when Rudi at the end asks our approval for whatever it is he's about to do (what he actually says, chillingly, is "This is the only solution, isn't it?") we are thoroughly implicated. Also, we wouldn't know what to tell him. Psychologically, this is truthful; morally, it's just; dramatically, it's terrific.

The play's first half is disconcerting: not in subject, but in tone. Moscovitch's sense of humour, dark but not cynical, is at full alert here, but as we don't yet know whom Rudi is talking to, or why, he comes off more glib than troubled. It's only in retrospect that we understand; and even at that a line like "I thought you were resettled -- I mean dead" sticks in the gullet. The evening doesn't fall into what I'd call theatrical present tense until Sarah appears, from then on it moves like fate on wheels, prompting lumps in the throat and knots in the stomach.

Alisa Palmer's direction is both sensitive and tense. Camellia Koo's set is magnificent: a long, ashen book-lined wall that does duty for any number of drawing-rooms and libraries, culminating in the Auschwitz archive and, by extension, the camp itself. Michael Walton supplies superbly ominous lighting, and John Gzowski a haunting sound score, full of half-heard melodies.

As for the acting: Brendan Gall's Rudi, like the play itself, takes time to come into focus, but it develops into a wrenching study of gangling guilt and of hope perpetually deferred. Paul Dunn's Hermann, edgy and chipper, brings a surge of energy whenever he appears. Diana Donnelly, who has moved with increasing assurance since her initial misstep with Major Barbara, hits full stride here; her Sarah has exactly the right measure of wariness and abandon. Moscovitch has usually been lucky in her interpreters, and they in her; the match here is just about perfect
