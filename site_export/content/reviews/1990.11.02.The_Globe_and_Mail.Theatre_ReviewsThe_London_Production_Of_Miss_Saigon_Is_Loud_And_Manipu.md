THEATRE REVIEWS"The London production of Miss Saigon is loud and manipulative and the score makes one nostalgic for Andrew Lloyd Webber. But the West End boasts some other choice pickings A hit that's actually a big miss

Friday, November 02, 1990

ROBERT CUSHMAN

BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail WHAT'S entertainment? In the old movie The Band Wagon Fred Astaire and friends proclaimed, joyously and irresistibly, that anything could be. "The lights on the lady in tights . . . the scene where the villain is mean . . . the plight of the children in flight. . . ."

The last one is my own invention, based on a much-applauded number - the second-half opener in fact - in Miss Saigon. The musical, just into its second year at London's Theatre Royal, Drury Lane, is now set for an April opening in New York, complete with Jonathan Pryce as the Eurasian pimp the Engineer. This is what I hoped, expected and argued for in an article recently in The Globe and Mail. Having done my bit as a cultural reporter and running dog of Western imperialism, I would like to revert to theatre criticism, and explain why I hated the show.

Miss Saigon doesn't flinch from showing us the fall of its eponymous city at the end of the Vietnam war. Great gates swing back and forth, evacuating Americans and desperate inhabitants mill back and forth on either side of them, and finally the famous helicopter takes off, brutally separating Miss Saigon, an updated Madam Butterfly, from her Pinkerton. It is all expert, exciting and perfectly legitimate. But though the show trusts itself to portray violence theatrically, it loses its nerve when it comes to pathos. Miss Saigon has a child and flees with him to Bangkok. At this point we are shown film of Vietnamese children in Thai refugee camps. It hardly rates as atrocity footage by today's competitive standards, but these are still real children in distress, being used in the service of a fictitious story. The esthetic gaffe is nothing to the ethical offence. I kept averting my eyes. I doubt if I would have flinched from the same material on the TV news or in the interstices of a Live Aid concert: which does not mean that the suffering would have affected me less. Here it is used as illustration to a song, so of course it gets applause. It's a number.

Not all of the show is on that manipulative level, but much of it is. Claude-Michel Schonberg, whose music might have been designed to make us repent any uncharitable thoughts we may have had about Andrew Lloyd Webber, has a reflex reaction when emotion seems called for - up the volume, up the key - but his garish idiom is at least more appropriate to the Coca-Cola ethos of the Americanized East than it was to the streets of nineteenth-century Paris in Les Miserables. Alain Boublil's libretto is also more specific about historical cause and effect than the previous show.

Despite the frantic toughness of the treatment, the Butterfly story remains as droopy as it always was. And yet this version does offer one unforgettable image. Across the seemingly vast expanse of a hotel room, Miss Saigon confronts her lover's new American wife, who thinks for a moment that she must be the maid. Characters and audience look simultaneously into an abyss of shattered hopes. The authors then spoil it all with a long song, but silence has said more than all the noise they can generate.

Nicholas Hytner's direction and John Gunter's designs are as remarkable for their poise as far as their fluency; this is the best- staged of all the giant British musicals. The embattled Pryce is commanding, tireless, sly and very likeable: maybe too much so. The character he plays is meant to be a creep, using the heroine and her boy to hitch a ride to the place where he knows he belongs, the corrupt United States of his dreams. Pryce is a heroic actor and too big for the Engineer's sneakers. He is, in fact, miscast, and not for ethnic reasons. But he was irresistible in London, and will be so in New York. A Welshman, he has been replaced at Drury Lane by a Scot (and another classically based actor), Hilton McRae. Occidents will happen.

Meanwhile, the London dramatic stage has got religion. If the local press is to be believed, the value of a play is in direct ratio to the number of clergymen it has in its cast. Simon Gray's Hidden Laughter is currently the biggest dramatic hit in the West End, thanks partly to the participation of Felicity Kendal and Peter Barkworth, both fine stage actors who are also popular TV stars. Barkworth plays a country vicar who becomes the friend and confidant of a weekending family from London. Devoid of faith themselves, they expect him to have it for them: a professional duty he finds onerous since he has barely enough for himself. The play somewhat resembles the same author's Quartermaine's Terms, whose central character was the selflessly ineffectual centre of a mean and striving world. Here the divisions are less clear-cut, with the emphasis equally on the crumbling marriage of his charming neighbors and on their horrible children. As the author digs deeper, both the charm and the horror wear off, which is a relief; Gray is almost too good at depicting the casual cruelty of what are currently known in Britain as the chattering classes, and the first half of the play seems in consequence a bit glib. What comes over in the second part is pain and honest bafflement. Kendal plays a woman who belatedly discovers herself as a writer, Kevin McNally her literary-agent husband: obviously a doomed match. McNally ends the evening surveying the wreckage of his home and family and asking what went wrong: a commonplace ending but maybe inescapable.

Religion seems to stand in this play for something that people crave but cannot trust. In David Hare's Racing Demon, at the National Theatre, it is whatever the characters would like it to be. Some 20 years ago, a British bishop created a furor with an un-doctrinal book called The God I Want. Each of the characters in this play is granted a short soliloquy addressing a deity created not so much in their own image as in their own physical and spiritual environment. The rector of a poor London district, with little trust in ritual or in anything except his own limited capacity to do something practical for his parishioners, is crushed between his right-thinking bishop and his evangelical curate. Conservative and radical join forces to defeat the lonely liberal: a traditional, and still potent, British theme.
