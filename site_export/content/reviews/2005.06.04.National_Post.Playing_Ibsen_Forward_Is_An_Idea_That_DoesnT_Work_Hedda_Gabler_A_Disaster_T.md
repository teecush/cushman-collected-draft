robertcushman@lycos.com; Hedda Gabler runs through June 12 at Buddies in Bad Times Theatre, 416-975-8555.

There seem to be two reasons why Hedda Gabler gets more productions than almost any other Ibsen play. First, it has only seven characters; and second, one of those seven, Hedda herself, is a role every leading actress feels she owes it to herself to play. God knows why.

For a woman who's been tagged as the female Hamlet, Hedda displays a remarkably narrow range of feelings, nearly all of them disagreeable. She's snobbish, she is -- to put it very mildly -- manipulative, she's frigidly flirtatious and she's cruel. That might seem to put her in good dramatic company: "Macbeth, after all, is a mass murderer, but he has a conscience about it, and his conscience, to a great extent, is the play. Hedda is determinedly self- righteous about her faults, though she does, at what is probably her most engaging moment, admit to being a coward, unable to break free from the conventions she professes to despise. Somehow, though, in the world outside the play, she has gained the reputation of actually being the rebel she would like to be. Either that, or it's all society's fault."

Somewhere in the reams of interviews that preceded the Volcano company's disastrous new production there is a reference to Hedda's being "ahead of her time." She isn't; she's behind it. Within the play itself we see her old schoolfriend Thea Elvsted earning Hedda's hatred by doing what she herself is scared of doing -- leaving her marriage to befriend the dissolute but presumably gifted writer Eilert Lovborg.

A full decade earlier, and even more decisively, Ibsen had shown us Nora slamming the door on her doll's house, in a play that used to be denigrated as an ephemeral pamphlet compared to Hedda Gabler but that now generates far more electricity.

There is another sense in which Hedda is old-fashioned, to the point of being an anachronism, and it's much more interesting. Like other Ibsen protagonists, Hedda, daughter of a general, dreams of being a tragic hero, and finds herself in a context that has no use for such a thing. In Ibsen's fallen, tragicomic world, vainglorious master builders lose their footing and tumble off towers, and disgraced industrialists, dreaming of a comeback, keel over and die in the snow.

Hedda, to avoid a scandal and the threat of sexual blackmail, makes a cleaner end by shooting herself -- offstage -- with her late father's pistols. Judge Brack, her would-be seducer, famously greets this event with the shocked observation that "people don't do such things," a remark whose irony is doubled by the fact that Hedda had used exactly those words to mock the behaviour of her husband's aunt in leaving her hat lying on the sofa. Suicide is reduced to the level of a social faux pas. You can see why Hedda would want out, even though she has been complicit in the system when it suited her.

That at least is what happens in the play Ibsen wrote. In Judith Thompson's adaptation, as directed by Ross Manson, Brack's last line has gone. In exchange we get a long, hallucinatory speech about horse-riding that Yanna McIntosh as Hedda is required to deliver between gunshots while dragging herself bleeding around the stage. The lady is thus deprived even of the final freezing dignity that her author granted her, though I have a suspicion that Manson and Thompson believe that in giving her a dying aria they are building her up rather than bringing her down. The speech itself connects with Hedda's earlier demand that her husband buy her the horse he promised and that he can no longer afford. That incident takes up one line of the original; in this version it seems to go on for petulant pages.

The play was written in 1890. Here its first half has been advanced to the beginning of the 20th century. After the intermission we find we have jumped still further forward; the time is The Present and the place (to judge from a single interpolated joke-line about Queen's University) is Here. The inspiration for the time shift seems to be the wonderful new book Lovborg has written about the future, a choice of subject audacious enough to put the fear of God into Hedda's placid husband, George, who is in competition with Eilert for a university post. That's the dramatic point, not whether Lovborg personally thinks the future will be good, bad or indifferent; he doesn't say, not in Ibsen. Thompson, though, has decided the matter for him: The future is going to be awful. Accordingly she takes us first to within hailing distance of the First World War, and then to the world as it is today, and we all know what to think about that.

The idea panders to our modern mania for making old authors agree with us, but it does nothing to illuminate Hedda herself. It also emasculates the play's shock-horror climax. Hedda's new living room affords no stove in which she can burn the manuscript she thinks of as Lovborg's and Thea's child; she has to mess around with a fire bucket instead.

Anyway, it's impossible to believe she is destroying the only copy: not in a world in which Kinko's stays open 24 hours in case, as stated by the late, great Mitch Hedberg, you should wake up in the middle of the night feeling you need two of something. The updating is distracting for the audience and, I would guess, a hindrance to the actors.

Manson and Thompson have of course a perfect right to use the basics of Ibsen's play as a jumping-off point for their own original creation. But that isn't what they've done. They've stuck close to their source, while pausing from time to time to pad it out with the occasional new line or even scene of their own. Some of these underline what's already there; others strike out from it, but without getting very far. It makes for a long evening.

Like everyone, I looked forward to seeing McIntosh as Hedda, and like everyone I seem to have been guilty of arbitrarily matching star actress to star role. McIntosh looks striking in her red dress, and Teresa Przybylski's deep white set allows her to take some commanding upstage poses. I admired, too, the convulsive movement of her hands when she begins to contemplate literary infanticide. But what McIntosh has always projected best is fearlessness and generosity, qualities that are pretty much the antithesis of Hedda. Nor does she do justice to the brittle comedy inherent in a woman who makes jokes at everybody else's expense while life plays its bitterest joke on her. She offers instead a kind of majestic resentment, monotonous and rather forced. Even more effortful is the boyish Brack of Nigel Shawn Williams, floundering in a vain attempt to do suave.

Ann Baggley's Thea is wooden in everything that pertains to Ibsen but a touch livelier in some added moments that allow her to get cross with the heroine. There is lovely work from Alon Nashman as a conciliatory George, a worrier wed to a wife who wanted a warrior, and a charismatic turn from Tom McCamus, whose Lovborg is a tousled charmer in the Dylan Thomas manner. He is acting in a different style from everybody else, more obtrusively modern (i.e. he gabbles), but that may be part of the concept; he is the Harbinger of the Future, after all.

Tanja Jacobs seizes every opportunity afforded by the role of Aunt Juliana and then a few over. George's auntie, fussy but kind, is the nicest of the nice people to whom Hedda considers herself superior. Another of her butts is the maid Berthe, played by Cynthia Ashperger, who in this adaptation gets to state at great length her objections to the new lady of the household. Come the new millennium she gets real bossy, and starts addressing her employer by his first name. So I told a lie; the play does have a new subplot -- the development of the servant class.

The fast-forwarding also permits far more detail than the original provided, not only about the contents of Lovborg's book but about what he and the other guys get up to when they're out on the town. I confess I always used to find Ibsen's 19th-century reticence on such matters rather trying. I see now that I didn't know when I was well off.
