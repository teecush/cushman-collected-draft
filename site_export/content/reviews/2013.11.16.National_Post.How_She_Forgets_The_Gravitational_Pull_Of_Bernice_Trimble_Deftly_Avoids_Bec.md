Among other things The Gravitational Pull of Bernice Trimble is a tale of two kitchens. They occupy the same stage space, and our visual clue as to which we are in at a given moment is the presence or absence of salt and pepper shakers. Bernice herself is a great shaker-collector, and Camellia Koo's set, one of her best, gives us shelves and shelves of them, stretching in every direction as far as our eyes can see.

Bernice is also a lively, organized, recently widowed woman who, in her mid-50s, is diagnosed with earlyonset Alzheimers: the same disease that took her mother. She now has to communicate this news to her own three grown children. Theres Peter, a confessedly awkward" statistician, who takes conflict-avoidance to artistic levels; Sara who's just the opposite, constantly spoiling for a fight; and Iris, whom Sara fights with the most, and who falls temperamentally between the other two. Iris is also the inhabitant of the other, shakerless kitchen, and our narrator. We see and hear the action entirely through her eyes and ears. We find her at the beginning of the play preparing one of her mother's recipes, a sodiumheavy dish that Bernice jovially calls "the everything-that-is-bad-for-you casserole, and that consorts rather oddly with her extreme phobia about dirt. The casserole preparation also serves as a kind of countdown, something cunningly clarified in the plays last wracking moments.

One of the subtleties of Beth Grahams script is that while Sara is violently vociferous about the chances of Bernice's condition being passed on to her and eventually to her own baby daughter, Iris obviously feels equally threatened but keeps quiet about it. She is, she tells us, an expert at biting her own tongue; she's found that, in her family, its the best way to survive. She talks to herself, or to us (the play can get too cute about this) as compensation. The family tensions are as vivid and believable as in any play since Death of Salesman, and theyre faultlessly realized in Philip Akin's pitch-perfect production. Akin is the artistic director of Obsidian Theatre, who present the play and who are Toronto's leading black company. This isnt a black" play; the only ethnic touches in the dialogue seem to be Italian, and the author is white. What we have are four good actors, presenting a credible family in a situation that could affect anybody and tear them, individually and collectively, apart.

Making Iris rather than Bernice the centre of the play was a smart move. It means we get a drama rather than a clinical study. The play, as the title indicates, isnt so much about her as about her effect on those she loves and who loves her. Karen Robinson, indomitable even in disarray, shows us as much about Bernice as we need to know; she's almost cheerfully (and movingly) determined even when contemplating suicide, thus administering the ultimate family litmus test. Iris' narrator role is taken to another kind of extreme; she describes the others actions and even their tones of voice as theyre happening, as if she were doing closed-circuit captioning. The sheer length of the role places a heavy technical burden on the actress; Alexis Gordon, who's a year out of drama school and has spent most of that year doing musicals, shoulders it phenomenally well. There isn't a false or faltering moment in her performance.

Sara presents an opposite problem, which Lucinda Davis attacks, successfully, by aggressively going for it: by revelling in her compulsion to impart all the physical details about her little girl Heaven (great name for a future stripper, Iris muses sardonically), about her rage and denial and unforgivingness, all of it understandable if hardly endearing. As Peter, Peyson Rock is taciturnity eloquent, and occasionally hilarious. This is, for obvious reasons, an upsetting play; its also a riveting one.

Joan Macleods The Valley, another four-hander, ranges wider but not as deep. It's set in Vancouver. Each of the characters begins by telling us of his or her first encounter with the police, usually in childhood. One of them, Dan, grew up to be a cop. He married Janie, an ex-addict, and they have a baby son. One day Dan finds himself called to a troublescene; a young man, Connor, clearly disturbed, is alarming his fellow passengers on a train by brandishing what might be, but actually isn't a dangerous weapon. Connor is a possible prodigy, who wrote a whole fantasy novel as a youth, then dropped out of his first term at university, and is now living with his mother, a divorced bookstore employee named Sharon. Connors jaw gets broken in the confrontation, and Sharon accuses Dan of using excessive force. What the truth about this might be I cannot testify, as in Richard Roses production, with all locations simultaneously depicted and the audience on two sides, my view of the incident was completely blocked by a large sofa. That, I should add, is not typical of the staging, which is mostly clear and fluid and stunningly lit by Graeme S. Thomson.

The train scene comes towards the end of the first act. Before then, the two sets of characters have existed in separate alternating compartments, coming to life, like the play as a whole, only in fits and starts. The men get the best of it. Ian Lake's Dan, certainly not a brute, is an unshakably true-to-life conflicted cop (or maybe I should say he's true to the image of the cop in the best TV shows). Colin Mercer is equally unassailable as Connor, equal parts stubborn and volatile, railing at his mother for using adverbs, which she does, shamelessly and repeatedly. She also mysteriously tells him not to make fun of Thomas Hardy. Susan Coynes Sharon is fine when given specific strong emotions to convey, but has too often to fall back on generalized concern. As Janie, Michelle Monteith adds to her list of terrifying meltdowns, but earlier is something of a blur; her climactic scene is one that the play hasnt really earned. It gestures toward broad issues - strains on and by the police especially in Vancouver, lack of social services especially in Vancouver, the rift between those who live high up and those lower down, literally and figuratively, again in Vancouver - but the gestures aren't followed through. Where the play works, it works domestically.
