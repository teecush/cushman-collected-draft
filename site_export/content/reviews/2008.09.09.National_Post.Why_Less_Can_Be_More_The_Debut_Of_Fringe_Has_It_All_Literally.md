The first episode of the new sci-fi-cum-terrorist show Fringe ends with the following remarkable exchange:

"How long has he been dead?"

"Five hours."

"Question him."

Well, it can't be vampires, because we already have a show about them. In fact, I know after watching the 90-minute pilot pretty much what's led us to this point, but to tell you too much would be spoiling. But I think that I can safely quote another couple of lines of dialogue that occur a few frames earlier. A supporting-role cop, male, remarks to a starring-role cop, female, "corporations have higher security clearance than we do." He then adds: "The truth is, we're obsolete." You mean, no more tense conversations betweenlaw-enforcement twosomes sitting in cars? That can't be. His other observation, though, is probably correct. So far -- and the plot could easily thicken--most of the skulduggery in this show is traceable to a multi-billion dollar concern with gleaming white offices called Massive Dynamic, who are into chemicals. Whether they're also in league with aliens remains to be seen.

Let's go back to the top. Fringe comes from creators who have worked on Lost and Star Trek, and it has the marks. But it begins more like 24, starting as that show did all those years ago with a mysterious incident on an aeroplane leading to the grisly demise of all on-board. This time, though, the captain has the presence of mind to put the plane on autopilot before succumbing himself, and it makes a safe landing at Logan Airport in Boston. (Dead, if you'll pardon the pun, on schedule: "Unlike" says a cop -- the same one, I think -- "any flight I've ever taken.") The 24 vibe returns later in the episode when a rogue civilian, who nevertheless is on the side of the good guys, goes Jack Bauer on a suspect from whom he requires vital information in a very short time. I hate to tell you that it works.

Our central character, though not so far a very involving one, is Olivia Dunham (Anna Torv), FBI Special Agent. What a pleasure to be able to type those last two words again; they bring back a cozier past. We first meet Olivia at a motel where she's having a romantic tryst with a colleague. I'm not clear why they couldn't go to his place or hers, but this rapidly ceases to be of any moment, as the guy inhales a dose of whatever it was that laid the passengers and crew so low and spends the rest of the episode hovering between life and death. Up to this point, Olivia has been professionally interested in finding who or what killed a whole planeload of people. Now, it's personal.

She's working, not too comfortably, with a high-up from Homeland Security (the new glamour-outfit in television land) who addresses her as "li-ai-son"; he's played by Lance Reddick, who seems to have been hired to repeat his upstanding policeman performance as Lieutenant Daniels on The Wire, but with less nuanced material; Daniels could be tough on his men, but this guy just seems rude. When Anna discovers a promising lead, he more or less tells her to stuff it and get back to her desk. She perseveres and the trail leads her to what the titles helpfully identify as "Baghdad, Iraq." (Oh, that Baghdad.) I doubt that the show itself went there; indeed I hope it didn't, not just for the sake of one brief scene in an anonymous hotel lobby in whose background men in headdresses may occasionally be glimpsed. Also hanging out there, and the object of the trip, is Pacey from Dawson's Creek. Well, I'm sorry but, given the basic level of competence that most TV shows can be trusted to deliver, one tends to respond to the actors less for what they're doing now than for what they've done before. Joshua Jackson, who was the best thing in Dawson's Creek, has aged (not too noticeably) into the best thing so far on this one. He's Peter, the near-genius son of a total-genius father whom Olivia needs to track down, and whose cooperation is secured by means of some simple blackmail. He and Olivia display the on-sight animosity that is an almost infallible fictional sign of deep romantic attraction. I'm not saying they're in one another's arms yet; I mean, she does have an out-of-action boyfriend.

Peter's dad is, literally, a mad scientist; he's been locked up for 17 years for nefarious chemical experiments. But he seems to want to atone, and one of his helpful actions is to devise a cocktail of drugs, including but not limited to LSD, that will en-able Olivia to make mental contact with her comatose partner. This is one of the many points in the show that requires Jackson to signal "you can't be serious." (He does it very well.)His father, who looks like Solzhenitsyn until he shaves his beard off, is John Noble, an Australian actor playing an Englishman. (Torv is an Australian playing an American; Jackson is a Canadian playing an American. Nobody's accent gives trouble.) The doctor is allowed to practise in his old underground lab, which since his enforced departure has been used as a storage room. The contrast of these surroundings with the glistening quarters of The Company (sorry again, wrong show; same idea) is its own moral comment. Representatives of both sides remark, ominously, on there being "a Pattern." Unravelling it is the mission to which Olivia, Peter and Dr. Frankenstein (his son's pet name for him) are summoned at episode's end by the former Lt. Daniels, who seems delighted to have his very own special detail again.

Is it good? It certainly moves fast. I'd quite like to know what happens next, which is not quite the same as dying to know. It's the difference between suspense and mere curiosity, which is also the difference between shows with human content and shows without. As a die-hard fan of 24 and Prison Break, I can hardly complain about lack of credibility, but those shows survive by making you invest in the people rather than the technology. That's as true for science fiction as for any other kind. It's amazing what they can do these days. Sometimes the art is to refrain from doing it. - Fringe debuts tonight at 8 p. m. on CTV and A.
