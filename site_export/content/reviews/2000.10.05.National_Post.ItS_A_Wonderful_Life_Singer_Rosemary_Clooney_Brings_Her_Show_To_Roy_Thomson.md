Rosemary Clooney and her band, Big Kahuna and the Copa Cat Pack, perform tonight at Roy Thomson Hall, 8 p.m. Call (416) 872-4255 for ticket information.

I have always wanted to hear Rosemary Clooney sing The Way You Look Tonight. She never has: Not on record, anyway.

But a week or so ago I got to interview her on the phone -- she was at home in Los Angeles, preparing for a tour that brings her tonight to Roy Thomson Hall -- and mentioned the song to her. She immediately started singing it, beautifully and with confidence.

Talking to Ms. Clooney, I discovered, is a little like attending one of her shows: In any setting, she exudes intelligent friendliness.

Her career began in Maysville, Ky., where she was born in 1928, and made her first singing appearance there at the age of three on the stage of the local movie theatre.

Somewhat later, she and her sister, Betty, began singing on local radio stations. Just after the Second World War they got themselves a job with the popular Tony Pastor band. Rosemary went single, had a colossal hit in 1951 with the novelty song Come on-a My House -- which she recorded pretty much under duress.

She stayed on top through the 1950s, but her career began to slide in the 1960s, when pop-music tastes changed radically and when her own life -- including her marriage to actor Jose Ferrer -- fell apart.

She had a breakdown, and was committed to a mental hospital. She now simply refers to this event as "my illness. When she got out, she embarked on two new and enduring relationships. One was with an old friend, a former dancer named Dante DiPaolo (a couple of years ago they finally got round to getting married). The other was with a small California-based record label, Concord Jazz, which in 1976 asked her to record an album with a small jazz group. The album was called Everything's Coming Up Rosie. Twenty-five years later, she is still recording for Concord, at the rate of one CD a year.

It is an amazing thing to be with one company for as long as I have," she says. "I can choose the material I want to do.

This material -- which she characteristically performs with humour, warmth, insistent rhythm, and a unique kind of triumphant bittersweetness -- centres on what has become known as the Great American Songbook," the standard repertoire drawn largely from the songs of such composers as Cole Porter, Richard Rodgers and Irving Berlin. She and Tony Bennett are considered the foremost modern interpreters of these compositions.

It isn't exactly the world she was born into, but its one she speedily became part of. Indeed, even her home in Los Angeles resonates with the ghosts of some of Americas most important pop artists.

"I'm sitting now in the same room where I last saw Billie Holiday, she told me. I'm sitting looking at the sunshine just as I did then. Only then the room was full of smoke. And Billie was drinking gin."

The house she was talking from -- the one shes lived in for decades -- once belonged to George Gershwin. For years, Georges lyricist brother Ira was her neighbour.

These days, Love Is Here To Stay, the last song George composed, is one of her two favourite closing numbers. (Her other choice is James Taylor's philosophical The Secret of Life.)

At 71, Clooney has become one of the singers people go to for wisdom. She's also one of the few who can dispense it palatably.

In her Toronto show, shell be doing some things from her new album, Brazil, but shell also be harking back to "things I did with [Count] Basie.

I'll probably do I Concentrate on You -- a Cole Porter song that's achieved honourary Latin status --"but I havent spoken to [pianist] John Oddo yet." (I stole him from Woody Herman.)

She obviously cant sing precisely as she used to do. "The breath- support began to go" when she was 55.

"I cope with the changes in my voice as I plan the show. I try not to change anything about the song itself; I want to sing it as it was written. That's what I've always felt most comfortable with."
