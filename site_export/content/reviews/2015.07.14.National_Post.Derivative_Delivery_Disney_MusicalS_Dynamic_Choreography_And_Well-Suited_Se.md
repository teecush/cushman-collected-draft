THEATRE REVIEW

Newsies Ed Mirvish Theatre, Toronto

It's 1899, and Joseph Pulitzer fears that the World may be coming to an end. He isn't thinking globally. He's referring to the New York World, the paper of which he is the proprietor and whose profits are declining. He proposes to rescue it on the backs of the "newsies" - the young boys who hawk his papers in the streets. He will charge them an extra dime for the privilege, reasoning that this will make them even more desperate than they already are and that circulation will go up accordingly. Mr Pulitzer is a double-dyed capitalist villain, a point that the musical Newsies illustrates by giving him a song called The Bottom Line. When Newsies played on Broadway a few seasons ago, it won Tonys. It did not win a Pulitzer Prize.

The newsies of Manhattan respond by forming themselves into an impromptu union and going on strike. Pulitzer's competitors, all except a certain William Randolph Hearst (unseen but much mentioned), are only too pleased to report on Joe's troubles, and for a brief intoxicating moment the kids who shouted the headlines are making them. It doesn't last; the rival press barons decide that their bread is buttered on the same side as Pulitzer's.

The oxygen of publicity is turned off; the cops and strikebreakers are called in. Despair threatens. But the boys rally, and call in reinforcements from the other boroughs. They bring the city to a halt. Pulitzer caves.

It's a stirring story and its outlines are true. The newsboys' strike also had a knock-on effect; it helped bring attention to New York's other exploited youngsters, though it's doubtful this happened as quickly as the musical would have us believe. This is a Disney show based on a Disney movie so it's history at two removes.

Both versions take as their hero a charismatic 17-year old strike leader, considerably more wholesome and clean-cut than his historical original seems to have been. On both stage and screen he has a love-interest. In the show, the lady is an aspiring reporter with a secret. Her situation proves to be similar to that of the heroine of Urinetown, and this is far from the only thing in the show that's derivative. Christopher Gattelli's choreography, the evening's main selling point, looks like West Side Story plus back-flips. The newsies, whether defiant or despondent or triumphant, are constantly breaking into lengthy acrobatic routines, one of them incongruously incorporating the leading lady. The leading man, meantime, is conspicuous by his absence.

Our hero, name of Jack Kelly, turns out to be a golden-hearted rascal with a tough veneer that's easily pierced. He also has a rather arbitrary yearning to move to Santa Fe. He expresses this in a song of that title, with which he starts the show and which he reprises, with vein-bursting insistence, at the halfway point, when he feels that the strike has failed and he just has to get away from it all. It's obvious that he's not going to, so the intended suspense falls flat. "This 'go west, young man' stuff is getting really tired" says his sweetheart, earning our gratitude by staving off a third rendition.

Aspirational ballads are not what Alan Menken's score is best at, but then very few scores are. His rhythmic rouser numbers for the juvenile chorus are more pleasantly if generically infectious; one of them, King of New York, I haven't been able to shake. They come with appropriately punchy lyrics, by Jack Feldman, though I had to listen to the CD to find this out; the sound quality in the theatre was awful.

The heroine has a number called Watch What Happens, about the stops and starts of filing a story, which is conversationally clever in a Sondheim style; unfortunately it doesn't connect with anything else in the show, including its own reprise. The sets, by Tobin Ost, make heavy use of newsprint, which is both appropriate and agreeable. Dan DeLuca as Jack is full-throatedly efficient, and the attendant newsies dance up several storms. The acting of the grown-ups is, by and large, atrocious.

Until August 30.
