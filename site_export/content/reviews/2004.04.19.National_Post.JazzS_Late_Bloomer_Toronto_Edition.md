Andy Bey's recording career took a while to get going. But his new CD proves it has been worth the wait

In the Fifties and early Sixties, there was a fine sibling vocal group in the United States known as Andy and the Bey Sisters. One of the sisters, Salome, subsequently moved to Canada, to become a bright light on the Toronto musical and theatre scene. Andy Bey, the brother and the main musical mind behind the group, embarked on what has proved to be a distinguished but troubled existence as a singer- pianist.

Some of the troubles were matters of temperament; he seems to have had little taste or talent for the politics of the music business. Some were to do with health; he was diagnosed HIV- positive, though he has not developed full-blown AIDS. His recording career hardly got going until the 1990s, by which time he was in his sixties. But it has been worth the wait. His new release, American Song (Savoy Jazz), strikes me as the best ballad album by a male singer since Frank Sinatra's late masterpiece She Shot Me Down.

The first Andy Bey solo CD I heard, a live set recorded in Zagreb and released on a Polish label, was called As Time Goes By (Jazzette; good luck in finding it) and one thing it revealed was that, for Bey, time went by very slowly. I would continually come to the end of a six-minute cut and realize that, not only had he spent all that time singing just one song but he had sung only one chorus of it. I also realized that, despite this, I had not been bored. He has a hypnotic voice and delivery.

The delivery is, nearly always, quiet and exploratory. The voice itself, though, suggests other possibilities, being a capacious bass- baritone in the tradition of earlier black crooners such as Billy Eckstine and Johnny Hartman. Bey has something of Hartmans uncanny smoothness, though where Hartmans reflected the suavity of a drawing room, Bey's suggests the contemplative ambience of a half- empty bar. It is, now at any rate, a somewhat ravaged voice. There are shards of gravel in the vibrato that add texture without hurting the tone. But he can also move, without apparent strain, into an unblemished falsetto that presumably owes something to early singing in church. The contrasting heights and depths suggest the flexibility of Sarah Vaughan but applied with the stillness and economy of Shirley Horn.

His American revival (few people can have heard the Polish disc, though in some ways it's my favourite) began in 1996 with Ballads, Blues & Bey (Evidence), his haggard voice, weathered but forceful, matched to his musing piano. It includes a desolate Willow, Weep for Me, in which the falsetto becomes truly eerie and which is marred only by one of those interpolated codas that singers seem compelled to throw in to convince us that theyre singing jazz. (The obediently tearful willow is exhorted "better do it while you can as if there were some danger it might stop.) Theres an amazing Embraceable You in which the words come to papa, come to papa, do" - - more or less spoken -- just about jump off the disc; on the repeat chorus, he sort of growls them.

His superb assurance does have its downside. When he goes off on a wrong tack there's no way of stopping him, and on the two faster tracks here -- You'd Be So Nice to Come Home To and Yesterdays -- he seems to be confining his accompaniment to the same bass riff played over and over. In the liner notes he professes himself extremely proud of this device, but it gets very monotonous. What the disc most splendidly reveals is his affinity with the music of Duke Ellington and especially of Ellingtons collaborator Billy Strayhorn. In a Sentimental Mood is perfect for him; he revivifies Im Just a Lucky So and So, on which most singers knock themselves out to be cheerful, by sounding perilously surprised, as if he can't believe the luck; and his Day Dream -- credited to both composers but sounding, in its pent-up yearning, overwhelmingly like Strayhorn -- is probably definitive.

For his next outing, Shades of Bey (1998), Evidence gave him a band. The opening track is Like a Lover, a Brazilian tune fitted out with a roseate English text; it's in the category that I think of as nature-ramble lyrics. He manages to make it sound sensible, with an economical, matter-of-fact delivery. He even, with a new rumble in his voice on the low notes and remarkable purity in the high ones, gives it gravitas. Going outside the usual parameters of the Great American Songbook, he discovers, among other things, Nick Drakes River Man, which fits the canon very nicely; it sounds like Nature Boy, only intelligent. Thelonious Monks Straight No Chaser (songified as Get It Straight) doesn't suit him; it's far too quick and abrupt. But there are two lovely Ducal things. Pretty Girl (originally Star-Crossed Lovers, from the Shakespearean suite Such Sweet Thunder) is calmly beseeching; Blood Count, Strayhorns last tune, written while he was dying of cancer, is very moving. Retitled The Last Light of Evening, its been fitted out (by another singer, Dominique Eade) with a lyric that cunningly keeps its options open; it might, just, be a love song but it keeps faith with the composition's identity as a premonitory requiem.

Bey did record at least one album in the Seventies and Koch Jazz has reissued it. It's called Experience and Judgment, and it shows more of the former than of the latter. The cover photo shows the singers head encircled by what appears to be one of the rings of Saturn; and the music, shapeless period funk, is appropriately spaced-out. The songs are agonizingly inspirational, and even more agonizingly inept. Bey wrote several of them himself, and the opening of his Celestial Blues should give you an idea: "We must get closer to the essence of life/ But be aware that it takes courage and strife. The advice is topped off by a resonant howl. Beys voice, naturally, was springier then that it is now and one would like to know how it sounded on real songs.

The new CD, though, is loaded with them. The voice is more haunted than ever but also warmer. (The mix seems to favour it more than usual.) There are a couple of voice-and-piano tracks; the others feature a small group, with probing arrangements by fine pianist Geri Allen, who also plays on two of the best cuts. The opener is Never Let Me Go, a remarkable song full of short naked lines, that gets to the core of Bey's talent; he is dignified and vulnerable, simultaneously.

He sings Angel Eyes complete with verse, which freshens the song considerably; moves wonderingly through the unearthly superlatives of Midnight Sun while still keeping some hold on reality; and even brings out a hard-bitten quality in It's Only a Paper Moon that is certainly there (its a Barnum-and-Bailey world/ just as phony as it could be") but that escapes most singers. A Latinate Speak Low is only half successful -- it gets melodramatic -- and the Ellington numbers this time are uneven. He pays more attention than is wise to the hack words of Caravan (its possible he thinks they're spiritual; God figures heavily in his liner-note acknowledgements), and though he approaches the decidedly worldly heroine of Satin Doll with appropriate wariness, Johnny Mercer's gloriously hip-and-flip lyrics are really beyond him.

He remains best when slowest. Ellingtons Prelude to a Kiss comes over beautifully contemplative, with a Frank Wess tenor solo maintaining the questing mood. Lush Life, Strayhorns upmarket barroom soliloquy, might have been written for him, and his performance challenges Hartman's great version with John Coltrane -- an end-of-tether narration that moves from defeat to despair. It develops at the end into an extraordinary series of wordless monosyllables ("heh heh" is prominent) that isn't quite scat singing, though it keeps the beat going superbly, more like scat acting. It takes over where the rather gauche lyric, very much a young man's work, leaves off; this is experience and judgment.

Late comebacks are fairly common in jazz, especially perhaps in jazz singing. This is a late arrival, and every bit as welcome.
