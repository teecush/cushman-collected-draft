Vincent Lam's Bloodletting & Miraculous Cures is a collection of short stories about young doctors. We glimpse them at various stages from their undergraduate days, applying to get into medical school, to their first years in practise at a Toronto hospital. The operative word is "glimpse;" the book is a series of vignettes, presented in approximate chronological order, sometimes told in the first person though not always the same first person, some of them focusing on particular cases, others more concerned with the relationships between the principal characters. There's a triangle involving Fitz and Ming, whom we meet as students in Ottawa and who become lovers, and Chen who, like Ming, comes from a Chinese family and who displaces Fitz in her affections when she, with better grades than her boyfriend, moves to Toronto a year ahead of him.

Fragmentary in structure, the stories are also very uneven in quality. Some of them come to life through the quirks or psychoses of the patients, but of the doctors Ming comes over as merely cold and manipulative while Chen is a presumably well-meaning blank. Fitz is the most fully drawn, and his self-tormenting attempts to cling to Ming are painfully believable.

The TV mini-series drawn from the book pretty much maintains this character balance. Ming (Mayko Nguyen) is a bit more likeable in the flesh than she is on the page, partly through the addition of a storyline that's only hinted at in the book. Chen (Byron Mann) remains flat, but Fitz (Shawn Ashmore) is magnetic, an alcoholic running on self-deprecation on the edge of self-hatred.

Jason Sherman, the adapter, has had to pull this trio consistently into the centre of the narrative, while reorganizing much of it and inventing the rest. He's done this by starting where the source material leaves off and then doubling back. In the book, Fitz leaves the hospital for a job with a private plane company that ferries insured travellers back to Canada when they've been taken ill abroad. The TV version finds him delivering a patient to his old haunt, Mercy Hospital (played very convincingly by St. Mike's, Toronto, at least for exterior scenes) and bullied into getting his

old job back by a no-nonsense triage nurse (an invigorating performance by Alison Sealy-Smith). This is awkward because he's previously agreed to act as sperm donor for the child whom Ming and Chen are unable to have. Fitz aptly describes this situation (invented for television) as "f--ked up." It's also extremely implausible; why would they choose him? Doubtless much will be revealed but at the moment -- I've watched four of the series' eight episodes -- I'm having difficulty reconciling what we're told in Part One with something that happens in Part Four. But maybe I've got the timelines scrambled.

That would be easy to do, because in showing us what has gone before, the script goes in for flashbacks, flashbacks within flashbacks and fantasies within flashbacks within flashbacks. These various levels aren't quite as confusing as they may sound, and some of the fantasies are very effective. There may even, if you've read the book, be yet another level, as you try to reconcile the differing chronologies of the two fictions.

The book's penultimate chapter finds Chen and Fitz as SARS patients in neighbouring isolation cubicles. The scene is cryptically flagged in an early episode, but I would guess that TV too is saving it up for near the end. In the meantime, Sherman has been clever about reassembling, and sometimes reassigning, the individual cases that dot the original. Fitz has appropriated one of the best from the book's Dr. Sri, who on TV has been relegated to supporting-player status, though the incident plays less disturbingly than it reads. So, too, does one of Fitz's lowest moments when, post breakup, he lets himself into Ming's apartment and then has to beat a humiliated retreat. In other scenes Sherman improves on Lam's flat dialogue, and he has some nice inventions of his own, including a sweet scene in which Ming, longing for motherhood, finds herself presiding over the birth of triplets. Ming and Fitz also get to meet cute -- or, in this case, re-meet cute -- in good movie tradition.

In less agreeable Hollywod style, Ming's sneering cousin Karl has been turned into a gloating monster. Apparently the story needs a villain. But in supplying one the show pushes itself further in the direction it was probably always fated to go: that of medical soap opera, with heavy musical underlining, in which we're expected to place exaggerated value on the protagonists' emotional states, simply because they're doctors.

It isn't as enervatingly glossy and antiseptic as House or Grey's Anatomy (or as it's known in my neck of the woods, Desperate Housemen) -- it's Canadian, after all -- but it isn't as much fun as Nurse Jackie. Or even, by virtue of that show's central character, as gritty.

- Bloodletting & Miraculous Cures airs Sundays at 8 p.m. on HBO Canada.
