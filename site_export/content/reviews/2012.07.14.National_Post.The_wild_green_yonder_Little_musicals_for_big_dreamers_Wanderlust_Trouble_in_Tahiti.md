Meet Robert Service, bankster and rhymester. In Wanderlust, Morris Panych's admittedly fanciful account of his life and work - and Stratford's fourth, smallest and best musical this year - Service is putting in time among the urban money changers while dreaming of life in the gold fields and mining camps of the Yukon and writing poems about them. The opening moments of the show find Service, in the person of Tom Rooney, boldly singing one of his verses and then getting stuck for a rhyme. While he stumbles and fumbles, the music, composed for the occasion by Marek Norman, goes implacably on, and so does he, and somehow, triumphantly, they manage to finish together. It's hard to resist the show after that.

Robert, a dreamer with an impulsive streak but only a streak, is surrounded by people who either don't understand him or who understand him all too well, and he makes all of them nervous. The bank manager, one Sam McGee, is a disconcertingly benevolent tyrant who continually seems on the verge of dispensing with Service's services but always ends up giving him sympathetic advice instead. Randy Hughson, finally getting his due at Stratford, endows Sam with his own unsurpassed capacity for saying things very fast and without apparent inflection, while still making them devastatingly funny. I also caught him at one point, standing in a far corner of the stage playing the mouth organ, adding atmosphere to a scene in which he does not otherwise belong. The assistant manager, well sneered by Dan Chameroy, is an unpleasant fellow called Dan McGrew, with whose fiancee Robert is in possibly requited love. She's a lady known as Louisa or, as it might be, Lou. I think you can see where all this is heading, but I'll spell it out. The show presents Service's best known poems, and some of his lesser known ones, as fantasies spun out of his bank-bound existence.

The show has resemblances to such other musicals as She Loves Me (whose source play, Parfumerie, Panych has memorably directed) and The Producers, or the bit of it before Leo Bloom breaks his chains. Wanderlust's hero is afforded no such apotheosis; Panych points out in a program note that Service never set foot in the Klondike until after he had written most of the verses in which he mythologized it, mainly for the benefit of readers even further removed from the scene than he was. It's a fair criticism of the show that, like its hero, it never goes anywhere. It spins out variations on a single situation. (Service's jingliness must be catching, though I admit that he would have come up with a perfect rhyme.) But it's so well done in every department that I can't say I mind.

Panych has written one of his wittiest scripts, especially on the subject of finance ("a sensible man earns his money or, preferably, borrows it" says the bank manager), and given it one of his most humane and inventive productions. He also takes credit for "additional lyrics." I don't know if he intends to make a career, or yet another career, as an additional lyricist; I would guess that on this occasion he's written the romantic stuff, which is well enough turned but cloudier than the versified narratives of Service himself that furnish the bulk of the score. Norman's settings for these are always spirited, sometimes beautiful; though even verse this undemanding can be quite hard to follow when sung. The most effective number is The Cremation of Sam McGee, which is spoken, by Rooney, to music with Hughson managing to transmit human warmth even as frozen corpse. The Shooting of Dan McGrew becomes a more elaborate set piece; it has firecracker saloon choreography by Diana Coatsworth, who in the office scenes works more decorous but equally bewitching wonders with desks and chairs.

And there is Rooney, a star at last, or, more exactly, given a star role at last. He combines the patient physical grace of a Buster Keaton with the relaxed vocal attack, in both speech and song, of - well, of a Tom Rooney. He presents shyness advancing, via sulkiness, into defiance, and then sidling or shuffling back again. Never sentimental, he's sharp and shining and moving. I suppose there are things he can't do, but I've yet to see them turn up. Robin Hutton makes an affecting, ambiguous Lou; theirs is a relationship that at first seems pure romance fiction, but later reveals honest and discomfiting depths. Lucy Peacock gives a Hogarthian account of Robert's amorous landlady, while back at base we get Ken James Stewart as the office sneak, Xuan Fraser as the office perennial, Troy Adams as a bank clerk turned balladeer, and a similarly admirable ensemble. Ken MacDonald's sets are economical enough to please Mr. McGee himself, and imaginative enough to delight everyone else.

The Shaw Festival's small-scale musical is also a beaut. It's Leonard Bernstein's one-act opera Trouble in Tahiti, a yearningly sardonic portrait of a post-war pre-Mad Men suburban marriage. Bernstein wrote the lyrics, more notable for point than polish, as well as the music, notable for both. The show is not, of course, set in the South Seas; the title refers to a song and-sarong movie that the heroine, a more desperate wanderluster, wishes her life were more like, while still dismissing it as romantic rubbish. The hero, meanwhile, commutes and also, on occasion, yearns.

Elodie Gillett and Mark Uhre are very fine as the couple; an eight strong chorus swirls around them, wittily and menacingly directed by Jay Turvey and choreographed by Linda Garneau. Turvey also puts in a very effective silent appearance as the lady's shrink. Not since Floyd Collins has the Court House space been so inventively used. There's the usual impeccable musical direction from Paul Sportelli. One thing: Husband and wife upbraid one another for missing Junior's high school play, but the show ends with them trying to save their marriage by spontaneously going off to see a movie: in fact, that movie. I hope they have a short-notice babysitter, or there'll be trouble a lot closer than Tahiti.
