THEATRE REVIEW

Billy Elliot The Musical Canon Theatre, Toronto

The signal achievement of Billy Elliot The Musical is to be even phonier than the movie it's based on.

Like the film, the show tells the story of an 11-year-old boy from a mining village in the North of England who wants to be a ballet dancer. His father is bitterly opposed to such a cityfied and sissified idea, because this is how Northcountry working men are expected to behave in popular fiction. He then undergoes an even more arbitrary conversion and becomes staunchly supportive. The background is the epic miners' strike of 1984-85, but we're given no reason to believe that Billy's dad and his even more virulent older brother would have reacted to him any differently if they'd still been happily employed. In fact, we're well into the second act before the personal and political stories make any contact at all. The strike at least gave the film some grounding in reality. In the stage version it's a decoration, to be alternately displayed and forgotten.

The obvious reason for making the story into a musical is the presence of dancing. It's also the greatest trap, and the show falls right into it, over and over again. Actually it's less a show about Billy than about whoever happens to be playing him; and there's no doubt that Cesar Corrales, who played him on the first night in Toronto, is a prodigy. (So, I dare say, are the three other kids who will be alternating with him during the run.) He acts appealingly, sings well and dances like one both possessed and selfpossessed. He also, for a 14year-old Canadian from a Mexican-Cuban background, copes amazingly well with the Geordie accent; He isn't always quite clear, but all around him more experienced players are having the same problem. He deserved the cheers he got at the curtain. The show, though, isn't content to wait that long. I've never seen a musical in which the performers get to take so many calls at the end of numbers.

Billy's life changes when he blunders into what is otherwise an all-girls' dancing lesson and finds, like the young man in A Chorus Line who crashed his sister's dance class, that he can do that. The story's whole basis is that he's the only person in town who can, and it might have been interesting if the show had run with that. But in this one, everybody dances, including the miners and the police who are sent in to break the strike. The coppers, doing their time-honoured knees-bend moves amid industrial unrest, might be called The Full Monty Python. And they're meant to be a threat. There's an especially dumb number, ironically entitled Solidarity, in which the worlds choreographically collide, with the schoolgirls crawling over the coppers' laps. It makes no sense of any kind.

There's a much better number in which Billy, at the height of his frustration, hurls himself against the cops' riot-shields. It, too, recalls A Chorus Line -The Music and the Mirror to be exact -and it's the one time that Peter Darling's choreography surpasses its model. But it isn't as if it's the police who are thwarting Billy's ambitions. Good dancing meets catchpenny thinking.

The show has the same author (Lee Hall) and director (Stephen Daldry) as the film, and together they've embraced every possible cliche. It isn't enough for Billy's mother to be dead; she has to keep coming back to him in dreams. His forgetful gran is a kitchen-comedy caricature. There's equal-opportunity stereotyping; the posh Southerners who greet Billy when he goes to London to audition for the Royal Ballet are ludicrously toffee-voiced. Back home, Billy's cross-dressing chum Michael involves him in a drag number in which they're joined by a stage full of huge ambulant frocks. Welcome to Broadway. So Michael's a dancer, too? A big guy with Afro horns in on one of Billy's dance lessons, though he's supposed to be playing iano. In an honestly frivolous musical, such inconsistencies could be fun. But this one has pretensions.

It pretends, for example, to be about ballet, though the most energetic number is called Born to Boogie and much of the other dancing is tap. There's one major exception: Billy imagines performing the pas de deux from Swan Lake, partnered by the dancer he hopes to become. This involves him dancing the girl's part, which you would think might confirm his dad's worst suspicions. In fact, it immediately precedes his change of heart. Now wait a minute: a man is motivated by his son's dream sequence? And wait another minute: Corrales' dancing is yearningly superb but what really brings down the house is the sight of him flying on wires: a mechanical effect having nothing to do with dance. And wait a third minute: much of the power of this scene is due to having the Tchaikovsky music piped in, which rather takes the shine off the show's official credit "music by Elton John." Shine happens to be the name of the score's big song; at least it's the one they reprise at the curtain call, though I had already forgotten hearing it before. John's music throughout is inoffensive (no power ballads) and thoroughly unexciting; Hall's lyrics are hard to judge, since they're mostly inaudible, but they don't seem to be forwarding plot or character.

The show has an uplifting story, a child hero and a presold title; no surprise that it's swept the world and that the first-night standing ovation was tumultuous. It has a good cast, too; Kate Hennig is outstanding as Billy's sweet-andsour dance teacher and surrogate mum, Armand Schultz and Patrick Mulvey are solid as his family, and the kids are more than all right. There are some legitimate goosebumps when the striking miners, plus one scab, rally round to raise the money for Billy to go to London, the father-son dynamic strikes some nice chords, and there's an occasional reminder of what life will be like for those in the village who, as Billy's brother bitterly puts it, aren't dancers. But none of this is done with music. It could be the right time for a stirring musical about a strike, but this one just goes through the motions. The extended curtain call finds the whole cast in tutus. So they are all dancers after all. Anything for a happy ending.
