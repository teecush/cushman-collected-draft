THEATRE REVIEW

THE BOOK OF MORMON

Princess of Wales Theatre, Toronto

The best thing about The Book of Mormon is that it all comes right in the end. Or nearly. I'm not talking about plot; this is a hugely successful Broadway musical so obviously it has a happy ending. I'm talking about comic tone.

The Book of Mormon, as if you didn't know, was written by Trey Parker and Matt Stone (from South Park) and Robert Lopez (from Avenue Q, which I think of as a tidier section of the same neighbourhood), and concerns two eager young Salt Lake Citizens, just graduated from missionary college and sent out together on their qualifying two-year mission. ( "God loves Mormons And he wants some more.") There's elder Kevin Price, the star of his year and the straightest and most self-satisfied of arrows, and elder Arnold Cunningham, a plump well-meaning misfit with a weakness for, as he himself says, "making things up." elder Cunningham doesn't mind where they're sent; he's just tickled pink at the thought of going there with elder Price. elder Price was hoping -- as he tells us many, many times -- for a posting to Orlando, Fla., where he once spent a dream vacation. Instead, they find themselves in an especially squalid village in a remote part of uganda, plagued by AIDS and other diseases and threatened by a neighbouring warlord intent on performing mass clitorectomies.

Hard as it may be to believe, most of this played for laughs; even more incredibly, some of the laughs are forthcoming. The young elders win the villagers' confidence but are in danger of losing it; the tribal chieftain's brave and beautiful daughter, who had taken their teachings very literally, is especially disillusioned. Then one of her friends says something so sensible and so heartening and so hilariously surprising that the whole audience dissolves. We're about 20 minutes from the final curtain, and we ride there on a wave of goodwill, right up to the closing number, which is a reprise with variations of the opening number, which was the best number in the show anyway.

What goes between is mostly fun but underwhelming, never quite sure of its ground and trying desperately hard to pretend otherwise. This surprised me since I had detected no such insecurity in New york where I greatly enjoyed the show, far more in fact that I had expected to. (I'd entered determined to resist the hype: a kind of double bluff.) In this touring production it seems muffled: literally so, as whenever more than one person is singing, the sound system makes it impossible to understand the words. It isn't the fault of the performers, nearly all of whom are at least as good as their Broadway counterparts. Christopher John O'Neill's shambling Arnold is actually better since he seems from the start like a human being rather than a collection of nervous tics. Mark evans is equally good as his chin-jutting, strong-singing friend, the one who, in a reversal of expected convention gets humiliated (quite graphically, at one point). At least, it would be a reversal if the show were not so obviously a spoof in an age of spoofs. It's like a Hope and zzCrosby Road movie with Bob re-cast as the one who gets dorothy Lamour, who in this case is the delectable Samantha Marie Ware. They share a song that begins "I'm about to do it for the first time And I'm going to do it with a girl." It's about baptism.

The relationship of the two young Mormons, which includes some quite bitter soul-searching on the part of elder Price, is the strongest element in the show. They're backed by an entertainingly campy group of slightly older elders, who do a high-kicking routine about resisting temptation; like most of the songs it has only one idea, but it's one that Casey Nicholaw's choreography pursues with inventive vivacity. The show also pays tribute to, or borrows from, or just bears an uncanny resemblance to such earlier musicals as Guys and Dolls (the mission that will be closed down if it can't save a few souls) and The King and I (the natives who act out their own interpretation of a Western story and shock the authorities by doing so). There's also some broad parody of the Mormons' own founding fables. The show, though, isn't really a satire on Mormonism. It ends up an agnostics' celebration of faith: an assertion that there are things to be done in the world, and they can be done in the name of God, even if you're not sure that he exists. Its own great saving grace is that -- as opposed to such earnestly mushy entertainments as Wicked or Billy Elliot -- it's about something.

The trouble, at least on a second viewing, is that half the subject, the African half, has gone missing. The show has good fun with the patronizing white folks ( I Am Africa) and it gives the villagers their own cynical anthem (title untranslatable in a family newspaper). And there's that great debunking moment at the end. But it's still hard to see how, in converting them, these latter-day Latter day Saints have actually solved any of their problems. Things such as AIdS can't be laughed or sung out of existence; while, especially in the light of recent headlines, things like raping babies just can't be laughed at, though the show tries to pretend they can. But black humour (no racial reference intended) needs to have some depth; it can't just be a matter of blithely mentioning the unmentionable.

If the book is scattershot, the score is slapdash (the three authors take no separate credits for the various elements). The tunes are nothing much; the lyrics have their share of funny lines, but they rarely build and some of the rhymes are slipshod. On the occasions when even the basic joke doesn't work, no amount of production can flog it into life; a number set in Hell is truly infernal. Some local specifics: Kevin Mambo is excellent as the village headman; the sadistic warlord carries no threat whatever (he did in New york), which seriously impacts the overall impact. (It's not as if there's any shortage of real-life models.) There's a running gag about Arnold's mispronouncing his beloved's name, which I confess I fell for nearly every time. There's also a thread about Africa's being nothing like The Lion King, which pays off explosively well near the start but frays later. It does connect, though, with Arnold's creative substitution of themes from pop culture for the scriptures he's supposed to have studied. Who, he and the show implicitly inquire, needs Mormon when you have Mordor? I tend to agree. But then I'm the guy who liked the musical Lord of the Rings.
