No, but I saw the movie. That is: Up until the new production at CanStage, my only acquaintance with John Patrick Shanley's Broadway success Doubt was via the screen version with Meryl Streep as Sister Aloysius, the iron-clad principal of a convent school in the Bronx, and Philip Seymour Hoffman as Father Flynn, the priest whom she suspects of improper conduct with an altar boy. I liked the film; apart from the merit of the central performances, I thought it one of the most successful examples I've seen of opening up an enclosed original. The glimpses we were shown of the school's daily life, of the students, of the contrasts between the comfortable, convivial dining table of the priests and the constricted refectory of the nuns, seemed to me nothing but enriching. The play, with four adult characters in narrow focus, is small beer by comparison. Quite possibly, though, if I'd seen them the other way around, I'd prefer the play. The point is, this is not material that stands up well to a second look.

The play's timeframe is the JFK early '60s. Its subtitle is A Parable, and it starts with Father Flynn delivering one, on the merits of doubt as an honest, flexible way of getting through life. Father Flynn is a modernizing priest who grows his fingernails long, writes with a ballpoint pen and wants to open up the annual carol concert to more contemporary material like Frosty the Snowman. All these things, especially Frosty, are anathema to Sister Aloysius, who rules with a rod of iron and believes that it's more beneficial for her students to fear her than to love her. Naturally, our sympathies are all with the open-hearted father (whose attributes include being a first-class football coach) and against the censorious sister. Cunningly, the play then begins to make us doubt, as strong evidence arrives that the priest has plied a 12-year-old boy with communion wine and may have gone further. The boy is especially vulnerable, as the first black pupil the school has ever had. Counter-complications arise when, in the best scene, the victim's mother pleads to let sleeping dog collars lie, while displaying a startling tolerance, for this time and place, of her son's possible gayness. Sister Aloysius won't be dissuaded and persists in what she regards as a crusade for truth and justice (and against an all-too-believable male clerical establishment), which Father Flynn regards as a witch hunt. Their cat-and-mouse game is absorbing at a first viewing, humdrum on repeat. Because beneath it, all is pretence and pretension.

In the play's last line, Sister Aloysius confesses that she, too, has doubts. Well, all very nice, but doubts about what, exactly? About the priest's guilt? Hardly; she seems totally convinced of it. About his capacity to wreak havoc in another parish? The matter is mentioned, but not pursued. About the underhanded tactics she's employed in entrapping him? (She admits to having sinned, and this may be what she means.)About the boy's fate, when his abusive dad finds out? About Frosty and fountain pens? About her own possible loss of vocation? All or any of these are conceivable; none is explored. It's a cop-out. The play ends where it ought to be beginning.

What we're left with, in Marti Maraden's production, is efficient but bloodless. As the big sister, Seana Mc-Kenna is excellent, as we knew she would be, but not beyond what we knew she would be. She doesn't take the same risks as Streep, who pushed the character to the brink of caricature and then pulled back, making her seem more human in the process. Nor does David Storch have the massive fleshly confidence that Hoffman brought to Father Flynn, and especially to his sermons; in other respects, though, he's fine, especially in the locker-room pep talk that shows us the man in his element, absolutely confident and unimpeachably Irish-American. As a young nun whose reactions mirror the audience's, Daniela Vlaskalic is over-the-top naive in her early scenes, but more persuasive when chastened. As the mother, Raven Dauda rides the obvious surface of every speech without connecting one mood to the next; emotionally, she's constantly ahead of herself.

It's been a week of shadow boxing. Hal Linden, the star of Tuesdays with Morrie, has said in some remarkably frank interviews that he finds Mitch Albom's original book "maudlin and sentimental," but that Jeffrey Hatcher's stage adaptation has cured all that. In fact, the play seems as cracker barrel as the book presumably is. Linden plays Morrie, sociologist and sage, now dying of Lou Gehrig's disease. Rick Roberts is Mitch, a former student who visits him weekly and finds his own life unspecifically transformed in the process. Mitch is an in-demand sports reporter, of whose success Morrie seems suspicious. Mitch has abandoned earlier ambitions to be a jazz pianist, though he has married a singer --an interesting wrinkle that the play doesn't seem to notice. Still, Emilie-Claire Barlow's (offstage) wifely singing of The Very Thought of You is the show's highlight. Both actors, in Ted Dykstra's production for the Harold Green Jewish Theatre, are good, though Roberts is initially too strident and Linden is your all-purpose secular rabbi. He gets his laughs, and the evening is painless, but all it leaves is a feeling of generalized goodwill.

Jonathan Garfinkel's House of Many Tongues tries to dramatize the Middle East conflict by showing us a house uncomfortably shared by the Palestinian who used to live in it and the Jewish general who owns it now. The house speaks, as does a camel (the house's would-be love interest). In Richard Rose's production, the actors, given impossible roles, vary only in their degrees of unnaturalness. The title refers not just to language barriers but to the proposal of the General's 15-year-old son to bring peace by having everyone practice cunnilingus. An alternative pun, given that the boy is said to have been found floating on the river in his cradle, might be Go Down, Moses.
