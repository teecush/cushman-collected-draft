Arinze Eze is the writer, director, narrator and also the subject of Where I Belong, which was made under the auspices of the National Film Board, and is screened tonight on CBC Newsworld's The Lens. Eze's parents are Nigerian, and he grew up in Nigeria where he trained to be an engineer. However, he was born in Canada, and having a Canadian passport -- "my escape card" he calls it -- he was able to come here to pursue his career. It didn't prove that easy, and anyway his life took a turn. He ditched engineering to fulfill lifelong dreams of being an artist. He's a versatile one; his film shows him making music, making theatre and --particularly and most impressively -- painting. But the principal job description that he offers is filmmaker.

Where I Belong begins with the stark statement "I don't believe in Canada any more," voiced over shots of Eze trekking through the snow. He didn't just come to Canada; he came to Winnipeg in, he says "one of the worst winters." No wonder there was temperature shock. It doesn't seem to have lasted, though; later scenes show him braving the climate in an open-necked shirt. His deeper disillusion also seems to have been temporary. It makes an ear-catching opening, but that's about all.

The film, in fact, is very bitty. Much of it concerns Eze's relationship with his girlfriend Tina, who is not only white but Jewish, and their worries about how their respective families will react. Eze's parents are all set to visit him, though they're delayed for a long time by visa problems. "I was born in Canada," says Eze at this point, "but my parents can't come and visit me -- because they're Nigerian." This is a subject that deserves more investigation than it gets. Anyway, the restrictions were apparently lifted because the parents arrive, separately, mother first. Subtitles -- "3 days before Mom's arrival" -- provide what must be meant as a suspenseful countdown. But it's all build-up, no payoff.

That goes, as it so often does, for the publicity as well. "An African-Canadian man's interracial relationship with a white woman suffers parental disapproval when his Nigerian parents come to visit." So says the press release, which must have been watching some other film. When Mom does arrive, she couldn't be nicer, smarter or more understanding; and Pop isn't far behind (in either sense), insisting that love is the most important thing. Both parents, it's true, have warnings about the problems that may face the children of interracial couples, but nothing they say could be construed as in any way intolerant or obstructive. They actively support the relationship.

The irony is that it's already broken down: a casualty not of anything Eze's parents say but of what he and Tina fear they might say. It's left very vague; the couple remain close and are obviously still fond of one another. (As they say, "we have five years of history -- that doesn't go away.") The "pressures" that have driven them apart are, again, left unexplored. Tina, whom we always seem to see gazing into a mirror applying lipstick, admits that Eze is clearly not "a nice Jewish man" such as her own family would have wished for her, but how they dealt with the situation is never revealed. Tina is worried about Eze's mother being a born-again

Christian: "I hope," she says, "the religious conversation won't come up". (It doesn't: not that we hear, anyway.) Tina also says that she "doesn't want to come in the middle of [Eze's] family" -- but what about hers? Maybe her folks just didn't want to appear in the film, maybe they weren't asked, but their absence makes the picture very one-sided. I must confess to an antediluvian suspicion of people who like to enact crucial scenes from their private lives while the cameras are rolling. But if it's going to be done, I think it ought to be done thoroughly.

We all place an exaggerated value on our own experiences. Where I Belong, which won in the NFB's Reel Diversity Competition, "a national initiative in partnership with CBC Newsworld," looks like a film made because its director wanted to make a film, and found his autobiography the handiest subject. According to that press release, "a recent Statistics Canada study reports a 30% increase in mixed-race marriages since 2001, but family acceptance can be slow." That's worth making a film about. But Eze, the NFB and the CBC haven't made it. - Where I Belong airs today on CBC Newsworld at 10 p. m.
