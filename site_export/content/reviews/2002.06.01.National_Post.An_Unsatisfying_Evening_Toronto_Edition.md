THE THREEPENNY OPERA

Avon Theatre, Stratford

- - -

STRATFORD - After My Fair Lady, The Threepenny Opera; after the most popular American musical set in London, the most popular German musical set in London -- the latter, admittedly, not a category for which there is huge competition.

If you think that Lerner and Loewe's view of the British metropolis was more mythic than real, you should consider the one that Bertolt Brecht and Kurt Weill dreamed up in 1928. Ostensibly set at the coronation of the young Queen Victoria in 1837, it presents a social, moral and legal structure that seems equally indebted to the authors' own Weimar Berlin and to a rather hurried reading of the novels of Charles Dickens.

It also gives off a pungent 18th-century odour -- naturally, since it is based, quite closely, on John Gays thieves kitchen classic, The Beggar's Opera -- while bits of it sound like the Germanic idea of 1920s America; Kurt Weill's music has the ferocious twang that at the time was thought of as jazz.

All these elements do come together to create their own world, but its very hard to recreate it, now, on stage. Stephen Ouimette, who directs at Stratford, is a fine actor who has hardly ever directed before. Considered in the abstract, as a debut production on a premier stage, his work is impressive. As a specific evening in the theatre, it is deeply unsatisfying.

There are two main problems with Brechts text. (There really aren't any with Weill's music.) One is its narrative incoherence, a fault it admittedly shares with its source, which, however, is more engagingly insouciant about it. Ouimette, I think, deals with this as well as anyone could.

The other is that it wants very much to shock, but 70 years of progress have made this goal increasingly hard to attain. This production starts with what has become one of the tiredest aggression gambits in the book: An apparently drunk, and certainly disorderly, actor pushes his way down the aisle, scattering restraining ushers in his wake. This one climbs up on stage, as they always do, and proceeds to sing the world-famous opening number, Mack the Knife.

However, what with his lurching gait and diction and the efforts of the other performers to stop him, the song gets drowned out. This is a pity, since Marc Blitzsteins translation (first heard in the shows legendary off-Broadway production in 1956 and the standard text ever since) turns out to contain rude bits that never made it on to the original cast recording, never mind the pop versions by Louis Armstrong, and Bobby Darin, and such.

I guess that hearing a hit song in a comfortable theatre (the Avon has been gleamingly refurbished) might count as bourgeois gratification, and the production is virtuously determined to deny it to us. Anyway, the device is never followed up; as the evening proceeds, the actors push about the various elements of Peter Hartwell's set, but that kind of thing happens in a lot of shows.

The story is the familiar one of Macheath, a.k.a. Mackie -- a criminal of impressive versatility, cold-bloodedness and sexual appetite -- who marries (or that's what he calls it) the daughter of the more respectably crooked Soho mastermind J.J. Peachum. Peachum, with the help of his wife and a bundle of whores, gets Mackie arrested and sent to the gallows. Thats all, except that it happens twice. The ending is a reprieve for the hero, the unlikeliness of which is paraded in all its ironic glory. (As it is in Gay; Brecht never claimed to have invented the alienation effect.)

Along the way, the actors pause to draw attention to how appallingly their characters are behaving and to how, society being what it is, they have no other choice. There are a lot of songs that say this, and even Weill cant altogether escape from the resultant grinding quality. All we can do is reflect on how deliciously shocking it must have sounded in 1928 and how often we have heard things like it since. It's not so much that it isn't true (though its not as unvaryingly true as the show would like us to believe it believes) as that its generalized. Only one line strikes freshly home, as spoken by Peachum, whose profession is the provision of rags and deformities to aspiring beggars. He says that the ruling classes are quite happy to force people on to their knees but can't stand looking at the results.

Brecht claims to be indignant at human depravity but, plainly, he digs it. This being the case, the best way to play both text and songs is lightly, so that we can share in the cynicism and, maybe, occasionally be caught off guard and bruised by it. This production, hoping to up the original ante, tries instead to rub our noses in it.

It doesn't help that though some of the cast can act and some can sing, very few can do both. George Masswhohl as Tiger Brown, the sentimental police chief, may be the closest to an exception; his line readings are a bit stiff, but this rather suits the character. He sings his portion of the Army Song duet with superb attack. (Credit too, and again, to Donna Feore, who has provided this number with discreet but brilliant choreography, mainly for rhythmically hunched shoulders.)

His duet partner is Tom McCamus as Macheath himself, and his far lighter but incisive voice makes a good match. But other parts of the score tax him cruelly, and the strain takes the bite off his delivery; even in speech, he gabbles. This shark has pretty teeth, dear, and a wicked smile and elegantly steely demeanour, but it isnt enough. (I break the rule of a lifetime by mentioning it but, for musical and other reasons, I would love to see him swap roles with Colm Feore in My Fair Lady.) Peter Donaldson as Peachum, too, lacks energy in his song; and here again it takes a toll on his talking, while Sheila McCarthy as his lady seems uncomfortable in both her aspects, pushing for a malevolence that isnt her.

There is some fine choral singing, though too little opportunity for it, and the younger women all have great voices. Blythe Wilson's Lucy, a jailer's daughter with riding crop, brings the house down with her Barbara Song; Diana Coatsworth as Polly sings a good, stark Pirate Jenny (admirably lit, by the way, by John Munro); and Susan Gilmour, as the non-piratical Jenny, makes the Solomon Song the most together sequence of the evening. But none of them makes much impact as a character; and Gilmour and her fellow sluts are disappointingly genteel.

The show pays off in its last act. Peachum squaring off with Tiger Brown threatening to flood the streets with beggars and ruin both the Coronation and the policeman's career is a sequence with real tension and authority. (Donaldson hits his stride here.) McCamus desperate in his cell while friends, enemies and cast-off loves line up on the other side of the bars is a memorable picture. The queen's mounted messenger, rising up from the orchestra pit, makes a delightful impression. After all that hard work, some authentically chilly winds do finally get to blow.
