THEATRE REVIEW

Berkeley Street Theatre, Toronto

The best parts of Kristen Thomson's Someone Else are very good indeed. Thomson herself appears in it, opposite Tom Rooney; they play a couple in their forties whose marriage is crumbling, and they give lacerating performances. The most painful thing is that they arent playing people who hate one another. Theyre playing people who love one another, even though each of them has turned, in the other's eyes, into someone else. At least, that's how I take the title. Other interpretations are possible.

She, named Cathy, is a stand-up comic, he, Peter, is a doctor: not perhaps the most obvious match, come to think of it, though Ive known doctors who thought themselves comedians. Within the structure of the play - and heres where it begins to run into trouble - their two jobs function in different ways. Cathy's is a dramaturgical device; Peter's is part of the plot.

The play begins with Cathy doing her act; or maybe just rehearsing her act. Chris Abrahams production features a bare white room of a set featuring a lot of doors and not much else. With the house lights still on, she picks up a microphone and starts prowling the stage, telling us how much her life sucks. She makes especially fierce fun of the state of her marriage and the size of her bum.

Cathy takes her self-deprecation further still, into self-therapy; theres no "between us girls" element in her routine, no presumption of sympathy. It's all her. It's a brilliant, bravura performance that she intensifies later on, pouring out anger and desperation in a second monologue for which she doesnt have the stage to herself; her husband is running around behind her, apparently in search of a ceaselessly ringing phone.

Effective though it is as commentary, Cathys profession doesn't have much bearing on the story. Peter's does, partly as a source of jealousy. Cathy suspects his interest in a self-destructive 19-year-old called April. Peter does indeed become obsessed with her, though not, as far as we can tell, sexually. Part of the problem between him and Cathy is that hes as reticent as she is needy and effusive. Aprils demands are easier for him to cope with because he can at least tell himself that they're professional, and he doesn't have to live with them. April has a history of cutting herself; Peter asks her to teach him how, as a means of awakening his own frozen feelings.

Maybe hes attracted to women who talk a lot. April makes long speeches about her abused childhood and her attempts to transcend it, but they arent as amusing or arresting as Cathy's confessions. In fact they stop the play in its tracks. This is partly because the young actress (Bahia Watson) hasn't the vocal chops to get them cleanly across in this exposing space, but more because the character seems like an intrusion: The play isnt about her, but she takes up a lot of its time.

The productions geometry is also odd. Or maybe its arithmetic, since in principle all its elements could add up, they don't come out as a satisfactory sum. The cause of Peter's withdrawal is revealed to us when, like a recovering alcoholic, he engineers a meeting with someone hes wronged in the past. This is David, a paraplegic who has sat mute and presumably accusing in his wheelchair for several scenes before his presence is officially recognized.

Damien Atkins gives a tremendous performance in this role, a man who disdains sympathy and works himself into a blazing outpouring of pride and scorn. And, yes, it makes theoretical sense that Peters encounter with him could have had the effect we're told it had. But, coming as and when it does, it seems too pat.

There are some wonderful scenes throughout, though. Thomson as writer, she and Rooney as actors and Abraham as director strike deep at the raw pain of opposites not just attracted but bound - two incorrigibly smart people wrong-footing one another with every step. Early on we see Peter and Cathy in a therapist's office, answering regulation questions that are spelled out for us on a screen: Shes flip but anguished, hes logical and impatient, a cross-talk routine that only one of them is equipped to play.

Much later we see them suddenly reunited by shared terror at the apparent disappearance of their daughter; Thomson's hysteria, Rooney's desperate mask of practicality, the galloping speed of the whole thing - all are agonizingly believable, even a little bit funny, though I couldnt quite believe that she wouldnt have snatched the phone out of his hand rather than relying on him to relay information. As everyone knows, and as Cathy would certainly agree, there are some situations in which men just can't be trusted.

Someone Else runs until Feb. 2 at the Berkeley Street Theatre in Toronto. For tickets and more information, call 416-368-3110 or visit canadianstage.com.
