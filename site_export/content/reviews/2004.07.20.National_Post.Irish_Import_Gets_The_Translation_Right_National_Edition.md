Theatre Review

TRANSLATIONS

Premiere Dance Theatre, Toronto

- - -

With a few exceptions such as Uncle Vanya and the current Dumb Waiter, Soulpepper shows have not been noted for distinguished design. However, Francis O'Connor's set for the new production of Brian Friels Translations raises standards not just for Soulpepper but for Toronto theatre in general. The play takes place in Ireland in the early 19th century, in a cottage that doubles as a hedge- school supplying a liberal and largely classical education to the surrounding peasantry. Here, executed in some holy mixture of stone and Styrofoam, its an edifice that exploits stage dimensions of height and depth one hardly knew existed. Imposing though it is, it's neither overpowering nor unbelievable. Its textured walls and floor frame, cradle and very likely inspire the actor; a big rickety staircase calls cruel attention to the fact that one of the inhabitants is lame and another habitually tipsy; wide doors at the back swing open to reveal glimpses of the country with whose fate the play is centrally concerned and to give periodic entrance to the English army that controls that fate. Whenever redcoats appear upon the stage (O'Connor -- who despite his name is English, not Irish -- also designed the costumes) the dramatic temperature changes. The play is also wondrously lit, by Kevin Lamotte; his opening burst of morning sunshine is fair dazzling and his evening shadows are burnished.

Friels play, now just over 20 years old and incomparably his best, is about the political power of language, not just in making the rules but in defining the territory. The hedge-scholars (all of them adult) speak only Gaelic and, in an impressive number of cases, Latin or even Greek. The dominie and his son alone speak English, and that mainly for interpretative purposes. Into the district comes the army, conducting an ordnance survey, which means they are rationalizing the local place-names, which in turn means they are, most of the time, Anglicizing them. The soldiers of course speak only English, so they have brought their own interpreter, who happens to be the schoolmasters other son, a character whose loyalties are ambiguous. Though the details are only hinted at, the situation is explosive; a major Irish rebellion lies in the recent past and the spectre of the Great Famine hovers, with grim irony, over the future.

Whether the characters are supposedly speaking English or Gaelic, we always hear it as English, and Friel's achievement in establishing and maintaining this convention is remarkable. There is never any confusion, at least for us; the characters' confusion is richly exploited -- first for comedy, then for polemically charged drama in a debate between an Anglophile Irishman and a Hibernophile Englishman, finally and most unforgettably in a love scene poignantly conducted across the linguistic divide.

The setting, and the authors use of it, are marvellously original and suggestive; its tempting to describe this as the kind of history play that Chekhov might have written had he ever delved into his country's past. One hopes, though, that he might have been tidier about developing his narrative. The political action is largely in the hands of two off-stage characters called the Donnelly twins; as a friend of mine once put it, speaking with the double authority of critic and teacher, they never made it to class, so the bloodier part of the action never comes into focus. Neither, and more crucially, does the possible part in it of the schoolmaster's two sons; and the faintness of the action infects the characters. It ends very finely, with a scene that begins by echoing the finale of an earlier Irish classic, Juno and the Paycock (two sozzled old men intruding unawares on a scene of tragedy) and then expands and explodes in unexpected directions. It includes a great speech by the teacher, Hugh, who seems, liquored or not, to be the authors mouthpiece; he reveres the past, lives in it in fact, but he also knows both the inevitability of change and the probable cost of it.

Hugh is played by Diego Matamoros at his best, meaning that he has the authority to take over the stage from the moment he sets foot on it, and the intelligence and technique to keep it in his grip. His performance is a monumental study in wryness. Indeed, in this production by Ben Barnes (artistic director of Dublins Abbey Theatre) all the Irish characters are well played, both as individuals and as a group; the sight of this motley group lining up defensively against the wall when the soldiers come is a memorable one.

David Storch, who has one virtuoso tirade, makes an urbane, troubled figure of the brother who got away, and Gordon Rand a complex one -- hopeful, bitter, crushed -- of the one who stayed. The play is in part a love story; Patricia Fagan, after an initial bout of arms akimbo acting, blossoms romantically before settling on a note of tough heartbreak, superbly sustained. Michael Simpson gives a pleasantly fey account of an old man whose classical studies have led him to serious contemplation of marriage with the goddess Athene (living in the past can be carried to extremes); and Liisa Repo-Martell is expectedly piercing in the emblematic role of a village girl traumatized into silence.

The English, as so often in Ireland, are a problem. Oliver Dennis offers a gentle cartoon of the commanding officer; pleasing enough at the start, but underpowered for his later roll call of the new English place names, which should fall like a series of hammer blows. This is the climactic scene of the play, its image of colonialism in action. Its rendition here is one of the few points at which this production falls short of the London premiere two decades back.

Another, more lengthily damaging, is the performance of the junior officer who falls in love with Ireland, with its topography and with at least one menber of its population. He's meant to be a naive young man; as Philip Riccio plays him he's merely silly, unbearably so in the first act, compensating somewhat but not enough in the second. The performance admittedly spares us the burden of worrying too much about the character's ultimate fate, one of the things that Friel, undoubtedly by intention, leaves vague and open- ended. The technique is effective up to a point but frustrating beyond it. It makes the play, to my mind, less than the masterpiece it was proclaimed at birth, but still undoubtedly a marvel.
