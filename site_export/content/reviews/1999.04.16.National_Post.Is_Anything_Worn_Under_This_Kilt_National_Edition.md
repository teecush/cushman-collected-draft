THE KILT

Tarragon Theatre, Toronto

You can bump, you can bump, you can bump, bump, bump

Until your privates wilt.

Me, I bump and I bump and I bump, bump, bump

But I do it in a kilt.

I offer the above, with apologies to the strippers number from Gypsy, as an anthem for Tom, a Toronto table-dancer of Scottish descent who is the hero of Jonathan Wilson's new play.

Tom uses his Scottishness as the gimmick every professional undresser, male or female, gotta have. His actual theme-song is Donald, Where's Your Troosers?

Before introducing him, however, the play transports us to the Second World War where we meet Mac, another young man in a kilt, serving as a private in the North Africa campaign and having a brief but happy homosexual affair with an equally young officer. At the end of this scene Mac performs a fierce and vivacious Highland dance, and by the end of that scene, the actor has turned into Tom, performing 50-odd years later for his Canadian clientele. After discarding the idea that he might be Dorian Gray, one realizes that Tom is Macs grandson. I thought for a while that he might be his son, but Im very dim about these things.

In fact, Tom's dad died when Tom was a child, and it was at this point that he and his mum, whose name is Esther, emigrated to Canada from Glasgow. Esther now teaches Scots dancing in Hamilton, and is pretty intense about it. She is gorgeously played by Maja Ardal as a miniature

dragon-lady with darting suspicious eyes and a mind to match. Although proudly tolerant of her son's sexuality (she chirpily informs him that a friend of his has "come out of the cupboard"), she doesnt know about his job and is livid when she finds out.

Old Mac has just died, so Esther and Tom return to Scotland to attend the funeral and see the family. This seems to consist of Esthers sister, Mary, and the two of them indulge in the lovingly prolonged recriminations that are customary on these occasions, especially in plays. (Deborah Lambie plays Mary and, spikily generous, she gives as good as she gets.)

Tom rushes out on to the street where he encounters David, an elderly man who turns out to be Mac's wartime love, also in town for the obsequies. David spots the family resemblance (not surprising in the circumstances) and a tender, funny and, let me add, chaste scene ensues. All then repair to the burial, which turns out to be a cremation, and is a hoot.

The only thing wrong with the play is its structure, which is a shame since the author has obviously worked very hard at it. In order to keep the relationship of Mac and David in our minds, he keeps wafting us back to Africa, distracting us from the engrossing present while having nothing new to say about the past.

Tom is a decorous stripper; his granddad may have served under Montgomery but he himself stops somewhere short of the full Monty. In fact -- despite kilt, gayness, and casting -- Mac and Tom do not illuminate one another much, although Paul Braunstein plays both of them well: openfaced and open-hearted as Mac, wryly smouldering as Tom. Even the Scottishness is not much of an issue. We meet Canadian Scots and Scottish Scots, but when they fight they fight like any other relatives, although more entertainingly than most.

Tom's sex life has resolved into encounters with customers, which have left him disillusioned, but the feeling is announced, rather than dramatized. Meanwhile, David, played with wistful courtesy by Gerard Parkes, has kept his love for Mac bright through the years; this is moving, but it isnt connected to anything.

Wilson seems to have a huge talent for making his characters live in the moment but not much as yet for digging beyond it. We are left with a vague feeling about the persistence of love -- family love, erotic love -- which is comforting but hardly profound. There is less beneath The Kilt than seems promised but whats on the surface should keep most people happy.
