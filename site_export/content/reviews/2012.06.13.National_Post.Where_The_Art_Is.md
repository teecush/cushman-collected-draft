THEATRE REVIEW

Home

Young Centre for the Performing Arts, Toronto

David Storey's play Home begins with two men conversing in polite but somewhat stilted tones: Two middle-aged English gents, names of Jack and Harry, who seem to know one another, but not very well. The play was first produced in London in 1970, and Storey has claimed that when he started to write it he had no idea who or where the men were; they and their words just came into his head.

I used to distrust, or flatly disbelieve, this idiot-savant approach to playwriting, but seeing the play again, in a fine production by Soulpepper, I have to confess theres something in it. It obviously worked for Storey, who does indeed seem to be discovering his characters and their background as he goes along. It also works, mostly, for me.

Jack and Harry, natty representatives of the southern English professional class, are shortly joined by two ladies, Kathleen and Marjorie, of roughly the same age but from a lower social stratum. Later we meet Alfred, a muscle-bound young man who enjoys lifting tables and chairs in the air and then walking off with them. This annoys the four older people, because there never seem to be enough chairs for them all to sit down on in the first place, and hes making it worse. All five characters, it seems, call this place home.

As far as I remember, no great mystery was made at the time of the original production as to what kind of home it might be; it might have taken Storey himself time to find out, but critics who gave his conclusions away were not accused of ruining anybody's fun. This was before the word "spoiler" came into common use. Now it's considered bad form to reveal anything.

What does rapidly become clear, though, is that the quintet, who have no previous association, have been thrown together in some kind of institutional setting, with regular communal meal times and with therapeutic activities laid on. In theory, it could be anything from a holiday camp to a mental hospital. I dont think myself that theres much doubt on which end of the scale it belongs, but then I've seen the play before. I don't think that my prior knowledge lessened my enjoyment: quite the opposite in fact.

Some years ago Albert Schultz directed a memorable Soulpepper revival of Harold Pinters No Mans Land, and it's conceivable that his attention was drawn to Home by the fact that the leading roles in both plays were created by the same two great English actors, John Gielgud and Ralph Richardson. (Home came first.) Whether that's the case or not, Home ranks with No Mans Land (and the more rambunctious A Chorus of Disapproval and The Time of Your Life) among Schultzs best productions. In fact, despite having no theatrical knights on tap, his production of Home improves on the original, which came all too self-importantly from what was then the Royal Court Theatre tradition of Plays That Are Good for You.

All I really remember from it is the sight and muffled sound of Gielgud crying: something that he could always do more affectingly than anyone else. I presume that Richardson cried, too, since both of Soulpepper's actors do, but Sir Ralph was always a more discreet and enigmatic sufferer than Sir John. Anyway, it was hard back then to think of the play as anything more than a wispy vehicle for its stars, and I doubted if it could be of any interest without them. Michael Hanrahan (Harry) and Oliver Dennis (Jack) prove that it can.

They play seemingly affable, cultivated men, whose gentle banter proves to cover great depths, certainly of sorrow and probably of shame. Both are dapper, but Dennis is the more dapper of the two, with a neatness and precision that have to be covering something up. Hanrahan is outwardly the more open and relaxed, the ruddy squire to Dennis's pale citizen, but he seems to have done something to his face; it isnt quite all there.

They exchange bluff anecdotes about their military or commercial experiences, in an amiably competitive manner that thrives on free association. Harry floats the theory that the Garden of Eden was actually located in Englands Vale of Evesham, though he stops short of claiming that Eve was named after it (or even vice versa). This, apparently, is Dennis's 50th production for Soulpepper, and his versatility is something we've long taken for granted; Hanrahans seems to be growing by leaps and bounds - compare this outwardly bland performance to his crazy gangbanger in Half Life - and on this occasion he is ultimately the more moving and disturbing of the two.

What pricks the two mens precarious bubble is the advent of the two women, who greet their stiff attempts at courtliness with the deepest suspicion. Where the gentlemen are elaborately reserved, the women are aggressively outspoken. Maria Vacratsis (Marjorie) is ceaselessly censorious, not least with her companion whom she is continually adjuring to "pull your skirt down, girl." Brenda Robins (Kathleen) complies, with a suggestive giggle: Her response to anything suggestive of a double entendre, which of course means just about anything. Individually and as a duo, these are far sharper performances than I recall from London. Andre Sills' eruptions and interruptions as Alfred are massive; he's abrupt in what he does, deliberate in how he does it.

Ken MacKenzies set consists of a brick wall, in front of which is lowered a screen with a slowly shifting cloudscape, It suggests an endless English summer afternoon. The hints that the play might be an allegory for the homeland itself strike me as facile, though 40 years on, when these social types have mostly disappeared, they can seem pleasantly nostalgic; Denniss performance, in particular, carries a whiff of Ealing comedy.

What keeps the play alive, and is captured in this production with musical exactness, is its feeling for the rhythms of the lost, the socially and psychologically unmoored. If everyone in it is yearning for a home it seems, sadly, that they have found it.
