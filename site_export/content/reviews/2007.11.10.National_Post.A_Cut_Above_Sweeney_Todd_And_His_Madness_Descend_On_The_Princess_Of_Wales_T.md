Almost the last thing that happens in Stephen Sondheim's Sweeney Todd is that the boy Tobias goes mad. This comes just after Sweeney, the vengeance-crazed demon barber of Fleet Street, has dispatched his two arch-enemies, Judge Turpin and his beadle, and after a more innocent bystander has also bitten the combined dust of Todd's tonsorial parlour and Mrs. Lovett's pie-shop. (The two are partners. In every sense.) It also follows Sweeney's turning on Mrs. Lovett and killing her, too; in most productions he does this by shoving her into her own baking-oven, but in John Doyle's staging, which comes to Toronto after successful seasons in London and New York, he has one last recourse to his trusty razor. Tobias then kills him, after informing him that he really oughtn't to go killing people, which is the only moral statement the show makes and the only one it needs. It's hardly surprising that the kid ends up off his rocker.

Which is how and where we find him as Doyle's production begins, held in a straitjacket, from which he is presently released to enact the story whose finale I have just given away. (I haven't spoiled anything. Sweeney is not about mystery but about Fate.) This version has two major conceits. One is narrative: The show is set in an asylum whose inmates are assisting in Tobias's theatrical psychotherapy. The other is technical: In this musical, which is virtually through-composed, the actors are also the orchestra. And so cunning are Sarah Travis's minimalist orchestrations, and so pointed the disposition of the actor-musicians about the stage, that the musical device also becomes a dramatic one.

A major consequence of the concept is that the show becomes about Tobias, who here gets to start it off with the uniquely haunting Ballad of Sweeney Todd. (Apparently the song's been cut from the upcoming movie. I can't imagine. Though I have seen the trailer, which seems reluctant to admit both that the film is a musical and that there is anyone in it besides Johnny Depp.) Edmund Bagnell, this production's Tobias, is vocally compelling, with just a couple of cracks, and visually compulsive; we watch the story through his eyes, noting in particular his trembling devotion to Mrs. Lovett, the protectress whom he in turn wishes to protect; their duet Not While I'm Around has never made more sense, has never indeed seemed more of a duet.

The weight of the show still falls on Sweeney and his co-worker. The current Todd is David Garry, newly promoted from standby. He gets through very creditably, looking every inch the fanatic Victorian tradesman, but he doesn't have the madness that propels both the character and the work, so some of the crucial sequences become monotonous. I've only seen one great Sweeney and that was the original, Len Cariou. I've rarely seen a Mrs. Lovett who was less than superb, but Judy Kaye may be the best of them all. She does make heavy cockney weather of the dialogue, but there isn't much of that; she gets every ounce out of the songs, not just melodically (she has one of the best voices in musicals) but dramatically and comedically. She knows exactly when to point a line with parlando, without ever breaking the flow. Not even Angela Lansbury was funnier with The Worst Pies in London, and no one has done nearly as well with By the Sea, which here becomes not a specialty turn but a delightful window into the woman's genial but fatally limited soul. She also stomps around the stage playing the tuba, with what looks like gusto, and, to remarkable effect, tinkles on the triangle.

Doyle's players-in-a-madhouse idea seems to owe something to Marat/Sade; certainly one recurring image -- the pouring of blood into a bucket to denote a death -- directly recalls the legendary Peter Brook production. No gore actually spills from throats here; the stage is bathed in red light instead, while the victims don spattered white coats. It's effective, as is Doyle's set, a bare room with one towering set of shelves, filled with knick-knacks. The whole production is gripping, but it's also limiting; it's fashionable to decry Harold Prince's original gigantic staging, but its physical size suited the show's emotional size. It released the tragedy in the melodrama. Perhaps a vibrant Sweeney, which this production has never had, would make the difference.

The performance level in general is down from New York, with a callow expressionless judge and a beadle whose physical slightness allows him to spend time standing in an upright coffin but who is vocally and dramatically thin as well. Doyle's New York production had the best beadle I've ever seen in Alexander Gemignani who's about to join this touring version to take over Sweeney. It also had the best Johanna in Lauren Molina; she's still playing it but her dizzy, spunky heroine has inexplicably dwindled into a thing of shreds and patches. The best of the supports is Katrina Yaukey, wittily playing the rival barber Pirelli in "push", which someone once suggested as the opposite of drag. I've never quite believed that the piece itself, tour-de-force of musical-theatrical writing though it is, is Sondheim's masterpiece; it lacks the intellectual and emotional range of Pacific Overtures and Into the Woods and the fun of Forum. It still dwarfs most other musicals of the last ... it still dwarfs most other musicals.
