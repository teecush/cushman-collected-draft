WHAT THE BUTLER SAW

Young Centre for the Performing Arts, Toronto

---

The title What the Butler Saw refers to a peepshow machine that used to be found on British seaside piers. Customers could insert a coin and see pictures of high-born ladies and gentlemen enjoying themselves and one another in variously titillating poses. Joe Orton's 1969 play, his last, makes the audience the butler. We gaze at the antics of a more modern kind of English aristocracy, drawn from the professional and governmental classes.

The setting is a mental hospital, or as most of the characters unabashedly refer to it, a madhouse. The arch voyeur, watching the farcical proceedings in gleeful disbelief, is Orton himself, the young playwright famously dubbed "the Oscar Wilde of the welfare state:" a Wilde whose Bosie, apparently jealous of his lover's success, bludgeoned him to death, before taking his own life. The play, accordingly, reached the stage in a posthumous unrevised state, the source of some of its charm and some of its untidiness.

Dr. Prentice, the clinic's head head-shrinker, inaugurates the action by instructing Geraldine, a phenomenally innocent secretarial applicant, to take her clothes off for a medical examination. No sooner does he have her where he wants her (on his couch, behind a curtain) than he's interrupted by his wife, who's arrived in a comparable state of undress, having survived an attempted rape by a page boy at a local hotel.

Much swapping of clothing ensues at, as befits a farce, an increasingly frantic rate. Two more intruders raise the stakes. One is a police sergeant, investigating the disappearance of the private parts of a statue of Sir Winston Churchill. The other is Dr. Rance, whose opening line is "I represent Her Majesty's government -- your immediate superiors in madness" and who proceeds to take what passes for control. The play's epigraph, drawn from Jacobean tragedy, is "surely we are all mad people, and those we take for mad are not."

The play accordingly takes shots at the official pieties of medicine, the law and the civil service. But it's more deeply and personally subversive than that. All the sex described or attempted in the play is straight, but Orton clearly regards all such relationships as both a joke and a fraud. What he's actually saying is "surely we are all gay people and those we take for straight are fooling themselves."

"I'ma heterosexual" protests Dr. Prentice, to which Dr. Rance replies, "I wish you wouldn't keep using these Chaucerian words; it's very confusing." He's equally capable, mind you, of upholding orthodox morality, if his author can get a joke out of it; the play's greatest weakness is its inability to see beyond the next epigram. But it builds a funny and incipiently frightening world. It is, for its time and place, the ultimate outsider's play; Orton, aggressively uncloseted and with a prison record (for defacing library books, which seems deliciously English) was not so much the Oscar Wilde as the Jean Genet of the welfare state--though a Genet with a sense of humour, which makes all the difference.

Jim Warren's Soulpepper production is very lucid, which in this play is both a virtue and a vice. He sets the action and the characters up very neatly, and he does a fine job on the phantasmagoric finale, which finds most of the characters bloodied, drugged and transvestite (the policeman descending a rope-ladder in an off-the-shoulder leopard-skin dress) while Sir Winston's now-discovered portions are waved proudly aloft. There's also a multiple, and conceivably incestuous family reunion, in which comedy itself gets parodied. Warren gets the Orton tone more consistently than in last year's Loot, but not the tempo; in the middle of the play, when the pressure should be on, things get very slow.

There is one triumphant performance: Graham Harley, resisting all temptation to play for eccentricity, makes Rance the ultimate civil servant. A moustached mole, impervious to argument and smug with power, he rolls out his terrifying pronouncements like points on a memo. Meanwhile, his eyes dance with ambition and self-satisfaction. Harley has both the style and the character down to perfection, which includes making some of Orton's less polished phrases sound natural if not witty.

Brandon McGibbon's page is admirable, Nicole Underhay's shaken secretary is admirable cubed. Oliver Dennis is his sterling self as the copper, particularly good at registering silent bafflement shading into indignation; he doesn't manage, though, to rescue a long speech that must be the most inert thing Orton ever wrote. (Dennis made magic with a similar aria in Loot, but this time the task was probably impossible.)

Brenda Robins' Mrs. Prentice is excessively brittle and unyielding, but then she is being asked to play a frigid nymphomaniac, depending on which laugh her author is after at any given moment.

Blair Williams as her husband exemplifies the good and the bad in the production. He makes a dapper first impression, establishing the doctor's professional assurance and personal frustration. But when frustration becomes desperation, he remains strangely imperturbable. This is damaging, since he's the character who keeps trying to put things right while getting them even more wrong. To put it another way, he's the farce. It's doubly surprising since Williams is an accomplished comedian who also showed, as director of The President, that he knows all there is to know about theatrical frenzy and farce's eternal race against time.
