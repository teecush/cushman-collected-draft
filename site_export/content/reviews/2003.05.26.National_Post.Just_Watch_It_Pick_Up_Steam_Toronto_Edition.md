ON THE TWENTIETH CENTURY

Royal George Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

I missed the train. I'm referring to the gleaming art deco monster that devoured the stage, dividing and combining and revolving and all but jumping in the air, when On the Twentieth Century was given its Broadway premiere in 1978. This is a musical about monsters, mainly of the sacred theatrical variety, and the train, the 20th Century Limited that chuffed its luxurious way between Chicago and New York in the Twenties and Thirties, is its emblem as well as its setting. Its a show whose keynote is extravagance: extravagance of decor, of expenditure, and of personal behaviour. Placing it on one of the Shaw Festivals smaller stages is, in some ways, to deny its nature. The production, by Valerie Moore and Patricia Hamilton, is mostly delightful, but you do sometimes feel the pinch.

The year is 1932. Oscar Jaffe, a habitually impoverished but unsinkably buoyant actor-producer-director, is fleeing from the collapse of his last show while already planning an even more spectacular next. He cannot afford to board what the title song calls the luxury liner between N.Y. and Chi at the station, but once it's moving he climbs in through a window and takes possession of the most desirable compartment.

Also in transit is Lily Garland, once Oscar's discovery, protege and mistress, now a movie star. Oscar schemes to pry her loose from Hollywood, from her current beefcake boyfriend, and from a rival manager, and re-sign her, personally and professionally. Ben Hecht and Charles MacArthur (the authors of the greatest American play, The Front Page) created Oscar and Lily in a play, Twentieth Century, that subsequently became a movie with John Barrymore and Carole Lombard playing roles that might have been written not so much for as about them.

The musical is the adaptation of one crack duo by another. The book and lyrics are the work of Betty Comden and Adolph Green, and theatrical monsters were their stock-in-trade. (One has, sadly, to speak of them in the past tense since their extraordinary 60-year- and-some collaboration ended when Green died last year. Its good that he should have this production as a memorial, though it obviously wasnt planned that way.)

As screenwriters they provided the fuel for Jack Buchanan's self- proclaimed genius director in The Band Wagon and for Jean Hagen's silent movie queen ("if we have brought a little joy into your humdrum lives, it makes us feel like it aint all been in vain for nothin ") in Singin' in the Rain. You can see traces of both these characters in Oscar and Lily. Comden and Green surveyed the world they grew up on, and later in, with affectionate irony, and they did a lot to keep it alive, at least in our thoughts. They were also high on professional eccentrics: as in this show Mrs. Letitia Primrose, a crazed moral reformer plastering the locomotive with stickers announcing the end of the world and warning sinners of the consequences. She has a song on this theme called Repent: Like you I once was wild./Men shouted 'oh, you kid!/A life of shame I led/ And dirty doings did. That last line is pure Comden and Green. They adored mock formality; they loved to take a cliche and twist its tail. Maybe the funniest lyric line in this show is "But, but, but, but, but, but, but ...", sung by several characters in succession when in receipt of unwelcome news. You might expect to find it in a very bad novel. Set to music in all its shamelessness, its artifice proudly exposed, it scintillates.

The music to which its set is Cy Coleman's. Coleman was a natural collaborator for Comden and Green. Like them he's urbane, a wit, and one of the last upholders of a great tradition. Hes the jazziest Broadway composer since Harold Arlen. In this show, however, he put the rhythm largely on hold. Lily, Oscar and their cohorts, who behave operatically, are given opera and operetta parodies to sing. Its a lovely notion, and sometimes it works: when the stars, temporarily reconciled, are cooing Our Private World at one another, or when Oscar stages a mock-death scene out of Puccini. The title number that sets the train in motion is marvellously elegant and propulsive, and occasionally the jazz breaks through, which is a relief. But rather too frequently the show comes over as the idea of a wittily over-the-top extravaganza, instead of the thing itself. Some of the score is inert, including a quarrel duet that notably fails to end the first act with a bang.

Inevitably, some of the weaknesses are accentuated in this production. Paul Sportelli's cut-down orchestrations are very neat, but one does crave a bigger sound. It's a pleasure, as always at the Shaw, to hear unamplified singing (and there always seems to be some actor in the cast whom you never suspected could sing, and can), but though most of the performers can project their voices or their personalities over the band, they cant always get both across at the same time. Which brings us to the matter of star power, in a show thats supposed to be about it. Every actor I have seen in the role of Oscar has had to struggle uphill, trying hard to be dynamic and debonair and not bringing it off, and Gary Krawford here is no exception. But then he doesn't have the material; though the script keeps insisting on the chemistry between the two leads, it never makes us feel it, and Oscar's solo songs always seem to be in a losing competition with Lilys.

One reason for this is that his are static proclamations, while hers tell stories. We first meet her in flashback, a catarrhal rehearsal pianist propelled into stardom by Svengali-Jaffe and let loose on a song called Veronique, about a French heroine who inspired a nation by refusing her favours to Bismarck. ("Veronique, she close her door/And start the Franco-Prussian War"; its like a parody of the Les Miserables view of history, avant la lettre.) Later she gives us Babette, in which she's a Somerset Maugham heroine: the only example I know of a song that successfully lampoons a whole style of spoken drama. Patty Jamieson is especially taking as the earlier, humbler Lily (real name Mildred Plotka) of whom of course we don't see much. There are some blanks and glitches in her prima donna stage, as if she were saving herself for her coloratura moment, which admittedly is stunning when it comes.

Brigitte Robinson cannot quite sustain Mrs. Primroses big number, but she has a manic determined twinkle thats thoroughly right. Patrick R. Brown, long and lugubrious, and William Vickers, rotund and desperate, do terrific work as Oscar's aides, for whom life is one long loyalty test; Brown looks rather like Adolph Green, which is not only appropriate but helpful. Evan Buliung is funny as Lily's narcissistic performer though he doesnt equal the ecstatic acrobatics that made a star of Kevin Kline when he created the role.

Valerie Moores choreography is excellent; the more people she has to move, the merrier. Especially nifty are the mass embarkation, and the whirlwind imbroglio She's a Nut, which sweeps up the cast, the storyline, and the audience. We even get -- something I'd hardly dared hoped for -- the prescribed glimpse of Mrs Primrose clinging indomitably to the front of the engine. Indeed, whatever qualms I may have about its scale, Yvonne Sauriol's locomotive design is a sleek treat in its own right, with the journey helped on its way by playful little devices like a toy train (used rather too often) and what seems to be a tunnel at the end of a light. Her costumes are superb. Unusually in a musical, the second act is better than the first. By the end joy is unconfined, even if the show isn't.
