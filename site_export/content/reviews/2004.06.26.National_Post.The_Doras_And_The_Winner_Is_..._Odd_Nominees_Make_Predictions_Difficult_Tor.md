Picking winners for this year's Dora Awards is a more than usually frustrating occupation, because the nominations in most categories are more than usually grotesque. True, the 2003-2004 theatre season in Toronto was not a bright one, but it wasn't quite as gloomy as the Dora voters, by both their inclusions and omissions, have managed to suggest. Something is wrong with the electoral process -- maybe it's log-rolling, maybe it's the theatrical equivalent of tactical voting, but it should be looked into.

We start with the General Theatre division. "General" here means larger, a capacious domain that can accommodate anything from the Princess of Wales to Theatre Passe Muraille, but that does include most of the venues most people, including this critic, are likeliest to have visited.

Outstanding New Play: This, as is perhaps appropriate, is the most vexatious category of all. I thought the best new work of the season was Jason Sherman's Remnants, followed by Ronnie Burkett's Provenance, which was one-half disappointing but the other half superb. Neither play was nominated. The omnitalented Burkett, who might have qualified in three other categories, has this year been shut out of all of them. Could this be backlash after all his previous acclaim?

Of those that are up there, my money would be on Trey Anthony's da kink in my hair, even though I didn't see it. Or maybe, given the competition, because I didn't see it. The most respectable of the rest is Marjorie Chan's China Doll, though I doubt it made anybody's heart sing.

Confederation's claim to newness rests on a technicality, as it's a reworking of an earlier play in Michael Hollingsworth's ongoing cartoon history of Canada. The whole cycle probably deserves a special prize, but singling out this installment looks like an act of desperation.

The two remaining contestants are unbelievable. Peter Froehlich's Simpl, a tribute to an old German comedian, was a decent evening's entertainment but only marginally a play. Michael O'Brien's Restitution - An Irish-Canadian Rhapsody was generally regarded as appalling; I actually liked it more than most, but not to the point of calling it good. The most surprising omission is Carole Frechette's Helen's Necklace, which everybody else seemed to think was wonderful; I suppose I should feel vindicated but I actually feel vaguely indignant.

Outstanding New Musical: This always occasions a certain amount of barrel-scraping, sometimes reaching as far down as Ross Petty Christmas pantomimes. Not this year, though Petty's Cinderella (one of his better shows) was as deserving of a nod as the nominated Pelagie, which was deadeningly worthy, or Hello ... Hello and Top Gun! The Musical, which were deadeningly worthless.

That leaves Tequila Vampire Matinee, which was original and fun. Betcha Top Gun gets it, though (its camp apparently struck a chord), unless there's an Acadian sympathy vote for Pelagie.

Outstanding Production of a Play: This, if it means anything, means Best All-Round Show (Without Songs).

This, by any sane measure, was Soulpepper's No Man's Land. It wasn't nominated. It's tempting to accuse the Doras of an anti- Soulpepper bias (the company, along with CanStage, is regarded as Establishment and therefore suspect), but I'd say that in most respects Albert Schultz's bunch have had fair treatment this year.

This oversight, though, is a huge one; and paranoia has a right to be rampant. And then again, just look at the competition. For thoughts on China Doll, Confederation and Simpl, see under Outstanding New Play above.

Remnants (Tarragon) deserves to win for its combination of writing, acting and direction. The Syringa Tree (CanStage, so I contradict myself) probably will win. You alternate two admired actresses in a solo turn that goes straight to our liberal hearts and what else do you expect?

Outstanding Production of a Musical (or Best All-Round Show With Songs): How exactly do you compare The Producers, brought in on a big scale, with the home-brewed product? Well, if you have to, The Producers clearly wins (and at this point it could probably use the comfort). I certainly wouldn't repine if the glory went to The Last Five Years (American piece but new Canadian production), but there's a better chance that it will go to Cookin' at the Cookery, though it's nothing like as good a show, and -- lest we forget -- is also brought in. I see Tequila Vampire Matinee is in there too, so maybe it has a better shot at New Musical than I'd thought.

Outstanding Performance by a Male in a Principal Role -- Play: The standard here is very high. Jim Mezon (Copenhagen) was very fine, and I am happy for David Ferry (Hockey Mom, Hockey Dad), Rick Roberts (hilarious in Hotel Loopy) and, even though I didn't see him, Dennis O'Connor (Le Visiteur). And I lament the exclusion of Kevin Hanchard (Blue/Orange). All the same, if it doesn't go to William Hutt (No Man's Land) we have forgotten what both words and acting mean.

Outstanding Performance by a Female in a Principal Role -- Play: This must be a headache. Yanna McIntosh and Caroline Cave are both up for The Syringa Tree, and the canniest thing would have been to finesse some kind of joint nomination. Forced to choose, I imagine the jury will vote for McIntosh, though if it were me I'd be inclined to avoid the issue and plump for Martha Burns (Happy Days), who was just as good. (And dare I say that I was more impressed by McIntosh's unlisted number in Phedre?) Nicola Lipman (Simpl) was good, but doesn't stand a chance; likewise Jane Spidell, nominated for Blood, but deserving it more for Rune Arlidge. Department of Seriously Uncanny Coincidence: The author of Rune Arlidge this year won our awed admiration by donating part of his liver to the author of Blood. That certainly puts awards in perspective.

Outstanding Performance by a Male in a Principal Role -- Musical: Michael Therriault in The Producers, and I don't want to hear any arguments. (Therriault's co-star, Sean Cullen, is also nominated and will be hosting the ceremonies. Hello, Sean.)

Outstanding Performance by a Female in a Principal Role -- Musical: Jackie Richardson (Cookin' at the Cookery) will get this in a cakewalk, though it's a shame that it's at the expense of her co- performer, Montego Glover. Why wasn't she entered under Supporting? Or, as they call it here, Feature? The rest are filler, though can anyone explain to me why Susan Gilmour, Amy Rutherford and Sarah Cornell (the wondrous sex-object of The Producers) were nominated, while Blythe Wilson (The Last Five Years) wasn't?

Outstanding Performance in a Supporting -- sorry, Feature -- Role in a Play or Musical: Another bulging category, the more so as it contains the entire cast of Confederation, billed as Ensemble. So joint nominations are allowed. Assuming they don't win by weight of numbers: C. David Johnson (The Play's the Thing) was a repeat performance and Brandon McGibbon (The Producers) honestly wasn't that good. It's down to William Webster (Phedre) and Juan Chioran (The Producers). Chioran's was the flashier role, if that means anything. Actually, it was the flashiest role of the year, and he did it better than the guy on the Broadway-cast CD.

Outstanding Direction of a Play: Albert Schultz (No Man's Land) isn't there. Richard Rose is, twice: deservedly, for Remnants, and undeservedly, for Simpl. Sarah Stanley (Restitution) boggles the mind. Larry Moss (The Syringa Tree) -- well, he didn't do anything wrong that I could see. What about Morris Panych (Amadeus)?

Outstanding Direction of a Musical: Again, two different worlds. Susan Stroman (The Producers) beggars, in every sense, the local product. If the home team gets it, then Daryl Cloran (The Last Five Years) deserves to win; Marion Caffey (Cookin' at the Cookery) doesn't, but very likely will. While we're at it:

Outstanding Choreography: I don't see how this could not be Stroman.

Outstanding Musical Direction: I want it to be Marek Norman (The Last Five Years).

When we get to the various design awards, we're even further into the comparison of incomparables. Let me say merely that Robin Wagner's sets and William Ivey Long's costumes for The Producers were all they should have been; and the costumes, by Astrid Janson and Julie Renton, for Restitution were well up to VideoCabaret's fantastical standards. Andrea Lundy picked up three of the six lighting nominations; I'm rooting for her work on Phedre.

In the Independent (or Very Small) Theatre division, I didn't see enough to have many opinions. I was no fan of Dear Boss (up for New Play) or of (nod), another Production nominee).

I did like Rebecca Northan (Female Performance) in Dear Boss, though she faces stiff competition from Kate Meehan (Tape). Eli Batalion has a deserved Male Performance nomination for Job: The Hip- Hop Saga, though his partner in this two-man show mysteriously doesn't. Clinton Walker has one for The Trials of John Demjanjuk; Frank Moore hasn't, but should have.

The various nominations for Theatre for Young Audiences managed to ignore both New Canadian Kid and Number One and Jamie at the resurgent YPT (now renamed the Lorraine Kimsa Theatre for Young People). Opera is none of my business, but I would choose the Canadian Opera Company's Die Walkure for Production, and, beyond a doubt, Laura Claycomb's heavenly Gilda in Rigoletto for Performance.
