THE ATTIC, THE PEARLS AND 3 FINE GIRLS

THEATRE REVIEW

THE ATTIC, THE PEARLS AND 3 FINE GIRLS

Saturday, March 8, 1997

Created by Jennifer Brewin,

Leah Cherniak, Ann-Marie


  


MacDonald, Alisa Palmer


  


and Martha Ross


  


Directed by Alisa Palmer


  


Starring Leah Cherniak,


  


Ann-Marie MacDonald


  


and Martha Ross


  


Rating: **

Sisters wrestle skeletons in attic

AS a title, The Attic, The Pearls and 3 Fine Girls has an attractive lilt, not to mention a cute internal rhyme. It also boasts a pun. The piece that lurks beneath concerns a trio of sisters who are indisputably Fine by name and, more debatable, by nature. When they apply the adjective to themselves (and nobody else gets the chance, since they are the only characters) they do it with a certain irony. They have, in short, their hangups. But then who hasn't?

Theres Jo-Jo, an academic with uncertain career prospects ("If I were a lesbian, Id have tenure"); Jayne, who is a corporate raider and has the cellphone to prove it (she is a lesbian, but seems uptight about it); and Jelly, who is some kind of artist and has a floppy kind of personality (she, to her sisters' mild surprise, is pregnant). Their father has just died, and they have reunited in the family home for the most chaotic wake since Finnegans. In between the festivities, which are off-stage, they indulge in recrimination and remembrance, which are very much on stage.

To do this they retreat to the attic, suggested with brilliant economy in Dany Lyne's design by opening a trap in the stage floor and tilting two flanking sets of window frames to a smart and threatening diagonal. This upper room was also the site of the most intense scenes of their girlhood, one or two of which are recreated. As for the pearls in the title, they belonged to their late mother and are much coveted and much mentioned, although they never take on any compelling dramatic reality. Neither do the deceased parents, or the family friends and connections (including one ex-husband) hovering below. And, most damaging, despite their continuous visibility, neither do the Fine girls themselves.

The actresses playing them do. The show, moderately witty and physically very accomplished, has the feel of an acting exercise. The three performers are also three-fifths of the credited creative team; the others are Alisa Palmer, who directed, and Jennifer Brewin, whose job description is contributing artist. Leah Cherniak as Jelly and Martha Ross as Jo-Jo devote a lot of energy to being competitively flaky. Jayne is set apart because she belongs to what the business pages call "the real world." This doesnt make her any less desperate -- more, if anything -- but it does enforce a trim exterior to set off the inside churnings, and Ann-Marie MacDonald has the style, the poise and the technique to bring it off. She gets some of her best effects in off-hand sotto voce (theyve given me mergers and acquisitions, she mutters proudly),and when she and her siblings take us back to the wild days of their youth, it's she who can take fullest advantage of the contrast. The sight of two women twisting to an old record of Roaming in the Gloaming is striking enough; when one of them is MacDonald in a tartan beret, black bra, skirt and leopard-skin coat, it stakes a claim to the history books.

One scene does have autonomous life. It's a flashback in which Jelly, the youngest, is terrorized by her siblings when they tell her their parents will not be returning from an evening out. "Are they angels yet?" she asks. Its a brief, imaginative sequence that says much about childhood fears and cruelties, and also hints at why Jelly has wobbled so much in later life. Nothing else has that concentration. Its a foregone conclusion that, after all the jokes and the angst, the evening will end in some kind of sisterly affirmation. But if it's a self-indulgent occasion, the actors themselves have probably earned the right.


  


The Attic, The Pearls and 3 Fine Girls is at Buddies in Bad Times Theatre on Alexander Street through March 30.
