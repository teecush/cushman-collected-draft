THEATRE REVIEW

His Girl Friday

Festival Theatre,

Niagara-on-the-Lake

The Shaw Festival has mounted a very good production of what may be the most unnecessary play ever written.

His Girl Friday is the play of the movie of the play. In the beginning, which was 1928, there was The Front Page, written by Ben Hecht and Charles MacArthur: not only the best of newspaper comedies but one of the great American plays of any kind. (For sheer, jazzy energy and wit it ranks with Once in a Lifetime and Guys and Dolls.) Its been filmed three times: twice under its original title, once under the one that's now on the marquee in Niagara.

The name change is the result of a gender change, one that according to legend came about when a Hollywood producer discovered the lines for the play's male star reporter sounded even better when spoken by a woman, and then presumably hollered for someone to get him a rewrite. Because the character was already called Hildy Johnson (the name of an actual, though male, U.S. journalist of Swedish descent), the rewrite was off to a propitious start. The picture was made in 1940, with Rosalind Russell as Hildy, looking superb in stripes, and Cary Grant as her former editor, and now also former husband, Walter Burns. What emerged was a classic screwball comedy with social-conscience underpinnings and maybe the fastest dialogue ever filmed. Its as great a movie as The Front Page is a play.

In 2003 the British National Theatre had the bright idea of combining the two by, in effect, putting His Girl Friday onstage. They got John Guare, the noted U.S. playwright, to adapt the adaptation, but he and they were on a hiding to nothing. The movie hasnt disintegrated, so why compete with the incomparable? The Front Page still exists, so why not just revive it? As well as being unfair to its predecessors, the new hybrid is unfair to Guare, whose House of Blue Leaves and Six Degrees of Separation rank among the best modern American plays and whose Atlantic City is my favourite screenplay ever.

Jim Mezon's production at the Shaw Festival is a huge improvement on the one at the National; it's better cast, zippier and it got rid of the extra layer of superfluity that weighed down the British version and that involved the pretense that the onstage proceedings were themselves being filmed. Making it the movie of the play of the movie, Mezon maintains most of the spirit of The Front Page, even without being allowed to deliver the letter. (As far as I could tell only one too-good-to-lose line of Hecht and MacArthur has been retained by Guare; its tell him his poetry stinks and kick him downstairs. He has had, alas, to jettison Grants immortal and presumably ad-libbed line from the movie: "The last man who spoke to me like that was Archie Leach, the day before he cut his throat.") He can't conceal, though, that the original play has suffered more than a sex change. It's been updated, which may not matter, and sentimentalized, which does.

The Front Page is set in the press room of a Chicago courthouse on the eve of a hanging. (Oh yes, its also the best play ever written about Chicago.) The condemned man, a clueless self-styled anarchist, has unintentionally shot a black cop, and the mayor and sheriff, who are up for re-election and are courting what they call the coloured vote, are determined to see him swing. They even go to the lengths of concealing his governmental pardon.

The killer escapes, and stumbles into the arms of Burns and Johnson, the citys star muckrakers. For the sake of a scoop, and because they hate the mayor, they conceal him - in a rolltop desk. Guare's version, like the movie, brings the action forward to the eve of the Second World War, but makes far more a point of it. The accused is now a German-Jewish refugee, his police victim an American Nazi. Walter Burns, the stopat-nothing newshound, is now, at least in his nobler moments, a crusader for justice and the little man, and even makes speeches about it, while Hildy is a positive paragon. Their originals had no such scruples; their mission of afflicting the comfortable - they weren't so hot on comforting the afflicted - had desirable political side effects, but they were basically in it for fun and profit. For which we loved them.

Guares version makes us love them less by trying to make us love them more, partly by making them love one another. Their romantic involvement worked fine in the film, but it weighs, and slows, the play down. Hildy wants to quit, Walter wants to keep her; and that dynamic would work just as well, probably be more interesting, if they were just colleagues and comrades-in-arms.

Thats not to knock the performances here; Benedict Campbell is prowling, Nicole Underhay is crackling and their collisions and collusions are smashing. Kevin Bundy is delicious as Hildy's new fiance, an all-around wimp who seems to have lapped up fascist sympathies along with his mother's milk. Hes one of Guares more felicitous inventions; mama herself is one of his least, a battle axe who made me feel embarrassed for Wendy Thatcher, who plays her, especially during a speech denouncing women writers that must rank as one of the unfunniest set pieces ever written.

As in any version of this material, much of the running - and very speedy running it is - is made by the supporting corps of reporters; Kevin Hanchard and Neil Barclay are stand-outs among an excellent team, with Ric Reid in a class of his own as the hypochondriacal poetaster Bensinger. Thom Marriott's bullying mayor and Andrew Bunker's desk-bound fugitive are great cameos, and Lorne Kennedy has a show-stopping, if somewhat indulgent, turn as the inebriated Bearer of the Pardon. Peter Hartwell's set is a fine sparse machine for rushing around in.

The show offers a roaring good time. Only it might have been a roaring even better one.

His Girl Friday runs until Oct. 6. For tickets and more information, visit shawfest.com.
