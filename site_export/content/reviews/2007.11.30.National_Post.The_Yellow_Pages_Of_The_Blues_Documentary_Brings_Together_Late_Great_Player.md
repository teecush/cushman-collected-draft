We were old, says Solomon Burke, summing up the glories of the concert we've just watched, we were all together. And," -- pause for a laugh -- "we weren't getting paid.

Burke is one of the headiest of the blues headliners featured in Lightning in a Bottle, which memorializes a 2003 show at Radio City Music Hall in New York City, which itself memorialized an entire American musical tradition.

Martin Scorsese, whose The Last Waltz is one of the most celebrated of all concert movies, is the executive producer of Lightning, and he appears on stage to make a brief introduction. The film, however, is directed by Antoine Fuqua, and very well.

The film cuts between performances on the night of, and rehearsals for, the show; with some stills and newsreel clips to fill in the sociological background, plus footage of artists like Muddy Waters and John Lee Hooker who, for the best of mortal reasons, couldnt make the gig.

Otherwise, everyones there. As one participant says, they must have looked in the Yellow Pages under blues."

"We try, says Scorsese to tell the story of this music from the beginning," and we start with Angelique Kidjo rapturously chant- singing Zelie, identified in a caption as adapted from a West African source. Fast forward to Mavis Staples and a 1928 injunction from Blind Lemon Jefferson, See That My Grave Is Kept Clean, her husky voice grazing the title phrase and turning it into gospel- blues.

David "Honeyboy Edwards, introducing his own Gamblin' Man, tells us that he hustled all over Mississippi" and that he started performing when he was 13; that too was in 1928. ("Every time I got tired they gave me a drink of whisky. I guess he got hooked.)

Odetta, whom I think of as a folksinger and who still looks amazingly young when she talks, but like a tragic seer when she sings, does Leadbelly's Jim Crow Blues, whose title speaks for itself.

We also get to hear Odetta sweeping in on a rehearsal with the great, and now late, Ruth Brown, and exclaiming I dont know why this brilliant woman has to compete with the band."

The musicians -- among whom Dr. John is repeatedly prominent -- look suitably chastened, and cool it. Brown herself, who was recovering from a stroke, muses that "most of the great singers of blues are men, because that allows them to vent their feelings.

For herself, she harks back to her 1950s R & B hit Mama, He Treats Your Daughter Mean, a time when, I didnt understand the blues. Now I have a reason to sing them." Singing, we learn, saves her from screaming; she reprises that teenage hit in a mature rasp.

Brown also joins forces with Staples and Natalie Cole to deliver the ultimate female put-down, Men Are Like Streetcars (with Bill Cosby onstage as the bemused object of their attentions). Cole, looking torch-singer glamorous in a beaded dress but sounding wildly good, is also the performer entrusted with W. C. Handy's St. Louis Blues, the centrepiece, as it happens, of one of her father's best albums.

Handy, we are told, did not so much write songs as transcribe them; and in rather the same way the film downgrades the "classic blues of the 20s and the women who sang them -- even Bessie Smith, whose likeness is briefly glimpsed but whose name is never mentioned. Shes lost somewhere between the early rural blues and the later varieties that cross-pollinated with country, jive and rock.

Or, to put it another way, in the gulf between acoustic and electric. The concert, and the film, pretty much divide into those two halves. For home viewers, the first half is the better, with the musicians' faces as compellingly gnarled as their voices. There's even a health warning, from Vernon Reid: A lot of these guys weren't sympathetic figures. Leadbelly shot a man dead. We project a lot of sentimentality on them -- because they made a great sound."

The second half, a cumulative jam session, is more a case of "you had to be there. There are still many moments to make you feel you were. Steve Tyler and Joe Perry look and sound like the Rolling Stones must have wished they did.

Burke, looking like a house with a white fedora for a chimney, sits in a straight-backed chair and delivers Turn On Your Love Light, a hand-clapper and audience-swayer that possesses even him, so that he stands up and takes his hat off. He also has a bizarre, scary anecdote about having to sing for the Ku Klux Klan.

King also has a story, about singing in the more liberal precincts of the University of Maryland and being booed because he was blues and the students wanted soul and R & B. (It was like being black twice.") "They thought, he says, taking the final spot, they would save the oldest till last."

Not quite accurate, or at least not in terms of his age: It may be more of that sentimentality, because he has so much reputation and presence, but when he sings and plays Sweet Sixteen, the same number with which he eventually won over the Maryland campus, you would swear that he extracts the sweetest and strongest sounds ever to come out of a guitar.

After all that, it's something of a come-down to learn from the final credits that the whole thing has been a glorified commercial for Volkswagen. (Something about the blues moved from the Mississippi Delta to Chicago and New York, and they can take you the same route.) But at least, as Solomon Burke testifies, the proceeds went to charity.
