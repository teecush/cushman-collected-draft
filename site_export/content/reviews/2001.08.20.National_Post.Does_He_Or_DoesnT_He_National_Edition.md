LOVE FROM A STRANGER

Royal George Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont.

- - -

NIAGARA-ON-THE-LAKE, Ont. - A character who proclaims in the first scene of an Agatha Christie play that "I want to live before I'm old and grey and dead" has almost a moral duty not to survive into the second. It's called irony. There may, though, be a greater irony to be mined in defying our reasonable expectations of the thriller genre by letting her stay alive awhile, and this is the path taken in Love from a Stranger.

It is not, strictly speaking, a Christie play; it is a 1936 adaptation, by actor and author Frank Vosper, of a Christie story that had been published a couple of years earlier. This may account for the dialogue, and even a couple of the characters, having more weight and flavour than those of your average Agatha.

The girl who tempts fate at the beginning of the action is named Cecily Harrington. After years of scrimping in a London office, she and her roommate, Mavis, have won a large cash prize in a sweepstakes. Mavis intends to use her share to travel. Cecily was all set to wed a stodgy fiance just returned home from two years in Sudan, but now she is having second thoughts. These are reinforced when a flaxen-haired young man calling himself Bruce Lovell appears from nowhere, ostensibly to rent her apartment, and sweeps her off her feet -- or at least off to lunch. (At the Savoy Grill, he remarks with impressive casualness.)

By the end of the first scene, and on 10 minutes' acquaintance, they seem mutually smitten. By the end of the first act they are engaged. At the beginning of the second act, they are married and happily ensconced in a very remote house in the country.

Here Bruce begins behaving very suspiciously. He commandeers the basement as a dark-room for his photographic habit and will let nobody else enter it. He claims that his own money is tied up and he must borrow Cecily's in order to buy the house. He gets her to sign documents that she does not appear to have read; there also seems to be a discrepancy between what he says the house cost and what it actually cost. He buries bottles of peroxide in the garden, leading us to speculate as to whether his hair colouring is truly God's work. He suffers unexplained dizzy spells and refuses to see a doctor about them. He removes from sight newspaper clippings, with pictures, concerning the recent trial in America of a serial wife- killer who got off by having a clever lawyer.

In fact, he acts so like a man with something serious to hide that we have a hard time believing him innocent, though by all the accepted rules of the game we know he must be. The truth of the matter I will not of course reveal. I will only say that the play takes shape less as a whodunnit than as a willhedoit (andifsohow). And that an early reference to Scheherazade and the Arabian Nights turns out to be thematically and materially crucial.

It takes its time, but the suspense steadily builds, and the last section, a masterful series of reversals, plays brilliantly with and against our expectations. Micheline Chevrier's production, barring a couple of weak performances, is well up to the standards of tautness and impeccability established by the Shaw Festival in these matters, and Brian Perchaluk's rural-fastness setting is as cozy a site for possible murder as could be imagined.

Lisa Norton and Mike Shara, as Cecily and Bruce, have some problems keeping their characters plausible in the early stages, but they squeeze every drop of narrative tension from their climactic duet.

Psychological tension, too: The criss-crosses here of attraction and repulsion may not quite be worthy of Strindberg, but they at least compete with the best of American film noir.

The supporting roles are no more than functional, but Laurie Paton's Mavis is an efficiently solicitous best friend, and there is more broadly entertaining work from Richard Farrell as the village gardener, who says things like "my rheumatics have been that bad," and from Helen Taylor as his buxom niece, who gets taken on as a raw but willing servant girl (and is roundly patronized for her pains).

Jennifer Phipps, as Cecily's busybody Auntie Loo-Loo, offers a model display of what might be called constructive scene stealing. As a genteel lady in reduced but not quite straitened circumstances, she is by turns protective, practical, overbearing and -- when the right expensive brand names are mentioned in her presence -- snobbishly coy.

Her instant changes of direction are very funny; she summons a world bounded at one end by tea at Harrod's (a reward earned by having registered her niece's apartment with the store's real- estate office) and at the other by cheap day-excursion tickets to the country, in the days when trains were trains and a coach with a corridor was a luxury. She is, as ever, real to her fingertips.

I was hoping that at the end she might ride to the rescue at the head of the U.S. Cavalry, or at least the local constabulary. But, as I said, this is a play that defies expectation.
