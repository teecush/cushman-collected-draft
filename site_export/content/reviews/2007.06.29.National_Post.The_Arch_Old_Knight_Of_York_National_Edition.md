It has passed into both civic and theatrical legend that Camelot and what was then the O'Keefe Centre opened at the same time. The Alan Jay Lerner-Frederick Loewe musical began its pre-Broadway tour in 1960 as the opening attraction of Front Street's cavernous emporium. Deals were done to make it so, but the inevitable result was two sets of teething traumas happening at once. The show opened unhappily, and key members of the creative team were whisked in and out of hospital with heart attacks as they attempted to fix it. Still, whatever the problems on that first night long ago -- which included a four-hour running-time -- what appeared on the stage must have been a miracle of excitement compared with what's going on there now. We have another touring Camelot, though this one is probably not headed for Broadway.

It's one of those catchpenny revivals with a sort of star in the lead. Michael York is playing King Arthur, and he's the main reason why the show's a disaster. It seems cruel to say so, since musical theatre has never been his thing (nor indeed has any other kind of theatre), and he does seem to have been cast for niceness. But that's the problem. His Arthur goes through the entire show with a self-deprecatory smile and vocal inflections to match. It's meant, I suppose, to be disarmingly charming, but it becomes physically painful to watch and to listen to. He seems to be apologizing for his emotions rather than feeling them. If we cannot believe, passionately, in Arthur's agony and in his idealism, the show makes no kind of sense.

In this very free version of Arthurian legend, the king's idealism consists in his introducing to Britain the concepts of law and justice, to be administered by the knights of the structurally egalitarian Round Table. The agony comes when his queen, Guenevere, betrays him with his best friend and perfect knight, Lancelot. Guenevere is put on trial and condemned to the stake, leaving Arthur to face what his devilish bastard son Mordred calls the "magnificent dilemma" of sparing her, thus destroying his ideals, or killing her, thus destroying his love.

There's tragic force there, and even this production can't quite destroy the soaring poignancy of the show's ending, in which Arthur, via a reprise of the previously jingly title-song, sends off a young hopeful called Tom of Warwick (i.e. Thomas Malory, future author of the Morte d'Arthur) with instructions to keep the Camelot story alive. It doesn't, though, come anywhere near disguising the weaknesses inherent in what has gone before. If ever there was a flawed musical with elements of greatness, Camelot is it. Lerner's book, admittedly with a fearsome amount of ground to cover, varies between solemn and flippant, generally settling on arch. Archness also pervades many of his lyrics. Loewe's music similarly veers between stately and stodgy, occasionally alighting on lovely; though, it's somehow typical that the loveliest song of all, Follow Me, should be sung, mostly offstage, by a character we barely meet to one whom, a mere half-hour in, we are about to lose. Michael A.M. Lerner (A.J.'s son) has adapted the script, and somewhat streamlined it, though he's added a baffling silent prologue and has cut a couple of the merrier songs from a score that couldn't really afford to lose them.

The staging (Glenn Casale) and sets (John Iacovelli) are very makeshift. Rachel York has charm and spunk as Guenevere, while showing again that it's impossible to play this role without sounding exactly like Julie Andrews, not only in the range and colour of her singing, but in the roguishness of her speech. That said, her singing of Before I Gaze at You Again, not previously a song to which I've ever paid much attention, is the evening's high point. An actor with the unlikely name of Time Winters gets all the available fun out of King Pellinore, a transplanted English clubman who arrives at court in search of the Questing Beast, like (he says) all the Pellinores before him, and then forgets about it for the rest of the show. He's fortunate, for purely technological reasons, in not having to sing. We have suffered much recently from overbearing sound systems; now here's one that's underbearing. It makes everyone, from the vibrant Ms. York to the modest Mr. York, sound as if their vocal cords have been wrapped in cotton wool. - Camelot runs until June 30.
