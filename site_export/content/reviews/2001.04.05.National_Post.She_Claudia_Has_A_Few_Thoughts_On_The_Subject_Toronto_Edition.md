I, CLAUDIA

Tarragon Extra Space, Toronto

Kristen Thomson is the real theatrical thing. Her solo show I, Claudia demonstrates that she is a magnetic and versatile actress, but after her performances in Oleanna, A Streetcar Named Desire and Hysteria, that is hardly news. It also shows her to be a terrific writer, and that is news since she hasn't done it before. Her script has its faults -- an overall structure that doesn't work as it's supposed to and a wide sentimental streak -- but in a debutante, such lapses are forgiveable. What counts is that she can rock her audience -- she certainly rocked me -- on a regular basis with lines and moments that are hilariously funny, totally unexpected and unimpeachably true.

The title character of I, Claudia (that arch title is, I guess, another flaw; it suggests that if the pun were not available, Thomson would have been just as happy to call her heroine Gertrude) is a 12 1/2-year-old schoolgirl suffering the triple assaults of puberty, unpopularity and her parents' divorce. The last affliction has developed its own painful sequel -- her father's re-marriage to a lady named Leslie, whom he has introduced to Claudia as "a special friend."

Claudia, whose accounts of her own thoughts and feelings happily occupy the bulk of the stage time, recalls this declaration with devastating adolescent scorn: "I hate to be precocious, but I know a euphemism when I see one." She is even more dismissive of Leslie's physical endowments ("two flying saucers landed from outer space") and just incredulous about Leslie's suggestion that they should be girlfriends: "I thought, 'Leslie, look at yourself." I quote from memory, but I'll vouch for the tone if not the actual words.

Thomson lets Leslie have her own say, which we hear while she is being fitted for her wedding dress -- stressed herself and the cause of stress in others. She also plays two male characters -- Claudia's paternal grandfather, as disapproving of his son's new match as she is, and Drachman, her school custodian, who once ran a theatre in some mythical European country and is now our master of ceremonies.

To effect these impersonations, the actress appears before us in a series of half-masks, the kind that cover everything except the mouth. (They are part of a classic set, devised for drama schools by the noted Anglo-Algerian stage designer Farrah.) As usual with masks, one initially finds them forbidding and uncalled for. Also as usual, they proceed to impose themselves and to seem not only acceptable but essential. And as always with a good masked performance, you could swear that the flat faces -- Claudia's own anyway -- are changing expression as you watch. Much as I usually enjoy looking at Thomson's own face, the sacrifice seems worthwhile; she is neither an old man nor a 12-year-old girl, and the masks enable her to distance herself from the characters' incidental qualities while immersing herself in their realities.

That at least is the idea. It doesn't altogether work out because the subsidiary characters still don't seem very real or indeed very necessary. They are there to provide alternative views of Claudia's situation and they do to the extent that they provide physical evidence that there are other people involved. But the men offer very little in the way of perspective or cross-lighting; it's their presence as excess baggage that causes me to accuse the play of sentimentality. Drachman's accent, coming on top of his mask, amounts to a fit of the mumbles, while his philosophical showman's presence seems like something Ronnie Burkett might have dreamed up on an off-day, though it does give director Chris Abraham a chance to apply some modest but useful theatrical colouring. (How much he contributed to the performance is impossible to assess, but I suspect it was substantial; the show is tight.)

The grown-up characters we really get to know are those we never meet -- Claudia's parents -- but only and subtly through what Claudia says and doesn't say about them. Leslie's vulnerable- aggressive presence is entertaining, but even she operates at a tangent to Claudia rather than in tension with her.

It is she, Claudia, who makes the play; and when she is on this is incomparably the best show in Toronto. (There's no competition, come to think of it, even when she's off.) She has subsidiary problems: Her science project for one, and, rather closely tied to it, the fate of two pet goldfish whom she has named Romeo and Juliet, the wrong way round as she later discovers.

She is also aggrieved by the efforts of her mother, whom she calls "over-protective," to restrict her movements around town. She is also capable, after spending most of the play talking about the father, whom she clearly adores, of describing her mother as the nicest person in the world and concealing a sad poem she has written because she knows it would worry her. This is a revelation that tears at our emotions without exploiting them, as does her description of finding inalienable evidence, through pillaging her father's apartment, that he was involved with his new lady long before the divorce. She puts another stolen token beneath her mother's pillow in the hopes that this will bring her parents together: "I learned it in voodoo class," she says, and we believe her.

Claudia is at what you might call an aggravated awkward stage. She is immersed in teenage culture, as only an imminent ex-pre-teen can be, and her verbal reaction to any situation that affronts her is the ubiquitous "duh!" There are two riotous sequences of her dancing with self-consciously furious abandon in front of a mirror, and her career ambition is to be a VJ, but she doesn't feel cool enough to admit it to anyone. She feels especially intimidated by two contemporaries named Tracey and Stacey, whom one imagines as later versions of the twins who periodically make life hell for Bart Simpson; she also knows herself to be brighter than them in many respects.

Claudia is genuinely bruised but not mortally injured; she herself, with colourful variations, employs the analogy of a cocooned butterfly. As a writer Thomson seems to have observed many Claudias, then created this unique one; as an actor she hurls herself at the role with hair-trigger accuracy and a joy that envelops the house. I laughed till I cried, though the chances are I would have cried anyway.
