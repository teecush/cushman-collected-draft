There are three theatres in Toronto that are pleasant to visit, irrespective of what happens to be playing in them. All of them are recent buildings. One is the Four Seasons Centre, more generally thought of as the Toronto opera house. Another is the Young Centre, home of Soulpepper. And the third, though apparently not for much longer, is the Princess of Wales.

This week David Mirvish, who owns the Princess of Wales and most of the adjoining territory, announced that the theatre is to be demolished as part of a new King Street development incorporating an art gallery that will make Mirvish's own legendary but largely unseen collection available to the public, a new campus for OCAD University, and three condominium towers, each a different shape. According to a letter sent to the media by Mir-vish, the Princess of Wales is "a beautiful facility of which I am proud, but it happens to be situated in the middle of the new project's path."

Well, it's Mirvish's property, he has a right to do what he likes with it, and he and his late father Ed have made huge contributions to the theatrical life of the city, indeed to its life in general. I share the widespread reaction that the last thing downtown Toronto needs is more condos, but quite possibly the new development will be the transformative addition to our cityscape that we are being promised. It's hard to tell from the preliminary sketches we've been shown; and as Frank Gehry, the architect, has said, they are only sketches. Much may change in what is apparently a multi-year project; one only hopes that there won't be too many second thoughts after the theatre has been levelled.

But if a theatre has to be sacrificed, why does it have to be this one? The Royal Alexandra is also a Mirvish property, and it's just down the street. Actually, I'm less shocked by the plan than by the cavalier reaction of some of my fellow theatre writers who claim that to shed tears over the loss of the Princess of Wales would be sentimental.

My objections aren't sentimental; they're practical. The late 1980s were Toronto's golden age of theatrical renovation. The Pantages - later the Canon, now the Ed Mirvish - was restored and reopened in 1989, as was the theatrical duplex of the Elgin and the Winter Garden. But the Princess of Wales was the first theatre, not just in Toronto but in Canada, to be built as a private enterprise since the Royal Alex back in 1907. (The Toronto Centre for the Arts, previously the North York Centre for the Performing Arts, was opened in 1993 as a combined private/ civic initiative. It's a great space in the wrong place. It's far easier to persuade North Yorkers to go downtown than the reverse.)

The Princess of Wales was opened, we're now told, merely as a "temporary facility" to house the Toronto production of Miss Saigon. Well, some temporary facility. It's lasted for 20 years, during which it's established itself as the most beautiful, audience-friendly big house in Toronto. The famous Frank Stella murals are the least of it, though we're promised that they'll mostly be retained in the new developments. The really rare achievement is the intimate contact between audience and performers, extraordinary considering the vastness of the stage and the spaciousness of the auditorium and equalled among big theatres only by the Festival Theatre at Stratford, which has a totally different configuration.

It's comfortable in less rarefied ways as well. Its lobbies are spacious. Despite its several levels, it's fully, and almost uniquely wheelchair accessible, which counts morally and, increasingly, legally. It has abundant legroom and excellent sight lines. Compare the cramped and cramping seats at the Royal Alex and the impassable crush in the foyer of the Ed Mirvish, Or, going outside the Mirvish empire, think of the Sony Centre, which for all its facelifts and name-changes, is an irreducible barn. Or the St. Lawrence Centre, which hardly ever feels right. The restored Elgin/Winter Garden has never established an identity. Even the Young Centre, which has the friendliest lobby in town, still has visibility problems in the actual theatres. Theatre architecture is a hard thing to get right. To take a successful example that's only two decades old and reduce it to landfill seems sort of wasteful.

Now it's true (though it hasn't been mentioned in the official announcements) that Toronto has more outsized theatres than it can use. They were built, or rebuilt, by competing impresarios at a time when it was thought that huge musicals, and the huge audiences for them, were forever. It turns out they weren't; the megamusical is becoming extinct (and just as well, if only for the extreme ugliness of the word itself). But big shows in themselves are unlikely to disappear, and the Princess of Wales is the best place in which to house them. The Royal Alex has history on its side, and as for the Ed Mirvish, one can hardly expect the organization to sacrifice a theatre named in honour of its founder. But if something has to go, I would rather it were one of them. If a beautiful new theatre is dispensable, while old ones aren't, then maybe our ideas of heritage need some re-examination.

It's true, too, that Toronto really needs purpose-built medium-sized theatres, able to originate work or - something the Mirvishes are already starting to do - house transfers from our small independent houses, whose best productions too frequently die on the vine. (Those small theatres are almost uniformly, if understandably, ramshackle. It's notable that the dispute at the Factory, Toronto's preceding theatrical furore, was sparked by opposing schemes for renovation.) No plans have been announced for incorporating a new theatre in the King Street development, but David Mirvish has said that he's always willing to build a theatre if it's needed, and I imagine that a smaller one would fit that bill. But I spend a lot of my time in theatres, and I'm going to miss the comforts, creature and otherwise, of the Princess of Wales. Even critics have the right to agitate for an agreeable workplace.
