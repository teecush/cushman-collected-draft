THE PLOUGH AND THE STARS

Court House Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

Sean OCaseys status as a world-class playwright rests on his first three full-length plays, an informal Dublin trilogy written in the early 1920s and providing a worm's-eye view of the birth of modern Ireland.

The Plough and the Stars was the last to be written, following on The Shadow of a Gunman and Juno and the Paycock, but the earliest in chronological setting. It unfolds around and against the Easter Rising of 1916, the event that sparked off those Irish troubles" that have yet to exhaust themselves. The play's skeptical view of what had become a cherished patriotic moment caused riots at its first production in 1926, and led to its author spending the rest of his life in England.

OCasey called the play a tragedy, as he did its two predecessors. Everybody else, though, thinks of them as tragi- comedies, and its their humour, and the way it leavens and thus highlights the pain, that has kept them alive.

Neil Munro's Shaw Festival production of The Plough has some powerful moments, especially towards the end; but it is penitentially slow and morose. This earnest approach may be due to an understandable horror of the comic stage Irishman, but however honourable its motives, it has caused much of the play's appeal to leak away. Several of the cast seem to have their mouths full of cotton-wool, and the small quadrilateral of an acting-area proves unkind to a play thats a kind of off-centre epic.

All three Dublin plays show history impinging on a group of tenement dwellers who have more than enough to cope with already. Those in The Plough fall into three inter-connected groups. There are Jack Clitheroe, a former officer in the Irish Citizen Army (precursor of the IRA) and his young wife, Nora, who is pregnant and lives in justified terror of Jacks returning to the colours. There are Nora's uncle, Peter Flynn, and her cousin, the Young Covey, and the carpenter Fluther Good. Their main occupations are arguing and drinking, though Fluther, when we first meet him, has been three painful days on the wagon.

Finally, there are the older, husbandless women: Mrs. Gogan, who has a consumptive daughter, and Bessie Burgess, the only resident whose sympathies, about which she is relentlessly outspoken, are with the British. The First World War is, of course, in progress and Irish boys, Bessie's son among them, are fighting abroad in the trenches as well as here on the streets. The others run what might be called an apolitical gamut: Most of them would be Fenians if push came to shove, but they would rather it didnt.

OCasey's own attitude to the warring factions is to call down a curse on all their houses and a blessing on those trying to survive inside them. He himself was a lifelong Socialist, who abandoned the nationalists when they turned out to have no interest in economics. But he still reserves his sharpest satire for the character whose views most closely reflect, or at least caricature, his own.

This is the Young Covey, who carries around "Jenersky's Thesis on the Origin, Development an Consolidation of the Evolutionary Idea of th Proletariat, a title he pushes on all he meets, including a prostitute who is consolidating her own proletarian role by trying to pick him up.

This encounter occurs in the second of the play's four acts, which is set in a pub. Outside, an orator is delivering an authentic, blood-curdling speech of the period: When war comes to Ireland, she must welcome it as she would welcome the Angel of God!" (Patrick Galligan, looking sinister, plays his onstage doppelganger.) Inside, the characters are fighting their own wars: mostly verbal, though the two widows come to actual blows.

It's a tame contest, though. Wendy Thatchers Bessie has a gaunt presence that comes in useful later, but neither she nor her antagonist (an excessively lugubrious Kelli Fox) raises much dust. Neil Barclays publican seems forever to be halting brawls that haven't actually started.

In the third act, the rising happens: Offstage, the rebels occupy the post office and the British shell them. Onstage, the tenement people gleefully seize the chance of a little looting -- though not, in this production, gleefully enough.

They aren't the soaks and pretenders of OCaseys earlier plays; they hold down jobs and they display a real, if erratic, solidarity that cuts across barriers. Nevertheless, O'Casey, the most unselfconsciously Shakespearean of modern playwrights (with the blessing that he didn't write in verse), wrote them as clowns, with identifying routines that need to be played to the hilt.

They arent. Uncle Peter, as played by an unsuitably healthy Simon Bradbury, sinks without a trace. Hes meant to be a wizened old man with an excitable streak: He imagines that everything said by the Covey is intended to annoy him, whereas in fact only half of it is. Ben Carlson fires off a few effective rounds as his tormentor, but their comic relationship never happens.

Benedict Campbell comes off better as Fluther, and his silent sinking under the influence is a miniature tour-de-force; or, considering his weakness, a tour-de-faiblesse. But there should still be more gusto, more verbal energy, in a character who can tell the Covey, "It would take something more than a thing like you to flutther a feather o' Fluther. And so it goes for all of them.

O'Casey, who could write comic poetry, fell flat when he tried for the romantic kind, but here -- in the garishly coloured scenes between Jack and his little red- lipped Nora," as they both insist on calling her -- the productions restraint is an asset. Mike Shara and Fiona Byrne act with tact and discover passion; she even brings off a descent into madness at the end.

The last scene, showing the risings aftermath with the army clamping down, is altogether the best; we finally get to feel the pressure of outside events. I don't know if the Cockney Tommy is aiming for impersonal menace, or if he's simply having trouble with the accent, but the Dubliners scurry and rally admirably; and Fluthers decision, when evacuated for the night, that there would be nothing "derogatory" in playing cards in a Catholic church, is a lovely grace-note. Then theres a shocking climax, and an equally shocking release of the tension.

It was O'Casey's own climax as well: None of his later plays is as good. So at least it seems when you read them -- they are hardly ever staged. It might be interesting to try.
