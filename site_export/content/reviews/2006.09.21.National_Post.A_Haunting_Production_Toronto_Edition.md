GHOSTS

Tom Patterson Theatre, Stratford

- - -

If the shade of Henrik Ibsen ever casts a spectral glance in the direction of Southern Ontario, it should be feeling uncharacteristically cheerful. Each of our three major classical companies has, in the last two years, mounted a first-rate production of an Ibsen play. Following Soulpepper's scintillating, heart-rending Wild Duck and the Shaw's spring-cleaned Rosmersholm, we have Stratford's account, obviously less adventurous but equally powerful, of the one about syphilis.

Sorry, that's a very reductive way of describing Ghosts, and one that Ibsen scholars have been shooting down for at least a century. The play's ineradicable final image is of a young man's brain softening before us, while his tortured mother debates whether to administer the killing medicine. But the disease itself isn't mentioned until the play is two-thirds over, and even then, as a Stratford program-note reminds us, never by name. It's only the ultimate manifestation, the ultimate symbol, of the subject at which the play has been banging away all evening: the ills we inherit from the society, and the family, into which we are born.

Oswald Alving, a Norwegian artist who went off to live a free life in Paris, is felled by an illness passed on by his much- respected but thoroughly dissolute father, the Captain, in whose memory his widow is about to open an orphanage. This is a self- consciously ironic act on Mrs. Alving's part, since she was on to her husband long before his death. The orphanage is the last chapter in a cover-up story that included her taking in the Captain's illegitimate daughter as her maid. When we first meet her, Mrs. Alving is a strong, intelligent woman who has shed many illusions. In the few hours covered by the action of the play, she will be compelled to shed many more. She may well be the unluckiest tragic protagonist since Oedipus, that other enforced seeker of truth, and like Oedipus she is still alive at the end of the play, still condemned to the agonies of choice.

Ibsen's reluctance to call Oswald's malady by its name didn't spare him the maledictions of Victorian critics. The play's London notices from 1891 -- "an open drain; a loathsome sore unbandaged." - - have passed into legend: These jaded days, one rather envies those reviewers their capacity for shock and the vigour with which they expressed it. It wasn't just the VD that repelled them; the play has an incest theme as well, which didn't charm them, and to top everything off, it puts the outraged moral majority on stage in the person of Pastor Manders, who recoils in horror when he discovers Mrs. Alving reading "advanced books." Ibsen is as skittish about saying exactly what might be in these books as he is about calling syphilis by its name or giving us details about the late Captain's dissipated habits or the temptations that Oswald encountered in Paris. Once upon a recent time, this evasiveness seemed very irritating, and actors of the Pastor had their work cut out to keep him from appearing a mere buffoon, an Aunt Sally set up for us to feel superior to. But not any longer: Time has caught up with the play and Manders, with his condemnation of things he hasn't read and his blanket appeals to scriptural authority, not to mention his advocacy of abstinence for young people who can't afford to marry, is back in the saddle. The parallels between the STDs of Ibsen's age and our own hardly need underlining -- they're in danger, indeed, of seeming glib -- and the play ends with what is, in effect, a mimed debate about euthanasia, fought out within the anguished soul of a mother.

Diana Leblanc's production, her best since her celebrated Stratford grand slam with Long Day's Journey into Night and The Cherry Orchard, beautifully mines the play's twin veins of irony and inexorability; it unfolds on a constructively cluttered domestic set (by Charlotte Dean) and is suffused with the sound of piano music and the sight of distant firs. Mrs.. Alving is a wonderful role for Martha Henry, who appears grey-haired and in a lush purple dress, sense and sensuality in a context rigidly confined. Perhaps the most revealing thing the character says is that those "advanced books" had told her little that she had not already learned from experience; like many women, in and out of plays, she is instinctively ahead of a society that insists on keeping her behind. She is also both watchful and witty. For all her articulate passion, some of the finest moments in Henry's performance are the silent ones: her tolerant smile as she watches Manders make a fool of himself, the tightening of her knee as she passes the moral point of no return. She has spent her life surrounded by clowns and cheats; the play shows her shedding them, leaving her with only her son to cling to, and then facing the cruellest disillusion of all: Enlightenment doesn't come cheap.

Peter Donaldson gives the Pastor his full measure of unthinking authority, blinkered sincerity and, rather delightfully, gullibility. He also adds a note of affronted, repressed sexuality; Mrs. Alving once threw herself at him (and he is, believably, a man she could have loved) and now her maid, for quite other reasons, seems likely to do the same. He's thrown into confusion by both of them. Every so often there's a sign that his mind might open, and this becomes a rich part of the pattern.

Brian Hamman, a find, is a touchingly open-faced, bewildered Oswald, and Adrienne Gould gives a striking account of the maid Regina, especially in her last ruthless scene. Gary Reineke completes the cast as her adoptive father, the carpenter Engstrand, an unscrupulous Dickensian creep, with a sanctimonious talent for pressing the Pastor's buttons; the performance unfolds slowly (that's quite independent of the man's walking with a limp) and with possibly excessive relish, but it makes its points.

But what it doesn't suggest is that Engstrand may, somewhere, have a legitimate case of his own. The other characters all do, and it's part of the greatness of the play, faithfully reflected in this production, that though the author's own stance is hardly in doubt, he doesn't make anything easy.
