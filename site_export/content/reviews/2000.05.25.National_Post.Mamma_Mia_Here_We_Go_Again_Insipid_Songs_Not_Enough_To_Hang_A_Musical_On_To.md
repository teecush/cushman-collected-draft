MAMMA MIA!

Royal Alexandra Theatre, Toronto

The signature sound on the first night of Mamma Mia! came not, as is usual in a musical, at the end of each number but at the beginning. The score is drawn from Abba hits of the Seventies, and the audience greeted each one with the thunderous clap of recognition. But they didn't only applaud; they laughed.

They seemed endlessly tickled at the cheeky way in which free- standing songs had been shoe-horned into a story that in no way concerned them.

Count me out, twice. Abba's tunes (lets not even get into the lyrics) have always seemed to me like music to rot the teeth, when it isnt setting them on edge; and though songs like Money Money Money and Thank You for the Music actually sound better divested of the original group's saccharine harmonies, they still have no dramatic life. The show itself seems divided as to whether it wants to them to have any; the songs generally seem intended to forward the action, or at least reflect it; the ironic approach may well be more in the minds of the audience than in the intentions of the creators. But it's there, if only by default, and very irritating.

Theres good precedent for musicals in which the songs are irrelevant to the plot; the theatre of the Twenties and Thirties was full of them, if you believe the history books. More recently, Crazy for You, whatever its flaws, was a decently entertaining attempt to revive the tradition; the difference is that its songs, being by the Gershwins, had stage presence, while its book was a self-confessed piece of fluff.

The script for Mamma Mia!, however, has pretentions and sometimes even comes close to realizing them. It raises issues about marriage and parenthood, and it is has a 20-year-old heroine -- an incipient bride, name of Sophie -- who claims at the end to have learned something, though its unclear what. It also has an older heroine, Donna, the younger one's contrastingly unmarried mother; she is suddenly confronted with three men who played rapidly consecutive roles in her past, and any one of whom may be her daughter's dad. She is naturally vexed by all this, convincingly enough for us to want to hear her emotions explored, either dramatically or through a suitable song. What we dont want is to hear two of her old girlfriends launch into a chirpy cheer-up number whose lyric just happens to include a reference to a wedding, and at the end of which the narrative is picked up exactly where it was let drop. The two pals, who share with Donna a disco-singing past (they were Donna and the Dynamos) seem, in their present swacked and sexually ravenous states, to have lurched out of an episode of Absolutely Fabulous; as, come to think of it, does the central relationship between hip mother and square daughter.

Catherine Johnson, who wrote the book, is a tough and talented British playwright. She is slumming here (profitably, the show is a huge success in London), with second-hand characters and an arbitrary happy ending. Still, once past some stilted exposition (shamelessly employing a couple of characters we never see again) she manages efficiently to craft a story around 30 pre-existing songs and throws in a couple of good jokes.

The problem is not that her work isnt good enough for its surroundings but that it isn't bad enough. It's in competition with the score, and since decibels beat dialogue (which is why someone, out of desperation, invented the rock-opera) it inevitably loses. But it puts up a good enough fight for us -- well, some of us -- to resent the intrusion of each new number; which is a fair definition of musical-theatre hell.

The story is propelled by Sophies learning of her mothers three old flames and inviting them to the wedding. Each becomes happily convinced that he is the father, though -- since nobody suggests anything as unromantic as a blood test -- the matter has to remain permanently in doubt. One of them, however, gets Donna: again and for keeps.

The action takes place on an Aegean island, where Donna runs a taverna, and the situation is somewhat mythic, though perhaps less Greek myth than Italian: Variants of its triple-threat who's-your- father situation have turned up in the play Filumena, the film Buona Sera Mrs. Campbell, and an earlier musical, Carmelina, a flop with some lovely songs.

Interestingly, none of these versions has rushed to acknowledge a debt to any of the others; they too seem to want sole paternity.

So this is a Canadian production of a British musical with a Swedish score, set in Greece and with an Italian title. The British flavour -- a sort of low-down heartiness -- prevails in Phyllida Lloyd's production, but otherwise its remarkably un-ethnic. But that may be inevitable with this music; you have to hand it to Benny Andersson and Bjorn Ulvaeus, a couple of Scandinavians churning out songs that are blandly and effortlessly mid-Atlantic. Well, perhaps not effortlessly; I mean, has there ever been a dance hit as deadly static in its rhythms as Dancing Queen?

Anthony Van Laasts choreography doesn't move things around to much effect; it's cute the first time the chorus poke their heads over a parapet to join in a number, but not the dozen times they repeat it. Mark Thompsons sets are disappointingly picture-book pretty.

The performances, however, are good. Louise Pitre, silver-haired and real, gives a storming account of Donna, incisive when she talks, electric when she sings. She cant, though, fuse the music and drama, because the score doesn't. The power-ballads she gets at moments of crisis would be terrible even if they were relevant: All she can do is freeze and belt, on the fashionable principle that emotion equals noise: "Seeing me so tense/ no self-confidence" as one song puts it with typical lyrical finesse.

Rangy Mary Ellen Mahoney, repeating her man-eating routine from Crazy for You, and plump Gabrielle Jones, amusingly encroaching on it, are fun as her chums.

Tina Maddigan is sweet as Sophie, and Adam Brazier as her intended shows early signs of rising above juvenile-lead stereotype, though they aren't fulfilled. And everyone else is everyone else.

At the end, after the lovers have walked into a 360-degree sunset such as they presumably have in Greece, the show prolongs itself for a lame-suited reprise of most of the numbers: a pop concert in fact. This seemed to me to be, on its own terms, the most enjoyable part of the evening, and certainly the most honest. It has of course nothing to do with musical theatre.
