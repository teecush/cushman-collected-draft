The title of Lynn Nottages play Intimate Apparel refers to a series of elegant undergarments run up by Esther, a 35-year-old black seamstress in 1905 New York. Esther is gifted but unlettered and, she has come to believe, unloved and unlovable. However, she strikes up a correspondence with George, a Caribbean man working on the Panama Canal. Since she can neither read nor write her letters have to be ghosted, and George's read to her, by two of her best customers, one a white society woman and the other a black prostitute. George comes to New York, and the two pen-pals embark on what is, to all intents and purposes, an arranged marriage.

Well, at least they've arranged it themselves. Mr. Marks, an orthodox Jewish cloth merchant, is waiting for a bride, about whom he knows nothing, to arrive from Romania; meanwhile his beliefs forbid him to let another woman so much as touch him, which Esther, for all her apparent prudishness, would obviously like to. Meanwhile Esthers white client, Mrs. Van Buren, is trapped in a loveless marriage, while Mayme, her black client, has sex and nothing else. That leaves Mrs. Dickson, in whose house Esther and her sewing machine reside; she married late to a much older man who has left her a widow. This loquacious lady comes on like the leading lady in the black Broadway production of Hello Dolly!. Everyone in this play is childless; everyone acts as Esthers friend and confidant, with the notable exception-- at least, once they've actually met and the knot has been tied--of her mail-order spouse.

Nottage is a rising and much-decorated American playwright, and this has been her most successful play. It trades in patterns and parallels, rather like the plays and novels of the time and place that it depicts. Both Esther and George are the children of slaves; the other characters are in other acquired or inherited chains. The title too has ramifications; Esther's personal as well as professional relationships are based on the clothes she makes; these include an eye-catching smoking-jacket, made from silk bought from Mr. Marks, thats her wedding present to George. It plays a pivotal role in the subsequent action. Theres also a sense of style in the dialogue, along with some lines and situations that pierce the heart. There is also a weakness for coincidence in plotting, that stops bashfully short of full-blooded melodrama. The challenge, as the action roams, along with Esther herself, from one room to another on a compartmentalized set, is to avoid becoming diffuse and episodic. The play doesn't quite manage it, and the Obsidian Theatre Company's production cannot pull up the slack; in fact, it lets it drop even further

Philip Akins direction has its heart in the right place. The wed- ding-night scene of George and Esther is handled with notable delicacy, as a mixture of promise and shadow. But the productions pacing is off; most of the time it trundles, meaning not only that it's generally slow but that when it should pause, it doesn't. The scenes between Esther and Mayme include a couple of what should be heart-stopping revelations; here, both actresses just carry on without blinking an eye. The result is that all the performances, with the exception of Alex Poch-Goldins Mr. Marks, sometimes fall out of focus; Marium Carvells hearty Mrs. Dickson hardly ever falls into it. Individually, the actors are fine. As Mayme, whose boudoir includes a piano and who should by rights have been a musician, Lisa Berry is warm with flamboyant flashes of anger. As Mrs. Van Buren, dreading another visit to the opera and reaching out to Esther as her only link to life, Carly Street is glamorously cool with a rage that's subdued but that burns even hotter when released. It's good to see Street, condemned in Lord of the Rings to be lacklustre in a lacklustre role, back on form, "form being the operative word since Esthers custom-made corsets look especially good on her. The problems posed by mounting this play in this elongated space may be insuperable, and Tamara Marie Kucherans set-design fails to solve them, but her costumes are, as the script requires them to be, superb.

Kevin Hanchard's George is difficult to judge in the first half of the play, when he's still in Panama; hes in shadow, barely glimpsed, and an actor you cant see rapidly becomes an actor you don't hear. When he finally emerges, he paints a sharp portrait of goodwill giving way rapidly to frustration (New York is cold, especially when you're unemployed) and then to outright selfishness and irresponsibility: Just Like a Man, in fact. The decline, though, both of his character and of the marriage, is presented in predictable terms; and this rebounds on Raven Daudas Esther, who certainly manages to centre the play but whose transitions from Point A to Point B often leave us guessing, even though both points are fully present in their own right. Daudas Esther is full of incipient passion: a woman who is far from frigid but who has held herself in for so long that she doesn't know the code for letting go. The story is told almost entirely from her point of view (she's hardly off stage) so any blame that may attach to her is hard to register.

As for music: I wish the production had found something less obvious than Scott Joplins most famous tune to evoke the ragtime era; and though its delightful when Mayme tempts Esther into joining her in one of those bawdy blues about a handy man", their vocal style is surely anachronistic. It's a decent evening, one that holds out the frustrating promise of being more.

- Intimate Apparel runs through Feb. 3 at the Berkeley Street Theatre Upstairs, 416-368-3110.
