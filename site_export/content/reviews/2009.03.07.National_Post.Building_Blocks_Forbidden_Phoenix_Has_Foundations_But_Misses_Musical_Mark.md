Two shows for young audiences, both set in, from a Canadian perspective, exotic climes, both imaginatively staged, both with a message rather loosely attached. The Forbidden Phoenix, which comes to the Lorraine Kimsa Theatre for Young People, via the Citadel Theatre in Edmonton, is the more exotic of the two, perhaps surprisingly since much of its action depicts events that happened right here.

The inspiration of Marty Chan's play is the building of the Canadian Pacific railroad, with its heavy reliance on exploited immigrant labour from China. Chan filters this historical material through the traditions of China's classical theatre, in particular, the story of the Monkey King who, Chan tells us, "promised to find food for his tribe, and travelled west until he found a waterfall." Beyond this waterfall lies, surprisingly, Canada, and the railroad magnate Horne, performed here as a tyrant-figure from Chinese legend. It looks as though Sun Wokong, the Monkey King, has hardly left home at all.

This puts a dent in the playwright's declared purpose of writing a cautionary tale about racism and how to defeat it. There is no sense of differing cultures or even of differing ethnicities. It's true that Horne does occasionally speak in sneering tones of "cool-ees" (that's how he pronounces it) whom he delegates to do such suicidally dirty work as blasting open recalcitrant mountains with sticks of dynamite, but Sun Wokong is the only cool-ee we meet, and when he's not available Horne seems perfectly willing to sacrifice the lives of employees of his own race. Meanwhile, the civilization that the Monkey King has fled seems even more repressive than the one he's reached, ruled over as it is by a Dowager Empress, who grabs all produce and profits for herself and treats any opposition as blasphemy. Sun Wokong only goes west because she has banished him there, for hoarding rice to feed his son: a boy whom the Empress later kidnaps and whom she brainwashes into rejecting his father. This is foul but I can't see how it counts as racist.

The titular phoenix sits atop the hill that blocks the railway line, defying Horne's efforts to dislodge her and providing Sun Wokong with moral and sometimes physical support in his battle for justice. Her precise significance, though, baffled me, whatever it may have done for the young audiences for whom the show's intended. But they reacted enthusiastically to the look and sound of the show, though seemed lukewarm, as well they might, about its songs. Purportedly this is a musical, but the numbers (music by Robert Walsh, who also collaborated with Chan on the lyrics) do nothing to advance the story. The form entails freedom and fluidity, and Ron Jenkins's production, gorgeously designed by Leslie Frankish, brilliantly attaches this to the techniques of the Chinese opera. The conventions may muddy the show's politics, but they do great things in the imaginative sphere. Also in the personal: The relationship between Sun Wokong (Richard Lee) and his Monkey Son(Shannon Kook-Chun) is expounded in roughly equal proportions of dialogue, dance and acrobatics, with an emphasis on martial arts, and it's as moving as it's delightful. Michael Dufays and Lori Nancy Kalamanski are imposing villains, and the support-work is excellent.

Binti's Journey, revived by Theatre Direct as the opening production at its sparkling new premises in Wychwood Barns, is more single-mindedly set in Malawi. Adapted by Marcia Johnson from Deborah Ellis's novel The Heaven Shop, it presents a society living and, from what we see, coping with the omnipresent threat of AIDS. "Your father has AIDS" is almost the first line we hear, a common schoolyard taunt here directed at Binty, whom we meet as a child radio star and whom we follow, through her own narration, as her world and her family falls apart and is then, in a sort, reassembled. The heaven shop is the family business, and it makes coffins. Jajube Mandiela's Binti doesn't change much through these supposedly maturing experiences, but she's an appealing focal point. Among her colleagues, who double indomitably, Patrick Amporah is especially good as males ranging from Binti's brother to the uncle who takes her in when her parents die. He's an unsympathetic guardian, and Binti's life in his care becomes an updated Cinderella story. She later finds refuge with her grandmother, whose power and wisdom are signified by having her played by three actors speaking more or less in unison, a device that's impressive when it arrives and wearisome when it persists. The production, by ahdri zhina mandiela, works very well as a tale. It's a kind of love story. The play can be quite blunt about the brutal ways in which AIDS can be contracted, but at times it's in danger of treating it almost as a beneficent force. The perpetual dilemma for children's theatre is to be uplifting without being preachy or sentimental. Neither of these shows avoids the trap. Technically, though, they're on a high level.
