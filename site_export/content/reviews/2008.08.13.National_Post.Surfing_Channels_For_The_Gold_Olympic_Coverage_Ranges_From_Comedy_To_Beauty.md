Hearken to the words of American essayist Chuck Klosterman, from four years past: In order to enjoy the Olympics, you can't think critically about anything; you just have to root for America (or whatever country youre from) and assume that your feelings are inherently correct. It strikes me that every wrongheaded sentiment in this country derives from the culture of inherent, unconditional rightness. I agree.

Im not sure that the feelings Chuck describes are shared by every spectator of the Olympics -- if they were, viewers from many countries would surely have switched off by now --but they certainly seem to permeate the TV networks. CBC and NBC have both been as patriotic in their Beijing coverage as events have allowed them to be.

In Canada, this hasn't been much. Here I must quote Chuck again, admitting his own susceptibility to the general malaise: "I'm sure Ill unconsciously find myself not-so-secretly hoping that America wins every

event -- except for speed walking-- for some reason, I always hope Canada dominates speed walking." He also referred to the speed walking juggernaut that is Canada. That was then. No speed-walkers, Canadian or otherwise, have travelled across my screen so far this year. Weve had to be content with the whitewater kayaking. "We want to know where Canada stands," insisted Ron MacLean. Where we stood, it turned out, was with David Ford in 18th place. This didn't sound all that impressive, but they put the best face on it, even if he wasn't going to get any medals. Ill say he did well. I think anybody who trusts himself to one of those things in those currents and comes out alive is a hero.

The Slovenian contestant did very well, and we learned -- these games are proving very educational-- that his country is full of white water, just like Canada. Maybe that counts as transferred patriotism.

In some ways, our most consistent export to the Olympics has been MacLean himself. The U. S. anchors have been bumptious by comparison, very gung-ho for their own boys and girls. (Except in the commercials, which on NBC have been remarkably free of Olympic content. On CBC, every advertiser wants to get into the act. Rona gives the impression of having sponsored the entire games themselves.)

Both countries have to face the problem of what to do on-screen when there are no actual events going on. Canada has opted for comedy, which after all is one of the things at which we are supposed to have reached Olympic standards. "Every Western town has its Chinatown," said one funny fellow. Does Beijing have its Western town?

It turns out that Beijing does. At least, we were treated to an excruciating saloon routine, in which a Chinese cowboy instructed a Canadian tenderfoot in the finer details of Wild West-ern lingo and etiquette. "Thank you, CBC comedian," said Ron, obviously wishing that the selectors had been a little more rigorous.

NBCs equivalent is The Olympic Zone (or The O-Zone, though nobody could call it layered) which is a sporting version of Entertainment Tonight. This, too, attempts to embrace the local culture; we've had a crash course on chopsticks. For something more immediately relevant, we had A Day in the Life of Michael Phelps, described as "uncomplicated but utterly remarkable;" by his own account it goes eat, sleep, swim, though the footage made it look like "eat, sleep, swim, pat dog." The eating is gargantuan. But maybe he was having us on.

That said, what has usually greeted me when I've turned to the Olympic channels has been beauty. For some reason, Ive always seemed to coincide with the synchronized diving, where the contestants vary between those who are merely fantastic and those who are superhuman. Of course, some people have higher standards than I do. Ive watched perfectly matched pairs perform impossible feats, only to hear an expert female commentator (who "dove herself") telling me what they did wrong, and sometimes commiserating with the judges on not having her advantages (i. e., having to award points without seeing the athletes in slow-motion). When you do watch them in slo-mo, they're unearthly. Maybe the Chinese could devise a way of having them perform that way in the first place. They'd probably have the best shot at it, because the things theyve been doing in diving, swimming (where their female champions are said to be 16, and look younger) and in gymnastics -- the most exciting Olympic event to happen on dry land -- have defied belief. Maybe it has something to do with playing at home; it certainly has a lot to do with being trained, from an early age. And, as one impressed but skeptical lady implied when discussing the opening spectacle on BBC News, perhaps its the product of a regimented society.

From what I've seen of the Olympics, they hug their winners, especially the young ones, every bit as affectionately as we do; they do seem to shun those who lose. I wonder what the Chinese TV coverage is like. Apart from being -- I assume -- technically perfect.
