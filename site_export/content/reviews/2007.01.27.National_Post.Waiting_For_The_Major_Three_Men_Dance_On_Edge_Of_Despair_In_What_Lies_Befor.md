Morris Panych has written a historical play, though he has claimed not to be much interested in getting the facts right. On the strength of What Lies Before Us, I believe him; but I also believe that on this occasion his indifference has done him in.

He gives us three men marooned in the Rockies in the 1880s while doing surveying work on the Canadian Pacific Railroad. One of them appears to be English, another Scottish and the third is definitely Chinese. We are some way into the play before we get any precise information about why they're there, and it isn't that precise even then. I don't think the word "Canada" is spoken from one end of the evening to the other. The Englishman, an empire builder by temperament if not by ability, sometimes speaks rhapsodically, not to say deliriously, about what their project will do for the country when it's completed, but what actually lies before him and his companions is less glorious and less susceptible to factual verification. For the most part, we have a play about these three guys stuck in a tent.

It's a gloriously realistic tent, with its camp beds and its tin bath. Panych has not directed this play himself, but he has sent along his regular designer Ken MacDonald who, in collaboration with Robin Fisher, has done his usual great job. But what goes on inside it is unanchored behaviourism. The two Brits can't stand one another, what with Ambrose the Scot being an unhappy cynic and Keating the English chappie an optimistic idiot. They cannot, however, get away from one another. It is impossible while contemplating them not to be reminded, as in so many other plays, of the indissolubly united tramps in Waiting for Godot. They, too, are waiting for someone; in their case it's an army Major who is going to rescue them. The certain knowledge, gained from looking at the program, that the Major is not going to arrive is dispiriting in a way that the non-appearance of Godot never was. This play could really use another character. The ones we have are stick- figures, who bicker away at each other on the apparent principle that if they keep at it long enough, one of them will say something amusing. Occasionally one does -- this is Panych, after all -- but not enough to keep the entire first act from being painfully tedious.

The second is unexpectedly better. Ambrose -- Vladimir to Keating's Estragon -- has defied expectation by not only talking about getting away but actually making a serious attempt to do it. I don't think it's giving much away to say that he fails. After the intermission we find him afflicted with gangrene and Keating with, believe it or not, rabies. One faces amputation, the other insanity. We've spent the first act watching them dancing aimlessly on the edge of despair. We now see them sunk in the pit itself, and it's far more satisfying. Ambrose, always miserably self-aware, keeps a journal which he hopes others will find after his death: "I want the world to know the terrible things this country can do a man's writing style." So the play does have things to say about Canada, after all.

And while it may not have a Pozzo figure, it does have a Lucky: Wing, the Chinese cook who infuriates Keating by not being able to speak or understand a word of English. He is especially riled by Wing's inability to run him a bath, which he treats as a new phenomenon, though we're also given to understand that they've been there for ages, and the situation must surely have presented itself before. Beckett's Lucky, you'll remember, has a stunningly effective speech constructed entirely of gibberish. Panych's Wing has an equivalent aria, the last thing in the play, spoken in his native language, which, of course, for most of the audience, might as well be gibberish. It, too, is effective, even moving, quite possibly more than it would be if we did understand it. Wing has spent the entire play being insulted and abused, but the last words are his. This, I guess, is What Lies Before Us: Canada united and multicultural, with former hierarchies reversed. Wing is played by Wayne Sujo, who is working on this particular railroad for the second time; he was in the opera Iron Road, which played in Toronto a few years ago and really was an exciting piece of theatre.

What Lies Before Us really isn't. It's tempting to say that the play would seem better if its author had directed it but I'm not so sure. The current staging is Jim Millan's farewell production as artistic director of Crow's Theatre, a company that crested with the premiere more than a decade ago of Brad Fraser's Unidentified Human Remains and the True Nature of Love. I've often felt that Millan and Crow's have been living on Unidentified Human Remains ever since but I cannot see what they could have done here that they haven't. David Storch and Matthew MacFadzean certainly do their damnedest as the Anglophone duo; MacFadzean, in particular, as the manically insular Keating comes close to making a human being of a caricature. Keating is satirized as a walking compendium of received ideas, but he's one himself--a startlingly simplistic portrait from a writer of Panych's sophistication.

Cabaret-U-Mano is a puppet show, hailing from Montreal, in which the dolls dance to records, and their handlers move right along with them. It's at its best when the people and the puppets seem to merge; when, in effect, you cannot tell the dancers from the danced. Most of the accompanying music consists of camped-up pop classics, which are then camped ever further up by the show itself. The flavour is insistently faux erotic; a bony marionette doing a striptease isn't my idea of a turn-on but chacun a son gout. The puppeteers, some of whom are very clever, seem to be making fun of the puppets, which is a change after several centuries of the reverse.
