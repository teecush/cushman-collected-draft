NEW CANADIAN KID

Lorraine Kimsa Theatre for Young People, Toronto

The title of "gibberish coach" is a novelty among Toronto theatre credits, though we can probably all think of past productions that sounded as if they might have employed one. The job is serious business, though, in Dennis Foon's 20-year-old play, New Canadian Kid, now available in a sparkling new production at Torontos young peoples theatre.

Gibberish, in this play, is what comes out of the mouths of people whose actual language is English. English, meanwhile, issues from people who, in reality, can't speak a word of it. It's an aural equivalent of the reverse games that Peter Shaffer, in his Black Comedy, played with darkness and light. That play was a farce, and much of Foons piece is funny as well. But it has, this being the YPT (all right, the LKTYP), an instructive purpose. We are taken inside the heads of a boy named Nick and his mother, new immigrants to Canada from a place called Homeland. In particular, we follow Nicks progress in school.

Every child's first day at school is mystifying. Nick's is mystifying multiplied. Two classmates, a boy and a girl, seem well- disposed enough toward him and are certainly curious. They are revolted (read, surprised) by the aroma that comes from his lunchbox; he seems to be bumping up against a meaningless wall of sounds and symbols. Foon is considerate enough to throw us some entertaining clues as to what their words mean. Hockey, for example, is called, as far as I could catch it, schlamstick while "mapleleafo" seems a slamdunko for the Canadian flag. Nick, in one of the shows less convincing moments, seems uncertain whether to stand or sit while O Canada is being played, though surely after the first couple of seconds he would just imitate the other kids. He begins very soon to make connections; not unexpectedly he makes them far quicker than his mother. At first it was she who had to quiet his fears when he longed to go back home. Pretty soon the roles are reversed. There is also a father who never appears, since hes too busy making a living, though not, as we are carefully informed, in his old job as a teacher -- an example of theatrical economy overlapping with real-life economy.

Of Nick's two peers, the girl is called Mench while the boy is called Mug. What is it with those names? Anyway, they both live up to them. Mench acts like a mensch, as they used to say in my family, and becomes Nick's staunch friend, while Mug turns out to be a bully. He ends up scrawling the word sgak on Nicks wall. This may be a generalized insult or it may be an epithet reserved for Homelanders. The latter seems the likelier but the point, though danced around at length, is never very firmly made. Sean Baek, who plays Mug, is of visibly Asian descent, leaving you wondering whether he was cast simply because he was the best for the role (hes certainly good, sliding very believably from neutrality to aggression) or whether the production intends some comment on the attitudes of older immigrants toward newer ones. There is much to be said for what is usually called "colour-blind casting" but the phrase itself is ridiculous. It suggests that an audience can wilfully edit the visual information with which it is presented; and it can't.

Richard Harte, clad in various shades of green, makes a charming Nick, feeling his way through a minefield with great humour and delicacy. Claire Calnan as Mench has the same qualities in even greater abundance, and her role may be trickier because her character has more choices. She is especially touching, and especially sure-footed, when Nick brings her home to meet his increasingly isolationist mother, setting off what threatens to become an incipient culture clash.

Laurel Paetz's Mother is less convincing than her juniors, maybe because the play has been written from their point of view rather than hers. There is something dutiful about the writing of her role - - she remains the outsider in ways the author may not have intended - - and hence something forced about her playing. "Chillex," as they say in gibberish when they want someone to back off.

Leah Cherniak, in a theatre notorious for good intentions muddily transmitted, delivers a production that impresses immediately with its clean-edged elegance and subsequently with its wit and resource. Its mounted on a new thrust-stage formation that encourages economy and rewards concentration. Victoria Wallaces design, incorporating a revolve on the platform and a giant blackboard behind it, is neat, and Todd Charlton's sound score is terrific.

At a 60-minute running time, the play's conceit never becomes coy or its message strident. It ends on an honest note of qualified hope. The audience, mostly nearer eight than 108 (those are the stated parameters), were gratifyingly quiet while the show was on and gratifyingly noisy when it was over. The gibberish coach is Michael Kennard, of Mump and Smoot, thanks to whom this new profession begins its life at the top.
