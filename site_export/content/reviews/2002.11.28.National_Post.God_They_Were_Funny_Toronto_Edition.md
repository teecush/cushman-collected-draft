THE PLAY WHAT I WROTE

Wyndham's Theatre, London

DAMSELS IN DISTRESS

Duchess Theatre, London

- - -

I have seen Harry Potter live. At least I have seen Daniel Radcliffe, who plays him in the movies. Last Saturday night he was the guest star of a London stage production called The Play What I Wrote. He was very good.

You deserve an explanation. The Play What I Wrote features, and was written by, a pair of young British comedians named Sean Foley and Hamish McColl, known collectively as the Right Size. The show what they do is their tribute to an earlier, and frankly superior, English comic duo: Eric Morecambe and Ernie Wise.

To say that Morecambe and Wise were better than Foley and McColl is hardly an insult to the younger team. Morecambe and Wise were better than any double act since Laurel and Hardy. Their nearest Canadian equivalents would be Wayne and Shuster; both teams were national favourites whose TV shows made them national institutions. Both specialized in sketches that took off from familiar literary sources. In this line, W&S's "Wipe the Blood off My Toga" was a far more sustained and sophisticated effort than anything M&W ever attempted, but the British pair -- both, like so many great clowns, from the north of England -- had by far the more original and distinctive personalities. They were funny, so to speak, outside their material.

This was especially true of Eric Morecambe, a tall and well- padded man in shiny horn-rims, who was something of a genius. Ernie Wise, smaller and fairer and the straight man of the outfit, existed like all stooges to be tormented; in Ernie's case this meant being affectionately mocked by Eric for his "short, fat, hairy legs" and his literary ambitions. Every week Ernie, the most prolific of authors, would arrive bearing what he announced as "the play what I wrote." Every week, despite his partner's earnest attempts to stop it happening, Ernie's play would be put on. And Eric, generally cast as some variety of historical menial, would sabotage it.

Each play would contain a role for a visiting thespian -- Glenda Jackson, say, or Diana Rigg -- to whom Ernie would pay sycophantic court. Eric, appalled by this display, would stand on one side and exclaim, "You crawler" -- as subversive a comment on the celebrity culture as has ever been heard on network television. He would also disrupt the proceedings by sticking his head round the curtain and beaming. He might also, when subtler methods failed, slap his partner's face -- a ritualistic gesture that was surreal rather than violent -- or take the guest star by the throat. He once did this to Andre Previn. (When Andre questioned Eric's piano-playing abilities, Eric replied, in a sentence that passed into folklore: "I'm playing the right notes, sunshine, just not necessarily in the right order.") They were totally unsentimental, and totally unmalicious.

Morecambe died in 1984, Wise in 1999. In their heyday, the 1970s, more than half the population of the UK watched their BBC Christmas specials. Two generations of subsequent comedians have grown up in their shadow, among them Foley and McColl.

Apparently, when it was suggested to these two that they impersonate their great predecessors, they said the sensible thing - - i.e., no. It was then suggested that they do a show about two comics who were asked to play Morecambe and Wise and refused, and this they accepted. So their own theatrical entertainment mimics the shape of the classic TV shows.

Hamish is the Ernie figure -- an earnest amateur Shakespeare. Sean, the Eric equivalent, is bribed by a producer to pretend that Hamish is going to get his play on so that it can be turned at the last moment into the desired homage. A further twist is that the producer himself is a fake who is only looking for a chance to play his harmonica. When the show is at its height, there are three levels of ambition and dissimulation operating simultaneously. Or rather there are four, since Hamish, like Ernie, enlists a guest star to help out with his masterwork -- a different one at each performance.

The stage production trumps the original TV shows by keeping the guest's identity a secret until the moment of appearance. An actor who has filled this spot described it to me as "the biggest ego trip available"; the occupants have included such heavyweights as Ralph Fiennes and Simon Callow. (A running gag is that the man they're really after is Ian McKellen.)

This is the place that Master Radcliffe occupied on the night I went, and a smart, self-possessed kid is indeed just right for the job. The star's role is to take sides alternately with the stooge and the buffoon: to help humiliate one and be humiliated by the other.

The show -- directed somewhat unexpectedly by Kenneth Branagh, who must himself be on the guest-star short list -- has been a huge success; it is now on its second London run, and next year it will brave Broadway, though one can't help wondering how it will go down with an audience that doesn't get the references.

I myself spent the first, expository half wondering what all the fuss had been about, since the Right Size seemed to be the wrong fit. Morecambe and Wise were supernaturally natural performers, grand masters of the off-centre colloquial. (Eric, in particular, may have had the soul and grin of a zany, but he had the frame of a solid citizen. Both men were known for their sharply tailored suits.) McColl and Foley seemed, by contrast, cramped and monochromatic, though the third man, Toby Jones, was brilliant.

In the second act, though, they start to perform actual M&W material; within five minutes I was weeping with laughter and I hardly stopped until the final curtain. The superb original scriptwriter, Eddie Braben, has dusted off and augmented one of his superb original scripts, a French Revolution epic entitled "The Scarlet Pimple." Variety material -- stand-up, crosstalk, sketch and the occasional sitcom -- accounts for some of the best comic writing of the past century; work that, like all classic texts, stands up to new interpretation. McColl and Foley offer a loving comment on the prototypes, one that increasingly partakes of their glory and eventually becomes one with it.

The last scene shows Sean and Hamish lying blamelessly in bed together, as Eric and Ernie were wont to do onscreen. (Their TV shows depicted them as inhabiting a Platonic -- in all senses -- bachelor menage; in some ways Eric and Ernie anticipated Bert and Ernie.) A police car, siren blaring, tears by outside their window, and the two intone, in unison, one of the immortal duo's most famous lines: "He won't sell much ice cream going at that speed." They then turn toward one another and say, still in unison, "God, they were funny."

It's a perfect moment. Tears of laughter turn into tears of joy. And then the band strikes up, and the joy reaches sublimity level, as the boys go into their heroes' regular soft-shoe sign-off, a ditty called Give Me Sunshine. They recreate it perfectly -- a considerable achievement considering that Morecambe and Wise, who mastered all the skills of the halls before transcending them, were ace song-and-dance men.

There is more comic genius on show in London in Damsels in Distress, a sequence of three plays by Alan Ayckbourn, all set in riverside apartments in the city's newly fashionable Docklands. There are no continuing characters, but all the plays are performed by the same seven actors, who, for the first time in the author- director's long career, have been imported in bulk from his home base theatre in Scarborough, Yorkshire.

He has run this with imposing dedication for more than 30 years, while turning out double that number of plays -- an output as remarkable for its quality as its quantity.

The cast make a delightful ensemble, and though much of the fun of the project must lie in seeing each of them take on three widely varied roles, I found that the one piece I saw, Role Play, stood very well on its own. In fact, it ranks with Ayckbourn's best. What is most impressive about both play and performance is their ability to take characters over the edge of caricature and then somehow bring them back alive.

An engaged couple, believable and obviously at risk, are surrounded by grotesques (his drunken mother, her smugly philistine parents) and outsiders (the lap dancer from upstairs and her punch- drunk minder). The action is farce, with a couple of unfortunate gaps in credibility, but the people have real problems, and by the end even the most outrageous of them have been restored some measure of dignity.

Trilogies are in the London air this year, with Ayckbourn's trio matched by Tom Stoppard's epic political cartoon The Coast of Utopia. More on that to follow.
