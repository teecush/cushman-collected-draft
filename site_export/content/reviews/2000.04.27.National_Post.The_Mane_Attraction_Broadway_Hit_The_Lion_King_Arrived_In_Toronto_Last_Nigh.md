THE LION KING

Princess of Wales Theatre, Toronto

The beginning of The Lion King is, as everybody has said, wonderful. It shows animals assembling, African jungle animals. They come down the aisles; some of them appear in side-boxes. There is always a certain pleasure in seeing four-legged creatures impersonated by two-legged ones, but the ingenuity of Julie Taymors staging (not to mention her design of costumes, masks and puppets)is of a different order from any we have seen before.

Two humans jammed together to create one elephant is not perhaps so very surprising (not that it happens in many shows), but we aren't usually allowed to see the actors through the skin -- and the frank artifice makes the result even more persuasively elephantine. The big elephant has a baby elephant in tow; and this, of course, is entrancing.

Then there are giraffes, each heart-stoppingly represented by a single actor on stilts, and both the sight and the movement here really are extraordinary. All these creatures are rendered with a grace that one might normally compare to that of gazelles, especially, of course, the gazelles.

Grace (extending even to a rhino) and wonder and a touch of humour: Like all good things, it's over too soon, but it is, of course, reprised at the end. Taymor, who went on in Titus to show civilization as a jungle, here gives us the jungle as a paradigm of civility.

There is a song going on during this prelude, Circle of Life, but one hardly notices. (Were more aware of it at the end, when the circle is completed.) There is also a dramatic situation -- the animals are gathered to render homage to the newly born cub who is to grow into the title character -- but one hardly notices that either. As the evening proceeds, both the plot and the score of the show remain negligible but, unfortunately, less easy to ignore.

There have, however, been magical diversions en route. There has been a stampede of wildebeests, represented by puppets (plus a few masked mortals) of increasing size and menace, silhouetted against a doom-heavy sky. There have been journeys through the veldt, in which rapidly moving actresses with grass growing out of their heads play the scenery. There has been a drought, represented by a pool of blue cloth that shrivels to vanishing point. (Richard Hudsons set -- a very traditional bare arrangement of three parallel sets of wings -- is most notable for what is put inside it.)

There have been lionesses in mourning, a majestic sight, with white ribbons flowing from their eyes for tears. There has been some hypnotic dancing; Garth Fagan's choreography is at its best in a celebratory set-piece that kicks off the second act, and in which muscles ripple in time to the music.

There has been some authentic-sounding African music, hypnotically sung and chanted, and most of it composed by Lebo M. This part of the score, written for the show, sits oddly with the songs carried over from the Disney movie, which are the stalest white bread. An occasional flicker in Tim Rice's lyrics recalls his younger, funnier days, but they rapidly descend to the standard contemporary level of gush and uplift, in which abyss they find Elton Johns tunes all ready and waiting.

With them go the story, which concerns the assassination of Mufasa, king of the jungle, by his jealous brother Scar; Scars successful attempt to pin the blame on Mufasa's son, Simba, who flees into exile; and the return of Simba, grown into young adult lionhood, to reclaim his inheritance.

There is the ghost of a love story, between Simba and a decisive lioness called Nala (a paler version of the new-look heroine who started coming off the Disney assembly-line when the studio discovered feminism).

There is also comedy. Initially this is supplied by Zazu (Jeffrey Kuhn), a horn-billed bird who acts as the king's supercilious advisor, and seems to have slipped out of Gilbert and Sullivan. He fades out, to be displaced by a trio of hyenas, Scars henchmen, who are fairly engaging; and by Timon the meerkat (Jonathan Wilson) and Pumbaa the warthog (Mark Terene), who befriend Simba and arent engaging at all.

Timon and Pumbaa belong to an old cartoon tradition, and their routines -- one squeals, the other lumbers -- are of the kind that can work on the screen, even when the material is as terrible as it is here, because of the distancing effect; it's cute to hear animated drawings talking and singing like old-time vaudeville acts. Real live actors, even if they're dressed as animals -- especially if theyre dressed as animals -- dont have the same charm. (Much of the schtick seems like a retread of earlier, better Disney pictures, especially The Jungle Book. The cheer-up jingle Hakuna Matata is The Bare Necessities, much diluted.) The fact that Taymor has dressed these scenes with the same visual sophistication as the others -- these characters, too, are half puppet, half visible actor -- actually makes them worse.

The nearest thing to acting comes from Richard McMillan, who as Scar combines the elements of pantomime villain and neurotic Shakespearean usurper, topping the mixture with a pungent whiff of camp and enjoying a spectacular death-fall. He also manipulates his mask quite beautifully. Eugene Clark is a stolidly imposing monarch, Phinda Mitya an even more imposing and not at all stolid female- baboon-as-shaman.

The nominal leads -- Araya Mengesha and Steven Allerick as the young and older Simba, Georgina Johnson and Saskia Garel as the young and older Nala -- do what's required, though the kids seem as usual to be having more fun with it than the grown-ups.

For all the fanfares about the happy union of Taymor and Disney, they aren't really a match. The bad parts of the show don't merely detract from the good; they travesty them. I would gladly sit through the highlights again, though I think I would prefer to stand; at the back, so that I could slip out easily.
