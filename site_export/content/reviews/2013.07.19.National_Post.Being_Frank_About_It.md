Faith Healer Royal George Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake Enchanted April Festival Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake Brian Friel's Faith Healer consists of four lengthy monologues, only one of which contains any jokes. Its probably reprehensible of me to have taken against the play on such superficial grounds, but there it is. Still, of the three productions of it that Ive seen, the new one at the Shaw Festival is the best. It makes my vestigial respect for the piece somewhat less grudging.

The faith healer of the title is Francis Hardy, who tells us early on that his choice of profession may have been influenced by his sharing its initials. (This doesn't count as one of the jokes. Francis, a.k.a. Frank, doesn't go in much for humour. Mordant self-deprecation is as close as he gets, and he gets there often.) Irish by birth, hes mainly plied his trade in the village halls of Wales and Scotland but, with money and audiences drying up, he returns to the old country for what turns out to be a fateful gig in Ballybeg. His entourage consists of Teddy, his cockney manager, and Grace, his wife. At least she says shes his wife, and Teddy says so too. Frank himself prefers to call her his mistress.

This is the most glaring but probably the least consequential of the several discrepancies in the trio's recollection of events. Frank's on first, then Grace, then Teddy, then Frank again. It seems obvious that Teddy and Grace are telling the truth on the marriage question, while Franks version is a way of humiliating Grace while appearing to be quite the lad himself. Murkier are the precise circumstances in which Grace, unattended and in a remote Scottish village, gave birth to a stillborn child while Frank blithely absented himself. Most mysterious, a cliffhanger left hanging, is the course of events at the pub in Ballybeg, when Frank was asked to work his magic on a hopeless paraplegic, and to face some very threatening music from the mans friends if he failed to deliver.

The conflict of memories isn't nearly as compelling a theme as it's cracked up to be. The question that really fascinates is why Grace and Teddy stick with Frank over some 20 years; and the answer - that they love him - is no less powerful for being self-evident. Teddy, the ultimate small-time agent (God knows how hes survived on a percentage of Frank) ranks Frank with the great ones: from rob roy the Piping dog, most successful of his own former clients, to Sir Laurence Olivier. What they all had in common, says Teddy, was that none of them knew how they did what they did.

At Niagara this theory is tested by the presence as Frank of Jim Mezon, who really is a great actor. (I didnt realize this till about five years ago; others have known it longer.) At ground level, I'm sure that Mezon knows exactly what he's doing; but I can imagine that he, like all performers, might live in fear of the magic not working on a particular night, and equally of its working unaccountably and unrepeatably well on another. Even if he doesnt, he uses his physical bulk and vocal delicacy to tap unerringly into Franks twin veins of vanity and insecurity. He calls himself a mountebank; he also knows he has a gift, however unreliable, and he clings, eyes and voice glinting, to the memory of the one transcendent night when everything mysteriously clicked and he cured 10 out of 10.

In Craig Hall's production, this compulsive oscillation is illustrated by having Frank spend his first soliloquy setting out the chairs in Christina Poddubiuk's fine composite re-creation of all the draughty halls the act can ever have played. realistically, this makes no particular sense; Franks current address would seem to be limbo, and even when active he would never have condescended to be his own roadie; he would have left such drudgery to Grace and Teddy.

But it gives the actor something to do, and us something to look at; it also makes a fine contrast to the same rooms uncluttered aspect when Frank returns to complete his narrative. There's a wonderful image of Mezon standing upstage, his hat newly placed on his head and his back to us, before turning and advancing to confront and/or surrender to his demons. Frank tells us that he's in a state of unworldly suspension, and Mezon makes us believe it.

Grace, hugging the bottle while apparently re-enacting a therapy session, is another in Corinne Koslos fine line of wrenchingly devoted wives (or mistresses). As Teddy, Peter Krantz is handed two gifts: he has the only funny lines, and his gold smoking-jacket is the evenings one splash of colour; Bonnie Beecher's lighting, jabbingly stark elsewhere, brightens up to greet it. His performance, though, is so emotionally and rhythmically limited that his protestations, of caution or loyalty, amount to a series of exclamation marks. Teddy has been known to walk off with this play; this time it belongs, as it should, to Frank, an exemplar of star quality in a play that's about it.

If Faith Healer is excessively demanding but somewhat nourishing, its balanced by the Shaws other opening, Enchanted April, which is excessively undemanding and not nourishing at all. It's a Broadway-endorsed adaptation (by Matthew Barber) of the celebrated novel by Elizabeth von Arnim, which I haven't read.

Its first act is quite interesting; two young wives, frustrated by their lives in post First World War London and especially by their husbands, team up to rent a villa near Genoa, country of sunshine and wisteria for a month. (They dont tell their spouses about it until the day before their departure, which strikes me as behaviour more typically male than female.) They take two other ladies with them to share expenses: one a free-spirited young woman with a title and perhaps a past, or at least a present, the other an older, censorious widow. These scenes, especially the marital ones, set up some intriguing, even troubling, relationships. They are nt followed through. After intermission, we and the cast arrive in Italy to find the wisteria and sunshine present as advertised. Indeed, apart from minor problems with the plumbing and cooking, all is idyllic, which is nice for them but rather boring for us. A little matter of conjugal infidelity raises its head, but is swiftly swept under the rug, though the play doesn't seem to realize that there is a rug; it lurches in the direction of farce, and then lurches out again, a frustrating progress that Jackie Maxwell's production does nothing to iron out. Tara rosling provides some subtlety as the more reserved of the two central vactioners; Moya OConnell as proactive companion is certainly vivacious, but its hard not to sympathize with the character, who says that her high spirits "would make Pollyanna blush." Marla McLean smoulders in an underwritten role; donna Belleville, as the dragoness who predictably melts when someone offers her walnuts, gets the regulation laughs from the series of disparaging oneliners that seem to make up her role. Sharry Flett also gets laughs, though not from me, as a maid who demonstrates her ethnicity by periodically breaking into excerpts from Italian opera's greatest hits.

William Schmuck has designed a first-act set that reduces multiple London locations to a uniform drabness, and a contrastingly colourful second-act successor that provokes instant applause, even though, as they say in The Musical of Musicals, it hasn't done anything yet. What this second-rate confection is doing on the main stage of one of our two premier theatre festivals, I cannot say. It doesn't even have the excuse of being a musical.
