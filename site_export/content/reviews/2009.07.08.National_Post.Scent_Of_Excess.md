One of the callers whose comments appeared at the bottom of Much Music's screen during the Michael Jackson memorial was speculating on who would be the first on-stage mourner to break down in tears.

"My money's on Jennifer Hudson, God bless her" the message said. She (I have a feeling it was a she) would have lost that money because Ms. Hudson didn't break down, for which God may bless her even more. The first tears I noticed were shed by Brooke Shields, who may have earned them by making Mr. Jackson sound more like a human being than did any of her fellow celebrities. Their friendship, she said, stemmed from their both having been child-stars; he started when he was three but she, having made her debut at eleven months, was in the unique position of being able to accuse him of being a slacker. "We had contests" she said "to see who could make the other laugh more."

It was reassuring, in the midst of tributes that painted Mr. Jackson as everything from an icon to a martyr, to hear that he could laugh. She spoiled it a bit by revealing that his favourite song wasn't one of his own numbers but the old Charlie Chaplin tearjerker Smile ( "though your heart is breaking"), a choice that even I, a pre-rock person, find suspect. It was probably the sentiments rather than the music that he was reacting to. Marlon Jackson then proceeded to sing it, without changing my mind.

The event itself, considering what it might have been, was remarkably dignified. It struck its most moving note at the beginning, with the surviving Jackson brothers bearing the flower-draped coffin, and never noticeably descended from it thereafter. But the scent of excess could never really be dispelled, less from what happened inside the auditorium than from the reaction outside: sometimes hundreds (and probably thousands) of miles outside.

In the Toronto streets, a presenter gushed about "the most talented people on the planet, in the universe, paying tribute to the most talented person in the history of music." I think we can put the bit about the universe down to over-excitement, but it would be interesting to know when people started thinking it more impressive to talk about "the planet" rather than simply "the world." I guess it sounds more grandiose, even spiritual.

Even those who believe "the history of music" only dates back 50 years may have found that "most talented person" claim for Mr. Jackson a bit excessive. And if you do go back further: well, roll over not just Beethoven, but Bach, Mozart, Louis Armstrong, Frank Sinatra and one or two others. The rhetoric inside the hall could get overheated as well. Even Berry Gordy, the boss of Motown, having begun with a persuasive estimate of Mr. Jackson's on-stage metamorphosis from shy boy to "kill-or-be-killed" performer who "loved it all, every moment," escalated to saying "King of Pop wasn't big enough for him; he was the greatest entertainer that ever lived." This estimate, patently unprovable since there were centuries of entertainers of whom we have no record, provoked the first standing ovation of the occasion.

The less we remember history the more we feel compelled to invent it, and to inflate ourselves. Al Sharpton ( "Michael taught the world how to love") seemed to be claiming that his breaking of racial barriers made possible the acceptance of both Oprah and Obama, though even if we confine the roll-call to show business, Mr. Jackson was part of a continuum that included Armstrong, Duke Ellington, Ray Charles, Aretha Franklin and (his predecessor as song-and-dance man and infant prodigy) Sammy Davis Jr, all of whom, coming earlier, had more barriers to break.

The hype--a word that has come to mean merely "publicity" but is short, after all, for hyperbole -- has other progenitors. Does Stevie Wonder know that with his "we need Michael here with us, God must have needed him more" he was echoing the 1920s Tin Pan Alleyman who wrote "they needed a songbird in heaven so God took Caruso away?"

Probably it is only technology that makes today's grief-orgies seem more excessive than those of earlier periods. Sorrow swept America when George Gershwin died at 37, but it couldn't be transmitted as well. There needs to be a perception of youth and/or vulnerability; Sinatra, an immeasurably more distinctive singer, would have had to keel over in his bobbysox days to provoke comparable lamentation.

Mr. Jackson was technically middle-aged when he died, but his absence from the performing limelight, and his peculiar persona, kept him young in people's minds. Prince of Pop he may have been, but he had to vacate the throne to be proclaimed King. robert.cushman@ nationalpost.com

THEY SAID

"Ever since I was born, Daddy has been the best father you could ever imagine. And I just want to say I love him. So much."

--Paris, Jackson's 11-year-old daughter

"I want to say to Michael's children, there wasn't nothing strange about your daddy, it was strange what your daddy had to deal with. He dealt with it anyway. He dealt with it for us."

--Rev. Al Sharpton

"He was indeed a shining light like my father Martin [Luther King Jr.]"

--Bernice King

"God has now called for you to come home, collect your wings and to fulfill your demands in Heaven and continue your magic amongst the angels."

-- sister, La Toya Jackson

"With the abrupt absence of our treasure, though we are many, each of us is achingly alone, piercingly alone."

-- words written by poet Maya Angelou and read at his memorial

"How much pain can one take? Maybe now, Michael, they will leave you alone."

--brother, Marlon Jackson

"Somehow when Michael Jackson sang, when he danced, we never felt distant. Michael was the biggest star on Earth."

--Queen Latifah

THEY SAID

"All over the world today people are gathered in love vigils to celebrate the life of a man that taught the world how to love."

--Rev. Al Sharpton

"We understand laws, and we know that people are innocent until proven otherwise. That is what the constitution stands for."

--Sheila Jackson Lee, Texas Congresswoman

"On June 25th, because he was the best, I believe Heaven and Earth did pause indeed to say of Michael Joseph Jackson, 'Here lived a great entertainer who did his job well.' "

--Martin Luther King III

"Michael Jackson went into orbit and never came down. Though it ended way too soon, Michael's life was beautiful. Sure there was some sad times and maybe some questionable decisions on his part, but Michael Jackson accomplished everything he dreamed of."

--Berry Gordy, founder of Motown Records.

"Michael saw everything with his heart."

--Brooke Shields
