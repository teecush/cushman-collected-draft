JOLSON

Princess of Wales Theatre, Toronto

I greatly enjoyed Jolson when it played in Toronto two years ago. A biographical fantasia, it was really just a cunning and elaborate excuse to revive a bunch of old songs, but it was done with exceptional sharpness and vivacity. I wasn't expecting a second visit to reveal any previously hidden depths, but I would have been happy to revel on the surface again. What we have now, though, turns out to be a different and much inferior show.

Most obviously we are denied any indication Al Jolson, the song- and-dance man still ritually described as "the world's greatest entertainer," spent almost his entire career performing in blackface. It's obviously a ticklish matter, and the original production found a witty and ingenious method of dealing with it. We saw a scene from a terrible musical of the 1920s or earlier, with Jolson playing a stereotypical funny servant scoring points off julep-sippin' white folks. At a certain point he stepped out of character, dismissed the other actors, and told the audience that rather than subject them to the rest of the plot he would just sing for the rest of the night. This is truly the kind of thing Jolson did, although he never took off the burnt-cork to do it. The show managed to elide his (real) desire for the solo spot with his (fictitious) discomfort at blacking-up, and by this sleight-of-hand honour was satisfied, history acknowledged, and blushes spared.

Even this seems to have been too much for the U.S. audiences at whom this touring version is aimed. (Political correctness now seems thoroughly reactionary, the politics of the apolitical: You can ignore real-life abuses as long as you act properly shocked by words and images on stage or page.)

We see Jolson in his dressing-room applying a touch of black and immediately wiping it off; we hear him complaining about playing "Gus the butler" without being given any idea of who Gus the butler was. This has ramifications. It means that Mike Burstyn, playing Jolson, has nothing to act. By the time we get to intermission Al has divorced one wife and married another woman, gone solo on Broadway and made the first talkie, but it still feels as though nothing has happened.

The show's authors (Francis Essex and Rob Bettinson) are English, and Jolson in its origins is a very English show. I mean that, for once, as a compliment. The British have a huge sentimental appetite for mainstream U.S. showbiz. The British approach can be bland and get the idioms wrong, but it cares and sometimes it works. It did with Jolson, first time round, thanks to a central performance by Brian Conley that transcended impersonation. It was cavalier with details but totally commanding: a star turn in its own right.

The most cunning device of the show as written is that, for most of its length, it is sparing with the famous songs and unleashes most only at the end, when we are supposedly present at Jolson's last one-man show. When Conley played it, I actually believed by this point that he was Jolson. I even found myself believing in his dresser, the most English of all the performances and charmingly camp, who hung around most of the evening singing snatches of The Spaniard Who Blighted My Life and was finally dragged on stage to perform it with the maestro. In this version, the character, the number, and the set-up all go for nothing.

The show's second act shows us Jolson during the war, when he exhausted himself performing for the troops, and, after it, when he became hot again after Hollywood filmed his life, using his voice and a younger actor's body. This does work up a certain amount of steam, although we do seem to keep watching the same scene -- one in which Jolie's comparatively estranged wife and his long-suffering manager try to talk sense into him. The wife, who is supposed to be Ruby Keeler, has the evening's most resonant line when she tells him that the movie will present his life "not as it was, but as you want it to be," which is a charming acknowledgement of the show's own strategy. Heather Mazur plays her as a mechanically efficient good- hearted belter, a falsification a lot less forgivable this time around that it did before.

Burstyn's Jolson, meanwhile, is far closer to the original's vocal mannerisms, and to his unglamorous looks, than Conley was, but in his hands the show becomes a nostalgia-package. A good actor, he works hard at the character's egomania, but we never feel it; he seems a nice guy who boasts. To be honest, this show needs blackface; if you're going to impersonate, go all the way. Down on one knee without it, he looks incomplete.

Harry A. Winter plays the agent wearily and well. The young Gershwin who turns up in Jolson's dressing-room to audition Swanee ("don't give up the day-job, George" advises Al) looks amazingly convincing. Bill Castellino's direction is otherwise unremarkable, and Broadway choreographer Joey McKneely is in mundane form. The sets (James Fouchard) remain glittery, and of course there are the songs. One -- Harry Warren's About a Quarter to Nine -- is superb, and the others corny, well-crafted, and infallible.
