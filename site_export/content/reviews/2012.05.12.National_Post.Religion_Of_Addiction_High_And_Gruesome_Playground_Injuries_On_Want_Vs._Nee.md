In High, Kathleen Turner commands the stage with square shoulders and a hoarse but powerful voice suggestive of a heavy cold. She also has canny, forthright comic timing, which though the play is far from being a comedy, comes in very handy, given that her role is that of a nun who swears a lot. Her prime antagonist, a 19-year-old gay drug addict who would seem to have seen and heard it all, doesn't like her doing this. He says it creeps him out. I agree, though probably for different reasons.

I thought the language an easy shock effect, though I admit that its laugh value declines steeply as it gets repeated, and that it does have traceable roots in the characters early life. Turner plays Sister Jamison Connelly, who works in a Catholic rehab institution where shes acknowledged to have an exceptional record of turning difficult patients around. She meets her greatest challenge yet in young Cody who has been on just about every known drug; who has been found with the dead body of his 14-year-old boyfriend; and whose dead mother was both a user and a prostitute. The sister is reluctant to take the case, but is pressured into it by her priestly superior, who has a special interest in the boy. And not the one you might pruriently have suspected.

The play's author, Matthew Lombardo, is Catholic and gay and a recovering addict, though judging from his autobiographical program note, he comes from a background more settled than Cody's and has had, fingers crossed, a more fortunate trajectory. In fact, he has probably put more of himself into the character of the nun, who is a former street person - hence the salty vocabulary - and, though she isnt very explicit about it, has had first-hand experience of dope, drinks and sex. She was saved by faith, but is all too aware of the possibility of relapse, spiritual and physical. She has a particularly hideous secret in her past, which she helpfully reveals to us in the second act, when the story needs some pepping up. She is the only one of the plays characters who's allowed to have monologues, which seems unfair, especially as they aren't as revealing as theyre obviously intended to be.

The play does hit nerves on the subject of addiction, but its less persuasive when offering religion as an antidote. I don't deny the possibility; I just don't think its been effectively dramatized. To his credit, the author avoids a facile redemptive ending, at least on the level of plot. He does, though, go for another kind of easy out: Like Doubt, that other pseudo-serious play about a nun, this ends with the central character repeating the title word - here signifying both a trip and the empyrean - as if, all by itself, it meant something.

Turner, who comes out swinging and goes right on that way, gives a compelling if unvaried performance, and she has two good supports. Evan Jonigkeits Cody goes through the required motions - bedraggled, defiant, despondent, despairing - without transcending a stereotype that goes back at least as far as Equus. Father Michael's personal conflicts are potentially the most interesting in the play, but during most of it he's just a plot device. Tim Altmeyers best moments come at the end, when he gets to appear with his cassock off; the character comes out from under wraps and so does the actor. Though he has a neat line at the start, when Sister Connelly asks why hes guilt-tripping her and he replies, "I'm a Catholic priest."

High is the kind of quasi controversial play that can make it to Broadway (at least if there's a star involved). Gruesome Playground Injuries belongs in the growing list of imaginative, sometimes remarkable, American pieces that, commercially at least, stay in the margins. Like High, its about addiction, though not of the chemical kind. Also like High, it has characters who are Catholic, though by upbringing rather than conviction and certainly not by vocation. And both plays involve an offstage funeral scene in which people keep telling someone who doesnt actually miss the departed that they're sorry for his or her loss. High holds the attention; Gruesome Playground Injuries rivets it.

What the two people in Rajiv Joseph's play are addicted to is pain: their own and one anothers. We first meet them in the nurses room at school when they're both aged eight. Doug, inspired by Evel Knievel, has ridden his bike off the roof and bloodied his head. Kayleen throws up; she often does. This sets a pattern for their subsequent behaviour. We last see them, 30 years later, at an ice rink: a perfect place for Doug to hurt himself again, though by now he isn't up to it. In between weve witnessed other stages of their relationship, though not in chronological order. Each time one of them is in some kind of hospital. Generally its Doug, who seems perversely to enjoy being accident-prone. But it can be Kayleen, whose psychological wounds go deeper. Doug at one point falls into a coma; Kayleen seems constantly to be on the verge of one. Doug comes from a loving family; Kayleen from very much the reverse. They stay away from one another for years on end, but each returns when the other is most in need. They have, it seems, the power to kiss one another better: sometimes literally. Indeed they share a first kiss; Doug, who wants it, suggests it's practice for their real first kiss, though Kayleen, who's resistant, is quick to point out the logical fallacy. Whether their physical relationship ever went any further is something the play leaves in doubt; over the years they both acquire and shed other partners, with jealousy resulting. Whats clear is that this, in its bizarre way, is the finest and most touching love story our theatre has told in years.

Peter Mooney and Janet Porter give performances that manage to seem both disciplined and spontaneous; Porter may have the edge, if only because Kayleen is the one who has edge. Doug, whatever his problems, remains the good-natured goof; Kayleen is allowed anger and introspection. Shes also the one whose ages and stages are more clearly differentiated; perhaps girls really do mature more than boys.

This play, too, comes with a program note: an impenetrable one by director Stefan Dzeparoski that seems to be about urban blight and the human body. Well, the second part is relevant. His actual production is admirably clear; the stage, a wide open space, is adorned with bubble-wrapped torsos, dummies of mummies, a couple of which fall to the ground with a thud at the start of things, to put us in the mood. The detritus of future scenes lies all around, ready for use; at the back there's a wheelchair, which, one accurately feels, does not bode well. Don't be discouraged; this, on occasion, a graphic play, is also a tender one. It's presented by BirdLand Theatre whose track record, though marred by a few grievously injurious missteps, continues outstanding.
