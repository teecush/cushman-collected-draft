THEATRE REVIEW

Vimy Young Centre, Toronto

Four Canadian soldiers, wounded with various degrees of severity in the attack on Vimy Ridge, are in a field hospital, haunted by memories of the battle and also of their lives back home before they enlisted. Two of them are anglo, one French and one Indigenous; they represent Manitoba, Alberta, Quebec and Ontario. Attending them is a nurse who completes the pattern by hailing from Nova Scotia, and is also haunted by her own memories: of her fiance who initially resisted joining up but eventually became a casualty of the same battle.

Vern Thiessens play, Vimy, like its characters, has been all over Canada but Soulpepper's production, timed for obvious commemorative reasons, is its Toronto premiere.

Diana Leblanc's direction is visually very impressive, with a set by Astrid Janson and lighting by Andre du Toit that splendidly evoke both the terrors of the charactersrecent past and the makeshift conditions of their convalescent present. The production is also a fine display of ensemble acting; as plays about groups of people thrown together in tight situations tend to be.

One by one, the performances become less effective.

The four patients (Sebastien Bertrand, Tim Dowler-Coltman, Wesley French and TJ Riley) show little individuality, and when some of them have to double in flashbacks as people from the otherspasts, things just get confusing.

Characters in uniform do tend to come out uniform, and as most of the cast are new to Soulpepper, and fairly new to Toronto theatre, we can't import our memories of past performances to help tell them apart. Christine Horne, as both a familiar face and the only woman, does stand out, though her betrothed (Andrew Chown) comes to life, ironically, only when he becomes part of the carnage.

The hospital scenes seem mainly to be an excuse to number off some representative national types.

Things only get exciting in the second half (the play was originally in two acts but is done here without an intermission) which finds ingenious ways of dramatizing the battle itself.

First we see each man instructed in his division's role for the attack. Then we see each in turn doing his best to carry the instructions out, somewhat terrified, somewhat exultant, each amazed at his own survival, each in turn laid low. Even now, though, we aren't emotionally invested; we care for them only as figures in a pattern. The most effective moments come straight out of Oh What a Lovely War; twice the men join in a weary chorus of "here we are, here we are, here we are again." The song itself is one that Lovely War forgot but the effect -part cynical, part uplifting - is the same. The play, by the author's own admission, makes no attempt to assess whether, as legend has it, Canadian identity was really forged at Vimy. It merely tells us, once again, that war is hell. To its credit, it makes us feel it.

Vimy runs until Aug. 5.
