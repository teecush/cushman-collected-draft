Lady Augusta Gregory was a natural-born playwright. That's the revelation of this year's Shaw Festival lunchtime show, a double bill of her plays (she wrote short) under the umbrella title of The Kiltartan Comedies. Kiltartan, Scottish though it sounds, is in Galway, Ireland, and was the country seat of the lady's husband, a member of parliament and former governor of Ceylon. She figures in text-books as the co-founder, with W.B. Yeats, of Dublin's Abbey Theatre, the house that, beginning in 1904, made modern Irish drama possible. The books also say that she contributed importantly to that drama herself, but we've seldom had the chance to judge for ourselves on stage. Our loss; but now, our gain.

Of course, she wrote rich dialogue; she was Irish, wasn't she? (Anglo-Irish actually. And Protestant. And conflicted.) Unlike some of her compatriots, though, she didn't let her pen or her characters' tongues run away with her. She had a firm narrative sense and also a spatial one, too. She wrote pictures as well as words. The abiding image from The Rising of the Moon, the first and stronger of the Niagara duo, is of two men sitting back to back on top of a barrel, to which a reward notice has been posted. One of them is a police sergeant, nervously watching the coastline for a Home Rule agitator -- we are in the early, and comparatively benign, days of the Troubles -- thought to be very dangerous. The other is a ragged ballad-vendor whose presence makes the sergeant even more uneasy, but whose absence might be worse. It's obvious to the audience that the balladeer is in fact the wanted man, and it is eventually made clear to the sergeant as well. The question becomes, will the policeman let the man go or turn him in and claim the 100 bounty --equal, the program tells us, to $18,000 of today's money? On the one hand there is the cash, plus his professional duty. On the other, since, of course, he

himself is Irish, there is the memory, craftily played upon by his musical antagonist, of the yearning patriotic songs the sergeant heard and sung in his youth, and of all they stand for. He makes his choice, but the play's last line finds him still exquisitely uncertain of whether it was the right one.

Douglas E. Hughes delivers this character beautifully, crowning a delightful performance of a man beset by doubts and fears, trying to be inflexible. Patrick Mc-Manus confirms the promise held out by his Dunois in Saint Joan of being a first-rate romantic actor, whimsical and dangerous. He also sings like a bird. Harry Judge and Andrew Bunker complete the cast as a couple of raw, embarrassingly conscientious constables. The comedy in the play helps build the suspense, allowing Micheline Chevrier's production to be both tense and relaxed

Spreading the News, the second play, is a more conventional rural comedy, about the multiplying power of rumour. A minor altercation at a fair grows, by the time the story has been told at second-and third-and fourth-hand, into a full-blown case of murder with an agricultural implement. Not even the reappearance of the supposedly dead man can quite halt the villagers' suspicions; they believe in ghosts. The play covers some of the same ground as one of the Abbey's most famous products, The Playboy of the Western World, and it's hard not to feel that it does it far more economically. You may get a little tired of a stage full of simpletons, no matter how gloriously they talk or how wildly they fantasize, but this play, too, has a political sting. Its authority figure is an English magistrate (Douglas Hughes again, in contrastingly complacent mode) confident that he can untie the local Gordian knot with the same unflinching methods he used in other colonies. We end with the prospect of the imaginary opponents about to be consigned to the same real holding cell, there to beat the real daylights out of one another. And, of course, rhapsodizing about it. A ripe and ready team of locals include Mary Haney as an apple seller whose deafness is part of the problem and nothing of the solution, Guy Bannerman looking forward with some reason to another unlucky day, and Tara Rosling as his formidable wife. (She looks more like his daughter and acts more like his mother, which may be the point; these men are babies, whatever the chronology.) Maybe the Shaw could offer us another double bill, combining one of Lady Gregory's plays with one by her professional partner, Yeats, whose dramatic works are, because of the poet's non-dramatic prestige, better regarded and more often revived.
