The Rediscovery of Sex runs to May 28 at Theatre Passe Muraille Mainspace, 16 Ryerson Ave., Tuesday to Saturday at 8 p.m. Pay what you can Sunday matinees at 2:30 p.m. $10 - $28. Call (416) 504- 7529.

Unless interactive drama is your thing, the only sexual activity that the theatre can satisfactorily present is voyeurism. It can, of course, talk about all the other activities, it can simulate or even literally reproduce them, but it still leaves the audience in the position of watching other people at it. This is what has undone all the ambitious sex-shows -- or revues on sexual themes -- from Oh! Calcutta! down to this week's Toronto opening, The Rediscovery of Sex. No matter how many bases they try to cover, they get monotonous.

Oh! Calcutta! was very much into making us feel happy and liberated. Really, it preached; it took, as you must have known I was going to say, a missionary position. It said that sex was good, and even fun. The approach taken by The Rediscovery is more anthropological. It probably also thinks, somewhere inside itself, that sex is fun, but -- depending on your viewpoint -- it's either too cool or too square to say so.

It's a long-delayed sequel to I Love You, Baby Blue, a pioneering work mounted 25 years ago by the same director, Paul Thompson, and a show that briefly led to its cast and crew being locked up. It took off, in its turn, from some erotic movies that were being shown at the time on Citytv. Hence, rather belatedly, the present show's opening number retells some urban history in Old Testament language: "In the beginning, God created Toronto ... there was Bloor and Yonge, and it was good" -- that kind of thing. Arch Biblical spoof is not exactly a new device, but it has a sort of purpose: It's designed to set up a gag about Moses, a media tycoon who, like his celebrated namesake, led his people to the promised land, but couldn't stick around to escort them inside.

The style also signals that the show recognizes its responsibility, as a revue, to be at least intermittently funny. It doesn't, as it turns out, do this very well (for some reason, people who compile shows about sex always seem to know fewer good jokes on the subject than anybody else), and really its heart isn't in it. The best numbers here are a few that are outright sombre, as opposed to the many others that are just skittishly cynical. Six young people, in rapidly changing states of dress and undress, take us through the streets, bedrooms and public playing areas of the city, and return with the news that anything can be said, most things can be done, but nothing is being enjoyed. Whether it really means to say this, or just can't find ways of saying anything else, is anyone's guess.

Early on we meet a trench-coated detective who has been down our streets and has found them mean. He harangues us for our prurient motives in attending such a performance, compares our civilization to Sodom and Gomorrah, and generally hankers for some good old repression, and lots of it. He's a figure of fun, but the odd thing is that, as it develops, the show seems to agree with him. There are one or two other recurring characters, but it's hard to keep track of them, and you find yourself wondering if you have met this person before or if the authors have merely run out of names.

There is some kind of progression. The show begins by concentrating on heterosexual relationships, mainly between desperately lonely men and disdainfully lonely women. It gets steadily gayer, but no happier. The mood, in fact, gets darker, or maybe it's just that the background music gets heavier. (There are no musical numbers, which in any revue that doesn't happen to be Beyond the Fringe is a mistake.) Along the way, there's a fairly amusing item about a primly virginal researcher doing a thesis on porn, driving her male co-worker crazy as they watch videos together, and taking umbrage when he resists her disinterested attempts to give him a hand job.

There's a reasonably accomplished sketch depicting swingers who displease or embarrass their regular others by their choice of irregular ones. I forget where it happens, but there's a surprisingly successful pun on that well-worn word "hard." There's much mockery of brittle, driven types who never disconnect their cellphones and who dine at North 44. (The show seems to have a fixation on this restaurant, though I doubt that there's a promotional tie-in.) It's strongly implied that the real trouble with sex today is that yuppies do it, or at least would like to do it, or at the very least think that they ought to want to do it. They are, of course, obvious targets, but then the revue form thrives on obvious targets. It doesn't have time for anything else. That's why it needs music and humour. They're necessary lubricants, and they can also -- music especially -- be passports to emotion, which is as absent from this show about sex as it would be from a government report.

Bouncing cartoon-like from one subject to another, a revue can hardly explore feeling, but this one hardly suggests it. Almost inevitably, it ends up presenting sex as a mirthlessly absurd activity. I remember hearing the word "love" just once in the evening, and it was spoken by a rejected husband. It isn't sentimental to remark that people having sex, even in Toronto, even in the year 2000, quite frequently say, "I love you." What's more, they often mean it, even if sometimes they're deceiving themselves and/or their partners and harbouring baser thoughts at the same time: That, in fact, is comic and dramatic in itself. Oh, and sex has been known to be the cause of children; that isn't mentioned either. The piece, in fact, leaves a lot out; and isn't too illuminating on what it leaves in. It isn't unbearable, though it's much too long; the production is smooth, and although the performances vary from very good to very bad, the actors are all at least personable. Everybody seeing the show is likely to be faintly aroused at some point, although it probably doesn't see itself as an aphrodisiac. If anything, it's despairingly puritanical, or perhaps hedonistically disillusioned. It takes the received wisdom that sex is being destroyed for us by stress and disease, and does nothing to challenge it. Nobody is going to get arrested this time, and it's good that the show can happen. That doesn't make the happening fun.
