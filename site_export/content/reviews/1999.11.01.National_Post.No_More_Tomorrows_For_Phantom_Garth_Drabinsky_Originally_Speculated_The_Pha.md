Last night, in the lobbies of the Pantages Theatre, they were selling souvenir programs and other memorabilia like there was no tomorrow. Which, in a sense, there wasn't. After 10 years, and on Halloween appropriately enough, the Toronto production of Andrew Lloyd Webber's The Phantom of the Opera came to a close.

After the curtain came down, the world repaired to a masquerade ball and charitable silent auction at the Windsor Arms Hotel.

This was perfect. The show itself includes a stunning-looking masquerade, and it begins with an auction, though not a silent one. Some of the same artifacts were on sale at both the real auction and the staged one. What was being commemorated was the end of a theatrical, maybe even a civic, era.

"There was more life in it," said Garth Drabinsky, who produced the show in Toronto and kept a tight grip on it for as long as he remained at the head of the now- extinct Livent empire.

"My plans ... would have kept it running for at least another year. But with no resources to market it, you lose momentum."

It doesn't mean much to call Phantom's run record-breaking, because there were no records to break. Nobody had tried to run a big musical indefinitely before; the two shows that have rivalled its longevity in London and New York -- Cats and Les Miserables -- have appeared in Toronto as recurrent attractions rather than permanent ones.

The Lion King, the only more recent show that might seem to have the same commercial legs, opens here next year; Disney produced it, the Mirvishes -- who have always seemed averse to unlimited runs -- are housing it, and what their long-term plans are nobody knows.

Mr. Drabinsky initially thought that Phantom would last three years, but when he told that to Mr. Lloyd Webber and to the press, "they thought I was nuts."

From the start, the selling of the play was unprecedentedly aggressive.

"We did over 100 radio ads. In terms of print ads, we had 400 different looks," Mr. Drabinsky said.

The radio ads, all of them built around the menacingly inviting Phantom voice as recorded by the late Stratford Festival actor Graeme Campbell, became part of Toronto's culture.

And not just of Toronto's.

Research commissioned by Livent a year into the run resulted in the show being marketed in Detroit, Cleveland, Pittsburgh, Buffalo, Rochester. The results, according to Mr. Drabinsky, were "astonishing," and put Toronto on the map as a theatrical tourist centre.

The effect on the surrounding economy has been incalculable. People came here to see Phantom rather than going to New York. And they stayed to eat, sleep, shop and even see other shows.

Mr. Drabinsky believes Phantom would have picked up business from next year's wave of tourists coming to see The Lion King. It would have had to weather a traditionally bleak January and February to get there, but then it always has. And then, Mr. Drabinsky said, the "Disney factor" would have kicked in; Disney rereleases their classic movies every seven years because "every seven years there's a new generation of audiences." And that should hold true for live theatre as well -- if it can hold on long enough to become an institution in the first place.

None of which explains why Phantom has exerted such a hold. Mr. Drabinsky is certain that it isn't the chandelier that falls at the end of the first act. When he first saw the show, fell in love with it and vowed to acquire the Canadian rights, he was seated in the back stalls of Her Majesty's Theatre in London underneath an overhang, and couldn't see the chandelier properly.

I agree with him: Once you've seen that effect, you've seen it and you don't need to come back. And people have come back to Phantom -- some devotees have made the pilgrimage dozens, even hundreds of times.

They like the love story -- it's the only single-mindedly romantic musical of recent years, and audiences get caught up in the central trio of virginal soprano, ardent young aristocrat and transcendentally ugly genius. (Or, to quote Mr. Drabinsky, "the triangle is ineluctable.")

Beyond this, of course, is the lure of the Phantom himself. All of us on some level feel misunderstood and unloved -- and the Phantom is both, on a truly gigantic scale.

And, yes, there is the spectacle, by which I mean the feeling of haunted lushness created by Hal Prince's direction, Maria Bjornson's sets and Andrew Bridge's lighting, which transports us from the Paris Opera to the Phantom's lair via a candle-lit subterranean lake. Visual splendour -- imaginative visual splendour -- is the one thing the theatre is about.

And the music helps. David Rogers is an actor who has been with the show since it opened. Starting as an all-purpose understudy, he graduated to playing Raoul, the hero, and has most recently been standby for the Phantom himself.

In the last week, he reverted to playing Raoul, with one shot at the Phantom when Paul Stanley took off on Friday for a one-shot gig in Las Vegas. (Mr. Rogers' audience probably got the better deal; Mr. Stanley, with his raucous rock-star voice, was nobody's "angel of music.")

Mr. Rogers told me that he still shivered when he heard Raoul's big song, All I Ask of You, welling up in the pit.

The show also contains excerpts from three imaginary operas: Each of these begins with some expert pastiche but in the last -- supposedly the Phantom's own -- the composer goes into his own melodramatic model, prompting the reflection that if the best aria Phantom could come up with was The Point of No Return, then maybe composing was not the greatest of his many talents.

Undoubtedly, though, the overall sound binds the package together.

And the Toronto production had one extra weapon: the theatre itself. The Pantages is a gorgeous piece of restoration, and an ideal frame for the show; it looks like the Paris Opera, and you can easily imagine the Masquerade number being staged on its main staircase.

Sadder than the show's closure -- all shows have to close some time -- is that nobody knows what future, if any, the Pantages has. Sad for the theatre staff, with no jobs to go to.

Sad for all of us.
