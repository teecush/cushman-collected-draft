(From the National Post, Friday, November 10, 2000) I began my review in yesterday's Post of Dame Edna: The Royal Tour with the confession that I am constantly forgetting names. As my own byline had disappeared from the piece, I can only assume that the condition is catching. *****

DAME EDNA - THE ROYAL TOUR

Pantages Theatre

As someone who is always forgetting names, I sit amazed at Dame Edna Everage's capacity for retaining them, especially when the names are those of people in her audience to whom she has only just been introduced.

I apologize for that use of the passive voice. It was wrong. There is nothing passive about the Australian diva and megastar -- megadiva, surely -- with her mauve bouffant, her jewelled spectacles that peer and intimidate like a twin-set of Regency monocles, and her endless wardrobe of shocking pink frocks, including one with zebra stripes that you could cross a street by. Above all, there is nothing passive about her relationship to us, her possums. She certainly isnt INTRODUCED to us; she doesnt even introduce herself; when she finds victims in the house she introduces THEM, to themselves, forcibly. First, she ascertains and vocally fondles their names; then she asks them questions, chiefly about their homes and what they -- the homes, I mean -- are like inside. (I thought beige was dead, but now I find it's alive and well and living in North York.") By the end of the 2 1/2-hour show, she has acquired some dozen of these dossiers, and what is prodigious is that she never lets any of them escape. She remembers which person goes with which history, and she manipulates them throughout the evening like a juggler playing with his balls. (Sorry.) Nothing is wasted; if she orders dinner for a hungry looking couple in Act One, then it arrives in Act Two, and they have to eat it. Onstage.

"I think real people are so much more interesting than celebrities, she coos, diabolically. If her act is anything it's an expose of the cult of celebrity: an exploitation of the twin ideas that fame in itself is a licence for unbridled egomania, and that people will do anything to be in its presence and have some of it rub off. When she interviews (i.e. patronizes, or matronizes) someone, shes like Joan Rivers, only more real.

On a literal level, of course, she isnt real at all; she's a fictitious character, invented, written and performed by the amazing Barry Humphries. This adds sting, but cancels offence; her victims aren't humiliated because they feel themselves to be part of the fiction, part of the show. On Tuesday night -- her Canadian stage debut -- she made instant if transient stars out of Carmen and Julie and Frank and Barbara and Ann and Steve and Nancy (whos tattooed) and Sandy and Sandys husband whose name, Sandy told us, is Harley. Like the motorcycle," Harley volunteered without being asked, for which impertinence he received a freezing rebuke from the Dame: "Do you often interrupt theatrical performances, Harley?

That sudden change of gear (an obvious metaphor in the circumstances) is Dame Edna at match-strength. So is the subsequent routine in which she gave Sandy a precious stone that she herself had received from an admirer in New York, described the murderous jealousies that this would inspire in Sandy's spouse and best friend, and ended up snatching the bauble back for Sandy's own good. And it must mark a new frontier in something-or-other when Edna learns from another couple the phone number of their mother, and calls the good lady up, right then and there; were used to seeing TV hosts do this sort of thing, but its somehow different when we're all in the same room. The recipient, by the way, took the call beautifully in her stride, almost as if she'd been expecting it, but of course being phoned by importunate strangers is now one of the recognized features of modern life.

Comedians before Dame Edna have drawn attention to the patrons up in the cheaper seats, but I dont know of any others who have cheerfully dubbed them paupers" and gone on dubbing them right through the show. She also takes it into her head that the punters in the side boxes may be deaf-mutes, and for their benefit she helpfully (and rapidly) repeats much of what she says in mime; though she does have to own up at one point to forgetting the sign language for arugula. Her head movements throughout are well worth studying, as are her facial expressions; welcoming those who have brought their spouses, "or significant others, or same-sex partners, she is overcome at the last item by a half-smirk, half- grimace that suggests she is shocked by her own tolerance.

Dame Edna, who was once an ordinary Melbourne housewife, has two grown-up children; and its notable that though she accepts her daughter's lesbianism (which doesn't mean that she accepts her daughter) she cannot bring herself to acknowledge that her son, Kenny, whom she adores, is gay. She gets the audience singing a song called any friend of Kennys is a friend of mine" and which at one point rhymes Kenneth with zenith.

She works into her patter, and even into her songs, knowledgeable references to such local landmarks as St. Lawrence Market and Church- and-Wellesley. "You cant say that Edna hasn't done her homework, she crows. I wish she hadn't said that; it seems anxious rather than outrageous, while the self-satisfied laugh that now punctuates her delivery sounds less like the Dame crowing over her own effrontery than like her creator exulting that the show is going well.

For the first time in my Everage experience -- which covers stage, TV and interviews -- I felt a gap opening up between the two of them; usually with Dame Edna its impossible to believe that such a person as Barry Humphries exists, which is the impregnable strength of the act. I laughed pretty much continuously; once I felt tears running down my cheeks; but I never felt transported as I have in the past. The fantastic spirals seemed less inspired, and the points less sharp, as if age were having a softening effect.

At one time, when she identified people in the audience, with exaggerated compassion, as senior citizens" and then kept coming back to them with a brisk cry of "senior! she seemed to be satirizing the whole idea that one could make old people feel better by calling them something else. If the act has one overriding target, its the cult of euphemism. She still calls people senior, but it now seems automatic. The end of the show, too, is a letdown, almost a cop-out, though it does belatedly explain the title. She tells us the Queen has asked her to take over the royal role in Canada, and though she at first protested that she had no infrastructure (thank God I remembered that meaningless word") but she was told she could create her own royal family. So she invites her chosen possums on to the stage and puts them into funny costumes. This I thought was unfair and, yes, embarrassing; she has the expertise in dressing up and they don't. And any comic could do it.

It remains to say that though Edna is not a first-rate song-and- dance person, Humphries is; and the difference is very cunningly split in the routines the Dame undertakes with her two Ednaettes, all the while sporting a winded, manic grin. The songs are in the old-fashioned English revue tradition, a tradition I happen to like: verbally loaded and with billowing piano accompaniment. This is provided by Wayne Barker, whom Dame Edna never once acknowledges and who is also repeatedly employed -- due presumably to some loophole in the musicians union regulations -- as a Polaroid photographer. Maybe Toronto has not inspired Dame Edna to her highest flights but that -- as she would be the first to agree -- is probably our fault rather than hers. What awaits you at the beautiful Pantages Theatre has its roots solidly in tradition, but the flowering is unique. And I suspect that it will bloom more outrageously as the weeks -- six of them -- pass.

I haven't mentioned the gladdies. Long may they wave.
