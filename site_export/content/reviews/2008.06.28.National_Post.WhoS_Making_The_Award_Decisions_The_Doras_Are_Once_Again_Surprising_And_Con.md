One of this years Dora Award nominees is Tazed and Confused, a Second City revue. This isn't up, as you might think, for Best New Musical. It's in the running for Best New Play, authorship credited to the Ensemble.

Theres a part of me thats pleased by that. It introduces a pleasing note of irreverence into a procedure prone, by its nature, to sycophancy, solemnity and self-congratulation. Besides, the best Second City revues are better written than most plays. All the same, there's something about its selection that smacks of desperation.

Well-merited desperation, I have to say. All seasons are bad seasons for new plays, but 2007-08 was worse than most. There is a redemptive front-runner in Hannah Moscovitch's East of Berlin, which has to win, even though its victory will deprive us, come Monday, of watching the Ensemble troop onstage to claim the prize. (Almost as good as the Tonys, where every show has 17 producers.) Moscovitch might conceivably be hampered by the fact that shes running against herself, with her early play Essay also nominated because, though it has been seen in Toronto before, that was only on the Fringe, which doesnt count. Moscovitch's other revived (and better) early piece, The Russian Play, doesn't qualify because it got a rather brief run the previous season. OK, but why didnt Michelle Monteiths virtuoso performance in that piece get a Best Actress nod? I've always understood that though revived plays aren't eligible, revived performances are. (In fact, theres one nominated this year.) Keeping up with these rules is enough to make your head spin.

The rules may explain why Colleen Murphys The December Man, well represented in other categories, doesn't have a shot at Best Play; it may have been new to Toronto but it had already been done in Edmonton. Or did the jurors seriously think it inferior to Judith Thompson's Palace at the End, which was three monologues and maybe one-third of a good play? (And again with the acting: Maev Beaty, the best of the three monologists, is the only one not recognized.) The remaining contender is Brendan Galls Alias Godot: I dont think it should win, but I'm pleased to see it up there; it was a very funny play (and production, incidentally) that was generally underrated.

There's a category called Outstanding Production of a Play. It isnt the same as Outstanding Direction, though its hard to see how you could have one without the other. It means, if anything, Best All-Round Event. One that should certainly have been nominated, should very likely have won, is Studio 180's Stuff Happens, the Iraq show (and a far livelier one than Palace at the End). It hasn't received a single nomination in any category; even David Foxs sublime impersonation of Donald Rumsfeld has been passed over. Well, as Rummy himself said, stuff happens. Or, as just about everybody in Toronto theatre has been saying, its a scandal.

Especially in light of what has been nominated. Take the Obsidian Theatre Company's slow and unfocused staging of Intimate Apparel. (Though it deserves, as befits its title, its Best Costume recognition. Bravo, Tamara Marie Kucheran.) There seems to be a rule that Obsidian will be nominated whatever it does; surely black theatre in Toronto has got beyond needing that special treatment. Jewish theatre shouldn't get it either; what is the Harold Green Companys Rose doing there? A fine performance, certainly, by Lally Cadeau, but a mediocre play. And on the subject of logs being rolled, it can only be Top Girlss status as a feminist classic that has gained it more reclame (not just from the Doras) than Mary Stuart, The Time of Your Life or Three Sisters, which were superior Soulpepper productions of more challenging plays. Yes, Top Girls was an actresses' showcase; it still fell apart in its middle scenes.

On the topic of Soulpepper: I'm rooting for Joseph Ziegler ( The Time of Your Life) as Best Actor since a) he was marvellous; b) he was robbed a couple of years ago for A Whistle in the Dark; c) Id like that show to get a break. Id like it to extend to Stuart Hughes's Supporting Performance a category that also includes, pleasingly, Mr. and Mrs. Diego Matamoros, she being Robyn Stevan in private life. Both were delightful, but Mr. M was Dora-worthier in The Odd Couple, just as he was a couple of years ago in The Government Inspector. What is it about great comic acting that the Doras don't get? And why do they persist, insanely, in lumping supporting performances Male and Female, Play and Musical, in the same crowded category?

Oh yes, Musicals, Outstanding Production of. These include Can-Stages Little Shop of Horrors, directed by Ted Dykstra, and Fire, starring Ted Dykstra (wild as Jerry Lee Lewis). The former deserves to win. Dykstra (in the revived performance mentioned above) is up for Best Musical Actor and he deserves that, too, though Rick Roberts in the same show is competition. Id hate to choose between Nicole Underhay (also same show) and Patricia Zentilli ( Little Shop) for Best Musical Female. How could Fire have such great performances but still be such a drab evening? The script, that's why. (And the production, though the director's nominated nonetheless.) Most Ludicrous Category: Musical Direction. How do you compare The Time of Your Life with Eugene Onegin? No-Contest Categories: Opera Production. ( Pelleas and Melisande) and Touring ( Nicholas Nickleby). They Have To Be Joking Category: Anything including Dirty Dancing. Question of Questions: Given the flak that comes their way every year, from here and elsewhere, when will the Dora jurors get around to explaining their methods, their criteria and their identities?
