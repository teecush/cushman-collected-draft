The Factory Theatre's Naked  season ("Canadian Classics Reimagined") has so far been a matter of passing off economic necessity as aesthetic choice. Ravi Jains production of Salt-Water Moon is a departure; its the first on the list to turn bare-bones staging, ingeniously and imaginatively, into a style.

David French's play - premiered in 1984, set in 1925 - has two characters. Jain's version gives it three. This may seem a paradoxical kind of economy but it works. We have Mayko Nguyen as Mary Snow, young small-town Newfoundland lady currently in domestic service, and Kawa Ada as Jacob Mercer, her former lover who returns from a stay in Toronto to woo her for keeps. But there is another person on stage with them: Ania Soul, billed in the program as "Musician." She sings and plays songs appropriate to the time and place. Now, these are all very nice atmospherically, but whats more important is that she reads or recites the plays original stage directions. These give meticulous descriptions of the play's picket-fenced waterside locale and of the clothes its people are wearing: descriptions that are in flagrant and sometimes amusing contradiction of what we see. The scenic details on which the author was so insistent are gone, replaced by an array of candles that Mary spreads around the floor as the action gets started. Her mandated yellow dress has given way to sweater and jeans; Jacob wears the same actor's uniform. Later on our narrator will describe actions that the performers never actually perform. In this context non-traditional casting is a non-issue. Both actors are ethnically unconventional choices for their roles but in this productions neutral convention, as if we had dropped in on a rehearsal, it doesnt matter either way.

Thanks to those candles the production, with no designer credited, is visually beguiling, as befits a play about a moonlit courtship: one in which the lunar references point the contrast between the practical woman and the fanciful man. In Jacob's absence Mary has got herself engaged to a suitor with more eligible prospects: one, moreover, against whom Jacob has a portfolio of grudges, some related to the still-recent war. Mary is an orphan who needs to look out for herself and still more for her younger sister, currently immured in a brutal-sounding institution. All Jacob has to offer are charm, a love that we presume to be both real and reciprocated, an ambiguous touch of urban glamour, and - most importantly for the play's purposes - the gift of the gab. Here, though, the production and the performances fall down. Both actors are proven talents, possessed of magnetism and range. Ada has a remarkable capacity for compelling attention by simply occupying space; his smile doesnt hurt either. But his actual lines, especially in the earlier parts of the play, come out here in a fast monotone. Responding to them, Nguyen protests rather too much. Neither actor is attempting a Newfoundland accent, and this could be damaging. The rhythms are not perhaps as important as the similar ones in an Irish play (try reading a line of Synge or OCasey without falling into the brogue) but they still add flavour. Its absence makes this revival less effective than the one at Soulpepper a few years ago. That had a more conventional setting but also, frankly, a more effective one. The play isn't a timeless masterpiece; it needs its scenic context. It also needs, or at least benefits from, its wider dramatic context. The Soulpepper production was part of a Mercer family cycle; we had already seen Leaving Home, a play written earlier but set much later, in which French introduced us to Jacob and Mary as a fractious older married couple, uprooted and living in Toronto. It made Salt-Water Moon, and especially its ending, more pungently bittersweet.

On its own terms, though, that ending in the Factory production is magically done. Jain's staging unmoors it from the rest of the play, brings it closer to us (literally). Thus exposed, Ada and Nguyen find their best form, he spinning rhapsodically between truth and fantasy, whichever he thinks will charm and impress her the most at any given moment, and perhaps unsure himself of which is which; she giving in with full consciousness of what shes doing, with no illusions about his illusions, and getting a firm and funny last word. They end happy, at least for the moment, and so do we.
