ONE NIGHT STAND

Theatre Passe Muraille, Toronto

The words Canadian classic inspire more dread than any others in the murky world of theatrical hype. The history books tell us that in the 1970s there was a golden age -- not just a renaissance, but a whole new world -- of indigenous playwriting. It must have been great fun if you were there at the time. Now, however, as revival follows revival, it looks like a bad joke.

The latest evidence is a restaging of One Night Stand, a great success in 1977 for Carol Bolt, who, sadly, died last year. She had updated it somewhat but, sentiment apart, it now seems a senseless piece, one that in teasing out a single situation manages to make 90 minutes of playing time seem like eternity.

The situation itself is basic but promising. Daisy, who is fairly young, and Rafe, who is younger, go separately to a club and pick one another up. Who actually made the first move remains a matter of debate, which is lifelike enough, but anyway they end up going back to her place: a high-rise apartment at Yonge and Eglinton.

Daisy is in a reckless mood. It's her birthday, but her married boyfriend has excused himself from the celebration, as have her best girlfriend and her boyfriend. Rafe is a twinkling stud of indeterminate background; sometimes he seems to be Latin, sometimes not. He also carries a tape-recorder around with him, maybe because he is a musician, maybe not. He has any number of stories. If consummation is delayed its not because Daisy is coy but because Rafe is, or maybe just because he never stops talking. Daisys frustration begins to communicate itself to the audience.

"I hate surprises," says Daisy, who works in a bank. "That's your problem," says Rafe, and it's not just a snappy comeback; its a diagnosis. At this point we seem to be in familiar dramatic territory: Rafe is the free spirit come to liberate Daisy from her respectable rut. If this were the 1960s, the inhibited partner would be the man, and he would have his life changed by some male playwrights fantasy in the form of an adorable, kooky nymph. More recently, genders have been reversed, and counter-cultural Galahads have taken to rescuing damsels who claimed to be quite happy in their distress but were wrong.

Rafe's behaviour, however, goes beyond the limits of endearing non-conformity into actual craziness. These are some of the things he does: He grabs Daisy's phone and is rude to her friends. He climbs out over the balcony into the apartment next door and returns wearing a scary animal-mask; he does this twice, managing on the second occasion to frighten the life out of Daisy, who by now is feeling understandably vulnerable. He calls an airline and books a flight to Peru on her credit card. Like many crazy people, Rafe cannot be argued with; he always has another answer. So far, so clinically accurate. But its as wearing in the theatre as it would be in real life, and every bit as tedious.

Rafe also blithely mentions having done time in jail for killing a woman. At this point it becomes really incomprehensible that Daisy, who may be horny and discontented but is not suicidal, doesnt throw him out: if not for her own sake then for ours. Alternatively, she might call the cops. As a matter of fact she does and, after a surprisingly long delay, they come to the door. (They seem to be Toronto's slowest.) She, however, manages to have them sent away by getting on the blower and doing some fast-talking to police HQ, who are remarkably understanding about it. (They also seem to be Toronto's dumbest.) If you think that sounds implausible, you should see what happens later.

Here we enter the territory of that which should not be revealed; but I can disclose that the plays denouement depends on Daisy having spent an unconscionable time in the shower with the water at full pressure and plugs in her ears. That at least is the only way I can explain her ignorance of the crucial events going on outside her bathroom door, which must have taken some time and made a considerable noise. There is a grim surprise ending that seems intended to prove something or to be the logical result of something else.

The only apparent reason for reviving stuff like this, apart from title-recognition value, is that it provides showy roles for a couple of actors. Making the characters credible is a harder thing, and giving them enough variety to fill out an evening may be impossible. At all events, it doesnt happen here. Stephanie Jones at least makes Daisy believably ravenous; a tall lady who has gone to the ball in a boldly striped top, she has a strikingly zoological look about her. She's a giraffe in zebra's clothing. Jason Cadieux's unrelenting winsomeness as Rafe is probably built into the role.

In one respect, seeing the hits of 25 years ago is cheering. Things have improved, even if they're still terrible.
