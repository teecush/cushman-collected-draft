The Tarragon Theatre continues its Hannah Moscovitch festival with a double bill of plays concerned in some way with children. The first, Little One, is a remount and partial rewrite of a piece seen two years ago at Summerworks. It's the story of Aaron and Claire, both adopted by an ultra-liberal, ultra-tolerant couple in Ottawa. They need all their tolerance since Claire, who came to them when she was four years old, is seriously damaged and, it seems, sociopathic. Aaron is charged, as her older brother, with looking after and understanding her. This is a tough assignment since Claire routinely taunts and insults him, vandalizes his possessions, and drowns his goldfish. When, a few years later, he is given a pet kitten, we can only fear for its fate.

Little One is the name Aaron gives his cat, but it might also apply to Claire herself and to the young Vietnamese woman next door, a mail-order bride. She and her husband are the subject of some of Claire's more lurid fantasies - if, that is, they are fantasies. The play is a retrospective narration by Aaron, who has become a doctor in adult life, partly out of guilt at having failed his sister. (A similar compensatory mechanism cropped up recently in Kristen Thomson's Someone Else.) Claire appears in his reconstruction of their relationship; this includes the stories she told him that she delivers, in Natasha Mytnowych's production, from the auditorium, her face spookily illuminated by a flashlight.

As before, Joe Cobden plays Aaron and Michelle Monteith Claire, and they're both excellent. Moscovitch, as we know, writes sparkling monologues, however dark the subject-matter (this is the play in which the sound of suburban Ottawa is described as that of a million people being quiet together) and Cobden, trying furiously hard to be patient, makes his account as funny as it's sad; we never meet the parents, but we get a perfect picture of them. The play's most piercing moment remains the mother's realization of how much she's sacrificed her son to her daughter. Monteith is unexcelled at being haunting, haunted, frightening and frightened, also at playing young, though she now pitches some of her speeches too quietly for comfort or even, as seems to be the intention, for discomfort. The production in general takes less kindly to the Tarragon Extra Space than it did to its previous quarters at Theatre Passe Muraille; the piano-playing, child-like Asian neighbour (Kay-lie Lau), speechless but present, was invisible throughout, at least from my seat. Her whole function in the play, interestingly mysterious before, now just seems obscure, the ending abrupt in a way I don't remember it being previously.

The companion piece, Other People's Children, concerns what I cannot avoid describing as a yuppie couple who hire a Tamil nanny for their one and only baby. Wife Ilana is a lawyer; husband Ben travels a lot on business, mainly to Asia; nanny Sati comes direct from another posting in Washington, and has three children of her own whom she hopes to bring to Canada. She also has, or had, a husband who's moved to Japan, which leads Ben to suspect the worst of him. The play starts with Ilana being nervously welcoming, and Tami being nervously welcomed though ironically it's she, the newcomer, who's falling over herself to be reassuring. Michael Gianfran-cesco's set, imaginative and clinical at once, comprises Sati's bedroom, Ilana's bathroom, and a hallway that, spiritually at least, is Ben's domain; he spends a lot of the play arriving, from Vietnam or wherever, invariably with awkward results.

The marriage is plainly in trouble, probably always has been; Ben and Ilana each suspect what the other may get up to when they're apart. Ilana, though she furiously denies it, seems to resent motherhood; she certainly resents the bond that Sati - effortlessly, we gather - forms with the unseen child. A triangular drama seems inevitable, but it take subtle forms when it - to use too emphatic a word - materializes. Most of Mos-covitch's plays involve cross-cultural issues, but this may be her first stab at domestic realism; she takes to it as expertly as she seems to take to everything else; she's our most competent playwright, and I don't mean that as faint praise. There's plenty going on here beneath the surface, notably with Sati herself; Ben and Ilana never learn the facts about her marriage, her previous jobs, or even about the photos she keeps by her bed. (She could, of course, be the anti-Mary Poppins, artlessly creating confusion rather than clearing it up.) Here, as not in Little One, the ambiguities are stimulating rather than perplexing. The balance of Paul Lampert's production is upset by Niki Landau's stridently unsympathetic Ilana, but Gray Powell does a stalwart job on Ben, possibly making theatrical history by flossing onstage. Elisa Moolecherry's Sati is beautifully unfathomable.

4: 48 Psychosis charts some of the same disturbed territory as Mosco-vitch's Little One, though here it's mapped from the inside - a perspective that commands respect rather than enjoyment, or even understanding. It's the last play of Sarah Kane, the young British playwright who committed suicide shortly after writing it, and it has indeed been described as a dramatized suicide note. It isn't, in textual form, that dramatized; it's printed as a kind of prose poem, no lines allocated to specific characters. Vikki Anderson's production (for Necesssary Angel) divides them between three performers, each with a more or less consistent role. Laura Condolln plays the dev-astatedly depressed protagonist, for whom 4: 48 in the morning is the only moment at which she can find peace; Bruce Godfree is her friend/ lover, driven to desperation by his inability to alleviate hers; and Raven Dauda is the doctor who helps, but not enough, and is resented for it. This may be reductive, but it more or less makes sense; however Anderson then undermines her conception with a flashy production (literally so, in its sound and lighting) set on what look like the steps of a stadium, strewn with significant props, such as pills and wine bottles. Some of Kane's anguish, anger and bewilderment come through in Condolln's performance, though only intermittently; the other two are ineffectual (like their characters, I suppose), not much helped by having, for example, to deliver a catalogue of symptoms to a bossa nova beat. The piece, if it's to work at all (and I don't know if it could) needs to be chilling; here it's just cold.

Little One/Other People's Children runs through March 17 at the Tarragon Extra Space; 4: 48 Psychosis runs through Feb. 23 at the Berkeley Street Theatre.
