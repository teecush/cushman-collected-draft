John Murrell's 1977 play Waiting for the Parade is Soulpeppers fifth production this year, and it makes neat patterns with the preceding four. Like the companys two war shows ( Billy Bishop Goes to ...and Oh What a Lovely ...), it's a scrapbook piece about 20th-century global conflict, seen from a tributary -- i.e. non-mainstream -- point of view, though in this case the period is 1939-45 instead of 1914-18. In terms of casting, it's an all-female counterbalance to the all-male Glengarry Glen Ross. And it contrasts with Faith Healer in being too thin rather than too dense for its own and its productions good.

Waiting for the Parade gives us the Second World War as seen by five women of Calgary, of whom one has a man at the front, one has a man locked up, one has one of each of the above and the remaining two each have a man at home. The program gives them names but the dialogue, oddly, doesnt. Janet, whose husband has an essential job as a radio announcer, presides with brisk imperiousness over the volunteer activities of three of the others. Margaret is a widow, who has one son in uniform and one locked up as a pacifist agitator, and who despairs of ever seeing either of them again; desperation is, indeed, her natural mode, though she tries to keep a tight rein on it when in company. Catherine, married to a soldier, consoles herself in his absence with a temporary affair, to the disapproval of Margaret, and especially of Janet who turns out to have her own issues. Eve is a teacher whose husband hasn't enlisted but who wishes he would; he keeps his guns at home and, we gather, never stops talking about them. Enforcedly outside the social circle, although a neighbour, is Marta, who is German and whose father has been interned as a suspected Nazi sympathizer. Catherine and Eve befriend her; the others patronize or simply detest her. The quintet's various predicaments are outlined in monologues, and rubbed against one another in group scenes. If that sounds suspiciously neat, it is.

I described Soulpeppers earlier war shows as scrapbooks and they are avowedly that; one a true story, the other a montage of period songs and sketches. Theyre both musicals. Waiting for the Parade makes use of music; characters break into evocative snatches of popular song, while a prologue and epilogue involve frantic jitterbugging expertly choreographed by Robert Mc-Collum to bring out the participants' differing degrees of enthusiasm, embarrassment and downright sullenness. But this is only incidental; it's a play, and the characters seem too neatly selected as representative cases of The Women Who Variously Wait. Murrell is always a sensitive and sympathetic writer; he gives the actors things to do, and the audience cause to be touched and amused, though never really moved. I felt the lack more this time than when the play was revived a few years ago at the Shaw Festival; not, I think, through any great disparity in the productions (though I miss Kelli Foxs passion at the Shaw) than through some of the charm having worn off.

Joseph Zieglers Soulpepper production gets progressively better as the feelings, either of joy or sorrow, get more intense. Quarrel scenes, sketchy in the first half, strike deeply and fully in the second, and we feel the final weariness of victory. Nancy Palk is splendid as Margaret, a lonely ramrod; she's touching and she's funny, especially in her repeated complaints about the bossy Janet. "I cant stand that woman" she says, repeatedly, finding a different inflection every time. If she avoids caricature, Deborah Drakeford, as the object of her intolerance, too frequently falls into it; she seems to be playing her character through the others eyes, though when Janet is finally permitted a heart, a broken one, she rises to the occasion. Krystin Pellerin is vibrant as Eve, especially when figuratively sticking it to her offstage spouse. Michelle Monteith's Catherine is warm as well, though strangely out of period; fuzzy where the writing is precise. Fiona Byrne is bitterly powerful as Marta, who is indeed, by virtue of her outsider status, the most powerful figure in the piece, though neither she nor the production can clarify her relation to the rest of the play. Stuck in a sort of sentry-box at the side of the stage, she seems to wander into the rest of the play at arbitrary moments, when her author needs her as a sounding board. The play is partly about varieties of loneliness, but hers seems to need a play of its own.
