THEATRE REVIEW

THE TOXIC AVENGER MUSICAL

The Music Hall, Toronto

I greatly enjoyed the first five minutes of The Toxic Avenger Musical. Maybe the first 10 minutes. This show, about a New Jersey town to which Manhattan exports all its nuclear and other waste, starts with one of its five performers advancing bravely toward us to sing the opening number, and finding his first note collapsing into a cough. That's funny. The number itself, when he and his colleagues are able to embark on it, is funny, too. Its an over-the-top anthem about the town of Tromaville and how anyone fixing on staying alive would be well advised to give it the old go-by.

The music for The Toxic Avenger Musical is by David Bryan (of Bon Jovi), the book is by Joe DiPietro (I Love You, Youre Perfect, Now Change), and the two of them collaborated on the lyrics. The music is characterless clatter but the best of the lyrics, thanks I suspect to DiPietro's old-school show-smarts, bring off something otherwise unknown in rock-based musicals, by being a) decipherable and b) good. Its only predecessor in that regard has been Little Shop of Horrors, a show to which it stands indebted in other matters as well. Little Shop pioneered the fashion for basing musicals on junk movies. The new show also resembles Little Shop in giving us a pacific virginal nerd of a hero and a coy, well-used heroine who respects his values and his character but is slow to respond to him physically. In this case, the boy is Melvin Ferd the Third, who wants to clean up his polluted town without hurting anyone, and the girl is Sarah, a blind librarian.

This curious combination of qualities is due to the evil lady Mayor, who heads the corporation that's poisoning Tromaville and has cuningly placed the records of her misdeeds in the custody of someone who cant read them. But Melvin gets his hands on the evidence, and unwisely confronts the Mayor, who has two of her goons drop him into a vat of exceptionally toxic goo. He emerges from it a foot taller, green all over, possessed of superhuman powers, and not shy about using them. (He does tend to be sorry afterwards.)

From the moment of his transformation, the show, which up until now has remained fairly sprightly, takes a nosedive. It begins, perhaps in accordance with its environmental principles, endlessly recycling itself.

It recycles other musicals as well. The Mayor might have stepped out of Sondheims

Anyone Can Whistle (everyone hates her, yes, yes, being the Mayoress, yes) while the frustrated Melvin's more indiscriminate rampages give him a definite resemblance to Sweeney Todd, if without the same complexity of motivation. Its attempts at political and theatrical satire make it seem like a less sophisticated Urinetown. Like a depressing number of other recent musicals, it gives itself the airs of satire without ever taking the smirk off its face long enough to establish anything to be satiric about.

John Rando, who directed Urinetown with such efficiency and imagination, settles here for efficiency. He doesn't manage to turn low-rent into a style. He has an excellent cast. Evan Alexander Smith is endearingly earnest as Melvin, though he can hardly help being monotonous as his toxic doppelganger (Toxie for short). Brittany Gray flutters her lashes and her vocal cords in classic style as Sarah. She functions far more efficiently outside her home than in it. The show isnt into explaining this; it just wants the laughs. Some of these are bracingly tasteless, with great cruel lines like, If blind people didnt love ugly people, then who would?" But it then retreats with a number called All Men are Freaks, which strikes me as a cop-out.

Louise Pitre doubles as the Mayor and Melvin's mother, who is stock Jewish though she doesn't seem to have passed it on to her son. At one point, the two ladies sing a hate-duet which, the score having taken a nosedive along with the script, is entitled (and mainly consists of) Bitch/Slut/Liar/Whore. The intended panache of this quick-change act is negated by the use of doubles. Pitre also plays a nun, for reasons to do with little except camp; she's both resonant and resilient, though at this stage of her game she should surely have better things to do with her time. All other roles are divided between Darren A. Herbert and Jamie McKnight, billed respectively as Black Dude and White Dude.

The show lays great stress on its Jersey setting but the Toronto audience is indulged with references to Tim Hortons and the Don River. How dumb do they think we are? (Don't answer that.) And maybe the production exemplifies its own environmental thesis. This gets imported from New York while our own, insanely funny, An Inconvenient Musical languishes in neglect. - The Toxic Avenger Musical runs until Jan. 3.
