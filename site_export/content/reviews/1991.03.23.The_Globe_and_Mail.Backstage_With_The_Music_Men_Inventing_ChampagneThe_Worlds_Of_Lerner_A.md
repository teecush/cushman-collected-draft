BY MARTIN GOTTFRIED Bantam, 483 pages, $29.95

Backstage with the music men INVENTING CHAMPAGNE:The Worlds of Lerner and Loewe ALL HIS JAZZ: The Life and Death of Bob Fosse

BY MARTIN GOTTFRIED Bantam, 483 pages, $29.95

Saturday, March 23, 1991

ROBERT CUSHMAN

REVIEW BY ROBERT CUSHMAN "THE writing of a book begins long before the writing of a book begins," says Martin Gottfried at the beginning of All His Jazz, unbearably arch before we have even got started on the acknowledgements. But he pulls himself together and offers a riveting account of a life that might have been lived for the benefit of a biographer. Bob Fosse began his career at age 10 as a child hoofer, calling himself Bobby Ritz. At an impressionable age he was exposed to Depression burlesque houses and strippers' dressing-rooms. Life was a cabaret, old chum, and a seedy one. Gottfried makes much - maybe too much - of the unfulfilled performer striving to assert himself in the feted choreographer, but history does keep playing into his hands. Back in 1955, choreographing Damn Yankees, Fosse worked out an aborted routine for a compere-figure in a gorilla suit. In 1972 Fosse was able to take over the gorilla in Cabaret and embrace it like an old friend.

It wasn't all he took over. Fosse radically altered the structure of the show Cabaret for his film, but he kept its style and tone. Having discovered sleaze-as-metaphor he stayed with it, using it to fuel a gnawing self-hatred. In Pippin, Chicago, and All That Jazz, he brilliantly exploited all the theatrical tricks while reviling the audience for accepting them. This always seemed to me pretty self- indulgent (if you feel like that, Bob, why go on?) but now that I know its personal springs I feel more tolerant. All That Jazz proved prophetic of Fosse's own fatal heart attack, which argues a kind of terminal integrity, although his old rival Gower Champion stole some of his thunder by actually dying on his own last first night.

I still prefer the earlier Fosse: the choreographer whose tight neurotic formations - the terpsichorean equivalent of close harmony - so hilariously impaled the corporate life in How To Succeed In Business Without Really Trying, the director whose mobile image of New York in Sweet Charity (sleaze-as-fun) provided the perfect frame for his wife Gwen Verdon, the ultimate soubrette. Part of Gottfried's story is how, long after their separation, Verdon remained Fosse's professional aid, coaching all the substitute Charities and finally emerging as the official widow. The relationship is part touching, part chilling, like nearly everything about Fosse.

He was the best at his game, more stylish than Jerome Robbins, more individual than Champion or Michael Bennett. Only Mamoulian, Welles and Kazan moved between stage and film with comparable mastery. Gottfried knew the man and can evaluate all his work.

In 1971 there was a musical film called The Little Prince. Alan Jay Lerner wrote the screenplay and lyrics, Frederick Loewe the music, and Bob Fosse played the Snake. It was Lerner and Loewe's last collaboration, and the only time either of them worked with Fosse.

The three men had differing attitudes toward work and - as three of the musical theatre's minority of male heterosexuals - toward women. Loewe was a hedonist, businesslike but relaxed. Having known poverty in early adult life, he relished success and retired on it, leaving both his career and his one marriage for the sunbelt life of an elderly playboy.

Lerner went on without him; he had to work, though he was very undisciplined about it. The same might be said of his personal life; he was married eight times, one of his divorces being spectacularly messy. Fosse, similarly driven professionally, acquired only three wives but an uncountable number of girlfriends. Gene Lees thinks Lerner's masterpiece, My Fair Lady, is the greatest of American musicals: I hope he's wrong, since the show, except in the quality of its craftsmanship, is so very un-American. It lacks the Broadway tempo and the Broadway brass. Its genius lies in extracting from its Shavian source the romance that everyone but Shaw himself had always known was there; really it's the last great European operetta. Loewe was Vienna-bred, and though Lerner was native New York, he was happiest writing about un-American activities.

My Fair Lady and Gigi stand apart from Lerner's other shows in having solid literary foundations. The other musicals - Brigadoon, Camelot, On a Clear Day and lots of lesser work - are mystical or preachy or both.

Not that they lack qualities. Camelot is one of the very few musicals that approaches tragedy. The ending - King Arthur clinging to the hope that some tattered fragment of his achievement may survive in the world's imagination - is extraordinarily moving. Lees, though, is too busy clocking up the show's anachronisms to notice; but since Camelot is based on a 20th-century reworking of a medieval epic about a warrior from the Dark Ages, such pedantry seems triply ludicrous. But there's no stopping Lees, once he starts making lists. He can and does name half a dozen figures in the arts who anticipated Loewe in taking early retirement, and he attacks the myth of the Broadway songwriter born into poverty by supplying the backgrounds of 17 who weren't.

He is a resistible writer when he starts laying down the law, but he has a brain. Not until the book is over do you notice that he apparently never met either Lerner or Loewe. (At least, he never says he did, and he is not generally bashful about such things.) Also, he never quotes a line of Lerner's lyrics; the rights were presumably unavailable or prohibitively expensive. So he cannot analyze them, which one wants him to do since he is a craftsmanlike if excessively solemn lyricist himself. There are two or three factual howlers and the book is an ugly object, its chapter headings surrounded by spots that are possibly meant to suggest stardust but look like measles.

"I couldn't write My Fair Lady now," said Lerner, "because I don't feel My Fair Lady now." But he couldn't write what he did feel. Fosse fitted better into the sixties and seventies but he may have died at the right time; his last show, Big Deal, was the only Broadway flop he ever had. Fosse's kind of theatre, a machine that danced, ousted Lerner's, a toy that sang.Now we have a video game that counts: in all currencies.
