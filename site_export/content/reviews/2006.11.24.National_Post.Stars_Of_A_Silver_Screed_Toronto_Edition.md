DIRECTOR'S CUT

Factory Studio Theatre

- - -

Connoisseurs of unwitting irony will find an overflowing potful of the stuff at the Factory Studio. Director's Cut is a play about a writer-directors blighted attempt to make a movie, and it is itself the work of a writer-director. Jim Millan, founder and outgoing chief of Crows Theatre -- a company with a certain reputation but, to put it gently, an uneven track record -- wrote the play himself and has staged it in collaboration with Jillian Kelley, a Newfoundland director who two years ago won the highly prestigious Siminovitch Prize. Both these emperors may have impressive suits of clothes in their wardrobes at home, but they aren't wearing them here.

Stop me if you've heard this one before: A man with a vision sets out to make a film and finds that vision hopelessly compromised by mercenary producers, egocentric actors and -- let us be frank -- his own susceptibility to bullying, flattery and big budgets. The twist here, which I think they hope will kill you, is that the luckless visionary is Canadian while the philistine producer and airheaded stars are American. To be fair, the play never pretends that its all the Yanks fault. We can't tell whether our hero, Jeff, who seems in disarray throughout, has any talent or whether his initial idea had any merit. He wanted to make a lyrical flick about a picturesquely lost young woman and ended with something about a virus with the power to destroy the Internet. Worse, he tries to fuse the two. We know from the start that the movie is a disaster, because the whole thing is told in flashback. There is no suspense and no reason to take an interest, since the characters are not just cartoons but caricatures. (In theatre reviewers' jargon, a cartoon is an idea intentionally simplified to make a satiric point. A caricature is simple and nothing else -- a cartoon with the juice squeezed out.) The dramatic framework has Jeff and his trusty editor making off with the original footage to shoot a documentary about how it all went wrong. They make their way to Hollywood to confront their former producer, and the show ends with the three of them sitting down together for, in effect, a screening of the play weve just seen. Thats the second time in three weeks that this instant- retrospective device has surfaced on a Toronto stage -- the first was The Story of My Life -- and it doesn't look any lovelier the second time around.

The American actors Jeff is lured into hiring are an intense monolingual blond, bizarrely cast as a brooding Italian, and a wired dog-loving comedian who would be bizarrely cast as almost anything. Forest Whitaker is also supposed to be on board (we don't meet him) but he walks out, leaving the team scrambling desperately for someone close by to replace him. This is the cue for the play to start dropping the names of Canadian actors (Paul Gross? Colm Feore? Don McKellar? --No! shouts everybody in unison at the last suggestion) in a bid for laughs thats even more desperate. I can think of just one good play about the movies, Kaufman and Harts Once in a Lifetime; it's one of the funniest plays ever written and it doesn't mention a single real name. I confess I did laugh when the imported starlet expressed puzzlement at the fragrant names of Toronto theatre companies ("Tarragon? Pepper?") but, like everybody else, Ive been laughing at that one for years.

Given the obvious impossibilty of the project on hand, the money- man emerges as the most sympathetic character around; at least hes trying to salvage something and he isn't even particularly obnoxious. In fact he's notably controlled; this gives Ron White, who plays him, a distinct advantage over the rest of the cast, and very smoothly he takes it. Everyone else is left frantically mugging. Gord Rand is an actor who can seem different in every role he plays. He takes versatility to the point of unrecognizability, and as Jeff he is, at least in his first and most frantic scenes, unrecognizably bad. Hes a little more endearing later on when he quietens down. Chris Earle as his sidekick is cheerful in a neutral sort of way, except for a strange bout of monster-impersonation. I suspect that both these performances are fighting for their lives. Of those who act the actors, Amy Rutherford does the eye-fluttering bit efficiently if repetitively, while Ryan McVittie seems even more listless and scattershot than the guy hes playing. Michelle Latimer completes the stage cast as the token Canadian in the film cast; she has little to do and it works out for her. The stage has a false proscenium to make it look like a movie screen. VideoCabaret do all their shows in this kind of frame, but they usually find stylish things to put inside it. Also, their scene changes are virtuoso jobs; the ones we get here, though advertised as providing filmic fluidity, look no different from the hasty transitions we see in nine Toronto productions out of 10. "Be arch, be funny, be clever," the producer instructs his team. This show, a self-indulgent lampoon of self-indulgence, scores one out of three.

- Until Dec. 10. 416-504-9971
