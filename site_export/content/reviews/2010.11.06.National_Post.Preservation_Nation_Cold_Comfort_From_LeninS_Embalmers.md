The two title characters in Lenin's Embalmers are Vladimir Vorobiov, a prickly genius, and Boris Zbarsky, a twisty politician, though political by instinct rather than profession. They are, and historically were, the two scientists charged with the duty of keeping the body of the deceased leader of the Russian Revolution free from rot and fit for public view: not for just an hour, not for just a day, not for just a year, but always. This was a tall, indeed unprecedented order, but they met it. And, life being what it is, they suffered for it later.

Vern Thiessen's play about them starts with a joke about the Gulag. It then gives us Lenin dying, Stalin and Trotsky staking their claims, Stalin winning, and Trotsky fleeing,

all at farcical speed. This is in accordance with the established tradition, which I cannot logically defend but intuitively support, that stories about Stalinism can be funny in a way that stories about Nazism can't. (Yes, there's The Producers, but that's set a long time after the fact.) Stalin wants to preserve Lenin's legend in order to shore up his own. This will not be news to students of the period, though they might be surprised by the play's insistence that Trotsky got ice-picked while Lenin was still fresh in his grave. Or would have been, under normal circumstances.

Both Vladimir (not Lenin, the other one) and Boris were Jews, which accounts for their play being presented by the Harold Green Jewish Theatre Company. It also gives an extra edge to Stalin's animus towards them, though that isn't much explored, and it's not as if Stalin's animuses (is that a word?) ever took much encouraging anyway. For a time, he seems genial towards the embalmers, even pinning medals on them; then he turns, apparently because a drunken Vladimir has kissed him on the mouth while being decorated. He asks to see their files, which is tantamount to a death sentence.

So the play's a cartoon, an intended black farce. But it's jokey without being witty, and its development becomes an exercise in automatic writing. There are these techniques, there's this predestined historical ending (foreseeable even without foreknowledge), and the characters move along on tram tracks. There's a cross-talking pair of secret policemen, who fill in as other supernumeraries. There are three women all called Nadya, a point the play with incorrigible waggishness points out, one of whom keeps talking about her lovers, and who predictably betrays her main squeeze in ways that go beyond the sexual; another of the Nadyas is equally predictably loyal to hers. (Mrs. Lenin is the third Nadya.) The embalmers themselves fight, reconcile and fight again.

The play tells us nothing about the period, or about life in a terror state, that we haven't been told before. The only revelations concern the technical details of the embalming, a process that we see the duo actually carrying out in an ingenious if protracted mime sequence at the end of the first act. Otherwise Geoffrey Brumlik's production appears to be doing what the script asks of it, which is to be clownish and blatant and out front, though some of the supporting cast do this with less finesse than would seem desirable. There is a bit of tension, and even surprise, in the later stages; Vladimir's an ex-Tsarist and Boris an ex-Marxist, and we wonder which of the two will crack and inform on the other first. (The Cheka's techniques seem unhistorically mild, but that's probably part of the joke.) Hardee Lineham, an underused actor who emerges periodically from the shadows to give another excellent performance, gives one of them as Vladimir, angry and entitled, though he's more powerful sober than he is drunk. Martin Julien has less definition as Boris. Janine Theriault plays all the Nadias, and seems most comfortable as the shyest one. If ever an actor was born to play Stalin (I mean that as a compliment) it's the great David Fox, and he sort of delivers here; all the same, he's hemmed in by writing that mainly has him coming on and ordering people's deaths, apparently because it's expected of him. That he does it with an overlay of casual bumbling is obviously meant to add to the horror. It doesn't.
