Every father who is a soldier should think about his children ... They never will live a normal life. Those words are spoken at the beginning of the documentary film Inheritance, shown on PBS at the end of last year, by Monika Hertwig, daughter of Amon Goth, the Nazi death-camp commandant now best known for having been played by Ralph Fiennes in Schindler's List. The same words might also serve as an epigraph for Hannah Moscovitch's play East of Berlin, whose original production from last year is currently being revived at the Tarragon Extra Space in Toronto. Both the play and the documentary present a meeting between a Holocaust survivor and/or her child, and the child of one of the perpetrators.

And so does the movie The Reader. This does feature a perpetrator, and it also has two survivors, mother and daughter; in one scene, the two sides are present in the same courtroom, but they dont actually meet. Their interaction is conveyed to us through a mediating figure, the boy whose future is coloured by having an affair with an older woman who was once a camp guard. And the boys older self is played by the same Ralph Fiennes, whose portrayal of Amon Goth propelled Goth's daughter into contacting Helen Jonas, the Plaszow camp inmate who had initially escaped the gas chambers by being taken by Goth to work in his house and who was rescued from there by Oskar Schindler. Monika wanted -- well, what? To make amends? To seek forgiveness for crimes committed when she was a baby, but still in some sense hers? To seek some kind of common cause with a woman hurt, though far more grievously, by the same man who had blighted her own life? We can never know, and neither can she.

Inheritance brings Monika and Helen together at the site of the camp itself. James Moll's film is a fine and moving one, even if you may harbour some doubts about the TV companys role in setting the whole meeting up. Both women break down, but it seems that its Helen who has to do more of the comforting, even though Monika is a strong and striking figure in her own right.

Years after the war, Monika's mother committed suicide. So did Helen's husband, another survivor, with whom she had made a life in America. This is one of the correspondences between Inheritances true story and the fictitious one that unfolds in Moscovitchs impressive East of Berlin. Brendan Gall plays Rudi, a young German who grew up in Paraguay and discovered his father had been an Auschwitz doctor, responsible for selections at the ramp. Fleeing the knowledge, he goes to Berlin, where he meets and falls in love with Sarah, a young Jewish-American woman, the daughter of survivors; her mother, too, had eventually killed herself. Both feel an appealing streak

of perversity about their relationship, though Sarah is far from knowing the full truth of Rudi's background. When she does discover it, Rudi, who's narrator as well as protagonist, returns to South America. We leave him with a gun in his hand, about to shoot either his father or himself. It would be unfair to reveal which he does, though foreknowledge actually makes the play seem richer.

When Moscovitch saw Inheritance, she was, she told me, amazed and relieved to discover how much she had got right. She already knew, though, that what might seem the most improbable aspect of her play had happened in real life. The viewer of Inheritance develops a longing for Monika and Helen to become friends. It doesnt happen; Helen can muster respect and even sympathy for Monika, but not affection. Moscovitch had read books about second-generation reconciliations between Jews and Germans that did indeed lead to friendships and even love affairs. Writing a play about the Holocaust was, she says, "a terrifying thing to do because Im Jewish." The ethical problems are obvious; and it merges with a technical one. Writing about it directly means dividing your cast between victims and perpetrators. But victims aren't dramatically interesting, because they've been deprived of choice, and concentrating on the perpetrators might seem obscene. The best way to approach it, now, seems to be to focus on the inheritors, whose situation is a little easier to apprehend.

The Reader, Oscar nominations notwithstanding, fails at this, though theres a suicide in this one, too. It works initially as a sexual fantasy (initiation by an older woman) but when the identity of Kate Winslets ex-guard is revealed, it makes everything else in the picture seem trifling, even offensive. Why, in such a context, should we care about the petty torments and equivocations of Fiennes' emotionally blighted lawyer? Was Winslet's cruelty due in any way to her illiteracy, and is her learning to read supposed to be some kind of silver lining? If not, what is the movies conceit? What I feel, what I think, Winslet says, "doesnt matter; the dead are still dead." Well yes, but we knew that. If Winslet's thoughts and feelings are really so unreachable, which is indeed likely, maybe she shouldn't be the subject. When Fiennes goes to visit Lena Olins remarkably poised survivor in her remarkably swank apartment, she tells him that there is nothing to be learned from the camps. True or false, that statement blows up the whole of the preceding film.

East of Berlin leaves its Nazi offstage, but it makes him believable, through the words of his son whose shamefaced monologue is addressed, Moscovitch told me, to an imaginary audience of survivors. I hadnt realized that, but I can see how that premise makes the writing possible and makes it powerful. Approaching the Holocaust at two removes, it passes the Helen Jonas reality test. The Reader doesn't. "There's no catharsis to be found in the camps," Olins character tells us. Nor is there for Moscovitchs characters or for Helen Jonas or Monika Hertwig. But there may, in watching them, be some for us. - East of Berlin runs until Feb. 7. Visit tarragontheatre.comfor information.
