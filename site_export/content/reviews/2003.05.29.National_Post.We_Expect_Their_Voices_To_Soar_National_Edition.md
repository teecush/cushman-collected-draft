THE KING AND I

Festival Theatre, Stratford, Ont.

- - -

The Stratford Festival's opening week of politically incorrect theatre continues with The King and I, the fourth and last of the major Richard Rodgers-Oscar Hammerstein musicals. Rodgers' score is his last great one, and Hammerstein's book, which takes large historical and biographical liberties, is maybe his most ambitious feat of dramatic engineering. It's also an honest attempt, by a 1951 Broadway liberal, to present not so much a clash between two civilizations as a nervous encounter.

The King of Siam, in 1863, imports British governess Anna Leonowens, to help civilize (i.e. Westernize) his offspring and, eventually, his kingdom. This being a musical, the King and the teacher fall in love; this being an uncommon musical, their feelings remain not only unconsummated but undeclared. In the meantime, the show tells us, they learn from one another, and so perhaps, on a personal level, they do. Theoretically, their two cultures are meant to be mutually instructive as well. In practice, it's a one-way street. Everything the show and its audience would endorse -- free speech, the abolition of slavery, respect for authority rather than prostration before it, romantic love -- is here championed by the West and, at best, grudgingly accepted by the East.

The show's cuteness quotient is actually more troublesome now than its cultural complacency, though the two are likely to reinforce one another. I remembered the show's last scene as a deeply moving encounter between two people who never told their love and are about to be parted forever, without so much as a farewell duet to see them through. Very dignified, I'd always thought it. I must have blotted out the intrusive part played in it by the royal brood whose tears, on this occasion, pre-empted my own. I never got past the lump-in-the-throat stage, and I didn't even stay at that point very long.

Frankly, I have always found Getting to Know You, Anna's song- and-prance in the schoolroom, one of the more resistible items in the R&H canon; its jogtrot lilt and doggedly earnest lyric foreshadowing the later degradations of The Sound of Music. The other songs, though, are lovely: peaking musically in the sweeping serenity of Hello, Young Lovers and theatrically in the joyous, foreshortened Shall We Dance.

The show's artistic peak, though, is not a song: It's the ballet The Small House of Uncle Thomas, Tuptim's interpretation of Harriet Beecher Stowe. This sounds like a recipe for disaster -- two ethnic stereotypes for the price of one. But the masks, the sounds, the costumes, the Jerome Robbins choreography (with Eric S. Robertson executing fearsome acrobatic leaps as the wicked king) create a world and a style of their own. Tuptim's dramaturgy is as ruthlessly selective as Hammerstein's own, and her Uncle Tom is a minor figure, present only by right of title.

Debra Hanson's sets are imposingly palatial. The singing is mainly lightweight; the hallowed young lovers (Anne Marie Ramos and Charles Azulay) are pleasant, but their amplified top notes shriek. The two leads are not heavy vocal roles; the singing of Gertrude Lawrence, the original Anna, was erratic, and that of Yul Brynner, the once and forever King, bizarre.

It's the film, with its pitch-perfect dubbing, that has established false expectations, certainly in Anna's case. One does want the actors, though, to sing right up to their own limits, and neither Lucy Peacock nor Victor Talmadge does that. They tend to talk their songs, which makes them not more dramatic but less. Talmadge ruins his A Puzzlement song that way, not helped by fussy staging. Otherwise he makes an excellent king, fast and light without any loss of authority. Peacock, though, never goes out to meet him; she seems touched only at the start of Shall We Dance or when, with her back to us and her reddish-brown hair cascading down it, she listens to Helen Yu, as the King's number one wife, sing Something Wonderful. One wants Anna and the king to be funnier together. If they were, they might go on to break our hearts.
