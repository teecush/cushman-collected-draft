THE DUCHESS OF MALFI

Tom Patterson Theatre, Stratford

- - -

Anyone familiar with The Duchess of Malfi will grasp what I mean when I say that certain scenes in Peter Hinton's production are so dark you can't see your severed hand in front of your face. For those who aren't: John Webster's Jacobean tragedy concerns an Italian noblewoman, a young widow who incurs the murderous wrath of her brothers, a duke and a cardinal, by marrying a second time and marrying beneath her. They arrest her, imprison her and subject her to a prolonged regime of mental torture. This includes turning off the lights and placing the aforementioned hand in hers; showing her a waxworks exhibit of hanged bodies and telling her that they are the corpses of her husband and eldest son; and filling her room with the inmates of the local madhouse. Finally, they take pity on her and have her strangled, along with her waiting-woman and her two remaining infants.

In this magnificently atmospheric production, we don't see these two children, but we do see their tiny coffins afterwards. The sight brings home exactly what has been done. The garroting of the Duchess is carried out in merciless, efficient detail. The effect is horrific but it isn't sensational. It is what the story demands and no more. (A true story, by the way, or so the Jacobeans thought. It even gives a date, 1504. Did I mishear, or has the production changed the date to 1623, when the play was first published?) The same goes for the mad scene, filled with music and literally naked menace, and for the pitch blackness whose last descent is on the play's concluding massacre. The Duchess' death isn't the finish of the play; its last section finds her jailer and chief executioner, Bosola, an ex-galley slave with a discontented scholar's sensibility, repenting and seeking revenge on his employers. He succeeds, to the extent that they both die, but he also brings about his own death and that of Antonio, the Duchess' husband, whom he had hoped to save. It was, he says to the surviving courtiers, "such a mistake as I have seen in a play." It is, indeed, a farce that you don't laugh at. It's also, as Bosola himself knows, just.

Self-awareness runs through the play. T.S. Eliot's poem about the death-obsessed Webster who "saw the skull beneath the skin" is quoted in all the handbooks, but it may be more important that he heard the beating mind as well. Hinton's production, in black-and- silver designs by Carolyn M. Smith, is wonderfully alive to the sights of Webster's world; it is equally alive to the sounds, hauntingly assembled by Peter Hannan to reflect an author whose favourite image seems to have been the screech owl. The most important sounds, though, are the shifting moods of the text, the way in which passion, despair and metaphysical wit keep treading on one another's toes. This production knows when to roar, when to whisper, when to laugh, and when to be still. It is extraordinarily of a piece. The actors are white-faced, an effect that seemed intrusive in Hinton's production of Into the Woods, but works perfectly here. And he picks up superbly on details. The Duchess' invocation, in her farewell to Antonio, of the classical ferry across the River Styx inspires the staging of the whole scene in a boat crammed with aristocratic refugees. The Cardinal mentions a ghostly old woman who appears at moments of family doom; the text briefly supplies a gossipy old bawd: they are here combined into a living memento mori, sitting in wait for us as we enter the theatre.

About the only lapse in style is the scene after the Duchess' death when Bosola, a conscience waiting to happen, goads Ferdinand, the incestuously-driven Duke, into an awareness of what he has done, pushing him from the jealous edge of madness into the abyss. Both actors rant and rush it; the rhythm of the scene goes, so do the modulations, and so, incidentally, does the double cynicism of Bosola's demand to be paid and Ferdinand's crazed offer to buy him off with a pardon for the murder that he himself instigated. It's unfortunate, since this may be the greatest scene in the play, but it isn't destructive.

At any rate, Scott Wentworth's Bosola comes safely out the other side; sardonic, melancholy, mercurial, jovial and blisteringly honest, he is master of all the play's moods. None of the other performances matches his, except perhaps Laura Condolln's maid who goes biting and scratching to her death. Peter Donaldson's Cardinal is authoritative but hardly haunted; Paul Essiembre's Ferdinand is petulant rather than possessed; and Shane Carty's Antonio is just dull. He may suffer, though, from the director's conviction that the Duchess, even in love, is always the great lady, always in command. This unfortunately plays into the schoolmistressy side of Lucy Peacock's personality; this Duchess hardly seems to need a lover, let alone to marry one. She lacks mischief. She is very fine in captivity, rising above her persecutors, but she doesn't have much to rise from.

For once in my life, though, I can forgive individual performances for the sake of everyone's adherence to a magnificent conception (as opposed to a mere concept). Some things may be fainter than one would like, but nothing goes missing. Webster's plays, maybe more than those of any other playwright, demand that a production create a world. This one does, and right up to the ominous last-line coronation of the Duchess' and Antonio's surviving son -- who must be the youngest, smallest child ever allowed upon a professional stage -- it never lets go.
