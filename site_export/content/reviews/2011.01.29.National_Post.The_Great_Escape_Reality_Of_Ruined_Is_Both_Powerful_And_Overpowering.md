In Ruined, form and content are at war and content wins. Lynn Nottage's play is set in what it hurts to call the Democratic Republic of Congo, and its constructed on the naturalistic Broadway model: well-made except for some loose ends. But its subject is endless civil war with the issues hopelessly muddied and with nearly everyone an actual or potential victim. The women, of course, suffer the most. Its their agony that stays with you, rather than the specific story the author has chosen to tell.

The title refers to the state of a woman's body after months of being used as a soldiers' sex slave. (The forces may be government or rebel; from a civilians point of view, theyre interchangeable.) It can also serve as a metaphor for the whole country. By the end of the play it's attached itself -- in one of several moments that are short-term effective and long-term inconclusive--to the central character, who's known as Mama Nadi and who is a kind of stationary Mother Courage. Where Bertolt Brechts protagonist pulled a food wagon, Nottages runs a bar and brothel. Theoretically, it's a conflict-free zone; both sides are welcome, both have to lay down their weapons before being served. But, of course, things don't always work out that neatly or that safely. Like Mother Courage, Mama Nadi has a persistent male admirer and even the chance of marriage. Unlike her, she has no children of her own and--this being an American play -- a heart of gold beneath the businesslike exterior. Brechts Mama was complicit in the system that oppressed her; her compulsive haggling led to the death of her own son. Theres a comparable scene in Nottage's play, but here the shuffling has been displaced onto another character. And where Mother Courage went on hauling her wagon regardless, Mama Nadi -- this being also a Pulitzer-winning American play -- gets a redemptive coda that seems to have been tacked on from some other script.

There are, we're told, 10 girls in Mamas establishment but, for obvious if frustrating economic reasons, we only meet three of them. Theres Josephine, already in residence when we get there, and two who are brought in during the first scene by Christian, a long-standing friend and business associate of Mama's. One of these is his niece Sophie, raped by a bayonet and hence "ruined"; the other, Salima, though technically spared that state, may have suffered even worse. As she already has a full house Mama refuses to take them, but soon (see heart of gold, above) she relents. Sophie, who's educated, becomes her resident singer, her bookkeeper and her favourite; she is, in one of the plays most moving threads, desperately saving up for a curative operation. Salima, after escaping from months of servitude chained to a tree, was cast out by her husband; Mama protects her, too, when he comes looking for her. This, through a neat telescoping of plot lines, gets Mama into serious trouble with the army, but its another action that's set up and not followed through. Horror is defused by a "powerful" speech in which one of the characters turns herself into a metaphor. This supposedly shames all the others. If only.

Mama is terrifically played by Yanna McIntosh whose only conceivable flaw is that it's almost impossible to think ill of her. We know shes going to do the right thing, because shes Yanna McIntosh. In her actual performance, though, she never gives a sentimental inch; she's flinty and earthy and even her most generous moments have an appropriate air of grudge about them. She's also brilliantly expressive with the African accent, which isnt her own. The only other performance that approaches her level of reality is Sabryn Rocks Sophie, not so much when she speaks as in the way, wounded and distant, she moves among the men in the bar. She sings affectingly, too, though Philip Akin's direction seems uncertain whether to treat her songs as action or as commentary. The production (a joint venture by Obsidian and Nightwood) is sympathetic but slow; it moves haltingly from one scene to another, and though it builds tension it never squeezes it. It's weakest in the crucial scene in which Marci T. Houses jaunty, jealous Josephine listens to the stories of her two new companions. Each of the actresses seems to be in her own world. Salimas narrative can hardly help being harrowing, and in Sophia Walker's performance it duly harrows. But it would do so even more if she actually looked at the person she was talking to, and if she didn't keep pausing for effect like a character in a play.

Sterling Jarvis is quietly, sometimes too quietly, delightful as Uncle Christian, though he too suffers from his authors reluctance to follow a theme through. Hes a teetotaller, because he knows from experience what happens when he isn't; then, in a reasonably tense scene, he is, almost literally, driven to drink by a hulking suspicious general (Andre Sills). Thereafter, he drinks like a fish, but the play never capitalizes on the connection. He might as well be three different people. It's an OK play in an OK production, with one more than OK performance. The reality dwarfs it; but it also gives it power.
