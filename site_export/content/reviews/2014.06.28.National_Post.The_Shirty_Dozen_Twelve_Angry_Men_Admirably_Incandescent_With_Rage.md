It's no great surprise that Soulpepper's production of Twelve Angry Men should turn out to be a spellbinder, not to say a humdinger. This TV play turned movie turned stage play pours all the traditional excitement of a courtroom drama into the jury-room adjacent, where it shows the tension to be even higher. A conventional trial drama offers us a series of one-on-one confrontations, with only the judge permitted to interrupt. Reginald Rose's play gives us a dozen characters in non-stop interaction over a matter of, literally, life-anddeath, enmities and alliances forming and re-forming, everyone's thoughts and feelings in continual flux. All that's needed to make it work is 12 good actors and it's true - the company in the Distillery has a plurality of these on tap and can order in the rest.

Actually, it has 12 plus one, since there's a 13th character, the guard who ushers the jurors in and out of their conferring chamber. He's very well played, too, by Andre Sills; I don't know if Alan Dilworth's production is intending to make a point by casting a black actor in this purely functional role; but one is made anyway. In Rose's play, which in its original TV form dates back to 1954, the case on trial is one of murder; a 16-year-old stands accused of knifing his father and will go to the chair if found guilty. Father and son belong, according to the more truculent jurors, to "these people"; their precise ethnicity is never specified (black? Puerto Rican?) but whatever it is, it has no representatives among the 12 males charged with reaching a verdict. As is well known, 11 of the deciders begin the deliberations convinced that that verdict should be guilty. The one holdout takes very seriously the judge's instruction about guilt needing to be established "beyond reasonable doubt"; the play tracks his efforts to persuade the others of just how reasonable his own doubts are and why they should share them.

Now, as in the 1950s, we accept his side as being that of the angels. It's a valid criticism of the play that it makes a safe case, one that few members of any likely theatre audience would dispute. The reasonable doubter, known to the program as Juror 8, is an architect by trade, though you may feel he missed a vocation or two; he should have been a private detective or a public defender. He ferrets out all kinds of possible holes in the prosecution's case, all of them mysteriously ignored by the prisoner's own court-appointed lawyer who had either decided the case was unwinnable or was just staggeringly incompetent. The play needs these assumptions in order to function, and it piles them on pretty thick. But we forgive them; the play's moral passion still glows while its mechanics are both physically and psychologically thrilling.

Dilworth's production renders the play's melodrama exciting by initially downplaying it. Yannik Larivee's set is a plain room on a strip of stage in the middle of the auditorium, with the audience on two sides. It's a hot day, waiting to rain; the storm will break, spectacularly, as we watch: the show's one technical indulgence, unless you count the pastiche TV theme music that Richard Feren has designed in cock-eared tribute to the play's origins. The actors troop on and take their places at a long table, reeking (the air-conditioning's on the blink) of the 1950s; with their shirt sleeves and their hair-partings, the performers are so completely absorbed into the period that even those we know by heart are hard to recognize, (Larivee's costumes are wonderful). The least colourful, at least initially, is the one who might be called the hero; as played by Stuart Hughes he's not the Henry Fonda type of the decent crusader (Fonda created the role) but a man whose conscience nags first at him and then at everybody else. It isn't that he believes the boy innocent, just that he isn't convinced he's guilty. He's fighting for the principle of reasonable doubt and also, as the debate goes on, for the souls of his opponents. This is a modest, tenacious performance that doesn't fully declare itself until the end - in one sharp mind-clinching line and then in compassionate silence - and that grows in the mind when the play is over.

Some of his conquests are fairly easy, men with sympathetic experience of being underdogs. Others are good-hearted or maybe just affable enough to be won over. The play is very hot on civic duty; the clearest exemplar of this is the jury-foreman who says little beyond calling his unruly, and often violent, flock to order but whose role, as played by Tony DeSantis, is shown to be pivotal; when his vote changes, so does the play.

This leaves three holdouts. One, played with brutal, loud-mouthed virulence by William Webster, is the room's one out-and-out racist, accurately pegged, in what may be the play's best line, as a man who always needs to have someone to hate; today, he'd be a compulsive contributor to online forums. At the opposite extreme of propriety is a businessman who prides himself on clear thinking, and is the only one who declines to take his jacket off; Tim Campbell's performance, implacably courteous behind his gleaming glasses and seemingly baked into his suit, would be the completest performance, if it were not for Joseph Ziegler as the angriest man of all, Hughes' ultimate opponent, the one whose weak spot he finds, probes, and may even attempt to heal. Ziegler's rage has a sociological veneer, but is deeply personal. Like another paterfamilias, the Irish patriarch whom Ziegler so devastatingly played in A Whistle in the Dark, this one self-destructs while ranting on top of a table. This being a more liberally hopeful play than Whistle, he's afforded a kind of redemption in surrender. It might all seem too neat, but the acting and the writing make it seem logical, raw power burning itself out before our eyes. This is a frightening, pitiful, self-transforming performance in a play of which the word "decent" can, for once, be honourably used.

Twelve Angry Men is at the Young Centre for the Performing Arts until July 19.
