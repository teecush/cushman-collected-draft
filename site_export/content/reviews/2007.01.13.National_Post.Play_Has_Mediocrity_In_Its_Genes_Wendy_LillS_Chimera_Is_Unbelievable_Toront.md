Hows this for a sample of realistic telephone conversation? It's drawn from Wendy Lill's new play, Chimera.

FIRST MAN: Youre not serious. This is a joke, right? Where did you hear this?

SECOND MAN: I heard it from my son-in-laws best friend. He grew up in White Cloud.

FIRST MAN: Your son-in-law's best friend? Grew up in White Cloud? Unbelievable.

Unbelievable is right. We don't, in the play itself, actually hear Second Mans contribution to the exchange, and phone chats in which only one of the participants is audible are notoriously hard to dramatize. One character always ends up repeating, for the audiences benefit, what the other has just told him. All the same, this specimen is quite exceptionally clunky. The clunkiness is unfortunately typical of the way in which the people in Chimera talk to each another, even when they're both in the same room with only a desk or table between them. Which is the unexciting formation in which these characters, in various combinations, find themselves for far toomuch of the evening.

The play is one of those in which the author takes an Issue, and dumps a collection of representative types on stage to deal with it. In this case the topic is stem-cell research, or a particularly contentious subset of it: the possible emergence, sometime in the future, of a crossbreed of human and animal, named after the mongrel monster of Greek mythology known as the Chimera. What may be setting off this possibility is the implanting, for medical research purposes, of embryonic human cells into the brains of rhesus monkeys. The author's method of humanizing her characters is to endow each of them with a few hand-me-down hang-ups to be dragged out at convenient moments. This has the unlooked-for effect of short circuiting the debate and preventing the ethical questions from being properly aired. Discussion may not be the highest form of drama (Shaw, with whom its generally identified, was less a dialectician than a crazy comedian in whiskers) but it is preferable to soap opera.

Lill is helpfully frank about her own method. She has one of her creations, a lobbyist who at first seems irrelevant but is later revealed as the plays evil genius, outline his strategy: "First, one assembles a cast of characters. The inexperienced cabinet minister, the fundamentalist MP and we mustn't forget the muckraking reporter trying to get back in the game." (That we mustn't forget is sinister cardboard-villain talk at its most outrageous.)Well, then. The inexperienced minister is saddled with the Justice portfolio, and, though all in favour of progress, shes faced by a confusion in the law over whether it might be permissible for monkey cells to be imported into humans as well as vice versa. The fundamentalist member (for White Cloud -- "Alberta" as he has to keep explaining), who sits opposite her, spearheads the Unfair to Apes and Unclean for Humans campaign and amasses a lot of popular support until he gets up in the House and a) is outed as a former geography teacher who taught his pupils creationism; and b) starts quoting from that fine old anthem Who Put the Bop (In the Bop-She- Bop-She-Bop), which on his lips sounds ridiculous. The muckraking scribe attracts the hostility of everyone in the play, which suggests hes doing his job right, and seems intended as the play's protagonist without having been properly groomed for the position.

He forms something of a bond with the scientist conducting the actual research, a septuagenarian lady from the North of England, who believes that she may be on the way to finding a cure for autism. This seems to me an unanswerable argument, but nobody else in the play ever engages with it. This, I suppose, is true to life, or at least to life as presented on talk shows where people keep pushing their own line without taking any notice of anyone else's. One feels like shouting, as off-stage voices in this plays parliamentary scenes constantly do, Answer the question. Of course nobody ever does, in the real world or on stage, but on stage, especially in what purports to be a play of ideas, is one place where one would like them to try.

The playwright, herself a former NDP MP, has decent impulses. She extends sympathy to most of her characters, including the rightwing Christian with whom she can have little politically -- though maybe something philosophically -- in common. She also builds up a reasonable head of narrative steam by the end of her first act. But it goes off the boil, done in by flaccid dialogue and by-the- numbers construction. This is another of those plays, assembled in short fidgety scenes, that leave you feeling that their subjects would be better treated on television. The writing probably wouldnt be any better, but the production values might lend an air of documentary urgency. And maybe it would be better in the theatre to approach such a subject as documentary rather than to invent a Chimerical case and try to pump feeling into it

Presumably by way of reflecting the genetic theme, all the characters have personal issues about the issue. The scientist is estranged from her son, an actor. The childless minister, putting her career first, once had an abortion and wonders if she did the right thing. Her aide squabbles with his wife about daycare and ear infections. Even the lobbyist flashes pictures of his grandchildren. And the journalist, himself a childhood friend of the minister's, had a twin brother afflicted with Down's syndrome and carries a load of guilt about how he behaved to him. This gives him a repentant curtain line thats meant to touch the heart but comes across like perfect kitsch.

Mary Vingoes staging switches from one conversation to another, and from one side of the stage to the other, in a manner that seems trudging and even grudging. Nor does her direction do much to help the performers who are left trying to put a brave face on material in which they seem to find it hard to believe. That at least was the impression left on Tuesday when actors one knows to be good were continually fumbling (not forgetting) their lines, which is generally a sign of underlying discomfort. In fact, the one who did best was the only one new to me, Joan Gregson, whose tart accent helps her authenticity no end. David Fox as the Bible-thumper imposes himself, as he usually does, through force of personality (by the way, I've yet to read a review of Snow Cake that does justice to -- or even mentions -- his massively moving performance in that picture) and David Jansen puts some cynical flesh on the hungry bones of the reporter. Geoffrey Pounsett and John Dolan are somewhat defeated and Philippa Domville almost entirely so. The biologist lady, presumably speaking for her author, cites Darwin, not as preaching the survival of the fittest but as saying, more or less, "only connect." It's a noble idea, but connective tissue is precisely what this show lacks.

As many readers have pointed out, the "unidentified woman" seen with George Gershwin in the picture that accompanied last week's column was in fact the young Ginger Rogers. I have also to apologize for a mistake in the article itself: The neo-Gershwinian confection My One and Only is being staged this year at the Stratford Festival, not the Shaw.
