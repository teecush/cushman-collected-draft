LEO

Tarragon Extra Space

- - -

There is a special joy in hearing and seeing a piece of theatre unfold at top speed with all its moments packed and none of them blurred. That's what happens in Leo, an unflaggingly accurate production of an outstanding new play.

The playwright, Rosa Laborde, is the Canadian child of a refugee Chilean family. Her play depicts three young people growing up in Chile before, during and just after the Allende years. There is Rodrigo, an idealistic political activist, and Isolda, a pragmatic political activist. Finally there is Leo, a poet, for which Rodrigo admires him, and apolitical, for which Isolda despairs of him. "Thank God," says Isolda to Leo, "there are the Rodrigos in the world. Imagine what would happen if there was just you and me." To which Leo quietly replies "I do."

That, in a deft and devastating way wholly typical of the play, shifts the ground, from the political to the personal. Rodrigo, Isolda and Leo are close friends; they are also interlocked lovers. In the course of the play each of them has sex with each of the other two. Isolda and Rodrigo are engaged -- since the age of 11, apparently -- but there are times when Rodrigo's benevolent fanaticism is too much for her and she turns to Leo instead. He reciprocates, but she is concerned when she hears him murmur Rodrigo's name in bed. Each member of the trio has a moment of jealousy, finding out about the other two. The tensions disturb them but do not destroy them. They have, after all, other, desperate things to worry about.

Leo may be a political skeptic but he has an interest in fashioning a rebellious ancestry for himself. He claims that his father fought alongside Che Guevara in Bolivia and vanished in the Bermuda Triangle. (The truth is considerably less romantic.) One way or another, he's an easy mark for Rodrigo's enthusiasm, and when the Pinochet coup comes, he finds himself following the love of his life (Isolda, after being captured, presumably tortured and released, has fled the country.) In this respect the play resembles Kiss of the Spider Woman; ideologically uncommitted men sacrificing themselves for their male lovers' principles seem to be a Latin American tradition. But this may be a coincidence. And if it is derivative, who cares? The play unfolds within the title character's memory; at any rate it begins and ends with Leo, tied to a chair and beaten by invisible hands, spitting out possibly meaningless words like "imperialism," "socialism" and 'totalitarianism" ... "words to live by ... words to kill for." Philosophically, I would guess that Laborde is closest to Isolda, the humane empiricist, but she has entered into each of her characters equally.

The play's emphasis on triangles -- Bermudan and eternal --has inspired Graeme S. Thomson to design a three-cornered set, with the audience on all sides, and Richard Rose to direct a similarly shapely and hard-edged production. Scene succeeds scene with an exhilarating combination of velocity and detail, grace and force. (The play is billed at 75 minutes and comes in shorter.) We are never allowed to forget that these doomed exploding characters are terribly, pitiably young.

Cara Pifko, very fresh and immediate, does her best stage work as Isolda; Sergio Di Zio captures both the strength and the naivete of Rodrigo (watch his shoulders shake as he gets stoned for the first time); and Salvatore Antonio, whose sharp nose and inquiring chin give him a bizarre resemblance to Rowan Atkinson, is a brilliantly mercurial Leo. Actually the three work so well together, both in the writing and the acting, that assigning them individual qualities feels arbitrary. Their chemistry is infused with what I take to be an authentically South American kind of wit. "The truth is," says Isolda to the boys, "that alone neither of you is all that much but if you were combined you would make the perfect man."

"I've had that same thought," says Rodrigo.

"It might not," says Leo dryly, "have been what she meant." Marcelo Puente's guitar music suits so well that I hardly registered it was there. It's embarrassing to admit but I can't think of a thing to say against any aspect of the show.
