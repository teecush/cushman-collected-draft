HOCKEY MOM, HOCKEY DAD

Factory Theatre Studio, TORONTO

- - -

TORONTO - A lonely man and a lonely woman, each of them separated from a former partner, find themselves occupying the same space for one morning every week. So what do you think is going to develop between them? Well, in real life, probably nothing: People are notorious for their skill at missing boats. But this is a play, and it isn't by Chekhov, so something has to happen.

The options are of course limited. We know there will be some kind of romantic involvement. It will end happily, or unhappily, or with a question mark; everything has been done before. But there are good reasons why it has; given charm, humour and performing skill, we can hardly help being interested, even involved.

Michael Melski's Hockey Mom, Hockey Dad has these qualities, plus a setting that's commonplace in the outside world but unusual to the point of being exotic in the theatre -- a double recommendation. As you will undoubtedly have guessed, our two characters, Teddy and Donna, encounter one another at ungodly hours on Saturday mornings while escorting their respective sons to hockey practice. The boys are already on the ice by the time each scene has begun; we never see them but we hear a lot about them. We watch their parents shivering in the bleachers, and making whatever supportive noises come naturally to them. Teddy bellows encouragement; Donna lets hers out in a deliciously self-conscious monotone.

They live in a small town in Cape Breton. Teddy is a school janitor and Donna a supply teacher. Teddy is rough, in a cuddly sort of way, and very ready. Donna is quiet and withdrawn. So it's Teddy who has to make the running, while Donna, who carries more emotional baggage than he does and certainly hugs it closer, begins to thaw -- as she must if there is to be any action at all.

The author is adept at filling in details of the characters' off- stage lives, Donna's especially; when she hints what a struggle it has been to find the money for her son's equipment but how she had to do it anyway, we very much get the picture, economically and emotionally.

Whether we are intended to see the whole Teddy-Donna relationship as a kind of hockey game, I cannot say. It seems to me the play toys with the idea whenever it wants a handy metaphor, but drops it very quickly. It's much better at showing how the practicalities of the game -- from carpooling to violence -- affect the lives of all four characters: the two on stage and the two just off it.

David Ferry's Teddy looks his name: chubby, weathered and enduring. A specialist in the comedy of desperation, he feels disappointment keenly, and has obviously known a lot of it, but he tries breezily to rise above it. (As cheerleader for a team that loses every game, he needs to.) Ferry's ebullience rarely lets up, and his energy never, but there are nuances within the bluster.

Nothing in Irene Poole's performance of Donna can top, or even equal, her first fish-out-of-water moment: one that leaves it ambiguous whether she is doing her earnest best to play the approved version or is simply making mock of the whole concept. But her subsequent delicate wariness, hardening under pressure, is both accurate and effective. Her character is given more of a chance to develop; she may seem the more passive, but in fact it's she who acts and Teddy who reacts.

It was probably a mistake to give the slender play an intermission -- then again, it may go with the subject matter -- but the first, funnier half, since we are forced to consider them that way, is his and the second hers. It's in this half that the play comes nearest to making the game itself resonate. We learn things about Donna that seem predictable in retrospect (a tricky concept, I know) but strike home at the time. Violence is the issue; she hates it and blames Teddy for exposing her son to it; he argues that it's a good toughening process. Both have a case, and as they talk passionately past one another you want them, more than at any other point of the evening, to be together.

Rosemary Dunsmore's direction wastes not a single moment, either during the scenes or between them; and Glenn Davidson's set is good enough to freeze your toes off. The play has already had an extensive life out East, and looks a very commercial proposition. I mean that in the nicest way.
