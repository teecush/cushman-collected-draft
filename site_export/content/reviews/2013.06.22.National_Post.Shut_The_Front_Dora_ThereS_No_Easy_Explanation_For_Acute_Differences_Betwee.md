Next Monday sees the annual presentation of Toronto theatre's dora Awards. As on all such occasions, the list of nominees provokes a mix of headnodding ("now, that's a good choice"), brow-smiting ("hey, I'd forgotten that one"), and eye-rolling ("what were they thinking?").

For the city's newspaper critics, these reactions can be especially acute, since for the last three years we've been giving our own prizes. The processes are rather different. The doras have a complicated nominating and voting process, involving multiple jurors from within the business, climaxing in a starstudded ceremony, for which you can buy tickets. The critics - three from the daily papers, two from the weeklies - foregather informally in some place of refreshment, and argue among ourselves until a consensus is reached. We announce the results, and a few weeks later re-assemble to hand out certificates to the chosen, all of whom know why they're there. There is absolutely no suspense. It's rather pleasant.

In each year so far, there's been very little overlap between critics' choices and dora nominations; which, awards being fallible by definition, is probably a good thing. This year, for example, the critics' awards in four categories went to a production called Terminus, a ticklish situation for me, since the play was directed and partproduced by a member of my own family. This meant I had to keep recusing myself. (I didn't have to have leave the room; I just sat in a corner, occasionally murmuring, "Oh well, if you insist.") In the doras, by contrast, Terminus appears in only one category.

The critics' choice for best new Canadian play was This Is War, Hannah Moscovitch's tight and powerful Afghanistan piece. It doesn't even figure in the dora nominations though another Moscovitch piece, Little One, does. Among the five plays on the dora list, Little One might well have my vote too, given that they've also left off Michael Healey's political satire Proud; I liked that one less than I liked Generous and Courageous, the two previous instalments of Healey's adjectival trilogy, but I still liked it.

The doras' other best play nominees range from bemusing to stupefying: Kristen Thomson's Something Else, which had stunning scenes of marital combat, but didn't hang together as a whole; Melody A. Johnson's Miss Caledonia, a lightweight acting vehicle for its author (also gaining her a deserved best actress nomination); and daniel MacIvor's Arigato, Tokyo which, despite a striking production such as we have come to expect from Brendan Healey at Buddies in Bad Times, was paralyzingly boring; though betting men might be well advised to consider it, given the huge number of categories (eight in all) for which it's been nominated. disgracefully absent from all dora categories was Jordi Mand's Between the Sheets, which was pin-drop tense, deserving nods (at the very least) for writing, acting, direction and design. It really wasn't such a bad season for new plays as the doras would have us believe.

Outsiders have long berated the doras for squeezing all acting nominees into two categories, male and female, without regard to whether they were leading, supporting, musical or framatic; they then further confounded the issue by including votes for ensemble, meaning they didn't like to choose between the actors in a show. This year they've gone some way to remedying this by giving the ensembles their own category, the nominees including such worthy candidates as Canadian Stage's THIS (though I would have singled out Alon Nashman, as the critics actually did) and Soulpepper's Rosencrantz and Guildenstern Are Dead (though I would have preferred a solo shoutout for Ted dykstra). There's also a citation for the Second City gang, though I can't see that their teamwork was any more admirable this year than it usually is. It all seems very arbitrary. I'm rooting for THIS, though, because I thought it was the season's best imported play, and the doras don't have a category for that.

As for the solo actors: Among the ladies, Maev Beaty is running against herself, as in truth she was in the critics' poll; we gave it to her for Proud, though she was equally deserving for Terminus. (I think I can allow myself to say that.) Actually she won our prize for supporting performance - we do allow ourselves those distinctions - while our best leading actress choice was Michelle Monteith in Little One, though it was frankly a toss-up between that and her equally uncanny performance in The Lesson - for which she has been doranominated in the independent (i.e. smaller theatre) division. yes, I know it's confusing. Anyway, Beaty and Monteith have been the two most exciting young actresses in Toronto for some years now, and it's good to see them being widely recognized, especially alongside Moscovitch, in whose plays they both first surfaced. Also in the dora actress stakes are Johnson, as mentioned, Clare Coulter for her remarkably moving King Lear, and Irene Poole in The Little Years: a strong team.

The doras don't agree with us so much about the men. Among those they've nominated, my faves would be Nigel Shawn Williams in Race and Tom rooney, taking time off from his apparently effortless brilliance at Stratford, to supply similar in Something Else. Stuart Hughes, critics' choice for The Crucible, isn't up there, though Albert Schultz's production is, deservedly, both for best direction and best production. (That means best all-around thing.) directing competition includes Chris Abraham, twice. richard rose should be there for This Is War, but isn't. unsurprisingly unnominated was Alan dilworth's La Ronde, the most underrated staging of the year, with a brilliant set by Lorenzo Savoini, who is also not nominated, though he is for The Crucible.

The doras may be stingier than the critics with acting prizes, but they're more generous regarding design; they have separate categories for sets, costumes, lighting and sound, while we lump them together in a single package. And maybe that's fairer. Actors get a lot of attention anyway,whilebackstagepeople don't. Maybe one day there'll be a dora for stage managers. As Adam Brazier has recently pointed out, they work harder than anyone. each of the dora design categories is dominated by a single person, Kimberly Purtell (lighting) getting three nominations out of five.

There's some confusion in the musical theatre and opera divisions: sometimes they overlap, sometimes they don't. Let me just note that best musical actor nominees include critical winners Bruce dow and darrin Baker (leading and supporting respectively, for us); that I wish some space could have been found for Christopher Siebert in La Cage Aux Folles, even if he was just passing through; and that I was glad to see Falsettos (musical) and Dialogue Aux Carmelites (opera) nominated for best production in their respective categories, though I was sad to see no mention of their respective directors, robert McQueen and robert Carsen. In the latter case, I'm more than sad; I'm incensed. Dialogue aux Carmelies was the COC's finest production of a fine season, maybe their finest production in years. It may also have been the best thing seen last season on any Toronto stage. robert.cushman@hotmail.com The Dora Awards are at the Bluma Appel Theatre on Monday June 24.
