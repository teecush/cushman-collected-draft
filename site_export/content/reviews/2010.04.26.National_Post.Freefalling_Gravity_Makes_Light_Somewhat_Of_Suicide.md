Robert Louis Stevenson wrote a sequence of short stories called The Suicide Club about an organization of people bent on doing themselves in, and about the heroic detective -- a European prince in private life -- who stops them. The stories have been adapted, with varying degrees of freedom, many times. The new TV series Gravity is, knowingly or not, the latest variation on the theme.

The suicide club it presents has aims the very reverse of Stevenson's. It's a court-ordered support group for people who have tried and failed to kill themselves: a sort of Suicides Anonymous, aimed of course at discouraging them from trying it again. In the opening sequencetwoprincipalcharacters are seen qualifying for membership. Robert Collingsworth (Ivan Sergei), a New York ophthalmologist grieving the death of his wife, drives off the edge of a cliff, to which he has been meticulously guided by GPS. There must, though, be a mercy clause built into the system, because he takes a nose dive into the pool of a gay cruise ship, whose clientele are none too pleased at having their space invaded.

Meanwhile, Lily (Krysten Ritter), who also works in the healing industry but at a less prestigious level ( "I sell makeup at a department store; I change lives," she says repeatedly), tries to off herself with, as far as I could make out, chocolate cake chased with codeine. She's rushed to hospital in time, her near-death experience involving falling in love with a non-suicidal fellow patient who seems to be a minor criminal. So at least says the policeman who spies on her, and seems to have been appointed, or to have appointed himself, her caseworker. He's as seedy and possibly disturbed as Stevenson's princely sleuth was elegant and efficient. He's played by Eric Schaeffer, whose other credits on the show include writer, director and producer. It is, I think we can assume, his baby.

It's a mongrel. The promotional material calls it a "dramedy:" a literally unspeakable word signifying a misbegotten thing. Comedy and drama can of course be combined to rich effect, as much of the best TV, current and recent, can testify. But, as any many suicide survivors could also testify, it can be hard to get the dosage right. Like a lesser Six Feet Under, itself my least favourite of the major HBO shows, Gravity, which might be subtitled "or levity," alternates rather than combines, resulting in drama that keeps undermining itself and comedy that's not so much dark as queasy. Robert's predicament is set up for laughs, Lily's for chills. Nevertheless, the two of them, right after their very first group encounter, become an item. Or at least they get it on, right there in the street. This leads to the most entertaining scene in either of the first two episodes. Robert's a Catholic, and his lapse sends him hurrying to confession. The officiating priest doesn't see how it can be adultery, given that Robert no longer has a wife, and is bored with the conversation anyway; he'd rather be placing bets over his iPhone. His message is less "go and sin no more," than, "get a life, preferably sexual." This is certainly the funniest confessional scene I've ever seen. It also features the most Jewish-sounding of Catholic priests. He's played, in what sadly seems to be a one-off guest appearance, by Robert Klein, who comes honestly by both the comedy and the Jewishness.

The group itself is presided over, and out-acted by, Ving Rhames as a former baseball player in a wheelchair. He will doubtless get even more commanding in the weeks ahead. The other members include a former model and apprentice cougar (Rachel Hunter) who is obviously being set up for future development; and a housewife (Robyn Cohen) whose problem, in stark contrast to those of her fellows, is that her life is too perfect. She gets a lot of attention in Episode 2 but doesn't really justify it. Robert has issues with his mother; Lily with her father, whom it's possible she misjudges. Gravity isn't terrible; I'm not actively opposed to finding out what happens to its characters. But I can't say I care much either.
