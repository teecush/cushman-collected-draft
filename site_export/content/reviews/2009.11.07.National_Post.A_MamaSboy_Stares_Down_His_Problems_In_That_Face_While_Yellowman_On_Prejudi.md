Ishould have sent you to boarding school" (or words to that effect) says Hugh, whos something successful in high finance, to his teenage son, Henry, who at that moment, and not altogether by his own choice, is wearing one of his mother's dresses. Hugh's words are weighed down with old irony since Henrys sister, Mia, has been sent to a boarding school, and weve already seen her there, assisting in the torture of a younger girl, apparently as part of an initiation ceremony. Henry isn't even in day school anymore, having dropped out to look after his mother, Martha, a pill-popping alcoholic mental case. Hugh hasn't been around to help, having taken off for points East and an Asian girlfriend.

This happy family is the subject of That Face, a prize-winning play by the then 19-year-old British writer Polly Stenham. In its Canadian premiere, from Nightwood Theatre, it comes over as a talented work but not an achieved one, tense and sparky in much of its dialogue but lopsided in construction. The torture scene at the beginning turns out to have no purpose beyond getting the play off to an arresting start. It begs questions: If this initiation is traditional practice, did Mia herself once undergo it and, if so, how did it affect her? Her partner-in-crime is briefly pressed into service as Henrys first girlfriend, thereby bringing out all the helpless possessiveness in his mother, and both girls may be meant to illustrate a pervasive callousness in privileged British youth: not that recent cases in Canada have given us any reason to feel superior. But the plot and character points could have been made in other, more economical ways.

Its the relationship between sick mother and caregiving son that turns out to be the play's main concern. We first discover them, the morning after one of Martha's binges, Henry sprawled across the foot of her bed. It looks for the longest time as if we have incest on our hands but this turns out to be a false trail. Technically, that is; Martha, who puts the "mother in smother," is happy to believe that Henrys gay, since it robs her of the threat of female competition. Meanwhile, Mia, whos long given up on Martha but loves Henry, stalks the outskirts of the play, giving off alternating signals of cruelty and compassion. Suspense hinges on whether Martha will go voluntarily into care or whether she'll be consigned there by Hugh, a cold but, in the circumstances, forgive-able fish.

Kelly Thornton's production is choppy, both physically and psychologically; the evenings pitted with the blank spots that actors feel compelled to fill with unfocused emoting. Sonja Smits has attracted a hailstorm of abuse but in fact her performance is the best in the play, admirably exposing Marthas imperiousness, vulnerability and the shaky sense of entitlement that sustains her until her final collapse. Nigel Bennett also seems at home, faithfully rendering Hugh as self-satisfied philistine, until breaking out with the play's most resonant moment: his terse admission that Henry is "a good boy, with bad parents. As Mia, Bethany Jillard plays another in her line of hair-trigger adolescents, but too often falls back on playing the (supposed) style rather than the character. Kristopher Turner has a fearsome task as Henry, and he doesn't really get into it until his scenes away from his mother, which allow him to be mercurial. The plays main problem: It keeps re-stating the same situation with a horror-struck look on its face and an almost total absence of humour.

That Face, downstairs at Berkeley Street, is part of Nightwoods 4x4 season, dedicated to showcasing female directors. Another of them, Weyni Mengesha, has done a triumphant job one floor up on Yellowman, an American play by Dael Orlandersmith about colour prejudice between blacks. We meet Alma and Eugene, who grew up together in South Carolina; she's Gullah-Geechie, dark-skinned; he's light, high-yellow." They both come of mixed parents; they develop from best friends to lovers; they face hatred within and without their own families, and one of them succumbs to it. The play is written as their parallel monologues; a "fluidity in the landscape, mentioned in the very first line, infects the writing and, even more, the performances, by Dean Marshall and Odena, which are just about perfect: always spontaneous but never out of control (well, Marshall may force the odd note), winning, funny, moving. Nothing sounds or feels literary," though the narrative method has its limits: the pole-axing climax lacks the inevitability it might have if we saw it rather than heard about it. But thats a very minor flaw. Yellowman is a co-production by Nightwood and Obsidian, and its the best thing I've seen from either company. Unmissable.

- That Face runs through Nov. 21; Yellowman runs through Nov. 14; both at Berkeley Street Theatre. For tickets, call 416-955-0101.
