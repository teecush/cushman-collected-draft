CRITICS' PICKS

POP MUSIC

In this period of post-election recovery, we decided to forgo the angry ragings of Marilyn Manson (yesterday at Massey Hall), for the softer sounds of Sufjan Stevens. The quiet singer/songwriter has, perhaps hastily, been lumped in with a number of similar souls as leaders of a new folk. The young man from Michigan became something of a curiosity when his third album, Greetings from Michigan, was touted as the first in a 50-album ode to every American state. Wishful thinking perhaps, but endearing all the same. The follow- up, Seven Swans (released this year), delved deeper into his pursuits of spirituality and storytelling, somehow making God bearable for the usually Christ-phobic indie hipsters. Winning raves from sea to shining sea, he was even hailed as a worthy successor to the dearly departed Elliott Smith. No pressure, though. Stevens plays Lee's Palace tonight. Tickets are $14 (416-532-1598).

THEATRE

There are two weeks left in which to catch Hairspray (Princess of Wales, 416-872-1212), a show that's tempting to overrate simply because it's so much fun: fun for the audiences and fun, one imagines, for the bang-up Canadian cast. Anyone who finds this just too frivolous can rest assured that Serious Theatre is still alive and ... well, alive in Toronto. In Omnium Gatherum (CanStage, 416- 872-1111), the New York dinner-party play, terrorism and allied issues are discussed as earnestly as if the diners were so many walking op-ed columns. In No Great Mischief (Tarragon, 416-536- 5018), a family saga set mainly in Cape Breton, the stage is awash in homely local colour. The program prints a couple of pages of fascinating Scottish and Canadian history, but remarkably little of it surfaces in the play.

DANCE

When you have a winner on your hands, why not work it? That's what Toronto Dance Theatre is doing this week with a revival of Christopher House's critically acclaimed Persephone's Lunch. Inspired by Homer's The Odyssey, House takes off on a witty, contemporary and existentially personal voyage that has his crew of 12 excellent dancers working through a series of evocative episodes. So far as there's a message, it is simply to relish life's journey. Runs to Nov. 20, 8 p.m., $17-$38. Premiere Dance Theatre, Harbourfront Centre, 207 Queens Quay W. 416-973-4000.

CLASSICAL

THE TSO

The Flying Karamazov Brothers make their Toronto debut in a concert with the Toronto Symphony Orchestra, conducted by Earl Stafford. The first half features the orchestra with Grieg's Morning, from Peer Gynt Suite No. 1, plus Rossini, Prokofiev and Victor Davies. The second half includes an homage to Japanese festival drumming, played on meticulously tuned cardboard boxes; a suite in four movements for juggling ensemble; a Marimba rendition of a classic Chinese folk piece and an excerpt from Symphony No. 9 by Beethoven, using radio transmitters and hockey helmets. Tonight and tomorrow 8 p.m. ($30-$91), Tomorrow 2 p.m. ($25.25-$60). Roy Thomson Hall, 60 Simcoe St. 416-593-4828. www.tso.ca.

THEATRE

HELEN'S NECKLACE

Susan Coyne and Raoul Bhaneja presents John Murrell's English translation of Carole Frechette's play set in a hot, chaotic Middle Eastern city scared by recent war. Searching for a loved-though- inexpensive necklace, Helen goes on a journey through the ruined city. Directed by Eda Holmes. Opens tonight and runs to Dec. 12. $20- $30. Today to Thursday 8 p.m., Saturday 2:30 p.m. 30 Bridgman Ave. 416-536-5018. www.tarragontheatre.com.

CHINESE ART OF PLACEMENT

Stanley Rutherford's story of an ex-poet who has chosen to live his life according to feng shui. But balance may be difficult what with his obsession with Tina Turner, a problem with ants and ongoing troubles in dealing with women. Starring Stephen Najera. Directed by Rusty Owen. Runs to Nov. 28. Today and Wednesday 8 p.m., Thursday to Saturday 8 p.m. and 10 p.m., Sunday 2 p.m. $15-$20. The Alley Theatre Workshop, 12 Ossington Ave. (enter off the first alley west of Ossington, North of Queen Street West). 416-703-9211.
