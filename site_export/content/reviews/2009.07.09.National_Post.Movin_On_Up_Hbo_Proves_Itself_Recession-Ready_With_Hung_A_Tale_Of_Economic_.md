As Ray Drecker's wife Jessica drives away, out of their marriage though not quite out of his life, she screams at him through the open car window: You used to be beautiful and athletic and talented and smart and popular and hung." (Her mothers in the car, too, but Jessica seems uninhibited, at least about this.) "What am I now? Ray asks. Now," she says, "youre just hung. Ray ponders this and -- to cut a long pilot episode short -- decides that if he now has only one marketable asset, he may as well market it.

Ray's terrible luck is to have a mid-life crisis at the same time that his country, and especially his city, are having an economic one. Ray is a high-school basketball coach and history teacher living in Detroit. One of the strengths of the show's pilot -- which, breaking the usual rule, is the best of its first four episodes -- is the way in which it fills in the civic background. We see car wreckage, stadium wreckage. We also, coming in closer, see Rays own home-wreckage. After the divorce, Ray moves into his dead parents lakeside house, now heavily mortgaged due to Ray's gullible trust in the subprime market. Really, the best thing to happen to it from his point of view would be for it to burn down. Which it does, due to faulty and overloaded wiring, but unfortunately Ray has been equally careless about keeping up the insurance. Pending the house's reconstruction, which he cant afford, he takes to living in a tent at the waters edge. This costs him his twin son and daughter, whose earlier decision to live with him had been the one bright spot on his horizon. The kids have to move in with their mom and her new man, a doctor. He saves lives!" she says proudly. "Honey, says Ray, his voice dripping equally with weariness and scorn, he's a dermatologist." I didn't spot it myself, but experts tell me that this joke has already been used on Seinfeld, though that show typically upped the ante by revealing that the doc in question had in fact saved at least one person from skin cancer.

To top everything, Rays team is losing every game and he fears being fired as a result. In desperation he takes off to a you-can-be-whatever-you-want motivational workshop where hes told, unsurprisingly, to follow his dream. Trouble is, he doesn't at present have one. But another attendee gives him an idea. She's Tanya, a poet currently working as an editorial temp, an employment Hell from which she hopes to escape by baking cakes and placing her poems inside them. As we learn in a flashback she and Ray have met before, and it ended up in bed, where she was vocally

impressed by the size of his equipment but dismayed by the discourteous speed with which he removed it, along with the rest of himself. Anyway, this and a repeat encounter, which plays out almost identically, leads circuitously to Rays decision to sell his services to deprived ladies, with Tanya as his agent.

All this is set up in the first hour-long instalment. This has been criticized as overlong, but in fact its leisureliness is the best thing about it. Slow is sitcoms new speed, and this episode, generous with scene-setting and character quirks, handled it well. There's a problem with Ray himself. Richard Jane, who plays him, is a solid actor but not overly charming or charismatic; he reminds me of Tim Allen, the bleary over-the-hill Allen of Galaxy Quest, but without the built-in character association (and, face it, the super script) that made that performance funny. Jane Allen is a definite asset as the super-conscientious Tanya, standing in the street right outside her own house loudly protesting, "I'm an excellent pimp! Well, yes, its hard out there. Youd think she might prefer old-fashioned words like madam" or even "bawd, her being a poet and all. Ray himself wrestles with his job description; in his mind he goes from whore" to "male prostitute to, at my last count and after an encounter apparently gratifying to both parties, gigolo."

The question raised by Episodes 2, 3 and 4 is can they, so to speak, keep it up? Can they make a whole series out of an idea that Flight of the Conchords once got through in half an hour, including songs? Do they want it be shocking? Do they want it to be wryly compassionate social commentary? I'm not saying it couldn't be both, but at the moment the Hung jury is out. Theyve had to devise complications to delay or derail Rays professional assignations, and already they're beginning to seem routine or contrived. In other respects, though, the initial careful spadework is paying off. Plotlines are intersecting more neatly and ambitiously than is usual in sitcoms. Ray's teenage kids are refreshingly unglamorized, in their exasperated loyalty to one another and their plain exasperation with their elders. Ray also has a promising conflict going with his upscale lawyer neighbour, who objects to living next to two burned-out ruins -- i. e. the house and its owner, the latter of whom has taken to urinating in Lake Michigan, which most surely has seen worse. I think it could be good for a season. Im not sure, to invoke a production with a similar background and a similar premise, that it could ever go the Full Monty, but then Im not convinced that The Full Monty ever went it, either. - Hung airs Sunday nights at 10 p. m. on HBO Canada.
