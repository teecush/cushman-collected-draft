In a dismal season for new plays, homegrown or imported, CanadianStage is doing something to pick up the slack, at least in the second category. On the heels of London Road it brings us Tribes, another British piece, and a magnetically interesting one.

The easiest way of describing Nina Raine's play is to say it's about deafness. Which it is, but it branches out from there to be a play about families and about communication, or the lack of it. With some acknowledgments to Twelfth Night: Some tribes you're born into; some you join; some absorb you. Billy, the central character here, experiences all three.

Billy, deaf since birth, is the twentyish son of an intellectual family in northwest London. (Their intellectual status is established by their above-average rate of swearing. The location is never precisely stated but trust me, I've lived there.) Billy's parents are Christopher, a former teacher who's now a full-time critic, by profession and by temperament; and Beth, who's started a new late-life career as a mystery novelist. So, they're both concerned with words; Christopher indeed is obsessive about them, and about their proper usage, and is merciless towards those who don't live up to them. Billy has two older siblings, Ruth and Daniel, who have moved back home in recoil from failed relationships and stalled careers. Ruth is an aspiring opera singer, currently doing cabaret gigs. Daniel is working on a thesis about, wouldn't you know, language. Billy has never learned sign language; his family thought it would make him feel self-conscious. Or maybe it's that they couldn't be bothered to learn it themselves. They are all, with the possible exception of Beth, very self-absorbed.

Then, Billy meets Sylvia, who is an expert signer; she comes from a deaf family, and is losing her hearing herself. Her signing and his lip-reading bring them together, in a scene that's witty, edgy, romantically charming and - like all first encounters - awkward, all at the same time; any failures of comprehension on the audience's own part are dealt with, as they are throughout the play, by projections of the words in question; the characters' problems become, by translation, ours. They become a couple and, through Sylvia, Billy becomes increasingly involved in the deaf community - to use a phrase that his father would, and does, volubly hate. Indeed, when Billy brings Sylvia home to meet the folks, Christopher in particular gives her a very hard time. This turns out, though, to be a standard rite of passage, one that she passes with (to use another expression Chris would probably hate) flying colours. They like her, though Daniel at least seems to have issues. Nor is Billy's and Sylvia's own relationship free of abrasion; she brought him both a new independence and a new sense of involvement, and both can work against her. Besides, her condition is in many respects harder than his; she knows what she's losing. When music plays, as it constantly does both between and during scenes, he may feel left out but he doesn't have to tune out; she, progressively and enforcedly, does.

Tribes is billed as a Theatrefront production in association with CanadianStage (who house it) and Theatre Aquarius. Daryl Cloran, artistic director of Theatrefront, directs the play; Lorenzo Savoini designed the barebones but sufficient set, whose major component is the all-important family dining table. There are especially fine performances, sympathetic but thoroughly unsentimental, by the central couple. Stephen Drabicki, a hardof-hearing actor himself, makes us feel Billy's hope, pain, his sharp humour and - perhaps most important - his stubbornness. The play's story takes Billy into a new world; Drabicki takes us into one as well. Holly Lewis shows Sylvia's attractive strength and also its frightened, and frightening, limits.

Billy's brother and sister are less well served by their author. Patricia Fagan vividly depicts Ruth's anger and frustration, but isn't given much chance to play anything else. If she has too few notes in her score, Dylan Trowbridge as Daniel has too many. He gives a high energy and highly watchable account of a young man who hasn't quite grown up. He tells us, though the play never really explores it, that he cares deeply for Billy and a) doesn't want him to be hurt and b) doesn't want to lose him. (There seem to be echoes here of the fraternal love-hate in Long Day's Journey into Night.) Maybe that's why he makes a pass at his brother's girlfriend: to defuse their relationship before it can cause any harm. It seems an awfully long-winded way of going about it; and anyway, from the moment Daniel first sets eyes on Sylvia, the production has suggested an attraction between them.

In its second half, the play loses its concentration, as if it were trying desperately to avoid becoming an "issues" piece, or had simply run out of ideas. In fact, the issues have been so strongly established and so artfully varied that they become themes. Every time somebody mentions writing, or texting, or talking or singing, it becomes another part of the pattern. When Joseph Ziegler's Christopher puts on a pair of headphones to shut out a conversation he wants no part of, it's the perfect image, multiply reverberant. Ziegler's performance is the upmarket counterpart of his great Irish dad in A Whistle in the Dark: an articulate man who, whatever his protestations of open-mindedness, will only converse on his own terms. His timing is a joy as, in a more sympathetic key, is that of Nancy Palk, whose role as Beth also recalls one of her own earlier appearances, as the wife in You Can't Take It with You, who took to playwriting because a typewriter had been accidentally delivered to her door. And was also, in her way, a caring mother. As for this playwright: Raine has had a meteoric career in London, and also in New York; it's good to have been introduced to her here, in a play and production that are alight and alive.
