TIDELINE

Factory Theatre

Long plays are back in fashion. Hot on the heels of The Trials of Judas Iscariot, a brilliant three-and-a-half hour show whose characters are in purgatory, comes Tideline, which goes on for three hours and forty minutes, is not brilliant and whose characters are also in a kind of purgatory. In this one, though, the audience joins them there.

Wajdi Mouawad, a French-Canadian playwright of Lebanese extraction, sets the last two-thirds of his piece in a recently war- torn land that he coyly refuses to name, but that is distinguished, at least in this staging, by the rockiness of its terrain and the readiness of its inhabitants to behave like symbols. Before we get there, however, we have an hour or so to kill. We start, promisingly enough, with a young man named Wilfred bounding onstage to tell an unseen judge about how he was having the best sex of his life when the phone rang to tell him that his father had just died. You may wonder what he said about this to the lady in his bed, or she to him, but such prosaic concerns are far beneath the play's notice.

We do learn that Wilfred's mother's family won't let his father be buried next to her because she died giving birth to Wilfred and they blame the father for it. This genuinely haunting situation, occasioning a couple of touching flashbacks, is set up at length over the play's first act and is then never alluded to again.

What happens instead is that Wilfred, with the court's permission, takes the body of his immigrant dad back to the old country for burial. He first meets a highly dispensable blind poet who recites the opening verses of the Iliad, though we don't appear to be in Greece. He then encounters a lady singer, and they gradually accumulate a whole retinue of the young dispossessed, who go tramping the land in search of a suitable gravesite so that Dorothy -- I mean Wilfred -- can fulfill his quest and go home. The corpse they carry is represented sometimes by a body bag and sometimes by a living, talking actor. Finally, after an especially interminable lyrical interlude, they commit the old man to the sea. Hence the title.

Wilfred's companions have all lost their own fathers, bloodily. So there's a bond. But there's no way that his condition matches or illuminates theirs. This attenuated fantasy could only be passed off as a universal fable by visionary direction and actors able to turn the lines, some of which have potential, into poetry. Bill Lane gives it a painstaking, plodding production, staffed by a cast who aren't up to it. Michael Rubenfeld does well as the Wilfred of the first act, blushing and gesticulating like Woody Allen thrust into Hamlet, but he merges into the crowd later on. His companions preach, mutter or -- in the case, after a couple of neat cameos, of Dylan Trowbridge -- shout; true, his character, a would-be terrorist, is supposed to yell, but it must be possible to do it with more nuance. Andrew Moodie has some fun as an imaginary film director who seems to represent Wilfred's narcissism, and no fun at all as an equally imaginary medieval knight who represents his childhood and whom he grows out of.

It's that kind of play, bypassing the specific to aim at the universal, and achieving only a kind of ponderous whimsy. It's intended as an act of remembrance. Mouawad, as stated, was born in Lebanon; you'd think there'd be a play there, one that might illuminate atrocity instead of just referencing it from time to time. If the besetting sin of Anglo-Canadian drama is to be parochial, then that of French-Canadian drama is to be cloudy and portentous. In this instance, I'll take le vice Anglais.
