THEATRE REVIEW

The Sunshine Boys Young Centre for the Performing Arts, Toronto

Soulpepper is bringing us American playwrights in matching pairs. They followed their production of David Mamet's Glengarry Glen Ross with his Speed-the-Plow, an attenuated variation on the same theme. They've now given us Neil Simon's The Sunshine Boys, which stands in rather the same relation to his The Odd Couple. Both the minor Mamet and the simpler Simon differ from their predecessors in introducing an explicit showbiz setting dear to the author's own heart and experience. Well, dear to his heart anyway.

His title characters are an oldtime vaudeville comedy duo called Lewis and Clark, names almost too perfect to have been made up. We meet them in the early 1970s, long after the act has broken up. Al Lewis went into stockbroking, and thence into comfortable retirement with his daughter and her family. Willie Clark lives, mostly in his pajamas, in a seedy New York hotel room where he's visited every Wednesday by his nephew, who's also his agent - and who brings him soup, the latest issue of Variety and no offers of work. Even Alka-Seltzer, a word that Willie declares inherently funny because it has a "k" in it, won't use him in a commercial because the word is out that he can no longer remember his lines.

On this particular Wednesday, however, there is some good news. CBS is presenting a nostalgic tribute to vaudeville, and they want Lewis and Clark to reunite and perform their famous doctor sketch. The only problem is that the two men, who were together professionally for 43 years, can't stand one another. At least, Clark can't stand Lewis.

The first scene gives us Willie, on the subject of the absent Al and practically every other subject, kvetching; and I use the term advisedly. This is one of Simon's more explicitly Jewish plays; its characters, and their whole world, are steeped in borscht and bagels and there's a problem if the actors aren't.

Eric Peterson has Willie's tetchiness and crankiness and transparent insecurities down perfectly; he gets the evening off to a fine start, nodding off in front of a recalcitrant TV set, awakened by the whistling of a kettle, and feverishly answering a phone that hasn't actually rung. Vocally he's fine on the rhythms but he doesn't get the tonality, with the result that the audience sits in glacial silence. The wisecracks as written aren't exactly vintage Simon but they should still make us laugh, even if we hate ourselves for doing so.

At that, Peterson is better than was Walter Matthau in the mechanically disagreeable film version. All the movie had going for it was the presence of George Burns, who had reached a state of comic grace in which every word he uttered sounded funny and who justified Willie's grudging admission that no one could time a line like Al could time a line. However, Kenneth Welsh, who plays Al here, is equally wonderful in a completely different way, one that has more to do with how he looks than how he sounds. He's patient and reserved behind thick glasses; an actor I've previously thought of as wiry has become preternaturally solid in bulk though intriguingly gelatinous in texture. His Al is, as indeed George Burns was in his own career, the ultimate straight man, guaranteed, in a reversal of traditional comic logic, to get all the laughs and his partner's goat.

Ted Dykstra's production takes off when the two men come together for a reluctant rehearsal, and it has a fair stab at the doctor sketch itself, although this is a style of comedy gone almost beyond recapture, even by the most conscientious actors. Or perhaps especially by the most conscientious actors. It's burlesque rather than vaudeville, the American stage equivalent of a British seaside postcard; its walking, or wiggling, emblem is a blonde and all-around pneumatic nurse, here deliciously, indeed toweringly, embodied by Sarah Wilson; funnier than either of the two men who spend the routine leering at her, she beats sexism by joining it.

She's one of two characters billed simply as Nurse; the other, a juxtaposition that probably makes a point in its own right, belongs to the real world, and is called in to minister to Willie in the second act. She will take none of his nonsense, and is rousingly played by Quancetia Hamilton. The play needs her even more than Willie does.

Like The Odd Couple, The Sunshine Boys keeps restating the same central dynamic. The earlier play, however, buttressed its two leads with solid supporting characters; the guys' poker buddies, and even the two English roses upstairs, were roles worth playing. Most of the support work here has to be done, with less grateful material, by Jordan Pettle, who does it nicely enough; he makes a more convincing nephew than he does an agent, but that's pretty much his uncle's complaint about him anyway.

Unlike The Odd Couple, though, the play does get better as it goes on. As often with Simon, it hints at depths that it can't bring itself to explore. In this case, the intimations are of a Sartrean hell composed of other people, or at least of one other person. Those depths can be pretty murky or pretty funny. It depends on how you look at it. Or on which Sunshine Boy you are.

The Sunshine Boys runs until Sept. 22. For tickets and more information, visit soulpepper.ca.
