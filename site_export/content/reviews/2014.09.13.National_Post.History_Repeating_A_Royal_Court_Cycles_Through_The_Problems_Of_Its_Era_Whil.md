If you are the monarch of a strictly Protestant 17th-century country, and you want to keep the job, try not to be pacifist, intellectually curious, sympathetic toward Catholicism, homosexually inclined or female. Queen Christina of Sweden could tick off every one of those boxes on her resume, and thus had a rough reign. It's chronicled, somewhat selectively, in Michel Marc Bouchard's Christina, The Girl King, given its Englishlanguage premiere at Stratford in a translation by Linda Gaboriau.

It seems that Christina, like her English predecessor Elizabeth I, spent a lot of time fending off counsellors who wanted her to produce an heir and suitors who were eager to help her do it. (She even, like Elizabeth, had to repel advances from a Philip of Spain, though not the same one.) The play starts with an adoring close relative throwing himself literally at her feet, to her intense annoyance. He renews his attentions at intervals throughout the rest of the play, and she goes on dismissing them.

This typifies the problem with the play. It holds the attention, firmly, but it does go around in circles. As tends to happen in history plays, supporting characters are selected for the causes they represent. Christina's chief adviser is an ardent Lutheran who sees himself as her substitute father, she having lost her real one an early age. He puts forward his own son as a possible consort. Visitors at court are the French ambassador, who seems to be acting mainly on behalf of the Vatican, and the philosopher Descartes, the importance of whose stay in Sweden the play probably exaggerates. (We wait for him to say that he thinks, therefore he is. Eventually he obliges.) Both these foreign influences are regarded with deep suspicion by the natives, but the primary threat comes from within. Christina falls in love with one of her ladies-in-waiting. The charting of their mutual attraction, and of their enforced separation, is the most compelling element in the play.

Bouchard has described the play as his "classical" piece; which, apart from the fact that everybody is wearing pretty clothes, seems to mean that the characters converse largely in aphorisms. In Vanessa Porteous' production, handsomely designed by Michael Gianfrancesco, this too often means that the actors stand around, screaming abstractions at one another. The abstractions, given the kind of play this is, are justifiable; the screaming isn't. Jenny Young as Christina is especially prone to acting in italics. Admittedly she has to spend a lot of time being exasperated, often with reason. Her problem as a protagonist is identical, really, to her problem as a ruler; she spends more time being acted upon than acting. Her best moments come when she reaches out, in perplexity or, occasionally, in joy. Christina eventually finds her own kind of freedom. The choice she makes seems arbitrary when it happens; it could have occurred at any point in the play. But its sequel finds her radiant, with the whole stage, in Kimberly Purtell's lighting design, brightening in sympathy.

The outstanding performance, though, comes from Claire Lautier, who brings to the queen's love interest a spontaneity and sensitivity nothing else in the play can match. Wayne Best makes a stern, judicious chancellor, except when he too gives in to shouting. His son, an interesting blend of sadistic politician and amorous poltroon, is Graham Abbey, in glittering form; he courts his sovereign by calling attention to the length and beauty of his legs, in a scene that is itself both beautiful and long. Another character exploited for short-term effect, gothic this time, is Christina's mother, who hates her, and apparently tried to kill her when she was a child; she also slept with her late husband's body for a couple of years, and has an albino attendant. Patricia Collins is certainly impressive in the role, but the first impression is enough. On a more human level there is nice work from Brigit Wilson as a high-born mixture of attendant, narrator and spy (as she says, "I hate to miss anything") and from Rylan Wikie as the devoted wooer whose attentions pay off in unexpected ways.

Monday Nights is the creation of five actors, roguishly billed as 6th Man Collective, who have been playing basketball together once a week, and are re-creating the event for an audience who also get to participate. The Theatre Centre has been dressed up as a basketball court; I'm not sure whether this counts as site-specific theatre or the reverse. Each of the actors heads a team, though one of them, on a rotating basis, gives that up and acts as ref. Each audience member joins a team, gets some instruction and then gets to play, though I cravenly stayed in my seat and settled for being my team's scorer. (Hey, we won. Well, we tied.)

Is it a play? No, but it doesn't pretend to be. Is it even theatre? Well yes; it's a live event, involving performers and an audience. Does it need actors? That's a tougher one; much of what these five do could be done by nonacting basketball players, amateur or professional. The nearest approach to conventional drama comes when each actor in turn is addressed by one of his fellows on the subject of his good and bad personal qualities. It's also the least effective part of the show. Presumably we're meant to connect these character analyses with how each guy handles himself on the court, but I couldn't make any connection. Earlier on, we've heard, through headphones, the merits of our own captain being confidentially discussed by his three rivals. The consensus on our man, Byron Abalos, seemed to be that he's a very nice fellow, which I have no reason to doubt; he's certainly a good actor, as recently seen in Twelve Angry Men. His four colleagues are Colin Doyle, Darrel Gamotin, Richard Lee and Jeff Yung. They may be all male, but females are allowed on their teams and did, on the whole, better than the men. It's a good-natured entertainment, whatever it is, with plenty of excitement on the court. It also brings out a universal truth in human nature: once you've been assigned to a team, you can't help cheering for it.

Christina, The Girl-King runs through Sept. 21 at the Studio Theatre, Stratford; Monday Nights runs through Sept. 20 at The Theatre Centre
