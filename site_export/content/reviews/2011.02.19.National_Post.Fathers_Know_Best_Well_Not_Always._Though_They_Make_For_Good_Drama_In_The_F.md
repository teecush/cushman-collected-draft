With their production of The Fantasticks, Soulpepper are in charming and benevolent mode, a mode they do well and often. This is the modest musical that made a modest start, off-Broadway in 1960, and then ran there for 50 years, which was not quite long enough for me to have got around to seeing it. I cannot say that seeing it now is a revelation; the show's qualities are well known, as are many of the songs. It's still fascinating, and mostly delightful, to see how they play out.

The Fantasticks gives us Romeo and Juliet in spite of themselves, twice. It's based on The Romanticks, a 19th-century bonbon by Edmond Rostand ( Cyrano de Bergerac), that's set in the familiar sweet-and-sour pastoral landscape of French classic comedy, but given what was then a modern tweak. The musical changes the locale to a non-specific rural America, but keeps the play's central conceit. This is that the fathers of the young couple are all in favour, but determine to put obstacles in their way, so that their children may have the pleasure feeling star-crossed and defying their elders. As far as we can see, the boy, Matt, and the girl, Luisa, were going to fall in love anyway, but their fathers, who are next-door neighbours, have built a wall between their properties, just to make sure. This is less like Romeo and Juliet than like Pyramus and Thisbe, while the old-time locale, use-your-imagination setting and presence of a hospitable narrator, also seem to look forward to Our Town -- especially as both that play and A Midsummer Night's Dream are on this year's Soulpepper schedule. The scheme involves arranging a fake abduction of Luisa, so that Matt may thwart it. To this end, the seniors engage a professional abductor, known as El Gallo, who in turn engages a couple of strolling actors. By intermission, everything has worked out very nicely. In the second half, it all starts to unravel. This, in addition to the other analogues, makes the show a precursor of Into the Woods; it shows what happens after happily ever after. There's also a resemblance between these young romanticks, at least in the first stage of their relationship, and the amorous dimwits of A Funny Thing Happened on the Way to the Forum. And I wonder if the young Tom Stoppard ever saw The Fantasticks; its thespians-for-hire, emerging from a property hamper and complaining about having fallen on hard times and offering death scenes as their specialty, look like ancestors of the itinerant players of Rosencrantz and Guildenstern Are Dead.

Harvey Schmidt, the composer, and Tom Jones, librettist and lyricist, are charter members of the last great generation of American musical theatre writers; and they may -- Schmidt may, anyway -- be the most purely melodious. The score's most famous number, Try To Remember, is of course impossible to forget, but it's rivalled as a standard, and surpassed as a song, by the beautiful Soon It's Gonna Rain. The songs that register most strongly, though, are I Can See It, which surges, and It Was You, which rapturously heals. These both occur in the second act, which needs them. The first half preserves a perfect balance of innocence and experience. The second, which departs more from the original, sets out to prove that you need to suffer before you can truly love; or, as the song has it, "without a hurt, the heart is hollow." As wisdom that's unexceptionable, but in acting it out, the show turns schematic. Not only the lovers but their fathers start squabbling. The actors return in the guise of pirates, though they may just be doubling; anyway this time they abduct Matt instead of Luisa, and they do it for real. Though how much of his misadventures with them are meant to be real, and how much is fantasy, is uncertain; at least, I couldn't follow it. The action begins to feel forced, though the music and the production eventually bring it to a satisfying conclusion.

Under Joseph Ziegler's direction, the performers are tender and funny and hardly ever sentimental. Albert Schultz as narrator gives the musical version of his Our Town performance, soothing and understanding; as El Gallo, he's a flamboyant but vulnerable swashbuckler, who had me puzzled as to which moustachioed Hollywood hero he most resembled. The lovers, I guess, could be more full-throated; Jeff Lillico's singing sounds oddly less assured here than it did in Light in the Piazza, which I would imagine to be the more testing score. But he gets the mixture of doubt and ardour exactly right, just as Krystin Pellerin's Luisa bewitchingly balances the demure and the determined. Michael Hanrahan and William Webster as the two not-so-heavy fathers, both devout gardeners more jealous of their vegetables than of their offspring, provide expectedly endearing duets, vocal and otherwise. The real show-stopping performance, though, comes in a non-musical role; this is Oliver Dennis' old tragedian, wispy-haired and down-at-heel, parading past glories and mixed-up Shakespearean soliloquies. In a show whose humour mostly provokes gentle smiles, he sets off uproarious belly laughs, but without disturbing the delicate equilibrium. He's ably seconded by Michael Simpson, got up as a squaw in case there should be call for one. (There is). The original harp-and-piano score is impeccably played, onstage, by Erica Goodman and musical director Paul Sportelli, whose appearance, beneath a battered hat and with his back turned stubbornly to us throughout, is fantastick in itself.
