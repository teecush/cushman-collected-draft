In the opening sequence of The Invisible Woman, the movie (screened at TIFF) about Charles Dickens late-life affair with a young actress, we meet the lady concerned, Ellen Ternan, in her own later life. Shes married and has given up the stage, though she's putting her early training to use by supervising juvenile amateur dramatics. Somebody asks her whether she was the inspiration for Lucie Manette, the heroine of A Tale of Two Cities. She doesn't deny it, but doesnt seem to want to dwell on the matter. Neither does the film, which is a pity.

A Tale of Two Cities is one of Dickens last three completed novels, the others being Great Expectations and Our Mutual Friend. It's generally believed that Dickens' relationship with Ternan influenced his portrayal of the young ladies in those books; theyre generally more temperamental, more teasing, than the monochromatic love-objects of the earlier stories. Lucie, admittedly, doesnt quite fit the new pattern; she's a pretty, virtuous nonentity, only interesting because she has a father, released from the Bastille, to mentally support. But two men love her: two men so identical in appearance that one of them, the dissolute Sydney Carton, can take the place of the other, the noble Charles Darnay, on the guillotine and thus do the far, far better thing that supplies the novel's celebrated climax.

Note that one of them has the same initials, indeed the same first name, as the author himself. And if ever an author was name-conscious, it was Dickens. So he gets to split himself between two look-alikes, and to enjoy both the satisfaction of getting the girl and the sweet noble agony of giving her up. Which, for a man experiencing both the guilt and the pleasure of an illicit affair, must have been quite a release. Though I have to say that The Invisible Woman goes easy on the guilt.

In fact The Invisible Woman makes dickens himself a none-too-visible man, or at least an opaque one. Its reasonably enjoyable, but ralph Fiennes, in his second outing as actor-director, brings to it neither the visual flair nor the performing authority that distinguished his brilliant modern-dress version of Coriolanus. Admittedly a writer writing is even harder to make dramatically interesting than a painter painting or a composer composing. but the film keeps hinting at connections that it doesnt pursue. Its main story is told to us in flashbacks, prompted by ellen's staging of a play written by dickens and his friend Wilkie Collins, and also by the friendly questioning of a quite unnecessary clergyman who fancies himself as an amateur psychiatrist. (everyone in her new life seems to know of ellen's association with dickens, but not how intimate it was.).One thing she tells him is that she was the model for estella, the heroine of Great Expectations whom Pip, the very autobiographical hero, loves but cannot have, up to and very much including their final meeting on the books last page. At least thats how dickens originally wrote it. he was persuaded by his close friend and future biographer John Forster that his public would never stand for so unromantic a conclusion. ellen, impressively anticipating generations of literary critics, says that the change was an artistic mistake.

What it's supposed to mean to her is uncertain. Is she bitter that dickens could never marry her, as Pip marries estella? Is she wishing that their affair had never happened at all? (There's some doubt as to whether the relationship was consummated, but the film, following Claire Tomalins book, takes the persuasive viewthat it was, and that ellen bore a child that died at birth.) And how does she feel about the novels whole depiction of estella who, raised by the crazedmisshavisham to break men's hearts, might be described as the tease of all time? dickens went on to create, in Our Mutual Friend, another, more everyday flirt in bella Wilfer who rejects the supposedly poor hero's addresses because she has her heart set on marrying money, and has to be educated into love. ellen, estella, bella: the names chime but, from what the film shows us, the fictional ladies are very unfair depictions of the real one, who doesnt lead dickens on, initially resists him as any well brought up young lady of the period might, gives in because shes come to love him, and is no kind of gold digger. (her mother apparently is; she urges ellen to become the great man's mistress, as she's never going to make it as an actress. Though it does seem for a moment as if Kristin Scott Thomas mrs Ternan, a fine figure of a woman, is about to offer herself to dickens in her daughters stead.) Fiennes' performance does capture the energy of the man who wrote like a demon and also edited, did public readings, socialized and even hiked like one; you can see why he would have needed a comparable sexual outlet, but there's no investigation of why, having found it, he would translate his lover into the tormenting figures of those last novels.

Maybe it was guilt. he certainly had plenty to feel guilty about, and not just by Victorian standards. The great glorifier of hearth and home treated his wife atrociously, sending her as a go-between to ellen and letting her learn of the marital separation from a selfrighteous letter published in the Times. This much the film does show us, very effectively; but mrs. dickens then disappears from the screen and, it seems, from her husbands mind. We arent shown his behaviour to his children, raised to emulate him and then frozen out when they failed to measure up to his own great expectations.

We are shown his social, as opposedto personal, conscience at work; and that's to the film's credit. he raises, with all the charmandpassion in the world, impressive sums for the rescue of fallen women. Sometimes the social and the personal coincide; hes embarrassed when a passing copper mistakes ellen for a prostitute, shocked when an actual prostitute accosts him and cant believe it when he offers her money not to have sex. These are the kind of scenes that lurk beneath the surface of dickens' novels and that the pieties of the time wouldn't allow him to depict Throughout dickens' work courts and prisons were to remain the symbols of organized injustice, and the happy endings were to get less definitively happy; the favoured enclaves into which the heroes and heroines and their friends and relations withdraw themselves in the last chapter seem more and more like fragile defences against an inhuman world. by the final books, the convention has been abandoned almost entirely; Our Mutual Friend, last and in some ways greatest of the novels, hardly has formal ending at all. Its image for society is a garbage dump, politely called a dust heap, and it offers its definitive comment on Victorian values in the character of mr. Podsnap, the businessman who combines pomposity, insularity and philistinism; who literally waves away all unpleasant subjects; and who judges life and art on whether they will bring a blush to the cheek of some mythical young Person.

None of this of course stopped dickens from doing very well-socially, financially, artistically - out of the world he criticized and, as far as we can tell, from enjoying his success. but he must have felt the contradictions; they may have fuelled the frenzied activity that shortened his life, and of which the ellen Ternan affair, which he must have seen as a refuge, was actually a symptom. his comparatively early death-he was 58-is something else that The Invisible Woman never gets around to mentioning. Consciously or not, the complexities of his life found issue in his books. Somebody should make a film about it.
