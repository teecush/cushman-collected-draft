In his new book, My Singing Teachers, his specific comments are sensible and enlightening, but there aren't enough of them BOOK REVIEW MY SINGING TEACHERSFriday, March 17, 1995ROBERT CUSHMANBy Mel Torme Oxford University Press, 228 pages, $27.95 BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail MEL Torme says in interviews that he sings better in his 70th year than he did at 35. I would dispute that - the records he made in the early 1960s seem to me unsurpassable - but I certainly think he sings better at 69 than he did at 67 or 68. On his last two appearances in Toronto his voice sounded cloudy and nasal, as if the Velvet Fog of his youth had resettled, and in a thicker formation. But on Wednesday at the George Weston Recital Hall, it had lifted, and his joyous flow of musical ideas came through pristine and uncluttered. He was magnificent.The concert was named after Benny Goodman's old warhorse Sing, Sing, Sing -and a tune, ironically, that no one ever sings; Torme himself took to the drums for the duration. It was the climax of a substantial Goodman tribute, Torme and his musicians forming, as he said, a new Goodman sextet. (He has, in Ken Peplowski, an urbanely excellent clarinetist for the job, but his vibes player has some way to go before matching Lionel Hampton.) But it was only part of a set that otherwise had no particular theme except Tormes relish for, and knowledge of, jazz-flavoured popular song.The knowledge is important, since Torme is a commentator as well as a performer: in the way he sings, in what he says between the songs, and - increasingly - in print. Reviewing his autobiography in these pages a few years ago, I hoped for a subsequent volume that would tell us less about his wives and more about his music. His new book My Singing Teachers is, I guess, the answer to my request: a quick trot through the singers, composers, instrumentalists and arrangers whom he most admires and who have most influenced him. There are a lot of them; he deals with so many vocalists, from Ella Fitzgerald on down, that one starts speculating on the few he has left out. His specific comments are sensible and enlightening, but there arent enough of them. One wishes he had chosen fewer subjects, dug deeper into them, and made more detailed connections between their work and his own. He is often most instructive - maybe because they're not direct competition - on people who stand outside the pop-singing mainstream: on Fred Astaire, who seems to bring out the best in everybody; on Woody Herman, the beloved singing bandleader; on minor Broadway figures like Stubby Kaye, the original Nicely-Nicely of Guys and Dolls who "held that company together like Krazy Glue."This all-encompassing joy in the different show-business traditions is the glue that binds his own act. It enables him to start out Pick Yourself Up as a straightforward Astaire-Rogers tribute, funny and rhythmic, and then with no break in the performing logic to turn it into a scat showcase. With flawless pitch he negotiates every twist and leap in the alarming melodic line of Stardust and also - something I've never known before - manages to act out the lyric. Ballads bring out the best in him; his version of My Foolish Heart (another vocal back-breaker) with superbly sensitive piano backing from John Colianni, is spell-binding. He also tells you what film it came from and when he saw it; and you can believe him. Such factual reliability - in both his patter and his book - is almost unheard of.He prides himself on his lyric-readings, which are always intelligent, always alive. But though his phrasing is impeccably conversational, his tone isn't - which may explain why he has never quite achieved the recognition afforded Sinatra, Tony Bennett, or Nat Cole who, with their more fallible instruments, always seem to be talking directly to you. A certain architectural remoteness is the price he pays for that beautiful voice; you can hear him taking a song and consciously threading it through his pipes, his arrangements. In the late sixties he recorded Beatles songs, but he seems now to have given up any strained attempts at modernity. This tubby, wizened septuagenarian is not nostalgia, he is living history: making and recording it. He seems undeniably pleased with himself but he deserves to be; and he turns that pleasure outward.Word count: 797
