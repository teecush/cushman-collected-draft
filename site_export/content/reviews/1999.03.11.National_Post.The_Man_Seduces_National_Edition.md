Tony Bennett

Roy Thomson Hall, Toronto

Tony Bennett began his mara-thon 90-minute set on Tuesday night with The Best Is Yet to Come, the magnificent Cy Coleman-Carolyn Leigh song about the beginning of an affair -- a song he introduced 40 years ago but which, in my ear, has always belonged to Frank Sinatra. He proceeded to reclaim it, and in significantly different terms: When Sinatra sang it, with a big-band Count Basie backing, it was an assertion of prowess; Bennett, accompanied only by his long- time pianist Ralph Sharon, made it a tender, confident promise.

Bennett has long been known as the nicest guy in popular music, and there seems no serious reason to doubt the proposition. With Sinatra dead, Mel Torme sadly silenced by a stroke, and Bobby Short a minority taste, Bennett -- in his handsome, grey-haired 70s -- is now the official elder statesman of the American popular song.

He is also one of its most fervent disciples, and though a packed audience had obviously come to worship him, his own devotion seemed to be devoted outward, making him a fans fan. Other singers have built programs around individual composers; Bennett's recent CDs have been tributes to other vocalists -- to Sinatra, to Astaire, and to the great female singers, especially Billie Holiday.

Though his Toronto concert must have been meticulously planned (there were no last-minute conferences with the musicians about what they should do next), it seemed spontaneous -- a relaxed ramble through the songbook, using those other singers as markers. Classics and rarities rubbed shoulders on equal terms. In his Astaire segment, he moved from a buoyant You're All the World to Me (a little-known song from Royal Wedding) to the two big ones from Shall We Dance: They All Laughed, sung with sly, driving wit, and They Cant Take That Away from Me, done with a yearning warmth that caught the audience delightedly off guard.

He also took a few brief canters on his own warhorses. I have sometimes wondered why I Left My Heart in San Francisco, an ordinary song that had been lying around for years before he got to it, should have caught the public imagination above all the others he had sung in much the same way, (I sometimes wonder the same about Ray Charles and the considerably better Georgia on My Mind) but he gets through it now with a nice blend of seriousness and self- mockery.

He had fun rocking I Wanna Be Around to a climactic "oh, yeah" that sounded like a homage to somebody (Louis Armstrong?), but was a bit weird considering that its been Bennett's own song from birth. He revisited his first hit, Rags to Riches, with a gentle swing far more appealing than the earnest nasal throbbing he gave it in the '50s. And the same understatement enabled him to give the only palatable performance of People, the Barbra Streisand anthem, that I have ever heard.

It may be a matter of age, economics, or simple good taste, but simplicity becomes him. He introduced himself as a jazz entertainer rather than "a jazz singer." If I can follow him into that minefield, it seems to me that he gets closer to jazz -- to the truthful reshaping of a loved piece of material -- the less obviously "jazzy" his surroundings. Singing with a big band, he often sounds choppy; but with just a piano, he can be magic. He did his first four songs with Sharon alone and they were enchanting; the hall became a supper club. After that, he added guitar, bass, and drums, but the intimacy remained.

This is the time to hear Bennett. He still has most of his bravura high notes, and the grey at the edges of the throat only enhances them. Sinatra made every song an individual event; Bennett, by contrast, offers a constant wash of good music with the occasional surprising peak. Like all good pop singers, he talks to you in song; like all great ones, he does it in an unmistakable voice, in his case gravelly and urgent. His tone can be world- weary, but his spirit is profoundly hopeful.

He still has his faults: Conversational singing shouldnt mean talking the songs -- not when you can actually hit the notes -- and Bennetts shouts and whispers are often overdone. (Speak Low, despite its title, was a casualty; it's a great lyric, and he rightly loves it, but he didn't have to demonstrate it so insistently.) His dynamics often seemed governed less by interpretative intention than by how far from his face he happened to be holding the mike.

But you wouldn't want Tony Bennett to be slick. He told us at the end that he was doing a long show because he loved Toronto and didn't get here often. I bet he says that to all the cities. But I believed him.
