robertcushman@lycos.com

All this week we're updating membership in the Academy of the Overrated, the pantheon introduced 25 years ago in Woody Allen's Manhattan.

- - -

Let us be clear. Overrated is not the same thing as Bad. Billie Holiday is overrated. That doesn't mean she wasn't a fine singer. It means that, for reasons that have more to do with politics and biography than with music, she has been accorded excessive reverence by people who would hardly acknowledge the existence of other singers -- Frank Sinatra, Peggy Lee, Rosemary Clooney -- who have sung the same repertoire to equal effect.

With the exception of Randy Newman, every pop and rock act since 1965 has been overrated. As Aaron Wherry pointed out here on Wednesday, there is no way, irrespective of their actual talent, that any of them could have lived up to their hype.

Laurence Olivier and Marlon Brando are overrated, since they were neither as moving nor as funny as some of their contemporaries. And to turn from interpreters to originators: Modern Drama is overrated. I don't mean mere "modern drama," the bread-and-butter new plays that our theatres turn over on a regular basis; that is routinely overrated, or the reverse, according the whims of journalism. We know that. I have in mind the founding fathers, the dramatists who flourished in the last quarter of the 19th century and the first half of the 20th, the ones who were canonized by American theatrical academic Robert Brustein in a 1960s book called The Theatre of Revolt.

Brustein's pantheon consists of Henrik Ibsen, Anton Chekhov, August Strindberg, Luigi Pirandello, Bernard Shaw, Eugene O'Neill, Bertolt Brecht and (a desperate choice, since here overrated does mean bad) Jean Genet. Samuel Beckett didn't make the cut, on the grounds that he just didn't write enough plays. For my purposes, this was just as well, as there is no way Beckett is overrated. You may not like what he did, but it was necessary and he did it perfectly.

Brustein's main criterion for selecting his writers was that they made audiences feel uncomfortable. Or most of them did. Here I want to drag in another American critic, John Simon, who once asked rhetorically "Do you think Ibsen was a nice man? Or Pirandello? Or Strindberg? Or Brecht?" with the clear and correct implication that they were not. He then pointed out that Chekhov, bucking the modernist rules, probably was a nice man. This is reflected in his attitude toward his characters (which is charitable, but not sentimental) and may explain why he is the only one on the list whose plays audiences consistently enjoy. He isn't overrated. Indeed, when you add on his short stories and his work as a doctor, you may conclude that we have yet to do him justice.

Shaw seems to have been a nice man, too. He only gets into Brustein's book on sufferance, treated with deep suspicion for having had the gall to write comedies, and optimistic ones at that. The others -- Strindberg, Pirandello, O'Neill, Brecht -- make excellent clubs for critics to beat their readers (and directors their audiences) over the head. I am not dismissing any of them -- though in Brecht's case it's tempting -- and I am not trying to discourage anyone from reviving their plays. Last year in London, I saw a Pirandello (not about the theatre) that justified the claims that are made for him; Stratford's Long Day's Journey Into Night is deservedly legendary. I would be happy to be further persuaded. But I do think that they, and the vision of theatre they have bequeathed to us, are limited.

Robert Fulford this week outlined a couple of the reasons why critics overrate particular artists. He left out the least flattering to our trade, which is identification. To promote a writer is, vicariously, to share in the glory, or to hope to. This can lead to resentment. John Osborne, it seems, never forgave Kenneth Tynan for the widespread belief that Tynan's review of Look Back in Anger had been crucial to the play's success and hence to Osborne's whole career.

Sometimes identification can go further. F.R. Leavis, a critic once hugely overrated and now the reverse, seems on some level to have believed he actually wrote the novels of his hero, D.H. Lawrence. Certainly, he used Lawrence as a stick with which to beat all novelists except the few whom he could accept into his Great Tradition. Unfortunately, he made these few seem like moralizing bores, which is what, among other things, they are.

The most insufferable of them, by far, was Henry James, parading his exquisite sensibility in never-ending, minutely qualified sentences. The only Great English Novelists who are not overrated are Jane Austen, whose sparkle not even Leavis's approval could quench, and Charles Dickens. Anthony Trollope isn't overrated, partly because he's still recovering from decades of being underrated. It goes without saying that all living novelists are overrated, with the exception of Zadie Smith. And maybe Philip Roth.

Anne Kingston, introducing this series, came up with the complementary category of the Untouchable, meaning unquestionably great. Proust plainly belongs there, even though I've yet to finish Remembrance of Things Past. I can tell from what I have read that it is not overrated. I'm surprised by the inclusion of Freud. It seems to me that people have been tearing him down for ages, to the point where he has become underrated.

That leaves Shakespeare. His reputation has been endangered by the critics whom Harold Bloom calls the School of Resentment, and even more by the unfocused rhapsodies of Bloom himself, which are enough to make anyone resentful. But he remains the greatest field of energy in the English language, maybe in any language. Theatre critics are able to put his reputation to the severest practical test; there are no other authors whose work we could watch so often without going mad. Despite all the praise that has been heaped upon him, he is not overrated. (I suspect he was a nice man, too.)
