Until March 25. (416) 504-9971

CLOUT

Factory Theatre

---

One of the two principal characters of David Young's new play is called Lionel K. Biggar. He is a press baron, and a vociferous apostle of material success who practices what he preaches; you might call him an apologist for the rich except that he never apologizes. The role is played, with great wit and presence, by R. H. Thomson, who starts the evening off by addressing his shareholders while looking like Lord Nelson. His enemies, he explains, have denounced him as a relic of the 18th century so he is humouring them and amusing himself by dressing up as one.

This opening routine is written, as well as played, with mischievous panache. It happens that the publisher of this newspaper, on whom L.K. Biggar seems to be closely modelled, has never appeared in naval uniform, past or present, at any office party that I've been invited to; but I was prepared to overlook this inconvenient fact and go with the flow.

Unfortunately, the author turns out to have his own misgivings; he reveals that Biggar's masquerade and vainglorious oration are things in somebody's dream: not his own dream, but that of one of Biggar's life-long opponents -- Trent, a left-wing journalist once fired by Biggar.

Trent, who comes before us bald and wearing the tattiest of hospital gowns, is dying of cancer, and the play is his fevered vision of Biggar behaving as he would like Biggar to behave -- except of course you can't control the way people and things turn out, not even, or perhaps least of all, in your dreams. But even with this mitigating subtlety, from the moment Trent declares himself, Clout loses its clout.

"Show me a good loser," says Biggar to the throng, "and I'll show you a loser." The play unhappily bears him out; it is the victim of its own chivalry. Politically it is on Trent's side; dramatically it is concerned not merely with giving the devil his due, which would be admirable, but also with stripping the wings off the angels, and it does this so remorselessly that it ends up as reverse agitprop, as devoid of real conflict as the conventional variety.

The two men find themselves chained together in a cellar, with diapers added to their former get-ups. Left and right, it is borne upon us as the characters thrash inseparably around the floor they need one another; it's an image, all right, but where do you go with it?

Plainly they have been kidnapped by some breed of terrorist; Biggar thinks it's an unscheduled installment of an ongoing toughening program that he has been inflicting on himself. Eventually their real captor appears: an implacable young female ecologist who loathes Biggar and despises Trent for having provided him with such feeble opposition.

After treating them both to some playful electric shocks, she gives Trent an assignment: torture the tycoon into seeing your point of view. We see Biggar pinioned to a slab of wall that has risen obligingly to the vertical for the purpose (what excitement there is in Richard Rose's production comes from the movement of the scenery) while Trent whips him.

But Biggar is contemptuous of Trent's ideas of cruelty, and certainly Trent's heart isn't in it; beating Biggar is his way of beating up on himself. The play, too, seems to be self- flagellating, desperately searching for some way of illustrating the personal inside the political. It comes up with the tiredest of devices. The two men, when young, loved the same girl. We flash back to the 1960s; Trent, a radical student editor, sends his girlfriend to discover the secrets of Biggar, the campus plutocrat. His scheme predictably misfires, and he becomes predictably jealous, while she becomes predictably pregnant. Trent behaves like a cad and Biggar, to Trent's great discomfort, like a gentleman. He can afford it, but still ...

So Biggar is better. That is what the script seems consistently, if surprisingly, to be saying; and the acting reinforces it. The writing does not permit Thomson to go on being funny but it does give him a shot at vulnerability, and he takes it very well, as he does the scorn and bluster that go with the territory.

Eric Peterson's performance of Trent, done on a single shrill and hectic note, makes a tiresome character even more so. Waneta Storms plays all the women, either because they're all faces of the eternal feminine (and the playwright does have the nerve to call one of them Eve) or because it's cheaper; she is woefully unconvincing as the tough terrorist, but very fine as the disputed decoy. There is one sequence in which she has to keep hurtling from one section of the past to another, and she keeps them beautifully distinct; she even blinks in character.

Trent gets the last speech, and it's an impressive one: tired, purged, presumably at the point of death, he speaks of opening up to some new truth "just beyond the scope of my thinking." Peterson kicks his tics and delivers it finely, as a serious balance to Biggar's comic overture. It might almost be the start of a play.
