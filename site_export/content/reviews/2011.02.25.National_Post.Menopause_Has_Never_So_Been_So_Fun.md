THEATRE REVIEW

Sandra Shamas's Wit's End III: Love Life

Winter Garden Theatre,

The first thing to know about Sandra Shamas' Love Life is that it isn't about her love life. The title is an admonition, not a description. Thankfully, though, it isn't until the last section of the show that we get to the uplift. That is, unless you count an earlier and very funny section about the quest for a post-menopausal bra.

Puberty and menopause are the show's main concerns, and Shamas is funny about both of them. She is very skilled verbally and, uncommonly among stand-up comics, visually; she can contort her body at some quite remarkable angles. Refreshingly, she does not roam the stage haranguing us through a hand-mic; though this admittedly is something that male comedians are likelier to do than females anyway.

Her comedy, like comedy through the ages, is largely concerned with the infirmities of the flesh, especially as it ages, and its attendant social and psychological embarrassments. She doesn't sound angry about it; her predominant tone is something between accepting and regretful. She's friendly, making sure we're all present and comfortable before she gets started. ("Are we all in?" she enquires solicitously, adding, "It's a question we girls ask." It's also an atypical line in a largely asexual show.)

She also compliments us on our memories, to which she self-deprecatingly compares her own. Christopher Plummer, she reminds us, is playing downstage in Barrymore, and if he at his age can remember all those words about someone else's life then the least she can do at hers is remember all the words about her own. She doesn't seem too confident that she will. And though this may just be schtick, it did actually take practical effect on the first night. She began her second act by informing us that she had forgotten an important segment of the first, and that she would do it for us now. Being a natural and accomplished chatter-upper, she got away with it, but it accentuated what is already an imbalance in the show. The first half is short, though presumably not always that short, and sweet. The second does go on.

She begins the story proper by taking us back to when she 11 and was shown, along with all the other girls in the class, what seems to have been a frightening and not terribly helpful film called Now You're a Woman -narrated, as she pointedly points out, by a man. She adds, even more pointedly, that the film wasn't shown to the boys, with the result (I paraphrase) that they've been playing catch-up ever since. Later, when she gets around to menopause, she suggests a possible sequel entitled Now You're a Hag. Not that she herself looks or acts in the least haggish. In her fifties, she seems sleek and handsome and comfortable. She hasn't dyed her hair, though she tells us she once considered henna: "It's so festive." That seems to be a favourite word.

The show itself becomes less festive as it proceeds, though that probably isn't its intention. Men, perhaps as a consequence of their early educational deprivation, figure only marginally, though she does reach out by including a lengthy digression on Cialis, and the improbability of its being advertised in the afternoons during Oprah. Much of the second act, though, is about life on the farm.

The show is the concluding part of Shamas' Wit's End trilogy, Wit's End being the name she's given to her reallife rural retreat. All those Wingfield plays are there to remind us that farming can be funny, but of course they're all fictitious. Shamas, waxing autobiographical, doesn't get too much comic mileage out of butternut squash. But that may not be her intention. She seems more concerned to provide examples of how she does in fact Love Life. It gets rather cloying; and, after the sharper stuff at the beginning, like a short-changing of her talents.

Still, she remains likeable. She rounds everything off with a pun about moving from her summer garden to the Winter Garden, and then she's gone, not to return, her end as unceremonious as her beginning. I've never seen a solo performer do less of what is known in the trade as "milking." But then, she isn't a dairy farmer.
