robertcushman@lycos.com

One thing can be safely prophesied about the Soulpepper company's opening season at its new home in the Distillery District. The Young Centre, which Soulpepper will inhabit on what its artistic director, Albert Schultz, describes as "a time-share basis" with the George Brown Theatre School, will have the most inviting lobby in Toronto. Open even now for unofficial peeking and official ticket-buying, it's a long, spacious-seeming room with a bookstall promised at one end (an outcrop of Leonard McHardy's beloved Theatrebooks) and -- it warms my heart just to type it -- a functioning fireplace already blazing at the other.

Those who can tear themselves away will find that beyond the lobby there are theatres: two main stages, of which the larger looks like a more flexible version of the Harbourfront Centre Theatre that has housed many of Soulpepper's best productions in the past, plus a black box and a cabaret. Nine plays have been announced for the two principal spaces, amounting to a year-long repertory that will give the company a continuous presence unprecedented in the annals of non- commercial theatre in Canada -- or even, with the single and more grandiose exception of the National Theatre in London, England, outside it. (Urban institutional houses usually take summer holidays, while festivals shut up shop in the winter, as Soulpepper itself has previously done.) I think that's exciting.

Exciting, too -- maybe satisfying is more the word -- is the lineup of actors, which includes nine of the 12 founding members, as well as a bunch of others who have become closely associated. If I single out the young Martha MacIsaac, it's because I should have done so last week when retrospecting about The Wild Duck, for whose emotional impact she was largely responsible. There are also -- borrowed or plundered or whatever from the Shaw and Stratford festivals -- Ben Carlson and Jonathan Goad, of whom young actors hardly come any better.

To clarify: I like it when companies stay together -- I think it's the best way for a theatre to establish an identity -- but I think, too, that its members benefit from getting out and about now and then. I also feel, perhaps contradictorily, that our best actors, of whom there are a remarkable number, constitute a floating super-ensemble anyway.

Soulpepper's other strength has always been its programming, of plays that you can't see anywhere else. As far as I can tell, of those they're doing next year, only one (The Importance of Being Earnest) has been given a major production by any other Toronto company within the last 20 years. And yes, that does include King Lear. This is par for their course; of those they have staged in their preceding eight years of existence, only one (Twelfth Night) has turned up anywhere else in the past two decades. And yes, that does include Hamlet. The cause is not exactly timidity in other theatres -- they have their own, more domestic, fish to fry -- and the picture is of course different if you bring in Stratford and Niagara, but Toronto is supposed to be a theatrical metropolis in its own right. Some of the plays Soulpepper has done (Our Town, The Bald Soprano, The Lesson, a couple of one-act Chekhovs) even I had never seen before, anywhere, ever. One (Olympia), I'd never even heard of. They got to some of the rarer European classics in their catalogue (Don Carlos, Platonov, Nathan the Wise) ahead of London, which is supposed to set the pace in these things.

The coming season plucks only one play (Gogol's The Government Inspector) from that old Continental shelf. It's contrastingly heavy on plays from the second half of the last century: Ionesco, Pinter, Stoppard, Mamet, all of whom have been at sometime tagged with the absurd "absurdist" label, and who do have family likenesses. Something's been left out, and consistently so. Of plays written in English before 1900, Soulpepper has done only a few Shakespeares and She Stoops to Conquer. I have a hunch that older English plays are harder than older foreign ones, which get done after all in modern translations, but there are some rich pickings out there, all unpicked.

I spent a few days in November at the pleasantest theatre I know, the Swan in Stratford-upon-Avon, watching the Royal Shakespeare Company's Gunpowder season (now transferred to London), consisting of Elizabethan and Jacobean plays that were considered, in their own time, censorably dangerous. They still seemed apposite now. There were two rapier-sharp studies of villainous realpolitik, both prudently set in and around ancient Rome: Ben Jonson's Sejanus, which I'd never thought would work but did, and Philip Massinger's Believe As You List (retitled, for good reason, Believe What You Will), which I'd always thought would work, and did. There was Thomas Middleton's The Old Law (retitled, for no good reason, A New Way to Please You), a riotous comedy about enforced euthanasia for octogenarians (already an endangered species in 17th-century England, one would have thought), leading to all kinds of heartless scheming by hopeful heirs. The only mild misfire was Sir Thomas More, which has one startling speech that's probably by Shakespeare but otherwise played like a lesser Man for All Seasons. And these, remember, aren't even the recognized classics of the period. We have a long way to go.

The Stratford Festival, at least, is making a start. It staged Marlowe's Edward II this past year, and is doing Webster's The Duchess of Malfi, directed by Peter Hinton, who seems ideal casting, next. It's throwing in a Moliere and a Corneille as well, but I don't know how much credit it will get. Stratford can program Aeschylus, Aristophanes, Giraudoux, a couple of Sartres and Peter Hinton, all in one year, and still be accused of playing safe. It can cram six of the riskiest Shakespeares into two seasons, and still be found wanting against the RSC, though the latter balanced its Gunpowder adventure with a Shakespeare lineup consisting of As You Like It, Twelfth Night, A Midsummer Night's Dream and -- as a mad experimental gesture -- The Comedy of Errors.

Endnote: Several members of the super-ensemble mentioned above are featured in the upcoming Lord of the Rings, of which you may have heard. One among them is Carly Street, a newcomer who's relevant here for a coruscating performance in a rare Marivaux (is there any other kind?), The Dispute, that was a jewel on the 2005 fringe. She went on to play a fine Portia in Shakespeare in the Rough's Merchant of Venice. Look out for her. I'm told she can sing, too.
