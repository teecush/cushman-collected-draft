THEATRE REVIEW

My Granny the Goldfish

Factory Theatre, Toronto

Death? Communication? exclaims the title character of My Granny the Goldfish. (She really is a granny, though she isnt any kind of fish and there seems no reason beyond authorial whimsy for saying that she is.) "These are themes! Why are we talking about themes?" It's a good question, but since it doesn't get asked until quite late in the second act, the only answer can be that the play is afraid of being thought pretentious and is attempting some last-minute damage control. It doesnt work. And, sadly, little else in the play works, either.

Anosh Iranis play is not in fact pretentious. Rather it's unpretentious to a fault, or way beyond. It's an empowerment drama sharing the stage with a sitcom. The sharing is literal. On one side of the stage theres a Vancouver hospital room occupied by Nico, a young business student from India whos also a self-confessed hypochondriac; he's found a lump and is awaiting the results of a biopsy. On the other, there are his parents in what all the characters still refer to as Bombay (the date is 2005); mom and dad are both alcoholics, and though they know their son is in hospital, they seem cheerfully unconcerned as to what might be the reason. The mother's mother, less irresponsible despite having a severe drinking problem of her own, flies to Canada and sets about teaching her grandson some life lessons.

The plays first-act climax is worth noting. Granny has been informed by Nicos doctor that the boy has cancer. She phones home, and the audience assumes she's going to tell his parents the terrible tidings. What she actually tells them is that Nico, who has confessed to a one-night fling with a Chinese nurse, is going to marry the girl. This news so appalls Nico's mother that she and her husband get on the next plane to their sons bedside. The state of his health would not, apparently, have been sufficient reason.

The psychological implausibilities here are only exceeded by the physical ones. Were asked to believe that a doctor would give a third party medical information still undisclosed to the patient himself. (The doctor seems remarkably cavalier about this violation of professional ethics. Later on in the play he does it again.) Also, though getting it on with your nurse is a common hospital fantasy, in real life it's apt to present certain difficulties, especially when the patient in pumped up with post-op morphine. The information about intra-Asian racism is interesting, but as it's presented solely as a series of gags, its hard to take it seriously as motivation.

Rosemary Dunsmores production has a few things going for it. One is the bright white hospital portion of John Thompson's set and lighting design. Another, once he gets past an introductory soliloquy that plays like failed stand-up, is the performance, at once full-bodied and restrained, of Kawa Ada as Nico. He's helped, I think, by being required to register exasperation with his parents and amused indulgence of his gran, both of them feelings well expressed in the writing and likely to be shared by the audience.

Granny herself is played by the Sri Lankan actress Yolande Bavan, whose presence intrigued me partly for nostalgic reasons. I remember her replacing Annie Ross in the legendary jazz vocal group formerly known as Lambert, Hendricks and Ross, and also for a less-publicized gig as a jazz DJ for the BBC. Her performance here suffers from an excuse of the cutes, but thats excusable given that shes playing one of those supposedly irresistible life-force characters: a woman who regards her daughter's alcoholism as pernicious but her own as endearing, and who waltzes around arranging people's destinies while showering them with philosophical advice.

Well, maybe not waltzing; she confides that "I wanted to be a tango dancer but my mother wouldnt let me," and does finally dance with her grandson in a kind of choreographic epiphany. Bavan manages to carry most of this off with grace while looking ageless; shes an aerating presence.

Back in Bombay, and later in Canada when Bombay gets there, things are a lot grimmer; the production takes crass and jerky writing and makes it crasser and jerkier. Veena Sood has apparently given up on any possibility of making the mother credible; she merely bashes through from one supposedly hilarious bit of insensible insensitivity to the next. As her husband, an unsuccessful bookie, Sanjay Talwar seems to be forlornly waiting for a chance to act believable, a chance that never arrives.

A lot of the action, in both locales, depends on unseen characters: the doctor, the nurse, Nico's hospital roommates, the inhabitants of the red-light district where he grew up and where his parents still live, and a woman who keeps showering his mother with wrong-number phone calls and eventually, of course, gets treated as a confidante. It isn't just sitcom, its old-fashioned sitcom in need of a laugh-track.

There are moments, in Nico himself and in some of his interaction with his granny, that hint at better things. But theyre swamped by everything else.

My Granny the Goldfish runs until April 15. For tickets, call 416-504-9971 or visit factorytheatre.ca.
