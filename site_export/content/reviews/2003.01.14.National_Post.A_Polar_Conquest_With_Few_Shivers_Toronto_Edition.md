TWO WORDS FOR SNOW

Artword Theatre, Toronto

Commander Robert Peary, the celebrated Arctic explorer, and his assistant Matthew Henson have turned up on our stages these past few years with surprising frequency. They were minor characters in Ragtime; Father, that musical's self-proclaimed "forward thinker" went off with them to discover the North Pole, and was mightily surprised to find that Henson, holding the exalted position of first mate, was black.

Having done their bit to expose the token liberals inbuilt racism, Peary and Henson sailed right out of the show. (There is, of course, more about them in the novel.) But they have a whole play to themselves in Richard Sangers Two Words for Snow, which explores the ice trek's racial implications in more detail, and which premiered at the 1999 PlayRites festival in Calgary. It has now been remounted in revised form.

Sanger is a poet, and when I saw his play in Alberta, I described it as a work that was planned as a fantasia but emerged as more of a meditation. The meditative element is even more pronounced in this new version. In paring down his material, the author has refined it almost out of existence. We seem to be watching the preparations for, and the aftermath of, a drama that never actually happens.

As before, we shuttle between two parts of the past. We are in New York, in the Eskimo Room of the American Museum of Natural History. An aged Henson, almost an exhibit himself, receives a visit from Peary's son, anxious to preserve his fathers place in the history books. It seems the Commanders claim to have been the first man at the Pole is being disputed by Henson, in his own favour. Back we flash, principally to the Arctic-moored ship from which Peary and company plan their polar conquest.

The part of Henson is played, as it was four years ago, by Nigel Shawn Williams, who is most impressive in his older, more tired incarnation, looking weary and bleary at the younger Peary. As the fresher, idealistic Matt he is little more than nice. This is despite the fact that, even at this point, the character has a lot to be angry about. Not only is Peary denying him his place in history, conceivably even usurping it, he is also robbing him of the Inuit woman whom he possibly loves and certainly fancies. I recall this relationship carrying considerable emotional weight in the earlier production -- here it goes for very little. It seems to have been much abbreviated in the writing; and the actress, Lucie Idlout, looks sweet but is otherwise inexpressive.

This typifies the show as a whole. The Calgary staging was uninspired but lucid, with the occasional line that jumped out at you. Ross Manson's production, for his Volcano company, is arty and opaque, with a lot of moaning acting. Teresa Przybylski's set is bright enough to make you snow-blind, but the human confrontations are muted. The crucial showdown between the mate and the commander is staged apologetically and off to one side.

Some of the overall muffled quality can be traced to David Foxs playing of Peary. This actor can hardly set foot on a stage without dominating it, and his best performances are transfixing. Vocally, hes always ready to rumble. He invariably sounds as if he's acting through his whiskers, whether or not he's wearing any. Here he gives us sounds rather than words, attitudes instead of thoughts.

He is accompanied by Jerry Franken and Tom Barnett, the same two actors who supported his massive performance in The Drawer Boy. (The three of them were also together at Stratford in The Trials of Ezra Pound. I occasionally wonder if they have the same agent, and if he or she has negotiated some kind of package deal.) Barnett, the youngest member of the team, always seems to spend most of his time listening to the effusions of his elders. He certainly does so here, in the thanklessly functional role of Peary junior. Franken, the most versatile, appears in the more modern scenes as Dr. Boas, an ethnologist. His role, too, comes over as thankless going on pointless; and again, if memory serves, truncated.

This is odd, because Boas scientific work is theoretically central to the plays concerns. On which his own position, as a Jewish refugee from Nazi Germany where eugenics are about to take their most sinister turn ever, should cast an especially ironic light. The play erects a totem pole of racial oppression, carried out by comparatively civilized people. The whites -- to put it gently -- patronize the blacks, and they both exploit the Inuit.

It's revealed that Peary had brought back half a dozen Arctic natives from one of his earlier expeditions and put them on show. They had all died in New York -- of the cold. When I saw the play in Calgary, during a remarkably clement February, that revelation froze us, too. In Toronto -- where most of us are currently able to think of several words for snow, few of them printable -- it passes by with hardly a shiver.
