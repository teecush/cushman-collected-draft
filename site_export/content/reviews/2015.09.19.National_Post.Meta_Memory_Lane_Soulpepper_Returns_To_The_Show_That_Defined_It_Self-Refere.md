Soulpepper first staged The Plays the Thing in 1999. It was the first production of the company's second season and it's the show that defined them. Their first two-play season had been a great success but it was still possible to attribute that to Robin Phillips who had directed both productions. There was then an interregnum production of Our Town: another success, and one that was to be often revived, but still a play that one would expect North American actors to know their way around. The Plays the Thing was something else: a Hungarian comedy from the 1920s, glorying in its own artifice. It gave Diego Matamoros his first Soulpepper leading role. And from the moment he stepped forward to deliver, with consummate cool, the plays opening speech, we knew we were home. Except that home had never been like this. There was a level of sophistication and assurance here unprecedented in Toronto. And so it went - in Beckett, Chekhov and Tennessee Williams - throughout the season.

The Play's the Thing was directed by an actual Hungarian, Laszlo Marton; his production was revived in 2003, and now it's back again. Matamoros still plays Sandor Turai, the boulevard dramatist who starts the play by remarking to his colleagues on how difficult it is to start a play. How much easier it would be if you could bypass all the customary contrived conversations and simply tell the audience who was who and what was what. He then proceeds to do just that, informing us that he, his longtime collaborator, and their new protege, a brilliant young composer, have just finished writing an operetta, and are currently holed up in a friends castle, awaiting the arrival of their leading lady who is also the composers fiancee. Exposition over, the action starts. The trio overhear their star in the next room, receiving the advances of an older married actor, and not resisting them as firmly as might be hoped. The composer is devastated; his friends fear for him, and fear even more for their own chances of having a hit. Sandor, forever unfazed, stays up all night and writes a play, incorporating all the shameless words that had emanated from next door. He then blackmails the guilty couple into performing it, so that his young musical genius will believe that he had unwittingly eavesdropped on a rehearsal. All ends happily, according to the ancient comic maxim that happiness consists of being well-deceived.

The production isn't quite the polished jewel it used to be; Matamoros, though stylish as ever, is almost too relaxed, and the first act moves slowly. The second, in which Sandor's overnight masterpiece gets rehearsed, is hilarious. There are two other survivors from the first staging: William Webster as second playwright, perpetually perplexed, and C. David Johnson in a gorgeous performance as the errant actor, sadistically saddled with an endless series of unpronounceable place-names. (The translation is by P. G. Wodehouse, the English master of pure comedy, and he really went to town here; or to a succession of towns.) Among the newcomers, Oliver Dennis is a perfect punctilious servant, Gordon Hecht a delightfully ingenuous young maestro, and Raquel Duffy the productions best prima donna to date. As a neurotically conscientious prompter Gregory Prest is funnily hysterical rather than hysterically funny; I preferred the quieter desperation of Jim Warren the first and second times around.

From old to new: Soulpeppers concurrent production of Happy Place is its first thoroughgoing world premiere, neither an adaptation (Of Human Bondage) nor a transfer from the fringe (Kim's Convenience). Pamela Mala Sinha's play does owe something to an earlier work; it takes off from her previous play Crash, a solo piece about a young woman surviving a rape. Happy Place gives us six women reeling from the same or similar traumas, all of them on suicide watch in a private care facility. But where Crash was a sustained creation, Happy Place has the feel of a diploma piece: a self-imposed assignment to write good roles for half-a-dozen actresses. In this it succeeds: a fine cast gets a succession of opportunities, some solo, some shared. But it doesnt add up to anything more organic.

It starts, like The Plays the Thing, magically, with the arrival on an empty stage of Samira, the patient whose experiences bear the strongest resemblance to the story of Crash; she proceeds to call the facility's communal area into being, one furnishing or appliance at a time. The elegance and simplicity of this prove typical of Alan Dilworth's production, but the device turns out to be structurally misleading. It suggests that Samira will be the focus of the play, but she turns out to be just one among equals; which is democratic but unsatisfying. Samira is played by Oyin Oladejo, who has tremendous presence but sometimes strays into orating rather than talking. The same pitfall periodically ensnares most of her colleagues, the shining exception being Diane DAquila who is scarifyingly good as an older woman with sulphurous reserves of anger. Liisa Repo-Martell is contrastingly, uncannily quiet as a patient nursing a possibly phantom pregnancy. Caroline Gillis is everybodys friend whom nobody likes; Irene Poole is a recently estranged lady apparently tormented by the fact that her adored son is only her stepson; and the author herself plays another woman with maternal issues. We learn more about some of them than about others, but not enough about any. Two of the inmates are discharged, or discharge themselves, but it's far from clear that they have become any less suicidal than those who remain. (And talking of loose ends, we're told that the facility charges $800 a day. Few of the characters look or talk as if they could afford it.)

There is a seventh character, the staff doctor; a role in which Deborah Drakeford looks right but sounds unconvinced. The ebbs and flows of institutional life are well captured, in writing and production, but there is little beyond that for the audience to hold on to. Sinha obviously didnt want to write a collective disease-of-the-week movie, and she probably wanted to avoid any kind of melodramatic climax, but if we are to be moved by the thought that life goes on, we need to be more fully involved in the lives in question. Soulpepper, true to eighteen years of form, offers another excellent ensemble but that, however much we enjoy watching actors interacting, cannot be an end in itself.
