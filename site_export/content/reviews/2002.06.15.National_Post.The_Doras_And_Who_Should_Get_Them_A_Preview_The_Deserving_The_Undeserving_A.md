Robertcushman@sympatico.ca

This year's Dora Mavor Moore Awards show appears to be the splashiest ever. The prizes are being dished out at the Princess of Wales Theatre on Monday -- The Lion Kings night off. The proceedings commence with a cocktail reception and end in Metro Square with something billed as Party with the Stars -- at least it does for us freeloaders and for those members of the public willing to shell out $150 for a VIP ticket.

With the Doras plainly shooting to be up there with the Tonys or even the Oscars, I feel like emulating my colleagues on the movie pages and offering my thoughts on who will win and who really deserves to.

Let it be said that these matters are less clear-cut in Toronto than on Broadway or in Hollywood. We dont breed blockbuster hits (though we import them), and we don't have enormous advertising budgets for kneecapping the opposition. We are a community, and it's a sure bet that at least half-a-dozen speechmakers will say so on Monday.

Still, the prizegivings have certain things in common. All are presented as races between the various suits (or, in our neck of the woods, the sweaters and jeans) who put the shows on; press releases and reports count the nominations garnered by each studio or theatre organization. I dont know why they think we care. Do most moviegoers even know which conglomerate financed their favourite flick?

In Toronto theatre, a company like Soulpepper may have a recognizable identity, since it presents classics and does so with a recognizable core of actors. But when a notable Canadian play comes along, I doubt that anyone by seasons end remembers whether they saw it at Tarragon, Factory, Passe Muraille or Canadian Stage.

Anyway ...

Best Play There are five contestants. There is no winner here as obvious as, say, Michael Healey's The Drawer Boy a couple of years ago. Healey's new and more complex Plan B deserves to win, but it had a mixed reception and besides, he won every conceivable prize for The Drawer Boy. I was glad to see that Alex Poch-Goldins witty and imaginative This Hotel got a nod, but I dont think it stands a chance. Nothing else was as entertaining as these two, but earnestness counts for a lot in Toronto, so I expect to see Daniel MacIvor's You Are There, Carole Frechette's Elisas Skin or Djanet Searss Adventures of a Black Girl in Search of God walk off with the trophy, in that order of likelihood.

Best Production of a Play I don't know what this means: presumably something to do with getting all the elements together and packaging them nicely. It's separate from best play or best direction, but in practice the best-produced show is going to look like the best-directed, and you wouldnt know the difference unless you were at rehearsals. In fact, the best-assembled shows were both at CanStage -- Indian Ink (the best play, as opposed to best Canadian play, of the season) and Picasso at the Lapin Agile -- but since these had the bad taste to be British and American plays with British and American directors, neither was nominated. Those that were: In On It, Skylight, Black Girl (my bet, though not my preference), The Island, and This Hotel (for which, lets hear it.).

Best Direction of a Play Laszlo Marton, when available, usually wins this, and should again for Uncle Vanya. My own runnerup: Kelly Thornton for This Hotel. Most vexing omissions: Kate Lynch's Midsummer Night's Dream and Herbert Olschoks La Ronde.

Best Actor in a Play Should be Alon Nashman (Picasso at the Lapin Agile). Diego Matamoros in Uncle Vanya was wonderful, but there were so many terrific performances in that show that it seems wrong to single him out, except perhaps symbolically. (Why not Charmion King as Best Supporting Actress? Too small a role to be noticed, I suppose.) John Kanis performance in The Island has been a legend for 30 years (as has that of his overlooked colleague, Winston Ntshona) so somehow doesn't count -- though we'd certainly all cheer for him. Peter Donaldson (Plan B) and Tony Nardi (The Lesson) are fine actors but theyve done better work elsewhere.

Best Actress in a Play This is the toughest: eight nominations. I can see nothing to choose between Yanna McIntosh (Skylight) and Fiona Reid (Indian Ink), but McIntosh had a potentially deadly role to enliven so Id go with her. So will the judges, since she's the newer girl and has two nominations. Though, as the other is for Belle, a repeat from last year, I can't see how its eligible. Tanja Jacobs is in with a chance as the performer of a highly regarded monologue (Elissas Skin), though in that area I preferred Jenny Young (The Shape of a Girl); I thought she was amazing.

The Musicals This is a farce. With the Mirvishes inactive, and Tarragon's Anything That Moves a revival, there were no new musicals. In the "best category, they've been reduced to nominating Damien Atkinss Real Live Girl, a standup act. I didnt much like The Piper but that surely deserved consideration at least as much. Why not simply suspend the category for a year?

In the production and direction areas, they've been further reduced to considering a Ross Petty pantomime and a thoroughly undramatic show about the Mamas and the Papas. I see no reason why Anything That Moves shouldn't clean up in every category allowed: direction (Alisa Palmer), actress (Glynis Ranney), actor (Tim Howar, though Damien Atkins is competition here) and -- probably -- choreography (Valerie Moore, though plays are also eligible here and there were nifty moves in a couple of them).

Real desperation: the appalling Saturday Night Fever is down for best touring production" though the Scots Medea (at World Stage) will obviously wipe it off the map.

Supporting Actor This can be male or female, play or musical. It has to be David Storch (The Lonesome West).

Design The years loveliest scenic design (Susan Bensons for Indian Ink) isn't nominated, though at least her costumes are. Also ignored: Scott Bradley's great set for Picasso .... Cheers, then, for Michael Levine (Uncle Vanya) closely followed by Steve Lucas (This Hotel).

All the above are in the "General Theatre, or big boys, division. In the smaller, or independent" (of what?) section, there is more of a tendency for two or three shows to sweep the board, like -- to name the most incongruous exemplars I can think of -- Titanic or The Producers. This year, the front-runners are Chekhovs Longs ... In the Ravine and -- my own preference -- The Walls of Africa. Though if Dennis OConnor doesn't win the best actor prize for his performance in The Dragonfly of Chicoutimi, I may very well get violent.
