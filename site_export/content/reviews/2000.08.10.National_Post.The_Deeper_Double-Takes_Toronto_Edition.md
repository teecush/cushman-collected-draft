THE SCHOOL FOR WIVES

Premier Dance Theatre, Toronto

Moliere's The School for Wives concerns a man who lives in mortal fear of being cuckolded. To avoid this terrible fate, he adopts a young girl and brings her up in total seclusion. He intends to marry her when she is of age, and assumes she will never betray him because she wont know how.

Laszlo Martons Soulpepper production is maybe not the most hilarious Moliere ever seen on a stage, but it goes unusually deep and it makes a gripping and rewarding evening.

The set, designed by Julia Tribe, takes the metaphor of imprisonment seriously. When the play begins, the jealous Arnolphe has brought his sheltered ward from the country to Paris, attended by a couple of blockish servants. The house in which he immures her looks at first glance like a cage: It's palisaded like an ancient public park, and as Arnolphe's obsessive jealousy grows he encloses it still further, personally inserting barricades between the spikes.

This is a dark production, but not a morbid one. It neither giggles nor gloats, and thats refreshing. Marton is a humane, grown- up director and in bringing him over from Hungary on a regular basis (this is his third Soulpepper production), the company is performing one of its greatest services.

Of course, a young man catches Agness eye, and of course they fall in love. The twist is that the lover is an acquaintance of Arnolphe's and, not knowing him to be his beloved's captor, confides in him every step of the way. The scenes between the two of them are a wonderful balance of forces: Arnolphe wounded but still feeling himself to be in control; the boy, Horace, acting like a dope but still carrying a biological guarantee of victory.

The play is better built than most Moliere and its confusions mount with steady logic. The knot is cut at the end by the usual revelations about mistaken identities and long-lost children. Its a true epiphany, but a cruel one -- bitter for Arnolphe, happy for everyone else. This production, rather remarkably, does justice to both sides.

Moliere wrote the part of Arnolphe for himself (as he wrote most of his star roles) and that of Agnes for Armande Bejart, whom he had married when he was 40 and she was 20. She was doubly his pupil, a member of his company and the daughter of his former mistress. (Its something of a Woody Allen scenario.) Many of Moliere's plays concern jealousy of one kind or another, but it's in this one and The Misanthrope (given an overly facetious production by Soulpepper two years ago) that art mirrors the real couples life most uncomfortably.

Forty is hardly over the hill, even in the 17th century, and part of the power of Joseph Zieglers Arnolphe lies in his being, on the face of it, a perfectly presentable suitor. He isn't a senile pantaloon (though there's a tradition of playing him that way) and he isnt a fool. His best friend pays tribute to his "good intellect." But he has undone himself: As both his friend and -- most devastatingly -- Agnes herself point out, by denying her worldly knowledge, he has left her without any defences against the first pretty face that comes along. Worse than that, by taking the girls love for granted, he has forfeited any chance of gaining it.

Moliere, we assume, was a natural clown. Ziegler, an excellent actor, is not. There are quite legitimate fits and double-takes written into the part that he doesn't even try to bring off. But he is sharp in the ones he does attempt, and he captures the deeper double-takes, of a man brought up against his own nature, and that's more important. His great confrontation with his too-carefully tended intended is very fine, with Liisa Repo-Martell perfectly capturing Agness funny simplicity and her amorous readiness and her deep sense of deprivation and hurt.

Most admirable, too, is Robert Persichini, who seems to have a droll, slow timing all his own. He plays Arnolphes confidant, whom I would expect to be played (not that I've spent years in thinking about it) as a city cynic, but who is here a wise and corpulent countryman, taking deception to be the way of the world and no reflection on the deceived. (Arnolphe's main problem is that he has a very fragile ego.)

Matthew Edison is a goofily agreable Horace, while Kristen Thomson (rather wasted) and Raoul Bhaneja are serviceable servants. A bemused Jim Warren and a twinkling William Webster satisfyingly round out the ensemble.

The production is as strong on story-telling as on theme and character, piecing out the action with some helpful silent interludes. (A couple of bits with dolls are heavy-handed.) The translation is one of Richard Wilbur's rhyming-couplet versions: not his best, and with some padding that sometimes leaves the actors gasping for air, but better than any other. Moliere in English took a great step forward when practitioners finally accepted -- I think it was sometime in the '60s -- that this is the form in which he wrote, and that we had better learn to deal with it.
