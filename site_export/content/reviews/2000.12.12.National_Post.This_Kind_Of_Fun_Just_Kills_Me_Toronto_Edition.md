MURDER ON THE AIR

Limelight Dinner Theatre, Toronto

I see that last time I visited the Limelight, I complained about the food. However, sometimes standards in the arts can rise, and on this occasion I had no problems with the establishment's roast beef, which was very tender and juicy. My two companions tell me they were happy with their chicken, and we all liked the green salad. Likewise, the house red.

If the dinner at this dinner theatre has improved, the theatre has made no progress whatever. Only some names have been changed, presumably to protect the takings. Last year, the title was Murder on the Limelight Express. This year, it's Murder on the Air. The format, the atmosphere, even the storyline and the assembled character types, remain essentially the same. It was fun then, and it is fun now.

Jean Daigle -- star, director and co-writer for the company that calls itself Mysteriously Yours --was last seen, by me anyway, as Hercule Poirot. Now he's a tough American-style private eye, somewhere between Philip Marlowe and Mike Hammer. More of a Hammer, I think. He wouldn't have the patience to negotiate Chandler's tortured thoughts and tortured prose: He has a tendency to shout at people and he is rather stout -- something like Tony Soprano gone over to the other side.

Once again, he has a murder to solve, and once again he does it by interrogating the broadly drawn genre figures who make up the rest of the cast.

He also picks on members of the audience to assist him in his investigations, strolling round the room to find appropriate victims. Far more of this show happens between the tables than happens on the stage.

The story is set in and around a radio studio. Time and place are left vague but we seem, spiritually at least, to be in the 1940s, the Age of Welles. The station's presiding genius is called Orson Kane; its leading actress is named Rose Bud; and the joint is owned by a plutocrat, one (how could there be more than one?) William Randolph Wurst, who starts the action by turning up in his office, dead.

The Wurst, in fact, is over. Everything else, of course, has only just begun.

Everybody who knew William Randolph had, naturally, a motive for bumping him off -- and also had, inevitably, the opportunity to do so.

The late unlamented was about to sell the station out from under them, thus infuriating the entire artistic staff (one director, two actors) plus his own daughter, who hoped to inherit the operation, and the much-put-upon 40-year-old pageboy who wanted to marry her and had been told he could do so only over her father's dead body. (If I, too, may be allowed to do some recycling, of a sentence from my review of this show's predecessor: discarded mistresses, disgruntled employees, disninherited offspring: the dead man has been dissing everybody.)

So our detective subjects each of them to merciless verbal probing, interspersed with wisecracks, and we in the audience try to keep up with the clues.

At the end, we are asked to say, via ballot papers, who we think has done it. I, as usual, had no idea (I knew who I wanted it to be, but I was wrong, again) and naturally I cannot reveal the truth -- though I may hint that the guilty figure in this piece turns out to be not dissimilar to the guilty figure in the previous one. The intervals required for us first to ponder and then to vote seemed rather drawn out, but maybe I should have ordered more wine.

Daigle seems, if anything, better suited to his gumshoe role than to the Belgian sausage he played last time round. He seems completely unfazeable, even when audience members devise snappier comebacks than the ones he had planned for them.

The rest of the company are equally adroit. Not since Moliere, or maybe Gilbert and Sullivan, has a theatrical troupe reacted with such apparent enthusiasm to the task of playing the same stereotypes in the same cartooned storyline, every time.

They get their best chances, though -- and we get our best entertainment -- in a scene-setting prologue when everybody, at least as far as we know, is still alive. We watch them putting on their radio show, and it is very enjoyably spoofed.

Patrons are drafted to read small roles and supply the sound effects, and since they have never done these things before, the players have plenty of opportunity to ad-lib to their mistakes. ("I hear him coming up the stairs" -- pause as the new technician fumbles with her maraccas -- "drunkenly.")

I'm a sucker for this kind of thing and, judging from the reactions around me, I'm not the only one. In fact, this show, by giving its actors a chance to satirize themselves as well as the people they're playing, may have an edge on its locomotive predecessor.

Pondering the inevitable sequels, I look forward more keenly to Murder in Hollywood than to Murder on the High Seas. Though how about Murder in the Newsroom, with one of the suspects a gibbering, neurotic critic? That should please everybody.
