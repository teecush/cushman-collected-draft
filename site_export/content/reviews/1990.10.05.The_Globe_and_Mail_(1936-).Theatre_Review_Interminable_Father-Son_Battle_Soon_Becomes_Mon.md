THE NORTHERN QUARTER

Written and directed by Alex van Warmerdam

Presented by The Mexican Hound

At the MacMillan Theatre in Toronto

---

It can be tough trying to escape from your father, especially when he is the kind of man who refuses to let you out of the house on your own, even when you are 43. This is not a situation too often encountered in everyday life, but nothing is impossible, especially when all concerned take a deep breath and agree to call it metaphor.

In The Northern Quarter, a Dutch production that opened Wednesday at the MacMillan Theatre in Toronto, the metaphor is pursued even in the casting. The play, which will close tomorrow, is presented by a long-established group whimsically called The Mexican Hound, whose guiding spirit is Alex van Warmerdam, writer, designer, director and actor. He appears here as the father, dominant even when dead. For when his oppressed son understandably takes a shotgun to him, he is still on hand to lecture the boy in the same tone of maddeningly silken sweet reasonableness he has adopted throughout. It's worse if anything, since as a ghost he now appears on film, eternally preserved and eternally re-runnable. His son is still a live actor, subject to all the ills and uncertainties performing flesh is heir to.

The son, whose name is Faas, wishes to play to be a painter. But, in real life, van Warmerdam is himself a painter so the situation is even more oppressive: when, in his stage role, he offers patronizing comments on Faas's work, he can do so with real conviction. And though Jack Vecht, the actor playing Faas, is not himself related to van Warmerdam (at least, not that I know) he is surrounded by people who plainly are. Credited with general management and production, and also taking a subsidiary acting role, is Marc van Warmerdam. At times the show aspires to the condition of rock opera: more convincingly, as regards both halves of that equation, than most claimants to the title. In control of the music is Vincent van Warmerdam. Vecht's performance is most charmingly and persuasively harried, and it is easy to cheer him on in his doomed struggle for independence. But obviously, when he chafes against family bonds and family pressures, he knows whereof he speaks.

The staging by the founding van Warmerdam is thoroughly painterly, in a minimalist way. It unfolds in tableaux, and each character is nattily and consistently decked out in a single color: grey, green, red. There is some wit in the varieties of parental torture on display. Mother, a subtler tyrant than father, declines to make her son his favorite fish-paste sandwiches on the ground that adding to their happiness would risk spoiling it.

Faas is informed that children, no matter their age, never go anywhere without their parents, unless of course those parents are dead. This comes under the heading of putting ideas into people's heads, and father's final fate shows where that leads. On the whole, ideas are things with which the family has no truck, and books are banned for that reason. Faas's parents do make him a present of a dictionary, but with the dangerous words (the vast majority) scored out.

In a play written, say, two years ago, by Vaclav Havel, that last detail might count as a neat piece of subversive satire. And apparently, when The Northern Quarter was first seen in Holland, it was taken by some critics as a blast against the enforced conformism and designer drabness of, say, East Germany. Even then that interpretation must have seemed smug: now it looks impossibly dated. Either way, the reaction has to be that of the criticized African ruler in Beyond the Fringe: "Take the mote out of your own eye before you start messing around with our beam." But if author-director van Warmerdam is going in for decoding, his methods are of limited use.

Most adults are, to a degree, stunted children, so we can all identify with Faas. But as it becomes clear that he is never going to win, not no way, the show becomes monotonous. And even designer-director van Warmerdam's trompe l'oeil visualization of the urban desert begins to resemble that horribly patronizing Pete Seeger song about the people who live in little boxes and all look just the same. Yes, it's sort of true and, yes, the show is fluent visually and performed for our benefit in English, with no apparent strain -- aurally. But (a) enough already and (b) what else is new?
