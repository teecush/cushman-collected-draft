The key line in Travesties is spoken by Nadya Krupskaya, more helpfully known as Mrs. Vladimir Lenin, when relating an incident from one of her husband's periods of imprisonment. She positioned herself outside the jail where he should have been able to see her. But," she says, "something went wrong. I forget what. Tom Stoppards play, a classic since its London debut in 1974, is about things going wrong for people with the loftiest ideas, and being misremembered afterwards to boot. Its about history's clowns.

The Lenins spent some of the First World War years in Zurich. So did James Joyce. So also did Tristan Tzara, one of the daddies of Dada. They weren't there at exactly the same time, but Henry Carr, writing his memoirs some 60 years later, would like to think they were. These unreliable reminiscences, irresistibly confided to the audience, are the spine of the piece. Its a play-within-a-monologue.

Carr, too, really existed. He was a minor official in the British consulate whose one claim to fame is that he played Algernon in a production of The Importance of Being Earnest for which Joyce was the business manager and which resulted in the two of them suing one another over the cost of a pair of trousers. Carr remembers himself as being the consul; and the actual consul, Bennett, as being his manservant. He also recalls being the friend and confidant of all three revolutionary transients, joining in their heated debates about the nature, purpose and proper relation of art and politics. Also those of their female acolytes: Carrs sister, Gwendolen, is a disciple of Joyce's gospel of art for art's sake, while his sweetheart, Cecily, a librarian, supplies books and worship to Lenin. The two ladies names may clue you in to the fact that Carrs dreamworld draws heavily on his role in The Importance; he pictures himself as an Oscar Wildean dandy, obsessed with his tailoring (those disputed pants). Large sections of the play are written as close and pointed pastiche of the parent comedy. (Joyce to the exhibitionist anti-artist Tzara: I advise you to acquire some genius and, if possible, some subtlety before the season is quite over.")Add to this, among much else, a musical crosstalk routine, a bump-and-grind on a library desk, and a scene written entirely in limericks. It's a brilliantly witty, and brilliantly intelligent, carnival.

It also has its drawbacks, chief being that it has no emotional arc. It certainly has heartfelt moments, notably Joyce's impassioned advocacy of art as magic, at the climax of which he literally produces a rabbit from a hat. Stoppard has said that he agrees with Joyce, but he gives equally persuasive arguments to Lenin and Tzara, of whom he disapproves, and to Carr, the plain man as philistine. But nobody in the play actually goes through anything; there are no doubts, fears, or disillusions, and the only plot is Oscars. This makes it less satisfying than Jumpers, its Stoppardian predecessor and companion-piece. Jumpers is a moving as well as a sparkling work, but in Travesties everything depends on sparkle: a daunting assignment that Joseph Zieglers Soulpepper production gets an impressive four-fifths right. It gets full marks for logic and lucidity, less -- after a delightfully deadpan opening sequence -- on bravura. Christina Poddubiuk has designed an elegant and versatile set for the craziness to bounce off, including a clock that can move smartly backwards to underline the glitches in the hero's head. But the limericks, for example, go slow and unmusical, and the show in general could use more showbiz.

It is, though, splendidly acted: far better than the last London revival, in which Antony Sher's old-man act as Carr killed off most of the laughs. Soulpeppers Carr is Diego Matamoros, whose gift is to establish a rhythm and identity for every role he plays and explore from there. Here he does it twice over and, barring a few lapses in comic phrasing, superbly. As the old Henry, hes the image of a dry, tetchy English pundit, brooking no contradictions except his own. As the young one, he's a self-satisfying man about town, more priggish than debonair and more Wodehouse than Wilde: especially with Kevin Bundy ineffably wonderful as his man, a Jeeves with socialist leanings who's also a dab hand, and foot, at a hat-and-cane number.

Jordan Pettle brings a welcome flamboyance and precision to Tzara (Jack to Henrys Algy) and David Storchs Joyce, though sometimes too muted, is fine in his prophetic utterances, as well as sporting a great cartoon outline. Oliver Dennis and Maggie Huculak are pitch perfect as the Lenins who, despite Stoppard's extensive rewrites, remain largely sealed off in their own part of the play, as in the sealed railway carriage that carried them back to Russia. Sarah Wilson and Krystin Pellerin are treats as the two young ladies, who end up butting heads and minds in a vaudeville duet ("oh, Gwendolen...oh, Cecilee) that might be even more pointed with more talking, less singing. Pellerin, as a librarian successively stern and melting, is especially fine, surpassed for endearingness only by this production's bunny, definitive in its role. - Travesties runs through March 21 at the Young Centre.
