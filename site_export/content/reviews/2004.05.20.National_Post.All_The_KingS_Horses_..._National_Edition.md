DEATH AND THE KING'S HORSEMAN

Artword Theatre, Toronto

- - -

The Nigerian laureate Wole Soyinka wrote Death and the Kings Horseman in 1976, and it became an instant classic. A Toronto production was planned, and then cancelled, in 1987. It has finally reached us in a joint production by AfriCan Theatre Ensemble and the Artword Theatre that might, it if it had more distinction, be called a disaster. As it is, its simply a disgrace.

The play is set in a Nigerian town in 1945. The king of a Yoruba tribe has died and custom decrees that his Horseman, one of his highest officers, follow him in an act of ritual suicide.

The play has a five-act structure, on the Greek model, and Act I shows the Horseman, egged on by his Praise-Singer, announcing his intentions to the women of the market, who form the singing-dancing- drumming chorus of a play that's both tragedy and comedy.

Act II takes us to the British Residency, where the plans of the District Officer to attend a party at the European Club (its first in ages, apparently) are disrupted by the sounds of uproar in the town. These two acts are written, obviously, in disparate styles -- the first florid and ceremonial, the second prosaic and clipped.

The two modes begin to impinge on one another in Act III, in which a black police sergeant, under white orders, makes a comically ineffectual attempt to stop the immolation. They collide in Act IV, which sees the return of the Horseman's son, back from studying medicine in London and finding himself caught, angrily, between two worlds.

Act V is full verbal confrontation between two modes of life, not to mention two views of death. There is a final twist that is not in the least surprising -- and was probably never meant to be.

The performing challenges it presents are obviously enormous. They are faced here by a catastrophically unpractised cast who seem to have been given no help whatsoever. The director, Ronald Weihs, is probably right in claiming the play has no subtext and that the actors should simply "let the words do the work.

This, though, as many Shakespeare productions have all-too- grimly revealed, can be perilous advice. However rich or vigorous the language (and lack of subtext is not always a virtue unalloyed), it still has to be pitched, directed, shaped. At the very least, it has to be spoken clearly. This production gets off to a typically thoughtful start by playing a vital expository duologue on the steps of the auditorium, behind the backs of most of the audience. Baffled at the outset, it takes us ages to catch up.

I cant believe it was Soyinkas intention to shut out a non- African audience (he writes in English, after all), but we do need help. Not that the script always gives it: When the exasperated District Officer asks the Horseman, Must you people always speak in riddles?" all but the most committed multiculturalists are likely to sympathize.

I confess I found the strictly English scenes easier to understand. I did not, though, find them any easier to enjoy. They are just as feebly acted, and if anything -- since they don't have the crutch of ritual to help them along -- even worse staged. The Officer's wife is condemned by the vagaries of the set to play an extended scene half-hidden behind a downstage grille.

I remarked last week, when reviewing Consecrated Ground, that some of its performers were amateurish without being bad. Those here are amateurish and bad, at least at delivering lines. Some of them do have other talents. The singing is all right, the dancing lively without being especially inventive and Donald Carr, playing the Horseman, gives a remarkable physical account of the long self- hypnotized approach to sacrificial death.

"Your eyes are glazed like those of a courtesan says one of the spectators, but its true not just of his eyes but of his whole gleaming, twisting body. That, though, is just one side of the character. The other is the sensual man who has relished his life (the juiciest fruit on every tree was mine") and who still clings to it, especially when it takes the form of a new young wife. I said the play was both tragic and comic: The Horseman is a man whom Fate has cast in a tragedy who often seems to feel that he would be more at home in the other genre. Like his son, though in very different ways, hes conflicted, and the results are agonizing. At least, that's what I suspect was going on. But I can only guess.

I have always thought it more of an insult than a compliment to call a performance "sincere." But, when skill is lacking, sincerity can be something useful to fall back on. And there are moments late in the evening when both Carr and Ian Morfitt (as the District Officer) communicate an awareness of what their characters are going through and of what they stand for. They can't project it, but we know they care. At such moments, the cultural clashes that are at the plays heart (and which could hardly be more topical) make themselves felt. The plays bold classic outline, and the way the action is disposed within it, are convincing proof that this is an author who aims high and has the right to do it.

I am not convinced this is a great play, as opposed to a play with greatness in it. To believe that, I would have to see a production that turned all the fine words into weapons. In the current circumstances, even accepting that they're fine is an act of faith.
