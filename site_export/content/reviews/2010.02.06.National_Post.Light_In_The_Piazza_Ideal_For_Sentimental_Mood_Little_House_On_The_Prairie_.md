An overture in a musical is a rare thing these days. The Light in the Piazza had me in its corner from the first notes played by a quintet, with a harp predominant, from behind a scrim. The opening theme they offer is a ravishing one, heralding a score and a show that turn out to be uncommonly beautiful and peculiarly moving.

It's peculiar in two senses: scarcity and strangeness. Honestly romantic musicals, of which this is one, are an endangered species, and this show, which ran for about a year in New York, tells an unusual and theoretically difficult tale. The Light in the Piazza, music and lyrics by Adam Guettel, is based on a New Yorker short story (by Elizabeth Spencer) that afterwards became a novel. Margaret, middle aged and middle American, comes to Florence where she spent her honeymoon, with her daughter Clara in tow. Clara is in her mid-twenties but her mental development, for guilt-inducing reasons, stopped short when she was 12; her physical and emotional development obviously didn't and neither, it becomes clear, did her perceptions. They meet Fabrizio, aged 20; he falls heavily for Clara, as she for him, even wants to marry her. His family offer few objections, but then they don't know the whole truth about Clara, and Margaret can never quite manage to tell them.

The unfolding of this story is remarkably un-queasy. It is, though, creatively disturbing, because not only does the audience not know what's going to happen, it doesn't know what it wants to happen. We have our own prejudices to overcome. The emotional effect isn't always due to the songs; Craig Lucas's book is exceptionally skilful and discreet. In Robert McQueen's very fine production for Acting Up Stage, one of the most powerful scenes involves Jacquelyn French as Clara simply rushing distraught about the stage; another has her, lost and frightened in a strange city, accosting strangers and reciting her name and address, the safety code her mother obviously taught her.

The New York production was by all accounts a visual feast. This one isn't quite that, but it has a good spare look, and it makes us listen. Guettel's music, flavouring and pervading the evening as in a chamber opera, is alternately bracing and sensual. The Italian characters sing mainly in Italian (this isn't confusing) and are joyous and full-throated, even when cynical; Jeff Lillico's Fabrizio, ardent and naive, and Juan Chioran as his witty and worldly father are beyond good, while Christina Gordon, Tracy Michailidis and Michael Torontow supply some darker sidelights as their relatives (of whom one would like to know more than the show tells us). The two American ladies, let loose in their and most other people's dream country, have numbers that veer between spiky and abandoned, with something of Richard Rodgers (Guettel's grandfather) and something of Stephen Sondheim (Guettel's mentor). The only song that misfires is the title number; the most conventional item in the score, it seems to be trying strenuously to justify a title, and an image of happiness, bequeathed by its source. The major love duet, Say It Somehow, is musically a marvel, though its lyrics ( "I hear the sound of wrap your arms around me") seem to have lost touch with what these two young people might actually be saying. It's like they've been listening to too many love songs (and Fabrizio's English, I must say, seems to improve spectacularly over a very short period.) Elsewhere, Guettel's words, if not the equal of his melodies, are intelligent and aware.

There's a crucial shortfall in the presentation of Margaret. This is really her story, though at one remove; her own marriage is decomposing, and she's torn between rejecting it, reliving it through her daughter and simply letting Clara, after all these years, go her own way. (She also seems to be nourishing a crush on Fabrizio's papa.) It's through her eyes that we're meant to see the conditional quality of happy endings. It's all there in outline, but not really fleshed out; and Patty Jamieson, though acting and singing well enough, cannot take us deep enough inside the woman for us to believe in some very abrupt transitions. Too much of her is shell. All the same, I haven't witnessed a standing ovation so heartfelt and deserved since the Shaw Festival's The President. That show overwhelmed us with laughter; this one does it with unsentimental tears. Guettel may not be the future of Broadway, things being as they are, but he ought to be.

From the near-sublime to the indisputably ridiculous: Little House on the Prairie, based as it is on a popular series of books that became a television series, seems to be America's answer to Anne of Green Gables, only less gritty. Set in pioneer South Dakota, it doesn't even work on its own tear-jerking terms; we can't be moved by the simple fact of a girl going blind because the show insists on singing inspirational songs about it. For rural flavour, the music strains after Aaron Copland and the dancing after Agnes de Mille. Melissa Gilbert, famous for having played Laura the tomboy heroine on TV, reappears as her mother, for no other discernible reason. Kevin Massey stands out as the love interest of the present Laura (Kara Lindsay, pretty good considering); they have a quirkily shaped second-act duet that seems to have fallen out of a better show. It even hints, briefly, at some sustained narrative interest. Otherwise, it's just one tarnation thing after another, so unspeakably saccharine that for long stretches I could hardly look at the stage.
