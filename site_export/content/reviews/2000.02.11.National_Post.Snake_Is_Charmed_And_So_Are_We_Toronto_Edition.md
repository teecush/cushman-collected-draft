MELLA MELLA

Young People's Theatre

Lusciously imaginative, Mella Mella had been promoted from the Young People's Theatre studio to the organization's main stage as a contribution to Black History Month.

Gail Nyoka's play is a dramatization of a Zimbabwean legend. Mella (Joanne Degand) is the daughter of a village king who lies sick and apparently incurable. Two bullying young guards (Martin David Peters and Gneon Douho) are already preparing to take power, ignoring the hereditary claims of the princess. She, however, is modest but resourceful. On the advice of a village wise woman (Layah Davis), she goes in search of an even wiser woman, the medicine lady, N'anga (Djolei Gogoua).

N'anga, in turn, points her in the direction of a powerful cave- dwelling serpent known as the Python Healer. This is something of a kill-or-cure proposition, since the snake has a fearsome reputation. Indeed, when Mella's two male rivals make the trek to the python's cavern, he has them running away screaming, and they have to tell lies about their adventures after they have made it back to the village.

Mella, however, being a good person to begin with and having received much valuable instruction both from N'anga and from her father's loyal counsellor, Gamyambadzi (Beche Ako), charms the snake and, indeed, returns home with it wreathed round her neck.

It restores her father, who settles down to a long and healthy reign with Mella in unopposed line for the succession. She has proved herself both a strong woman and a dutiful daughter.

Many points in this story are legendary favourites the world over. On their respective journeys to the python's lair, both Mella and the guards encounter a sick merchant begging for water. Mella, as befits a heroine, takes time out to fetch him some; the men -- as befits greedy, impatient, uncharitable louts -- do not.

In patriarchal European fairy tales, similar incidents function as an infallible means of telling the good brother from the bad. The suppliant usually turns out to be some sort of supernatural benefactor in disguise; and even here, though the Python never actually alludes to the incident, we can be fairly sure he knows about it.

The tale, however, despite vigorous acting all round, is less important here than the telling, which is magical. Pierre Tretault's production looks and sounds splendid from the outset. Drums beat out complex and compelling patterns as a visual pattern no less compelling, made up of silhouetted branches and a golden sun, unfold on the cyclorama. Shadows, it turns out, will tell much of the story: shadows of vegetation, of masks, of human and animal figures. The prize silhouette, naturally, is that of the python itself, which has a sinuous and challenging neck and a darting, quivering tongue.

Groupe Bassan and Glenn Davidson are responsible for the shadow puppet design. Davidson is also credited with the set and lighting, which keep a constant stream of arresting pictures before our eyes and also make it worth our while to glance down to a mosaic stage floor in yellow and black.

The robes are gorgeous; they include one especially striking creation for a thin and apparently headless actor who looks like a walking clothes closet; he or she presumably symbolizes the kingdom deprived of its ruler.

Costume designer is the same Djolei Gogoua who imperiously narrates the play in the role of N'anga and who also does the voice of the Python, for whom she supplies some suitably intimidating sibilants.

A remarkable team also includes Beche Ako, choreographer, and Gneon Douho, who as an actor is one of the bad guys, but as a composer is on the side of truth, good order and a commanding beat.

A treat for the children for whom it's intended, the show also rewards the attention of any passing adults.
