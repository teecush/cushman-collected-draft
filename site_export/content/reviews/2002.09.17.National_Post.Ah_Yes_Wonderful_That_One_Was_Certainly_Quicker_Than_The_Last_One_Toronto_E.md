ARTURO BRACHETTI

Canon Theatre, Toronto

Arturo Brachetti's one-man show calls to mind a mildly salacious joke that was going around when I was an undergraduate:

First Guy: "Doesnt Sally dress well?"

Second Guy: Yes, and so quickly, too.

Brachetti dresses very well and very fast. He is a virtuoso quick- change artist, a magician who does it with himself instead of with rabbits. He starts off with a bit of symbolism. He takes a white mask off his face; beneath it is another white mask, and then another. One has lost count long before he discards the last. In its own small way, this looks impossible: How can there have been room for so many layers? And this is a pointer to what he does for the rest of the evening. He will go offstage in one complicated costume, and return -- apparently instantaneously -- in another. However many dressers he has in the wings (the program only lists two), this is impressive. And it goes on impressing. For the whole two-hour show, our breath stays taken. That, however, is all there is to the evening. And it is not enough.

Brachetti, who began his career at home in Italy, nevertheless made his major breakthrough here in Canada -- in Montreal, at the Just for Laughs festival. This, on his current shows evidence, is surprising, because he doesn't seem to be much of a comedian: not a verbal comic, at least not in English, and not a great physical clown, either.

His director, Serge Denoncourt, and his designer, Guillaume Lord (both French-Canadian) have staged his act imposingly; it all takes place within and around a kind of revolving house.

The big set piece, cued by Brachetti's informing us that, like all Italian children, he grew up on American movies, is a long Hollywood montage in which he appears as one familiar icon after another. Its the ultimate drag-queen extravaganza, though more innocent than some. I dont mean it lacks sexual innuendo, merely that Brachetti shows no inclination to sneer at his subjects. This is refreshing but limited, since he neither characterizes nor impersonates; he simply shows up, suitably attired, and assumes an appropriate position.

The same goes for an earlier routine in which he acts out a western, switching between five archetypal characters. There is wit in the costumes (for which he himself is presumably responsible, since no one else is credited) and in the transitions between them, but little in what he does while wearing them.

He has the frantic eagerness-to-please of his compatriot Roberto Benigni, but he does not have much personality. Nor does it help that in his chats to the audience he draws attention to this shortcoming -- and even gets metaphysical about it. In the show's first half, he tells us about his childhood; he shows us supposed film footage of his family (and, of course, steps off the screen dressed as each one) and tells us about the local priest whose hobby was stage magic and who got him started.

In the second, he talks about a last conversation with his mother, who told him how proud she was of his success as the man of a thousand faces, and then added, (a little too conveniently for comfort) "But, Arturino, who are you really?" It sounds like one of those questions best not asked, or at least best not repeated in front of an audience.

Anyway, it gets dodged. Apparently, the television was on during their conversation, and they were interrupted -- oh coincidence! -- by the news of the lifetime-achievement Oscar being given to Federico Fellini. This prompts another ingenious, and technically impeccable, movieland tribute that culminates in Brachetti crying out, Thank you, Federico!

One would like to echo this with "Thank you, Arturo," but we are as far away from discovering the real Brachetti as ever we were. Which might not matter if he hadn't raised the subject in the first place.

He also includes some more mundane interludes, which, lacking a transforming spark, remain mundane. One of them involves a three- cornered hat, squashable into various shapes, whose versatility the maestro nonetheless overrates. (I wasn't surprised that a child in the audience failed to identify one of its wearers as Captain Hook. Who, trying to identify Hook, would look at his head?) Another is some shadow hand puppetry, which is clever but still the stuff of a hundred variety shows.
