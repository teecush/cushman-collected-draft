THEATRE REVIEW

More Fine Girls

Tarragon Theatre, Toronto

My memories of the 1995 Theatre Columbus hit The Attic, the Pearls and Three Fine Girls are rather dim, though I do recall that arriving late in the run I couldn't see what all the fuss had been about. The show seemed, to put it charitably, slight. That, though, would place it several steps above its new sequel More Fine Girls, which is dire.

The three fine girls are three sisters whose surname is Fine (which sounds Jewish though we're led to believe they aren't) and whose first names are Jojo, Jelly and Jayne: an arrangement that should have led them to hate their parents more than they actually do. Not that they don't have their issues. They are the creations of Martha Ross, Leah Cherniak and AnnMarie MacDonald, who wrote and played them the first time around, and who now, as then, share authorship credit with Jennifer Brewin, Theatre Columbus's new artistic director, and with Alisa Palmer, director of the actual shows.

The first play found the three Fines on the top floor of the old family home, acting out memories from childhood and later, while their father's funeral happened downstairs. The new play balances its predecessor to the extent that it ends up in the same house, but with the revelations occurring in the basement. And if part one had an overload of whimsy, this one has nothing but.

It's aimed, obviously, at audiences eager to discover what happened to the characters in later life, rather than at those like me who had completely forgotten what happened to them earlier. However, research reminds me that Jojo was a theatrical academic, Jelly an artist and Jayne a high-flying exec. Fifteen-odd years later, Jojo and Jelly are still in the same lines of work, but Jayne has come down with a severe case of back-to-nature and has retired to a farm. Jojo and Jayne each keep expressing anxiety about the other's love life, or lack of it, and as much as they try to sound well-intentioned, they end up sounding schadenfreudian. They have similar, though greater, worries about Jelly who has inherited the house. She's turned it into an outsize art installation and hopes, as a birthday present to her daughter whose name is Jessie (of course), to levitate it. There are chairs and tables hanging on the walls of Judith Bowden's set, which turns out to be not a metaphor but a naturalistic depiction of how Jelly has done the place up.

The whole performance aims at being engagingly offthe-wall, but other than scenically, it doesn't have any wall to be off. It's weightless and aimless, a play with no moorings, just a series of gags loosely tied to presumed emotional baggage that nobody gets around to unpacking. Jojo, whose professional tic is to drop the names of major modern playwrights, demonstrates terminal chutzpah by remarking that Chekhov at least gave his three sisters somewhere to sit down. He also of course gave them relationships, passions and motivations, but Jojo is tactful enough not to mention it. She and her siblings do get to sit down together in one scene, set in a restaurant where they frustratingly fail to order anything: a frustration that the audience finds it all too easy to share. The only other emotions to make themselves properly felt are Jelly's annoyance at her sisters for invading her space and her fervent wish that they would go away.

Part of the reason for siding with Jelly rather than with either of the others is the way that Severn Thompson plays her; or perhaps just the fact that Thompson plays her. She is, to put it brutally, the only person onstage who can act: the only one, that is, who delivers her lines as if they were actually addressed to someone and who grasps that, even in the role of an eccentric artist who claims contact with extraterrestrial forces, underplaying can be a viable option. Thompson came late into the cast when Cherniak left it for what are described as "personal reasons." That, though it may be a breach of critical protocol to mention it, sounds suspicious; it suggests she had realized that there was no there there.

Anyway, Thompson, though playing in a different mode from her colleagues, manages to mesh with them perfectly in matters of narrative and timing, which is admirable. The other two are not actors but performers, at least MacDonald is. I remember thinking that she was much the best thing in The Attic, but her wide-eyed, jolly-hockey-sticks, hyper act has worn very thin. I will admit, though, that she's very funny when placing a call to Ikea and having to deal with their automated operator; we've all been there. (I mean to the operator, not to Ikea, though on second thought and since we're at the Tarragon .) Ross just wafts, all over the stage. Though she, too, has her empathetic moment when she reveals that, even though he's been her bread and butter, she hates Brecht. Like an AA confessional, it should do both speaker and listeners a power of good.

More Fine Girls runs until April 3. For tickets, call 416-531-1827 or visit tarragontheatre.com.
