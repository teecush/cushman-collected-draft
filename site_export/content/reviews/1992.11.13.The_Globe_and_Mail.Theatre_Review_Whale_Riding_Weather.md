THEATRE REVIEW WHALE RIDING WEATHER

Friday, November 13, 1992

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Toronto ONT -- WRITTEN BY BRYDEN MacDONALD Directed by Annie Kidder At the Factory Theatre Rating: Bryden MacDonald's Whale Riding Weather is dedicated to the propositions that all we have is one another and that love will conquer all Whale Riding delivers maximum voltage BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Toronto ALLAN Gray is the first actor I have seen who can act with the whites of his eyes. Or maybe, since Im terribly unobservant, he is just the first actor I have noticed doing it. In Whale Riding Weather, performed now by the same cast that originated it last year, there is a moment in which Gray looks at a lover - by this time an ex-lover - with pupils that are absolutely dead. That may be expected. What surprises and alarms is that the sockets encasing them look so blank, weary, hopeless: whiter than white.

The more dead beat the characters the livelier Bryden MacDonalds writing gets. Gray plays Lyle whom we find in a cluttered, decrepit apartment that he has not left in years, lying on a couch from which he has probably not risen in days, and wearing underpants on which I prefer not to speculate. His mind and his tongue, though, remain in brisk working order. They are exercised on various subjects: his past (in another life he had a wife and son), his current life. Remember, he says, unarguably and with relish, "mediocrity rises."

He is addressing his younger companion Auto who sits near-catatonic at a table covered with pills and booze, and whom Lyle once, in every sense of the word, took in. Auto turns out to be capable of speech, and even of going outside to do the shopping. This time he returns with more than cat- food. This time he brings home a man, name of Jude. (Patron saint of lost causes, sneers Lyle: you've come to the right place.) A pick-up is something Lyle has satirically been urging on Auto, but he is of course disagreeably surprised when it actually happens. He can see how things will go.

So can we. Jude, first off, is predatory, and alarmed by Auto's desperate physical and emotional state: his walking zero-ness. He then becomes moved by it, involved with it, which of course is medicine in itself, and the two go off together. But predictable is not necessarily bad; when its good we call it inevitable. Inevitability is implicit in every mocking suffering glance, or witty suffering word, that Lyle directs at the newly happy couple, and it sustains the play. Also there is an exploratory excitement about the love scenes themselves; or about the mere fact of encountering love-scenes, of any gender, in the theatre today.

As long as the play remains in the present, it is riveting. It loses its grip when it ventures into the past, and I do not mean flashbacks. The confrontational scenes in which, with a great show of indignation, characters tell one another things they already know or, even more alarmingly, things they unaccountably dont. As always these scenes, because of their inherent improbability, are stuffed with Fine Writing, here of the lyrical great-outdoors type. This is where the whale-riding comes in. It derives from Lyle's Maritime past. What it signifies, I leave you to discover for yourselves. And when you have found out, please tell me.

I am praising and blaming this play. That is reasonable. Despite its sexual orientation, despite its Pinteresque triangular warfare over a tattered piece of ground (The Caretaker, the classic of the genre) this is a mainstream work, dedicated to the propositions that all we have is one another and that love will conquer all. Auto is an original creation, who in Randy Hughson's performance never loses his mystery, but Jude, far from obscure, is a standard stud, though Patrick Galligans performance individualizes him. Lyle we have often met before, though generally with his clothes on; ageing (i.e. older than other characters) homosexuals in plays can usually be relied on to greet visitors, and delight audiences, with quizzical put-downs. Of course theyre in pain underneath it all; here gay pride and straight morality find common satisfaction. Still it is remarkable how much resigned anguish, and how little self-pity, Gray's Lyle can inject into a line like "for an instant you thought I was beautiful," spoken very quiet and quick.

In fact, any reservations I have about the text are effectively quashed by Annie Kidder's direction. An electric current flowing from stage to auditorium and back again, growing stronger with each transaction defines successful theatre. Here it is at maximum voltage. Whale Riding Weather continues until Nov. 29.
