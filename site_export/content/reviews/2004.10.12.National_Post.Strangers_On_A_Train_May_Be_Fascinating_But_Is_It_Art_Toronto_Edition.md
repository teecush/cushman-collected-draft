THE UNEXPECTED MAN

Tarragon Extra Space, Toronto

- - -

If what we're told about North American tastes is true, then Yasmina Rezas plays are, or ought to be, deeply unfashionable. Experience, though, proves they arent and oughtn't. This French lady writes the kind of plays that used to be described as "civilized": plays about economically comfortable Europeans with mainstream cultural concerns. Despite this handicap, her play Art has been a worldwide success. The Unexpected Man has done less well, which is understandable, but it has done all right, which is deserved.

It's a more challenging piece than Art. It has two characters instead of three, much less action and fewer jokes, though it may have more wit. Where Art was concerned wholly with the foibles of cultural consumers, this play has a creator as half its cast. Hes a novelist, and the plays title is also the name of his latest book. It's also a description of him, at least in his relation to the other character, who is a female admirer of his work and with whom he finds himself alone in a confined space. She knows who he is, but to him she could be anybody. As they wonder whether or how to make contact, we listen to their interior monologues, or -- since they are sharing a compartment on the express from Paris to Frankfurt -- their trains of thought.

He describes himself, right at the start, as an embittered man, ill at ease with the world of things; he makes a fetish of it, though one suspects he would like to be lured out of it. Meanwhile, he is having a good discontented time, complaining to himself about his sleep habits, about his bowels (whatever, he wonders rhetorically, happened to Ex-Lax?), about his daughter, who has committed the unforgivable sin of getting engaged to a man who's inaudible, and about his oldest friend, who has in effect deserted him by taking up with a comparatively young woman. (Youth, it is clear, is the enemy.)

His travelling companion, too, has lost a close male friend -- in her case, to death. He wonders why she, unlike any other woman on a train, isnt reading. She in fact has a copy of his latest in her bag, and is pondering whether she dare bring it out and, if so, when. In his head, hes defensive but curious; in hers, she's flirtatious but scared. In ours, we're fascinated but impatient, wondering whether either will make a move, whether the monologues will ever become dialogue. We may even, if weve travelled on English trains, remember that the alarm system there is called the communication cord. We make silent bets on whether they will, metaphorically, pull it.

Its a slender thread, but it neatly ties together the intellectual, the emotional and the mundane, and -- in Christopher Hampton's more than admirable translation -- it has just enough suspense to hold us. It's surprising, given the authors reputation, that the play hasnt been given a higher-profile Toronto staging. Physically, it demands little; spiritually, it could take more.

Chris Abraham's production, though perfectly intelligent, is economical to a fault. The performances, by a grumbling Les Carlson and a musing Deborah Kipp, are accurate but low key. It should be possible to probe further into these characters' interiors -- into her yearnings and his discontents -- and emerge with more colours.

In London these roles were played by Eileen Atkins and Michael Gambon, two of the best, and though it's considered bad critical form to suggest alternative casting for a given production, it's hard here not to start thinking of possible Canadian equivalents. (Eric Peterson and Seana McKenna were the two who tugged hardest at my imagination.) But this is still the most recommendable play in Toronto, and I recommend it.
