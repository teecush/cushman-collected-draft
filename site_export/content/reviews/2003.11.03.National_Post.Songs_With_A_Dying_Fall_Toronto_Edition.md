GOODBYE, TIM HARDIN

The Poor Alex Theatre, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - Right at the beginning of his first hit song, If I Were a Carpenter, Tim Hardin rhymed "lady" with baby. Four hundred years earlier, Shakespeare, through the mouth of Benedick in Much Ado About Nothing, had expressed withering opinions about people who do that, and they are still valid. To be fair, the meandering folksy quality of Hardin's tune -- and indeed of his lyric -- is not of the kind that leads you to expect great rhyming precision, and the song does have its own plaintive appeal.

He was to improve on it, though, with the tender, bemused Misty Roses, a song of adoration shot through with previsions of loss; and with Reason to Believe, an honest look at a lovers infinite capacity for self-delusion. In Goodbye, Tim Hardin, a play in which he is the only speaking character, Hardin includes these two songs on the very short list of his own favourites. He has good taste.

But then we dont know if it's really his taste, or that of Bill Ballantyne, who plays him here and who is also the author. The two men apparently met once, in the 1970s when Hardin was playing a gig in Toronto. He suggested tea at the King Edward Hotel (Ballantyne got stiffed with the bill), and it may be that confidences exchanged over the sandiwches and pastries found their way into the present script. Otherwise we don't know what the sources are. Since the play is subtitled An Imaginary Life we can assume a fair amount has been made up.

Hardin was a junkie who acquired his habit in the army, before his musical career even started. The havoc wrought by his heroin habit on his life and his talent is, if youll pardon the expression, the substance of the play. His greatest successes were in the mid-1960s. We meet him in 1980, in a rundown apartment in Los Angeles, on his last night before dying of an overdose. He was 39. If Pete Postlethwaite in Scaramouche Jones is the spryest centenarian imaginable, then Ballantyne here is about the most grizzled guy never to have made it to 40. Hes in danger of having his poetic licence endorsed.

It's a two-man show. In one half of the stage, Ballantyne plays Hardin; in the other, David Matheson plays -- and sings -- Hardin's songs. At the outset, Ballantyne seems a delightful performer, telling his story in tones of gentle amazement, as if he didnt quite believe his own life; his voice and eyebrows arch in unison. It gradually dawns on us, though, that this is all he can do -- or at least all that his own text will allow him to do.

His Hardin is fun when reminiscing about his poverty days, hanging out in Greenwich Village with his fellow folkies Fred Neil and Phil Ochs; the three of them would eat substantial meals at upscale places like the Algonquin and the Russian Tea Room, before making their escape through the mens room window. (It's left unclear whether they were ever able to pull this stunt more than once at any one restaurant, but in retrospect Hardin seems to have been practising up for tea at the King Eddie.)

They would also spend time in clubs, listening to Thelonious Monk and Miles Davis. Again, there's a suspicion that Ballantyne, a jazz fan (and a pianist in another life) is projecting his own tastes onto his subject, but its true that Hardins own songs are more musically interesting than those of most of his contemporaries, which is probably why they attracted good people like Bobby Darin. Ballantyne has Hardin talking lovingly about harmonies, as well as quoting with relish, if no particular relevance, from Shakespeare and Yeats. We can also cheer him on when he mocks the fresh- scrubbed protest-folk that immediately preceded him, and especially when, contemplating a later generation, he excoriates "that idiot Scotsman with no sense of rhythm" -- this turns out to be Rod Stewart, who was to have a hit with Reason to Believe, a song that deserved a better fate.

Ballantyne-Hardin is out of line, though, when he implicitly tars Darin with the same brush; you have only to listen to their respective albums of standards to hear the difference -- Darin was all rhythm. And without Darin's hit recording of Carpenter and his subsequent versions of other songs, Hardin would hardly have had a career.

It would be good to hear from the play something about how Darin came across these songs, and about how these and other covers affected Hardin's immediate prospects. The whole period of his success is skated over; he goes from hopeful to has-been without a beat. Ballantyne blames everything on the drugs, but one would like a few more specifics.

A lot of time is given to Hardins marriage to an actress, Susan, who we are told was perfect. (She inspired The Lady Came from Baltimore, a song Matheson doesnt sing.) Their relationship started out idyllic and ended wretched; after trying her best on many occasions, Susan left him. Again, it was the drugs' fault. It may well be true, but the only insight the play has to give us, delivered as solemnly as though it were a revelation, is the familiar one that heroin destroys trust. The same kind of hollow generalization is applied to the subject of crooked managers: "They don't just want your money, they want your soul." No, they dont; they just want your money.

Royalties alone should have kept Hardin in reasonable comfort, even if he could no longer write or perform, but he died bankrupt. Again, one wants less verbiage and more facts. Meanwhile, Matheson sings 10 Hardin songs, and the wistful innocence of his performances, set against the regret of Ballantynes monologues, establishes the counterpoint that was obviously intended. It exists but doesn't develop. I should add that Ballantyne's own command of the stage stays firm until the end, and that on the occasions when he goes for laughs, he gets them.
