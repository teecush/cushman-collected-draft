We are someway into The Sheep and the Whale before we hear of either a sheep or a whale. The sheep turn out to be sacrificial offerings in a Muslim ceremony. The play is largely about ethnic traditions and ethnic differences. It takes place on a ship that's nominally Russian but is actually controlled by a conglomerate, and whose crew and passengers are from all over. It's roaming around the Straits of Gibraltar, surrounded by separate civilizations. I don't know quite where the whale fits in. He's a blue one, who at some point in the past -- the play is full of flashbacks -- was sighted by a ship's doctor, the most sympathetic and enlightened person on board, who swum out fascinated to get closer to him. Maybe he (the whale, not the doctor) symbolizes the natural world that makes racial conflicts seem not only obscene but absurd.

The Sheep and the Whale is the first play of Moroccan-Canadian writer Ahmed Ghazali; he wrote it in French and it was translated by Bobby Theodore. It arrives in Toronto bearing golden opinions and a prize. It also arrives as a verbose mess, with a cast made up of poses rather than people. It starts with a storm at sea, which as in other plays from The Tempest on down, is confusing but exciting. Once the storm is over, the excitement lets up but the confusion doesn't.

The Russian freighter has hit a smaller boat, killing all but one of its human cargo of Moroccan refugees. Having thus acquired the bodies, the captain is obliged to find a home for them. None of the surrounding countries, however, will take them; a reflection, presumably, of what would have happened had the victims been alive. The big ship has, or had, its own stowaway: a black African whom the doctor befriended and who has now vanished; he's the object of a vicious manhunt by the sailors. There is also a tourist couple on board, the man Moroccan and the woman French. They are in a state of attraction-repulsion about one another and one another's cultures, demonstrated by some rough sounding sex they have just offstage. Once she wanted to learn Arabic, but he was against it. Now she only wants to go home and so, incompatibly, does he. It's thanks to him that we learn about the sheep sacrifice; as a boy he was very squeamish about cutting a ram's throat while facing Mecca. We also get a glimpse of the vessel's owners, represented by an American huckster-tycoon who delivers the company spiel in a cartoon scene that, in writing and performance, takes failed satire to an unnerving level of ham-handedness. All these elements are thrown in together but so inconclusively that when the end comes, it feels like an intermission. Relief, as the house lights come up, dukes it out with incredulity.

Some of the shapelessness can probably be blamed on Soheil Parsa's direction. (The show is a co-production between Cahoots Theatre Projects and Parsa's own Modern Times company with input from the host house, Theatre Passe Muraille.) The actors lurch clumsily across the stage, even in good weather, and they handle the dialogue in much the same way. Faced with lines too formal and too weighed down with ideas to pass as normal speech, they do what actors usually do when denied stylistic guidance: try hard to make everything sound realistic. That means forcing passion onto words that won't take the weight. It also means the most atrocious anthology of forced laughter, both individual and choral, that I have ever heard come off of a stage. (Some of the laughs in the auditorium sounded pretty forced as well.) It may be unfair to blame the performers themselves, since at least some of them are known to be good: Julian Richings, for example, who, as the idealistic doctor with a thing about whales, here puts himself through great gyrating corkscrews in an attempt to convey conviction. Or Soo Garay, whose solution to playing a walking symbol is to never stop shouting. Jim Warren as the skipper survives best, buttressed as he is by a handy ship's telephone and a ripe Russian accent. He proves his ethnicity by asking the French lady down to his cabin to view a rare edition of Anna Karenina. That's one funny line we can be reasonably sure was meant that way.

Blind Submission is confusing in a less grandiose fashion. It's the work of three actor-playwrights who wrote three separate plays, each about a person either suicidal or a rescuer of suicides. These have been sutured together, so we watch them in alternating chunks. Each performer, as well as playing his or her own protagonist, appears in other roles in the other plays, making the whole thing extremely hard to follow. Nor do the plays uncover much new ground: How much more is there to say about the travails of growing up both gay and Jewish? There are sparks of good writing, and the creative three --Dmitry Chepotevsky, Heidi Waters and especially Jenn Griffin -- wear their actors' hats with grace.
