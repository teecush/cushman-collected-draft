Recently, This Hour took on The Hour, and you can still see the results on YouTube. This Hour Has 22 Minutes aired a sketch entitled "Strombeshi," in which an actor impersonating George Stroumboulopoulos gets together with an actor impersonating Jian Ghomeshi for a half-imaginary TV show in which "we talk about ourselves." It's a love-in between two guys congratulating themselves on being what it's probably no longer cool to call cool: on being young and successful and having daily or nightly shows of their own. ("You're great." "No, you're great".) It's pretty funny, especially when faux-Jian congratulates faux-George on the number of bands he wears on his wrist to signify his devotion to various charitable causes, one of them being "rubber depletion due to excessive wristbands." George also spends a lot of time expecting a text-message from Bono, which impresses Jian even when no message arrives.

Do you detect an imbalance here? George is the primary target, maybe because a television host is easier to satirize on television than a radio host. The best they could do with Jian was ask him why his radio arts show Q -- devoted to "Politics, Performance, People" -- wasn't called P. George comes with visuals. "Strombeshi" was set on what looked like George's set, complete with hideous (and, to judge from the way in which the host always seems to be straining forward while sitting on it, hideously uncomfortable) red plastic furniture. Even more fearlessly, it reproduced the tiny tuft of hair that sprouts beneath George's lower lip, whose function seems to be to supply a lonely note of gravitas to balance the boyishness.

There is also the fact that Jian's radio program is harder to make fun of because it's usually good, with an interviewer-host who sounds informed and entertaining; it certainly makes a refreshing change from the traditional CBC arts shows. (This estimate has, of course, nothing to do with the fact that Ghomeshi writes a column in this very section of this very paper.) The Hour with George Stroumboulopoulos is far more problematic, the problems beginning with the fact that this hour goes the full 60, minus commercials. That's a lot to fill every night of the week, especially when you're going up against Jon Stewart and Stephen Colbert and covering some of the same topics. Why, last week, would anyone but the severely cable-deprived want to hear George on President Bush's five-years-on Iraq speech when they could be hearing Jon? (To be fair, Stewart on this subject turned out to be surprisingly uninspired, like he'd done it all already. But it's the principle of the thing.) George's stock-in-trade on these occasions is very much like Jon's; he runs clips and interrupts them with incredulous expressions, visual or vocal. But he doesn't have Jon's delivery or Jon's personality or, from the sound of it, Jon's writers.

But then, is he supposed to be a comedian? Officially maybe not, but if the news is your pasture and irreverence your image, then it's a role you're more or less forced into.

You can't just pass yourself off as the alternative Peter Mansbridge (though that may well be what the Corporation originally had in mind). And if you have a studio audience ....

At the beginning of George's show, he invariably gets the same blast of sycophantic applause as Stewart or Colbert. The difference with George is that after the opening. The volume -- in terms of both

loudness and amount -- steeply declines. On Tuesday night, he interviewed a couple of Canadian residents who also happen to be American Democratic super-delegates. I lost count of the number of times he said he expected them to turn up wearing capes, but it wasn't funny even once. When they did arrive (in suits) they turned out not to have much to say, and he wasn't very successful at drawing them out.

There are some good things about the show. The brief sections, labelled The Set Up or The Bio, which set up the next subject or the next guest, are more helpful than the scrambled eulogies delivered by most talk-show hosts -- though we maybe don't need, at this date, to be informed that Senators Clinton and Obama are running for the Democratic nomination. And, as jumpy and off-putting as he is when talking to camera, George can be a decent interviewer when he meets somebody with whose flow he can go.

Sometimes he goes too easy. Last week he was chatting with Jenny McCarthy, the former Playboy model whose life changed when her son was diagnosed with autism and she herself became an activist for proper treatment. You'd think this would make her impregnably sympathetic; but she came on as if she were plugging her latest movie, and George was content to schmooze right along with her.

Things were much better on Tuesday when he met Chantal Kreviazuk; she's the most sensible and appealing of pop singers with a conscience (she slipped once into talking about her "artistry" when she meant her career, but we can overlook that); George let her make the running, which was the decent and clever thing to do. But he shouldn't have said that after the break, we'd be taking "our nightly nosedive into glitz and glamour." What does he think he's doing the rest of the time?
