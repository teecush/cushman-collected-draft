Obituary of Urjo Kareda.

Urjo Kareda went through three outstanding careers in and around the Toronto theatre.

First he was theatre critic of The Toronto Star, possibly the best Canadian critic there has ever been on a daily paper.

Next he was literary manager at the Stratford Festival, working closely with Robin Phillips, the artistic director, through one of the most fertile and energetic periods in that theatre's history.

Finally, he was an artistic director himself, in charge of the Tarragon Theatre from 1982 until his death early on Boxing Day.

The death was shockingly sudden, even though everybody in the professional worlds he had moved in -- worlds that are close but distinct -- had known of the drastic worsening of his health.

For the last few weeks Urjo's condition (friends, foes and neutrals always referred to him by his first name) had been the principal topic, spoken or unspoken, of every theatrical conversation in Toronto.

Apart from the threatened personal and artistic loss, there was the horrible irony of a man whose mind was his salient quality succumbing to cancer of the brain.

His career as a theatre critic was a classic case of seizing the moment. The years between 1971 and 1975 -- his time of office at the Star -- saw an upsurge in new playwrighting; really the birth of a new Toronto theatre. Kareda was its chronicler and its champion; he was searching, vivacious and enthusiastic and the impact of his reviews helped new plays and new audiences to find one another.

He became identified with the new movement but he ranged far beyond it.

He was an incisive, sometimes savage, analyst of the Jean Gascon regime at the Stratford Festival.

And he was versatile. Passing through Toronto on two occasions in 1974, long before I came to live here, I encountered his reviews of two major American musicals at the Royal Alexandra: A Little Night Music and Gypsy.

The music and lyrics of Stephen Sondheim, the acting and singing of Angela Lansbury, were anatomized with the same seriousness and described with the same excitement that he would have brought to either a new play or, another area of expertise, an old opera. His reviews had rhythm -- a great and rare quality.

His central passion, though, was for words. His move to Stratford -- Canada's theatrical establishment -- was seen by some in Toronto as a desertion but in fact it was logical: he liked working with texts and he got to edit the old as well as commission and encourage the new.

Much of a dramaturge's work is inscrutable from the outside but Phillips testified that, next to himself and the actors, Urjo had the greatest influence on what appeared on the festival's three stages. More new plays were presented than under any previous Stratford regime; none of them have turned out to be classics (for that matter, few of the new plays he acclaimed as a critic have stood up well to revival) but that was to be expected: masterpieces are rare. He did however commission new Canadian translations -- notably John Murrell's translations of Chekhov -- that are still in use today.

In his 20 years at the Tarragon (the roundness of that number is another ironic shock) he brought on such playwrights as Judith Thompson and Jason Sherman, and also gave a Toronto showing to American writers like Wallace Shawn and Tony Kushner.

In my own three-year term of office as a Toronto critic I have detected a weakness at the Tarragon for frail, earnest, over- literary scripts but I have also found that at least one of the most exciting shows of a given year would invariably emanate from one of the theatre's two stages: Sherman's It's All True, in Richard Rose's production; Daniel Brooks' amazing five-actor staging of Goethe's Faust; the very funny two-hander Sibs; Yanna McIntosh's coruscating performance in Skylight.

Innumerable playwrights have paid tribute to the nurturing that they and their scripts received from Kareda.

Less publicized has been the care he took with actors.

Randy Hughson got his first Toronto break in Urjo's production of Thompson's The Crackwalker and received constant encouragement from Kareda thereafter, culminating in the brilliant performance Hughson gave this year in Morris Panych's Earshot -- a show that was itself an all-round Tarragon Theatre triumph.

Kareda maintained a strong presence as a journalist, writing an arts column in Toronto Life and especially fine opera reviews in The Globe and Mail. He was also a valuable broadcaster, especially about music.

Two of the last things he wrote were a celebration of Toronto's actors and an everything-nailed-down rave review of Robert Lepage's Bartok-Schoenberg double bill at Canadian Opera.

Combined with the imminent return to Tarragon of Kristen Thomson's enchanting I Claudia -- a double Dora-winner and one of the theatre's great successes -- they make a fine memorial.
