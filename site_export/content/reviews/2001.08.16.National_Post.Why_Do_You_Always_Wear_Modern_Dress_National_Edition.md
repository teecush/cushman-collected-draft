THE SEAGULL

Avon Theatre, Stratford, Ont.

There are more ways than one to stuff a bird, especially when its The Seagull.

Soulpepper's current full-blooded Uncle Vanya, superb though it is, is not the only valid way of doing Chekhov. There is room for a cool diagrammatic laying-out of the characters and their relationships, or even for a nostalgic trip back to the mournful genteel tradition of which we have all heard but few of us have ever seen.

Certainly, after her fine and unsparing Cherry Orchard of a few seasons ago, one hoped for something definite from Diana Leblanc. But her Stratford production of The Seagull is frustratingly hit- and-miss, unrooted not only in any recognizable style but also in any recognizable time or place.

The set, to begin with, is a definite downer. For the first two scenes, which happen outdoors, Astrid Janson has supplied broad green strips of what looks like canvas, stretched out, one above another.

They are shifted around a bit when we move inside, and room is even made for a couple of doors. These supply a welcome touch of solidity, and may have something to do with the production's second half being a marked improvement on its first. Actors and audience get something to hold on to. We lose, somewhat, the sense that everything is happening in a void -- but only somewhat.

It doesnt even seem to be a purposeful void. The actors are in modern costume, and yet again I find myself fretfully observing that in a still-modern play, realistic as these things go, this is worse than confusing.

The characters keep dropping references we understand but that have nothing to do with the people we are looking at. They retain their Russian names, but who -- in Russia now or at any time in the last 70 years -- would be comparing a current star actress to Eleanora Duse?

Where now, in fact, is this plays whole world of resting or aspiring performers, driven or frustrated writers glumly measuring their achievement against that of Turgenev, of country estates whose visiting owners are at the mercy of their live-in managers who delight in denying them horses for a drive into town?

The structures have gone. The people remain, or their equivalents do, but we really could be trusted to notice that for ourselves. I know the effect is meant to be timelessly suggestive, but all it actually suggests is that the real costumes got lost in transit and that the performers are gallantly making do.

To be fair, a few of those in the smaller roles seem to benefit. Aaron Franks, in spectacles and leather jacket, makes far more than is usual of the financially challenged schoolmaster Medvedenko, who moves in the course of the action from suitless suitor to henpecked husband.

It is he who has the play's famous opening line, Why do you always wear black?, addressed to the contemptuous Masha, whom he loves and has the bad luck to win. Her even more celebrated reply -- "I'm in mourning for my life. Im unhappy" -- is delivered here by Sarah Dodd with fascinating snappishness, as if to say surely any fool could see that.

Thus begins an uncompromising performance, sad and funny, of a woman consumed by her own unrequited love for the young author Konstantin, and drinking to make sure the pain stays in place. (She has a secret passion and doesnt care who knows it.)

Lally Cadeau, too, makes much of Masha's mother, Polina, with her less demonstrative yearnings: She is married to the bullying bailiff but carries a permanent torch for the philandering doctor Dorn.

The richest of all these performances, though, is Brian Bedford's as the rapidly ageing Sorin, the one character in the play who has never been in love, though he has always wanted to be.

Indeed, as he tells us, he has never achieved anything he set his sights on, and he is now dying of it. (This is often held to be true of all Chekhovs characters, but it fits only a couple of them here.)

Bedford, mustering perfect technique in the service of perfect truth, presents an amiable joviality that gets eroded. In the course of two years, he goes from a dapper spectator to the prisoner of a wheelchair. He is also ineffectually, but not unperceptively, kind.

The only actor in the cast to make every line of Tom Stoppards jaunty translation tell, Bedford makes us hang not only on what he himself says, but on what everybody else says about him. We notice, for example, that his relationship with his nephew Konstantin is the only fulfilling one in the play, the only one in which the balance of affection is just about equal.

The play is full of allusions to, and quotations from, Hamlet (though I don't think these are as revelatory as the production, or at least the program note, would like us to believe) and Stoppard, who knows this territory, has added a few more.

He has not included the line about the pangs of despised love, though it's in depicting those pangs that Leblancs production is at its best.

There are numberless moments here of quiet heartbreak as the characters watch the passions of those they adore whirl inexorably away from them. Its when those passions come to the boil that the orchestration falters.

Martha Henry's crop-haired Arkadina seems uncertain just how actressy -- in the derogatory sense of being hollow and effusive -- this successful actress really is. We can see she enjoys playing lady of the manor, especially when she is just about to leave it. Dispensing a solitary rouble to be shared among the servants, she is in her element. (And they, silent roles all, are excellently played.)

We can guess what Konstantin means when he says his mother is good with the sick -- and when she tries to tend to his head wound while unable to stop her resentment of him showing through, at least some of the ambiguities get explored.

But when she abases herself in front of her wayward lover, Trigorin -- a fiendishly layered scene in which she is consciously acting what she genuinely and desperately feels -- she goes either too far or not far enough.

Peter Donaldson's Trigorin, a shambling predator disguised as an easy prey, has a similar problem in a different context.

He conscientiously avoids making an aria out of Trigorins great speech on the frustrations of second-rate authorship, but he loses some of its pain in doing so. He is fine, though, in his moment of sensual surrender to the adoring young Nina.

Konstantin and Nina may be the two hardest juvenile roles ever written, if only because the tragic ending is split between them. One gets the madness, the other the suicide. It would be so much easier to combine the two.

Michelle Giroux pins her Nina firmly on the girls hero-worship of Trigorin, with increasingly disdainful side-glances at a Konstantin whose existence she barely registered in the first place. ("A symbol if ever I saw one" she says --or Stoppard says -- when he presents her with the dead bird.)

This carries her logically through the first three-quarters of the play, but for the last quarter, something more than logic is required. And perhaps greater toughness: We don't quite believe this Nina will survive, and we should. She, unlike Konstantin, is a professional.

Michael Therriault cannot breathe life into the boy's literary theories -- love, on the whole, fares better than art in this production -- but his emotional, if not his creative, vulnerability is believable. Why does he kill himself? Perhaps to stop himself from turning into his uncle.

To round things off: Brian Trees insensitive drama-fancying bailiff is remarkably similar to his insensitive drama-fancying professor in Tempest-Tost. This actor never does anything false, so you cant call him mannered, but I wish he could find a different rhythm.

Rod Beattie as Dorn, the least self-lacerating of Chekhov's doctors, cuts a surprisingly slight and seedy figure. His good opinion of Konstantin's writing ought to count for more than it does here.

And so, unless I'm being impossibly sentimental, should his invocation of what other translators have called the "magic lake." This production could use some magic. It could even use a visible lake.
