Era of intelligent musical gaiety, gone but not forgotten

Spectator

Era of intelligent musical gaiety, gone but not forgotten

Saturday, February 8, 1997

LAST November I was in New York preparing a radio special for the BBC. (I felt terribly cosmopolitan.) The subject was the Cabaret Convention, an annual week-long songfest in which singers show off their wares while celebrating the American theatre and supper-club repertoire. One of the highlights was a concert devoted to the lyrics of E. Y. (generally known as Yip) Harburg, 1996 being the 100th anniversary of his birth. Among those billed to appear was the composer Burton Lane, Yip's collaborator on Finian's Rainbow and other pleasing, if less famous, things. Lane was a good pianist and a fair singer, and we expected to hear him give out with Old Devil Moon or How Are Things in Glocca Morra ?

In the event, Lane, who was then 85, didn't appear, at least not on stage. He was noticeable in the auditorium, looking frail though not alarmingly so, and smiling: The evening had after all been full of his tunes and they had stood up beautifully. Then, on January 5, came the news of his death which, coming just after the Harburg centenary, seemed to set the seal on an era.

As a team, Lane and Harburg never had the public visibility or the durability of Rodgers and Hammerstein or Lerner and Loewe. They could both be difficult men, and after Finian in 1947 they collaborated infrequently. But they were a good match. Each exemplified to a high degree the Broadway tradition that crystallized in the 1930s: intelligent gaiety against the odds. They wrote the kind of love songs -- flippant or heartfelt or both at once -- that sounded good on stage or screen, as vehicles for solo singers or swing bands. Harburg, who wrote Brother Can You Spare a Dime and the songs for The Wizard of Oz ,was the master equally of fantasy and of social comment; indeed, he regarded the first as a vehicle for the second. (A fervent Roosevelt supporter, he always maintained that If I Only Had a Brain and its subsequent stanzas -- "if I only had a heart . . . if I only had the noive" -- were a coded comment on the Depression and the New Deal. Eventually, and traumatically, he was blacklisted.)

He was also the grand master of lyrical wordplay; it suited his taste for whimsical satire and could also be a delightful end in itself. Harburg could call a song I Like the Likes of You or reel out such a string of compliments as "you are so lovable, so livable, your beauty is just unforgivable."

Once Harburg had bestowed Over the Rainbow on Judy Garland, therainbow -- an image he had previously used as automatically as any other lyricist might -- became his conscious symbol for hope and romance. Hence the governing motif of Finian. The score contains an admonitory song called Look to the Rainbow ; and 10 years ago, when I wrote and directed a Harburg anthology show in London, that seemed the perfect title-number. It's a sweet tune -- part optimistic, part homesick -- with an intentionally Irish lilt; speeded up it goes very naturally into a jig. All the characters in Finian are Irish wanderers, Southern sharecroppers or rednecks; to accommodate these specialized parameters Lane wrote swinging folk or gospel tunes as well as a couple of classic ballads. He set Harburg's virtuoso conceits ("when I can't fondle the hand that I'm fond of, I fondle the hand at hand") to an intoxicating waltz.

If Burton Lane was the last of the thirties songwriters it's partly because he began so early. When his first song was published, in 1927, he was 15. He was 18 when his first songs were interpolated on Broadway; 21 when he broke into the movies with Everything I Have Is Yours . In Hollywood he wrote with Frank (Guys and Dolls ) Loesser who, in those days was strictly a words man and just about the funniest, even when he wasn't writing outright comedy songs. The Loesser-Lane songs have a special kind of unbuttoned joyousness; I especially cherish How'dja Like to Love Me , a guarded preview of domestic bliss ("would you rise in wrath if I beat you to the bath each day?") that they wrote for Bob Hope and Martha Raye. Even greater fun musically was How About You (as in "I like New York in June, how about you?") on which Lane's partner was the fairly obscure Ralph Freed. The bubbling profusion of notes in the middle, when the lyric goes "I like potato chips, moonlight and motor trips," is the kind of logical surprise that makes a song's fortune.

In the 1950s, Lane teamed with Alan Jay Lerner, Harburg's heir as commentator (liberal rather than radical), compulsive word-juggler (he never got this bit quite right) and doctrinaire romantic (what else do you call a man who married eight times?). Together they wrote Royal Wedding ,a deeply unmemorable movie that nevertheless contains one song, Too Late Now ,that is as sublime as a pop ballad gets. In 1965 they reunited for the stage show On a Clear Day You Can See Forever :a consistently elegant work. The title song was an instant classic. I never liked it; but musicians kept telling me how great it was and finally, hearing Lane's tune away from Lerner's mystically preachy lyric, I heard what they meant. The melody soars on one continuous sweeping arc.

Lane's last Broadway musical, again with Lerner, was in 1979: Carmelina ,which flopped but contained some lovely songs. He wasn't finished even then. A few years ago there was a complete recording of Strike Up the Band ,a 1927 musical by George and Ira Gershwin. As is usually the case, the original materials were widely scattered. One song, Meadow Serenade ,had a full lyric by Ira but of George's music only the chorus survived; Lane supplied a new setting for the verse, and it's as beautiful as anything in the original score.

In 1990, Michael Feinstein released a recording of Lane tunes with the composer at the piano. One song, I Can Hardly Wait ,had been written only that year and it, too, is a beauty; though its lyricists, the compulsive Oscar-winners Alan and Marilyn Begman, clearly belong to a later and more solemn era than Lane's own.

Of course, eras don't so much end as merge and overlap. The work of Lane and Harburg is still abundantly alive, and the book is hardly closed on the generation that preceded them. I see that we have just lost Gerald Marks, who wrote the music for All of Me ,and before that Irving Caesar who, as is the way of songwriters named Irving, had lived to be 101. His last decades were comparatively unproductive, but in his youth he wrote the lyrics for Swanee , Tea for Two and Crazy Rhythm : none of them deathless verse but all apparently indestructible. Caesar and Marks collaborated on Is It True What They Say About Dixie? a question that has always seemed to me to beg for a disrespectful answer. Yip Harburg, preferably with a Burton Lane accompaniment, would have been the perfect man to give it.
