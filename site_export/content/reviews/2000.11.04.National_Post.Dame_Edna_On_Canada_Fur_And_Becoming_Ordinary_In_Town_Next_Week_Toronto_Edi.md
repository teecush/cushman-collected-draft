I was privileged to obtain an audience with Dame Edna Everage on the day of her memorable meeting with the Toronto media at the Royal York Hotel. After the press conference, she received me in the hotel's Royal Suite; our meeting was suitably blessed by the presence of Dame Edna's good friend Queen Elizabeth II, in the form of a large framed photograph, obviously taken some years ago.

Dame Edna answered my questions with the frank fearlessness and the superb linguistic aplomb for which she is famous. Our in-person colloquium was supplemented a few days later by a telephone interview "to fill in the lacunae," as she put it. At this point Dame Edna was in Minneapolis, Minnesota, where she had gone to fine- tune the show -- Dame Edna, the Royal Tour -- which she brings to the Pantages Theatre this Tuesday. It was mid-morning and she was (she said) still clad in her peignoir. What follows is an edited transcript of our two conversations.

How does it feel to be returning to the colonies?

Well, I happen to be in many ways very innovative, very radical and in other ways very conservative, and I loved the old Commonwealth. When we were at school in faraway Mooney Ponds, Melbourne, we learned about Canada. I could actually draw the lakes. Like a little bunch of oak-leaves they were, sprouting. I've had a lot of invitations to come to Canada, and I've won an award in Banff -- lovely name, isn't it, Banff? Sounds like a brand of exclusive toilet paper -- and when I was young, my favourite show that I was taken to see was Rose Marie, and of course there were Mounties in that and I always had a picture of Canada infested with Mounties.

Did you feel that you were destined to be a performer?

Well, I did. In school I was the cynosure of all eyes. And of course my autograph book was full of things like "You Have That Spark, Magnetic Mark."

They called you "Mark?"

No they didn't. It was a figure of speech, Mr. Cushman. But people are drawn to me. You must admit, hardened old journo that you are, that was a good turnout this morning, and they listened, and they were, generally speaking, not impertinent. To return -- after a very long parenthetical route -- to your question, I didn't set out to be an actress. I never ever dreamt that one day I'd become a friend of Ingmar Bergman who is at this moment planning a film comeback -- Edna Gabler is the name of this picture -- and I think as Scandinavian shows go it will certainly have the edge on Mamma Mia.

Would it be fair to say you had a suburban upbringing?

My father sold vacuum cleaners from door to door, and he spent long periods in the country. It's not easy selling vacuum cleaners to people who don't have carpets. He'd save up and occasionally he'd get my mother a new attachment, a new optional extra. I'll never forget when he came home one night with his very first crevice nozzle. And my mother cherished that crevice nozzle.

I do feel that the moral values of Melbourne suburban life are very good guide-lines for the world. I think the next stage for me is to simplify, to go down, to say "I'm casting aside all of this and becoming an ordinary person again." I'm thinking of renouncing my damehood and just being Mrs. Everage. I rather miss that status. It would be courageous of me, in fact, to give all my lovely dresses to charity and wear just the ordinary, rather dreary clothes that you can see in Toronto any day of the week.

But obviously you didn't always feel this?

No, I didn't. I was very flattered when the Queen bestowed my damehood upon me for my services to culture. A lot of people, as you know -- well, you probably don't -- but the fact is that when you're in a position like mine there are a lot of envious people, and a lot of people say it's a spurious title. They claim I've bestowed it upon myself. I stay at Buckingham Palace when I'm in London. I have my own key. And I even have my own shelf in the refrigerator.

And of course the Queen and I meet. Now, it would be to betray a confidence if I told you what we discussed, but she's always sought my advice and then ignored it. So I take no responsibility for what that woman wears. Though I've helped certain American women. I persuaded Hillary to stop wearing those rather horrible little Alice bands. I nipped that in the bud. Tipper is a bit of a different story. She's really rather willful and femininity is not her specialty, I'm afraid.

How did the Queen convey the news of your damehood to you?

Well, she didn't say "I hereby confirm that you have a royal title." She just accepted it; she's a little bit in awe of me, frankly. I'm not in awe of anybody. I used to be a big fan of little Margaret Trudeau when she was quite an independent kind of a woman - - do you remember those days? And of course there's Margaret Atwood, whose books I adore. Danielle Steel and Margaret Atwood are my favourite writers. I don't think they've been together in the same sentence for a very long time, if ever.

I know how people receive a knighthood but what is the equivalent ceremony for a dame?

This is a little bit of an obsession of yours, isn't it, Mr. Cushman? There's an unpleasant probing tone in your voice. Well, you go along to Government House in Australia and they give you some little dry sandwiches and a little drink, the Governor shakes you by the hand and presents you with a velvet box and in there is a lovely ribbon with something you can hang round your neck. But if I returned my own damehood I'd do it very tastefully and it wouldn't spoil my friendship with royalty at all, I don't think. Because I think, between you and me, the Queen would like to be Mrs. Windsor. She'd give anything for anonymity. I haven't positively decided to return my damehood . But it's part of my becoming more accessible, becoming more ordinary. I've enjoyed my fame but now I think obscurity is beckoning me.

You could become famous for obscurity.

I could. I could be the most highly profiled obscure person you know.

There must be other dames, though, of whom you approve?

Well, little Dame Kiri Te Kanawa is a friend of mine. She's a little New Zealand singer. And Dame Judi Dench is a friend but I never thought her damehood suited her. She's very fond of hopping into royal garments, and she played Queen Elizabeth in that thing about the Globe, but she didn't convince me, and many people, including the critics, said I should have had that part.

Do you feel you have been typecast?

I'm myself in the theatre and I will be in Toronto, but I want people to know that I'm an actress. I'd love to join the company in Stratford, Ontario. I wouldn't mind playing small leads; but walk- ons, no, not at my time of life.

You got a Tony on Broadway?

A special Tony. There was no category I could fit into; I am uncategorizable. And I got the Drama Desk Award, the Outer Critics Award. I collected a shelf-full of hideous sculptures.

Do you feel prepared for the Canadian climate?

I've got sone humanely culled Australian animals who've donated their pelts to keep me warm.

Your manager, Barry Humphries, when you discovered him, he was just a struggling actor...

Struggling is the word. He was playing the part of a housewife -- he had to -- and I gave him some tips. And in the end the producer insisted I play the part. Well, can you imagine what a blow to his pride that was? It was a long time ago but the scars are still there.

How often do you go back to Australia?

Well, every year I revisit my roots. Fundamentally I'm Australian, but then I belong to the world.
