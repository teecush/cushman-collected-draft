Craig Ewert is, or was, a professor of computer sciences in Chicago who took early retirement and, to get out of Bushs America" moved to Harrogate, a spa town in the north of England.

There he was stricken with ALS, or Lou Gehrigs disease. Facing a life of deteriorating paralysis -- in his terms, with no life at all- -he decided to kill himself, with as little pain and as much dignity as possible. He went to Switzerland, one of two countries in the world (plus the state of Oregon) that permits medically-assisted suicide and the only that allows it for non-citizens.

He met in Zurich with Ludwig Minelli, popularly known as "Doctor Death but actually a human-rights lawyer, who runs Dignitas, an organization devoted to providing precisely this service.

The documentary The Suicide Tourist (CTV, tomorrow) tells the story of Ewert and his extremely supportive wife Mary.

It also follows, in counterpoint, the story of another pair of Dignitas clients: George Coumbias, a Greek-born immigrant to Vancouver who rose from janitor to president of L'Oreal in Japan. Now in his seventies, he has suffered four serious heart attacks and expects a fifth: I can't play tennis or golf, I cant have sex. What are they going to tell me next, no more good food and wine?"

He has serious fears: "Im a very private person; I want to be able to feed and clean myself. So far, George's story and Craig's parallel one another. But there is a difference. Georges wife Betty (theyve been inseparable since 1958") is in perfect health but doesn't want to survive him. They have an assisted-suicide pact.

Craig's case seems -- to me, anyway -- perfectly cut-and-dried; as he says, confronting his choices, "Ive got death, or Ive got suffering and death, and he's perfectly entitled to choose the former.

George's, or rather Bettys, is murkier, and its understandable that the doctor to whom Minelli refers both couples should have no hesitation in writing a prescription for Ewert but balks at giving one to Mr. and Mrs. Coumbias.

Simply, it goes against his training. But the question remains: Doesn't a sane woman have a right to end a life that she believes she will find insupportable? And who's to say that, when her husband dies, she wont kill herself anyway?

John Zaritskys film won the audience vote as best Canadian picture at this year's Hot Docs International Documentary Festival. It isn't a debate. Dissenting voices are only heard through the comments of their opponents; Craig Ewert trenchantly observes that without modern technology he would be dead anyway, and the Christians who complain about doctors who practise euthanasia playing God" ignore the fact that doctors play God all the time by preserving lives that in earlier ages would have been lost. "My ventilator is playing God, he says. (You could of course extend this argument to every medical advance since Hippocrates, but I dont think that invalidates it.)

These touches apart, the film is an elegy and -- at two hours -- an unduly prolonged one; a judgement that may sound heartless but is in the circumstances appropriate. Ewert, whose gets the bulk of the screen and voice-over time, is an intelligent and sympathetic man, but in the end he has only one thing to say: that his life has become intolerable and, having weighed the options, he has the right to end it. I wouldnt give him an argument but, in the interests of variety if not of balance, I would have liked to hear someone else give him one.

Minelli makes the surprising but rather persuasive point that Dignitas can actually prevent suicides; by offering desperate people a civilized option, it can prevent them from rushing into things and give them the opportunity to change their minds.

Still, if there's one really disturbing thing in the film, it's Minellis admission that, to maintain objectivity," he never actually attends a death: "I do not mix the potion.

We, however, do attend Craig Ewerts death. We hear the designated attendant hand him the draught and say, Mr. Ewert, if you drink this, you're going to die," and we witness him swallowing it and asking for apple juice to drown the taste.

It's solemn enough, and revealing enough, for me to swallow my own customary doubts about the effect of film cameras on supposedly spontaneous moments, and the thought-processes of the people who allow or even welcome them.

Theres a momentary cheat when were treated to a montage of what are presumed to be the dying man's last memories; it isn't exactly sentimental, but it breaks the code of realism that otherwise justifies the film. But it regains its dignity when Mary Ewert holds her late husbands hand, then gets up, takes off her glasses off and kisses him. She doesnt cry, or not on camera. And you think, "yes, it would have been worse if she'd had to watch him die painfully." And we all die.
