For the second time in a row, the main stage at Theatre Passe Muraille is given over to a show that doesn't deserve to be there. The two cases aren't identical. Last month's Last Days of Graceland placed an implausible story in an impossible locale. The credibility of People Power, the new occupant, cannot be challenged either on the grounds of narrative or of setting. This is a history play, set in the Philippines during the Marcos years. It covers the 14-year period between the assassination of opposition senator Ninoy Aquino, and the flight, by popular demand and with American assistance, of the president with his family, and his replacement by the murdered man's widow, Cory. That's a lot to take on, more than this production has the resources for or, as far as I can determine, the ability to bring off.

Last Days of Graceland was scarred by bad acting. So is People Power, more consistently -- omnipresently, in fact -- but also more forgivably. For one thing, the players, members of a Filipino-Canadian troupe called Carlos Bulosan Theatre, are dealing with a subject close to their own hearts; a lot of emotion bounces off the stage and a certain amount of sympathy ripples back. For another, the actors are hobbled by their script, though since it's literally their script -- they wrote it themselves -- this really is a matter of withholding blame with one hand while bestowing it with the other. Which hand is which is hard to decide; maybe the woodenness of the acting is due to the banality of the script or maybe it's the other way around. Either way, it rarely sounds like real people talking. Best just to list the five creators (Leon Aureus, Rose Cortez, Nicco Garcia, Christine Mangosing, Nadine Villasin) and their director-dramaturge (Nina Lee Aquino, presumably no relation and certainly none that the program mentions) and move on.

People Power is a tribute to a popular movement that resulted in a non-violent revolution. But it portrays it in a sentimental-grotesque fashion that's not so much simplified as simplistic. The Marcoses themselves make token appearances, but they aren't even cartoons, just cardboard cut-outs. Also, though the show makes a point of impugning their attempts at populist ingratiation-- trying to win over the populace with fake smiles and sanctimonious slogans -- it uses exactly the same tactics itself. I've rarely seen so many excruciating excursions into the audience.

The majority of the characters are representative fictions. The constant doubling and the low-definition performances make the permutations difficult to follow, but mostly they seem to divide into heroic rebels, government stooges and in-betweens. There are a couple of society ladies who sip expensive drinks, complain about the help and share enthusiastically in the first lady's celebrated footwear fetish. One of them is a newspaper editor's daughter who's severely critical of her photographer-husband's penchant for taking pictures that might be embarrassing to the regime. Later she changes sides, though whether that's due to an access of conscience or to a sense of where her bread might henceforth be buttered is left unclear.

One corner of the stage houses recurrent scenes in a brothel where GIs stationed in Manila humiliate the girls, who alternately dream of revenge and escape. ("Yankees go home -- and take me with you;" it's the best line of the evening, though reminiscent of Miss Saigon without the songs, just as much of the rest suggests Evita.)

On an upper level, and in a scene that might take some kind of prize for confused staging, Filipino emigres in a far country that might be California are intimidated by agents of the CIA. We meet a prosperous family -- self-made father, self-righteous mother, one idealistic daughter and one complacent one -- apparently designed to symbolize the entire conflict. "What tortured people?" asks mama, newly informed of human-rights abuses and gazing around the stage as if she expected them to be visible from her dining table. "I don't see them." It's an all too typical moment. Intentionally or not, the show caricatures the people it disapproves of and sentimentalizes those it likes. Its feelings may be sincere, but its methods are second-hand.

Camellia Koo's set, well-furnished with oil-drums and festooned with stickers marked either "Marcos" or "Aquino," gives us the trappings of conflict, but if the people don't carry conviction either as symbols or as individuals, we've no reason to care.

And there's an additional handicap, one that's unusual in political drama: The story has a happy ending. What the play wants to happen, happened. Generally, shows designed to involve us in struggle leave us with the feeling that the struggle is still going on and that we'd all better sign up for it. I'm usually a sucker in the theatre for Popular Front rhetoric, but this time my blood refused to race. It just didn't feel the need. I have to report that there was a standing ovation on the opening night, but I suspect that it was partisan. It was cheering the Cause, not the effects.
