THEATRE REVIEW

PARTICULARLY IN THE HEARTLAND

Enwave Theatre

- - -

The TEAM (acronym for Theatre of the Emerging American Moment) begin their contribution to WorldStage by hanging around the stage in casual, mainly rural gear giving out with off-key renditions of The Star Spangled Banner, Take Me Out to the Ball Game, American Pie and other anthems. They are indeed from the U.S., though they have done most of the work on this show in the U.K. They then invite us, as a reciprocal gesture, to sing O Canada. On the first night we sang, on the whole, better than they did.

It seemed, for the longest time, as if we might also be able to act better than they do. This proves in the end not to be the case; there's talent on that stage. But we have to travel an obscure and whimsical road to find this out. The particular stretch of heartland depicted in Particularly in the Heartland is Kansas, known to all of us as a region of strange and terrible storms. The latest twister, or whatever it is, has left the three Springer children -- Sarah, Todd and Anna -- alone in their homestead, their parents having vanished on a trip to Wal-Mart. They receive three visitors who become part of the household. One is a heavily pregnant young woman named Tracy Jo who, at her first appearance, turns to the audience and confides, "I'm an alien-- ssh." The second is an older woman called Dorothy who has reversed the trajectory usually associated with her name by being blown to Kansas from Oz, or at least from New York. (She seems to have been in a plane crash.) The third is the ghost of Bobby Kennedy.

Some kind of allegory is obviously intended. There has, perhaps, been a nuclear devastation that has cut these characters off from the outside world. The values of Middle America are being tested. The set-up, though, is confusing, as well as being almost interminable. It involves a lot of not especially expert whirling about; the company boast of their "exuberant physicality," but the results suggest that we have been taking the physical skills of some of our own groups entirely too much for granted. Kristin Sieh, playing Sarah, does get to do some fairly spectacular skipping.

It isn't clear what the intruders are meant to add. Tracy Jo, the earliest arrival, does get to tell Dorothy, "I was poor, tired and huddled once too -- but I was here first, just remember that," which is sort of neat, but what is gained by making her an alien? Is Bobby meant to represent the lost hopes of liberalism? He doesn't make much of a case, though when RFK says of JFK, regarding the Cuban missile crisis, that he believed in "America's right to moral leadership of the planet," and hence resisted a pre-emptive strike, something stirs.

Something stirs, too -- indeed it's a turning-point -- when one of the young Springers says, "I'm a Christian," and Dorothy, a sophisticate from the financial sector, murmurs, "That's awesome." Strict belief has always been a fertile ground for both drama and comedy, and it proves so here, with Todd placing his hands over his ears when he hears swearing and Sarah protesting at the use of the word "evolution." But it's hard to see why a family of evangelical Christians should be standing in for the whole of Kansas or indeed Kansas for the whole of red-state America. These characters aren't being mocked, but they aren't being much tested either. The show includes a question-and-answer session, where on the first night someone -- not a plant, I'm sure -- asked the Springer kids what they thought happened to their parents. They replied that they must have been taken up to heaven in what they call the Rapture leaving the children to carry on living on Earth. They admitted to being puzzled as to why they themselves should have been thought unworthy of apotheosis, but put it down to childish misdemeanours. They referred us to a series called Left Behind, depicting children left in just such a situation. Anyway, I was glad that question got asked. Without it, we would have been hopelessly lost.

The sequence also showed the actors' capacity to stay completely in character while improvising. Libby King and Frank Boyd touchingly complete the sibling trio, Lucy Kendrick Smith's more sympathetic moments as Dorothy are very good, and though I don't remember the original well enough to be sure, I suspect that Jake Margolin may well be doing a first-rate impersonation of Bobby; he's authoritative, anyway. In the show's final sequence, Bobby's ghost gets to play Marley's ghost in a group reading of A Christmas Carol, or parts of it, whose mixture of admonition and benevolence seems to be the show's own message to us. It's an impressive end, and the people up there seem genuinely nice.
