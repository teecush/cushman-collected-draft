LARRY'S PARTY

Bluma Appel Theatre

---

Carol Shields' novel Larry's Party is a cool, inward account of the first 50 years in the life of a man. Larry Weller, born and raised in Winnipeg, accumulates two marriages, two divorces, one son and two careers.

Having taken a course in floral design, he rises to become manager of a flower-store. But on his first honeymoon he visits the Hampton Court maze in England, and there, "getting lost and then found," he stumbles into his vocation. He builds a labyrinth in his own backyard, exasperating his wife and precipitating their breakup.

He becomes a professional, and very successful, maze-maker; he moves to Chicago and meets his second wife, an academic in women's studies who specializes in the lives of female saints. When this union, too, exhausts itself, he sets up home and shop in Toronto. Finding his two exes in town simultaneously, he hosts a party for them, his current girlfriend and a small assortment of relatives and clients. And here his life, which seemed to have bottomed out, takes another turn.

Crisply and enchantingly written, the book is a minefield for dramatic adaptation. It has a story but no plot. It has a hero who, though no pushover, is essentially someone to whom things happen; even his floral education is the result of accident and maternal intervention.

The book, narrated in the third person, tells us much about his thoughts and feelings, but always in a guarded way, as if there were some final court of appeal yet to be heard from. It has little dialogue, except towards the end, and what it has reads more like a precis of conversation than like the thing itself; this is allowable in a novel but not in the theatre, where language has to stand up and be counted.

So if you are going to stage Larry's Party, then a musical seems a reasonable way to go, if only because of music's ability to speed things up. Here, though, it does not work.

The party that ends the novel is now used, not too surprisingly, as a framing device. Apart from that, everything is very literal. Richard Ouzounian, who wrote the musical's book and lyrics, simply squeezes in as much of the story as he can, in chronological order, where the original keeps pausing and recapping.

The novel, in fact, is something of a labyrinth, and that's rather satisfying. It also, I suppose, tells us that life is a maze; the novel dispenses this hardly revelatory information with a certain teasing grace, but the musical seems deadly serious about it.

Larry himself is somebody who wants to learn -- who does learn, about himself and about the limits and the potential of his own mind and body. What songs can do is make us free of his thoughts and feelings; and that's what these songs attempt, engagingly at first.

But the further inside Larry's head we go, the more we find it stuffed with the remnants of other musicals. Marek Norman's music is a continuous high-pitched drone (the orchestrations seem to consist entirely of plucked strings and plunked keyboards), and the lyrics buckle under pressure. Larry sings generic arias about being in love and being confused; the women in his life fare even worse. His first wife has a self-realization number that is one cliche after another; his Toronto lady follows it with something similar about being lonely yet defiant.

When Larry goes into a three-week coma, his son hastens to his bedside to unleash another power ballad; in the book he simply reads The Toronto Star (obituaries excepted) out loud, because he can't think of anything to say, and that might have made a more affecting number. All the strains and ambiguities in their relationship have been edited out in favour of bland affirmation; indeed, much of the material's quirkiness, starting with its very first episode, has been toned down. And when we get to the ending (not too satisfactory in the novel) we simply feel that, of three possible women, Larry has wound up with the most boring.

There are some brighter things in the score: a jolly song about botulism, for example, occasioned by the story of Larry's mother having once unintentionally poisoned her own mother-in-law, though staging the number in the presence of the guilt-stricken woman makes everybody seem remarkably tactless.

One of Larry's rich clients is a country and western singer, which occasions not the expected musical parody but a love song that gets touchingly reprised later on. Wit, though, is generally lacking, both in the writing and in Robin Phillips' direction and design. This is about the most lugubrious-looking musical I have ever seen. Black space surrounds a tall, transparent revolving structure whose maze-like qualities are rather quickly exhausted; a gift of a scenic metaphor seems to have gone begging. The arms of Larry's party guests stretch out from within it, and that is striking; things are less happy when the effect is reversed and the ensemble stands outside the action, doing aerobics while the principals emote. They also lie down, holding shrubs, to signify Larry's first, home-grown, maze.

No actor could do more for Larry than Brent Carver. He isn't obvious casting for Everyman -- he is too extraordinary an actor to seem an ordinary man -- but he contributes an undimmed spiritual generosity, a sense of easy tolerance, and of course a ringing voice. His face seems somehow rounder than usual, and his shoulders subtly and progressively hunch as he gets older. (The production is not otherwise much concerned with denoting changes in period or locale.)

Michelle Fisk adds some welcome astringency as his sister; Julain Molnar (academic wife) and Barbara Barsky (last chance) are also helpful. So, as Larry's father, is Gary Krawford, whom I coincidentally first saw, many years ago in New York, playing the lead in Stephen Sondheim's Company, a show to which this one bears some structural and spiritual similarities. If ever an actor could bring that musical's recalcitrant hero to life, it would be Brent Carver. Do it, someone.
