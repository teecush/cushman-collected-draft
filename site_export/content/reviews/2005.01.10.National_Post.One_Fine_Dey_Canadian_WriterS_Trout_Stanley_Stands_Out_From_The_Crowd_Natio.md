Trout Stanley runs until Feb. 8 (416-504-9971), Little Dragon until Jan. 30 (416-504-7529) and Bea's Niece until Feb. 6 (416-531-1827).

I was gearing up, through most of a week loaded with unsatisfactory Toronto premieres, to denounce the whole idea of encouraging new Canadian plays as being cruel to audiences. Then at the last moment came Claudia Deys Trout Stanley at the Factory Theatre to blow my ill-humour to smithereens.

Dey, whose language has always been striking and whose dramaturgy has sharpened with every play shes written, here delivers a masterwork. It depicts an outlandish world and makes us believe in it. In remote B.C., somewhere between Grizzly Alley and Misery Junction (names that are apparently as authentic as they're evocative), live Grace and Sugar Ducharme, 30-year-old twin sisters whose parents, referred to as the holy mother and father, died 10 years ago, bequeathing them the town garbage dump.

Grace -- a glamorous figure in thigh-high boots, sky-high hair and provocatively zippered front -- now runs the business single- handed; Sugar has worn her mother's dirty-pink tracksuit every day since becoming orphaned and in all that time has never left the house. She manufactures what she calls "tragic figurines" for which, she says, there is quite a market. Factor in that the twins were meant to be triplets (they call their stillborn sister Duckling) and that the day on which we meet them is their birthday, on which terrible things usually happen.

Sugar is about to end her life of quiet desperation with a rope but is foiled by a male intruder who, despite appearances -- he wears a stolen cop suit -- is probably not the suspected killer of a local stripper-cum-Scrabble-champion. He, too, has lost his parents, but then they treated him appallingly. Among other things they named him Trout Stanley. (Asked about this, he says simply, its a fish name.) Having rescued Sugar, he woos her in one of the funniest and most ecstatic love scenes in recent, or any, memory.

Grace returns bearing a gun, and a three-way battle ensues, in the course of which it becomes clear that Grace, who may not be as liberated as she looks, has engineered Sugars reclusiveness from motives both selfish and concerned. The play turns out to be about love and release. The details may sound Gothic, but there is not a hint of camp. This is simply a play about people who live at extremes, of climate and feeling. The narrative gets a little muddy toward the end, but for the most part it unfolds logically, confidently and surprisingly.

It is also joyously written. Tense, witty verbal duels, in which repetition is a signpost to the characters' desperation and the author's control, erupt into soaring prose arias. The play pays homage to the fierceness that lies around us without ever becoming folksy. It even gets away with fiddle music.

Eda Holmess production is all but faultless, with a great ear for phrasing and eye for placing; Kelly Wolfs set and costumes are delightful and Andrea Lundy's northern-lights lighting is glorious. Melody A. Johnson sometimes has you hunting for her words, especially when they come at the ends of sentences, but she makes even this seem right for Sugar, who in her hands is a perfect compound of woe and wonder. It's hard, too, to imagine any improvement on Michelle Girouxs Grace, who sounds as good as she looks, or on Gord Rands Trout, a shy guy who has made himself bold.

One thing that bodes well for Trout's and Sugar's future together: They both dislike martial arts, finding them "too sudden." This would make them an unsympathetic audience for Keira Loughrans Little Dragon (Passe Muraille), whose Chinese-Canadian heroine, a first-year student at Queens, decides rather late in the play that she is the illegitimate daughter of Bruce Lee. Even before this, she has begun to find her identity through kung-fu and its analogues, all of which are helpfully differentiated.

In Marion de Vries' production, every scene is staged as a martial-arts contest; the fights are directed by Richard Lee, who might well pass for the son of Bruce and in fact gets to play him, or his apparition. The choreography ingeniously underlines the action -- we appreciate just who is kicking who's ass, psychologically speaking -- but over a whole evening it gets to feel mannered. Anyway, the plays first half is a lively though unfocused romp through campus life with race-and-gender undertones; when the main issue arrives, it seems contrived. Loughran is a promising writer and she remains an interesting actress. Strangely awkward at portraying her protagonists conversational life, she excels at bringing her thoughts to the surface. Michelle Polak gives fierce support as a jealous-Jewish-lesbian co-student.

At the Tarragon, third stop on our theatre's Axis of Good Intentions, an evening yet more diffuse: Bea's Niece whose title, apart from being a false rhyme and a false pun, is a false pointer to what David Gows play might be about. Anne (Maria Ricossa), a novelist on a psychiatric ward, does indeed have a wise and wacky Auntie Bea (Patricia Hamilton, minus the proper zaniness) prone to offering worldly advice, e.g., "Scotch, a gun and some opium are the three things you need most to be a lady." Or maybe other-worldly, since she may not really be there; she also doubles, or the actress does, as a female rabbi. But she doesnt seem any more central than does Anne's husband (John Bourgeois), who certainly is dead, or her harried, pregnant shrink (Fiona Highet, floundering), whose fractious relationship with her patient may really be the play's spine. But the characters and their concerns, plus references to the kabbalah, are just dotted about the dialogue as the equivalent literary and religious symbols are dotted about the stage. The play never gets a grip, and so neither does the audience.

Richard Roses production seems little concerned with orchestrating text or performances, but on April Viczkos essence- of-clinical set it does look good, with an especially dashing trompe l'oeil effect, which, coming right at the start, proves the best thing in the evening.
