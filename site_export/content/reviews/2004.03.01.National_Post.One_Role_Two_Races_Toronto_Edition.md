THE SYRINGA TREE

Bluma Appel Theatre, Toronto

I have now sat through The Syringa Tree three times in six months, and the fact that I have been able to do so without going mad argues a solid strength in both the play and its subject. The piece, a quasi-autobiographical monologue about growing up white in apartheid South Africa, was brought to last year's World Stage by its author, Pamela Gien, who gave it a fine, physically liquid performance marred by an oppressive winsomeness.

The protagonist-storyteller is, at the start of the action she recounts, six years old, and the actress-author, though she had no trouble impersonating some 30 other characters en route, kept her in a state of pucker-voiced innocence practically through adolescence.

Now at CanStage the role is being alternated by two Canadian actresses, Yanna McIntosh and Caroline Cave, between whose performances I find it almost impossible to choose. One at a time or in conjunction, they blow their authors performance out of the water while lifting her text up to the sky.

What neither of them can do is disguise the plays lack of focus. It's never too clear whose story it's meant to be. The narrator is Elizabeth Grace, whose father, a doctor, is a self-described "Jewish atheist" and whose mother is a cheerfully lapsed Catholic. The couple are exemplary white liberals, doing their very best for the surrounding community and especially for their own black servants. Chief among these is the Xhosa nanny, Salumina, who dotes on her Miss Lizzie and is doted on in return. The first significant thing that happens is Saluminas giving birth to a "pickaninnie" (thats the word they all use) of her own, a girl named Moliseng. Lizzie bonds with her as well; probably her first real inkling of the system she lives under comes when she instructed that the presence in the house of Moliseng, who lacks the proper papers, has to be kept secret. Her first real fright comes when the child is seen by their neighbour, an Afrikaans minister.

Two traumatic sequences of events mark Lizzie's growing-up. One is the temporary loss of Moliseng in the chaotic local hospital system; the story of Mrs Grace driving to the rescue, car windows tightly closed, is the tensest sequence of the evening. The other is a bloody attack on the farm of Lizzie's grandparents, carried out by insurgents from Rhodesia whom a white policeman describes, with improbable restraint, as freedom fighters; this, you may feel, would hardly have been the time or the place for political correctness, but it is a forcible reminder that the play comes to us from the United States, where the author now lives and where theatrical punches are still habitually pulled.

Showing us apartheid through the dawning awareness of a well- disposed child is a cunning way of mediating its cruelty; its also, presumably, true to Giens own experience. But its usefulness diminishes as the child grows older, which may explain why the later scenes seem rushed. We flash forward to Elizabeth at university; it's the time of the Soweto riots and Moliseng is in the thick of them. The upshot sickens Elizabeth and she, like her author, leaves the country -- a development that was foreseen by her father who loves his country but had warned her "don't ever make this place your home."

She doesnt come back for years; and I wonder now, as I did last year, what took her so long. Not even the collapse of the hated regime can sway her, let alone any desire to see the man she still affectionately refers to as my old dad. (The play teases us, too calculatingly, with the possibility that her mother may be dead. I was reminded of the episode of The Simpsons, set in the future, in which Lisa phones home to announce her engagement, to be greeted by a delighted Marge saying "I wish your father could be here to hear the news" and then adding but he isnt back from work yet.) What draws Elizabeth back is news of Salumina and Moliseng; and it makes both her and the play look self-indulgent.

Moliseng's name is literally the play's last word, which by both actresses is first affectingly crooned and then stirringly proclaimed. But for this to be any more than sentimental, Moliseng would have to be the central figure (as opposed to figurehead, which she briefly is). And for this to happen, Elizabeth would have had to hog less of the limelight than she does. Even the story of her stricken grandparents is interrupted by her memory of an earlier, happier trip that netted her a nice new pair of pickaninnie pants. This is downright confusing, and anyway, weve had quite enough cuteness already. The play is also pretty cavalier with its symbolism. The tree of the title is Lizzies special childhood refuge: a place to swing from, a place to hide. But then it sort of spreads. Moliseng in Soweto is rhetorically exhorted to "hide ... climb the tree." Any tree, apparently, will do.

The play is another of the off-Broadway semi-serious type -- heartfelt, moderately funny, and safe -- that CanStage brings us every season. I'm not knocking this; somebody has to do it, and they do it well. On this occasion, under the play's original director, Larry Moss, theyve done it superbly, and twice over. Of the two performances, Caves is perhaps the more surprising. One expects McIntosh to be magnificent (she has a track record of it) and a standing ovation is guaranteed here from the moment she opens her mouth; it takes five minutes to realize that Cave, a Shaw Festival ingenue whose acting muscles are rippling with unsuspected strength, is headed in the same deserved direction. Both of them use the little-girl voice, but discreetly. McIntosh simply states it as a fact; Cave plays with it more. Both can be hilarious. McIntosh is black and Cave white; this doesn't matter -- both are required to play characters of both races, and anyway one-person plays make their own rules -- but it does make a difference, to both their perception and ours: Which is why I don't find "colour-blind casting" a useful term. Cave brings more harried, humorous detail to the Grace parents who, as characters, are the play's richest creations, or recreations; McIntosh is a more rooted Salumina, and a more mischievous Moliseng, roles that she inhabits but that Cave has more visibly to impersonate. As the older Moliseng at Soweto, McIntosh is thrillingly defiant, Cave movingly tremulous. McIntosh has richer fun with the young Lizzie, Cave is the more lucid as the narrating Elizabeth. But frankly, from the first moment, which finds Lizzie hanging upside down from her swing (superbly lit, like the whole play, by Louise Guinand) you know that you're safe with either actress and blessed with both. To indulge in one final quibble: McIntosh, on this stage, is born incandescent, Cave achieves incandescence, and the play, through their ministrations, has incandescence thrust upon it.
