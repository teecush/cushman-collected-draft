CROSS CURRENT THEATRE Robert Cushman sees Kiss of the Spider Woman as a rare serious musical

Wednesday, June 24, 1992

ROBERT CUSHMAN

ROBERT CUSHMAN The musical Kiss of the Spider Woman ends with a posthumous triumph for its leading character. He (for those who have not read the book, seen the movie, rented the video, or attended the show) is Molina, a window dresser imprisoned by a despotic South American regime for homosexual activity and forced to share a cell with a revolutionary activist named Valentin. The two move from mutual distrust - actual repugnance on Valentins part - to love. When Molina is released he carries a message to Valentin's girlfriend. The prison warden, who has planned it all, has him rearrested, tortured and killed. Cue for song.

Molina is a film buff, and this closing number is called Only in the Movies. For me, though, its hook was in its first words, which are Optimistic Ending. They made me very nervous. Up to that point I had been thrilled, gripped and moved. Now it seemed that the show's honesty was in jeopardy, about to be sacrificed to a musical-comedy finale. The composers, John Kander and Fred Ebb, and the director, Harold Prince, were too bright and too experienced not to know what they were doing, and they presumably intended irony. That did not mean they could bring it off. Could there be such a thing as a serious musical?

The answer, to put you out of your misery, is yes. The number keeps its balance: between the form of song-and-dance, which entails celebration, and the facts of the situation, which are appalling. Love does not conquer all. Molina keeps getting up, but he is still dead.

To see how rare this is, look at history. A Chorus Line, masterfully staged, ended with exhilarating glitz. It was intended to expose the pitiful plight of hard-working hoofers condemned to prance mechanically behind a star, but who cared? At the other extreme, Kander and Ebbs Chicago turned the form against itself, apparently to accommodate the disillusionment of its director, Bob Fosse. Dismissing its own accomplishments as "razzle-dazzle", it ended up blaming show business for the state of the world.

Between those two shows esthetically, before them historically, came Cabaret, the first Kander-Ebb-Prince musical, which put the birth of Nazi Germany stealthily and scarily on stage, and did it in musical terms. But it too faltered at the end, sending its characters scurrying around the stage mouthing significant reprises of earlier lines. One of the mouthers was a Jew, and my awareness of what was in store for him left me uneasy at the curtain call, when they tried to send us away happy. There is an unwritten rule that you dont applaud plays about genocide.

At its best Cabaret was history on the move: a rare achievement in the theatre, and one that Prince repeated in Pacific Overtures. That, and his other collaborations with Stephen Sondheim, showed musicals could connect intelligently with the real world: a challenge variously flunked by Aspects of Love (underwritten), Miss Saigon (tasteless) and Les Miserables (mindless).

Kiss is the best prison musical since Fidelio, and I'm not kidding. Are musicals escapist? Well, this one is about escape, physical and psychological. It doesn't transcend the medium; it uses it. So Chita Riveras Spider Woman - a diva playing a diva - is a chanting, swaying symbol of fate and of freedom. When the prisoners lust for her its like South Pacific's "There's nothing like a dame" once more with desperation.

Brutality doesnt sing; and the Wardens one number is a mistake. So is the explicit protest song; the love story is its own protest (it makes amends for the silencing of the gay theme in Cabaret) and the jail shows what there is to protest against. It falls short of the real horror - but then I once saw a Solzhenitsyn play that made the gulag look bearable. What it does show - men crammed inhumanly into a cage, for example - is done with the quick fluidity of musical staging. That is putting the form and its technology to serious use; just as the same director, with equal brilliance, put them to decorative use in The Phantom of the Opera. Love them or hate them, shows of this complexity don't get onto the stage on their own.

Above all, Brent Carver's Molina, though real enough for any play, belongs in a musical; there is rhythm in everything he does. Prince once described Guys and Dolls as one of the few mindless musicals he loved; he was wrong - not to love it but to call it mindless. It respected its source by transmuting it and by respecting itself; My Fair Lady and Cabaret did the same, and Kiss of the Spider Woman is their descendant. Robert Cushman writes and hosts Songbook, Sunday afternoons on CBC Stereo.
