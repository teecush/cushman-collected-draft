A COMMON MAN'S GUIDE TO LOVING WOMEN

Canadian Stage Theatre, Toronto

Glossy magazines remain full of articles for women on how to attract and hold onto men, though now, of course, they focus less on matters of dress and appearance than on sexual performance. Soberer organs, such as newspapers, are equally full of articles denouncing the assumption that keeping men happy should be a womans primary aim in life. It is seldom pointed out, though, that men are subject to much the same pressures and react in much the same way, thrashing about between dependence and whatever its opposite may be.

A Common Man s Guide to Loving Women presents the case -- or the conflicting cases -- for this neglected half of humanity. Andrew Moodie has written a conversation piece for four guys in their late 20s, all of whom seem to be doing rather well for themselves. One is a lawyer, another a corporate executive, a third works in television. But when something goes wrong in their relationships with women -- when a phone doesn't ring or a date fails to show -- their confidence evaporates, and they revert to confused little boys. And the other things they have in their lives, and even in their apartments -- video games, computers, basketball, strip- clubs, booze, and one another -- are apt to make them seem more boyish than ever.

All four are black, a fact that is sometimes of incidental importance and at others of no importance. Two of them are facing sexual and emotional problems of real magnitude. The lawyer is being dumped by his fiancee. The exec has been accused of rape and is liable to lose his job even before he comes to trial. To hear him tell it he is innocent. And even that is somewhat punctured by his lawyer friend, whose talk around this point has a decidedly journalistic air, redolent of the things people read and write rather than how they speak. Mostly, though, the dialogue is sparky and the issues compelling to sustain us through some rocky areas of narrative and characterization.

The characters are paper-thin, and the author jerks them around. One actor abruptly lets loose with a long speech about having been abused as a child. It's powerfully written and acted but once its over the speaker reverts to his previous status, that of a jokey commentator. We dont feel that hidden depths have been uncovered, merely that someone has been drafted to fill in a theme.

At other times, there is some tentative mixing of fact and fiction. We are told that the lawyer' s ex is an actress, and we hear that she has been seeing more than he would like of an actor called Randy Hughson. That happens to be the name of a real Toronto actor, so there were in-joke giggles on the first night. I thought the idea desperate rather than helpful, but I laughed as loud as anyone when our jilted guy consoled himself with the reflection that at least he wouldn't have to go to see any more of those damn plays. He should think himself lucky he hadnt been dating a critic.

Talking of art and life: After the premiere, there was the customary reception in the lobby and -- for the first time in my experience of these festivities -- there, laid out beside the regulation carrot-sticks and chicken wings, was a selection of brightly-coloured condoms, along with literature on how to use them. This was generous of someone, as well as being a praiseworthy reinforcement of the plays concern with safe sex. (Though the implied estimate of the amount of fun people have at first-night parties seemed to me wildly optimistic.)

The actors are lively (we're talking about the play again, now) though three of them -- Derwin Jordan, Andrew Jason Wade, and Conrad Coates -- are stronger on enthusiasm than on finesse. The author is the fourth player, and in strength, urbanity, and emotional grip he excels. His role is that of the only married man in the gang, though there's little sign that he spends much time at home. He talks about his wife in tones of propietorial affection but he never actually mentions her name.
