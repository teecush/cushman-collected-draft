CROSS CURRENT Sure, Bernstein was gifted, but was he any better than Gershwin, Kern . . . ?

Monday, November 12, 1990

ROBERT CUSHMAN

ROBERT CUSHMAN THE late Leonard Bernstein was a gifted composer, in several genres. His five Broadway shows are a distinguished bunch: On the Town, Wonderful Town, Candide, West Side Story and the one everyone forgets, 1600 Pennsylvania Avenue. That's one dud, one cult classic, two orthodox hits and a landmark: statistically stunning.

The exhilarating impact of New York, New York, at the start of On the Town, is a typical Bernstein opening. Arresting openings are de rigueur for a successful musical, but the first rumble in West Side Story and the Candide overture are exciting in a manner peculiar to Bernstein. There is a density of musical texture, a richness of allusion, beyond what we expect on Broadway, and that has to be traced to Bernstein's involvement with - to use a term I hate but can find no substitute for - classical music.

The musical establishment has various strategies for dealing with those of its members who stray across the lines. Gershwin, patronized during his lifetime, was posthumously deified; since he wrote Por gy and Bess and the concert pieces, his popular songs were accorded a sort of grace-by-association, respected by people who would consider Richard Rodgers intolerably middle-brow and may never have heard of Harold Arlen. Kurt Weill, when he wrote for Broadway, was accused of selling out. Nobody ever levelled that charge at Bernstein. He was cheered on, in the cause of reclaiming popular art for real art.

Bernstein's four big shows fall between the years 1944 and 1957. That is to say, they are part of the Rodgers and Hammerstein era; they rode Broadway's most self-confident wave. They were a vital part of that wave; it would have been poorer without them. But it is a jump from acknowledging that to assuming, as did one British obituary tribute, that they were the best Broadway had to offer. Globe and Mail music critic Robert Everett-Green declared that "as a writer of popular music, he was unbeatable, and a truly original artist." Well, he owed a lot to the example of Gershwin and Rodgers, but that's no crime. Still, "unbeatable"? Consider the above-named, plus Kern, Berlin, Porter, Loesser, Sondheim. Not all as well-trained as Bernstein, but all capable of writing music that stands up at least as well as his, both in and out of its theatrical context; all of them (and I don't except Sondheim) his superiors as melodists.

Everett-Green is right that Bernstein would have found post-1960 Broadway a less congenial place. I doubt, though, if he would have found it impenetrable. Sondheim, whose shows are more experimental than any of Bernstein's, Candide excepted, still gets them on. And is it true (Everett-Green again) that Bernstein's shows "changed the nature of the genre?" Wonderful Town is a celebration of New York, not much different in form or spirit from Guys and Dolls, which preceded it, or Bells Are Ringing, which followed it. On the Town showed how much of a story could be told in dance, but Oklahoma! had pointed the way. Candide, a comic opera, was one of a kind: brilliant, but it has had no successors.

There's a delicious moment in Wonderful Town at which an innocuous tune reveals itself as an Irish jig and dances - the music, not just the choreography - all over the stage. The entire score of that show is scintillating 1930s pop pastiche, as Candide is wonderful operatic pastiche. But the fun is always about how wittily Bernstein can imitate other composers; it rarely, as it does in Sondheim, serves a dramatic purpose beyond itself.

That leaves West Side Story; and it is this show, maybe with side- glances at Candide, that people have in mind when they speak of Bernstein as a Broadway trailblazer. Its "serious" subject matter was no great departure; South Pacific, which anticipated it in killing off its juvenile lead, was thought just as contemporary and hard-hitting in its time. (Today's realism is tomorrow's escape.) Like On the Town, it crossed choreographic frontiers and achieved a new, and certainly influential, fluidity of staging. But that, again, was Jerome Robbins; he could have done it with any composer. No, that's not fair; Bernstein's fierce, jagged dance music is vital to the effect.

However, West Side Story is supposed to be a musical, not just a ballet. When its love songs were plugged for the movie (though not before), they became hits. But they are terribly syrupy. The most famous one, Maria, is finally not about the boy's love for the girl but about the tenor's ability to hit a climactic high note. Maybe that's what is meant by raising musicals to the dignity of opera; and Bernstein's heir may not be Sondheim but Andrew Lloyd Webber. West Side Story may have reached its apogee in the "operatic" recording, conducted by the composer, which unerringly emphasized everything that is worst about it. The Pavarottis and Domingos have gone for the score, but the best popular singers - Sinatra, Fitzgerald, Mel Torme, Peggy Lee - steered clear of it. That may not be conclusive but it's significant.

It points to a lack of jazz in Bernstein: a lack that once led me to propose Andre Previn as a superior example of the popular composer with classical credentials. That was foolish of me; Previn may have been a more relaxed songwriter than Bernstein but even his best tunes are slight and, theatrically, he barely exists. There's a wistful masterpiece of a song in On the Town - We'll catch up - Some Other Time. I'm not sure Bernstein ever did, not in that area. But we are the richer for the chase.
