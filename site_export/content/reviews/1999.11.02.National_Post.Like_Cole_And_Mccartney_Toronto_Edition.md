THE JOHN PIZZARELLI TRIO

George Weston Recital Hall, Toronto

John Pizzarelli, jazz singer-guitarist, is the son of the noted non-singing guitarist Bucky Pizzarelli, and when he began his career he was billed as John Pizzarelli, Jr.

It was a good time to be a junior, and young Pizzarelli's record company promoted him as their answer to Harry Connick. Both came to prominence performing standard songs in ways that reminded people of older singers; Connick was touted as the new Sinatra, Pizzarelli as a latter-day Nat Cole. The two Wunderkinder have since gone in diametrically opposed directions.

Connick still sounds somewhat like Sinatra, tonally at least, but he has changed his repertoire; and the more he sings his own plodding songs, the duller his records get. Pizzarelli has dug further into the Cole mine, and his act might be described as a homage, but it is not an imitation; the more of Nats songs he does, the more he sounds like himself.

Like Cole in the 40s, he appears in a piano-bass-guitar trio format. The difference, of course, is that the leader and focus of attention is now the guitarist instead of the pianist. Instead of a reproduction of the King Cole lineup, we get a stimulatingly skewed revision of it.

The equation comes out about the same; the sweet and rhythmic guitar of Oscar Moore was as important to the original Cole trio sound as the leader's piano so Pizzarelli, who's a formidable instrumentalist quite apart from his singing, is a more than plausible leader now. And if his pianist, Ray Kennedy, has a self- effacing manner he turns out to be a furious player, as arresting as Cole but far more extrovert; more in fact like his apparent idol Oscar Peterson. The trio commenced on Friday night with a Pizzarelli original called Oh How My Heart Beats for You; unusually long for an opener, it filled its space, with Kennedy taking three fleet and seamless choruses in succession. In the second half, the three players -- the bassist is Pizzarellis brother, Martin -- did a storming instrumental of Sweet Georgia Brown.

Pizzarelli himself is never likely, I think, to escape from behind his guitar as Cole did from behind his piano. His singing is charming but small; indeed its charm lies in its modesty. Within it he can be witty -- as in his self-mocking treatment of the line about being "a rowdy-dowdy" in When I Take My Sugar to Tea -- and he can be touching; his Im in the Mood for Love was bashfully romantic, as if it were going on inside his head. He also did a trio of Beatles tunes. One, Honey Pie, he converted into his own currency; it could easily have been a swing-era novelty. And I Love Her he left, sweetly, much as it was -- but then his nice hip young man persona is not that different from Paul McCartney's. The third, Can't Buy Me Love, was indeterminate; it could have swung harder, as on the old Ella Fitzgerald record, without doing anyone any harm.

As a raconteur, Pizzarelli proved both voluble and valuable. He was very much on, spinning out finely-woven routines about opening for Frank Sinatra and almost meeting him, or about the indignities associated with making a vestigial appearance in The Out of Towners and about that movie's rapid (and merited) demise. These did sound spontaneous, even if they weren't.

Mimicry, however, is not his strong point; as he proved with an over-extended routine on I Like Jersey Best, which left to itself would be rather a funny song. This though was his closer, and by the time it came round he had earned far too much goodwill for us to hold it against him.
