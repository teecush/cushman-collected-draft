It is said Cirque du Soleil has scouts at the Olympics, but one wonders if it should be the other way around

CIRQUE DU SOLEIL: ALEGRIA

Ontario Place, Toronto

- - -

Early on in this Cirque du Soleil extravaganza, a revival of one of their greatest hits, there is a trapeze act. It starts quite gently, making audience members feel that they too might have swung that high and that wide in their playground days, if only someone had had the stamina to push them. Even when the performers pass ever more wildly across one another's flight paths, while hanging in impossible positions, there is still something very soothing about the grace and confidence with which they do it. They tell me that the Cirque people have talent scouts at the Olympics. Personally, I think it should be the other way around.

Indeed its hard, while watching the assorted gymnasts of the Cirque, not to conclude that we sent the wrong people to Athens.

Reviewing a Cirque du Soleil show is a tough gig, because the best parts, which are most of them, put you into a state of trance, and their worst into a state of boredom; and neither state is conducive to acts of critical analysis. Alegria (a Spanish word meaning "joy") does give us a way in by pretending to have a structure, maybe even a story. It purports to present an equivalent of the medieval Feast of Fools, at which social ranks changed places, kings becoming clowns and vice versa. So it seems that all these magicians of the body -- whose tricks depend on skill, not legerdemain -- have invaded some kind of castle, at which they are understandably welcome guests. But I dont recommend that you worry about it.

Marvel instead. Marvel at the somersaulting contortionists, who don't seem to have anything as inflexible as a bone in their bodies. Then there are the men who hold the Russian bars, a receding set of flexible horizontal springboards from which another set of men take off on vertigo-defying leaps, only to find that the first set of men have, quite literally, raised the bar, so that they have to land on a higher plane and then take off from that, even more extravagantly.

But then raising the bar is what the Cirque du Soleil is about. They have a fire-eater whose fire-eating is actually the least of his accomplishments. Anyone, he seems to be suggesting, can put flaming brands in their mouth; what really separates the men from the boys is the ability to encircle your body with rings of the stuff and juggle them at lightning speed. There's the man who seems to be flying through the air supported only by two large red ribbons. And there are the daredevils, whom I assume to belong to the same family -- this is a circus, after all, and theirs is a very traditional circus act -- who spin around near the highest point of this biggest of big tops, before dropping down to trapeze-level where other relatives, swinging equally hard, will catch them by their legs. I dont think there are ever more than three people in one of these formations at any one time, but even family dependency has its limits.

There is also a strong man who looks like hes employed as a bouncer but, as it turns out, wouldn't hurt a fly. More to the point -- since there are, famously, no flies on the Cirque, nor any other form of non-human life -- he wouldn't hurt a person. I guess that he, and the shows friendly though not over-hilarious clowns, are part of the narrative of which, as I have said, I could make little. There is, though, a scene at the end of the first half that is probably a part of it and that is extensively enchanting. It involves the sprinkling from a sack, onto the stage-floor, of a myriad of little slats and widgets that form into a miniature circular railroad track. Behind this there is a big black slab with a hole in it, suggesting a wall designed under the influence of Henry Moore or Barbara Hepworth. There is a snow-storm; it huffs and it puffs and it blows the house down.

The onstage characters have, I think, scurried to safety; but, just to let us know how it feels, with a mighty wind and a massive blower, the snow, in the form of numberless grains of confetti, is propelled at the audience. We rouse ourselves for the intermission, happily covered in the stuff. I have seen this effect employed before, in an entertainment called Slavas Snowshow in which it was used more lyrically, but that doesn't make it any less effective here.

The worst feature of Alegria is its music, which consists mainly of a trite titlesong, endlessly reprised. The sets, costumes, lighting and choreography, however, make glowing amends. I doubt if even the Cirque has a performer able to hold his breath for two hours, and it certainly isn't possible for a mere spectator, but most of us will sit there imagining the feeling.
