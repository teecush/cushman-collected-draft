For hardened Dora explorers, awards night 2010 could be an unusually beatific occasion, as befits the prize-giving at the end of an unusually good Toronto season. There are, as always, quibbles and questions over what has been nominated, and there will doubtless be more over what actually wins, but it's comforting to look at the key category -- general theatre division, outstanding production -- and realize that all five of the designated shows have a right to be there. They are: Soulpepper's Parfumerie, Tarragon's If We Were Birds and Courageous, Necessary Angel's Hamlet and Canadian Stage's 7 Stories. Since this category really means best all-around show, it should follow that the nominees will turn up in other slots as well. And so they have, apart from 7 Stories, which, if the Dora judges are to be trusted, arrived at its eminence without any of its participants having done anything individually remarkable. This is obviously nonsense, but at least Morris Panych, who wrote 7 Stories and directed Parfumerie, can take comfort in being up there twice, at least in spirit.

7 Stories, being a revival, could not of course qualify for outstanding new play. Erin Shields' If We Were Birds and Michael Healey's Courageous could, and have; and I expect them to duke it out. The only possible contender, Brad Fraser's True Love Lies, is deemed ineligible on a technicality (it's already been done elsewhere), and the nominations for Judith Thompson's Such Creatures and Anusree Roy's Letters to My Grandma (respectively skewed and skeletal) ought to be jokes, though they probably aren't. George Walker's And So It Goes is a surprise omission -- doesn't sentiment mean anything anymore? The Shields and Healey plays are both very strong, but I expect the former to win since a) its tragic fusion of classic myth and modern reality make it a more unusual kind of play, and b) its author is a comparative newcomer. In fact, I expect, and indeed hope, that If We Were Birds will sweep the board in every category it's up for.

I loved Parfumerie, which is just as well, as they'll probably be reviving it every other Christmas. All the same, if I'd had to choose an outstanding Soulpepper production it would have been Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf; the Doras' snubbing of Diana Leblanc (direction), Tim Campbell (supporting performance) and above all of Nancy Palk (actress) is, to put it nicely, incomprehensible. Diego Matamoros is up for best actor, and he was fine, though his un-nominated performance in Faith Healer was finer. And finest of all, though not eligible for perfectly legitimate reasons, was his solo version of Jorge Luis Borges' The Aleph; watch out for it in the future.

In fact, the best actor award will go, almost beyond a doubt, to Eric Peterson in Billy Bishop Goes to War, one of those occasions on which sentiment and actual achievement beautifully combine. Viola Leger in La Sagouine was another such instance, but she, most surprisingly, has been ignored. Tara Rosling, in If We Were Birds, should romp off, though that may not be the appropriate phrase, with best actress; she was stunning, and besides, what should have been her strongest competition -- Fiona Reid in Rock 'n' Roll, Palk (see above) -- has been ignored. Meanwhile, the actresses who played Gertrude and Ophelia in Hamlet have been nominated, though neither could ever be a leading role, however radical the adaptation. And Hamlet himself, Gord Rand, has been slighted, though without his uncanny performance the production couldn't have succeeded as well as it did. Rand has been nominated, in the independent section, for his performance in Goodness, and I hope he gets it; this has been his breakthrough year.

The Dora people continue their stubborn (again I'm being polite) custom of lumping together male/female/ dramatic/musical "featured" (i. e. supporting) performances in one category, and have compounded it by lumping in something called "ensemble." This is a cop-out, and anyway the two finest ensembles of the season haven't been honoured. I refer to those in Assassins and Top Girls; the latter, probably because it was a Mirvish (i. e. commercial) production, might as well not have existed, as far as the Doras are concerned. Anyway, Peterson is in that category, too, twice, and more power to him; but it should, and will, go to Maurice Dean Wint in Courageous. The sublime "featured" perf of the year, though, was completely ignored; I refer to Melody Johnson in 7 Stories. They got something against women, all of a sudden? Actually Johnson is sort of there, as director of Second City for Mayor, one of the "ensemble" nominees, though it was actually the less good of Second City's two shows. Johnson is also co-author of Mimi, which, unless some opera gets in the way, should split the vote for best musical with Leslie Arden's The Princess and the Handmaiden, my own vote going --slightly--to the latter. Mimi is also up for best musical production, which is different, along with Assassins and Light in the Piazza; there's riches, seriously. The Toxic Avenger Musical is also nominated but, to adapt John O'Hara, you don't have to believe it if you don't want to.

There's now a separate musical theatre division though it overlaps confusingly with the general one. Thus Adam Brazier ( Assassins) is up for best general director, and though all five contestants are more than worthy, he is the only one who should have a prayer over Alan Dilworth ( If We Were Birds). The year's best musical performance, Erica Peck's in The Boys in the Photograph, hasn't been anointed; of those who have, I'd go for Jacquelyn French ( Light in the Piazza). I have, for once, seen all five nominees for independent production; I'd plump for The Mill. (And, please, for its designer, Gillian Gallow.) Best touring production should probably go to The Walworth Farce, which I didn't see but wish I had. No nominations at all for August: Osage County; I'm surprised, but relieved.
