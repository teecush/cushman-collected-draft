This is a non-spoiler alert. There have been no advance screeners of tonights season premiere of Lost, so I am unable to drop any subtle hints of what might be going to happen. Or even, which would probably be more likely, any clumsy hints.

So let's go back to last season's finale. Actually, lets stop flattering the networks by echoing their self-aggrandizing audience- baiting talk of "premieres (which are usually extended recaps) and finales" (which, rarely finalize anything, because of there is another season to consider) and just refer to first, last and, when necessary, in-between episodes. Last seasons last episode of Lost found Jack, the show's nearest approach to a hero, at the point of leading his fellow plane-crash survivors, and other acquired flotsam, off The Island. He seems nearer to doing it than ever before, due to the descent from the skies (by parachute), of an English chick -- "bird, we used to call her --named Naomi, who has access to a boat and the complicated methods of getting in touch with it. At least, she had these things; she doesn't have them any longer because shes been shot.

Anyway, she may not have been all she seemed. On this show, as on so many others, it would be surprising if she was. Which of course means that she may be. Arguing against her authenticity is a television message, received in another part of the island and another part of the plot, from a woman with a much posher English accent, whose name is Penelope and who doesnt seem to have heard of Naomi or her boat. And then there's Ben. He's the shrivelled- looking guy in charge of the Others who were on the island first and have been making the castaways lives -- and sometimes their deaths - - miserable ever since they first got there. It took Ben a long time- - the better part of two seasons, really -- to reveal his full evilness, so maybe the writers didnt know about it then, either. It's getting quite hard to remember the first season of Lost, when the central conflict had yet to be declared and the whole thing seemed to be a cross between Survivor and a maturely-cast Lord of the Flies, complete with mysterious Beast. Ben, who is currently a captive of the good guys, has been warning them that if they trust Naomi, even posthumously, they'll all be killed. Well, of course he would say that. But one of the many irritating things about Ben is that he sometimes tells the truth when he ought to be lying. (Another irritating thing is that there seems to be no good reason for his underlings to render him unquestioning obedience. Its not as if he could beat them up. But thats often the case with criminal masterminds in fiction.)

I don't like Lost. I'm tempted to call it the triumph of style over substance, except that mystification and hand-me-down glamour arent stylish. Decadent" would be more like it, though, without the depraved fun that might suggest, I cant in all honesty dismiss it out of hand, as I usually end up at the end of an episode wanting to know what will happen next. What I don't want to know, and what the show insists on telling me, is what happened last. Something like half the series' footage consists of flashbacks to the characters lives before they made their fatal, or at least fateful, flight plans. The latest episode gave us a twist on this with an elaborate flash-forward: Jack, back home in Los Angeles, unsuccessfully attempting suicide, and then roaming around as a junkie unable to get his prescription filled. Which is really humiliating because hes a doctor. It's uncertain whether this is meant to be a literal prophecy, or just a projection of Jack's fears while still on the island of what will befall him if ever he gets off.

Its meant, obviously, to add depth to his character. But it doesnt work, just as the flashbacks don't work for him or any of the others. In their marooned state, the characters are as miserable a bunch as have ever threatened to go into indefinite syndication, but at least they have some action to sustain them. In their previous lives, they sit around feeling sorry for themselves. And they do it in the same glossy, airless television-land inhabited by the characters of all television soaps with aspirations to seriousness. It fits that Jack was a doctor in his former life, as was Juliet, one of his two bait-and-switch love-interests on the island; medical series have always been the shows with the most pretension to reality and the least connection with it. We are meant to think that the show's random collection of stereotypes have significance beyond themselves because they all have skeletons in their closets and because the landscape they currently inhabit is full of unexplained secrets and -- in the bad peoples lair -- complicated technological gizmos. Well, not always that complicated: Its rather charming, in the age of most series' multi-tasking cellphones, to find that Ben's gang still rely on the humble walkie- talkie. I mean, when did you last even hear that phrase?

Well, all right, there are some people I like. I cheered when Hugo a.k.a. Hurley, the fat fellow whose help is spurned by the tough guys, came to their rescue by driving his van right through the ranks of the opposition. Then theres Desmond, the ex-monk who was also a designer for the Royal Shakespeare Company. You have to love the details; I suspect that one was dreamed up by some studio- bound American scribe with fantasies about the British classical theatre. (Ive met some RSC designers, and none of them struck me as possible action-heroes. Still, you never know till you're tested.) Desmond has also read all of Dickens except Our Mutual Friend: Shame he missed that one, it's almost the best, but at least he has something to look forward to. Unlike his friend and compatriot Charlie the rockstar, whom he has failed to save from drowning. When, I wonder, did British characters become indispensable accessories for American TV shows? Does it go back to Daphne on Frasier? Or further still?

The isle is full of noises. The background music does most of the acting. So of course it always has, until the best HBO shows taught us to do without it. Now its pretty much a rule:Music that substitutes for drama means a show that cant be taken seriously. That doesn't always matter; many shows don't need or want to be taken seriously. Some even have a sense of humour: Lost probably thinks it has, but it has yet to yield a single intentional laugh. Its ultimate source, I think, may be the 60s cult series of all cult series, The Prisoner, an interlocking series of mazes. Lost aspires that way, but gets -- well, lost. It wouldnt be surprising if the whole thing turned out to be a dream. That would be the ultimate decadent touch.
