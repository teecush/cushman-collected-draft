BORN YESTERDAY

Festival Theatre, Niagara-on-the Lake

---

It's 1945. Harry Brock, a tough operator who made a fortune in scrap during the war, has arrived in Washington to corner the junk market for good and all. He has an ex-idealist lawyer to counsel him and a tame senator to sponsor the necessary bill. Harry doesn't break laws so much as bypass them, though he could certainly break legs if pushed. Ensconced with him in his very luxurious hotel suite is his girlfriend Billie Dawn, former Broadway chorine, who in addition to her obvious uses comes in very handy for putting her signature to documents she hasn't read. But then she doesn't read much of anything. That would mean putting on glasses, which she's convinced would ruin her looks.

Billie is the girl played by Judy Holliday in the movie Born Yesterday; they immortalized one another. The Shaw Festival has revived the Garson Kanin play on which the film was based, a play that might, especially at the Shaw, be subtitled Eliza Doolittle Goes to Washington. Harry gets to worrying that Billie might embarrass him in front of the high-toned people he needs to impress, so he hires someone to educate her. The someone is Paul Verrall, a journalist whose idealism is emphatically not ex-(man, he writes for The New Republic) and who wears glasses all the time. It comes as no surprise that Paul's pedagogy so transforms Billie that she gets to questioning her protector's business practices, especially the ways in which they run counter to what she has now learned about the U. S. Constitution. Along the way, I need hardly add, Paul and Billie fall in love.

The play is part screwball comedy, part melodrama and part -- a large part -- Democracy 101. It's also, of course, topical. The senator promises "no interference with free enterprise -- foreign or domestic." A hotel maid, taking in Sue LePage's supremely enviable set, observes, "While I'm getting $18 a week, I don't see why anyone should spend $235 a day," to which Paul dryly replies, "I know some people who'd call you a communist," to which she replies, "Tell them I'm thinking about it. Seriously." I'm not sure that the play ever improves on that for trenchancy: not when Harry's corrupted mouthpiece, who's drinking to forget, starts remembering; not when Paul starts quoting improving books, and dropping names like Tom

Paine; and certainly not when Billie starts quoting and name-dropping right back. The play is unquestionably on the side of the angels, but it gets mighty self-congratulatory about it.

Gina Wilkinson, directing, cannot unify the play's different styles, but she manages each very well on its own terms; hers is a very assured festival debut. An interpolated bellhops' ballet near the beginning points us down a blind and campy alley; and a prolonged game of gin rummy that's a classic on the screen just seems mystifying in long shot. But a symbolic book-burning is a fruitful invention, as is the decision to have Billie not merely boast of the five lines she once had in Anything Goes, but recite them. Funny in itself, it also feeds very nicely into Billie's adoption of that show's title song as her personal theme, which in turn guarantees that the audience's toes will be tapping for the curtain call.

The staging is smart and teamwork impeccable. As for individual performances, my only major beef is with Deborah Hay's Billie, a curious reservation to have since she's very talented and seems to be doing everything right: the teetering walk, the hard-boiled innocence, the corncrake voice. I blame the play. It banks heavily on the charm of hearing liberal verities delivered in a traditional dumb-blond squawk, thereby patronizing both the character and the audience. Eliza in Pygmalion didn't merely lose an accent, she gained a soul or rather discovered one that was there all along, but Billie only has a set of circumstances. Holliday humanized her, but she was unique.

The men in her life actually offer more scope. Gray Powell, latest in the Shaw's uncommon line of intelligent juvenile leads, is a perfect Paul, the picture of humorous integrity, while Thom Marriott makes Harry into a surprisingly complex brute, and unable at bottom to believe that he could be doing anything wrong. It isn't only his pride that is hurt when Billie deserts him, it's his ambitions. The dread that he too might be thought stupid really gets to him. And he does have his own smarts; when he's told that he can't have his cake and eat it, he says that it don't make sense, which it's about time somebody said.

There's reliable support work from Patrick Galligan and Lorne Kennedy, and Ali Momen makes a neat impression as Harry's cousin, a reluctant thug. The first night audience raised the roof, and the show does offer a good time. But Shaw, O'Neill and Sondheim offer better ones. - Born Yesterday is in repertory until Nov. 1.
