BULLY

Theatre Centre, Toronto

This seems to be the theatrical season of Stephen Hawking, the year in which the chairbound genius not only haunts the dreams of playwrights and their characters but bursts right out of them onto the stage. In Andy Jones' recent one-man show about good, evil and Newfoundlanders, an imaginary Hawking is featured, via video screen and sound system, as an occasional disembodied antagonist, his voice, or his voice box, piped in to pull the hero down from his more winsome flights. His role in Bully is more central, and he even appears in the flesh. Even here, though, he is a thing in the protagonist's dream.

Bully is the work of two young playwrights, Stephen Guy-McGrath and Steven Mayoff. As well as being half of the authorship, Guy- McGrath is half of the cast. He plays Eugene Michael Carter 2nd, a 10-year-old boy whose monogram spells out the most celebrated equation in modern science. This must have been a very thoughtful decision on his parents' part, though they haven't done too considerately by him otherwise. His father has left home; his mother spends most of her time bemoaning the fact.

Things are equally bad at school; Eugene is being persecuted by a 12-year-old who used to be his friend, and he ends up being seriously injured. He is a scientifically gifted boy (or, as his ex- mate would say, a geek), and he turns for consolation to imaginary conversations with the author of A Brief History of Time. In the light, if that's the word, of black holes, quantum mechanics and chaos theory he tries to make sense of his own predicament, and to imagine a way out of it. Alex Poch-Goldin plays all the other roles: Eugene's mother, his tormentor, the priestly and (it goes without saying) repressive teacher at his Catholic school, and Hawking.

This is a lively and ingenious piece that never makes full delivery on its promises. Eugene spends much of the play strapped into a harness, poised for flight. We acknowledge the boldness of the image and the intrepidity of the actor, but then we start to wonder what it's there for. We can see that Eugene has a lot to escape from; thoughts of the myth of Icarus invade our minds, and you might argue that Icarus, too, was let down by his dad (though, in all conscience, the old man did his best).

Ted Dykstra, the director, argues forcefully in the program that the play is really about the plight of a boy abandoned by his parents: physically by his father, emotionally by his mother, who thinks she's loving him when what she's actually doing is using him as the sounding board for her own grievances. But this doesn't really come across as central, not with Mr. Carter absent by definition and with the cross-gender casting giving us a Mrs. Carter who is stylized to the point of caricature. And though we can see why Eugene, himself a wheelchair case (we're never sure how seriously), should have adopted Hawking as a fantasy father, our understanding of their relationship is blurred by the fact that most of the time we don't know what they're talking about.

Or maybe I should speak for myself, as somebody who never even tried to read A Brief History of Time, much as I enjoy the title. Early on in this play the authors made me feel as if I really understood the Big Bang theory (though didn't I read somewhere that grave doubts have now been cast on it?), but the euphoria didn't last. I floundered, and so, as far as I was concerned, did the play.

At least, though, theory and dramatic practice unite in the central scenes, the ones showing Eugene's encounters with his playground nemesis, Harry Glassco. Maybe they aren't meant to be as central as all that. The play's title might well serve for any number of well-intentioned docudramas of the kind that get toured to schools. Probably the authors intended it to have a more complex resonance; it's possible to see that, in some sense, Eugene is being bullied by everyone else in both his physical and mental life, and that he is also capable of playing the bully himself. (The ending of the play, if I read it right, is scarifying.)

But in the end it's Harry's behaviour -- what you might call the classic bullying -- that carries the charge. It comes, though, doubly twisted. One variable is the suggestion that the two-year difference in the boys' ages, which once meant nothing, now means everything; if Time is the play's theme, then this is maybe the most striking observation on the subject that it offers. The other is that Eugene, testing out theories of infinite possibility, keeps re- playing the scene in his mind, testing out the different strategies he might have used. Whatever he says or does, the end result is always and ominously the same. What this is meant to prove scientifically I'm not sure, but theatrically it is very economical and very potent.

Guy-McGrath plays a very persuasive and engaging Eugene, victimized but not a victim, and Poch-Goldin complements him as a Harry with a similarly ambiguous glint, vicious but himself unmistakably boxed in. He also does sharp work as Hawking, though he has made some strange vocal choices; as a dream figure this Hawking is allowed both to walk and non-mechanically talk, but the accent he comes out with here is Central European. Did the actor actually want us to think of Dr. Strangelove? Or is this how Eugene, whose actual experience of genius scientists must be fairly limited, would think of him?

Anyway, this is not a squawking Hawking. And it seems to me that to make him a comic figure, as two shows have now done, is the highest and most merited of compliments. It's to acknowledge that his achievements have been so great, and his triumph over his afflictions so complete, that no amount of laughter can detract from them -- to treat him, in fact, like anybody else.

Some terminal thoughts, conceivably scientific: This play has a running time of 60 minutes. That may make it sound modest; nevertheless, it has been in the works since 1999, and has had three workshop productions. For what is presumably its prime outing, it has been staged way out west on Queen in a small basement theatre that, when I saw it on a Saturday night, was less than half full.

Now consider the production's credentials. Dykstra, its director, is one of Toronto's most talented and versatile theatre people: actor, director, composer. Poch-Goldin is one of our most highly rated young actors, and a promising playwright on his own account. They and the authors must wonder at times what it's all for.

That is the occasion as seen through a glass, a half-empty one, darkly. Lighten up. Treat the glass as half-full and it's admirable that they should all be so fully involved, and that a show worth catching should be there to be caught. Guy-McGrath and Mayehoff are clearly writers (and Guy-McGrath an actor) to watch. Bully for all of them.
