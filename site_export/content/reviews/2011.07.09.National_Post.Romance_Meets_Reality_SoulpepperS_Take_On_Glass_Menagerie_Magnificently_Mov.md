Ontario theatre is throwing Tennessee Williams a great 100th birthday party. On the heels of the Shaw Festival's Cat on a Hot Tin Roof, wonderful for its sustained firepower, Soulpepper gives us a production of The Glass Menagerie, no less wonderful for its sustained delicacy. The two productions have in common that they are both, on occasion, very funny. In Cat,the comedy serves momentarily to relax the tension. In Menagerie, it tightens it. The more we laugh, the closer we come to tears.

The Glass Menagerie, Williams' breakthrough play from 1944, is also his most overtly autobiographical. There are degrees, of course. Williams gives us a reminiscing narrator figure who's clearly a surrogate for the author, but his play still isn't as dependent as Eugene O'Neill's Long Day's Journey into Night on our knowledge that the author is writing about his own family. All the same, it's hard to believe that a situation at once so hard and so fragile as the one Williams conjures up could have been imagined by a playwright who wasn't drawing on his own experience.

To refresh memories of this memory play: The setting is a run-down apartment in 1930s St. Louis where live Amanda Wingfield, former Southern belle forever lamenting a luxurious plantation past that may or may not have existed as she remembers it, and her grown-up children Tom and Laura. Amanda married a telephone man who, as she says more than once, "fell in love with long distance" and hasn't been heard from in years. Tom supports the family by working in a warehouse, disappearing every night to the movies (or so he says) while practising on the side to be . well, to be Tennessee Williams. Laura is physically crippled, or at least has a bad limp; she's also, and perhaps consequently, emotionally crippled, shy to a point beyond the pathological. Amanda frets that her daughter has never had a gentleman caller, whereas she in her own youth was besieged by them (or so she says). Finally, Tom brings to dinner a colleague from the warehouse who also happens to be Laura's unspoken crush from high school days. When he meets her this time, he's nicer to her than anybody has ever been, which makes it all the more brutal when, through no real fault of his own, he has to let her down. Laura's hobby, her lifeline, has been her collection of tiny glass animals, her favourite of them all being a unicorn. We know, even if we've never seen the play before, that the mythical treasure is going to get smashed, taking Laura with it; waiting for it to happen is a source of exquisite tension.

There's less of the Southern Gothic in this play than in Williams' others; the language, though finely tuned and finely turned, sounds more natural than usual. It certainly does in Ted Dykstra's production, which doesn't have a slack moment and manages to be both tough and magnificently moving. It has a functional and atmospheric set by Patrick Clark, superb moody lighting by Lorenzo Savoini, and haunting music by Dykstra.

It also has actors who play off one another like co-operative virtuosi. The most surprising stroke of casting is the entrusting of Tom to Stuart Hughes, who must be about the same age as Nancy Palk, who plays his mother. It works, through Tom's role as self-admittedly unreliable storyteller. In this guise he's pretty much ageless, as well as being wry and witty and cracklingly sardonic to a degree I would not have believed possible in this play. So when he's playing his younger self, we accept him as a retrospective projection. Anyway, he's so good in these scenes, nobody's going to quibble. He's witty here, too, touchingly urgent in his concern for his sister (expressed at embarrassed second hand), and superb at charting the ebb and flow of his exasperation with his almost uniquely exasperating mother. This, I realize for the first time, is one of the richest mother-son relationships in world drama.

Like Cat, though less spectacularly, this is a play in which the characters take turns at centre stage. The first half is mostly Amanda's, and she's kaleidoscopic. She's the starry-eyed nostalgic, the passionately worried mother, the sweet-talking saleslady peddling subscriptions to a ladies' magazine while conscious of how far she's declined that she should have to be doing this, the overweeningly gracious hostess, the unreasonable matriarch blaming everybody (which usually means her son) for everything. She's a romantic tumbled into reality, knowing it for what it is even if she can't always cope with it. These qualities don't just alternate, they coexist; and Palk, operating at tremendous voltage and with a marvellous sense of shifting tempo, renders them as a seamless whole.

The second half chiefly belongs to Laura and her gentleman caller. Their long candlelit duologue may be infallible -at least, I've never seen it fail -but it could hardly be better done than it is here by Gemma James-Smith and Jeff Lillico. She's less physically ethereal than most Lauras, and more uncompromising in her neurosis (which here sounds partly catarrhal) but there's nothing in the text that contradicts that, and her final silent meltdown is a heartbreaker. Laura's real-life model spent most of her life institutionalized, and you can see it happening here. Lillico exactly captures his character's boyish enthusiasm, born of insecurity and thwarted hopes. With the best of intentions, he destroys Laura at least as surely as Stanley Kowalski, in Williams' next play, destroyed Blanche DuBois. Indeed, you might say that Williams created Blanche by combining Laura with Amanda.

There are cracks in the play; it's hard to believe Tom would have walked out on the broken Laura quite as abruptly as he says he did, and indeed their whole relationship, the subject of his valedictory speech, is underwritten in the play itself. Some of the social comment is overwritten; it's odd that this most domestic of Williams' plays should be the one to engage most explicitly with the world outside, and some of the attacks on '30s escapism and isolationism are facile and unfair; it's not as if America didn't have its own problems. But those are specks.

Writing about this production, I'm tearing up all over again. Happy 100th. Blow out your candles, Tennessee.
