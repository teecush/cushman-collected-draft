Those of us living in temperate zones like Toronto can stop complaining about what a terrible winter we're having. Ice Road Truckers, the new TV documentary show set in the Northwest Territories, proves to us that we just don't know from cold. You freeze all over just looking at it. And somebody like me, who flinches at the idea of a nighttime drive on the 401 even in the summer, can reflect on the truck drivers who are the central characters on this show. Their job is to drive supplies from Yellowknife to diamond-mining operations 300 miles farther north. And the roads they drive on, which have to be newly built every year, are made of ice. (The diamonds are described as "another kind of ice.") Some of us are terrified of driving on ice. These guys are scared -- well, apprehensive, they don't scare easy -- of driving off it. Or, more accurately, of falling beneath it. The establishing shots for each episode of Ice Road Truckers include 18-wheelers sinking, drivers presumably included, into the deep waters of instant hypothermia.

It's strictly a seasonal job. The ice road, built over permafrost, is good for only 60 days, which is just twice the time it takes to construct it, at an annual cost of $10-million. The rewards for the truckers are comparably big. One of them says that he can make a year's wages in eight nights; another, aged 25, looks forward to being a millionaire by 30. "The dash for the cash" is how they describe it at the start of each episode. It's nature's own reality show -- or nature's and commerce's -- and the narration is delivered in reality TV's exclamatory, heavily italicized tones.

We focus on a half-dozen truckers, and each episode concludes with a chart showing how much each of them has earned so far. "Some of them," we are lipsmackingly told, "will hit the jackpot, others will jack-knife." However, the jackpot contains legitimate earnings. There's no suggestion in the first two episodes that the TV company is offering any kind of top-up. (Well, on the History Channel, you'd hardly expect it.) This isn't some contest that's been artificially dreamed up by a network. It's what these guys would be doing anyway. That gives it its kick. The line between "reality" and documentary has been getting increasingly blurred, but this show --however it wants to bill itself and however unctuously it sometimes comes on--is on the respectable side of it.

The road will crack if they all start off at once, so they draw lots for departure times. The winner is Alex, the most experienced of the bunch, grizzled and jovial and something of a good-luck charm for the others. He sets off with a 220-pound load of diamond-processing material. ("Everyone's in a good mood," he says at the start, "unless you don't like my jokes.") Alex tells us, persuasively, that he loves being on the ice, but this time out he hits one of the many occupational hazards: an air leak. It's 40 below (Fahrenheit or Celsius, remarks a local radio announcer, at these temperatures it hardly matters) and has to keep moving, otherwise he'll freeze. But he also has to keep stopping; he's in for 13 hours on frozen roads.

At the other extreme is T. J., 21 and an ice-road virgin, who tells us that he "despises the cold," which initially suggests that he may be in the wrong line of work, or at least have chosen the wrong place in which to practise it. And wouldn't you know, first time out, he encounters the most dreaded of weather conditions, a whiteout. Here, as at several points, you get to wonder how the camera crew, unacknowledged in the cab with him, must have felt. Well, at least they had the satisfaction of providing T. J. with company. And the other guys, too: Long-distance driving encourages soliloquy -- these heroes have an audience for theirs

Existential drama? You bet. It's like a factual climate-reversed version of The Wages of Fear, the classic French film about truck drivers bearing loads of nitroglycerin, sometimes going up in flames when they hit a patch of rough ground. I don't know if comparable mayhem is in store for any of the designated protagonists on this show. We do meet the rescue teams who descend beneath the ice to salvage whatever, and presumably whomever, they can; fatality figures are quoted, but it's not clear whether they refer to divers, drivers or both. The truckers emerge with the expected blend of bravado and practicality, surface camaraderie and basic competitive solitude. The narration does its best to cheapen things with the reality show's patented rhetorical questions ("Is this rookie really cut out to go the distance on these roads?") and compulsive scoreboarding ("After 14 days of the dash for the cash, Alex is in the lead with $120,000"). But this hysteria is shamed by the guys themselves. "There's a whole lot of stupid rolling on the roads this year," says one of them. That's poetry.
