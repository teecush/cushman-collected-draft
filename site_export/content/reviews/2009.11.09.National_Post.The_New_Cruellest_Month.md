THEATRE REVIEW

AUGUST: OSAGE COUNTY

Canon Theatre, Toronto

Tracy Letts' August: Osage County has been acclaimed as the best American play of its decade. (Broadway, in defiance of statistics, throws up one of these every season.) What it actually is, at a running time of three-and-a-half hours, is a Gothic soap with pretensions.

The title, curt with rumblings of summer thunder, seems designed to bestow an ominous significance on an otherwise arbitrary sequence of family explosions. The time turns out to be irrelevant and the place hardly less so. The month is mentioned, I think, just once in the play itself, though we do gather that its very hot out and in (and the plays matriarch doesn't hold with air conditioning). Sausage County, as I can't help thinking of it, isnt name-checked at all, though the program tells us that its located in Oklahoma, about 24 minutes from Tulsa. There is some talk about the isolating nature of life on the plains, and there is one especially meretricious scene in which two of the characters get uppity and talk as if

they were some kind of metaphor for America, but mainly we have to take them for what they are.

They are the family of Beverly and Violet Weston. Beverly is a university teacher and once-acclaimed poet who spends the first scene drinking and maundering about T.S. Eliot. The acting in this touring production is generally better than that of the take-over cast I saw in New York, but I have to make an exception for Bev, who bestows a winking self-satisfied emphasis on the close of his every line. The only thing that made the scene bearable for me was knowing that at the end of it he'd be going away and not coming back. He drowns himself in the nearby river.

Violet faces widowhood through a blur, caused by galloping pill addiction. At other times, though still under the influence, she can be blisteringly clear, candid and cruel: in her own words, a truth-teller. In Estelle Parsons' performance, and indeed in Letts writing, she does this very well; Violet is a woman for whom attack is just another means of evasion, and Parsons is in total command of both the character and her carapace. Its a swinging, swingeing performance; and just to see her bounce or creep up and down the steps of a three-storey set is a treat. (The fact that she's 81 shouldn't, strictly speaking, influence our admiration, but of course it does.)

The brunt of Violets truthtelling is born by her three daughters. Oldest sister Barbara has also married a professor, and theyve brought their daughter along; Barbara's reeling bitterly from her husband's infidelity with a student. Middle sister Ivy, whos stayed home, is grabbing for what seems her last chance of happiness. Youngest sister Karen arrives for the funeral towing a thrice-wed fiance whom everybody else knows to be trouble. Theres also Violet's own down-home sister Mattie Fae, with whom she seems to have astonishingly little in common; her atypically good-natured husband Charlie; and their son, who's believed to be dim-witted and is known as Little Charlie. Big Charlie introduces us to Little Charlie in a short scene that may be the best in the play and is certainly the tenderest.

Letts can do other things as well. He can deliver melodramatic revelations when and how you least expect them. Theres one in the last act thats a humdinger: not the thing itself but the way it's sprung. He needs this talent because his method is to hop from one shock-horror episode, past or present, to another. You've had enough of Violet and her drug habit? Well, heres an older man hitting on a teenager, to take your mind off it. And, hey, how about some incest? And more unhappy marriages than you could shake a cocktail at. The trouble with all this is that it has no foundation. Theres occasional talk about Beverly's and Violet's marriage but no clear sense of what it was like; hence, no reason to care about them or, by extension, about anybody else. Maybe it is all meant to be about the locale. Or the month.

Theres humour, though less than the show or its first-night audience might like to think. Anna D. Shapiros production seems to have coarsened on the road, and to be aimed at people who think it shockingly funny to hear mature actresses using four-letter words. Most of the cast give in at some time or other; even Shannon Cochran, who otherwise dispenses a fastidious brand of swaggering self-hatred as Barbara, goes for an easy laugh at the second-act curtain. (She gets it; the public appetite for bad acting seems inexhaustible.) There's one character I haven't mentioned: a Cheyenne housekeeper (DeLanna Studi) who spends the first scene giving comfort to Beverly, the last giving it to Violet, and in between proves a very good cook and a great defender of virtue. Even the toughest American plays can wind up sentimental, and the final curtain of this one finds Violet the hitherto-untouchable curled up in the lap of the Indian (her word, though the others try to persuade her it's "Native American"). Perhaps this is a metaphor for America. Or it could just be a gimmick. - August: Osage County runs until Nov. 15.
