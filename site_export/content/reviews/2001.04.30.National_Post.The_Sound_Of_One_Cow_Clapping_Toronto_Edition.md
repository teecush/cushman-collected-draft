MOO

Alumnae Theatre, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - Sally Clark's play Moo won a Chalmers award in 1988, and though I refuse to believe that it was ever very good, I imagine that a skilled production would at least make it look slick. The Alumnae Theatre, which used to be a respected professional company, has given it an amateurish revival in which all the actors are over- stretched, but if you can decode it you may discern in the text a recognizable Canadian quality, one that I would define as cute heartlessness. It can get cloying, but experienced actors on Toronto's classier stages have frequently managed to shine it up and pass it off as wit.

And, undeniably, the play starts with a smart idea. Sometime in the 1920s, to judge from the background music, Harry has his wife, Moo, committed to a mental hospital, claiming that she is actually his sister and that her furious denials only prove her insanity. Strangely, it takes the authorities the longest time to check out Moo's story, and by the time they do Harry has skipped town and shacked up with somebody else.

We are not, though, watching a tract about a man persecuting a woman. As far as we can tell, Moo is crazy, the proof being her continuing obsession with Harry, who first won her affection by shooting a gun at her. "I have this compulsive need" she says, "to be screwed around by someone. Not just someone -- no ordinary Tom or Dick will do; it has to be Harry." Treasure that line. It's as good as the play gets.

The attraction is symbolic; Harry is obsessed with Moo, too. The action unfolds over a further 40 years, much of which he spends mailing her postcards from exotic and unaccessible locations. She sets off in pursuit, generally finding that by the time she gets there, he has moved on. Occasionally she does track him down, and one of these reunions produces a son. In the course of Act Two this boy, tired of being reviled by his mother as ugly and stupid, revolts and goes off to war.

We also see Moo interacting with her family, especially with two sisters who disapprove of the goings-on, and whom she of course regards as pitiable prisoners of convention. The longer the play goes on, the easier it becomes to sympathize with the sisters' point of view.

When her relatives give her a 60th birthday party, Moo turns up accompanied by an admirer who gropes her furiously under the table, and whom even she admits to finding disgusting. She ends the play institutionalized once more, senile, visited briefly by a still- infatuated Harry and at greater length by a niece who used to admire her but who now reviles her, accurately enough, as a selfish woman who has wasted her life on a futile, not to say degrading, passion.

The niece also proclaims that she has no intention of letting the same thing happen to her. This may be a moment of genuine hope for the future of women, or it may be crushing dramatic irony; we simply don't know enough about the young lady to tell.

The play does not exactly endorse Moo, but it does present her as a force of nature whom we will enjoy spending two hours with: an Auntie Mame with consequences. Women like her certainly exist, as do men as helplessly feckless as Harry. Compulsive behaviour, though, is not necessarily compelling behaviour, and the play is one of that common breed that state a situation and then just keep repeating it (cf., currently, Hedwig and the Angry Inch, which at least is shorter and has songs).

Kristen Schiener's production is unable to keep the play in the style to which it would like to become accustomed. It has trouble moving with any kind of fluency from one short scene to the next, its furnishings sometimes misbehave (when I saw it, Moo had to bestow a sharp kick on a recalcitrant retractable bed), and it's sloppy at staging crucial physical actions. At one point Harry, stationed far upstage in the hope that we won't notice, gives his second wife a vague shove. We learn later that what he was actually doing was pushing her downstairs; who would have known?

Even worse, the actors -- mostly non-Equity -- seem to lack any sense of verbal timing, and the scenes between Moo and Harry completely lack sexual tension, which undercuts the play's entire premise. Brandon Barre as Harry has more ease than anyone else on stage, but not too much else. As Moo, Risa Gittelman seems (probably unfairly, since presumably she didn't cast herself) to be at the centre of a vanity production; she doesn't have the range, the authority or the sparkle for a character who sees herself and is seen by the play as a virtuoso creation.

The others, though they appear terrible, may simply be too young for their roles; the two who actually get to play young -- Darren Stewart-Jones as the son, Stefanie Drummond as niece -- are noticeably better than the rest.
