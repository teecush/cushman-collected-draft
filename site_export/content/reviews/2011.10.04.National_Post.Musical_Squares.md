THEATRE REVIEW

Chess

Princess of Wales Theatre, Toronto

The best thing about Chess is its title. The worst is that the title has nothing to do with the rest of the show.

OK, that's an exaggeration. The storyline does involve a world chess championship match - two of them, in fact - and the musical begins with a company number outlining the history of the game, though the information is bewilderingly irrelevant to what follows and - this being a high-decibel, low-definition pop opera - is mostly incomprehensible anyway.

We then narrow down into a personal contest between an American chess master, Freddie Trumper, and a Russian, Anatoly Sergievsky, whose story is "inspired," as they say, by the encounter of Bobby Fischer and Boris Spassky. The twist here is that the American's coach or "second," Florence Vassy, tired of her principal's hysterical narcissism, falls for the Russian and he for her. In other words, it's the same old trite love story, and the contestants could just as well be playing Tiddlywinks. Or at least tennis.

Which seems a waste of a terrific metaphor. A checkerboard stage (which this show doesn't have) always looks good, and the game itself is the purest of power plays, the more powerful for being conducted under a code of chilly politeness. Chess, the game, is politics, or at least diplomacy. The musical does have this somewhere in mind. There is pressure on both players to throw the game, and in the first act - set in Italy - Freddie literally does so, hurling the pieces to the floor. Boris is pronounced world champion, and promptly defects, to England and to Florence.

A year later, in Bangkok, he defends his title against another Russian. Boris's wife, who wants him back; his country's government, which wants him back; and a TV company that wants ratings, all put the screws on him to lose the game and come home. The Russians also blackmail Florence, who's Hungarian, by promising to release her father who's being held in Moscow. The Americans, for their own nefarious reasons, are in bed with the Russians on this one. So it could be quite the game of chess, but it doesn't even get started till the second act, and the connections between microcosm and macrocosm are never pushed through in writing or staging.

The action takes place in 1979 and 1980 and the show, after being a record album, was first staged in London in 1986. The current production is also British and, like Private Lives next door, comes to Toronto en routetoNewYork.Thedirectorchoreographer, Craig Revel Horwood, makes a determined metaphorical effort by dressing up his chorus as chess pieces, black and white, individually denoted by rank (in the program anyway) and costume.

When, at the back of the stage, an actual game is in progress, the ensemble down at the front knock one another about, from which we may deduce that a piece has been taken. But that's as far as the idea goes. There's no sense of hierarchy among the chess persons, and most of the time the performers could be anyone. What is impressive about them is their ability, in a production conceived on the John Doyle model of actors-as-musicians, to sing, dance and provide the entire accompaniment all at the same time. Playing the violin while lying flat on your back is probably not a skill much taught in music schools, but these people really have it down.

And the music they play (by Benny Andersson and Bjorn Ulvaeus of ABBA) is demanding, though that isn't necessarily the same as good. The curse of the poprock-through-sung-musical is that it equates drama with noise. Internal conflict means a character screaming prepackaged feelings at the audience. External means two characters (seldom more) bellowing them at one another.

Admittedly, traditional musicals aren't always much better at the interpersonal relationships, but at least they have dialogue to help out. The lyrics here, plus the almost non-existent book, are by Tim Rice, whose considerable sense of humour (his program bio is the second best thing in the show) nearly always goes missing in his musicals. A semiclever couplet such as "have you an appointment with the consul/if not, his response'll be no" sticks out like a comparatively healthy thumb.

The general tone is starchy, though some overarching irony seems also intended; how else to explain the presence of the Arbiter (David Erik), a slinky referee-narrator figure who suggests the MC from Cabaret, starved of any function? Also somewhat in the Cabaret tradition is One Night in Bangkok, the original breakout hit number about how decadent the city's nightlife is. It has nothing to do with the rest of the show, which could be happening anywhere.

The other famous song, I Know Him so Well, duetted by Anatoly's wife and mistress, is perhaps the answer to Rice's earlier I Don't Know How to Love Him; the advance in knowledge may be admirable, but it can't make up for the fact that we don't know him at all. Or anyone, except perhaps Freddie (James Fox) whose childishness gives him a certain flavour and who has an interesting trajectory in the second act.

The end of the Cold War has removed some of the show's initial urgency, but historical drama is still a respectable genre, provided the people and the issues are clear. But here the characters are pawns, in an unintended sense, and the narrative impenetrable; as the inclusion of a detailed synopsis in the program virtually admits. Maybe they should go the whole operatic hog and add surtitles.
