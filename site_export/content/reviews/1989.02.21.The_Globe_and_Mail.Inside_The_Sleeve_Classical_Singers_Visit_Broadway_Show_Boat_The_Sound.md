INSIDE THE SLEEVE Classical singers visit Broadway Show Boat The Sound of Music

Tuesday, February 21, 1989

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Telarc DG 10162 Of Thee I Sing" Let 'Em Eat Cake CBS F2M 42522 ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail For some years now, opera has been trying to do musical comedy a favor. Record companies have signed up divas, symphony orchestras and assorted Placido Flamingos and unleashed them on the Broadway repertoire. For obvious reasons, they have concentrated on the graver or more pompous musicals, and the results have been grisly. We have had a West Side Story with Kiri Te Kanawa that unerringly emphasized everything most unbearably overbearing in the score; a South Pacific in which the same dame sang as though English were her third or fourth language; and a My Fair Lady on which I cannot comment. Te Kanawa singing Cockney, in a show that is actually about authentic accents, was more than I could face.

For some reason, classical singers in complete shows sound more grotesque than when they record solo albums of pop standards, which at least give them the chance to stretch out and impose a personality. The disciplines of a score throw them back on technique, and it's usually the wrong technique. But there are always exceptions; and the box-set three- record complete Show Boat is a glory.

Frederica von Stade plays the heroine Magnolia, and Jerry Hadley her gambler lover (and later husband) Gaylord Ravenal. The crucial point is that they have been engaged as the servants of the show, rather than the show being recorded as a vehicle for them. The presiding genius is conductor John McGlinn, who has made it his scholarly mission to restore classic musicals to their original form. Broadway's habits are chaotic, and McGlinn has had to cut through the detritus of revivals, adaptations, cuts and rewrites. The results are revelatory, right from the start; Jerome Kern's overture, generally replaced by a medley of the show's half- dozen hits, turns out to be a murkily ominous Mississippi tone-poem. The first sung line, "Niggers all work on the Mississippi," jolts as it was meant to. Lyricist-librettist Oscar Hammerstein was telling a serious story about race relations in the old South and he knew what he was about. The restoration later on of a prophetic chorale, Mis'ry's Comin' Aroun', raises the score to Porgy and Bess level.

In fact, McGlinn gives us every note of music: songs full-blown and fragmentary, dance-interludes, even underscoring - which means we get sizeable chunks of dialogue as well. These suggest that Show Boat, if not precisely a tragedy (its story develops but its characters don't), is still a drama. It was first staged in 1927, and not for decades did Broadway attempt anything comparable. McGlinn also throws in an appendix, of songs deleted from the first production or added for subsequent versions, including the 1936 film. This is a Variorum Show Boat.

Hadley is sound and round, if stolid. (But Gaylords always are; their glamor has to be taken on trust.) Von Stade is delightful, without a hint of condescension, though some of the later jazzier numbers tax her a bit. I'm prepared to believe that she has rhythm in her soul, but she cannot always get it through her lips. McGlinn claims that pop singers have swung and"or torched these songs out of recognition, but they were responding to something that is really there, whether Kern himself knew it or not. (And whether he liked it or not.) The real cross-cultural dynamo here is Teresa Stratas as the doomed mulatto Julie. She acts with the sad worldliness of a Kurt Weill heroine, and sings with a purity and ease that transcend categories. She leads an ensemble rendition of Can't Help Lovin' Dat Man that is the most joyous thing I have heard in ages. (It's a happy song, whatever nightclub tragediennes may have done to it.) You yearn for a full-scale stage revival, harnessing talents from opera, drama and musical comedy; the show deserves them. Recording quality is crystalline. Neatly balancing Hammerstein's first major show is his last: if The Sound of Music could ever be described as major. This is another musically complete version (every chirrup of birdsong and every clang of the church bell), but this time with von Stade as unchallenged star. She is an expectedly pristine Maria, though she has surprising trouble with breath- control; her Favorite Things are interspersed with audible pants. The entire show has been cast operatically, which is a mistake. The show contains two bracingly cynical numbers (it's always a shock to realize that this, of all Rodgers and Hammerstein scores, contains anything cynical, and certainly the film had no truck with them), but they are ruined here by a stodgy buffo and a vapid mature soubrette. Accompaniment is by Erich Kunzel and the Cincinnati Pops, who are appropriate. The treat is the presence of Eileen Farrell, America's Schwarzkopf and the greatest of all cross-over artists, as the Abbess. She was always able to cope with both classical and popular material by having, as she says herself, two voices; and her Climb Ev'ry Mountain, supremely relaxed, is the most tolerable I have heard. More in the Show Boat line, for authenticity and importance, is the double-headed recording of George and Ira Gershwin's two political comic operas. Of Thee I Sing, a whimsically disrespectful spoof of the U.S. presidency, was a prize-winning, record-breaking hit in 1931; Let 'Em Eat Cake, its dyspeptic successor, was a quick flop in the more defensive atmosphere of 1933.

There is plenty of good wisecracking though, both in book and lyrics. Union Square, with its refrain, "Down with everything that's up," anticipates and deflates the all-purpose protest song. The Gershwins are audibly flexing their muscles, George especially, though the constant references to Gilbert and Sullivan become a crutch rather than a satiric device. On the other hand, when a Southern belle strikes up a lament, the music is limbering up for Porgy. (She's lamenting because the President, having promised to marry her as part of his election platform, has turned her down for a girl who can bake corn muffins. You get the picture.) The score, despite brisk conducting from Michael Tilson Thomas, is tamer than it might be because as the opportunist hero Larry Kert, the original Tony of West side Story, still sounds like a blameless juvenile lead. However, Maureen McGovern is an unexpected pleasure, soaring sweetly like a pop Barbara Cook; there is smart comedy singing from David Garrison and Paige O'Hara (both heard to less advantage in Show Boat); and there is the gentle presence of Jack Gilford as the perpetually overlooked Vice- President Throttlebottom, gracefully resigning his one chance of glory with the words, "You can be President and I'll go back to Vice."
