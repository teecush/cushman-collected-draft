THEATRE REVIEW"The publicity for The Noam Chomsky Lectures at Theatre Passe Muraille may be high-flown, but it is a good 70 per cent accurate as well Critics hit with tactical, strategic attacks THE NOAM CHOMSKY LECTURES

Friday, March 22, 1991

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Toronto ONT -- Written and performed by Daniel Brooks and Guillermo Verdecchia At Theatre Passe Muraille Backspace in Toronto BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Toronto THE press handout for The Noam Chomsky Lectures describes the show as a meta-theatrical onslaught . . . unrelenting and sometimes rabid, adding that it "reaffirms the theatre as a place of dissent" and will radically alter your understanding of the theatre, the mainstream media, yourself and the world you live in. But I went anyway.

Just because the publicity is pretentious garbage, I told myself, it does not follow that the play will be. But I admit it, I was wrong. Not about the play, which isnt, just as I expected it wouldn't be, but about the publicity, which isn't either. It is high-flown, yes, but it isnt pretentious; and, fairly far from being garbage, it is a good 70 per cent accurate.

Ill get back to that, as writer-performers Daniel Brooks and Guillermo Verdecchia would say, and do. (I mean do say, not would do, although that would work too.) What really lured me to Passe Muraille was the prospect, well publicized in the press though not in the official handout, of hearing some of my fellow critics roasted.

Brooks and Verdecchia deliver. They read out, and also project onto a screen, extracts from reviews written by The Globe and Mail's Ray Conlogue and the Star's departed Robert Crew and current Geoff Chapman, extracts they plainly believe display the authors as priggish and"or reactionary buffoons. I didnt say a word, thank you very much, and why should I defend them, theyre big boys. As for me, I knew that I was far too young and inoffensive to draw any flak myself. If you must know, halfway through, one of them said, in reference to some passing flippancy I emitted last week: "Yes, Mr. Cushman, this is an intermission. Honestly, I was enjoying myself too much to want an intermission, and The Noam Chomsky Lectures is not the kind of piece that needs one.

The attacks on the critics are tactical, designed to secure the audience's complicity in the baiting of a common enemy; and strategic, setting up the creators' main theme, which is the manipulative power of the press. They have little to say about television, despite the generalized references to the media." Maybe they dislike that word as much as I do. Or maybe its because there is hardly any theatre criticism on TV to give them a handle.

The show consists of two fellows at a table haranguing us, and sometimes one another, with arguments, facts and jokes. They are confessedly performers and we are voluntarily an audience, so the event is theatre, but its distance from what usually passes under that name gives it the latitude to question its own identity and, in so doing, to reaffirm it. This is, as per the blurb, meta-theatre. It is also known as double- bluff. And what you are reading is its literary equivalent, which we might call para-journalism, designed to evoke the theatrical experience by simultaneously miming and mocking it - without malice, since I really liked it, but without ever allowing the show to distract attention from my main preoccupation, which is, as Brooks and Verdecchia will be the first to recognize, myself.

(What I agonized most about in constructing this review was where to insert my reference to the shows reference to me. I contemplated leading off with it, and I also considered inserting it in an arch parenthesis like this one.) I don't think I would describe the show as "rabid - I didn't catch Verdecchia foaming at the mouth more than once or twice - but it is unrelenting. The polemics never let up. At one point the actors instruct us in their understanding of the word terrorism" - which is different from the newspapers - by shouting at us while simultaneously assailing us with lights and noise. It hardly compares with the average rock concert, but it did reduce an infant in the audience to tears. (My respect for the actors was somewhat lessened by their failure to take account of this.) What they are banging on about mainly is U.S. foreign policy and Canadian complicity in it. I guess this "reaffirms the theatre as a place of dissent, though doing so may not be that novel. And here I call Geoff Chapman to witness. Brooks and Verdecchia wouldnt be having such a go at him if he hadn't been having a go at plays whose attitudes they approve of. And, by the way, I don't think the laugh-count mentality they attribute to Geoff - yucks for bucks" - is such an outrageous criterion for judging most plays. They subscribe to it themselves, as they admit, and then satirize themselves for admitting it, which is all the funnier, and puts us in the mood for absorbing their anger, and I imagine youve got the idea by now.

Both the anger and the humour ring true. And when they attack their own hypocrisy (i.e. their greed and professional ambition), that rings true too. It does seem to justify their attacks on larger hypocrisies. They are more enraged by governments sanctimonious excuses for inhumanities than by the inhumanities themselves. Or maybe I'm projecting my own feelings onto them.

Brooks does a virtuoso job in outlining the ties that bind print and finance in Toronto. This is done with the aid of a chart, on whose showing I have been working for some of the fattest cats on two continents. So how come I ain't rich? If the guys want conspiracy theories, have they noticed that all four of the theatre critics they mention have the same last initial? This may mean that arts editors have a suspicious regard for the ghost of Nathan Cohen, or just that they interview candidates alphabetically and get bored early on. Or is that too rational? I knew before I went near the theatre that I would end up describing the show as a cross between My Dinner with Andre and Rosencrantz and Guildenstern Are Dead, and now I have. I think that both Brooks and Verdecchia, especially Brooks, have prosperous careers ahead of them as licenced gadflies. No, it wasn't Chomsky who coined the phrase "repressive tolerance," but I have to get him in somewhere. The boys do quote him, constantly, but I gather that he had his doubts.
