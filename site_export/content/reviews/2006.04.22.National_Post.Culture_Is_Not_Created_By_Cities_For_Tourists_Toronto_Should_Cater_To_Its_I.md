Critics are always being asked about their responsibilities. Recently, we have been handed a new one. A few weeks ago, I was on a panel at which one of the speakers from the floor asked whether, in keeping with the new spirit of the age, we reviewers didnt feel a special obligation to be nice to shows that might bring tourists back to Toronto.

The show my questioner had in mind was, of course, The Lord of the Rings, a show that, as it happens, I liked. But I hadn't been prejudiced in its favour by the advance publicity: rather the reverse. That long buildup, you may recall, was all about the economic benefits that would accrue to Toronto from the presence of a big hit musical. We heard a lot about the unprecedented amount of money and technology involved, and about the show's being based on a hugely successful book that had already spawned three hugely successful films. It seemed to be trading heavily on name recognition; it was, in fact, a spinoff. Really, its a wonder it turned out as well as it did.

David Miller, the Mayor, approves of it. In an article in last Sundays Toronto Star, outlining a vision for the city's golden future, he looks to a time when Lord of the Rings is still playing." It's Exhibit A -- indeed its the only theatrical production mentioned by name -- in his eulogy of the citys arts- boosting program.

The Lord of the Rings may or may not be successful in attracting the foreign hordes, but I cannot believe that it forms part of any vaunted "Toronto cultural renaissance, if only because it has so little to do with Toronto, or indeed with Canada. Yes, it does include some of our best actors, a couple of whom are doing some of their best work. But the material, the production and the initial impulse and financing are all British. It's a close call, but I'd say that the big Livent musicals from a decade ago, Show Boat and Ragtime, were slightly more Canadian. They were American in their writing, their staging, their subject matter and most of their casting, but they were at least produced here. And they did attract American audiences, as did the same companys money-spinning import The Phantom of the Opera and the equivalent shows that have been brought in by the Mirvishes.

But none of this constitutes a culture, let alone a renaissance. Nor, despite all the fuss thats being made about it, does the concurrent opening of a batch of new performance and exhibition spaces: What somebody once called the edifice complex." Personally, after years of enduring the cramped seating and barnlike acoustics of the Hummingbird Centre, I'm delighted that the Canadian Opera Company is about to have a new home. But the exciting thing here is not that the COC will have "a world-class facility (whatever that means) but that it has a world-class production of the Ring cycle to put inside it. The only art form for which buildings, in themselves, are a major achievement is architecture, and in that respect Toronto has a long way to go before it becomes Chicago.

A culture is not something that a city creates for tourists. It's something that a city creates for, and with, its own inhabitants. If tourists want to join in the fun while theyre passing through, fine, but thats not what it's about. Toronto keeps talking about becoming a theatre centre to rival London or New York but it seems prepared to skip a few stages to get there. Both those cities had strong theatrical traditions long before the visitors arrived. Britain's National Theatre has been, by and large, a roaring success. But it had already been one for 10 years, in makeshift quarters at the Old Vic, before moving into its purpose-built complex on the South Bank. (And that Old Vic success grew from uncounted years of previous tradition.) Broadway has become something of a joke, and it certainly is marketed to tourists, but those tourists are only interested -- certainly I was when I first visited New York -- because the place has an aura, accumulated over decades during which it catered primarily to New Yorkers.

Tourists, anyway, are fickle. They are only around for part of the year. Managers in London know this, and in the off season they concentrate their marketing strategy on attracting the home audience, or a larger proportion of it than would be coming anyway. Tourists are limited, too, in what they want to see. They want to see musicals or, given the appropriate surroundings, classics. They dont want to see new plays unless theyre established commercial hits, which Toronto has never found a mechanism for creating. And, frankly, why should they? The plays are unlikely to speak to them. New plays don't have to be parochial, but they do imply a shared frame of reference, which a theatrical metropolis has the time, and the resident audience, to develop.

The Toronto success story of the past decade has been Soulpepper, which neither plays nor markets itself to tourists. So I was told a couple of years ago by Albert Schultz, the artistic director. I wondered whether the move to new premises in the Young Centre might have changed that, but when I spoke to him last week he assured me that it hadn't. He did say that subscription audiences -- local by definition -- had doubled, and that his focus was on attracting the four million people who live within 25 minutes of us." (He also said, and I heartily agree with him, that Toronto has spent far too much time feeling sorry for itself over the fallout from SARS and 9/ 11.) Soulpepper, by the way, shares the Young Centre with the George Brown Theatre School. One of the most exciting of all Torontos new spaces is the National Ballet School on Jarvis. These cant be tourist attractions. They're investments in the culture of the future.

Soulpepper doesn't do new plays, but it has built a new Canadian company. The best prospect for our citys theatre is the simultaneous flowering of Soulpeppers classical policy with the fostering of new plays at theatres like the Tarragon, Factory and Passe Muraille. This past season has been a terrible one in the latter department, though before rushing to judgment we should remember that a) always and everywhere, more people have been good at acting in plays than at writing them; b) history teaches that any theatre that devotes itself to new plays will spend 90% of its time producing junk. The best we can hope for is that some of it will be lively, temporarily engaging junk. And, to be fair, some recent seasons have come closer to meeting that quota. It's an unglamorous hope, but it's the best we have. And tourists wont help us achieve it.

Near Bay and Wellesley, theres a condo development where the new opera house was once intended to be. It's called Opera Place -- in other words, what we have in place of an opera. We can wave all the flags and chant all the slogans we want, but if we put all our faith in new buildings, imported musicals and an audience of transients, they will be what we have in place of a culture.
