Toronto's Theatre Centre, about to move from its long-established address at Queen and Dover-court, takes leave of its old premises with a show that is itself a requiem for a shuttered place of entertainment. Boblo takes its name and its subject matter from an amusement park that flourished for nearly a century on an island in the river between Windsor and Detroit. Its name was a corruption of Bois Blanc. It finally shuttered in 1993. Among those who cherish its memory are Erin Brandenburg, writer, and Andrew Penner, musician and composer. They crafted this memorial tribute together, and their company Kitchen-band is presenting it.

It's an impressionistic piece, not a history. In fact, as somebody who had never heard of Boblo, let alone visited it, I left with a very hazy idea of what the place was like, or how it may have differed from any other carnival park.

Penner's music is the best part of the show, and as front-man and vocalist, he himself is the dominating presence in it. He provides, among other things, a great vocal that leads into a Charleston routine that itself leads into a challenge dance. I'm not sure why the 1920s should be privileged here above all other periods; maybe it's just because Charlestons generally look and sound like fun. Certainly this sequence is the liveliest part of the evening. Other times the music is mainly moody and folky, most effectively so in a number called Pelee String Club that comes right at the end: after the curtain call, in fact.

Boblo - the fairground, the island and the encircling Detroit River - are presented as sources of pain as much as of pleasure. Almost the first thing we hear is the lament of a parent, mourning the loss of a young son, desperately hoping that he will somehow resurface alive and unharmed. It's wrenching, and so is the near-concluding moment when the show circles back on itself to tell us what actually happened.

Similarly haunting is the testimony from beyond the grave of a drowned woman, buttressed by accounts of the man who tried to save her. The victim is played by Amy Rutherford, who's the only actor to make much of an impression. (That's as opposed to the musicians, who all have personalities and revel in them.) Rutherford also appears, posed in a picture frame, as one of a quartet of piquantly mismatched summer lovers; and she also spends a lot of time behind a scrim, meditating on radio waves and on the fact - possibly frightening, but more likely consoling - that they never disappear.

The show in fact is less concerned with specific memories than with the persistence of memory itself. This provides a few haunting moments, but as a theme it could apply to any place, any time. What we're told about Boblo itself seems vague and arbitrary. It's as if the authors expected their own nostalgia to be automatically self-validating. It's more like self-defeating.
