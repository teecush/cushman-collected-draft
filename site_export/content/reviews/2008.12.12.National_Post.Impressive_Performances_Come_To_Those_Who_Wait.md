THEATRE REVIEW

SOMEONE WHO'LL WATCH OVER ME

Berkeley Street Theatre Upstairs, Toronto

---

Frank McGuinness's Someone Who'll Watch Over Me is partly a play about performance, so it's appropriate that its current revival should be, taken all around, the best-acted play in Toronto. Which at this moment is saying something.

Somewhere among Tom Stoppard's juvenilia there's an apprentice play that he later summed up as "Waiting for Godot in the condemned cell ... I'm sure you get the idea." The piece went professionally unperformed, but Stoppard's description of it would do very well for McGuinness's play. Samuel Beckett's Godot, the greatest technical as well as philosophical influence on all drama since the Second World War, reduces the form to something like its basic necessities. He gave us two men, in a roadside limbo and without benefit of personal biographies, filling in the days with conversation and the occasional physical exercise to give themselves, in their own imperishable phrase, "the impression we exist." Mc-Guinness, another Irishman, gives us three men whose identities are comparatively lavishly documented: an American doctor, an Irish journalist and an English teacher. And we do know where they are, though it's part of their torment that they don't know whether anybody else does. They are hostages, chained to the wall in a cellar-like room in Beirut. It may not quite be a condemned cell, but they have no way of being sure. Any one of them might be taken out and shot at any moment. Or any one of them might be released.

The play has a geometric structure; it gives us a pyramid and then inverts it. First there is one captive, then two, then three, then two again, then back to one. The games the men play are, obviously, meant to serve the practical purpose of keeping them sane; you could not perhaps say quite the same of the routines enacted by Beckett's tramps, but then again maybe you could. (And Beckett's play is more realistic than you might think; it was partly inspired by his experiences in the French Resistance.) McGuinness's prisoners write imaginary letters home, invent movie scenarios, recreate horse races and Wimbledon tennis finals. What start as competitive rituals develop into mutually supportive ones.

Initial conflict is guaranteed by the presence of the Irishman and the Englishman, dragging out ancestral hatreds that, in any real sense, they probably hardly feel. Edward, the Irishman, is a vulnerable loudmouth who sought a dangerous posting partly out of bravado, partly to escape an uncomfortable marriage; he's also a voluble atheist who refuses to read the Bible or the Koran, the only two books available, but whom we eventually find -- perhaps for want of anything better to do -- deep in one of them. David Ferry brings a high level of both energy and intelligence to the role; it's as close to being a rampageous performance as is possible in his confined position, but it's precision-controlled. Vocally he never lets up, whether he's being wistful or aggressive, but he never loses sight, or lets us lose sight, of the fear that grows within him every minute. You can track a lot of this play's progress through the actors' eyes: Ferry's blaze, those of R. H. Thomson as Michael, the Englishman, are pale and blinking. Michael, a widower, came to Lebanon as a language teacher after being laid off at home. He's prim and apparently vulnerable: a self-acknowledging sad sack, kidnapped when on his way to market to buy pears for a flan (his most prized recipe). He also seems to have gone through life fending off suspicions that he might be gay, suspicions raised again, at least momentarily, by his fellow prisoners. His lonely life (no children, wife died young) and unfashionable scholarship (he's a medievalist)may in fact have made him the best qualified to survive. Thomson's performance is often very funny, especially when he proudly summons up Virginia Wade's victory at Wimbledon, complete with sound effects: a proud virtuoso turn by a man who's learned not to cower. There's a touch of Monty Python in Thomson's assumed Englishness, and it doesn't hurt a bit. Defensiveness gradually gives place to strength, which combines with the humour and adds up to grace. This is as fine a performance as I've seen him give.

As Adam, the American who serves as part-time mediator, Ryan Hollyman is less well defined than the other two, but so is his character. He's a blank slate to whom his companions assign qualities that may or may not be there. His terrors, though, are as vivid as theirs. The director (and producer) is Tegan Shoet, a newcomer who apparently supports her theatre habit by working as a lawyer; she gets as much variety into the staging as the constricted circumstances permit, and the mental contact between the actors is superb.

The hostage-takers are kept offstage, like a collective Godot -- though one whose appearance is dreaded rather than desired. We don't even hear much about them. Here the play cheats; it may be the captors' strategy to keep the captives physically in the light (it's never switched off) and mentally in the dark (they don't even know what day it is) but they must have said something. They must be making demands of some kind, or why take hostages at all? But the characters say nothing about it, apparently keeping quiet for our benefit. We don't even know if they've been physically ill-treated; there are bruises on Ferry's legs but they're never explained or even referred to. The tensions and resolutions between the cellmates are finely dramatized, their ongoing fight with their jailers not so much: a limitation that's frustrating but that may also, paradoxically, be a strength. There is a fourth voice written into the play, that of Ella Fitzgerald, whose recording of the almost title song (the Gershwins called it Someone to Watch Over Me) is both Edward's choice of imaginary desert island disc and a key to the theme of both play and production: interdependence. - Someone Who'll Watch Over Me is at the Berkeley Street Theatre Upstairs in Toronto until tomorrow.
