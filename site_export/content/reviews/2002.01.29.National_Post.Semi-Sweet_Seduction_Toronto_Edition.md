THE GRADUATE

Canon Theatre, Toronto

The ways of titillation are strange. I considered crossing the ocean to see Kathleen Turner's momentarily nude appearance as Mrs. Robinson in the stage version of The Graduate, but I would not have crossed the street to see Jerry Hall, who succeeded her in the role in London. The presumed difference in their acting skills may have had something to do with this, but only in an indirect way; Hall, an ex-model, may have a better body than Turner, but the attraction lay in seeing a woman who wasn't best known for showing it off.

I never made the transAtlantic trip, but Turner and The Graduate have now obligingly saved me the trouble, touching down in Toronto en route for New York. I'm glad I saved my money. Turner, as rumoured, makes a brief and discreetly-lit appearance without any clothes on, and at other times looks very fetching in a slip. But her actual acting is on one note. It is quite an attractive note, with a foghorn quality good for getting off the occasional wry one- liner, but it never seems to belong to a real person.

Nevertheless, one misses her when she isn't there. She may not be giving much of a performance, but The Graduate isn't much of a play. Terry Johnson, one of Britain's best comic playwrights (Dead Funny, Hysteria), has adapted it from the Charles Webb novel and the 1968 Mike Nichols film, and he is also the director. I have never read the book, and largely forgotten the movie, but my impression is that Johnson has done a decent job of assembling the narrative into a series of short and reasonably shapely scenes. But he cannot, under either of his hats, prevent the story from running out of steam, both sexual and dramatic.

The closing sections of the first half are an especial drag, the audience applauding hopefully at the end of each episode only to discover that there is still another to sit through before they, like Mrs Robinson, can find relief in a drink.

The piece, with its West Coast setting just anticipating flower power, remains a prime example of that 1960s specialty, generational pandering. Now, however, it has two generations (maybe more) to play to: the Baby Boomers nostalgic for their own tame gestures of rebellion, and their successors who can convert the message of young- folks-good old-folks-bad to their own purposes.

Mrs. Robinson, not only alcoholic but unforgivably rich and middle-aged, seduces the virginal Benjamin Braddock, 22 years old and just out of college with all sorts of honours; and they both seem to be having a good time. Who could object? If there is any guilt, then presumably they share it. Well, Benjamin's parents are upset by his behaviour; and Mr. Robinson, understandably vexed, calls him a degenerate. But nobody expects us to take their opinions seriously; they're old. (And a clumsy farcical scene in which the three Braddocks visit some kind of guru-shrink makes damned sure we don't).

Benjamin gets off. But Mrs. R turns into a vengeful harpy, an evil force from whom the hero has to escape. She is shunted off to the sidelines, while Benjamin sets about wooing her virtuous daughter, Elaine, with whom he has fallen inexplicably -- or at least unexplainedly -- in love.

All three central characters, even though played by more-or-less movie stars, are wafer-thin, so one hardly cares what happens to any of them, except to wish they would hurry up about it. Jason Biggs doesn't have the chunky oddity Dustin Hoffman brought to Benjamin -- he is more of the conventional dreamer type, a latter-day Holden Caulfield signalling sensitivity -- but he does very well with the character's early awkwardness, which does suggest a kind of integrity, and with his later oddball determination. Alicia Silverstone squeaks and squawks with great conviction as Elaine, though I doubt if she does anything that fifty less celebrated young actresses could not have managed.

The two end up sharing breakfast cereal in bed, as some kind of ceremony of purity. This is in contrast to the behaviour of Elaine's mother, who devoted her energies to getting Benjamin into the sack and to stopping his mind from wandering when he was there. The latter activity provides the best scene in the play, as it did in the movie, though the preceding embarrassment in the hotel lobby is funny as well.

The play has, by current standards, an unusually large cast, and everyone else in it falls smartly into line, especially Victor Slezak's indignant husband and father, goaded into wielding an axe. Rob Howell's set, mainly composed of louvred doors, is witty and versatile, though somewhat chilly; it often leaves Turner, in particular, looking stiff and marooned.

Music of the period links the scenes, the selection going beyond the irritatingly wholesome Simon and Garfunkel tunes that adorned the film. Maybe the property would have worked better on stage if they had turned it into a full-scale musical. We could have had a real celebratory bash to start with ("We're throwing a party for our graduate/With Californian food that you'll be glad you ate") instead of remaining confined in Benjamin's bedroom while he avoids the revels.

I was about Benjamin's age when the film came out, and I remember my contemporaries thinking it a fine satirical thrust when a well- wisher sidled up to the hero and said "plastics." Here this famously economical career advice is delivered, more plainly and tamely, by Mr. Robinson; but I think now, as I did then, that it's the authors who come off smug and heavy-handed. We are meant to take the ironic point that the whole adult world and the people in it are plastic. But they could hardly be more plastic than the play.
