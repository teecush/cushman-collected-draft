In the Canadian Stage production of Venus in Fur, which has just opened, there is a scene in which a woman who has her sights set on a man seizes the man's cellphone to yell at his girlfriend and make her jealous. Who says our theatres don't have consistent artistic policies?

David Ives' Venus in Fur arrives here as a big American success. It is, in both writing and staging, a much slicker piece than the homegrown Flood Thereafter, and it's far better acted, but its total effect is equally confused. It's a play about the mounting of a play based on a book. Thomas Novachek, a NewYork playwright with a European accent, is casting his own adaptation of Venus in Furs, the 19th-century novel by Leopold Sacher-Masoch about a dominating woman and a submissive man, whose subject and whose author's name inspired the psychiatrist Kraft-Ebbing to coin the word "masochism." (Poetic justice: Krafft-Ebbing's own name has given rise to its fair share of puns.) Curtain rise finds Thomas lamenting that he can't find an actress intelligent and attractive enough to play his female lead; it's been suggested that Ives is here presenting Thomas as criminally sexist, but actually his demands and his disappointment seem perfectly reasonable. There enters an actress with a ripe New York accent and an unquenchably enthusiastic manner who, despite auditions being officially over for the day, prevails on Thomas to let her read for the role. The character's name is Wanda and the actress' is Vanda, a coincidence that strikes Thomas deeply. He's also impressed by her having dressed, or more accurately undressed, for the part. And once she actually starts reading, he's knocked out; as are we. Her voice and manner change completely, and it's obvious that she knows the play - and, as it turns out, the novel - inside out. She also knows Thomas outside in.

"Who" he asks, "are you?" He seems to think she might be the novel's Wanda come to life, or maybe even the Venus whom the novelist invoked. Or the Aphrodite who was the Roman goddess' Greek equivalent. Thomas starts talking about The Bacchae, the Euripidean tragedy in which women tear a male voyeur to pieces, though he rather glosses over the fact that they were acting under the influence of a male god rather than a female one. (There's another link to The Flood Thereafter: Greek myth roots.) Anyway, what Ives' Venus infers is that Thomas is a masochist waiting to happen. She has him read his play's male role, and tells him that he's great in it and should play it himself. In fact, she starts directing him. She also, it seems, sets about seducing him: hence the call to the girlfriend. The play embarks on a series of role and even gender reversals, the latter linking up with The Bacchae, whose doomed hero was in drag when he was dismembered. But they seem mechanical and self-cancelling: titillating rather than interesting. They certainly don't go as deep or as disturbing as this season's other S&M play, Pig.

The play, and Jennifer Tarver's sharp direction, work best as black backstage comedy: the actor's revenge. Rick Miller, best known for switching between multiple lightning-quick sketches in his own one-man shows, here gets to play one character in depth, and he brings it off beautifully; first obstinate, then pliable. (I can do literary references, too.) As Vanda/Wanda/Venus/Aphrodite, Carly Street blazes. At first sight and sound, she seems almost too broad a broad, but that just throws her instant transformation into sharper relief. From then on, she alternates between voices and personae, and is in perfectly-timed command throughout. Street is probably best know for her appearances in musicals (Lord of the Rings, Bloodless) but she's also done superb work in little-seen productions of Shakespeare and Marivaux; she could, if anybody cares, be one of the best classical actresses we have. Meanwhile, in her flaming red hair and black undies, she comes closer to justifying this play's erotic pretensions than the text itself ever does.

From furs to hosiery: ...And Stockings for the Ladies is the arbitrary name - the line only crops up once and in passing - given to the new production by the Harold Green Jewish Theatre Company. The author, Attila Clemann, had drawn on the letters and reminiscences of two Second World War members of the Royal Canadian Air Force: a squadron-leader, Ted Aplin, and a sergeant whom he has renamed Daniel Friedman. Both were present at the liberation of Bergen-Belsen, and the play is mainly concerned with their efforts to provide succour to the inmates, beyond and in defiance of official guidelines. For no apparent reason except economy, both men, and everyone else involved, are played by the same actor, Brendan McMurtry-Howlett. He does pretty well, but as a ventriloquial actor he's hardly a Rod Beattie, or indeed a Rick Miller. His director, Zach Fraser, doesn't help by insisting that he perform everything; a scene in a taxi in which he has to keep shuffling between seats as passenger and driver is one of the clumsiest pieces of staging I've seen in ages. Concentration camp prisoners are represented by puppeteer's dolls; one can just about see the reason, but it doesn't justify the break in convention. Besides all this, and despite the horrors it relates, the script itself isn't very interesting. Of course, Squadron-Leader Aplin missed, and was missed by, his wife and children back home, but just reading out their letters isn't enough to make us care. There's one compelling passage: a quarrel between the two men while engaged on an apparently pointless rescue mission. McMurtry-Howlett (a singularly, or doubly, un-Jewish name) does get some tension into this. But he'd do far better if he had someone else to act with and either a real play or a thoroughgoing documentary to act in.
