There are two musicals and a poem called The Wild Party. The poem, rhyming coupleted, was published in 1926; it's the work of a New Yorker writer, Joseph Moncure March, and it's a Jazz Age wallow, a fascinated deadpan view of a louche backstage world where everything was publicly Prohibited and privately indulged. By some quirk of fate or rights availability, the two musicals based on the poem both debuted in New York in 2000, one off Broadway, the other on. By some other but probably similar quirk, it's taken another 15 years for either musical to be presented in Toronto. But now, finally, and courtesy of the Acting Up Stage and Obsidian companies, we have a production of the Broadway one, the more adventurous, musically and theatrically, of the two. It's an evening that starts out terrific but gradually runs out of steam, even while remaining steamy.

"Queenie was a blond, and her age stood still /And she danced twice a day in vaudeville." So begins March's poem, and so also begins Michael John LaChiusa's musical; he wrote the lyrics and music, which are practically continuous, and collaborated with George C. Wolfe on what there is of a book. The scene-setting opening number is performed by the full company, louche and loud and scornful and aggressive.

We meet Queenie and her boyfriend Burrs, who's also in the theatrical profession; he has a blackface act. Queenie asks Burrs to make some coffee; his reply is to threaten to beat her up. She says she'd like to throw a party, and that goes down much better. All their friends attend and a few of their enemies, most of them performers of one kind or another, and all of them sexually or professionally on the make. In its use of showbiz as metaphor, and its unwaveringly acrid tone, the show does for 1920s New York what Chicago does for Chicago. It also suffers from the same encroaching weakness; it tells us so unrelentingly how rotten everyone is, that it leaves itself no place to go.

The score, unlike Chicago's, doesn't go in much for straightforward pastiche. LaChiusa has his own style: his music, in its mocking mordancy, sounds like a George Grosz painting looks. There's a matching visual style to Robert McQueen's production, designed by Michael Gianfrancesco, costumed by Alex Amini, lit by Kimberly Purtell and choreographed by Stephanie Graham. Their apparently seamless collaboration is matched by that of the performers, so electrically confident as an ensemble, that we wait eagerly for them to declare themselves as individuals. Some of them do.

Queenie, who "was sexually ambitious," is played by Cara Ricketts, a usually fine actress in what appears to be her first musical. Her singing doesn't always get across; she also seems vulnerable from the outset, rather than becoming so, which hurts the show's most important narrative arc. Her best moment is a beautiful anomaly, for her and for LaChiusa's score; Queenie, to her own surprise, has fallen in love with an equally surprised gigolo, stylishly played by Dan Chameroy, and on the classic urban-pastoral setting of a fire escape, they share a guardedly rapturous duet. This sends her official lover, into a fury of jealousy; Burrs, as played by Daren A. Herbert, burns up the stage.

He has other reasons to feels incendiary; in this production, though not in the New York original, both Queenie and Burrs are black. So her blondness becomes more piquant, and his enforced blackface - as with many real black performers of the time, including the great Bert Williams - more grotesquely explosive.

"Uptown is looking more like downtown," sing a black voice-and-piano duo, who provide the party's official entertainment. "Black folks are sounding more like white folks /who are sounding more like black folks every day." The production reinforces their point, though in the reality of the time, they would probably have been the only blacks present, hired as cafe society's pet exotics. As brothers, they're biliously jealous of one another; by J. Cameron Barnett and David Lopez hit all the right buttons, musically and dramatically. Their counterparts, played with matching pungency by Josh Epstein and Larry Mannell, are a couple of Jews, would-be producers, both of them called Gold-something-or-other and set on changing that.

There are other arresting performances by Sterling Jarvis as a boxer, another kind of pet exotic, and Stephen Patterson as a playboy, sexually and socially voracious: intellectually to, if name-dropping counts.

The women blur, whether they're arrogant lesbians ("I made Alice B. Toklas cry"), exploited ingenues or jealous leading ladies; some are vague in outline, others short on detail. Susan Gilmour, as a desperate aging star, comes off best, Queenie's amorous rival is the weakest. Here, the cast's weaknesses conspire with those of the material. None of the characters are what you would call likable, but the men are at least interesting; still, when the show belatedly moves from genrepainting to narrative, it's hard to care, especially when the story turns to mayhem. (Chicago, never my favourite show, nevertheless knew better than to even try to move us.)

Involvement is further hampered by too many of the lyrics being indistinguishable, not because they're too soft but because they're too loud. Apart from this matter of balance, Bob Foster's musical direction is scintillating. There is a huge amount of talent at work here, but you have to sort through it.

The Wild Party runs through March 8 at the Berkeley Street Theatre.
