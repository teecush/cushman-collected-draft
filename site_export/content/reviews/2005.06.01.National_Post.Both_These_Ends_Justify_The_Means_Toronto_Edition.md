Journey's End is in repertory at the Court House Theatre, Niagara- on-the-Lake, until October 8; Happy End at the Royal George Theatre until October 28. 1-800-511-SHAW

NIAGARA-ON-THE-LAKE - The Shaw Festival production of Journey's End is, to use the appropriate vernacular, a jolly good show. This famous play about the trenches of the First World War (written 10 years after the event) was the first English theatrical word on the subject, and -- at least until Oh What a Lovely War came along, to do for the enlisted men what Journey's End did for their superiors - - the last one.

R.C. Sherriff, whose first play it was, had an astonishing success with it that he never managed to duplicate. He went on writing into the 1960s, usually plays on ostensibly serious subjects that emerged as irredeemably cozy, with all problems easily solved. Journey's End is cozy too, in a way. It focuses almost exclusively on the officer class, all of them apparently dedicated to preserving the social and moral codes they learned at public school in new and appalling circumstances. But the play transcends its apparent limitations. Sherriff, writing from personal experience, shows how desperately these men, mostly very young but a couple in early middle age, need the memory of home comforts and home beliefs. Sometimes it seems that that's what they're fighting for.

We are in a dug-out in France in 1918, the British troops awaiting the last major German offensive. Captain Stanhope, the company commander, barely in his twenties, is a good and popular officer who can only keep himself and his men together by drinking. Osborne, his second-in-command and indispensable confidant, is a placid ex-schoolmaster who reads Alice in Wonderland. Their comrades include Trotter, jovial and unimaginative, who came up through the ranks; Hibbert, who has cracked and wants out by any means available; and Raleigh, the youngest and newest recruit, who knew Stanhope at school and hero-worships him. He also happens to be the brother of Stanhope's fiancee, and Stanhope, already prey to self- torment, lives in fear that the boy's letters home will expose him as the alcoholic wreck he feels he has become. War, from these men's perspective, lives up to its reputation of being 90% boredom and 10% terror. They live for the next revolting meal and the next essential cup of tea. What lies ahead is the near-certainty of injury and death, a prospect brought even closer when orders come for a reconnoitering party to get information by taking a prisoner. Osborne and Raleigh are chosen to lead it.

Christopher Newton's production, relaxed and amused in its first half, gets very tense in its second. It does take a few surprising short-cuts. The Colonel (Anthony Bekenn) is far too reminscent of Stephen Fry's C.O. in the War According to Blackadder, jovially unperturbed about sending his subordinates into harm's way. Evan Buliung's Stanhope carries the play, as he should, but he does so by hitting the same high-strung note with hardly any variation; still, when he brings out the good in the terrified Hibbert by appealing to him to do the decent thing and stay with the other chaps, he gets well away with the trickiest scene in the play. Conversely, Patrick Galligan's Osborne could afford to let up on the sweet reasonableness just occasionally, but his is still an affecting performance; when, left alone and sick with fear, he lets down his guard for a silent moment, and it's a very moving one. Jeff Meadows' Hibbert is good, William Vickers' Trotter even better and Jeff Lillico's Raleigh ideal, progressing from juvenile enthusiasm by way of a shocked forced maturity to a final uncomprehending agony. The trench cook, Private Mason, is a role that Simon Bradbury could probably play in his sleep, but he is as wide awake as the character himself. When he too goes up the steps to battle, a whole wider world of combatants comes into focus. We never do find out exactly which, if any, of the characters gets out alive. It's the most honest ending possible.

Tadeusz Bradecki's revived production of the Bertolt Brecht-Kurt Weill musical Happy End looks as good now as it did two years ago, given a stylish visual bite by its designer Peter Hartwell, and with every move precisely plotted. It's the piece itself that continues to disappoint; the songs are some of Weill's best, but though they help set the mood, they have almost nothing to do with the action. This, tossed off by Brecht and his collaborator Elisabeth Hauptmann and then ascribed to a mythical "Dorothy Lane," is slight to begin with: an encounter between some fairly inefficient gangsters and a deatchment of the Salvation Army in a mythical Chicago. The confessedly free adaptation by Michael Feingold makes it seem even more drawn-out, and is as laborious as the electronic contraption through which the gang's mastermind, The Fly, sends them their orders. Feingold's lyrics aren't that great either, though they're now the approved English version. The piece ends, in a thin echo of The Threepenny Opera, with the two sides uniting to battle the common enemy, Big Business. But the mob, even in the 1920s, was big business. (I'm not sure about the Salvation Army.) Brecht seems, by the way, not to have heard of Prohibition. His hoods are small-time shakedown artists.

Cast changes are up and down. Ben Carlson, standing in for Benedict Campbell until mid-June, hasn't the misanthropic weight for public enemy Bill Cracker. His singing voice, so pleasing in She Loves Me, here sounds hoarse, which might seem right but isn't. Something about his presence leads us to expect a serious love- plot, which doesn't materialize. This might be intentional alienation but I don't think so. Glynis Ranney, though, far supasses her predecessor in the role of army sweetheart Hallelujah Lil, especially in the way she builds the great torch song Surabaya Johnny from innocence to ashes. She's sweet voiced, but believably tough. It probably isn't Jay Turvey's fault that I couldn't wait to see his sinister Japanese mastermind get whacked and was severely disappointed when the whacking proved temporary. Among the other original-cast survivors, I especially liked Jeff Madden's Baby Face and Donna Belleville's Major Stone. She, incidentally, is the first of two Salvationists of her rank to appear at the Shaw this season; Major Barbara follows shortly. Times being tough all over, as both Brecht and Shaw keep reminding us, I wonder if the two shows can share the uniforms.
