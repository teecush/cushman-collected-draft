OLIVER!

Princess of Wales Theatre, Toronto

The great thing about Oliver! is that it doesn't play like an adaptation. Unlike most stage versions of classic books, musical or otherwise, it has its own identity, its own shape and its own jaunty tempo. Things do get rushed toward the end when hundreds of pages of novel are crammed into about 20 minutes of playing time. But even here the choices made are both clear and clever. And though Lionel Bart described this show -- for which he wrote book, music and lyrics -- as being "freely adapted from Oliver Twist, he was in many ways remarkably true to Dickens.

Oliver Twist, like many great books, has always led a double life. Its a horrific expose of crime, poverty and institutional cruelty in Victorian England, especially in relation to children. Its also a terrific adventure story, a kids' classic, and I suspect that if you asked most people to summarize their memories of reading it, they would come up with something very similar to the musical.

They would remember the hungry boy in the workhouse asking for more, and his subsequent flight to the excitement of the big city. They might remember that he turns out to be somehow related to the toffs who eventually rescue him, but would forget the complicated details; in this version he's revealed to be his benefactors grandson and thats that.

They would recall Fagin as a lovable thief, a funny if shady old man who gives Oliver shelter; they would filter out his involvement in Bill Sikes' murder of Nancy and his own eventual execution. They would not filter out the murder itself, a brutal battering that is one of the huge melodramatic climaxes of 19th-century fiction.

Whether by instinct or cunning -- I suspect it was mainly the former -- Bart made all these choices, arranged them logically, and made the whole thing move to music. His songs vary in quality but they all advance the action, present a character or glorify a moment.

As an unschooled musical dramatist -- a pop-meister who conquered the theatre -- Bart was something of a British Irving Berlin, if with nothing like the same talent, taste or discipline. Each of them spoke showbiz like a native, though by 1960, when Oliver! premiered, it was a more debased lingo than it had been when Berlin started up. Bart could articulate popular sentiment because he shared it; like Berlin, and indeed like Dickens. When Bart has his Oliver Twist pipe up where-ere-ere-ere is love/does it fall from skies above?" he is being more authentically Dickensian than we might care to admit. (Dickens' Oliver doesnt talk like that but he has a friend who does, a saintly orphan named Little Dick, foredoomed to an early grave.)

Sam Mendes production, which started out in London in 1994, has been widely hailed for putting some of the original Dickensian darkness back into the show. That's a matter of opinion. There is a new silent prologue, showing Oliver's mother staggering into the workhouse in the snow to give birth.

There is also a new scene for Fagin and Bill Sikes -- new to the show; the dialogue is in the novel -- well ahead of Bills originally scheduled entrance at the top of the second act: Fagin chatters nervously while Sikes remains hulkingly, terrifyingly silent, and the relationship between them is immensely strengthened. (Bart, who died this year, worked on the revisions for this production.)

But there was always terror nibbling at the edges of this show, and in some ways this revival is more of a jolly English pantomime than its predecessor. Matthew Bournes staging of the Consider Yourself number is awash in knees-up-and-see-the-sights-of-London choreography, and it all comes round again in the double-time reprise of Who Will Buy, effectively killing what used to be the most pulsating moment in the score.

The city itself is spectacularly rendered in Anthony Ward's sets. They don't unify the show as effectively as Sean Kennys legendary revolving original, which literally and figuratively swept all before it, but they have their own wonders; two separate bridges rise and fall, characters cavort in front of daintily-rendered Nash terraces (probably inspired by the film), and St. Pauls Cathedral makes an imposing appearance at the back. Fagin's den, somewhere beneath the sewer-line, is especially impressive.

This production's first Fagin was Jonathan Pryce, a classically trained actor having fun in a musical. We get the opposite extreme in Russ Abbot, a television comedian going straight -- which is what Fagin himself does at the end of the show, in Barts biggest but most logical departure from his source.

Abbot, a towering figure in the traditional hat and tatters, is actually at his best when trying least hard to be funny. His comic routines are pasted on but his moments of fear -- of Sikes, old age or the rope -- are very powerful. His voice, though, doesnt have the music-hall strength for which his songs cry out, especially the Reviewing the Situation soliloquy ("So at my time of life I should start turning over new leaves?) that is Bart's comic peak.

Sonia Swaby's Nancy, busty and red-dressed, is a production-line tart-with-a-heart; she, too, seemed to be having voice problems at the preview I saw, skirting the big notes of her big numbers, which include some of the best songs in the show. She did, though, gather all her forces for As Long as He Needs Me and was rewarded with cheers; its a shameless showstopper and a truly terrible song.

Everybody else is competent without being transfixing, and the orchestral playing is fine. The kids are all locally recruited, with Mitchell Moffitt appealing in the title role; and since it is the title role, I wonder why he isnt accorded a better showing, either in the billing or at the curtain call. (Are we still exploiting our children?)

Musical fanatics might note that one song has been cut, though its orchestral reprise mysteriously remains, and that Bart added some new lyrics to others. He was never what you'd call fastidious about rhymes, and in one of these late additions he couples utterly" with "gluttony." So at his time of life he should start turning over new leaves?
