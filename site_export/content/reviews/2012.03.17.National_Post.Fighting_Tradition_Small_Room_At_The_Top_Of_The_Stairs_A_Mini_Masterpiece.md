Ihaven't been a great fan of Carole Frechettes plays; theyve seemed to me like tiny surfaces, obsessively polished. Her new one (new to Toronto, anyway; it premiered in Montreal in 2008) is small too, at least physically - its very title is The Small Room at the Top of the Stairs. Its reverberations, though, are immense: immense and unforced. It's a miniature masterpiece, and it comes to us in an astonishingly good production.

It's a contemporary variant of the Bluebeard legend, which may account for it possessing a narrative strength beyond any of Frechettes previous plays. Grace, our heroine, is a beautiful young woman who has married into the extreme 1%. Her husband Henry is an investment banker who tells her, I dont know how to create beauty, but I recognize it when I see it. He sees it in her and in the new 28-room house ("designed by the world's best architect, furnished by the world's best decorator") in which, following a whirlwind courtship, he has installed her. Henry has, of course, been married before: three times, though he doesnt care to talk about them. Also, of course, there is just one room among the 28, the attic of the plays title, that he forbids Grace to enter. And again, of course, Grace can't resist looking into it.

We find her, as the play begins, standing on the stairs outside the prohibited door, with her doubts and fears, and other people's voices, ringing in her ears. One voice is that of Henry, extravagantly protesting his love for her. Another is that of Joyce, her mother, gamely making do in her own mere five and a half rooms, overjoyed that her daughter has found wealth and happiness, especially wealth, and living for their weekly shopping trips together. And a third belongs to Anne, Graces older sister, a dedicated aid worker, who detests (her word) Graces values and her new lifestyle and the way she has always been her parents' favourite, but who loves her, in a rather patronizing way, nonetheless.

Henry, returning early from one of his many business trips, is furious - frighteningly so - to find that his wife has disobeyed his one command and entered the room. What she has found there is not to be revealed in a review, though it's fair to say that its not exactly what a reader of the original fairy tale might expect. As to what it means: There are various interpretations of that (there was vigorous debate in the lobby on the night I went) but clearly what Henry has kept locked in there represents everything he himself has had to lock out, everything he has had to banish from his mind and his existence in order to live the life he does. And everything that Grace has to decide whether to deny or admit. The play is a parable but an open-ended one.

Weyni Mengesha has given the play a hypnotic L-shaped staging, the audience on two sides at right angles to one another. (This must have been done before, but not often.) Astrid Jansons set is a shiny black floor. There is no practical staircase; the all-important steps are suggested by lights and white lines, enough to make Grace's succession of approaches and retreats into occasions of heart-stopping suspense. Thomas Ryder Payne's sound design takes a further toll on the nerves. Bonnie Beechers lighting is extraordinary; it creates a whole geography for the house to which the production meticulously adheres. It also creates a separate pinpointed world for each of the characters, whether theyre in the heroine's head, in the next room or - in Anne's case - on the other side of the world.

There is a jewelled precision to Frechettes writing here, certainly as transmitted through John Murrells translation. There's also considerable psychological complexity; the characters may be representative figures, often seen at one remove, but they spring to life in relationships that are subtly imagined and deftly drawn. The performances are very fine, collectively and individually. Nicole Underhay's amazing Grace starts as Goldilocks crossed with Cinderella. Spoiled from the cradle, even her growing compassion has a sense of entitlement about it. That doesnt make it any less moving; you feel for her and fear for her. Rick Roberts Henry is a dream suitor with a terrifying crack inside, all charm and solicitude, Champagne and roses, he can also unleash a sudden and ferocious anger that seems fundamentally directed at himself.

Sarah Dodd's Joyce gets a laugh, mainly at her own snobbery, almost every time she opens her mouth; hers must be the wittiest performance in Toronto. It's also a very human and surprisingly sympathetic one, of a mother fighting for what she perceives as her childs best interests that just happily coincide with her own. Claire Calnans Anne, by contrast, takes positions that the play intellectually endorses and emotionally doubts; with her meetings and her causes, she can seem as narrow as her mother and rather more selfish.

The people to whose desperate real-world problems she caustically compares Grace's privileged ones are represented onstage by Raquel Duffy as Jenny the maid, an immigrant of unspecified nationality who seems to have designs, if not on the master himself, then on his mansion, which she seems to be running singlehanded. She functions as a litmus test for everyone else onstage: Henry depends on her; Joyce bullies her; Anne never meets her but would probably regard her as a statistic; and Grace first condescends to her, then desperately tries to ingratiate herself with her, and condescends even more in doing so. She fits in with the play's themes, but not quite with its style; unlike the others, she seems to be standing for something rather than embodying it. The strength of the other characters, and of the whole play, is that they feel themselves to be figures in a traditional story and keep fighting it. We have the brilliant clarity of a fairy tale and the murky depths beneath it. There have been some great things in the Toronto theatre recently, but this is the best, the very best.
