To bring Monty Python and the Holy Grail to the stage, Eric Idle had to convince his colleagues, raid the back catalogue and reinvent the musical

Eric Idle says, when asked, that he is not an ex-Python. "I always deny that. I say Graham's an ex-Python." That would be Graham Chapman, the one who died: an ex-Python in the same way that the Norwegian Blue is an ex-parrot. Im more of a former Python. You cant escape it till you're dead. There's no end.

He warms to his theme. "Are you an ex-Beatle? No, youre a Beatle." Well, if youre Paul or Ringo. That's your game. You're a Liverpool player. Youre reminded of it every day. People yell at you in the street.

And what, I asked him over a King Street lunch in Toronto, do they yell at you?

"Hello, Mike, I love your travel books." That would be Michael Palin, whose most visible pursuits post-Python have been a notable series of TV globetrotting shows that afterwards went into volume form. He and I are always mixed up because we play the same kinds of roles. Well, they were the two Pythons with the greatest air of scrubbed innocence. In a raddled sort of way, they still are. "And they say, You're John Cleese's little friend, arent you? They yell all sorts of things. Like, 'Hello, Monty.' "

Cleese, as actor and writer, went from the anarchy of Python to the traditional farce of Fawlty Towers and A Fish Called Wanda, diversifying along the way into pop psychiatry and health education. He seems to have become the closest thing to a reluctant Python; he wasnt in the last TV series. The two Terries, Jones and Gilliam, have done direction. Of them all, Idles career has been the show- busiest, culminating -- at least to date -- in his authorship of the hit Broadway musical Spamalot. Which is what brought him to Toronto, where the touring production opens officially on Sunday.

Spamalot is based on Monty Python and the Holy Grail, which Idle says has always been the most popular of the team's movies. What, not Life of Brian? Apparently not: People love the Judean Liberation Front scenes -- as well they should -- but maybe a picture that ends with a mass crucifixion was never going to be a popular favourite. Grail has now been a hit with several generations. "Everyone knows the story, has seen the movie. They see it in college. It feeds into the whole Dungeons and Dragons thing."

In fact it was while he was working on a CD-ROM thing of Grail in the States that it occurred to Idle: "If you can adapt it as a game, it would make a very good musical." It was clear that a Python reunion tour -- a perennial pipe dream of Idle's though not necessarily of all the others -- was never going to happen. A musical seemed a promising idea to pursue instead. Then came the sobering thought: Ill have to deal with all the Pythons. When he did contact the other four, they gave their consent, though it seemed obvious that "they didnt think it would work. They were concerned to distance themselves; I'd be exposed as a charlatan, trashing their legend." Now that the show is on, and a huge success, they love it.

Idle himself wrote the show's book and lyrics, and collaborated on the music with John Du Prez. He is, in truth, the only one of the Pythons one can easily imagine doing a musical. He was responsible for most of the songs that cropped up in the TV shows.

But as far as turning Holy Grail into a musical: Well, it sort of starts with Gilbert and Sullivan. Jonathan Miller came to me in 1986 and said, Would you like to be in The Mikado, at the English National Opera? I said, 'What are you going to do with it?' He said, Well, Im going to get rid of all that Japanese nonsense for a start.' So I said, 'Count me in, because a Japanese-free Mikado has got to be something worth seeing. He played Ko-Ko, the Lord High Executioner, and "I just had the best time, because theres a tradition that you can rewrite the Little List Song, and I'd rewrite it every night. And I thought, well this is it, musical comedy is a form I really enjoy, something that's really appropriate for me. So Ive been trying to reinvent musical comedy ever since then."

When he did it, he caught a wave. The Producers had revived the fashion for funny musicals, something that had lain largely dormant during the long years of the British Ascendancy. In Spamalot, Idle and Du Prez seem to be lampooning their more solemn compatriots with The Song That Goes Like This, a number that everyone takes to be a parody of an Andrew Lloyd Webber ballad, though Idle swears it isnt. He has also raided his own back catalogue for Always Look on the Bright Side, the Life of Brian finale number that has fitted into any number of celebratory scenarios from mass crucifixions on.

To create the stage show, all the roles Idle had played in the film were combined into his major one of Sir-Robin-the-not-quite-as- brave-as-Sir Lancelot; similarly, all of Michael Palin's original roles have been collapsed into that of Sir Galahad.

In the musical, King Arthur is definitely the star part. Tim Curry originated it on Broadway; Simon Russell Beale, the British classical actor, took over from him, and will be playing it again later this year in London; Michael Siberry, another British classical actor, plays it in Toronto.

Simon plays him like a man who doesn't really believe he has a horse, Idle says. "Siberry plays him with dignity." Theres one other obvious casting choice thats yet to be realized. At some time I'd like to have a shot at playing Arthur myself. I've been tempted to do it for a few weeks in London. Only for a few weeks, since he and his wife and children live in California. Cleese lives in California, too, but one gathers they dont do much socializing; it is, after all, a very big state.

American resident or not, Idle can still talk and feel like a Broadway-struck Englishman. "Its a 30-year period since we did the movie. And now we're doing this show on 42nd Street. It's very moving."
