The question posed by Goodness is what is the proper response to a genocide. Is it impersonal justice or personal vengeance? Most people, looking at it from the outside, would opt for the former, and Michael Redhill's play really does nothing to alter that conclusion. But he does let us see, and more importantly feel, that for a survivor the issue may not be that simple. Or that it might be all too simple, especially when the perpetrator at hand may be about to escape.

The play gets to this point by a circuitous route. Its central character is its author, or at least a writer with its author's name. "I'm being played tonight by Gord Rand", he tells us, "you'll be in good hands with Gord." I bet he says that to all the actors, or has all the actors say it for him. Jordan Pettle said it, when Volcano Theatre first did the play in 2005. It was true then, and it's even truer in Volcano's current revival, which follows on a triumphal progress through Edinburgh and New York. In Rand's hands we have a portrait of a physically and psychologically dishevelled man, trying to reconcile his beliefs with his emotions, and facing up to events he has read and thought about but with whose human consequences he has never before been brought face to face.

He knows about vengeful feelings; he has them himself, towards his wife and the best friend for whom she left him. This hardly seems an issue on the same scale as mass murder, and I can't believe that the real Redhill thinks it is, whatever may have happened in his own marriage; but the stage Michael does keep bringing it up. Its immediate effect was to send him to Poland, where his Jewish great-grandparents and their family were slaughtered by the Einsatzgruppen. He finds that the present-day Poles aren't interested; to them, it's just history. Stopping off in London on his way home, he meets a strange man who directs him to a strange woman. Her name is Althea, and there was a more recent genocide in her country; her family was among the victims. Later, one of the principal instigators was put on trial, and she was the prison guard. As she tells the story, her younger self acts it out, along with the accused, his daughter, and the prosecutor. And our author-surrogate gets more appalled and more confused.

The prisoner, Matthias Todd, has evaded prosecution on the main issue; he's being tried instead for a single killing, which he represents as a crime of passion, prompting a certain shamefaced empathy from Michael. That's insofar as Todd professes anything at all, since he claims to be suffering from Alzheimer's, or his daughter passionately claims it for him. The prosecutor is just as passionately determined to break these defences down. Michael's liberal principles are additionally battered when he's asked how he'd deal with his own Nazi spectre if it rose up before him; which, in a powerful hypothetical scene, it does.

The site of the crimes for which Todd stands trial is left unspecified; it might have been African, it might have been European. Cross-cultural music (live and superb) and cross-cultural casting keep the issue in doubt. This vagueness bothers me less now than it did four years ago, except when characters start batting around the numbers killed; if they can be bothered about specific numbers, then why not about specific places? There have been some textual cuts, but most of what's best about the writing then was there before; as when Michael asks of the atrocities in Althea's country "were there camps?" and she replies "no; they spared themselves that expense." Ross Manson's direction, though, is much sharpened, partly by being presented on a three-sided stage, with the actors rising like witnesses from the front rows of the house. The climactic sequence, in which all the personal and political issues collide and explode, has grown enormously in tension. As well as Rand, there are vivid performances from Layne Coleman as the artfully pitiful Todd and from J.D. Nicholsen as his implacable inquisitor. The women do less well: as old Althea, Lili Francks plays with a self-righteous stridency that diminishes what should be the most searing speech in the play; as young Althea, Tara Hughes can be strong but is too often merely sullen; and Amy Rutherford as the daughter supplies little except a look of haunting heartbreak at the final tableau. And though the play often transcends its own cleverness, it can't finally surmount it. The heart of darkness remains stubbornly, and perhaps inevitably, unreachable. However, they're taking it next to Rwanda (disgracefully, without proper Canadian support) and there, if anywhere, will be an audience qualified to judge.
