Video Cabaret shows usually feature around a half-a-dozen actors but look -- with their short scenes and quick-change black-box stagings -- as if they have casts of thousands. Of course, these days half a dozen might as well be a cast of thousands. The latest re-staged instalment in Michael Hollinsgworth's tragic-comic-strip History of Canada is The Red River Rebellion (Cameron House, to March 20) with Louis Riel, Wifred Laurier and John A. Macdonald among those caught in VidCab's invariably unflattering light. Other recent Toronto arrivals are small-scale and make no bones about it. Cast Iron (Tarragon Extra Space, to March 13), written by Lisa Codrington and performed by Alison Sealy--Smith, presents the memories of a woman from Barbados dying in a nursing-home in Winnipeg. It's a joint production by Nightwood (women's theatre) and Obsidian (black theatre). Question: does this make it twice as specialized, or only half? There are two speaking actors (and a third who's almost mute) in "Pinter & Pirandello" (Artword, to March 6), which is not an imaginary confrontation but a double-bill in which each half concerns love, jealousy and fantasy. Pinter's The Lover is the better-known piece, originally as a TV play (it still bears the traces); Pirandello's I'm Dreaming, But Am I? will be new to all but experts, though its title would, of course, fit nearly everything its author ever wrote.
