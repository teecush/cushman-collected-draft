THE HUMAN VOICE / THE ELEPHANT SONG

Studio Theatre, Stratford, Ont.

- - -

A character is on the phone, and we can hear her but not the person to whom she is talking. Drama offers a writer no challenge like this, or so experience suggests. In 99 plays out of 100, it's met with a monologue that sounds like:

"No, I don't love you any more ... Yes, I have burned all your letters ... Yes, I can tell that you're angry ... Oh, I see, you're coming straight here, by taxi, with the pearl-handled revolver that you keep in the top right-hand corner of your desk, underneath the box of typewriter ribbons that you never got round to throwing out after you bought the computer, and you're going to shoot me because you've decided that I'm a heartless bitch, and you should be here in half an hour, traffic permitting ... Yes, I'll put out the cat ..."

The blessed 100th play is Jean Cocteau's The Human Voice, which premiered in 1930 and is entirely made up of one of those one-sided phone chats. A woman is talking to her ex-lover, who has dumped her quite recently. One of the play's signal virtues is that the man never seems, on a technical level, to be putting words into her mouth.

Emotionally, he is; she is the one who is still in love, which means she is, to a great extent, at his mercy. So she tries to accommodate him. We can make a very good guess at what he is saying at the other end, but neither she nor the author stoops to quoting him directly. Character and dramatist retain their dignity. She is no puppet, and he no puppeteer.

There is no indication her current malleable state is her normal one, or that she might not snap out of it at some future time. We get vivid glimpses of a tougher woman in the peremptory way in which she addresses the other callers who intrude on her party line, and who sometimes cause her to lose her connection. (And we thought our phone system was a pain.) These interruptions are expertly used to vary the texture and even to inject a little humour.

From what she says, and from the look of her rumpled but elegant bedroom, not to mention her silk pajama suit, we judge her to be a sophisticated lady, probably with a career, who has put her glossier self on hold.

Her ex is apparently a lawyer. She is terribly understanding as she tries to keep him on the line while avoiding sounding jealous or dependent -- two incompatible objectives. We can work out not only what the man is saying, but also what he's probably thinking. One guesses that a part of him is concerned while a larger part wishes she would stop clinging, and we can sympathize, though more for her.

We get to see her clearly from inside and out. By the end of the conversation she has the telephone cord wound round her throat, which of course suggests suicide but also emotional envelopment. "I'm wearing you", she tells him, with emphatic playfulness. She's also wearing the play.

Lally Cadeau plays her as a woman whose flesh is ruling her head, and whose anxiety is trying desperately to pass itself off as cheerfulness. This is a seamless, generous performance that never hits a false note. It shows us both what the character is and what she was.

Jim Warren's direction and Sue LePage's design are perfectly key. The new English version is by John Murell, possibly our best playwright and certainly our best translator. I had thought of this play, on the rare occasions I thought of it at all, as a mere exercise for its author and actress. I was wrong.

Its companion piece, Nicolas Billon's The Elephant Song, does come across as an exercise, though a talented one. Its author is 25 and this, his first play, lacks a defined centre. It eventually emerges as a play about mother love, or the lack of it. A young man was denied affection by his mother, an opera singer more concerned with the affection of her public. She gave her son a stuffed elephant called Anthony, but that is no compensation. When we meet the boy he is in a mental hospital, still holding fast to Anthony.

The most powerful moment in the play -- though one that completely breaks with its otherwise naturalistic conventions -- comes when the mother (Barbara Dunn-Prosser) appears from out of nowhere, singing, beautifully and unaccompanied, a Puccini aria. But to get to this point we have to go through a prolonged cat-and- mouse game between the boy and the head of the mental hospital, who is not the psychiatrist who's actually been treating him. That doctor has disappeared, and the action begins as an investigation into his absence. In the course of this the patient -- who as he says himself is crazy but not stupid -- gets to tease and taunt the physician, even to twit him a bit on the state of his marriage.

We have met this kind of institutional duelling in plays before: in Equus, in Blue/Orange and even -- to stretch a few points -- in David Mamet's Oleanna, while the effects of living with a demanding diva were explored, recently and at some length, in Michel Tremblay's Impromptu on Nuns' Island.

I am not suggesting plagiarism or even influence, merely that the playwright is bound to have a tough job making this territory seem new. In this context, some of his accomplishments work against him. He seems too clever, too good at dialogue and building up tensions, to get by on youthful freshness. The protagonist's delight at setting false trails seems to have infected the author. By the time we get to what's really on their minds, it's too late for us to care.

The play has the same direction and design team as its predecessor, here doing more conventional work. Mac Fyfe as the troubled young troublemaker is an arresting addition to the theatrical gallery of the beautiful and the doomed. Stephen Ouimette, who plays pouch-eyed professionals as well as anybody, goes conscientiously through the required paces as the head head- shrinker but cannot bring him to independent life, perhaps because the character, for all the time he spends on stage and all the lines he gets to speak, has been endowed with very few qualities.

Maria Vacratsis seems uncomfortable with the remaining role, that of a nurse whom the patient mocks unmercifully -- though usually behind her back -- for being overweight, and who finally emerges as a substitute mother. Or maybe it would be more accurate to say that he emerges as a substitute son. Either way, it needs more investigation than it gets. Played as a surprise climax, it just seems glib.

The play inspires the mixture of future hope and present disappointment for which the standard word is "promising."
