HAUNTED MASTERS

Spadina Museum,

Toronto

- - -

As a treat (I wouldn't call it a trick) for the Halloween season, a new company billed as The Troubled Souls Co-op has taken over the top floor of Spadina House, next door to Casa Loma, and mounted a quadruple bill of phantasmal playlets. The title Haunted Masters derives from the fact that each segment is based on a short story by a celebrated writer. This is a touring production, in the sense that the audience goes walkabout among different rooms, depending on which chamber is deemed most conducive to scaring the life out of us with the material at hand.

The plundered scribes are, in order of appearance, Henry James, D.H. Lawrence, Edith Wharton and Charles Dickens. Not having read any of the tales in question, I cannot judge how faithful the dramatist, Gene Franklin Smith, has been to his originals. From the fact that he has set the Dickens episode in 1952, some 80 years after its authors death, I deduce that he is not above taking certain liberties. At any rate, each of these pieces stands dramatically on its own feet. None of them plays like an adaptation. Each moves fluently from scene to scene within its confined space.

The James is the subtlest, and also the most fully realized. In its authors peculiarly uptight Anglophile New York, a man tactlessly asks his former betrothed to act as his go-between with another woman. Both incipient partners have a fascination with death, which the spurned one balefully exploits. Somehow, the intended couple never get to meet; every date is broken, and the relationship dies of attrition, as do both its constituent parts.

Lawrence's The Border Line is a more literal ghost story, at least as done here. A young man named Alan marches off to war (the First World one) and is killed. Katherine, his enthusiastically pawed beloved, takes up with Alan's old chum Philip, whom the late hero has always despised as being "on the wrong side of the border." Katherine is German, and after the war she drags him off to the fatherland where she, her sister and Alans ghost treat him with the charming contempt reserved in Lawrences fiction for men who don't measure up to their creator's exacting standards of ramrod virility.

Il Canile is a Wharton story, re-titled and also (Ive looked it up) re-located. It now takes place in a Renaissance Italian world in which noblemen entrap unfaithful wives and their suspected lovers and have them stabbed, garrotted or cast into dungeons. That seems to be the game of the husband in this play, but hes foiled by his wife and her dangerous pet dogs. Not that that lets her off any hooks; She finds herself on trial for witchcraft and murder, with the hounds cited as her familiars. The transposed setting has inspired some heavy-handed stylization, with everyone in masks except the heroine; even she spends much of the time heavily veiled.

The last play is the most cheerfully ghoulish. The Bride's Chamber (Dickens called it The Ghost Chamber) finds a young British couple with an exorcism to arrange spending a night in a room that 100 years earlier was the site of dreadful deeds. A Victorian miser who had arranged his wife's demise appears to plead for absolution. His auditors seem unwilling to indulge him. They presumably are Smiths invention, tossed together with a ghostly host (or ghostly guest -- Im not sure who has the property rights here) who is authentic Dickens.

Patrick Conner's direction is weak on period style but strong on murky atmospherics; the final piece is positively Grand Guignol. It suffers from having no role for Soo Garay, who is the most striking of the four performers, whether as Jamesian vampire or Lawrentian vamp; the Wharton, in which she has to appear faceless, bent and croaking as the entire Venetian judicial system rolled into one, defeats her.

Jaclyn Francis, pallid in the first exhibit and understandably oppressed in the third, is lively in the other two, especially when acting vindictive. Stephen Sparks, absent in Part One and neutral in Two and Three, gets an enjoyable outing as the guilty spectre in the last. Craig Stanghetta seems overtaxed by the demands made on him, and especially by the cockney accent that he seems to think is the voice of 20th-century England.

The author presides over the evening, ushering us upstairs into the performing space and jauntily informing us that the house itself is haunted. I saw no sign of it myself but I emerged from the evening pleasantly spooked.
