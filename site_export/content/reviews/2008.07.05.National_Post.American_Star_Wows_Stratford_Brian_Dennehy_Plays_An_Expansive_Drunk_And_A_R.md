At the Stratford Studio we get Brian Dennehy -- a star in the American theatre but a previously unknown quantity in ours -- in a double-bill of one-act classics. In the first, he plays a compulsive talker; in the second, a compulsive listener whose particular fix is the sound of his own recorded voice from the days when he too was a compulsive talker.

Eugene O'Neills Hughie, the first play, is a miniature hung round the neck of the authors marathon masterpiece The Iceman Cometh. That play is about a collection of lushes in a New York barroom, barricading themselves against the horrors of reality with booze and what O'Neill refers to -- with compulsive repetition -- as "pipe dreams." Hughie zeroes in on a guy who might be one of them: Erie Smith, small-time gambler and big-time drunk, returning after an extended bender to the cheap Manhattan hotel he calls home. What sent him off was the death of a Mr. Hughes, a. k. a. Hughie, the hotel night clerk and the regular recipient of Erie's nocturnal boasts and confessions. Without this sounding board, Erie feels desolate. But hope beckons. The desk is now manned by a new clerk, a wizened fellow whose name is also Hughes. Could he be the new Hughie? Erie tries his best to make him so, going on about his own past triumphs at the tables and with the ladies, and about how Hughie, trapped in wedlock and terminally timid, lived vicariously by hearing about them. In fact, Erie is reminiscing about his own reminiscences.

Dennehy, swaying expansively, captures Eries tarnished swagger, his frantic attempts to keep the daylight out and his possibly new acknowledgment that it may just be time to let it in. But his thunder is somewhat stolen by Joe Grifasis clerk, which perhaps is as it should be since his, when you come to think of it, is the title role, if only by right of inheritance. Grifasi, whose face is a medley of lines and pouches and whose manner makes unobtrusive seem like a bumptious overstatement, might at first sight be part of Patrick Clark's faultlessly persuasive set, but he has an identity of his own, and it's an ambiguous one. He could be humouring Erie, seeing right through him; he could be impressed. He might even -- to me it was the dominant impression -- be identifying with his predecessor; ruefully, silently acknowledging that his own predicament and personality, not to mention his marriage, are very similar to those of the late Hughie -- even while Erie addresses him as a man of the world whos above all that.

The clerk, because of his secrecy, is really a more interesting character than Erie, whose blowhard tactics are transparent from, as he himself might say, the get-go. What saves them is the salt and humour with which he expresses them; as Robert Falls, the plays director, says in a program note, O'Neill isn't usually credited with being funny but here, regurgitating the Broadway slang of his seriously misspent youth, he is. Certainly Dennehy, spouting the lingo, and Grifasi, absorbing it, make him seem so.

"Do you know Arnold Rothstein?" asks the new Hughie, impressed by this possibility at least, of an irritated Erie who would much rather not go there. Rothstein, plainly, is out of Eries league. He was the gangster whom Damon Runyon portrayed as The Brain ("however much money there is around, The Brain sooner or later gets hold of all of it") and though ONeill obviously lacked Runyon's comic genius, there's a sense in which he got there first and more authentically. Hughie, like The Iceman, lets us sniff the real stench that Runyon turned into perfume.

Hughie -- the real, the ex-Hughie, I mean -- must rank among the great non-appearing title characters, along with Mary Chases Harvey, that other legendary drunkards companion, and of course Samuel Beckett's Godot. Which gets us to the evening's other and probably even better half, Becketts Krapps Last Tape, in which Dennehy, having played a duet with another actor, plays a duet with himself. This is the play in which an old failed writer, who's been logging his own life on a tape recorder, listens to an entry from decades earlier, a time at which he still had love and hope but turned his back on them: on the former anyway with predictably dire effects on the latter.

Sam Beckett was a gentleman and a showman; he laid out his plays meticulously, with an infallible sense of timing. Jennifer Tarver, an expert Beckett director whom Stratford was smart to scoop up from the Toronto fringes, obeys his instructions implicitly and still emerges with a production that has an identity of its own. Beckett knew that when he had Krapp begin the play by eating a banana and a half and leaving the peel on the floor, we would wait for him to slip on it; so he does, but at a moment when we don't expect it. He doesnt fall; he merely flails, which Dennehy does brilliantly, before going on to fall foul of lifes more metaphysical banana skins.

His physical transformation is remarkable, from the expansive Erie to the reclusive Krapp whose closest contact with the outside world is in his jutting eyebrows. Phenomenal too is the contrast between the gnarled voice that issues from his lips, and that of the hopeful young litterateur that we hear on tape, so shockingly authentic that one almost suspects Dennehy of having laid down his vocal tracks some 30 years in advance. Both are authentically Dublin, but at vastly different stages of development. Every time one sees this play, its progress seems more ineluctable, towards a bitter dismissal that one prays in vain won't happen. Krapp, a more universal play than Hughie, is also a better one technically (Both, incidentally, were first staged in 1958, O'Neills posthumously.) Hughie pretty much
