'Who would have thought we were so important?" Rosencrantz asks Guildenstern towards the end of Tom Stoppard's theatrical memorial to the two of them. Or maybe Guildenstern asks Rosencrantz; one of Stoppard's running gags is that nobody, including themselves, is ever sure which of them is which. Whoever poses it, it's a pregnant question through which all the play's levels get levelled. It reflects on the two guys' literary status as minor characters in Hamlet; on their social status as functionaries or hangers-on at the Danish court; and on their status as human beings, condemned from birth to an end that, being universal, is statistically insignificant. "They really had it in for us, didn't they?" says one of them, and "they" could be Hamlet, Shakespeare or fate.

The title Rosencrantz and Guildenstern Are Dead is a direct quote from the last moments both of Shakespeare's tragedy and Stoppard's comedy, the words dispassionately delivered in both contexts by an English ambassador. Stoppard's signal achievement is that in the 45 years since his play appeared, it's become impossible for us to hear them dispassionately. We want to protest, say that they didn't deserve to die, that it isn't fair.

Stoppard shows us Hamlet's two university buddies on the road to Elsinore, ignorant of why they've been summoned, and wondering and worrying about it; then, at court, doing their ineffectual best to probe the cause of Hamlet's lunacy; and finally, escorting him to England for what is supposed to be his execution and turns out to be theirs. When they are, so to speak, off the Shakespearean stage, they talk in scintillating Stoppardian prose, mixing high philosophy and low verbal farce, a cross-talk of which they're both the executants and the victims; but every so often they fall subserviently into pre-determined blank verse as characters from the larger play sweep in on them and, even more disconcertingly, sweep out again.

In Shakespeare's play the duo really are indistinguishable; they are never on their own and, since they never get to say a word to one another, they have no relationship. Stoppard obviously had to change that; his characters share a predicament, and they both feel oppressed by it, but Rosencrantz is both the more innocent and the more transparently woebegone, while Guildenstern takes refuge in displays of intellectual superiority, like a Samuel Beckett tramp who may actually have seen Waiting for Godot. In Soulpepper's revival, Rosencrantz is wonderfully played by Ted Dysktra, a towheaded embodiment of existential dread, filled with boyish eagerness and boyish despair, and with almost unfaultable comic timing. Jordan Pettle, his opposite number, doesn't have the same verbal punch and precision, which means that some of the philosophic crosstalk threatens to outstay its welcome. But he comes into his own, as does Joseph Ziegler's whole production, in the last movement, where all the talk of death becomes more than talk; becomes indeed rather moving. It's the first time I've known this play ripen rather than fade as it progresses.

At every stage of their journey R&G, as they tell us in Hamlet, meet the Players, whose specialty is death and who are there to point out the difference between make-believe mortality and the real thing, each having its theoretical advantages; Kenneth Welsh, as First Player, brings a leathery down-at-heel authority to a role often played as farouche trickster. Visually the production is very impressive, from the pyrotechnic coin-tossing of the opening to the pirate attack of the last act.

This has been a week for Shakespearean knock-offs. It would have been even more of one if WorldStage's Othello, C'est Qui hadn't been cancelled, but we have more Moor at Stratford later in the year and, right now, in Joseph Jomo Pierre's Shakespeare's Nigga, a co-production of Obsdian and Theatre Passe Muraille. This gives us the poet himself looking back on his career, in high dissatisfaction. He dreams of having two black slaves, with the same names as the two leading black characters in his own plays, Othello and Aaron, the gleeful villain of Titus Andronicus. At least, a synopsis included in the press kit (maybe as last-minute insurance) says that he's dreaming; it's not at all clear in the production. Othello is, in the play's appropriation of Deep South parlance, the house nigga, entrusted with a whip; Aaron is the field one, on whom he uses it, and who spends most of the play chained-up and snarling. Othello nurses a love for Shakespeare's daughter Judith; Aaron actually has her. Both these passions, for different reasons and in different measures, displease Shakespeare, bringing in more potent echoes of the Prospero-Miranda-Caliban-Ferdinand dynamic in The Tempest than of anything in the other plays.

Obviously this isn't intended as a serious contribution to Shakespeare biography; he's there as a symbol of European culture and of what it's done to the disenfranchised. To that extent the play resembles Edward Bond's Bingo, but that has a real action, grounded in real documents. Pierre complains, in both play and publicity, that Shakespeare failed to give Aaron (whom he plays himself, beneath his previous good form as both actor and playwright) a voice: an odd accusation, given that he has the second longest role in Titus, and much the liveliest; if he isn't a fully fleshed character, then neither is anyone else in that play. And it's absurd to claim that Titus, a totally neglected piece until the last 60 or so years, has influenced how anybody sees anything. Othello probably has but, aside from a few moments that may reflect obliquely on the protagonist's relations with his wife and father-in-law, this play does nothing with them. Pierre's Othello and Aaron have nothing in common with Shakespeare's but their names and their colour. Anything goes, which means that nothing does.
