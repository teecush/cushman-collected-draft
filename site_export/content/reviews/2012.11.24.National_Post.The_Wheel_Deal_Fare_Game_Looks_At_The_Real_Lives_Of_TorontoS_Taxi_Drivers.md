Judged as a play, Fare Game is feeble to the point of non-existence. To be fair (ouch) it isn't trying to be a play. Its an assemblage of documentary film clips, for which the links happen to be provided by live performers in front of a live audience. Even on that basis, it leaves a lot to be desired.

The shows subtitle is Life in Toronto's Taxis and its subject is the working conditions of Toronto taxi drivers. Three people with collective credentials as actors, writers, directors and video artists - Marjorie Chan, Ruth Madoc-Jones, Alex Williams - put it together as part of Theatre Passe Muraille's current Theatre Beyond Walls project, which is aimed at making a connection between the playhouse itself and the area around and outside it. Sometimes they take shows into the surrounding streets; other times, they bring the streets into the show. Fare Game does both. Its creators went out with cameras and recorders to interview drivers both in and out of their cabs, and they now run the footage for us.

One of the shows stated aims is to make us give more thought to the man or woman at the wheel (all but one of their interview subjects are male) the next time we get into a cab. In that, they probably succeed. They also leave us in no doubt that taxi-driving is a tough life: very long hours for, once all overheads have been deducted, very little money. Its also, we're told on two separate occasions, the most dangerous job in North America. The most effective of the video segments is the first-hand story of a driver who was knifed by a passenger at Yonge and Sheppard, which he might reasonably have believed to be a safe area; he could see the knife coming but still couldn't manage to escape.

We are told, which most of us probably already know, that cab driving has traditionally been an immigrant occupation with its constituency changing with each successive wave of newcomers; also that this results (and is probably more the case now than ever before) in a large number of cabbies working far below their educational qualifications. We learn - something we probably should have known already, but I have to confess I didnt - that the big taxi companies arent employers at all, but middlemen connecting drivers with passengers; which makes you wonder about the drivers, who are also the car owners, having to sport the company logo.

There's a lot about licensing: about the difference between "standard plates" and "ambassador plates" and how this can lead to cab drivers being exploited. Unfortunately, most of this information is anecdotally supplied to us by the drivers themselves, which doesn't make it too easy to follow. Maybe the show should have expanded its video resources to include some charts, or at least some captions. Some of the film sequences show drivers and their representatives, in search of a better deal, demonstrating outside City Hall and attending a hearing inside of it. They all come off as good-humoured and articulate. As for their civic and industrial opponents (who must exist, otherwise what are they protesting about?) they are neither seen nor heard from. This is a pity. The case for including them is not so much that it would be fairer (pun, this time, not intended) but that it would be more interesting. Or, that it would be more dramatic, which a documentary - on stage, film, TV or any combination thereof - needs to be, every bit as much as a scripted play.

Some of Fare Game actually is a scripted play, or a scripted something. As well as introducing the video segments (and, as interviewers, appearing in some of them) the three originators deliver statements from subjects who didnt want to appear on camera. This unavoidably entails the actors functioning as actors. They also do a few unexplained physical exercises, presumably to establish their theatrical cred, and each of them gets to jump on and off his or her own personal set of steps and rostra: again to no discernible artistic or informational end. They vary in their efficiency at fulfilling their dual functions. Chan, both as actor and narrator, goes through the whole evening sporting the same broad and uncommunicative grin. Madoc-Jones is engagingly informal as link-person but freezes when speaking other peoples lines. Williams, the least experienced performer of the three, makes a wooden, apologetic master of ceremonies, but comes to surprising life when playing characters. On opening night, though, the biggest hand went to the taxi drivers themselves, present in the audience, and that was certainly fair. (Ouch again, but a good ouch.)
