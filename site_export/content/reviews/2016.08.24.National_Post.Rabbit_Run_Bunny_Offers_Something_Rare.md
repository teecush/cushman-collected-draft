THEATRE REVIEW

Bunny Studio Theatre, Stratford

"Show, don't tell" is one of the cardinal rules of playwriting, and like most rules it can be broken.

Nobody has broken it more consistently and successfully than Hannah Moscovitch. She first came to wide notice with The Russian Play, which everybody remembers as a monologue but was actually a firstperson narrative with inset scenes involving two other actors. She retained and refined this format in several of her subsequent plays, and in her new one, Bunny, commissioned and premiered by Stratford, she returns to it, full-strength. The result is an often-scintillating piece that only slackens when, perversely, it doesn't tell us enough.

Bunny is the nickname that one of her friends gives to the play's protagonistnarrator

on account of the caught-in-the-headlights look that comes over her in social situations. Her actual name is Sorrel, bestowed on her by left-wing academic parents who "disapproved of beauty unless it was painted by Van Gogh." She inherits their politics and their scholarship but not their intellectual puritanism. (They approve of sex, at least in principle and when it isn't too loud.) Shy and gawky growing up, she has a passion for Victorian novels. At 17, she discovered boys and they discovered her. She found kissing very enjoyable, and did as much of it as possible, shortly making the logical progression to more intimate encounters. She became known as "a hot dork," the apparent contradiction making her all the more desirable; other girls came up with less affectionate descriptions.

We watch Sorrel as a highschooler, losing her virginity to a football hero; as an undergraduate, beginning a long-running affair with a married professor; as a career academic, marrying a politically ambitious businessman of whom her family would disapprove, whoever painted him. By the time we take our leave of her, she's a professor herself - of Victorian literature, unsurprisingly - responding, or not, to the advances of a young man in a canoe. This last scenario is forecast at the beginning of the play as part of a montage that's confusing at the time but falls into place later. All the while, Sorrel keeps up a retrospective commentary on herself and the men in her life, a running soliloquy whose tone varies between humorously self-deprecating and even more humorously self-deprecating. The sharper the humour, the deeper the sting.

This is a mode in which Moscovitch has always done well, and in which she does so again. It's also a formfitting style for Maev Beaty, who gave her first great performance in a lesser-known Moscovitch play USSR (which really was a monologue) and for whom this new one may well have been written. She uses impeccable technical poise to convey a character in constant apprehension of losing whatever poise she has. Every word, every wince, every nervous smile, is perfectly timed, perfectly true: wit in the service of feeling. Her male partners, all excellent, are Cyrus Lane as the adulterous lecturer; Tim Campbell the pink-shirted image of a robust politician in waiting; and Emilio Vieira and David Patrick Flemming as the younger guys at either end of the chronological spectrum.

The writing, the acting, and Sarah Garton Stanley's direction together offer something rare: good, frank theatre about sex, erotic without being exploitative.

We watch and listen to Sorrel trying to find her moral compass. She's vague about where it may be; the only trouble is, so is the play. The constant references to her Victorian literary heroines aren't much help. They might be more so if she was more specific about which ones they are; it's not as if all the female protagonists of Victorian novels were identical. My own guess is that Sorrel would like to be Dorothea Brooke (Middlemarch) while fearful of turning into an unexpurgated version of Becky Sharp (Vanity Fair), but, voluble though she is, she never lets on.

And then there's the one character in the play about whom Sorrel, in her storytelling capacity, says nothing: Maggie, her best, maybe her only, female friend, the one who names her Bunny, and who first appears riding a bicycle and offering cautionary advice. She offers more of it, though not from a bike, in later scenes, even as tragedy begins to envelop her own life. This, Sorrel eventually tells her, is the most important relationship she has (there's no suggestion that it's sexual), but we have to take that on trust, because their scenes together provide no proof beyond the bare statement. In fact, Sorrel as narrator seems to consider their friendship too strong or too sacred to require comment. It isn't, even though Krystin Pellerin as Maggie adds another to the production's roster of fine performances. (As does Jessica B. Hill in the late-arriving role of Maggie's daughter.)

Nonetheless, what seems meant to be the point is blunted, because the play won't show and Sorrel/Bunny won't tell. But what precedes and surrounds it should charm you.
