NAC production misses the details, while 'da Kink needs filling out

Marti Maraden, outgoing director of the National Arts Centre's English Theatre, has recruited an extraordinary number of our best actors, including at least four of my personal top 20, for her valedictory Shakespeare, Love's Labour's Lost. Unhappily, the whole doesn't amount to more than the sum of the parts, which means, by cruel theatrical arithmetic, that it adds up to less.

This early comedy, a word-drunk piece trying to get sober, is magical but tricky. As a pastoral it needs a sense of place, which isn't achieved here by a platform set built from anonymous timbers and a louring backcloth dotted with strange shapes, neither of which suggest parkland or much of anything else, except perhaps dead fish. John Pennoyer has also designed 18th-century costumes that are elaborate but, especially for the women, constricting.

The themes and relationships are accurately sketched in but not fleshed out for the most part. There is, tonally speaking, insufficient light and insufficient shade, and little of the speed with which one keeps giving place to the other. Juan Chioran comes off best as Don Armado, knight of the bedraggled countenance; the mournful Hispanic accent gives him, of course, no trouble, but it does sometimes seem to be taking over the characterization. Ben Carlson has all the pace, energy and intelligence for the jesting Berowne, and it's a pleasure to hear him phrase the big speeches; but he misses out on the sheer glee of self-mockery when he, too, falls in love.

There are also surprisingly few sparks between him and his dark lady Rosaline, played by Yanna McIntosh. Casting a black actress in this role sounds intriguing but works out awkwardly. When Berowne's friends cast aspersions on his lady's complexion, by which they textually mean just her hair and her eyes, they sound not merely rude but racist. It's especially perplexing since the arch-mocker (Nigel Shawn Williams as the King of Navarre) is himself black. Maybe some comment is being made on intra-racial prejudice, but I rather doubt it. Colour-blind casting and colour-specific casting seem here to have collided. Of course one wants to see McIntosh in Shakespeare, but I doubt if Rosaline, an underwritten joker, is her part, any more than Kelli Fox is a natural for the Princess of France, whom she plays with a charming but unvaried smile. I would like to see these two gifted actresses exchange roles.

The veteran David William makes a properly courtly, if excessively detached, Boyet; Peter Froehlich and David Schurmann make little of the pedants' cross-talk act but have nice moments, of confusion and rebuke respectively, in the concluding show of the Nine Worthies. The whole production, indeed, perks up in its last movement, which is the acme of bittersweet. The messenger of death makes his infallible effect, allowing Fox to play naked pain, which she does superbly, and McIntosh to administer an extra silent sting in the play's tail. And the song of the Owl and the Cuckoo has been beautifully set by Marc Desormeaux, with its verses cunningly distributed among the players. This, if nothing else, is a labour won.

The last show that Ed and David Mirvish presented at the Princess of Wales was Hairspray. They have now replaced it there with another hirsute extravaganza. This suggests a consistency of vision usually deemed rare among commercial managements, and we should acknowledge it.

By all accounts 'da Kink in My Hair was extravagant only in spirit when it made its bow two years ago on the Toronto fringe, and when it subsequently appeared at Theatre Passe Muraille. It was a homely, rambling comedy-drama about a Toronto hair salon with an exclusively black, and exclusively female, staff and clientele. Cheerful enough on the outside, it got its real grip by allowing the customers to deliver a series of monologues that celebrated the joys of life while detailing the stark odds against them, in the shape of prejudice and cruelty both without and within their own community.

The pattern is still there, but it has been complicated by the producers' turning the show into a musical. This in itself is a very good idea; and it has obviously been carried out with the enthusiastic co-operation of the author, Trey Anthony, and her director Weyni Mengesha, who has herself supplied music and lyrics. It has not, though, been carried far enough; the numbers are good, and excellently performed, but there are too few of them to create a style. Nor has much thought been given to the way they affect the rest of the show which goes on as if it hadn't been informed about the changes.

A musical can afford even less than a play to waste time, or to stop and take deep breaths. We now notice how thin the connecting scenes in the salon are. The author herself plays the proprietress, and is very warm and entertaining, though she seems happier talking to the audience than to the other characters. But then, apart from some funny business over botched take-out orders for lunch, she hasn't given herself much to do except to be welcoming. She's everybody's confidante, but she apparently isn't meant to hear the other characters' deepest revelations. So there's neither contact nor conflict; the women merely come and go.

The confessors, all well played, include an elderly woman joyfully re-discovering sex with a neighbour, a career-woman who, in order to prove herself, is running dangerously on empty, a movie- star returning to the old neighbourhood and letting us know about her lesbianism (this sequence is the most forced, as if the show had suddenly decided that it was being way too heterosexual and had better get more inclusive) and a church-sister who has lost her son to schoolyard violence. This last is the most painful, but not altogether for the right reasons. It's too raw a situation to be trotted out and then discarded. It feels formulaic.

That doesn't stop it feeling real, as do most of the stories. This is a rare example of our city's inhabitants getting to state their case on a big stage. More music, and better musical staging, might turn it from a series of fragments into the rich social tapestry it needs and deserves to be. More imaginative design might make the salon itself into a glistening image of community rather than the arbitrary setting it now seems. (Both the dialogue and the program notes affix symbolic value to hair, but you have to take their word for it.) At the moment, the alternation of songs and monologues makes for a half-baked Chorus Line without the overriding suspense.

One speech, and one performance, take the show to a different dimension. The last soliloquy comes from a Jamaican girl, recently arrived, whose openness first wins our heart ("why Canada so cold?" she asks with enchanting plaintiveness), then breaks it with a harrowing tale of family abuse, then warms it again with hope. The role is played by d'bi.young, who inhabits each of its moods so completely as to make you feel in the presence of a new major actress. Her speech melts into an ensemble number and, this time, that's the wrong decision. She deserves her own space and her own applause.
