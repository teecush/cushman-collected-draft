HYSTERIA

Bluma Appel Theatre, Toronto

An aged psychiatrist, who only happens to be Sigmund Freud, is bent over with his hand firmly stuck inside a wellington boot. Beside him, standing on one leg, is his doctor, who appears to be trying a female undergarment on for size. There are logical, even respectable, reasons for all of this, but a casual visitor coming upon the scene would have no way of knowing that.

Naturally, in strict accordance with the rules of farce, such a visitor promptly appears. He's Salvador Dali, easily identifiable by the most distinctive moustache south of Groucho Marx. Another man might be appalled by the tableau, but he is entranced. What Dali merely dreams, he exclaims, "you live!"

I have been describing a moment from Hysteria, by the British playwright Terry Johnson: An extreme moment, but not an unrepresentative one. It is also a moment with antecedents. Tom Stoppard built a one-act piece called After Magritte around just such a stage picture, and he works several of them into his metaphysical farce, Jumpers. This is the second time in five days Ive invoked Stoppard, but it cant be helped: Blame the zeitgeist. He also, in Travesties, established the genre of the confrontational extravaganza by bringing together -- on a slender historical pretext -- James Joyce, Vladimir Leni, and Tristan Tzara, whose Dadaism, by blessed coincidence, prepared the ground for Dali's Expressionism. Johnson, though certainly his own man, has been following in Stoppard's footsteps for years. His first notable play, Insignificance, corralled Albert Einstein with Marilyn Monroe, bringing on a couple of more average Joes -- McCarthy and diMaggio - - in supporting roles. A few years ago, he had a charming though maybe unexportable piece about the stars of the Carry On movies. Before that, he had built another play, Dead Funny, around the same rude tradition of British comedy, though in that one he kept the famous comics off stage. It has never been seen in Canada, though its central icon, Benny Hill, is surely enough of an international name for audiences here to get the point.

Dead Funny is his best play. Hysteria, however, is a brilliant one, and its been given a superb production by Morris Panych, though the two are not invariably in perfect sync. The action has some roots in historical fact: Freud and Dali did meet in England in 1938 when both were refugees from imminent war and -- in Freuds case -- more than imminent persecution. Dali visited Freud, now dying of cancer, as an act of homage toward his spiritual father -- the man who prided himself on painting the unconscious acknowledging the man who, crudely speaking, discovered it.

In fact, the play is a triple study of Freud and the idea of paternity. The old supershrink receives two other callers at his Hampstead home. One, a composite of two historical figures, is Yehuda, his physician. Like Freud, he's a Jew; unlike Freud, he's a believer, and he takes great exception to his distinguished patients last work, Moses and Monotheism, which would rob Judaism of its spiritual father by claiming that Moses was actually an Egyptian.

Hes prepared to do anything to stop these ideas being published at such a time, including destroying the manuscript. Finally, and crucially, there is Jessica, a young woman who crashes into Freud's study and demands that he analyze her -- or rather, that he re- enact with her his 40-year-old analysis of a woman in Vienna.

At first, he had attributed this woman's hysterical symptoms to her having been raped in childhood by her father. Then, noticing he seemed to be making this diagnosis with suspicious regularity, he changed his mind and said shed imagined this abuse, in fact desired it, and that her condition was in effect her own fault: another blanket diagnosis and no less suspect than its predecessor. (The phenomenon of false-memory syndrome suggests were still paying the price of both.)

Jessica has a strong personal stake in this case, and she is not about to let Freud off the hook. She accuses him -- surely unfairly - - of trimming his sails to the prevailing Viennese winds: He didn't want to antagonize his wealthy male patrons. More uncomfortably the point is Freud's realization that he himself both had, and was, a father.

One other historical, perhaps even hysterical, footnote demands mention: Apparently, shortly after arriving in London, Freud was taken to see Rookery Nook, a classic English farce. He tells Jessica it had made him laugh; she says he lacks taste. (She seems humourless enough to be a theatre critic.) Anyway, the experience seems to have affected him: His own life becomes farcical. Jessica sheds most of her clothes and hides in a closet. He cannot afford to let her be seen, because that would give Yehuda a blackmailing handle against him. So doors furiously open and close throughout. Dali, when he appears, is instantly aroused at the sight of Jessica, and joins happily in.

Johnson, unlike Stoppard, does not write farce. He uses farce, which is a trickier proposition. He doesnt tie up all his loose ends with Stoppards dazzling neatness (though he does pretty well) and he doesn't have the same sustained gaiety of tone. Stoppard, even at his gravest, writes happy plays, making him probably unique among modern playwrights. Johnson writes dark plays and they are likely to break up into comic slabs and serious ones. Jessica's concerns here push Dalis and Yehudas into the background. Not everything in this play is what it seems. Or rather, it is what it seems but you have to wait until near the end to discover just what that is. There are one or two hints on the way, through. The time seems to fluctuate between 1938 (Kristallnacht is in the papers) and '39 (there are air raids). And you may notice, when you return from the intermission, that Ken MacDonald's set has reversed its position.

This set, which has a family likeness to the one MacDonald designed for Panychs production of The Overcoat, is gorgeous: a spacious white chamber that goes golden under the lights and with which the costumes, Freuds especially, are colour co-ordinated. The wall is composed entirely of filing cabinets. It isn't quite what the author ordered -- he wanted to disarm us with naturalism and I know Hampstead well enough to assure you this isn't it -- but its a trade-off worth making.

Toward the end, the play unleashes a surrealist maelstrom that this production, in a veritable Panych-attack, magnificently realizes. The physical comedy, too, is deliciously done, though the verbal sometimes suffers. There is more snap in the lines, and there should be more pace, than appears here.

The visuals may have soaked up some of the actors energy. Eric Peterson's Freud has great intelligence and a fine, sharp outline, but he doesn't command the stage as he should. The plays coherence is that its his agony, in all its aspects, but here he seems just one among equals. Peter Donaldson's Yehuda is somewhat stiff and not noticeably Jewish, though that's what the character is there for. David Storch, though, is a delightful grown-up schoolboy of a Dali. One of the plays charms is that he, of all people, functions as the audiences representative, the nearest thing on stage to an average man. He also gets a memorable come-uppance when Freud dismisses his painting as a conscious representation of the unconscious, rather than the real thing.

Kristen Thomson's Jessica is absolutely the real thing, a performance to rank with her work in Oleanna and Streetcar. Funny and determined, capable of both bravado and bravura, she can switch in and out of farcical gear in a second. And when it comes to the traumatic re-enactment at the heart of the play, she is movingly inside it and lucidly outside simultaneously.

At the beginning of a new season, CanStage, with this and Outrageous, has the two best shows in town. And Hysteria is not only the more hysterical but also the more truly outrageous of the pair.
