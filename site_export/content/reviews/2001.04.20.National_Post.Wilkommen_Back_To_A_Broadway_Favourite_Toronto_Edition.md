CABARET

Pantages Theatre, Toronto

- - -

Alma Cuervo was the best thing in Cabaret when it played at the Princess of Wales a couple of years ago, and in the somewhat reduced circumstances of the musical's return engagement at the Pantages, she incomparably is. (The show is part of SFX's "Best of Broadway" season, and it still comes closer to justifying that label that anything else in the package.)

She appears as Fraulein Schneider, the middle-aged Berlin landlady who breaks off her engagement to a widowed Jewish fruiterer under the looming threat of Nazism. Cuervo, whose career has been in plays more than musicals, gives a fine, strong account of a shruggingly accepting survivor of a woman, sheltered but worldly, and delivers her two solo songs with devastating power, musical and dramatic.

In one, So What, she psychologically kicks up her heels and proclaims that "you learn how to settle for what you get," especially in the matter of rent. In the other, What Would You Do, she reverses the coin, showing how a decent but unheroic person can settle because she's terrified of what she'll get, and dares her auditors, onstage and off, to condemn her.

These songs, written with an awareness of Brecht and Weill but not in their shadow, show Fred Ebb, the lyricist, and John Kander, the composer, at their best, unhysterically grown-up. And if there were a taste of justice in the world, they would bring down the house more conclusively than any of the score's more famous and flashier numbers. It's difficult, though, to get an audience on its feet for a question mark.

Cuervo also has a couple of duets with her admirer Herr Schultz, admirably played this time round by Hal Robinson. In the first, he presents her with a pineapple from his store, and they go into a tango with Hawaiian overtones. The second, a waltz, comes after he has impulsively proposed, and it sweetly celebrates their future marriage. Both numbers are charming with a touch of the absurd, and the two players convey this beautifully, but to make sure that we don't miss the point, the master of ceremonies of the eponymous Berlin cabaret is onstage behind them, silently -- but not gently -- mocking their feelings.

What chance, we are implicitly asked, does a mature Jewish- Gentile romance have in Germany, c. 1939? The production's overriding nihilism, though, takes the point further, acting superior to the possibility of any tender feelings between any two people, anywhere, anytime. That may not be the intention. It is the flavour.

When Cabaret was first produced on Broadway in the '60's, the numbers in the Berlin nightclub -- the sequence that begins at the top of the show with the emcee singing Wilkommen and climaxes near the end with Sally Bowles doing the title song -- were a commentary on the historical action, rather than a metaphor for it. The same was true of the film version in the '70s. The cabaret was only half of Cabaret. It was the more eye-catching, enjoyable part, so it's understandable that this revival -- directed in New York by Sam Mendes and choreographer Rob Marshall after an initial London staging by Mendes alone -- should make it the whole show.

"Life" according to Sally, "is a cabaret, old chum," and this production takes her at her word. The stories of Sally and Cliff (the musical's stand-in for Christopher Isherwood, who wrote the original Berlin Stories) and Herr Schultz and Fraulein Schneider and all the others, not to mention the rise of German fascism, are an extended cabaret-number; though, since Sally herself works at the club, some of this presumed onstage activity is actually happening backstage. I may be literal-minded, but I find this confusing.

It's swings and roundabouts -- Cabaret, in either of its major stagings, is a brilliant but problematic musical -- but the Mendes- Marshall version is the one we have here, and its flaws are the ones we have to reckon with. One of them is that it turns a supporting character into the star role, and by doing that makes it less rewarding. I remarked before that the emcee here cannot steal the show, because he already owns it. He's always around, leering and preening, and I got tired of him.

I suspect that the production concept determines the performance, but Jon Peterson seems more slinkily androgynous than his predecessor and, however much he may bare his fangs, he does not use them to bite into his songs. He also indulges in some below-Everage banter to the audience that may be a road-show addition -- I don't remember it from before -- and that probably bears little relation to how they carried on in the hotspots of old Berlin.

The conceit seems to be that the emcee, cavorting with his girl- and-boy band and chorus line, makes reductively uncommitted fun of everything, including the Nazis, but doesn't know what he's getting into. Giggles won't cut it anymore; a last tableau -- unique to this production -- shows him in a concentration camp, doubly stigmatized as a homosexual and a Jew. (Echoing the play Bent, he impales himself on the electric wire.)

You have to work very hard to earn the right to use Holocaust imagery, and it's difficult to think of a musical that could qualify. It's nice to know that the creators of this show think Hitler was a bad thing; it's also hard to believe that anyone in the audience needs to be told. What actually comes across is that the production, having run out of ways to shock us, is going for the ultimate.

Meanwhile, much of what was shocking in the original production, which eased us seductively and economically into the horror, has lost its sting; the emcee's gorilla song, for example ("if you could see her through my eyes, she wouldn't look Jewish at all"), which here comes right on top of, and is cancelled out by, a dialogue scene in which a stone is hurled through Herr Schultz's shop window.

The scene is very well staged. The song isn't; Marshall's choreography doesn't have -- presumably doesn't want -- the insolent finesse of Ron Field's (for Hall Prince, in the original) or of Bob Fosse's (for himself, in the movies). In the film, the Mein Herr number involved a lot of girls bending over chairs; here there are no chairs and we just get protruding posteriors.

Still, scenes and songs flow into one another very smoothly. The production knows what it's doing, even if it's confused about what it's thinking, and the staging is very assured. Joe Masteroff, the original librettist, still gets book credit, and he or someone has threaded an astute path though the myriad texts that this material has accumulated.

There are good performances from Drew McVety as a Nazi and Lenora Nemetz as a prostitute, and Hank Stratton does a decent job as the decent Cliff. Sally is Andrea McArdle who, eons, ago, was Broadway's first Annie. I was musing just a few weeks ago on what happened to feisty little musical orphans when they grew up. On this showing, it's nothing good. McArdle makes an earnest stab at the character, and she gives a touchingly tremulous torch-song rendition of Maybe This Time. But the English accent defeats her -- she seems to be groping her way from phrase to phrase, usually landing on the wrong word -- and the amoral playfulness of songs like Perfectly Marvelous (in character) and Don't Tell Mama (ostensibly out of it, but in it really) escapes her utterly. I hadn't realized before how good Michael Gibson's orchestrations are: seedy and reduced but capturing all the essentials.
