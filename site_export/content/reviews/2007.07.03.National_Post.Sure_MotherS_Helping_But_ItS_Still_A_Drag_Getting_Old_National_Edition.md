THE CASSILIS ENGAGEMENT

Court House Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont.

---

In 1909, at the age of 39, St. John Hankin, playwright, drowned himself. It may be the worst loss ever inflicted on English drama by an early death. The Shaw Festival tipped us off to this a few years ago with its production of Hankin's The Return of the Prodigal and it now reinforces the point with The Cassilis Engagement.

Hankin wrote Edwardian society comedies that owe something to Shaw and more to Wilde, but are trimmer and more mordant than either. They show greed, jealousy and snobbery bubbling beneath a placid surface and finally boiling over. The Cassilis Engagement is subtitled "a play for mothers," and it turns on the efforts of Mrs. Cassilis, a widowed member of England's county gentry, to prevent her son Geoffrey from contracting an unsuitable marriage. Geoffrey's fiancee is a lower-class London girl called Ethel Borridge, which in name alone is enough to condemn her. Mrs. Cassilis determines to kill the match with kindness. She invites Ethel down to her place in Leicestershire for a prolonged visit, asking her to bring her own mother, and trusting that the sheer incongruity of their presence in such surroundings will both alarm Ethel and awaken Geoffrey. It turns out to be a fruitful scheme, in ways that even its progenitress might not have foreseen.

Hankin was anything but a sentimentalist, and Ethel is anything but a victim. Her mother has scrimped to give her an education, hoping that she can use it to marry money; there's a senescent earl waiting in the wings, but Geoffrey will more than do. Meanwhile, Mrs. Cassilis' friends and neighbours look on aghast, horrified that she should give such creatures houseroom, let alone be friendly to them.

Christopher Newton's production contains three marvellously complex performances. Mrs. Cassilis is a mixture of long-suffering and long-sighted: a nice woman who hatches a nasty plan that requires her to act nice. Goldie Semple lays out these layers superbly. The feral maternal protectiveness, the flickers of conscience, the natural graciousness purposefully perverted, are all there; so is a sense of loss in the midst of success. Mrs. Borridge is a hearty vulgarian whose transparent opportunism and dropped "aitches" (she's only too 'appy to make herself at 'ome) embarrass her daughter as thoroughly as they amuse or appall her hosts -- which, since she herself has trained Ethel to be upwardly mobile, is choice indeed. Mary Haney plays her vigorously and successfully for laughs until her ambitions begin to fall apart, at which point she becomes enraged, desperate, and -- in her terror -- terrifying. Social climbing may be a joke; poverty isn't.

Watching mother and daughter exhibiting their tricks and manners to the scandalized toffs is rather like watching Eliza Doolittle's "not bloody likely" scene from Pygmalion extended over most of an evening. (Hankin's play came first.) The greatest of the play's many ironies, though, may be that Ethel is so full-blooded a character as to make much of Mrs. Cassilis' best-laid plan superfluous; she was bound to revolt against this environment even without her mother on hand. Trish Lindstrom makes her hard, vivacious and fundamentally honest, able to buck the expectations all around her rather than deny her own nature. A scene in which she entertains the company with a music-hall song (lyrics by Newton himself ) is a real set- piece, delightful in itself and laying bare all the conflicts.

The men's roles are decidedly subordinate, but David Leyshon almost saves the baffled Geoffrey from complete and utter drippiness, and as an ageing rake playing devil's advocate, Patrick Galligan has a diverting swagger, though he seems excessively aware, not so much of his place in the world as of his place in the play. There is a third mother in the cast, a countess who communicates mainly via epigram and represents the minor aristocracy at its intolerant worst. There's a similar character in The Return of the Prodigal, and the problem with both of them is to stop them turning into cut-rate Lady Bracknells, revelling in their own outrageousness; Donna Belleville, like her predecessor, fails to solve it. Her daughter is the girl everybody thinks Geoffrey ought to marry, a mouse in jodhpurs; Charlotte Gowdy plays her dowdy, but in an appealing sort of way that includes one modest moment of genuine suffering. As Mrs. C's confidante who gets drawn gradually into the plot, Laurie Paton conveys subtle and supple gradations of shock and fascination with both those on both sides of the tracks. In a good Shaw season, this show is the best yet. - In repertoire until Oct. 5. Call 1-800-511-SHAW or visit www.shawfest.com for tickets.
