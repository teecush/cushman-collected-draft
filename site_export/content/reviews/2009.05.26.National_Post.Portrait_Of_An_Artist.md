SUNDAY IN THE PARK WITH GEORGE

Royal George Theatre, Toronto

---

Of the two musicals that Stephen Sondheim and James Lapine wrote together in the 1980s, the first, Sunday in the Park with George, has generally been held in higher critical esteem than its successor, Into the Woods. The latter, based on fairy tales, has been disparaged as a kids' show, while the former is self-consciously about important things like Art. In fact, Into the Woods houses a seamless masterpiece, the modern theatre's nearest equivalent to A Midsummer Night's Dream, while Summer in the Park greatness, though it certainly exists, flashes disconcertingly on and off. Alisa Palmer's Shaw Festival production is uneven, but it's still the most satisfying I've seen, the only one in which the emotional resolutions of the evening's second half top the more cerebral pleasures of the first.

Like Into the Woods, Sunday in the Park has a first act that seems complete in itself until you experience the second; and that would in fact be cute but unsatisfying if you didn't experience the second. The first act climax is a tableau vivant representation of Georges Seurat's giant group painting A Sunday Afternoon on the Island of La Grande Jatte, a modern pastoral for a new industrial age. We've spent the evening thus far watching Seurat -- George to us -- on location, sketching the various weekending figures who make up both the picture's and the musical's supporting cast, and then refining the sketches in his studio. Now, we watch the final assembly. George moves the other actors around the stage until he has them where he wants them; meantime, they sing a soaring chorale at whose final tolling notes the painting has taken shape before our eyes.

Standing at the front of the group, placed there at the last moment as a form of amends, is Dot, George's former model and mistress, whoin real life is about to leave Paris for a new life with a new man in America. She's pregnant with George's child, but has been turned off by his absorption in his work. History tells us little about Seurat, and it naturally tells us nothing about the people -- soldiers, domestics, assorted bourgeois --in his painting; Lapine's libretto has had to invent biographies for them, none of which amount to more than grudging vignettes. A minor exception is an old lady who turns out, with endearing abruptness, to be the absentminded George's mother; a major one is Dot, who takes her name from Seurat's own pointillist

technique. (If Andrew Lloyd Webber had written this show, it would have been called Dots.) And that revolutionary style is reflected in Sondheim's score, its melodies marked out in stabbing, iridescent shafts of sound. And these motifs build and echo from one song to another so that a late number (Move On) becomes, not a reprise of an earlier one (We Do Not Belong Together) but an expansion and glorification of it.

The ache, the yearning and the discipline that distinguish Sondheim's music also find wonderful expression in Finishing the Hat, which might be called George's vocational number. It's hard, the song and the show tell us, being an artist. (Indeed. But then, it's hard being anything.) But it works more as a window into George's craft than into his character. He's aloof and dedicated, Dot is vivacious and instinctive, and the others are lay figures: by definition, of course. This thinness of personality is obviously intentional (it's telling that only George and Dot have numbers of their own) but it's also, in a Sondheim show, frustrating. Act Two moves us from 1884 to 1984 and from Paris to New York: Seurat's (fictitious) great-grandson, also called George, is a laser sculptor who calls his creations Chromolumes, his latest being a homage to his namesake's masterpiece. He's accompanied by his wheelchair-bound grandmother Marie, the daughter Dot was carrying. Unlike George the Brushwielder, who never sold a painting, George the Chromluminary is professionally feted and artistically frustrated;

he has to spend most of his energies raising money (his art, he sings sardonically, "needs a Foundation"). The satire on the art world is fairly facile -- these people, too, are dummies -- but when young George makes a pilgrimage to the Parisian park, where the spectral presence of Dot gives him the understanding and encouragement she could never offer his predecessor, the show takes on a new life. At least it does here, as I've never known it do before. This is largely due to Steven Sutcliffe, who may not have the vocal plangency of Mandy Patinkin, creator of the double role, but is a far more sympathetic actor. A trifle stiff as George One, he makes George Two a restless, vulnerable and wholly believable figure. When he sings the soul-searching Lesson #8 (title courtesy of Dot's old grammar book) the show's themes come together as his personal quest: past and present, tradition and innovation, Children and Art. That last coupling is the title of Dot's last great song: an example of unifying a show by main force. (Though what of childless non-artists? Are their lives nothing?)

Julie Martell as Dot/Marie may have a tougher job than her co-star, since she has to compete with memories of Bernadette Peters, who was incomparable. Surprisingly, for a young performer who's mainly done musicals, she's stronger as old Marie than as young Dot, and comes across better in speech than in song. The latter isn't just her problem; the Shaw tradition of unmiked voices is a noble one but Paul Sportelli's orchestra, giving an otherwise exemplary account of what sound like the original orchestrations, often drowns out the words. Two supporting actors stand out: Kyle Blair in what turns out to be the key role of American George's technical assistant (who opts to work for some less demanding employer, like NASA) and especially Sharry Flett, a revelation as French George's mama whose longings for life, and especially trees, as they used to be, have enormous resonance. The Act One finale is not, because of fussy staging, to be as stirring as it should be. The Act Two finale, immediately due to Sutcliffe but with Flett having helped prepare the ground, is moving even to tears. - Sunday in the Park with George is in repertory until Nov. 1.
