FILLER UP!

Artword Theatre, Toronto

The season that began so alarmingly with Godzilla has hit a further low with Filler Up!, a one-woman show so embarrassing that for long sections of it I could hardly bear to look at the stage.

My reluctance had nothing to do with the physical appearance of Deb Filler, the writer-performer: this despite the fact that her lifelong weight problem is the most prominently featured of the three things she is here to tell us about. (The others are her lesbianism and her Jewishness.)

Actually, Filler's shape is best described as comfortable, and she proves it, at the end of the show, by pulling on a pair of leather pants that, from all she tells us, would previously have been too small for her. This is one of the evenings two climaxes.

The other is her refusal to take more than a single bite of a freshly baked loaf of challah bread. Freshly baked, I should add, by her. We have watched her assemble and blend the ingredients with her own hands during the first half of the show, and during the second it has browned merrily in an onstage convector oven. It is, I suppose, a metaphor of sorts. But who kneads it?

Unmediated autobiography is the most perilous of all theatrical forms. If you dont warm immediately to the person doing it, it's all up. Nothing else is going to come along to tweak your interest; it's just you and her and her self-absorption. My own failure to invest in Filler has to do with the fact that, though she presents herself as a comedienne, and does funny voices to prove it, she seems to have no idea of how to weight a joke.

There are certain clowns (the late George Burns was one) who attain a state of comic grace in which literally everything sounds hilarious. Filler seems to have reached the opposite state. Trying to be ingratiating, she just sounds bossy; instead of throwing lines away, she belabours them. She didnt exactly win me over by breezily demanding a bigger welcoming hand than she actually received before doing anything to justify it.

She then started in on the jovial Jewish allusions that seemed, coming out of nowhere, like a calculated pitch to the hall. (Some of which, to be fair, responded. At the end -- critics are always being told they should mention these things -- there was even a standing ovation. As if you were surprised.) Im a Jew myself, and I still felt outnumbered.

I also felt shame: Shame at having hinted earlier this week that Sandra Shamas, who really knows how to do these things, might be less than a transcendent genius. Listening to Shamas' first-person narratives, you sometimes wonder how much of what she is saying is true. With Filler, you never doubt that all of it is, because you cannot imagine that anybody would have taken the trouble to make it up.

Anyway, the story she tells us begins in New Zealand, where she grew up as the daughter of two refugees from Nazi Europe. Both parents, mother especially, encouraged her to eat, and then worried when she got fat. So they sent her off to visit an aunt in the United States, who, by prior arrangement, deposited her in fat camp. She escaped from this after a day and ran back to auntie, a raucously vivacious soul who continued the family tradition by filling 'er up. Deb seems to have spent the intervening years shuttling between home, where she discovered her destiny as a performer, and New York, which is where she has pursued it.

A sub-plot concerns the present, pressing question of whether her now-widowed mother should come to live with her, a proposition on which both, courtesy of the intercontinental phone lines, blow ceaselessly hot and cold.

She tells us her mother has continued to call her even on the opening night of her Toronto run. Presumably, she will continue to do so on all the other nights. In other words, what was set up as a free-wheeling monologue turns into a play.

This is a miscalculation of unimaginable ghastliness. The shows only saving grace has been our shared pretence that she was talking directly to us, making it up as she went along. Now it thinks its a DRAMA. It's a mess: Neither fish, fowl nor good Yiddishe herring. (I didn't tell you that her mother was in the audience, in person, at the premiere. I didnt want to confuse you.)

Now, what can I salvage? Fillers recreation of her professional debut, singing an appalling homemade song to her boyfriend -- she had one then and he was a mistake -- is nice in its remembered awfulness. (Her other musical interludes are also awful but less intentionally so, despite the fact that she can actually sing. She picks up a guitar and does snatches of famous tunes with parody lyrics that are both heavy-handed and, well, half-baked.)

She does an amusing impersonation of her first lesbian partner, who accepted her tentative drug-propelled advances with a couple of off-hand monosyllables. She entertainingly recalls the name of her first movie role (Pudgy Earthquake Victim). She does a real routine about a hefty Hebrew maiden called Zaftige who personally parted the Red Sea, though Moses "accepted the award on her behalf and with it the perks. This should have been the end of the first half but, like the Red Sea post-miracle, it rolled relentlessly on.

Later on there is dignity in her telling of her father, a concentration-camp survivor, who persuaded the Russian liberators to let him enlist the help of the imprisoned German guards in baking bread. It's meant to offset the frantic jesting about diets and support groups but it merely overpowers them.

The recipe seems to have been exhaustively pre-tested. It boasts a co-writer (Lowery Marshall), a director-dramaturge (Molly Thom) and three other workshop dramaturges." It is produced by Filler herself and John Karastamatis, Toronto's smartest theatrical press agent, who must have taken leave of his usually impressive senses.
