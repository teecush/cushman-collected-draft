This has been a miserable year in the Toronto theatre. In terms of new Canadian work from established writers, it was frightening. We had Adam Pettles Mosley and Me, unambitious and mildly disappointing; Florence Gibson's Home Is My Road, ambitious and seriously disappointing, and Jason Sherman's Remnants was panoramic and the only play of the year to be both funny and moving on any kind of scale. It gives me no pleasure to say any of the above, but the truth is the truth.

Otherwise there wasnt much. Susan Coyne, who has been coming into her prime as a character actress, has also been making stealthy advances on the profession of playwright, progressing from translator to collaborator to TV scriptwriter to adapter of her own childhood memoir Kingfisher Days. This was charming, as was her own narrating performance in it; next step awaited with interest. Another actor, Matthew Edison, made a promising writing debut with The Domino Heart, a series of linked monologues: a reasonable way to start though not, one hopes, the way that hell go on. Yet a third actor-playwright (we grow them here), Chris Earle, gave us Russell Hill, a portentous revue with a stunningly persuasive, not to say versatile, subway-platform set. From Montreal, Carole Frechette's Helen's Necklace was one of those ultra-sensitive pieces that make me wish I were home with a book.

Most of these plays were stuck in their own past or present back yards. So it was a relief when, at the end of the year, two plays turned up with, by our standards, exotic settings and resonating themes. Tiger of Malaya, about Japanese war crimes trials, was no masterpiece, and Bhopal, about murderous multinational malfeasance in India, was weak and muddled in both writing and production, but their stories at least set the adrenalin running.

Im not claiming things are much better elsewhere, though the ever-accumulating backlog of foreign plays makes it hard to be sure. (When are we going to catch up with the Tom Stoppards and Terry Johnsons?) The American This Is Our Youth (Kenneth Lonergan) and the English Blue/Orange (Joe Penhall), both given good Canadian productions this year, were lively evenings but in familiar moulds: the nihilistic teenage play, the mental-hospital power-play. (The latter is actually an ingenious melding of social and generational conflicts, doctor-patient crossed with doctor-doctor.) One sat through both crossing off their good points but without surrendering. And they were the best. We saw nothing else from the U.S. except the documentary Laramie Project, in which content again trumped form. The British import (or touring stopover) Scaramouche Jones offered a fine, gentle solo performance from Pete Postlethwaite but little else; it was an essay in the sentimentalization of once-disturbing themes; no wonder it was a hit. Peter Shaffers Amadeus, an earlier example of the same kind, was revived by Canadian Stage, and so imaginatively, under Morris Panych's production, that one almost forgot to question it's being done at all. Maybe it was to keep us up to date with Shaffers revisions; he is a tireless rewriter, and most of his changes here were for the better.

Panych at CanStage also directed the musical highlight of the year: A revival of Stephen Sondheims Sweeney Todd with a worthy performance by George Masswohl and a wonderful one by Fiona Reid. For size and power, this was the highlight of the Toronto year. (It even compared favourably with the COC production of the Peter Grimes that may partially have inspired it.) This year's Dora Awards reached a new plateau of irrelevance by denying prizes to Sweeney while heaping them on a smaller homicidal musical Little Mercy's First Murder, which was passingly stylish but cumulatively monotonous. It derived prestige of course from being done at Tarragon, which gets automatic respect, rather than at CanStage, which never gets any. Next years jury, obeying this rule, may even give musical prizes to Karen Hines Hello...Hello.

Actually, the best new small Canadian musical of 2003 was the hole-and-corner This Must Be Love, traditional in wit and style but innovative in thought, with honourable mention for the determinedly oddball Tequila Vampire Matinee which blew up quite a storm once it got started. Though here's a rule for aspiring musical makers: don't make fun of the form -- certainly not in your preliminary pronouncements -- until youve mastered it.

For sustained fun we had to wait, as usual, for the summer, when the classical companies get going. Soulpeppers revival of Harold Pinter's No Man's Land was the show of the year, something to make you purr with delight. The coup of getting William Hutt back on the Toronto stage was matched by the sublime wit and craft of his performance, which in turn was complemented by Peter Donats self- transformation and his antagonist, and by the sheen of Albert Schultzs production. Beckett's Happy Days, with Martha Burns, was nearly as good. The other new shows weren't. Phedre, staged by Daniel Brooks, was an imaginative raid on the near-impossible, but Nancy Palks performance looked increasingly like a fine actress beating up on herself. Schultzs She Stoops to Conquer tried much too hard to be funny. A word for John Neville's emrbyonic Classical Conspiracy, whose Ibsen readings pointed out peaks still to be scaled; Soulpepper's choices are getting dangerously cosy.

The Shaw Festival, under its new director, Jackie Maxwell, had an up-and-down year. I liked Maxwells own production of Three Sisters, with its use of a wide stage to emphasize loneliness, and applaud her courage in mounting a newish Canadian play, Michel-Marc Bouchards uneven The Coronation Voyage, though I can't really blame the tourists who stayed away: What's in it for them? These were the departures; other shows fell into the established categories. Shaw himself was decently served by Joseph Zieglers production of Widowers Houses, though I found it less revelatory than most, and by Misalliance, which sometimes seemed to succeed in the teeth of its director, Neil Munro. Blood Relations was a good home-bred alternative to the traditional English thriller (production better than play) and the two musicals could hardly have been more contrasted or, in their own ways, more ambitious: On the Twentieth Century, a dauntingly coherent comic opera, and Happy End, which survives on its Brecht-Weill songs and nothing else. I also had one of the best hours of my life at a reading, directed by Christopher Newton, of Cole Porter's blessed musical, Jubilee.

Stratford came in for some belabouring: I'm not sure why. They gave us Sartre and Aristophanes, even if they didnt seem to know what to do with them, and Aeschylus and Giraudoux, whom they got very right. The home-grown House of Atreus -- one story from three authorial points of view -- was a triumph. There were first-rate productions of Troilus and Cressida and Pericles, and good ones of The Taming of the Shrew, Antony and Cleopatra and Loves Labour's Lost. The Swanne trilogy continued on its infuriating and fascinating way. Only the musicals were confounding; I doubt that Stratford needed either The King and I or Gigi, except financially, and I'm certain it didnt need them both in one season. For boulevard theatre at its best, though, you could hardly beat Brian Bedford in Present Laughter. Stratford still offers our best display of actors: Peter Donaldson, Seana McKenna, Graham Abbey, Scott Wentworth, Diane DAquila, Jonathan Goad, Thom Marriott -- and Michael Therriault who limbered up at the festival for his Toronto appearance in The Producers, a show that, what do you know, damn near lived up to its hype.
