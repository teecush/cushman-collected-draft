RODGERS AND HAMMERSTEIN BY ETHAN MORDDEN Abrams, 224 pages, $57

Rodgers and Hammerstein: fresher than springtime

RODGERS AND HAMMERSTEIN BY ETHAN MORDDEN Abrams, 224 pages, $57

Saturday, February 20, 1993

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Review by ROBERT CUSHMAN RICHARD Rodgers and Oscar Hammerstein are 50 this year. Their first show, Oklahoma!, opened in New York in 1943, changing the American musical. First past the post in the anniversary stakes is this luscious and intelligent coffee-table book.

To many people Rodgers and Hammerstein have always seemed to be 50. Coming between the giddy irresponsible musical farces of the twenties and thirties and the fleet-footed theme-and-variation shows of today, they look like Broadway's middle-age: sober and moral and carefully plotted. For most of us they have always been there, if not on stage then on screen: lengthy square-cut roadshow movies, devoutly respectful of their originals.

Ethan Mordden persuades us this pair of stodgy uncles were actually daredevil innovators, not revolutionaries but evolutionaries. This was certainly true of Hammerstein. In his youth he wrote the words for the most durable American operettas: Rose Marie and The Desert Song, shows with strong if ridiculous stories. From this followed Show Boat, with a strong story that was quite sensible. After that came 15 years of failure; but when Rodgers was asked to put music to a Western play originally called Green Grow the Lilacs it was Hammerstein to whom he turned for lyrics and libretto.

For Hammerstein this meant picking up where he had left off. For Rodgers it was an about-face, since with his previous partner Lorenz Hart he had typified Manhattan smarts. Rodgers and Hart did not write rural shows, and Hart refused to do this one, thereby signing his own death- warrant. (He was already far-gone in alcohol and despair; the success of Oklahoma! seems to have pushed him over the edge.) Apparently overnight, Rodgers turned off his nervous energy and tapped a new motherlode of bucolic charm. By the end of his career this style had curdled - nearly every song sounded like a jog-trot nursery rhyme - but for a good 10years it was theatrical magic. After all these years the score of Oklahoma! still sounds miraculously healthy.

As Mordden points out it is the score, rather than individual songs, that became the hit, and though Oklahoma! was not the first show to have a full cast recording ("an idea made of lightning") it might as well have been. "Oklahoma!'s score arrested the ear because, from one end to the other, a story was being processed, characters came forth, ideas were told." The story may not have been complex but it was strong, the characters were unsophisticated ("not one of them has a brain in his head" said Hammerstein cheerfully) but they were winning and the overarching idea - the birth of a state - is extremely powerful. Oklahoma!, more than any of its predecessors, was all of a piece; in Rodgers' words "the orchestrations sound the way the costumes look." And it is something unique: a lightweight epic.

Some wag once quipped that R&H did not so much bring the musical to life as bring it to death. In Oklahoma! they kill off the villain, an authentic psychopath. In their later, more ambitious entertainments the victims are more central: the anti-hero in Carousel, the male juvenile in South Pacific, finally in The King and I the hero himself. (Women, in these shows, are survivors.) Two of these are very moving. We cry buckets at The King and I because the leads have never told their love, even to themselves; we however have seen it graphically demonstrated in their Shall We Dance duet. As for Carousel, where the protagonist comes back from the grave to witness his daughter's graduation and to find grace in a small-town sermon: well, it sounds gooey (and in the film it looks it) but Mordden puts up a persuasive case for it as a dark masterpiece. ("Still, the guy is dead.") Reports from London suggest that a new and uncompromising production at the National Theatre has proved him right.

He also writes temptingly of the team's three lesser-known shows, the ones that never got filmed or toured or revived. Finally he faces up to The Sound of Music where the old themes come back softened and nobody dies: where nice nuns balance nasty Nazis. After saying some pleasant things about it he convicts it of hypocrisy - its happy ending comes too easy - and concludes, what is surely true, that its sugar and its success have made people think that all of R&H is like that.

Actually Mordden's last two pages are confusing. "Listen. European art is honest. . . . American art is dishonest." (Take that, Mark Twain. Wipe that grin off your face, Eugene O'Neill.) Hence the musical. But R&H "brought it close not to what life is like but what life is about." Does that make them uniquely honest, or forgivably dishonest, or is there some level of irony here that I don't get? In fact - Mordden is thoroughly convincing on this - they told what they believed to be the truth and if they never dwelt on the ugliness they seldom denied it either. Which sounds honest to me.

A sprightly writer with a gift for distilling slang into elegance, Mordden is oddly suspicious of other people's light-heartedness; it would, I fear, be hard to convince him that America's greatest musical is actually Guys and Dolls. He also, in this instance, goes light on the biography; Rodgers' more curmudgeonly traits are underplayed. But he is great at dramatizing main issues, and as an omnivorous buff he also explores any number of byways. Omnivorous but not omniscient - and I cannot resist pointing out a couple of gaffes. Howard Dietz did not write the words for the TV musical of High Tor; Maxwell Anderson did. (I bet you're glad to know that.) More alarmingly, he describes Rodgers and Hart's Love Me Tonight as an Ernst Lubitsch film. It was actually the work of Rouben Mamoulian, who a decade later directed Oklahoma! and Carousel on stage: a vital connection. I don't know if Mordden selected the pictures himself (233; 58 in colour) but whoever did, they're wonderful.
