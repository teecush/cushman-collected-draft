It is only in the last stages of RARE that there is any explicit mention of Down's syndrome. This is despite the fact that nine of the play's 10 performers have the condition, making it, inescapably, the subject. Judith Thompson, who assembled and directed the show from the actors' own testimonies, made a canny decision here. We think, for the greater proportion of the piece's one-hour length, that we are being let off the hook, and probably feel guiltily grateful for it. When, finally, we are impaled, the process turns out to be bracing and then healing.

Partly it's because by then we've got to know them as individuals, which also, in the way of theatre, makes them more powerful as a group. The actors begin by putting on masks, giving the impression that they are looking at us: also, perhaps, shielding both us and themselves. Then they take the masks off and introduce themselves by their real names: Sarah Carney, James Hazlett, Nicholas David Herd, Michael Liu, Dylan Harman Livaja, Suzanne Love, Nada Marie Christiane Mayla, Krystal Hope Nausbaum and Andreas Prinz. Those are the full versions, as listed in the program. In the performance itself, they only use their first names.

They also tell us their ambitions. The method recalls, of all not too unlikely things, A Chorus Line, and the show does indeed include some song and dance. At the end, after the mood has changed and the stakes have risen, they give their names again, and again tell us what they most want. The terms are different, and they themselves seem older. Instead of "I'm looking for a girlfriend" (James), we have "I hope one day to have sex" (Dylan).

The first part of the show works by making the familiar strange. At times this spills over, perhaps unavoidably, into cuteness; when Suzanne does a dance routine, enthusiastically and well, the audience response is vociferous almost beyond the call of duty. The vocal and instrumental contributions of Victoria Carr, a singer-songwriter who's the 10th performer, supply an air of muzzy uplift to proceedings that are otherwise very precise. A friendship-into-love scene, tentative but determined, between Sarah and Michael, is charmingly specific to the characters themselves; it also seems like everybody's fumbling, hopeful youth, caught forever.

The text includes some formal poetry. What may be the two most famous speeches in Shakespeare - Romeo's "but soft, what light through yonder window breaks" and Hamlet's "to be or not to be" - are spoken, to remarkably freshening effect. In the first, especially, the careful, emphatic delivery, and the bold but spare accompanying gestures, make you hang on every word; this may even be how Elizabethan actors spoke the lines, when they were new and when acting would have been natural by its own lights, probably stylized by ours.

The Hamlet speech comes in the production's last movement, and is shared among the performers as they grapple with their own lives. Up to now, they've seemed cheerful, resilient, even innocent. The resilience remains, but they now talk unsparingly about being bullied and insulted, about their love for lost siblings and grandparents (this is especially moving), about their fears of what may happen when their parents die, and about their determination to survive it. There's an impassioned plea to impending parents of Down's syndrome children not to have those children aborted: A plea, it seems to me, that only those children and their parents have the right to make. There's anger, too. Dylan swears about his condition barring him from acting school. If ability to transmit a powerful emotion is a criterion for admittance, he has a case.

The pleasures and the pains recounted and enacted here should relate to everyone's remembrances of childhood, with the confounding difference that we're experiencing here through people who, physically and intellectually, are not children. (They're mostly in their twenties.) It's possible - actually very likely - to approach this production feeling queasy; or, equally, with a determination to avoid that feeling. Both apprehensions feel groundless. I'm not sure if the title is meant to be a description of the show itself; but if it is, it's justified.
