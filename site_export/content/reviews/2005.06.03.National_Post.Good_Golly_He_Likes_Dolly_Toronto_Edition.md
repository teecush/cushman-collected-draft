HELLO, DOLLY!

Festival Theatre, Stratford

- - -

Lucy Peacock doesn't find herself in Dolly Gallagher Levi, the matchmaking factotum of Hello, Dolly!, until she reaches her big number. I don't mean the title anthem in the restaurant; I mean the first-act closer, Before the Parade Passes By, in which Dolly -- one of the show's two potentially merry widows -- affirms her decision "to rejoin the human race."

You can feel Peacock seizing on the preliminary dialogue in which she asks her late husband's blessing on her new departure; at last the actress has something to act, and it carries her right through the song, for which she mysteriously finds more voice than she had for her any of her earlier excursions, when the orchestra regularly drowned her. (William Hutt in The Tempest also husbands his resources, but more excusably and more productively.)

She also excels, when she gets to the title routine, at showing the woman's delight in returning to her old haunts and being courted; but the truth she brings to it only exposes the falseness of the number itself. It isn't about the character who's supposed to be singing it, and it certainly isn't about acting; it's about star status, justified or otherwise, getting cheered for coming down a staircase.

It doesn't help that the staircase here is less impressive than the one the designer, Patrick Clark, has provided for the miserly Mr. Vandergelder's hay-and-feed store back in Yonkers. Peacock does fine when she gets to sit down and vamp Vandergelder in the restaurant, and she's the only Dolly I've seen to take genuine pleasure in the adventures of the young lovers in the other part of the plot -- one thing she has in common with Prospero, for those who crave thematic parallels.

But most of Dolly is shtick, for which she isn't equipped. To play Dolly you don't have to be able to sing, exactly, but you must move the Earth with your voice. Peacock doesn't come from that tradition or have those skills, whether for the outrageous camping of Motherhood or the ragtime raucousness of So Long, Dearie. (An 11 o'clock number if ever there was one; it comes from nothing and goes nowhere.) Even the earlier dialogue bits defeat her; she sounds foggy and choppy, with an accent, Gallagher rather than Levi, that comes and goes.

The best all-round performances come from Peter Donaldson, whose Vandergelder is a sustained display of wit, and from Christina Gordon in a minute but evocative turn as a street trader. There is some delight, too, from Dayna Tekatch and Kyle Blair as the snivelling Ernestine and her swain, dragooned into exhibition dancing. As Mrs. Molloy, the other widow, Robin Hutton sings sweetly but hardly acts at all. Laird Mackintosh has some spirit, plus prancing-pony legs, as Cornelius Hackl, and Lawrence Haegert and Amy Walsh fill in nicely enough as the fourth couple.

None of them, though, can really compete with the athletic dancers in the Waiters' Galop, especially the ones who wield spatulas as if they were Indian clubs. Michael Lichtenfels' choreography throughout is in a different world from his work on The Tempest, though the number that's simply called Dancing is less rapturous than it should be; when the late Gower Champion staged it, it just about defined the spirit of musicals, or at least of musical comedy.

Susan H. Schulman's direction is rather more prosaic than that, though it tells the story well and has a good sense of humour. It has plenty of colour, too, especially in the costumes.

I never understand why audiences infallibly applaud revolving stages for revolving, or dancers for high-kicking in unison; surely that's the least you'd expect in either case. But I confess to being a Pavlovian sucker for anything that appears on stage in the likeness of a train, and the one here is dandy. It's featured in Put On Your Sunday Clothes, a joyously expectant number that's one of my two favourites in Jerry Herman's score. (The other is Elegance, which isn't by Herman at all but by Bob Merrill; which may explain why it has a couple of rhymes that are spectacular. Or, as it has to be pronounced here, spectackular. Its spruce pleasures are, in this production, spoiled by over-elaboration.)

Which brings me, at last, to the show itself. Its source, Thornton Wilder's vintage farce The Matchmaker, provides wit and style and a modicum of wisdom; the adaptation, by Michael Stewart, adds a bit more wit and considerable slickness. The score, if not the most distinguished in Broadway history, is like the whole piece an honest and successful attempt to make an audience feel happy. The tradition it inagurated could easily turn stupid and mechanical, as was demonstrated in Herman's very next diva-showcase Mame. But Dolly, anchored to earth by its setting in turn-of-the-century melting-pot New York, has substance. It's taken me 40 years to admit it, but Hello, Dolly! is a great musical.
