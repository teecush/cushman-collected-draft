HELEN'S NECKLACE

Tarragon Extra Space, Toronto

- - -

Helen is a Canadian woman, at large in an unnamed Middle Eastern city, and she has lost her necklace. "Lighter than air" is her description of it. Its pearls are plastic, and it has little monetary value, but it means a lot to her, and she is understandably upset at its disappearance. So she goes all over town looking for it.

En route, she meets a number of locals of both sexes, some of them more fluent in her language than others, all of them with problems of their own. A mother, in denial over the death of her young son, finally admits that the corpse that possesses her mind is that of her own child, not that of a neighbour's. Helen, abashed, recognizes that, compared to such a loss, that of a mere necklace matters little. But she still wants hers back, and her quest next takes her to a building site. Here a man lectures her on the state of the city and on what it's like to live in what is in effect -- maybe in reality -- a bombed-out refugee camp. His cry, and that of his people, is "We cannot go on living like this." He advises Helen to adopt it as her own, and she does.

I can't see that she has earned the right. The play is by Carole Frechette, the Montreal dramatist whose Elisa's Skin was seen in this space two years ago. She writes with a certain grace; or so at least I judge from John Murrell's translations. And she has won the Siminovitch Prize, which in Canadian theatre is the most prestigious award going, or at least the most lucrative. So I am probably in a minority in finding this piece tenuous, pseudo-lyrical, and -- in its arbitrary linking of large and small sorrows -- meretricious.

It's slightly more fun to watch than its predecessor, which was sternly anchored to a cafe table; this one's heroine gets to move about, mostly in a mimed taxi with an amusingly cavalier attitude to other traffic. But after the encounter with the bereaved mother, everything is anti-climax. Helen may come to accept the unimportance of her necklace in the scheme of things, but the play doesn't. It continues to treat the necklace as a potent symbol of something, and so, more alarmingly, does Helen herself. It stands, I would guess, for all the meaning that has leaked from her life.

But if I am seriously to believe that Helen cannot go on living like this, and to be affected by it, then I want to know how she is in fact living and how she has been living in the past.

We gather that she was part of a legation and that she stayed behind when her compatriots went home; the play also treats itself to a brief flashback about a break-up. It isn't enough. The indigenous characters are in specific predicaments. Helen seems to be suffering from a generalized Western malaise, calculated to appeal -- lazily -- to the audience's feelings of privileged guilt. We end on the customary note of modified hope, the two sides -- epresented by Helen and her driver, who has been patiently dropping her off and picking her up all day -- giving to, and learning from, one another.

Susan Coyne is Helen, insecure but determined; she has a shy smile that speaks volumes. I cannot fault her performance, but I have to confess that after her funny and passionate work of the last couple of years (at Soulpepper and elsewhere), I find its built-in literary droop is something of a letdown -- not in skill but in what's demanded of it. It has a sober charm to it.

As does Sanjay Talwar's assumption of all the other roles, though he seems so determined not to overact that they tend to blur, not at their edges -- you always know which is which -- but at the approach to their insides. Why his overtaxed taxi driver speaks much better English at the close of the play than he does at its beginning I cannot begin to explain.

Eda Holmes' direction is sensitive. The event as a whole is mildly interesting, mildly boring, and very Tarragon.
