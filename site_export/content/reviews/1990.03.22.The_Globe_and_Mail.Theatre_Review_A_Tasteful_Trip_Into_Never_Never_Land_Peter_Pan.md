THEATRE REVIEW A tasteful trip into Never Never Land Peter Pan

Thursday, March 22, 1990

ROBERT CUSHMAN

A musical production of the play by James M. Barrie Directed by Fran Soeder Starring Cathy Rigby, Stepehn Hanan and Cindy Robinson At O'Keefe Centre in Toronto BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail PETER PAN may not be one of the great U.S. musicals, but it is one of the most efficient and effective. It was done on Broadway in 1954, and conceived as a vehicle for Mary Martin, whose spirit - puckish rather than pannish - still hovers over it. It certainly does in the first-rate touring revival that opened Tuesday night at O'Keefe Centre in Toronto. The star, Cathy Rigby, as befits an ex-athlete on wires, does a pretty terrific hover herself.

The musical hews closely to the J. M. Barrie play. All the dialogue seems to come from source; there is no separate credit for a librettist. The songs, about 10 of them, are thriftily slotted in to accentuate the high points of the drama and they do this very well. I first saw the show some 10 years ago in a Broadway revival with Sandy Duncan as Peter. Soon afterward the Royal Shakespeare Company in London mounted a sumptuous revival of the original play that left me, in comparison, strangely unsatisfied. It seemed to cry out for music and for musical staging, especially at those moments when the story literally takes flight.

The musical also irons out some of the originals psychological creases. It doesnt stint on the bloodthirstiness: Captain Hook still terrorizes his crew by flourishing his implement, plots infanticide on a grand scale (he even speaks chillingly of a holocaust of children) and, in the lagoon scene, puts in peremptory orders for his favorite cocktail - drowned-Indian-maiden-on-the-rocks.

Pan, equally murderous in intent and more so in achievement, blithely disposes of a couple of pirates before insouciantly tossing their skipper into the jaws of his nemesis, the clock-eating crocodile. Death in this story is never far off, even for fairies. The show, like the kids watching it, takes all this in its stride. Barrie's bleak ending, where a grownup Wendy cedes to her own daughter her place in Peter's affections, is defused by a reprise of the song Never Never Land. This is the point where the show comes closest to Disney - heavenly choirs over the final credits - with the crucial difference that the song, a long-limbed waltzing charmer, is sweet without being sugary.

What we have here is not a re-invention of the original (like My Fair Lady) but a showmanlike and surprisingly tasteful distillation of it, with a strong emphasis on story. Harmonious as it all now seems, the original production, masterminded by Jerome Robbins, must have been a bloodbath. The neophyte songwriters, Carolyn Leigh and Moose Charlap, had half their original score thrown out; Peters anthem I Gotta Crow and the simple but rushingly appropriate Im Flying are theirs. Reinforcements were supplied by the more seasoned team of Jule Styne, Betty Comden and Adolph Green; Never Never Land, Distant Melody, and Hook's musical soliloquies are theirs. Hook, a baroque comic monster, is a very Comden-and-Green conception.

Apart from a tiny, tinny orchestra, the O'Keefe version, directed by Fran Soeder, has all the elements to honor its ancestry. Lost Boys and Indians do some splendidly infectious hoofing when pledging blood- brotherhood; the architecture of this number is authentic Robbins, comedy and drama built from simple steps.

Sets, climaxing in a luridly purple sky over the pirate ship, and colorful costumes abound in their own adventure playground, with the Darling family nursery the most mysterious locale of all.

Cathy Rigby, a spunky and surprisingly tuneful Peter, not only woos but wins the audience, and Cindy Robinson is an authentically bossy Wendy. Stephen Hanans Hook, intentionally or not, comes over as a spoof of Laurence Olivier in his Restoration fop roles. Its done effectively, with a kind of cringing panache, and indeed "Brimstone and gall, what cozenage is here?" is a fairly typical example.

It might, though, be funnier and more frightening to play against this, and make him a businesslike villain. He suffers dramatically from the fact that none of his schemes works, but we ought to believe that they might. My own children's favorite character was the crocodile. This may reflect on their upbringing, but he is an endearing specimen, a bit undersized, but then an exclusive diet of pirate (when you can get it) and timepieces is probably not very nourishing. Maybe, too, the reptile's simplicity attracted them. The croc with the clock is, after all, the least complicated figure in the piece. We know exactly what makes him tick.
