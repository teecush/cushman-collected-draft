Naughty wives and rural lives: Fallen Angels was deemed quite shocking in the 1920s.

FALLEN ANGELS

Avon Theatre, Stratford

WINGFIELD'S INFERNO

Tom Patterson Theatre, Stratford

- - -

The Fallen Angels of Noel Coward's 1924 comedy are called Julia and Jane. Each of them lives in a frightfully, not to say frighteningly, expensive flat in Central London. At least, we can see for ourselves that Julia does, and we have no reason to believe that the circumstances of Jane, who resides on another floor of the same apartment building, are any less luxurious. Both women are married, and though we don't know what their respective husbands actually do that yields such riches, we assume that it's Something in the City. Off duty, they're solid tweedy chaps whose main preoccupation is golf.

Julia and Jane have more in common than a postal address and identically placid marriages. Each, long ago, had a premarital fling with the same fella, a debonair Frenchman (he must have been debonair) named Maurice, who whisked one of them off to Rome and the other to Pisa for romantic times they wish neither to forget nor to repeat. Or so they say.

Now, with their off-putting spouses off putting, the ladies learn that Maurice is about to arrive in London, news that leaves them all a-tremble. This is Act One and it's promising. They settle down to await his arrival, fortifying themselves with food, drink and memories; nostalgia turns inevitably to competitive recrimination, culminating in a spot of outright physical violence. This is Act Two and it's hilarious, certainly in Brian Bedford's Stratford production. It's also a tour de force, since it's kept going entirely by the two old campaigners, with a maid popping in occasionally to provide sustenance. The lover eventually turns up, and so, less eagerly anticipated, do the husbands. This is Act Three, and we won't go into it.

The play was deemed quite shocking in the 1920s, whether because it showed respectable married women with erotic pasts, or because they actually talked about those pasts, or because they got plastered while talking about them. There's still a kick to it, not in the sex but in the lovingly orchestrated rudeness. Seana McKenna plays Julia and Lucy Peacock Jane, and they are Absolutely Fabulous. (The resemblance is more than passing, especially in the falling- down-drunk bits.) Both ladies are wonderfully served by the designer, Susan Benson, especially when they put on their party frocks. They are not so much dressed as sheathed, and they do battle accordingly.

McKenna, a rapier in green, makes the initial running, scoring point after point with the nasal drawl, as of somebody who feels temporarily superior, that she perfected in Private Lives. The first act is hers, but then she is playing on home ground. Peacock, a blunderbuss in red, seems at an initial disadvantage; personally, I felt very relieved when she took off her formidable hat and began to make herself comfortable, and I wouldn't be surprised if she did too. She proceeds to go from strength to delirious weakness until, thoroughly sozzled, she becomes the helpless victim of inanimate objects that should by rights be on her side. The goings-on around this point are sublime; Bedford's direction combines the verbal precision of his previous Coward productions with the physical madness of last year's Noises Off.

The maid in this play was always a good role in a quiet long- suffering sort of way (they call her Saunders because her predecessor's name was Myfanwy, and who can blame them) but here she is something else. Someone, it seems, has got hold of a previously unknown text in which she is good, not only at her own job, but at everyone else's. She is musical, far more so than her employers, and she knows exactly which golf clubs you should take to the links at Chichester. Omnisicient and omnicompetent, she is a female Jeeves, the only one I have ever seen on a stage. (Male clones are quite common.) Joyce Campion, at once meek and imperturbable, steals the show, an especially neat trick considering that neither leading lady ever lets go of it. That's ensemble, I guess. The last act, by its author's admission, is weak; it depends heavily on the long-delayed arrival of Maurice, who here is not sensationally smooth; while the husbands seem to be duffers, whoever plays them. The play does show Coward practising up for Private Lives which has exactly the same shape, only better.

Wingfield's Inferno, the sixth of Dan Needles' plays-for-one- actor about the ex-stockbroker who turned to farming, takes its name most obviously from a fire that destroys the Orange Lodge at Persephone township, leaving our hero-narrator Walt Wingfield lumbered with the task of raising money for its reconstruction. There's a metaphorical meaning too, of course, drawn from The Divine Comedy; Walt seems uncommonly literate for both his spheres of action, which may partly explain why, though liked, he always feels something of an outsider.

I wouldn't say Walt goes through his own personal hell, but he does periodically invoke a particular circle of Dante's penitentiary where reside frauds, hucksters and, by logical inference, insurance companies. The last are a recurring theme in this show. It might have been called Wingfield's Insurance.

The theme, as is customary, is first broached by local newspaperman Ed before the baton passes to Walt himself via his Letters to the Editor. The structure remains the same as in previous Wingfield plays; a series of anecdotes, mostly farcical and each apparently self-contained, all find their resolution in the denouement, like a whole team of shaggy dogs kept on a single leash.

Once again Rod Beattie, under the direction of his brother Douglas Beattie, plays Walt and all who come in contact with him. Serene and quizzical wife Maggie, stuttering brother-in-law Freddy, an assortment of self-interested lodge members and over-ambitious horse trainers, a baby and a dog: All are brought to instant life with the crinkling of a brow, the twisting of a larynx or the popping of a pair of eyes, the last being the distinguishing feature of a glorious new addition to the cast, a glad-handing member of parliament with a porous memory and a capacious thirst

The previous episode, Wingfield on Ice, reached its epiphany with the birth of Walt's and Maggie's daughter Hope. This new one tries for something of the same effect with less success; it feels like repetition. But there is plenty of joy en route, in Walt's slapstick encounters with skunks, animal and human, and in the tale of a dead- cert winner of a racehorse, done in by a change in the weather. Through it all Rod Beattie, a one-man carnival of baffled humanity, is reliably amazing.
