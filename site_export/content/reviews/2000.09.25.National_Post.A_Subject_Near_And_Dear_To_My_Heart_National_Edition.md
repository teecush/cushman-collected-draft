ST. NICHOLAS

Artword Theatre, Toronto

TORONTO - Conor McPherson is the young Irish dramatist whose play The Weir was seen in Toronto two WorldStage seasons ago, on which occasion it took the town by storm. That's criticspeak, meaning that quite a few of those who saw it liked it. I was one of them.

I also like the same author's St. Nicholas -- the play if not the present production -- which was written before The Weir and looks like a trial run for it. The Weir was a symphony of narratives; half a dozen people met in a pub and told one another stories, the last of which blew the mind of everyone who heard it, onstage or off. In St. Nicholas there is only one storyteller, talking directly to us. We never learn his name, but we discover most of the other salient facts about him. He lives in Dublin, where he works as a theatre critic, if you call that work. In the course of his story he takes another job as an assistant vampire, which the playwright, with the insolence typical of his trade, seems to regard as being a natural career progression.

Not until he got involved in the bloodsucking business, he tells us, did he know what real power was. As a critic he seems very hung up on power, he relishes it, unlike most of the reviewers I know, who don't think about it much and worry when they do. But then Ireland, and Dublin in particular, may be the last bastion of literary punditry -- a place where a critic can still cut a figure, even if an ungainly one, in pubs and clubs and coffee houses. Our hero is, he tells us, one of the five best-paid writers on his paper. (I've always liked the Irish.) He has got to this position by having opinions: not -- McPherson really goes for the jugular here - -by forming opinions, which would require serious thought, but simply by having them.

He drinks, as an Irish critic would; and he seems to consume beer and whisky indiscriminately and in stomach-churning quantities. He loves but neglects his wife and children. His endearing professional habits include writing reviews -- almost invariably hostile -- before seeing the play. On one crucial occasion he socializes with the cast, leading them to believe he liked the show so that their disappointment will be all the more exquisitely flavoured when they read him. Unfortunately he offers one of the younger actresses a lift home and on the journey finds himself seriously smitten.

I would say that McPherson has nailed some of the social and psychological quirks of our trade unnervingly well, but that his total picture is excessively dark. But then, as I said, things may be different in Ireland.

He has certainly created a character and evoked a milieu; and he does both without apparent sweat. The writing rolls captivatingly along, sharp in detail and rich in wit. The play that the protagonist has so impetuously slammed moves from Dublin to London - - for a shorter run than was originally planned, thanks to his review -- and he follows it there, intent on pressing his suit (which, from the rumpled look of him, could probably do with it). McPherson writes as potently of English seedy-bohemian life as of Irish. Eventually our critic tires of humiliating himself and accepts defeat. Subsequently he encounters a pale man named William who is always on the lookout for new blood and recruits our hero to procure it. William is no Dracula and his victims apparently don't become vampires themselves, and his new helper never partakes himself. (You can hear his mother objecting "they couldn't make you a real vampire?" Do Irish mothers say things like that?) Initially happy in his new position, he begins to revolt against it and eventually quits when -- inevitably -- one of his new recruits turns out to be his beloved. So he is saved -- giving up power in favour of unconsummated love -- though the details of the epiphany are vague. The play has a Christmassy title, so maybe he is like Dickens' Gabriel Grub or Ebenezer Scrooge, redeemed by contact with the supernatural. (Vampires, of course, are not good spirits, but William seems to wish he had a conscience, which -- as the narrator acutely observes -- is much the same as actually having one.) I found the second half of the saga less involving than the first, perhaps because I felt less personally implicated; I am not now nor have I ever been a vampire (I swear it) though I do know people who persist in writing musicals about them. But even if the latter part of the journey slackens, it's fun getting there.

The author is quoted in the program as saying that it's especial fun "when we have a well-known actor in front of us." In London and New York, the play was performed by Brian Cox, who is fairly famous and certifiably great: the best Titus Andronicus I've ever seen, one of the best Lears, and just a week ago, a disturbingly magnificent Hermann Goering on TV. He has massive presence. The Toronto performer is Adam Bramble, who isn't up to it. He looks right -- grey hair, pot belly, black shirt -- and he gets through his allotted 90 minutes fluently and comprehensibly. But he never sounds convincingly Irish, which immediately denatures the piece (I always though the rhythms automatically imposed an accent, but obviously I was wrong) and he seems to be having trouble with the idioms; he speaks of his first batch of a vampire victims as "a tidy half- dozen," emphasizing the adjective as if it were their appearance rather than their number that was in question. He is both emotionally and vocally limited.

Each of his lines seem to describe the same upward arc, and they all strike a pleading note that is both inappropriate and draining. What our theatre most needs: actors and directors who can put the iron into irony.

The director here, Dean Gabourie, provides occasional bits of production -- bringing the lights up for the hero to come among us, piping in crowd noises when he talks of entering a pub -- that are not helpful. If the play has a primary moral, it is that salvation lies in not being a vampire, and to that I would give instant agreement. Its secondary moral may be that salvation lies in not being a critic, and that I would like more time to think about.
