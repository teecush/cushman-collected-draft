THEATRE REVIEW LOST IN YONKERS

Thursday, November 05, 1992

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Toronto ONT -- WRITTEN BY NEIL SIMON Directed by Gene Saks Starring Mercedes McCambridge and Susan Giosa At the Royal Alexandra Theatre Rating: The New York setting and the presence of two brothers recall the charming fairy tale of Brighton Beach Memoirs Lost again in Simon's past BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Toronto LOST in Yonkers is Neil Simon being autobiographical at one remove. That, at least, is how I interpret its New York hinterland Second World War setting, and the presence at its core of two brothers, aged 13 and 15. These elements recall the charming fairy tale of Brighton Beach Memoirs, the first and by far the best of Simon's trio of self-confessed confessionals.

You can't be in two suburbs at once, and there is no way of reconciling Lost in Yonkers with the Simon chronology we already know. Put it that if the two boys of the earlier piece had lost their mother, been temporarily abandoned by their father, and been billeted on a belligerently reluctant grandma, Lost in Yonkers would be their story. They are, if anything, more convincingly drawn here than in the earlier play. Taro Alexander and Phillip Reese give remarkably unself-conscious kid performances, nourished by writing that is just precocious enough. Competitive but supportive, growing up uncomfortably fast, they make sharp, defensive jokes: the only jokes, though not the only laughs, in the play.

The kids have plenty to be defensive about. Dad has gone dangerously into debt to pay his late wife's hospital bills, and to make up the cash he must spend a year in the South, peddling scrap iron. His own mother's Yonkers apartment, above the family candy store, is the only place he can leave his sons. She is German, a refugee from pre-Nazi persecution whose life has been a personal crusade against sentiment, family or otherwise. She doesn't want them; they are terrified of her. Nevertheless, in grandmother's house they stay.

Two more of her grown-up children share the place. Uncle Louis, a smalltime hood, is one of nature's transients, but Aunt Bella is doomed to permanence. Strappingly in her 30s, she is mentally "a child. Not crazy. Not stupid. A child." Another sister has a bizarre vocal tic; Eddie, the boys' father, is weak but tries hard. Their upbringing ran to blows from their mother's cane, poisonously-tasting medicinal soups, threats of commitment to an asylum, actual confinement in closets, and some more ingenious refinements of blackmail masquerading as discipline. Their present deficiencies give the old lady more to blame them for. In her defence, Louis claims she made them tough, but Louis lies about a lot of things.

Simon sets all this up with breathtaking efficiency, and with great conviction. It feels lived. He is also pretty skillful at keeping it going. (Eddie's letters home keep hinting at a dodgy heart, a useful sword of Damocles to keep suspended over the characters' heads and ours.) Resolving it is another matter. Grandma is a splendidly imposing monster, secure in her own system and, when pushed, fiercely articulate in her defence. Mercedes McCambridge, seated most of the time, which she makes a position of power, embodies her to granite perfection. What is to be done with her? A comeuppance seems in order, especially from a playwright so committed to family values: maybe the belated revelation of a heart of gold.

This, of course, would be terribly sentimental, and Simon can be seen struggling with the problem rather like the critics who veer between reviling him as a jumped-up hack and acclaiming him for making money and art together. (Lost in Yonkers comes bearing Pulitzers, not generally a good omen.) Eventually, he compromises; not, I think, with his public - he never has - but with himself. He offers a feel-good ending; not quite a happy one but close.

This involves much speechifying. A lot of worms turn in this play - they have to, given the situation - and the spectacle is, as usual, heartening. It is not always convincing. Susan Giosa's Bella, a specialty turn that holds up surprisingly well, has a climactic outburst that is part affirmation, part cry for help. As she delivers it, it is moving. It has a great future as an audition piece. But its hectoring syntax, its throbbing delivery, its unheralded eloquence, are Hollywood sincere.

Bella also has psychic powers. She can casually turn on a radio, and catch the exact beginning of a song. It figures. This is a very neat show.

Lost in Yonkers continues until Dec. 19.
