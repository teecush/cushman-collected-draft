Amidst franchised mega-musicals, a selection of oft-overlooked plays by the Bards contemporaries gleams

LONDON - The Royal Shakespeare Company, rival of the British National Theatre since the two were created in the 1960s, has fallen on hard times. That at least has been the word these last few years; I have rarely been able to check it out for myself since the RSCs recent programming, in Stratford-on-Avon and especially in London, seems almost to have been designed to confuse. One never knows where they are, who they are, or what they're doing.

Adrian Noble, the artistic head and a fine director of plays if not of companies, has largely abandoned the RSC's London base at the Barbican (a space everybody hates, but they have to be somewhere), proposed tearing down the old "jam-factory" theatre on the banks of the Avon, and seems to have abandoned any idea of maintaining a permanent acting team.

Like the National, the RSC is about to change directors. Michael Boyd, youngish and still fairly unknown, is taking over, and he inherits at least one hopeful sign. Last year, in tandem with the regulation Shakespeares in the big house, the RSC mounted a season at the smaller, newer Swan Theatre of five lesser-known pieces by its patrons contemporaries, performed by a self-contained group of 28 actors. The whole season has now transferred to the Gielgud Theatre on Shaftesbury Avenue.

Its amazing. In the heart of the West End -- a theme park crammed with internationally franchised musicals and pop-song collections masquerading as theatre -- stand five plays, in rotating repertory, from the period academics insist on calling early modern.

The plays stand up, no question. All the same, I found myself at the Gielgud wishing I were seeing them at the Swan, which, of all the platform-stage houses built in the last half-century, is the only one to match the Canadian Stratford for impact and intimacy. It also became clear that, for all its enthusiasm and dedication, this was not a strong company. There was a lack, as in so much English theatre now, of actors who were a) experienced and b) good.

At the highest level, there was only one. Antony Sher is a star who, almost uniquely in his generation, has dedicated himself to the theatre -- and to the classical theatre (and this company) at that - - and the two plays in which he appeared were much the best. Sher is a gleeful and consummate technician for whom display has sometimes seemed an end in itself, but here he had roles that could soak up all the energy he threw at them.

In The Roman Actor by Philip Massinger, Sher played a mad emperor (if that isn't tautological), a sadistic tyrant with two fatally soft spots: for his empress (filched from another man who is executed for the impertinence of having been there first), and for the theatre. Naturally, his favourite woman cuckolds him with his favourite player.

Sher gave a silky, mesmerizing account of a man nine-tenths convinced of his own divinity, and if the barefaced audacity with which he switched between manhood and godhead seemed to belong to the actor rather than the character, the process was too enjoyable to occasion much complaint.

Massinger has fallen out of scholarly favour. The Victorians liked him because they thought he was moral; their successors have disdained him, probably for the same reason. (The Revels Plays, the premier publishing series in the field, has yet to issue any of his plays.) He thought this play to be his best, and the production (by Sean Holmes) suggests he was right. It has a good, clear political story, told by the company with great commitment, and its use of three plays-within-the-play, all impacting on those who act and those who watch them, is metatheatre as teasing and effective as anything before Pirandello.

The critical stock of John Marston, who flourished on the cusp between Elizabeth and James, fell in the last century as surely as Massinger's rose. Marston could never have been described as wholesome; his plays present a corrupt court world with lickerous relish, though he always pays lip service to some kind of moral order.

The Malcontent, his masterpiece, allows him the best of both worlds, being about a deposed and (by his own account) virtuous duke who returns disguised as a scrofulous and highly articulate cynic, a Prospero with a three-day stubble and a vocabulary virtuosic in its offensiveness. Sher seized on the part and gave it the soul of a mole, burrowing into human nature and finding it, to his delight, even worse than hed suspected. He looked mole-like as well, though straightening up, literally and impressively, for the happy ending. (This is a play in which murders are plotted, copiously, but never performed.) The production, by Dominic Cooke, moved the action to a Latin American dictatorship, which was not greatly illuminating, but at least bestowed an identity and some nice visuals.

Shers principal antagonist in both these plays was Joe Dixon, a strapping young actor not yet fully formed; he delivers strong outlines but sometimes seems to have boxed himself inside them. In The Roman Actor he had the title role, the unwordly star at the imperial court, entrusted with a speech in defence of his craft that became an anthology-piece. Dixon delivered it well but as a performance. It would surely be a richer irony if it seemed to come from his heart.

In The Malcontent, he was a ruthless but incompetent usurper, and gave us rather too much of the incompetence and too little of the ruthlessness; he was no threat. But he smoked a cigar with style.

Gregory Doran, the cheerful and formidably knowledgable young director who devised and ran the season, also directed The Island Princess by John Fletcher, the most obscure play in the quintet. (At least, it was the only one I'd never seen before.) It was also the trickiest, for Fletcher, the hit commercial playwright of the post- Shakespeare generation, is a very slippery writer. Facile is perhaps the word; he wrote easy, conversational verse that sits especially well on the lips of caddish young men, a type he seems to have drawn from life and that remains permanently modern.

The Island Princess is an adventure story set in what we now recognize as Indonesia. The Christians (Portuguese, but we're meant to identify) square off against the heathen (everybody else). The original audience would certainly have recognized and relished passing parallels to English colonial adventures, but to us its tempting to read the whole play as a progressive-minded satire, and a priest -- dressed up here like Osama bin Laden -- does have a bitter speech about Western exploiters. Hes also the villain in disguise, pushing his own agenda, but that irony, or whatever it is, went for very little since neither his villainy nor his rhetoric came compellingly across.

When the cultures square off at the end of the play, it's made clear that the Christians are always right. The titular heroine, formerly a fanatic, ends up embracing both the faith and the body of the handsomest invader.

The play, in fact, keeps falling in and out of seriousness. It's sophisticated by default, and though Dorans production, with a gamelan band, had fun and drive, the actors couldnt keep up with the currents. Maybe they lacked practice, both in their careers and in the preparation of this play. In Doran's scheme, all the plays were given short rehearsal periods, partly for economy and partly to recreate Elizabethan spontaneity. But the words can hardly come as speedily to us as they did to our ancestors. (There are those who claim the Elizabethans never rehearsed, but it seems unlikely. When Shakespeare wrote a burlesque rehearsal in A Midsummer Night's Dream, was he satirizing something that didnt exist?)

In Edward III, its the English who are always right, even -- or especially -- when they're laying waste to France, at an early stage of the Hundred Years' War. This chronicle, the earliest play in the bunch, has been attributed to Shakespeare, and has now been officially accepted into the canon. (Im not sure who makes these decisions.) But it seems unlikely. He would already have written the Henry VI plays, which are far more accomplished, and he is unlikely, at any date, to have come up with a phrase like "dusty heaps of brittle sand." (Come on now, which is it? Dust or sand?) The only passages that might sound Shakespearean concern King Edwards attempted seduction of the (married) Countess of Salisbury, and they are like the early word-drunk comedies like Love's Labours Lost.

Later on, the king goes to war, and faces other moral tests, but ends up always taking advice and doing the decent thing. There is a pattern here and it came through, but not very grippingly. It's like Henry V without the introspection, and David Rintoul, in the title role, gave it little more than a round voice.

There was one fine performance, from Michael Thomas as the vanquished French king, who passed effortlessly from a real man to the emblem of a ruined land -- the kind of double focus all these plays require. The same actor made an admirably despicable spy in The Roman Actor.

Weakest of the shows was the one outright comedy, Eastward Ho! which, as written, is a seamless collaboration between George Chapman, Ben Jonson and Marston. I had heard mixed reports of this show, and feared it might come out as a cheap romp. Watching it, I rather wished it had. This is one of the city comedies that were among the periods best inventions, partly a celebration of the thrift and honesty of London tradesmen, and partly -- perhaps mainly -- a satire on it.

Here it was hard to tell what it was, and the production was so slow that it was even harder to care. Much of the plays delight lies in its evocation of the town itself, complete with place- names; here, surely, was a case for projecting a map onto the back wall, rather than making us blink at a dark surround all night. The pivotal character, a scamp of an apprentice named Quicksilver, could hardly have been less like his name, but his goldsmith employer (Geoffrey Freshwater, an old RSC hand) at least spoke like a human being and, while on stage with him, other actors tended to do the same.

There were other good performances in all the shows, but not enough. (The women were uniformly weak.) And there are rewards in all the plays, enough to make the season seem more than the sum of the parts. I hope Doran does it again (he says he will) and I'd like to see our theatres -- Stratford most of all -- try some of the same. I can't imagine seeing any of these plays as often as Im required to see Shakespeare, without going mad, but there could be no richer or more helpful context for any Shakespearean theatre. In no other period do you get such a sense of drama inventing itself.

And we might even do them better. We dont necessarily have better actors, but our classical companies -- Stratford, Shaw, Soulpepper -- are better at keeping them together and letting them ripen -- better in fact at texture.

Coincidentally, the National Theatre has just opened a main- stage production of one of the best-known Jacobean plays, John Webster's The Duchess of Malfi. This is a more elaborate show than any at the Gielgud, and some of its ideas work well, especially the translation of the madmen, sent to torment the Duchess, into a sadistic video. This, you will have gathered, is a modern-dress production, directed by Phyllida Lloyd (Mamma Mia!), and the set seems to represent a football terrace.

Whenever somebody dies (which is often -- this is the classic tragedy) he or she gets up, walks to the stands and surveys the rest of the action. So the Duchess, killed off four-fifths of the way through, is kept in our minds, a neat solution to an old problem. Before retiring, each victim delivers a final speech to us -- an inspired notion, since these last words are proverbial meditations (Webster called them sentences), not natural speech.

Unfortunately there is little of this verbal sensitivity elsewhere, and the overall acting is poor. Janet McTeer's Duchess is diminished by whats going on around her and, as her brother, Will Keen, who knocked me out with his hysterical intensity in The Coast of Utopia, gives the same performance over again, augmented by a lot of crotch-grabbing. He leaves no doubt of his characters incestuous motivations but tells us nothing about them. He also sounds a lot less patrician than the jumped-up steward whose marriage to his sister he resents. Maybe the production is telling us that, in a pop- star age, all social roles are reversed, but it isn't carried through.

This, of course, is one advantage Canadian actors have. They are spared the crisis so neatly summed up by Alan Jay Lerner: "An Englishman's way of speaking absolutely classifies him/The moment he speaks he makes some other Englishman despise him." We are luckier, and should make the most of it.
