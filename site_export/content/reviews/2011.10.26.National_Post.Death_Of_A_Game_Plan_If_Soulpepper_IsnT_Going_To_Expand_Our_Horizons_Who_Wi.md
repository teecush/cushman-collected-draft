Soulpepper's 2012 season, its 15th, will contain a dozen shows. Two of these are revivals from previous years: Death of a Salesman, from 2010, and the perennial A Christmas Carol. Another is described as a "revisit": Daniel Brooks, who directed Samuel Beckett's Endgame back in the company's second season, is returning to it in what's announced as an entirely new production; I'm not sure how many different ways there can be of staging Endgame, but Brooks, whose previous production was a triumph, has certainly earned the right.

The new offerings include two Canadian plays: one, Lee MacDougall's High Life, about 20 years old, and the other - Kim's Convenience by Ins Choi, a former member of Soulpepper's acting academy - brand new, at least if you don't count a successful airing at this year's Toronto Fringe. There's also an English play from the 1970s, Home by David Storey, and an against-the-grain Soviet Russian work, Mikhail Bulgakov's The Royal Comedians, also known in English as Moliere and in fact about him. The balance of the season comprises, in order of composition, Kaufman and Hart's You Can't Take It with You, Eugene O'Neill's Long Day's Journey into Night, Arthur Miller's The Crucible, Neil Simon's The Sunshine Boys and David Mamet's Speed the Plow. Add the revived Salesman to those five and you have, next year as this, a predominantly American repertoire.

When I remarked on this in a telephone conversation with Soulpepper's artistic director Albert Schultz, he denied there was any particular plan about this and even seemed a bit surprised. I think I can see a rationale, one not limited to this company. There isn't, or at least there isn't yet, a Canadian canon, but there is an American one, composed of substantial modern plays that have name recognition and are more immediately accessible to both actors and audiences than the classics of Britain and Europe.

Long Day's Journey, by common consent the great American play, will have Nancy Palk and Joseph Ziegler in roles for which they have looked ideal for some years now, and will be directed by Diana Leblanc, whose legendary Stratford production of the same play looms in memory as something between a shining beacon and a hard act to follow. At the other extreme, though, I do rather relish the prospect of Kenneth Welsh and Eric Peterson as the Sunshine Boys, but I can't help feeling that one Neil Simon play, The Odd Couple, is enough. (Or, since it's been revived this year, more than enough.) We're talking after all about the company that first burst on us with Schiller's Don Carlos and whose signature production in my estimation (and, I discovered, in Schultz's as well) remains Chekhov's Platonov. Both were not just rediscoveries but revelations. This year's Soulpepper lineup has included just two plays from before 1900, A Midsummer Night's Dream and Ghosts, neither of them exactly rarities. Next year's doesn't have any.

Part of the problem, of course, is economic. The Soulpepper box office has never done well with Shakespeare; Schultz attributes this to Stratford being so close. Given the centrality of Shakespeare, I don't think it's healthy for Stratford or anyone else to have exclusive rights, but I see what he means. "If they're not going to come to see us doing Shakespeare, would they come to see us doing Middleton?" Schultz says, with reference to the rest of Elizabethan and Jacobean drama.

Maybe the only way to find out is to try; but, as he says, that could be expensive. Like nearly all plays written before the 20th century, indeed like most before the Second World War, these plays have a lot of characters. Soulpepper, by the way, this year and next, bunches its largest-cast plays together, which is a sensible use of resources.

More troubling, I think, is a second reason Schultz adduces: that the classics, as traditionally defined, are not the plays about which "the company" (defined as "a core of about 25 actors and directors," starting with the 12 founding members) "are coming to me and saying, 'I want to do this.' " The problem with that is it can be self-perpetuating; both artists and audiences lose touch with what's out there. If Soulpepper isn't going to expand our horizons, then who in Toronto will?

Maybe at this point I should bite my computer. When the 2011 season was announced, I looked at the list and thought the plays disappointingly small: physically and, for the most part, spiritually. I still miss the masterpieces, but in practice, and on a one-by-one basis, most of the new shows have been good and a couple (The Glass Menagerie, The Price) have been exceptional.

We also had a second chance to see The Time of Your Life, which should be everybody's favourite. It was one of four revivals, and the beginning of a plan to start an ongoing rolling repertoire, on the European model. I thought it was a great idea, on both artistic and commercial grounds; a show should be kept alive as long as people want to see it, while the flow of new productions should keep the old ones from going stale. But Schultz's team discovered that "you can't possibly keep the machine going; Toronto isn't Budapest" - people don't go to the theatre in the same numbers and with the same devotion, certainly not in a recession.

This year's most popular production has been The Fantasticks, but it isn't coming back; apparently it exhausted its audience. Also, scheduling became a headache, and patrons got confused.

"In a perfect world" says Schultz, "we'd keep Death of a Salesman in rep for two years." As a next best thing, it will be sharing summer and fall with four other plays in two theatres, the precise allocation of the last three weeks to be announced, depending on how Salesman fares against The Crucible. That sounds good, not least for the Arthur Miller estate; still, if the modification of the rolling rep ideal is logistically and financially understandable, it is also a pity, though a lesser one than the reining-in of the repertoire.

Soulpepper began with a season of two plays in rented premises. Now they do a dozen a year in their own complex. The more you do, the more misfires you're likely to have; besides, you can't be brand new forever. On the other side, productivity has its own excitement; and we have, uniquely in North America, a year-round rep with several of the country's best actors on tap, some of them for 15 years straight. It's a trade-off worth making. If, that is, the company can avoid sacrificing its soul and diluting the pepper.
