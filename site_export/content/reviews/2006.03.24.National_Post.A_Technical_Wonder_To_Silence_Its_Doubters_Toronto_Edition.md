THE LORD OF THE RINGS

Princess of Wales Theatre, Toronto

At the curtain calls for The Lord of the Rings, the stage itself rises up and takes a bow. It deserves to. It has been giving a flawless performance all night, and a breathtakingly versatile one.

Not only does it revolve, which perhaps is no big deal, it also rises, falls, divides and subdivides, frequently doing all these things at the same time. The multiplicity of levels and compartments is great for battle scenes, of which there are many, and for overall speed; the various strands of a complicated story can overlap, which is exciting in itself.

The theatrical version of J.R.R. Tolkien's trilogy has its faults, most of them coming unshakably with the territory, but scenically, technically and imaginatively, its a marvel.

Under Matthew Warchus implacably disciplined direction, hardly any of the effects are gratuitous. The bad guys look really bad. The first Black Rider, suddenly unleashed from nowhere, is as majestically scary an apparition as I have seen in a theatre, a giant skeletal amalgam of horse and man. This, like the presentation of the man-eating spider Shelob with visible actors pulling her giant legs, may owe something to the animal acts in The Lion King, but it's creative imitation.

The virtuous people get their share of spectacle as well. When Aragorn, the returning king, finds himself short of living soldiers to fight the armies of the Dark Lord Sauron, he calls on the Warriors of the Dead who arise, shrouded in mist, from what looks like a huge vat of seaweed: a thrilling finale to the second of the show's three acts and a really smart recruiting idea. One can think of many places now where it might come in handy.

Rob Howells sets, a forest of invention with withered branches as the governing motif, pull not just the stage but the auditorium into Middle Earth, as does Paul Pyants lighting, which, in its beauty and variety, calls for the coining of new superlatives.

With three books to get through, the show can hardly afford to waste time so it starts before it starts with its real heroes, the hobbits of the Shire, already on stage, indulging in simple hobbit pleasures, such as trapping fireflies and releasing them again.

We have a solid base to start from and, some three and a half hours later, to come back to, since the show includes the culminating episode of the Scouring of the Shire, which is more than the movie-version did. It's a noble choice, but as a payoff it doesn't pay off. It feels rushed, not so much in tempo as in lack of weight. Thats something that keeps happening, and its the main flaw.

The adaptation, by Shaun McKenna and the director, is outstandingly intelligent. Resourcefully, it brings in shadow-play and a disembodied narrator to handle the exposition that's left over from the earlier book The Hobbit. It later proves neat at telescoping: cutting out swathes of narrative and binding the remaining bits together. It keeps its eye firmly on the essential object, the Ring of Power, the original weapon of mass destruction; on the quest of Frodo to destroy it; and on the torments, physical and mental, of Gollum, who's enslaved himself to it.

Tolkiens books, of which Im an admirer but not a devotee, have two big things going for them. One is our involvement in a reassuringly simple struggle of good against evil, the Dark Lord Sauron and his underlings vs. everybody else. The other, which helps guarantee the first, is the sheer scale of the story, which amounts to a ready-made mythology, something you can get lost in. It makes up for any amount of cardboard characterization and sheer bad writing.

It isn't for song and dance. Everybody has denied that Lord of the Rings can be turned into a musical, including those who've actually been doing it, and its true that the songs here are mainly used as dramatic lubricants -- glorified incidental music, really -- rather than as things in themselves.

Mainly, but not always. Early on theres a full-scale, stage- filling hobbit hootenanny. It's irresistible -- Peter Darling's choreography is top notch-- but it relates to nothing in the show. It looks and sounds, by the way, very Cape Breton. The score is the work of an Indian, A.R. Rahman, and a bunch of Finns, Varttina, with an Englishman, Christopher Nightingale, co-ordinating; but its clear that while here theyve all gone native.

There is one song that works dramatically, holding our attention, deepening the action, projecting the characters. Frodo and his friend and servant Sam cheer up their weary selves by hymning one another's heroic deeds, then Gollum reprises their tune, turning it into a lacerating investigation of his schizoid self. Michael Therriault, whose Gollum suggests Ariel re-inventing himself as Caliban, here sets the seal on a performance that has already slithered into theatrical glory.

Comparably fine is Evan Buliung's Aragorn, a piece of genuine heroic acting, supple and plangent.

Some of their peers among Canadas best-singing actors -- or best actors period -- are shown to less advantage. Brent Carvers Gandalf is too little the tetchy teacher, too much the saintly sage. Carver's great qualities are vulnerability and mischief, and Gandalf doesn't have much of either.

Richard McMillan as the turncoat wizard Saruman has little to do but stand around in whiskers; and Dion Johnstones Boromir is killed off before he can come to life. The show simply doesnt have time for him, or for most of the other Fellows of the Ring.

Gabriel Burrafoto (Legolas) and Ross Williams (Gimli, Welsh like in the movie) are just token dwarf and elf respectively; Owen Sharpe and Dylan Roberts as the junior hobbits are lively, but I couldn't tell them apart. Shawn Wright gets some of the show's few laughs as the spokesman of the Ents, who guard the trees and, with the aid of wires and stilts, look like them.

James Loye and Peter Howe, the two principal British imports, play Frodo and Sam; and are admirable, though the show skates over Frodos inner struggles on Mount Doom (which, given the books exclusively male worldview, might not be too far from Brokeback Mountain). I'm not sure that what happens at these climactic moments would be clear to anyone who didn't already know.

Some of this has to do with the absence of an important character, the title character in fact. The lord of the rings is Sauron, who forged them and whose power depends on them. He doesnt make any personal appearances in the book, either, but he lives there in what people say of him.

We are told he has shrunk his being to a single malignant all- seeing Eye. Thats quite an image, one you would think a production high on visual coups would want to capture. But it doesn't even try. It proceeds to miss out on the one really moving scene in the book, the one in which the new king and all his court kneel to the dazed heroic hobbits.

As an unsatisfactory substitute we get a wedding ceremony at which the guests, in one of the show's less happy design choices, look like a convocation of beekeepers. But this is to carp. At its worst, the show is far better than doubters ever thought it could be. At its best, its wonderful.

BY THE NUMBERS

Here are some numerical breakdowns from the production:

$27-million Cost of The Lord of the Rings.

3.5 Running time in hours.

55 Number of cast.

500 Number of pieces of armour in the show.

150 Number of weapons.

7 Weight of the screen at the back of the set in tons.

40 Weight of the computer-controlled stage floor in tons.

30 Amount, in tons, of concrete that had to be removed from under the old stage to install the network of new steel supports to take that 40-ton weight.

17 Number of elevators in the stage floor.

20 Number of 4,000-cubic-foot sea freight containers used to ship the set pieces from England.

73 Number of people working behind the scenes on each performance.

808 Number of shoes worn by hobbits, orcs, elves and ents.

280 Number of gloves worn by the hobbits, orcs, elves and ents.

504 Number of costumes.

387 Costume changes.

5,000 Yards of fabric in the costumes.

37,155 Man-hours spent making the costumes and hats at Torontos Seamless Costumes.

400 Number of individual metal motifs hand-sewn on Elrond's costume.
