Until July 19. (416) 973-4000

PRESENT LAUGHTER

Premiere Dance Theatre

- - -

With Stratford, Niagara, Soulpepper and the entire Follows family currently presenting his plays, Noel Coward now unquestionably ranks as Canada's national playwright. It is a puzzling situation, since his best work is so firmly rooted in the milieu of theatrical London between the world wars, but maybe imperial nostalgia has something to do with it. That, and an appreciation, also nostalgic, for well- crafted comedies, shrewdly based on the twin actorish preoccupations of sex and self-absorption.

Perhaps taking a hint from the title, Soulpepper's production of Present Laughter - Coward's last major comedy, written in 1939 - makes an ambiguous attempt to drag the play out of its own time and into ours. The play is set in the house of Garry Essendine, matinee idol and partial Coward self-portrait, which here could pass either as a genuine glamorous home of the period or as some contemporary rich person's handsome recreation of it.

Garry's manservant has a spiky punk hairdo, his manager sports a ponytail. Costumes, to begin with, could pass muster as from either era, but gradually they cut loose and so, very much, do the props; in the second act laptop computers and Starbucks coffee mugs are flourished with a sort of casual bravado. It all seems, as Coward himself might say, very silly, and - as he probably wouldn't, but I would - giggly.

It is also, obviously, deliberate; and deliberation is very much a feature of Joseph Ziegler's production. "She is crying," says Garry's Swedish housekeeper of his latest conquest, who has been ordered to get dressed and leave, "which makes her slow." (My favourite line in the play; it's the Scandinavian accent that makes it hilarious.) I don't know if the Soulpepper actors have been shedding tears, but something -- maybe a desire to escape from the famous clipped Coward style, maybe a determination to give the characters and situations their serious due, maybe nerves -- has made their pacing glacial. They don't actually take all the fizz out of the play, but they certainly flatten it.

The production is not so much bad as wrong, and this is especially true of Juan Chioran's central performance. Garry is a light-comedy actor whose success has enabled him to build a personal theatrical empire, surround himself with a devoted circle of admiring colleagues and charm the pants off everybody.

Now, the modern trappings make most of this ridiculous; Garry's kind of theatre does not exist any more, in London or anywhere else, and his kind of actor would be in Hollywood making millions in sitcoms, or jetting off to movie locations. He would not be about to set off (by boat, yet) for a six-week tour of Africa.

More important than any of this: Garry is sunny. That's what draws people to him. He reveals a dark side under pressure; that's what's funny. Chioran is dour and devilish from the start; you may believe that every woman he meets will fall for him (there's no accounting for tastes) but not that all his friends would confide in him; and when the play shifts gears into farce, with him as its victim, we don't feel for him. Technically, he is fine -- he gets all his laughs -- but temperamentally he strikes at his character's roots.

Individually, most of the performances are good; though Brenda Robins' metallic vamp exhausts her charms -- which are initially very striking -- within a couple of minutes, and Martha Burns as Garry's ex-wife exudes a dispirited air of wishing she were in some other play altogether.

Nancy Palk, however, keeps things efficiently moving as the omnicompetent secretary, and Patricia Fagan gets the show off to an excellent start, lying on her back in borrowed silk pyjamas and phoning to boast discreetly about her night of passion with the great man.

I must admit, too, that in a few cases the modern dress helps; it enables Michael Hanraham and Neil Foster to make something more definite of Garry's producer and director than the usual stick- figures. I developed a soft spot, too, for Rick Miller's angry young playwright who comes to mock and stays to worship. Best by far, though, is Joyce Campion, in a league and a world of her own as the housekeeper who dabbles in spiritualism and so may be said to be outside time in the first place.

All the same, this will not do. It is the second consecutive occasion (A Flea in Her Ear was the first) on which Soulpepper has spoiled a perfect comic mechanism by disregarding its ground-rules.

Some playwrights -- I name no names -- are big enough, free enough, poetic enough and frankly old enough to exist in several styles or time-frames simultaneously. Feydeau and Coward are not among them.
