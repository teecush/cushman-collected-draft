FINDING REGINA

Theatre Passe Muraille, Toronto

There are Josh, Rae and Annabel, and they all grew up together in Regina. Josh still lives there; the two women have moved away, Rae to Vancouver and Annabel to Toronto. They're now in their thirties, and we meet them in a Regina hospital, where they have assembled in the waiting area outside intensive care. Beyond the door lies a friend and contemporary, who has tried to kill himself. The last time our trio were together was at a funeral. In fact, these days they only meet when one of their group has either attempted or committed suicide, but apparently this happens often enough to keep them in touch.

Finding Regina is a first play by Shoshana Sperling, much of whose previous experience has been in stand-up comedy. She is from Regina herself, and the most successful portions of this piece come when she marries her personal and professional experience: that is, when she manages to be funny about her hometown. Outside the hospital it's minus 78 -- that's with the wind chill, though there's heated debate, if that's the right phrase, as to whether the wind chill actually counts. Anyway, the characters seem to take it in their stride, even the two of them who have bunked it to more temperate climes. (Who says theatre can't be a moral force? It probably does the Toronto audience a lot of good for us to have our own winter put into perspective. I didn't know whether to laugh in guilt or shiver in sympathy.) They don't complain much, or slap themselves, or blow on their hands, and they don't seem that bundled up under their parkas. When the action shifts temporarily to the cab of Josh's truck, two of them even attempt to have sex in it.

Behaviour like this seems to have characterized their teenage days, and it's left its mark. They have made typical choices, typical anyway of people in plays. Josh is still leading what he claims to be the same carefree existence, indulging in sports, dope and everything female that's available. Rae made a smart marriage, whose smartness is coming into question. Annabel is doing a PhD in women's studies, and the author, who plays the role herself, has fun with the way in which she wrinkles her nose every time one of the others says something politically questionable; she combines the moral certitude of Lisa Simpson with the belligerence of Miss Piggy. The set (by Tamara Marie Kucheran) takes clinical sterility to new levels of barrenness. There are also slides (sharply done by Graham Powell) that make Regina look like everywhere else, only more so. They suggest this would indeed have been a rigorous place in which to spend one's adolescence. But then, what place wouldn't?

Regina, from what we see and hear, is as companionable, or as claustrophobic, as any small town. Annabel tells us that, on alighting at the airport, she got a full update on the suicide's condition from the baggage handlers. Josh claims to know everything that's happened to everybody he was in school with, and a sequence in which he asks the women to test him on this knowledge is very enjoyable -- remarkably so, considering we don't, of course, know any of the people he's talking about. The interest seems to transcend both geography and generation. Even those of us who've never been to Saskatchewan, or who could not care less about the relative coolness of Bowie and Pink Floyd, may find ourselves drawn in. But when the play tries to work on a level deeper than that of a game show, it comes undone.

This is yet another retrospective play. The characters' present existences are pencil-thin, but keep feeding us bits of their pasts, so they can have something to react to. But it's all very arbitrary. Their author can make them say whatever it takes to keep the party going. That's all right when the only object is humour, but not when we're expected to have some kind of emotional response. What we learn is commonplace. Rae, the pretty girl in school, could have boyfriends without putting out, while Annabel's position was the opposite; jealousy results. Josh was a mindless stud, whose prodigious memory doesn't extend to recalling the identities of all the women he slept with; resentment results. Josh may also be insecure about his sexual orientation; denial results. Many of their reminiscences converge on their comatose friend next door, but naturally where he is concerned, we have even less of a reality check.

The actors in Kelly Thornton's production do all right when required to be funny, and flounder when obliged to be emotional. Jeremy Harris comes off best since Josh, Regina unregenerate, is spared most of the soul-searching; at least it's directed at him rather than from him. The other two have set speeches. Rae's, as delivered by Teresa Pavlinek, are shrill, and Annabel's, as unrolled at length by Sperling herself, are strident. They demand our sympathy, but never give us grounds for it. Numbness sets in, and so, only a little later, does boredom.

This is a common occurrence in the theatre so, to cheer myself up, I have compiled a list of the plays that have opened in Toronto this year that, whatever else, have not been boring. It's longer than I thought it would be: Wingfield on Ice, Sunday Father, Little Mercy's First Murder, Dream Machine, Pinocchio, This Could Be Love, The Laramie Project, The Sea, Boy Gets Girl and -- to prove that not being boring is not necessarily the same as being good -- Vinci.
