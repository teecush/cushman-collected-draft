There was a beautiful musical on Broadway this season: a show gorgeously scored, cleverly staged, profoundly well acted, and honestly moving. The show was The Bridges of Madison County, and it has already closed; it didnt even, as has been the custom for shows in trouble, hang around on the chance of picking up a Tony or two on June 8. It has in fact been nominated in three categories, at least two of which - best score and best actress - it deserves to win. But it's not on the list for the all-important Best Musical, so the producers, understandably enough, gave up.

They must have felt they'd done the right things. Their librettist was a prominent playwright, Marsha Norman; the music and lyrics were by one of the best of the younger composers, Jason Robert Brown; and their star was Kelli OHara, one of the two top leading ladies to emerge in New York over the past decade. And they had an apparently pre-sold title, already popular as a novel and a movie. That last factor had in fact prejudiced me against the show; there was an air about it of, "What hasnt been adapted yet?" In the event, the musical proved to be its own creature, with a social and even emotional canvas greater than that of the film (I can't speak for the book, though I can't help suspecting that its less grown-up than the show.)

By contrast, the musical to which I had most looked forward - and the front-runner for the Tonys - was the one that most disappointed me. That, perhaps, is the way it inevitably goes. The show is A Gentlemans Guide to Love and Murder, based (in very small print) on the novel that also inspired the greatest of British film comedies Kind Hearts and Coronets. No official acknowledgment has been made of this joy of Guinness's performances lay in the subtlety and delicacy with which he sketched in each new murderee. Nobody could accuse Jefferson Mays, his Broadway equivalent, of tasteful understatement; he goes bull-headed at his eight roles, succeeding by my count at only a quarter of them. He is up for Best Musical Actor; so, more deservedly, is Bryce Pinkham as the man who, repeatedly, does him in, and who turns in a shyly witty performance, very different from Dennis Price's suave unruffled cynic in Kind Hearts but equally valid. Hes especially good in his scenes with the high-toned woman who wants to marry him, and the mercenary one whom he wants to marry, and whose attributes include having the most heartlessly funny songs. This guide is actually more reliable on love than on murder. The score has been much praised for reviving the Broadway tradition of literate cleverness, and it does try hard for Gilbert and Sullivan ingenuity; it actually sounds better on the cast CD (Ghostlight Records) than in the theatre. But it gets off on two wrong feet, and never quite rights itself thereafter; theres a monstrously coy tongue-twisted-incheek opener about how shockingly macabre the proceeding are going to be, followed by an expository number, unbearably overdone by Jane Carr, whom I remember from Britain as a delightful young character actress. Age and America seem to have coarsened her; it's ironic that an English (well, maybe Scottish) actress should exemplify the show's overwrought misconception of what constitutes English comic style.

Indeed The Bridges of Madison County, in addition to its other merits, was funnier. The emotional focus is naturally on Francesca, the Italian-born wife of an Iowa farmer and her one-night affair with the roving photographer who captures her heart and imagination while her husband and teenage children on Broadway and even with the Tonys; audiences and voters can take an unhappy ending provided it comes with uplift (see Les Miserables, Man of La Mancha and, though I hate to admit it, Carousel) but realistic bittersweet is upsetting. Francesca makes the choice, and lives with it. Browns lyrics are acute, his music sumptuous; and OHara was (is, if you get the CD, also on Ghostlight) magnificent. She already had me in tears with her opening number, in which she tells how she came to make a home in America while that home scenically grows up around her: tears, at that point, of joy at the glowing intensity with which she performed it (and, not incidentally, at how authentically Italian she both looked and - with no sacrifice of clarity - sounded) The joy extended to the Tony-unrecognized work of Bartlett Sher, the director responsible for the superb production of South Pacific that came through Toronto, twice, a few seasons ago.

I said above that O'Hara by the impressively versatile Jeanine Tesori who even managed a few years back to write a good score for Shrek. Violet isn't a new show but it deserves to snag Best Revival. As for the actual New Musical nominees: Im reduced to hoping that A Gentlemans Guide wins; at least it tries for something original while its competitors all have scores from prior sources: Beautiful, a Carole King songbook; After Midnight, the latest old Harlem anthology; the Disney Aladdin, much as I enjoyed it at its tryout in Toronto. I did unexpectedly enjoy the musical of Bullets Over Broadway; it was never my favourite Woody Allen movie, and the stage script, by Allen himself, is no masterpiece, though it has put him, perhaps surprisingly, on the Tony list. Up there too, less surprisingly since she's a favoured Broadway baby, is Susan Stroman's killer choreography: some of her best. This show too has a score composed of old songs: an idea thats fine in theory but always unfortunate seemed to be a plague, as well as bullets, over Broadway since of seven shows I saw in one week, three had understudies on in key roles; all were more than adequate.

As with musicals, so with plays: The smartest kid on the block has been ignored, in this case totally. Will Enos The Realistic Joneses, a quirkily disturbing comedy, rich in surprising character developments and delayed-action laugh lines, about two couples with the same surname who end up as neighbours for medical reasons. This play has been shut out of all categories, despite having a hot director (Sam Gold) and a cast consisting of a movie star (Marisa Tomei), two TV stars (Michael C. Hall, Toni Collette) and the author of August Osage County (Tracy Letts, a better actor than playwright). They all do impeccable work, and this show, unlike Bridges, seems to have beaten the system, at least for a limited season; It runs until July. I probably shouldn't pronounce on the nominated new plays by such established names as Harvey Fierstein, Terrence McNally and John Patrick Shanley, since I haven't seen them; I will venture that none of them sound very exciting. I did see Act One, James Lapines adaptation of Moss Tony Shalhoub as Kaufman and Hart and Harts father. Shalhoub, up for Best Actor in a Play, is in competition with, among others, a brace of Shakespeareans: Mark Rylance as Richard III and Samuel Barnett as Viola in Twelfth Night, both of whom I liked. Rylance is also nominated as Best Featured (i.e. Supporting) Actor for his Olivia in Twelfth Night, though he played it as if it were the lead; that isn't necessarily a compliment. He's up against two others in this all-male production: Stephen Fry (Malvolio) whom I would quite like to get it, and Paul Chahidi (Maria) whom I would really like to get it. This Twelfth Night was the production - imported from Londons Globe Theatre and directed by Tim Carroll, known in Canada for his work at the Stratford Festival - that New Yorks critics greeted as if they'd never seen any Shakespeare before. I actually preferred his ungarlanded Richard III production to his Twelfth Night and found the acting level, below the few performances cited, inferior to that in his excellent Stratford productions of Romeo and Juliet and King John, not to mention Peter Pan.
