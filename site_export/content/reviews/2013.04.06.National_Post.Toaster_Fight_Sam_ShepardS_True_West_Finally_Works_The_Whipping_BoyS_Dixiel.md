Aguy stands framed by the kind of swinging saloon doors that we're used to seeing in Westerns. Only we're not in a saloon. We're in a kitchen, in California: the non-Wild West. On the other side of it sits a man at a typewriter. (The year is 1980.) He's writing a treatment for a movie script. His name is Austin. The man in the doorway is his brother, Lee. Lee, best as we can tell, steals things for a living: small things, such as TVs and toasters. The play is True West, by Sam Shepard; and together the brothers represent what the West mythically used to be, what has become of it and, by extension, what has become of America.

I don't buy it. It seems to me that over the years Shep-ard has been given an easy ride by putting supposedly iconic characters, be they cowboys or rock stars, on the stage and expecting audiences and critics to fill in the gaps. This has resulted in some very tedious plays. True West is not one of them, but it has taken Soulpepper's crackling new production to prove it to me.

The point about these Western brothers, under Nancy Palk's direction, is not that they're western but that they're brothers. The upshot is an examination of sibling rivalry as searching and as funny as any in drama. A movie producer is apparently very interested in Austin's script. Lee, who's been asked to make himself scarce, bursts in at the end of their meeting (carrying his latest acquisition) and spins a yarn of his own invention that interests the producer even more. Lee, by his own admission, can't write, so Austin is invited to perform this tiresome chore for him. Austin says he would die sooner. Nevertheless, and of necessity, a kind of collaboration does develop. Inevitably it breaks down; and one of the play's choicer images is of Lee tearing out the typewriter ribbon when he tries to go it alone. Austin acts quite smug about this, as which author wouldn't?

It will occur to anyone watching that Austin and Lee are two halves of the same personality; their own mother virtually says so, when she makes a late return from a trip to Alaska, about as far as you can get from the traditional American West while still being in America, and is appalled to find the mess to which her sons have reduced her home. (Dad, whom we don't meet, is a toothless deadbeat, about whom Austin tells a very affecting story. He should turn it into a movie.) It's also a fair assumption that they represent aspects of Shepard himself, the cowboy-actor-playwright. That, though, is a matter of only passing interest; I sometimes suspect that you could take any two people and pass them off as opposite sides of something. These two are alight and alive as individuals and in their relationship: in their resentments and their jealousies and in the unbreakable bond between them. Also in their violence: both Stuart Hughes as Lee and Mike Ross as Austin can be truly frightening.

In fact, if Hughes is trying to present Lee as some kind of archetypal cowboy, he doesn't succeed; in fact he came far closer to it in The Time of Your Life. But he does excellently present a man with a grudge, a small-time con artist desperately (and sometimes hilariously) attempting to make his latest and biggest scam come true. There's a lilt in the way he carries himself. Ross may be the more surprising of the two, all very patient and civilized until he snaps, which he does in stages. Each brother attempts to muscle in on the other's territory: Lee because that's what he does, Austin because he's goaded. This initially results in a stage filled with toasters: a wonderful sight and, when all they prove operational, a wonderful smell; this is a play that appeals to all the senses. It's less persuasive when Austin begs to follow Lee into the desert; this plays as if the playwright were trying to live up to his own myth, or as if the Shepard were following the sheep. As satire on Hollywood, too, the play falls considerably short of David Mamet's Speed-the-Plow; indeed Ari Cohen as the producer seems to be giving a watered-down reprise of his performance in that play; he may even be wearing the same shirt. Patricia Hamilton, though, is beautifully brisk as Mom, who has returned home in hopes of a personal encounter with the already-late Pablo Picasso. You can see where her boys get it from. Ken MacDonald's set is, by some of his standards, restrained but still perfect; it also undergoes some very crafty quick changes during the blackouts. The final tableau, the brothers in terminal fang-baring mode, is unforgettable.

From the Wild West to the Deep South: The Whipping Man - by a new American writer, Matthew Lopez - takes us to a stately home in Richmond, Va., a few days after the end of the Civil War. Caleb, a Confederate officer, returns to the old family home to find it occupied only by a couple of former slaves. Simon, the more devoted of the two, performs a grisly but necessary amputation on Caleb's wounded leg. Caleb's family is Jewish, and they've brought their slaves up in their own religion. Well, it happened with Christians, hence all those spirituals; why not with Jews? The play's advertised key-scene is a Passover service. If this had happened before Emancipation, the juxtaposition of present servitude with a historic celebration of freedom, might have yielded a scene of huge ironic power, though admittedly it could have been a hard act to follow. As things are, the scene is delayed until the second act, by which time our noses have been rubbed in the situation, and the play has to rely on increasingly melodramatic revelations to maintain interest. The "whipping man" was a designated punishment artist to whose untender mercies John, the more rebellious of the black pair, was regularly consigned. These recollections yield a couple of quite powerful speeches, but hardly justify the play's title.

The Canadian production is, understandably, a joint venture by the Harold Green and Obsidian companies. Directed by Philip Akin, it's reasonably effective (as in never actually boring), though nobody could accuse Brett Donahue (Caleb) or Thomas Olajide (John) of underplaying. Sterling Jarvis gives a far subtler, and richer, performance as Simon but even he falls victim to the twists of plot. This is another of those prize-winning American plays that flirt with important subjects but never truly engage with them.

True West is in repertoire through May 4 at the Young Centre (416-866-8666); The Whipping Man runs through April 14 at the Studio Theatre, Toronto Centre for the Arts (1-855-985-2787).
