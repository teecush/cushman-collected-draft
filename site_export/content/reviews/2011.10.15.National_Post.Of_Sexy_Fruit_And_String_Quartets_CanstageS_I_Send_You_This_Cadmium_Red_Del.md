The Art of Time ensemble likes to merge all kinds of performing disciplines, sometimes throwing in some non-performing ones as well. Canadian Stage is giving its Berkeley Street platform to the ensemble's latest project I Send You This Cadmium Red, a double bill of which the first part is interesting and the second - which gives the evening its name - fascinating.

The unifying factor is the enthusiasm of Andrew Burashko, Art of Time's artistic and musical director, for the work of the British composer, whose music he describes as classical (I'm not sure that word means much anymore, but we know what he's saying) with a touch of jazz. The production takes two Bryars compositions, both of which appeared on CDs with spoken or sung components, and adds visuals to them. Burashko introduces the evening himself, and he's very informative, but I think he should either speak louder or invest in a microphone.

The first half is a revival of a preexisting piece, a ballet entitled Soudain l'Hiver Dernier, choreographed by James Kudelka and set to Bryars' music. The music itself was a response to a vocal performance: a four-line hymn, Jesus' Blood Never Failed Me Yet, sung unaccompanied and with no pretentions to technical accomplishment by an old man. This song is endlessly looped, while a string quintet provides its own instrumental commentary.

I suspect that if I heard it on disc the monotony would drive me mad, but here, of course, there's another element. Two dancers, Michael Sean Marye and Luke Garwood, perform a slow pas de deux of trust and dependence.

As for the singing - a continuous devout drone, dubbed in from the original disc, while Art of Time's own musicians provide live backing - I found that I was tuning it in and out; it took me time to accept that it wasn't gong to stop and wasn't going to develop, and I began to resent it. Gradually, though, I came to accept it as a necessary background that finally fused with the emotional effect of the dance. They illustrated one another. Oddly enough, considering that it was the initial spur for the whole show, it was Bryars' minimalist music that got squeezed out of the equation, at least in my consciousness. Intellectually, I knew that it was vital, but in practice it was one element too many for me to respond to.

I felt somewhat the same in the early stages of I Send You This Cadmium Red itself, but the feeling rapidly vanished. The musical piece (originally commissioned by BBC radio) that inspired this section was itself inspired by an exchange of letters between two British painters: one, John Berger, octogenarian and living in Paris; the other, John Christie, some 20 years younger and still at home. Christie is also a filmmaker and Berger one of the world's great art critics and art explainers. The title is the first line of a letter sent by Christie to Berger, extolling the glories and associations of a particular shade of red and enclosing a sample of it. This sparks off a long epistolary conversation in which they delightedly explore the properties of other shades and then of other colours.

This staged version, directed by Daniel Brooks, gives us two actors to recite the letters ("recite" is an unhappily lifeless word for what they do, but they mercifully don't "act" them) and two screens across which the colours they discuss appear, dazzle and dance. Julian Richings plays Berger and is wonderfully urgent yet relaxed; one of the hardest things for an actor to do is make a literary text, which these letters, however informal, have to be, and make it sound natural, conversational, without ever forcing or self-consciously dramatizing - and Richings brings it off perfectly. (Having music behind him probably helps; the onus isn't all on him.) John Fitzgerald Jay, an actor too seldom seen (in these parts, anyway) is Christie, patiently stoking Berger's fire. Among other things, we get a celebration of friendship. We hear of Christie's wife and of their new baby daughter of whom they are, obviously, joyously proud; the wife's name is Genevieve, and it's amusing to hear how Berger always gives the name its French pronunciation and Christie the English.

Among the things we learn from their back-and-forth is that yellow is the only colour that positively demands a form (though saffron, if I got it right, may be an exception); that blue, not just the blues, must have inspired and intoxicated Charlie Parker; and that, at least according to Berger, blueberries are the only kind of blue that is really sexy.

We're in on a kind of quest, which the music and the projections help make witty, urgent and eventually ecstatic. It made me wish that I could see the world, or at least see colours, as painters do. "Colour, and our need of colour," says Richings at the conclusion, as magisterially and yet as humbly as if it were a truth newly revealed, "is everywhere."
