BOYGROOVE

Diesel Playhouse, Toronto

- - -

Everything new is old again. If I were looking for a historical precedent for this amused deconstruction of the boy-band phenomenon - - and I suppose I am -- I'd go back to a musical of which the creators of this show have probably never heard and which they are certainly too young to have seen. Not to mention that it happened on another continent. I'm referring to Expresso Bongo, a British musical from the 1950s about a young rock-and-roller whose agent finagled him into stardom and got ditched and kicked in the teeth for his pains. That's Boygroove's story as well, multiplied by four.

The endearing similarity between that show's Bongo Herbert and the four close-harmonizers of Boygroove, who are called Boygroove, is that nobody, including themselves, thinks they're any good. They are among those chapters in pop history that the official culture refuses to take seriously; the show proceeds on that assumption and does nothing to challenge it. You can't imagine anyone writing a show this disrespectful about the Beatles or any other group or performer with pretentions; even the Supremes, in the Broadway musical Dreamgirls, got respect. But satirizing the Backstreet Boys is kicking at an open door. Not that kicking at an open door can't be fun.

It certainly is here; and, rather unusually, it gets to be more fun as it goes along. One reason for the delayed impact is that it takes time for the group to register as a group. And one reason for that is the performers' own cleverness. The actors who play Boygroove also play all the other people in their lives. In practical terms, this means that at most given moments the quartet is actually a trio, while the fourth member moonlights as their agent or their choreographer and harangues them.

There aren't many new jokes to be made about agents or choreographers, but this show does a spirited job of recycling the old ones.

Some things have changed since the '50s: Back then, the manager would have been a lone shark (and quite possibly a loan-shark as well); now, he's one of a whole school. He doesn't have to peddle his proteges to the Corporation; he is the corporation, or one of its spokes. He still gets disposed of, quite early on. His shafting is the agenda for the boys' first real scene together. They have, of course, had group numbers, but one of the more surprising things about the show is that most of the songs are filler. Aaron Machri's music and lyrics exaggerate the real generic thing just enough to seem comic, as do the moves laid on by Kay Grigar, but no song really takes the stage until the boys make their last goodbye (with The Last Goodbye).

By then, we've come to know and even like them. There is Andrew (Andrew Bursey), listed as The Sensitive One, a pudgy creation whose conscience troubles him because making a lot of money and laying a lot of girls hasn't left him with much time to save the planet; he has his own pet cause -- "Stop Right There, Hunger!" -- and when he mentions it, he gives a deeply-felt salute. There is Jon (Jon Paterson), The Bad Boy, lean and mean but not so much that he doesn't have dreams of being the first rock-star in outer space, which I can't help thinking is not the best idea either he or the show has ever had. (Well, maybe it is the best he's had.) There is Lance (Scott Walters), The Gifted One, he who spurns the groupies and whose belated emergence from the closet is the immediate cause of the group's implosion. And there is Kevin (Matt Alden) who is billed as The Leader, but whose most positive quality seems to be not being any of the others. The actor compensates, though, with a brief sketch of George Michael, skimming discreetly from a busted tryst to a waiting helicopter.

Boygroove's almost-nemesis is a rapper called HypeTastic; he tells the rock press how much he hates and despises the wholesome foursome, and they tell the pop press how they wish he'd just be tolerant like they are. I told you there was nothing new under the lights. It's just like Cliff Richard refusing to take the Beatles' bait when they made fun of him. But though with Cliff one always felt that he never quite got the point, Boygroove in these disputes make a lot of sense. In all, they're pretty realistic about their place in the world. They know they're a perishable commodity; at the height of their fame they reckon that they have three albums left in them, at most, plus maybe three movies "if our bodies hold out." I think it's Kevin who says that he wants to make $24-million before it's all over and frets that so far he's only made one. But those are the early days; he retires with $30-million. Clearly he has his eye on the ball, and so (despite a few wastes of stage time, like Kevin's girlfriend and an interlude involving Mr. and Mrs. Tom Cruise) do Chris Craddock's script and Kenneth Brown's direction. Both are fast, astute, and friendly with attitude. And if you want to find the Diesel Playhouse, it's where Second City used to be before moving across the street; and the chairs, tables and drinks remain the same.
