I've lost count of the number of adaptations Ive seen in recent years of August Strindbergs Miss Julie, each of them transferring the 1906 Swedish original to some closer time and place. After Miss Julie, by the British playwright Patrick Marber, is the only one of them that works. If it weren't for the awkward fact that it couldn't exist without its predecessor, Id say that it was better than Strindberg.

In Strindbergs play, Miss Julie, a nobleman's daughter, loses her virginity to Jean, her father's valet, in the heat of a Midsummers Eve celebration. Daybreak finds her ashamed but inflamed, careering between excoriating her new lover and planning to run away with him, a project they both, for different reasons, realize is hopeless. Marber tells the same story, sometimes word for word, but sets it down in England, on VE Day, with a Labour government just voted in: so, a double celebration. The unseen father is now a Labour peer, though one whose politics are never going to get in the way of his privilege; as his chauffeur, John, the reincarnation of Jean, bitterly remarks. John, a demobbed soldier returned to the estate on which he grew up, is both a rebel against the class structure and a prisoner of it. So, in her way, is Julie, the English rose with thorns rampant. At every turn, and with nothing seeming forced, the new local and historical references make the play more immediate than Ive ever known it. Also more economical: always before, I've grown impatient with these class-crossed lovers' twists and turns. This time, I hung on every word and every silence.

In David Ferrys production (for the new-to-me Red One Theatre Collective) the intertwined tensions, social and sexual, could be cut with a knife. Ferry, who must be our most undervalued director (witness The Last Days of Judas Iscariot, and the similarly titled Life of Jude at this years Summerworks, and now this) knows when to linger things out and when to slam them violently shut. He also designed the set and lighting, which are moody and evocative. There's a radio onstage, playing 1940s dance music and rarely turned off; at first it threatens to be a distraction, but as the action progresses, its unheeding smoothness does more than provide an ironic counterpoint; it actually has the effect of turning up the heat, which in these performances is already extreme. They're

also dead accurate in time, place and status. Claire Armstrongs Julie is the image, visual and vocal, of ravaged English entitlement, temptress and tempted in one. Christopher Morriss John is a burning compound of resentment, calculation and desire. Amy Keating brings a biting northcountry practicality to Christine, the cook and John's fiancee, in whose kitchen the play takes place and who, alone of the three of them, knows her place and how to take advantage of it. Marber gives her one hilariously vindictive moment that Strindberg never thought of; though, to give the Swedish devil his due, his ending does possess a tragic inevitability that isn't quite encompassed here. In all other respects the emotional logic is impeccable; the first night audience, in a tiny makeshift space, was spellbound. This, with the possible exception of The Double, is the best show in Toronto.

Speaking of endings: The Sacrifice Zone, a words-and-action piece from a Canadian company working with an Australian playwright, has a denouement that rocks. It makes sudden sense of plot-points that until then have been kept mysterious. Its so effective that what has gone before seems for a moment more substantial than it did at the time. Then the spell lifts.

The play deals with an explosion at an industrial plant, fictitious but obviously meant to be emblematic, in which five people were killed. Most of the victims families accept a compensation package from The Company (thus referred to throughout, shades of Prison Break) that runs the town, but one bereaved husband and one bereaved wife hold out for a government inquiry. Meanwhile a Company geologist falls in love with another employee and marries him; but is dismayed by what she discovers about the firm's ecological practices. Her husband is less idealistic; so is nearly everybody else in town, faced with the possible shutdown or removal of the industry on which they all depend. The play weighs the human and moral costs of justice, and it recognizes ironies and complexities. But it doesn't flesh them out. Theatre Gargantua, a self-described multi-disciplinary theatre company, surrounds Suzie Millers skeletal script with electronic music, a set consisting of constantly reconfigured door-frames, and a great deal of mime. Well, all right, thats what they do; but it mostly seems fussy and repetitive, especially in the domestic scenes. Under Jacquie P.A. Thomas's direction, a cast of five, all grown-ups but playing characters of all ages, throws itself enthusiastically about; Michelle Polak and Sam Benson can be quite touching in scenes involving a mother and son, the latter with a superhero fixation that turns out to be trouble. But they all have trouble making the semaphore text sound natural.

Layne Coleman and Linda Griffiths face similar problems in Griffiths' play Heaven Above, Heaven Below, though for different reasons. Here the dialogue is too arch and brittle to be credible as conversation but not witty enough to be enjoyable for its own sake. Its a sequel to Griffiths 20-year-old play The Darling Family and maybe you need to have seen the earlier work (I haven't) to get invested in the newer one. Two people named only as He and She meet in a boutique hotel room to discuss the abortion She had as a consequence of their brief affair, also 20 years ago. So the play makes a pair with George F. Walker's Moss Park, the previous Passe Muraille twohander, also a sequel and also involving an abortion, though in that case contemplated rather than achieved, hence involving suspense and possibility rather than just recrimination. As He and She contemplate their comfortable surroundings, compare their comfortable careers, do pot and coke, and wonder whether theyll have drinks from the minibar or sex on the bed, I found myself in unexpected sympathy with Ford Nation. (Well, maybe that doesnt work for the drugs.) Coleman at least manages to give He an appealing shaggy slyness, though I found it hard to believe in him as the musician he claims to be. But maybe that too is all in the past.
