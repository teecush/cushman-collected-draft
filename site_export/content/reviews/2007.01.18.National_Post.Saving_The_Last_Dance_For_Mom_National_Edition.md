The departed banned in China THEATRE REVIEW

Singkil

Factory Studio Theatre

Catherine Hernandez's play Singkil is set in present-day Scarborough, but takes its title from a centuries-old Philippines dance. It remains a living tradition and depicts, so we are informed, the travails and eventual triumph of a princess lost in a forest. She eventually finds her way out with the help of a prince, though the play also tells us that the prince was a later addition and that in the pristine version she worked out her own salvation. This may be very empowering but it makes what already sounds like a vague all-purpose scenario even mistier. In this play it's a metaphor that won't do what its author wants it to do, and so turns into an unwanted superstructure.

The princess here is Mimi, who is in her twenties, the daughter of Filipino immigrants. Her mother Maria recently died, though she gets plenty of stage time in dreams and flashbacks. Maria was a former Singkil performer, who gave it up to get married, against the advice of her friend and dancing-partner Norma. Norma used to dance the handmaiden to Maria's princess and seems now to be carrying this role over into her real life. She arrives in Toronto in the wake, so to speak, of Maria's funeral with the declared intention of looking after Nestor, the husband, and the undeclared one of healing the breach between Mimi and her late mother. Mimi had hated Maria for reasons that, when revealed, seem less than Earth-shattering, at least in theatrical terms. (Most of us feel more tolerant about the behaviour of people in plays than we might about the behaviour of people in real life.) She also hates Singkil, for reasons that go beyond a young person's routine resentment of alien customs that set her apart from her peers; with Mimi it's personal. The play traces how she comes to love and forgive her mother; or, to put it another way, how she learns to dance.

The parts are all there, decently written but loosely assembled. The show is the second major offering, following Banana Boys, from the Asian-Canadian theatre group fu-Gen; and dance, as you would expect given the story, plays a prominent part in Nina Lee Aquino's production. Indeed, it begins with a dance, an erotic pas de deux that's very striking, though it turns out to be not an illustrative introduction to Singkil itself but theatrical shorthand for some actual love-making between Mimi and her boyfriend Chase. Or maybe -- I doubt

it but we aren't given enough information to decide -- what we are watching is one of those later Singkil accretions, with Chase cast as the unwanted prince. He does a lot of sticking around, though Mimi keeps changing her mind about whether she wants him or not. She can certainly act like a princess, in the uncomplimentary sense, and so, we gather, could her mother before her, whom marriage and motherhood in a new country left feeling bored and marooned. We are left, when all is finally explained and atoned for, with the feeling that the love of mother and daughter is a fraught but beautiful thing. But we knew that already and the play does little to make it new.

The later, more emblematic dancing never seems more than decoration, though it's tastily done (choreographer, Clare Preuss). Real Singkil is apparently performed on a symbolic forest of bamboo poles, and Camellia Koo's set pays tasteful homage to that concept, at the expense of suggesting any convincing Canadian domesticity that might set it off. Nadine Villasin's Mimi is very immediate, if occasionally rough at the edges, while Karen Ancheta's Maria smoother but more distant (even given that she's dead). Rose Cortez finds it hard to get beneath Norma's aggressively chatty exterior, and Leon Aureus, required to play the unassuming Nestor at two widely-spaced periods of his life, seems stranded midway between them. Chase, the boyfriend, is given a lively performance by David Yee, an Asian actor who looks European, which in this play is confusing. Chase tries, a little too eagerly, to reunite Mimi with what he presumes to be her own culture, and it makes a difference whether he is doing this from inside or out.

The play bears a fortuitous resemblance to Pedro Almodovar's Volver; both have estranged generations of women finding reconciliation, skeletons in family cupboards (more numerous and extreme in the movie) and a mother with a musical past, though the Spanish one's talent is for singing rather than dancing. It's unfair to compare a beginning playwright with a brilliant director at the height of his powers: The liquid movie is obviously in a different class from the lumpy play. In one respect, though, Hernandez' vision is more inclusive than Almodovar's; it finds a place for men, and -- Singkil revisionism notwithstanding --a generous one. Both are fundamentally warm-hearted works, but the play has structural cracks that make it feel chilly. - Until Jan. 28. 416-504-9971
