THAT DOROTHY PARKER

Canadian Stage Theatre Upstairs, Toronto

Dorothy Parker wrote fewer funny poems and less funny prose than is generally believed, and her more earnest ventures in both forms can be dreadfully soggy. Her one-liners have become so familiar as to seem mechanical. But she maintains her hold on our imagination: as the most durable of the self-anointed New Yorker wits of the '20s and '30s, as the only woman among them, and as someone who suffered with style and made sure everybody knew about it.

Resume -- her eight-line masterpiece cataloguing the disadvantages of all available methods of suicide and concluding "you might as well live" -- ranks with Philip Larkin's "they fuck you up, your mum and dad." They may not be the greatest poems of the 20th century but they are surely the most unanswerable.

In her one-woman show about the lady, an evening that swings metronomically between mediocrity and atrocity, Carol Lempert rushes breathlessly through Parker's canon against self-slaughter while struggling into a pair of shoes. Naturally, the points get blunted. Some of the more lascivious Parkerisms -- "one drink more and I'll be under the host" or "if all the girls in Vassar were laid end to end, I shouldn't be in the least surprised" -- she delivers in a tone of self-conscious schoolgirl naughtiness, with matching smirk. She doesn't seem to have an ear, a failing bad enough if you're delivering formal poetry and absolutely fatal to light verse.

The neat little ode that ends "I'm due to fall in love again" is massacred by having the emphasis placed on "again," which is filler, instead of "in love," which is what it's about. She also speaks, in prose this time, of "the elevated eyebrow-school of journalism" instead of "the elevated eyebrow school of journalism." If you can't hear the difference I'm sorry for you, but there's nothng I can do.

Anyway, you're not up on stage reciting the stuff.

In addition, the show -- directed, to spread the blame around a bit, by Mark Wilson -- has structural problems. Its jumps in time and space are fidgety. It is supposedly anchored in Parker's old haunt, the Algonquin Hotel, on the day in the Second World War in which she learns of the death of critic and broadcaster Alexander Woollcott, her long-time friend and sparring-partner.

Their relationship is the pivot of the evening; she keeps reminiscing about it. I may be biased, since I once tried to get through a whole book of Woollcott's appallingly arch prose, but he seems to have been as intolerable a man as he was a writer, snobbish and witlessly rude. The attention he gets here, considering the show is meant to be about someone else, is wildly disproportionate.

Valiantly but awkwardly, Dorothy tries to fill us in on the other regulars at the Algonquin's famous Round Table. "Edna" she name- drops, then switches into the expositional-interrogative.

"Edna?" she repeats, her inflection insistently rising, "Edna Ferber? The author of Show Boat?" A few minutes later she's at it again, with "Mr. Benchley? Robert Charles Benchley?" Benchley -- a nicer man than Woollcott and a funnier, though at this distance almost equally perishable -- is invoked here as good uncle to Woollcott's bad, or second banana to his preening ego. He never comes to life, though. None of them do.

And, though I revere the New Yorker and love the Algonquin, I suspect that their joint legend does not stand up to close inspection. They were not, most of those guys at their wisecracking alcoholic lunches, all that good.

Parker herself seems to have suspected it. Her increasing disaffection is one of the more interesting things about her, and one of the better things about this show.

We hear about her championing of the framed anarchists Sacco and Vanzetti, of her journey to the front in the Spanish Civil War. There is a scene of her phoning her young husband, stationed abroad and obviously tiring of her, that begins touchingly but that the actress cannot sustain.

Missing, in whole or part, are Parker's two finest prose arias: one in which she waits distractedly for a lover's phone-call, raising brittle self-pity to something like art, and The Waltz, a hilarious stream-of-consciousness while dancing with the partner from hell.

They might be difficult to stage, especially the second, but not, I think, impossible. Done well, they might centre the evening. It needs it.

I learned one thing that seems all too appropriate to pass on: Harold Ross, editor of the New Yorker, arrived late at lunch, his excuse being that he had spent the morning writing obituaries. Woollcott rejoined that he, Parker, and Benchley were all theatre critics and had been doing exactly the same. It hurts me to say it, but I couldn't have put it better myself.
