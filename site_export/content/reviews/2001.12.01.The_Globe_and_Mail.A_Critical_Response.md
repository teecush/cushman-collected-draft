Simon Heath (letters -- Nov. 28), responding to Kate Taylors article on theatre critics, brings up again the old chestnut about the critics' (or reviewers') conflicting responsibilities to their readership and to the art form. We are always being asked about this, and I have never understood where the conflict is supposed to come in. If critics describe and explain their responses to the shows they see as lucidly and entertainingly as possible, they are doing the best they can by both parties.

People sometimes talk as if we were torn between the demands of the artists, who want us to say nice things about them, and of editors, who want us to pan everything because it makes good copy. This is a myth. For critics always to write raves might be good for individual artists, or at least for their egos, but it would not be good for the art form, and intelligent artists know that. Nor is it true that negative reviews are more fun to read, or even to write, than positive ones; in fact, the reverse is often true.

Michael Noon (letter -- Nov. 29) asks, although I cannot believe he means it seriously, why critics dont review a productions closing night and thus spare themselves and their victims the risk of "significantly reduc[ing] the revenues of many well-intended productions." (Aren't all productions well-intended?) There's a fairly obvious answer: If we like a show, and say so early in the run, we stand a chance of increasing the box-office takings.

My impression is that most theatres would rather take the risk and have us there. They do not give us free tickets because they love us personally or even because they revere our opinions. Reviews are a form of publicity; at the very least, they let readers know that a show exists.

Everything Ms. Taylor said in her article seemed to me 100-per-cent accurate. And it is not that common for the two of us to agree -- as I'm sure she would agree.
