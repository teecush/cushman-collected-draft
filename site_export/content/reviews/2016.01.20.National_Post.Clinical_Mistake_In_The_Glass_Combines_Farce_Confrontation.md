Through an incredible hospital foul-up, a woman becomes an unwilling surrogate mother. The embryo she is carrying is the biological property of Michael and Darah, a married couple in their forties who for the last six years have been trying, desperately and unsuccessfully, to have a child of their own by more orthodox methods. In other respects, they are extremely well off (Michael is a banker; Darah designs video games). The bearer of their test-tube offspring is named Linda; she and her husband Scott are in their thirties, have a daughter of their own and are scraping by (Scott is a poet; Linda is a musician).

Anna Chatterton's Within the Glass shows us the two couples meeting in person for the first time. Michael and Darah are hosting, and they're doing it in style (Julie Fox's living-and-dining-room set is luscious). This is partly because they can afford it, and partly because they're very nervous. They don't know which of the options open to Linda, now at the end of her first trimester, she will decide to take. Will she keep the child as her own? Will she have an abortion? Or will she carry the baby to term and then give it to Michael and Darah to adopt? The last choice of course is the one that Michael and Darah devoutly hope she will make. Linda herself seems disinclined to oblige.

The Tarragon has mounted a first-rate production of a problematic play, one that begins amusingly, and moves on to some powerful confrontations, but destroys itself halfway through. This is when one of the characters makes, offstage, a crucial decision whose reasons are never made clear. By way of cover-up we get an extended foray into the comedy of over-eager embarrassment. Even before this, the play has been split in its tone. Half of it is anguished personal drama, focusing on the agonies of sterility and with assumed socio-political overtones. The other is a comedy of manners, or perhaps a comedy of awkward hospitality. The richer couple don't want to appear patronizing, the poorer couple make it clear that they feel patronized.

This is all very amusing to begin with, and it offers some rich double meanings, especially when the subject is food. Michael, announcing dinner, says "we'll all feel a

little better with something in our bellies," not perhaps the most tactful thing to say to the pregnant Linda. She, a vegetarian, objects strongly to the offer of a chicken dish: "It's never just a chicken, it's a genocide for chickens." The chicken, in another biological double-entendre, is a castrated one: a capon.

When the foursome stop smiling and start shouting, the conflicts tend to be vivid but free-floating, unmoored from what surrounds them. They do, however, provoke some really exciting acting, beautifully orchestrated under Andrea Donaldson's direction. As Linda, the designated earthmother alternately resenting and glorying in her fecundity, Nicola Correia-Damude gives an appropriately overflowing performance, drawing on impressive reserves of anger and longing.

Where she's all curves, Philippa Domville's Darah is all angles, coyly flirtatious as she sets the table and welcomes the guests. Brittle and apparently hysterical as the evening proceeds, she too finally gets to release her rage, storming at the woman who has accused her of delaying child-bearing for the sake of her career.

Michael, it turns out, is even more frantic for parenthood than his wife. Rick Roberts, who starts out as the picture of a suave accommodating host, loses the suavity while ratcheting up the accommodation to self-immolating levels. Paul Braunstein's roughneck Scott could hardly be further from the conventional idea of a poet, but it takes all sorts, and his jovial cynicism, biting the hands that are feeding him while relishing the food, is an excellent contrast to the earnest concerns of everybody else. While the wives attack one another, the husbands grow closer together: self-defence perhaps, given that both couples will eventually get around to airing some longfestering grievances. There's a suggestion that Michael and Linda are attracted to one another, or at least that he is turned on by her fertility, but it seems to be there because that kind of thing is expected in a play. At a certain level the action is farcical. Once we had comedies about children swapped at birth, now we have them swapped in test-tubes. If one of the play's purposes is to discuss the ethics and consequences of in vitro ("within the glass") fertilization, then maybe the discussion shouldn't have been based on a clinical mistake.
