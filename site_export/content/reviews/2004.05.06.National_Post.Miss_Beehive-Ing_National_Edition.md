HAIRSPRAY

Princess of Wales Theatre, Toronto

- - -

If you were to judge Hairspray by the coherence of its plot, which is not an exercise I recommend, you would not be impressed.

In Baltimore, a teenage girl, overweight by conventional beauty standards, conquers all rebuffs to get into the dancing chorus of her favourite TV pop show, and eventually gets crowned Miss Teenage Hairspray 1962. In beating down doors for herself she also manages to open a few for her black friends, who are being marginalized by the local network. Thats all. If the show weren't so energetic and entertaining, it would be unbearable.

It gets you on its side from the first moment, which shows you our heroine, chubby Tracy Turnblad, on her back -- in bed, which has been propped up vertically, and singing about getting up in the morning, which she proceeds to do. She shing-a-lings her way through Good Morning, Baltimore, a song whose lyrics pretty much set the tone for the evening in their lovably cheesy optimism ("every day is an open door") and in their opportunism (there's the flasher who lives next door, which is sort of funny in its casualness while letting us know that this show is to be squeaky clean with the just the occasional dab of sleaze to maintain its self-respect.) The music sets a tone, too; you cant, looking back, tell one tune from another but each one, or nearly each one, is infectious when it happens.

The hidden master of the current Broadway musical -- and indeed of the current Toronto musical -- may well be Thomas Meehan, who collaborated on the books of both this show and The Producers; and who long ago wrote the script for Annie; he knows, in other words, how to make musicals work as cartoons. (And probably Hairspray, like The Producers, tames the film that it adapts.) Cartoon is an apt mode for Hairsprays slice of pop history; the show takes us back to that peculiar rock 'n' roll interregnum between the reign of Elvis and the ascension of the Beatles -- a bubblegum time still sufficiently in sync with older modes for to use square old words like "square." One number is called Welcome to the 60s, and it sounds ironic; this is really the pre-60s, with Motown just beginning to break and Connie Francis still a force in the land. It's an era memorialized in its own time by Bye Bye Birdie; but for Hairspray, Marc Shaiman (music and lyrics) and Scott Wittman (just lyrics) have written almost an entire score in a style that Birdie used only for occasional spoofs. In this show, it's the odd traditional Broadway number that comes across as pastiche.

The TV show that Tracy so desperately wants to be part of seems to be an extension of her high school; indeed most of its performers are her fellow students, including Link Larkin, resident-heartthrob and aspirant Elvis. We have spent several scenes thinking that Tracy is worshipping Link from afar, before the show thinks to slip us the information that hes really been quite near all the time. (The fact that in this production he looks a good 10 years older than she does abets the confusion.)

The principal threat to Tracys ambition is the show's producer, Velma Von Tussle, who hates her for her unglamorousness, epitomized by her weight and her big bouffant hairstyle. Velma's ideal of beauty is her daughter Amber -- svelte, blond, untalented, and another of Tracys unexpected schoolmates. Velma and Amber are of course the shows villainesses; and though villains are supposed to be fun to watch and to act, these two just get in the way. They aren't people you love to hate; they're simply irritating. Velma herself is an ex-beauty queen, the former Miss Baltimore Crabs. That is not a funny enough idea to sustain a whole song, but she sings one anyway.

Apart from being a second-grade schemer who never looks like winning, Velma is a racist; she denies it (I love Sammy Davis Jr, and hes Negro and a Jew), but shes aghast at having black people or black music on her show, even though everybody else, including her star DJ, seems to think it a great idea. Tracy certainly does; she spends a lot of time in detention (her hair has obstructed her classmates' view of the blackboard) and the black kids she meets there help make her a dancing star.

Tracy's mother Edna, initially shocked by her daughters ambitions, soon changes tack and gets right behind her. If Tracy is plump, Edna is mountainous, and the show is about empowering her as well. At least, thats supposed to be the idea; though, as I noted when I saw the show in New York, your credibility in proclaiming that Big is Beautiful is somewhat compromised when you cast the biggest woman of all as a man. It's best, though, not to tell this show to get serious. On Broadway Edna was played by the gravel- voiced Harvey Fierstein, as endearing as hilarious. Jay Brazeau, who plays it in Toronto, goes through the same motions, voice included, but the life and the warmth and the outrageousness aren't really in him. Nor is Vanessa Olivarezs Tracy up to the standard of her predecessor; when she dances, which is what shes meant to be about, it isn't an event. But she sings well and acts appealingly, and it's neat to have a veteran of American Idol in a show about its innocent TV predecessor.

Susan Henley is bland as Velma, and Charlotte Moore, cast as a trio of dominatrices (if thats the plural), plays each with the same half-hearted Satanic grin. Fran Jaye has some vocal swagger as Motormouth Maybelle, a matriarchal black DJ who talks mainly in rhyme, and who also sings the inevitable "believe-in-yourself" number; these songs in musicals are all indistinguishable, and theyre all guaranteed to floor a white audience that thinks it's getting soul as surely as the folks at Cookin' at the Cookery think theyre getting jazz. Anyone who objects to the stereotypes in The Producers should really be shocked by Hairspray; at least The Producers has the wit to play them all for laughs. Hairspray stops being fun when it stops being fun. In this version, it has two performances that show absolutely the right way to go, sharp outlines delightfully filled out. One is Jennifer Stewarts as Tracy's tearful and eventually triumphant best friend; the other is Tom Rooney's as Turnblad Senior who runs a joke shop (the Har Har Hut) and dresses the part. He describes himself as "a crazy loon," so Rooneys three-month passage from Hamlet to Hairspray may not be as much of a stretch as it looks. But he gets maximum underplayed impact from every spoken line, and he is obviously having a whale of a time in song and dance. His affectionate duet with Edna -- a sort of loud soft-shoe -- is the number most improved from New York.

The most deteriorated is a womans prison routine that now looks and sounds like a blurred copy of the Cell Block Tango from Chicago. Some knowing echoes from earlier musicals (Edna keeps quoting from Gypsy) are clearly designed to keep the buffs happy: but I have no idea whether a scene on a compartmentalized beehive set that resembles The Telephone Hour in Birdie, is meant as an homage or merely indicates the director, Jack O'Brien, and the designer, David Rockwell, are running short on ideas. This isn't something they do very often; OBriens production has great pace and involvement, and Rockwell's scintillating backdrop is, by Kenneth Posner, even more scintillatingly lit.The costumes by William Ivey Long, usually a wizard, are disappointing, even the outsize fashions for Edna, though she's great in her working garb as a laundress. Jerry Mitchells choreography may not be strikingly imaginative, but it sizzles, as do the corps that performs it. The closing number is called You Cant Stop the Beat; and even if you could, you wouldn't want to.
