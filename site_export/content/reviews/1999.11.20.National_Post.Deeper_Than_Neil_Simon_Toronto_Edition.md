Alan Ayckbourn used to be called Britain's answer to Neil Simon, both of them being prolific comic playwrights hugely successful in their native markets but a hard sell outside.

The comparison doesnt hold; Ayckbourn ranks far below Simon as a wisecracker. In fact, he writes few quotable lines though he occasionally comes up with beauties. In Communicating Doors, a lady off-handedly remarks that no woman in her right mind wants to relive her honeymoon.

Theres a depth of casual misanthropy there that Simon would never aspire to. In fact what distinguishes Ayckbourn from Simon (or his Canadian step-cousin Norm Foster), is that he never balks at bad behaviour, never tells comfortable lies, personal or social.

He also plays far more complicated technical games. Simon wrote Plaza Suite, three disconnected episodes in the life of the same hotel room. In Communicating Doors, Ayckbourn takes Simon's conceit and crosses it with Hitchcock's Psycho, J. B. Priestleys time-warp plays and The Lion, The Witch and the Wardrobe.

We begin in the future. It is 2019, and Poopay, a young dominatrix, has been summoned to the hotel to minister to a rich old man called Reece. It turns out that he doesnt want discipline, or even sex. He wants her to witness his confession. He has been married twice, and connived at the deaths of both his wives, though the murders were actually committed by Julian, his sinister aide.

Learning what is afoot, Julian chases Poopay first into the bathroom (definitely a place of ill omen), and then into an adjacent store room, which she locks against him. She finds it not only a refuge but a time machine. She emerges to find that it is 20 years earlier (now, in fact) and occupied by Reece's second wife Ruella, whom she warns of her future fate.

Ruella is finally convinced and goes back a further 20 years, by the same route, to perform the same service for Jessica, the first wife, who proves harder to persuade. And so it goes, the doors constantly swinging, the time constantly changing, and the women charging through both in order to rewrite history and save their own and one another's lives.

Time-travel stories always make my head ache; if you could go back and change things, the ramifications would be endless. Youd need parallel universes. Ayckbourn cleverly builds these quibbles into his scheme, and the piece is a structural delight. Its also a thriller that thrills -- though this production seems uncertain how far to stylize the melodrama -- and a comedy that's funny, though less so here than it might be.

It is indeed one of a kind, even in its author's capacious canon (53 and counting). He has sometimes been gratuitously dark, as if saying "you didnt expect a boulevard playwright to pull this, did you?" He may be, apart from Aristophanes and Feydeau, the only comic dramatist never to write a love scene (as opposed to a sex scene). But he has his feelings, and they seep through the cracks. The title refers to more than one kind of communication. The play expresses an unforced concern over an increasingly uncaring and dehumanized world (in Ayckbourns future even video stores have given way to "virtuality shops"). It's also a female-solidarity play: one in which the dominatrix really learns control. And it ends with a touching, and utterly unexpected, epiphany.

Ayckbourn's flaw is that he doesnt create memorable characters. They do what is required of them, but only the old couple in Bedroom Farce (his best play, and a minor classic), escape their authors jurisdiction and insist on a life of their own. On their home ground, his plays benefit from top-of-the-line casting. This doesn't happen here; Janet Wright's production is long on pace but short on flavour.

The performances -- notably Aviva Armour-Ostroff's Poopay -- markedly improve when the action takes over and they can do rather than just be. Kate Trotter briskly controls the proceedings as Ruella. Bruce Hunter is the actor charged with putting together the errant Reece's pieces; the character is really three separate roles, and he falters in the first two but does the last beautifully. The most in style, though not quite there yet, is Robert Persichini as a sad-sack hotel detective; he is, embryonically, a joy.
