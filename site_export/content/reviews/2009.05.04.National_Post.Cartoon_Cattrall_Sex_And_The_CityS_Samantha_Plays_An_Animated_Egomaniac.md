An oddity of Producing Parker is that the title character isn't the star, though she is the protagonist. This new animated series is named after Parker Novak, a hard-working and capable TV producer in her late twenties, working on a daily TV program called The Dee Show. The Dee in question is an ego-driven talk-show host who soaks up all the attention, onscreen and off, while relying on underlings like Parker to do all the work. Kim Cattrall is the voice of Dee, and she's the only performer with a credit in the opening titles, even though its Kristin Booth, playing Parker, who does most of the heavy lifting on the show itself. No reflection on Cattrall but this really does seem a case of life imitating art. If you call it life; if you call it art.

Producing Parker begins niftily enough, with a good old-fashioned theme song. A 1950ish vocal group informs the heroine "you have to work your butt off / but you dont regret it / now you're in your element / with a full screen credit" not to mention you've got your staff / and the last laugh. Its catchy, though it does recall the title song of a certain Broadway musical, one that also concerned producers. Once it gets to story, however, the show is in the uncomfortable position of satirizing a genre that satirizes itself.

A lot of it has been done before, sometimes by shows with live actors. Parker, the plucky professional at the mercy of exploitative no-talent employers, is the cartoon version of Tina Fey in 30 Rock. She has an office full of wacky associates but no real friends and no love life. She does, however, have a talking dog, a device even more irritating here than it is on Family Guy. Its also borrowed from that show a habit of going off on split-second tangents. Whimsy can be used for satire, but it isn't the same thing. The commentary on The Simpsons -- back in its good days --depended on our believing, at some level, in the characters and in a sense that the show meant what it said. South Park, Family Guy and, now, Producing Parker don't bother to mean anything; theyre too concerned with being the cool beyond cool. They arent as smart as they think they are, and they aren't as fast. They're sitcoms that take advantage of animations limitless capacity for special effects, but not of its potentially dizzying depth of wit. They mistake frenzy for invention.

The format of Producing Parker is that Dee, while doing her show, will discover that someone has something she doesnt and that her whole team, mainly meaning Parker, will have to devote the next segment of their lives to getting it for her. The first show finds Dee hosting on a discussion on "what matters most to women" -- i. e. jeans -- and discovering from a British designer called Indigo Jones that she herself is posterior challenged --i. e. she doesn't have a fashionably big bum. (If that is the fashion, but plainly Dee will believe anything if it makes her feel insecure enough.) "Indigo Jones," says Dee indignantly, can kiss my ass. "If she can find it," a subordinate murmurs. That's quite funny, but the show goes on making the same joke for the next half-hour, with commercials for something called Booty Barn, scenes at a bar named Heinies and the loyal Parker trying out a posterior pump which gives her so much ballast that she goes flying through the air like Mary Poppins and gets mistaken for a terrorist. Talk about forced.

Episode Two is better (strange how often I find myself saying that) since Dees obsession is a little more rooted in reality -- hence more fruitfully comic -- and she herself puts in more than token appearances. This time, after hosting a show on model moms, she decides that what she wants is a baby. Too busy to do it the orthodox way ("You told them I'm a celebrity?" she asks indignantly when Parker relays the information about the nine-month waiting period), she settles instead for adoption. Chicago, Parker's pneumatic blond intern (amusingly voiced by Sarah Cornell, who played a similar role, in person and with a Swedish accent, in the Toronto production of the actual Producers), rings round the agencies and finds one that guarantees delivery in 24 hours or no charge. Once the blankets are off, the baby turns out to be Siamese twins, prompting Chicago to say its got two heads -- its gonna be so smart. So indeed it is -- I mean, they are: genius level, in fact. Of course it's Parker who has actually to look after them, and who proves a born mother, and of course they go missing just as Dee's about to present them to her TV audience, leaving the bereaved star to scream the reasonably immortal line, "This is the worst thing thats ever happened to me -- were talking about dead air, people." Dee, by the way, literally breathes fire when she's upset, like when someone mentions her age.

Others laying about the place include Simon (Aaron Abrams), a would-be writer who's relaxed to the point of total disengagement and is Parkers nearest thing to a two-legged buddy, and the studio head, a Martini-sipping dandy named Blake. Hes yet another Brit (though played by Peter Keleghan, who as far as I know is Canadian) and would rather be doing reality shows, featuring either sharks (he keeps one in his office) or zombies. Parker has a crush on him; strangely, as she's supposed to be so sensible.

Making Parker, rather than Dee, the pivotal figure is a bit of a cop-out and also self-defeating. If you have a monster as your central character, then you should make her central, rather than trusting that your audience will root for the underdog. (Parker is everyone's underdog, including the dog's.) Egomaniacal TV hosts can offer infinite comic potential. Remember The Larry Sanders Show. Think of what Stephen Colbert does four nights a week. And those shows were/are tough. Producing Parker only pretends to be, like so much TV comedy. Well, like so much comedy.
