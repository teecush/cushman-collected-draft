Elaine Stritch at Liberty opens tonight at the Elgin Theatre, Toronto, and runs till Saturday. At the box office, all Ticketmaster outlets, 416-872-5555 or ticketmaster.ca.; robertcushman@lycos.ca

After a lifetime on the stage, Elaine Stritch feels she is finally 'free up there' to do and say as she pleases - hence the title of her show

Elaine Stritch performs her autobiographical show, Elaine Stritch at Liberty, this week in Toronto, looking very liberated indeed in a man's shirt and black tights, the latter revealing a truly remarkable pair of legs. On more formal occasions she dresses up, casual but elegant. Her previous booking was in Chicago, and when I met her there last week, at the Ritz-Carlton Hotel, she was a symphony in beige. She wore taupe (her word) gaberdine slacks, a silk blouse, a Paul Davis knit tie, her favourite sweater -- given to her by her maid in New York ("her name is Nancy Torres and she knows how to pick 'em") -- and a lapel pin held over from her first- act costume in the Toronto and New York production of Show Boat, 10 years ago.

And does anyone still wear a hat? Stritch does. She's her own answer to the withering question that she asks in The Ladies Who Lunch, the Stephen Sondheim tour-de-force that she introduced in Company, in 1970, and that remains a cornerstone of her repertoire. It tops off the ensemble perfectly. It's also utilitarian ("I can put eight rollers in front of my hair, put a hat on, and I don't have to have my hair down when I'm not working."), and that combination is basic Stritch.

When she is working, she wears the tights-and-shirt outfit ("it's very comfortable"), causing John Lahr, theatre critic of The New Yorker and her writing collaborator on Elaine Stritch at Liberty, to look up at her during one of their sessions and dub her "an existential problem in tights" -- a remark that went right into the script and that she calls "a gorgeous line." Lahr also threw in references to "things like Samuel Beckett," and indeed Stritch, with the yearning cragginess in her voice, could be very good in Beckett.

Right now, though, and for the last two years, she has been offering what may be the most personal of one-woman shows: some of her signature songs from musicals, linked by the story of the consuming passions of her life -- for the theatre, for the men she has loved, and for the bottle. They are intertwined, though not, as it turns out, interdependent. She has been able to do without the last of them. The show is its own provisional happy ending; she has been able to score the biggest success of her career after staying on the wagon for 15 years.

She was talked into doing the show by her producer, John Schreiber. "He said school will start at 10. Somebody hits me with a schedule, I'm there. It's when I'm left to my own devices I am scr- r-ewed. Royally screwed."

When Sondheim came to see the show, and she told him how terrified she was, he said, "Elaine, terror becomes you."

I asked her if performing was where she went to get away from her insecurities, and she referred me to the name of the show. "One day John Lahr put a little note in his pad, At Liberty. He said, 'I've been kicking around some ideas about what this show means, and it means you're at liberty to say any damn thing you want.' I said, 'That's the title, John, that's the title.' But I don't actually mean that I'm free to say anything I want ... Because I'm on the high side of 70, and older people can say exactly what they want, but I don't feel that's what the show's about. I feel that I'm free at last by feeling secure, and I accept my life, and I'm not sore about anything, and I do feel free up there. I do not feel that liberty offstage. I hope I will someday. I'm working awfully hard on feeling secure after the curtain comes down. That is so difficult for me, because I'm leading a sober life in every way, so I'm fully aware of how tough it is to relax. It is tough for a dame like me to put her feet up and say, 'I'm gonna have a bran muffin and a cup of tea or look at the ocean or take a walk.' I can't get there."

She seems to have the tea part down all right. Our meeting at the Ritz took place over a civilized tray of Earl Grey (it followed a prolonged period tramping the hotel's capacious lobby and cafe area to find a table quiet and comfortable enough) and it all seemed rather English. This seems fitting, since Stritch lived in London -- at the Savoy -- during the '70s and '80s. A TV series, Two's Company, in which she played the rich and hyperactive employer of Donald Sinden's imperturbable butler, made her Britain's favourite American broad. Her only marriage was to English actor John Bay. His nephew, George Bay, joined us at the end of the interview; he lives in Chicago and produces English muffins -- a commodity, by the way, unknown in England.

The marriage, by Stritch's own account, was a great one. "And maybe it's like all things that were just great, it was brief." It was cut short by her husband's early death. "So I don't know about that love business. I know I had something very special with John. And there wasn't a lot of emphasis on the obvious -- I mean the sexual -- it was just wanting to be with somebody. I haven't had anything like, since John died, the feeling of calm I had when he was alive. I almost reverted right back to the nut that I was before I married him." Nowhere during our conversation did she sound more serious and, well, sober than when she said that.

Her feelings about the brevity of love are somewhat contradicted by her memories of her own parents, who were married, lovingly, for 74 years (she emphasizes the number, with what sounds like a mixture of pride and incredulity) and who died, at the ages of 95 and 96, "within 12 hours of each other."

It sounds like an ideal way to go, and Stritch's supportive Catholic upbringing should not, by all the rules, have sent her off the rails. But she was experimenting seriously with alcohol when in her teens, while nourishing "a basic desire to be just about anybody else. If I'd meet a nurse, I wanted to be a nurse. I don't mean act a nurse, I wanted to be the nurse. It's only very recently that I'm looking in the mirror and saying, 'You're OK.' I mean, I had to get to be 77 before I could say this, but I don't care how long it took. But, you know, where the hell have I gone? I haven't played my part, ever, until now."

Taking this last remark literally, I pointed out that not many actors get to play themselves the way she's doing now. She agreed. "And let me tell you something, it's the best part I ever had. Because an awful lot of rehearsal went into it (I'd wanted to say that, but she beat me to the punch).

"A guy said something to me at the opening-night party in New York. He just brushed by me and said, 'Elaine, you're the best thing in it.' Isn't that divine? I've been looking for him ever since because I would marry him, by mail, on account of that one line. But he hasn't got in touch, so I don't think he wants to spend the rest of his life with me."

As the show reveals, she has spent two-year portions of her life with Gig Young and with Ben Gazzara; and also, which the show doesn't reveal, with Joe Allen -- yes, there really is a Joe Allen - - "the restaurant guy. He was cut from my life, for time reasons, and he never came to see the show. (Which sounds like poetic justice. Gazzara did come, and said, "Elaine, I didn't know you were that talented.")

With all this high-powered competition, I wondered how John Bay, who wasn't that well-known, had plucked up the courage to propose. "He didn't. I did. I knew it was on his mind, so I just made it easier for him. He replied -- and I credit him in the show -- 'Well, why not?' " The way she tells it, he said it a lot like Jack Benny. He was a connoisseur of comedians and achieved his greatest success playing Groucho Marx in A Day in Hollywood -- A Night in the Ukraine. He was Groucho: "most extraordinary," as Stritch says, "because he looked like Jack Kennedy." He also did a one-man show as Groucho in which, with the author's "be my guest" consent, he sang Send in the Clowns with altered lyrics: "Isn't it rich/Isn't it queer/Finding the deeds to the ranch/In your brassiere?" Hearing that sung by Stritch imitating Bay imitating Groucho is an experience.

An admiring journalist in Chicago wrote of her, "She's straightforward, she's honest, she's upfront, she's foulmouthed," and Stritch relished all the adjectives except the last.

"I thought, God, if there's one thing -- and it hit me, it's an example of me being the strong woman that everybody thinks, and I cried when I read this. There's no word in the English language I hate more than foulmouthed, and to call a woman that ..." Aggrieved, she spelled out for me, but did not actually say, the four-letter words she has never used in her life. "Foulmouthed?" she concluded. "Jesus!"

The strong-woman image derives, of course, from the roles she's played in musicals -- take-charge dames in the Ethel Merman tradition garnished with her own flavour of acidulous: Joanne in Company, or the luxury-cruise hostess of Noel Coward's Sail Away. Her sound is an individual cross between musical comedy and drawing- room comedy. In fact she's only done seven musicals in the whole of her life, not all of them hits, but they've been enough to establish her as one of the queens of Broadway. "As a singer," she says correctly, "I'm a hell of an actress" -- and I'd add that, though she may not have the greatest range or the purest tones in the world, the notes she does hit are in tune.

She's actually done more plays than musicals, her favourite being Edward Albee's A Delicate Balance, which she played in a New York revival some half-dozen years back. Chicago's Steppenwolf company has asked her ("and it's funny they would") to do Sweet Bird of Youth. "And to do that, at my age, would be to fulfill Tennessee Williams' advice, because he told me before he died (here she adopts an accent best described as Southern-nasal), 'I don't care how old you get to be, Lainie, Sweet Bird of Youth -- it'll never be done till you do it.' Isn't that marvellous?"

It could be. Between such engagements she remains, like any actor, At Liberty but with a twist. She can go on doing this show for the rest of her life, energy and longevity feeding off one another in a woman who knows she's a legend but has figured out how to work around it.
