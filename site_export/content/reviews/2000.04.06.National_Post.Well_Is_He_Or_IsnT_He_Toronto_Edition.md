I MIGHT BE EDGAR ALLAN POE

Alumnae Theatre

One has read of performers so versatile and energetic that they do everything but hand out programs before the show and sell drinks during intermission. Christopher Morris, sole acting proprietor of I Might Be Edgar Allan Poe, goes one better and actually does these things, or approximations of them.

This play doesn't have an intermission, but before the action begins there he is, wandering around the set and sometimes off it, to take our tickets. (He doesn't have to wander far: The upstairs studio at the Alumnae, where the show happens, is a bare room without a platform.)

This is not, it turns out, just an economy measure. The play solves the problem that dogs every extended dramatic monologue -- just whom is the actor meant to be talking to? -- by casting the audience as things in his dream; naturally, he has to welcome us. Apparently we play our roles pretty well. He judges it a good dream, and we can agree with him, though its contents are nightmarish.

Morris plays Joseph, a young man confined to a mental hospital who believes himself to be the Gothic fantasist invoked in the title and who is capable of reciting at least one long poem (right at the beginning of the play) and one short story (near the end) straight through, without mistakes and with an impressive variety of accent and gesture. This talent, which should have got him at least a radio contract, instead gets him locked up.

There have been other problems. He has lived in a world of his own since childhood, and has had one especially traumatic past incident involving a fire. His doctors have kept pressuring him to tell them about it but he has resisted. Finally, though, he does tell us about it, and it turns out to be more frightening, because more soberly realistic, than anything in Poe.

Morris, who was an appealing clown in Love's Labour's Lost last summer, plays Joseph as a good-natured innocent lacking a skin, sure of himself but puzzled by everybody else. He seems to have been banished to an attic room, like the madwoman in Jane Eyre. One of its few amenities is an old wind-up gramophone on which, before the play proper begins, he plays a varied selection of 78s. They don't seem especially relevant to what follows, but they do have charm.

So does Joseph, and he makes, much of the time, a lot of sense. Naturally, he sees things differently from his doctors. He complains, contrary to what might be the general impression, that they see life in extremes, take it far too dramatically, while he just accepts.

When he was a child he had a pair of hamsters who ate their young. He wasn't bothered by this but his parents expected him to be, so he obliged. Now he uses the same methods with his psychiatrists: He tells them what they obviously want to hear, and it seems to work.

Sometimes, though, they disappoint him. One shrink has told him that appearing in the hospital talent show would be good therapy for him. He takes her advice but she doesn't turn up for the show. His hurt, though he doesn't dwell on it, is very believable. He says, very pertinently: "Doctors don't have my thoughts -- it's not like they cut me open," so how can they presume to interpret what's on his mind?

The Poe story Joseph performs includes a definition of madness as "overacuteness of the senses." One can see that this fits his own case, and that Poe's affinity with the macabre reflects Joseph's acceptance of it. (He's able to throw in some funny impersonations of academics coming to grips with his favourite author. In Morris' performance, Joseph has a definite gift for mimicry.)

But the central identification seems strained, the quotations overlong (since the play is presumably meant to be more than an excuse for a couple of party pieces) and the title rather cheap. At their best, the writing and acting escape the frame: Joseph is more interesting than Poe.

There may, though, be a tale of mystery (and even of imagination) to be told of Dawson Nichols, who is named as the play's author. The program tells us nothing about him, though it supplies biographies of the director (Daryl Cloran), producer, actor, designer and stage manager. I cannot shake the suspicion that the author is one of the above, working under a pseudonym. On the other hand, he might be Dawson Nichols.
