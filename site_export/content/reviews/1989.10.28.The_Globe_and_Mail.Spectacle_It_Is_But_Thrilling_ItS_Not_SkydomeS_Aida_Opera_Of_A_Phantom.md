Spectacle it is, but thrilling it's not SkyDome's Aida: opera of a phantom

Saturday, October 28, 1989

ROBERT CUSHMAN

BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail YOU ENTER the SkyDome for Aida to find yourself confronted by a Sphinx: a large Sphinx, sure - did you ever see a small one? - but cuddly-looking, with a blandly optimistic expression on its face, and great welcoming paws. Any prima donna would find it a comforting perch from which to agonize and, as the night wears on, several of them do.

When Frank Sinatra and Liza Minnelli played the Dome, the place seemed huge but that literally wasn't the half of it. For Aida the whole arena is in play: the biggest chocolate-box since the Colosseum, housing one of the world's most renowned confectionery entertainments, a luxuriant tale of pageantry and pointless suffering.

This production, with its well-publicized cast of hundreds, may be leaden but it is not inappropriate. What did Verdi expect to hap pen during that endless processional music? The problem is that the rest of the opera is not built to the same scale. Half of it is Racine, and the other half Barnum and Bailey. A production as heedless of expense as this one should probably transport the audience, by a fleet of private helicopters, to some more intimate space - the O'Keefe Centre, say - for the scenes of in-fighting and self-immolation. They could always fly us back again for the curtain-calls.

But then the Dome people have not yet figured out how to deal with the cultural mafia. They supplied the critics with press kits (these are files of waste-paper stuffed with statistics) instead of programs, and seated us in a balcony which gave a panoramic view of the whole enclosure but was several hundred miles away from the action. It is probably a great angle for reporting ball games, but it is no way to respond to live performance. The best that can be said of this perspective on Aida is that those hundreds of ant-sized extras did give some feeling of the kind of society that must have built the pyramids.

Oh yes, there were pyramids on stage, three of them, as well as the Sphinx. They were not as impressive, though. As a back-drop for the old vaudeville cross-talk act of Mr. Gallagher and Mr. Shean, they would have been swell; for a tale of dynastic passion in old Egypt they seemed a bit weedy, a bit camp. There is some attraction in the arguments of the produc tion's conductor and presiding spirit, Maestro Giuseppe Raffa (though none at all in his compulsive use of that title), that opera needs visual allure, though I doubt the wisdom of his statement that if it fails in this regard it will go the way of the Pharaohs. The Pharaohs, on this production's own showing, were very much into spectacle; and where are they now? Where, in particular, is the one whose name was Ozymandias, king of kings? Look on his works, ye mighty, and despair. Or if that doesn't get you, look at the myriad empty seats at the SkyDome Thursday.

Look at the myriad full ones, too - in the SkyDome's house are many myriads - all their occupants glued, as usual, to the video screen, the only way of seeing the singers. Film critic Pauline Kael once noted that one of the few rules in movies is that all pictures set in ancient Egypt are ridiculous. Aida as seen here is one of those pictures. Viewed in close-up, the show seems to be trying for some kind of archeological fidelity, sabotaged by the grandiosity of the music which is unmistakably nineteenth-century European and by the visible discomfort of the extras, cast for costume-size rather than acting ability.

Weight of numbers does not in itself amount to thrilling spectacle, not even with the addition of dangerous animals. (There are fleeting glimpses of a lion in a cage-on-poles and a python draped round somebody's shoulder.) Any decent opera-house gives you more to look at, qualitatively, than this. Half-way through, the Sphinx cracks apart, and the applause this raised was slightly more justifiable than the equivalent raptures at Les Miserables when two bits of scenery slot together. But compared to the witty and sophisticated visuals of The Phantom of the Opera this Aida is a joke: the opera of a phantom.

And how does it sound, through its million-dollar system? Not bad. The orchestra is tinny in the martial passages, which may be Maestro Raffa's fault or even Verdi's, but individual ardors and agonies inspire considerable sweep. One winces, superstitiously perhaps, at the idea of miked opera voices, but Grace Bumbry has enchantingly rapt moments, and the video camera catches her at becoming angles. It doesn't do the same for Amneris or Radames, but all concerned sing on with their professionalism admirably unruffled.

There are also dancers, whose efforts are indecipherable on stage but are revealed on screen as balletic yoga. The screen also gives us surtitles which are admirable. It is good to know from moment to moment what is being sung. Otherwise, subversive thoughts arise concerning the frivolity of a form that employs words while rendering them incomprehensible. The same screen is used during the 30-minute interval to credit the production's sponsors. The show's official dry-cleaners called to ask for a mention. We refused, of course, very much on our dignity, but that call gives the flavor of the event as well as anything could. Actually, as it turned out, their work looked spotless.
