SNOW WHITE AND THE MAGNIFICENT SEVEN

Elgin Theatre, Toronto

In Robert Altman's film California Split, which took overlapping dialogue to new heights of incomprehensibility, there was a just- audible recurring sequence in which George Segal and Elliott Gould challenged one another to name the seven dwarfs. Somehow, neither of them could ever manage to recall all seven at one time, and it was plain, from the frustrated murmurs all around the movie theatre, that nobody else could, either.

A few years later, there was a newspaper competition to name an eighth dwarf. My own nomination (un-entered) for a fitting companion for Sleepy and Dopey and Grumpy and, um, the others, was Filthy.

None of which is very relevant to Snow White and the Magnificent Seven, but relevance never seems to be of crucial importance to Ross Petty when he assembles his annual holiday entertainments. And besides, re-labelling the small septet, individually and collectively, has obviously been a major concern for him. Since the old title is politically beyond the pale, he has named them, as a group, after a movie of which you may well have fond memories. (Your juniors have probably never heard of it. Mine hadn't.)

Mindful of who holds the imaginative copyright on these characters, he has cloned the Disney figures as Pops and Duddy and Snoozy and Wheezy and Jolly and Blusher and Fingers (a startling departure, but he plays the balalaika) and has given them a theme song just far enough removed from Heigh-Ho, Heigh-Ho to discourage litigation. Actually, they seem to get rather tired of this number, and of whistling while they work, and they declare they would rather sing show tunes.

So, I'm sure, would Petty. His shows, built on the anything-goes model of English pantomime, keep making disconcerting lurches into sophistication. He himself adds to his gallery of villains by playing Snow White's wicked stepmother. It isn't a drag performance, exactly; he merely puts on a frock and does his usual dastardly stuff from inside it. He calls himself Queen Lola, just so he can vamp the hero while singing Whatever Lola Wants.

Snow White, played with unusual brightness and vivacity by Melissa Thomson, gets brighter and more vivacious than ever when she sings I Can Cook Too, from On the Town. She sings it to convince the seven of her housekeeping qualifications, so the song's double entendres go begging (even this new-millennium Snow White isn't that kind of damsel) but it livens the show up, no question.

Prince Charming and Muddles (both refugees from other fairy tales; you won't have heard of Muddles but I'll explain later) keep treating us to choruses of Brush Up Your Shakespeare. This is both a running gag and an in-joke. The Prince, in accordance with the Petty tradition of ritually sacrificing one classical actor every winter, is played by Graham Abbey, the last Stratford Festival's Henry V and the next one's Romeo.

He takes the song's advice to heart (though Snow White loves him anyway) and keeps favouring us with bits of Bard, from "my kingdom for a horse" to "soft, what light through yonder window breaks." (As they say in the trade: He isn't really going to do it like that, is he?) He does the good sport thing very well. He, like Snow White, is encouraged to be a lot funnier than panto juveniles ever were in my day, and he sings (a lot) and dances (a bit) with justified abandon. I think he should get together with the Shaw Festival's Ben Carlson and work up a club act.

Snow White is not one of the basic pantomime titles, and is in fact rather a thin story to take up an evening. Once you've done the mirror and the apple and the cottage, you're through. The Petty script, uncredited as usual, leaves out some of what tradition does supply. (No coffin, no nuffin'.) To make up the time, the show borrows from other pantomimes, mostly Cinderella. Muddles, sweet on the heroine but knowing better than to compete with the hero, is Buttons by another name; Eddie Glen plays him, well.

Other padding includes a mystifyingly irrelevant and extremely long scene in a gypsy encampment. Other characters include a moderately unscrupulous cat (Peter Deiwick) whose position is obscure but who seems to belong to Fred Penner.

Penner plays someone with a French accent called Oscar the Obvious and is also the compere. Thanks to him, the customary village green opening is withheld for about 15 minutes, and you may hope that you have escaped it entirely, but it finally clocks in, the boys still holding out their hands hopefully to the girls and the girls still tripping in dainty circles, before giving in and sitting on the boys' knees. I wonder if new choreography would be unconstitutional.

The magnificent ones hardly live up to their adjective; their timing is slack. Petty's own timing remains a pleasure, though it demands major co-operation from his audience. He was in luck on Saturday night when a disparaging reference on his part to Harry Potter produced ringing cries of "stupid" that had him digging deep into his fabled store of withering ad libs. This is not his best show (that would still be Robin Hood) but it's an improvement on the last few.
