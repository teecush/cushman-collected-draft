The received wisdom on Hair is that it has great music and a terrible script. I wonder. Galt MacDermot's score must be the most copious ever written for a musical - the program lists 41 separate songs, in a show that isnt even throughsung - and most of the tunes have attractive hooks. The majority, though, fail to develop - Good Morning Starshine, which has a surprising but logical middle, is an exception - so they end up irritatingly weightless, whether as theatre or as pop. Theres also, especially considering the show's implicit claim to represent the youth movement of the 1960s, something curiously uncommitted about them; they feel like pastiche without purpose. It's a fragmentary score, only with an exceptional number of fragments.

The lyrics, by Gerome Ragni and James Rado, are similarly shapeless, rarely deigning to come to anything as conventional as a point. Again, there are exceptions: Frank Mills is one, and What a Piece of Work is another, though with that of course they had some august assistance. Pity the writer whos hailed as a pioneer: Just as John Osborne, after the success of Look Back in Anger, was lumbered with being the revolutionary saviour of the British theatre when he was in fact rather old-fashioned, so the authors of Hair were thought to have overturned the traditional musical and were expected to go on overturning it. In fact, their subsequent shows were major flops, and MacDermot, at least, seems to have been miscast. Hair billed itself - still bills itself - as "the American Tribal Love-Rock Musical (how many trendy bases can you cover in one subtitle?) but its doubtful that he was a rock composer. His roots were in jazz and swing - he composed African Waltz, theme of the Johnny Dankworth orchestra and a better tune than anything in Hair - and his best theatre score, for The Human Comedy, was persuasive big band 1940s.

There is, however, more to the book of Hair, also by Ragni and Rado, than previous stagings, including the original, have suggested. There is, for example, humour. That's one of the revelations of Diane Paulus' production, which comes to us on its postBroadway tour. Its visually and sonically vibrant; you can actually hear the words, not just because of technological advances but because the actors deliver them with both feeling and timing. The cast seem happily unselfconscious about enacting their parents revolution, and they make the most of what story there is.

It concerns, you may remember, the forcible enlisting of Claude, gentle member of a community of Central Park drop-outs. His parents, especially his dad, are all in favour of his going to Vietnam and proving himself a man; his friends want him to burn his draft card as a gesture, and then escape to Canada as a practicality. The friends include Jeanie (Aleque Reid) who, being both pregnant and ecologically conscious, might be described as an earth-mother-tobe; Sheila (Sara King), the clearest thinker of the group if you discount her claim to have seen the Pentagon levitate; and Berger, an all-purpose anarchic prankster. They're all well played, and if Steel Burkhardt as Berger comes over as insufferably smug and smirky, that probably means he's doing a faithful job. Claude was played, when I caught the show, by William Joseph Lewis, an understudy whose performance, vulnerable and unknowable, I cannot imagine being bettered.

All these characters, and several others, are engaged in an overlapping ritual - literally, at one point - of love and lust, but none of these emotional or physical entanglements are explored in any depth. Neither is anything else. The girl who sings about Frank Mills never mentions him again, and even Claudes predicament is ignored for lengthy periods; though it does give rise to a secondact dream (or nightmare) sequence thats here more intelligently and comprehensibly presented than ever before. Another surprise is the weight given to the show's elders. What do you want to be, besides dishevelled?" asks Claude's mother (Liz Baltes) and its the best line in the show, not because we agree with it but because its smartly turned. Her partner (Will Blum) turns up as another, more tolerant adult, in an initially funny scene that can't resist an extra twist.

The show wants to be - is neurotically afraid of not being - too hip for the house. It makes a great display of tearing down the fourth wall, but it wants us to cry over Claude. All the rushing down the aisles and inviting the audience onstage for a post-show dance that may have felt liberating when the show was new, now just looks like something that every show does when it runs out of ideas. And maybe it was never all that spontaneous. It's worth consulting William Goldmans book The Season in which he contrasts the shows off-Broadway premiere, when it did seem fresh and even brave, with its Broadway makeover, which arrived when the end of the war was already in sight and which has perforce been the source of all subsequent revivals. He also contrasts the predictably shocked responses of the more conservative critics with the far more vitriolic comments of an underground paper whose reviewer actually knew the scene the show was supposed to be depicting. He accused the producers of turning it into a kind of hippie theme park. It still feels like that, even in a production as good as Paulus's, only with 40 years of nostalgia thrown in.
