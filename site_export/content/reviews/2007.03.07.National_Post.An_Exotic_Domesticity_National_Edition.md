THEATRE REVIEW

JOHN AND BEATRICE

TARRAGON THEATRE

- - -

Beatrice sits munching an apple in a sparsely furnished room at the top of a 33-storey building with a broken elevator. She is awaiting a suitor. She had advertised for one, placing posters all over town that promise a substantial reward to any man "who can interest, move and seduce her. In that order." Up the stairs puffs John, who, as a self described bounty hunter, feels that this is an offer right in his line. When he enquires about that reward, Beatrice informs him that her dad, a plastic garbage-can king, was decapitated in a freak auto accident, that she is the heir to his fortune and that the building and most of the block on which it stands belong to her. John takes up the triple challenge and does very well at it, mainly by telling stories, both piquant and poignant, about his own early life. In fact, it looks as if he's won.

The situation in Carole Frechette's John and Beatrice may not seem the most plausible you have ever encountered, but it plainly wasn't meant to be. Beatrice is a fairy-tale heroine: a latter-day Rapunzel with a tower block for a tower, a Sleeping Beauty whose slumbers are broken up into abrupt little naps. At least, that's how she sees herself. The play is a fantasy about a fantasist. John's a yarn-spinner as well, but his stories seem more grounded, even if his way of life--helping old ladies find lost pets, hunting down chainsaw killers, living off the rewards and only accepting payment in $20 bills -- sounds a trifle exotic. He might be the modern version of the woodcutter who wins the princess. But times have moved on, and we might guess from the characters' names that they are mismatched. Their ideal partners would probably be called Dante and Janet.

There's a preciousness about the play, especially in its second half, that can get on the nerves, but I never felt like giving up on it. There is a human current running through it -- far more so than in some of the more elaborate plays that have been fetching up in Toronto recently and more too than in Frechette's other (and in my mind over praised) plays like Elisa's Skin and Helen's Necklace. There is also wit. I actually believed in John's story about how he got started on his strange career: rescuing a young woman from drowning and finding, after he had moved in with her, that she had become something other and less romantic. "Her voice was too shrillher dress was too fancy." (The translation is by John Murrell, who habitually gives Frechette a head start over other Francophone Canadian playwrights arriving on Anglophone stages.) John has learned something about love, if only not to look for easy definitions of it. Beatrice, despite having had, she says, 14 men in her life, is still clinging to received ideas from some age of innocence. "Someone told me," she says inter alia, "that if you love a man you cook him a rack of lamb." Ah, that fine old French culture.

After John has theoretically won Beatrice and she has theoretically succumbed, the two perform a sped-up version of what married life might be, looking forward in much the same way that the nonagenarian couple of Ionesco's The Chairs look back. It isn't encouraging. Their emotional vocabularies don't match. ("Don't push me," says John, metaphorically. She does, literally.) The ending is trite, but the play survives. It's a paradox: You never believe in these people, but you still want to know what happens to them.

It's very well done. Leah Cherniak's direction keeps the juices flowing and makes the most of the few sequences of violent action. Yannik Larivee's set, with a checkered floor and walls tapering in exaggerated crooked perspective, is an eye-catcher. Under Lesley Wilkinson's lighting the colour of those walls changes from faded ochre to orange-red, while constantly changing vistas tease us through the solitary window. Otherwise, the most notable visual features are the bottles of drinking water, brand undetectable, that litter the floor, and on which Beatrice seems to subsist. (What good is sitting alone in your room? Not much, apparently.) But it's the acting that makes the evening. Rick Roberts' John is a fine compound of burliness and sensitivity. Caroline Cave's Beatrice is a woman passionately determined to be disappointed by life. There are even moments at which, moving from grinning incredulity to squeaky- voiced disparagement, she seems to be channelling Jon Stewart responding to something more than usually outrageous in the news. This is a visually compelling, vocally rich performance that draws on both the princess and the clown: a psychological striptease -- a physical one too, in one rather specialized regard -- that still leaves a secret at the core.
