The playwriting debut of J. Karol Korczynski occurred three years ago with a piece called Canada House. He and the Canada House Artistic Co-op have now come up with a successor piece, Canada Steel, with a third, Canada Square, on the way. Canada House focused on drug-dependency, crystal meth mostly, presented as a means for the bosses to keep the workers docile. In Canada Steel, the only drugs at issue are legitimate medicinal ones, with the main subject the wreckage, institutional and human, of what used to be a great industry, the steel mills of Hamilton.

Gus, a former steelworker prematurely retired for mental-health reasons, has already lost his house; he now finds that his union will no longer pay for his medication. He tends to go off his meds anyway; and his resultant towering rage is much exacerbated by his attempts to phone the union to discover what has happened to his health plan. His calls get routed to India, where they are fielded by a young lady named Bhopal; initially unhelpful, she gradually becomes more and more invested in Guss problems, partly because she has similar ones of her own; her outsourced job is about to be sourced even further out, to Burma. In his lighter moments, Gus pursues a Homer Simpson-ish vendetta against an unseen next-door neighbour, and dons a Leafs jersey to watch the team play on TV, going berserk when they actually win the Cup. (The play, like Canada House, is set in the future.)

Meanwhile Guss wife, Rose, discovers, in a dumpster, an outsize piece of progressive art depicting the steelworks; idealistically, she thinks it should be exhibited, and pragmatically, she suspects it's worth money. Still further meanwhile, Gus and Rose, who aren't that progressive, worry that their daughter Roxie may be taking up with a lesbian. This occupies a lot of space in the first act and is totally forgotten in the second.

Les, the yuppie smoothie, in whom Rose is trying to pique an interest in the painting and with whom she goes to bed a couple of times, is by a strange coincidence the very same official who holds her husbands fate in his hands. The union is now virtually indistinguishable from the company. Act 2 finds most of the cast -- with the virtual inclusion of Bhopal, on the line from Mumbai but visible in person -- converging on Less pad, a situation for which the technical word is "farcical. And some of it is played for farce, though without either the preparation or the development to make it work in more than isolated flashes. Les instructs his aggrieved visitors on the ways of the new globalized world, telling them that they don't get it." In fact, they do, and so do we, but that doesn't stop Les from going on telling us. Mayhem ensues, with a coda that seems split between the authors instincts for apocalypse and his ideological obligations toward hope.

The play straggles and bellows, and Graham Cozzubbos production just sits there and lets it happen. Some of the acting is disgracefully bad, with a succession of fluffed lines on the second night suggesting a general lack of confidence. Gus's unstoppable anger may partly explain the hammering monotony of Daniel Kash's performance, but there is no equivalent excuse for Alison Woolridge's Rose, played on a single note of aggrieved aggression. Brian Marler's Les is a reprise of the role he played in Canada House, and as uneasy now as he was then, but Caroline Gowdy has reasonable moxie as Roxie. It would not normally be a compliment to say that an actress phones her performance in, but Pragna Desai, who has to do just that, progresses delightfully from anonymously unhelpful to earnestly concerned to blazingly defiant. But then, as befits a telephone operative, she has all the best lines.
