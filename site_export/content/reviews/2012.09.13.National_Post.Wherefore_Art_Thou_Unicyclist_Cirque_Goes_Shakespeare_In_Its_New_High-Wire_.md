THEATRE REVIEW

Cirque du Soleil's Amaluna Grand Chapiteau, Toronto Portlands

Theres a scene in The Tempest in which the magician Prospero, as an engagement present for his daughter Miranda and her princely intended Ferdinand, produces an entertainment to all of whose characters are goddesses. Its generally referred to as the Masque Scene. Attending Amaluna, the latest extravaganza from Cirque du Soleil, is like seeing that masque stretched out over an entire evening.

There's good reason for this. The Cirque show is confessedly based on the Shakespeare play. It also professes to be a celebration of women; it has a high proportion of female performers - which, this being Cirque, means contortionists, gymnasts and such - plus what was known in less enlightened times as an all-girl band.

The trend starts at the top. Prospero is now Prospera; she's still the ruler of an island - though this one is populated, mostly by unicyclists - and she still conjures up a storm, literally. For her prototype, this and the resultant shipwreck were part of a revenge plot; for Prospera, a devoted mother, its just an elaborate way of finding the right young man for her daughter.

Up until now, this Miranda seems to have been leading a happy enough existence, frolicking with an upstanding man-sized lizard who swishes an impressive and, it subsequently transpires, detachable tail. He suggests, at least initially, a cuddlier Caliban, and he is indeed called Cali. Im not sure which of Prospera's many attendant sprites corresponds to Ariel, but I figured it to be the one who hangs down from an air-borne hoop, which I thought of as Ariel's aerial.

Ferdinand, when the sea washes him up, turns out to have been renamed Romeo, presumably to reassure both Miranda and the audience that he is indeed lover-boy material. He even, alone in the cast, gets name-checked in the course of the action; Cirque shows being understandably short on dialogue, we can only work out who the other people are by checking with the program, but Romeo has apparently been hanging out with the clowns, and they do get to talk, at length.

Theres a male clown, who might be Shakespeares Trinculo or Stephano or both, and a female clown, who's in residence from the start, if not before; she does the welcoming-the-audience and turn-offyour-cellphones bit. When he and she collide, we temporarily leave the world of The Tempest and enter that of The Magic Flute. They shack up, and before you know it have produced a dozen babies, pretty much before our eyes. All this while Romeo and Miranda are still at the hand-holding stage; some relationships just move faster than others.

In themselves the clown scenes are every bit as tedious as these Cirque interludes usually are, and even more protracted. But at least they're used to provide a conscious counterpart to the central romance, thereby bringing the show to a level of dramaturgical sophistication hitherto unknown in these parts.

Of course, dramaturgical sophistication is not necessarily what you go to Cirque du Soleil for. A problem with Amaluna, though, is that its short on the things - the breathbating, credulity-defying things - that you do go there for. Its a case of swings and roundabouts, except that the swings aren't swinging very wildly and the roundabouts are turning slow.

The director, Diane Paulus, arrives with a solid record in non-circus entertainment (she's responsible for Broadways current Porgy and Bess and its recent Hair) and shes at her best here when she's telling the story. Most Cirque productions claim to have a story, and it usually boils down to someone going on a nebulous quest and arriving at a mysterious castle where people do tricks as if they were in, well, a circus. Amaluna, with its classical underpinnings, has far more of a spine.

Sometimes it relaxes too much for comfort; I had a hard time sorting out the various lady deities who are apparently meant to be assisting at, or presiding over, Miranda's transition into womanhood. Prospera herself (Julie Andrea McInnes) seems to be controlling everybodys destinies with the help of a magic cello, but she doesnt really do much except to extend a hand over whatever is going on as if to say, "Hey, I did this."

However, Miranda (Iuliia Mikhaylova) is a delight, whether as a bewitched spectator of the magic, or a bewitching participant in it; the fact that she's both is the show's greatest claim to unity. Shes an aquatic acrobat (an aquackrobat?) who performs her choicest feats inside a tank shaped like a giant goldfish bowl. Romeo (Edouard Doye) crosses a tightrope to win her, just as we were expecting him to do, but his most spectacular talent is for climbing; put him in Verona and hed be up that balcony in no time.

Cali (Viktor Kee) reverts unexpectedly to jealous type, pushing young Romeo into the fishbowl, shutting the lid on it, and then doing a gleeful juggling act on top; it isn't an especially impressive juggling act (not by Cirque standards, that is; just you try it) but we get the point.

There's some group-juggling much earlier on, some of it done by performers who are niftily balancing on the soles of other performers feet. Some local Amazons do some tumbling on uneven bars that involves them playfully kicking some minor male castaways in the face. The guys get their revenge, however, at the start of act two with a tumbling routine on a seesaw, the one act to induce the sheer physical exhilaration that has traditionally been this troupes trademark.

A less boisterous kind of magic is administered by Lara Jacobs who, using her feet, raises and meticulously reassembles a set of bamboo shoots. It's the most hypnotic display of picking up sticks you are ever likely to see.

Amaluna runs in Toronto until Nov. 4 and runs in Vancouver starting Nov. 23. For tickets and more information, visit cirquedusoleil.com.
