FOSSE

Pantages Theatre, Toronto

There is a telltale moment in the first act of Fosse. The band strikes up the opening bars of Hooray for Hollywood, and the cast of dancers, doing their job, perform a few steps to it. There are only a few because this song is the only one in the evening that never featured in a Bob Fosse stage or screen musical. It has been inserted, rather desperately, as narrative glue. It precedes a number that Fosse choreographed during his own time in Hollywood (his very first, actually, from the film of Kiss Me Kate), which itself is followed by a routine he devised for his stage show Dancin' as a tribute to Fred Astaire, set to a song that Astaire performed in another film Fosse himself had nothing to do with.

So this sequence represents, very roughly, the subject's movie period. To appreciate this, however, you would have to be an expert on Fosse's career, or at least be able to read the program notes in the dark. It is the same later on with a number done to the old song Dancing in the Dark (the real one, not the Springsteen pretender), apparently based on routines Fosse used to perform on television, with and without his first wife. Again, who would know? One can understand the show's creators -- Chet Walker, Richard Maltby Jr., Ann Reinking, in that chronological order of involvement -- wanting to avoid explicit narration, which might smack of the schoolroom or at least the PBS documentary, but they haven't come up with a comprehensible alternative.

The biographical approach, such as it is, is only fitfully employed. The idea seems more to illustrate Fosse's showbiz philosophy, which might be summed up as the world is rotten so enjoy it. We start with a languorous world-weary rendition of Life Is Just a Bowl of Cherries, and we end with a reprise of it. That's about it for structure. A mournful crouching creature at the side of the stage during the opening is probably the same one who reappears near the end as the downcast hero of Mr. Bojangles, a broken-down hoofer who spends most of his time in jail and probably represents Fosse's worst nightmare of the dancer's life. But we have had no sense of him in the interim.

So what we have is a staged anthology of song-and-dance routines - - the emphasis far more on dance than song -- that were originally created, for a variety of projects, by a man who was one of the two or three greatest Broadway choreographers. He was certainly the most distinctive -- the only one whose work can be identified at sight as being his -- and he was also the only one to parlay his stage success into movies and television. Some of these are still exciting. Big Spender, the dance-hall hostesses' massed come-on from Sweet Charity, remains a masterpiece: a symphony of sleazy costume and garish makeup, mockingly brassy music and minimal crooked- finger movement. Rich Man's Frug, noses and cigarette holders thrust aloft in a massed snobbery contest, is a satirical delight; it is also the only number that works better in this touring version than in the original production that we saw at the Ford Centre (as it then was) in North York, before it went to Broadway. This, too, derives from Sweet Charity: the last fun Fosse show before he decided that he had tidings to give the world, as in Pippin, Chicago, the film of Cabaret, and other explorations of his love- hate relationship to The Old Razzle Dazzle.

We get the trademark bowler hats, splayed feet, dark sets (reduced by Santo Loquasto to one dark set, and a gloomy one) and threefold formations. Fosse, having discovered the erotic potentialities of the trio in Steam Heat, went on exploiting them. He did it to great effect in individual shows, but when the results are placed end-to-end, it becomes wearing, however much he shuffles up the sexes. Cool Hand Luke (two males, one female) does not look all that different from Me and My Shadows (one male, two females), which immediately follows it. (And why in the latter do the two chicks keep singing, in counterpoint to the main lyric, about somebody called Dancing Dan the Safe-Breaking Man? The guy doesn't look like particularly like a break-and-enterer. You'd need to have seen Big Deal, the flop show in which the number originated, to answer that question. Most of us, of course, haven't.) A voice in my head began muttering, in the disjointed accents of Gollum from Lord of the Rings, "threesomes are tiresomes."

Unless my memory is failing me, the Glory sequence from Pippin, in which Fosse solemnly lets us know he was against war, and the orgy number Take Off with Us have been radically trimmed for the tour; I never liked the second one much, and I always hated the first, but there now seems no point in having them there at all. I Wanna Be a Dancin' Man, the Astaire homage, remains a delightful union of two apparent incompatibles, Fosse's tension and Fred's cool; they meet in the tilt of a hat. How To Succeed in Business Without Really Trying and Little Me -- two of the funniest shows Fosse or anyone else ever worked on -- have always been excluded: a pity, since the show could use some extra joy, especially now.

Even the wonderful extended closer, a skin-tight choreographic realization of the Benny Goodman warhorse Sing Sing Sing, is a shadow of its North York self; when the massed dancers perform the ultimate Fosse move -- the one that consists of leaping in the air while throwing out both your arms and letting your head follow -- they lack the proper abandon. Most of the dances here come off tight- limbed and heavy-footed.

Reva Rice, who does much of the singing and sounds a bit like Nancy Wilson, is a definite improvement on her predecessor; the other vocals, individual or group, manage to sound simultaneously loud and muffled. The orchestrations, even in their current tinny reproduction, are terrific; they are the work of Douglas Besterman, who recently won a Tony for his work on The Producers (as who didn't?) and, predominantly, of the great Ralph Burns, whom I would be inclined to nominate the hero of the evening. Livent, which originated the show, is nowhere mentioned in the program, and I suppose one would have to be naive to expect otherwise.
