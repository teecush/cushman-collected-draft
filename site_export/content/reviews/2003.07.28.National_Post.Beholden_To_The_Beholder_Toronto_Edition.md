PORTRAIT

Artword Alternative Space

- - -

A man in his 30s and a woman in her 20s stand side by side. He does most of the talking. He tells her, and us, that he is a success, a man of destiny, adding charmingly that I'm important. You arent. She, politely unimpressed, says that she, a rising young artist, can give him true immortality by painting his portrait.

This little scene, schematic but crisp, kicks off Portrait, a new play by Marisa Victor. What follows is that he (no names are given) accepts her invitation and visits her studio, a poverty-row garret with a rotary phone and a dysfunctional toilet. She professes to be a dedicated artist, emphatically uninterested in material rewards, and to have innovative techniques that will allow her to capture on canvas the man within. Skeptical but intrigued, he goes along. He, of course, fancies her (she seems ambivalent about him) and their involvement, whether or not its sexual, eventually harms his marriage. She meanwhile gets a taste -- a substantial taste -- of what it's like to be fashionable.

In some ways, the play resembles The Shape of Things. A woman professing to be an artist takes a man and, in making him the subject of his art, changes him. In this case, though, the subject knows from the outset that he is the subject, and he starts, unlike Neil LaBute's nerd, from a position of power. We get role reversal rather than violent humiliation.

The painter, meanwhile, most unlike LaButes scarily undeviating heroine, has her doubts. She doesnt know whether she's any good; she doesn't know whether she wants to paint portraits or devote herself solely to abstracts; she lives in constant fear of having sold out. Or she says she does; maybe shes just being manipulative. But even her manipulations point to actual insecurities. The audience, which starts out thinking its been summoned to watch a shiny idealist put a crass materialist in his place, gets its preconceptions knocked about. The woman also believes herself to be privileged, by virtue of her potential if not of her achievement. She's a believable study in arrogance.

So, also, is the man, but less interestingly. He goes through credible but predictable motions. It might help if we had a clearer idea of just what it is he does that's made him rich and famous at 37. (He seems to be some kind of captain of industry, but one cant be sure.)

Then again, the casting may be skewed. Stephen Near, though perfectly competent, seems young and light for his role, and sticking a scrub of beard on his chin does not bestow charisma. Grania Maguire, on the other hand, looks and sounds exactly right as the blond transformer, convincing enough to override some first- night insecurities. In fact, the whole production, under Lindy Zuckers direction, appeared unfinished, with the actors still too far from one another's throats.

The play is the second show from Allegoria Productions, whose "mandate," it says here, is to produce provocative and entertaining new and classic plays interpreted to focus on Canadian social, political and environmental issues (i.e., to do the same as everybody else). I don't know who told them to do it; if nobody did and its all their own idea, then it isnt a mandate. A mandate is, by definition, something you get handed. There's a comic comment on this kind of language in the play itself, when the heroine says, "I have a commission for a self-portrait." At least I hope it's comic. The authors ear is, in general, very good -- and talking of ears, the script contains a mini-lesson on the right way to pronounce Van Gogh.

Theres a very sharp first-act ending. It feels, indeed, so conclusive that it's disconcerting to find it followed by an intermission rather than a curtain call. To put it more brutally, the situation is slight and the development prolonged. The play holds the attention, even so.
