Last week's penultimate episode in the current season of Prison Break featured an unpretentious, but still spectacular, procession down the streets of Panama City. Strutting out in front, jaunty and dapper in white duds and, naturally, a Panama hat, was T-Bag, the serial killer and bisexual sex offender, now being used as a decoy to catch others who escaped with him from Fox River Prison at the end of the first season.

Behind him, keeping tabs and ready to pounce, were two minor characters, toughs in the pay of the mysterious Company that seems to control America if not the world. A safe distance after them came Bellick, the disgraced prison warder turned bounty hunter, itching to deprive T-Bag of US$5-million in formerly buried treasure or at least to collect on the reward for his apprehension.

Keeping their eyes firmly fixed on him were Sucre-- the nicest of the original escapees, pressured into an unholy alliance with Bellick -- and Michael, the show's hero and mastermind, lured here by an e-mail from Sucre that Sucre never actually sent. He's the real object of the Company's trap, but being super smart he's already figured that out; he wants, though, to turn in T-Bag, since he has on his conscience the people T-Bag has killed before and since Michael reluctantly engineered his release. (Michael had actually been an engineer before getting himself locked up.)

Michael is being trailed by Mahone, the haunted federal agent whom the Company has blackmailed into killing all of the Fox River Eight; at the last count, he'd offed three of them.

And Mahone was being tracked by Linc, Michael's brother, whom the Company framed for murder and whose escape from the chair was Michael's motive for organizing the prison break in the first place.

The show obviously relished its own wit in parading these characters, so ingeniously united and re-united. Wit, verbal and structural, is one of Prison Break's most attractive features. Among TV drama series, it occupies a space between The Sopranos, which is completely character-driven, offering not so much a narrative arc as a narrative mosaic, and 24, which is completely plot-driven, so much so that when the plot falters, as it has this season, we have nothing left to cling to. Playing for time, 24 goes down narrative blind alleys that seem hopelessly arbitrary because the people, Jack Bauer included, are puppets. And they aren't funny --not even Chloe this season.

Prison Break also has its tangents and dead ends, but we enjoy them because we like following the characters. That goes even for the despicable ones. There are, it's true, many fans who heartily wish T-Bag dead as soon as possible, but his debased insouciance, and Robert Knepper's brilliant Southern- fried performance, have the rest of us hooked. And a flashback to his own abused white- trash childhood humanized him without too much sentimentalizing him.

Strangely, Kellerman, the company hit man now hung out to dry, has never aroused the same public hatred, though he must have killed as many people (and nursed incidentally a similar hopeless passion for one unattainable woman); he too, as played by Paul Adelstein, has style and a believable streak of humanity. There is a surprising degree of complexity here. The driven Mahone (William Fichtner), Nemesis on barbiturates, keeps winning our sympathy. Even the brutish Bellick (Wade Williams) has his sad-funny moments; he's a middle-aged man still living with his mother with whom, be it noted, he enjoys the only affectionate parent-child relationship on the show. He has also now been arrested, twice, for murders actually committed by T-Bag.

Prison Break's prison break came at the end of the first season. I asked at the time if a sequel would have to be called Prison Broken, and doubted that it could possibly work. I was wrong. The second season has surpassed the first. It's been fascinating to see the characters dispersed, their storylines separating out and then crisscrossing with what seems a fair imitation of inevitability. The central motifs -- the conspiracy, the framing of Linc, the treasure, the price on the fugitives' heads --act like magnets.

The show also boasts one of the most compelling romances in TV drama history: Michael (Wentworth Miller, who's turned wry watchfulness into a style) and Sara (Sarah Wayne Callies), the disgraced prison physician, belong together precisely because they're both so withdrawn, so guarded (pun partially intended), so painfully intelligent. The plot keeps pulling them apart, and they've barely got to kiss, so one roots for them all the more.

Last Monday's episode -- maybe the best there's ever been -- ended with Sara, about to get 12 years for aiding and abetting, finding an unlikely saviour in the repentant Kellerman, entering the courtroom flashing a smile that's a patented blend of sheepishness and arrogance, promising to reveal all about the Company. Bets are being taken on whether he, unlike all the others who have tried, survives to tell his tale. It's hard to believe that the show would have the heart to kill him off, or that the villains would have the opportunity. But if he comes through then bang must go the conspiracy, so what will the promised third season have to beat its head against?

Something, plainly, will have to go painfully wrong for somebody in tonight's season finale. We'll be left, once again, hanging from a cliff for several months, cursing and blessing Prison Break for leaving us there.
