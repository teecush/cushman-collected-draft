THE LITTLE FOXES

Royal George Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

---

This year's Shaw Festival plays are arranging themselves in pairs. Alongside An Inspector Calls, with J. B. Priestley calling down vengeance on his whirling Birlings, we have The Little Foxes, in which Lillian Hellman wields the hatchet on her horrendous Hubbards.

The Hubbards are a post-Civil War Southern family, rich but definitely not old money, a fact that gnaws at them even as they express their contempt for the soft slave-owning gentry whose immediate descendants they've dispossessed. They don't plant 'taters, they don't plant cotton; and them what plants 'em are best forgotten.

What the Hubbards are good at is trading in the stuff, the cotton anyway, and they have a deal brewing with a Chicago tycoon who would like some southern comfort in his brand. There are two Hubbard brothers, the domineering Benjamin and the smarmy Oscar, and a sister, Regina. Regina is married to Horace Giddens, a banker whose consent is needed to seal the deal. Horace is not, however, about to give it. He is a sick man, and says that he has done quite enough bad things in his life without adding to them now. Besides, he hates the Hubbards, especially the one he married. Regina reciprocates, her most pressing concern right now being to keep his ailing heart from beating for good and all.

Rounding out the clan are Oscar's wife Birdie, a good weak woman whom he terrorizes; their son Leo, a rotter and sneak who's his dad's spy in Horace's bank; and Alexandra, daughter of Horace and Regina, who stands an awful good chance of being married off to Leo. Or, considering what Leo's like, an appallingly bad chance.

Like Priestley, Hellman never stops telling you how terrible her terrible people are, and she's even less subtle about it. Where Priestley is insistently righteous, she's insistently self-righteous. Eda Holmes, director of this revival, quotes her as saying "I am representing for you the sort of person who ruins the world for us," and that us-and-them tone is significant. An Inspector Calls suggests that we're all implicated; Hellman seems to have dreamed up her characters in order to feel superior to them.

It can be a pleasure shared, since she's also supplied an efficient melodramatic plot for them to rattle about in: one that sets Regina against her brothers as well as the three of them against all decent -- or, in Horace's case, half-decent -- folks. But both plot and play can be hard to activate. There was a star-studded Stratford production--Martha Henry and William Hutt and Brian Bedford -- that I missed but that must have been roaring fun; the two versions I have seen --one with Elizabeth Taylor, one without -- were crashing bores. Holmes, however, keeps the play alive and suspenseful by the unexpected method of treating the characters as human beings. This goes especially for Regina, a woman who in middle age exhibits all the most endearing maternal and matrimonial features of Joan Crawford and Lady Macbeth, but who suffered a maimed childhood on her own account; Laurie Paton, whose season this is (so far), plays her with a creamy malice beneath which the scars still fester. Ric Reid is ideally rough and gruff as Ben and Peter Krantz sufficiently creepy as Oscar. Gray Powell and Krista Colosimo, the two juveniles from Getting Married, make far better impressions here with, to be fair, far juicier material; he as the mewling low-life Leo, she as Alexandra, whose declaration of independence cows even Regina, leaving us to wonder whether it heralds a new era of selflessness, or just more of the same.

David Jansen's Horace is a weak link, too lightweight and, beneath his grey hairs, too young for the company he's required to keep and the fight he's supposed to put up. Lisa Codrington also seems a little young but is otherwise splendid as Addie, the black servant who runs the Giddens house with all the authority of an ancestral retainer. The Hubbards have made their pile, we are told, by "cheatin' the coloureds;" Regina's coloureds take their revenge by being very selective with their loyalties: none for her, some for Horace and all the world for Alexandra who, Addie says, will marry Leo "over my dead body." So that's where the power lies. Sharry Flett, in a part that might have been written for her, finds a fluttery dignity nestling beneath the clipped wings. Like Blanche, Scarlett or any other self-respecting Southern heroine she can't help thinking of her old Kentucky home, or whatever part of the Dixie The Little Foxes is supposed to be happening in. Hellman wrote a sequel called Another Part of the Forest. Someone should revive that, in tandem or just for a change. - The Little Foxes runs until Nov. 1.
