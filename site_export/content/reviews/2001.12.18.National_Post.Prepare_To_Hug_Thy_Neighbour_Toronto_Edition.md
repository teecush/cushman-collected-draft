A CHRISTMAS CAROL

Premiere Dance Theatre, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - Practice, in Michael Shamata's production of A Christmas Carol, seems to have made perfect. He has directed his own adaptation of the Dickens story three times before -- twice at Theatre New Brunswick, one at the Grand in London -- and it arrives in Toronto, care of Soulpepper, as calm, inventive, funny and moving.

John Ferguson's set takes the eye from the first moment: a handsome but neutral yard enclosed by three elegant walls, each incorporating three double doors. The resulting space can be inside or out, Scrooge's office or his house or street that takes him from one to the other. It can also be any or all of the locations to which the Ghosts of Christmas Past, Present and Future transport him in his providential dream. Here the production, much assisted by Graeme Thomson's lighting, becomes very fast and very fluid.

More exactly, it is fluid in executing transitions -- the doors closing on one scene as they open on another -- but very solid once the changes have been accomplished. Each episode flashes up and makes an impact. The picture of Scrooge as a boy at Christmas abandoned in a schoolroom is indelibly presented, the child alone in a pool of light.

Each of the various Christmas parties has its own separately joyous identity, even though each is attended by the same group of hard-working actors, playing an average of four roles apiece.

The sequence in which the dream turns to nightmare is a stark montage, as Scrooge sees visions of his own death and of the reactions -- flippant indifference at best, relieved loathing at worst -- that it inspires in the survivors.

A Christmas Carol usually takes well to the stage; it has an instantly appealing flavour and is short enough not to need hacking to pieces. But few versions have been both as faithful and as lively as this one.

Joseph Ziegler's Scrooge is perhaps too soft a touch, seemingly well on his way to joining the human race from the moment Marley's ghost gives him his wake-up call. But he is very affecting as he sees his past mistakes unveiled before him, and quietly harrowing when he pleads with them to stop happening.

After his awakening he is a delight, bubbling with relief and with the happy sense of his own absurdity. He doesn't reach the giddy heights scaled in the movie version (I mean the movie version) by Alastair Sim, who made you want to hug not only him but everyone within a radius of 50 miles; with Ziegler you would settle for 25. But he does execute cheerful contortions -- cartwheels, almost -- that the late Mr. Sim might well have had in his soul but could never have realized with his body.

John Jarvis plays all four ghostly visitants (Marley included) in a vivid assortment of fantastic get-ups. He is best as the silent, menacing Christmas Yet to Come; his Christmas Present, though authoritative, is a bit of a bully, bellowing admonitions that might be more powerful if they were understated. (They are certainly topical, especially the vision of the two children, Ignorance and Want.)

Tanja Jacobs is vigorously, toughly Dickensian as Scrooge's housekeeper, who ends up robbing his corpse. Oliver Dennis is a briskly sympathetic Bob Cratchit, and Susan Coyne, sharp and tender, authentically Dickensian in another mode, as his wife.

Note how sensitive the production is in the matter of Tiny Tim: It solicits our tears, not by parading a cute crippled child in front of us but by letting us see the effect on his parents.

There are four children in the cast, of whom Ross Ward is especially good; he plays the boy outside Scrooge's window on Christmas morning. He delivers his part in this dialogue from offstage, another tactful touch; the production then rewards us for our patience by letting him appear in Scrooge's room, staggering under the weight of the bird he has been instructed to buy. It isn't quite as big as he is, as he described it, but it will do.

George Orwell described Dickens as a man "generously angry," and though it's the generosity that registers most strongly here, the anger can be felt, too. Contributions to the Daily Bread Food Bank are collected on the way out, so we have a chance to put the moral glow that this story inevitably induces to some practical use.

I could have done without the Harlequin masks on the scene- shifters, and the scene of young Ebenezer's abandonment by his fiancee falls flat, though Ziegler's reaction to it doesn't. Otherwise all is splendid.

This Christmas Carol is accompanied by a generous selection of Christmas carols, including one that's better known as Greensleeves. The late Michael Flanders had a theory about the ubiquity of this tune in productions of period plays: He claimed it was composed by Henry VIII, and that every time it was used as incidental music, the royalties went to Royalty.
