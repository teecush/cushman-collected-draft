Theatre Review

Julie Madly Deeply; The Boy with Tape on His Face

Panasonic Theatre, Toronto

Despite its awful title, Julie Madly Deeply is a delightful show. Part tribute, part biography, part impersonation, part affectionate satire, it's about Julie Andrews. But it's also about Sarah-Louise Young, who wrote and performed it, and it's that double focus that keeps it interesting and buoyant.

Young is a British cabaret performer who has mastered the art of being self-assured and selfdeprecating at the same time. She tells us frankly when she first comes on that she isn't Julie Andrews and never could be, thus striking the note that keeps her going throughout: She keeps a distance from her subject but not from us. Andrews is a singer with a - sometimes overlooked - flair for comedy. Young is a comedienne who can sing.

The distancing note has been struck before Young even comes on stage. Michael Roulston, her accompanist and an invaluable partner throughout, has played a brief overture consisting of songs that indeed come from Andrews' two biggest hits, My Fair Lady and The Sound of Music, but that were sung to or about her rather than by her.

When we get into the body of the show, too, Young contents herself with nibbling at the edges of the Andrews repertoire. When she gets to My Fair Lady she neglects the obvious "I Could Have Danced All Night" in favour of the less familiar "Show Me" ("say one more word and I'll scream") which she cleverly uses to illustrate her heroine's supposed impatience with voice teachers and directors. She gives us "Say a Prayer for Me Tonight" which was written for Andrews to sing as Eliza Doolittle off to the ball, but was cut (and later turned up in Gigi). Much later on she sings Stephen Sondheim's acidic "Could I Leave You," which Andrews performed in the anthology Putting It Together, and does it as well as anyone I have heard since Alexis Smith in the original Follies.

Pardon my show-buffery. One thing Young shares with her subject, or persuades us she does, is a very British no-nonsense briskness. She admits that her main strategy for getting inside the skin of her heroine is to wear a Sound of Music flaxen wig. She disparages her own vocal talents, though in fact she sings very well, even if she can't quite soar like Andrews in her prime. She does a very good Liza Minnelli impersonation (Minnelli subbed for Andrews in Victor/Victoria on Broadway), though perhaps not good enough to justify the number of times she repeats it; and the fact that she self-mockingly apologizes for the repetition doesn't make it better. She admits to having a limited repertoire of accents, though that couldn't reconcile me to the borscht-belt voice and delivery she imposes on the sophisticated Moss Hart (doesn't she know his plays?) or to the roughneck tones of her Richard Rodgers. (Oscar Hammerstein, in this rendition, is a silent partner.)

She's especially apologetic about the voice she uses for Julie's stepfather Ted Andrews, who was abusive, exploitative and Canadian. One thing that distinguishes Young from most visiting performers: When she pays compliments to Canada or makes jokes about Toronto, she actually sounds as if she means it. She may even have done some research.

Anyway, my own nostalgia kicks in here; growing up in Britain in the 1950s I used to hear the Andrews family on the BBC, billed as "Ted and Barbara Andrews and Julie." Then, suddenly, it was just "Julie Andrews," mother and stepdad having realized where the voice and the talent and the meal-ticket were.

There was Educating Archie, which had Julie playing straight-girl to a ventriloquist's dummy (on radio) and which I regret to say Young fails to treat with the respect it deserves. Still, her breakneck commentary on the teenage Andrews' rise to fame as if it were a horse-race (Petula Clark coming up on the inside) is irresistible. Then it's Broadway, with My Fair Lady celebrated and Camelot slighted, I think unfairly, even if Young does admit to a crush on Richard Burton; there should have been room for Alan Jay Lerner's tribute to Andrews' fortitude during the wreckage of that show's previews: "makes you wonder how the English ever lost India." Then the movies, of course, and Blake Edwards, and the decline from box-office sovereignty, and the tragic voice operation.

Young, both amused and amusing, keeps it all funny and honest and unsentimental and never gets cheap or manipulative. She doesn't manage to explain or explore - though she sometimes promises to - just why we all adore Julie Andrews, maybe because not all of us do. Some of us just like and admire her. (Others of course hate her, but that isn't reasonable, and isn't part of the present equation.)

At the end of the show Young gives us "Something Good," the song that Rodgers without Hammerstein wrote for the Sound of Music film and that is actually better than most that were carried over from the stage show. She sings it with the same restraint and simplicity as did Andrews himself, and it makes a lovely summation for both of them. They've both done something good.

Julie Madly Deeply, which isn't mad and isn't deep, comes to us direct - well, sort of - from the fringe of the Edinburgh Festival, as does The Boy with Tape on His Face; the two shows were shown to the critics on the same night but are now alternating.

Sam Wills, a New Zealander based in the U.K., is another writerperformer. He's also a mime, and he has masking tape over his mouth lest he should ever yield to the temptation to be anything else. He does some ingenious things with props, and gets props of his own, right off the bat, for consistently failing to perform a simple juggling trick. (Well, simple if you're a juggler. I assume. Actually it must take skill to get it wrong every time.)

But his main foils are his audience, whom he invites on stage in an unending succession and whom he directs by gesticulation. He gives them moves. When they get them wrong, he's scornful; when he gets them right, he's aghast. He communicates these feelings through what may be the most expressive eyeballs in show business. One spectator gets asked up repeatedly and is then summarily dismissed; until the end when he becomes the subject, or maybe that should be object, of an extended routine. The first-night victim exhibited a charming gift for resignation. Indeed, all the dragooned spectators turned out to have great stage personalities, thereby reinforcing my suspicion of performers who rely on the unpaid help to do most of the work. I also don't much like mimes. Nobody else seems to like them much either. And yet there's a public for them. So somebody must be lying.
