For 45 years, give or take, Harold Pinter has written the most commanding language in the English-speaking theatre. This is true whether or not you understand his plays, or even whether or not you like them.

There is no dialogue that matches his for snap, rhythm or resonance. He acts, too; and in recent years his acting has displayed the same ironclad wit and authority as his writing. Both talents were on display, full blast, on Wednesday at the Liberty Grand, where Pinter was the night's feted subject in Harbourfronts World Leaders series.

He read his most recent play, Celebration, which was staged last year in London. It is one of his lighter works, and conceivably his funniest. Two groups of people are celebrating at separate tables in a swish restaurant. Seated at one are an aggressively self-made businessman, his brother, and their wives, who are sisters. At the other is a smoother top dog and his wife. Between them flit the owner, his wife and a waiter.

I wouldnt claim that Pinter, flying solo, managed to keep all these nine characters absolutely distinct all the time, but the moments of doubt were remarkably few. Certainly he knew what he was doing, and his own enjoyment of it fairly bounced off his lectern.

Pinter was an actor before he was a playwright, and it would be interesting to know what his young performances were like. I suspect that success as a writer is what released his power and relaxation as an actor, and in recent years, with his dramatic output having slackened, a lot of the energy seems to have been re-channelled into performing.

In earlier years, he appeared occasionally in productions of his early plays, usually playing the aggressive wide-boy characters: Mick in The Caretaker, Lenny in The Homecoming. It would be great now to see him play Max, the overpowering father in the latter play. The vein of casual brutality that everybody has noticed in his plays is something that he brings out with relish in his acting; a few years ago, in a revival of his early play The Hothouse, he played the corrupt and hard-drinking director of a mental-hospital, and his barrel-housing power was, appropriately, intoxicating. Nothing, by the way, could have had less to do with the traditional, attenuated Pinter style."

But there is delicacy there. Both aspects were on show in his film performance in Mansfield Park, and both were rivetingly there in Celebration. The supposed top dogs here, as in other Pinter plays, spend a lot of time verbally humiliating their women, who respond in kind, but more subtly and hence more effectively. Pinter managed the coarse male aggression smashingly, but he was equally successful, roguishly feline, with the women, especially the one who taunts her partner with the exploits of her secretarial youth. ("I've been behind a few filing cabinets in my time.) Equally fine were his discreet, solicitous restaurateur, and a waiter liable to interrupt any conversation (May I interject?") with a jaw-dropping account of the famous people supposedly intimate with his grandfather: a roster that takes in most of the great literary names of the early 20th century, peaking with perfect comic pitch at "Thomas Hardy -- in his dotage."

Pinter's plays have always been about power (its one of the reasons actors like them so much) and most of his recent works have concentrated this theme into brief, naked parables of state oppression. There are political undercurrents in Celebration, but the focus is back on the sex-war. (The Caretaker, perhaps his gentlest play and still possibly his best, is one in which women are unseen and barely mentioned.) The new play revisits old Pinter territory with new joviality and expansiveness; and to watch the authors fabled reticence give way to the actor's needled flamboyance was an electric experience.

It eclipsed the tribute part of the evening: a staged dinner- party conversation about the Pinter oeuvre written by David Macfarlane and performed by some top Toronto actors. It had its clevernesses in its allusions to Celebration itself and to the dinner which most of the audience had just eaten as part of the deal, and it featured a delightful irruption-from-the-audience by Pinter's old actor friend Henry Woolf, now a theatre professor in Saskatoon. (At the risk of sounding Toronto-centric, couldn't anybody invite him to do a play here?). Mainly, though, it seemed forced.
