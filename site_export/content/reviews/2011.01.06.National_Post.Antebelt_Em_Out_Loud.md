What we have here is a delayed Canadian premiere. Parade was originally commissioned by Livent Inc., who workshopped it in Toronto, a dozen years ago. Livent then produced it in New York, but the company imploded and the production was seen no more. The show now returns to where it started in a joint production by Acting Up Stage, who specialize in musicals, and Studio 180, who specialize in plays on public themes. Parade being a musical on public themes, the combination ought to be ideal, in the event the show remains what it always was: interesting, ambitious and flawed.

The main problem is that, in trying to tell two stories, Parade shortchanges both. The subject is the case of Leo Frank, an American Jew from the Northern states who settled in Atlanta, Ga., and who in 1913 was tried and found guilty of the murder of a 13-year-old girl, Mary Phagan, who worked in the Atlanta pencil factory at which he was the supervisor. It's clear that Frank was railroaded, the victim of a xenophobic culture still licking its wounds from the Civil War. The show's first act takes us as far as the trial. The second is mainly concerned with Frank's efforts, and especially those of his wife, Lucille, to appeal the verdict. The marriage of Leo and Lucille, who come closer

in adversity than they had ever been before, is one of the two stories. The other is that of the town itself, and by extension of the whole Southern consciousness. In theory, of course, the two narratives should be one. In practice, it feels as if they're alternating.

Leo, in particular, seems alienated from the action, and not just because he spends most of it in jail. He's sketched in for us, but only sketched, as a pettish, repressed man whose demeanour attracts as much suspicion as his religion. He also has the perennial problem of victims in drama: Because he doesn't get to do anything, it's easier to feel sorry for him than to feel moved or involved.

TheFranks'relationshipgetsfuller treatment from Jason Robert Brown's score than it does from Alfred Uhry's script; the couple have a propulsively hopeful duet, and then a serenely rhapsodic one, to both of which Michael Therriault and Tracy Michailidis bring the requisite urgency if not the hoped-for glow. Therriault excellently depicts the buttoned-up Leo of the beginning (this, after The Producers, is his second musical role as a nebbish called Leo) but he doesn't get much chance to take it further, and his growth in courage and dignity is stated rather than explored. His final moment of heroism comes out of nowhere. Michailidis has a more active role, but she's done in, at least in part, by the authors' second thoughts, which strike me as unwise.

The script performed here is one reworked by Uhry and Brown for a London production at the Donmar Warehouse. It streamlines the second act, most of which revolves around Lucille's successful attempt to get Georgia's governor to reopen her husband's case, which he had previously regarded with urbane disinterest. He's potentially the most interesting character in the piece, a man conflictedly doing right being always more compelling than one who's being done wrong, and the scenes in which he cross-examines the various perjured witnesses are the most exciting in the show. But the complexities of his relationship with the case and with Lucille have been ironed out.

Gone are any hints of flirtatiousness, including his escorting her to a state house ball. Gone are the conscience-stricken second thoughts of the judge at Leo's trial. Jay Turvey plays the governor sympathetically, but the reduced circumstances are unfair to him, to the story and to the audience. Also to George Masswohl who is twice wasted: as the judge and as an emblematic Old Soldier who appears at the beginning and the end as a representative of the embittered Confederacy, but has no existence in between, as man or symbol.

This brings us to the show's second weakness: its failure, certainly in this production, to do justice to injustice, both inside and outside the courtroom. Brown has musicalized the trial itself resourcefully, but without capturing the full outrageous scope of the proceedings; as often, you learn more about these from the program than from the play. You find, for example, that the viciously anti-Semitic demagogue played by Mark Uhre was actually a newspaper editor; he seems here to be a deranged evangelist. Joel Greenberg is usually a good director of political drama (and of lynching dramas, cf. The Laramie Project), but the manoeuvrings in and around the governor's mansion hardly register.

Paul Sportelli's musical direction is especially taut here; so is Daren A. Herbert whose double performance as a terrified janitor and a contemptuous convict is the strongest of the night. He shares with Alana Hibbert a rousing second-act opener in which two black Atlantans get to exult that, for once in the South, another minority group is getting it. It's one of too few occasions on which the piece takes us inside the thoughts of the community it's depicting.

Meanwhile, the plain staging shortchanges the imagery implicit in the title. The murder takes place on Memorial Day (described by Frank as a typical Southern notion -- glorifying a defeat) and two more Memorial Day parades come and go before the drama is done. This doesn't quite unify the action, but it strengthens it. Parade needs that strength. Its subject-matter is hardly grimmer than that of Sweeney Todd, Assassins or Cabaret, but those shows leave you with irony or tragedy or both. Feeling drained is good; feeling depressed isn't.
