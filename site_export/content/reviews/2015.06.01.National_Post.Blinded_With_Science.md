THEATRE REVIEW

The Physicists Tom Patterson Theatre, Stratford

Our first sight in The Physicists is of a dead female body. Police are hovering around, doing due diligence. There has been a murder. The scene of the crime is an extremely plush Swiss sanatorium, for mental patients. One of the inmates has strangled his nurse. This is not the first time such a thing has happened. Nor will it be the last.

There are three patients in this particular wing. All are former physicists with impressive track records. One thinks he is Sir Isaac Newton and dresses accordingly. Another believes himself to be Einstein, complete with facial hair and violin. The third answers to his own name, which is Mobius, but claims to be on speaking terms with King Solomon, who is dictating to him what may be the most important scientific document of all time, on the principle of universal discovery. And I may already have said too much, because Friedrich Durrenmatt's play operates on its own principle of universal surprise. Much of it functions like a John le Carre novel, continually uncovering new levels of deceit. Its a terrific thriller, with its suspense, along with its wit, played to the limit in Miles Potters scintillating Stratford production. Trapdoors keep opening beneath the feet of characters and audience alike. The traps it keeps springing, though, are as much moral as narrative, and the joy of the play is that they happen simultaneously. Ultimately, it's about the fate of the world.

Durrenmatt, Swiss himself, crested as a playwright around 1960. The Physicists was first produced in 1962, and the play was greeted around that time, somewhat simplistically, as a plea for nuclear disarmament.

The issue went into comforting hibernation for decades but now of course it, like the play, is with us again full-force. Michael Healey's snappy adaptation allows itself some industrial and technological updating in its final section, in which the hunchbacked lady who runs the asylum takes centre stage. Hitherto she has been at most a domestic dictator (I decide who my patients think they are); now she reveals far more extensive powers.

The plays key line, spoken by Mobius, is "what is thought cannot be unthought." A

program note calls this a warning the world must never allow to become a prophecy. Actually its something more terrifying than either: it's a truth. It's what has made this play a sleeping dragon. Its implications and his own conscience have driven Mobius, apparently the greatest scientist ever, into a madhouse. Geraint Wyn Davies, in what may be his finest performance, plays him as gentle, honest, with an iron will and capable of great suffering. When hes visited by his ex-wife, along with their three children and her new husband, theres a wrenching contrast between what his words say and what his face betrays. The tension is tighter still in his scene with the nurse (Claire Lautier, attractive and attracted) who adores him and whom he loves. Open of countenance, a credible genius, he carries with him the darkest of secrets: the King Solomon who is his avatar, ended in despair. Wisdom decays, even though knowledge doesn't.

Of his two colleagues, Graham Abbey's bewigged Newton is elegantly, hilariously feline. Mike Nadajewskis Einstein has less authority than the others (his disguise doesnt seem to have penetrated beneath the surface) but he fits in well. Randy Hughson brings knife-edge timing, and wonderful world-weariness, to the perplexed, disillusioned police inspector, who couldn't care less about drinking on the job; his is another personal-best performance, at least at Stratford. Sean Arbuckle does a delightful minor turn as Mobius' marital successor, described as "a passionate missionary." (He has six children of his own.) Both of these two supporting characters are at the least, well-intentioned - if, in the inspectors case, beaten down. Indeed, the one flicker of hope the play offers is the number of people in it who, chips down, are capable of behaving decently. How much good it does is another question.

The virtuous do not, if I may spoil, include the Fraulein Doktor-in-charge whom Seana McKenna, with blond wig and hump, plays as a variant of her Richard III, all barbed wit and feigned innocence. Shes very funny, and somewhat chilling. What she surprisingly lacks is grandeur, a quality that I dimly remember as emerging strongly in the concluding stages of Peter Brook's London production. Potter's version doesnt get that; its lightweight, with a kick. Within those limits, on Peter Hartwells economically perfect set, it's a spellbinder. It opened at Stratford, by the way, the night after The Sound of Music, with which it shares not only an Alpine setting but a corps of singing kids, though the Mobius Family Singers prove less adept than the Trapps at melting their dad. Otherwise, the two shows have little in common.
