THE TRIUMPH OF LOVE

Artword Theatre, London

The set for the first-ever Canadian production of Marivaux's The Triumph of Love is like a do-it-yourself kit for modest productions of 18th-century French comedy.

At the back is a flight of white steps leading up to a narrow entrance through which we glimpse a small rectangle of sky. A versatile vista, this changes from blue to mauve to back again, and once takes a dashing turn at being green. There are tall screens at the sides of the stage, bearing patterns of branches -- this play, like many another artificial comedy, is set in a garden. A few spindly trees stand up on stage, and there are also -- this is a philosophers garden -- a couple of formal but comfortable chairs.

A mirror confronts us from high on the back wall, reflecting the action before and below. Im not sure why, except two images for the price of one always make a striking effect. The trees, as it happens, look more convincing in reflection than they do in reality: Maybe there's an intellectual point there. The various parts do not really cohere, and a brown false proscenium that must be meant to unify them is downright ugly, but the design serves.

John van Burek's direction of his own translation more than serves: It is a considerable achievement. Its great virtue is nobody in it seems to be treading water. Every actor seems to know at every moment just what he or she is talking about and how it relates to what everybody else is talking about. This may not seem much to ask, but it is a surprisingly rare occurrence, certainly in a play as remote from us in time, place and convention as this one. I wont pretend my concentration never slackened, but I dont think theirs ever did.

In France, Marivaux's airy, trenchant studies of amorous deception have been making a comeback. Elsewhere, they hardly arrived in the first place, and though we know more of them than we did (this production follows on the same company's Game of Love and Chance last year), we still need careful guidance.

This is the story: The Princess Leonide wishes to make amends to the young prince Agis, whose family was disinherited -- and, it seems, decimated -- by hers.

She also has every intention of marrying him. So she seeks him out in the country house of the philosopher Hermocrate, who has watched over him since his childhood.

Since she would hardly be welcome in her own shape, the princess disguises herself as, as if you hadnt guessed, a man. Calling herself Phocion, she professes to be a travelling student, a sort of guru-collector who wishes to stay with Hermocrate in order to soak up his wisdom. With her she brings her servant, similarly transgendered.

To the ironies that traditionally accrue in a situation like this, Marivaux adds one of his own: Half the people Leonide encounters see through her masquerade and half dont, so the levels of deceit and awareness vary dizzyingly throughout. Shakespeare never thought of that.

We, inevitably, do think of As You Like It, what with deposed princes living in woods and disguised princesses conducting tutorials in passion and misanthropic scholars who claim to be above all that. However, unlike Rosalind, Leonide-Phocion is a ruthless flirt. To get close to Agis, she has to charm and disarm both Hermocrate, who knows she is a woman, and Hermocrate's bluestocking sister, Leontine (Marivuax can be brutally confusing with names), who thinks she is a woman. Coolly and successfully, she sets about making them both fall in love with her: Imagine Rosalind making a play for Jaques rather than laughing at him, and encouraging Phebe instead of rebuffing her.

She also has to buy and keep the co-operation of two of Hermocrate's male servants. Her own female attendant helps out here. Biology and the rules of theatre have their predestined way, and the play ends happily for some, cruelly for others. That is what comedy means.

I can imagine a production that went deeper than this one both psychologically and scenically, one that pointed up the pain and the sexual ambiguities, that made more use of emotional and pictorial chiaroscuro (the lighting here is very flat) -- one, frankly, with more money to spend.

Van Burek goes instead for speed and lucidity and achieves both. His translation is crisp, though occasionally coming up with words such as "whippersnapper" and wiseacre and "shenanigans" that are meant to sound colloquial, but dont. (A test: Can you imagine anyone using them in a new English play? They dont sound 18th- century either.) On the other hand, an observation such as "it puts a sort of fungus on your manly lineage," addressed by a gardener to his employer, is in context priceless.

In the virtuoso role of the faux Phocion, Amy Price-Francis, who was promising earlier this year with the Montreal Young Company, is endlessly charming and endlessly intelligent. She is not endlessly resourceful: She has to spend most of her time seducing one person or another and she does run out of changes to ring. Her few moments of royal whip-cracking are effective, and she governs the play: Without her or somebody comparable, it would fall apart. It will be interesting to see which major company snaps her up first.

She gets witty support from Helen Taylor as her maid, a role that disappointingly fades out, and rather unformed romantic opposition from Allan Hawco's young prince, though he does redeem himself with a moment of plain-speaking exasperation near the end. Michael Spencer-Davis is a nicely gnarled and mercenary rustic and Paul Fauteux a merry Harlequin, though excessively given to twirling himself around tree trunks.

The production has its tricky moments and most of them are inflicted on or by him. But then what do you do with a character called Harlequin? Is he a convention? Is he real? This version tries for a little of both.

That leaves the two crucial roles of the scholarly siblings. Ross Manson agreeably sketches in the outlines of Hermocrate, but he is not the absurd and noble presence that seems called for. Philippa Domville, retreating from her superbly full-bodied work in Time after Time into her Shaw Festival genteel mode, makes a chill spinster of Leontine.

Less funny and less incipiently passionate than they might be, they are also less sad. They do, though, share a fine proto-Wildean duet when each declares the intention of marrying the same person and puts on a fancy hat for the occasion.

In summary, this Triumph of Love is not quite a triumph but it is to love.
