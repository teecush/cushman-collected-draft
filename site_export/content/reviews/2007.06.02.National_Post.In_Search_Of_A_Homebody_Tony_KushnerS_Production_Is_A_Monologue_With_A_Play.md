No Canadian actress does language, especially curlicued, high- comic language, like Fiona Reid. She has the first hour of Tony Kushner's Homebody/Kabul entirely and entrancingly to herself. She plays a London woman, the homebody of the title. She tells us that she is married, though the most intimate contact she and her husband now have consists of sampling one another's antidepressants. She also has a daughter, and there isn't much communication there either. Her chief delight is reading: "I have," she confesses, "read too many books. I haven't read many books, but I've read too many."

The books she favours are slightly out-of-date travel guides and histories; she's currently engrossed in one about Afghanistan. Its style has infected her own; she speaks in long and ornate sentences rendered even longer by her constant fear that she may have misspoken herself and must therefore go back and start again. Reid doesn't drop a stitch of this dense verbal knitting; she renders confusion with devastating, hilarious lucidity. More than that, she creates a character out of syntax, a print-dress sort of lady who's modest and worried but has a questing soul. She tells us about visiting a store and finding to her amazement that she can understand the proprietor, even though he's speaking Pashtun. Soon, so the rest of the play informs us, she will journey to Kabul herself and never come back. The year is 1998. The Taliban rule; the Americans have not yet bombed.

Kushner is a wonderful writer of lines, speeches and sometimes even scenes, but his plays, including the trophied Angels in America, are a mess. This one, as per its title, is two plays, or a monologue with a play attached. The monologue is superb, the play is confused and at times banal. Husband Milton and daughter Priscilla journey to Kabul to bring the homebody's body home. They're told she has been killed, hideously. Milton wearily believes it, Priscilla angrily doubts it. Weariness and anger are their character notes. They squabble for much of the play over their feelings about the vanished woman, a subject in which it's impossible to take much interest since the homebody herself told us very little about their relationship and we've never seen it for ourselves.

Priscilla, donning a burqua, hits the Kabul streets in search of her mother, or at least her mother's body, and runs almost immediately into potentially deadly trouble. She's rescued by a Tajik poet, who offers to act as her guide and interpreter, though not without his own agenda. Sanjay Talwar plays this role with an appealing blend of politeness and passion. He also codirects with Jon Michaelson; their production, once Reid is offstage, takes a long time to gather fresh momentum. Lesley Faulkner, whom I remember being iridescent as the surrogate Marilyn Monroe in After the Fall, is allowed to play Priscilla on a single grating note, even when going rapturous over the Afghan landscape. Her dad experiences a similarly shop-soiled transcultural epiphany by doing drugs. He's your stereotypical uptight Englishman, though Michael Spencer-Davis gets to transcend the type. The family's surname is Ceiling, and the play tries to show all its members breaking through theirs, but there are no clear connections between their successive actions.

Speaking of connections, Milton's supplier is a British consular type named Quango Twistleton, who has gone native for the narcotics; Kris Holden-Ried, new to the stage, gives him a quiet (i.e., semi- audible) charm. Deena Aziz brings the show back close to its opening level as a former librarian seeking Western asylum and desperately conflicted about it. A speech in which she rails against oppression, both homegrown and imported, constantly switching between languages as between targets, is a tour de force.

Words themselves are a recurring motif. The Tajik poet writes his verses in Esperanto, which he prizes for its "hyper-rational ungainliness." Milton works in computer language, another rational and ungainly tongue, which, when he speaks it, becomes its own poetry. Language, as this play's best writing and acting show, is a powerful tool. But it doesn't make a play.
