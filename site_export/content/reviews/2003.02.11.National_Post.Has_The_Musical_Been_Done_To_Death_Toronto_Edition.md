Anything Goes continues in repertoire at the National Theatre, London; My Fair Lady has an open-ended run at the Theatre Royal, Drury Lane.; robertcushman@lycos.com

The new director of Britain's National Theatre brings down the curtain

Nicholas Hytner, who is about to take over as director of Britain's National Theatre, has announced in advance that under his regime the National will no longer be producing musicals. They don't really belong there, he says, and anyway the company has already done all the good ones.

This seems, at first blush, somewhat dog-in-the-mangerish of him. As a guest director, he was responsible a few years ago for a production of Carousel that ranks as one of the National's most successful and enlightening musical revivals. It's as if, having had his fun, he's all set to stop other directors -- not to mention other audiences -- having theirs.

He has a point or two, though. If you wanted to be mean, you might say that, in this department, the National Theatre started at the top when it put on Guys and Dolls in 1982 and has been going downhill ever since. It's not just that that show, as written, is the best of all Broadway musicals; it's also that it offers uniquely rich opportunities to a company of actors rather than an ad hoc collection of song-and-dance specialists.

Since that date the National's own company strength has fragmented, and its productions -- of plays as well as musicals -- have increasingly become one-off affairs. When a musical appears in the repertoire -- as has happened with increasing frequency -- it has no longer been as a jeu d'esprit but as a building block, taking up a lot of time and space and holding out at least the possibility of transferring and making money.

Hytner's own opening lineup includes Jerry Springer -- The Opera, so we can take it that when he says he'll be abandoning musicals, he actually means mainstream Broadway musicals. (The English National Theatre has never revived an English musical, unless you count The Beggar's Opera.) For the most part, the National up until now has staged the brand-name shows everybody knows, a policy similar to that of the Stratford Festival, whose move into musicals was partly inspired by the National's example.

This has certainly been the case under the regime of the departing director, Trevor Nunn, who came to the National with a double reputation: as Britain's best interpreter of classic drama, Shakespeare especially, and as the director of Cats and Les Miserables. His National Theatre shows have included My Fair Lady, which has transferred to the West End, and Oklahoma!, a brilliant staging which followed Hytner's even more revelatory Carousel by conquering New York itself. (A confession: Years ago, the National commissioned me to write a report on the possible musical repertoire. In the course of it I asserted that there would be no point in the National Theatre doing Rodgers and Hammerstein. I hang my head.)

Nunn's valedictory musical is itself a departure, not one of the post-war behemoths but Anything Goes, Cole Porter's chef-d'oeuvre of 1934, set on that quintessential musical comedy location, a luxury liner. It has been rapturously received. I found it high on extravagance and short on personality -- which is exactly the reverse of how the show must originally have been conceived. Anything Goes enshrines the spirit of the '30s musical -- one of defiant, sardonic gaiety -- more completely than any other show, but while it trumps its genre, it does not transcend it. With their great songs and farcical scripts, these are wonderfully unimportant shows that originally tumbled onto the stage at the rate of a dozen a season. As far as I can tell, dancing was a lot less important to them than clowning.

Now, however, a revival of a '30s show is governed less by the nature of the piece itself than by generalized expectations about "the musical" as a machine for killing the audience with kindness. The pattern may well have been set by the overweening stage version of 42nd Street, which sacrificed all the grit of the movie in favour of an endless series of roof-raising production numbers. We are assaulted by the idea of entertainment, as if you could somehow manufacture innocence.

The highlight of this Anything Goes is the title number which, after its introductory vocal, builds into a full-stage, full- company tap number (Stephen Mears is the choreographer) that goes on and on and on through any number of styles and any number of combinations. It's irresistible, not least when Susan Tracy, a distinguished actress previously untaxed by the role of a battleaxe mother, stands in front of the line and shakes a leg that's as mean as her disposition. It provides, in its unexpectedness, a perfect illustration of what the song is about. It tumultuously closes the first act. But it's devalued when, 10 minutes into the second, a very similar treatment is given to the mock-revival meeting number, Blow Gabriel Blow, this time with minimal reference to the subject of the lyric.

Some of the smaller numbers, too, are fussily staged. To take a minute but significant example: In You're the Top, the line "you're Napoleon brandy" is here cued by the singer's catching sight of a glass of the stuff on a nearby table. Nobody delivering this paragon of list-songs should have to pause, for inspiration or anything else.

A major problem in reviving these shows: They were written, very specifically, as star vehicles, and we don't have the stars. Reno Sweeney, the nightclub evangelist, was the role that first defined Ethel Merman; here it's done by Sally Anne Triplett, who dances well but is so lacking in individuality that, whenever she came on in a change of costume, I failed to recognize her. Her leading man (John Barrowman), who should be cutely hard-boiled, has an unfortunate tendency to simper.

Simon Day, though, is first-rate as a titled buffoon of P. G. Wodehouse provenance (Wodehouse was one of the show's original writers), and Martin Marquez is fairly funny as the obligatory gangster, though he doesn't have the crumpled innocence the role calls for; when he disguises himself as a clergyman, he should look the part. As originally written, this hood was a superstitious soul, worried by his position as Public Enemy Number 13 and determined to rub out another mobster in order to promote himself.

That joke, alas, is no longer with us. The current, heavily adapted script was first staged at Lincoln Center in 1987, and I recall it being much more amusing there, partly because of more adept performers (Howard McGillin was a perfect cad of a hero, and Patti LuPone had charisma, even if she unforgivably swallowed Porter's consonants) and partly because the surrounding production, though grand, was not grandiose. The huge Olivier stage is no place to be intimate, and I suppose Nunn has given it and his audience what they both demand. But I wish he had approached the show as he might a minor Restoration comedy; gone back to the original, kept its structure if not all of its dialogue, and refrained from augmenting what was already a classic score. That would be a task for a National Theatre, and even Hytner might agree. The Shaw Festival used to do something of the kind with vintage musicals, though going to the opposite extreme of enforced economy.

Nunn's production of My Fair Lady (transferred to Drury Lane, where the Lerner-Loewe musical made its first London bow in 1958) is far more assured and has of course much more solid material to build on; not better, necessarily, but firmer. He does fall again into the British director's habit of treating the American musical as a giant box of candies of which not one can be left unsampled. Thus we get two extended Cockney knees-ups, where tradition and common sense call for only one. Higgins (Alex Jennings, wonderfully boyish in face and mind) looks set to deliver I've Grown Accustomed to Her Face all alone in the middle of a huge empty stage, and it promises to be tremendous, but then the director gets him travelling through London, haunted by the sight and sound of other flower girls. When it comes to Alfred Doolittle, the production seems to be mining a dark subtext that doesn't actually exist, either in the musical or in Shaw's play; the actor (Dennis Waterman) is plain surly.

But there are some inspired ideas, of which the best is perhaps the simplest. The interval has been moved forward so that the first- act curtain descends on a radiant but petrified Eliza departing for the ball -- a cliff-hanger, in fact. The Ascot scene has been revitalized by locating the action at a time of royal mourning; a scene we're used to seeing in white is now all in black.

Joanna Riding, a plucky Eliza, does especially well in that scene, showing off her new accent and her old ideas, but it's worth getting hold of the cast CD to hear her predecessor in the role, Martine McCutcheon. McCutcheon, a star of East Enders, brought a modern Cockney sound to the early unregenerate Eliza that made her songs and her whole character live as never before (though for the later, posher Miss Doolittle you're still best off with Julie Andrews).

Early on, a lyrical staging of Wouldn't It Be Loverly, full of character detail that's relevant rather than redundant, perfectly stakes the piece's claim to be a musical. Up to then, it's been Pygmalion with a patter-number stuck in; now, suddenly, it's a romance.

The end is as I've never seen it. Eliza stands impassive at Higgins' new demand for his slippers; she raises her hand, as if to throw them again; he raises his; they stand, locked; then they both burst out laughing. Maybe this will be a partnership of equals. It's about text and acting, two large parts of what a National Theatre is for.

Nunn's final farewell production is to be Love's Labour's Lost, deploying his Anything Goes cast. Shakespearean romance and Broadway romance have much in common -- both are composed of youth and cleverness -- and I hope the equation works.
