End of Civilization runs in repertory until June 12 at the Factory Theatre. 416-504-9971. Evita runs until June 5 at the Princess of Wales. 416-872-1212.

And more revivals. One of them is revelatory, or at least one scene in it is. Theres a duologue in George F. Walker's End of Civilization, one of the two plays from his Suburban Motel cycle that Factory Theatre is remounting, that's unlike anything in any other Walker piece I have encountered. The thing is, its normal. Or at least, its about people who have recently left a state of being that most audiences would accept as normality, and who are still reeling from the shock, unable to adjust. Most Walker characters are at the end of their tethers; and though this is routinely described as being "disturbing, it really isn't, because we can't imagine them ever being at the other end.

Here we meet Henry and his wife, Lily: Henry has lost his job, and has reached the desperation stage as regards finding a new one. So he has come to the same anonymous motel room we know from previous plays (excellently realized, in all its just-short-of- seediness, by Shawn Kerwins set design), so that he can use it as a base from which to present himself for even more interviews; and Lily has come along, to give him support. But of course, with their house on the line and their kids staying with relatives, all the strains of their marriage are coming to the surface. Lily resents Henrys assumption that, just because she's never had a job, she would be incapable of finding one now to help out. Henry is fearful of being mocked, by his wife or the world. Already dangerously angry, he has begun to take pills.

As well as being brilliantly written, this scene is cunningly placed so that it functions not as exposition but as exploration. The performances, by Dennis O'Connor and Brenda Bazinet, illuminate every beat of conversation, every flicker of tension; a partnership turns, before our eyes, into a pair of resentful solitudes.

OConnor, a weighty actor in every sense with a shock of white hair, comes on like a cross between Falstaff and Lear; his railings against the state of a greedy, heartless world take on a farcical kind of grandeur. It has to be farcical because this is a Walker play, after all, and one of the marks of a Walker character is an insouciant acceptance of the awful. (The characters revel in it; the author, somehow, doesnt.) As he sinks deeper into Walkerland, Henry comes under suspicion of mass murder -- not of the men denying him work, but of his fellow applicants, whom he despises as disgusting ass kissers... depressing."

Lily, however, doubts he did it: "I just can't see him killing people. Especially if they weren't management.

Investigating the case are Max and Donny, the same two world- weary cops whom we can meet alternate nights in the companion- piece, Adult Education.

Here they are onlookers rather than prime movers, and it suits them better. Ron Whites Max, not so much hard-boiled as over-easy, is especially good, now that hes allowed to stick strictly to business. Donny is more involved; it turns out he was at school with Lily, and he gets very upset when she discovers the easiest way for a woman alone in a suburban motel to make some money.

Not that Donny has anything against professionals (matter of fact, he's obsessed with them) but he has standards.

Donny is crazy" in Max's estimate, "sick in Lilys; and David Ferry is, as before, expert but perhaps too endearing. But then his place in the action is rather strange; he isnt at its centre, but he won't stay on the periphery either. Walker delights in messing with his audience's expectations, both of chronology (this play has an ultra-confusing time-scheme, of which I cannot even now make perfect sense) and of symmetry. Donny, by common consent of all who meet him, needs help. One thing he could maybe use is a play of his own.

The cast, in Ken Gass unimpeachable production, is completed by Jody Stevens, obvious but not objectionable as the hooker down the hall. End of Civilization is the best play now in Toronto, as bleak, funny and pertinent now as at its premiere seven years ago -- when, incidentally, it had a definite article to its name. More downsizing.

From further back in theatrical history, not to mention historical history, Evita returns to confirm its reputation as the best musical to come from the firm of Tim Rice and Andrew Lloyd Webber, whether working separately or together.

Rices more sardonic lyrics wear particularly well; the syllables tend to sit jerkily on the notes, but they have point and attitude, especially in the opening Oh What a Circus, the commentary of someone called Che (sort of Guevera, but only sort of) at the funeral of the temporary secular saint Eva Peron. Musically the number is a pre-echo (a preprise, perhaps) of the show's hit tune, Don't Cry for Me Argentina, and its good to hear it so comprehensively desecrated.

And when Che remarks of all the mourning that its good for the country. In a roundabout way" we are hearing political analysis with more edge and resonance than we usually get in musicals, especially in the 25 years since. Also it sounds unforced, not overly impressed by its own toughness. Rice was always a joker. These are his best, and most serious, jokes. They are the wittiest lyrics Webber has ever had to set. And yes, that does include Cats.

Rice is less impressive when he tries for Broadway wordplay of a more traditional kind. When Eva in her glory sings "The people adore me. So Christian Dior me, it's like bad imitation Dave Frishberg. (Frishberg wrote Peel Me a Grape: Best way to cheer me -- Cashmere me... New Thunderbird me -- you heard me." Hear the difference?) And when, in the Big Song, the lady proclaims herself "Dressed up to the nines. At sixes and sevens with you, it's pitiful. Its also a shock to realize that the tune itself consists just of its title phrase and some plodding padding. Webbers Latin pastiches, though, have energy, especially the tango and the brisk parade of Eva's discarded suitors as she sleeps her way to the top. Her rise from direst poverty is very well presented, evoking both sympathy and revulsion. Keeping these divided aims in balance proves much harder, though, once she's made it. There are gaping narrative holes -- the bane of the sung-through musicals, where everything has to be conveyed through lyrics which arent up to the job. Why did Eva throw herself at Juan Peron, rather than at any other of Argentinas ambitious soldier-politicians? What did she see in him? What did they promise, that made the people support them? What did they see in her -- or, as Che puts it, all too rhetorically who is this Santa Evita?" Just a Latin-American Princess Diana? The answers to these and other questions can be found in the program more easily than on the stage. Che's verdict -- "she did nothing for years -- seems all too accurate, dramatically if not politically. That does not make for a satisfactory heroine. Or even a satisfactory anti- heroine

And that's another, even more important, thing. The show has no characters. Peron himself is a cipher. The most emotional song goes to his discarded mistress, whom we have never met before and never meet again. (Im told that in the movie, they gave this number to Eva. Smart move; she could use it.) That leaves Che and Eva, a commentator and a symbol. They never meet, never make contact, not even when they sing a duet. It leaves an emptiness.

His is the better role, a brave cynic who keeps getting beaten up for being ironic. Bradley Dean sings in the choppy falsetto that now seems inseparable from the genre (it wasnt originally) but he has the right mocking posture.

Eva Peron was, obviously, a hard case; Kathy Voytko sounds as hard as the nails that proverbially get scratched on chalkboards. But of the charm that she must also have had -- what Rice's scansion forces her to describe as a little touch of star-qualitee' -- there is no trace. She can go dreamy when required, but her passion is petulance.

The shows strongest point was always its staging and that remains, with Larry Fuller, the original choreographer, "reproducing" the original direction of Harold Prince, who now has an "entire production supervised by" credit. The emblematic groupings are still striking, especially the nine naval officers who move in tight formation, three abreast, and signify Power. Theres also a recurring tableau vivant of Argentinian nobs, looking and sounding as if they had dropped in from the Ascot scene in My Fair Lady. It's a quarter century since the show's London premiere, but half the cast still sounds English. Evita is the best of all the pop operas, or rock song-cycles, or whatever you want to call them, but that doesnt constitute a claim on immortality. You let down your people, Evita. You were supposed to have been immortal. But in the end you could not deliver.
