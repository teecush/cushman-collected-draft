THEATRE REVIEW

The Homecoming

Avon Theatre, Stratford

In 1958, the British theatre critic Irving Wardle, later to become the revered reviewer for The Times, wrote an essay entitled "Comedy of Menace," in which he identified a group of playwrights devoted to raising laughs by raising chills, for purposes satirical, metaphysical or both. His prize exhibit was Harold Pinter, whose only full-length play at the time was The Birthday Party. In this play, he noted, menace, in itself a meretricious and easilymanufactured device, stands for something more substantial: Destiny.

Wardle did not, contrary to claims in the Stratford program, invent the "comedy of menace" label for Pinter's The Homecoming, which didnt come along until 1964, though it certainly is plenty comic and plenty menacing. Its a far tidier, more accomplished play than The Birthday Party, but it doesn't have the same sense of inevitability. Its characters are the playthings not of destiny but of their author's caprice. It ends up feeling forced and false.

Morally, this may be to the playwrights credit. The most acute comment on the play may have come from another playwright, Joe Orton, whose specialty was the comedy of bland outrageousness. Theres a very Ortonesque moment in The Homecoming when a character is felled by a heart attack and another character says what a shame that is because he'd been hoping the victim would drive him to the airport. It brings the house down, but it isn'tcredible. The trouble, according to Orton, is that he really believed people were that heartless but Harold doesnt. The line plays like imitation Orton. And so, more importantly, does the major plot development in which a father and two of his sons conspire to turn the wife of a third son into a call girl, to her acquiescence and that of her husband.

Its not that such a thing couldn't happen. It's not even that the play hasnt prepared the ground; its dropped all kinds of hints about the wife's previous life and present discontents, and it's positively revelled in depicting the men of the house as competitive misogynists. But it still seems to be happening because the author, against his natural grain, is willing it to happen. And yes, the menace is manufactured, though how easily only Pinter himself would know.

To be more specific: Were in the North London house of Max, a retired butcher; his brother Sam, a chauffeur; and Maxs sons Lenny, who we gradually gather is a pimp, and Joey, an aspiring boxer who, according to his dad, has now only two things to learn: how to defend himself and how to attack. In this household, not knowing those things is fatal; psychologically everyone in it, Max and Lenny especially, is always on the attack and had better know how to defend. The place has been womanless since the death of Max's wife, whose memory he alternately reveres and reviles; Max now does the cooking and this, plus a gammy leg, undermines his status.

Another British critic, the late Ronald Bryden, described the play, and Pinter's work in general, as a fight for territory; as a theory, this has the disadvantage that you could apply it to almost any play ever written, but it is fair to say that The Homecoming gives us the battle for stage space in its purest form, with Max as the wounded lion surrounded by ambitious cubs. The eldest of them, Teddy, got out and became a philosophy professor in America; he now returns with Ruth, his wife and mother of his own three sons. The family, whove never met Ruth before, greet her as Teddys whore. She, though, is a match for even the impenetrably street-smart Lenny: "you take this glass," she tells him during their major altercation and I'll take you. That line is, in its self-consciousness, the first false note in a hitherto pitch-perfect text. It's like imitation Pinter.

This chiselled, oracular tone dominated the first production of The Homecoming, and its become the recognized "Pinter style," though oddly enough Pinters acting in his own plays was nothing like it; he was wonderfully forceful and brutal, barrelling through with never a trademark pause in sight. He would have made a magnificent Max.

Jennifer Tarver's Stratford production is in the older, heavier tradition but strong and exact on its own terms. The nearest thing to a weak link is the Max of Brian Dennehy, who's physically too tall for the role and vocally too slight; he negotiates all the transitions from bullying to cringing without carrying absolute conviction at either extreme. Since the play literally begins and ends with Max, this matters.

Aaron Krohn is a brilliant Lenny, in absolute command of the flash, needling idiom, and Ian Lake an enchantingly vacant Joey. Stephen Ouimette makes a real flickering moral force of Sam who, like Petey in The Birthday Party, is the small disregarded voice of decency. Mike Shara, ineffectual rather than distant, softens Teddy who should probably be as callous as his siblings, but he does what he does very well. Cara Ricketts, handed the most opaque role in the play, makes Ruth a living woman, intelligent as well as beautiful, a believable lust-object for the men but more than a mystery object for the audience.

Leslie Frankish's costumes are spot-on, though Teddy's turtleneck does seem to be about to devour him; her set is big and brown and brooding, fittingly devoid of any feminine influence whatsoever. The Homecoming has been described as "the ultimate Pinter play," and so it is: with all the brilliance that implies, and all the limitations.
