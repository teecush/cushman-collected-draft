River Road and Other Stories runs tonight and Wednesday to Saturday at 8 p.m. at the Canadian Stage Theatre, 26 Berkeley St. Tickets are $20 and can be ordered at (416) 368-3110.

The two most haunting songs in Sylvia Tysons one-person show are Womans World and Driftwood. More precisely, they are the two that I thought while I was hearing them would be the likeliest to make me require the services of Ghostbusters. Not coincidentally, they are the two songs that open the evening's first and second halves. My reactions had not yet been contaminated by a succession of other songs that sounded very much the same. Torpor, in other words, had yet to set in.

I came fresh to all this material, and that should make me either the best or worst audience in the world. Probably, it made me the worst. River Road and Other Stories is a quasi-autobiographical entertainment that depends heavily on the spectators' nostalgic affection for the performer. I am just about aware of Ian and Sylvia, the married folk-singing couple of which Tyson was formerly half, but thats as far as I go. If there is such a thing as active apathy, that is the feeling that folk and country -- the two modes in which the lady specializes -- arouse in me. Both forms can work theatrically, if dramatically juiced up, but here we get them neat. Even when Tysons words sounded like they might be interesting, the monotony of the music generally destroyed them for me before the song was over.

She herself comes before us as a pleasant, sensible, middle-aged lady with a nice singing voice. Her presentation, though, is stiff, stranded awkwardly between formality and its opposite. She sometimes picks up a guitar or one of two accordions, but most of her accompaniment is piped in, and that immediately has a deadening effect; visible interaction between singer and musicians can sometimes be a way of giving a club act the semblance of theatre. A face-mike snakes round her cheekbones, and since this is neither a pop concert nor a production of Rent, it looks weird: a sinister adornment for a raconteuse, which is what part of the time she sets out to be.

Her reminiscent script is carefully written and as carefully delivered, but her speaking voice obstinately refuses to fly. She sounds as if she's delivering memoirs into the void on some late- night radio arts show; she hasn't yet learned to make the live audience her co-conspirator.

Sometimes, in a gesture towards staging, she wanders over to a kitchen table at the side of the stage, sits down and tells us things her mother told her back in her small-town childhood. Later on, the same location serves as the setting for a routine about the horrors of being madeup for an early-morning television appearance. She sits there, suffering and grimacing, while we hear her own prerecorded voice playing the role of the makeup artist, cheerily patronizing her. This routine comes out of nowhere -- it corresponds to nothing else in the show -- and it is not especially funny. And her remark that TV is really just about commercials is not the revelation she seems to think it is.

A pattern begins to emerge. She will tell us about some old friend or acquaintance; about Maggie, say, who first taught her about sex -- not that Maggie knew all that much about it personally but, being Catholic, she had access to all those racy confessions. (This bit is quite funny.) She will then tell us that she wrote a song for or about the person in question, and will sing it.

That leaves the songs themselves, which, as Ive said, I can leave, though I appreciate that many people will take them. I noticed one recurring device; the repetition at the end of a song of a line from an earlier stanza, in lieu of a punchline. Theres one song about feeling awkward writing a song -- it's over too soon or it goes on too long" -- which sounds curiously (though I suspect unintentionally) like Monty Python. There's a persuasive monologue on the thesis that "love makes you stupid," followed by a corroborative song that I liked very much. I wish there had been more like it. There was no program available on the first night, so I can't tell you its title, but it confirmed that Tyson is observant, and knows how and when to rhyme.

As in the concurrent Stan Rogers show, the down-home title song is used as a teaser at the beginning and performed in full at the end. In her curtain speech, the performer thanked us for turning out to support Canadian music. A worthy cause, doubtless, but I'm not sure that customers who have paid (not me, of course) to be entertained should be addressed as if they were subscribers to a charity.
