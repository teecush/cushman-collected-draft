GIGI

Avon Theatre, Stratford, Ont.

- - -

STRATFORD, Ont. - There is a line in Gigi, both the movie and the stage show, that always sticks in my throat. "Paris," it says, "is a suburb of Maxim's." It's meant as a joke, of course, but to an uncomfortable degree it's where the show is coming from. Alan Jay Lerner, who wrote the script of both versions, had years before penned the screenplay of An American in Paris, and in its viewpoint Gigi is the ultimate American-in-Paris musical. It takes a populous and complicated city and reduces it to a set of wealthy, well- dressed people with nothing on their minds except their palates and - - overwhelmingly -- their own and other people's love affairs. All they have to worry about, apparently, are where their next truffles and Veuve Clicquot are coming from and where their last lovers are going.

The expounder of this vision, the musical's narrator and raisonneur, is Honore Lachaille, belle epoque boulevardier extraordinaire; or, to put it in the most convenient shorthand, the Maurice Chevalier role. Honore is the uncle of Gaston Lachaille, a playboy less comfortable with the routine, who finds solace in the humbler home of a lady known as Mamita, and her grand-daughter Gigi. Gigi's precise age is left coyly unspecified, but she's a schoolgirl. She's rapidly maturing, though; and Gaston, as all the world knows, comes to realize that she was trembling on the brink while he was out yonder somewhere blinking at a star. He falls in love.

The film, based on the novella by Colette, came out in 1958. It was Lerner's and Frederick Loewe's first work since their stage triumph with My Fair Lady, and it was another instant classic. It's like My Fair Lady without the Doolittles; it shuts out the workaday world. A stage version, with a few extensions to the script and score, reached Broadway in 1973, looking exactly like what it was -- an attempt to cash in on an already famous property. It was, as things have turned out, the first in a long and depressing line of such things, but people were choosier then and it flopped.

The show does try to distance itself from Honore's viewpoint more than the film did. Gigi's great-aunt, a hugely successful courtesan who elects to tutor the girl in such crucial matters as the distinctions between diamonds, is a dragon more monstrous here than she was in the movie; Honore himself admits, or complains, that his world is changing. But its existence in the first place is probably a figment of his author-lyricist's imagination. Lerner was married eight times (is this the definition of a romantic?) while Honore avoids wedlock like the plague. But the author admitted he identified with the old roue, or with roues in general, and that's where the show's heart is. Some of the suspense hinges on the question of whether Gigi will become Gaston's mistress or his wife. Audiences now might question whether the latter would be necessarily the happier fate.

You have, in short, to put some of your feelings on hold to enjoy Gigi. The film made it easy: It had sumptuous settings, an archetypal cast, a tight screenplay, one of the best scores ever written for a movie musical, and Paris itself. The show forfeits some of these advantages and dilutes others. The authors did not reinvent their original creation; they merely made it longer. Of the five new songs, only one (The Earth and Other Minor Things) is very interesting, and the additions to the book are padding. The second act seems to go on long after the story is essentially over.

Since the film itself has yet to disintegrate, Gigi is in the strictest sense a useless show. But comparisons seem to matter less the further away you get from the Main Stem. Broadway and Hollywood were in direct competition in a way Stratford and Hollywood are not, though to admit as much may be to relegate the festival to the level of superior summer stock.

Richard Monette's production is hardly at pains to disguise its origins -- it begins by projecting credits on to a pretty water- colour front cloth -- but it's livelier than either of the others I have seen (both British). Cameron Porteous' revolving sets make up an enjoyable pastiche of Parisian paintings, from Dufy to Lautrec, and the couture is the height of haute.

There is even a scene with real emotional resonance. Everyone's favourite number in the film is I Remember It Well, the duet of the two old lovers Honore and Mamita. So it is here; the temperature changes in the theatre from the moment they meet, and when the music starts you can feel the audience silently singing along. But we get more than a reprise. Domini Blythe has her own way with Mamita, not gently contradicting her amnesiac partner as Hermione Gingold did but complementing him, rapt in her own reminiscence. Hers is a lovely, delicate performance altogether, and her concern for her grand-daughter's fate is the most touching thing in the evening.

James Blendick's dapper Honore, by contrast, hews close to the Chevalier model, though in a voice more crusted with port than champagne. His singing sounds much stronger, full of new highs and lows, than it did last year in My Fair Lady. (It may help that Honore Lachaille's songs are more laid back than Alfred Doolitle's.) A minor puzzle in the production is why these two players and most of their colleagues sport French accents, while a couple do not. Patricia Collins' Aunt Alicia is imperiously English, assaulting all of her lines like some Lady Bracknell of the demi-monde, and reaping a reward on the few occasions when they're actually witty.

As Gaston, Dan Chameroy's point of origin is uncertain, and his performance also wavers. He deals resoundingly with the title number -- the one in which he admits, more or less, that he has grown accustomed to Gigi's face -- but he misses the sardonic snap of She Is Not Thinking of Me, a song I much prefer. Nor does he look quite right for the most eligible bachelor in Paris; there is a sulkiness to his lip and an aggressiveness to his chin that suggests the other, rather less sensitive Gaston whom he played in Beauty and the Beast. (Can an actor make a career out of playing characters with the same name in stage versions of movie musicals?)

As Gigi herself, Jennifer Gould acts and sings most refreshingly, but her final transformation works no magic; she is more attractive as a hoyden than as a heroine. Gaston's previous flame is played as such a screaming ninny as to cast serious doubts on our hero's taste, and there is too much anxious scampering about, with Donna Feore's choreography confining itself to regulation twirls and can- can lines. Nor is the production exactly chary of ethnic stereotypes, but then neither is the material; a guy can't even install a telephone without interpolating a "vive la France." "There must," proclaims Gigi, when failing to understand the lovestruck Parisians, "be more to life than this," but the show never even hints as to what that might be. Paris is Paris Again: a suburb fit for tourists.
