THEATRE REVIEW

Mary Stuart

Tom Patterson Theatre, Stratford

Stratford's opening week reaches its peak with Mary Stuart, a play that, in the special dramatic world of counterfactuals, reigns supreme. Friedrich Schillers drama, first staged in 1800, has as its centre piece an encounter between Elizabeth I, Queen of England, and her cousin and captive commonly known as Mary Queen of Scots. This never happened. Mary, having fled her native Scotland, was indeed imprisoned for years under Elizabeths orders, and was eventually convicted of treason - at a trial she was not allowed to attend, on the extorted evidence of witnesses whom she wasn't permitted to confront.

She, a Catholic, was certainly the figurehead and inspiration for a succession of mostly young conspirators who dreamed of putting her on the throne (to which she had a certain dynastic claim) and restoring the old religion. Whether she connived at these plots is still a moot point; Schiller has her protest her innocence under seal of the confessional, so we're obviously meant to believe her. The play begins with her trial already over, but her sentence unpronounced. She dreams of meeting Elizabeth in person, knowing that the ties of blood and the spirit of queenly co-existence would have to result in her being pardoned. Elizabeths hard-headed counsellors know this too, and are determined to stop it happening. Others, with their own agendas, flatter her that it will be a golden opportunity of personally humiliating her rival. So, in the grounds of Fotheringay Castle, where Mary is imprisoned and near to where Elizabeth just happens to be hunting, the two come face to face.

Or, it sometimes seems, knee to knee. In Antoni Cimolinos hypnotically tense production, the scene is marked out by who gets closer to the ground. Lucy Peacock's Mary, painfully swallowing her immense pride, kneels and begs forgiveness. Seana McKenna's Elizabeth bends her own body downwards, but not in sympathy; rather in a spirit of mocking incredulity. She lays on the sarcasm until Mary can take it no longer, draws herself back up to her full height, and exultantly proclaims Elizabeth a usurping bastard (literally). Elizabeth is the one humiliated; Mary has metaphorically signed her own death warrant. Neither has got what she wanted. Its a superb scene.

Its a peculiar quality of the play that it makes something that didn't happen a stepping stone to something that famously did. Schiller, who was not innocent of sentimentalism, also wrote a Joan of Arc play in which the Maid dies on the battlefield rather than at the stake, but his Mary does indeed go to the block. Actually sending her there remains a political and psychological problem for Elizabeth, which she resolves by signing a non-metaphorical warrant and handing it to a hapless secretary with no specific instructions on what he's to do with it. The play is, among other things, an overlapping set of highly suspenseful cat-and-mouse games: a Schiller thriller.

Its also, of course, an unsurpassed vehicle for two star actresses. The governessy tones that sometimes afflict Peacock in her classical roles (though never in her modern ones) hinder her here in her opening scenes, but she blazes in the central duet, and is spare and moving in her concluding journey to the scaffold, where her self-abnegation becomes real. In its final sanctification of Mary, receiving the last rites from an old friend whos taken holy orders expressly for the purpose, the play cheats a bit; after triumphantly flouting historical facts, it starts relying on them; it dredges up Mary's past sins, involving people we haven't met, purely for the purpose of absolving her of them. But it does give the actress the opportunity to play hard-won serenity, an opportunity that Peacock beautifully takes. McKenna, from her first sweeping entrance (eo Sharps costumes are gorgeous and Elizabeth naturally has the best of them) is both superbly regal and rackingly insecure. Her wit coruscates; it is, on the surface, always at someone elses expense, but underneath it's at her own.

There is, or so it seems in this production, a third major character almost as important as the two queens; this is the earl of Leicester presented, not too historically, as Mary's admirer as well as Elizabeths and emerging, in Geraint Wyn Davies performance, as a master of real politik and of playing one side against another, almost - but not quite - as ruthlessly successful as the Tudor monarch herself; there's something shabby about his manoeuvres as there isn't about hers. (That, by the way, is a compliment to both performances.)

Schiller was a great political playwright, and his mastery of argument is electrically embodied in Ben Carlsons Burleigh, implacably hounding Mary to her death without, as far as we can tell, harbouring ashred of personal ill-feeling towards her. His counterpart is Brian Dennehys old earl of Shrewsbury, counselling compassion and exemplifying it, even towards his opponents; it's this actor's finest Stratford performance.

Then there are James Blendick as Marys immovably honourable jailer, and Ian Lake as his nephew Mortimer, a fictitious prototype of all the men who went to their deaths to save Mary, equally enflamed by religion and by her, and likely unable to distinguish between the two. When Soulpepper produced this play, I wrote that Mortimer might be a suicide bomber in the making. Six years on, the resemblance seems closer and scarier, especially given that the character initially seems to be, and seems to himself to be, the romantic lead.

Cimolinos production encloses its period sets and costumes within a modern fence of barbed wire; but even without this, and given Peter Oswald's equally barbed translation, the resonances are overpowering: what with Mary languishing unheard in her own private Guantanamo, and Elizabeth throwing her hapless bureaucrat under the Tudor equivalent of a bus. He's given a delightfully, even poignantly, bewildered performance by Dylan Trowbridge, whom I should have included on my roll of honour for Measure for Measure; Patricia Collins, too, comes up strongly in her quieter moments as Marys devoted nurse.

Like Measure, also at the Tom Patterson, this production offers the special luxurious thrill of a first-rate company operating at close quarters, with one another and with us. Its also the re-discovery of the world's great dramatists, one of whom we still know almost nothing. It's Stratford at the top of its bent.
