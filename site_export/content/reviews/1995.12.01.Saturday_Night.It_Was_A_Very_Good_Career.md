Arthur Miller turned eighty this year. So did Saul Bellow. And so, on December 12, does Frank Sinatra. Guess which one is getting the publicity.

That is not a complaint. Miller has meant something in my life, Bellow not much, but Sinatra is the pre-eminent singer of the songs I love and its difficult for mere prosewriters to compete with that. For a start, he may be the only singer ever to have sung Cole Porter's Night and Day (on six different commercial recordings, and change). Everybody else sings "Night an' Day." Sinatras attention to final consonants is extraordinary, and no other singer, popular or legit, can ever have bestowed as much weight on the word and as he does, making you hungry to learn just what the conjunction will be conjoining. It happens to be the very first word of the song that became his personal anthem, "My Way," and he sings that opening so quietly and powerfully that its almost possible to forgive him for the rest of it. He sings the same word, as powerfully but less quietly, as he surges into the last stanza of his earlier and far superior testament It Was a Very Good Year: "and I think of my life as fine old wine from vintage kegs." He savours the wine, and the vintage, and the kegs, each word given its due as a separate sound but with no break in the thought. He does the same at a happier tempo (actually it's ruefully happy) when he encounters the phrase that ancient adage in a less-known song, "The Moon Got in My Eyes"; it is not often after all that a pop singer gets to roll his tongue around the word adage.

This ability to take a sung text apart and put it together again before our very ears gives his singing a majestic, sculpted quality. I imagine that it's what the late Alec Wilder (composer, critic, and musical conscience to a generation or two of listeners and performers including Sinatra himself) had in mind when he said, "Frank Sinatras singing mesmerizes you. In fact, it gets so symbolic sometimes that you cant make the relationship with him as a man, even though you may know him." There is a paradox here. Sinatra sings colloquially, in a personal voice that has gone from sweet to warm to metallic to haggard and has always been inimitable (even by Sammy Davis), but he also and at the same time sings formally. His singing is loaded with personality, and it carries the added freight of his audience's strong and possibly ambivalent feelings about him as a man, but it alsotranscends personality. Which I guess makes it art.

It makes sense to write of Sinatra's singing in the present tense. His records, the earliest of which go back fifty-five years, have been continuously available, and are now more so than ever with CD box sets making giant demands on our listening and storage capacities. Nor do we know that he has actually retired. He last sang (very well, apparently) at a charity concert in February, and there seems to be a fifty-per-cent chance that he will do so again. The three birthday books on hand are cautiously valedictory, but their main note is celebration-biographical, critical, or a mixture of the two.

The biography is his daughter Nancys Frank Sinatra, An American Legend (General), though one might more accurately call it a chronicle or, better still, a calendar. Year by year, sometimes even day by day, Nancy with the Laughing Feet--thats what we called her in the sixties when she had that hit song about brutes in boots-guides us, very proficiently, through Dad's personal and professional schedule: from record date to movie set to nightclub stint to board meeting to charitable extravaganza to political fund-raiser. It's exhausting just to read about, even given the use of his private jet. The personal history is as affectionate, and generally as protective, as you would expect. (Sometimes the sweetness and light spill over into unexpected places; the dictatorial Louis B. Mayer is described, just after he has fired young Frankie from MGM, as this lovely man. This must be a first.)

One thing Nancy doesnt discuss is "Nancy," the song that the comedian Phil Silvers in his only excursion into lyric-writing dreamed up in 1944 with the composer Jimmy Van Heusen, probably Sinatras longest-serving friend. The omission is surprising for professional as well as personal reasons. Singing so tenderly about his four-year-old daughter did the singer's image a lot of good, just as leaving his children's mother for Ava Gardner five years later did it an incalculable amount of harm. (This was the 1940s.) Nice song that it is, and remarkably un-sticky in the circumstances, it has remained an essential part of the Sinatra armoury; in the 1960s BBC listeners, resisting the attempts of a smart DJ to fob them off with something swinging, chose it as Sinatras greatest record. This it certainly isnt, but the English are notoriously sentimental, and they make very faithful fans. Those who voted had probably been bobbysoxers in 1946.

The fans, wrote E.J. Kahn, Jr., in that year, have always considered it one of Sinatra's lovable assets that he married his childhood sweetheart, Nancy Barbato." This was the era when teenage girls (and a few boys) were piling into New York's Paramount Theatre and staying there, clutching their lunch boxes, for six separate Sinatra performances a day; a considerable display of stamina on both sides. Kahn was reporting on the phenomenon for The New Yorker, and he wrote in that magazines best vein of urbane bewilderment: "His fans seemingly will do almost anything he tells them to, and it is fortunate for the rest of the population that he does not have a hankering for, say, arson. Their obedience falters only when he asks them to keep quiet.... The article, a real delight, is included in The Frank Sinatra Reader (Oxford University Press), an anthology of essays, reviews, and news stories from then till now, assembled by two Sinatraphile academics, Steven Petkov and Leonard Mustazza. Though they claim that their focus is the music, many of the pieces they reprint are given over, inevitably, to speculation about the man. The late Ralph Gleason, jazz turned rock critic, writing in 1974, still digs the singer but considers his appeal and even his musicianship to be compromised by that rug on his head... the body-guards...court of sycophants...his attitudes." (Gleason, you may note, can strike an attitude or two himself.) In 1989 Jonathan Schwartz, disc jockey and occasional Sinatra confidant, offers in the languid tones of Tom Wolfe journalism a few basic reasons for strange behaviour: "This fellow Sinatra has been applauded around-the-clock since he was twenty-five years old.... Sinatra, since, lets say, 1942, has been shielded from the way things are. Gene Lees, critic and lyricist, experiencing both admiration and misgivings, asks himself in 1967, What would I be like, what sort of things would I do, if I had that much power?" The journalist Pete Hamill, summoned abruptly to Sinatra's table and limo in 1974, looks at him and finds him "desperately lonely. In the same year Martha Weinman Lear, once one of those sandwich-toting' soxers, declares herself still smitten but admits that it has got Tacky.... A lifelong Democrat, he got chummy with Reagan and then, good grief, with Agnew." (She left out Nixon.)

Now, thats what hurts. Personally, I dont care how many gangsters he's been photographed with, or even how many journalists he insults. His cruelties and crudities are more than balanced by his fabled generosity towards friends, acquaintances, and total strangers. The tedious truth seems to be that he is capable, like most of us, of both good and bad behaviour but that his position gives him a lot more scope both ways. He once embarked on a worldwide concert tour for children's charities saying, convincingly, that an "overprivileged adult ought to help underprivileged children, and, even excluding the music, he has done more good in the world than harm. However: politics that may sit well on Charlton Heston look bizarre on Frank Sinatra. The hip cat should not have joined the fat cats. I cant speak for the generation of the 1940s, but I discovered him in the late fifties and having been bowled over by his singing--first by the insouciance of it, then by the emotion of it, and always by the sheer intelligence of it--I was additionally pleased to discover that he had been an outspoken fighter for racial equality long before Hollywood liberalism became fashionable. Later he tried to hire a blacklisted writer to script a movie, and found his road blocked in a John Wayne town. (One time when he failed to do it his way.) Then the switch came, and we had to comfort ourselves by likening his new allegiances to the amorphous soft-rock songs that invaded his repertoire from the mid-sixties on; they didnt really suit him and underneath he knew it.

The Frank Sinatra Reader contains nothing written by Sinatra himself, but there is one interview quotation that, for very good reason, keeps recurring: Whatever else has been said about me personally is unimportant. When I sing, I believe, I'm honest." This honesty is not a matter of simple autobiography. Sinatra's listeners respond to the sound of a complicated sensibility revealed through song, and very likely surprising itself in the process. His greatest quality is that he takes nothing for granted. In the 1940s--his real pop-star period--he was a fervent, boyish singer with a black-and-white take on romance, but in 1947 he recorded "It Never Entered My Mind, a regretful masterpiece by Rodgers and Hart, and, without becoming maudlin, made it a gentle, searching display of a quality for which the word vulnerability" seems inadequate. Twenty years later, the acknowledged king of his world, he sang "Dindi, a mysterious longing song by Antonio Carlos Jobim, and the humility he conveys in the face of beauty--the beauty of the song and of the person sung to--is unlike anything he had ever done before. I would bet its unlike anything he even thought he could do before; you can hear the wonder in his voice. Will Friedwald, who calls it miraculous," rightly says that "you almost have to listen twice to be sure that its Sinatra. And yet it couldn't be anybody else.

Friedwald's Sinatra! The Song Is You (Scribner) is entirely and intelligently concerned with the music (including the vital contributions of instrumentalists and arrangers) and is therefore the best Sinatra book yet written. His previous book was Jazz Singing, a capacious study that took in a lot of pop as well. He is even less tolerant of rock and roll than I am, but then at his young age (he was born in 1961) he can afford to be. He is bracingly withering about the two sets of Duets that took Sinatra to the top of the CD charts by pairing him with current pop favourites in rehashes of his most familiar tunes.

Friedwald takes us breezily and in detail through every phase of Sinatras career: from his early days as a band-singer to a septuagenarian decade marked by a pitifully small number of records and a punishing concert schedule in which he has still, incredibly, delivered. The book is a long read, because apart from having 500 pages of its own it keeps sending you back to the music. Just to maintain my self-respect I have to point out a couple of omissions. His discussion of the album A Swingin Affair--the 1956 sequel to the legendary Songs for Swingin' Lovers and even more enjoyable--omits I Wish I Were in Love Again," the paragon of anti-love songs dispatched by Sinatra with elegant understated wit over-laying a ferocious sense of loss, and "I Guess I'll Have to Change My Plan, the singer as urbane loser. These are key tracks showing Sinatra at his coolest and wittiest, a master of understated irony.

Sinatra once described himself as an eighteen-karat manic depressive" (another omnipresent quotation in the Reader) with the implication that this is directly reflected in his work. Of his top collaborators the brassy, exuberant Billy May (Come Fly with Me) reflects his manic side, and the stringy, melancholic Gordon Jenkins (No One Cares) the depressive. But the most satisfying was the great Nelson Riddle; he and Sinatra could take each mood to its extreme, while allowing one to admit the possibility of the other. Swingin Lovers, only medium fast and not terribly loud, is stoic as well as intoxicatingly happy; Only the Lonely, the definitive collection of saloon songs, uses wry humour as a hedge against despair. The latter album occasions Friedwalds best single insight: he points out that Bill Miller's piano accompaniment on "One for My Baby (And One More for the Road) acts out the role of the unconcerned bystander.

It's this complexity thats missing from My Way," not only a rotten song musically and lyrically but a negation of everything thats best about Sinatra; it protests too much. (Much, much too much.) The best criticism of it was made by Sinatra himself, when he first heard it; he said it was "too on the nose. The real Sinatra works by indirection. He swings, without hitting you over the head with rhythm; without sobbing, he moves you. When he was fifty he recorded September of My Years, a wistful set of songs about getting old; when he actually got old he stopped singing them, thereby sparing all our blushes. He now sings a straight romantic repertoire; and interestingly, though some people have said that he's too old to be singing at all, none have questioned his right to sing love songs per se. He knows how to position them. He is not a nostalgia act.

In 1981, having just attained to senior citizenhood, Sinatra released one of his very best albums, She Shot Me Down. It took its title from Bang Bang," a former Sonny and Cher (and Nancy Sinatra) hit, on which he bestowed a startling stark dignity. Like his masterpieces of the fifties, it has its own identity: not just a collection of torch songs but a set in which each item recalls a recently ended affair, depicted in detail. It includes "Thanks for the Memory, with a breathtaking new lyric by the original author, Leo Robin; and a new song by Gordon Jenkins, I Loved Her." This is chiefly a clipped account of incompatible tastes ("She was Boston, I was Vegas), delivered with a fine, mellow weariness, but its middle section, which contains the line, I wish that we had been a little more the same," comes out with an extraordinary clenched anger, the syllables of the word "little" stretched right across the beat, in vocal italics. By the main theme he is back again to aching compassion. There are a few other singers with a comparable feeling for words, some whose musical inventiveness is greater or at least more obviously displayed, but none in whom words, music, thought, and emotion come together with such magisterial force. It makes him, even in the December of his years, impregnable and incomparable.
