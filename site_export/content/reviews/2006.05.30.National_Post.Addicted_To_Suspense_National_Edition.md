Last week saw the end of a months-old television tradition, by which Fox TV kept our nerves pleasurably jangled for two solid hours. At 8 p.m., they gave us the first season of Prison Break, the story of Michael Scofield, design genius from Chicago, who gets himself consigned to the citys Fox River penitentiary so that he can spring his brother, consigned to Death Row for a murder he didn't commit. Michael framed himself, so to speak; his brother Lincoln was framed by crooked politicians, a phrase that here, as in most dramatic latitudes, is a tautology.

This was followed at 9 p.m. by the longer-established 24, which has just completed its fifth season of the adventures of Jack Bauer, the wild-card super-agent of Los Angeles' Counter Terrorism Unit (CTU). The common device here is that the action of each series is supposed to unfold over a single day and in real time, one hour per episode. Over the years, Jack has saved the city and the country from bombs, viruses, nerve-gas, presidential assassinations and -- this last seasons ultimate weapon -- presidential treason. Both shows run on the same fuel: the plan that, once per episode, goes catastrophically wrong, only to be rescued and then threatened anew the following week. Both have wildly implausible storylines but extremely persuasive underlying assumptions. Both feature affectless superheroes with an occasional glint of fallible humanity.

It seems like a dream now, but the very first time we saw Jack Bauer he was peacefully teaching chess to his teenage daughter. It may be the last peaceful moment hes had, certainly the last involving any member of his family. Before that first hour, or episode, was out, his daughter was well on her way to being kidnapped by terrorists. A short time later, his wife was in the same predicament, and by the end of the day, or the season, Jack, a broken man, was cradling his wife's dead body in his arms, mumbling I'm sorry.

His anguish was understandable. So was his shock, though it was hardly greater than ours; were hardened now, but five years ago popular TV series didnt kill off sympathetic major characters. That he should feel guilty was also understandable, though it wasn't quite rational. It's true that his wife was killed by his traitorous ex-mistress but the treachery, though obviously not the lechery, was something he had no way of knowing about. (Neither did anybody else on the show, probably including those who write it.) Its also true that Jacks family had only been in jeopardy in the first place because his enemies wanted payback for his activities as a super- counter-terrorist; but he'd rescued them and placed them in what he had every reason to believe was a safe place.

The surprising thing is that Jack's authors, too, seem to hold his familys fate against him. Or at least they do occasionally, when it suits them. The producers have been quoted as saying that the final season of 24 will have to end with Jacks death "because he's a tragic hero."

John Doyle, The Globe and Mail's TV critic, has coupled Jack with Tony Soprano as men who are shown to be rotting within, even if some unthinking viewers are dazzled by their machismo. Hes right about The Sopranos, a morally aware show that never takes its eye off the object. But 24 doesnt have that kind of texture or that kind of seriousness; it's all about suspense, at which it's brilliant, perhaps unprecedentedly so. Tragedy needs a protagonist with some talent for introspection; serious drama needs writers with some capacity for analysis. Jack and his creators dont have time for either.

The moral crux is supposed to be Jacks readiness to inflict torture; we are seduced into applauding behaviour from him that we would condemn in the real world, and then we feel ashamed of ourselves and start asking questions. But in practice the show lets both us and its hero off the hook. Jack mistreats only people whom the viewers know in advance to be thoroughly bad guys with specific information about imminent outrages. This is in fact the ticking bomb scenario that U.S. authorities invoke whenever they want to justify torture (or whatever they, at that moment, feel like calling it) but of which they have yet to adduce a single concrete example. Life, it seems, is not as neat as 24.

Only once does Jack seem to have suffered pangs of conscience while on the job. It was in season three. On the orders of the president, who was himself yielding to the whim of some blackmailing fanatic, Jack put a bullet through the neck of CTU's then-current boss, murmuring "God forgive me" as he did so. It looked like a real character-crunching moment. It has not, however, been alluded to since.

One corollary of the real-time convention is that it doesn't permit flashbacks. It doesnt seem to allow for much in the way of mental retrospection either. Once youre dead on 24, you are, with rare exceptions, dead; and unmourned to boot. The amount of collateral damage Jack's exploits leave in their wake has almost become a running gag. There should be a permanent memo in all CTU agents' e-mail: "Never go on a mission with Jack Bauer. He will survive. You wont." Civilians, too, have bitten the dust only to be instantly forgotten, this season in record numbers.

The show derives much of its force from connecting with threats that are all too real, and it probably performs a therapeutic service by exhibiting and temporarily defusing them. It never mentions 9/11, but it obviously exists in its shadow; this season, it even risked what can best be described as a mock hijack. But Jack himself strides through it all, leading 95% of an unexamined life. Hes an action-hero, combining agility and cunning (no wonder he's good at chess) in a tradition that stretches all the way back to Gilgamesh via Odysseus. And like his predecessors, he has a weapon with quasi-magical powers that only he can wield. In his case, it's a cell-phone that can download or transmit anything with stupefying efficiency.

24 has obviously been a great influence on Prison Break, which went on hiatus with the escape effected but everything else still to play for. The next season should perhaps be re-titled Prison Broken, though that sounds like something you do to incarcerated pets. There was a new last-minute wrinkle; an old con revealed the whereabouts of a $5-million stash on the outside. Judging from the looks on the faces of the guys who overheard him, the next season could turn into Its a Mad, Mad, Mad, Mad World minus the slapstick. For its part, 24 looks like its turning into The Manchurian Candidate. Last we saw, Jack was on a slow boat to China, in the hands of sinister captors who have their own uses for him.

Im addicted to both shows, but I find that my withdrawal symptoms are worse if I miss an episode of 24. I'm desperate to know what happened but I know that if I catch up later, having already been given a summary of events, there will be very little there for me. The pretense that all these events are happening in one day is no longer believable; though it was in the first series, the only one in which you actually felt night giving way to day and day to night.

Prison Break isn't as viscerally exciting, but it has a richer spread of characters, what with Michael having involved half the prison population in his plans. And with character comes humour, which may actually be detrimental to suspense; it allows all concerned to relax.

Politically, both shows could be said to be ahead of their time. 24 has featured a black president, who was a saint, and Prison Break has had a female vice-president, who is anything but. For a time, 24 seemed to be grooming its own villainous Veep, and its perfectly possible that the writers, who notoriously make it up as they go along, abandoned this idea only when they realized that Prison Break had beaten them to it. So they broke another taboo, and put their own president Logan through a series of U-turns. First he was a weasel, then a villain, and finally -- with virtuoso efforts from authors and actor -- he was a just-about-credible villainous weasel, Nixon with a touch of Bush. Seeing which, the Prison Break team struck back and -- in a dazzling display of fancy narrative footwork -- had their own Commander-in-Chief assassinated (another network convention gone bust) and speeded his deputy into the Oval Office. Where she now sits, all ready to wreak havoc in Season Two, as corrupt and right-wing and vindictive as they come.

These are odd politics for conservative Fox, but then this is the network that brought us The Simpsons, which in its prime was the most subversive show on television. It knows the value of being in touch with the times. And both Prison Break and 24 are very much of their times in their preoccupation with corporate conspiracies.

President Logan was obsessed with oil, and his strings were being pulled by a still-unmasked bunch of smoothies who were probably rolling in it. Prison Breaks VP was originally in cahoots with something called The Company, which employed her to put paid to environmental bills. Obviously, energy conservation is not a priority for the evil masterminds of either show, but I think they might conserve some of their own by simply joining forces across the commercial break. Their aims and methods seem remarkably similar.

Actually, there's something at work here more fundamental than the zeitgeist. There's the nature of drama. It is, as all the textbooks say, about conflict; and theres no excitement, moral or physical, in being on the side of the overdog. Drama is, by definition, against authority, whether thats government or something beyond. So Prison Break takes its characters from the most marginalized section of society; while CTU, though an official organization, is a maverick one, in constant danger of being wiped out.

24's long-term prospects seem infinite; there's always another day. Prison Break is unlikely to achieve comparable longevity, due to its confining premise, not to mention its confined premises. Even if they contrive a means of throwing the whole cast back in the slammer, nobody is entitled to play more than one Get Out of Jail card. On a personal-relations level, I think Michael, who after all is not a career warrior, may have a faint shot at happiness, even though his lady of choice rounded out the season by taking a drug overdose that may or may not be fatal.

Jack and Audrey were granted so many tender reunions that one knew for certain their relationship was doomed. Jack, even if he has to spend the whole of next season in China, will just go on as one of the walking wounded. But he will walk tall, or as tall as Kiefer Sutherland can, and his wounds will only bleed on very special occasions.
