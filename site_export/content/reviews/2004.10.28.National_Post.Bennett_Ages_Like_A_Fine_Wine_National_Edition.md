THE ART OF ROMANCE

Tony Bennett (Columbia)

- - -

I didnt go to Tony Bennett's recent concert at Roy Thomson Hall because my previous visits had suggested that he wasn't varying his repertoire much, a feeling that reviews of his current tour had done nothing to dispel. On record, though, he continues to explore new songs -- new to him, anyway -- and even at 78, to refine his singing style. He is one singer whom age becomes; the husk in his voice is still some way short of becoming a croak and he sounds, at his best, generously thoughtful.

His new CD, The Art of Romance, plays to his strengths as a balladeer, with one excursion -- The Best Man, a Nat King Cole hit from the 40s -- into the ruefully comic. Bennett has probably explored the American songbook more thoroughly than any big-name vocalist short of Ella Fitzgerald, though unlike Ella he has mainly steered clear of composer collections. He hasnt yet reached the all- passion-spent philosopher stage, and chances are he never will; he still sounds emotionally in the thick of things.

Sometimes too much so; Harold Arlen's Don't Like Goodbyes, one of his more recondite selections, is a theatre song that exists in two variants, to be sung either by a mature character or a young one; Bennett opts for the young version, and confuses the matter still more with a reckless choice of pronouns. The best are two old warhorses that he refreshes simply by taking them gently and intently through their paces: I Remember You and the finest Gone with the Wind I have ever heard.

Over-dramatization remains his besetting sin; he kills the ambiguities of Stephen Sondheim's Being Alive by talking, sometimes shouting, its key lines. He also goes in far too often for self- deprecating chuckles. Johnny Mandel, last of the classic arrangers, supplies rich charts and is composer of three of the tunes: Little Did I Dream, which has a sub-par Dave Frishberg lyric; Where Do You Start, a short-winded song about divorce that Bennett almost transforms; and Close Enough for Love, which is quietly, perfectly done.
