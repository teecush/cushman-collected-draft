THEATRE REVIEW

Alice In Wonderland Uncle Vanya Mrs Warrens Profession Shaw Festival

Our first sight of the Shaw Festival's Alice in Wonderland raises expectations and raises them high.

A drop curtain depicts a Victorian proscenium theatre, enchantingly drawn in playingcard colours, with our heroine's face, as in an old photograph, surveying the scene through a peephole on top of the arch. When the curtain itself rises, we behold an equally magical riverside scene, complete with a rowboat on which Lewis Carroll will escort the three Liddell sisters - of which, Alice is the youngest - while he tells them the tale of Wonderland.

When that story begins to take shape, the pictures become more complex before our eyes: a mixture of live action and projection. Through the magic of video, Alice herself falls dizzyingly down the rabbit hole, though she does give the odd impression of falling upwards. Her constant changes of size are wittily depicted while, in more sombre vein, theres a beautiful seashore scene for Alices encounter with the Mock Turtle.

There are a few eyesores, mainly in the costumes, but the production, directed by Peter Hinton and designed by Eo Sharpe, is for the most part visually amazing.

In all other respects, it's a mess.

Hinton's adaptation takes the story to be a meditation on Victorian society and on growing up; which it may well be, but we dont have to be hit over the head with it. The satire here is very heavy-handed, especially when we get to the Queen of Hearts and her trial scene. Theres a lot of new dialogue, and not only is it less fun than the original, it's also less trenchant. Whatever happened to sentence first, verdict afterwards? The rewrites and additions extend to the songs; their words, to Allen Cole's music, are often difficult to make out but enough comes through to suggest that Hintons lyrics, sloppy in rhyme and meter, would have had Carroll, a master of light verse, reeling and writhing and fainting in coils.

The overall tone is literal rather than logical: precisely the reverse of the Alice books themselves. It is also unforgivably unfunny. Much of the original text remains, but few of the actors seem to know how to point it. Its a blessed relief when the Cheshire Cat appears, in full grin and in hologram form, with Jennifer Phipps' face clearly recognizable behind the whiskers, and with her voice speaking the words as if she knew what they meant and relished the knowledge. Graeme Somerville is also fine, as a gentle troubled Carroll whose commentary is acceptable because it's outside the story proper, and as a Mock Turtle whos similarly gentle and troubled and who even gets to sing his Beautiful Soup song with the proper mournfulness. His is the best sustained scene in the show containing, in the Lobster Quadrille, the best-looking and best-sounding musical number. He also makes the most of an apocryphal courtroom speech for the Mad Hatter, though the tea-party scene is soporific enough as to turn the audience into so many dormice. Finally Tara Rosling, flying on wires like Peter Pan, makes an intrepid forthright Alice: maybe too forthright, but the books Alice is never slow to lecture the creatures she meets and the play's is even less so.

There are modern classic plays in the Shaw's smaller theatres. Jackie Maxwell has directed a fine production of Chekhovs Uncle Vanya, in an adaptation by the rising American playwright Annie Baker. Its one that retains the original's time and place but whose dialogue is aggressively here and now. Waffles, the family hanger-on, now gets called a moocher, which is OK, though I found Dr Astrov's repeated description of himself, and practically everybody else, as a creep somewhat infelicitous; but then I dont know Russian.

Anyway, this is a close-quarters bare-knuckled production in which the actors respond beautifully to one another while making clear how the characters fail to. Everybody is suffering, and hardly cares who knows it; and the occasional well-intentioned effort to make things better only makes them worse.

Patrick McManus, radiating self-contempt through a droopy moustache, is the angriest Astrov I have ever seen, if not exactly the subtlest. His diatribes about deforestation can never have sounded more urgent.

Neil Barclay in the title role doesnt establish himself as the play's central figure, but titles aren't everything; understated at first, he works himself into the hysterics of his attempt to shoot the Professor, a scene that just about defines tragic-comedy, or rather tragic-farce. His subsequent agonized sullenness is touching and credible. Marla McLean is a Sonya less longsuffering than most; she can rage with the best of them, and its invigorating. She meshes beautifully with Moya OConnell's Yelena, a vibrant portrayal of a woman afraid of vibrancy, terrified by emotions (her own or others'), and desperately pleading to escape from them.

I believed why she, young and beautiful, had married her old professor, smug and irritable as David Schurmann properly plays him. Peter Millard strums along nicely as Waffles, and as everybodys nanny Sharry Flett is concern personified. About the only flaw is the servant who, while moving the sets around, sings what sounds like "ha, ha, ha," but maybe thats an ironic comment on Chekhov's insistence that his plays were comedies.

Eda Holmes has staged Mrs Warren's Profession - the seasons one actual Shaw play - as a rehearsed reading (though none of the actors carry books) in a present-day London club that has yet to admit women to membership.

The idea is to show that misogyny, theoretical and practical, is as alive now as when Shaw wrote his expose of prostitution and its economic causes back in 1899, when the play was banned and had to be presented by a different kind of private club.

I wouldnt have thought any audience could miss the contemporary point; it belongs to what Basil Fawlty called the special subject of the bleeding obvious, especially given a production as vividly acted as this one.

The concept, as sometimes happens, has had beneficent side effects: the constricted setting, with plush sofas doing furniture duty for scenes inside and out, focuses attention, ours and the performers'.

Nicole Underhay plays Mrs. Warren, the working-class girl who became a prostitute because it beat getting paralyzed in a factory and who now runs a string of brothels herself. She sometimes overdoes the coarseness and bravado but she's excellent when vulnerable, pleading for the understanding of her daughter Vivie, who has unknowingly gone through college on the proceeds of the trade.

Vivie, all pertness and practicality in Jennifer Dzialoszynskis performance, applauds her mothers initial decision when she finally learns of it, in a confrontation that seems more than ever one of the strongest scenes Shaw ever wrote. It's when she learns that the business is still going on - Mrs. Warren being too good a businesswoman ever to give it up - that Vivie revolts.

Sir George Crofts, business partner and gentlemanly blackguard, is toweringly played by Thom Marriott. He certainly towers physically over Vivie, to ironic effect since she bests him in every other way. Wade Bogert-O'Brien is ideally feckless as Frank, the younger suitor whom she rejects along with anything else that might reek of romance.

The plays four gentlemen - the others are Gray Powell as an ineffectual artist with an undefined place in the Warren orbit and Shawn Wright as Franks father, a caricature of a clergyman - act as the production's chorus, welcoming us to the club and reading some of the stage directions, including those at the end when Vivie settles down to her destiny in an actuary's chambers.

This is ambiguous. Are we meant to think that, even as she asserts her independence, the male sex is still pulling her strings? Or is the laugh on them? After all, the play was written by a man.

Alice in Wonderland is in repertory through October 16 at the Festival Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake; Uncle Vanya through September 11 at the Court House Theatre; Mrs Warren's Profession through October 16 at the Royal George Theatre.
