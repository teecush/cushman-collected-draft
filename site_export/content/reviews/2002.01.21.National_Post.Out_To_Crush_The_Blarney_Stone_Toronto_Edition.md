THE LONESOME WEST

Bluma Appel Theatre, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - How are things in Connemara?

Martin McDonagh, living in London but of Irish name and descent, is the most naturally gifted playwright to have surfaced in the British Isles since Harold Pinter. He excels at all the basic things -- narrative, dialogue, character -- that a previous generation, in Britain as here, couldn't manage and pretended didnt matter. The result was 30 years of desiccated drama. Once again, the Irish have had to ride to the rescue.

McDonaghs The Lonesome West returns us to the remote Irish village that furnished both a setting and a title for his superb The Beauty Queen of Leenane. For sure the ol' place hasn't changed much. Like its predecessor, this play concerns people who basically live in a kitchen. Once again the burning of a characters hand on the all-important stove (stage right this time, rather than stage left) is a climactic event. Once again the reading aloud of a letter by the person who has just written it provides a showpiece scene and also has a crucial influence on the succeeding action. And once again that action concerns two family members living together in physical and emotional deadlock. It is conceivable that at some level they love one another, but what actually keeps them going is conflict.

Coleman and Valene, the two brothers of The Lonesome West, get rather more fun out of life than the mother and daughter of The Beauty Queen. They go out more, and their antagonism is slightly less deadly. Admittedly most of their excursions seem to be to funerals, and when we meet them they are just back from burying their father, whose head Coleman had playfully shot off in retaliation for a criticism of his personal appearance.

Valene agreed to pass this off as an accident (not that anybody in Leenane is fooled) on condition that Coleman sign over his half of their inheritance. So now Coleman, the wilder of the two, lives on the indulgence of his primmer brother. Meanwhile, Valene spends money on a brand new stove (in bright red, with the letter V imprinted on the front) and on an extensive collection of religious figurines -- acquisitions that Coleman regards with rancour.

But then these two men, now in their forties, have spent their lives destroying one anothers toys; and they are frozen in childhood rivalry, never happier than when rolling around in combat on the floor. Like the two women in The Beauty Queen, they delight in taunting one another with their sexual inexperience; their favourite insult is virgin feckin' gayboy.

The last scene finds them under orders to be nicer to one another -- if possible, to go over all their past grievances and apologize for their misconduct. So they try, but, as surely as happened to Elyot and Amanda in Private Lives, their attempted concord only leads to more, and more violent, discord. Some of the old cruelties they dig up really hurt. Besides, this is how they enjoy themselves.

This scene, like much of the rest of the play in Jackie Maxwell's CanStage production, is both uproariously funny and deeply painful. Colemans idea of home comfort is to pull his shirt out over his pants, revealing a momentary expanse of pot-belly; Randy Hughson takes full advantage of the rolling, childlike brutality, and of the sly, lacerating wit. Valene, less cunning than he wants to believe, is a mixture of violence and gentility. Benedict Campbell, beautifully pitched, gives him a prim walk that accelerates into a waddle.

As in The Beauty Queen, the central couple is supported by two other characters. One, played with poignant vivacity by Tara Rosling, is Girleen, a 17-year-old distributor of her fathers illicit whiskey. She nurses an unrequitable, almost Chekhovian, passion for the local priest, Father Welsh (or would that be Walsh? - - nobody seems quite sure, and neither, by the end of the play, do we), who suffers from what seem, in this region, to be fairly standard vocational agonies.

As Coleman kindly tells him, "You're a bit too weedy and you're a terror for the drink and you have doubts about Catholicism." (Hughson rolls off this reassuring catalogue with superbly off-hand assurance.) If he has lost his faith, the priest still has his conscience, and it racks him; David Storch, with a grey quiff incongruously crowning an otherwise sleek head of hair, makes him both tragic and absurd. He also talks as colourfully as anyone else in Leenane: "You didnt even cry when Holland knocked us out of the fecking World Cup" is his apocalyptic judgment on what he ironically takes to be Girleens heartlessness.

In The Beauty Queen the subordinate pair, though interesting, were kept subordinate. Here they are not quite secondary, not quite equal, and the play suffers from the split focus.

Nor, despite painting an apparently broader canvas, does it say as much about this desperate reach of Ireland as its predecessor managed subtly to suggest.

But if its overall structure is defective, what might be called its local craft -- its subtle banking of narrative detail to be explosively cashed in at a later date -- is often superb.

There is a third Leenane play, The Skull of Connemara, that we are still waiting to see and that may bring the whole trilogy into focus. (Don't hold your breath: The Lonesome West has taken five years to reach us.) McDonagh, riffing on earlier presentations of an idyllic rural Ireland, has created his own counter-world. It may be extreme, but you believe every word of it. The action may be dark but, because its inhabitants are so richly and idiosyncratically alive, the experience is exhilarating.
