At the start of Blackbird, a man and a woman confront one another in charged silence across a very messy room. It turns out that their lives, and especially their prior relationship, are even messier. The man, Ray, looks to be in his late fifties, early sixties; we learn that the woman, Una, is 28, though in the current production she seems about five years younger.

They're in a staff room at Rays workplace; the reason it looks like a pigsty (I quote) is that the employees leave the detritus of a days snacking lying all over it. Ray's own position in the company (dental floss and allied products) is uncertain, but he claims it's something senior, though at one point he gets so intent on clearing up the garbage from the floor and tabletop that Una twits him with being the janitor. This he vehemently denies, and I think we can take it that he is telling the truth. Its typical of David Harrowers engrossing play that we can be more certain of what Ray isn't than of what he is.

The salient fact about the two characters is, however, undisputed. There's a certain amount of fencing and stonewalling before were told what it is, but the revelation comes early enough for it to be re-revealed here; besides, it would be impossible to discuss the play intelligibly otherwise. Ray and Una had a sexual relationship when Una was 12. Ray was sent to prison; since his release he has, he says, rebuilt his life. Una hasnt seen him since his arrest but now, having spotted his picture in a trade magazine, she's tracked him down. For revenge, retribution, reconciliation or just some unspecific notion of "closure"? We don't know and neither, probably, does she. As for Ray, he could be terrified to see her or pleased or intrigued or some combination. All the possibilities are there.

Blackbird was first staged at the Edinburgh Festival in 2005; it was subsequently staged in London and New York, winning all kinds of awards in both places. For the first 20 minutes of its Toronto production, its reputation seems overblown. Its tense, certainly, and intriguing, but it unfolds with the kind of freighted, sawn-off, semi-enigmatic dialogue thats become contemporary drama's default style. We think we know where we are with it: Basically, this being a British play, in oblique Pinterland, with a dash of Mamet menace. Our mistake. Those influences, or at least those resemblances, are certainly there, but to the play's own ends. It may seem secretive, but it isnt obscure. Harrower has, on this evidence, an impeccable sense of what can and cannot be said without cheating. He applies it to character, narrative and even to morality. His remarkable achievement is to move us beyond disgust.

There is no doubt that both Una and Ray are deeply damaged, and our instinctive reaction is to blame Ray. I wouldnt say the play discourages that reaction, but it does complicate it. We're made to contemplate the unsettling possibility that what happened all those years ago was love, which would have made the betrayal all the more intense. Then, in the play's last brilliant quarter of an hour, theres another reversal, one that changes all our perceptions. Were forced to recognize something that we have been tempted to forget -- what a 12-year-old child is actually like. Here, Harrower is, indeed, harrowing. I've said that he knows just how much to reveal. He also knows how and when to spring the revelations; he times his surprises for maximum impact. And still he leaves us wanting to know more. The play ends with the most unnerving set of unanswerable questions since Ibsen dropped the curtain on Ghosts without telling us whether or not Mrs. Alving was about to mercy-kill her son.

I would guess that both roles in Blackbird are fearsomely difficult, but Ray's is probably the more easily approachable. Hes in the more obviously dramatic situation, a cornered man governed by fear, the predator become the prey. That, at least, is the impression given in this Studio 180 production in which Hardee Lineham plays him, superbly, as a man built out of jelly. You believe in the facade he has constructed, as a pink-faced middle manager, and shudder at how easily it can be dismantled. Una suffers more from the productions decision to sever the play's connections to its national origins; there's no reason, of course, why it couldnt happen in Canada, but her manner of speech is hard-wired to the British tradition of opaque threat. She does sound like someone out of Pinter, and Jessica Greenberg seems to be playing a style rather than a person. She starts blankly aggressive, before switching to plaintive, a tone that dominates a long retrospective monologue, which, perhaps unwisely, is staged and lit as a set-piece. Its only in the last stretches, where Una's at her most open and vulnerable, that the performance finds some flexibility. This imbalance apart, Joel Greenberg delivers a taut production on a wonderfully anonymous set by Michael Gianfrancesco, an environment disdainful of humanity whose human inhabitants have clearly reacted in kind. The last of the play's open questions, or maybe the first, is its title; the author has referred offhandedly in interviews to Bye Bye Blackbird ("no one here can love or understand me") and to the Lennon-McCartney song, but I think he was kidding.
