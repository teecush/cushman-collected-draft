THE MOTHER'S SAINT

Edward Roy's new play is about the abuse of children in Catholic institutions in Quebec 60 years ago, and about the effects on those children's own children. The author's other works include The Other Side of the Closet, seen last year at the Toronto Young People's Theatre, which was about growing up gay in contemporary Canada.

This form of dramaturgy -- selecting an issue and then trying to find a story and characters to illustrate it -- may be a legitimate approach when writing for children, though I have my doubts; its appearance on the main stage of a supposedly adult house is a reminder of how much growing up our theatre still has to do.

The Mother's Saint is actually much inferior to The Other Side of the Closet,which was at least honestly didactic and clear in its objectives. It was also, of course, short. The new play is long, chronologically complicated and confusing.

It kicks off during the 1998 Montreal ice storm; a young Torontonian named Robert is discovered in an otherwise deserted house, unconscious and in the company of a dead body. Revived and taken into custody, he is questioned by a police inspector who has reason to believe that a drug-related murder has taken place.

It transpires that the suspect has come to town to seek information about his late mother, whose erratic behaviour as an adult can be traced to her nightmarish experiences as a child. She had been abandoned to an orphanage run by an order of nuns; their regime, already oppressive, was much worsened when the province, in order to secure increased federal funding, encouraged the orphanages to reconstitute themselves as mental hospitals.

Since Robert's mother had been seeing unauthorized visions of what she called "the Lady" -- hence the play's title -- she was a sitting duck. (She was one of the "Duplessis orphans," named for Maurice Duplessis, Quebec's premier at the time; there were thousands of them.)

As Robert is interrogated, the earlier stages of his quest are played out in flashback; it is hard to tell which of these episodes are part of his statement to the inspector and which are stopping- places for his own train of thought. Interspersed with these, but operating on an entirely different convention, is another set of flashbacks showing the mother as a child, with the sisters of no mercy.

The author, who is also the director, throws in a few other devices. There is an introductory film sequence showing Montreal iced up; though if there is meant to be anything symbolic about the weather, it doesn't register. It doesn't even contribute atmosphere; we just forget about it. There are also a couple of cabaret turns by an audience-baiting drag queen. One of these routines ends the play, with breathtaking feebleness.

The drag artist's relevance to the action is that he is the lover of the guy in whose house the protagonist is discovered. Robert's reason for being there in the first place -- he certainly hadn't been invited -- is what the play slowly reveals; he says near the beginning that he feels like he's in a jigsaw puzzle, and so do we. Most of the pieces do fit in the end: all, in fact, except the hero's recurring encounters with the child who would grow up to be his mother, which must be hallucinations but are staged with the same prosaic dullness as everything else.

With its long succession of short scenes, the play is yet another theatre piece to play like a hack TV script minus the hardware; the technical problems it presents are insoluble, or at any rate unsolved.

It's especially hard on the actor playing the cop, who is constantly left on the edge of the stage, staring fixedly at scenes that don't involve him and trying to look as if he belonged. It's also hard on the audience. Our theatres seem to think that we will actually enjoy watching an endless series of furniture-removals carried out in a dingy half-light. (At one especially hilarious moment a bath, which has done double duty in two separate locations, is found to be no longer necessary and is unceremoniously pushed through a trick wall by one of the nuns. Presumably this is a standard architectural feature in convents, installed in case of Protestant persecution.)

The performances vary in skill; 13-year-old Tess Benger comes out in first place, with Steve Cumyn as Robert and Terrence Bryant as his unwilling host tying for second. But none of them seems very convincing, or indeed very convinced. They can't even get up and walk round a room, or perch on the edge of a table, as if they believed in it. (Actually I don't think I've ever seen a play in which an actor did perch believably on the edge of a table; it's one of those supposedly casual actions that always looks contrived.)

All of them are imprisoned by colourless dialogue that is mainly exposition, and by characters who are cardboard; nor does it help that half of them are playing two roles apiece. There seems no reason for either the drag or the drugs, except that the latter is a laborious device for getting the hero into a situation where he will be forced to talk.

He is also granted a girlfriend. Both the gay and the straight romances in the play have their problems, the latter especially. It's all about lack of commitment, and that kind of thing; these are the kind of relationships that consist mainly of talking about the relationships.

The point, presumably, is that sins committed against the parents will be visited upon the children and probably beyond; but it isn't dramatized. (The play is apparently autobiographical, but so what?)

Meanwhile, the really interesting point goes missing. Just what happened all those years ago when the orphanages sold their souls to become the wrong kind of asylum? How did the people most involved react? The moment is passed over in a bald exchange between a comparatively nice young nun who has qualms and a nasty old one who doesn't. If you're going to deal with issues, deal.
