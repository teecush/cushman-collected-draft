Bus Stop is at the Royal George Theatre until Nov. 27; Belle Moral is at the Court House Theatre until Oct. 7. 1-800-511-SHAW.

BUS STOP

Royal George Theatre

BELLE MORAL

Court House Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont.

- - -

Everybody is nostalgic for roadside diners, and the one designed by Sue LePage for the Shaw Festival production of Bus Stop looks good enough to be franchised. The play itself, William Inge's Broadway hit of 1955, also calls up memories of a neater if not happier time, a period in which dramatic characters got together to interact in confined spaces for tightly limited periods -- and emerged the wiser for the experience. Truths are told, destinies are linked and even the remaining loose ends have a tidiness about them. Of its kind, Bus Stop is about as good as it gets.

It has the double advantage of seeming both fresh and familiar since, though famous as both a play and a film, it is not often seen, at least not in these parts. A blizzard brings a small busload of people -- a driver and four passengers -- to an overnight halt some 30 miles west of Kansas City, which, as the local sheriff has patiently to explain, isnt in Kansas even though they themselves now are.

First to disembark is young Cherie, a self-pronounced chantoose who claims that she is being abducted. Bo, an equally youthful cowboy from Montana who was in K.C. for the rodeo, caught her club act and was dazzled. She liked him, too; at any rate they went to bed together. (Everythings up to date in Kansas City. They've gone about as far as they can go.) He woke up convinced they were in love and engaged and wouldn't hear otherwise. So here they are, en route to Bos ranch, with his protective older buddy, Virgil, along for the ride. The remaining traveller is Dr. Lyman, an old soak of an ex- teacher, who quotes Shakespeare and comes on to young girls, in this instance to Elma, high-school student and part-time waitress. She too gets dazzled but only temporarily, thanks to the good advice of Grace, whose diner it is and who herself is taking advantage of the extra-long stopover to have a fling with the bus driver.

All in the course of the night discover something about love and tell us what it is. Grace learns to settle, Elma learns to wait, and the doctor learns to give up.

Bo and Cherie, the central couple, share a more complicated educational process. He, with some assistance from his friends wisdom and rather more from the sheriff's fists, discovers that you can't always have what you want, especially if what you want doesnt want you. She, finding that hes more vulnerable and less experienced than he let on, melts. Her decision is also influenced by her realization that neither her past nor her probable professional future bear much thinking about. (Her performance of That Old Black Magic as part of a wayfarers' cabaret halfway through the night doesn't suggest any kind of threat to Doris Day.) So the expected Broadway happy ending has its dark underside.

The buckaroo and the chantoosie-floozy are American legends, with Cherie carrying additional mythic baggage from being played on screen by Marilyn Monroe. Its a tribute to the two actors here that each of them can embody the archetype and still find the person within it.

Nicole Underhay, in a fluent and firmly centred performance, explores all Cheries permutations of hurt and pride; Martin Happer's Bo has a blithe swagger that's a very plausible anagram for innocence. Hes even, by the end, managed to make the characters boorishness likeable, which is quite a feat. (My hazy memory of the movie is that it finessed this matter by filling in all the back story, and showing it mostly from Bo's point of view.)

Jackie Maxwell's production gets the personal rhythms and the rhythms of the diner flowing smoothly into one another, with Grace and Elma brought firmly to life by Mary Haney, shaking her feathers like a satisfied mother hen, and Diana Donnelly, a perceptive listener and eager participant. Michael Ball is a fine drawling sheriff and Norman Browning a good self-hating lush. Peter Krantz supplies a melancholy ground-bass (actually, he plays guitar) as Virgil who, like Antonio in The Merchant of Venice, bows out of his young friends life when a lady bows in.

Virgil may supply a literary subtext as well as a homosexual one. He shares a name, after all, with the Roman poet who was resurrected to become Dantes guide, philosopher and friend through the first two-thirds of The Divine Comedy.

Or maybe my mind is running that way after seeing Ann-Marie MacDonald's Belle Moral, a new play that's loaded with literature. The title, puns apart, denotes a big old house not far from Edinburgh, and the time is the end of the 19th century. The references run from partially submerged prophetic gags about Kafka and Proust to more topical and explicit invocations of Wilde and Shaw, to outright quotation from times past; Shakespeare gets paraphrased, while the plays best speech is a poem by Robert Burns, the one about mice. Paul Sportellis score includes strains of Clair de Lune, which turn out to be a musical pun. And then there's Darwin.

Pearl MacIsaac, the de facto mistress of Belle Moral, is an amateur biologist concerned to push evolutionary theory further forward, or perhaps backward; her project emerges as the endeavour to find a genetic link between humans and dogs. Or, failing that, nicer ways for the one to treat the other. "We still organize our societies" she says, "as though we alone had been created in the image of a god in whom we profess no longer to believe." I wish the bulk of the play were as good as that line at taking ideas and rubbing them together.

Pearl's father recently died; her mother, who was thought to have carried a strain of insanity, committed suicide shortly after giving birth to Pearls brother, who has grown up wild and whimsical. But it never works up any steam, and when its resolved the surprise is less in the resolution itself than in the fact that it has one. The jokes are almost continuous but seldom funny, and they don't add up to a comic structure.

It reads better than it acts; better anyway than it acts here. The director, Alisa Palmer, has given a weightless play a heavy- handed production. Judith Bowden's pannelled mural of a set looks impressive at first glance, but it proves very impractical for the plays succession of short, shifting scenes; we spend ages watching furniture being trundled around in the half-light. The actors, too, are stuck in glue; the art of conversation sounds, most unexpectedly at the Shaw, in steep decline. The Scottish accents may be a hurdle even Fiona Byrne, whose overall lucidity as Pearl is a mercy, is sometimes strangled by hers. Jeff Meadows antic flourishes as the brother are exhausting. Peter Millard plays the family doctor with a rank lack of conviction. Brogues apart, he has a tough job functioning as the play's melodramatic villain and all-purpose reactionary. Donna Belleville does reasonably well by a barely characterized maiden aunt. The best writing goes to, and the best acting comes from, Bernard Behrens as a decrepit retainer amusingly addressed as Young Farleigh.
