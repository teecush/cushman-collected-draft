Sir Laurence Olivier On stage, he seemed indestructible

Wednesday, July 12, 1989

ROBERT CUSHMAN

BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail A few years ago Laurence Olivier played King Lear on television and made me cry. This should not, on the face of it, have been surprising. Here was the official great English actor of his generation, maybe of the century, in the greatest tragedy in the language. But among all the qualities attributed to Olivier that of being moving had never ranked very high. Exciting, witty, hypnotic, athletic, protean: these were adjectives nobody ever denied him. He was, from the 1930s to the 1970s, the master of the British stage. But the Lear, a decade after his last appearance in the theatre, was something else.

Physically shrunken, he seemed to have a new moral authority, a new simplicity, an amazing lack of self-consciousness. Part of the effect was in the implicit homage paid him by the other actors in the cast. Leo McKern, a major actor of the generation immediately junior to Olivier's, played Gloucester; and scuttled to his masters side with the duty a fine actor might pay to a great one. Players like Dorothy Tutin, Diana Rigg, John Hurt and the late Colin Blakely, added to the tributary effect; and the faithful, loving Cordelia was played by Anna Calder-Marshall, an actress who almost personifies goodness. Her tears sparked everybody elses. Like Lear, Olivier had lost his kingdom and discovered love. That may seem sentimental, but the parallel was close enough to be affecting. And for those of us who grew up on his name, and admired him without ever being able quite to surrender to him, it was a cathartic release.

There is a story about Olivier, rehearsing for a charity matinee at London's Royal Court Theatre. He was due to re-create one of the Court's, and his own, greatest triumphs: Archie Rice, the leering, self-hating music-hall comic in John Osbornes The Entertainer. He suddenly appeared in the stalls in full costume and makeup and muttered to the director, "this is really me, isnt it?" He identified, apparently, with the hollow mountebank in Archie; and the identification allowed the performance to go beyond the obvious thrill of seeing a classical actor impersonate a low comedian. When the film version resurfaced on TV recently, I reflected that I had never had less trouble believing in an Olivier performance. All the glittering technique was on display but it never obstructed the truth. In fact, it was part of the truth.

Some of this may, at this moment, sound ungracious. It isn't meant to be. But I have to acknowledge a difficulty in dealing with Olivier: one that I think may be characteristic of my generation. The classical performances that placed him on his pedestal, at the Old Vic just after the war - Oedipus, Hotspur, Richard III - were before my time; though when the Richard appeared on film I was ready for it and was duly devastated. I wasn't moved; but then I wasnt asked to be. I was hypnotized into aghast complicity with this lank-haired monster with the iron-filing voice. Above all, I was amused. His soliloquies, delivered straight at the camera in defiance of convention, were a cinematic breakthrough. On reflection, they were a breakthrough period. I have rarely known an actor to achieve such audience rapport, even in the theatre.

The makeup, the cleverness, the impersonation - this was acting. His contemporaries, John Gielgud and Ralph Richardson, by contrast seemed distant, mannered, always themselves. As time went on, this unchanging humanity is precisely what I came to treasure in them. When Richardson died, I felt a sense of personal loss. I found Oliviers centre harder to locate. This is, of course, precisely what some of his greatest admirers - most notably Ken Tynan, who should have written his biography - most valued. One problem is that Olivier was largely absent from the London stage during the 1950s. I didn't get a good sustained look at him until he took up residence as first director of the National Theatre in 1963. And by then he was a living legend: a myth that dared you not to believe, and in doing so made belief harder. You sat there silently saying, Prove it.

He must have felt this burden himself. Certainly he never ducked a challenge. The burden of running the National for 10 years he shouldered magnificently, establishing a genuine classic repertoire and bringing on new generations of actors, playwrights and even directors. There were rocky periods but some of it seemed like a golden age, and in retrospect it all does. There has never been such a glittering muscular acting ensemble in Britain, and nobody else would have had the technique and the application to bring it off. He directed few plays himself, but one of them - Chekhov's Uncle Vanya in the first season - was the greatest production I have ever seen. He himself played Astrov and his duets with Michael Redgraves Vanya were, among much else, a superb display of mutual respect at the highest level.

His classic performance at the National, though, was his Othello. This was a triumph of vocal and physical control, as well as being a white mans consummate appropriation of negritude. It turned off as many people as it turned on. I felt, and still feel, hopelessly equivocal about it. The performance wanted to impress, and it did. It wanted to overwhelm, and sometimes it did. I assume it wanted to move, and it didn't. But I realize, 25 years on, that I have never seen an Othello to match it. Actually, I have seen few Othellos at all; the Olivier dust has yet to settle. The role needs the size that he gave it; the words shrivel in anybody else's rendition. And perhaps everything else is unimportant.

His other major roles at the National - Shylock, James Tyrone, the Captain in Strindbergs The Dance of Death - evoked the same mixed feelings. I resisted what were plainly intended as their emotional peaks, while acknowledging that nobody else could even have built the scaffolding. He played some gleeful cameos in Restoration and other comedy; again it was difficult to judge if they were more or less funny for having his name attached. He went out, predictably unpredictable, as a working-class ideologue in Trevor Griffiths highly political play The Party. Officially, it was a supporting role and Griffiths spoke to me endearingly of this "ambulant black hole" entering the rehearsal room and effortlessly sucking all energy toward it while making elaborate attempts to be inconspicuous.

Actors who worked with Olivier, even younger ones theoretically distrustful of him, invariably ended up worshipping him. (Directors tended to be warier.) My only personal encounter with him concerned a TV documentary for which he vetoed the idea of filming National rehearsals because "it always makes the director look clever and the actor a fool." He was always protective of actors, and they, as the Lear showed, returned the compliment. He ran the National from an actor's point of view which, in these days of director-power, was a valuable gesture, maybe an invaluable precedent. His last years, as everybody knows, were wretchedly shadowed by illness, but he seemed triumphantly to overcome one setback after another. He seemed, in fact, indestructible. As with his life so with his death: I am not sure whether I am moved by it, but I feel an immeasurable sense of shock. Robert Cushman was theatre critic of The Observer in London from 1973 to 1984.
