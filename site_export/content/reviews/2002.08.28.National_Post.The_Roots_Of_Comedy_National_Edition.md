HAIRSPRAY

Neil Simon Theater, New York

OKLAHOMA!

Gershwin Theater, New York

- - -

I loved the first half-hour of Hairspray, and I quite liked the rest of it. Its a new Broadway musical, definitely a hit, based on the cult 1988 film. (My definition of a cult movie is a picture I heard a lot about but never got round to seeing.) It opened, improbably and unprecedentedly, in the middle of August -- between seasons, really -- and if nothing else comes along it will clean up at next year's Tonys.

As everybody but me already knew, it's the story of a well- rounded girl who makes good. Here is teenage Tracy Turnblad, in Baltimore in 1962, seen right at the top of the show, greeting the day from inside a bed thats been hydraulically raised to the vertical. (Prologue: Good Morning, Baltimore.) Tracy has a dream, about which she enthusiastically sings; its to dance on her local white-bread TV pop show, of which we immediately get a generous glimpse. (Ensemble number: The Nicest Kids in Town.)

Tracy confides her ambitions to her mother, a laundry worker. If Tracy is chubby, Mom is awesomely ample. (Character song: Mama, I'm a Big Girl Now.)

So far, so wonderful: a Bye Bye Birdie book married to a Little Shop of Horrors score. This pre-Beatles era was the last time pop music was of any use to the theatre, the last in which the music had narrative drive and the lyrics could be comfortably understood and might even conceivably be funny. This show's music (by Marc Shaiman) and lyrics (by Scott Wittman and the composer) are an amiable pastiche that propel the show along and raise a few laughs. The direction (Jack OBrien), choreography (Jerry Mitchell), sets (David Rockwell) and costumes (William Ivey Long) are rousing essays in the colourful-grotesque, and they never stop moving. Thomas Meehan, who, after Annie and The Producers, is Broadways man of choice for writing a cartoon, collaborated on the book.

The show hits its first roadblock about the same time Tracy herself does. Her dreams are blighted by the TV producer, who has her own hidebound ideas about what pass for good looks in Middle America, and by her daughter, who exemplifies them. They are a shrill duo -- the Wicked Witch of the West and child -- and they keep striking the same attitudes. (They have to, for the sake of conflict, but that doesn't help.) Mother has a number about her glamorous youth (When I Was Miss Baltimore Crabs) that takes us to the depths of dismal; it may be impossible in a musical to laugh at someone you loathe. Also, they seem more like an irritant than a threat. Even their star and host, Corny Collins (Baltimore's local answer, presumably, to Dick Clark), doesnt like them much, and he must have more clout than they do.

Worse still is when the show gets token-serious. It isnt just stereotypically unglamorous white people whom the villains are determined to keep off the air; once a month, their show has something called Negro Day, but the rest of the time, Baltimore's black population is unrepresented and unimpressed. (As one of them says, "every day is Negro Day at our house.") Tracy meets the black kids at her own school, mainly in detention class, and they teach her to really dance. She joins her cause to theirs, and -- along with a high proportion of the rest of the cast -- goes mock- heroically to jail for it. (The on-the-spot shuffle-and-tap routine in the women's prison -- The Big Dollhouse -- is a definite highlight, mainly for its tongue-in-cheekiness.)

Now, this particular battle for integration was a real one, but it was won a long time ago and Hairspray has no call to act so self- congratulatory about it now. As a friend remarked, it rings especially hollow when you look round the auditorium and notice a marked absence of black people. (There are plenty of fat people, though.) We take for granted, now, that any musical that can manage it will squeeze in an inspirational gospel number, and that the white audience will greet it with hysterical applause. Patronizing, I call it, and the anthem here (I Know Where Ive Been) is indistinguishable from all the others of its kind.

Its the only point at which the show gives up even the pretense of comedy, and it jars. Otherwise, energy and goodwill carry the day, especially when they cohabit in the person of Edna, Tracy's mother. On screen, she was played by Divine, the drag queen. The stage version honours this tradition, while modifying it in the direction of user-friendliness, by giving the role to Harvey Fierstein. (Whether they'll be able to keep the transvestite convention going when the show becomes a franchise is something we wait agog to discover.)

Now, you might argue that a show which goes on at such length about the acceptance-rights of big women is guilty of serious cop- out when it casts the biggest of them as a man. You might also note that Ednas first line in the show is to the effect that people like her and her family dont belong on television, and that she spends the rest of the evening fighting that assumption, and that nobody remarks on the inconsistency. It is also true that her role in the show really amounts to a series of detachable routines, and that what should be their highlight falls rather flat; this is an old- time vaudeville duet with her twinkling shrimp of a husband (the very admirable Dick Latessa) in which the writing runs out of wit when it is most needed. It doesn't matter; every word Fierstein's Edna says, or sings, or (this covers both the preceding) growls is funny: endearing, too. I wonder which Tony category this performance will be eligible for. As Tracy, Marissa Jaret Winokur has plenty of punch but not much variety, so her performance wears rather, how you say, thin.

In its essence, Hairspray is an old-fashioned 1950s comedy musical: songs and sketches marshalled into a mildly satirical plot line. But, coming in the wake of The Producers and Urinetown, it confirms a return on Broadway to American musical comedies that are recognizably American and recognizably comic. (Youve spotted the missing ingredient: Still, two out of three aint bad.) Such shows are still a (highly profitable) minority. The EuroDisney behemoths - - some are Euro, some are Walt -- continue to clutter the place up immovably: Beauty and the Beast, Les Miserables, Mamma Mia!, The Phantom of the Opera, Aida -- I am condemning the latter without seeing it and feel no guilt whatever. There is also 42nd Street, the revival of a revival, and Thoroughly Modern Millie, which is an imitation 42nd Street.

The best musical evening to be had on Broadway is the more honest, and certainly more thoughtful, revival of Oklahoma!, directed by Trevor Nunn. This started at the National Theatre in London and, by many accounts, was better there. But it's plenty good enough here, panoramically scenic (if the corn isn't as high an elephants eye, it would take an elephant to disprove it) and with Susan Stromans best choreography to date. Her dream ballet may not be as balletically impressive as the Agnes de Mille original, but it is more dramatically immediate, largely due to having the heroine, Laurey, dance it herself, rather than relying on a silent substitute.

Josefina Gabrielle's Laurey has been much criticized but I found her charming, and very detailed in the approach-avoidance games she plays with Patrick Wilson's Curly. Shuler Hensley is a wonderfully sung and acted Jud Fry, a gigglingly neurotic villain so understandable as to be downright troubling. Listening to the Rodgers-Hammerstein score, you don't just nod at a procession of famous songs (as you often do at classic musicals -- Kiss Me Kate, for example), you have a theatrical experience. After 60 years, these songs work.
