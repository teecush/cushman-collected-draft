History books may record this as the Shaw Festival's Clare Boothe Luce season. Luce, New York society beacon and journalist (edited Vanity Fair, married into Time-Life) is mentioned in One Touch of Venus as a symbol of style and wealth and smartness, and is author of The Women, a play that both skewers and worships the same qualities.

One Touch of Venus is a 1943 musical assembled by the heady team of Kurt Weill (music), Ogden Nash (book and lyrics), and S.J. Perelman (book). It's based on a Victorian English comic novel, The Tinted Venus, in which a statue of the goddess comes to life and wreaks havoc. The musical updates and transports it. The Anatolian Venus is brought to Manhattan by Whitelaw Savory, a super-rich art expert who sees it both as an investment and as a reminder of a girl whom he once loved and lost. The statue is accidentally reanimated by Rodney Hatch, a shy young barber on whom she immediately sets her sights, sights that he speedily comes to enjoy. And whats more, shes got arms.

This is a post-Oklahoma! musical, though so immediately post (they opened the same year) that the narrative revolution had hardly had time to take. Some of the show is story-driven, some is haphazard; it opens, for example, with an arbitrary, not to say philistine, number about the faddishness of modern art. Much of the action is a chase: Savory and his goons in vengeful pursuit of the barber and his divine doxy, with additional input from Rodney's bullying fiancee, her yet more monstrous mother and some late-arriving Turkish guerrillas. It's a thin but frantic story, sometimes funny, occasionally touching, the latter quality much enhanced by the best of Weills music. This is the most "Broadway" of his American scores, and some of it is stuff that Cole Porter could have done better, but it also boasts such a driving, jagged tune as Venus perplexed I'm a Stranger Here Myself, and the great ballad duet Speak Low, Shakespearean in its title, its imagery and its compression. Nash, the laureate of comic verse, was paradoxically best as a lyricist when writing love songs. Speak Low deepens the evening, or should; the approach to it at the Shaw is strangely giggly.

Most of Eda Holmes' production is delightful, especially in its evocation, via Camellia Koos sets and act drops, of wartime New York: something that the original audiences must have taken for granted but is helpful to us. Ryan deSouzas musical direction is excellent, as is Michael Lichtefeld's choreography, especially in the show's two satirical ballets. The one major problem, the one that has always dogged this show, is how to cast the goddess of love. Robin Evans Willis, whos a bit too young for divinity, plays her as a self-conscious seductress, and comes off coy. Venus wouldnt have to act sexy; she is sex. There must be subtler ways of showing a woman's feelings for a man than the current Shaw practice (it happens in Harvey, too) of having her rub herself up against him. Kyle Blair's Rodney -- half eager-beaver, half nerd -- is spot-on. Mark Uhre doesnt have the mileage for Savory, but he sings beautifully, especially in a yearning ballad that has nothing to do with the rest of his character. The toughs are very sharply played by Jay Turvey and Neil Barclay, whom the authors of Guys and Dolls must have foreseen when they wrote Sit Down, Youre Rockin' the Boat. Julie Martell and Gabrielle Jones play the barber's thwarted in-laws as arrant grotesques, which is perfectly fine. The most commanding performance of all comes from Deborah Hay as Savorys sharp-tongued secretary who informs us in show-stopping song that "one way to be very wealthy / Is to be very, very rich."

She carries the same message, along with the rest of the large all-female cast, in The Women. She also, in both shows, adheres to the Niagara convention by which all American women below a certain social level -- and sometimes, as in Hays two roles, above it -- are presumed to sound like gangsters' adenoidal molls. The Women elaborately depicts a rarefied slice of the inter-war Big Apple in which wives take their husbands' infidelities for granted, and compensate with shopping, card games, workouts and, in extreme circumstances, divorce; one scene takes us to Reno where, were told, the actual residents seldom use the facilities. Its a sharp moment in a generally flabby script. Mainly we follow the story of Mary, the solitary innocent in the pack, who finally learns to sharpen her nails and reclaim her spouse from the gold-digger by whom he's been appropriated. It's a long journey to nothing very much. Jenny Young as Mary and Moya OConnell as her antagonist give recognizably human performances, along with Kelli Fox, whose voice cuts like a knife through a blur of an opening scene, placed so far upstage that the complicated task of establishing the characters becomes impossible. The other rich bitches are caricatures, Wendy Thatcher being the most entertaining. The lower orders double, treble and quadruple like rabbits, throwing up believable performances from Melanie Phillipson as an Irish maid and Patty Jamieson as several people. Theres a submerged class-war in the play, but in Alisa Palmer's production, it's mostly fought in the wrong way. A society dame complains from her hospital bed about the inconveniencies of her latest childbirth; a nurse tells her grimly how bad pregnancy can be for poor people. It's acted here as a tantrum meeting a lecture. The main attraction is watching the massive components of William Schmuck's set form and reform; sometimes, as in a deluxe bathroom scene, the finished product looks witty. Mostly, it just looks expensive.
