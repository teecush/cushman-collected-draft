UNIDENTIFIED HUMAN REMAINS AND THE TRUE NATURE OF LOVE

Buddies in Bad Times Theatre, Toronto

- - -

I have found that in the minds of British critics who have some acquaintance with Canadian drama, Brad Frasers name leads all the rest. Its a little hard for us to think of him as our breakout playwright, simply because in the last 15 years he hasn't written many plays. But this new production of the piece that made his name explains why he has gone down so well all over the world, while also serving as a bracing reminder of how welcome his voice was here back in 1990. Theatrical writing with this much wit and presence is still uncommon. At Buddies in Bad Times, where this revival has landed, it's practically unheard of.

There are long titles and there are cryptic titles: Unidentified Human Remains and the True Nature of Love manages the unusual feat of being both, though the play itself is not especially either. The remains are those of the female victims of a serial killer at large in Edmonton. (The location may well have significance for those who have lived there, but for the rest of us it could be any city.) Love is sought, in some form, by each of the plays seven characters, most of them sexually active though not as much as theyd like to be, all of them in a mess.

In terms of plot, the multiple-murder story has not worn well, least of all in the gloating narrative vignettes of death and dismemberment that set the tone for it and for which the cast turn into a chorus. The play has few pretensions as a whodunit, and though it's being billed as a thriller, it only comes to life in that vein toward the end when one of the characters, confronted by some clues best described as bloody obvious, is abruptly transformed into both a detective and a one-person vigilante squad. (The implicit comment on the efficiency of the Edmonton police force is not flattering.)

In terms of theme, however, the two halves of the play go very well together. The image of sexually motivated murder looms over the everyday action, an extreme instance of the potential for cruelty latent in any relationship. Most of the characters come to recognize this in themselves; some confront it.

To get down to cases: We meet David and Candy, who once experimented as lovers but are now better cast as roommates. (Their apartment is a point of relative stability on Philip Beesley's urban jungle of a set, whose other components change identity by the minute.) David, who greets his co-habitant with "honey, Im homo," is described as a professional faggot, which means he gets all the best lines.

He is also a professional waiter, though he once had passing success as an actor; his new career derives from what I remember a film character in a similar situation describing as "a wild, untamable desire to keep from starving to death." Besides, hes good at it. He's sought out by his busboy Kane, who plainly adores him but is scared of doing anything physical about it. Part flattered, part annoyed, David reaches his lowest point by starting to humiliate Kane and maybe his highest by stopping.

Meanwhile, Candy oscillates between Jerri, a lesbian admirer whose attentions are as persistent as Kane's and who gets treated with equal capriciousness, and Robert, an intermittently abusive bartender. Candy also has a career: Shes a book critic, though very little is made of the fact; she isnt, for example, branded as a metaphorical serial killer on her own account. (This is an admirable gesture of self-restraint from Fraser, who has posted his own frank opinion of critics on his Web site.) And in yet another meanwhile, there is Bernie, who can't do without women but who also seems to hate them and who desperately craves his friend David's reassurance.

The foibles, dates and destinies of these people are interwoven in quick-changing scenes, as if the concept of Friends or of Sex and the City were being stripped of glamour and played for real. The play also makes the most resourceful use yet of phone messages recorded and overheard. The comic pressure, partly because of the seriousness of the stakes, is raised very high, and in one sustained sequence erupts into farce. Both of Candys current attachments visit her at the same time. David, who was all primed to go out for the evening, changes his mind. Miss all this? he says. "You must be crazy." He could be speaking for us all.

Crows Theatre, which first produced the play in Toronto and has yet to do anything else as good, remounts it now under the same director, Jim Millan. I remember his original production, with Brent Carver as David, as being sleeker and more precise than this one; here, cast and audience alike seem to be clinging to the comedy because they are unsure of everything else, with the inevitable result that it's less funny. (And time, probably, has nibbled at the play's edges.)

The performances are mostly fine, but they all have moments of flabbiness -- times when theyre mouthing lines with no clear sense of who theyre talking to or why. Damien Atkins, barring the odd lapse into sentimentality, is impressively and enjoyably in command of David; likewise Mary Krohnert as Candy, continually getting knocked off her perch and climbing back up again, and Jenny Young as Jerri, unrecognizable from her last Toronto appearance, in The Shape of a Girl. As Kane, Philip Riccio grows steadily in definition as the character grows up. Tony Nappo is blusteringly, and blisteringly, believable as Robert, whom the play, if not Candy, drops with excessive abruptness. Michelle Latimer copes gamely with a psychic hooker who beats the brunt of the more portentous writing. Only Brendan Wall falls short; a good actor, he cannot muster either the weight or the torment that the play seems to demand of Bernie -- without, admittedly, giving him much to go on.

Some things that once played inevitably now seem arbitrary. Most of the play's muscles, though, still flex. The appetite is whetted for Fraser's new one, which has the enticing title of Cold Meat Party and is due next season at Factory Theatre. Already, we hear, it has been a wow in Manchester.
