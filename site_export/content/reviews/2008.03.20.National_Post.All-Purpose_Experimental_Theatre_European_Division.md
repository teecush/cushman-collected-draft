THEATRE REVIEW

THE MANSFIELD PROJECT

Factory Studio Theatre

---

After eight years spent excavating the short stories of Anton Chekhov, the remarkably talkative mimes of Theatre Smith-Gilmour have turned their attention to a slightly more recent tale-teller. The Mansfield Project is a dramatization of four pieces by Katherine Mansfield; there is no narrative overlap between the sections but they have been arranged so that each melts into the next.

The show begins with two men digging a grave, an image that when clothed in anything approaching modern dress, plants us firmly in the land of All-Purpose Experimental Theatre, European Division, Chekhov to Beckett. Two black-clad females stand at the back of the stage in mourning; First Gravedigger remarks to Second Gravedigger that now these women's lives will really start. He doesn't make that sound like a happy thing.

He's not wrong. The ladies are The Daughters of the Late Colonel. That's the title of the first Mansfield story before us, though you'll search in vain to find that information in the program, or anything at all about the author beyond vague generalizations concerning her "delicate words" and the "challenge" she presents.

Here's a radical suggestion: Suppose our theatre programs start devoting less space to beautiful mission-statements and declarations of how happy the actors are to be working with one another (or just working) and more to basic facts about the authors and the works they're plundering. Anything less seems rather rude.

I'm no Mansfield scholar myself, but I think I can see that this New Zealand-born writer who spent much of her short life (1888 to 1923) in England was trying to make universal art out of the British class system. The specifics count. The colonel's daughters find themselves part-liberated, part-terrified. Their father bullied them, and there are plenty of flashbacks here to show us just how he did it. They even worry about whether they have the right to bury him. (As opposed to what, you may ask. Well, this is not a wholly naturalistic story, and Theatre Smith-Gilmour wouldn't have it any other way.) They have a brother who's out in Ceylon, doing the work of the Empire; a mention of there being no proper postal system out there brings out the sound effects of pattering hooves. And they have a nephew, to whom they are devoted and whom they ply with cake that he, with some embarrassment, declines to eat. Above all, they have a maid, on whose cuisine they are dependent and of whom they would now like to get rid: understandably, since she appears here as a cooking monster out of Strindberg or Lewis Carroll.

As usual, directors Dean Gilmour and Michele Smith have come up with a staging that is swift, suggestive and economical. Having paid that ritual obeisance to their ingenuity, I have to note that in between the quick changes their shows are largely dependent on words, and that when it comes to delivering them they, as actors, are invariably less effective than the colleagues with whom they share the stage. In this instance Adam Paolozza, whose maid here is a really scarifying skivvy, and the consistently incisive Claire Calnan.

Gilmour is a stolid actor and, however hard his Colonel may bark, he doesn't have much bite. Calnan and Smith play the sisters, without establishing much family resemblance. Cal-nan is bracingly Anglo-Saxon; Smith, as always, relays her lines in a thick Gallic accent that makes everything she says come out twee and twittery. This has its charms, especially when her character gets to agonizing over whether, by not leaving crumbs out in the kitchen, they may be starving the mice. But it helps propel the whole thing into Theatre Smith-Gilmour's favourite mode of Gothic farce; and since Mansfield's stories, unlike Chekhov's, don't have a born playwright inside them struggling to get out, it's farce that fails to pay off. In terms of length The Daughters of the Late Colonel is the most substantial part of the evening, but it simply fades out in favour of the next bit.

That would be Carnation, a fairly pointless piece of sexual fantasy, which in turn gives place to Prelude, about a group of bloodthirsty children. This comes off like something out of Second City, a troupe who are always good as horrific kids (cf. the Dungeon Master sketch in their current revue); it's less amusing than the Second Citizens would be, but it probably has higher aspirations. Equilibrium is somewhat restored in Six Years After, which has Smith being touching as a bereaved mother on a cruise, with Gilmour as her oblivious, though apparently well-meaning, husband. It could be a nice dying fall, if it had something more solid to fall from. - The Mansfield Project is at the Factory Studio Theatre until April 13. Call 416-504-9971 for more information.
