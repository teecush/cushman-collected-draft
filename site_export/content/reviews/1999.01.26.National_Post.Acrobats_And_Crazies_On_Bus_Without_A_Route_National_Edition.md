The Number 14

Royal Alexandra Theatre, Toronto

Axis Theatre, a Vancouver company, has taken The Number 14 from its home base to Hong Kong and New York before getting off in Toronto. That's quite a bus trip.

The show is set on a bus; Pam Johnson, the designer, gives it to us as a cut-out, a cross-section in attractive shades of red and white. There are six actors in the cast, all of them virtuosi of the quick change, and they come and go at a furious rate. We are told that they play sixty characters between them and although nobodys counting, it seems credible.

Sometimes they wear masks; there may be a system governing when they do and when they dont, but it is never made clear.

Their physical and rhythmic skills are immense. This is obvious from the start, when they all play corporate commuters standing, sitting, crackling their newspapers, and flourishing their cellphones in perfect unison. It seems a bit patronizing, but it's fun. Later on, they transform themselves, almost before our eyes, from a party of grumbling seniors to a party of grumbling kids, presided over by a monstrous teacher on stilts. Her physical bulk is used as a facade behind which the others can work their transformation scenes. It's very clever.

One man -- dressed as a woman, Monty Python style -- does a spectacular acrobatic ballet, vaulting over seats, hanging from rails, in time, presumably, to the jerking of the bus.

The ensembles names are Peter Anderson, Darlene Brookes, Colin Heath, David Mackay, Wayne Specht, and Beatrice Zeilinger; there is no way of telling who does what. They also sing well, mostly a cappella.

Theyre less good at dialogue, both the speaking and the writing of it. (Most of the actors share a "conceived and written" credit with Roy Surette, who directed, and Melody Anderson, who designed the masks.) Great claims have been made for the show; the bus trip has been touted as a metaphorical journey through the heart of the modern city. In practice it seems hardly to have an idea in its head. The point of buses is that they have routes and destinations; this show seems happy just to make it to the next stop.

We gather that the bus goes to the end of its line, turns round, and then comes back again, but the place is a void and the people are cardboard.

Half the passengers are presented as crazies, so that they can bore, scare, or irritate the other half: This effect is repeated with grisly regularity.

Every so often the authors feel an obligation to say something about urban decay, so we get a rap number or a schoolgirl's song about all the things there are to be scared of in the world.

The latter item, which comes out of nowhere and goes right back there, is the show's serious low point. Its comic low point is a verbal and physical duel between a couple of unemployed Shakespearean actors. There is no reason for this routine to take place on a bus (theres no reason for it to take place anywhere, but thats another thing), and the same goes for far too much of the show. Its premise is nowhere near as strong as its creators would like to believe.

A surprising weakness in the mise en scene is that it never makes us aware of anyone on the bus except the characters we actually see, even when we're told that it's rush hour. The ability to people the stage with unseen presences is one traditional mimes talent that this group appears to lack.

Toward the end, they try their hands at a good old alienation effect. A derelict wanders through the auditorium and up on to the stage, notes disgustedly that only half a bus seems to be present, and tells us that the whole thing is an illusion. In the interests of destroying it, he even drags one of the stagehands into the light. (This is justice. Running this show must be a very demanding job, and those doing it deserve recognition. The stage manager is Darren Adam; the assistant stage manager is Annette Devick.)

Much of the show in fact has the coy, hectoring tone of bad childrens theatre. My own kids liked the first half a lot. After that they decided -- correctly, I think -- that the show was willing to throw in anything for the sake of a laugh, and they clammed up for the rest of the evening.

The creators -- or their publicists -- acknowledge a debt to Mr. Bean as well as to Python; they have kept their models' cute trappings, but have jettisoned their bite, their wit, and their sense of structure. Invoking such classics in this context is hitching a free ride.
