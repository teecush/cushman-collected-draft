The film version of the 1975 Broadway musical Chicago works because we are fascinated by the intersection of celebrity and evil. Thats why it worked in 1975. And 1942. And 1927

Here is a bit of musical comedy lyric that Im fond of, a delectable slice of heartless insouciance. It's sung by a pair of theatrical agents to a nubile young woman who has recently been the death of her male protector. The chorus begins:

So here's a good tiding:

If men you are killing,

A talent youre hiding

To be a performer.

So be a performer

And soon youll be riding

(If God should be willing)

The crest of your life.

And it ends:

Unless you, God forbid,

Get hung for what you did --

Oho, you naughty kid.

Just fulfil this small request of your life.

You'll be happy all the rest of your

Long and healthy

(Knock wood)

Life!

That does not come from Chicago, show or movie, though it well might. The situation depicted and the philosophy advanced would fit right in there. It's from a stage musical called Little Me. The late, great and underrated Carolyn Leigh wrote the words, to music by the still-flourishing Cy Coleman. Little Me was staged on Broadway in 1962. The first Broadway production of Chicago was in 1975.

Im not accusing anyone of stealing (though I do remember being struck, when I saw that 75 production in previews, by the similarity of its first cynical spoken line to that of another, earlier show set in a criminal milieu, Irma la Douce). I'm merely suggesting that, in equating criminal and showbiz celebrity, Chicago was not as ferociously ahead of its time as it has become fashionable to believe.

Little Me's Belle Poitrine puts on a good show in court, is acquitted of murder, and becomes a star. She and the show then go on to other things. Chicago shows a jailful of jazz-age murderesses, chief among them the aspiring performer Roxie Hart and the experienced trouper Velma Kelly, who owe their eventual freedom, and stardom, to the grandstanding tactics of the razzle-dazzle lawyer Billy Flynn. It tickles me to note that it takes Chicago more than two hours to deliver the same message that Be a Performer socks over in less than four minutes, and that Be a Performer is funnier.

Chicagos prescient amorality -- or its prescience about amorality -- is generally held to account for its initial failure. A major flaw in this theory is the fact that the first production of Chicago was a success; it received generally admiring reviews and ran for more than 900 performances. It didnt take home many awards, but that was because it opened in the same season as A Chorus Line.

Revisionist history holds that A Chorus Line was a feelgood show that the sentimental audiences of the time lapped up in preference to the tough medicine offered by Chicago. Actually, A Chorus Line won because it was the better show (though Chicago has the better score). It also faces unpleasant truths just as squarely and -- since its characters are people rather than cartoons -- to more disturbing emotional effect.

Today, of course, a musical season that could boast both A Chorus Line and Chicago seems to belong to some unimaginable golden age. In fact, its riches extended even further than that. This was also the season of Pacific Overtures, the Stephen Sondheim/John Weidman musical about America's opening-up of 19th-century Japan to Western markets, layered and mournfully ironic. If you were looking for intelligence and adventurousness in musicals, that was the place to turn, but few people did, then or since. Some 15 years later, the same team came up with Assassins, a far more searching examination than Chicago of the links between violence, celebrity-worship and the cult of entitlement. I don't see Hollywood taking up either of these musicals in the near future.

Spectators at Chicago, in 1975 as in 2002, were having a good sophisticated giggle at the idea of universal corruption. This is the reaction the material has always provoked. It must have done so in 1927 when it first surfaced as a play based on an actual case, and it certainly did so in 1942 when the film Roxie Hart (starring Ginger Rogers but not a musical) took its place in the great tradition of hardboiled screen comedy.

Meanwhile, Broadway had given the world Anything Goes, Cole Porters 1934 ocean liner musical, in which a chorus of first-class passengers are tickled pink to find themselves on board with Public Enemy Number One, and sing a hymn in his honour; and Rodgers and Harts 1940 Pal Joey, an unwaveringly dark musical that happens to be set in a decadent Chicago clubland. That's another show always quoted as having been ahead of its time, but which was in fact a solid success the first time around.

Audiences are versatile animals; they can enjoy heart on one night and heartlessness on another, with no reference to what is supposed to be the Zeitgeist. Beware of pundits who tell you what you, as a product of your times, ought to respond to; they are, without exception, idiots.

The 1975 production of Chicago was brilliant. Even people who hated it could tell that. I know, because I was one of them. As a tourist, I appreciated the opportunity to collect three certified Broadway legends -- Gwen Verdon, Chita Rivera, Jerry Orbach -- in one evening and to see all of them operating at top form.

My problem was with the show's director, choreographer and presiding genius Bob Fosse. He had done a magisterially inventive and imaginative job, but I thought he was being self-indulgent. Not theatrically -- nothing could have been more disciplined -- but personally. When Orbachs lawyer went through his Razzle Dazzle number, he seemed to be standing in for Fosse, excoriating his own genius for playing theatrical tricks while continuing to play them, offering us delight while despising us for accepting it. (Rivera welcomed us back after intermission with a cheery, "Hello, suckers.")

If that was how Fosse felt, shouldnt he just put up or shut up? And what were those lines in Fred Ebb's lyric: Long as you keep 'em way off balance/How can they spot you got no talents? Did Ebb really want us to believe that he, or his composing collaborator John Kander, or the performers, or Fosse himself, were untalented? He knew they werent; we knew they werent. Wasn't all this mock self-flagellation rather sickly? And didn't it make the storys simple satirical point unnecessarily pretentious?

Fosse had been playing this love-hate game with his own abilities ever since directing the film of Cabaret. The original show (which he had nothing to do with) had handed him a metaphor, and he had run with it, all the way to his autobiographical movie All That Jazz. It made me angry, the more so because I admired almost everything else he did.

I no longer feel so strongly about it. Fosse and his few peers (Jerome Robbins, Gower Champion, Michael Bennett) have died, leaving no successors. They are, to put it mildly, missed. Chicago returned to Broadway in 1996, in a blown-up concert staging, and is still there. So the revival has run much longer than the original, and has generated the usual international spin-offs.

The word is that audiences have now caught up with the shows savage indictment of society, and have only to turn on the news to find contemporary parallels. The O.J. Simpson trial is frequently cited, though I can't imagine why. Simpson was a superstar before being indicted; Roxie and Velma are eternal wannabes.

But the real reason the show now slips down more easily is that, with Fosse no longer in control, it has shed its weight of personal obsession. It is a smaller show, but a more enjoyable one; an uncomplicated fable, rousingly performed. I had a great time at it. I caught the revival's first Velma, Bebe Neuwirth, who turned in the sleekest, most dynamic song-and-dance act I have seen in years. Watching her go through I Cant Do It Alone (she could), I found myself hoping that Ann Reinking, who both directed and played Roxie, was a lady with no malice in her heart; for she had to sit at the side of the stage during that number and watch Neuwirth steal the show from under her nose, eight times a week.

And now there is the movie, inheriting the old problems and adding new ones. People are still saying how topical it all is and how rotten we all are (or at least how rotten everybody else is), but not as loudly. A more pressing question seems to have taken over: How can you con a modern movie audience into sitting through a musical?

All the reviews I have read have said two things: a) this is the best film of a stage musical since Cabaret, and b) thats not saying much, considering the competition. I would not contest either point. But I am bothered by another repeated assertion: that Chicago's presentation of all its musical numbers as fantasies is proof positive that no other way is possible. We are told that audiences presented with characters gliding from speech into song as part of a story would hoot with derision, and probably tear up the seats.

This seems to me completely unproven, another example of theorists telling audiences how to behave. My, they're hip. Its certainly true that the numbers in Chicago wouldnt work as quasi- realistic character songs, but that's because that's not how they were written in the first place. Theyre the authors and director's commentaries on the action, and to perform them the actors assume the position of jazz, burlesque or vaudeville icons, fitted out with parodies of 1920s songs. (The parodies are exact: one-on-one, in some cases.) Both stage productions have been done on abstract sets, complex in the Fosse original, bare in the revival. In both we were, all the time and unmistakably, in the theatre.

This isn't really an option for a movie, and Rob Marshall, the director-choreographer, puts us where the story says we are: an apartment, a courthouse or (most of the time) the Cook County jail. The sets are stylized but theyre still places. The numbers can no longer be Fosses dreams, so they have to be the stagestruck Roxie's.

Most of the time, the writing and direction make this work. The one number in the original that is conventional conversational -- a funny female duet, very Kander-and-Ebb, called Class -- has been deleted. Dialogue that seems to be leading up to it is still there, so the cut must have been a last-minute decision. Presumably they realized there was no way of making it fit.

But something is still wrong. The picture looks like a musical -- the screen is full of flailing limbs and tapping feet -- but it doesn't feel like one. For all his Broadway experience (perhaps even because of it), Marshall seems as afraid of the genre as everybody else is supposed to be.

A review in Torontos Eye Weekly magazine has congratulated him on continually switching from the numbers to the plot "because theres nothing more tedious on film than four straight minutes of singing and dancing." Well, it's an opinion. I doubt if it would be shared by many people who've seen Astaire and Rogers, or Gene Kelly and Donald OConnor (separately or together) or, come to that, Joel Grey and Liza Minnelli in Cabaret, and it seems a strange position from which to judge musicals.

Its also a strange position from which to direct musicals, but it seems to have been Marshall's. He cuts so relentlessly and frenetically that we never actually get to see the performers perform. That negates the best reason for going to see musicals; it certainly negates what this show, in either of its incarnations, was about in the theatre.

Or maybe it's his stars whom Marshall distrusts. A couple of supporting players (Queen Latifah, John C. Reilly) get a chance to stretch out, and take it. But Catherine Zeta-Jones Velma is a blank with a slinky body attached; lacking the magnetic desperation with which both Rivera and Neuwirth flooded the role, she just seems unlikeable.

She has, admittedly, lost two of her best songs. Renee Zellweger has more to work with, and she makes a fine, fluffy Roxie, whose schemes seem to pop out of her head like speech balloons. Shes at her best in the trial which, significantly, is where the movie most departs from and expands on the show, and in which all she has to do is act.

Marshall has abandoned Fosse's louche choreographic trademarks (there are, as one reviewer has pointed out, no derbies) in favour of a more generic licentiousness, but in some ways he comes closer to the stage original than the revival does. The old relentlessness is back. This, as I may have hinted, is not necessarily a good thing. It draws attention to the show's main flaw: It keeps saying the same thing, over and over, in song after song.

So we're all either corrupt or gullible, except for those of us who are both, and every one of us harbours the jealous ambition to Be a Performer. Guilty. And what else is new?
