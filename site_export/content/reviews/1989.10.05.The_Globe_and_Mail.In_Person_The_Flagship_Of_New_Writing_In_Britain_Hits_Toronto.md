IN PERSON 'The flagship of new writing in Britain' hits Toronto

Thursday, October 05, 1989

ROBERT CUSHMAN

BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail TONIGHT, THE Canadian Stage Company presents the English Stage Company. It all happens at the Bluma Appel Theatre in the St. Lawrence Centre; Toronto theatre has become very confusing.

As for the English Stage Company, nobody in Canada has ever heard of it. Thats forgivable; neither has anybody in England. But if mention were made of the Royal Court Theatre, where the ESC has hung out since its inception in 1956, then there would be recognition. For some reason the name of the company has never taken, but the theatre always has.

Max Stafford-Clark is not shy about his theatres importance. "We are the flagship of new writing in Britain," he says. I call it his theatre, since he has been its director since 1979. He also directs the two linked plays that the Court is presenting in Toronto. One is a Restoration comedy, George Farquhar's The Recruiting Officer. The other is a new play, Our Country's Good, by Timberlake Wertenbaker, which upheld the best Court traditions by winning all the major awards for best new British play of 1978.

The new play is actually about the old one. Based on The Playmaker, a novel by the Australian Thomas Kenneally, Our Countrys Good deals with the first play ever to be presented in Australia, which just happens to have been The Recruiting Officer. It was acted in 1789 by a group of transported convicts. It was undertaken as a form of prison therapy, and some of the tension in Our Countrys Good comes from the conflict between those officials who foster and approve the experiment, and those who regard it as the dangerous mollycoddling of proven criminals.

The sympathies of Wertenbaker, Stafford-Clark and company are hardly in doubt and the play ends as an affirmation of, in the director's words, the power and enduring worth of theatre. The Recruiting Officer is a good play to be affirmative about. Stafford- Clark's synoptic view of English drama is "if Shakespeare takes the gold medal, Farquhar is well in there for the silver." This may sound extravagant; Farquhar died at 29, having written a handful of comedies. But they are among the most likeable and actable in the language, and his maturity might have been staggering.

He does have the Shakespearean quality of humanity; he paints a broader social canvas than his contemporaries, he is concerned with sex as more than boudoir-hopping, and his dialogue can make you laugh even in reading. Actors seem to love playing him. Stafford-Clark has become not merely his fan but, in a strange way, his friend. During rehearsals he kept a journal in the form of an imaginary correspondence with his deceased author, which has been published under the title Letters to George.

Its a great play, George, writes a relieved director after the first run-through.

Affirmation is needed at the Royal Court these days. "In the last few years," Stafford-Clark says, weve dropped from seventh to sixteenth place in the national subsidy league. This is alarming for what is generally regarded as Britain's third national theatre, the contemporary counterpart to the classic orientation of the National Theatre itself and the Royal Shakespeare Company. The Court has been caught in the crossfire between local and national subsidy, each refusing to give more money until the other makes a move.

The Court used to present up to a dozen productions a year; now it's down to four. Is there a danger of its closing altogether? Stafford-Clark says no, though he admits that Napoleon would have said as much about the likelihood of a retreat from Moscow.

Thirty-three years ago, two months after its inception, the Court presented Look Back in Anger, and as far as popular mythology is concerned its still John Osbornes theatre. It has been patron and protector to a long line of subsequent writers: Arnold Wesker, John Arden, David Storey, Edward Bond, Christopher Hampton, Caryl Churchill. Not all of them still write for the Court, and its record is marked by bitter feuding as well as by fruitful collaboration.

Stafford-Clark, who is in his late 40s and has been in the driver's seat for 10 years, is still regarded by some of the old guard as an intrusive juvenile. Educated in Dublin, he made his reputation in the sixties as director of an experimental theatre in Edinburgh. Many of the plays of the seventies and eighties that I most enjoyed were Stafford- Clark productions: Howard Brenton's Magnificence, Snoo Wilsons The Glad Hand and, the most widely known and exposed, Caryl Churchills Top Girls. He also forged a valuable exchange link with Joseph Papp's Public Theatre in New York.

He is unusual in having made a front-rank reputation purely as a director of new plays. The Recruiting Officer was a new experience for him, the first time in 10 years he had worked without a living playwright at his elbow. (Hence, in compensation, the charming and informative Letters to George.) But doing occasional classics is a Court tradition, inspired by "the importance of measuring the standards of new writing against the past," (his phrase) and by box-office necessity.

In this case the economic imperative was twofold; doing The Recruiting Officer in tandem with Our Country's Good enabled him to engage a comparatively large cast for both. We have 10 actors. Its a knife edge. You couldnt do The Recruiting Officer with 9, and we couldn't afford 11. Even so, there is a bit of doubling, some of it across the gender line. You might say that Our Country's Good was created in workshop except that the director has come to distrust the term. "Its become a new verb - to workshop - being a way in which limping plays are eased into existence."

Joint Stocks best work transcended that, and so does Our Country's Good. It's the accredited work of a rising playwright. Wertenbaker, incidentally, is of Basque-Canadian extraction; and the plays designer, Peter Hartwell, rated by Stafford-Clark as brilliant but too self-effacing to be fashionable, is wholly Canadian.

Research is a directors prerogative as well as an author's. Stafford- Clark's imaginative involvement with Farquhar has led him to research eighteenth-century theatre, and to realize that the classic comedies were written for a theatre at least as intimate as his own. When the Theatre Royal, Drury Lane, was rebuilt it became a forcing-house for spectacle, and dramatic writing went into a long decline. We're still suffering, he says, from the building complex that has made the major new theatres in London and elsewhere into arenas that can be filled only by star turns, whether from actors or designers.

The Royal Court is a 400-seater inhabiting a part of Chelsea in southwest London that was fashionable in the days when London supposedly swung, but is off the beaten show-biz track these days. The productions will have to be blown up a bit for St. Lawrence Centre. But if they live up to the promise inherent in their authors, director and cast, they should be treats.
