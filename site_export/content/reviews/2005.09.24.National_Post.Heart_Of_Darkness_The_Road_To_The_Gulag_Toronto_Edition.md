Robert Cushman is the National Post's theatre critic.

THE PEOPLE'S ACT OF LOVE

By James Meek

Harper Collins

391 pp., $34.95

- - -

Most English readers confronted by Russian novels have a love- hate relationship with the characters' names, so evocative and so confusing. Them and their patronymics. James Meek's The People's Act of Love is an English novelist's brilliant reduction and provocative updating of the world of the Russian classics, and it begins with an implied joke on the subject. At the age of 12, Kyrill Ivanovich Samarin, a natural rebel, asks if he can take his uncle's name rather than his father's and become Kyrill Pavlovich instead. He's told this isn't allowed. So he asks to be called just Samarin, and so he is, right through the book.

The same first sentence, packed but not forced, also mentions dynamite. Flash forward nine years and the Russian Revolution has begun. We meet Samarin again, a fugitive in Siberia, claiming to have escaped from the White Garden, a Tsarist prison camp in the Arctic, along with a fearsome criminal known as the Mohican, who chose Samarin as a companion so that, when their food ran out, he would have someone to eat.

The publisher's blurb speaks of "a story of communism and cannibalism," and while the novel may not exactly equate the two, it does present them both as, shall we say, extreme tendencies. Not that they are the only ones on view. At the time of the action, the Red victory is assured but not yet secured. The Whites are still fighting and the town of Yazyk, where most of the story happens, is controlled, along with the neighbouring railroad, by a brigade of Czechs who came to fight alongside the Russians in the First World War and now cannot wait to get back to their own country, which has only just come into existence.

All that keeps them in Yazyk is their commander, Captain Matula, who is both crazy and malevolent, and who cannot bear to give up his dominion over men, territory and -- his special passion -- horses. "It's pleasant to be king of Siberia, sir," says his second-in- command, Lieutenant Mutz, "no-one's surprised you don't want to go back to being a sales representative for a light-bulb manufacturer in southern Bohemia." Some English reviewers have compared Matula to Kurtz in Heart of Darkness, but Matula is actually the more convincing creation. In his case we know what "the horror" is.

Yazyk is principally inhabited by a cult whose members have castrated themselves (or, in the case of the women, hacked off their breasts) to be closer to God, and who refer to themselves as "angels." They, like the Czech Legion, actually existed, and Meek brings them superbly to life in all their pettiness and all their fervour.

The saner people in town include the majority of the Czechs themselves, and Anna Petrovna, generally known as "the widow," a self-trained photographer with a son who has moved here for personal reasons and is, or has been, an object of desire for at least three of the surrounding men. "Anna Petrovna," muses Samarin on first hearing of her from the chief of the castrati, "that's the kind of name that allows you to imagine any kind of woman." In fact she's every Russian heroine, a warm-blooded Tolstoyan woman who has strayed into a nightmare Dostoevskian landscape. Humanly fallible, humanly angry, she is one of the book's two identifiable points of reference.

The other is Mutz, a dogged and conscientious secular Jew whose ambitions are to win Anna, to get rid of Matula and to lead his men safely home. He can't win 'em all, but he does a very respectable job against crazy odds. Not all this novel's antecedents are European: It has a definite whiff of Catch-22, and not just in the military scenes.

The cast is completed by the approaching Reds, already split between decency and the rulebook, and a shaman from the forest who dies of drink, maliciously supplied by someone unknown. The book is in part a murder mystery, and one whose neatness takes a second reading to appreciate fully; it's also full of surprises that actually surprise.

Meek, as well as being a novelist, is a prize-winning foreign correspondent for The Guardian, and he plays the reporter-as- historian with glittering precision. One section is a roll call of the Czechs who have gone missing, and it's done with gaunt unsparing wit. His prose reflects the harsh, dazzling white light of the tundra-and-taiga setting.

If much of the action is horrific, the tone is humane. "The people's act of love" is Samarin's phrase for revolution; the castrates of Yazyk might describe their own self-mutilation in the same terms. In the end, Samarin comes to them, begging them to take the knife to him, to destroy the human impulse that, twice in his life, betrays him. We judge these people by their failure to live up to their own terrifying ideals; even Matula has a grotesque moment of generosity.

For Samarin, who preaches what would later be called "perpetual revolution," the Reds are only temporary allies on the road to a gulag of which the Tsarist White Garden is a mere prophecy. And in fact his dream is shared by the last local remnant of the White regime: "It doesn't matter who rules, whether it's a prince of the blood or a Marxist, as long as they're Russian, and as long as the peasants fear them ... fear should shine on them like light."

The book is a grim study of fanaticism and an act of faith in its limits. It is also funny; the author's command of irony is pitch- perfect, but he can also be more tenderly amusing; the Czech legionaires, beneficiaries of both styles, are a gallery of deft sketches, and the last pages make you feel glad for the survivors among them, the humane pragmatists. A book whose author likes to play with names closes with the feeling that the Meek might inherit the Earth after all.
