It's a shock to hear a sentence of death by hanging passed, as recently as 1959, in a Canadian court, on a 14-year old boy: not just to hear about it, but to hear it. The boy of course was Steven Truscott, convicted of the rape and murder of his classmate Lynne Harper. His trial and sentencing are the first-act climax of Innocence Lost, a semi-documentary play by Beverley Cooper, first staged at the Blyth Festival, not far from Clinton, Ont., where the events took place. It's been given a new, joint production by the National Arts Centre in Ottawa, where it's now playing, and the Centaur Theatre of Montreal.

Truscott himself is a vivid presence in the play, but the real focus is on the small town itself, and on the military base at its edge: the two communities existing in a state of mutual distrust and suspicion. Most of the characters are the actual individuals involved, and some of the words are their own, but the most prominent figure is an invention, or at least a composite; she's Sarah, another classmate, who acts as narrator, and who goes on what seems to be a representative journey. At first, she thinks it impossible that Truscott should not be innocent; after the trial, she's as obstinately convinced that he's guilty. In the second half, with the sentence commuted to life imprisonment and with evidence mounting that there's been a miscarriage of justice, she's gradually brought to question her own certainties.

Sarah is well played by Jenny Young, but she isn't too satisfying as a device; or rather, she's unsatisfying because she so obviously is a device; she's acted upon rather than acting, and her final epiphany plays like a hopeful but hasty contrivance. The play's strength is in its evocation of an apparently amiable small town, traumatized into the hunt for a scapegoat. It's like seeing Our Town turn into The Laramie Project, with maybe a dash of Parade. The title is double-edged; in denying the presumption of Truscott's innocence, the town sacrifices its own.

All this comes out clearly and absorbingly in the first act. In the second, as the post-trial investigations come into play, along with the entrenched resistance to same, things get trickier. There's a lot of narrative ground to cover, some of it retrospective; it becomes hard to remember just who said what at the trial and why. Some of this is compounded in Roy Surette's generally lucid production by the amount of rapid doubling that's required; it's not always easy to work out who everybody is.

There's still powerful stuff in the later scenes, notably in a confrontation between the dead girl's angry, stricken father (Allan Morgan) and Isabel LeBourdais, the writer who's infuriating him by trying to clear Truscott's name. Her campaign fuels much of the second act; it's conducted here, with both reason and passion, by Fiona Reid, who also adds a touch of visual wit that I suspect, as there's nothing else like it in the show, of being her own invention.

Truscott himself is, for obvious reasons, largely absent from the second half, but Trevor Barrette's very believable and wholly sympathetic performance makes one gladder than ever that his case at least led to the abolition of capital punishment.

Clare Coulter is giving fragments of one of the best King Lears I have seen, certainly one of the most moving. It's fragmentary because Philip McKee's Lear, a Canadian contribution to Worldstage, cuts the original play down to 80 minutes, eliminating everybody except the king and his three daughters, though the Fool does appear as a ventriloquist's dummy, sitting in his master's lap and voicing (we assume) his deepest misgivings.

At first Coulter seems rather a mischievous Lear, peering or maybe glowering at the audience through a set of red curtains. These drawn aside, we embark on the abdication scene and note the uncanny conversational strength that Coulter, speaking very quietly, brings to the text.

Coulter plays it all sublimely, each detail freshly discovered. She's playing Lear as a man, but gender here hardly matters; she's simply old age, in all its dignity and all its helplessness.

In her one-woman show Dancing with Rage, Mary Walsh does some reversing of the stage-auditorium relationship, entering down the aisle, apologizing for being late and worrying that her Mirvish producers might have taken advantage of her tardiness by turning the theatre into a condominium.

This monologue, genuinely and un-forcedly hilarious, turns out to be the highlight of the evening, which otherwise switches confusingly back and forth between two stories, in one of which she recites a fairy-tale for today, while in the other she plays Marg De-lahunty, Warrior Princess, on the hunt for a lost child whom she fears may have grown up to be Stephen Harper.

There's also some standard stand-up stuff about clothing choices for older women. Walsh's script has some good jokes, but not enough, and the production (by herself and Andy Jones) is overly reliant on video footage that's both mucky and jerky.

It's risky for a comedian to become an institution, especially when much of her show is set in one. Walsh also does a bit of ventriloquizing, less effectively than Coulter. It might be fun though, some night, to have the two of them reverse roles. After all, Lear dances with rage, if man (or woman) ever has.

Innocence Lost runs until March 16 at the NAC Theatre in Ottawa; Lear runs until March 10 at the Harbourfront Centre in Toronto; Dancing with Rage runs until March 24 at the Panasonic Theatre in Toronto.
