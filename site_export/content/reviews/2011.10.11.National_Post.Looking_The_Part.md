THEATRE REVIEW

The Ugly One

Tarragon Theatre, Toronto

Sex, Religion & Other Hang-Ups

Theatre Passe Muraille, Toronto

There's an old comedy routine in which a man who has lost his memory is returned by the authorities to the bosom of his family. "Is it really true," his wife asks, on her way up to bed, "that it's as if you'd never met me before?" He confirms that it is. "Hmm," she says - it's all in the timing - "don't be too long, will you dear?"

I thought of this while watching The Ugly One, whose protagonist undergoes radical facial reconstruction, with results that have a similar aphrodisiac effect, not just on his wife but on every woman he meets. Men go for him, too; either they want him or they want to be him.

Our hero's name is Lette, and he's probably German; anyway he's the creation of a German playwright, Marius von Mayenburg. Lette is a well-regarded inventor who's shocked when his boss informs him that the trade show presentation of his latest brilliant creation is to be entrusted to a smarmy young subordinate. Lette himself is apparently too villainously ugly to be allowed out in public; his spouse confirms that this is indeed the case, and that she's only been able to love him by steadfastly refraining from looking him in the eye.

Lette takes himself off to a plastic surgeon, who regards him as the biggest challenge he's ever had but manages to turn him into something transcendently beautiful. Lette's life changes. He becomes first choice not just for his own presentation, but for everybody else's. Women throw themselves at him, notably a widowed CEO who is 73 but, thanks to repeated procedures of her own, doesn't look it. Unhappily for Lette, the surgeon realizes that he can perform the same operation on literally anybody else, with identical results. The world is soon full of Lette look-alikes; and the original's stock falls as rapidly as it had risen. It doesn't help that, in his new-found arrogance, he's been pricing himself out of an increasingly crowded market.

The play keeps up the same tone of deadpan extravagance all the way through, but in other respects it's uneven. Its premise is, even for a dramatic fable, unbelievable. I hate to be so literal, but if Lette really is, as everybody else keeps insisting, the worst-favoured thing since the Elephant Man, wouldn't somebody have told him already? I mean, people aren't that nice. Structurally, the evening begins with a bang. It then gets to feeling obvious, telling us over and over that we live in a beauty-obsessed society, as if this were news. Later, as the multiplying paradoxes of Lette's situation affect everyone around him, it becomes really interesting and inventive. Finally, it overplays its hand, its last development being too clever for its own good, or at least for mine. The pluses, all told, beat out the minuses.

Ashlie Corcoran's production, for Theatre Smash, is brisk and sharp; much of it, like When the Rain Stops Falling but to less poetic effect, is performed on top of a table and in a confined space.

Lette is played by David Jansen, who is neither the handsomest man in the world nor the ugliest but manages, without benefit of makeup, to convey how it feels to be both; from the audience's perspective the character's degree of likeability varies in inverse proportion to his imagined looks, and Jansen grades this process acutely.

Three other actors divide all the other roles between them; and, in another example of too-cleverness, the characters portrayed by each one have the same name. Thus, the wife, the boss lady and the surgeon's nurse are all called Fanny; maybe it's this uniformity that causes Naomi Wright to make each of them aggressively unpleasant. Hardee T. Lineham doubles surgeon and industrialist with sabre-toothed attack but also with excessive jerkiness, even given that both of them are jerks. Jesse Aaron Dwyre completes the company as Lette's upstart rival and rich Fanny's mother-loving and mother-smothered son.

I was reminded of Eugene Ionesco's Rhinoceros, which satirized conformity by having everyone turn into rhinos. Here everyone opts to become very lovely instead of very ugly, but the message is the same. At one hour and five minutes, though, von Mayenburg delivers it more economically.

Sex, Religion & Other Hang-Ups, a one-man play - really an extended stand-up comedy act - by James Gangl, is also about a guy becoming more attractive. Avowedly autobiographical, it tells of how the gangling Gangl finally got his break both as an actor and as a lover.

The professional breakthrough came when he got the part of Buddy No. 1 in a Coors Light commercial. The personal came when, at a comparatively advanced age, he lost his virginity - or, as he puts it, neatly tying the two themes together, got his union card - after a doomed romance with the girl of his dreams, who'd appeared in the same commercial as Bunny No. 3. His failure to score with her he puts down to a Catholic upbringing taken too earnestly to heart.

Some of this is very funny, most of it is charming, and nearly all of it feels truthful. It proves once again that the great thing about sexual confidence is that it does wonders for your sexual confidence.
