Eleanor of Aquitaine, wife of Henry II of England and mother of Richard the Lion-Heart and that unhappiest of royal villains, King John, had a busy life. She has also had, posthumously, a busy stage career. She has played juicy supporting roles in scripts by William Shakespeare, Jean Anouilh, Christopher Fry and even Alfred, Lord Tennyson. In the 1960s she attained to Broadway and subsequently Hollywood stardom, opposite her husband, in The Lion in Winter. (Henry, of course, was the lion.) She has now received the ultimate accolade: her own one-woman show.

Eleanor of Aquitaine: Mother of the Pride, written by Catherine Muschamp, gives us the lady on what we assume is her deathbed having what we assume is her last dream. She imagines herself defending her life and works before a tribunal composed of her deceased opponents, of whom there were many, Eleanor being a combative personality who lived into her 80s, by 12th-century standards an astonishingly ripe age. The charge on which she is arraigned is one of heresy. As far as I know, no ecclesiastical court of Eleanor's own time ever accused her of such a thing, but in this play the word has a meaning that's vaguer in one sense and more specific in another. Eleanor is on trial for being ahead of her time, for being a Strong Woman.

So she was, but she wasn't unique in that, even in her own time. Her mother-in-law, Matilda (familiar to readers of 1066 and All That as Matilda-or-Maud), was a great rattler of kingdoms. Eleanor, though, had a bigger kingdom to rattle than most. She was in fact married twice. Her first husband was Louis VII of France. They divorced after failing to produce an heir, something that was generally taken to be Louis's fault. (I have brushed up on the period in Simon Schama's marvellously racy History of Britain.) She then married Henry, an alliance that gave them dominion over much of France and most of Britain. Of the children she bore him, eight survived at least some way into adulthood, a phenomenal figure for the time. Eleanor was also responsible for introducing the French cult of courtly love into England, a bit like Jackie Kennedy bringing culture to Camelot. Henry and Eleanor were a great power couple, but they grew apart, politically and sexually, Eleanor spending many of the later years of Henry's reign locked up in a castle.

Here she gets to state her case, with no one to answer her. This is a miserable way of doing business dramatically, and it short- changes history as well. As often happens in this kind of piece, we feel not that a conflict of people and issues is being explored but that a writer is championing a character through a feeling that she - - it usually seems to be a she -- has been neglected by everyone else. Frankly, I don't believe that Eleanor was prophetically championing the cause of women; I think she was championing the cause of Eleanor. Good for her, and maybe good theatre, too, but not when her principal antagonist is presented only as a buffoon who used to be good in bed but has aged into a tyrant who takes mistresses, one of whom Eleanor arranged to have killed. (The Middle Ages may well have thought of this as murder. They wouldn't have called it heresy.)

Many of Eleanor's previous theatrical appearances have been on the fringes of plays about Henry II and Thomas Becket, and we get to hear something about their quarrels here, probably because they're too famous to leave out. We learn little of what part Eleanor may have played in them, and nothing about their importance to the separation of church and state. Nor would you guess that Henry virtually founded English common law, which has influenced the world. Harping on their domestic troubles, Eleanor comes off as a whiner.

She also seems to be a liar. She accuses Henry of murdering two of their sons, whom she had encouraged to rebel against him. (These were not nice people, despite the efforts of The Lion in Winter to pass them off as endearing squabblers in a medieval sitcom.) She says that one of them, Geoffrey, was killed by the forcible insertion of a poker "in his orifice."

This is the identical nasty end that awaited a later king, Edward II; for graphic details, see the current production of Marlowe's play at Stratford. But the historical Geoffrey died, without foul play, in a tournament. Why has Muschamp borrowed somebody else's horror show? Is she setting Eleanor up as an Unreliable Narrator? That would be a useless exercise, since the play gives us nothing against which to measure her. Or has the author decided that that is just the kind of thing Henry would have done, and consequently that he must have done it? Muschamp, a feisty octogenarian in her own right (raised in Canada, incidentally, though now living in France), seems to have identified with her heroine altogether too closely.

Eleanor has a heart, not to mention a poetic streak. She says that when a boy was flayed in her presence, her tears fell "like mineral water poised on stalactite." Here, as at many points in the show, I wondered whether the play was written in blank verse or whether, like much half-baked historical drama, it just fell into the rhythms. Visual interest in Jean LeClerc's production is confined to the red heraldic cloak from which Eleanor unwraps herself at the very beginning of the evening and in which she re- swathes herself at the end. The actress, Chapelle Jaffe, seems confident enough but delivers most of the text on one note, a peremptory staccato bark.

In Richard Rose's production of The Comedy of Errors, the Shakespeare play with a double dose of twins, Ephesus is a town composed of transparent cages. In the first scene, the refugee merchant Aegeon is confined in one of them while he tells his long tale of woe. It seems more monotonously woeful here than is strictly necessary, while the Duke who judges him is a cardboard tyrant. The cages are rearranged to form adjacent chambers, from one of which the jealous wife, Adriana, harangues her patient sister, Luciana, who is doing aerobics in the other. For street scenes, the frames that define the cages are realigned into an urban maze through which the Ephesian natives obliviously rush, each one minding his own business at a furious rate.

It all seems very inhuman, as much in itself as in the society it depicts, and not much fun at all. But as the show progresses it develops personality, humour and even warmth. The frames are manipulated with an agility and inventiveness that matches the constant reshaping of the furnishings in Rose's production at Stratford of The Brothers Karamazov.

The identical Antipholus brothers -- one a resident, one a tourist -- suffer identical identity crises and carry identical briefcases, which they use as offensive weapons. Master-servant relationships are defined here by the varying ways in which beatings are given and received. The serving Dromios (Kyle Horton and, with a slight edge, Michael Rubenfeld) take their punishment with grimaces and jokes, each in an individual rhythm. The Antipholi (Brandon McGibbon and, with a definite edge, Brendan Murray) are respectively piqued and outraged. The highest-definition performance is Philippa Domville's pregnant Adriana, whom the pressures of farce bring ever closer to premature delivery; she gives a new meaning to the term "hysterical pregnancy."

The last scene is a joy, Victor Ertmannis's condemned Aegeon entering into the spirit of things by laying his own head upon the executioner's block, then wondering why nobody notices. There's a lovely surprise ending that, even with only one performance to go, I shouldn't give away. Let me just say that it's the perfect way to end a play about twins.
