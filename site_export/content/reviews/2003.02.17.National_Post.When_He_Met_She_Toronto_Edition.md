THIS COULD BE LOVE

Poor Alex Theatre, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - There are a fair number of reasons why This Could Be Love could be terrible, which makes the fact that it's good all the more remarkable. Its a two-character musical, which is not a sub- genre with a very inspiring history, and the couple in question are identified to us only as He and She, which seems like economy gone insane. When we meet them they are at adjacent tables in some kind of bar (the sets are economical, too), each in the same position, seated but stood up.

Now, its probable that in real life both would feel too depressed and humiliated by their non-appearing dates to start making conversation, which is what these two do. If we grant that they might, though, it's quite possible they would, in a mood of defiant desperation, go off on an impromptu date themselves and that the night would find them in bed together, which sequence of events is what happens here.

What really challenges belief is that they should immediately decide to get married, even driving a considerable distance the very next morning to find a Cree chief empowered to officiate at weddings. (You never know when arcane bits of Canadiana may come in handy.)

The reasons they give for this behaviour may be even harder to get your head around. He and She, having both been burned by the traditional courtship rituals, jointly resolve to bypass all that. Theirs is a whirlwind anti-romance. They draw up agreements as to the conduct and goals of their relationship which He, the more anal- retentive of the two, insists on having written down in list form. (His hobby, the key to his character, is memorizing the names of capital cities that he has no intention of visiting.)

Of course they -- or should I say They? -- really are in love, and We -- I mean we -- settle in for an evening of waiting for them to acknowledge it, while they go through all the regulation hoops that tradition decrees for the occupants of two-handed plays about relationships, with or without music. I don't think Im giving away much by revealing that jealousies are caused by one partners previous experiences and the other's lack of them. Nor should it come as a great surprise that the pair split up in the course of the action and reunite at its end. We'd feel cheated if they didnt.

So we have a predictable story trying to pass itself off as unconventional; a double recipe for disaster. And yet the writing and performance make it work.

The author -- of book, music and lyrics -- is Brock Simpson, a charter member of the amoebic group of musical-comedy practitioners responsible, in various combinations, for two of the most delightful shows in recent memory: Honest Ed -- The Bargain Musical and The Drowsy Chaperone. This bunch have now formed their own company, Harry Gadsby Theatricals Inc. There is no such person as Harry Gadsby, and This Could Be Love is his first production.

The first thing you notice about Simpsons lyrics is that he knows what a rhyme is. This is quite rare. Russell Smith, a generally smart writer, had a recent column in The Globe and Mail lamenting such current deformations of the English language as the apparently incurable confusion between its and it's, and spoiled it by saying at one point that canine rhymed with mainline. It doesn't. Train-line rhymes with mainline, and if you cant hear the difference, theres nothing I can do for you. Pairing canine with mainline is not an indictable offence, but it could never give the same aural satisfaction as Lorenz Hart's mating of Yonkers with conquers.

This Could Be Love's first number, a mutual-attraction song thats invigoratingly breezy in words and music, is pure Rodgers and Hart (and the shows title may well be a homage to R&H's This Can't Be Love). Its second-act opener is a traditional softshoe duet of the kind Broadway audiences are currently lapping up as the eleven oclock number in Hairspray, though Simpsons is better written. You do wonder, though, what place this kind of pastiche has in a supposedly contemporary show, or whether a man who comes up with a couplet in which love is compared to a prick from an amorous hatpin, following it with "and this could be that pin," could ever sound at home in the 21st century.

The rest of the score proves that he can; that he can fit his old- fangled verbal dexterity into a varied succession of modern rhythms. This is something that neither of the previous Gadsby-group shows has tried. The heroine has a number called I Want (I think -- there are sadly no song-titles in the program) that sounds free-form but is in fact very tightly controlled, and it legitimately brings down the house. It's cleverness turned into joy.

Beside his songwriting talents, Simpson writes nippy dialogue and has a lively, if uncomplicated, sense of character. He has also given his characters interesting jobs. She is a department store's house detective, with the difference that her brief is to spy not on the customers but on her fellow employees; this breeds self-hatred, that the show explores, somewhat, but somehow cannot link to the main action. He has a career that is far more musically amenable; he writes advertising jingles, and we actually see him at it and believe what we see.

(Compare Theyre Playing Our Song, another two-character musical that was ostensibly about a couple of songwriters, but that could actually have been about anyone. This one is far more specific, and therefore far superior. Also, it doesnt have lyrics by Carole Bayer Sager, who, as somebody once said, probably thought that her names rhymed.)

It is predictable, but not stultifyingly so, that He, faced with writer's block, will make professional use of a tune that was supposed to be the couple's own private theme, thereby causing serious grief to She. (I curse Simpson for forcing me into these grammatical contortions. Why couldnt he give them names? What does he think he has here? Archetypes?) It looks like The End, but of course it isnt. The end, which is also a beginning, occurs in Helsinki (capital of Finland), where He finds personal and musical liberation -- "there under an alias/I'll be humming Sibelius" -- and where He and She solemnly commit themselves to not being committed, in a song that sounds like Sondheim's Being Alive turned inside out.

John Mitchells production is brisk, though spartan to the point of grubbiness. I imagine he would like to be able to afford more furniture, just as Simpson would probably appreciate more spacious musical arrangements than his own piano accompaniment can accommodate; his tunes deserve them.

Most of the costume budget seems to have gone on Krista Sutton (She), who is allowed an early release from the fluffy pink number that she wears to be stood up in. Jonathan Wilson (He) goes through nearly the whole evening in the same yellow bow tie with polka dots - - other things, too, of course, but thats what you notice -- and when he sheds it, it's a life-altering decision.

Both players seem to have been hired for their comedic rather than musical talents; both have a tendency to sing flat, and Wilson in particular seems fazed by harmonies. (I sympathize, but he's the one whos up there.) Both, however, compensate with energy, likeability and acting skills. Wilsons rabbitty enthusiasm, as a man who has survived a hundred put-downs, is very appealing, and Sutton is a fair stormer. Neither of them yields an inch to sentimentality.

The show seems aware of its own limitations in style and scale, and capitalizes on them. It satirizes itself, without ever cancelling itself out, which is quite a trick.
