GLENN

du Maurier Theatre Centre, Toronto

Near the beginning of Glenn, the young Glenn Gould calls to his mother that he has just worked out the structure of the Goldberg Variations. There must have been a parallel moment for the playwright, David Young, when he called to someone -- conceivably his director, Richard Rose -- that he had worked it out, too, and was going to build his play around it.

The resulting piece has become a Canadian perennial; first seen at the du Maurier in 1992, it was revived at last years Stratford Festival, and it returns to Harbourfront by way of the Centaur Theatre in Montreal. All these productions have been directed by Rose, and of the four actors in the current company, three have appeared in previous stagings of the play, though not always in the roles they play here.

The play does not precisely have four characters; rather, the one enigmatic figure of Gould himself is split four ways. There is the Prodigy, the precocious boy pianist; there is the Performer, who, still young, enjoys tremendous success, plus some severe criticism, on the concert platforms of the world, and eventually finds it all either too much or too little; there is the Perfectionist, retreating to the technological monasticism of the recording studio; and there is the Puritan, Gould at the end of his life, surveying his earlier selves with a sardonic though not embittered eye.

A partial hook is that Gould recorded the Goldbergs twice in his life, less floridly the second time than the first, and his life can be seen as a process of paring down. The play borrows something of the musics fugal form, alternating and intercutting the four Glenns (fair enough: Gould was fanatical about the possibilities of tape editing), letting them spy and comment on one another, and even indulge in the occasional four-way confrontation.

It sounds stimulating, and that's how it seemed when I saw it at Stratford, which was my first time with the play; it was dense, and sometimes confusing, but I assumed a second visit would clear some of that up. This time, however, I found myself tuning out more often than before, and not always feeling greatly rewarded by the parts that did hold me.

Sometimes the multiple focus twitches enjoyably. Gould did, undeniably, watch himself living his life, both at the time and afterward. So it's amusing when one Glenn watches another Glenn take a taxi, which turns out to be driven by yet a third Glenn (conceivably in psychological disguise) whose steering wheel is actually a red vinyl LP and who wants to discuss the maestros theories about music, to the maestros own embarrassed disgust. This is funny; I was reminded of Alan Bennett's evocation of Lawrence of Arabia, who "wanted his complete indifference to public notice to be universally recognised." Lawrence, Bennett said, used to don flowing Arab robes complete with scimitar and hoped, in this uniform, to pass unremarked in Oxford Street; Gould, a passionate hypochondriac, seems to have entertained similar expectations regarding his familiar uniform of cap, overcoat and muffler, but when he wore it in Manhattan in June, people talked.

The sight of the Gould quartet bound together in an entanglement of tape is an apt and witty visual image. There are even allusive sidelong references to Gould's questionable tastes in popular singers (Barbra Streisand and Petula Clark). But often the play seems the victim of its own mechanism, especially when all four constituent parts are involved. You grow to dread the shout of canon! invariably followed by the demand from one player that the others join him in some kind of word-association game.

There are two Goulds in public mythology: the musician and the eccentric. The musician, as Young himself is too smart not to know, is largely irretrievable in dramatic form, and giving the play itself some kind of musical shape doesnt solve the problem. Of course there can be occasional lines about technique and interpretation, but no sustained discussion and not much illustration. (We do, naturally, get to hear recorded snatches. Their main effect is to make us wish that the actors would shut up and let us listen to Gould playing Bach. Or to anyone playing Bach.)

The eccentric, of course, is far more theatrically accessible, and the play delivers him in bulk; we hear a great deal about Goulds reclusiveness and misanthropy and pill-popping. The most obvious thing about Gould is that he was a tease, and the script has enough of a sense of humour (I'm not so sure about the production) to deliver on this. Most of what we learn, though, could equally well be delivered to us biographically or anecdotally. That kind of conventional dramaturgy would probably come off very banal, so one can see why the author would go for something more ambitious. But it doesn't seem very much of a return for all that structure. And hints that Gould was some uniquely Canadian kind of genius merely prompt the question, "So what?"

If the play seems more distant than it did at Stratford, that may be because the audience in this space confronts it head-on rather then enveloping it. The staging looks very similar to before, the actors threading their way through a multiplicty of high-tech equipment.

The acting is down a notch in intensity, though Duncan Ollerenshaw (who has been with the play since its first workshop) repeats his appealing tremulous-sensitive performance as the Performer, who moves from loving to hating every moment of it. Brandon McGibbon's Prodigy is rather too much of an ordinary kid, but John Koensgen does a good, gnarled job as the Perfectionist. R.H. Thomson doesn't sound the depths of fright and pain that Richard McMillan (much missed since he disappeared into the jaws of The Lion King) brought to the Puritan, but he makes an excellent master of ceremonies, both crabbed and relaxed.
