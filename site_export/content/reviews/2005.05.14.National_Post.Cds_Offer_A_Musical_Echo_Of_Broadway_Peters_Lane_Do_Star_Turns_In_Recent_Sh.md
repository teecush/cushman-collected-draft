I've been catching up with some CDs of Broadway musicals, most of which we have yet to see in Canada.

The big exception is Gypsy, just revived at the Shaw Festival. The album of the 2003 Broadway revival, starring Bernadette Peters (Angel -- that's the label, not the lady), confirms my belief that those who have criticized Nora McLellan's Niagara Rose for being too soft, vocally or temperamentally, are off-track. There are many ways of playing Rose. And not even a managing mother has to yell all the time to get her way. It can be done with charm.

Peters in fact has less voice, or anyway less belt, than McLellan. And yes, a Rose whose most fully realized number is a buoyant Together Wherever We Go, closely followed by a flirtatious Small World, may be said to be missing something.

But Peters is a superb actress (though I don't know of her ever doing a non-musical play) who works through text as well as music. And one advantage of the CD over the LP format is that it has more space for linking dialogue -- even, occasionally, for whole scenes.

The climax of any performance must be Rose's Turn, in which her vicarious fantasy of burlesque stardom gives way to breast-beating recrimination and then to triumphant and/or grotesque affirmation.

That's already quite a palette. But the defining moment -- the one that nails the interpretation -- comes just afterward. Rose's daughter, now famous as Gypsy Rose Lee, has overheard her and says "you really could have been something, Mama"; to which mama replies "If I could have been, I would have been, and that's show business." I don't know how Ethel Merman delivered that line, but I imagine she sounded matter-of-fact and not too bothered. Angela Lansbury, my favourite Rose (one of the half-dozen best performances I've seen of anything), made it accepting and therefore healing. Tyne Daly infused it, memorably, with bitterness; she wasn't reconciled to anything.

Peters offers a synthesis; she knows she should give up but it still deeply hurts. This is one torn, neurotic lady. And the lead- ins to Everything's Coming Up Roses and to Rose's Turn itself can never have sounded more terrifyingly driven -- which, riding over the rough bits, is pretty much how she does the songs themselves. What you get is a thin-skinned woman willing herself to be thick- skinned -- trying, if you like, to be Ethel Merman. Merman's own recording (now on Sony, including some bonus tracks) remains the benchmark, as original-cast albums nearly always do, not just for her performance but for everybody else's, orchestra included. But this is a worthy supplement.

- - -

I wonder if The Frogs (PS Classics) would qualify for the Shaw Festival. GBS is a character in it, but it doesn't take place in his lifetime. (I'll get back to that.)

"The time," says Nathan Lane, sounding perplexed, "is the present. The place is ancient Greece." Lane is playing the god Dionysus, in an adaptation of the Aristophanes comedy by Stephen Sondheim and the late Burt Shevelove. (A Funny Thing Happened on the Way to the Parthenon?) In the original, Dionysus, whose job description includes responsibility for the theatre, journeys to the underworld to bring back Aeschylus and/or Euripides to revitalize the drama, and also public discourse.

Here the candidates are Shakespeare and Shaw, which may not seem like much of a contest, though Shaw himself is both the title and the subject of one of the best songs, joyously sung by Lane. ("The mind with such velocity--/All right, there's the pomposity./But that's just Shaw.") Shakespeare gets a beautiful setting of Fear no more the heat o' th' sun, which must be the most notable of Sondheim's rare collaborations with another lyricist. Then, as in the Greek, there is the Frogs' chorus, delivered as Dionysus crosses the Styx. The amphibians here ("not your hoity-toity intellectuals/ not your hippie-dippy homosexuals") represent the forces of solid conformity, or perhaps liquid complacency, i.e. they stick in the mud. The satire may be somewhat generalized, but this is still a wonderful piece of choral writing, witty and relentless. If the show seems to run out of steam in its second half, that may be Aristophanes's fault; he wasn't hot on plot. (I am not quoting.)

The musical started out in 1974 in the Yale Swimming Pool -- good for frogs, bad for acoustics. It reached Broadway 30 years later, "even more freely adapted" by Lane himself. He is becoming omnipresent in musicals, and especially on recordings of musicals, which is fair enough as he must be one of the most musical comedians ever. (His hysteria was wrong for his namesake in Guys and Dolls, but maybe he was frustrated by having so few songs.) He's very good in the title role of Sherry! (Angel), a surprising recorded resuscitation of a 1967 flop, based on The Man Who Came to Dinner.

The play, in a revival of which Lane appeared a few seasons back, didn't exactly cry out to be musicalized, then or now, but given that the thing was done, it doesn't sound bad.

James Lipton, better known now as the obsequious host of Inside the Actors' Studio, wrote the lyrics, which are quite adroit. To his clout in getting this project on a major label, add that of the cast, which also includes Peters (rather wasted), Carol Burnett (doing a luscious cartoon of a predatory star), Tommy Tune and, though he barely sings, Mike Myers. His character is based on Harpo Marx; who can say whether he got the voice right?

Sweet Smell of Success (Sony) should have been a Toronto musical; it was one of Livent's lost projects. It reached New York in 2002, and was generally attacked. The CD makes it hard to see why. This adaptation of the classic '50s movie about Broadway columnists, press agents and other underworld figures relies too much on a gossips' chorus, but is otherwise loaded with atmosphere. Also with melody: Marvin Hamlisch comes up with ballads (I Cannot Hear the City and Rita's Tune, though the latter seems to have suffered damage since the Toronto workshop) that I wouldn't have believed he had in him; the moody '50s sound seems to have liberated him. (Though I've always suspected that his strictly '70s score for A Chorus Line would have got more respect if somebody else's name had been on it.) Craig Carnelia is maybe too gentle a lyricist for this milieu, but his work is still good.

John Lithgow, as the Burt Lancaster-Walter Winchell figure, took most of the knocks; they said he couldn't sing. Well, he doesn't have the voice -- or, more important, the flair -- for his 11 o'clock number, a mocking song-and-dancer titled Don't Look Now. But he sounds superb, deadly accurate, as the merciless potentate with the steel-trap column and the steel-trap mind. Listen to him ticking the day's items on or off; he's a dictator in a double sense. Again, it's about the acting.
