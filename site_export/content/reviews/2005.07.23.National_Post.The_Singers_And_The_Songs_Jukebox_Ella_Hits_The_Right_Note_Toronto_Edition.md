About 40 years ago Billy Wilder made an especially cynical movie titled Kiss Me, Stupid that everybody loathed but that I rather liked.

Its about a couple of small-town amateur songwriters (one of them Ray Walston) trying to sell some material to a visiting singing star, an alcoholic womanizer (played by Dean Martin and named, lest there should be any misunderstanding, Dino). The price of success for the writers is to put Walston's wife in Martin's bed, though they finally jib at this and substitute the local good-time girl (Kim Novak). The film wasnt really a musical, but it had of necessity a few songs, and it counts as the last movie to have an original score by George and Ira Gershwin.

George had been dead for a quarter of a century, and Ira was in retirement, but every so often he would come out of it to fit new words to his brothers unused melodies. In this instance he faced the challenge of writing songs that would sound as if they might be the work of neophytes without actually letting down the Gershwin family name. I'm not sure that he found the right balance, and at one point he seems -- gracefully -- to have given up altogether. There's a sequence in which a conscience-stricken Walston, probably dubbed, sings a heartfelt ballad written for his wife, and its a beauty, certainly in that context. Its called All the Livelong Day - - Ira had wanted to add and the Lovelong Night" but his prudishness overcame him -- and I left the theatre longing to hear it again.

I never got the chance. Nobody, it seemed, recorded it, then or in all the years and all the Gershwin tribute albums since. In fact, Ella Fitzgerald had made a single of two songs from the film, but they were never reissued. They are, however, available now, on a two- disc anthology of her '50s and '60s singles, all re-released, titled Jukebox Ella (Verve).

All the Livelong Day is not, in truth, quite as lovely as my memories of it, and I think the tempo is too fast. But its still worth hearing, with an especially tender last section, and Ella, of course, sings it beautifully. Its one of her soothing numbers. The pairing, I'm a Poached Egg (Without Toast), is great fun. It's meant to be a parody of the kind of losers list-song that Ira perfected in I Cant Get Started, but it's jaunty enough to pass as the real thing. It could easily have turned up as one of the obscurer numbers on Ella's epic Gershwin songbook with Nelson Riddle; indeed, both arrangements, though uncredited, have Riddle all over them.

The rest of the package shows that Fitzgerald could be as infectious and inventive with ephemera as with classics. None of the songs became hits, though some of the 50s ones (Too Young for the Blues or The Silent Treatment) are quite good. There are also a couple of wonderful oddities: versions in German -- recorded, perplexingly, in Denmark -- of Youre Driving Me Crazy and Mr. Paganini, which swing more, if anything, than they do in English. (They also, when Ella abandons words, give the bizarre sensation of a singer scatting in a foreign language.) A 1962 recording of Desafinado reminds us that Ella was one of the first North American singers -- maybe the first -- to latch on to the bossa nova. There's also something she wrote herself, in '64, called Ringo Beat, whose message, more or less, is "we used to swoon over our idols; now let the kids have theirs." Coming from a more calculating singer it would sound, then or now, like unconscionable pandering (and why choose to celebrate that particular Beatle?); from her, obviously meaning it and having fun with it, its sweet.

At about the time Ella recorded that, Mark Murphy was beginning to break through as the jazz singer of his generation, with a beautifully controlled voice and an unfettered imagination. Hes gone on ever since, and has sometimes seemed to be progressively -- a double-edged word -- losing touch with his material. He has made some very forced and ugly sounds, no pleasanter for the fact that he was obviously making them on purpose. But his new one, Bop for Miles (HighNote), actually recorded in 1990, shows him near his best. It was made live in Vienna, and seems to be a seamless suite of songs associated with Miles Davis, though some clumsy edits suggest that originally there were pauses. It works best, though, when one song plays directly off another; when a fast, ruminant (as in both thoughtful and cud-chewing) All Blues goes into a wild Summertime that refers to the melody just often enough to keep itself grounded, and thence, by seasonal association, into a super-speedy Autumn Leaves, which has little to do with the mood of the song (these leaves aren't drifting, they're driven), but works anyway. Murphy blurs the line between words and sounds, and sometimes between song and speech, and on the later tracks this gets wearing. Squeezing the songs out in an alternating assortment of grunts and falsettos, he sounds as if he might be in physical pain. But he ends in fine form, with a barely lyricized version of Milestones, one of his warhorses from way back. He is backed by a small, fierce band that, strangely for a Davis tribute, has no trumpeter. But perhaps Murphy himself is the trumpet.

A far quieter, but in her way equally spiky, performer is Patti Wicks, who sings and plays (very good) piano. Love Locked Out (MaxJazz), her first album on a more or less prominent label, won me over immediately with its first and title track, a fairly rare Ray Noble song with an ultra-rare verse. The tone of this is admonitory, which seems to suit her well; hers is a stark, guarded kind of voice that sends the lyrics at you in short, clipped phrases. She sounds oddly like the 50s cabaret performer Frances Faye, who played mostly for laughs. Wicks deals mainly in ballads, so she needs the right ones, songs with an edge. Cole Porter suits her perfectly; she sings After You, one of his more romantic avowals, but still with a presentiment of doom behind it. She also does well by Rodgers and Harts great It Never Entered My Mind, Cy Coleman's self-mocking Would You Believe and the vengeful An Empty Glass. (She can't match Peggy Lees fast, clipped version of this, but I doubt anyone could.) On a more sentimental song like Blue Gardenia, you long for something more liquid. Wicks can also be heard, in more informal form and working with an Italian trio, on Basic Feeling (Jazzmates).

Wesla Whitfield is a singer who combines fine technical equipment with a probing and highly intelligent approach to lyrics. On her latest, In My Life (HighNote), she finds new, unforced ways to phrase songs ranging from I Have Dreamed, done rhythmically for once, to Wrap Your Troubles in Dreams, which here sounds like hard- won wisdom. Her accompanist, Mike Greensill, is one of the best there is.

Freddy Cole is his own accompanist. Hes spent most of his career trying both to escape and exploit the shadow of his brother Nat. He sounds far more gravelly than King Cole ever did (though who knows how his voice might have aged, had it been given the chance?) and now sounds pretty much his own man. His new one, This Love of Mine (HighNote), is mellow rather than memorable, but it's pleasant and has fine musicians and interesting songs. The latter range from the fairly new, such as Bob Dorough's Just for Now, to the long- neglected Out in the Cold Again, on which Cole sounds sad, certainly, but still warm.
