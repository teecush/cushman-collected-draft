ZADIE'S SHOES

Winter Garden Theatre, Toronto

BELLE

Factory Theatre, Toronto

The Factory, which in the last few years has had the best record for new plays of any theatre in Toronto, has two of its greatest hits on view again.

Florence Gibson's Belle is back at the home base, while Adam Pettle's Zadie's Shoes is being given a commercial run by the Mirvishes.

Both these authors were virtually unknown when their plays were first produced and the flaws in the two works stand out more sharply the second time around. But Belle remains the biggest play born in this city in years and Zadie's Shoes one of the most thoughtfully funny.

Zadie's Shoes stems, as its author has testified in interviews, from two traumas in his own young life: a diagnosis of cancer and an addiction to gambling. Both, we gather, are now in remission.

In the play, these two conditions are divided between two characters. Benjamin, an aspiring writer and practicing waiter, is an apparently nice Jewish boy and a devotee of the track as were his father and grandfather (or "zadie") before him. The cancer-victim is his girlfriend Ruth, who, having exhausted -- and been exhausted by - - the resources of conventional medicine, is about to fly off to Mexico on a more radical gamble: her life on an alternative cure.

Benjamin snaffles the money she was saving for their tickets and blows it on a bet, an act that is, to quote Ruth in a different but related context, "beyond selfish." He spends the balance of the play trying both to reverse his luck and win back her respect. One task proves easier than the other.

His road leads him, for the first time in years, into synagogue, where he meets an old man who doubles as tipster and confessor, a role in which Paul Soles delivers a lovely shrug of a performance.

He has, as you would expect, the lion's share of the play's jokes. Some of these are glib ("Jesus Christ!" exclaims Benjamin; "Wrong prophet," snaps back his new friend) but the best are splendid.

Equally funny, in a wilder and shaggier manner, is Randy Hughson as a recovering gambler-junkie-alcoholic, pressed reluctantly into service as Benjamin's partner in crime. Nobody is better at the comedy of bedraggled exasperation and he is, as he was before, especially fine when the character emerges unexpectedly as a moral force.

Benjamin himself is a problem. He talks a lot and -- as played by Jordan Pettle, the author's brother -- the character tends to disappear behind the words. He is uncomfortably real when he tries, like any guilty man, to charm himself back into his love's good graces. He plays with a kind of feverish casualness that stops short of a genuine addict's drive, and the feeling remains that, for all their good intentions, author and actor are letting Benjamin off the hook too easily. (Words run away with them as well as with him.)

Kelli Fox is admirable as Ruth, a believably good woman willing herself into desperate cheerfulness and genuinely moving when forced into anger.

The weakest elements of the play were always Ruth's two sisters and so, despite some re-casting, they remain. Deborah Drakeford is wooden as Beth, a curling champion preparing for her most challenging day on the ice. Tara Rosling tries far too hard as Lily, the kind of feckless spiritual eclectic we would once have called a hippie, now about to exchange generalized Earth-motherhood for a more specific maternity.

They are both formula characters with, especially in Beth's case, formula conflicts. Lily fits neatly into the play's plot, and Beth - - enslaved, like Benjamin, to the superstitions of good fortune -- into its theme.

Adam Pettle has an impressive sense of structure; but it is one thing to devise a pattern and another to bring all its elements to life. His vision, too, may be darker than his skills. Jackie Maxwell's production, with its bleachers setting, has been blown up without damage.

The major achievements of Belle remain remarkable.

Florence Gibson has dramatized and made comprehensible, without any hint of the schoolroom, a juicy chunk of American history: the reconstruction period that followed the end of the Civil War and of slavery.

She has told it through four representative characters who move from the South to the North -- two men, two women; two black, two white -- without ever appearing schematic. We follow the parallel, and sometimes conflicting, agitation for black rights and women's rights, and watch people with individual passions aligning and betraying.

She has fashioned a rich lyrical style, by spirituals out of Tennessee Williams, that usually avoids self-indulgence. The characters don't usually relax on it; mostly they hurl it at one another. The exchanges, in Ken Gass's production, are very well orchestrated.

Belle and her husband Bowlyn come from Georgia to New York after eight years' rock-hard saving, he to drive a cab, she to scrub floors. He loses his horse and she his job, both losses unintentionally caused by Nance, who is another kind of southern belle, the bleached kind, reborn as a suffragette.

She enlists them in her cause and for a time they are united, till sundered by sex and politics. Nobody gets what he or she wants - - not even Lackey, small-time exploiter turned small-time functionary -- but all survive for future conflicts.

Yanna McIntosh's Belle remains a shining success, not least because she humanizes an ideal: a character who stands, by right of race and gender, at the epicentre of discrimination and whom everybody admires.

I see that I originally described the play as "devastatingly funny"; I don't quite see where I got that from, but Soo Garay's Nance is still a marvellously witty creation, as well as a sad one, what with her brave but tattered rhetoric and her casual reference to Bowlyn's future fleet of taxis as "your vehicular conveniences."

Nigel Shawn Williams as Bowlyn lacks their precision, but does all right. Alex Poch-Goldin's shifty Lackey (or, as he wincingly insists "LackLEY") attracts the sympathy that invariably accrues to someone whom everyone else on stage considers despicable.

There is a fifth character. As in Zadie's Shoes, the heroine's sister proves the weakest link. (No fault, I think, of the actress, Janice Robinson, who is new and an improvement.) She stays down in Georgia, where she can usually be reached by flashback. The play seems to regard her as a prophetess, but Bowlyn calls her "your crazy sister" and he has my sympathy.

It's in her that the imagery and its delivery are the thickest -- she sounds like she's singing even when she's talking -- and most of the time I failed to understand her.

She takes over the last section of the play, leaving me wondering whether the other characters' return home was metaphor or reality for life or a visit, victory or defeat.

A small point: when Lackey alludes to the Ku Klux Klan, is he just acknowledging their existence or planning to join them? Probably it's the latter; he's a born loser. But why is he planning to go fishing? Of course, it's a nice thing to do in the South.

Note that Belle is a double act of cultural appropriation -- a white author writing about partly black history, a Canadian writing about wholly American history -- and that is a doubly good thing.
