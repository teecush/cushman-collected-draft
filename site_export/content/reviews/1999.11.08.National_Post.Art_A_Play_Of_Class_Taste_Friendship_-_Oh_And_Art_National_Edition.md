ART

Royal Alexandra Theatre, Toronto

Maybe Art really is about art. As you may have heard, Yasmina Rezas play -- a hit in Paris, London and New York -- concerns Serge, a dermatologist who spends the French equivalent of $50,000 on a medium-sized all-white abstract painting. This arouses the violent scorn of his friend Marc, an engineer and self-proclaimed defender of traditional cultural values. There is a third party, Yvan, an insecure chap who used to work in textiles and is now in stationery and who likes to agree with both of the others, which on this occasion proves both agonizingly and hilariously difficult.

Everyone -- including, on a previous occasion, me -- has said that the painting is just an arbitrary pretext for exposing the latent tensions within this trio, and that any other would have done just as well. Now Im not so sure. Art, or taste in art (I don't just mean the visual arts), occupies a unique position in human discourse; it is something about which we are all supposed to feel passionately but on which we can also agree to differ. This creates a strain. Politics doesn't do it, or religion; they create insurmountable barriers or none at all. The same with personal relationships: Of the three men in this play, Serge has been married, Marc still is, and Yvan is about to be.

Eventually -- the arguments about the painting having damaged the amour-propre of each -- they get around to badmouthing one anothers past, present or future spouses. But they would never have begun that way; if they had, there would quickly have been no friendship to salvage. As things are they can climb down, blame their indiscretions on that damn picture, and carry on more or less as before.

Serge even offers to let Marc deface his precious, vulnerable canvas, and Marc takes him up on it. Serge has a hidden agenda here and he is going to have to keep it hidden, for life.

Friendship stands revealed as being very like art: something good and necessary, but neither as holy nor as honest as we might like to believe. Each may convey a truth; each is rooted in deception. Each involves self-interest; Yvan has a spot on his hand that he would like Serge to take a look at.

The painting itself is generic-modern: the kind of thing people have been attacking and defending for ages. Serge claims that he can discern white lines on the white background (a whiter shade of pale, perhaps); and maybe he can. Maybe the painting gives him pleasure, or maybe he bought it as an investment, or maybe he just wants to annoy the possessive Marc, who claims to be his mentor. (It could of course be all of the above.) If its the last, he certainly succeeds; Marc sees red.

He has some very good arguments, does Marc. Accused of being old- fashioned, he asks "what use is being representative of your era? and, even more pertinently, how can a man live in any time but his own?" In other words, anyone alive is as much part of the age as anyone else; it's good to hear the boot being put into those who claim special privilege for their own tastes and beliefs by calling them "contemporary (or, God help us, relevant"). But it's just as good to hear Serge lay into Marc for his reverential and equally elitist use of the term "the Artist, i.e. someone, preferably dead, who doesnt paint rectangular slabs of non-representational monochrome. Marc is the intellectuals anti-intellectual.

In North America he'd be subscribing to, maybe writing for, Commentary or The New Criterion.

Like a good playwright, Reza spreads herself around. She claims to have got the idea for the play when she saw an all-white painting on a friend's wall and laughed; she seems to identify intellectually with Marc, while temperamentally lining up with the debonair ironic Serge.

Her heart, though, is probably with Yvan who is at sea through most of the debate, since he doesnt have any tastes or beliefs worth speaking of, and who is anyway preoccupied with his approaching wedding and with keeping his buddies on board for it. He has a long, frenzied speech -- one that never fails to bring down the house -- in which he recites the agonies he is going through just trying to get his mother, fiancee and prospective in-laws to agree on the wording of the invitations.

He suspects that the other two despise him, him with his spinelessness and his lack of success; he can hardly compete, socially or culturally, with his rich friends rich friends. (We hear of a dentist who only likes conceptual art.") His fears are well-founded; but it's also true that they need him as much as he needs them. Yvan quotes some gnomic lines from his shrink about the perils of letting yourself be defined by other people; Serge and Marc scoff but it's the boat that theyre all in, one that gets thoroughly rocked but cannot be allowed to capsize. Their relationship, like the play itself, is less about characters than about checks and balances between characters.

This elegant and original piece is the first French play in decades to have made any impression outside France. Marc the classicist would approve, since Paris used to be the theatregoers Mecca. Christopher Hampton has given it a splendidly springy translation; William Joseph Barnes' production (previously seen in Winnipeg), hewing closely to the London original by Matthew Warchus, is sharp, tense and funny. Scott Hylands plays a silvery schoolmasterly Marc, increasingly outraged by the unassertive independence of Richard Poe's Serge. Passions flare beneath their subfusc exteriors, though not enough to make us hold our breath as we should when they finally go head to head.

Stephen Ouimettes Yvan meanwhile takes his time; his big aria has less pace and definition than it might (it still gets applause, though), but his later wide-eyed writhings are delicious torture.

Mark Thompsons set is certainly about art; it makes its own minimalist statement, with each character in turn inhabiting the same pale room that is differentiated only by lighting and, naturally, the picture on the wall. Casual black sweaters and MJQ- ish music take us comfortably back to the days when Modern was still modern; and the radio ads are by Sean Connery, the show's co- producer, putting his mouth where his money is.
