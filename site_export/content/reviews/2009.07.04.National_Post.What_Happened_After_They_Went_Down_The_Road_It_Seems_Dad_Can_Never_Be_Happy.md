David French tells us that he wrote Of the Fields, Lately (1973) because audiences kept asking him what happened later to the characters of his previous play Leaving Home (1972). The answer seems to be: the same, only more so. Not in terms of volume -- Of the Fields is a shorter, sparer, more concentrated play than its predecessor --but of intensity.

Leaving Home introduced us to the Mercer family, origins in Newfoundland, now living in Toronto. Principally they were Jacob, a carpenter; Mary, his wife; and Ben, their university-age son. Ben was the one who left home, ostensibly to live with his just-married brother, and to the great displeasure of his father. It was made plain, though, that Jacob would have been equally displeased had Ben stayed. He could be displeased at anything; love fought with resentment, the education-gap fused with the generation-gap, to produce a wound that never ceased to fester.

Soulpepper, which revived the earlier play two years ago, has now added the later one, with Ted Dykstra again directing and with the same actors playing the Mercers. About five years have passed (it's 1961) and Ben, having moved even further away -- to Saskatchewan, in fact -- has returned. The immediate reason is that Mary's sister Dot has died, and Ben is back for the funeral, though we're told they were never close. (An oddity: Dot was never mentioned in the preceding play. Another oddity: Ben's brother Billy and his ill-omened marriage are equally absent from this one. In a play concerned with family recriminations, that's strange.) It seems, though, that Ben's real purpose is to try and mend fences with his dad. Fate seems to present him with the perfect opportunity. Jacob is ill, too ill to work, though he angrily refuses to acknowledge it. Ben could move back in, get a job, pay the bills. Jacob, as might be expected, is partly delighted to have his son back, partly incensed. Jacob is an expert at denial, and what he denies most is his own mortality. Or as the ever-anxious Mary puts it, rather too explicitly for dramatic comfort, "He can battle all he wants; we're all up against the same enemy: time."

The play is bookended by Ben's reminiscence of the day his father paid an unprecedented visit to his school baseball game, and of how he, aged 12, was too ashamed and embarrassed to acknowledge him: a memory that has gnawed, unspoken, at both of them ever since. It's a fine speech, delivered here by Jeff Lillico with both subtlety and force, and it's obviously meant to underpin the play -- "the emotional cornerstone" as Ben says "of the wall between us." But it doesn't work that way. The play has nothing more to tell us about fathers and sons, or about this father and this son, than we had already learned from the first one. In autobiographical plays, which these confessedly are, the author's surrogate usually comes off as the most shadowy figure, and Ben, despite his actor's best efforts, remains a plot device with a bit of back-story about his life away thrown in towards the end.

The real interest lies with the older generation. It is, perhaps surprisingly in a play written by a man in his early thirties, an elegiac piece. Jacob and Mary, with loss hanging over them, are juxtaposed with Dot's widower Wiff, who is Jacob's fellow-worker, fellow-Newfie and enthusiastic fellow-drinker. In fact he never made it to his wife's deathbed, having stopped to take a drop first and then another drop. He had reasons for this, which are revealed in a funny affecting speech that's closely followed by another telling how he returned home to find Dot, in effect, haunting it. So he comes to the Mercers for refuge, rather like the old couple in Edward Albee's A Delicate Balance (though for reasons that are easier to follow). If anything, we learn more about Wiff and Dot's marriage than we do about Jacob and Mary's, but then of course, with only one survivor, the situation is less complicated. Anyway, Eric Peterson makes a three-dimensional person, both crafty and concerned, of a character who starts out looking like comic relief, with his vows of abstinence repeatedly made and as repeatedly broken. Part of the evening's pleasure of course is to see him square off against that other veteran Kenneth Welsh: Peterson's Wiff dry (considering he's an alcoholic) and contained, Welsh's Jacob explosive, juicy and hurt. Both are wounded men, but where one can acknowledge hurts both given and received, the other can only lash out. Welsh's performance is the same one he gave in Leaving Home (except that the accent's laid on less thick) but so it should be; his brief spurts of joy, when he feels his son actually loves him, are of course very moving. As Mary, Diane D'Aquila starts the play ironing endless shirts --a perfect image--and she goes on to be properly anguished and enduring, though she's also too ready to be aggressively aggrieved and seems upscale for the company she's married into. French continued the Mercer saga in Salt-Water Moon, which took us back to Jacob and Mary's courtship (Soulpepper, scrambling the chronology even further, revived this play in between the two others, with Lillico playing his own father as a young man) but, with all allowances for the ravages of time, I can't relate the two people we met then to the two we see now. And though I can appreciate the older Jacob's constant harking-back to his old haunts and friends, the theme of geographical displacement seems to me more taken for granted than explored. These are good Canadian plays that still stand up, certainly in productions as good as Dykstra's. I've still to be persuaded that they're universal classics. - Of the Fields, Lately plays through July 30 at the Young Centre for the Performing Arts.
