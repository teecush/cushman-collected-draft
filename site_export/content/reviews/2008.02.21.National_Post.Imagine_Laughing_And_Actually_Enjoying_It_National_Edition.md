THEATRE REVIEW

THE ODD COUPLE

Young Centre for the Performing Arts, Toronto

---

There are many compliments to be paid to the Soulpepper company's revival of The Odd Couple, but the chief among them -- the one that contains all the others -- is that it refuses to be a gag machine. Neil Simon's comedies can hardly help being funny (at least the best of them can't, and The Odd Couple, though an early work, may be the best of all), but they can also leave you feeling pummelled with wisecracks and starved of humanity. In the words of a Broadway sage: You laugh, but you don't enjoy it. The screen adaptations of Simon's plays have just this soulless quality. If they are to breathe, the plays need a live audience to breathe along with them, and Tuesday's first-night house was in harmony with the actors from our first sight of the supporting quartet of poker players in Oscar Madison's smoky, dusty apartment.

The story, by now, is folklore. Oscar takes in his friend and fellow-divorce Felix Ungar, who is as compulsively neat as Oscar himself is fecklessly untidy, and the two set up house together. In effect, they re-enact their marriages, with the same disastrous results.

There isn't much plot; there isn't even much character development once the basic psychological facts have been established. But there is a mastery of incident, and of verbal and physical knockabout, hardly any of which feels externally imposed. (Simon gets an amazing amount of mileage from having somebody unexpected at the other end of the phone or ringing the doorbell. Unexpected by the characters, that is: The audience can nearly always guess. It's remarkably satisfying.) Above all, there is the friction between two men who really like, but cannot stand, one another.

Stuart Hughes' direction gets the most from every word and every situation, without forcing a thing. He has a set, by Lorenzo Savoini, that conjures up not only the room in the apartment that we do see but the half-dozen that we don't, plus the flat across the hall. He has costumes, by Erika Connor, perfectly attuned to the personalities of those who wear them and to the period -- just before the 1960s became "The Sixties" -- that they inhabit. Above all, he has a central couple whose oddities come out enchantingly even.

Albert Schultz is a gentler, goofier, paunchier Oscar than we are accustomed to. He isn't, in other words, Walter Matthau looking like a human razor blade and making with the one-liners as if they, too, were weapons, of the automatic variety. Even a rank showbiz line like "what is that, the Curse of the Cat People?" -- Oscar's response to one of the verbal guilt-trips that Felix lays on everyone around him -- comes from Schultz like a genuine, baffled inquiry: angry, yes, but also concerned. Diego Matamoros's Felix is pettishly perfect, from his trim receding hairline and uptight specs on down, not to mention out. He hugs his neuroses while giving them plenty of geographical space. This actor is, among much else, a champion clown, whether terrifying his pals with gestures in the general direction of suicide or executing a dainty ballet when proudly setting the table for a dinner of London broil. An overweeningly proud cook, he looks his happiest in an apron. Still, what really unites the two men, and the two performances, is an underlying sadness. Though in Felix's case it isn't all that underlying; he parades it, and it's hilarious.

One of Felix's domestic achievements is to kill his buddies' poker-game with acts of culinary kindness. The visiting gamesters are Michael Hanrahan, Oliver Dennis, Derek Boyes and Kevin Bundy, in roughly that order of effectiveness as individuals and with impregnable credibility as a collective. These are good New Yorkers' parts -- the production's big surprise is the impact made by Amy Rutherford and Krystin Pellerin as the coo-coo Pigeon sisters, two English birds nesting in the apartment upstairs whom Oscar thinks ripe for the plucking but who start unexpectedly coo-cooing over Felix. They are perfect, in voice, manner and -- in dolly dresses that look quantifiably Quant -- appearance.

It's Felix's virginal thwarting of Oscar's dreams of scoring that drives the great rift between them. Once that has been established and the recriminations have mutually and furiously flown, the play has little more to say and starts to run out of gas though not, thanks to what has gone before, of goodwill. There's no flagging in the performance; the stage remains charged with energy, and so does the auditorium.

I wouldn't want Soulpepper to embark on a Simon cycle; the appeal of this show has much to do with seeing this company's two signature actors in joyous partnership. But everything else backs them up, down to the inspired choices of music between scenes and even at the curtain call. You laugh, and you enjoy it. - The Odd Couple plays in repertory at Toronto's Young Centre for the Performing Arts until April 19. Call 416-866-8666 for information.
