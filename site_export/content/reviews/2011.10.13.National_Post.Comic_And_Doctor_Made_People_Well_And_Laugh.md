Note: Obituary of DR. Robert Buckman

Robert Buckman, doctor and comedian, divided his life between making people well and making them laugh. Sometimes he did both at the same time. Its hard to think of a life more productively or benevolently lived. It's also hard to conceive of a better, kinder or funnier friend.

It isn't strictly true, perhaps, to say that he always made people well. He worked in a field - oncology - in which that often isnt possible. But, by the general testimony of both patients and colleagues, he always made them feel better. He was a pioneer in the neglected art of helping doctors to talk, honestly and sympathetically, to patients. The sympathy part was probably helped by his being ill himself for much of his life. But having known him for even longer than that - we both grew up in London, went to the same university, and emigrated to Canada at around the same time - Im sure that those qualities were there from the start.

He belonged to a distinguished English line of funny doctors of whom the most famous is probably Jonathan Miller; unlike Miller he remained a practising physician and a practising performer throughout his career. Like Miller and others, he first came to notice as a member of the Cambridge University Footlights. He arrived in Cambridge a year after I left, but I heard about him through mutual friends, and I saw him in a Footlights revue on the Edinburgh festival fringe.

Later I heard him as a DJ on BBC radio while he was working his way through London medical school. He formed a comedy partnership with another doctor, Chris Beetles, and the first time we really met was when I went to see Beetles and Buckman in a cabaret booking at Quaglino's restaurant in the West End. Except that I didn't see them, or not both of them; Beetles was off sick, and Buckman performed, hilariously, with a cardboard cut-out of his partner. I was able to write afterwards that Id seen an intelligent, original stand-up comic.

Much of the humour was medically related, and the duo went on to a TV series in similar vein which they called The Pink Medicine Show. This involved, among other things, Rob lecturing on the Ascent of Man: Some specimens learned to stand erect and became homo sapiens; others crawled on their hands and knees and became estate agents.

There was also a thighslapping song-and-dance routine entitled "Medical Men" ("and thats how they discovered schizophrenia"); and I also recall B&B, at an Amnesty International benefit, performing a Cossack dance in the background of the legendary John Cleese-Michael Palin exparrot sketch. He convulsed audiences (and guests in his living room) with a story of how, as a junior doctor, he had to deliver the baby of a woman who spoke no English, showering her with encouraging phrases erratically gleaned from a Berlitz phrasebook. And he was writing comic columns and collecting them in books.

All this time, he was advancing as a cancer specialist and researcher, which is what brought him to Toronto, as a consultant first at Sunnybrook and then at Princess Margaret. By then he had mostly abandoned formal performing, but his comic talents were irrepressible - I doubt that he ever tried to repress them - and he was in constant demand as a radio and TV pundit and as an afterdinner speaker.

He taught and lectured all over the world. He made a highly praised series of instructional videos with Cleese, and was at work on another series with another Python, Terry Jones; he was returning from shooting from one of these in England when he died, on a plane, at the appallingly early age of 63.

He was also at work on a novel. At his death, he had published 14 books, including a tongue-in-cheek autobiography (title I'm Not Dead Yet) and several devoted to what must have been his greatest cause: demystifying and, as far as possible, de-terrorizing illness. This concern stretched far beyond his own practice and his own specialty.

If I or any member of my family had a health problem, we always knew we could call him, wherever he might be (and he was always travelling), talk to him and count on a sympathetic, serious, compassionate and unsentimental response. I know there must be many others who relied on him in the same way.

The greatest loss of course is to his wonderful family. More selfishly, I still can't believe that I shall never again pick up the phone to hear one of his quizzical, funny-voiced greetings. He is, in so many ways, irreplaceable. He led a rich and loving life, and he led it by example, suffering from two potentially crippling conditions in succession and refusing to be limited by them.

His last major book was called Cancer Is a Word, Not a Sentence; and that title in its concern and practicality and its humour is quintessential Rob.
