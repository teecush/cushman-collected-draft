PLATONOV

Du Maurier Theatre Centre, Toronto

When Soulpepper first mounted Platonov last year, it was as a glorified --and glorious -- workshop. The production now returns with virtually the same cast but with a more durable set and with costumes more eloquently suggestive of the late 19th century, which is where Chekhov's play, his first, was written and is set. Both help.

The period frocks counterbalance a translation that is aggressively and exuberantly here and now: We can welcome the actors as Canadian while still believing in the characters as Russian. The setting -- designed, like the clothes, by Victoria Wallace -- is a high, bare room done up in what appears to be hessian.

Sometimes it is dotted with benches, the title character being a schoolteacher. At other times, it is just a mess; it looks unliveable but it feels lived in. These are middle-class people who have humour and intelligence and even, in a couple of cases, money. They still exist in chaos, or squalor, or both. Their voices echo in this spare environment, shouts ring and whispers pierce the air. The opening moments -- in which Platonov himself is discovered, quite characteristically, asleep -- are done with an extraordinary overheard naturalness. They set a tone.

The volume level of director Laszlo Marton's production is to rise considerably. The play -- which would take seven hours uncut but has here been reduced by half -- becomes a switchback ride between farce and tragedy. The people behave exorbitantly, though they never seem surprised by much, and the more extreme they get, the more we accept them. It all seems --how you say? -- wildly probable.

The production has lost a little in edge (or maybe just in surprise), but gained in richness. Albert Schultz's Platonov has developed from an acute sketch to a painting. He is a drunken, lecherous, ineffectual, cynical, self-hating man who, at 35, is in a permanent state of mid-life crisis. He is, in short, someone with whom we can all sympathize. Admittedly, he gets luckier than any man has a right to (every woman in the cast falls for him), but he compensates for this by not enjoying it much, or at least by having terrible remorse, sometimes before the fact. His position is complicated by having nearly all his acquaintances, male and female, regard him as some kind of idol -- a feeling that, in a warped way, he shares. He is the first to chastise himself for failing his own ideals and of course he tries to use this as absolution; meanwhile, he spreads his guilt around on everyone else. He can yearn for a better life while in the act of seducing a friend's wife. Mostly the women come to him, but he makes an effort for this one, probably out of sheer perversity.

Schultz, untidily bearded and moving sartorially from dishevelment to decrepitude, plays him from a permanent bedrock of charm, from which spring serpentine rivers of deception, which can convince everyone but himself. He spends a lot of time lying down, the victim of alcohol or inertia or sheer physical and moral exhaustion, but he can never stop his mind working -- which is both his saving grace and his destruction.

He has a brother-in-law, a doctor who has gone even further than himself in vodka and futility (he has to be literally dragged to a sickbed) but can always pull back from the edge. Diego Matamoros maybe enjoys his antics too much in the earlier scenes but is magnificent when he has to pull himself together to deliver the final, bitter epitaph ("Who will I get drunk with at your funeral?")

Stuart Hughes' tearful cuckold in a cream-coloured suit has perhaps benefited more than anyone else from the show's enhanced sense of period. It gives him a handle on an indeterminate character, one whom his author seems to have made up as he went along.

Of the women, Nancy Palk is gorgeously commanding, and rather sensible, as a local landowner who spends a lot of time trying to arouse Platonov by lying on top of him. Only the occasional touch of self-conscious irony betrays her in these encounters, as if she can't quite believe a lady could behave like this.

Susan Coyne (who adapted the play, with the director) has some trouble connecting the widely spaced dots that make up the role of the debauched wife. She is best at extremes, at the moments of surrender and of revenge. Robyn Stevan remains perfect as Platonov's own wife, a seemingly placid figure who nevertheless throws herself (non-fatally) under a train because it seems the thing to do. Her scenes with Schultz, in which he flounderingly and compulsively tries to restore harmony in the home, are among the funniest and saddest in the play.

Liisa Repo-Martell is tremulously earnest as Platonov's last and least likely victim, a biology researcher. And Michael Hanrahan is strong and supple as the local brigand, with whom everyone seems on remarkably easy terms, but whose death they take thoroughly in their stride.

The student whose idealism (or priggishness) Platonov especially loathes, and who returns the compliment, is now played by Christian Lloyd, whose performance augurs well both for himself and for Soulpepper's Young Company, of which he is a member.

There is, as before, a fine quartet of servants, including William Webster as a major-domo who submits to humiliation with scalding decorum, and Patricia Fagan as a maid who delivers a message to the protagonist as if applying to be his latest flame. Marton's staging seems as fluent and unforced as life itself, but can still form indelible stage pictures, especially at the end.

In a righteous world, this production would be permanently available, or would at least return at regular intervals. In the world we have, grab it while you can.
