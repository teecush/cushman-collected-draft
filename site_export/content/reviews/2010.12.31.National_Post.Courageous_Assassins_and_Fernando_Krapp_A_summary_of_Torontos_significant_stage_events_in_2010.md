A few trends, some of them interlocking, from the departing theatrical year, in and around Toronto.

Desperation/Disintegration As they sang in Assassins: "Everybody has the right to be happy/even if at times they go to extremes." I can't remember a year in which there were so many powerful performances of characters driven to such extremities, physical and/or psychological. Two of these occurred in Buddies in Bad Times' production of Blasted, the British play by the suicidal young writer Sarah Kane, about all kinds of apocalypse. Michelle Monteith played a young woman in a self-protective shell, going in and out of fits. She's an uncanny actress. David Ferry is a very canny actor, who let his defences totally down as a fearful, predatory man whose later turmoils included being brutally blinded. There were profounder echoes of King Lear when Ferry presented us, naked and fearful, with what Lear recognized as "unaccommodated man," still preserving a kind of dignity. Jane Spidell went hauntingly mad, after a soigne start, in two different plays: Soulpepper's Doc and Peggy Pickit Sees The Face of God, the latter the central and much the most impressive panel of World Stage's Africa Trilogy. As the sequentially trusting, violated and vengeful Philomela of If We Were Birds, Tara Rosling was astonishingly present in all her moods. Jacquelyn French wrung tears, without begging for them, in The Light in the Piazza. Anusree Roy was devastating at the end of her own play Roshni (a duologue twice as good as her earlier monologues). Mike Ross, breaking new ground, had a virtuoso farcical melt-down in Jitters. Earlier he had been one of that corps of Assassins, who, with perfect musical and dramatic control, lost it, individually and as a collective. Graham Abbey was especially wonderful. Another National Anthem was the greatest musical number of the year.

Germans Nobody has bothered much with Teutonic drama, since they stopped doing Brecht. This year, it made a rewarding comeback. The aforementioned Peggy Pickit was the work of the divinely-named Roland Schimmelpfennig; it revitalized the middle-class domestic-battleground format by giving it a global context. It was given a superb production, directed by Liesl Tommy, from South Africa via the U.S.A. Franz Xaver Kroetz's Through the Leaves, which finds the lyrical in the prosaic, was finely done by Company Theatre, Philip Riccio directing. Maria Vacratsis' performance, perfect, showed us a woman surmounting desperation; Nicholas Campbell's vied with Ferry's in the broken-machismo stakes. (Good year for Campbell; he stood out in The Red Queen Effect, a show otherwise dismal.) Matthew Jocelyn's regime at CanadianStage got off to a brilliant start with Fernando Krapp Wrote Me This Letter, which also had notable extreme performances from Ashley Wright (extremely unpuncturable, until the end) and Ryan Hollyman (extremely physical, especially when terrified). All these plays had size, scope and imagination. And any games they played were serious, not silly. It strikes me that in this they resembled the best Canadian play, Michael Healey's Courageous, which arrived at the beginning of the year; only a couple of plays ( If We Were Birds, The Clockmaker, both also at the Tarragon) arrived to challenge it.

Mention of games brings me to Meta "Shows with The Musical in the title are always terrible." Those words of wisdom are from something called Stephen Harper The Musical, which unfortunately proved them true. So, on a much larger scale, did Rock of Ages, which didn't have the subtitle but behaved as if it did; it was a spoof of a spoof. So was Young Frankenstein. So was Legally Blonde The Musical and, probably, Priscilla Queen of the Desert, though those last two, to be honest, were fun. The meta trend may be spreading to more august addresses. Stratford's production of Shakespeare's weakest comedy might have been subtitled The Two Gentlemen of Verona The Play.

Miranda Two plays -- Judith Thompson's Such Creatures and Zorana Kydd's Soulseek-- involved characters with unreliable memories of having played the heroine in The Tempest. More meta, I guess. The real deal turned up at Stratford, in what was essentially the Christopher Plummer show, in which he magisterially acted both through and around the words.

Festivals Stratford's company was unevenly divided. The Tempest was essentially Plummer, plus Julyana Soelistya's extraordinary Ariel, diminutive and blue-bleached. The Winter's Tale (plain) and As You Like It (fancy) were more solidly cast and performed, all down the line. The season's best shows: Kiss Me Kate ( Brush up Your Shakespeare was the year's second best number) and, leaving nothing to be desired, Peter Pan. The Shaw similarly did well by its patron saint, but even better by others, with Harvey and The Cherry Orchard; Jim Mezon proved himself a great actor in the latter, and re-proved it in John Bull's Other Island. Most worrying trend: the retreat, at Stratford and Soulpepper, from un-obvious classics --other than Soulpepper's A Month in the Country, another case, in Laszlo Marton's production, of actors going rewardingly to extremes. Soulpepper also had a series of lab productions that weren't available for review but, I can now reveal, were among the year's highlights. One was an experimental Cherry Orchard, cut down to its major characters, one of whom -- Brendan Gall's Lopakhin -- was the best I've ever seen. The others, Diego Matamoros' solo version of Borges' The Aleph and the academy's bewitching Window on Toronto, will be back next year, in the main bill.
