Everyone who has written about Art has said that it isn't really about art, it's about friendship, and I shall undoubtedly say it myself when I review Canada's first production of the play in English, which opens tomorrow. It will hurt to be echoing everybody else, and it will hurt even more to be repeating myself, but there's no help for it. I want to deliver some prefatory ruminations about Art, and I see no way of starting on them without telling you that this play concerns the relationship between three male chums.

There is Serge, a Parisian who buys a very expensive abstract painting, and there is Marc, who pours violent scorn on it, and there is Yvan, who bounces between the other two, trying to be nice to both. I saw the play in London, where it has now been running for three years, and about halfway through it I had a vision. I felt I wanted to see it performed by the three guys from Seinfeld. It seemed to mirror their relationship exactly.

Jerry would bring the picture home, ostensibly proud of his purchase, but full of doubts underneath. Kramer, acting very man-of- the-world, would ask him just what on Earth he thought he had spent his money on and would lecture him loftily on his lack of taste; to be sure Kramer has never, as far as I know, set up as an art expert, but his self-confidence has always been boundless and there's a first time for everything. George, meanwhile, would rush around flashing his insecurities and would keep trying to bring the conversation round to his own problems, just like Yvan, who in the play is largely preoccupied with his forthcoming wedding and with his friends' opinions of his fiancee.

I still think they'd make a great cast for some future TV production of the play, though there is obviously no chance they would ever do it in theatre. Even if he were willing to commit the time, I can't imagine Jerry Seinfeld sustaining a stage performance in a play for a whole evening, and I have no idea whether Michael Richards, who as Kramer seemed to be a clown rather than an actor, could do it or not. Jason Alexander, though, gives me the impression that he could do anything, and he would be a magnificent Yvan, who always steals the play -- just as Alexander's George, who became painfully real, used to steal the TV show -- and who has that rarity in modern drama, a show-stopping speech.

It's a shame there's no part for Elaine, but, at least as it stands, Art is an all-male play, even though its author, Yamina Reza, is a woman. (Incidentally, Seinfeld was unique among sitcoms in boasting four absolutely first-rate characters. Even Fawlty Towers could only muster three.)

Somebody else's similar fantasy, though, did nearly come off. There were plans for the three male stars of Frasier to do the play in Los Angeles and perhaps subsequently in London or New York, but it never happened. Certainly the roles would fit them up to a point. Connoisseurship is very much in Frasier's line and philistinism is very much in Martin's, though Serge and Marc ought really to be contemporaries. Niles into Yvan could be hilarious, though I'm not sure that the junior shrink's punctilious elegance would be of much use here; the two characters are different kinds of losers. Then again, Kelsey Grammer, David Hyde Pierce and John Mahoney are all practising and accomplished stage actors, and they wouldn't have to repeat their TV personae. And the characters in Art are very malleable.

There have to date been 12 separate casts in the London production of Art. It started out in 1996, with Tom Courtenay as Serge and Albert Finney as Marc. These two actors, like John Gielgud and Ralph Richardson, have increasingly become a double act as they have achieved doyen status. It was, however, the lesser-known Ken Stott who stole both the notices and the audience's hearts as Yvan, and this has been the routine ever since.

When the play appeared on Broadway in 1998, the usually urbane Alan Alda revealed an unaccustomed mean streak as Serge, and Victor Garber -- Canadian-born but part of the New York landscape by now -- was Marc. But it was Brit Alfred Molina as Yvan who captured the lightning and the Tony nomination. (Art, by the way, having lost most of the subsidiary awards to The Beauty Queen of Leenane, surprised everyone at the end of the evening by winning the award for best play.)

The characters in the New York production remained French; nevertheless, Molina, presumably to fit in with his colleagues, played with an American accent. There is an appropriateness about this; there is no way that these characters, with their intense notions about art and friendship, could be English, but they could just possibly be New Yorkers. They might even be Torontonians, if you confine your gaze to the areas within easy reach of the Art Gallery of Ontario. We will see what the Canadian cast -- Richard Poe, Scott Hylands and Stephen Ouimette -- make of them.

Molina's appearance also signalled an unusual period of transatlantic entente as actors commuted between the London and New York productions. There were four Broadway casts; the second one featured two wonderful character men, Henry Goodman and David Haig, who had been in the play in London but in different roles, confirming that in this piece an actor's individual qualities matter less than how he balances those of his colleagues. The third man, new to the play, was Brian Cox, Britain's best recent King Lear, who played the Finney role and looks rather like him.

Meanwhile, London played host to American actors such as Stacy Keach, David Dukes and Judd Hirsch. The West End production these days is peopled, as long-running shows tend to be, by solid backbone- of-the-profession kind of actors, bolstered by the odd former TV star. The current London Yvan is comedian Chris Langham (son of the great Stratford Festival director, Michael Langham), and I should think he would be wonderfully put-upon.

The final New York cast was heavy on comedians: Buck Henry, George Segal and as Yvan -- bringing my Seinfeld dream as close to realization as it's likely to get -- Wayne Knight, alias Newman. (I confess I find that one hard to visualize, but what do I know?)

Knight, of course, is also the cop in Third Rock from the Sun, but a Third Rock cast doing Art -- or anything else -- is more the stuff of nightmare. If you want science-fiction casting, imagine the play turning up as the cabaret at an intergenerational Star Trek convention: William Shatner being hectored by Patrick Stewart, and both of them making life hell for Leonard Nimoy.

That, of course, would mean upping the ages a bit; if you want to lower them, we could have the angst-ridden Dawson's Creek version, with the script revised to include a bit more navel-gazing and a lot more polysyllables: Pacey clicking his tongue over Dawson's ideas of how to spend his discretionary income, and Jack going really berserk over his wedding plans because he knows he's still gay.

We could even, if we could secure the services of a good medium, have a Marx Brothers version: Groucho as Serge, Chico as Marc, Harpo as Yvan. This, of course, would mean losing a third of the dialogue so that the play, already short, would run about an hour; but audiences' attention spans are growing notoriously short.

We have yet to see women in Art (as opposed to Women in Art), possibly because of the author's insistence that she was concerned with male relationships, thank you very much, and if she'd wanted to write about her own sex she would have done. Still, she can hardly expect to buck the Zeitgeist indefinitely, and I await Art: The Talk Show, with Rosie O'Donnell, Oprah Winfrey and -- for charity's sake - - Joan Rivers.

There could be the Canadian Stage Company version: I want to see Fiona Reid purchasing the art, Clare Coulter aghast at it, and -- I know -- Sandra Shamas going spare over the arrangements for her wedding reception.

Further along yet: when Friends finally comes off the air, and its cast are in need of a buck, they could all take to the road in a nostalgic production with rotating casting, segregated or co-ed as the mood or the fashion takes them. They could even use their old theme tune.

And then some curmudgeon of a critic could come along and ask them where they got the idea that the play is about them, because titles don't lie and it's meant to be about Art.
