The 1965 musical On a Clear Day You Can See Forever is rightly revered for its score; Burton Lanes music is glorious and Alan Jay Lerner's lyrics, though uneven like all his work, include some of his best. The book, though, is generally agreed - not least by Lerner himself, who wrote it - to have been a mess.

It concerns reincarnation, ESP, hypnotism and psychiatry, all of them among the author's own obsessions at the time. Its heroine was a greenfingered kook, appropriately named Daisy, with a smoking habit that led her to consult a shrink, one Mark Bruckner, who discovered that she had existed in a former life as an English regency heiress. He fell in love with her, in both her incarnations.

In the recently opened, and rather unexpected, Broadway revival (at the St. James Theatre) much has been changed. The shows present-tense period has been updated to the 1970s, whose fashions here look even more hideous than they actually were; and Daisy has become David, a gay florist. His previous self, though, is still a woman: a 1940s big band singer named Melinda. (The name could hardly be changed as its also the title of one of the most bewitching songs.) Mark, a widower, again falls for Melinda though not, he is at somewhat awkward pains to reassure us, for David.

To the extent that the original show worked at all, it seems to have worked as a vehicle for its leading lady, Barbara Harris. Bisecting, or bisexualizing, her double role deprives the show of its main motor, while making Mark the protagonist by default. That, the default part anyway, seems an apt description of Harry Connick, Jr., the production's marquee attraction, who is either not much of an actor or too embarrassed by his character's situation to try.

Hes a friendly stage presence, though, and his noncommittedness has an appeal of its own. As a singer he sounds more like Frank Sinatra than ever, which is fine in itself but doesnt blend well with the more traditional musicaltheatre attack of his fellows.

David Turner as David, alternating between flounces and hysterics, is probably obeying director's orders; Jessie Mueller performs very well as his alter or earlier ego, without quite justifying the script's insistence that Melinda could have been the second coming of Billie Holiday, Judy Garland and Ella Fitzgerald all at the same time. (The world would never know, because she died in a mysterious plane crash, just like Glenn Miller, who makes an appearance under a slightly altered name.)

The original stage score has been augmented with additional songs supplied by Lerner and Lane for the movie version, and with significant others from their earlier film musical Royal Wedding. Ironically, the numbers from the latter source often fit better into the rejigged book than those from the authentic Clear Day. There is a fascination in seeing which song will turn up next, and how; and for the first half-hour or so the show does look as if it might actually work.

The ending, too, is rather touching; we finally get to hear the title song and Connick does it very affectingly. Peter Parnells adaptation was commissioned and staged by Michael Mayer, director of Spring Awakening among other things, and its been designed, in psychedelic headache style, by Christine Jones. The sum is a disaster but not a disgrace. Anyone who collects golden age musicals should see it.

They will certainly want to see the latest revival of Follies (Marriott Marquis), a show whose first production, in 1971, is a legend difficult - or, given economic and other constraints, impossible - to live up to. It's equally impossible not to try. Stephen Sondheim's score - half of it pastiche with a sting, the other half elegant contemporary with an even greater sting - remains a tour de force; the lyrics may well be the best collection that he, or anyone else, has ever written for a single show. But without the magnificent fusion of the original Hal Prince-Michael Bennett staging, plus the unbeatable original cast, the central metaphor, tracing the human follies of the present, to the Ziegfeld Follies of the past, tends to fall apart.

The central quartet, vainly trying to obliterate their youthful mistakes but doomed to relive them, comprises Bernadette Peters, vocally ravaged at the performance I saw but still very moving; Ron Raines, correct but stolid; Danny Burstein, superb; and Jan Maxwell, who has won plaudits and awards as the super-sophisticated Phyllis but seemed to me to be having too good a time. The other three all have their meltdowns; why doesnt she?

Eric Schaeffers direction is lucid without being inspired; and it shirks the full darkness of the ending. In fact its most poignant moments come at the beginning, as the showgirl veterans assemble at their reunion, and exchange the fond embraces of incredulous recognition. Reencountering the show itself, 40 years on, yields something of the same sensation. Not only has Follies become its own myth, it's become part of its own subject.

RelativelySpeaking(Brooks Atkinson Theatre) is a triple bill of one-acts by famous cutups: Ethan Coen, whose opener is unspeakably bad; Woody Allen, whose closer might be called speakably bad, meaning that even its good jokes are tired; and Elaine May, whose middle piece is brilliant. Its title is George Is Dead. He was also very rich, and his death has left his widow, who's never had to do a thing for herself, dependent on a childhood acquaintance, whose own marriage is in vividly depicted trouble but who cant prevent herself from trying to help.

The play is a study of entitlement, financial and emotional, and hence both topical and eternal. Its farcical realism, superbly sustained up to but not including the very last moment. It's perfectly performed by Marlo Thomas, woman as grown-up child, and Lisa Emery, weary with maturity. This play needs to be rescued from its surroundings; May must have written something else it could be paired with.

A final New York holiday recommendation: Up until New Year's Eve, you can see Barbara Cook and Michael Feinstein performing together in Feinsteins own cabaret room at the Regency Hotel. The mutual congratulations are laid on too thick, though they do manage to sound surprisingly spontaneous, but the songs are the things.

Feinstein has acquired vocal weight over the years, certainly as a rhythm singer; Cook is less stunning than when performing solo last year in Toronto, but at 80plus, shes still the singing actress beyond compare, sacrificing neither half of the equation to the other. They end, though this will presumably change next week, with White Christmas; and for once it's a pleasure rather than an embarrassment to sing along.
