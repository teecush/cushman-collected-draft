THEATRE REVIEW pH

Thursday, September 28, 1995

Robert Cushman

Created and performed by Dumb Type At the Du Maurier Theatre Centre, Harbourfront, until Saturday Rating: Glazed eyes ride an infernal machine Reviewed by Robert Cushman DUMB Type, a performance group from Kyoto, is among us as part of the Today's Japan at Harbourfront. According to its moving spirit, Teiji Furuhashi, the name "dumb type" refers to "a society that is overstuffed with information but cognizant of nothing." He obviously doesn't believe that he and his colleagues are cognizant of nothing; at the very least, they know that nobody else knows anything.

The show pH is concerned, says the director in the same interview, "with the relationship between technology and human beings. My inspiration for that set was the idea of performers being trapped in a copy machine." The set actually occupies what would usually be the auditorium. The audience sits in the theatre balconies and looks down on a long room empty but for a couple of lighting gantries. These stretch from wall to wall, one fixed high and one low, one with its lamps on and the other dark; and they sweep the stage, back and forth, ceaselessly, from before the show starts to just before it ends. There are five actors present, and they are very well drilled. This, according to another program note, is because they are portraying "automatons, robots, persons devoid of will and initiative." There could also be a more prosaic reason: If they didn't know exactly when to duck, they'd be decapitated.

One of the infernal arms projects crisp images on the stage floor: giant dollar bills and such. But it takes some time to realize that we are in the presence of a giant photocopier. (Some time for me anyway; I'm bad at these things.) Other symbols of alienation put in appearances, some on a screen and some in person. Actors push supermarket trolleys which threaten to take over their lives. At the end, the stage is invaded by little pink mechanical pigs. Piggy-banks, I thought, but I was wrong again; they actually represent the ascendancy of the Japanese toy industry. Still, the overall pitch is not exactly unfamiliar. If the Unabomber were to demand access to a theatre space he might come up with something like pH -if, that is, he had the flair.

The constant motion of Furuhashi's infernal machine is a genuinely original spectacle. It's also hypnotic; the show has rhythm. If it's monotonous, and if the technical content far outclasses the human, that presumably comes with the territory. The title refers to a chemical formula, used to denote the proportion in a substance of acid to alkali.

Other polarities ("question-answer, image-word" and 11 others) are said to underly each of the show's sections, but I wouldn't have known. To me it all looked much the same, and I have quoted so liberally from the official propaganda because it obviously knows far more about the show than I do. There are three white-clad young ladies who spend their time scurrying around looking oppressed, and a man who faintly croons a lyric about the benefits of modern civilization to the tune of Time on My Hands.

I was generally baffled and mostly bored, but not in the soul- destroying way that's induced by stupidity or incompetence. The show is probably about boredom: a Te Deum to tedium.
