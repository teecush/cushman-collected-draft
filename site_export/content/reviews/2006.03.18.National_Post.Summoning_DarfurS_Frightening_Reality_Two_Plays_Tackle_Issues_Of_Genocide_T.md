Colleen Wagner's The Monument begins with a long monologue, spoken by a teenage soldier named Stetko who awaits execution. He had been convicted of participation in multiple officially sanctioned rapes and murders, committed during a guerrilla war. He admits his crimes, looks back on them with shame mixed with a certain amount of pleasure, expresses some resentment at being the object of victors' justice, and claims, predictably, that he was mostly motivated by peer pressure and the need to prove himself sexually. He also talks about his girlfriend who should have visited him but hasnt.

The speech is somewhat generic but compelling; both the narrative and the emotions are believable. At the end of it, Stetko is approached by a woman named Mejra who will let him escape if he will then do whatever she wants. He agrees, and the rest of the play depicts their wanderings through a war-torn landscape. The transition takes the play to a different level, and a far less satisfying one.

Obsidian Theatre has revived the play, which was first seen here 10 years ago. The original production was set somewhere in Central Europe. Obsidian, restoring what were apparently the authors intentions, sets it somewhere in Africa. Either fits -- which indicates the play is lacking in specifics, the things that make drama happen.

Stetko's promised submission to Mejra involves walking around with his hands tied. Having him thus powerless, she starts hitting him, hard. At first this is rather satisfying; the man who committed unspeakable violence against women is getting a dose of his own medicine. But as it goes on, our sympathies shift. Whatever may have happened in the past, in the dramatic present, the victim is now Stetko or, as Mejra calls him, Stinko.

We learn, as Mejra herself does, that violence begets violence and that revenge turns hollow. But we already knew that, and the play keeps repeating it, with the minimum of human content that might make it seem fresh. The couple eventually arrive at the mass grave of Stetko's victims, one of them, unsurprisingly, Mejras only daughter, and they erect a grisly makeshift memorial, the one in the title. Symbolically presented, it makes for a powerful image, one on which the play should really end. But it doesnt.

Nigel Shawn Williams's production, designed with appropriate bleakness by Shawn Kerwin, is atmospherically strong if sometimes gratuitously underlit. If it gets monotonous, that's probably unavoidable. The same goes for the performances. Garnet Hardings sullen boyish bravado in his opening speech is exemplary; his subsequent oscillations between subservience and defiance give him little room to manoeuvre. Yanna McIntosh, avenging fury crossed with grieving survivor, has even less; her authority cannot be faulted, but the role has her punching far below her weight, even if the punches are literal.

For a contrasting lesson in how to bring atrocity home, see Hanas Suitcase. This production by the former Young People's Theatre is based on Karen Levine's book about two Tokyo schoolchildren who became fascinated by an exhibit in their citys Holocaust Education Centre -- the suitcase of Hana Brady, a young Czech girl sent to Auschwitz via Theresienstadt -- and, aided by a teacher, set out to learn more about her. As their investigations proceed, Hanas story takes shape in their minds and before our eyes.

Emil Sher's dramatization is remarkably successful, give or take some strenuously chatty exposition, and Allen MacInnis's direction is superb. On Teresa Przybylskis clever and elegant set, worlds collide. The screen doors of the Japanese house slide open to reveal silent grey figures trudging their way toward the camps. Thats all it takes: that and the brief use of a balcony level as the perch for an impassive SS guard.

The perkiness of Siu Ta and Richard Lee, grown-up actors playing the Japanese kids Maiko and Akira, can be hard to take, but it serves a purpose; they initially see the plight of the European Jews as a kind of video game. Only gradually do they come to recognize the horror and sheer helplessness of it. They and their teacher (admirably played by Jean Yoon) dominate the first half; Hana, her brother, George, and their parents are silent presences.

In the second act, they come to vocal life. Hana and George's incredulity at the remorseless restrictions as the Nazis crack down (the most frightening part of the story, because it's the easiest to identify with; the camps are beyond being moving) is echoed by Maiko and Akira, shocked for them and recoiling with them. Their words become interchangeable, and the ripples spread out among the Canadian children, watching from the auditorium. George, the only survivor, now lives in Toronto, and has attended a performance. The reverberations on that occasion -- he appears in the play as an adult as well as a child -- can only be imagined.

Jessica Greenberg plays Hana, Paul Dunn the young George, Eric Trask (who is especially fine) and Sarah Orenstein their parents and other adults. The re-enactment takes us all the way to Hanas consignment to the gas chambers. Its all handled with great tact, but no soft-pedalling.

There is another dimension to the story and to the production. Hana was one of a group of child artists who documented life in Theresienstadt, and some of their drawings, hidden and recovered, are projected. The show has undertaken its own project of research and reclamation. It confirms the rocketing of standards at this theatre under MacInnis's stewardship. It also, by concentrating on one reality, summons the corresponding reality of Darfur far more powerfully and movingly than The Monument, which is explicitly angled to do so. It also makes you wonder if, in Israel, schoolchildren are undertaking similar projects on Hiroshima.
