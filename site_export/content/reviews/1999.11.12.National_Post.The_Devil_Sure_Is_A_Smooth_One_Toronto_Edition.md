FAUST, PART ONE

Tarragon Theatre, Toronto

---

Daniel Brooks' production of Faust is amazing. Its very existence is a cause for wonder: Goethe's long dramatic poem is only dubiously a play for the stage, despite its author's lifelong association with the theatre, and even in Germany productions of it are special events; outside, they are practically unheard of. When it is done, it is unlikely to be performed by a company of five, which is the total strength mustered at the Tarragon. It is even more unlikely to be done this hypnotically well.

The hardest thing to get one's head around is that this Faust is never in any danger of what we usually think of as damnation. That's not just because we are only being offered Goethe's Part One, which by definition leaves the hero on his feet; even at the end of the more densely philosophic Part Two he gets off scot-free. It's what he's escaping from that remains mysterious.

In Christopher Marlowe's Doctor Faustus, the English play that predates Goethe's by more than 200 years, the protagonist boasts that he thinks hell's a fable, to which his Mephistopheles sombrely replies, "Ay, think so, till experience change thy mind." You know where you are with that version, and where you are involves both tormenting fires and the eternal absence of God. Goethe, who just about personified the Enlightenment, never mentions the first and is at best equivocal about the second. He offers us a Faust without theology; he is elaborating on a legend, while ignoring or debunking the beliefs in which it was rooted. This makes the conflict stimulatingly, but sometimes fatiguingly, abstract. It also produces a mocking, questioning comic tone that this production superbly captures.

Goethe's Faust, like all Fausts, begins in his study, bored with it all. He has learned everything, cured everyone, won all the prizes (though not apparently much cash) and is 80 years old. Weary of earthly wisdom, he turns to magic and seeks union with an Earth spirit (the spirit of creation, I think), which rebuffs him. He thinks of suicide and then of the Devil, who appears first as a dog and then as a man. Asked what he is, this Mephistopheles suavely identifies himself as the spirit of denial; in other words, a universal cynic.

Faust makes a deal: Mephisto will become his servant and feed him with pleasures; if ever Faust confesses to a moment of real satisfaction, his soul will be the Devil's. He embarks in effect on a lifetime of asking, "Am I having fun yet?"

One thing Faust has been missing is sex. Mephisto takes him to a witch whose potions knock 30 years off his age. He then has a vision of a young country virgin and literally says to his attendant, "You gotta get me that girl." That line is the first-half closer, and gets a big laugh -- legitimately, since it's a huge anticlimax. Getting and abandoning that girl, Gretchen, takes Faust the rest of the play, and I've always thought it weird that a vast questing drama should decline so abruptly into a commonplace seduction piece. (It's far worse in the Gounod opera, which sentimentalizes everything.)

I was, it seems, wrong. In this production, the play still breaks into two clear halves, but they balance one another. Both are triumphantly lucid -- the first intellectually, the second emotionally. Faust's ruin of Gretchen and his degrees of remorse over it are the test of his surrender to the Devil's spirit of negation.

Brooks' staging is bracingly severe and wonderfully imaginative. The set (designed by Julie Fox, Jan Komarek and the director; lit by Andrea Lundy) is a wooden platform; it can divide laterally, and for his opening monologue Faust, placed between the two sections, seems in the half-light to be standing in a trench. When the lights go fully up, the front half of the stage is revealed as a long refectory table, at which the professor can receive worshipful students or diabolic house guests.

The supernatural effects are handled with dazzling simplicity; the spirits can be disembodied heads in fish bowls or red lights on the drapes (the devil dog's eyes) or echoing voices, booming or whispering.

Brooks is only defeated by Walpurgisnacht, but probably anyone would be; his version is seriously underpopulated, but I'm not sure that more would mean better. With the original's crowd scenes drastically and even cheekily reduced, five actors -- three male, two female -- actually seem a reasonable complement; there are, after all, only half-a-dozen named characters.

The five have to work phenomenally hard. Randy Hughson as Faust threads his way through the long debates -- internal, external and not all, to be frank, immediately theatrical -- while creating a sharp-tongued image of the scholarly mind at the end of its tether. It's a relief, though -- for actor, character and audience -- when the witch's Viagra lets him shed his beard and spectacles and offer what is almost a second performance: a callous, amoral young traveller (never without his suitcase) with the brain and recurrent scruples of an old one.

Chris Earle, by contrast, is enchantingly smooth and slippery as the tempter of the early scenes, but tends to repeat himself as the sinister valet of the later ones; even cynicism, it seems, can become monotonous.

Both these actors are a good deal more than good; the two actresses are downright magnificent. Maggie Huculak plays Martha, the heroine's friend and neighbour, and makes a full, sad, warm- blooded woman out of a conventional neglected wife and unmerry widow.

Waneta Storms' Gretchen is unforgettably real: In her blunt amazement that the glamorous stranger could possibly want her, in her progressive surrender, in her terror when she becomes pregnant and the village turns on her and in her madness when she has murdered her child and awaits execution. She is still sane enough to be instinctively repelled by Mephistopheles; her values are, in fact, traditionally Christian, and the play, after all its earlier skepticism, ends up endorsing them. I confess to finding this confusing, but its embodiment is very moving.

Ross Manson admirably completes the troupe as the aspiring pedant Wagner and as Gretchen's brother, in whom religion becomes a self- righteous, and murderous, code of honour.

The translation used is by Randall Jarrell and it sounds terse or expansive as required.

There have been some fine productions in and around Toronto this year, but I think this may be the finest.
