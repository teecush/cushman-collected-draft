NOT IN THIS LIFETIME, JEAN HARLOW

Theatre Passe Muraille Backspace

We have seen real-life husbands and wives acting on stage together, brothers and sisters and lovers of all persuasions. The two performers in Not in This Lifetime, Jean Harlow, however, break new ground in consanguinity. Michael Rawley, who both wrote and appears in the play, is a victim of kidney disease who, after three years on a dialysis machine, became the beneficiary of a transplant. His donor, a miraculous match, was Wendy Krekeler, an actress- friend who is also currently his co-star.

The play obliquely reflects their off-stage relationship, both in its subject matter and in the mere fact of its existence. It is not, however, directly about it.

In this piece, which also covers a three-year period, both characters are undergoing dialysis and are hooked up, for reasons of both bureaucratic and dramatic convenience, to adjacent machines.

Three times a week they clock in, for four hours of excruciating inactivity. We observe them trying, in diametrically opposite ways, to prevent the experience from defining their lives. He does it by fighting his condition. She does it by ignoring hers.

He, who goes by the uncommon name of Chaelan, is a fitness freak who rollerblades to treatment. He conscientiously obeys dietary restrictions though, as a gourmet, he finds them especially agonizing. (One of his speeches is a valedictory ode to the tomato.)

She, called a little more prosaically Erdra, would also hate to watch her food if she gave it the slightest thought, which she doesn't. She also smokes, drinks and is diabetic; lack of circulation is slowly destroying her feet. He is toned, and she is beefy. He counts the years to a possible transplant; she isn't even on the list. She has decided that, when the doctors decree amputation, she will simply take herself off her machine and call it quits.

In the meantime, as the clinic's oldest inhabitant, she lords it over the staff and other patients and makes life especial hell for her immediate neighbour. The two of them bicker and worry one another and eventually, predictably but plausibly, establish a sort of sympathy.

They are, after all, in different stations on the same boat.

Nobody could accuse Rawley of writing a sentimentally sympathetic part for his benefactor.

Erdra is self-destructive, which theatrically can be a compelling quality, and loud-mouthed, which generally isn't. Krekeler plays her, I suppose, uncompromisingly; her character certainly supplies the most gratingly repeated laugh I have ever heard on a stage.

But both the writing and the performance of the role are heavy- handed; a character who doesn't know when to back off maybe requires an actor who does. Rawley's own role, the model patient and health- activist, seems to be autobiographical (the basic situation obviously is) and he is able to point up what's absurd as well what is admirable in Chaelan's undeviating right-mindedness. But he too remains a diagram rather than a person.

(He looks like Tim Allen and he sounds, at least in this role, like Alan Rickman, so he seems to be mounting a one-man Galaxy Quest.)

The play manages to be gripping and boring, simultaneously. It grips because this is, after all, a life-and-death situation, one that most of us will contemplate with a sense of worried involvement, of there-but-for-the-grace-of-Godliness. We also get the chance to feel indignant about a health system controlled by management rather than medicine; and to rage, with accustomed impotence, against the idea that pain and sickness should exist at all. In Erdra's case, the play makes a strong implied case -- and I am not being either callous or ironic -- for euthanasia. (We are also affected, of course, by our extra-mural awareness of Rawley's endurance and Krekeler's generosity.)

It bores because it's banal. The dialogue never lifts. Erdra's favourite diversion, both in the treatment room and at home, is to watch vintage movies featuring her favourite feisty female stars: Jean Harlow (who died of kidney disease) and others. "See you in a while, me darlin' Bette Davises," she says before settling down for the day's draining. People don't talk like that, and it isn't even much fun pretending that they do.

Everything here is too explicit, too obviously designed to convey information or to set up another verbal tennis rally. Maybe the off- stage reality has overpowered the on-stage fiction. I don't mean the clinical reality. I mean that these characters carry on like actors.
