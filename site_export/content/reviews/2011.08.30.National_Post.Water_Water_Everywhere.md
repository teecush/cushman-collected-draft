THEATRE REVIEW

When the Rain Stops Falling Studio Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont.

The year is 2039, and in Alice Springs, Australia, a dead fish falls from the sky. This is even odder than it might seem, because the man at whose feet it falls, whose name is Gabriel York, informs us that this kind of fish has been extinct for years. For him, the miracle couldn't have come at a better time, because it means hell now have some dinner for his son, whom hes about to meet for the first time in two decades.

Gabriel had walked out on his family as, in a way, had his father before him and, in another way, his father before him. Gabriel's father was also called Gabriel and his mother Gabrielle, which the couple themselves admit to be quite a coincidence. Andrew Bovell's When the Rain Stops Falling is composed of echoes and parallels, parents and children, husbands and wives. Its a tremendous play, and the Shaw Festival has given it a superb production.

The program tells us that Bovell belongs to a theatrical wave, distinguished among other things by characters who . acted like full-blooded Australians, that started to gather in the late 1960s. As an ignorant Pommie, I find this claim surprising; I do know that as far back as the 1950s there was a full-blooded Australian play called The Summer of the Seventeenth Doll that conquered the world. Besides, more than half the characters of When the Rain Stops Falling are English, either by birth or recent descent.

After its futuristic prologue, the play takes us back to London in 1959, and it keeps switching between years and countries. Its two female characters are each played by both a younger and an older actress. It presents a cats cradle of relationships, a mosaic of themes; and I'm more than usually reluctant to give away details of plot, because so much of its fascination depends not just on what is revealed but how and when.

We watch people discovering things and concealing things. We arrive at the realization, explicitly stated in the dialogue and honestly backed up by the action, that when they don't talk to one another its often not because they have nothing to say, but because they have too much. We watch them wrestling with appalling fears and guilts, their own or their parents or partners'. It's all, once youve latched on, surprisingly easy as well as satisfying to follow.

We also, as per the title, see and hear a lot of weather. Many entrances are made with umbrellas. The Australian drought seems by 2039 to have ended, to be succeeded by conditions even more cataclysmic. A recurrent flippant line in the dialogue is "there are people drowning in Bangladesh" (or East Pakistan, depending on the date); by the last scene, which returns us to the first, they actually are - and everywhere else, apparently. The play manages - and I wouldnt have thought it possible - to rhyme, without glibness, environmental catastrophes with human ones.

Shaw has paired its great choice of script with an ideal choice of director. Peter Hinton, picking up on the author's stage directions, stages the play around, and sometimes on, a great table, at once epic and domestic. The space that frames it is square, backed on Camellia Koo's set by movable, translucent screens. Kevin Lamottes lighting and Richard Ferens sound are magic, even for them.

What marks Hinton as a great director is that the performances and the images seem to have grown up together. The evening feels wonderful from the moment when Ric Reid, a fine taken-for-granted actor, launches into his opening monologue, in a play otherwise composed almost entirely of duologues, and mines all of its wryness, cragginess, bewilderment and pain.

There are a few performance flaws, and the play's central incident strains - not so much coincidence as the characters' recognition of it - a few inches too far. But this is a show that excites while you're watching it, and just keeps on resonating. It has to be revived or transferred or both. Shaw, like Stratford, has had an unusually good season and this belongs, with Cat on a Hot Tin Roof, at the very top of the rock.
