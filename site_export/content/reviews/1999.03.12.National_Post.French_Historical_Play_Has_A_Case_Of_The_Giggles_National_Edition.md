Monsieur D'eon is a Woman

Buddies in Bad Times Theatre, Toronto

History's most celebrated hermaphrodite gets a going-over in Monsieur D'Eon is a Woman, a moderately interesting chronicle play with recurring fits of the giggles.

The Chevalier d'Eon de Beaumont was an 18th-century French aristocrat who achieved some reputation as a soldier and diplomat under Louis XV and XVI. He also insisted that he was a woman. Nobody was certain until after his death; an autopsy established his masculinity.

Mark Brownell's play shows us d'Eon, at age seven, taking Joan of Arc for his role model, she being a woman who fought and dressed like a man. In other words, d'Eon is a woman trapped in a man's body, pretending to be a man trapped in a woman's. (Or, as Stephen Sondheim once put it, "wait, I think I meant that in reverse.") When the Chevalier grows up, he wins battles and is sent on missions to Russia and England; but his success arouses the jealousy of the older Louis, the hostility of the younger, and the resentful enmity of just about everyone at the French court. He moves to London after being released from the Bastille by the forces of the revolution. To survive, he takes to giving sword instruction in skirts.

D'Eon doesn't seem much bothered by his own sexuality, and neither really does anyone else; it's just another handle for his foes to use against him. So the various bits of the play don't hang together and it's difficult to see what, if anything, the author is trying to say. Sue Miner has staged it capably, and it's always a pleasure to see a large cast (11 good actors, furiously doubling) disporting itself in fancy dress on a wide-open stage. Brownell has a talent for aphoristic prose, and the brief scenes between d'Eon and his patron saint, whom he calls his "shiny lady," strike sparks.

Ron Kennell gives a light, dignified, technically excellent performance in the lead, keeping a gracious distance from the other characters and thus explaining why they all hate him. The author helps set the tone in his performance as George III, not yet mad, whom he plays as an animated pudding. This is history in the style of Blackadder or Monty Python but without the wit or content. I thought we had outgrown this kind of show, but they keep coming.
