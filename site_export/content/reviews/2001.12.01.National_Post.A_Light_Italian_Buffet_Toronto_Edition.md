Until Dec. 16. (416) 368-3110

CIAO, BABY

Berkeley Street Theatre Upstairs

- - -

Some notions, however attractive, may be too thin to make a play. Ciao, Baby is based on three volumes of verse by Gianna Patriarca, who came to Canada from Italy at a very young age.

They have now been worked into a four-person show whose subject, as you might guess, is growing up female and Italian-Canadian.

Toni Ellwand, one of the actresses in the current cast, has been trying for a few years to get this material onto the stage. Recently she called in the assistance of Paula Wing, an actress and playwright who also appears in the show and has arranged Patriarca's poems to tell a story.

At the beginning, a young girl arrives in Toronto with her mother and sister to rejoin the father of the family who has moved from the old country ahead of them. We watch this girl grow up on and around College Street, see her swap longings and experiences with two of her contemporaries, until we find the three of them grown up with daughters of their own, giving them the same headaches they once inflicted on their own mothers. Along the way the three of them also play other ladies of the community, sometimes to confusing effect.

The mood is generally cheerful and Patriarca, we are assured, avoids the confessional bleeding-heart mode in her writings. That, obviously, is all to the good. But it turns out here to have its downside. The emotions seem generalized, all the more so when a passage is split between three speakers. When we hear a monologue, we are presumably getting the poet's voice straight. When solos give way to trios the feeling is diluted and the verse, if it still exists, must be very free indeed.

The heroine's friends occasionally tease her about her habit of dropping out of social situations in order to sit down and knock out an ode, but her characterization doesn't go much beyond that. There is no real narrative, only chronicle, and the things that are recorded are commonplace.

There are problems at school, trouble with poppa for staying out late, worries about religion and dancing and sex and men: the stuff of any woman's life but of only passing interest unless you know her as an individual. Here the individual merges into the crowd, even at the moment of asserting herself. There is no binding motif: An early sequence about making wishes while watching trains pass is repeated at the end but cannot justify the prominence it is given.

Sue Miner's direction keeps it moving and alert, and the three actresses -- Ellwand, Wing and Lucy Filippone -- convey a pleasing air of quick-witted, sensual solidarity.

The token male is Charly Chiarelli, throatier than I remember him from his one-man show, Mangiacake, earlier this year (perhaps he has a cold), variously appearing as father, teacher, brother, husband -- all of them figures of mild oppression: Patriarca on the patriarchy. (The pun is hardly my own; the producers identify themselves as Matriarca Productions.)

There is mandolin and accordion music and at the end the women take a trip back to their roots. I love Italy but I still felt distant. This seems to be an entertainment for insiders.
