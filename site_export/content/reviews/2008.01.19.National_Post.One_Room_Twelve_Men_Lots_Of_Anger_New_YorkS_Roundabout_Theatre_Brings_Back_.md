Reginald Rose's Twelve Angry Men is justly famous as a prize exhibit from one of those recurring Golden Ages of American Television: in this case, the 1950s era of the live, original drama. The TV play became, with minimal alteration, a movie, and Rose subsequently wrote a theatre version that has been in heavy rotation ever since. The stage play does make changes to the original, mainly for the worse; they pad and sentimentalize. But they don't interfere with the impact of the piece, which remains the non-stop attention- grabber it has always been.

You probably know the set-up. A jury settles down to consider its verdict in a New York murder case. A teenager is on trial for murdering his father. (Both are probably black, though the word isn't mentioned; we only hear, with unctuous delicacy, about "these people." I don't know if the author was trying for universality, or trying to spare '50s sensibilities, but I suspect the latter. Anyway, the crime plainly occurred in one of the inner city's virtual ghettos.) There appears to be a mountain of evidence against the accused, starting with the fact that he was overheard threatening to kill his father. Eleven of the jurors are disposed to cut the discussion short and send the boy to the chair -- which, this being a first-degree murder, is undoubtedly where he would go. But the 12th, an architect, if it matters, has doubts. He never claims to know definitively that the prisoner is innocent, but he cannot convince himself that he is guilty either. Gradually, in the face of ridicule, indifference and belligerent hostility, he wins the others over to his side. It's a thrilling and heartening process. Also, in a world in which the presumption of innocence is increasingly being called into question, a newly pertinent one. It's a feel-good evening, in that it vindicates all our best liberal sentiments, but democracy deserves its demonstrations, too.

The play is one-of-a-kind, providing all the excitement of a courtroom drama while dispensing with the judge, the lawyers and the prisoner at the bar. There turn out to be some very leaky components in the prosecution's case, and the jurymen, once aroused, take delight in pulling them apart. This should, of course, have been the defence's job, but we are told that the accused was represented by a court-appointed advocate who really couldn't be bothered. This element of the play is a little hard to swallow -- not the defence's negligence, but the acumen and enthusiasm of the jury, who practically re-stage the proceedings as they should rightfully have proceeded.

They turn out to be first-rate sleuths, so we get the thrills of a trial-play and a detective-play at the same time. There is much pacing-out of floors, and examining of weapons, and discussion of how much of a crime would be clearly visible from across the tracks of an elevated railway. (There's nostalgia for you. New York's Third Avenue El was mostly closed down in 1955, one year after the date of this play.) And the excitement mounts when the remaining reactionaries among the Clean Dozen are provoked into attempting their own counter-demonstration. One of them actually yells at an opponent "I'm going to kill you." Aha.

Along with exciting action goes compelling characterization. Most of the 12 angries only become so when they start getting enraged with one another, but you might call them the Cross Section, an account of the varied sociological strata they represent. (Given, that is, that they're all white and all male.) They start out, inevitably, as types, but their personalities become more sharply defined according to how they agree or disagree.

The present production, directed by Scott Ellis, comes from New York's Roundabout Theatre (it opened there in 2004 and has been touring for a couple of years) and it's a display of steadily gathering force; the ensemble grows tighter as the individuals become more distinct. I wasn't crazy about Richard Thomas's performance as Mr. Valiant-for-Truth, which is in a style I've seen described as Hollywood-sincere, all italics and exclamation-points. But no one could say he isn't sympathetic.

Mike Boland is an unobtrusively authoritative foreman and Alan Mandell a dignified octogenarian reproach to some of his younger, brasher colleagues (the characters, that is, not the actors). But the two stand-outs are the two hold-outs, the last to be converted: Kevin Dobson as a blithe bundle of prejudice who wants to bring the debate to a speedy end so he can get to the ball-game, and Julian Gamble, a massive presence as a more complicated kind of loudmouth, one whose personal issues poison his view of the case and who eventually dissolves in what looks like a heap of blubber. His collapse is one place where the stage adaptation weakens the original script --his motivation is made too explicit -- but it still works. And there's a nice developing irony: The man who couldn't see what there was to discuss becomes a fanatic for driving things to a conclusion; he may not mind being on a hanging jury but he doesn't want a hung one. It's a little sentimental when he and his opponent reach a kind of silent rapprochement, but not much. This is one of those rare occasions on which the phrase "never a dull moment" is the literal truth.
