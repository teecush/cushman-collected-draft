THE SOLDIER DREAMS

THEATRE REVIEW

THE SOLDIER DREAMS

Saturday, March 29, 1997

Written by Daniel MacIvor

Directed by Daniel Brooks and Daniel MacIvor


  


Rating: **½

Soldier comes off vague and arid

Special to The Globe and Mail


  


TWO Daniels have come to judgment. The Soldier Dreams was written by Daniel MacIvor, adapted for the stage by Daniel Brooks, and directed by the two of them together. The refining process has been going on through three years of trial runs and the final accomplishment is decidedly mixed. The form is often brilliant, the content banal to non-existent.

It's about death: doing it and watching it. A performance piece rather than a play, it has an exquisite score (by Richard Feren) and wonderful lighting (uncredited). Right at the start the two combine in a remarkably powerful theatrical statement: total darkness, daringly prolonged, over wrenchingly beautiful music. When at last the lights go up, they reveal a rectangular slab of stage, set well back and harshly illuminated against a wall-and-floor surround of implacable beige. Within this compartment are a sick bed and its attendants. Some of the images in this show are worthy of Robert Lepage, though he would have given us more of them. Lepage's motor runs extravagantly fast; Brooks and MacIvor are more economic.

Dying in the bed is David; around him, bickering, are his two sisters, his brother-in-law, and his boyfriend. In turn, they step to the front of the stage and deliver valedictory statements on David, who is now deceased. Whether they are speaking to us or an imaginary audience at some kind of memorial is uncertain; either way, we get the point that they are far more concerned with themselves than they are with David. David himself also gets to talk. The body remains on the bed but a second actor, MacIvor himself, strolls down to utter the dying man's thoughts and to re-enact a remembered scene from his past: a one-night stand in Ottawa with a German medical student.

Verbally as well as visually, the piece is quite cunningly patterned. Somebody tells a funny story that Richard, David's lover, reckons to be homophobic. Later on we discover how the joke came into the family in the first place, and it casts a whole new light. Like citizen Kane mumbling about Rosebud, the dying man mutters a phrase that sets everybody guessing. In his case it's "the German doctor." With the benefit of flashback, we know what he's talking about, but the other characters are baffled. Richard's professions of ignorance are especially emphatic, so he may be covering up. We don't know or, speaking for myself, care. The encounter itself is written and acted -- by MacIvor and especially by Volker Burger as the German -- with considerable humorous delicacy, but it's an oasis. The main action is arid.

The idea is apparently that we will see these people appropriating David's memory to their own ends, but his memory, even in distorted form, hardly gets a look in. We merely listen to unexceptionally selfish people being unexceptionally selfish; that is no great revelation. Jim Allodi gets some interest out of the gangling brother-in-law and Blair Williams gets rather more out of the prickly Richard, whose role seems more vigorously and specifically written than the others. The women are unhelpfully stiff. There have been deathbed-vigil plays before. This one is less agonizingly portentous than Edward Albee's All Over ,less sentimentally uplifting than the musical Falsettoland ,but it doesn't have a compensating identity of its own.

We're never told that David is dying of AIDS, but audiences are bound to think so and wonder whether he caught it from the German. To the play's credit, it never tries to make aggrieved moral capital out of this, or anyway not much; it's the fact of death that's important here, not the cause. Given that, it might have been helpful to tell us and get it out of the way. The characters would know; why shouldn't the audience? Mystification is an element to be used with caution, and with the utmost rigour.

The general mood is despairing, in an unpushy sort of way. "Life," we are told, "is a war; even when the soldier dreams, the war goes on." David's time in Ottawa was apparently a brief time out of war, but death, we later learn, is the only real peace. Seems logical, if bleak, but the characterization is too vague to be truly tragic. David, everybody tells us, loved dancing, and it's no surprise when, in the play's last moments, he goes into a dance: a frantic, desperate one. Maybe he's raging against the dying of the light; maybe he's frantically embracing it.


  


The Soldier Dreams runs at Canadian Stage until April 12.
