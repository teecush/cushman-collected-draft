THEATRE REVIEW THE MONUMENT

Saturday, January 07, 1995

ROBERT CUSHMAN

WRITTEN BY COLLEEN WAGNER Directed by Richard Rose Starring Rosemary Dunsmore and Tom Barnett At the Canadian Stage Co. Berkeley Street Rating: Treating the horror of war and violence against women as synonymous robs The Monument of its potential power Compelling play not very interesting BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to the Globe and Mail WHEN the lights go up, not that they go up very far, it looks like a scene from a Shakespeare history, brutalist style. A sombrely-clad man harangues us in the gloom from what might be a throne. Slowly we discern that it's actually an electric chair, and that he is strapped to it; the pointy thing on top of his head is not some kind of exotic crown. In the moments before execution he is making confession, though he does not seem very penitent. He is a contemporary soldier recalling the 13 women he has raped and then killed. There is one who especially attracted him and of whose fate he leaves us uncertain. He also speaks of the pressure from his peers that set him off on his career as a war criminal, of his increasing sexual difficulties, and of his own still-virgin girlfriend. What he really regrets, though, is having been caught. It is, as you may well imagine, a powerful speech.

Spotlit beside him, a woman appears. She offers him his life, if he will follow and obey her. We now get to see the entire stage, and a very bleak lunar landscape it provides. She chains him up, beats him unmercifully, forces him to drop a heavy stone on his own foot, chains him to a plow and has him work the barren field on which she apparently lives. Finally she takes him to the mass grave of his victims, and makes him dig up their bodies. One of them is her own daughter. These corpses will be a monument, a testimony against war. She wants him to speak to the women who come to view it, but he can't find the words.

The piece is compelling, but not very interesting. If Colleen Wagner were to write an article on the all-too-compendious subject of Atrocities Today, I would probably agree with every word (though I might enter a reservation about a world-view that apparently divides the population into Soldiers and Women, ignoring the fact that modern warfare takes in plenty of male civilian casualties.) But a play is, or should be, something more. Wagner's Soldier may well need the lesson she teaches him, but it's far from clear that the audience does. Though she claims in a program note that the media have neglected the horrors she depicts, and their psychological implications, news reports on the first trial of a Serbian war-criminal took me at least as far inside his head as her play does.

I have been calling the characters the Soldier and the Woman, but she gives them names: Stetko and Mejra. (We're never told it's taking place in Central Europe, but we're bound to think so.) The play is just far enough from being a simple parable to get us asking awkward questions. By what authority does Mejra strike her deal with Stetko? (You can't just walk out of a condemned cell.) Are there really supposed to be women gathered at the end just offstage, or is this a dress rehearsal in her mind?

If we take the play realistically, we spend a lot of it sympathizing with Stetko, whom she humorously addreses as Stinko. To begin with, I thought Tom Barnett's performance too hardened - he is supposed to be a kid scared into becoming a monster - but his choices turn out to be the right ones; the play supplies his vulnerability for him. Whatever he may have done before it starts, what we actually see is him being victimized. That he deserves it, makes little emotional difference. I was surprised that the author hadn't realized this would happen. On reflection I think that she did, and that she wanted to unsettle our preconceptions; at the end Mejra, too, is meant to learn something, presumably about forgiveness. But this isn't clearly presented, and it comes too late to save Rosemary Dunsmore's performance from its single note of strident implacability. She spends the whole evening being right. Richard Rose's production, which stakes everything on starkness, never allows her to play against her lines.

The play treats war, and violence against women, as if they were synonymous, rather than two overlapping horrors. At times it echoes Euripides' Trojan Women, at others Death and the Maiden, but mostly it summons the simplistic self-righteousness of Buffy Sainte-Marie's The Universal Soldier, one of my least favourite songs. Colleen Wagner deserves better, not least from herself.
