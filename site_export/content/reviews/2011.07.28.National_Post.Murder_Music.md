THEATRE REVIEW

The Kreutzer Sonata

Young Centre for the Performing Arts, Toronto

This is Ted Dykstra's time. He is the director of all three of the plays in Soulpeppers current repertoire: the superb Glass Menagerie, the perennially successful Billy Bishop Goes to War, and now The Kreutzer Sonata, of which he is also the adapter and sole performer. In the latter role, his achievement is unimpeachable. He sits in an armchair, in the persona of an upper-class Russian from the late 19th century, and tells the story of how he murdered his wife. He tells of how he loved her and of how he became jealous of her relationship with a Frenchified fop with whom she played violin-and-piano duets. Music, he observes, fosters a dangerous intimacy between those who perform it; it also, he laments and proves, arouses dangerous emotions in those who listen to it. Like Richard II, Dykstras narrator might have cried, This music mads me, let it sound no more. So he stops it, dead.

All this, and more, Dykstra tells us in the course of just over an hour, without ever standing up. He's wearing a rather elegant dressing gown, and he has a few props: a glass of water, a Bible. (His character is very religious; he also keeps relevant documents between the pages.) His hand movements are studied, sparing and revealing; he uses them to make or underline points. The visual effect is enhanced by Lorenzo Savoini's fierce, concentrated lighting. Dysktras vocal delivery is virtuosic, generally suave and measured but breaking into an intensity that we can recognize as hysterical even though the character doesnt. He is a crazed man priding himself on his lucidity. It's a perfect performance.

The script may also be perfect in its own terms, but those terms are limited. The play is based on a novella by Tolstoy, that in turn takes its title from a composition by Beethoven. It's the principal piece that the narrators wife and her supposed lover play together, and whose sensuous first movement in particular drives the husband to frenzy. One of the most obvious differences between play and book is that were more than halfway through the latter before the sonata itself is even mentioned. In the play, we not only hear of it, but hear it, right from the start; it would be theatrically unthinkable for us not to.

The music becomes the play's theme, in more senses than one and in a way that isn't true of the novel, despite its title. Dykstra first performed his adaptation for the Art of Time ensemble, as half of a double bill of which the other half was a dance piece performed to the Beethoven music. And I suspect that it was more satisfying in that context than it is all on its own.

Theres more context in the book, too. The protagonist tells his tale to an anonymous narrator whom he encounters on a train and who has already heard other fellow-passengers expounding their views on women and on marriage: Some of them, to present-day ears, sound agreeably advanced, others fearsomely obscurantist. Dykstra can hardly be blamed for ditching this framework; indeed, nearly all his alterations are understandable and most of them were probably inevitable. They include getting rid of the characters account of his early life. In fact, his story begins about halfway through Tolstoy's. And this does have consequences, both visceral and intellectual.

Like his author, Tolstoy's hero led a wild brothelhaunting life in his youth and is now revolted by it; he belongs in the line of repentant sinners from St. Augustine to Malcolm Muggeridge who, having had their fun, are intent on denying it to everyone else. Although he still finds the temptations physically overwhelming, he finds sex, inside or outside marriage, spiritually and intellectually abhorrent, tolerable only when it produces children, and dubiously so even then. So what if the human race dies out? There are higher considerations than that.

Tolstoy wrote a "postface" (opposite of a preface) to insist that no, he wasnt kidding, those were his beliefs; though he did rather hedge his bets by admitting that, humanity being what it is, his ideals were unlikely to be realized. Being an artist as well as a theorist, he couldnt help complicating things in the actual story; there are too many layers for us to take the protagonist on trust. You do read him, though, disturbingly swayed by the hero's intensity while noticing how many of his positions - the horror of contraception, the idea of marriage as legalized prostitution - still recur at different points on the political map. Dysktra gives them passing mention, but mainly what he offers us is a straightforward story of jealousy and revenge, brought on by music, here standing in for the carnal temptations that Tolstoy found in absolutely everything.

There are some more minor alterations, notably in the matter of names. Tolstoy's protagonist Pozdnyshev is just Yuri to us; his wife gets a name, too: Sonya. And she now plays violin in the duets rather than, as might seem more natural in an amateur, piano. Maybe (I'm not being frivolous) Dysktra is trying to establish an independent identity for his own version. His text certainly seems to be his own paraphrase, and an effective one, rather than an adaptation of an existing translation. Like the story, he's at his most gripping in the account of the murder itself, which has a horrible inevitability to it. It also makes you wish that, for all his other skills, Dykstra would act more often.
