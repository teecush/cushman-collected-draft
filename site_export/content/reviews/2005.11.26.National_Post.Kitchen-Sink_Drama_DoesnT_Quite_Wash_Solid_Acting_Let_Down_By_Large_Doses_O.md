robertcushman@lycos.com; The Dishwashers runs through Dec. 18 at the Tarragon Theatre, 416-536-5018. Crowns runs through Dec. 10 at the Bluma Appel, 416-368-3110.

Morris Panych's The Dishwashers is the second play in succession (The Optimists was the first) to arrive on the Tarragon stage with an idea, a setting, a group of characters and no development.

Panych directs it himself, and it comes, like all his productions, with a startling set by Ken MacDonald. On this occasion the most startling thing about it is how uncharacteristically realistic it is. MacDonald takes us into the bowels of a vauntedly classy restaurant, down to the cheerless depths where the crockery is reunited with the cutlery and both get resparkled. It's tempting to describe the space as cluttered but in fact, close-packed though it is, its extremely ordered. And the detail is extraordinary, from the bits of tomato obstinately clinging to a plate to the steam that rises from the machine as yet another cleansing cycle nears completion.

Presiding over this process is Dressler, who alternates between a grinding cynicism about the way the world works and a touching faith in the higher culinary motives of his bosses upstairs. He takes enormous pride, for example, in being a part, however lowly, of a trade that could recognize an artichoke as a potential gourmet treat where others might have seen just a prickly green thing." Being a dishwasher seems here very much like working for a newspaper.

Labouring alongside him is Moss, an old and very sick man ("riddled with cancer, Dressler says briskly, and thats the best thing about him"), kept on apparently through charity, though whose charity is long left a mystery. Into this setup there is thrown, as there always seems to be in workplace plays, a young newcomer: not, for once, a student but a hotshot investor, name of Emmett, who had found he wasn't so hot.

The play is in part a tribute to Panych's father, who slaved for years in a comparable environment. Its problem is that environment is all, apart from a handful of really funny lines, that it has to offer. The space between the beginning of the play and its end is filled by having the characters revolve in their predetermined patterns. Dressler oscillates between obsequiousness and officiousness, without ever quite losing his humanity; hes an ex- con, and still has a few scams going. Emmett is sometimes resigned to dishwashing for life, and sometimes rebellious; his attempt, capitalist though he is or was, to form a union, is the nearest thing to a plotline, but it, too, seems like filler. His conflicted state is indicated by his inability to finish a sentence without contradicting himself; this trait is especially evident in a final scene thats genuinely surprising but that twists its screws a few times too many to be satisfying.

As director, Panych begins the first act by having the stage furniture play some poltergeist tricks of its own, and the second by having the three principals do a little dance. Both these devices are delightful, but they have nothing to do with the rest of the show. The second, though, lets the rubicund Randy Hughson, who plays Dressler, show how very light he is on his feet. This actor is a puzzle. Most of his recent performances have been the same, filled with desperate bluster, but each has seemed absolutely right for the character he is playing. His pace and comic timing are great helps, of course. Ditto Eric Peterson as the aged Moss, though he has in the circumstances to go for slowness rather than speed. Jonathan Crombie is good, though fuzzy in outline, as Emmett; and Daniel Karasik makes an excellent impression as a last-minute recruit.

Crowns is about hats. They line the side of the stage, and they also weigh down most of the text. Half a dozen black ladies, and one black gentleman, discourse in disconnected monologues on the importance of wearing gorgeous headgear to keep up morale and appearances in the segregated South. Regina Taylor's script -- there's no way its a play -- is adapted from a book by Michael Cunningham and Craig Marberry, and I can see that on the page, with photographs, the vignettes could have considerable anecdotal and historical appeal. Recited on a stage with no continuity, they are paralyzingly dull. There is a sliver of a through line, about a teenager from the North sent to live with her high-hatted relatives, but it doesnt amount to anything. She does a bit of rap; most of the show's music is gospel. The audience is dragooned into clapping along. This must be the most desperate of CanStage's annual attempts to build a show around Jackie Richardson. She is in great voice, as are all the cast, but theres no way this is a musical, either. The show is dire.

I could happily have devoted my entire column to The Last Days of Judas Iscariot, but it has already come and gone. The director, David Ferry, roped off a corner of the yawning Fermenting Cellar in the Distillery District to serve as a suburb of Purgatory, where a catatonic Judas comes to trial, and animated it amazingly. Dramatist Stephen Adly Guirgis is a lapsed Catholic, though he might equally well be a vexed secular Jew. With exhilarating wit he applies New York street talk, leavened with much learning, to the problem of Judas (ultimate villain or ultimate victim) and all the moral and political problems that radiate from it. He doesnt have any answers. An evening in which the surprises never stopped coming was peopled by, among many others, Irene Poole, racked and ringing as Judas's lawyer; Sanjay Talwar, her fawning opponent with a fez; Alon Nashman, Sigmund Freud as coke fiend and, as surprise-witness Satan, Diego Matamoros, immensely powerful, imperturbably cool. Pray that some at least of them will be back.
