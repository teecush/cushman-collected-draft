Among the credits in her program bio, one of the cast of 9 to 5 The Musical lists an appearance in Hair The Musical. Gee, I can't remember when Hair wasn't a musical. But maybe this nomenclature has become compulsory. It's got to the point where shows list themselves as The Musical even when they're spoofing the idea of shows listing themselves as The Musical.

Well alright, 9 to 5 The Musical, isn't a spoof, though its plenty derivative -battening, among other things, on its composer's name and image. It's the show of the film of the song, the song being of course Dolly Parton's hit number of the same title. That song, with its peppy and flavourful description of the office grind, always sounded like it might make a good opening number for a musical, and so it proves, though its effectiveness as scene-setting exposition is somewhat diluted by some standard strap-hanging, briefcasebrandishing choreography.

It does serve to introduce us to the three leading characters: Violet, the office manager, five years widowed; Judy, the office newbie, freshly divorced, from a heel; and Doralee, the pneumatic office eye-candy, a happily married Texan who bears a strong vocal and physical resemblance to Idon't-think-I-need-to-complete-thissentence.

The film wasn't a musical, but Parton has supplied a full-length original score for the stage version. I wouldn't exactly call it a good theatre score, but it does suggest that, given more of a source to write into, Parton might well come up with a very good one. Country music, being rooted in story and character, not to mention aural clarity, has a habit of working in the theatre in a way that rock usually doesn't; and Parton supplies some attractive tunes, matched to observant and even funny lyrics. One number, a duet between Violet and the junior accountant who's been laying hopeless siege to her, actually advances story and character. The actors are in a different place at the end of it than they were at the beginning, and we believe in the journey. That's the way theatre songs are supposed to work, but unfortunately it's an exception. More typical is the stentorian ballad sung by Judy, who started the evening shy but isn't any more, after she's discovered that she no longer needs her ex. However strenuously she sings it, and however overpowering the amplification, it doesn't tell us anything we don't already know.

I blame the book, written by Patricia Resnick, which is both skimpy and incredible; it does sort of own up to the incredibility but with more desperation than wit. It's 1979, which in consciousness-raising terms, and indeed in showbiz terms, is depicted as somewhere between How to Succeed in Business Without Really Trying and Mad Men. Assistants are still called secretaries, and the boss is a Mr. Hart, who is described at least four times (thrice in the show and once in the program) as "a sexist, egotistical, lying, hypocritical bigot." He's also an embezzler, something on which great store is set for much of the evening, but which turns out to be strangely irrelevant by the end. He patronizes Violet, who obviously does all the actual running of the office, denying her a long-promised promotion because, he says, guys prefer to do business with guys. He hits on Doralee, grossly and persistently, while rather inconsistently inviting his buddies into his office to admire her attributes. Judy doesn't like him much, either.

The three fantasize, each in her own way, on taking revenge; and it's here that the show, despite making a great pretense of being energetic, grinds to a halt. Each of their fantasies is sung, danced and acted out. So we watch the same situation staged three times; and none too inventively at that. And then, their fantasies come true, sort of. I'll spare you the full details, but they do include a protracted farcical interlude in a hospital that's quite agonizingly forced, as well as Mr. Hart's prolonged absence from work.

In his absence, the three heroines take over, and institute many lifechanging reforms, such as lunchbreaks. How they manage to do this is unexplained, given that Violet is the only one of them with any seniority, and one of the supposed mainsprings of the action is that she's been passed over, in favour of a toadying male creep whom seasoned musical buffs may recognize as the re-incarnation of Bud Frump. The authors have to banish him from the stage lest we ask awkward questions about why he hasn't taken over.

Jeff Calhoun's staging is standardissue second-rate Broadway, office furniture division, but this touring version has first-rate principal casting. Dee Hoty, a musical-theatre treasure these 20 years, is a Violet as tasteful and spirited and economical as the lady she plays, with just a dash of poignancy. Diana DeGarmo is a roof-raising Doralee, revealed at the end though never before to be herself a country chanteuse in training, and Mamie Parris does everything possible for Judy. (For the record, I saw the show in preview, though admittedly, the same day as the opening night.)

In its sexual politics, though, the show may not be quite as advanced as it thinks. Why does Violet, imagining herself as a future CEO, sing about being One of the Boys. (It isn't even an original title). And then there's Kristine Zbornik, a talented comedienne, who's been lumbered with the role of Roz, Hart's secretarial sycophant, who sings an embarrassing number about how much she adores him, and whose attempts at bumping-and-grinding are meant to be hilarious because she's so plain. And this in a show that claims to be empowering women. Parton herself appears via video-clip to reinforce the claim, and to begin and end the story: fittingly perhaps, given that even in absentia she's the chief selling point. They could call the show Hello Again, Dolly (The Musical), but it might not be legal.

- 9 to 5 The Musical runs through July 10 at the Toronto Centre for the Arts.
