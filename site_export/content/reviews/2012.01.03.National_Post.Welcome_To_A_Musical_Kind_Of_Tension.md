THEATRE REVIEW

American Idiot

Toronto Centre for the Arts

The title American Idiot almost invites its own rejoinder, something about sound and fury and what it signifies. To be fair, the shows authors are probably smart enough to have seen that coming. To be further fair, the production does signify something, and something important. It signifies a generation's impotent rage. That much comes over loud and clear. Loud, anyway.

It signifies, but it doesn't illuminate. Officially entitled Green Days American Idiot, it represents both a nadir (so far; things can always get worse) and a culmination for the rock musical. It takes an album of minimal dramatic content, and diffuses that content even more by splitting its story three ways. We have Johnny, living a frustrated teen-or-twenty-something life in the suburbs of whats bitingly identified as Jingletown, U.S.A., and then moving to the city where things feel even worse.

He has two mates: Tunny, whose despair leads him to enlist for war, presumably in Iraq, from which he returns an amputee; and Will, who decides at the last moment to stay home because he's got his girlfriend pregnant. Will, who doesn't seem to have any, is the ultimate couch potato, staying in that position for the entire evening.

Different things may happen to these guys, but theyre still interchangeable blanks. The writing gives them no chance to be anything else. The music, credited to Green Day en masse, is mainly a series of indistinguishable ear-splitters, sometimes throwing off a stabbing or appetizing melodic phrase but never developing any of them. Interspersed are a few gentler tunes; the most appealing, Last Night on Earth, sounds, intentionally or not, like a pastiche of a 50s teenage ballad.

As always the music drowns out the words; though on examination, they don't add much. Billie Joe Armstrong's lyrics work best through simple repetition, as in, I dont care if you dont care," reiterated as a bonding ritual between the three heroes. Anything more ambitious is defeated by a mixture of pretentiousness and technical incompetence, two things that tend to go together; a lyricist who doesn't know where the stress falls on the word "today or how it should sit on the music is unlikely to have anything interesting to say on weightier matters. Let's not even get into the question of rhyme; why use it, if youre not prepared to do it right? Anyway the overall result is of portentous vagueness: OK for a rock concert, but fatal for a show with narrative intentions.

Example: Sept. 11 is never mentioned, but is presumed to hang over the action. So I guess the group lament Wake Me Up When September Ends is supposed to have historical resonance, rather than just being the latest in a long line of wistful September songs. Its defeated, though, by the rock lyric's traditional inability to hold a thought or an image from one line to the next. Further example: Johnny calls himself, and goes on calling himself, the Jesus of Suburbia. The show seems to agree, or at least makes a very big deal of it. On what grounds? He's no kind of preacher, no kind of teacher. Its the latest manifestation of the messianic complex rock has had since the 60s. But Johnny isn't the messiah; he's just a very sulky boy.

He does have a tempter: an apparent emanation of himself called St. Jimmy who, as befits a musical Mephistopheles, likes to make sashaying, head-tossing entrances down a fire escape. He turns Johnny on to heroin and off his girlfriend, affectionately known as What-sername, with whom he had earlier seemed truly smitten.

The performers do what they do, and do it well; I cant write about them individually because none of them is allowed to show any personality. The best part of the production is Steven Hoggetts choreography, which is practically continuous and which finds an expressiveness in brute force for which the rest of the show seems to be straining.

Otherwise, the most notable feature of Michael Mayer's production is its use of multiple video screens: I counted 33 (I needed the distraction) and I bet I missed a few. These are used for atmospheric and commentative purposes: as in, when the lyrics mention the 7-Eleven, a 7-Eleven sign pops up on the screens. For a show that rails, from its second line on, against the media," this one seems awfully proud of its own technology. But maybe this is all very subtle and self-reflexive point. Maybe the show is saying that Johnny's protest is not only pointless, but deserves to be.

Underneath he seems to be a nice old-fashioned boy; he sends postcards home, for Gods sake. Not emails, postcards. The funniest of the few spoken lines concerns not robbing a convenience store. And Johnny ends up back where he belongs, reunited with his friends. Regrets, he has a few; but if theres irony here, it's very hard to detect.

We have, who'da thunk it, an endorsement of Norman Rockwell's America: an anti-youth musical. It makes a change. But for it to be interesting, we would first have to care.
