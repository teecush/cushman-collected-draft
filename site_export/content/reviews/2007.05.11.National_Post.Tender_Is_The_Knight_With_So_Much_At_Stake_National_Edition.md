THEATRE REVIEW

SAINT JOAN

Festival Theatre,

Niagara-on-the-Lake

- - -

A veteran theatre critic once divided the many performances he had seen of George Bernard Shaw's Saint Joan in to tough and tender Joans. By that yardstick, Tara Rosling, in the opening production of this years Shaw Festival, must be ranked among the tender. When she makes her first appearance at the castle of Robert de Baudricourt, whom she cajoles into sending her to the French court, she has the invulnerability of total innocence. Shes sublimely confident, hardly needing to raise her voice to make a point, and in perpetual possession of what can literally be described as a winning smile. It wins her the awed respect of craven kings and seasoned soldiers, virtual command of the French army, and a series of bruising victories over the occupying English. Rosling makes all this credible, and any lingering doubts as to whether her initial brightness can darken, when Joan discovers that not everybody loves a winner after she's won, are conclusively dispelled. Her resolve when Joan finds herself deserted by her allies is both effective and affecting; so is her desolation when, on trial by the Inquisition, she briefly believes herself deserted by God as well.

It's a shining performance, but some things are missing, as they tend to in tender Joans. Cutely cropped though she is, it is hard to believe in this Joan as a soldier among soldiers or even as a military pragmatist. Also, that smile does not translate into actual gaiety, the common-sense humour with which Joan deflates so many of her adversaries. Roslings voice pipes rather than chuckles; when a judge at her trial rebukes her for giving pert answers, it rings oddly as what we have been hearing from her are mystified sobs. There are times when this Joan seems reduced to a supporting player in her own drama. And though the final cry of the rejected saint, How long, O lord, how long? might well pierce heaven, it is less successful at piercing the theatre.

This is partly because it lacks context. As Shaw wrote it, Joans cri du coeur concludes an epilogue -- the Dauphin's dream -- that brings us up to date on the burned heretic's rehabilitation and canonization and shows her former friends and enemies first worshipping her and then, once again, deserting her. Its a scene that directors and commentators have traditionally found troublesome, though in fact it does its job well. Jackie Maxwells solution at the Shaw is to turn the epilogue into a prologue. Before the official text gets started, we have a ghostly parade of characters mouthing a condensed and re-ordered version of what would normally come at the end. It is clever (though I should think anyone who does not know the play must be wondering who all these people are) and it does cast an interesting ironic pall over what follows; we know from the start that Joan does not stand a chance, before or after death. But it means that when we get to where the epilogue should be, there is virtually nothing left of it. The finale does not have a build.

The prologue is performed, against a hideously starry sky, in costumes suggesting the First World War, and the period keeps switching thereafter between the early 20th century and Joan's own early 15th. It makes a sort of sense; Shaw's play premiered only five years after the so-called Great War, which has in any case become accepted theatrical shorthand for any kind of armed conflict. But if it does not do any harm, it does not do any good either. The message seems to be that all wars are essentially the same and equally damnable. Shaw, the pacifist, might well have agreed but it does not happen to be what this particular play is about; if it was, Joan would have to be one of its villains, and the production certainly does not present her that way. There is a jokey line about Hell being preferable to the French wars; its delivered here with an ominous solemnity, which as always with Shaw makes it not more effective but less.

The productions great virtue is the overall strength of its acting, and even more of its interacting. Ben Carlson, unexpectedly cast old, brings all his pace and lucidity to Bishop Cauchon, and as his hostile ally the Earl of Warwick, Blair Williams is suave perfection. Their debate on Joan's spiritual and political heresies is the Shaw Festival doing what it does, or should do, best. Though placing this scene, the only one in which Joan herself does not appear, just before the interval does not do the heroine's status any favours. The scenes actually a three-hander; Peter Krantz, as the fanatically patriotic English chaplain, splendidly humanizes a one-joke role, while his later breakdown when confronted by the reality of her execution brought me closer to tears than the Maid herself ever did. Reaction to tragedy is often more moving, because its more accessible than tragedy itself; a fact also demonstrated on a smaller scale by Andrew Bunker as the more constructively traumatized Brother Martin. Harry Judge is a fey, funny Dauphin, Patrick Mc- Manus a terrifically likeable Dunois and Thom Marriott makes so much of the bullying La Tremouille (who "pretends to command the army") that his early exit, however a fair judgment on the character, is downright cruel to the audience. - In repertoire through October 27.
