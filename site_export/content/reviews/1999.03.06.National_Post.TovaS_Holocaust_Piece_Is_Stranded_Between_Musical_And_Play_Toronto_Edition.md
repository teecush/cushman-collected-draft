STILL THE NIGHT

Factory Theatre, Toronto

Theresa Tova's Still the Night is the story of the author's mother, and of her ordeal in wartime Poland as a Jewish teenager in flight from the Nazis. It comes wearing the badge of authenticity, and this is its greatest -- or anyway its most obvious -- strength. It is also a musical, and this, though useful in some ways, is its greatest weakness.

There are actually two girls on the run, cousins: Bryna and Little Bryna. Mary Ellen Mahoney, last seen by me as the hero's man- eating fiancee in Crazy for You, now wades into radically deeper waters to play both Big Bryna and her daughter Tybele who is the author-surrogate. The show's framework depicts mother and daughter living in Calgary, the daughter teasing out the mother's memories, which are then enacted for us. This -- following How I Learned To Drive, Stone Angel and Alberta's Two Words for Snow -- is the fourth play I have seen this still-young year that requires its leading actor to alternate constantly and instantaneously between an older and a younger self. It is the first that also requires her to play two separate characters who have conversations together, and Mahoney -- switching positions and personalities like a ventriloquist switching voices -- does it niftily.

We get a leisurely look at the two Brynas in the Polish countryside before the nightmare starts. Big Bryna's father is a factory man and a prominent local Communist; Little Bryna's is a farmer and apolitical. Little Bryna is unsophisticated, goes to a Jewish school, and loves Yiddish-American movies starring Molly Picon; Big Bryna talks tough and smart, goes to a Christian school and prefers Ginger Rogers. All this is a frame within a frame, and the show devotes rather too long a time to setting up the normality of their lives before it is brutally shattered. But once it is -- once the Nazis have taken both girls' parents and they set off to join the Yiddish partisans in the forest, it is as gripping as you would expect.

Along the way they are raped (with a resultant childbirth), tortured, and almost hanged; their successive ordeals are played out in passages of jagged mime, backed by harsh percussive music from the band. But they win through, their friendship inevitably stretched but apparently unbroken. They are the only characters we see, but one of the show's achievements is that it never feels like a two-person show. Script and performance make us aware, without any awkwardness or strain, of the hostile forces all around.

At the end of the war, in comparative safety, they do break up. Little Bryna emigrates to Israel and is apparently contented. Big Bryna goes first to France, then to Canada, and grows up embittered, hugging her pain. The show finds plenty to admire in her wit and resourcefulness but it does not pretend that suffering necessarily ennobles. When the two heroines are reunited after 30 years, they have nothing to say to one another: one believes in forgiveness, the other in grudge. It is left to daughter Tybele to make her own pilgrimage to the kibbutz and achieve some kind of reconciliation with her aunt.

We see Big Bryna keeping a store in Calgary, fighting, haggling and wheedling as she learned to do in the war. We can look the other way, remember the girls sneaking into movies as children, and see it as a rehearsal for the deadlier games ahead. Liza Balkan as Little Bryna is pertly convincing when playing young, and even better -- weathered, stooped and grandmotherly -- when playing old.

But there are holes in the development. Mahoney, tall and rangy in red, looks and sounds good but still offers a display of technique rather than a character. The ventriloquial technique doesn't allow for any real confrontation between mother and daughter, though that's what the show seems to be heading toward. Tom Diamond has staged the show incisively, on a set by Mary Kerr dominated by a tall step-ladder and a huge hammer-and-sickle and reminiscent of the expressionist theatre of the `30s. But the dialogue scenes are sometimes out of focus.

The play was first seen (though not by me) in 1996 when it was acclaimed. There have been two major changes this time around, and I suspect that they are both damaging. Mahoney's role was originally played by Tova herself, and though I have no idea how their performances compare, the author's presence must have upped the emotional stakes considerably. Then more songs have been added. The score comes in three varieties; there are authentic Yiddish songs in translation, there is specially written material, and there are a couple of barely relevant American standards. The old songs never quite fit the context; they might work as Brechtian commentary, but they're sung in character. They may add some ethnic flavouring, but dramatically they get in the way. The new ones, collaborations between Tova and John Alcorn, are lightweight: off-Broadway music and lyrics whose images and sentiments are taken from stock. They simply aren't up to the subject, and no wonder: with the partial (and special-case) exception of Cabaret, no musical has touched the Holocaust without cheapening it.

Music is the most powerful of all stylistic devices, and its presence allows dramatists to take chances, narrative short-cuts, that might not otherwise be possible. It can also raise the temperature, but here the emotions come between the songs. The script is stranded somewhere between being a fully-realized play and a musical book. That doesn't destroy it; the show, which is touring the country, is well worth seeing. But don't expect a masterpiece.
