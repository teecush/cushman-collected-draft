THEATRE REVIEW

Onegin Berkeley Street Theatre Downstairs, Toronto From Russia with love, as it keeps pausing to remind us, comes the new Canadian musical Onegin.

The Russia part: the show is based on Alexander Pushkin's novel-in-verse Eugene Onegin, published in 1833 and one of the foundational stones of Russian literature. It's been a musical before of course, courtesy of Tchaikovsky, whose 1879 opera gets equal acknowledgment with Pushkin's poem as the source material.

The love part: Evgeni Onegin (no truck here with Anglicising his Christian name) is a fashionably bored young nobleman who comes into an inheritance and is bored with that too. He gets invited to the estate down the road by his friend Lensky, who is betrothed to Olga, one of the two daughters of the house. Olga's older sister Tatyana, a great reader, beholds Onegin and sees in him the hero of every romantic novel she has ever devoured. Cool cat that he is, he finds the intensity of her attentions alarming, and backs off. At a subsequent ball, he flirts instead with the spoken-for Olga. Her fiance Lensky is a decent type but what chance does that have against the local incarnation of Lord Byron? A duel ensues. In its aftermath Onegin discovers that he too has a heart. But of course it's too late.

So, it's high emotions in a fabled literary landscape, a combination that may move contemporary sensibilities to envy or to mockery. Veda Hille and Amiel Gladstone, who wrote words and music here with Gladstone also the director, seem to want it both ways. A spirited but overextended opening number sets us up for spoof. The actors introduce us to the characters they're playing, laying jovial emphasis on how passionate and Slavic their story is going to be. They even invite us to take a drink, every time the Russian word for "love" is mentioned; which, the show being in English, turns out to be not that often. They also interrogate members of the audience about their relationship status - "yes" they threaten "it's going to be that kind of a show."

Again, and mercifully, it turns out not to be, or not much. There are, when the story proper starts, a few more hints at jokey anachronism. The band (three pieces including cello - unusual) is announced as the Ungrateful Dead, and when one of the characters perishes he is said to have joined their immortally thankless ranks. But it isn't made clear if the whole thing is meant to be taking place in some kind of limbo, and it's even less clear why.

It is possible to make fun of a classic while still preserving its emotional core - especially, or so it seems, a Russian classic. Stupid F--ing Bird did it recently with Chekhov's The Seagull. The current Broadway hit and strong Tony contender Natasha, Pierre and the Great Comet of 1812, to judge from the cast album, manages the same for War and Peace, no less. (Of course that book gives a lot of room for manoeuvre.) In Onegin, though, the facetious trappings throttle the feelings. The story and the characters never come to life. And since the show has virtually no book, in the sense of spoken dialogue, this has to be the fault of the score.

More exactly, it's the fault of half the score. The music, mostly melodic rock, can be very expressive. The lyrics, though, can't. They're often difficult to hear (but this is Berkeley Street, one of Toronto's several homes of the awful acoustic) but what it does get through is generalized and opaque. The show follows Hamilton in including not jut a duel but a number about the rules of same; it seems intended to be at least partly comic but it's hard to be sure.

The day of combat does however furnish the score's best song, a fearful threnody beautifully sung by Josh Epstein's Lensky as he contemplates his fate. Onegin himself is a surprisingly subdued Darren A. Herbert; his best moment is his first, a silent appearance as a baleful figure perched in a casement on Denyse Karn's ruined pile of a set, caught as in a bolt of lightning. Tatyana (Hailey Gillis) and Olga (Elena Juatco) are similar beyond the demands of sisterhood, except that Tatyana is much the taller of the two. The cast is completed by Rebecca Auerbach as a managing mother given nothing to manage, and by Shane Carty and Peter Fernandes as various supporting males; Carty is a genial commentator-inchief; Fernandes as a conceited French suitor has a long solo that is probably intended to be funny.

The show is produced by the Musical Stage Company, formerly Acting Up Stage, the enterprising group whose previous productions include the same writers'Craigslist Cantata. I wasn't that show's greatest fan, but on current evidence its revue format suited the authors better than the narrative format they've swung for here.
