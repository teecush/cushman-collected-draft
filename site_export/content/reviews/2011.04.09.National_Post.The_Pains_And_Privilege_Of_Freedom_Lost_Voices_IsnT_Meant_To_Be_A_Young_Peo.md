Isaw Lost Voices at a students matinee, which was probably the best way to see it. Ed Roy's play is the kind that takes an issue, and then attempts to build a drama around it. In this case, the subject is the plight of child refugees. Two early teenagers -a girl from India, a boy from Pakistan -arrive, separately and unaccompanied, at Pearson airport, where the authorities have to decide what to do with them. The emotional stakes are high enough, and the narrative tension strong enough, for the students -schoolkids actually -who comprised the audience to have sat quiet and attentive. I was held myself.

However, Topological Theatre, which presents the production, is not primarily a young people's company -at least it makes no claims to be -and if the show is judged as a fleshedout drama for grown-ups, then big holes start appearing. The play might actually do better as a documentary piece that concentrated on the fleeing kids. In fact, it devotes as much time to the Canadian childrens aid workers who are handling their cases, and who have their own problems. Its obviously intended that the two strands will enrich one another, and there are occasional moments when this kind of dramatic cross-fertilization does seem about to happen. Mostly though, the stories just get in one another's way. Each prevents the other from being explored at anything but the most rudimentary level.

The youngsters are Nabeela, whose abusive father has sold her to a trafficker who has a buyer for her in Canada, and Wakeed, who is fleeing from the Taliban after they have burned a school with his two sisters inside it. The officials investigating them are Terri, a veteran in the field who's experiencing burnout, and Phil, whos just off his training course and whom Terri is supposed to be mentoring, something for which she warns him at the start she has neither the time nor the patience. Terri gives Phil a very hard time, something for which he thanks her in the end, though its not very clear why.

For the most part, Terri deals with Nabeela and Phil with Wakeed; whether the gender alignment is deliberate or coincidental is something else that remains unclear. Their tasks are made harder by the fact that initially neither of their clients will say a word; their interviewers aren't even sure they understand English, though the audience has reason to think otherwise. We've heard their inner monologues addressed to the more sympathetic of the folks back home (Nabeelas mother, both of Wakeeds parents), and these have all been in English. These may, of course, have been translated for our benefit, but it's still obvious that the characters are going to have to find some words if the play is to proceed. In fact, their initial silence -part caution, part trauma -doesn't strain credibility; what does is the extent of their bilingual facility once their lost voices are found. With Wakeed it all starts with the word pizza; Terri has told Phil that everybody likes pizza, and it may be the soundest advice she gives him. Wakeed even likes it for breakfast, which takes Phil aback.

Nabeela learned English by running errands for an Englishwoman, Wakeed by fraternizing with a Canadian army sergeant, in scenes that suggest some future CBC radio series called Pakistanada. (The plays view of the Canadian military mission seems to be that its a good idea that doesn't work.) The details surrounding Nabeela's arrival in Toronto are murky. She is said to have been accompanied by a mysterious "uncle" who then deserted her; in which case, how was the transaction supposed to work? Both have mixed feelings about remaining in Canada: Nabeela fears for her younger sisters left to her fathers mercies, Wakeed fears for his "honour." Their background scenes are written in flat, stock terms but their situations, and all the other blighted childhoods that they represent, are strong enough to involve us, just as, despite all professional protocol, they involve Phil and Terri.

Those two, however, do not have their charges built-in dramatic advantages. We learn that Phil is a son of privilege, trying to compensate; we learn what motivated Terri's choice of profession (and it turns out to be the biggest cliche in the whole play). The two of them lecture one another on their respective failings, without convincing us that these exist for any other reason than to fill out the play. The pressures on them, ferociously overworked and under-supported as they are, arouse our concern; they themselves don't. Its neat that they both should achieve a breakthrough when they temporarily swap cases; its frustrating, to put it very mildly, that the resolution of both those cases should take place offstage. Sarena Parmar and Owais Lightwala give sympathetic performances as the young refugees. So, as far as the play allows, do Soo Garay and Shaun McComb as their elders; if Garay's flights off the handle sometimes seem forced, that's probably unavoidable. Both actors also play the older characters in the flashbacks, more earnestly than persuasively. Its perhaps in recognition of their awkwardness that the author, whos also his own director, keeps most of these scenes to the side of the stage half-hidden behind curtains. The centre, meanwhile, stays bare: an apt if unintended metaphor for the play itself. Still, it isn't pretentious.
