THEATRE REVIEW THE SERVANT

Monday, June 11, 1990

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Written by Robin Maugham Directed by Allan Davis Starring Keir Dullea and David Ferry At the Bayview Playhouse Stage version of Servant better than the 1960s film BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail The Servant is best known as a movie: a typical early 60s piece of swinging Gothic, directed by Joseph Losey from a script by Harold Pinter. Before that, however, it was a novella by Robin Maugham, Somerset's nephew. He subsequently turned it into a play, which on Friday became the first offering at the re-opened Bayview Playhouse.

Pinter or no Pinter, the play is more satisfying than the film, which was high on atmosphere and low on story. The present staging is generally lacklustre, sometimes inept, redeemed by a couple of good performances, one of them mercifully in the star role. Nevertheless, its grip on the audience is hammerlock.

Tony is a young Englishman, back home after several uncomfortable years in Africa and possessed of "a moderate sum" of money. This seems an understatement; everything's relative, of course, but Tony's modest fortune enables him to buy and maintain a large house in one of the most expensive parts of London and to engage a full-time manservant to maintain it and him. The man in question is Barrett, an opaquely obsequious Midlander (actually from D.H. Lawrence country) with a Jeevesian degree of domestic competence. He also cooks like Antoine, the fabled Frenchman who cosseted and tyrannised Bertie Wooster's Aunt Dahlia. This puts him ahead of Jeeves whose culinary talents may have been limited to the preparation of hangover cures - admittedly a service which Tony stands, or reels, in increasing need of as the action proceeds.

The play might be called The Secret Life of Jeeves and Wooster. It shows the man's manipulative skills employed to subjugate and humiliate the master, with the latter's increasingly enthusiastic co-operation.

It seems that Barrett can give him everything, including sex. There is an implicit homosexual bond between them, and an explicit heterosexual one; Barrett instals in succession two of his own girl-friends, each with instructions to minister to the young master while remaining in thrall to the servant. Submissive by profession, he gets his kicks from dominance.

Territorial struggle, sado-masochism, the idle rich: no wonder the material attracted Pinter (and was baroqued asleep by Losey.) Maugham's own treatment is straightforward and economical, the erotic games outlined but never demonstrated. So a production needs to be stylishly suggestive. Allan Davis's hovers between blatancy and embarrassment.

The program gives the setting as "Chelsea, London, England, Today" and we get "floppy disks" in the dialogue, current pop on the speakers (with actors writhing zombie-like to their Walkmans), and remote control for the onstage television. But the play is a period piece, immutably ensconced in the innocent decadence of post-Profumo Britain. It precedes the boom that preceded the slump. Tony is extinct, replaced by young men equally callow but of tougher fibre, who made their own fortunes and probably have no souls to lose. Even more antediluvian are his bovine long-suffering fiancee and old school chum, forever turning up to receive confidences and - in this production - perching on the arm or back of the sofa for the purpose. Yes, people do that in real life but not very often and not in other people's homes. It is meant to denote casual ease but actually suggests a director at his wit's end to vary the blocking.

However, there is Keir Dullea. He is best known for his leading non- role as one of the astronauts in 2001, and second-best known for having elicited from Noel Coward the salutation "Keir Dullea, gone tomorrow." But, no, he is still here, and an excellent actor.

As Barrett he is socially and geographically exact (lone distinctions in this cast), with an exact balance in his early scenes between courtesy and insolence. Initially he seems ageless and maybe sexless but still a threat; anyone this confident has to be. Above stairs he is content to hover and shimmer and, in an extremity, apologise. His moment comes when Tony takes the fatal step of invading his kitchen. This is a hostile kingdom and Barrett, who has spoken in humble sentences, unlooses a tirade. Dullea takes it thrillingly. Then he subsides, without ever relinquishing his authority. "Mister Tony" who was once "Sir" becomes "Tony": not even an equal, an inferior. It is finely noted in the text, firmly nailed in the acting.

The only performance of comparable command is Erin McMurtry's as Second Bimbo. Unfortunately her role is brief and her appearance long delayed. Her predecessor, Sally Cahill, flounces and bounces - which is an improvement on Eric McCormack's earnestness and Jayne Lewis's plain immobility as the two upper-class stooges.

Which leaves us with the lad himself. If you remember the movie and James Fox's elongated Tony, David Ferry's stumpy, uneasy equivalent will confound you, being devoid of social graces in a play that largely hinges on them. However as Tony descends his personal slope, he comes within the actor's reach; his coarseness becomes convincing, and piteously naked. He is a lost boy. You resent and feel sorrow for him at once, which is as it should be.
