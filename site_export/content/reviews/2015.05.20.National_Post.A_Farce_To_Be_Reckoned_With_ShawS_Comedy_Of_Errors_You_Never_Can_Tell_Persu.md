Theatre review

You Never Can Tell Royal George Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

The Shaw Festivals opening production of You Never Can Tell begins as a pain and ends as a delight. The pain doesnt have anything to do with the first scene being set in a dentist's office. Valentine, the philandering hero of this early and exceedingly popular Shaw comedy (it may hold the record for productions at the festival) does perform a couple of extractions there, but they're both under anaesthetic: with gas, five shillings extra, laughing gas being Shaws stock-in-trade as well as his autobiographical protagonists. Valentine plies his trade in an English seaside town, and we all like to be beside the seaside. But Jim Mezon's production lays on the atmosphere excessively thick and also with excessive coyness. We start with a jerky bit of ancient home movie (the play's date is 1897); and when we get to the beachfront hotel thats the principal setting, we find it done up, in Leslie Frankishs designs, with corresponding cuteness. When the characters gather for a confrontational lunch on the terrace, the seating arrangements turn out to be oversized beach balls. After intermission, however, all is transformed. The hotel interior is done up in a stunning dark blue with black stripes (or vice versa) and in the last scene, with a fancy dress ball happening just off the stage and occasionally erupting onto it, the magic multiplies. The design throughout has favoured mobiles and motifs; the principal ones at the end are bisected slices of orange, plastered all over the walls. The stage is drenched in carnival which, with characters appearing in masks and false noses or decked out as Harlequin and Columbine, is just what the playwright ordered and what his play - a romantic comedy that's at once disenchanted and celebratory - demands.

Or perhaps call it a romantic farce. The chief narrative thread is the penniless, or shillingless, Valentine's wooing of Gloria Clandon, the forward-thinking daughter of a forward-thinking mother. Mrs. Clandon Senior (not all that senior in this rendition) has authored a series of 19th-century books on 20thcentury living. She has also taken Gloria and her twin siblings off to Madeira, thus removing them and herself from a husband and father she claimed was abusive. Returning to England, she finds herself in the same town as her ex, who also happens to be Valentines landlord and onetime-only patient. One of the twins refers unabashedly to "the long arm of coincidence."

I have never been able to take much interest in the courtship of Valentine and Gloria which, even more than most in Shaw, is a matter of words, words, words. In this production, its more than usually askew. As Valentine, the self-proclaimed Duellist of Sex, Gray Powell, all mind and mischief, could hardly be bettered.

But his instant attraction to Julia Course's Gloria is a mystery; it can't be intellectual, since when they first meet, shes virtually silent, which is the playwrights fault; and it's unlikely to be physical, since she's practically invisible between her huge hat, her stern glasses, and her bun of red hair, a combination that can be blamed on the director, the designer, and a character concept, reasonable in theory, that in practice has been carried altogether too far. One keeps waiting for the moment, familiar from old movies, when she will let down her hair, take off her specs, and become radiant. And it does indeed happen, all but the radiance part.

Even when supposedly surrendering, she never melts, never unbends. Theres no laughter in her voice, and in Shaw, thats a disabling defect.

There are plenty of compensations, however, in both script and performance. The crown jewel is Peter Millard as William, the omniscient and omnicompetent waiter, the hotel's master of ceremonies and, in this production and with increasing effectiveness, the play's as well. Besides wit and tact, he conveys genuine agony - its more than just embarrassment - when his guests momentarily insist on treating him as an equal; and overpowering relief when they restore him his identity by ordering drinks; its both moving and amusing, and sends ripples of acute social comment out into the auditorium.

It's also good to see a takenfor-granted Festival actor getting a main chance and taking it. Others in full control of what they're saying and doing are Peter Krantz as a solicitor with a heart and Jeff Meadows as a barrister apparently without one but with a terrifying complement of booming common sense; being a renowned legal magician he comes to the party got up as something out of the Arabian Nights. The estranged couple (played by an actual non-estranged couple) are patchier: Tara Rosling communicates all Mrs. Clandons anger and pain but is less persuasive as an oracular authoress; Patrick McManus, as the one who done her wrong but has suffered himself, has to work far too hard at being a bully but comes into his own when allowed to be vulnerable, accommodating and happy, in that chronological order. That leaves us with their offspring, the twins, written in a style of heightened precocity that seems almost impossible to recapture. Jennifer Dzialoszynski has the easier task of the two, and after an initial five minutes of exaggerated pertness, she settles happily down into being bouncy and teasing. Stephen Jackman-Torkoff has to bear the brunt of the archness and, being very tall, settles for High Camp; it isnt right, and it can be cloying, but it's some kind of solution and, like practically everything else in the show, it gets better and more persuasive as it goes on.

You Never Can Tell is in repertory until Oct. 25. For tickets and information, visit shawfest.com
