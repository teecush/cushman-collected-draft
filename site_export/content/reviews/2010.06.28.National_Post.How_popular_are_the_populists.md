PETER PAN, EVITA & JACQUES BREL IS ALIVE AND WELL AND LIVING IN PARIS

Stratford Shakespeare Festival

---

To be sung to the tune, if you know it, of Bobby and Jackie and Jack, the pastiche revue number about the Kennedys, from Merrily We Roll Along: There's Peter, Evita, and Jacques:

The final three cards in the pack.

One is the boy with a magic touch,

And one's a dictatress who spent too much,

And one wrote a song that's about the Dutch -

That's Jacky and Eva and Pete:

One flying, one dying, one beat.

Tim Carroll's production of J.M. Barrie's Peter Pan is just about perfect. It's spectacular without being overpowering, tender without being cloying (a feat with this play), insidiously disturbing without getting in anyone's face. It's also expensive without being profligate; every cent looks well spent. Carolyn M. Smith's sets take us from a spacious-but-cozy Edwardian nursery to a Neverland whose every detail is playfully evoked -- Barrie, like Shakespeare dreaming up the Forest of Arden, wrote an adventure playground that left nothing out.

The casting is unbeatable. Michael Therriault's Peter is boyhood personified: all charm, all brashness, all conceit, with just enough empathy to feel lonely. He is ever so friendly, makes whimsy seem practical, and is scarily unreachable. The production's final image, showing him outside the forever-barred nursery window, is the logical culmination of the performance as well as of the story, the more haunting for not being forced. All the children's roles are played by adults, all of whom seem to having a roaringly good but perfectly disciplined time. Sara Topham may have found her ideal role as Wendy, whom she makes enchantingly bossy and more self-aware by the moment, while Paul Dunn, has certainly found his as the touchy adolescent John; he's a man playing a boy playing a man. Stacie Steadman rounds out the Darling siblings as a piping Michael, proudly possessive of his teddy bear. The Lost Boys are as distinctive as the parented ones, with Shane Carty a stand-out as Slightly.

The pirates are, except in their costume and makeup, less colourful than the kids, maybe because they're written that way; Barrie seems to have spent more energy on describing them than on dramatising them. Tom McCamus, like every other actor I have seen in the role, fails to solve the conundrum of Hook, which is that he's spoken of as frightening but written as almost entirely comic. His death, flinging himself into the jaws of the croc-with-the-clock, seems to come out of nowhere. McCamus still makes a fine fop of him, but the real delight comes from his other role as narrator: Barrie himself, trying out the stage directions and experimenting with different versions of a text with many variants. It's been done before, but never to this extent or with this finesse.

The attendant bestiary includes the longest, meanest, crocodile you'll ever see, not to mention the best motivated; we know what makes him tick. The director, who's British, has a lot of Shakespeare in his resume; Stratford must bring him back.

It's been a fine batch of Festival openings; if the remaining two shows let down the side a bit, that's mainly because of the material. Not quite so much with Evita: Andrew Lloyd Webber isn't the musical Antichrist and this may be his best score. Tim Rice's lyrics, especially in the opening Oh What a Circus make intelligent satiric points, though his words don't usually sit well on the music; some of his jokes would sound funnier spoken, which is a working definition of bad lyric-writing. Also, his Che's initial indictment of his Eva ( "she did nothing for years") turns out to be, dramatically speaking, all too true; once she's bed-hopped her way to the top, she subsides into inaction, until it's time for her to expire. The show sometimes assumes she's done stuff, but that's not the same thing. It's all very static.

Gary Griffin's production is handsomely staged, and as lucid as possible, and Chilina Kennedy does something unprecedented in rock musicals: she doesn't act the style, she acts the character. Instead of all-purpose brass, she offers vulnerability, believable in the early scenes and invaluable in the later ones. Juan Chioran does everything possible and more than most for Peron, and Josh Young is a decently charismatic Che. Josie Marasco is the discarded Mistress who exists solely to sing another Another Suitcase in Another Hall, a rotten song that serves no purpose. So Pan, Mrs. Peron, and Brel: From Heaven, through Limbo, to...

Well, you get the point. The mendaciously titled Jacques Brel Is Alive and Well and Living in Paris has always been one of my least favourite shows, and it remains so even in Stafford Arima's unusually inventive production. Brel wrote monotonous melodies, that fake emotion by getting progressively faster and faster -- and, as sung by the two ladies in the cast, Jewelle Blackman and Nathalie Nadon, louder and louder.

Thewords, theEnglishwords anyway, are awful; the few sung in French go with more of a swing, but the translations, by Eric Blau and Mort Shuman, are best described in the tag-line of their lyric for Jackie: "Completely cute, cute in a stupid-arse way." That song itself doesn't seem to know what it's about from one sentence to the next; it's incoherence masquerading as profundity. Brent Carver sings with devout intensity, but the evening belongs to Mike Nadajewski, who appears to have nabbed all the best material, including one song ( Next, about an army recruit lining up for STD treatment after a first encounter) that even I liked. He has ferocity and he has humour. Double, even triple, commendation for Rick Fox, musical director for Evita, musical supervisor and arranger for Brel: this Fox knows many things.

- Peter Pan is in repertory through Oct. 31; Evita through Nov. 6; Jacques Brel Is Alive and Well and Living in Paris through Sept. 25.
