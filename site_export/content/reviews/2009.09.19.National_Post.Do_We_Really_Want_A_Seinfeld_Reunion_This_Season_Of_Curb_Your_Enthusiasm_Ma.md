It's ancient history now, but I've always thought that the finale of Seinfeld was underrated. I don't think it quite worked, and it certainly shouldn't have taken two hours, but it wasn't the cop-out that it was accused of being. It ended, you may recall, with the four central characters being lectured by a judge on their anti-social behaviour and sent down accordingly. After years of making us laugh at, and sympathize with, the quartet's irresponsibility, the show seemed to be turning around and condemning it. Steady on: The courtroom was filled with the stars' friends, relatives, ex-lovers and former victims, all dissociating themselves as speedily and sanctimoniously as possible. If they were respectable society, you could have it. The laugh -- the show's laugh -- was on them. Our heroes -- well, three heroes and one heroine -- may have ended up behind bars but they were happily, and rightly, unrepentant.

Well, that's how I saw it, and Larry David agrees with me. David, of course, wrote the show in collaboration with Jerry Seinfeld, and is often credited with being the brains and the sensibility behind it. Since then, also of course, he has had his own show Curb Your Enthusiasm in which he incarnates in his own person all the homely vices that previously had to be split four ways. I bring this up now because, as has been amply advertised, the new seventh season of Curb Your Enthusiasm revolves around the question of a Seinfeld reunion. And when Larry tries to get Jerry interested in the idea, one of Jerry's responses is that it might at least make people forget that awful finale. To which Larry says that he doesn't think it was awful at all.

Curb your enthusiasm. The Seinfeld connection doesn't crop up until the third episode. The first two find Larry behaving in the self-serving manner we have come to love and identify with. His new girlfriend Loretta has been diagnosed with possible cancer (she looks perfectly healthy; the show isn't that brave), and Larry is trying to get out of the relationship before a final diagnosis comes through and leaves him looking an irredeemable heel. The second show, which is entitled "Vehicular Fellatio," also incorporates a running gag about guys who appear to be getting blow jobs in their cars from wives or girlfriends, and how Larry keeps jumping to wrong conclusions, or at least to the right conclusions at the wrong moment. The publicity describes his character as "brutally honest," but I don't think he'd lay claim to that kind of courage. More often he's just tactless.

So, flashing forward a couple of weeks: Larry meets with a network chief (very convincingly young and smarmy) who desperately wants a Seinfeld reunion show. Larry refuses on the grounds that reunion shows are lame. This of course is double bluff-- the show's, not the character's -- designed to set up a reunion that we will accept as not being lame, as if the accusation could be deflected by being admitted. Or that we will enjoy its lameness, as good post-modernist fun. Or that just seeing the foursome reunited will be pleasure in itself, regardless, in thinking which they may well have a point. Anyway, back in the plot, Larry's objections are overcome by the realization that he may be able to cast his estranged wife (Cheryl Hines) as Jerry's new girlfriend, and hence get her back. Suspense and frustration (the same thing, really) ensue because Jerry, having bought the idea, sees Meg Ryan in the girlfriend role. And the cliffhanger, plus the chance for a star cameo, is that she wants to do it. So the reunion story still has some way to go. Word is that it will be threaded through the rest of the season.

So is it lame? So far it is, fairly. We see Larry talking with all the principals, which means we have to get used to seeing Jason Alexander, Julia Louis-Dreyfus and Michael Kramer addressed by their real names rather than as George, Elaine and Kramer. Of them all, Alexander comes off the best, because he's the best actor; he makes himself into a character, one who's disconcertingly self-possessed. By contrast Jerry is, for a stand-up guy, surprisingly awkward at playing himself.

Also, the reunion idea invites awkward comparisons. Seinfeld still seems, in re-runs, amazingly fresh, canned laughter notwithstanding. It is, after all, the only TV comedy to boast four first-rate characters. Few episodes of Curb withstand a second viewing; their improvisatory style, however winning in itself, becomes very slender, and Larry is essentially on his own; that his best friend's his agent seems only too likely. And maybe even misanthropy has its limits. Larry is selfish, cowardly, stingy, envious, obsessed with sex and status: all the qualities one loves and identifies with. And he's honest about them, at least by comparison with the people he mixes with. But there is a limit to what you can do with them, especially when the protagonist doesn't need to work another day in his life. David is said to have based George on himself, and here he's a George who's rich and successful, which is sort of a contradiction in terms. The show gets by with it, but at a cost. My favourite-ever Seinfeld moment had Alexander-as-George being asked how he lived with himself and replying "it isn't easy." David-as-George has no one to ask him that question, and no need to reply to it. Nobody's going to send him to jail, even in a series of finale. Larry is George without shame, and so without a vital spark. Curb Your Enthusiasm is a paradox: comedy of embarrassment featuring a man who can't be embarrassed. - Curb Your Enthusiasm, Season 7, debuts tomorrow at 9 p. m. ET on HBO Canada.
