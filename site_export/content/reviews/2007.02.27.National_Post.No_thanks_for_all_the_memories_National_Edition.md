THE POLISHED HOE

Enwave Theatre, Harbourfront

- - -

In theory, Colin Taylor and Alison Sealy-Smith have done a neat, economical job of adapting Austin Clarke's novel The Polished Hoe for the stage. The book is, for the most part, a long, purposefully rambling monologue in which Miss Mary Mathilda, doyenne of the Caribbean island of Bimshire, confesses to having murdered the white owner of the local plantation and gives the reasons why. The stage version retains the novel's confessional format, but makes some alterations, logical and sensible ones, to the order in which the information is handed out.

That is not enough to make a play, let alone an enlivening evening in the theatre. Reading the novel is like taking a long walk through very rich mud. The exertion is caused not so much by the intricate patois of Mary Mathilda's speech as by her habit of going off on long digressions. There is a reason for these: They amount to a miniature history of black-white relations in the West Indies. The dead man, symbolically named Mr. Bellfields, had exercised a droit du seigneur on any plantation woman who took his fancy, but Mary had been his long-time favourite (he was her master, she was his mistress) and had fathered her son -- now grown- up and an English-educated doctor. Everybody on the island had reason to hate Mr. Bellfeels (as they call him in the book), and Mary tells us what those reasons were. The way in which she does it is fine on the page, at least for a reader equipped with stamina and patience. Digression in the theatre , though, is death.

And so, even more, is what Kenneth Tynan, reviewing another novel adaptation, described as "exposition in its least dramatic form -- retrospective narration in the first person." That, unrelieved, is what the dramatized Polished Hoe gives us. The only variation comes in there being two police officers to take down the protagonist's statement. First there is the young Constable; he is then relieved by his superior, the Sargeant, a contemporary of Mary's and one who has long admired her from afar. He has his own memories, and the best scene in the play comes when the two go out from the living room of the Bellfields' Great House into the fields themselves; here we at least have the tension of two sets of recollections working against each other.

Otherwise there is no present tense action, no present-tense conflict. This won't do. There are many plays in which much of the action has occurred before the curtain rises, but -- apart from brilliant sketches like Hannah Moscovitch's USSR -- there are no good ones in which everything has. The Polished Hoe often bears a remarkable resemblance to Ibsen's Ghosts, what with its exhumation of an abusive relationship, its hints of an unfulfilled attraction between the two principal characters and its incorporation of a saucy maid throwing herself at an older gentleman (though the Sargeant is far more responsive to Gertrude, Mary Mathilda's young domestic, than Ibsen's Pastor Manders would ever have allowed himself to be). We also, as in Ibsen, get spectral presences located just off the main acting area, but here they aren't the younger generation preparing to repeat the mistakes of their elders and so kick-start the plot into new life; here they really are ghosts, or at least thin flashback figures acting out fragments of Mary's story in a desperate attempt to add some variety to the evening. Things happen in Ghosts. Even in the supposedly action-less Chekhov we get such exciting events as the selling of a cherry orchard -- and The Polished Hoe (a weapon, incidentally, not a woman, though funnily enough that might work) recalls that play, too, with its magnetic chatelaine and her inarticulate admirer. And don't give me Waiting for Godot; stuff happens there too, if only in the ebb and flow of feeling between the two tramps. But nothing happens in The Polished Hoe.

The adaptors don't do much for their own script in their other guises of director and leading player. Taylor's staging and pacing are ponderous. Sealy-Smith, young for her matriarchal role, gives an efficient account of Mary Mathilda, but considering the weight of personal and racial tragedy the character is required to bear and the reverence in which she is said to be held by everybody else on the island, efficiency is not enough to cut it. Nor does it take in the sly humour that permeates the novel and could surely find a place in the play. Lacking these elements, the performance becomes monotonous. Tony Thompson's Sargeant is monotonous too, but his is a rather splendid kind of monotony. This Barbados actor (the show is a co-production by our Obsidian Theatre Company and their Frank Collymore Hall) has a vast, slow, respectful presence, impassive but not unfeeling. Indeed his recollection of a long-lost love (three rejection letters and out) is the one touching moment in the evening. Jamaal Grant also does his bit for the honour of the force as the Constable, just graduating from being wet behind the ears.
