Soulpepper's production of The Threepenny Opera gets to the core of the piece. I would say that it gets to the heart, except that this Bertolt Brecht-Kurt Weill classic doesn't have one. It might, I guess, be accused of having compassion. There are songs in it, especially towards the end, that depict the misery of the poor with an angry bitterness that feels authentic. But they aren't really about social justice.

Brecht wrote the piece in 1928; and in later years, after he'd got religion (i.e. Marxism), he tried in rewrites and prefaces and notes to make it politically correct. But he wasn't a socialist here. He wasn't even an anarchist. He was a nihilist, seeking a righteous pretext for his own violence. It can get monotonous (how many times in one evening do you want to be informed that the world is a heap of excrement?) but it's certainly compelling. There's a consistent vision.

It's Brecht's vision even though Weill, a brilliant chameleon of a composer, carries it along with a score that's alternately abrasive and seductive. It's Brecht's, despite the fact that he doesn't seem to have written much of it. He got the story and characters from John Gay's The Beggar's Opera, the first British musical, whose 18th-century whores and highwaymen he transformed into whores and bank-robbers of no identifiable period. He delegated most of the actual work to his longsuffering mistress and amanuensis, Elisabeth Hauptmann, while taking, in his usual endearing fashion, all the credit himself. The Soulpepper program makes some amends, crediting Hauptmann with "German translation" and Brecht with "adaptation and lyrics." The present English translation of the German translation is by the late Robert David Macdonald, a mischievous Scottish fellow; he, or somebody, has accentuated the apolitical by squeezing into one of the songs a skeptical reference to Karl Marx that I don't remember from any other version.

Something else about the program: It announces a play "in three acts" while also telling us, with perfect truth, that there will only be one intermission. Tim Albery, the director and something of a tease himself, stops the action right in the middle of a scene. It's the one in which Captain Macheath, a.k.a. Mack the Knife, visits his favourite brothel and is betrayed to the police by his favourite doxy whose name, like that of most Brechtian sexworkers, is Jenny and who has been bribed by Mack's unwelcoming new in-laws the Peachums. The break works, as does the re-positioning of two important songs to places where they make greater narrative and atmospheric sense.

The Beggar's Opera was cavalier about plot; the Threepenny update is downright insulting. It takes place in a fantasy London that's Edwardian as much as it's anything but keeps referring to the coronation of Queen Victoria, some 70 years earlier. It thinks the Old Bailey is a prison rather than a courthouse, and has a man condemned to death at an hour's notice and without a trial. Albery tidies up, somewhat, the ramshackle structure while keeping faith with the play's savage inner chaos.

Nothing is more savage than the ending; Mack is saved from the gallows by a royal pardon. In Gay, this is a joke on the audience ("give 'em a happy ending"); in Brecht, it's a joke on the world ("man eats man, and money talks"). In both, the implausibility is freely admitted from the stage. So Gay anticipated Brecht's alienation effect by 200 years. The show puts paid to the idea that Brecht used alienation as a means of keeping his own passionate feelings in check; he took to it naturally, as the perfect vehicle for imposing his own icy coldness on his audience.

The first thing we see in the Soulpepper production is a side of beef, hanging; d'bi.young.anitafrika as the narrating Ballad Singer seems to be playing a butcher's assistant. She rasps rather than sings Mack the Knife, underlining a brutality in the song (this isn't the pop version) that could probably be trusted to do its own work. But she's a glittering presence, and she moves furniture on and off stage with a splendid air of contempt. Crime in this world is a business like any other; Mack, who is thinking of diversifying into banking, is the thug and seducer as tycoon. He's played by Albert Schultz, sleek, slicked-down and heartless. (That's a compliment.) He's only roused to emotion, and some especially powerful singing, by the prospect of his own death. Patricia O'Callaghan is his bride Polly, dainty of voice and walk and steely of grip, while the senior Peachums are gleefully and vindictively played by William Webster and Nancy Palk; Webster punches home his anti-moralizing songs in great style. Stuart Hughes is delightfully Caledonian as Mack's buddy, the Chief of Police (by a careful irony, the only character with feelings) and Jennifer Villaverde as his daughter rises impressively to a vengeful spot of mock-coloratura, she being another of the hero's cast-offs. Mack's gang is excellent.

Lorenzo Savoini's set consists of two platforms. In front, in keeping with the pervasive imagery of gluttony and hunger, is a giant dinner-table; behind is an abattoir or anything else you fancy. The only staging flaw is that too much singing happens beyond the great divide, tough on vocalists who are untrained (mostly) and unamplified (blessedly). Sarah Wilson's performance of Pirate Jenny (the second most famous song) is a blur when it starts upstage, a triumph when it finishes down. This apart, Paul Sportelli's musical direction, with a couple of actors doubling in the band, is superb. The vengeful, menacing ensembles, tightly and fiercely choreographed by Patti Powell, are tremendous; they hate, but the hatred is earned. This is a devil of a show to get right, and this production is almost the best I've seen. On the first night it got cheers, which were deserved; it didn't, despite being both a musical and an opera, get a standing ovation. That's because it chills.
