TOO TRUE TO BE GOOD

Court House Theatre,

Niagara-on-the Lake

Shaw Festival

- - -

The last Bernard Shaw play to begin at Niagara with a parade of masked medics was, logically enough, The Doctor's Dilemma. The same device is now being used to launch the later and lesser-known play Too True to be Good, the pretext being that the action begins in a sickroom. Here lies a very rich, very spoiled and very protected young lady who is suffering from the measles. Suffering still more is the human-sized Microbe who -- big laugh -- has caught it from her. He keeps up an embittered running commentary on the first act, which in print at least, is very funny. It is destroyed here by a ghastly performance from William Vickers who shouts, moans, coughs - - does everything except simply and humorously talk. Shaw once told an actor that it was impossible to overact in his plays. He had a point. But there is a difference between purposeful extravagance (this play is sub-titled "a political extravaganza") and the underlining, and consequent undermining, of every joke.

A gaffe like this is likely to be the fault of the director rather than the actor. Which is strange because, in most other respects, Jim Mezon's production is exemplary: It's the Shaw Festival's best Shaw production in the last couple of years and a cast-iron example of what this institution is for. It has a terrific trio of central performances. Nicole Underhay is a ferocious ball of perfectly-targeted energy as the Patient rescued from hearth, home and hypochondria by a couple of neophyte crooks who steal her insanely valuable pearl necklace so that all three of them can live on the proceeds. Kelli Fox is a lot of fun as the Nurse, a cockney con-artist who cheekily passes herself off as an Italian countess. Blair Williams plays the brains of the operation, or the intellect minus practicality, though his true vocation has always been for preaching.

The burglary takes up the first act. At the end of it, the Microbe announces that "the play is now virtually over but the characters will discuss it at great length for two more acts." ("The exit doors" he adds, "are all in order.") This of course is Shaw poking characteristic fun at his own loquacious reputation. It also happens to be pretty much true. The three tricksters arrive in an unnamed "mountainous country" that could be in North Africa, the Middle East or the Caribbean. There is a British force here commanded by Col. Tallboys, an amiable blimp with ambitions for a knighthood. He is played by Benedict Campbell, who has never been better and who wields a teapot with true-blue English aplomb, at one point jabbing it an opponent's ribs. The actual work of the regiment is done by the omnicompetent Pte. Meek, based on Shaw's friend Lawrence of Arabia and played by Andrew Bunker with sublime self- effacement. This is a master-servant role-reversing relationship as old and infallible as the hills. It also contributes to the play's intended air of vertiginous dislocation. It's 1932 and a world still reeling from the last apocalypse is preparing, half-knowingly, for the next. The Burglar's speeches about having destroyed cities and civilians hit very hard. He also discourses on the higher centres (the mind) that govern him and the lower (sex) that preoccupy the Nurse, and the impossibility of satisfying either without the other.

The play itself must carry some of the blame. It was, in the '60s, the first of Shaw's late plays to be rehabilitated, the first to show that the farce of ideas, increasingly divorced from social reality, carried a greater kick than critical tradition had acknowledged. But the traditional objections remain; Shaw's characters can say anything at any time, and it's a challenge to keep them rooted and consistent. The paradoxes -- a microbe infected by a human, an atheist who casts out his believing son -- become mechanical. To counter this, a production has to fire on every cylinder of personality, not just -- like this one -- on most of them.

A lot depends on the final speech. The preacher, left alone, preaches, his situation being that he has to, despite not knowing what to say. Traditionally this has been taken as Shaw's own confession, the author once more tipping his hand, this time in despair. He always denied it, but we don't have to believe him. Certainly the speech works best with that tension behind it. Williams' skill remains; so, very much, does his likeability. But the passion, though it sometimes appears on the horizon, isn't there yet.
