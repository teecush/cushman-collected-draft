THE CURE FOR EVERYTHING

Theatre Passe Muraille Backspace, Toronto

---

We start with Maja Ardal, standing in a corner upstage with her back to us, shaking her hips to The Hippy Hippy Shake by The Swinging Blue Jeans, an early 1960s group who have not crossed my mind, or I would imagine many other people's, in a very long time. The Cure for Everything is the sequel to Ardal's previous solo show, You Fancy Yourself, another chapter in her semi-autobiographical saga about growing up awkward in Edinburgh. It's very skilled, very charming and very slight.

It's also rather approximate about chronology. Elsa, Ardal's presumed alter ego, is shown as being hippy-happy-hypnotized by that song, a British hit in December 1963, while simultaneously being traumatized by the Cuban missile crisis, which happened a full year earlier. Just thought I'd mention it. Elsa, generally regarded as an oddball, has gained unaccustomed credibility by being the first girl at her school to wear Beatles tights. If we're to take the Cuban missile date as our benchmark, this would put her very much ahead of the curve, since Beatlemania hadn't really broken then.

She's precocious in other ways, too. The possibility of a nuclear holocaust, which keeps her immigrant parents glued to the TV set in the kitchen and pretty much neglecting her, spurs her to catch up with all the things she hasn't yet done in her life, primarily getting drunk and losing her virginity. Elsa is 15; and in the Britain of 1962, or even 1963, her innocence at that age, in those matters, would not have been all that exceptional. (Though I suppose things may have been different in Scotland from what they were in England.) Anyway, she sets about filling in the gaps, with the assistance of her new and very unexpected best friend Sheena, generally regarded as the most dazzling and desirable girl in the school, but gradually revealed to be in a far sadder state than Elsa herself.

Elsa's relationship with Sheena is one of the play's poles. The other is the background political threat. In between there are Elsa's Saturday job in a butcher's shop (greeted by Sheena with the '60s equivalent of "ew, gross"); and Elsa's worship-from-afar of Sheena's male equivalent Brian Baxter who is similarly revealed, in the most touching moment in the play, to be less than he might appear. There is also music. Elsa, revealed in the previous play as the best singer in the school, is selected by an appreciative teacher to play the French horn in the school orchestra; it is, he points out, the most difficult instrument, befitting a natural musician like herself. This gets her further grief from the bullies, all brandishing their graceful easy flutes, but what starts as a ground-bass ends as an epiphany.

All these strands are neatly tied together, but they don't make as rich a mix as we had in You Fancy Yourself, a lumpier play but one with more texture and sounding greater emotional depths. This one is linear and simple. It's delightfully performed. Under Mary Francis Moore's direction, Ardal makes Elsa live as both anguished adolescent protagonist and wide-eyed adolescent narrator. She also nicely sketches in the other characters, doing canny instant switches from Elsa's pawky Caledonian to Sheena's throaty tones, somewhere between finishing school and BBC, and then to the shy and wholly unexpected tones of the longed-for Brian. I'm not sure what the play's title means, but I note that Elsa's peers no longer accuse her of fancying herself, even though she, and certainly her interpreter, has a right to. Elsa is still insecure, but there's been progress.
