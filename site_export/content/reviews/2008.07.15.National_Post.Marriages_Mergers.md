THEATRE REVIEW

THE PRESIDENT

Royal George Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

---

Farce is always a race against time. Seldom, though, is that so literally the case as it is in The President, the insanely enjoyable one-act piece that is this year's lunchtime show at the Shaw Festival.

The title character, a Mr. Norrison, is president not of a country (he'd consider that small potatoes) but of a company. He runs a tight, happy and incredibly profitable ship but, at the very moment of taking a supremely well-deserved vacation, he finds both his plans and his livelihood threatened. A lovely young woman, entrusted to his care for a season by her father who just happens to be our tycoon's most vital potential investor, has got herself wooed, wed and impregnated by a poor and bearded taxi-driver who is also, wouldn't you know, a card-carrying Communist. (And she was supposed to be in school, getting finished.) Her parents are about to hit town. Hurricane Norrison has just one hour to transform the ill-chosen love of her life into a suitable son-in-law. He's up for the challenge. Just to keep himself and the audience on our respective toes, he has his secretary run in and announce the time every few minutes.

His secretary: The play is full of them. That dates it, though to exactly which date is hard to say. It was written in 1929, by Ferenc Molnar, head Hungarian honcho of the Continental boulevards, whose works have been the foundation of diverse English-language triumphs from Carousel to, well, to The President. The latter's original title was One, Two, Three, under which name it was filmed by Billy Wilder; the play, at least in this version, is much, much funnier. Molnar was as at home in Manhattan as he was in Europe, and Manhattan is where the play, in Morwyn Brebner's sumptuously snappy adaptation, is unquestionably set; if we didn't know it from the words, we'd know it from the Chrysler Building whose summit is located directly outside the presidential office window. The office and its staff seem rooted in the 1950s but the cabbie, volubly loyal to his Marxist principles even in the process of discarding them, is an emblematic figure of the '30s, as is the emigre Central European count, currently employed as a janitor but still empowered to sell ambassadorial positions to the highest bidder. It all merges into myth: the American dream with the velocity, and something of the undercurrent, of nightmare, one that hits us where we currently live. What after all could be more contemporary than a play about a makeover?

Working from a tongue-twisting text (try saying that fast), Blair Williams, a proven fine actor making as auspicious a directorial debut as I can remember, sets about making life as fiendishly difficult for his cast as possible. If the diminutive William Vickers comes onstage he will be plonked firmly down on a very high chair. When the actors aren't spinning around the stage, we must assume that they're spinning behind it. Those who double are required, having changed from one character to another, to come back as the person you first thought of. One of them, Mike Nadajewski, brings off a triple, his most eye-catching impersonation being of a secretary specially hired for her inability to provoke jealousy in even the most suspicious of spouses. Male, female or indeterminate, this company proves as impossible to intimidate as -- well, as so many Norrisons.

Norrison himself is Lorne Kennedy, dynamically dapper, going at a furious crackling pace without sweating a drop. He has other people to do that for him, lubricating their loyalty by a liberal application of what he calls "oil." He knows all his employees' private problems, and has a soothing word, sometimes even a soothing action, for all of them. He does not, of course, care an actual jot for any of them. For his transformation act, he needs a full force of helpful tradesmen, obsequious board members and tireless messengers. Manipulating them all, he can shift in a single breath from fixing a typist's broken engagement to ordering a barber to "shave the first man who walks through that door." His imperious rhythm even accommodates personal heartbreak over a faithless mistress, each spasm accorded precisely one second's indulgence in what becomes a sublime running gag, befitting Kennedy's sublime performance.

The production might be said to take place in the Kennedy era, since the unrelated Chilina Kennedy brings to the role of the impulsive young brideas muchingenuous edibility as she bestows on the darlin' Eileen of Wonderful Town, and even more bloody-minded determination. No wonder gentlemen prefer blondes; and if the Shaw feels like trying that piece again, there could hardly be a likelier Lorelei Lee. Jeff Meadows is suitably rubbery as her beloved, taught in record time to drive autos like the autocrats and drink pluto like the plutocrats, as Lorenz Hart put it in a song, entitled Disgustingly Rich, that might be this play's theme tune. Support is impeccable, with especially flavourful cameos from Thom Marriott and Michael Ball. Surveying what he has wrought, the president favours an especially admiring subordinate with a reflection on the malleability of "people -- as you call them." It gives a pleasantly acrid parting tang to what has otherwise been an hour of effervescence. Standing ovations at first nights, or first afternoons, have become debased currency. For this curtain-call, though, we were on our feet even before the actors were: a reception so rapid and unanimous as to put cheers and bravos back on the gold standard. - The President runs until Oct. 4.
