THEATRE REVIEWS TRELAWNY OF THE 'WELLS'

Monday, May 28, 1990

ROBERT CUSHMAN

NIAGARA-ON-THE-LAKE, Ont. -- By Arthur Wing Pinero Directed by Christopher Newton Starring William Hutt, Barry MacGregor Julie Stewart and William Vickers Time turns Trelawny of the 'Wells' into Pinero's best play BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail NIAGARA-ON-THE-LAKE, Ont. George Bernard Shaw, who had little time for the plays of his honored colleague Arthur Wing Pinero, made an exception of Trelawny of the 'Wells'. Pinero the problem playwright, the Pinero of The Second Mrs. Tanqueray, was in Shaw's view a fake, writing reactionary melodramas and trying to pass them off as challenging pictures of modern life. Pinero, thought Shaw, derived his views of modern life from the work of other playwrights as blinkered as himself. But Trelawny is a period piece - written in 1897, set some 35 years earlier - and Shaw was happy to applaud and endorse its nostalgia.

Time has agreed with him. Trelawny now looks like its author's best play, and 25 years ago (more nostalgia) there was a production at Britain's National Theatre that made it seem like a masterpiece. It gave the very rare and stirring sensation of history - theatrical history, in this case - on the move. Some of this was sleight-of-hand, taking place between the acts. Scenes were changed in full view, while a generation of actors melted into the wings to be replaced by a new vintage. This gave extraordinary resonance to what are anyway the key lines of the piece.

James Telfer, an old leading man fallen on hard times, complains that "this new-fangled stuff, and these dandified people are to push us, and such as us, from our stools!" To which his wife replies, "Yes, James, just as some other new fashion will, in course of time, push them from their stools."

The catalyst for the anti-Telfer revolution is Tom Wrench, once a despised walk-on in the same company at the Banigge-Wells theatre (a transparent disguise for the shabby but historic Sadler's Wells in North London), now emerging as a successful and revolutionary playwright.

His innovation is to put real people onto sets that incorporate real doors and real windows. Of course, the realism of one generation is the escapism of the next, and Pinero gives us a perspective on this. His model for Tom Wrench was the Victorian actor-playwright Tom Robertson, whose works are now charming Dickensian curios.

The Trelawny of the title is Rose Trelawny, an actress who leaves her precarious profession to marry a swell, but who finds her period of enforced probation with his family - an old judge and his spinster sister - too constricting to be borne. She returns to the boards, but her experiences have sobered her so that she no longer believes in the overblown texts she is required to inhabit. (Shaw would have seen a lesson for Pinero himself in that.) Wrench sees, however, that she is just the actress he requires. The fact that he has always been in love with her doesn't hurt - at least, it hurts him, but he rises above it to play Cupid for Rose and her intended. More melodrama: but we gladly give in to it.

Christopher Newton's production that opened on Friday is pictorially splendid but humanly hollow. No play has ever given a more detailed, loving account of the actor's life: the warmth and camaraderie, the jealousy and insecurity. Like Shaw, we are made to feel nostalgia for a world in which every player had his sharply-defined rung on the ladder, from the heights of Leading Man to the depths of General Utiliity: a world in which the most crushing condemnation of an aspiring actor was that he "lacked vigor."

Well, none of the Wells company in this version lacks vigor; they are falling over themselves and one another with it. What they lack is credibility, or any communicated belief in their own dignity. The old Telfers are caricatures, even in their downfall, which accordingly lacks all pathos. Sure, Pinero dictated that Colpoys, the low comedian, is "unable to forget" that he is one; but Simon Bradbury's antics in the role are pasted-on and very wearing. Nor is this a long-term strategy designed to highlight the "real world" outside. The landlady and grocer who attend the actors, the straight-laced toffs who are Roses's prospective in-laws, the new wave of players who arrive in the last scene: all are equally artificial. The play's lesson is surely that all are equally real.

It is not a matter of eschewing extravagance. There are a couple of cameos - Peter Hutt's overbred officer, Robert Haley's excitable Irish prompter - that work because they are affectionately judged.

Barbara Worthy's comedienne has some warm moments. And the parts that have to be taken seriously repay the effort. William Hutt's old authoritarian is beautiful: frail of body, stern of tongue, generous of spirit. Julie Stewart's Rose is poised and vivacious; so, more surprisingly, is Duncan Ollerenshaw as her shy cipher of a lover, though the suggestion that he may be a future Edmund Kean has to be ranked among the more improbable moments in world drama. (The same actor - Ollerenshaw, not Kean - was squeezed out of my notice of Misalliance, in which he throws several impressive tantrums.) William Vickers, after an embarrassingly overdone first act, recovers himself to make an affecting Tom Wrench, with the steel of achievement in him. These players dominate the last section; thanks to them and the author, we leave reaonably happy.

P.S. Two intervals are still one too many.
