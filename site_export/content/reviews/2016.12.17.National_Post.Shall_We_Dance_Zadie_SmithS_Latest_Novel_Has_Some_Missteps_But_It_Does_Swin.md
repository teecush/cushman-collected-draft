BOOK REVIEW

Swing Time By Zadie Smith Hamish Hamilton 464 pp; $34

He isnt doing that right." Fred Astaire apparently used to say that when watching himself dancing on screen. Zadie Smith tells us so in her novel Swing Time - or rather her narrator, unnamed throughout a long book, tells us. The quotation reverberates through the story on several levels.

The narrator, whom we follow from her childhood right through her 20s, is obsessed with Astaire. In fact, she's obsessed with dance generally, especially with dance on film, readily available through the VCR when she's growing up, in Smiths own northwest London in the 1980s. The book takes its title from one of the Astaire-Ginger Rogers movies of the 1930s: the one with perhaps the greatest score and performance, though hardly the greatest script. But "Swing Time also reflects the way the novel itself moves, swinging back and forth between different decades, different continents.

Astaires comment about himself resonates with the narrator when she encounters it as a pre-teen. For Astaire the person in the film was not especially connected with him. And I took this to heart, or rather, it echoed a feeling I already had, mainly that it was important to treat oneself as a kind of stranger ... I thought you needed to think like that, to achieve anything in this world." On the face of it, she has taken the opposite tack.

This, Smith's fifth novel, is the first to be written in the first person. But that too can be a way of distancing, at least to the reader. Who, after all, can see themselves whole? It may be significant that we never learn the narrator's name. She has a wonderful voice - the book is infinitely quotable - but an amorphous personality, especially given the kaleidoscopic narrative shes called upon to unify. The effect is partly intriguing, partly frustrating.

The narrator loves dance, but has no talent for it. Her best childhood friend - who does have a name, Tracey - is brilliant at it, and makes it her career. The two complement one another in other ways. The narrator has a black mother and a white father; Traceys parentage is the reverse. Tracey's father is almost entirely absent, apparently often in jail; Tracey fantasizes that the reason for his absence is that he's a back-up dancer with Michael Jackson.

The narrators father is around, at least to begin with. Hes kind but ineffectual, intimidated by his wife, a dedicated and ambitious Open University student and a determined achiever, other than domestically. ("My mother, who aspired to healthy eating, could not spend fifteen minutes in a kitchen without being reduced to a sort of self-pitying mania ... We would end up eating Findus Crispy Pancakes again. Round Tracey's things were simpler: you began with the clear intention of making Findus Crispy Pancakes.) We're told that Long before it became her career my mother had a political mind: it was in her nature to think of people collectively." A memorable scene shows the grown-up narrator having lunch with her mother, now indeed a professional politician, attended by an acolyte, colleague and lover named Miriam. Miriams "favourite thing in life was to see a conflict resolved, any conflict, and so my mother was a great resource for her: everywhere she went she made conflict.

One source of conflict is her daughters own job, as personal assistant to a pop megastar named Aimee, who suggests Madonna with a dash of Angelina Jolie. Aimee has announced her commitment to global poverty reduction" and wants to build - in fact does build, or at least finance - a girls' school in a West African village. "'White woman saves Africa says mother, is that the idea? Very old idea."

Aimee was born in Australia, but she acknowledges no roots and "has no tragic side. She goes through life as her own creation, and through the book as an entertainingly observed one. But when the story itself moves to Africa - followed, at carefully chosen photo-op intervals, by Aimee herself - the life goes out of it. There are some thematically crucial moments: the narrators exposure to African dance, which thrills her, and her realization that the villagers think of her as white.

She makes a new friend, Hawa, that relative rarity in the village, a middle-class girl." Hawa is the substitute for the definitely (and defiantly) un-middle-class Tracey, from whom the narrator has grown apart. The rift gets increasingly bitter. That's believable, but it isn't fleshed out. And yet its clearly meant to be the fulcrum of the book.

Maybe I should confess an interest here. Im a West Londoner myself; and though I grew up a couple of decades earlier than both Smith's narrator and Smith herself - and a little more south and east - Smith's novels, starting with the amazing White Teeth, still make me feel London as no other current writer does. I also share the narrators obsession with classic musicals and especially with their songs: one aspect of the book that Smith has admitted to be autobiographical.

The narrator may not be able to dance, but she can sing; with limited range but an intuitive sense for phrasing a lyric (like Astaire, though she has to be taught to acknowledge it). I believe it. That sympathy established, I feel entitled to pick a few nits. The book gets a couple of song-titles wrong: They All Laughed becomes We All Laughed, and All of You is confused with All of Me, mistakes which if followed through would make nonsense of the lyrics, and hence of a vital part of the narrators sensibility. Tracey gets into the chorus of a West End production of Guys and Dolls, her and the narrator's favourite musical - that shows their impeccable taste - but the number that's described, a pussycat routine called Pet Me Poppa, was only in the movie, where it replaced the far superior A Bushel and a Peck (Ive never known it to be done on stage).

Later, Tracey lands another chorus gig in a revival of Show Boat; the narrator is moved to wonder why neither Tracey nor any performer of colour (she calls that phrase, in another context, "the new euphemism) ever gets the plum role of Julie. But as far back as 1971 I saw the part played in a highly successful London production by the mixed-race Cleo Laine, and I dont know of any subsequent staging that has or would cast a white performer. This is important because race, and the degree to which it can or cannot be fluid, is a central theme of the book. The author's general point may be valid, but she's chosen the wrong example.

Show Boat proves to be Traceys last theatrical engagement. When the narrator pays her a last call, Tracey is a multiple single mother who throws class, and implicitly race, in her visitors face as she never has before. (Final musical irony: her kids are glued to the TV, watching South Pacific.) The narrator herself is childless, parentless and partnerless - though her romantic life has always been, as far as we can tell, shadowy. She's also jobless. The details have been hinted at in a prologue - it was the first day of my humiliation" - from which the rest of the book is a flashback. When you first read it, this introduction is mysterious to the point of confusion, but if you go back to it at the end (as I suspect the author knows you will) things fall into place. It includes a reviewing of the Bojangles of Harlem number from Swing Time the movie, in which the narrator notices - apparently for the first time - that Astaire, paying tribute to the great Bill Robinson, is dancing in blackface. "I'd managed to block the childhood image from my memory," she says, and I imagine everything else follows from that. I don't think it does; the book sprawls too much. But it does swing.
