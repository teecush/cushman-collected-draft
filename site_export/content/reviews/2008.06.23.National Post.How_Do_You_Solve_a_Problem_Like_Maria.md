How do you solve a problem like Maria? Maybe by not creating it in the first place.

It's obviously too late to wish that The Sound of Music had never been written. You could climb every mountain and still not find that dream, though the show is by far the least interesting of the Rodgers and Hammerstein musicals, including their three bona fide flops. The musical with the nuns and the Nazis is immovably in the cultural bloodstream, the object alike of swooning adoration and camp mockery. (Some people, I'm sure, combine both reactions.) It's also the only musical whose casting could become the subject of a TV reality show. Cabaret is a popular musical, too, a cult one as well, but it's impossible to imagine a show called The Search for Sally Bowles. The object of such a quest needs to have a certain innocence about her. We have to watch a parade of ingenues competing for the most ingenuous role of all.

And now we have the chance. Andrew Lloyd Webber produced a successful stage revival of The Sound of Music in London, having discovered his leading lady through a televised nationwide search, complete with voting viewers. Mirvish Productions are bringing the stage production to Toronto, and CBC is bringing How Do You Solve a Problem Like Maria? along with it, or rather, before it. Last Sunday and Monday, the vast field of contestants was narrowed down to 50, then 20, then 10. Starting next week, the hunt is really on: a further round of tests and auditions with Canadian viewers polled for their opinions on who will be Toronto's Maria.

It is, of course, a publicity stunt and, in essence, an old one. Think of the search for an unknown to play Scarlett in Gone with the Wind. But in those days, the public was supposed to be a hungry witness of the spectacle, not a participant. Now the electronic election has become the model, and nobody seems to find it in the least surprising.

It's frightening. For one thing, it abjectly denies the old showbiz wisdom that you don't give the public what they think they want, you give them what they never knew they wanted. This is a real live production we're talking about, not the nebulous chance of being acclaimed as a Canadian or American Idol. And the casting of its leading role is to be determined by a head-count among people with no professional expertise, in circumstances even more artificial than those of the usual audition. Lloyd Webber himself said it best, while mulling over the aspirants' videos before watching the Top 20 strut their stuff for him at the London Palladium: "On TV, everyone is reduced to the same screen size." It can't tell you very much about stage presence.

David Mirvish, interviewed in the Palladium lobby, said that it was all very fraught, they were all very talented, and he was glad he didn't have to make the decision. But he should be making it, or helping to make it -- it's his job. As for the show's director, whom one would expect to have something to say about the identity of his leading lady, he's so far been conspicuous by his absence. Do all these people feel happy about abdicating their responsibilities? The only professional whose opinion seems to count is the man everyone compulsively refers to as "Lord Lloyd Webber." In the words of the commentary the postulant Marias are offering up their talents before "the Lord himself." He, to his credit, is coming over shrewd and good-humoured -- the man who began his career with the air of an engagingly earnest schoolboy now seems like a tolerant, seen-it-all headmaster. Not bad for a mogul.

And, to be fair, the TV show is a cut above most of its talent-contest competitors. It has a sense of humour about itself, as demonstrated in a title sequence involving rival Marias biffing one another on the familiar mountain-top from the opening of the movie. The movie also inspires what the commentary describes as "a Maria essential-- singing while spinning," though the image of a twirling Maria derives entirely from Julie Andrews and her director's camera. You can bet that spinning wasn't essential when Mary Martin created Maria on Broadway. But that's another problem with Maria; her show has become a staged movie. Our host, Gavin Crawford, shows little sign of his comic past and the show has a disconcerting habit of photographing him curled up on windowsills like Peter Pan, but he probably copes as well as anyone could with the built-in sycophancy of the genre and with such portentous pronouncements on performance anxiety as "they may have excelled in Maria school, but this is the real world." "Maria school" is the jovial name given to the theatrical boot camp at which those on the long shortlist were put through the paces by a trio of professionals:a musical director, a vocal coach and a leading actor. Here, the program gets down to business, allowing us to see scenes being worked on and songs being worked through. Sequences like this were the best parts of last season's Triple Sensation, and Maria has so far been offering them in more concentrated form. It's a wall and we're the flies.

But it's still very artificial. The lucky Marias who got flown to London had to put on a show -- a carefully rehearsed production -- to get in a show. It's also, like all these things, protractedly cruel -- and it will inevitably get crueller. The Palladium culling process involved the young actresses parading before the Lord himself in unpredictable groups of one, two and three, left completely off guard as to which among them had passed and which had failed. The suspense, of course, was entirely for our benefit, and it was abetted by cutting between these individual triumphs and agonies and the spectacle of the entire group carolling about the hills being alive. Theatre has always been a blood sport, but traditionally it's only been the final stage of the game that gets acted out in public. I also wonder just how conclusive the popular vote will be. In the West End production, the role of Maria ended up going to a performer who was already fairly well known and well established. I suspect that the authorities have allowed themselves some wiggle-room. If not, well, that's their problem. - How Do You Solve a Problem Like Maria? airs Sundays and Mondays at 8 p. m. on CBC
