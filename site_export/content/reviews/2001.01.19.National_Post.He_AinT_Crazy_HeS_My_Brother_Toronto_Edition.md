VINCENT

Theatre Passe Muraille, Backspace

The Vincent in Vincent is Van Gogh, as usual. Like the sugary Don McLean song of the same title, the piece soothes us with the implicit assurance that we, a safe century later, would never be such heartless philistines as to call a great painter a madman and even lock him up for it.

Essentially a lecture, very thinly disguised as a one-man play, it was written by Leonard Nimoy -- Mr. Spock of Star Trek -- presumably as a vehicle for himself. (At least, I assume the author is that Leonard Nimoy, and that is why he wrote it; the program is silent on both matters.)

A short time after Vincent's death, Theo, his art-dealing brother, takes his stand behind a lectern to tell the truth about the deceased. Behind him stands a wall full of Vincent's paintings, some of which he will eventually bring downstage -- half boastfully, half accusingly -- as the climax of both his speech and the play. He illustrates his story with extracts from Vincent's letters; whenever he gets to them the lighting changes and the actor leaves his post to assume the role of the dead man, speaking to us from beyond the grave. So the actor gets to play two contrasting characters; the play might have been called Vincent and Theo if Robert Altman's movie hadn't got there first.

Unfortunately, the play, unlike the movie, tells us almost nothing about the two men's relationship; and what it does tell, it states rather than dramatizes. Given the format, this is almost inevitable; the play takes the familiar scissors-and-paste approach of selecting a few documents and setting them up. Theo does, of course, have the inside track on some things. As a brother, he knows all about the grief Vincent caused his family when he set up house with a prostitute who was pregnant with somebody else's child. As a dealer, he is only too aware of Vincent's refusal to allow any of his work to be sold. He tells us, plausibly enough, that Vincent had impossibly high expectations of himself in every department -- art, religion, love -- and in effect set himself up to fail. None of this, though, seems to have eaten away at Theo himself; he reacts, at most, with mild exasperation. He seems to have no unresolved issues about his brother. Most of the time, it could be any narrator up there.

I don't know what Mr. Spock may have made of this double role; on the face of it, he would have been better suited to sensible bourgeois Theo than to reckless saint-and-sinner Vincent, but maybe he took on the challenge precisely to demonstrate that there was more to him than cold rationality and pointy ears. (The matter of Van Gogh's own famous ear is here approached by Theo with an appropriate air of pained self-consciousness: Yes, here it comes, the organ you've been waiting for.)

Gabe Bettio, who appears in this production (originally staged in Fort Erie), is certainly happier playing Theo; he projects a dry indignation both with the straight world that rejected his brother and the Bohemian world that led him to further excesses he certainly wasn't equipped to withstand. Theo's anger at the more robust Gauguin, whom Vincent hero-worshipped, comes over especially strongly. But when it comes to projecting Vincent himself, the actor is way out of his depth; he turns up speed and volume, by way of suggesting abandon, and he fails in the crucial task of making written material sound like spontaneous utterance: this in a man for whom, we are asked to believe, spontaneity was the breath of life.

The show's view of Van Gogh as persecuted (and self-persecuted) genius is perfectly conventional, perfectly predictable and, in all probability, perfectly accurate. The story and the work are compelling enough for the evening to maintain a low-burning level of interest, but it remains doggedly unexciting: a star-vehicle without a star.
