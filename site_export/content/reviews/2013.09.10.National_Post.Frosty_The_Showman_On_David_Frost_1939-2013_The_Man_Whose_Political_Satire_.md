London, Saturday, Nov.

24, 1962. (I've checked the date.) I was in my last year of grammar school - high school, to you over here - and came home late in the evening to find my family around the television. This was not unusual. The set was tuned to the bbC; that wasnt unusual either. What was unusual was the program they were watching. It was the premiere of what seemed to be some kind of variety show, but unlike most in that genre it was obviously going out live. even more surprisingly, it was making jokes about politics and politicians, quite rude and personal ones: the same kind of jokes that people in real life made all the time, but that you never, ever heard on TV. It was more than surprising; it was liberating.

The show was titled That Was The Week That Was, commonly known in very short order asTW3. And presiding over it was somebody who looked and sounded like an uppity undergraduate, and whose name was david Frost.

He was indeed a recent Cambridge graduate, and had made a name there as a performer with the universitys legendary and, over the years, extraordinarily influential intimate revue club, the Footlights. Since coming down he had made some cabaret and TV appearances, but essentially he was unknown. He had come up through the state-school system and spoke in a voice that seemed both fresh and familiar, individual and generic; it hinted at no particular region, no particular class, and managed to combine elements of both squeak and sneer while still being ingratiating. The voice remained pretty much unchanged for the rest of his career, though naturally the appearance didn't. In his later years he came to look quite elderstatesmanlike, his hair soberly sandy, but back when he started it was dark, with an accusatory widow's peak that one reviewer declared (approvingly) to be an affront in itself. The obituaries that followed his death last week on board the Queen elizabeth have mostly concentrated on his later activities as a multiple interviewerof presidents and prime ministers and as a TV mogul on his own account: Frosty the Showman. but that young persona and the use he made of it remain his most important achievement, and the most far-reaching.

What the look, the voice, and the persistent wagging finger conveyed was an indictment of the british ruling class (and it certainly was a class), not so much for being corrupt or inefficient as for being absurd. In 1962, with Harold macmillans Conservative government in apparently secure power, this wasnt just startling, it was practically revolutionary. Nor were the broken taboos exclusively political. One thing that struck me on that Saturday night was that, for the first time ever on british TV, people were being funny about sex. mentioning it at all, except perhaps on science programs, was practically unheard of. Later on, TW3 presented "a consumer's guide to religion", anticipating by some 40 years the much-missed God-slot that Stephen Colbert used to do on The Daily Show before he got his own Report. TW3's venture into sacrilege prompted at least one clergyman to write to the newspapers, demanding that this horrible programme be swept from the screens of a Christian country. Interestingly Frost, like the devoutly Catholic Colbert, was aChristian; he was the son of amethodist minister, and had considered entering the ministry himself.

TW3 did not of course come out of nowhere. The previous year had seen the London opening of the incomparably hilarious revue beyond the Fringe, in which four other recent graduates - Peter Cook and Jonathan miller from Cambridge, Alan bennett and dudley moore from Oxford - had expanded the targets and techniques of british stage comedy beyond what anybody had thought possible. It was inspired; I had never laughed as much in the theatre (still havent) and neither had anybody else, at least nobody else I knew. Cook capitalized on its success to open what was described as "a satirical nightclub" named, with an irony no less effective for being thumpingly obvious, The establishment.

Satirical. Satire. Those were the words. everybody wanted a piece of them. Theatres and clubs, though, were privileged zones. Putting this kind of thing on TV took real cheek. The man who possessed it, and who recruited Frost, was Ned Sherrin, a young bbC producer who had come up through a lively news program called Tonight but was more of a showbiz creature than a current affairs one. (He was a political animal only in the professional sense.) He caught a peculiar cultural moment in britain; it was the immediate pre-beatles era and the shows preferred music, what was cool at the time, was jazz. The improvisational spirit extended to the visuals; cameras, cables and microphone-booms were proudly visible. That's a cliche now, but it was immensely refreshing at the time. The casualness extended to the running-time; the show was the last thing on in the evening and could overrun or underrun according to what was in the week's news that could be made fun of.

And presiding over it all, giving it its tone, was Frost. He wasnt a great comedian (his solo bits usually fell flat) but he was the perfect front-man. He had a fine team of actors supporting him, and what seemed an inexhaustible stable of writers. One of them was Peter Cook, though it seems that he and Frost werent friends. Cook described Frost, with whom he had overlapped at the Footlights, as a bubonic plagiarist. Later on, when the non-swimming Frost was taking a dip in Cook's pool, Cook did save him from drowning; but according to Alan bennett, speaking at Cook's funeral, he always maintained that it was the one thing in his life he regretted doing. (A related legend holds that michael bentine, a gentler and zanier comic of the preceding generation, once sent Frost a telegram that read "dear mr. Frost, I think you are a cult" and signed it "michael beltine.") TW3 got lucky with history. The John Profumo-Christine Keeler scandal erupted in 1963, providing an endless source of material that it gleefully and relentlessly exploited. The commercial stations of the time would never have touched the show, and it had plenty of enemies within the bbC itself. Schedulers did their best to sabotage the second season, and were only foiled by the top-level intervention of Sir Hugh Carleton-Greene, brother of Graham Greene the novelist and the most enlightened director General that the corporation known, fondly and otherwise, as Auntie bbC ever had. There wasnt a third season; there was a general election coming up and it was decreed that satire, or whatever it was, would be tactless. The show could at least claim to have been the author of its own destruction; it had been widely credited with bringing down the macmillan government. This is unlikely, but it had certainly captured and fostered an increasingly skeptical national mood.

And, it turned out, an international mood. In 1964, with the british version off the air, the u.S. produced its own TW3 series, with Frost again involved, inaugurating a bicontinental career that at one point had him commuting by Concorde twice a week. (It seems fitting that he should have died while crossing the Atlantic.) Canada had This Hour Has Seven Days, similar to the british show in title and tone, and also in being cut off in its prime. Sherrin, without Frost, went on to produce two similar though less successful series, that incidentally were as hard on the new Labour prime minister, Harold Wilson, as their predecessors had been on Harold macmillan: harder, probably. Frost, without Sherrin, hosted and produced a show called The Frost Report that was less topically centred and lampooned social types rather than individuals. Always a good talent-spotter, he assembled a supporting team that included yet another Footlights graduate, John Cleese, who soon went on to be one-sixth of Monty Pythons Flying Circus. I was at Cambridge, and in the Footlights, in the years immediately after TW3, and the style was already more pre-Python than post-Frost. Frost might have been grooming the stable of comedy that was to replace his own.

Except it didn't. The Pythons were actually the anarchic heirs of TW3 (though they also owed a debt to Lewis Carroll). They didn't have to establish that english public life was ridiculous; that had already been done. They didnt have to attack individual targets; why ridicule a particular politician when you can invent the ministry of Silly Walks? Theyve been the dominant influence on british and North American comedy ever since.

Still, something had gone missing. Saturday Night Live has been inadvertently proving it for nearly 40 years now, imitating the TW3 formula, right down to the time slot, without approaching either its bite or its free-wheeling spirit. It wasn't until 1999 that there appeared on TV a direct descendant of TW3, a comedy show explicitly based on the news. It is of course The Daily Show with Jon Stewart, with- since 2005 - its spin-off The Colbert Report. People in britain used to say that they had to keep up with the news in order to appreciate the jokes of Frost and company; they now say exactly the same about Stewart and Colbert. (Well, there are worse reasons.)

There are obviously differences. America now is a more bitterly divided society than britain in the '60s ever was, and the shows reflect that. Then theres the matter of technology: the TW3 team did impersonations; the newguys can rely on video clips to have their targets convict themselves out of their own mouths. They are also more talented comedians than Frost; indeed Colbert,who combines traditional performance skills with a razor mind and whose blowhard persona is a great and brilliantly sustained creation in its own right, is a comedic genius, a consummate verbal tightrope artist. Stewart is playing a less complicated game, and hes prone to excessive mugging, but both he and Colbert have it all over Frost in the underlying seriousness department. They do irony where Frost did sarcasm. Frost's manner on TW3 never suggested that anything much was at stake; Stewart is at his best when the humour is coming out of barely-controlled anger. (This also distinguishes him from bill maher, whose nervous giggling at his own jokes makes him unwatchable.) Stewart is also the best interviewer on TV: the only one who asks a question as if he would actually like to know the answer and then follows it up as if he were conducting something resembling a conversation. I imagine that at some point he'll tire of having to cram these encounters into 10-minute segments, a portion of which have to be spent proving that he can still be funny; and that he will devote himself to full-length, if not full-time, interviewing. If he does, he will of course be following in Frost's later footsteps, as he already has in the earliest, most valuable ones. david Frost, at the dawn of his career, showed television, and the world, that it didn't have to respect respectability.
