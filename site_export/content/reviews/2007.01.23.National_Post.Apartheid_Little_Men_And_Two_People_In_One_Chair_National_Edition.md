New World Stage: Combined festival brings a heady mix of global theatre Torontos Harbourfront Centre used to house, at different times, two theatrical festivals. There was the biennial World Stage season and there was the International Dance season. This year the two combine in the New World Stage International Performance season, which is planned to be an annual event accommodating drama, dance and the increasingly fluid and disputed area between them. As well as being a more diverse occasion than previously, the New World Stage is also a longer one. The old World Stage seasons were typically crammed into a few weeks. This one is spread over a more leisurely six months, beginning tomorrow and stretching into June.

At least, it should seem more leisurely to audiences and critics. The organizers, probably, will never let up. For Tina Rasmussen, the season's artistic director, this should seem like par for the course. The festival is the culmination of two years' heavy globetrotting that may have peaked at the Edinburgh Festival, where she saw 66 plays in nine days. (You can do this in Edinburgh, easily.) Its a peculiarly intense kind of tourism, with an equally intense kind of souvenir-shopping attached. "You want, says Rasmussen, to bring your travelling experiences back with you."

She didnt have to travel very far to collect her opening production, Dollhouse. This is Ibsen's A Doll's House, as rendered by the New York company Mabou Mines, a troupe so long-established and well-regarded that it must count as the Establishment of experimental theatre.

Lee Breuer, the director, offers a new spin on what the company calls Ibsens feminist "anthem. (Their quotation marks.) It certainly offers a bizarre take on the plays title by having all the male roles played by little people" (or, to put it another way, very short men) while the women are all around six feet tall. This might make the patriarchal system seem more outrageous than ever, or it may make the women's eventual victory somewhat predictable. We shall see. The idea as described certainly sounds good for at least 10 minutes of stunning theatre, though it leaves you wondering what they will do for an encore.

Mabou Mines has built-in Canadian content. It takes its name from a community in Nova Scotia, where the company's founding members planned their inaugural event back in 1970. So it seems fitting, or at least a nice coincidence, that one of the home-grown contributions to the season (World Stages world does include Canada) should come from Halifax. This is Revisited, presented by the 2b theatre company (as opposed, presumably, to the Not 2b theatre company), a show that started life as a "revisitation of Thornton Wilders Our Town but ran into problems over rights. So the canvas was extended to take in other (comparatively) early American writers including Willa Cather and Walt Whitman, making it in effect Revisted Revisited. The format is simple; a storyteller and his audience sit at a table, and the man calls up characters from his small-town memories, characters who proceed to people the stage. It's one of the shows about which Rasmussen herself sounds most enthusiastic. It has, she says, a terrifically honest, non- strident ending; it makes you recall why you fell in love with the theatre."

There are people who fell in love with the theatre in the early 1970s, or at least had their eyes more fully opened to its human and imaginative potential, when they saw Sizwe Bansi is Dead. This is the play created in South Africa by the white playwright Athol Fugard and two black actors, John Kani and Winston Ntshona, as an enactment of how it felt to live under apartheid. (It wasn't an explicit protest play; the protest was built in.) The play, which had to be virtually smuggled out of its native country and had a shattering impact across the world, has outlasted the system that provoked it into being. Toronto will be seeing a new production directed by Peter Brook, who merits the title "legendary theatre director if anyone does.

This isnt the same as the production of the other Kani- Ntshona- Fugard collaboration The Island, which played in Toronto with Brook directing the two original performers. This is a new, from-the- ground-up production with different actors and performed in French. It was first staged at the Theatre des Bouffes-du-Nord in Paris, which has been now Brooks base for more than a quarter of a century.

Technically small but has massive reverberations": That could be a description of Sizwe Bansi, but in fact it's Rasmussen's summary of Belgian show Aalst -- a True Story, which reduces staging to two people in a single chair -- a method she describes, undoubtedly accurately, as "something you wouldnt see anywhere else. Aalst is faction" theatre, based on newspaper and TV reports, about a crime -- the murder of two children by their parents -- and its effect on the town in which it happened. Im also looking forward to another more elaborate collective piece, Particularly in the Heartland, whose logo is a heart decorated with the stars and stripes. Presented by another New York company, The TEAM (Theatre of the Emerging American Moment), it's described as "a new play about losing sight of America, and trying to fall back in love with it. If the company can catch that emerging moment, it should be exciting. They are, according to Rasmussen, a very young, exuberant, athletic company," with an average age of 27.

And for relaxation after all this intensity, we can anticipate The Spiegel Show, starting June 1, which is happening in the Spiegeltent'n tavern. This is a space that doesnt yet exist but will be erected on the model of a "travelling, wooden circular dance hall, popular in the 1920s in Belgium. The entertainment within it, however, will not be Belgian; it will be Torontonian, drawn from local comic and musical talent. Rasmussen directs it herself, and she -- a passionate, quiet-spoken lady -- promises a salubrious salon and an evening thats sexy and salacious." Sounds just like what we'll need as summer sets in.
