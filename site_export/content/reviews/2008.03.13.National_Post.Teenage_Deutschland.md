The musical Spring Awakening would be a first-rate production of the play Spring Awakening if only the songs didn't keep getting in the way. This isn't an example, like My Fair Lady, of an adaptation subtly tweaking its original, and coming up with something different but related. It's a case of two styles and periods at war with one another. The fact that this was plainly the intention doesn't make the result any more satisfying.

The play was written in 1891 by Frank Wedekind, at the beginning of a literary career as one of Germany's most reverberant iconoclasts; this, his first play, feels, still, like the one he owed it to himself to write. The musical is the work of Steven Sater (book and lyrics) and Duncan Sheik (music), and it has a similar urgency; it opened on Broadway in 2006 after the customary extended gestation period, was an instant sensation, and is being brought to Toronto next season by Mirvish Productions. Both concern a group of tormented adolescents in a German provincial town; their tortures are caused equally by their school curriculum, which is as narrow in scope as it is cruel in its demands, and by their sexuality, which they are not so much encouraged as forced to deny -- vainly, of course.

One girl, Wendla Bergmann, whose mother cannot bring herself to impart the biological facts, is made pregnant by Melchior Gabor, who knows more but obviously not enough. Academically, Melchior is his generation's prize student. His best friend, Moritz Stiefel, is also his polar opposite; he's the boy most oppressed by both varieties of ignorance, and he winds up committing suicide. Wendla also dies, at the hands of a backstreet abortionist procured by her mama--an event that the musical depicts with lip-smacking relish where the play is far more laconic and far more powerful.

Wedekind has been tagged, like most of his time and place, as an Expressionist, but in this play at least was more of

a hyper-realist. It's constructed in a series of short, sharp scenes, some of which retain some shock effect even now. Two scenes involve masturbation, one solitary, one group; there's a lyrical homosexual seduction as well as a fumbling hetero one. The musical's structure hews fairly closely to the play's, and is powerfully, furiously staged by Michael Mayer on a set appropriately resembling a school gymnasium.

Though the spoken script is kept strictly in period, the score is way out of it. The lyrics include references to stereos and phone calls, and -- a far deeper anachronism -- the tunes are rock. That is, a music that screams sexual know-how is applied to characters whose tragedy is that they don't have any. There's no pretence that the songs are forwarding the plot; they'reabout thekids expressing their inner drives. These don't change much over the course of the show. In fact, they boil down to two, desire and frustration. Listen to the CD, and you can't tell which character is supposed to be singing. You can tell that they're using a vocabulary, and a frame of reference, that wouldn't have been available to them. The message seems to be "this is how these adolescents would have expressed themselves if only they'd had our advantages." This is the present not so much illuminating the past as patronizing it.

There's a sense in which Wedekind asked for it; his last scene includes a Man from the Future, who persuades Melchior to follow him into the world rather than following his friend and his girlfriend into the grave. But that's just one scene, and besides, it's written with the same dark wit as the rest of the play. The musical's closing anthem, The Song of Purple Summer, makes for a flatulently sentimental subsitute.

In its own right, some of Sheik's music is lovely (the opening Mama Who Bore Me and the twice-used love song The Word of Your Body) or poundingly effective (The Bitch of Living). Sater's lyrics are the customary self-important approximations. The songs in the older rock musicals were dramatically ineffective because you couldn't hear the words; those in the newer ones are dramatically ineffective because you can. No lyricist in or out of the theatre is obliged to use rhyme, but if he is going to, then why not do it right? Why pair "idol" with "Bible" and think you're clever? The smartest Sater gets is pairing "f--ked" with "self-destruct"; that has some force to it.

One area in which the show gets fruitfully away from Broadway tradition is Bill T. Jones' choreography, which isn't graceful and fluent but awkward and restricted, as befits the environment; for once the term "knee-jerk" can be used as a compliment. The older people are sternly marginalized: first by being divided among just two actors (Kate Burton and Glenn Fleshler, both new to the cast, both good), second by not being allowed to sing except in ensembles. Most of the original kids remain, with Jonathan Groff vibrant as Melchior and Lea Michele touching as Wendla. The new misfit Moritz (Blake Bashoff ) actually fits in very nicely. As the supposed saviour of the American musical, Spring Awakening is the new Rent, which itself was hailed as the new Hair.

Each was seen as an improvement on its predecessor for dramatic sophistication, and that claim is true. Each has also been called a breakthrough in musical style, and don't you believe it. Songs that don't tell the story or develop the characters have been with us nearly as long as Wedekind's play. And did you know --I wonder if the authors of the show do -- that he was a singer-songwriter himself? - Spring Awakening runs indefinitely at the Eugene O'Neill Theater, New York City.
