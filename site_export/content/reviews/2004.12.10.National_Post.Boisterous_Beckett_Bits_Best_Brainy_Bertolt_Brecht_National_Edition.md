BECKETT & BRECHT

Theatre Centre, Toronto

- - -

Newly decked out with a lobby that's almost welcoming and an auditorium that's almost comfortable, Theatre Centre at fashionable Queen and Dovercourt recommences operations with a double-bill that pays tribute to the two gods of 20th-century drama. In Beckett & Brecht, Beckett is represented by a selection of his own playlets, then Brecht by a newly-written piece in which he is the pivotal figure. For this and other reasons, the first part comes off the best.

Its contents are described in the program as Beckett Shorts, designed perhaps to be worn with Tom Stoppard's Jumpers. Beckett was notorious for insisting on absolute fidelity, not only to his dialogue but to his stage directions. He was also justified, at least for his later and briefer works. In these, the image defines the play; a production that tampered with it would have nothing left to work with. Jennifer Tarver, the director here, has her work cut out -- and does it very well -- constructing the pictures, pacing her three performers and engineering neat transitions from one piece to the next.

The first of the quintet, also the best-known, is Not I, in which a woman billed simply as Mouth, this being her only visible feature, pours out the contents of her memory, at fever pace, to a silent hooded Auditor, who looks like a human question mark. Every time the speaker slips into the first person, she corrects herself and reiterates that the experiences she's describing are actually somebody else's.

Scenically, Mouth's mouth is the merest pin point cut in a black drape. It's a striking effect, though the velocity of the words (flawlessly delivered here by Barbara Gordon, as defensive as she's desperate) and the eye-aching darkness make concentration difficult. In fact, one is quite often bored. But it's a creative kind of boredom, one that keeps jerking the mind back to attention. Guilt has its uses.

It doesn't work in That Time, the piece that lends its name to the collection and gives us a grizzled protagonist (David Jansen) sitting motionless while stereo speakers on either side of him offer two conflicting versions in two registers of his own voice, of a cherished and/or rejected incident from his past. Beckett keeps his images very specific and that, as always, is a blessing. He may set the interpreters endlessly arguing but, from moment to moment, he is the least cloudy of writers. All the same: I can't see that That Time adds anything to what he said earlier and better in Krapp's Last Tape. The knife is dulled, but he still persists in twisting it.

It's twisted further in the two mimed plays, Act Without Words I and II (done in reverse order). Here, of course, the stage directions are the plays. In one of them, a pair of adjacent sleepers are prodded awake in their sacks (this is emphatically not the Ritz) by a long pole that emerges from the wings like an old- style vaudeville hook, except it's to start the act rather than stop it. One man, very grumpy, stumbles resentfully into life, the other, far cheerier, begins his day with a brisk series of physical jerks. But ultimately it makes no difference. Each ends up precisely where both began. In the other, the torture is refined; a solitary figure (Paul Fauteux) is bullied by whistles and tantalized by an inaccessible bottle of Evian (the branding being, I assume, the production's tribute to the author's adopted homeland). Life is a series of Pavlovian reactions, culminating in the grave. Beckett has tapped in to the secret of farce -- human beings depicted as automata are funny.

The last bit of Beckett, Catastrophe, was new to me and a revelation. It has three characters, two of whom get to walk and talk. A male director and his assistant arrange a male model in a suitably humiliating pose. They might be arranging a gallery installation, but it turns out they're rehearsing in a theatre. This is not only -- who'd have thought it? -- Beckett going in for showbiz satire, it must also be his ironic apologia for the hoops through which he put his own actors or the holes and corners to which he confined them. It makes a superb first-act finale. Sam Beckett was a gentleman and a showman.

Bert Brecht was just a showman. And it's a bumpy decline here from SB to BEBE, which is about Brecht and his women, in relation to whom he was a bastard. We meet three mistresses and -- proudly announcing herself in the funniest line -- one wife: Helene Weigel, the original Mother Courage and co-founder of the Berliner Ensemble. Four actresses tell us how he exploited them domestically, sexually and professionally. The earliest, Elisabeth Hauptmann, claims to have done most of the work on The Threepenny Opera. How much and how vital remains unclear, but there was plainly no reason to deny her either credit or royalties, both of which Brecht did.

The show, a collective creation directed by Jennifer H. Capraru, retells some interesting history but is too fragmented and dispersed to work as drama. Stylistically it invokes the spectre of Berlin cabaret. Maybe it's time to acknowledge that this vein has been definitively mined. Of the new songs, only one (The Exile Song, lyric by Morwyn Brebner) gains from being heard rather than read. It's sung by Camille Stubel, whose angular Weigel is the most commanding performance. As Ruth Berlau, last and looniest of the partners, Ruth Madoc-Jones is undone by a patter-song that is too fast, too clotted or both.

Mention is made of Brecht's slippery testimony before the Un- American Activities Committee. Meanwhile, Beckett, who never struck political poses, was working heroically for the French Resistance. Brecht's best line is confined to the program: "A theatre which cannot be laughed in is a theatre to be laughed at." Right on, Bertolt. But Beckett's jokes are better.
