The best thing about Rock of Ages is that it doesn't even pretend to take itself seriously. Here it scores heavily over its most obvious predecessor, Mamma Mia!; both are story musicals, as opposed to revues, with soundtracks assembled from song-hits of the past. Rock of Ages' music is aggressively undistinguished, mostly 1980s heavy metal with a few sobbing ballads and the occasional blessed echo of the blues, but it isn't cloying like the ABBA catalogue. It also breaks new ground in turning inaudibility to dramatic account.

Since you can hardly hear the lyrics of any of the songs beyond their title phrases--the performance style takes care of half of that and the audience's screaming does the rest -- you don't have to be bothered by any disconnect between what the words are saying and what the action is that they are supposed to be illustrating. A title like We're Not Gonna Take It can be pressed into service for a political protest scene, and the audience can be sent home happy to the strains of Don't Stop Believin'; the latter worked better at the end of The Sopranos, but Rock of Ages doesn't do irony, though it sometimes seems to think it does. What we're not gonna take, or in what we're supposed to keep believin', don't matter none. We're spared the embarrassing duplicity of Mamma Mia!, which spent half its time pretending that its songs were irreverently irrelevant and the other half pretending that they were legitimately moving. Rock of Ages doesn't go that route. To repeat a distinction I invoked over the last big new musical at the Royal Alex: It's silly but it isn't stupid.

Well, maybe that's a bit generous. The show is set on Sunset Strip, Los Angeles, mostly in a club belonging to a moccasinned gent named Dennis. Two young people come to his establishment looking for fame, fortune and, just to be going on with, work. Drew is a would-be singer and composer; Sherrie is a would-be actress. They're obviously predestined to fall in love, but the obligatory obstacles arise; Sherrie comes on like such a good girl that Drew feels nervous to come on at all. Disillusion ensues when Sherrie falls for a rock star who can't remember her name even while he's banging her, let alone after. She becomes a Sunset stripper while Drew, in what the show clearly believes to be a descent to far lower depths, joins a boy band. There's quite a sweet book scene (author, Chris D'Arienzo) when the two encounter in their fallen states, though it's unclear why they don't clinch the deal then and there instead of waiting for the final curtain. Tradition, I guess.

Tradition may also explain the inclusion of a totally unnecessary subplot about a Teutonic property developer named Hertz who wants to tear down the Strip, or Dennis's part of it anyway, in order to build -- to be honest, I can't remember what it is he wants to build, but it must be something unwholesome. Or maybe it's something wholesome and that's the threat. Anyway, the way the show goes on, this could spell the ruin of rock 'n' roll. I think it's about time that rock, the big money in American music, stopped posing as some kind of persecuted innocent, a stance that hasn't been credible since, at the latest, 1957. Still, Rock of Ages isn't paranoid like We Will Rock You.

Story be, obviously, damned: What keeps the show going are the vivacity of Kristin Hanggi's direction and the enthusiasm of the performances. Beowulf Borritt's set is amusingly angled to permit maximum use of cock-eyed projections; he also designed New York's Sondheim on Sondheim, which makes comparably creative use of video, and his is clearly a name to remember-- one indeed that it would be hard to forget. Among the supporting cast, David W. Keeley has great style as Dennis who has, at the very end, the one moment of genuine wit in the show; the writing provides it but the actor rides it home. Victor brings some weight to the heartless Hertz, who may not even be that heartless; this isn't Urinetown either, not remotely. Cody Scott Lancaster brought the house down as his thwarted-confectioner son whose proclamation, "I'm not gay, I'm just German," struck me as dubious; I mean, listen to his accent. Talking of accents, and moving to the stars, Yvan Pednault has been given some lines to justify his French-Canadianness: a nice gesture, but in a show like this, would anyone care? Anyway, he acts with charm and sings with real fire. He crests the music; Elicia MacKenzie, though approaching it with admirable determination, tends to sink beneath it. But she too acts well, in a novice role not unlike the one she played in The Sound of Music: Maria climbed every mountain, Sherrie slides down every pole. No one could say that Aaron Walpole doesn't throw himself into the part of Lonny, a beer-bellied, tattoo-torsoed narrator, who keeps pointing out that the show's a musical. This is too much; not only is the show a spoof (what isn't, these days?), it spoofs itself being a spoof. It gets very tiresome. On the credit side, the show is refreshingly clear-eyed on the subject of happy endings.
