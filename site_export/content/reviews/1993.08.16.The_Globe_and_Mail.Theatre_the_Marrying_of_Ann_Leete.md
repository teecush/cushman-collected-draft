THEATRE THE MARRYING OF ANN LEETE

Monday, August 16, 1993

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Niagara-on-the-Lake ONT -- WRITTEN BY HARLEY GRANVILLE BARKER Directed by Neil Munro Starring Ann Baggley, Seana McKenna, Jim Mezon and Christopher Newton At the Court House Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake Rating: The Marrying of Ann Leete is so precisley styled - as opposed to stylized - that it presents great challenges to its audience and its interpreters. Haunting play never quite comes into the light BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Niagara-on-the-Lake THIS is a fascinating play that begins in darkness and never quite comes into the light. It starts shortly before sunrise in an English country garden. On a bet, an aristocratic house-guest surprises and kisses the daughter of the house. Whether this is a joke, a serious affront, or a sign of actual feeling, nobody seems quite sure, least of all perhaps the assailant himself. Halfway through the play he is engaged to the girl, though not before he has fought a duel with her father. At the final curtain the young lady, Ann Leete, is indeed married: to her father's gardener.

As actor, director, playwright, theorist and critic, Harley Granville Barker has a good claim to be considered the father of the modern English theatre. He wrote The Marrying of Ann Leete, his first play, in 1899, and set it 100 years earlier; every character in it is, in some way, reeling from the impact of the revolutionary events across the Channel. "France!" cries the conservative Mr. Leete at one moment of reminiscent anguish. But it's an isolated cry: isolated from the dialogue immediately surrounding it, and from the ostensible concerns of everyone on stage. What is most crucial goes unmentioned, and that is typical of the play: startling when it was new, and fairly surprising even now.

Barker's dialogue is hauntingly, searchingly precise. It also - a huge achievement - manages to keep faith with its own time and the one it's writing about; nothing is anachronistic, nothing is archaic. There are quotable lines too good - too humanly and dramatically truthful - to be epigrams: "When you consider it, no one lives in the polite world" "Why did you desert the army for politics? - Better fighting." And, as dawn breaks on the opening episode, "The sun has risen to show us to ourselves."

What it reveals, as the brighter among them are aware, is not preposessing. Ann's father is a member of Parliament who has quit his party and is being wooed back: hence the presence in his house of the noble lord, Ann's suitor. The engagement is a front for political reconciliation, and Leete Senior's ambiguous attitude to it reflects the self-disgust that has become his ruling passion. Some of it is diverted toward his son ("the begetting you, sir, was a waste of time" - it sounds like something out of Restoration comedy but is meant in deadly earnest) who professes Republican principles but is ashamed of having married beneath him. An older daughter, on the brink of divorce, looks sardonically forward to an enforced rural seclusion, well supplied with "milk, butter and eggs." As a breed, the Leetes are intelligent, but Ann is the only one in whom it hasn't curdled.

I love the play. But I have to admit that a piece so precisley styled - as opposed to stylized - presents great challenges both to its audience (maybe one should always be seeing it for the second time) and to its interpreters. Neil Munro's production is lucid, but its first half looks and sounds surprisingly tentative. Eighteen years ago the Royal Shakespeare Company inspirationally cast Mia Farrow as Ann, but for any other actress the precise blend of innocence and toughness must be difficult to find. Ann Baggley is frequently too knowing, actually too modern. Christopher Newton has all the urbanity for her father, but the corroding pain, though present, is subdued. Seana McKenna as the incipient divorcee bites the hardest; and her interview with her husband's emissary (Sandy Webster, painfully discreet) is the best-acted scene in the show.

The last act is Ann's marriage party. Her relatives are on hand to patronize the new son-in-law: a ceremony that Leete himself savagely orchestrates. Affronted servants look on, their snobbery even more thoroughly inflamed than their employers'. The scene plainly is meant to be a rout, out of a cartoon by Hogarth or Gillray; Barker wrote it on those lines, though he maybe threw in too much too quickly, and Munro takes him up on it. Silent at the back, stands the gardener-groom himself, not too subtly named Abud; as Roger Honeywell plays him, he has loomed through the action with increasing authority, and you can see why Ann chose him. At the end, the focus narrows to the two of them in their cottage; it's a chill, unromantic scene, but vestigially hopeful. Ann Baggley, at her best here, is still, painfully, but obdurately, learning.
