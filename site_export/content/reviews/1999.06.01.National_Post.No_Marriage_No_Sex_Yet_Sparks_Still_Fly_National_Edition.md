GETTING MARRIED

Court House Theatre,

Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont.

- - -

I would count myself a fan of Bernard Shaw, but I have my limits; and I have always drawn the line at Getting Married. This puts me in the same boat as many of the characters in the play; they draw the line at getting married, too. The play consists of them talking about it.

The Shaw Festival's new production just about converted me. There were still times when I could have echoed the cry of Eliza Doolittle (Alan Lerners, not Shaws): say one more word, and I'll scream. But none of the discussion here is tedious, and some of it is downright scintillating.

The play breaks down into a series of ensembles, trios and duets; and Jim Mezon, the director, has arranged them, both aurally and visually, to maximum effect. He has also, I think, made some crafty cuts. This could be part of the theatre's Y2K preparation, though for them the millennium is an occasion for celebration rather than crisis. For 2000 is the year in which Shaws copyrights run out. And then "wot larks," as the blacksmith said in Great Expectations.

Getting Married happens in 1908, in the house of the Bishop of Chelsea. (Chelsea is a suburb of London; it has never had a bishop.) His Graces sister-in-law is about to tie the knot, but both bride and groom cry off when they realize at the last moment just what they will be letting themselves in for.

He recoils at the prospect of being held responsible for his wife's debts. She has a far longer list of the rights and freedoms that she stands to lose. Ninety years on, her concerns resonate considerably louder than his.

Around them are grouped a selection of representative attitudes. There are the bishop and his wife, comfortable in their marriage. There is the wife's other sister, name of Lesbia, a self-described old maid, courted for years by a retired general who keeps repeating that each proposal is his last.

There is her other sister, who has just traded in her foursquare husband for a younger and prettier model, in effect exchanging a diet of roast beef for one of sole meuniere.

The bishop is a worldly cleric, but he has a celibate chaplain, Father Anthony, who decidedly isnt. He is deputed to draw up a new kind of marriage-contract which will satisfy everybody. The participants cannot even agree on a first clause. They decide to consult a different oracle: Mrs. George, the lady mayoress, known for her wisdom, her devotion to her coal-merchant husband, and her wide experience of other men. She arrives at half-time and takes over the play.

As usual with Shaw, the one subject not discussed is sex: at least not explicitly, and not with any kind of poetic suggestiveness either. Sometimes this reticence serves him well. He can deal, up to a point with flirtatiousness, and erotic fencing. He can deal with disgust; few things in the play are more effective than when Lesbia hints darkly at what repels her about union with a man. He cannot deal with consummation; he gives Mrs. George a trance-like aria, widely regarded as the most embarrassing thing he ever wrote, in which love is depicted as a one-way street, the woman giving pleasure and receiving none.

The play has been wittily set by David Boechler in a partially- curtained gazebo, backed by topiary hedges with large holes, through which we keep seeing the characters in new perspectives.

Acting in this kind of play is a matter of striking 12 at once; you dont sneak up on these characters, you reveal them -- and yourself -- the moment you open your mouth. The best performance comes, for the second time this week, from Sarah Orenstein who makes Lesbia's thoughts flash across the stage.

Second best is Barry MacGregor as the abandoned husband, half aggrieved and half complacent. Third best is Gordon Rand as his ostentatiously fastidious rival, who doubles as the latest conquest of Mrs. George. Fourth best, lively though somewhat untidy, is Severn Thomson as the bride who keeps everybody waiting at the church.

After them the deluge; they're an intelligent and personable deluge but they dont have the authority to see their roles through.

That doesnt include the two outsiders who deserve special attention. The constantly tempted (and inflexible) Anthony is a clever creation, a lawyer turned priest, and Simon Bradbury plays him cleverly, though with an uneasy edge of caricature. Sharry Flett moves from Mrs. Danvers to Mrs. George, an even more impossible role, and is frustrating i.e. exciting without being satisfying.

She gets triumphantly through the long speech, helped by discreet staging, and is fine as the seductive sybil. She is not convincing as the coal man's wife, as far beneath her admirer socially as she is beyond him sexually. I hate to sound like Professor Higgins (Shaw's and Lerner's), but she hasn't found the right accent.
