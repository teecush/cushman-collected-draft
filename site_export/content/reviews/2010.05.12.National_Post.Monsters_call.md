FRANKENSTEIN

Bluma Appel Theatre, Toronto

---

No sooner has Young Frankenstein departed Toronto than old Frankenstein appears to take his place. This production from the Catalyst Theatre of Edmonton shouts "art" as emphatically as the Mel Brooks spoof yelled "commerce," but the two shows have a surprising amount in common. The new arrival, based fairly closely on the Mary Shelley novel, is a tremendously talented piece of work, and I hated it.

That's an exaggeration. I only hated some of it, mostly before the intermission. The beginning of the second half is magnificent. Frankenstein's monster, a sad hulking figure in white, encounters an old man in a forest. The man is blind, and thus immune to the feelings of fear and loathing that the unfortunate creature arouses in everyone else he encounters. The old man's family, however, can see, so happiness is short-lived. While it lasts, it is extremely touching.

One great thing in its favour is that it's spoken, unaccompanied. Everything that has preceded, and much of what follows, is sung or recited to music, mostly in rhyming couplets. The show is not, by any conventional measure, a musical, and yet it's through-composed. The text, the music and the direction are all the work of Jonathan Christenson.

The music, unexpectedly, surpasses the words; the song that this Victor Frankenstein sings to his monster while in the process of creating him is beautiful. But Christenson's greatest achievement here is his staging. He and his designer, Bretta Gerecke, are jointly credited with "conceiving" the production; the choreographer, Laura Krewski, deserves to share in the glory. The sets are made out of paper and, along with twisted and fantasticated harlequin costumes, they create a mocking storybook phantasmagoria.

What Christenson's show has in common with Brooks' is a pervasive atmosphere of camp, though his is cold camp where Brooks' was warm and fuzzy. Brooks belly-laughed at the source material; Christenson and company sneer at it.

The show also moralizes, in a complacently nonspecific way. "We close our eyes, we cover our ears," sings the chorus at the beginning and end of the show, "this is the way we've done it for years." They could be warning us against misusing science (genetic engineering) or ignoring it (global warming). Or they could simply, like so many Fat Boys from The Pickwick Papers, want to make our flesh creep. Their Dr. Frankenstein bears no traces of the traditional Promethean overreacher. He's a capering, inbred neurotic, compensating for his incapacity for human contact: your standard mad scientist, in a context with delusions of higher significance. Given this conception, Andrew Kushnir turns in a virtuoso performance, especially impressive in the way he can spring from braying parlando to piercing falsetto in the course of a single vocal line. The only sympathetic figure is, naturally -- or, if you want to be literal, unnaturally --the monster himself, played by George Szigalyi as lovable, lumbering and lonely, i.e. in the same way he's played in every adaptation; he's not that far removed from Mel Brooks' junior version, winning all hearts while puttin' on the Ritz. The production's craft is dazzling; the content, or lack of it, is numbing.
