Susan Coyne is emerging as a very funny writer even as she has, over the past few years, emerged as a very funny actress. Her new play, Alice's Affair, contains a line about an unfashionably conformist university that I can barely restrain myself from quoting. It contains several more that wouldnt make much sense if I did quote them, but that work splendidly in context. Coyne, in short, can write verbal comedy and she can write situation comedy.

Unfortunately, the play in which these qualities are being exhibited doesnt add up. Part of it is about a middle-aged woman trapped in what seems to be a constricting marriage; another part of it is about the various relations between literature and life; and one part seems to have been superimposed upon the other.

The eponymous Alice was once a creative-writing student with a demanding teacher regarded by many, notably himself, as charismatic. He is dismissive of Alice's writing, though he would probably claim that he was being cruel only to be kind. He also, we gather, toyed with her emotions, and maybe with her person as well. She certainly wouldn't have been the only student he had had this kind of relationship with.

Flash-forward to now, or thereabouts, and Gregory, the predatory professor, is being buried. His ex-students, who for our purposes are Alice and her friends and contemporaries, gather at the graveside to pay tribute. Afterward, they adjourn to Alices summer cottage and interact. There are Jane, a gold-plated confidante who cant understand why Alice is so shocked at her having a string of boyfriends; Cheryl, who has forsaken her earlier ambitions for lesbianism and good works; and Marie, Cheryl's partner and a psychic. And there is David, who was once Alice's lover and -- her husband being tied up in the city -- would like to be again. David is the only one of the group who has stuck to writing, quite successfully, though he is haunted by the likelihood that Gregory would not have approved.

The trouble with all this is that Alice, the linchpin, is not credible as a writer. I dont mean that she couldnt write; theoretically, anyone could. I mean that her author hasn't told us much about what kind of writer Alice ever wanted to be, and that she lets us forget about those ambitions for most of the play. The mature Alice could be any discontented woman tempted by any importunate men. The ones in question don't seem to be any bargains (both the late Gregory and the extant David come over as unstable egomaniacs) but that doesnt get us very far.

In its attempts to bridge the gap, the script plays some games. We start, entertainingly enough, with one of Gregorys lectures, a series of showy put-downs. Then, as a sort of student's revenge- fantasy, we get the first part of Alice's current biography, presented as a series of scenes in which Gregory is dragooned into playing supporting roles. (In one, hes a waitress.) Then, more or less straight, we get the old buddies reunion; this, giving a stageful of talented actors a chance to pull together, is the most fulfilling part of the play, and certainly of Eda Holmes's production. It culminates in a seance, which in turn leads into a dream sequence in which everyone concerned seems at their wits' end. Much earlier mention has been made of William James (author of The Varieties of Religious Experience) and of Harry Houdini, and both in some sense materialize, but not to any clarifying effect.

Martha Burnss Alice is self-effacing almost beyond the call of duty, at least until she takes a late opportunity to lose her temper. Daniel Kashs Gregory is enjoyably arrogant while alive but an uncalled-for irritant when dead; and neither Brenda Robins (Jane) nor David Jansen (David) bring anything fresh to their roles. Brooke Johnson, though, is amusing as Cheryl, a super-malaprop who mangles not just words but whole proverbial phrases; the joke becomes mechanical but I kept on laughing anyway. As Marie, a disconcertingly practical oddball, Nancy Palk is toweringly delightful. The proceedings are rarely dull, but they're rarely satisfying, either.

Trying, an autobiographical piece by Canadian-American Joanna McClelland Glass, is as smooth a ride as Alice's Affair is bumpy, but its less interesting and equally lacking in any visible point. Unless, that is, the author, who for dramatic purposes has renamed herself Sarah Schorr, is out to prove what an exemplary secretary she once was. She performed that office nearly 40 years ago for Francis Biddle, former attorney general for FDR and a judge at Nuremberg; and, apart from a tendency to repeatedly split her infinitives, she seems hardly to have done a thing wrong. (She does get pregnant, by a husband who apparently no longer loves her, but thats a matter for sympathy, not blame.) She reminds her employer, now 82 and appearing under his real name, of an illustrious liberal past that he seems irascibly in danger of forgetting, and teaches him to use a Dictaphone. This cross-generational two-hander is painless but bland, right down to the obligatory final scene in which Sarah, through a carefully phrased phone conversation, informs us of her patron's death. One problem is that the fragments we're vouchsafed of Biddles former life are more dramatic than anything we see or hear him do now. Another is that an octogenerian hero is apt to be a drain on a plays energy level. Paul Soles, though, without any obvious cheating, keeps this one burning fairly bright; he is crotchety without being obnoxiously endearing. An actress less pretty than Caroline Cave might provide a stronger counterweight. Also, in a play that's full of quotations (as the lady said of Hamlet and might also have said of Alice's Affair), shes prone to attacks of poetry-voice. The play probably invites them, but it might have been better to refuse the invitation. Otherwise, Marti Maradens production effectively dispenses light and shade, while Christina Poddubiuk's study-set, crammed but not cluttered, looks a beautiful place to live and think in.

- - -

Steve Lucas has designed an equally fine though contrastingly bare set for Sky Gilbert's Rope Enough, the kind of vast empty room in which anything can happen and almost anything, including Gilberts production, can look good. The title pays homage to the Hitchcock film Rope, and also (though nobodys acknowledged it) to the highly revivable Patrick Hamilton play on which the film is closely based; and they in turn derive from the 1920s Leopold-Loeb case in which a well-heeled male couple, disciples of Nietzsche, murdered apparently for kicks. In this updated version an equally swank duo, disciples of Derrida, murder or claim to have murdered, for a kaleidoscope of reasons. Maybe they want to subvert conventional ideas about gays; maybe they want to confirm them; maybe they just want to have fun. Their author-director, as capricious as they are, plays with all the possibilities, to surprisingly provocative effect. He muddies the waters, though, by throwing in a subplot about a self-proclaimed gay role-model who goes about having unprotected sex. Perhaps we're meant to think of him as the real killer; perhaps we're meant to think him a cool joke at the expense of the plays raconteuse, a friend who keeps insisting she isnt homophobic and may be standing in for the rest of us. The acting is uneven, meaning that by conventional standards it's often awful but that it gets its points across and can sometimes be very tense; Gavin Crawford as the more articulate accused exemplifies all these things.

- - -

Chekhov's Heartache is the fourth, no less, of Theatre Smith- Gilmours dramatizations of the masters short stories. They have drawn this time on two of the bleakest, Peasants and Peasant Women, which here appear even bleaker since the original's spare, swift narrative movement turns into a trudge when acted out; no matter how swiftly and adeptly the four actors switch character, their patented style (lots of mime and vocalized sound effects) transforms questioning tragedy into dogged gloom. The junior players, Clare Calnan and Monica Dottor, are fine, but the troupe's founders weigh the show down. Michele Smiths face can transform itself into a mask of pain but the sense and flavour of English speech continue to elude her. Dean Gilmour lacks both the range and the power for the acting problems, which here include an especially damnable blend of cruelty and hypocrisy, that he insists on setting himself.
