CHEKHOV LONGS ... IN THE RAVINE

Factory Theatre Studio

- - -

The troupe known as Theatre Smith-Gilmour follow up its successful Chekhovs Shorts with Chekhov Longs. Note the subtle change in punctuation. The new show is uneven, but with searing moments of greatness.

The earlier program framed and adapted a bunch of the Russian master's short stories; it was omnibus theatre. The new piece concentrates on a single tale: the In the Ravine that gives the production its subtitle.

This staged novella, unlike the familiar Chekhov plays, concerns the peasants, not the gentry. We are introduced to the family of the Tsybukins, villagers who in the Russia of 1900 have done pretty well for themselves. They owe their prosperity to old man Tsybukin's talent for swindling his neighbours by selling them off-colour goods.

An ironic sign of this merchants success is that one of his sons, Anisim, has become a policeman. The other son, Stepan, is a simpleton but at least he makes an advantageous marriage. Father himself is encouraged to remarry. Anisim, who must have inherited some of the paternal talents, returns home in murky circumstances and there seems nothing for it but to marry him off as well. From here on, a cheerful narrative turns irreducibly, if still sardonically, tragic.

Theatre S-G is named for, and run by, Dean Gilmour and Michele Smith, who are this productions directors and, with the Company," its adaptors. They first hooked up 24 years ago while studying in Paris with the celebrated Jacques Lecoq, whose name is practically synonymous with modern mime. Their skills in physical performance are formidable and have been passed on to their fellow actors. All present are adept at sketching the physical outline of one person (or, on occasion, an animal) and then doing it just as adeptly for another. A wedding feast provides a showpiece opportunity; the cast switch roles and personalities at the drop -- or, more often, at the wearing -- of a hat.

They are less good -- at least Smith and Gilmour are less good -- at delivering text; and this play, which, despite the aesthetic trimmings, is a pretty straightforward rendering of its source, has a lot of text. Smith has a distractingly heavy French accent, though she still provides an amusing cartoon of the feckless Anisim.

Gilmour -- the only male on hand -- is more of a problem. He plays the central role of Tsybukin pere; he is also our host, establishing the village setting at the start with the aid of a couple of bricks. (The image is apt; brickmaking is the basis of the local economy, and a cruelly hard trade.) But his performance in both guises lacks weight and colour; also, though he has a reputation as a clown and favours us with a variety of conspiratorial smiles, he is not very funny. He is silently impressive at the end, when Tsybukin is a broken man, but he has not had much to fall from.

Three actresses complete the cast. Anne-Marie Kerr is properly agreeable as the new, and kindly, stepmother. The real excitement, though, comes from her daughters-in-law. Aksinya, the clever wife of stupid Stepan, is very reminiscent of another Chekhov character, the arriviste cuckoo Natasha, who fouls the nest of those politely helpless Three Sisters. Like Natasha, Aksinya enters into a family and, in the name of sound business and moral principles, devastates it. Colombe Demers makes her bloodcurdlingly implacable, though the fireworks they provide seem the province of the actress rather than the character. They are not quite rooted.

As a take-over artist, Aksinya gets her golden opportunity when the ex-cop Anisim gets arrested, leaving a bewildered bride, Lipa, who can now be described (and humiliated) as "the convict's wife."

In this role, Liisa Repo-Martell is astonishing. She enters, for inspection by her new family, with a shy smile that is more than an adornment; it seems to be her bodily essence. A person has come upon the stage, fully imagined and fully fleshed, and she remains there.

It is instructive to watch a scene in which she and her father- in-law converse while crossing the fields. The movement has to be stylized, and also true. Gilmour walks like an accomplished mime; Repo-Martell walks like Lipa, earnest, well-meaning, and striving to keep up. Her lamentation at her direst moment -- a sustained off- stage cry, unearthly but all too human -- is as harrowing a sound as I have heard in a theatre. It is like the stories one hears of Laurence Olivier's Oedipus: extremity made manifest.

Repo-Martell can also, as required of all the actors here, break off to assume other roles; and she does it, I think, more fully than any of them. (Her wedding guest, silently gossipy, is a hoot.)

In the three crowded years since she burst upon us, seemingly out of nowhere, in Soulpepper's Platonov, this is the finest work she has done.

To return to the show's punning title: the length can be excessive, the longings are superb.
