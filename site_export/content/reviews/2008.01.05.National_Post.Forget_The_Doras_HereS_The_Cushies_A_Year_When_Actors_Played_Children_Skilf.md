Alook back at some of the best performances of 2007 and the shows they came in:

Seana McKenna ( Orpheus Descending) The best came first, since this scalding performance was unsurpassed by anyone this year including McKenna herself; though Shakespeare's Will, her Stratford solo, confirmed that she can do anything--except, apparently, sing madrigals. The Orpheus ensemble in Toronto was slightly less starry than it had been at Stratford but hardly less good.

Eric Peterson, Carolyn Hetherington, Diego Matamoros and all ( Half Life) Another ensemble return, heart-rending and impeccable. Like the play.

Daniel MacIvor ( Monster, House) This retrospective season cemented MacIvor's fearsome legend as a theatrical soloist. His plays elsewhere, involving other actors, were less exciting.

Maude Mitchell, very tall, and Mark Povinelli, very short, mixing their own metaphor in Mabou Mines Dollhouse, a visiting show from New York whose Ibsen deconstruction was alternately exasperating and invigorating. In a contrastingly sober WorldStage import, Aalst (from Belgium), Felix van Groeningen and Lise Pauwels, as child- murderers, took naturalistic behaviourism as disturbingly far as it can go. Habib Dembele and Pitcho Womba Konga ( Sizwe Bansi Is Dead) History lived.

Anand Rajaram ( Tip of the Melting Iceberg), the best Second City revue in ages. Thom Allison (Elegies, The Wizard of Oz) Star quality. Yvan Pedneault, Erica Peck, Jack Langeduk ( We Will Rock You) Fresh Canadian talent in a stale British show.

Albert Schultz and Company ( The Threepenny Opera) Soulpeppers fresh and mature combining on a largely excellent production of the hardest tuner in the repertoire.

Jordan Pettle, Yanna McIntosh and Cliff Saunders ( Danny, King of the Basement) playing children, for children, as seriously and skilfully as if they were playing grown-ups for grown-ups, in a play that's a genre classic.

Michelle Monteith and Maev Beaty ( The Russian Plays) A revived Hannah Moscovitch play paired with a new one, both inspiring virtuoso acting. Monteith, an acrid waif, was the young actress of the Toronto year, sharing honours with Steven McCarthy in the beautiful Revisited (from Halifax) and with a fine team in the exacting Crave.

Nicola Lipman ( Scorched) Stonily impressive, in a play and production not so much scorched as overheated.

Joseph Ziegler, Family and Associates ( A Whistle in the Dark) Yet another perfect remount, with Ziegler's Falstaffian patriarch matched against Allan Hawco's terrifying tearaway.

Susan Bennett and Ryan Kelly

( Will the Real JT Leroy Please Stand Up), transcending camp in a Sky Gilbert play that does the same, even if its author doesn't want it to.

Jane Spidell and Oliver Dennis

( Leaving Home), turning marginal characters in one of those "Canadian classics" into newly polished comic jewels; in the central role, Kenneth Welsh was equal parts incomprehensible and enchanting.

Tara Rosling ( Saint Joan) Moving if not exactly soldierly, and surrounded by a sterling Shaw Festival crew. Opening week also found this company at its best in The Circle (everybody), A Month in the Country (nearly everybody), The Philanderer ( Ben Carlson and Deborah Hay). Later on and better still, Goldie Semple, Mary Haney and Trish Lindstrom were a transcendent trio in The Cassilis Engagement, a real discovery. As were The Kiltartan Comedies and their young leading man, Patrick McManus.

Fiona Reid ( Homebody/Kabul) She had a very good year but this was matchless virtuosity. Sarah Dodd ( Marion Bridge) The best in this, she also shone in Whistle and Age of Arousal, which also featured Clare Coulter in a world of her own.

Brian Bedford ( King Lear) Moving as he's never been (and more so than Ian McKellen's quizzical king in the U.K. and U.S.). Also Scott Wentworth's superb Gloucester. Elsewhere in Stratford's opening batch: Cynthia Dale and Laird Mackintosh in My One and Only, exhilarating, like the show. Lots of people in a provocative Merchant of Venice. Jonathan Goad, magnetic, and Lucy Peacock, overwhelming, in Othello, both also shone elsewhere and she was the actress of the year.

Two George Walker ensembles,

veteran ( Better Living) and juvenile ( Tough).

Soulpepper Unlimited. Four great shows. If Top Girls, the newest script, also seemed the thinnest, it called for and got seven superb performances. The Time of Your Life (Joe Ziegler again presiding), Mary Stuart (Nancy Palk v. a grown-up Yanna McIntosh) and Three Sisters had whole stagefuls of them.

Everyone ( A Delicate Balance, The Odyssey, Pentecost) Richard Monette's Stratford regime all-but-concluded with three superbly exciting shows on successive nights. Nigel Shawn Williams's Odysseus was a giant step.

Shaun Smyth and Paul Fauteux

( The Pillowman) Brothers in the best new foreign play. Brendan Gall, Paul Dunn and Diana Donnelly ( East of Berlin). Friends, enemies and lovers in the best new Canadian play. Though both pieces transcend boundaries.

Bob Martin ( The Drowsy Chaperone) Call him irreplaceable. Philip Graeme ( The Prince Hamlet) A partial text, a complete performance. Brent Carver ( The Elephan Man) Fresh light on a faded play. Randy Hughson ( The Drawer Boy) A new way to play a role thought to be definitively taken. David Ferry Ubiquitous.

Judy Kaye ( Sweeney Todd) The best Mrs. Lovett -- and there have been lots.

Ron Pedersen, Deborah Zentilli and a man-eating plant ( Little Shop of Horrors) Pedersen also good, along with Adam Brazier, in the earlier Rocky Horror Show, but second time was the charm.

Kurt Browning ( Peter Pan) Skating and flying. Kate Hennig ( White Christmas) A show-stopping surprise. And, in this show, a relief.

It was also the year we lost Bill, Ed, Bluma and Richard: One great artist, two great enablers, and one who was both.
