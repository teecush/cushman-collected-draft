Crisp and trenchant and witty, Margaret Atwood's The Penelopiad is a book that cries out to be read aloud. In Atwood's own stage version, the cries are half answered, but only half; the author's voice comes through from time to time but mostly the play steers an uneasy course between recitation and dramatization. Or perhaps, since Atwood's text is a response to The Odyssey, I should say that it reverses Homer by showing that if you try to sail between Scylla and Charybdis, you can still end up being dashed by one and drowned by the other.

The show is a first-ever collaboration between Canada's National Arts Centre and Britain's Royal Shakespeare Company. This is more notable historically than artistically, as the result displays neither organization -- and indeed, neither nation's theatre, -- at anything like its best. Nor does it hold out much hope for Atwood's proving as notable a playwright as she is a novelist. Both on page and stage, The Penelopiad tells the story of The Odyssey from the viewpoint of the hero's wife, that by-word for patience and fidelity. The fidelity remains in this version--at least Penelope, as narrator, says it does -- but the patience is wearing thin, certainly within her own head. She addresses us as a dead woman: current and permanent residence, Hades.

When Homer's Odysseus returned from 10 ten years of war and 10 years of wandering, he celebrated by killing the hundred suitors who had been besieging his wife and feasting on his foodstuffs. As an encore, he hanged a dozen female slaves who had been sleeping with the intruders. The latter act has struck many readers down the ages as, to put it mildly, gratuitous, and Atwood agrees with them. She structures her own book around the deed and the various characters' responsibility for it. Guilt is sloughed off by Odysseus, who ordered the executions, and his son Telemachus, who carried them out, but bitterly acknowledged by Penelope who, we learn, encouraged her twelve favourite maids to make nice with her tormentors in order to find out what they were thinking, but failed to warn anyone about it.

Atwood finds the Homeric story shocking, and with reason. But she can't do much with her shock except state it, and then re-state it. This works all right in a novella but not in a play. If we are to care about the maids' deaths, we must have felt that they were once alive, and these women have no dramatic life: not as individuals, not as a chorus. Atwood actually calls them a chorus in the book, and she gives them a series of odes describing how miserable the life of a slave girl, even a favoured slave girl, inevitably was. Some of these interludes are plaintive, more are sardonic. They are a lot more fun to read than they are to hear, at least in this production.

The same goes for Penelope's monologues, which are the backbone of both incarnations. Gone almost entirely, and much missed, are her commentary on everyday afterlife in the underworld, and on what she occasionally hears or glimpses of the continuing world upstairs. (She knows about the Internet, but who was Hitler?) There's an acid laugh on nearly every page of the book, starting with Penelope's observation that Odyesseus "got away with everything" and that getting away was one of his "specialties." The words are still there in the play, but they aren't funny. Penny Downie, the fine Australian actress (now living in Britain) who plays the queen, is the main reason for seeing the show; in fact, I would settle for re- naming The Penelopiad The Pennydowniad. (It's certainly easier to pronounce.) She can be very powerful, and she can tell a joke; like Seana McKenna, whom she most resembles among Canadians, she was once a virtuoso Chorus in Henry V. This evening's at its best when Downie just stands and delivers, but even she settles too easily and too early for doom and gloom.

The rest of the cast, and the production team, are divided about equally between both countries. Canada supplies some actresses with good track records (Corinne Koslo, Jenny Young, Kate Hennig, Philippa Domville) but both they and their British sisters could be anybody, whether gyrating in the chorus or stepping out of it to play individual supporting roles, of both genders; the British Sarah Malin is a very matey Odysseus. Music is by a U.K. composer, Warren Wills; his main contribution is to drown out the words of the songs. In one number, a sea shanty, this may be a blessing: Best not to have speculate on whether the awful lengths to which Atwood is prepared to go in seach of a rhyme are meant to sound horrible. It's performed in sailor suits twice; Penelope remarks that in Hades, "a certain amount of vapid dancing goes on," and Veronica Tennant, the choreographer (ours), takes her at her word. The direction, by Josette Bushelle-Mingo (theirs), is just dull: a visual and emotional void.

Chances are that it worked better in both respects at its first stop, the intimate Swan at Stratford-on-Avon (which is many people's favourite space) than it does at the yawning NAC, which is unlikely to be anybody's. But there's an irony here; it's impossible not to compare The Penelopiad to the dramatization of The Odyssey itself that the NAC's own director, Peter Hinton, mounted this year at the Canadian Stratford. That was an infinitely more imaginative production of a superior play, one that re-invented its original instead of plonking it down while losing the best bits. When it comes to specific elements the two stories have in common -- the killing of the suitors and the portrayal of Helen as a brittle minx - - the contrast is painful.
