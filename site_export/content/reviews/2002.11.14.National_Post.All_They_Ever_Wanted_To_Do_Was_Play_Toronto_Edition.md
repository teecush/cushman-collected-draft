SIDE MAN

Tarragon Theatre, Toronto

There is one scene in Side Man, Warren Leight's play about a lost generation of American jazzmen, that works better now in Toronto than it did four years ago in New York -- only one, but it happens to be the most important in the play.

It's the '60s. Three middle-aged horn men, hanging on to what's left of their careers after the onslaught of rock, hear for the first time a bootleg tape of the last tune played by the young trumpet prodigy Clifford Brown, just before a car crash took him in 1956. They are, of course, blown away. On Broadway the actors signalled their rapture by starting to finger imaginary instruments, in effect joining in -- an outrageously cute bit of business. The Toronto cast are far truer to the way in which actual musicians would behave and, incidentally, to the author's stage directions. They are very still. One of them occasionally slaps his fingers; another nods his head. The third, the play's title character, simply sits there, rapt. We can see right into him. We can hear what he lives for.

The scene comes halfway through the second act and it lifts the whole play. This is just as well, since for much of the time this production falls lamentably short of the mark. Its entire first act reminded me of miserable evenings spent in London watching English actors stumble through American plays -- a sensation I've never experienced in Canada before.

Side Man is one of those memory plays. It's narrated by Clifford, a jazz musician's son (as is the author). His parents had planned to name him Francis Albert, but something in the Sinatra connection went sour, so they named him after Clifford Brown, who died the year our Clifford was born. Gene, Clifford's father and the sideman of the title, met Terry, Clifford's mother, in the early '50s (though it feels more like the late '40s). She was studying classical flute; he was playing in clubs in New York and with big bands on the road, and it seemed as if the work would never dry up. Besides, he was good, and -- like many a fan -- she fell for him, his sound and his aura simultaneously. After Clifford's birth, the marriage goes violently sour. Gene is hopelessly unrealistic, believing there will always be gigs, that radio and TV will always need live musicians with jazz chops and that no reasonable soul would dream of leaving New York for, say, Las Vegas. Terry, the former ingenue, reacts by getting drunk -- angrily and bitterly and, on some occasions, almost fatally.

Gene hangs out with three fellow sidemen from the Claude Thornhill Band, a fine progressive outfit that Clifford oddly describes as "legendary" and "completely forgotten." (It can hardly be both.) It may seem strange that the four guys should find themselves working together on all their subsequent engagements, but have you never heard of dramatic licence? We meet Ziggy, who lisps, and Al and Jonesy, who ends up doing serious time for heroin and is therefore not on hand to hear the Brown tape. There is also Patsy, a waitress, who seems to go with every trumpet man in town, consecutively. (She marries several of them.) Between them these characters provide us with a capsule history of the bebop era and its aftermath; essential facts are fed to us with unobtrusive brilliance, and we get to feel what the life was like, why it ended and how its survivors are faring among the ruins. Or how they fared: "The present" in this play is 1977.

The domestic scenes take us nowhere we haven't been before, but they are tense and believable. If nothing in Terry's early behaviour foreshadows her later descent, then nothing forbids it, either. Anyway, the actors seem to find this aspect of the play much the easier to negotiate. Brandon McGibbon's Clifford is very plausible when interacting with his parents, usually with one of them at a time. He is thoroughly at sea in his role as storyteller, constantly sticking his hands into his waistband, in an apparent effort to look casual, and talking about jazz and the jazz life with a hushed reverence that suggests a square radio host trying to sound hip. Clifford isn't a musician -- he's a commercial artist -- but he grew up with the music and he would not be approaching it on tiptoe.

That's the trouble with Andy McKim's production as a whole. It looks all right; designer Sue Le Page has placed high grey windows in a brick wall that carries right on from the architecture of the theatre itself. But it lacks tone, it lacks rhythm, and much of the time the characters sound as if they don't know what they're talking about. Musicians wouldn't speak to one another in awed tones about Miles and Gil; they would all know who Miles Davis and Gil Evans were and would feel no need to put them in italics. Small things -- but they add up, destructively. Tony Nappo, Jim Jones and especially Oliver Dennis (the lisper) make a decent ensemble, but they are "characters" -- from any play -- rather than denizens of a unique world. Laura de Carteret as Patsy just overdoes things.

So, moving to the leads, does Jane Spidell in Terry's early scenes; she lays on the naivete with a trowel. She serves the later Terry, hardbitten and vociferously wounded, far better (though physically she hardly seems to age a day).

I have to say I missed Edie Falco, whom I saw play the role in New York and who is currently my favourite American actress. Actually, I missed her in two senses, since if I hadn't been at Side Man Tuesday night I would have been home watching The Sopranos. And it occurred to me that Falco's role as Terry was not unlike her later role as Carmela. Financial levels apart, both women's marriages follow the same, not uncommon trajectory: They fell for a maverick and ended with a brick wall -- though Gene makes Tony Soprano seem like the model of a solicitous spouse.

Gene doesn't get it; that is the gist of Oliver Becker's performance, which, though not immune to flaws of the production in general, is very subtle and complete. Compared to his buddies, Gene seems very sober and solid, a plausible husband and father. But that makes him even further removed from what the rest of the world calls normality; he doesn't need eccentricities to sustain him, he only needs the music. He really is a sideman; he's on the edge of life. Becker is a subtle and versatile actor who recently went grossly over the top in The Bear (in Absolutely Chekhov) but is here thankfully back on form. You believe that this unassuming, not to say empty, man can play like an angel. As indeed he does: I don't know whose records are subbing for him offstage, but they are gorgeous. The music, though heard only in snatches, is a huge emotional part of the play; like the Hamlet speeches in In the Wings (or come to that in Rosencrantz and Guildenstern Are Dead), these songs sound even more resonant in an unfamiliar context. For a last number, Clifford requests Why Was I Born, which, tellingly, is his mother's favourite. But what Gene plays, no less tellingly, is It Never Entered My Mind.
