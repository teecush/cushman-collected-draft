THEATRE REVIEW

Awake and Sing!

Young Centre for the Performing Arts, Toronto

Clifford Odets' Depression classic Awake and Sing!, revived by Soulpepper, is an odd duck: a political play whose conflicts are played out in purely domestic terms. In theory, that might be the best of both worlds -- we like to be made aware of wider issues, but we dont want to have polemic stuffed down our throats. In practice, theres a disconnect.

The play is set in the Bronx apartment of a poor Jewish family, the Bergers. Grandfather Jacob, from the old country (unspecified, but presumably Central European) is a convinced Marxist who lectures his daughter, Bessie, her husband, Myron, their two children and everyone else within earshot on the desirability of a life that isn't, in his recurring phrase, printed on dollar bills. And he does get through to the youngest generation. By the end, under his indirect tutelage, granddaughter Hennie has left her husband and baby for a new life with previous admirer Moe, a one-legged bootlegger who's also the lodger. Her brother Ralph declines to follow his love, preferring to stay and fight the good fight. These two paths to happiness, both probably doomed, would seem to cancel one another out. Anyway, some revolution.

In 1935, when Odets play was premiered by the pioneering Group Theatre, it probably added up better. Times were terrible, and audiences could extrapolate. (Yes, theyre getting terrible now, too, but "timeliness" can only take you so far. About two minutes into an evening, by my count.) Also, the play had a secret weapon. It was the first play to put working-class Jews on the American stage, and take them on their own terms. In this respect, it anticipates or parallels Lorraine Hansberry's A Raisin in the Sun which, 25 years later, did the same for American blacks, and which Soulpepper triumphantly staged last year. I would have expected Awake and Sing! to have the same impact, but it doesn't. Raisin has a firmer grasp of dramatic cause and effect, in terms of both plot and environment. Its characters predicament was intimately bound up with their being black. The Bergers has little to do with their being Jewish; anti-Semitism(amazingly, considering what was going on at the time) is hardly mentioned. They could as easily be poor Gentiles. Except, of course, that they wouldn't talk so entertainingly. Odets' dialogue, if not as consistently rich as its sometimes held to be, is what has kept the play afloat.

The current actors do well by it: Few of them are Jewish, at least as far as Im aware, but they're nearly all idiomatically fine. And yet, six years ago, there was a scratch production of this play in Toronto, with a mostly inadequate cast. I wrote at the time that it made me want to see the play done by someone else: the Shaw Festival or, so help me, Soulpepper. Be careful what you wish for, I guess, as with a first-rate director and actors, I'm still dissatisfied. Line by line, scene by scene, performance by performance, Miles Potters production, on a suitably brown set by Peter Hartwell, is impeccably sensitive, impeccably lucid; everybody responds intelligently to everybody else. But the evening crumbles away. The play isnt big on plot, but it does feature some far-fetched developments that the production can't make convincing or even very relevant.

The family is held together, and the play wants to be, by Bessie, a caring tyrant of a matriarch. She bullies her pregnant daughter into marriage, refuses to let her son's shiksa girlfriend in the house (which, unity of place and theatrical economics being what they are, means we never meet her) and heads off any disagreement at the pass. Nancy Palk plays her, in the first part of the evening, superbly: earthy, commanding, sensitive in her own way, on top but never over it. In the second half, handed a big bitter speech of self-justifying self-denial ( "I bought a new dress every week? A lover I kept?") she weakens, sacrificing anger for sentimentality and downplaying her scene of greatest vindictiveness. But her dwindling is partly the plays fault; it runs out of things for her to do. The big decisions are made behind her back, indeed after her last exit. She ought to be there.

Derek Boyes seems whimsically out of place as her perennially defeated husband, substituting a Celtic smile for a Yiddish shrug. William Webster might profitably be more whimsical as Jacob, who retreats by playing Caruso records, but he does the secular-prophet bit imposingly well. A shame that he has to make his greatest contribution to the plot from offstage. Michael Hanrahan, with a silk scarf wrapped around his vocal chords, is absolutely right as the familys comparatively rich Uncle Morty, and Ari Cohen righter than right as Moe whose amputation on the last day of the war made him a cynic, a pacifist and--as it transpires -- an unlikely do-gooder. The young people are very impressive: Jonathan Gould quivering with life as the irresolute Ralph, Sarah Wilson hard as nails as Hennie and Matthew Edison is lovely as the poor schmuck tricked into giving Hennie's child a father. It would be hard, and probably wrong, to play the last life-affirming moments for irony. But taken straight they seem wished for, tacked-on. Play and production are awake, no question, but they don't sing. - Awake and Sing! runs through July 31 at the Young Centre for the Performing Arts.
