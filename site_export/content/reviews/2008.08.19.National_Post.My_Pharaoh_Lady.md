George Bernard Shaw's idea of Julius Caesar was Shaw himself in a toga: an enlightened thinker and non-stop talker who was also a man of action. Shaw himself, of course, didn't command armies on the battlefield, but he got into a lot of political fights, and not just on paper. He was also a ladies' man who liked to present himself as above all that, or -- seen from another angle -- a prude who cultivated a reputation as a philanderer.

This is the image that, in stark variance with the historical record, he wishes on the middle-aged Roman general who in Caesar and Cleopatra encounters the teenaged Egyptian queen. In real life, they were lovers and had a child. In Shawspeak, they're teacher and pupil, he a little miffed that she doesn't fancy him, but only as a passing matter of pride, not passion. Their relationship is a dry run for that of Higgins and Eliza in Pygmalion, though a Pygmalion that nobody would have a hope of turning into My Fair Lady. (It was tried once, disastrously.)

Where Pygmalion has a plot, Caesar and Cleopatra just has history, most of it happening offstage. What happens onstage is debate, with Caesar winning every round. That sounds hopelessly undramatic, and yet the play -- certainly in Des McAnuff's Stratford production -- is knockdown entertaining. The trick is that Caesar -- the most enlightened of dictators and the most pacific of generals, one who likes to set prisoners free because it's so much more agreeable and less troublesome than locking them up, a man who doesn't flinch from killing but both abjures and abhors revenge and realpolitik --doesn't have an earthly clue. He's fighting a losing battle, not only against his own time but against all others. The moment he closes his mouth, human nature kicks in. It's about to kick him especially hard. Shaw teasingly claimed -- well, one hopes he was teasing-- that his Roman play was "better than Shakespeare," but in fact much of its effect depends on our awareness of Shakespeare. We are nudged about what awaits Caesar when he returns to Rome. We know Cleopatra will meet another, more congenial Roman and -- more cloudily -- that their story, unlike this one, will be a tragedy.

Ruthless, benevolent and with a hair-loss problem, this Caesar is the most engaging of Shavian supermen. Playwright and character were of a comparable age, even though Caesar was near the end of his career, and Shaw still practically at the start of his. At 78, Christopher Plummer is older than either of them, but he remains a heroic actor with a mischievous streak, and one who shows no signs of fading gently into the sunset. Not that he can't underplay: Caesar, like all the best Shavian heroes, expresses himself largely through deflationary wit; Plummer is a complete master of the throwaway that lands. (An increasingly rare talent, by the way.) He can also, and effortlessly, assume the grand manner; he's a self-puncturing figure, but the self that gets punctured, both visionary and practical, is real.

His mission with Cleopatra is to transform her from spoiled, frightened girl to poised, thoughtful queen. (Still spoiled, however, and murderous.) He gracefully introduces her to herself, and there is a definite sense that Plummer is performing the same service for Nikki M. James, who when she played Juliet at the beginning of the season was acting around the text, and now as Cleopatra is driving right through it. She has all the directness of the schoolgirl, all the imperiousness of the princess; in fact she only falters at the very end, when she gives us all of Cleopatra's sorrow at Caesar's departure but none of her relief. Here the production can't resist going for sentiment.

The best supporting performances are Steven Sutcliffe's prim British secretary, Gordon S. Miller's debonair art dealer, and -- best of all -- Paul Dunn's boy king Ptolemy, sulking and quaking on his throne. Peter Donaldson, as Caesar's chief officer, offers plenty of gruff loud loyalty, but surprisingly few grace notes; while Diane D'Aquila, failing to find the key to Cleopatra's bloodthirsty nurse, settles instead for battering the door down. And in a play that deals largely in types, some roles seem haphazardly cast; if the burning of Alexandria's library is to have either comic or dramatic effect, then the distraught tutor who brings the news has to be a really passionate pedant. Here Caesar, the author-as-philistine (again like Shaw himself ) has nothing to react to. The show, however, looks wonderful, in an appropriately witty way. The two leads meet between the paws of what Cleo calls "a baby sphinx," which here has a faint smile on its projected face. Elsewhere, Robert Brill's gracefully versatile sets are overseen -- and sometimes manoeuvred -- by an assortment of Egyptian gods in black and gold. Paul Tazewell's costumes adorn everyone except the minor Egyptian males, whom they tend to smother; the women are beautifully dressed (and, in a bathhouse scene that might have surprised the author, undressed).

The books say that Shaw was poking fun at the costume melodramas of his own day; I suspect that he also adored them, and this staging allows us the best of both worlds, spectacle worn at a rakish angle. The production is fast and easy. It's a lightweight evening. Which is both right and good. - In repertory at the Stratford Festival until Nov. 8.
