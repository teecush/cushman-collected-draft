Halfway through his five-part series My Israel, Ralph Benmergui says that "everyone who comes here feels they're a victim of something or other." He speaks as a Sephardic Jew visiting an Ashkenazi-run country. His family emigrated to Canada from North Africa, and he attended an Ashkenazic Hebrew school in Vancouver where, as he ruefully admits to a Sephardic journalist friend in Israel, "they treated me like dirt ... we [Jews] were invisible."

"We're still invisible," chimes in his mother, from her Canadian kitchen. We meet her at the beginning and end of the show, and keep flashing back to her in between. Like any good Jewish mother, she worries about her very grown-up son going off to such a dangerous place. She's also proud of him for doing it --With good reason as it turns out, since My Israel is an outstandingly good-natured and inquisitive piece of work.

It's poised, as just about any examination of Israel must be, between despair and hope. Talking to an old Canadian friend who emigrated to Jerusalem decades ago and is fiercely patriotic without being bigoted, he says, "I feel everyone's grievance is real, but no one feels heard," and she doesn't disagree. An especially determined female settler says, "We're all victims -- the Israelis and the Palestinians -- it's the governments' fault." As to what those governments should be doing, that they're not, she doesn't elaborate and Benmergui doesn't press her, at least not in the footage we see.

That's the program's biggest failing; it keeps cutting away just when you want it to ask another question. Two and a half hours is a lot of TV time, but it isn't much in the real world. But then even if the conversations went on forever, they likely wouldn't resolve anything. We're dealing here with irreconcilables -- and not just between Jew and Arab -- that have, somehow and sometime, to be reconciled. Benmergui must have known that before he left, but the knowledge gives his show the tension of a quest. He's resolutely cheerful in his approach, so we get an apparently lightweight show with heavyweight implications.

Monday's episode centred on the Wailing Wall, which he approached with a suspicion that seemed equally rooted in modern scepticism and old-time religion ("isn't this a form of idol-worship? God doesn't dwell in bricks and mortar"), with a side trip to the Holocaust memorial of Yad Vashem ("the place where the reason for Israel becomes clear").

He got to spend Christmas in Bethlehem: "I went from the Wailing Wall to the al-Aqsa mosque to Manger Square in

one day" -- and if you think a North American Christmas looks secular, you should see Manger Square. The timing of his trip means that he also got to celebrate the Christian New Year in Israel. It's a festivity that some younger Israelis (defiantly disengaged, they don't like to believe there's a war on), have adopted with enthusiasm but, to Benmergui's Canadian eyes, with little success; at a quarter to midnight, he still can't find a party.

Yesterday's program was about "the new wall," security barrier or land grab, depending on your point of view. In effect, it works as both. "I know it's evil and not good," says Benmergui's Israeli driver, "but my family comes first." The harassment at the check points is shown, and is shaming, though the sad point it makes is less about Israel than about humanity: power, backed by self-righteousness, will always be abused. "The other" can always be demonized, as Jews know better than most. The series' last episode introduces us to intransigents on both sides (though none of them seem to be what you'd call good haters) and then to an Israeli and a Palestinian family, both bereaved in the conflict, who have come together in a reconciliation program. It's genuinely moving.

The struggle is so wearily familiar that it's almost a relief to be shown, in tonight's "Race and Class" episode, the tensions within Israel itself, a country where the city skylines look increasingly American while, outside them, "many live in poverty because security trumps everything, including social programs." One in three Israelis, we're told, lives below the poverty line. Israel is a place where "you have to make a choice, religious or secular, left or right, Ashkenazi or Sephardi," though the last, of course, may have been made for you. Benmergui's journalist friend has been able, it seems , to make a decent life for himself.

On the other hand, we're shown a Sephardi village where "going into town means crossing the railroad track" with a train threatening to mow you down every 15 minutes. The inhabitants must often feel a kinship with the refugees living in camps "that were not supposed to be permanent but have been here for 60 years."

Indeed the most kindred spirit Benmergui encounters is Mohammed Alatar, an Arab filmmaker -- American-Palestinian -- who shares his own sad, smiling mix of fatalism and hope. In fact, Alatar may be the more hopeful of the two, perhaps because he has to live it. He tells us, at different times, that "the disease is the occupation," but also that "Palestinian hatred is unsustainable" and that he's sure "Israel will still be here, 50, 100 years from now." Everyone agrees. All the same, the last shot is of Benmergui, this nice and charming man, beating his head against the Wailing Wall. - Ralph Benmergui: My Israel airs tonight at 9 p. m. ET on Vision TV.
