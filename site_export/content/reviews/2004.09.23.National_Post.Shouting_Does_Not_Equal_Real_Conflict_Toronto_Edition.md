PRIVATE JOKES, PUBLIC PLACES

Tarragon Theatre, Toronto

- - -

Oren Safdie, son of a famous architect, has written a play in which two other famous architects -- fictitious -- pass judgement on the final-year project of a young student and are very nasty, both to it and to her. The title is Private Jokes, Public Places. I have no idea whether the play itself is a private joke, but it sits very uneasily in the public place that is the Tarragon Theatre.

I lied when I said that the two judges are architects. One of them, a German, is; the other, who's English, is only a critic but he carries on as if he had the authority of a practitioner that should, I suppose, be a lesson to us all. Margaret, the student, is a Korean-American (the play takes place in a city that might well be New York, though theres nothing to stop it being Toronto) and her diploma piece is a design for a community swimming pool. The fourth and final person on stage is Margarets professor, who likes her.

There are other characters, but they're unseen; they are Margaret's classmates, and they are presumed to be sitting where we are. This is a public judging, before an audience. The author sets a trap for himself here, and Alisa Palmers production pushes him enthusiastically into it. The characters dont have to talk to one another, because they're too busy talking to us. They -- the two pundits, especially -- are showing off. This may well be because of the kind of people they are. In practice, though, it's difficult to distinguish between the characters showing off and the actors doing it.

Even as written, the play suffers from a damnable lack of conflict. (Its full of people shouting, but that isnt the same thing.) There is this idealistic but also practical young woman and there are these two well-fed establishment bullies picking, and occasionally hitting, on her. It's obvious whom we're going to side with. Margaret wants her building -- imaginary but on an actual site -- to serve the people who would use it, and is also averse to tearing up a tree that would stand by the side of the pool. What could be more admirable? Her opponents are enraged by her refusal to fit into any recognized movement; anything with "ism" on the end would do. At one point Margaret, who gets increasingly emboldened as the action progresses, says something tactless about the failure of Modernism. The critic immediately rears up: "The Modernists? You criticized the Modernists." Thats the level of caricature were dealing with. Margaret, meanwhile, turns into Joan of Arc.

Maybe Safdie intended a little more light and shade; maybe he intended there to be some right on both sides. It's hard to tell here, because the actors' scrambled delivery makes the arguments impossible to follow. (Think of the lucidity of Copenhagen, which showed, both in its writing and in this years Toronto staging, how ideas and the people holding them can be made dramatic. Or of the Shaw Festivals Man and Superman.) The production tips its hand from the beginning by having the men deliver their opening speeches from off-stage, making it impossible for us to apprehend either who they are or what they're talking about. M. J. Kang -- whose performance, since she's played the role in the plays three U.S. outings, cant be blamed on the current circumstances -- never gets beyond playing Margaret's nervousness. Victor Ertmannis as the Englishman does begin by acting like a human being, but has turned by the end into an angry buffoon. Dan Lett, by contrast, starts out unbelievably snide and snarky as the German, but develops some subtlety in the final stages. David Jansen come off best; his role as the mild- mannered teacher effectively prevents him from screaming at anyone. His mildness is probably meant to be suspect, but his interventions come off here as so may draughts from an oasis.

This is the third time in two weeks that an American play with credentials has fallen flat on a Toronto stage. This Is Our Youth is a commercial production and Frankie and Johnny in the Clair de Lune an independent one, but from an institutional theatre with the Tarragon's tradition, one expects more. Then again: Maybe I missed something but I failed to see on Tuesday night any sign of tribute to the theatre's late founder, Bill Glassco. Not a picture on the lobby wall; not so much as a slip in the program suggesting that the night's performance -- the start of a new season -- might be dedicated to the man who got the place started. I was shocked.
