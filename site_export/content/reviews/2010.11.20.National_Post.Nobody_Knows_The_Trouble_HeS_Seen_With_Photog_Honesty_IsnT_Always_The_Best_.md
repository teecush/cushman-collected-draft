Shows about photographers seem to be all the rage in Vancouver. The Electric Theatre Company has the acclaimed (though not by me) Studies in Motion, soon to arrive in Toronto, care of Canadian-Stage. In the meantime, World Stage brings us a less-heralded work from the same city's Boca del Lupo group, one bluntly entitled Photog.

Studies in Motion has a cast of thousands and is aggressively hi-tech in its presentation. Photog is essentially a one-man show, and though it certainly mixes its media, its techniques are comparatively modest. Studies in Motion derives from the life and work of a real, though long dead, camera man. Photog concerns one who would be alive if he were real. It's subtitled "an imaginary look at the uncompromising life of Thomas Smith," and it would be quite possible to reverse those adjectives: to call the life imaginary and the look uncompromising. The imaginary Smith is a composite figure, a combat photographer whose story has been pieced together through interviews conducted by the show's creators on a trip to Brooklyn, which apparently is where guys in the trade hole up in between assignments.

We meet Thomas in his own Brooklyn apartment, from which he is about to be evicted: not for nonpayment of rent or anything but because his building is about to be pulled down in favour of a luxury condo. (The program makes more of a point about this than the play does.) While he fends off the landlords and cops who are knocking on his door, he rifles through his memories, which, of course, mainly means going through his pictures. Being a composite, Thomas has seen service in most of the world's worst trouble-spots: Liberia, Haiti, Afghanistan, Iraq, Ivory Coast. His professional souvenirs are displayed on the big screen that constitutes the set's back wall, searing his mind and, at least in intention, the audience's comfortable consciences.

The show can truthfully call itself uncompromising. It doesn't flinch from showing us pictures of devastation, or from having its protagonist describe atrocities that he has witnessed, recorded or feared. But uncompromising isn't the same as successful or well-planned. The enclosing scenario just seems contrived. It's difficult to see that a middle-class New Yorker's enforced departure from his home ranks anywhere on the same scale of suffering as massacres and starvation in Africa and the Middle East. I have a terrible feeling that it's meant to be a metaphor: that Thomas the truth-teller, having fallen foul of tyrannical regimes abroad, is now being dispossessed by more politely repressive forces at home. I hope I'm wrong.

Thomas begins the proceedings by recalling the old adage about a person's life passing before his eyes at the moment of death; and though he doesn't seem to be dying himself, he has seen enough other people do it, and has been in mortal peril often enough himself, for the feeling to be justifiable. Some of his memories stem from his childhood; he tells us that he chose his career because, at the age of 12, he already found things slipping away from him and wanted to preserve them. Now he is, plainly, at the end of his tether, and not just because of his imminent eviction. He has seen too much. Jay Dodge, who wrote the script, also plays the role; he has an appealingly gentle stage presence but not much range, so his overall agony doesn't count for much. He is best when soberly recounting the gradations of fear experienced by troops on a bomb-strewn road, or when telling a cohesive story, as when he relates an escape from near-certain death during the Ivory Coast civil war. The pictures can be powerful, too, though less than you might expect. The most haunting is shown to us early on, a shot of an Iraqi girl with frightened eyes.

Dodge spends two separate sections hoist aloft in a harness, describing experiences airborne or equestrian. These might be more effective if we couldn't see the stage-hand pulling on his ropes; it may count as full disclosure, but honesty isn't always the best policy. Most of the projections, in Sherry Yoon's production, are straightforward, though there's one snazzy sequence in which a photograph is overlaid with a functioning video. There's another in which an image gets draped all over Thomas's one armchair. His unwelcome callers are glimpsed on the screen in close-up, framed in a keyhole, which is not exactly original but is still menacing. Further menace, and other atmospherics, are supplied by a musician, Pietro Amato, doubling on keyboards and French horn (the second French horn to figure in a play this week). At one point, we get a shot of the Enwave theatre lobby itself. Since Thomas at this point is arraigning Western society for its indifference to the troubles he's seen, the effect is to include the audience in the indictment. But if we're at fault for watching something as bourgeois as theatre, isn't the Boca del Lupo company equally at fault for offering it to us? Indeed, isn't Thomas himself, for all his devotion to bearing witness, also part of the system? After all, he's doing it for a living (as he reminds us) and he could get out, close call though it may sometimes have been. If his martyr status is deserved, the play hasn't justified it to us. His war mementoes are turned into pretty pictures. I know that's not what they're meant to be, but that's theatre for you.
