GUMBOOTS

Pantages Theatre, Toronto

From the townships of South Africa comes the latest musical entertainment to wear its heart, and its title, on its feet. Gumboots are what were worn by black gold miners under apartheid to keep the water out. It came cheaper to the bosses than draining the mines. Since the workers were not allowed to speak to one another, they conversed by stomping their feet. And this, we learn largely from the program, was the origin of a dance style -- a physical equivalent of the blues, and equally adaptable. A group called the Rishile Gumboot Dancers of Soweto have refined, expanded and showbizified it, and their work is the foundation of this production.

Gumboots, then, is a celebratory show with elements of remembered protest -- you hardly get to be an international non-verbal hit any other way. (There is singing, plenty of it, but very little of it is in English.) It begins with half a dozen overalled and, of course, gumbooted men swarming on to the stage; four others will join them at various times during the evening, but this sextet remains at the centre of things. They begin the proceedings with an echoing hymn to Nelson Mandela; then they get down to business. The set is a scaffold resembling a mine-shaft; they take their places within it to mime the journey down. After rather lightly sketching in an essentially horrific existence, they break for an extended party sequence. Then they go back to work, more grimly this time. And they end, of course, with affirmation.

The physical vocabulary is initially compelling, but its the grammar that keeps us interested. The basic repertoire of foot stomping and leg slapping is extremely limited, but the rhythms that combine them are varied and sometimes exhilarating. (The director, Zenzi Mbuli, is both a choreographer and, though not in this show, a drummer.) The high-point, both visually and musically, is a train sequence whose prolonged percussive energy drives the audience happily crazy and sets them up for the restrained lamentation of the final section. This locomotive number is nearly as exciting as the best parts of Bring In Da Noise, Bring In Da Funk -- not quite, because the performers dont make the same individual connection with the audience.

Gumboots doesn't have the narrative and historical pretensions of Noise/Funk. It does, though, have a vein of self-patronizing cuteness the other show avoided. This is especially true, as you might expect, in the party scene. At one point, whether of its own volition or through commercial pressures, the show even flirts with the dread spectre of audience participation. Nobody actually gets dragged on stage, but a lady in the house gets congratulated on the whiteness of her teeth, as introduction to a song about it. (We also get asked to join in some singing, but it's not as if they lower a song sheet or get the sections of the house competing with one another. Besides, it doesn't happen till the curtain call, at which point anything rightfully goes.)

A guy sings a presumably self-mocking song called I'm Too Sexy, which seems to have put the camp into mining camp. Two more, imitating women, sashay and hip swing across the stage to the accompaniment of whistles (of the wolf variety), but are then wittily upstaged by another who simply walks. This celebratory sequence seems to make two points. One is human -- that people, even living in the direst circumstances, will still find means of having fun. The other is aesthetic -- a language born in sorrow will grow to encompass joy. Both are valid. But they are made at excessive length, to justify some rather commonplace fun and games.

The last sequence, though, is a real grabber, embracing a requiem for miners killed on the job and an ambiguous celebration that is also part lament, when the mine is finally closed. I like songs, and songs that join together to tell recognizable stories, so I am suspicious of dance revues passing themselves off as musicals. They seem to me part of the same homogenization of theatre that plunks popular musical movies on a stage and calls them shows; you appease the locals by giving them something they know and the tourists by dispensing with words. The SFX season at the Pantages is loaded with both; and Gumboots, already a hit in Edinburgh and London, has at the least been co-opted into the process. But its roots seem genuine, and the performing spirit is infectious.
