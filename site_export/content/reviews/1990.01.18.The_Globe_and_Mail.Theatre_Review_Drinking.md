THEATRE REVIEW DRINKING

Thursday, January 18, 1990

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Directed by Daniel Brooks Written by and starring the Augusta Company A high-speed merry-go-round for sorry players in a social circus BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail DRINKING, the Augusta Company's new group-authored play at Toronto's Poor Alex Theatre, is a quick and lighthearted look at human misery. The social and marital kinds are especially favored.

It begins forbiddingly. The Poor Alex is, to be frank, a rather dingy theatre, as befits its name, and on Tuesday night the house lights were still dimmed when the audience was admitted. Back in the dear old experimental days of the sixties, this kind of ritual humiliation of the spectator was common form. It was regarded either as well-intentioned therapy or as a suitable punishment for doing anything so bourgeois as going to the theatre in the first place.

Eventually the lights went up, leaving the impression that the whole thing had been a mistake and allowing the audience to read their programs. There was an evangelical tone to the cast bios, which tended to be light on theatre but heavily into health.

Dimly visible at the edge of the stage are seven filled wine glasses, all in a row. Presently the acting area brightens and, one at a time, the actors wander on: four men, three women. Some look bemused and some belligerent. None seems happy. One makes his way to a microphone and suggests getting proceedings under way with a drink. Like alcoholic vultures, the company descends on the glasses and disposes of their contents.

The man at the mike periodically returns to it and gives further orders. This is his party, and the guests are to be regimented into enjoyment. At his bidding, they sing, dance, tell stories: all the things we do to convince ourselves we are having a good time.

No characters are named, either on stage or in the program, but some identities begin to emerge. One lady requests her husband not to drink too much this time, and he sarcastically thanks her for drawing attention to his problem in public. Dancing becomes frenetic, seduction violent, some people fiercely disrupt other people's enjoyment, and in general a good time is had by few.

Oh, what a circus. When the merriment is at its depth, the ringmaster loses patience and tells everybody to start again "and to get it right this time." What ensues is a double-speed replay of the preceding action, foregrounded by a monologue from the host. He, too, has problems. We have already seen a shy young woman commanded to sing, and obliging with an ancient show-tune called Nice Baby. A surprising choice for 1990, but gratifying for musical-comedy buffs. (Early Gershwin? I'm not certain.) But more is involved than nostalgic titillation. The singer is the compere's wife, and the song a commentary on their relationship. She wanted children and couldn't have them. So she infantilizes him instead, and he is not happy about it. In fact, he is cracking up. Such is the state of their marriage. Such, or worse, is the state of all marriages. The show never actually says this, but its whole stance implies it. We drink to forget but all we do is bitterly remember.

Actually the title, despite the elegant introductory image, is a mistake. It suggests a mislaid intention. The show, as we receive it, is not about drinking. That is merely one component in a pretty generalized view of behavior. Still, we get the message; and, were there an interval, bar-takings during this performance might well reach an all-time low. Were there a bar.

There is something of Beckett here, recalling the peremptory stage- direction in Play ("repeat play exactly") and the classic synopsis of Waiting for Godot: "Nothing happens, twice." There is something of the Australian playwright Peter Handke: the opaque revels of The Ride Across Lake Constance. And there is a lot of Mike Leigh. Leigh i s the highly skilled British director of improvised plays and films (High Hopes) who throws people together in unfortunate social situations and watches them comically suffer. His worst fault is a tendency to patronize his characters. Drinking resembles choreographed Leigh; and the choreography (by Claudia Moore) helps to keep portentousness at bay. In a longer show it might have the opposite effect, but Drinking only lasts an hour. By paring down dialogue, Daniel Brooks' direction keeps a neat and clinical distance from any suggestion of real suffering. The show's notions of human conduct are shallow and conventional but the execution is so fleet and twice-over-lightly that you don't have time to resent it.

The actors are loyal and in step. They are Don McKellar, Tracy Wright, James Tait, Claudia Moore, Valerie Campbell, Chris Gerrard-Parker, and David Ramsden, who is sweatily effective as a fat and frightened guy with a guitar.
