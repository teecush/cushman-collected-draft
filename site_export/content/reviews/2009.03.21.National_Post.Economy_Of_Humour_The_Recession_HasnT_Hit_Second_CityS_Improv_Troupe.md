In dishonour of the economic situation, the new revue at Second City has christened itself 0% Down, 100% Screwed, a title that for once takes topicality above the level of the vaguely relevant pun (cf. Barack to the Future, Tazed and Confused and almost any others you may or may not care to remember). Not only that, but the show keeps its announced theme going right through the first number and even into the second. After that, it pretty much drops the idea, but then we wouldn't want things to get monotonous, would we?

About that opening number. It presents five of the cast of the six, lined up across the front of the stage as a pick-swinging chain gang of, to generalize a little, ex-yuppies. Lamenting their various falls from financial grace, they each conclude by intoning the same universal sentiment: "Blame it on the Crisis." Above and on guard stands the remaining performer, who tells us with great deflationary flair that being as he has a cushy job in government, he isnt really affected. Now, this might be satire of the crisis, or it might be satire of satire of the crisis, but either way its an appealing beginning, not least for the way it shows off a very strong team.

The strongest of all is Darryl Hinds, who is impish and unquenchable and seems, this time around, rather underused, possibly because he's so good that he's always going to seem underused. The second number is really his, as a Toronto taxi-jockey whose current passenger would be a prime candidate for that chain gang. As he moans about the problems in his life, our driver counters with his own, which are extreme as hes an immigrant from Gaza, but which he is chivalrously inclined to play down, which of course makes them sound all the more insupportable and his passenger all the more insufferable, because plainly nobody knows the troubles this cabbies seen because nobody wants to know, and I don't think I can type another comma. The sketch goes on too long, like that sentence, but it makes its point, and Hinds couldn't be more whimsically devastating. And thats about it for the economic situation. The show has other fish to grill.

Visually, it may be the most stylish of all Second City shows. The director is Melody Johnson, a performing alumna of the troupe and one of its most distinctive comic actresses. She has infused the production with her own quirky physicality, both between numbers (the transitions are exceptionally smart) and within them. Camellia Koo, similarly distinguished, designs. The evenings highlight is a sketch depicting a children's puppet show at a public library. Now this might be accused of topical relevance, as we're told that budget-cuts may make this troupes version of Little Red Riding Hood into The Last Puppet Show, but the real drama lies in the relationship-spat between puppeteers, and the effect their jealousies and recriminations have on the life-size creatures at the end of their strings who are left to dangle, languish and collide. The marionettes are played by actual actors, and the detail, precision and wit with which its all been worked out are extraordinary.

In a slightly related vein I also liked, though not quite as much, the chubby Marty Adams' turn as a Marco Polo incensed at having been turned into a kids' game. Reid Janisse is aptly manic as a diabolical emissary from American TV faced with the apparently hopeless task of getting Canadian politicians to be rude to one another on camera. (I dont think it is all that impossible but never mind.) Leslie Seiler features as a floating psychic -- she does seem to float -- whose easy deductions about members of the audience inspire her two male acolytes to delirious flights of professional sycophancy. Numbers like this, which have to be at least partially improvised, seem to get the best out of Second Citizens these days; while the proper improvs that follow the main show (and are free if you come in late) were, on the night I went, extremely good. They seemed, bizarrely, to have better punchlines than the written stuff. Caitlin Howden functions as gamine second woman and Kerry Griffin as more or less permanent straight man. Hinds later assumptions include a dainty Barack Obama, paying a state visit to Ottawa, with Seiler in tow as a very fierce Hillary; keeping a few intellectual feet above the ground, this president projects an appropriate air of glazed bemusement, as if uncertain whether it's he or the Harpers who have dropped in from outer space.

I had few belly laughs from this evening, but much pleasure. It isn't among Second City's very best shows, but they are few and far between. It is, however, among their second very best.
