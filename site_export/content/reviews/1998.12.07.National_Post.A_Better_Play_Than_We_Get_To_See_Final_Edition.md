One Flea Spare

Canadian Stage Theatre, Toronto

Half-hearted applause at intermission is always a bad sign. It suggests that audience members are uncertain whether the show is over but rather hope that it might be. Naomi Wallaces One Flea Spare is, almost certainly, a better play than it appears in the shapeless, nerveless production that Alisa Palmer has directed for Nightwood Theatre. The play has a sharp, original aroma, though it's not always clear or agreeable. The production reeks of sludge and good intentions.

Wallace, a youngish American playwright who lives in England, has written what may be the first play ever to address the Great Plague that devastated London in 1665 and 1666. It's a juicy subject, though the dramatists who lived through the event can hardly be blamed for passing it by; this was the Restoration period when drama meant either bustling sex-comedy, or heroic tragedy in rhyming couplets. Neither form would have lent itself to the depiction of ones friends and neighbours dropping dead behind bolted doors and being carted off to mass graves on the edge of town.

A house in which the pestilence was discovered was locked up for weeks. This play begins by playing a cruel joke on two of its characters. Mr. and Mrs. Snelgrave, a well-to-do couple about to emerge from quarantine, are plunged back into it again when their home is invaded by two strangers: a sailor and a 12-year old girl.

So four apparently ill-assorted people are condemned to interact, while a fifth -- a watchman, who might as well be a jailer -- prowls sardonically outside.

The Snelgraves may have stayed alive but their marriage is dead.

She was badly injured in a fire, years before, and he hasnt touched her since. It's left to Bunce, the sailor, to seek out the few patches on her skin that can still feel, and to awaken them. We are told two things. One is that in a world of suppurating flesh, the body can still be a vessel of salvation: a striking notion. The other is that sex is something indulged in mostly by the lower orders: a snobbish cliche of British political drama, and historically unsound. To judge from the literature, the toffs of the 1660s were at it like rabbits.

Satire, sex and heroics are still there but they've been chopped up and seasoned, in a determinedly lyrical style that is partly the authors own and partly a reflection of whats going around today.

Wallace has been highly praised by Tony Kushner, author of Angels in America, and like him she takes large historical themes and fillets them into scenes that are variously funny, pungent, and screamingly tiresome.

It's unfair that the tiresome parts are largely dumped on the young girl, whose name is Morse. The plague has taken both her parents but she rises above it. She spends some of her time scoring pert points off the men in the cast, and 13-year-old Natasha Greenblatt does this very well. But she also functions as a narrator and as the bearer of some kind of transcendental message. She is not yet able to give colour and form to such a torrent of text, and most of what she said lost me.

Her elders don't fare much better. Exchanges that look sharp on the page emerge drained of wit. Actors bear down on one another with what is presumably meant to be an air of menace but there is no danger, no combustion: merely a series of short walks around the stage. Set-pieces come off best.

David Fox as Snelgrave is sweetly self-satisfied when demonstrating to a barefoot and skeptical Bunce the historical inevitability of his own possession of a pair of good shoes. He is even better when inquiring about the horrors of a life at sea, and taking a keen vicarious pleasure in them. ("Scurvy, was there?") But as the symbol of an evil patriarchy finally overthrown by servants and women, he seems far too amiable and rather lightweight.

Woody Dalrymple and Brenda Robins play their erotic encounter with great tact, though earlier she seems more of an automaton than is strictly necessary. Sky Gilbert plays the watchman, who veers between sexual exploitation, democratic rumbling, and half-naked apocalyptic prophecy. He is a presence on stage, but technically limited. He does get to slip us scraps of historical background, and what he says in this line is so interesting that you want the author to can the fantasia and cut to the facts.
