Megamusicals cannot live by song alone

BELTING IT OUT / Toronto's two new blockbusters explore different traditions in musical theatre. Where Ragtime has spoken dialogue, Jane Eyre -- unfortunately -- is sung from beginning to end.

Saturday, December 14, 1996

Toronto -- IN 1971, when Andrew Lloyd Webber and Tim Rice unveiled their Jesus Christ Superstar on Broadway, they were putting a hit record album on stage. Speech doesn't sit well on pop records, so Webbber and Rice did without it on stage, and found that they had invented the through-sung musical. Their subsequent shows, together or apart, institutionalized it.

I've always been suspicious of the genre, and I'm pleased to note that Webber's new show Whistle Down the Wind (which began its try-out in Washington this week) is a book show, that being the trade name for a musical with talking in it. This may be a happy portent. Of the two big new shows in Toronto, Ragtime is certainly a book musical, though its book is properly and most effectively economical. It's crammed with song, but each number is allowed to make its own statement. Jane Eyre isn't a book show, but probably should be.

The rationale for the through-sung musical has always been that contemporary audiences, in their infinite sophistication, would find ludicrous any transition from speech to song and back again. There may have been some sense in this in the days when Webber and others wrote rock musicals. The noise and hypertension of rock might leave the spoken word echoing forlornly in a void. But rock, unable to communicate much on stage beyond its own energy, has long since given place in the theatre to nondescript-contemporary: a sound of which a whole uninterrupted evening is a great deal too much.

The through-sung musical likes to fancy itself pop opera, and it probably thinks of the expository bits between the set-piece numbers as recitative -- the semi-chanted sections which in formal opera carry the plot and fill in the gaps between arias. I used to amuse myself by saying that the only reason people stood for recitative in opera houses was that they couldn't understand the words, but the implied comparison isn't really fair. Aspects of Love, Sunset Boulevard and now Jane Eyre don't use the semi-chant of recitative. They just insert small songs between the big ones. Small in scale, not necessarily in length; some of them seem to go on for ever. After all, they have a lot of story to tell.

A good song can certainly handle quite complicated exposition. Stephen Sondheim's A Weekend in the Country (in his 1973 A Little Night Music ) is a virtuoso example. It takes over from dialogue, and is more entertaining than dialogue, but we still need the book scenes -- if only to underline how much fun the songs are. There is no such safety-net in through-sung musicals. The characters are stuck with singing short-breathed lines whose rhymes come round with numbingly predictable regularity. They make themselves and their authors sound silly.

Paul Gordon, who wrote the score of Jane Eyre, does this doggerel stuff more resourcefully than most, with few real clinkers. But he is short-changing himself as a lyricist, and even as a composer. I would like to hear him writing in a more relaxed manner and on a contemporary subject. I suspect that his music, to adapt the old joke about Wagner, is better than it sounds. Wall-to-wall song becomes wallpaper song. There are some affecting tunes in Jane Eyre , especially the proposal duet for Jane and Rochester, but they sink in the surrounding sludge. The whole show is nearly all in the same nerveless style: one that needs to have drama pumped into it by vocal and orchestral hysterics. (One song that gets bellowed -- twice -- has the incongruously intimate title of Secret Soul. ) What it isn't is sexy, which the Charlotte Bronte love story certainly is. I don't know quite how you would find a musical signature for Bronte's novel, with its tragic undertones and religious overtones. Maybe by using folk-song styles, as was done in Broadway's The Secret Garden in 1991, another period tale from the Yorkshire moors. The gypsy song in Jane Eyre does something in this line; it breaks the monotony, is refreshingly rhythmic, and even has some wit. But a single song does not a complex novel make.

I can think of only three successful musicals based on classic novels: Oliver! , Man of La Mancha and Les Misérables. (The last two are among my least favourite shows but I can hardly deny that they work for huge numbers of people) The curse of adapting most novels for the stage, musical or otherwise, is that you end up filleting them. While E. L. Doctorow's Ragtime hasn't yet had time to become a classic, it's a very special case. It has a complex narrative structure but Doctorow's ironic and (his own term) "reportorial" style means that the task -- a far more congenial one -- is to put flesh on the bones. The book has a pattern of ideas and characters that needs to be respected -- otherwise it ceases to be Ragtime -- but its form, its ambience, and of course its title are inherently musical -- and theatrically musical. I noted when reviewing the CD that "ragtime" here is a shorthand for a variety of styles that flourished at the beginning of the century; we are in at the birth of American popular song, on the forked road that led to jazz and to the American musical. The tradition has been neglected and abused but it is inherently very strong, waiting to be re-activated. Unlike Jane Eyre, which uses a fraying musical convention with no particular relation to its subject, Ragtime naturally comes on singing.

The last musical to use pastiche on so grand a scale was Sondheim's 1971 Follies ,which implicitly criticized the old forms even as it celebrated them. Ragtime is less ambiguous, more open-hearted. Its composer, Stephen Flaherty, may not be a great original melodist but his music dances joyously to familiar rhythms, without great wit but with much authentic passion.

It also fuses perfectly with Lynn Ahrens' lyrics. Lyricists don't get no respect unless they're either Sondheim or dead, but Ahrens is an excellent technician. If you want to hear how cleanly, pointedly and exuberantly rhyme can be used, listen to the hard, clinching monosyllables in Henry Ford and Buffalo Nickel Photoplay, Inc. Ahrens is also a dramatist. She doesn't expose her characters as ruthlessly as Sondheim but she digs into them deeply and sympathetically. Celebrating the allure of the ragtime pianist's "new music," or expressing different shades of baffled social anger, some songs moved me to tears. Others made me laugh. Like ragtime itself, and unlike the screeching melodramas of recent years, the score -- and therefore the show -- has real dignity, real sparkle, real pathos.

Ragtime isn't the definitive American musical that it plainly aspires to be and, by right of pedigree and performance, should be. But it's already a once-in-a-lifetime subject, rapturously grasped.

It isn't the first show to attempt such a subject, but the line of musical epics that capture the movement of history is short. Ragtime 's only major predecessors are Show Boat and Follies, with Fiddler on the Roof as a sort of immigrants' prelude to the American experience and Sondheim's Pacific Overtures and Assassins as something different but allied: what an American critic has called "epics distilled." All these shows have used old musical forms, but with freshness and purpose. (Used otherwise they yield only nostalgia trips, which can range from Hello Dolly to Tommy. ) I don't think it makes them much of a pointer for future musicals -- they are rarities, almost by definition -- and anyway I distrust those critics who are already offering Ragtime up as a musical for the ages: a) they can't know how it will age and b) what matters is how well it works now.

Evolving a form of popular song that can carry stories and illuminate character is one of the great American achievements (and it's unspeakably depressing that they should have surrendered that inheritance to the soulless English and Europeans) but I don't think it can accomplish anything very substantial without the support of the spoken word. Weill and Gershwin accomplished it in their Broadway operas, but only by using a different weight of music -- and they did know about recitative. Speech, song and dance make up a magical trinity; why sacrifice any of them?
