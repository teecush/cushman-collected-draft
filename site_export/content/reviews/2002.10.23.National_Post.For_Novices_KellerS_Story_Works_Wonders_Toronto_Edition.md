THE MIRACLE WORKER

Lorraine Kimsa Theatre for Young People, Toronto

There are certain plays I would rather see at a young peoples theatre than anywhere else, even though they may originally have been written for grown-ups. The Diary of Anne Frank, which was given an excellent production at Torontos YPT a few years ago, is one of them; William Gibson's The Miracle Worker, the current attraction, is another.

Both belong in the category of humanitarian warhorses, well- crafted but simplistic. The Anne Frank play has to strain a bit to extract a hopeful message from the Holocaust but manages, at least in its original Broadway version, to send the audience home with the heroine's belief in the essential goodness of the human race ringing in their hearts. The Miracle Worker has an easier task, coming as it does with a historically certified upbeat ending.

As everybody knows, its the story of how the child Helen Keller, stricken by an illness that left her both deaf and blind, was rescued from darkness and silence by an indomitable 22-year-old teacher named Annie Sullivan. Annie had once been blind herself and had been raised in an orphanage. She could not teach Helen to see but she could teach her to speak and to love -- or at least to express the love that presumably had boiled within her during all the years in which she had lived like an animal, both the pet and the terror of her helplessly indulgent family.

The plays climax comes when Annie, after a long series of mental and physical battles, succeeds, almost beyond her dreams, in getting Helen to pronounce the word "water" -- the very same word that, as a brightly precocious infant, she had learned to speak before tragedy struck. Now she not only utters the word, she connects it to its meaning. This breakthrough comes, in the play, at the end of an unusually crowded day in which Annie has had to battle not only with Helen but with Helen's relatives. The Kellers, an old Virginia family still mentally re-fighting the Civil War, are divided against one another, and within themselves as well. Annie snatches victory from defeat with astonishing dramatic neatness. Her victory is, always and undeniably, moving. It is also, as the author arranges it, glib.

Annie is determined at all costs to spare Helen the institutional hell in which she herself grew up; the point is made through a series of aural flashbacks involving a brother who died there and whose voice, accusing her of desertion, continues to haunt her dreams. Meanwhile the Kellers try to deal with the monster in their midst: not the silent, violent child but the self-opinionated Irish Yankee they have hired to take care of her. Captain Keller (Confederate Army, retired; now in business) is on his second marriage, and he seems in danger of losing and misunderstanding both his children. His son by his first wife is a sarcastic cub who, of course, really craves his father's love. Critics have, in general, written these domestic scenes off as a total loss, and they certainly seemed like hopeless padding in the one previous production (in London, about 10 years ago) that I have witnessed.

Leah Cherniaks production, though, does surprisingly well by them. Greg Ellwand is credibly gruff as the Captain who never knows whether his dignity lies in supporting Annie or firing her. Stephanie Belding beams appropriately as the angel in his house, weak when hes strong and vice versa; and Paul Braunstein is excellent as the redeemable rat of a son who wanders through the play like a subplot waiting to happen.

In other respects the performance is less happy. Cherniak's YPT production of Anne Frank was grippingly claustrophobic (which admittedly comes with the territory); this time, though, her staging rambles. The first half seems to go forever, maybe because it combines the first two acts of what was planned as a three-act play, with cliff-hanging curtains carefully provided. Charlotte Dean's set -- a little house on the prairie masquerading as a modestly fine Southern mansion -- looks pretty enough, but its crannies and levels fillet the action into inaccessible little bits. What may be the crucial scene of the play -- Annies fight to get Helen to fold her dinner napkin -- is confined to a corner of the stage; the centre is occupied by the rest of the cast, standing in the half-light, looking uncertain how or whether to react.

Thus confined, the central conflict lacks its full blood- curdling effect; and the production seems anyway to be pulling its punches. As Helen, Mary Krohnert does efficiently all that is required of her, but there is not much individuality in her tantrums, and correspondingly little joy in her release from them. As Annie, Carmen Grant is tart, bold and occasionally witty, but there is little glow in her determination. She is more forceful when laying down the law to Helens household than in confronting the child herself. There is little chemistry between these two, and the impact is a fraction of what it might be.

Or maybe this is a play that only works the first time you see it. There are certainly no subtleties to be revealed at further viewings. (I sympathize with my colleagues who apparently have had to sit through it dozens of times.) That's what makes it ideal for a young audience, or rather what makes a young audience ideal for it. It is also informative without being patronizing, and this production enhances our education by projecting photographs of Helen's later triumphant progress. It shows us little, though, of what happened to Annie. And thereby hangs an oddity. The name of Helen Keller is rightly talismanic. The name of Annie Sullivan -- despite the enduring and worldwide success of this play celebrating her and named in her honour -- is still, to most people, unknown.
