Alan Ayckbourn writes plays about Scarborough. For many years he ran his own theatre in the spa town of that name in the North of England. He tried out and directed all his own plays there, and maintained extremely high standards of performance and production. It sometimes seemed to me that, in a country that kept preaching about "community theatre", his was the nearest thing to it that actually existed. He wrote and presented plays about the same middle-class people who, as residents or tourists, made up his audience. None of the plays was ostensibly set in Scarborough, but all of them might very well have been. Occasionally he would pursue characters and settings from one play to another. Over the years - and he's been extremely prolific - the plays, linked or not, built up into a British equivalent of Balzacs Human Comedy. Theyre addictive.

The Norman Conquests, now being given a smashing production by Soulpepper, is an early block in the Ayckbourn building, but in itself it typifies the whole enterprise, sharp in perception, modest in tone, crazily ingenious in scale. First produced in Scarborough in 1973, and done in London the following year, it is in fact a trilogy, and the funniest ever written. (The competition, from The Oresteia to O'Neill's Mourning Becomes Electra, isnt any competition.) Its constituent parts are Table Manners, Living Together, and Round and Round the Garden, and they take place in the dining room, the sitting room and, naturally, the garden of the same country house - this one definitely in the south of England, near the coast - over the same summer weekend. Only they dont happen sequentially. Their actions are roughly simultaneous, each showing us what was going on in one area while we and the cast were occupied in another. The author's always maintained that it doesn't matter which of the three plays you see first, but Id recommend seeing them in the advertised order; though admittedly Ive never seen them in any other order. They're a jigsaw puzzle, and part of the fun is fitting the pieces together. It's also, as it turns out, part of the sadness; and I mean that in a good way.

There are six characters in search of a solution. Three of them are siblings: Annie, who lives in the house and takes care of their bedridden mother; Reg, an estate agent, who arrives so that Annie can take the weekend off; and Ruth, a businesswoman, who arrives. The others are Tom, a vet, whos been hanging around for years in vague pursuit of Annie; Sarah, Regs bossy wife; and Norman. Norman is an assistant librarian, when he has the time, and Ruth's husband, perhaps when she has the time. His conquests are the three ladies in the cast: all of whom he virtually or actually overcomes between Saturday and Monday, even though at least two of them claim, with some reason, not to be able to stand him. Norman has appeared, less furtively than might seem advisable, to take Annie for a dirty weekend in East Grinstead (one of those English place-names whose intrinsic hilarity doesn't really travel). It doesnt happen, largely because Annie is dissuaded by Sarah, who claims that she isnt a prude but does have standards. So they're all stuck in the house together. Two unseen characters are the mother up the stairs and a cat up a tree, both apparently with very disagreeable dispositions.

Ayckbourn's specialty is that apparent contradiction in terms, delicate farce; the physical comedy, which can be a scream, always tells something about the people. He is also, and perhaps uniquely, a boulevard playwright who never tells lies. He exposes his characters without mercy, but also without contempt. Ive heard him described as speeded up Chekhov and there are passages, especially those involving Annie and the terminally ineffectual Tom, that are like crazy-house versions of the failed proposal scenes in Uncle Vanya and The Cherry Orchard. The garden play, drawing on our investment in its predecessors, goes especially deep; though it does also include a prolonged sequence of forced misunderstandings that seems to belong in some other play: perhaps Relatively Speaking, Ayckbourns first success and probably his funniest in terms of belly-laughs but something that for better or worse he went beyond. The final curtain of this one, an uneasy calm after a storm, ambushes you with its bleakness.

It's underplayed in Ted Dykstra's production, though theres little other cause for complaint; it gets both the mechanics and the psychology unforcedly right. Albert Schultzs Norman, an overgrown self-tickling puppy-dog, may be going too far in his skittishness but he's going in the right direction; Norman, who alternates between self-promotion and self-pity, describes himself as "a gigolo trapped in a haystack" and Schultz, with encroaching beard and encroaching waistline, brings out both the charm and the selfdeception; he's perceptive about other people, blinkered about himself, so he gets what he wants in the small things, while always feeling frustrated in the big ones. Fiona Reid, mistress of manic gentility, was obviously born to play Ayckbourn, though apparently she never has before; Sarahs obsessive orderliness, especially with somebody elses house and mother, gives her no trouble, but the real treat is to watch naughty thoughts cross her mind, reflected in her swivelling eye and quivering lip, as she realizes that Norman may be hitting on her too. She's wearing two expressions at once. She's also a treat to watch while eating toast.

The great performance, though, is Laura Condollns Annie, a wonderfully subtle and expressive portrait of a self-cast wallflower who has no need to be, and is continually on the verge of breaking out; when she actually does, in anger or in lust, we cheer her on. In jeans or, self-consciously, in skirt, she looks exactly right: she makes impatience attractive. She also communicates that, though Norman may be the most intelligent person on stage, shes the wisest. Derek Boyes gets the beamish enthusiasm of Reg, an amateur games inventor, whose finest moment comes when he satirizes the unnatural movements of a knight on a chess-board. Oliver Dennis is admirably obtuse as Tom, most extreme of the play's trio of oblivious males, though I confess to missing Michael Gambon, the role's London creator, whose was genial bulk made him deliriously funny when seated on a very low stool at the dinner-table with Norman patting him, literally and metaphorically, on the head. Sarah Mennell draws an accurate honey-coloured outline of efficient (and myopic) Ruth, but doesn't mine the material as deeply as she might. And Norman conquers, thrice.
