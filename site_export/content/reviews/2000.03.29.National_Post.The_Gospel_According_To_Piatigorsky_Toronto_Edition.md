THE OFFERING

Artword Theatre

What did Abraham and Isaac say to one another as they journeyed to the mountain on which the father had been divinely commanded to sacrifice his son?

The Bible, which doesn't go in much for small talk, is largely silent on the subject. The first act of Anton Piatigorsky's The Offering, which transfers the story to contemporary Canada, offers to fill in the gaps.

Its second and third acts pursue the relationship of father and son unto the second and third generation, by way of two more of the most familiar Old Testament stories.

We see Isaac, now old himself, defrauded by his neglected son Jacob of the patrimony he intended to bestow on his favoured son Esau. Finally we watch old Jacob coming to request famine relief from an official who is actually his own long-lost Joseph. Abraham, Isaac, Jacob and Joseph: A remarkable family in anyone's book, as Tim Rice remarked in a somewhat similar context.

He might have said in everyone's book, since the Bible casts an inescapable cultural shadow on all of us, believers or not. Like any classic -- perhaps more than any other classic -- it is ripe for plunder.

Still its stories, like all stories, come with certain ground rules, without which they cease to make narrative sense. One thing that the Abraham of Genesis famously does say, when his son asks why they have brought along no animal to kill, is that God will provide a sacrifice. The Abraham of this play never mentions God, never even hints that he is acting under orders.

So why does he take a knife to his son's throat? It seems to be because Abraham doesn't like him very much. You get the impression the kid has repeatedly refused to clean his room.

It is rather on that level. Abraham is jealous of the easy relationship that Isaac has with his mother. (Later, Isaac will feel the same about Jacob.) In addition, the son's whole manner seems to mock the father's achievement; Isaac -- who is older than one has usually imagined him -- lounges around and smokes and is a musician, while Dad is a self-made tycoon. You don't have to be an uncritical fan of the nuclear family to find Abraham's actions improbably extreme (or extremely improbable).

A third character, billed as Ram, combines the Biblical roles of the angel who stays Abraham's hand and the male sheep who gets it in the neck as designated substitute. He is a hirsute, capering figure, who could be man or beast but who most suggests the Greek god Pan, with little horns on his head and a set of musical pipes at his lips. He might, come to think of it, represent the Christian God, preaching mercy and killed on account of it.

But neither the writing nor most of the acting can bring the fable to life. The original story is unpleasant on a grand scale, though it can be interpreted as instruction-by-extreme-case. (Abraham, and through him the human race, had to be taught the hardest way about the futility of human sacrifice; after all, every victim is somebody's child. Just telling him wouldn't have cut it.) This version is nasty on a small scale; Isaac, dizzy with relief, plunges the knife into Ram on Abraham's orders. The knife becomes a family heirloom; instead of a cycle of violence being ended, one has started.

We next meet Isaac blind, immobile and enthroned, and asking which of his sons loves him most: like a cross between Shakespeare's King Lear and Samuel Beckett's Hamm (himself a Lear derivative) from Endgame. Sycophantic Esau takes the Shakespearean hint, dresses up Elizabethan and speaks in the clipped blank-verse accents of Olivier's Richard III.

This is a stunt that British actor-writer Steven Berkoff has been pulling for years, and that is here sustained at wearyingly unfunny length, but Isaac seems to like it (and Piatigorsky, unlike Berkoff, does know how many syllables make an iambic pentameter). Jacob secures his hated brother's portion by putting on his cloak and aping his rhythms. This means he gets the knife; Isaac takes it and ritually cuts off the end of his tongue with it. So Jacob goes through the rest of this act, and the whole of the next, with a lisp.

The last episode, by far the strongest, departs from its source by having Jacob himself plead with Joseph instead of sending his remaining sons to do the job. Here at last we have a dramatic situation that's arresting in itself and fruitful in its implications. Joseph, a cold fish acting as the agent of an all- powerful businessman, takes down the old man's details (Jacob seems to have led a life both selfish and feckless) and realizes, though he does not disclose, that this must be his father.

His employer, Farrow, a.k.a. Pharaoh, is another figure out of Beckett (who is certainly another source worth stealing from): a ragged mechanical apparition who moves like a robot, needs to be wound up by his assistant, and when he eventually speaks, does so in a voice coated with rust. He is about to pull the plug on all charity but consents to let Joseph help Jacob, at a price: Joseph must bind his descendants to Farrow's service. This recalls some of Jacob's own shadier Biblical dealings, and looks ominously forward to the next book. It also suggests that Farrow may be the capricious Old Testament God who has so far failed to manifest himself.

So, on one side we have, finally, love between father and son, achieved by their never having had to live together; on the other we have its cynical exploitation by an outside force.

There may be a grand design when you mull it over but in the theatre you merely get a trio of revue sketches: one overblown; one cheaply thin; and one successful. Chris Abraham's austere production might be an impressive frame for a richer text.

The three actors divide the roles according to age rather than continuing character; much the best is Mark Ellis, playing the sons. As young Isaac he is a sharp-edged junior malcontent, deflating Abraham's grandiose schemes for land development on the sacrificial mount with the weary exclamation "Father, it's a national park!"; as Jacob he projects a cynical disgust worthy of Rowan Atkinson's Blackadder; and as Joseph he keeps the veneer and probes beneath it without ever letting it crack.

Gary Reineke, doing the three fathers, is monotonously mournful, though he manages some prickly pathos at the end. Dean Gilmour (of Theatre Smith-Gilmour, the high-end clown troupe) seems to have been hired as a performer rather than an actor: As the ill-fated Ram, he prances and pontificates; I could hardly wait to see him slaughtered. As Esau, he is doomed to repeat a single verbal joke to which he seems technically and temperamentally unsuited. He appears to be coming into his own as the spectral Farrow, a fine figure of a wind-up doll. He makes a superb visual impression; then, alas, he has to open his mouth.
