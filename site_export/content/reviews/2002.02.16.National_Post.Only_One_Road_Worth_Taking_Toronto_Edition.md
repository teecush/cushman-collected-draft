ROAD

Equity Showcase Theatre

- - -

HOLLYWOOD BOUND

Bathurst Street Theatre

- - -

Road is a fairly good night out about a truly terrible night out. It's set in England during the Thatcher years in a North country town that was once industrial but now, thanks to unemployment, is nothing. A narrator, a tramp called Scullery, invites us to walk with him down the main drag and to peer inside the houses: to look and listen as parents nag at children, husbands yell at wives, and all give back as bad as they get. Most of their energies are targeted at the approaching evening at the disco in the local pub. Already the threat of aimless violence hangs over the street, as does the promise of aimless sex; the local tarts are out in force, and it is hard to tell the amateurs from the professionals.

When I say that we are invited to take a walk, I mean it literally. This is a promenade production, in which the action unfolds in a series of scenes all over the theatre floor and the audience has its attention artfully directed to the next location or merely follows the actors around.

It's a form of staging that serves very well for plays about communities, as opposed to old-fashioned community theatre; we are voyeurs, not participants. It was popularized at the British National Theatre during the 1970s in revivals of medieval mystery plays and adaptations of Victorian novels about small rural towns. These were nostalgic shows; Road, which was produced at the Royal Court in 1986, was the first major attempt to use the technique in a contemporary setting. It was the debut piece of its author, Jim Cartwright (whose later plays include The Rise and Fall of Little Voice) and an instant success.

This Equity Showcase production mixes professional actors with graduating students of the George Brown Theatre School, and here, too, it can be hard to tell the amateurs from the pros. Everybody is very committed and adept. Everybody also sounds amazingly authentic: congratulations to Debora Joy, the dialogue coach, but the conviction here goes beyond vowel sounds. In Mark Wilson's production, the men tread barroom sawdust, the women flash suspenders, to the manner born.

All the same, the play, despite the premium it places on immediacy, seems remote, and I don't mean historically or geographically; we can understand these people, and we do not imagine things have changed much for them in the last 15 years. We start, sharply enough, with a young woman ironing her dress for the night while her mother shouts abuse and demands her money.

That sets the tone; the play's humour, which is real, is all rooted in insult or humiliation. When the author wants to prove that he has a heart, he writes lyrical speeches that certainly don't work as realism and aren't credible as a heightened expression of what the people might actually be feeling. "Joey's story" announces our host with an air of importance, and we are ushered into the bedroom of a young unemployed man who sees no reason to get up, ever, and of his wife who wonders where their lives have gone. You feel for their situation but not really for them.

The show's second half, when the joyless partying is over, contains a record dramatic amount of throwing up. A more or less catatonic soldier has been taken home by an overweight woman and just sits there while she tries to arouse him. There is obviously supposed to be a balance here of grotesquerie and pathos; what we actually get is a sustained leer followed by a brief bout of sentimentality.

The last big scene corrals two young men and two young women. The boys try routine seduction, the girls decide they want something more. So the boys give it to them: they pour gallons of wine and put on Otis Redding singing Try a Little Tenderness. This double stimulus induces a state of heightened awareness, prompting a series of truth-telling soliloquies. As an idea this is reasonable, but the actual words -- culminating in a group cry of "somehow I might escape" -- sound like things the author would like his people to say, rather than things they actually would.

The joylessness is laid on artificially thick, though some of this may have to do with Christopher Morris's performance as Scullery. He is a young actor worth watching but his performance here is a walking gloat with a scowl attached. His best moment comes outside the play proper; during the intermission we move to another room, representing the pub. Here Scullery is inveigled into singing Over the Rainbow. He does it beautifully (and, what's more, a cappella), conjuring up all the innocence that the song and, presumably the play, ache for.

The same anthem crops up in Hollywood Bound, a peculiarly aimless tale that originated at The Georgian Theatre Festival, where it was apparently known as The Company Cabaret: Meaford Goes to the Movies - - a more tactfully modest title in the circumstances. The new name looks especially rash when we see it emblazoned on the suitcases that the company of eight all insist on carrying early in the show. Their opening number, as you might have guessed, is Hooray for Hollywood, and you never saw so much choreographed hand-waving and knee-trembling in your life.

A through-line seems threatened but it rapidly disappears, as does any idea we might be paying tribute to the golden age of Hollywood musicals. Most of the stuff sung here is from films of the last 30 years: a period in which movie songs have, almost without exception, been dreck. Sometimes the show seems aware of this. A performance of the song from Titanic skewers not only the awful number and its awful singer but the conceit of the whole damn movie.

Krista Sutton, whose performance here is the bright spot of the show's first half, returns to even better effect in the second, when she tells Doris Day exactly what she can do with Que Sera Sera. That's just about it for wit, though Sandy Winsby, who seems to be the most experienced player around, manages to sing the song from Love Story with dignity. (No, I wouldn't have believed it either.)

The other performances come in various shades of gauche; sometimes this seems intentional, as when plump young Ken McClure sings the bashful Suddenly Seymour (from Little Shop of Horrors). He's also the one who does Over the Rainbow, in which he gives an accurate impersonation of Mandy Patinkin, whose overwrought interpretation seems to be supplanting Judy Garland's in the popular imagination, which is not a good omen. Nor is this show's benighted belief that it can take You Are Woman, I Am Man -- a funny song from Funny Girl -- and make it work by playing jokes other than the ones the authors had in mind.

Two even weirder examples of slapdashery: 1) Kevin P. Patterson, the show's writer (of what, exactly?) and director, wanders on at arbitrary moments during the first half to make announcements but is never seen in the second; 2) one of the songs is The Best of Times Is Here, which was never a movie song no how. It does, though, herald the intermission, so it might be accused of having relevance.
