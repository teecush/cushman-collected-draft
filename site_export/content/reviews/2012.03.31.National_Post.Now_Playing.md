Tennessee Williams' Vieux Carre is one of his late plays, worked on over many years and first staged in 1977. It harks back both to the New Orleans setting of A Streetcar Named Desire and to the confessed (and confessional) autobiography of The Glass Menagerie. A young man, identified simply as the Writer, tells of his intellectual and sexual coming of age in a lastditch boarding house in the old citys French quarter at the end of the 1930s. Its an engaging play, though as the title tacitly acknowledges, stronger on atmosphere than on story.

The World Stage offering billed as The Wooster Group's Version of Tennessee Williams' Vieux Carre is the work of a New York experimental theatre company with a history almost as long and revered as Williams own. Textually, their show is far less of a deconstruction than might be expected. Most of the plays original lines are still there, and in the right order; maybe they all are, but it's hard to be sure as Elizabeth LeCompte's production seems to be going out of its way to obscure many of them. Perhaps in compensation we get some of the text twice; in the plays second half the lines appear on a screen as the actors are saying them, though you may have to cock your head quite painfully to read all of it. The idea is presumably to remind us that the Writer is in fact writing - that these people, real as they may once have been, are now his creations, speaking his words. Or alternatively that theyre speaking their own words and he's a very fast typist. It also exemplifies the belief, by now rather tired, that using video on stage is automatically new and exciting.

Screens and cameras figure prominently in the staging, which is otherwise adorned with hooks, pulleys and naked bed-frames representing beds. (Then again, this is anything but a deluxe establishment.) Visually it's striking, though like much conceptual theatre it makes its statement early and then has just to go on repeating it. The plays two important female roles are doubled, and so are two of its three important males.

The leading lady is Kate Valk, playing Mrs. Wire, the landlady, and Jane, the one woman among her guests. Mrs. Wire sleeps in the hallway the better to keep an eye on the lodgers. Shes mean, suspicious and bullying, but a softie underneath; like nearly everyone else in the play she's in thrall to a glamorized past, and she comes to look on the Writer as a surrogate son. As written, she's the idea of a memorable character rather than the thing itself; as acted shes even less, since many of her lines are muted or unintelligible and all of them are delivered in a flavourless monotone.

Valks other performance is also monotonous, but the tone's more interesting. Jane is one of Williams' faded and fated genteel ladies (a Yankee this time) tied to an insensitive stud. Verbally at least, she gives as good as she gets, though delivered with more force than resource her outbursts begin to pall. Maybe theres a style here, but bad acting done on purpose is still bad acting.

And, by the opposite token, good acting triumphs whatever the context: can indeed justify the context, as proved by this companys two main men. Scott Shepherd plays Tye, Jane's guy, and Nightingale, a tubercular self-prostituting painter (refrain: You should see my serious work) who invades the nervous Writer's room and helps him come out. I dont mean out of his room. To make sure we dont miss the point he's equipped with a stuck-on and sticking-out phallus. Maybe this, along with his faded magician's dressing-gown, is a tribute to the spirit of old comedy; anyway Shepherds performance is bold and strong and pitiful enough to get away with it.

As is his Tye, a strip-club bouncer who is insistently heterosexual (he insists, that is, that nobody can blow him for less than $100) and delivers, rivetingly, a speech on one of Williams favourite late themes, the tearing apart of a human being by a pack of dogs. Shepherd is miked, as is Ari Fliakos who plays the Writer, but to opposite effect. The electronics make Shepherd sound more immediate, Fliakos more impersonal, as befits a character whose primary function is to observe.

We are told, by him and by others, that he changes and develops, but the change is told, not shown, and Fliakos can hardly be blamed by failing to convey it. Some other characters, and much of the action, would be incomprehensible to anyone who'd never read or seen the play. The potentially haunting figures of two starving and scavenging old ladies are reduced to one actor, in drag and on video and barely visible or audible, while the less said about Nursie, Mrs. Wire's black help, the better. The Wooster Group are always worth seeing, and they obviously meant to honour Williams play, but their "version" may leave you wanting a, for want of a better word, straight production on the lines of Miles Potters Orpheus Descending. Sia, by Matthew Mackenzie, is set in Ghana, where a well-meaning Canadian is kidnapped and held to ransom by a Liberian refugee he, rather patronizingly, thought was his friend. His captor wants to prevent the testimony of a key witness at the warcrimes trial of the Butcher (i.e., presumably, Charles Taylor) of Liberia.

This seems far-fetched to begin with, and hardly less so when it's explained. It gives rise to some intense (or, less flatteringly, semi-hysterical) acting from Brendan McMurtryHowlett and Thomas Olajide as hostage and jailer. Olajide is calmer in scenes he shares with his idealistic kid sister, delightfully played by Jajube Mandiela, though it's confusingly uncertain until very late whether these scenes are flashbacks or whether the brother is leading a double life.

The play touches on the subject, always timely and currently topical (not the same thing) of child soldiers, and the resolution is momentarily powerful, but only that. And if, as seems possible, the intention is to finger the disparity between the worlds indifference to African suffering and its sympathy when its a Westerner who's in danger, then there's been a basic theatrical miscalculation.

In the situation were given, its the Canadian who's the victim. He's the one we cant help rooting for.

The Wooster Groups Version of Tennessee Williams' Vieux Carre closes tonight at the Fleck Dance Theatre, Harbourfront; Sia runs through April 15 at Factory Studio Theatre.
