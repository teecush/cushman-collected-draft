Robertcushman@lycos.com

Michael Healey, stepping up to receive the Dora award for outstanding new play (general division), tugged at his hair and said, I don't believe this ... Robert Cushman seemed so sure I wouldn't win." Were I not even shyer than he is, I would have risen from my seat and shouted, "I wanted you to win.

I wrote last Saturday that Healey deserved the award for his play Plan B, but probably wouldnt get it since hed already won so big for The Drawer Boy, and the critical reception for Plan B had been mixed (i.e., none of the others seemed to like it as much as I did). But what do critics know? I've never been happier to be proved wrong.

The 23rd Annual Dora Mavor Moore Awards, as presented on Monday at the Princess of Wales Theatre, were among the slicker prize days in recent memory, though someone really must persuade the organizers that backcloths with stars on them do not represent the ne plus ultra in glamorous sophistication. Maybe they weren't meant to. One of the endearing things about the annual Dora show is that it seems intended as a parody of the Oscar and Tony shindigs.

Its also more egalitarian: People other than actors are allowed to make speeches and hand out awards; directors, producers, administrators, all sorts, though thus far theyve drawn the line at critics. As for hosting the evening, if the Tonys this year had Gregory Hines and Bernadette Peters trying to pass themselves off as a comedy duo despite looking as if they'd never met before, then the Doras had Diane Flacks and Richard Greenblatt, who are a comedy duo, or were a couple of years ago in their show Sibs. Needless to say, they wrote themselves better material than the Broadway scribes gave to Hines and Peters, and they delivered it better, though there is a limit to the number of jokes that can be squeezed out of Flacks's pregnancy, and they exceeded it.

Also, a certain self-consciousness clung to them; its hard for this kind of banter to sound spontaneous, and it didnt. They seemed to want to be a parody and the real thing simultaneously, especially when they went off into a musical number, celebrating the event's long list of sponsors to the tune (it eventually emerged) of Mack the Knife. This was the only warbling of the evening; music was otherwise confined to John Roby, pianist, saluting each of the winners by tinkling whichever show tune took his fancy at the moment. Next year, I'd like to see Healey host the event, either in his own droll solo person or in cahoots with his partner, Kate Lynch.

The show was also notable for a record number of ties in the voting (the award was split in four categories) and for the unpreparedness of the guest prize-givers in dealing with them. The award for male principal in a musical was presented to Damien Atkins in Real Live Girl, who reduced several participants, including himself, to tears by thanking his mother. (Yes, all award winners thank their mothers, but he really thanked her.)

Five minutes later it was announced he should have shared the title with Graham Abbey, usually of Stratford, for his work in Snow White and the Magnificent Seven. Abbey appeared and gave a good- sport performance to equal the one he offered last Christmas in green tights, tap-dancing with seven dwarves" (his description).

Later, a division of the spoils for outstanding touring production between Medea and Hamlet seemed to leave the representative of Harbourfront, which brought us both, uncertain of which tragedy she was accepting for.

The evening also featured no less than four acceptance speeches by Dean Gilmour -- a lot for a performer whose main talent is for mime. His (and his wife Michele Smiths) company, Theatre Smith- Gilmour, collected that number of prizes for their show Chekhov Longs -- In the Ravine. Three of them -- best play, production, and direction -- were plausible; that for the performance of Gilmour himself, who gave himself far more lines than he could interestingly handle, surely wasnt, especially since he was up against Layne Coleman (The Walls of Africa) and the magnificent Dennis O'Connor (The Butterfly of Chicoutimi). It was strange, too, given all the confetti aimed in its direction, that the really outstanding performance in Chekhov Longs -- Liisa Repo-Martell's -- wasnt nominated at all.

Among the musicals, as expected, Anything That Moves took everything that wasnt nailed down. Christopher Newton, presenting one of its prizes, referred to the show as Anything Goes, which the director, Alisa Palmer, seemed to take as a compliment, as well she might.

Glynis Ranney, outstanding actress in same, quipped how relieved she was that musicals were no longer lumped with opera. The rest of us were relieved, too, since it was a lunatic arrangement. The two operatic categories mainly consisted of the COC running against itself. The winners -- Ewa Podles, for her performance in Julius Caesar; and the Robert Lepage double-bill of Bluebeard's Castle and Erwartung for best production -- were predictable, pretty much in the best way. A prophecy: In the peripatetic world of opera, the winners are never going to be around to pick up their trophies.

Steve Lucas won the design award (independent) for Breath[e], which seemed inevitable as that show is nothing but design; it then transpired that he was meant to share it with the three gentlemen who designed The Girl With No Door on Her Mouth, a piece I regret having missed but will undoubtedly have a chance to see, since it also won for lighting (the same three guys), and two awards are more than enough to guarantee a revival.

Chekhov Longs shared its outstanding production citation with Hrant Alianak's The Walls of Africa, which also carried it off for sound design and best actress. The latter was Tedde Moore; and her prize -- the first of the evening -- was triply gratifying: a) she deserved it, though Dragana Varagic in The Vindication of Senyora Clito Mestres was stiff competition; b) its a comeback for her after years of virtual absence from the theatre; and c) shes the granddaughter of the actual Dora and it's nice that these things should occasionally be kept in the family.

Back in the general division, Garth Drabinsky also made a comeback when The Island, which he brought to Toronto, won for outstanding production of a play. I expressed my bemusement about this category last week, but if it has to exist it makes more sense to give the palm to a visiting show than to some arbitrarily chosen resident package. What, for example, makes Tarragon's Skylight, which was nominated, a more outstanding production than Soulpeppers Uncle Vanya, which wasnt? We all know awards are silly, but at least in the other slots there can be some attempt at objective judgment.

Uncle Vanya didn't win a thing, while Soulpepper's Ionesco double- bill, which I thought good but not on the same level, cleaned up: Tony Nardi, who made an especially generous acceptance speech, got best actor for his Professor in The Lesson; Jim Warren was feted for his direction of The Bald Soprano, and Victoria Wallace was dittoed for her costumes. David Storch in The Lonesome West won the award for best featured (i.e. supporting) performance, to my relief, and Yanna McIntosh in Skylight won for best female performance, to everybodys audible joy.

The Adventures of a Black Girl in Search of God, though not a musical, got best choreography (Vivine Scarlett), which is the award it deserved, and This Hotel got best sound design (Richard Feren, Torontos omnipresence of audio), which is something. Astrid Janson, winning set designer for La Ronde, paid tribute to the actors who had to trundle her creation around. It was a striking set rather than a beautiful one; where beauty is concerned, I still root for Susan Benson's translucent design for Indian Ink. And the Silver Ticket (or career achievement) award went to Bill Glassco, for many things but chiefly for founding the Tarragon.

Some obvious anomalies about the Doras scream to be corrected. It's proper that the authors of plays and musicals should only be eligible for awards at their works first appearance; its absurd that the performers and directors of the same shows can be nominated every time their show gets revived. (At this rate, the COC will be able to get prizes for that Bartok/ Schoenberg double-bill into infinity.) I have some sympathy with a colleague who inquires why The Island wasn't in the touring category (in which it would probably have won), considering it has been on tour for 30 years. And my own score as a prophet works out around 66.66%, especially if I'm allowed to count Michael Healey.
