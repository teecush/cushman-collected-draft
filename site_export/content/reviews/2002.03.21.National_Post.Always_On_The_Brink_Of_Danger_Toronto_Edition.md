MOJO

Berkeley Street Theatre, Toronto

Returning from holiday I find myself catching up with a seven- year-old British play that still provides a good, dark and dirty time. Also, for me at least, a nostalgic one. I never thought I'd see a Teddy boy upon the stage again: those teenage British toughs, or would-be toughs, of the 1950s.

Mojo is a first play by Jez Butterworth, who was born 10 years after the 50s ended. The play re-kindled a vogue for edgy, funny and nihilistic plays about small-time urban hoods. It offers the continuous possibility of violence, than which nothing is more dramatic. Butterworths handling of this constant threat and its occasional manifestation is brilliant. (That's a word his characters use a lot; it sounded anachronistic to me, though I guess he's done his homework. But I query his inclusion of laid-back. Very 60s.)

The scene is Soho, among the seedy clubs that battened on the rise of rock n' roll with its quickly manufactured local idols. The rising talent here is Silver Johnny, named for his suit, who up to now has been controlled by Ezra, a small-time mobster. Ezra never appears, for good and gruesome reasons. Somebody bigger, also unseen, has been bidding for Johnny's contract. We do meet Mickey, Ezras second-in-command and the oldest person on stage; and Ezras son, known as Baby, who seems to have been alternately abused and indulged and whose behaviour is consequently unpredictable; and a trio of minor menaces called Sweets, Potts and Skinny. Nearly everyone is on pills, and the second half shows them all under siege, little fish awaiting killer sharks.

If menace is the characters' style, it's also the authors; Butterworth is a beneficiary of Harold Pinters discovery that the cross-talk dynamics of Samuel Beckett could be mated to, and enriched by, the rhythms of cockney slang. Mojo begins with a long dialogue between Sweets and Potts, who understand little of what is happening around them, but sense that it's important. We sense the same, though we understand even less. But we're held, and we laugh. Butterworth is good at building up tensions and creating an atmosphere in which anyone will betray anyone else at any time, or would like to. This is not in itself, though, as rich or thick an effect as the author might think; like much thats aggressively hip, Mojo seems to have a few illusions of its own.

The first half is set in a small room, for which the designer, Lorenzo Savoini, supplies an exaggeratedly steep perspective that is more striking than helpful. The second half, in a bigger room, looks like standard realism, though there is no detectable change in the writing. When a full complement of interacting actors is on stage, Daryl Clorans production is excellent; when only two or three are gathered, it tends to the self-indulgent.

Dylan Trowbridge and Christopher Morris play the underlings' opening duologue for speed rather than sense, adding more mystification. Trowbridge at least looks good as the sickly Sweets, with a marionette walk and the face of an especially melancholy poodle. Morris develops considerable authority as the more confident Potts, half jocular, half jugular. But their Rosencrantz and Guildenstern act never quite pans out; if we are meant to see the action through their eyes, then the production is at fault, but I suspect it's the play. It wants to give equal (though no greater) time to Hamlet and Claudius -- Baby and Mick -- who are actually more interesting, but are cramped.

Blair Williams' Mickey anchors the play, as he is meant to, leaning hungrily forward. Mickey is a middleman trying for king, or at least kingmaker, though some of his actions remain baffling even after his motives are clear. Nor is it plausible that he should end up shedding tears over a relationship to Skinny -- a foul-breathed simpleton, much victimized by Baby and twitteringly well played by Damien Atkins -- that has never been established. The really sensational performance is given by Mike Shara as Baby, more childishly, insolently dangerous by the moment: dead in style, dead in period, and dead real.

Silver Johnny is played by Michel Protti, who also produces the play for Theatrefront. This company's self-proclaimed mandate is to push its members to "new emotional and physical limits." Protti certainly qualifies on the second count, since for much of his own appearance he is hanging upside down; it is good to meet a producer who is prepared to lead from the front and at such an angle. A refreshingly un-boring evening, worth hurrying to.
