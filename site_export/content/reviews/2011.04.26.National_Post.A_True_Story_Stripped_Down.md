THEATRE REVIEW

Calendar Girls Royal Alexandra Theatre, Toronto

Here comes another idea masquerading as a play. And in this case, it was somebody else's idea.

Calendar Girls derives from the well-known story of a group of mature members of a women's institute in the north of England who decided to pose sort of nude for a calendar, in order to raise money for a new visitors' sofa in the hospital where the husband of one of them had recently died of leukemia. Their initiative paid off beyond their wildest dreams, bringing in thousands of pounds for cancer research. It also gave rise to a successful movie, which in turn became a West End play though, possibly due to a Titanic act of selfdenial on someone's part, not a musical. Not yet, anyway. In the meantime, the play, which has now arrived in a Canadian production, does manage to squeeze in a few songs. These are pretty much padding, but then so is most of the play.

Writing that can pass on the screen can seem very exposed on a stage; and though I never saw the film, my impression is that Tim French, who has adapted his own film script, has made few allowances for this. All his play has to offer is the basic situation, and the goodwill that naturally attends it. The facts have presumably been fictionalized. They've certainly been pared down; there are only six ladies in the group, posing for a month apiece; how the other half waved is never explained.

The premature widow is named Annie; her bossy best friend, who originates the scheme, is called Chris. The others are Jessie, a retired schoolteacher; Cora, who sings and plays the piano; Celia, who ranks as the sex symbol of the troupe by virtue of having in her youth been photographed topless on a Harley-Davidson; and Ruth, who acts dowdy and repressed.

The actual photo shoot takes place at the end of the first act. After that, the play has nowhere to go. There are murmurs of puritanical objections from the community but they fizzle out as soon as mentioned. To fill out the time before we go home, the play has to give each of its central sextet a manufactured back story.

Marti Maraden's Toronto staging doesn't do much to fill out the gaps. It's the play's fault that what should be the crucial scene, the one in which Chris sells the grieving Annie on her idea, hasn't been written. But it's the production's fault that when the two of them confront their friends with it, the moment simply glides by. Even more, the real-life selling point has been blunted. These ama-teur pin-ups were respectable women "of a certain age." The actresses who have been cast here may be age-appropriate on paper, but few of them look it. The story is cut adrift from whatever realistic moorings it may once have had.

Given all that, there's some OK acting going on. Barbara Gordon takes the honours as the acid-tongued teacher. Fiona Highet is a watchful, touching presence as Annie; Fiona Reid enjoys Chris's wisecracks, as do we. Kathryn Akin brings warmth, if no great sense of character, to musical Cora, and Terri Cherniack's Ruth develops rather impressively from caricature to feeling person. Jane Spence, though, really does seem too young to be in this group and compensates by playing with a coarseness surprising in an actress I've previously admired in roles ranging from Ophelia to Ben Affleck.

There are men present, too. Dan Lett graphically depicts John's all-too-rapid decline, and Gordon Tanner hovers amusingly between delight and embarrassment as the designated cameraman. Robert Jones' sets, from the U.K. production, are unhelpfully skimpy. On which subject, whatever the posters may be trying to suggest, there's even less actual nudity in this show than there was in The Full Monty. Coming more up to date: after Billy Elliot and Ghost Stories, Calendar Girls makes Mirvish Productions' third British turkey in a row.

Calendar Girls runs until May 28. For tickets, call 416872-1212 or visit mirvish.com.
