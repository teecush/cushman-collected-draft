You know about metatheatre: a play or show that comments on its own nature or its own circumstances. The original production of Le Malade Imaginaire, Moliere's last play, is a strong contender for greatest metatheatrical event, ever. (Don't rush; it opened and closed in 1673.)

In this satirical comedy about a hypochondriac, the title role was played by the author himself who was himself very ill and who pulled off an unrepeatable coup at the fourth performance by dying, right there on stage.

That's the legend, anyway. When the audiences at the three previous performances heard about it, they must have felt cheated. But even they had been treated to a jolt of what we preposterously call post-modernism, though it's obviously as old as Aristophanes. They got to hear Moliere, who had already made fun of doctors in his other plays, railing in his protagonist's persona about the theatrical libels perpetrated on the medical profession by that intolerable scoundrel Moliere. Laugh? They thought he'd die.

This scene still comes off well in the Pleiades Theatre revival entitled Dying to be Sick. Unfortunately, it's about the only scene that does.

The translation, by Adrienne Clarkson and Pleiades' founder John Van Burek, may well be very good (it leaves in tasty gobbets of the original French, just as Van Burek's Goldoni translations regaled us with the occasional mouthful of Italian) but most of the actors mash it up so much that it's hard to be sure.

This isn't altogether their fault; the director, Brendan Healy, has put them into grotesquely exaggerated wigs and frocks, and ordered up strangled accents and coy balletic poses to match. Almost the only person on stage allowed to act like a human being is Hardee Lineham as Argan himself, the perpetual patient; he delivers a funny and credible account of a tetchy tyrant, high on his own imagined sufferings, living only for his next prescription enema.

The production must think that his humanity will be thrown into sharper relief if he's surrounded by shrieking cartoons who might be his bad dreams come to life.

It doesn't work that way, though. The play's satire on human credulity, especially in matters of health and mortality, may be forever pertinent, and its jabs at the price of drugs certainly strike home right here and now, but it's still a one-joke assembly- line piece that needs all the reality it can get.

Argan, like many earlier Moliere figures, is an obsessive who takes his delusions out on his immediate family. Like his predecessors he has a daughter whom he wants to marry off to a man who'll humour his fantasies (she, of course, is in love with someone else) and a wise maidservant who does her best to save him from himself.

In this production, the maid (Michelle Polak) is a cackling ninny, the daughter (Stephanie Broschart) a simpering gold-digger -- a shallowly cynical idea quite unsupported by the text -- and her true love (Karl Ang) a nondescript accomplice.

It may have been my fancy but I sensed that all these actors were having qualms about taking their director's medicine and would have preferred to play their characters undoctored. The actress playing Madame Argan, who really is a gold digger, seemed on the other hand to be enjoying herself going over the top, and I found it hard to forgive her for it. It is of course possible for actors to achieve truth through caricature, but they probably need to be more accomplished or experienced than most of those here.

The only one who doesn't let his trappings play him is Alex Poch- Goldin as the youngest of the play's parade of venal quacks. You can understand what he's saying and why.

There are no trappings on Victor Ertmanis as the hero's brother- in-law -- the one who offers to take him to a comedy by Moliere -- and his entry brings a breath of fresh air. But he dissipates it when he starts belabouring his points; as the play's voice of reason, he shouldn't sound so desperate about his own arguments.

The play ends with a harlequinade sequence -- it's officially called a ballet --in which the hypochondriac, cured of thinking himself incurable, is mockingly transformed into a physician.

William Hutt, in an otherwise forgettable Stratford production, once did this enchantingly, tossing off phrases in Latin as if in a dream. This production, true to its own logic -- and it does know what it's doing, even if what it is doing is wrong -- makes it more of a nightmare.

It doesn't have either the skills or the resources for the job, and it seems to go on forever, but my impression was that Argan died at the end of it. That would make it the author's own joke, trumped; at least one other revival has had the same idea.

But as Moliere himself was uniquely equipped to remind us: Dying is easy, comedy's hard. - Dying to be Sick runs until Nov. 4 at the Theatre Centre. Call 416-872-1212 for more information.
