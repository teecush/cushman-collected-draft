So far, this has been an exceptional new Toronto season. The Clockmaker, Billy Twinkle, Fernando Krapp, Through the Leaves, Blasted, Hard Times, plus Second City and Cirque Eloize, all within a month. There have been letdowns, too, of course, some of them severe. Though not a disaster, The New Electric Ballroom has, surprisingly, to be ranked among the disappointments.

The surprise stems from the fact that it is an Irish play, presented by a Canadian company, the weirdly named MackenzieRo, that specializes in such; and Irish drama, what with Martin McDonagh and Conor McPherson, has been riding high this last decade or so. Enda Walsh, the author of The New Electric Ballroom, has come in on a more recent wave; his play The Walworth Farce won high praise here when an actual Irish company brought it to World Stage, and I regret having missed it. This new one, though, despite some fine acting, seems cramped, mannered and contrived.

It starts with a woman standing with her back to us at a coastal cottage sink, delivering a high-velocity tirade whose only discernible specifics are that it sounds extremely bitter and makes recurrent use of the word "fish." It turns out that she is called Breda, and is the oldest of three sisters, all of whom are present on stage as the play begins and indeed never leave it. The others are named Ada and Clara: that's ABC, though the alphabetical order is teasingly different from their order of birth; Ada, who's pushing 40, is the youngest. She's also the only one who ever goes out; she works at a fish cannery. Fish is, so to speak, in the air: a recurring topic of conversation in the house (see above), the presumed economic mainstay of the surrounding community and the occasion for the only visits the sisters receive. These are from Patsy, a bashful youngish chap, who delivers the stuff on what seems to be a daily basis and lives in hopes of being asked to come in and sit down.

Breda and Clara haven't stepped outside the house in 40 years; not since they visited the New Electric Ballroom and were cruelly let down, both on the same night and both by the same guy: a local heartthrob of a singer, name of Doyle, who proved, like so many others, to be less of a charmer offstage than on. This means that they have had most of Ada's lifetime to warn her against men, which they have done by dressing up in their finery and ritually re-enacting the traumatic evening. We see them at it, and at some length. But either the warnings haven't taken or they're beginning to wear off. Ada is beginning to yearn for something else, and she thinks it might be Patsy. He thinks so, too, and finally invited to stop for tea, goes into an extended reverie of his own, complete with musical interlude. Maybe this strikes altogether too close to home; anyway it doesn't turn out to be the brightest of ideas. Like much of the play, it leaves the audience--this member of it, anyway -- asking "What exactly is going on?" and "Why should I care?" (Answer to both: I don't know.)

It's always tempting, when confronted by three theatrical sisters, to invoke Chekhov, but it doesn't seem too apposite here. Compared with his siblings, these three are zombies, who don't even want to go to Moscow, or Dublin, or anywhere. They bear more of a resemblance, and not just because they're Irish, to the wailing women of J.M. Synge's Riders to the Sea, or to their Hispanic cousins in Garcia Lorca's The House of Bernarda Alba, one of whom tried to break out from behind the shutters with tragic results. Or, coming back to the oul' country, they're like the enraged daughter of McDonagh's The Beauty Queen of Leenane, split and diluted three ways. Lorca's and McDonagh's heroines had a tyrannical mother to rebel against; Walsh's sisters are doing it, very efficiently, to themselves. In fact, the play is a, presumably unconscious, riff on Cinderella; the Ugly Sisters go to the ball, are disillusioned by the Prince, and take an especially cruel revenge on Cinders by warning her against following in their own dance-steps. And she believes them. And when she does meet Prince Charming, he smells of fish.

Stranger things have happened, and Walsh probably does have a personal vision. But he releases it here in an endless stream of words, words, words: some of them evocative, some funny (Patsy has a beaut of a line about haddock), but cumulatively numbing. To make one last Irish comparison, the play's conception of life as an endless loop of reminiscence makes Samuel Beckett ( Krapp's Last Tape especially) seem like stern, character-driven social realism. The characters here are just mouthpieces. Some of them, as played here, are very good mouthpieces. Rosemarie Dunsmore as Breda holds on to the mean ferocity she unleashes at the beginning, while thankfully letting up on the speed, while as the more pliable Clara, Sarah Dodd is quite spectacular, spinning her own memoir from hope to heartbreak and reliving every moment of it, while also keeping going a nice running gag about cake. She does suffer, in Autumn Smith's production, from having to give much of her performance in a low chair placed far downstage; it often looks, from the restrictive sightlines of the Tarragon Extra Space, as if she's left the stage, which would seem to contradict the play's point. But the two senior sisters' frozen faces at the end -- Dunsmore's a gorgon mask, Dodd's a doughy smile -- is quite a picture. Cathy Murphy's Ada doesn't have comparable definition, but Christopher Stanton's Patsy is a winning kind of loser; when he's left hovering, we share in his discomfort. Which I suppose is the intention; but a play that leaves its audience frustrated rather than moved has missed its aim. Like most plays these days, this one is short; like too many, it feels long.
