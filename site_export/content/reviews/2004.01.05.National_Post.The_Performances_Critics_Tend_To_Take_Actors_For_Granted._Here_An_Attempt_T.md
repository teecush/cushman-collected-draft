The theatre can do without theatres; actors, in the last resort, can act in open spaces. It can also do without playwrights; actors, if forced, can make up their own lines, sometimes as they go along. And God knows, directors are expendable.

Actors, though, cannot be got rid of. Plays that do without them have occasionally been written. Two examples that come to mind, oddly enough with almost identical titles, are Breath by Samuel Beckett (running time: approximately one minute) and Breath[e] by our own Steve Lucas. But the idea is unlikely to catch on. In the end, a theatrical event depends on just two sets of participants: a group of people doing it and another group of people catching them at it.

For all that, actors are neglected. True, they get applause, adulation, sometimes even money, but they also get taken for granted. The Siminovitch Prize, the most lucrative theatrical award in Canada, has now been given in successive years to a director, a playwright and a designer. This year the same cycle starts all over again, with no hint of recognition for the people who have to get up there and make the shows happen.

Harley Granville-Barker -- who was, successively and successfully, actor, director, dramatist and scholar -- declared in the last of those incarnations that "the art of theatre is the art of acting, first, last, and all the time." Critics, even if they pay lip service to this fact, tend to give precedence to playwrights and directors, whose work is easier to intellectualize and, very often, easier to describe.

Here, anyway, is my own attempt to make some amends: a list, more or less chronological, of the performances -- and, sometimes, groups of performances -- that most thrilled and delighted me, in and around Toronto, in 2003. It omits some that were highly praised in other quarters, but it is, all the same, substantial. We are living in a climate of good acting.

- Welcome visitor: Elaine Stritch (at Liberty)

- Most pleasant surprise: Nora McLellan in Blood Relations

- Most scandalously underused performer: Jennifer Phipps, the best actress in Canada (seriously), and a long-serving member of the Shaw Festival company, but whose only role last year was the minor one of Anfisa, the nurse, in Three Sisters. As always, she brought a world -- the world of the play -- on stage with her, unforcedly filling out every moment of her character's progress and connecting all the dots. Do you remember the moment in Shakespeare in Love when the hulking Jim Carter, as the actor playing the nurse in Romeo and Juliet, was asked what the play was about and replied "well, it's about this nurse"? When Phipps was on, Three Sisters was a play about this nurse.
