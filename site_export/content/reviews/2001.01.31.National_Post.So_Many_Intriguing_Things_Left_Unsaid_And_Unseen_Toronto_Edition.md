GIRLWRECKED

The Dovercourt, Toronto

Rana, a second-generation Canadian immigrant, remembers a relationship from her girlhood and its traumatic resolution. She was 11 when she arrived in London, Ont., having spent six years in her parents' home country of Mauritius. Initially assigned to Grade 6, she is abruptly promoted to 8 when the authorities discover that she can read Shakespeare, aloud and clear. Here she meets Roz, a red- headed girl, "skinny and scraggly," by whom she initially half- expects to be bullied. In the event, the two develop a friendship, and the beginnings of a lesbian romance. Both are distrusted by their peers: Rana for being foreign, Roz for just about everything - - the way she dresses, the way she smells. It all comes to a head with an especially vicious attack on Roz during which Rana behaves, in her own estimation, disgracefully. The memory stays with her forever, or at least up to the time that she tells us the story.

Storytelling is what this piece is -- a solo narrative written and performed by Soraya Peerbaye, who shares her protagonists ethnic background. Its a slight story but a compelling one, and might serve for a more elaborate play than we have actually been offered. We want to know more about Roz than Rana is able to tell us in this format. Even more importantly, we want to know more about Rana, knowing not being the same thing as being told. We can see that she wears a hijab, but though it's an important prop for the actress, we don't know to what extent it either intrigues or enrages her classmates. Rana is enraged and humiliated to find the word Paki traced on snow on her front lawn -- it seems her tormentors cant even get their racism right -- but the anger suggests complications in her, a hierarchy of prejudice, which is also unexplored, as are the lines of force that draw the two isolated girls together.

The framework for the play involves Rana reading, with caustically mocking restraint, a litany of how-to-hold-your-man principles from a teen magazine. This is intriguing, as is a later hint that Ranas and Roz's sexualities have diverged, but nothing much is made of either possibility.

It's not that one wants everything to be spelled out; rather, that a broader canvas might give the same subtleties more room to manoeuvre and more depth in which to resonate. And when it comes to violent action, or indeed to comic action, audiences have an unregenerate desire to see rather than hear about it. Even Greek tragic messengers delivered their casualty reports to someone; there was drama in the way their bulletins were received. The climax of the story is not that easy to take in as Rana tells it, so what might be called the post-climax -- when Rana tells us what really happened, as opposed to what she wants people to believe happened -- is in fact an anti-climax.

GirlWrecked is unsatisfying, but it is definitely talented. Peerbayes previous career has been principally in movement-based theatre; she and her director, Karin Randoja, have devised a choreographic staging built around the visual outline of a schoolroom. The outline presupposes a greater sense of academic place than the script actually supplies, but it enables Peerbaye, who seems to be making her professional acting debut, to move between, over and around two parallel rows of chairs with delightful, skipping grace. Her vocal authority is not as secure; words get lost, and the space, an echoing church basement at the Great Hall on Queen Street West, is not kind.

This apart, her delivery is delicate and acute, as is her writing. The main thrust of the show is verbal -- surprisingly so; and the words are precise, evocative and sometimes witty. When youre best friends, the early-teenaged Rana decides, "there can be no one else -- otherwise, it's polygamy." The later-teenaged Rana, a wallflower at a dance, refers to herself and her companions in rejection parading from the floor "like we're genuinely interested in examining the wall." Theres a vein of self-mockery here, gentle but merciless, that is very attractive. But it also seems too easy, and in the end too thin. As in nearly all one-person plays, however hard the narrator is prepared to be on herself, its still her story that we're stuck with.
