Two popular preconceptions about Newfoundland are that it's devoted to folk music and that it has a lot of bad weather. Both are reinforced from the very first moment of Rocking the Cradle. Storm clouds play on a transparent scrim curtain while, behind, the entire cast whoops it up in dance and, a little later, song.

They are celebrating the wedding of Joan and Vince, which takes place, as does the entire play, in what the program identifies as "an isolated outpost, accessible only by water." It's December, 1962. Five years later, to the month, Joan and Vince are still married but are also childless, a situation that makes them unique in their community. Joan is first philosophical about this, then puzzled, then angry and at last bitterly enraged. Vince isn't bothered. That's an understatement. He's aggressively indifferent.

Rocking the Cradle, a visiting production by the RCA Theatre Company of St. John's, is a free adaptation by Des Walsh, a Newfoundland poet and playwright, of the Spanish play Yerma by Federico Garcia Lorca, also a poet and playwright. Lorca's heroine, whose name actually means "barren," is in the same biological and sociological predicament as Walsh's, though of course she lives in a hot climate rather than a cold one. (An analogous disregard for climate change was demonstrated a few years ago by the Shaw Festival when it mounted another Lorca play, The House of Bernarda Alba, and entrusted it to a director from Poland.) The thinking seems to be that one enclosed community is as good as another. It doesn't work.

I'm not crazy about the original Yerma, either; I suspect that the exoticism (to us) of Lorca's plays may give them a freer pass than they deserve. Still, I'm willing to pay lip-service to the power of their Andalusian atmospherics, and I know that the right actress and/or director can make Yerma a study in tragic obsession to rival Phedre. But what seems single-minded and inexorable in Lorca here becomes merely repetitive. Joan has a series of meetings with her mother, who's more belligerent than understanding; and a concomitant series with a cheerful, and continually pregnant, best friend. She also conducts an inconclusive flirtation with the man she should have married, who eventually leaves for Toronto. The shrinking of the community, and its absorption into the outside world, occasionally threatens to become a sub-theme but never quite makes it. Vince himself is scarily insular; he sees no reason why Joan should even leave the house, despite his refusal to furnish her with any cause to stay inside it. Their marriage is profoundly dysfunctional, sexually and otherwise (some girl talk between Joan and her friends fills us in on some of the details); it's also dysfunctional dramatically, in that Vince doesn't appear much and says little when he does. It might be satisfying to see the situation explode, except that it all happens too suddenly. Having proceeded at an amble, the play finishes at a rush.

The writing is varied at one extreme by an occasional burst of willed metaphor and at the other by what sounds like self-parody that left me nostalgic for the Newfie newscasters on The Royal Canadian Air Farce. This reaches an extreme in the songs. All the characters except Vince (who doesn't have soul) break at the slightest provocation into folk-ballads that they seem to know off by heart. This isn't something that people often do in real life -- not even, I'll bet, in Newfoundland -- and Richard Rose's production totally fails to find a convention for it. (Rose is the artistic director of the Tarragon Theatre, which presumably explains the arrival on that house's main stage of a play underequipped for the exposure.) One of the wisest pronouncements on the theatre I've ever read states that if a production doesn't establish a style, "it automatically slithers into realism," and that's what happens here. Actors are left to sing whole songs at one another, or to react to them, as if they constituted normal dialogue. The fact that they're often out of tune may indeed be realistic, but it isn't helpful; one longs for moody accompaniment to help them through it. (It helps Lorca's plays to no end.) The odd attempt at imagistic staging misfires, too. Bolts of silk are unrolled and waved about to symbolize a storm. It's a tired device; worse than that, it doesn't connect with anything else in the production, unless you count a pervasive uncertainty about whether we're inside the house or out of it.

Ruth Lawrence begins solidly as Joan but, as the character progressively loses it, so does the actress. Darryl Avalon Hopkins is sullen as Vince, which seems to be what's required; he appears at a final Christmas party drunk and wearing a dress. If the play and production are trying here for a carnival dimension, they've left it a little late. Greg King and Didi Gil-lard-Rowlings have some charm and humour respectively. The rest are slapdash or worse.

- Rocking the Cradle plays through Dec. 13 at the Tarragon Theatre. For ticket information, call 416-531-1827.
