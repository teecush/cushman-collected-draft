THEATRE REVIEW BEDTIMES AND BULLIES

Wednesday, February 07, 1996

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Written by Volker Ludwig Adapted by Dennis Foon Directed by Sally Han Starring Derwin Jordan, Paul Braunstein, Kim Renders, Patricia Vanstone. At the Young People's Theatre in Toronto through Sunday Rating: Production adds insult to perceived injury YPT presents a dull and innocuous show that the board of education blames for not being innocuous enough. They deserve each other. BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail COLOUR-BLIND casting is a fine idea, but it has occasioned more hypocrisy and muddled thinking than any other issue in the theatre. Audiences can no more fail to notice whether actors are black or white than whether they are tall, short, fat, thin, bald or hairy. How much it matters in a given play is a thing infinitely variable, and the variables include the nature of the material and the skill and style of the production. The latest disagreement on this comes to you courtesy of the Toronto Board of Education and the Young People's Theatre.

The YPT, doing a play for and about children called Bedtime and Bullies , has cast a black actor as principal bully - though if that is really what he is, he must be the most amiable, unthreatening bully ever put on a stage. The board has accused the theatre, pardon my jargon, of reinforcing negative racial stereotypes. The YPT has mounted a dull and innocuous show that the board has blamed for not being innocuous enough. They deserve each other.

On the way out I heard a little girl asking, "Mommy, which was the bully?" Her confusion was understandable but it was not the fault of the actor. Derwin Jordan gives a quick-witted, vulnerable performance that is much the best thing in the production. He plays Peter, a boy who is habitually spanked by his father and reacts by scaring two other kids: a brother and sister. In the play's German original (by Volker Ludwig) these siblings were called Max and Millie, as was the play. Alliteration still lives in Dennis Foon's Canadian version, which has renamed the characters Kevin and Kelly, while bestowing a new and portentious title on the piece itself. That title fits right in with the current YPT house style, which, from what I have seen (and I haven't seen everything) is mushy, preachy and desperately unimaginative.

Peter allegedly terrorizes Kevin and Kelly. But what we actually see is Peter flourishing an obviously toy gun, which arouses Kevin's envy rather than his fear. (It also arouses the shocked disapproval of Kevin's mother, a single parent of weary but unshakable high-mindedness. In Patricia Vanstone's amazingly unmodulated performance she is rather like the Young People's Theatre rolled into one.) Peter swaps his gun for Kevin's bike. This is a smart move on Peter's part, and one that he seems surprised to get away with, but it does not constitute extortion. Kelly puts it down to her brother's being easily frightened but there is no sign that he's frightened of Peter. It could of course be going on between the lines, except that in Sally Han's production there is nothing going on between the lines.

It does, to give it its due, have a bright and reasonably versatile set, and it tries to forestall the question of Peter's colour by abandoning naturalism. The actor is black but the character isn't, or doesn't have to be. To emphasize this, a white actor plays Peter's father while Kevin, Kelly and Peter himself are played not by real children but by young adults. This approach, however, demands high style, in acting and direction. Paul Braunstein as Kevin has a fair stab at it, though he's overly winsome; Kim Renders, in the trickier role of his kid sister, limits herself to squawking and bouncing. But the production, bar a couple of seconds of attractive clowning, gives them nothing to do except horse vacuously around.

Some exceptionally ghastly songs stop action and tension in their tracks, though sometimes this is managed without musical assistance. The play's moral climax comes after Peter has been reformed by his new friendship with the two Ks. (A celebratory sleepover puts everything to rights; the feel-good factor is working overtime.) Peter's dad, seeing the light, offers to let his son spank him, and Peter declines. There is no pacing here, no weight; the moment doesn't seem to matter to either of them. Intended as a tribute to the imagination of childhood, the show comes off as an insult to it.

If they had cast real kids they might have run into all kinds of problems, but the play might also have been something. We could have believed in Kevin's fears; there's a well-written scene in which he torments his mom with his refusal to go to sleep. We could even have believed in Peter's bullying. As it is, with the production giving us nothing else to think about and despite all the actor does for him, he is a stereotype - not the threatening one deplored by the board, but its depressing opposite: he's a victim. But the way to defeat stereotyping is not arbitrarily to ban one group of actors from playing one kind of role. It's to think, write, act, and direct in terms of individuals, not of statistics: in other words to do it well, and to trust your audience to respond in kind. Theatrical freedom has to include the freedom to play villains. (They're often the juiciest parts anyway; I doubt that Derwyn Jordan had to be bullied into playing Peter.) Whoever started this rubbish about the theatre providing "positive role models?" Can anyone name a good play that does that?

The management have inserted a slip in the program, solemnly informing us that parental abuse is wrong and that momentary repentance solves nothing. Either they think their audiences too dumb to work this out for themselves or they are bowing to pressure from officials who think that everything in a play must be spelled out. If our bureaucrats want to get into theatre criticism, and I'd rather they didn't, perhaps they should learn how drama works. The YPT, who presumably know already, should brush up.
