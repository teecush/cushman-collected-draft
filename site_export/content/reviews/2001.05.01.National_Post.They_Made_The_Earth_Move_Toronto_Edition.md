THE EPIC PERIOD

Factory Theatre, Toronto

A native lawyer, reflecting on the difference between his ancestors' times and our own, decides that "vegetarian was the Ojibwa word for bad hunter."

I wish all of The Epic Period were like that, or at least that more of it were. The title suggests we are in for something dizzyingly ambitious, though the fact that there are only three actors in the cast (doing, to be sure, a certain amount of furtive doubling) should warn us to moderate our expectations. In the event, Sean Dixons play turns out to be a contemporary comedy of relationships, with a bit of the historic past clinging to it, and a bit of myth clinging to that. A pervasive air of tongue-in-cheek is meant, I think, to allow the elements to combine weightlessly into a whole. Its actual effect is to prevent any of them from really engaging the audience.

The comedy works best, especially in its romantic aspect. The embroiled couple are the lawyer, who is called Joshua but who also answers to the name of Bundle, and a soon-to-be-ex-nun, who was prophetically christened Moody. Just before the play starts, Joshua has helped Moody break her vows in the most decisive way; the action proper begins the morning after, with her attempting to throw him out. So this, at its outset, is yet another post-pickup play.

It turns out that their meeting was no random chance; Moodys mother has just died, Joshua was present at the deathbed, and he has been entrusted with a mission to the daughter. Its upshot is to send the two of them on a quest to a spot called Christian Island, a burial place for Huron converts of many generations before. While digging for treasure, they also continue to dig one another. Moody's discoveries in both these areas enable her to reach out toward her moderately estranged and thoroughly puzzled father, of whom, as it happens, Joshua goes in mortal fear. Not only has he debauched the old man's daughter but the old man happens to be his boss and he does indeed set out in pursuit of them, in a canoe and with a rifle.

When the three of them finally collide, bandying firearms and recriminations, the play -- 60 minutes into its 90-minute length -- comes to belated but exuberant life. We can even cut it enough slack to be somewhat moved by a scene in which Moody remonstrates with her father for the lack of attention he showed her mother, to which he more or less replies that he did the best he could. A kind of therapy ensues, facilitated by techniques of "shape-shifting" (which here seems to be a fancy word for role-playing) inherited by Joshua, a.k.a. Bundle, from his Indian ancestors. In the end, though, we dont know or care enough about any of the characters for it to matter.

The past is continually with us. Moody, through her mother, has Huron blood and her excavations bring her a message from her family past. (It may also relate to her own religious conflicts, though the point is hardly made.) There are brief historical flashbacks: A 17th- century Jesuit is seen admonishing a female member of his Huron flock, presumably Moodys ancestress. These sequences could probably stand bolder treatment than they get; in Brian Quirt's production, they are shoved to the side of the stage and intoned by robed and hooded actors who seem to be satirizing their own pretensions before even advancing them. The jokes here, whether past or present, come off nervous rather than joyous; neither period seems sure enough of itself to illuminate the other.

There was a similar uncertainty of tone in the recent An Acre of Time from the same director: the same see-sawing between solemn and jokey, as if to say we have all this Canadian history at our disposal and it's bound to be enriching, but we dont want to get too heavy about it. There is a lot of this in the current theatre. You may call it postmodern but you can hardly call it satisfying.

The play is also, perhaps less momentously, the second in succession on the Factory stage in which two male actors flank Waneta Storms. The lady appears on our stages as often as her husband, Randy Hughson (though rarely with him), and like him is always a welcome sight; she seems, though, to be generally less fortunate in her vehicles than he is. This time she takes as long to get into her stride as the play does, but when she hits it she is enchanting.

Herbie Barnes, last seen in the title role of The Hobbit, makes a cute bundle of Bundle, endearing in his insecurities, though passing them on rather too freely to his audience in the shape of diction that is swallowed or gabbled or both. And call me sizeist, or at least heightist, but I dont think the effect of his being slightly shorter than the object of his affections has been sufficiently allowed for in the staging. David Gardner's dad starts out disconcertingly low key but eventually reveals this to be a style, an effective way of conveying wounded bemusement. Steve Lucas's sets and lighting tickle the eye without much assisting the mind.
