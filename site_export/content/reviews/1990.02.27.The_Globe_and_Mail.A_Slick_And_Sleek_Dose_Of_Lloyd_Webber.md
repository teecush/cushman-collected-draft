A slick and sleek dose of Lloyd Webber

Tuesday, February 27, 1990

ROBERT CUSHMAN

BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail The Music of Andrew Lloyd Webber in Concert, now at Pantages Theatre in Toronto, is slick and sleek. Fourteen formally clad singers sit or stand in a straight line, before 50 musicians of symphonic bent. The vocalists do not get about much, but when they do they move with point. They exert themselves most in the songs from the two earliest Webber musicals, Joseph and the Amazing Technicolor Dreamcoat, and Jesus Christ Superstar.

Probably the concert format makes the songs seem more static than they really are, but we do seem to spend a lot of time listening to grandiose, slow-moving ballads. These, of course, are what make the hits.

Webber was famously turned on as a boy by the musicals of Richard Rodgers, and the one that seems to have influenced him most was South Pacific. Webbers love songs all seem to be struggling toward Some Enchanted Evening, conceivably Rodgers's worst song, certainly his most inflated. It has little to do with the actual experience of love, either the fun or the pain, but a lot with how people like to fantasize about it. Passion, tenderness, vulnerability are excluded in favor of a pre- fabricated vision of Romance: Love as the fabled Many-Splendored Thing.

Memory, the Webber variant on the theme, has rather a poignant middle section, but what sold it was its thumping, unrelenting opening, not so much an affair of the heart as a heart attack.

Amplification helps, of course. Webber may have left rock 'n roll behind him but he can still use rock triplets to pump up an emotion. The concert extracts from Joseph and Jesus remind us that once there was hope for the rock musical as a vehicle of theatrical playfulness.

Webbers music bounces, and Tim Rice's lyrics are delightful. You have to forgive the rhyming of "biscuit" with district, a venial sin by most contemporary standards but hurtful coming from a writer who knows how sharp words can be. Joseph's most celebrated couplet on the subject of Pharaohs Dream ("all those things you saw in your pajamas"were a long- range forecast for your farmers) is absent from this selection, but what remains demonstrates both the supreme value of insouciance to the musical theatre and how much it is missed.

There were still glimmers of it in Evita, but you wouldnt know it from this concert, whose mind is on higher things. There is an overture, with Latin touches that do not so much invoke Edmundo Ros and Xavier Cugat as succumb to them. And there is, of course, Don't Cry for Me Argentina, a boring song that has great ironic point in its dramatic context when you know Eva is lying, but which went over big with record-buyers who neither knew nor cared. What we do not hear is the tune's bustling reprise as Oh What a Circus, with its line about Peronism being good for the country in a roundabout way." That kind of mordant offhandedness is what makes a serious musical. Pretentious earnestness, though, is generally better box- office.

In subsequent Riceless shows Webber has had to work harder for his fun. There is some of it in Cats, though Eliots light verse was never really funny. Webber himself has said that the rhythms of the poems more or less dictate the musical settings, which may explain why the music sounds competent rather than inspired. True to form, the concert includes the one really portentous tune in the piece, The Addressing of Cats.

Starlight Express, a more entertaining show than is usually acknowledged, is similarly slighted. Instead of One Rock n' Roll Too Many or the C&W spoof Uncoupled, we are regaled with the reverential title song. This wasn't even a hit.

The second half of the concert is largely devoted to plugging The Phantom of the Opera. The ensemble number Masquerade, bereft of the Hal Prince staging and the Maria Bjornson designs, just dies; being able to hear the lyrics is no compensation at all. But the ballad All I Ask of You actually gains from being set adrift. Heard on its own, divorced from the two uninteresting characters who sing it on stage, it soars. It is not opera, whatever claims may have been made for it, but it is very good operetta.

That is more than can be said for Love Changes Everything, the lung- bursting hit from his latest show, Aspects of Love, which serves as an encore. It is back to Webber's impersonal style, which has given rise to impersonal singing.

The lady singers in this concert work firmly and selflessly in the tradition. Among the gentlemen, Sandy Winsby stands out as a helpful, tactful compere.

It is a good-taste occasion. And that, along with the obvious entrepreneurial genius, is a Webber attribute. (He put this show together, apparently.) At his most poundingly effortful, he never writes anything as desperately bad as The Impossible Dream or whatever you care to name from Les Miserables. Whatever the Broadway chauvinists may claim, this intrusive Englishman has not destroyed the American musical theatre. They did that themselves.
