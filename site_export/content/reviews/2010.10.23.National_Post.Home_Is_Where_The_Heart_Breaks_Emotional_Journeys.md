Joseph Ziegler is a heroic actor, and also a hugely sympathetic one: not such a common combination. This makes his Willy Loman in Soulpepper's Death of a Salesman (which I saw at a preview) an overpowering experience. He was already burrowing into my heart in his first scene; he had broken it, but also lifted it, long before his last.

That first scene, which shows Willy the travelling sales rep returning to his Brooklyn home in the small hours, having suffered a panic attack on the road, is played, in Albert Schultz's production, very quietly. Willy is dog-tired, his wife, Linda, is consumed with anxiety but trying her considerable best to be supportive, and both of them are trying not to wake their two grown sons in the bedroom upstairs. Even with his voltage down, his hair grey, and his body in an unaccustomed state of slump, Ziegler has bounce. Willy, as Arthur Miller drew him, is above all else a volatile creature; he keeps turning on the same dime. When we meet him, he's trying to keep desperation at bay, mostly by denial. But the play's flashbacks reveal that he's always been like that. An optimist by culture and profession, he's always feared that he might be missing out, even when he and his family were at their happiest. (This seems, counting back from the play's 1949 date stamp, to have been during the Depression. It's in the postwar boom that their world falls apart.) Some of Willy's mood swings are almost funny; one moment he's admonishing his son Biff to act serious in a job interview, the next he's advising him to crack a few jokes. Ziegler plays each moment to the hilt, lingering on none. Most moving perhaps is his rock-bottom humiliation when his boss, Howard ( "business is business") fires him; most powerful is the wrath he turns on his sons in their subsequent meeting at a restaurant; most terrible is the joy that suffuses him when, screening out everything the boy has actually said, he takes refuge in the love that shines through Biff's anger. It's a joy that leads him to commit suicide for the sake of the insurance. Well, not just for that: Miller himself said that Willy's motives were inextricably mixed, and some of the play's stature comes from making all the options both tangible and tantalizing.

It connects with economic tensions that are persistent, certainly now, and family tensions, especially father-son tensions, that are eternal. Nancy Palk has all the gentleness and all the fury of Linda, who's both appalled onlooker and tortured participant; high among this production's moments of electrifying rages is her "then make Charley your father, Biff" delivered from the door of the totemic family refrigerator. She also, through beauifully sharp timing, gets one perfectly appropriate laugh that I've never heard in this play before. Ari Cohen's Biff, the least defined character as written, comes superbly into focus when we scroll back to his crucial moment of disillusion with his dad; he's a heartbroken boy who gives birth to the passionate shell of a man that explodes in the concluding scenes. Tim Campbell's Happy, all set to follow in his father's footsteps, is an all-American glamour boy to rank with the one he gave us in Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf ?, though I don't think the production needs to give him its final moment; the point's been made, and the play isn't about Happy. Michael Hanrahan breathes Brooklyn as Charley, the charitably ironic neighbour. Soulpepper academicians fill out the cast to great effect: Gregory Prest as Bernard, graduating from geekhood to sober young maturity; Brendan Wall as Howard, embarrassed but impervious; Ins Choi as Stanley the waiter, wishing he was dead; and Raquel Duffy as the woman from Willy's past, first glimpsed as a ghost in a mirror. The only flaws in Schultz's direction are visual; Lorenzo Savoini's set crams two levels into one, promotes traffic problems, and forces the epilogue into a straight line. Otherwise, acting and

production are rock-solid, with Ziegler's Willy a triumph of character acting, raised to the highest power.

There's another fine American family, but rising rather than falling, in Soulpepper's other space, which now houses A Raisin in the Sun, revived from two years ago. The creaks in Lorraine Hansberry's play are more noticeable the second time around, but so are its strengths and those of Weyni Mengesha's production. The one major newcomer is Bahia Watson who makes the aspiring doctor-daughter both sweetly capricious and bracingly bright; the play has nearly as much to do with gender issues as with racial ones. Charles Officer's Walter Lee now smoulders as powerfully as he explodes; Abena Malika as his wife movingly suffers and passionately protests; and, dominating all as the matriarch with the South still in her, Alison Sealy-Smith continues to amaze with vocal and emotional depths beyond any she has previously sounded. This play, in this production, doesn't break our hearts; in a way that's neither shameless nor shameful, it warms them.
