Ghosts runs to Nov. 6 at the Canadia dell'Arte studio theatre, 186 Munro St. Call 465-7393

GHOSTS

Canadia dell' Arte

---

Just over 100 years ago, when Ibsen's Ghosts made its first appearance in English, the press gave it a notoriously rough ride. It was described in the London papers as "an open drain ... a loathsome sore left unbandaged."

It isn't as much fun as that makes it sound but it remains a taut, mesmerising play about a small group of people trapped by the mistakes of the past and by the way in which society continues to sanction those mistakes.

At the play's end Oswald Alving, a young Norwegian artist, sits slumped, his brain softening from inherited syphilis, while his agonised mother wonders whether to give him the morphine pills that will put him out of his misery. This scene, which so shocked the Victorian critics, was also responsible for the piece's glib dismissal by some of their successors; who needs a play about syphilis now that we have penicillin?

In fact, if Ghosts is a drama about syphilis, it's a pretty bad one; the subject isn't even mentioned till halfway through the action, and even then never by name. AIDS and the euthanasia debate have sharpened the impact for us, but Ibsen isn't asking us to build hospices or to put more money into medical research. He's asking us to live honestly, which is harder.

Peter Reitzel's production, for his Canadia dell'Arte Theatre Troupe (a cute name that sets my teeth on edge), is interestingly self-destructive. It is for the most part conventional and thoughtfully acted, and it traces the play's ideas with unusual clarity, but it has been placed in a self-consciously experimental setting and it features one oustandingly weird performance.

The play unfolds in a single bleak day. ("The weather," says the program breezily "is lousy.")

Mrs Alving has dedicated an orphanage to the memory of her late husband, a man much respected in the neighbourhood but known by her to have been a lecherous drunk. She herself, though, had never been much help to him; that is one of the crucial things she comes to realise in the course of her own long day's journey into a particularly appalling night. This production puts the play into modern dress while keeping the Norwegian references, though why it should assume that contemporary Scandinavia is a century behind the times is beyond me. The wide playing space, divided into three compartments, is littered with miscellaneous junk, presumably symbolizing the accumulated detritus of the Alvings' history. The living-room is dominated by a carpenter's work-bench - perhaps because one character is a carpenter, though he doesn't live there - and by a piano, which defeats me. Key scenes are acted on obscure parts of the stage and many of the moves are very awkward.

There may be beautiful intentions behind all this but it is not nice to watch.

The opening scene promises great things. Randall Lathier as Engstrand the wood-worker, and Julie Reitzel as Regina, his supposed daughter and Mrs Alving's maid, dig deeply but easily beneath the text, exposing all their characters' tensions, all their selfishness, all their distrust. She, as the play progresses, proves especially good, giving the girl -- for the first time in my experience -- a solid viewpoint of her own. These however are the easiest roles, the play collapses when Father Christmas comes on.

He is actually Pastor Manders, God's local representative, carrying a large cross and wearing what looks like a white muffler but proves to be a detachable beard. He takes it off (and parks the cross) during his more informal moments. Nobody else is acting in this ludicrously stylized convention, and the results are terrible.

Manders is the voice of stifling propriety (he's stifled himself, too) and any production's first job is to humanize him. Otherwise, even if you close your eyes and think of Preston Manning, he and the play just seem silly. In or out of mufti, Dennis Frey's performance is inadequate: bleating, inexpressive, and given to cartooned displays of sexual insecurity. Mrs Alving, a passionate and intelligent woman, is supposed to have loved this man.

This is like bad amateur acting.

The Alvings, mere et fils, are like good amateur acting: That is, they are clear and intelligent, but lacking in range and command. Jody Cummins' Mrs. A is a trim hausfrau (contemporary costume doesn't help her status) who learns and communicates her lessons bitterly and well; but when she speaks of the ghosts of the past she sounds impatient rather than haunted, as if she would like to get on with the hoovering. Michael Chipman is too subdued for Oswald's vaunted "joy of life" but his descriptions of his sickness and his final descent are wrenchingly believable. The two of them play their last scene with unforced concentration and harrowing force. The director then ruins it by junking Ibsen's last lines and having the company sing a hymn instead. I cannot imagine what this is intended to convey -- Ghosts, despite having a blinkered cleric and a Dickensian hypocrite in its cast, is a fiercely secular play -- but it is, as you may have gathered, typical.
