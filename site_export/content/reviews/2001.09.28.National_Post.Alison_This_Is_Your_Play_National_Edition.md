YOU ARE HERE

Theatre Passe Muraille

About halfway through You Are Here, Randy Hughson comes on stage and the audience wakes up. Partly this is the pleasure of recognition -- I haven't seen a play with Hughson in it for at least three weeks -- and partly it is because he radiates a contained energy unapproached by anybody else on this stage. It is a treat just to watch him rock backwards and forwards on his heels as he considers another characters dubious proposition.

He plays Thomas, a successful film director, and for an actor who rarely gets cast as a successful anything he is mightily convincing. He is far from being the central character. That position belongs to Alison, who makes most of her living as a celebrity journalist, and when Thomas first appears she is interviewing him.

Later she persuades him, not too plausibly, to direct a script that her psychologist-husband has written, and his initial reaction to it is worth recording: It is, he says, "very North American."

Asked to expand on that, he says that it goes on and on about its characters emotional states as if they were automatically of compelling interest.

Here Daniel MacIvor, the author and director, seems to be slyly mocking himself, or maybe trying to disarm criticism in advance. (Judith Thompson uses a similar tactic in Habitat, which opened the same night.) Because his play is an example of what Thomas is describing; it is all about Alison.

The first half of the play is her monologue, with illustrations. She sits on a chair in what looks, through an ingenious use of lighting, like a foreshortened black box. This has the effect of making Caroline Gillis, who plays the role and whose performance is technically unfaultable, look like a very big girl indeed, rather like Alice after drinking the potion.

Also like Alice, she recalls people from her past life, mainly her college days, and they pop up when she mentions them. They say something characteristic of themselves, she gives them a name-check (Richard! "Jerry!") and they go right on talking. It's like a sepulchral edition of This Is Your Life.

One of the visitants (played with some charm by Jim Allodi) is Alison's old roommate, who is defined, repeatedly, by his unwillingness to wear a watch since he wants his arms -- and, by extension, his life -- free. Another is the man she eventually marries.

There is also a female student whom she hates, all her life, for her apparent happiness and conventionality. We barely see this woman, learn nothing about her, but the author throws in an equally unrevealing final encounter as some kind of attempt at a climax. The point, probably a valid one, may be that we can all waste a lot of ourselves storing grudges against people who are really of no importance to us. But it isnt interesting, because Alison, however lyrically she soliloquizes, isnt interesting.

She is also steadily less credible, though her initial situation seems reasonable; she drifts into journalism, against her finer instincts, because she has to do something. Later, as the play begins to open out to include ensemble scenes, she becomes a film producer, and this is not believable, not of the woman we see.

Her experiences on set do permit the play to broaden into some mild satire on self-indulgent actors and manipulative directors: nothing very new, but at least there are some jokes.

Rather sharper is Alison's interview with the actress who eventually appears in her movie, and who adjusts her image, on a moment-by-moment basis, between brazenly commercial starlet and frustrated artist. Fiona Highet hits this off well.

All the characters become embroiled in a spiral of adultery, miscarriages, divorce, drink, drugs, the destruction of lives and the bitter evaporation of youthful ideals, displayed with civilized restraint across a restaurant table. It is, in fact, like a hundred other plays, but with an extra layer of self-consciousness.

Toward the end of each act, a character turns out front to reassure us that either the intermission or the final curtain will soon be upon us. These moments, presumably, are what MacIvor's program notes have in mind when they go on about honesty and breaking down the fourth wall and frankly admitting were in a theatre. Its kicking at an open door; Dr. Johnson pointed out, nearly 300 years ago, that no audience ever forgets it's in a theatre. Playfully underlining this can be fun; solemnly underlining it is a pain, though it seems to make those involved feel very cutting-edge. I think I might like some plays more if they didn't come wrapped up with sycophantic publicity and narcissistic manifestoes.
