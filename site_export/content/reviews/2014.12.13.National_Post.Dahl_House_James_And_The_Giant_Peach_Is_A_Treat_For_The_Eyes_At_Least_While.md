YPT's production of James and the Giant Peach, a musical based on the Roald Dahl book and directed by Sue Miner, has two great things going for it. One is the ensemble performance of the outsize insects who accompany the title character on his trip in the flying, floating title fruit. The other is the visual identity bestowed on the peach, and the creepycrawlies, and just about everything else in the show by its design team.

Yannik Larivee did the sets and Robin Fisher the costumes; Fisher also designed the puppets that represent the insects before a magic potion enlarges them to actor-size. They're a colourful bunch posttransformation, and they inhabit a colourful stage world in which the giant peach is first glimpsed as a silhouette on a screen before opening up and becoming the main acting area. Shadow play also serves to delineate the young hero's recurring nightmare about the death of his parents who, as you may or not remember, were eaten by a rhinoceros escaped from the London Zoo.

They must have been phenomenally unlucky. Rhinos in the real world have been known to gore or trample people to death, but never to eat them; they're vegetarians. Dahl is here indulging in the kind of brutal whimsy that makes his stories, as far as I'm concerned, so very resistible.

Take, for example, the two greedy and malicious aunts who have to adopt the orphaned James, and who mercilessly torment and exploit him. As with the sadistic school principal in the hugely successful stage version of Dahl's Matilda, we can't relate them to what we actually know about child abuse, because they are so camped-up and caricatured. These ugly sisters (one assumes they're sisters) make Cinderella's siblings look like thoroughly motivated case studies. They're spiritedly played here by Nicole Robert and Karen Wood, though their screechy pseudo-British accents hurt my ears. It's hardly their fault, though, that they aren't as darkly funny as the script clearly thinks they are. They're presented as petty cheats who think they've hit the big time when the giant peach in their garden. When it disappears they have to scarper, off to America on the Queen Mary, though since they apparently haven't yet collected on all the money they thought was coming their way, it's a mystery how they can afford it.

Meanwhile James himself, brightly and honestly played by Alessandro Costantini, is off on his own transatlantic trip, travelling by peach. His voyage is also one of self-discovery, shared with and prompted by his newly inflated friends. When marine disaster threatens, he proves a resourceful and inventive leader; they learn that not all humans want to tread on them; and they all become what they most want and have lacked, a family. In this new world of hug-a-bug, Lana Carillo's ladylike Ladybug naturally takes precedence, but both Saccha Dennis (Spider) and Stewart Adam McKensy (Grasshopper) are credits to entomology while Jacob MacInnis charms the house as a timorous Earthworm who, predictably but delightfully, proves a hero. Dale Miller is excellent as the incorrigibly suspicious Centipede; his conversion is the show's dramatic high point, and he can even do convincing Cockney. He's less happy as the Mysterious Man who gives James his magical recipe and then disappears without explaining himself, though not before delivering the most ill-fitting of the musical numbers. The score is by the off-Broadway team of Benj Pasek and Justin Paul, who wrote the pastiche songs for the TV show Smash and are still spoofing away here; you have your plaintive loneliness song, and your sneaky lowlife song, and your rhythmic rave-up (the highlight): nothing that's bad but nothing that feels inevitably right either. But if the material is sometimes lacking, the presentation is first-rate.

A much older story is the basis of The Brown Bull of Cuailnge, presented by the group known as The Room and written by their playwright-in-residence (though he actually resides in Buffalo) Neil Wechsler. In first-century Ireland, a queen sends an army to Ulster to capture a bull, the only one in the country bigger than the one owned by her husband. None of this exciting stuff actually happens on the stage, which is occupied by four soldiers in the queen's army wondering what they're doing in what seems to be the most frivolous war since The Iliad and trading doubts, fears, blows (eventually) and the occasional song. They also presume heavily on our knowing the legend, or at least the program's summary of it. The others accuse their leader, Fergus, of periodically deserting them, which indeed he does, though never for more than a minute at a time; they speak, though, in terms of thousands of years, so maybe they're meant to represent all soldiers in all wars. The conversation goes in frustrating, and eventually agonizing, circles; and Geoffrey Pounsett's production doesn't give it any contours. The youngest recruit is played, pointlessly, by a woman, Gabriella Colavecchio, who's monotonously plaintive; the angriest by Antonio Cayonne, who's monotonously loud. Dylan Roberts' Fergus is monotonously ferocious, but he is at least quite powerful at the start; Anand Rajaram, the musician in the case, keeps a quizzical distance which feels in context like a small blessing. However: Wechsler's bio tells us that he has adapted Ibsen's supposedly unstageable epic Emperor and Galilean, and that I would like to see.
