SIMPL

Tarragon Theatre, Toronto

- - -

Katl Valentin, whose name is just about familiar from social histories and theatre textbooks, was a German comedian who flourished before, during and after the First World War but more or less sat out the Second. He wasn't Jewish and he wasn't political. Peter Froehlichs play Simpl, in which he himself plays the leading role, is an attempt to bring the man to life, to recreate his routines and to investigate why he either chose or was forced into silence.

It makes for an interesting evening, sometimes an entertaining one, but it doesnt have much urgency. Froehlich is an authoritative performer with an impressive number of verbal, physical and musical skills, but he is not a great clown. Valentin apparently was; at least, the whole show rests on that assumption. He has been described as the German Chaplin, though from what we see here the two had little in common, even leaving Charlie's politics out of it. Valentin -- at least Froehlich's Valentin -- was not ethereal or balletic, and he was not primarily a mime. He did songs, monologues and sketches, and he also played the tuba, always a laugh-getter of an instrument. His hometown, which he seldom left, was Munich. He worked in that citys cabarets, from one of which, the Cafe Simpl, the show derives its vaguely punning name. An alternative title, on similar lines, might have been My Funny Valentin.

Karls chief comic persona, as presented here, is that of belligerent innocent, a bull with the world his china shop; a vocal Harpo Marx with a healthy infusion of Groucho, though with the physical solidity of a Sid Caesar (who comes especially to mind because he did very good Teuton). The prize number here shows Karl wreaking methodical havoc in a radio studio, careful always to enter through the effects door (and to close it behind him) and refusing to leave until he gets exactly what he wants. It's funny, but it's something short of uproarious.

The same goes for most of the material. The songs give off a pleasing air of craziness without being especially amusing, though its possible of course that they have lost something in translation. Valentins career is apparently well documented on records and in film shorts, and Froehlich put the show together from these sources, along with memories of his own father, who was a fan.

Piety, though, may have misled him. This doesn't seem to be the kind of rich comic text that another actor can walk into and make his own. Valentin's public, which included many famous intellectuals, seems to have thought he was a genius. This makes life difficult for anybody attempting to walk two and a half hours in his shoes.

It isnt a one-man show. Valentin had a female partner, Liesl Karlstadt, who, in accordance with a long tradition, discovered a gift for comedy while trying with all her might to be serious. Actually, its Karl who discovers it, and she becomes his off-stage counsellor and on-stage punching bag: If he's Caesar, she's Imogene Coca.

Nicola Lipman plays her efficiently and sympathetically. Liesl, we gather, is in love with Karl. He, as it happens, is married, a fact thats mentioned precisely twice, though you would think it would have had some bearing on his conduct in such turbulent times Liesls predicament is treated in hardly more detail; Karl's indifference leads her to attempted suicide and a mental hospital, but the build-up to this is perfunctory. She merely, as in some old biopic, asks plaintively why Karl can't see her as a woman.

There is a third member of the team, Karls accompanist, Gerhard Krachmann. He is played by Peter Tiefenbach, who supplements his reliable musical talents by acting as a solid straight man in the sketches. His dramatic gifts are somewhat overtaxed, though, when he has to a) fill us in on the march of history by treating his colleagues to tidbits from the newspapers and b) represent the rise of Nazism in his own person. He takes to strumming the piano in a brown shirt. He goes from doing this within the act to doing it in real life -- there is no clear demarcation, and the elision, though doubtless meant to be suggestive, is merely confusing.

So is much of the historical background. Karl doubtless lived to some extent in a world of his own, but it still strains belief to hear him wondering, in the late 1930s, where his old friend and admirer Bertolt Brecht could have got to. (Brecht, a wily bird, skipped the country in 1933, within 24 hours of the Reichstag fire.) Like Brechts Galileo, Valentin kept his options open; invited to tea with Hitler, he talked non-stop so that the Fuhrer wouldn't have the chance to ask him any embarrassing questions. But the rising pressure of terror, the possible cost to Valentin if he had got out of line, is bewilderingly underplayed.

He kept out of trouble but he got up the Nazis' noses anyway. In his comedy he presented anti-social behaviour without condeming it - - a tactic guaranteed to enrage totalitarians, or perhaps politicians of any stamp. Froehlich gives Valentin, or has found somewhere, a penetrating comparison between the audience response to political humour ("they laugh because they want to punish someone) and to his own (they laugh because they cant help themselves"). That sounds right to me, and I have no doubt that the second is preferable. It might not excuse Valentin if he had been an active collaborator, but apparently he wasnt; he never joined the Party.

The question of whether he stopped performing out of disgust or because he had no choice sounds as if it should be compelling but it never is. Nor is the question of what he should have done. Valentin's choices never concern us -- perhaps because the show has not decided how much of a biography, as opposed to a celebration, it wants to be.

We do see Karl and Liesl making a brief post-war comeback. They play red-nosed refugees, bombed out of their home, attempting to load their belongings on to a cart that keeps tipping. It's far from a barrel of laughs but it makes you understand why Samuel Beckett was another Valentin fan. The couple can't go on; they must go on. Richard Rose's production seems to be trying for a mixture of dourness and drollery, though the balance tips decisively toward the former.
