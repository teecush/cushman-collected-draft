Late in 1979, Britain's Royal Shakes peare Company, at a high artistic tide and a low financial ebb, issued a press release. It announced that in 1980 it would only be able to afford one new production at its London home, the Aldwych Theatre, to sit alongside the customary Shakespearean transfers from Stratford-upon-Avon. That one, however, would be a whopper: an adaptation, in two full-length parts, of Charles Dickens novel The Life and Adventures of Nicholas Nickleby.

This may sound like hindsight, but my first reaction was, "What a good idea." Dramatizations of novels often flounder, but I had come to believe that this was due not to any fundamental incompatibility between page and stage, but simply because stage versions never gave themselves enough time. Dickens was a writer with an obviously theatrical genius, who -- had he written when plays rather than novels were the dominant form -- might have been the greatest English playwright since Shakespeare. Stage versions of his books often dwindled into a collection of famous catchphrases and basic plot lines; the richness, the variety and the passion got lost. But this didnt have to happen. A fringe company called Shared Experience had recently mounted a version of Bleak House, which, with no sets or costumes and only six actors, managed to cover the whole book, no subplot or minor character left behind. It was breathtaking.

The RSC's Nickleby was obviously going to be a bigger deal physically; they had a company of 40. It was a very strong company, forged over several years by the recognizably great artistic director Trevor Nunn, who was to co-direct Nickleby with young protege John Caird. What distinguished Nunn's work was its humanity - - he seemed to love actors -- and its full-bodied intensity; his comedies were rib-breaking and his tragedies terrifying, and Dickens would give him huge scope for birth. He had recently directed Ian McKellen in the greatest Macbeth I or most other people had seen. McKellen was no longer with the RSC, but I encountered him in Aldwych at Nicklebys first official all-day performance.

Im looking forward to this, he said, "more than to anything I've ever seen." A couple of months later I asked if the event had lived up to his great expectations. Oh, much more, he said. He added that what he loved most was the feeling that the show had been created by the actors --his ex-colleagues -- to share with the audience.

The show did indeed unfold like a sumptuous gift, from them to us. Watching the impetuous, impoverished young Nicholas and his family progressing through the cruelties and the kindnesses of early Victorian England we laughed, we cried and were made to feel -- as George Orwell wrote of Dickens -- "generously angry." Part One lasted four hours, Part Two four-and-a-half; and since so much of the show was comic, one ended up feeling light-headed. I think it was at the third intermission of the second show that an acquaintance voiced my own thoughts by saying, I don't want this to end. We were completely caught up in the shows world. At the end, I happily defied critical tradition by standing up and joining in what appeared to be unanimous tear-stained cheers.

So it was a shock to find that the reviews were mostly not that good, with "not really a play" the burden of complaint. My own review in The Observer was a rave, beginning with the words theres a lot to be said for length. But what turned the tide was a column by the late Bernard Levin in The Times. There were lines round the block the next morning. The production was brought back twice within the year, then went to New York, on to television and into legend.

There have not been many revivals. But in 2006, Jonathan Church, the new artistic director of the Chichester Festival Theatre, finding himself in straits similar to the RSC's 28 years ago, mounted a new production with similar success. Co-directed by Church and actor-director Philip Franks, it was revived last year, toured in Britain, and now plays its only North American date at the Princess of Wales Theatre, starting today, all day. This has given David Edgar a chance to have a second look at his play. Some people are surprised to learn that Nickleby has a playwright, and Edgar, whom I reached by phone at his home in Birmingham, admits to mild irritation at the persisting legend that, back in 1980, "45 actors and two directors" did it all themselves -- though it was created on the basis of a five-week workshop before I delivered.

Edgar, then as now one of Britain's best large-scale public playwrights, was an inspired choice of adaptor. He reminded me that the original production happened "quite early in Thatcherism" when there was debate as to whether the vaunted Victorian values were truly "about self-help or about philanthropy. The social and economic divisions in society are now, if anything, stronger." And so it is in Dickens. One of Nicklebys villains, the aristocratic cad Sir Mulberry Hawk is able to bet Pounds 50, while Nicholas is offered a munificent pound a week by Crummles -- and Pounds 5 a year at Dotheboys Hall. Mr. Crummles, no meany, is the archetypal strolling actor-manager with whom Nicholas and his oppressed companion Smike find employment, and whose repertoire in Edgars version includes a riotous rewrite of Romeo and Juliet.

Edgar has reduced the plays' running times to three and three- and-half-hours ("more of a pang to me than it was to the public") and thinks some of his changes may be improvements. We've lost a whole subplot from Part One; Part Two was more difficult, more a matter of filleting. Part Two is now a better play than it was, cleaner and clearer. And darker.

Though, it always had its dark passages, and they were very powerful. Edgar once described Dickens theory of human and social behaviour as "wouldnt it be nice if every day were Christmas." The novels and the play recognize that most days aren't, but insist that they should be. Meanwhile, the show is the richest kind of present, any day of the year. - To April 20 at the Princess of Wales Theatre. For tickets, call 416-872-1212 or visit mirvish.com
