Sesame Street's charm lost on stage Sesame Street Live

Saturday, March 10, 1990

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Written by Jon Stone and Joseph A. Bailey Directed by Marilyn Magness Starring Jim Henson's Sesame Street Muppets At the O'Keefe Centre, Toronto BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail ONE of the perks of parenthood is being allowed to watch Sesame Street. Clearly, it is the best children's TV show in the world. It is also one of the very few U.S. television products that are suitable for adults. Significantly, it comes out of New York, not Hollywood. The version shown on TVOntario is fatally slowed by cumbersome Canadian content, but on Channel 17 (PBS Buffalo) you get the wittiest, most imaginative, most musical, most literate program on U.S. television.

Also one of the most self-indulgent. At least half the jokes, and most of the musical pastiches, come across as though they are being perpetrated for grown-ups. The hippest pre-schooler is unlikely to appreciate waiter Grover's injunction to the kitchen to "break out the good stuff" when his lunch-break rolls round, or the accuracy of the show's loving homages to every popular music style from vo-de-o to video. I don't think, though, that the show's creators do this just to curry favor with people like me. They do it to amuse themselves, and to keep themselves on their toes, in the honorable tradition of Lewis Carroll, A.A. Milne, Huckleberry Hound and Muffin the Mule. Not talking down to children means working to your own best standards, and giving yourself the occasional break.

To be honest, my own kids are no more addicted to Sesame Street than they are to the Care Bears or Polka Dot Door: the easy, patronizing stuff. But they are no less addicted, and at low moments I tell myself that regular exposure to brilliant entertainment will have an educational effect far more important than Sesame Street's declared purpose of teaching them their numbers and letters. They will learn to enjoy and expect the best.

Anyway, they clamored to be taken to Sesame Street Live at O'Keefe Centre. And my trio of critics were very nice about it. Anthony leaned forward in his seat, entranced by the experience of being in a theatre; Mitchell, who is very laid-back, coolly appraised the bright lights and ticked off the characters; and Chloe, whenever the music started, threw herself into choreography more inventive than anything on stage.

The show, in fact, is miserably unimaginative. The cast consists of actor-size replicas of the original puppets, miming to a pre-recorded track of dialogue and music. The voices are either those of the original performers or extraordinarily good mimics, but this is depressing in itself. The sound, and therefore the whole experience, is dead and distant; Jim Henson, Frank Oz et al have no business lending themselves to such a travesty of their own work. The story is How Sesame Street Came To Be On TV. Big Bird conceives the idea of a kids' show that will Also Teach Things. He is as earnest about this as the fund-raisers - my brood calls them "the Money Ladies" - who periodically solicit our alms on public TV: the ones whose devotion to "culture" is so schoolmarmish that you pledge yourself to watch nothing but game shows and commercials from here on in. This may be a necessary pitch for the foundations, but it should be kept from the kids. They should be watching for the stories, the fun and the characters. A dozen are on view here, but they have nothing individual to do or say, beyond the odd catch phrase.

Bert looks better than Ernie, whose large head does him in when blown up to stage-size. There is no Kermit; you could hardly blow him up without forfeiting his frogginess (and he demands good material). There is no Snuffy; he, I suppose, would have to be blown down. And, crucially, there are no human characters - making you realize just how much the TV show owes to its adult actors and continuing situations. They give it reality and, incidentally, femininity. (The puppet characters are overwhelmingly male.) Without them, the TV show would be, at best, cute. Apart from the occasional intervention from Oscar the Grouch ("hope you hated the show," he says encouragingly), the stage version isn't even that. It may have the official imprimatur, but it's still a ripoff. It even sinks to interpolating songs from other sources: a frantic, meaningless version of Kids, from Bye Bye Birdie, for instance. That is the stuff of cheap English pantomime: a mixture of instant recognition and total opportunism.

Infant middle-class Toronto was out in force, and it clapped and joined in at all the prescribed moments. But I think it felt cheated, and I hope it will grow up to know why.
