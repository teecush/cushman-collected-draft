ALL MY SONS

Festival Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont.

Arthur Miller's All My Sons was his first Broadway success, staged just after the Second World War. It concerns Joe Keller, who made a lot of money in that war manufacturing airplane parts. He allowed some faulty components to be sent out, resulting in the deaths of some 20 American pilots. His own son Larry was killed in action, though Joe takes comfort in the thought that he wasnt flying one of the defective planes. By the end of the play, however, old man Keller has been brought to realize that there is more to life than profits and family. The dead boys were all my sons. After making this affirmation, he stumbles off to commit suicide.

Now compare that title/line to the equivalent one in Millers next and most celebrated play, Death of a Salesman. Willy Loman is rhapsodizing about the old man whose example inspired his own choice of career, and he remarks parenthetically that "when he died, he died the death of a salesman." That death turns out to be very unlike Willy's own. So we have a play with a title drenched in irony, and one in which it's a climactic proclamation, bearing the plays full moral weight on its shoulders.

The ironic approach is the subtler and the more complex and, over time, the more satisfying. We never quite know why Willy kills himself -- we are offered a bewildering choice of motives -- but we believe it when it happens. Joes reasons are pretty clear, but his action comes too pat to be convincing. The pleasures offered by All My Sons are the simpler ones of moral melodrama. The morality is heartfelt and the storytelling extremely well-contrived, so it makes for an exciting evening.

This is how it works: Joe's business partner and next-door neighbour -- a weak man, easily intimidated -- took the rap and is just coming to the end of his jail sentence. The partner's daughter was engaged to Joes dead son; now she is about to marry his surviving brother Chris. This arrangement seems to please everybody except Joes wife. She clings to the belief that Larry is still alive, because if she admitted he was dead she would have to admit that, morally at least, Joe killed him. And if she believed that, Joe would be brought to believe it too, and that would be fatal. It's a convoluted course of reasoning, but it makes sense at the time -- emotional sense, anyway.

Back into their lives comes the partner's son, now a lawyer and newly convinced of his fathers innocence and Joes guilt. He also manages to convince Chris, which is what the mother most fears. For Chris' own war service has left him with a highly developed moral and social conscience; he is known throughout the neighbouring backyards as the local crusader. (For a CEO's family waxed fat on the war, the Kellers seem to be living on a remarkably modest scale. Maybe thats why Miller decided to set his next play further down- market; he obviously felt more at home there.) Chris duly turns on his father and, with a little help from his fiancee, gets him to acknowledge his responsibility.

Neil Munros direction is, as usual, intelligent, well-crafted and densely atmospheric, though he takes the last characteristic almost to excess: Joe and Chris have their crucial confrontation alfresco, with the shades of night falling so fast that we can hardly see the combatants. This may be intended to lend distance and texture to some pretty bald arguments, though time has already accomplished that; we swallow things in a period play that we would gag on in a new one.

Leslie Frankish has designed a pleasingly mellow variant on the house-with-a-porch setting that's de rigueur for American family plays of a certain age.

Most of the actors were in Munro's production of You Cant Take It with You earlier this season, and its intriguing to see them switch from domestic comedy to domestic drama; some of them play parallel roles.

Norman Browning, the dyspeptic tycoon of the earlier show, is the suicidal tycoon of this one, and he gives the role the same solid, chunky front, the same slow, lip-smacking delivery. Surprisingly, what worked for a caricature works equally well for a character. Joe remains a hollow man, but with the degree of hollowness that might indeed lead him to self-slaughter. He wouldn't have the imagination to figure out another way.

Ben Carlson again plays his son; he is maybe too sunny for the role, insufficiently obsessive, but he still has the authority to turn a juvenile lead into a person.

Mary Haney, who was previously a pleasantly batty mama, is now what Tom Stoppard once called "stark raving sane," a blend of delusion and absolute clear-sightedness. Miller's perception of the split maternal consciousness that desperately holds a family together is maybe his greatest insight (it was further developed in Salesman) and it strikes far harder now than the play's concern for our broader spiritual welfare.

Laurie Paton is as tough and as sympathetic as the fiancee should be, and Peter Krantz appropriately louring as her avenging brother. The various neighbours are so many rough edges, but the play's craftsmanship shines through: the guileful dispensing of some extremely complicated exposition, the early flashes of humour (a quality for which Miller is much underrated) before the claws begin to bite. And when they do take hold, they grip fast.
