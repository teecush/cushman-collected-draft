THEATRE REVIEW

DANCING WITH STARS CAN BE MURDER

Mysteriously Yours, Toronto

---

A few years ago in these pages, I suggested that Jean Daigle -- star, director and co-author of the Mysteriously Yours interactive dinner-with-murder operation -- should add Tony Soprano to his gallery of parody impersonations. Well, now he's obliged, but not, unfortunately, in the context of a full-fledged New Jersey gangster spoof. Dancing With Stars Can Be Murder, threateningly subtitled The Finals!, takes for its target exactly what you'd expect: a reality TV show with celebrity input. Daigle's Tony appears, for no particular reason, to investigate the poisoning (for once it isn't a shooting) of a contestant who, in his own mind at least, was the favourite to waltz off with the prize.

To be exact, Daigle doesn't play Tony: he plays James Gandolfony, an actor famous for playing Tony. He's turned up to be one of the celebrity judges, but he arrives late, as Daigle's character always does. This is so he can double as the victim, who this time around sports a startling black wig, voluminous enough for three Liberace hairdos piled one on top of another. The performance that supports this creation is suitably flamboyant; and when it comes to the main business of the evening, interrogating his fellow actors and selected members of the audience about their connection with the deceased, he's as good at the amiable bullying as ever he was. And it certainly fits the Tony Soprano persona, first seen here in undershorts, dressing-gown and smoking a cigar. There's probably an explanation of why this getup, so ideal for going down to the end of the driveway to pick up the morning paper, should be Tony's choice of attire for a social-professional engagement, but I forget what it is. Anyway he himself seems faintly embarrassed by it, and glad of the chance to slip off at the intermission and get into something less loose. Daigle's ability to be simultaneously inside the proceedings and detached from them is what makes these shows go.

All the same, this isn't one of the good ones. It's an arbitrary mix of two pop-culture flavours -- three if you count the Agatha Christie whodunnit convention that underpins all the creations here -- that gets little out of any of them. Tony out of context seems like a waste. Parody of TV dance competitions hardly gets beyond telling us that judges and competitors alike are, to a man or woman, murderously jealous narcissists. (Well, if they weren't murderous, or potentially so, they wouldn't be here.)What ingenuity there is has gone into naming them: we meet choreographer Bruno Tarantella, recording starlet Mary Kate Lohanspears and prima ballerina Tatiana Imasonova -- accent, for full effect, on the third syllable -- who does show off a routine involving hammers with a dancing handyman from the audience who, on the night I attended, was damned impressive. I was also mildly tickled by Mary Kate's revelations about some anti-social behaviour earlier in the day that had resolved in her spending a full 90 minutes in police custody, and by her fear that if found guilty of homicide she might be subjected to a sentence even longer.

The choreographic quotient is disappointingly low, especially coming from a company who a year or so back was able to convert their formula into a neat mini-musical. And even the detection part is perfunctory. There was a time at Mysteriously Yours when you could expect a denouement after the denouement; when, once the crime had been solved, there was some kind of twist in the criminal's reaction. Now, the guilty party is identified, those of us who got it right are congratulated and the rest castigated (Daigle still does this very well), and we all go home.

Still, it's Christmas, and one wants to be nice, and there are worse ways of spending a holiday night out. The cast still rise splendidly to the odd improvisational challenge when a punter comes out with an unexpected response, and even when one doesn't. And, something that sets this dinner theatre apart from most others, the dinner part of the equation is very strong. Order with confidence, from a staff who remain charming and considerate and who, in some cases, have been there for years. Such ensemble consistency is rare, in restaurants as in theatres. The management must be doing something right. - Dancing With Stars Can Be Murder is playing at Mysteriously Yours ...Mystery Dinner Theatre's New Year's Eve Mystery Gala.
