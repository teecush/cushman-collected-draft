IN PERSON " The turning point in Derek Jacobi's career came in I, Claudius, which made him the most recognizable classically trained British actor of his generation A survivor who knows exactly where he's going

Monday, January 14, 1991

ROBERT CUSHMAN

BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail WHEN Derek Jacobi was winning all hearts with his TV starring role in I, Claudius, somebody - my mother actually - asked me if he was really as nice off screen as he seemed to be on. With heavy heart I had to tell her the truth. Yes, he is.

Edmund Kean, the great English actor of the early 19th century, was, by historians' consent, not nice. On and off stage he was one of your hell-raising self-destructive actors. Jacobi, by his own estimate, is temperamentally light years away from Kean, certainly as a human being. Still, Jacobi recently scored a great success playing Kean at Londons Old Vic in a production that is now playing at the Royal Alexandra Theatre in Toronto.

Kean is not one of those biographical one-man shows. Its a proper play, of mixed ancestry: a translation by the British director Frank Hauser of a drama by Jean-Paul Sartre, itself based on an old play by Alexandre Dumas. The attraction of the material to Dumas, France's grand master of romantic swashbuckle, is obvious. Sartre should have been a much tougher nut, but of course he, like both Dumas and Kean, was a great womanizer; and is anybody really surprised that the existentialist's facade should have masked a susceptible bourgeois heart? Besides, any play about an actor is automatically concerned with appearance and reality and if that isnt philosophically respectable I would like to know what is.

One of jealous, passionate Keans greatest roles was jealous, passionate Othello; we get to see him playing that part and then, under pressure, stepping outside it. He talks directly to the audience, at which point, says Jacobi, there comes a further twist. "He starts talking as the actor playing Kean. It's that amount of passion. Total reality."

The play had been done in London some 20 years previously, with the late Alan Badel in the leading role. Jacobi remembered that production and especially recalled one line. Kean, having auditioned an aspiring actress, says to her: You were worse than very bad. You were quite good. That withering dismissal of mere adequacy has, he says, "a terrifying resonance for actors." Last year, when having a what-would- you-like-to-do-now conversation with London producer Duncan Weldon, Jacobi mentioned Kean; the upshot was this production.

So, on this occasion at least, Jacobi belied his assessment of himself as not a mover and shaker. What he is, he says, is "a survivor," and he has survived at a very high level. He was born during the Second World War into an East-end-of-London family with no theatrical connections. As a schoolboy, he was a star, both in school and with the fledgling National Youth Theatre, and played more leading roles in the competitive hothouse of Cambridge University. When he turned professional, he went straight to the prestigious Birmingham Repertory Theatre. In 1963 he became a founder-member of the National Theatre, under the direction of Laurence Olivier. Olivier's National is remembered now, a little rosily, as a glittering ensemble that commanded the loyalties of some of Britains best actors for astonishing lengths of time. Jacobi was one of the longest-serving of all. He stayed eight years.

In retrospect his career there was less than meteoric. He had good roles from the start - he was Laertes to Peter OToole's Hamlet in the inaugural production - but only at the end of his stay did he get an unequivocal lead: the saintly Prince Myshkin in a dramatization of Dostoevski's The Idiot. The production was generally (and somewhat unfairly) judged a disaster, but Jacobi came out of it well. He was believably Slavic and he had genuine authority; his holy fool did justice to both the noun and the adjective.

It was a suitable, if muted, climax to his National years. Everybody except himself had expected him to become a matinee idol, but instead he turned into an unusually attractive character actor. His contract with his audience was based on affection rather than hysterical devotion. He showed unsuspected strength; in the problematic role of Cassio, he held his own with Oliviers legendary Othello. And, to his own surprise, he became a comedian. Cast as the clown Touchstone in As You Like It (I read it and panicked - there wasnt a laugh in it), he borrowed some aghast mannerisms from National colleague Maggie Smith and somehow made them his own. They have been on call, when needed, ever since.

After the National he freelanced, on stage and TV. And then came the turning point.

I, Claudius was one of television's most successful prestige entertainments. Episode 9 - recalled by Jacobi as "the gory one, with John Hurt running amok" - is about to be rerun on PBS as part of Masterpiece Theatre's 20th-anniversary celebrations. I was, he says, tucking appropriately into a plate of Caesar salad, "far from the first person they thought of." I asked idly who was, and got the astounding answer, Charlton Heston. Astounding because Heston is no comic and it was the comedy, in the script and the playing, that made the series take off. But of course Heston was considered bankable by the American co- producers, and it was a long time before the BBC could persuade them to take a chance on Jacobi.

It was, he says, a big challenge, though apparently more technical than temperamental. It was shot in sequence but began with a flashback, so having established Claudius as old at the outset, they had to hold the image in their minds and return to it weeks later, a problem for the director, the makeup people and the actor. There were still six instalments to shoot when they began transmission. "The critics were lukewarm when it started," Jacobi says. But gradually it took over and became a cult. It got an instant repeat at home, and abroad it made Jacobi the most recognizable classically trained British actor of his generation, a position only to be challenged when Jeremy Irons achieved a similar TV success in Brideshead Revisited.

Jacobi returned to the classic theatre, this time in a position of strength. In one season with the Royal Shakespeare Company, he played three whopping roles. His skilled charm carried him through Benedick in Much Ado About Nothing, and even through Peer Gynt; and sounded newer and deeper notes, vocally and psychologically, as Prospero in The Tempest. The following year he added Cyrano de Bergerac.

This was another makeup challenge, and a temperamental one as well. "On the face of it," he says, not referring to the celebrated Bergerac nose, Cyrano wasnt me. His director, Terry Hands, told him cheerfully that if he failed in this one, his previous successes wouldnt mean a thing. With these encouraging words ringing in his ears, Jacobi set to work. Everybody knew that he would bring off the pathos of Cyrano, and his death scene duly had the audience weeping buckets. The flamboyance was more in question, but he brought it off through sheer energy and attack. The show was a sell-out success in London and New York.

Being ugly and sensitive, Cyrano looked backward to Claudius. Being demonic, he looked forward to Kean, and Jacobi acknowledges that the two roles presented him with similar problems of self-transcendence. "Kean says that you want to be what you can't be; you get fed up with what you are." The line reminded him of his own approach to Cyrano.

Jacobi has made his biggest cinematic splash to date as Arthur Clennam, the repressed Victorian hero of the two-part Little Dorrit. He has just completed another film called The Fool, based on the sociological writings of Henry Mayhew, chronicler of the London poor. I play two characters who are really halves of the same personality, Jacobi says. "One rich and powerful, one poor and downtrodden. It's basically a story about money. Very contemporary." Next, Pierre, the massively self-doubting hero in a two-part stage version of War and Peace. Sometime, I imagine, he will play a definitive Jekyll and Hyde. Offstage he gives the impression that he lets his career happen to him. Onstage he knows exactly where he is going.
