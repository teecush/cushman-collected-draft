NOT MY STORY

Artword Theatre, Toronto

- - -

The first lines of Silvija Jestrovic's Not My Story are actually from Chekhovs Three Sisters. It turns out that the woman delivering them is auditioning for the role of the middle sister, Masha. Her own name, also very Chekhovian, is Sonya. She comes from what was formerly known as the former Yugoslavia, and is now living in a city in Canada; we assume its Toronto. Her speech over, she laughs with her unseen judges about the obvious suitability of her Slavic accent for a play that is about Russians, even if it's going to be done in English. She laughs, but she probably isn't joking. More sort of desperate.

Flanking her are two other women, her expatriate compatriots. The three arent literally sisters, but if you go looking for resemblances to Chekhovs ladies, you can find them, in fractured form. In place of Olga, the reluctant headmistress, meet Lela, who announces herself as an art history teacher but has been forced into more exotic pursuits. For Irina, who planned a marriage of convenience with a man she didn't love, we offer you Nena, who actually ties the knot with a gay Canadian in order to get citizenship. He has his own agenda; she's a photographer, hes an aspiring model, though his real talent seems to be for mixing juices.

This is a play about people denied outlets for their talents and ambitions. The author comes from a background similar to those of her three heroines, and so do two of the actresses. The stage is repeatedly occupied by moments of anger, frustration and even fleeting happiness that feel authentic.

There is not much, though, to hold them together. Chekhovs characters, to pursue the analogy, existed within a society, and their drama is framed by the arrival and departure of a military battalion whose members crucially affect their lives. They also have a fire and a fatal duel to speed their lives along. These women are, by definition, on the fringes. They can't even yearn for Moscow because their Moscow is what they ran away from. Things indeed happen to them, but the events don't register, and so we get no sense of change or growth. It proceeds in brief, isolated scenes that frequently seem about to turn into something interesting but never quite make it; they end before they can take hold.

Dragana Varagic, who plays Sonya, is herself a formidable actress. Her own situation, as a performer whose ethnicity may be denying her some of the breaks she deserves, parallels that of the woman shes playing. Varagic fills her up with the bitterness of rejection and the wit of survival, and I would watch her play Masha or any other Chekhov heroine any time.

But Varagic is also the director, and thats a mistake. It looks as if her energies were divided, and the production exists in a permanent state of droop. This applies not just to the scenes but to the spaces between them, which are filled with really irritating piano music.

One of the play's misfortunes is that its central characters are all in essentially the same predicament; so Sonya's friends cant help but seem like paler echoes of herself. Given this, Cynthia Ashperger (Lela) and Rena Polley (Nena) play with constant determination and intermittent success. Ashperger comes on especially strong in a surprise-revelation scene that takes us down one of the plays more promising false trails; Polley is the only one of the trio not to sound authentically Central European, conceivably because she isn't. The production may be taking its cue from a theory Sonya advances at the start, about the irrelevance of accents to the higher scheme of things; she varies it later by saying what a gas it would be were she to perform pregnant.

Some plays -- Chekhov's, for example -- are big enough, and have become mythic enough, to stand up to that kind of thing. This one isnt exactly in that class. If it isnt specific, it's dead. It's about different cultures, for Gods sake.

The last words spoken in Not My Story are "lets begin." They're at the wrong end; we'd been waiting to begin all night.
