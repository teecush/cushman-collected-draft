The new Second City revue, most unusually for its species, has a title that bears some relation to the show itself. Click Bait Switch is a revue for the Internet age, with opening and closing routines that consist of the six-person cast delivering disconnected come-on lines designed to hook unwary or credulous surfers. Beyond that it has, intentionally or otherwise, a recurring theme. It depicts people behaving like automata.

Of course, a lot of comedy does that. Obsessiveness is what makes the wheels of comedy, and especially of farce, go round. But this show seems, well, obsessive about it. Its second half kicks off with Kyle Dooley, a notably tall performer with equally notable improv skills, as a muscleman ready to challenge all comers, real or fictitious, living or dead, and coming on like a cross between a cartoon figure and a wind-up doll. The same description might go, double, for the two senescent wheelchair-bound billionaires, married, whom we find wondering how to dispose of their wealth. This is an especially strong number whose major joke is all the more satisfying for being foreseeable a short way off. It also has that rarity in Second City or any other revues: a great punchline. And it has an Internet connection.

This is an unusually intelligent show: one that taps into today without going for easy topicality. (Such words as Harper and Duffy never cross a performer's lips.) The first extended sketch - to tell the truth, it's over-extended, but funny nonetheless - has the sextet at a barbecue, screeching "Don't Do That" - every time someone says something that might be considered shaming of someone else, which is all the time. A happy young couple, she an activist, he a CSIS agent, recall how they found compatibility,. ("Are you reading my mind?" "No, just your emails.") A darkskinned guy - Etan Muskat, versatile and ebullient - reveals to his two paleskinned buddies that his secret fantasy is to be white. ("I'd like to ask a waiter, how spicy is the pepper?") His friends aren't pleased. ("We're not racist, but without you - there's no proof.") This item also has a good payoff, physical rather than verbal. We visit a high-end restaurant that gives new meaning to the term "tasting menu", and where the dessert is rendered more delicious by having a hungry man (Dooley, on a leash and with his tongue hanging out) watch you eat it. There's a great ending to this too, all the better for connecting with an earlier scene.

There's also plenty of stuff that's just charmingly crazy. Becky Johnson, another tall performer, sabotages herself at a job interview, despite her potential employer's kindest intentions. (She refuses a relaxing drink on the grounds that alcohol makes her weird. She must have put away a few before she even got there.) Leigh Cameron sweetly delivers the evening's one and only song as a delectable pastry, cheerfully waiting to be devoured. ("It's OK, please don't cry /For I'm just a singing pie.") More manically musical - far more - is Kirsten Rasmussen as a demonically possessed clubber, whose moves drive the other guests to request music that she can't dance to. It turns out that no such music exists. Kevin Whalen, who has a knack for appearing simultaneously genial and worried, heads a trio of caterpillars all grossed out by the unexpected experience of turning into butterflies. Which probably they would be, not having had sexed. There's a sketch about that, too, and a good one, with very funny representatives of the puritanical, the permissive and the just-don't-know-yet There are creepy-crazy bits as well. Johnson and Whalen play a mother and son out for a drive, the former telling the latter things no boy should have to know. An ultrasound scene puts a new face, and body, on some familiar fears and feelings. (I know that sounds vague but I don't want to give everything away.) It has yet another effective ending; I don't know what I've been complaining about. Somewhere between the comforting and the forbidding is a twist on the old Lady and the Tiger theme: a traveller has to choose between two doors, one festive, one fatal, guided by two confusing oracles who are new on the job; this bit, like its protagonist, doesn't really know where it's going but has some fun on the way.

So it's a good show, mostly well written, always excellently performed, and with a tang that differentiates it from its predecessors. Paul Bates directed it.
