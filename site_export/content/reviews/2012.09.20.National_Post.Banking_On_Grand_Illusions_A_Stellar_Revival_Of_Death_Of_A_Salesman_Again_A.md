A man who, after working all his life for the same company, is no longer useful to them, is summarily dismissed, with the implication that really it's all for his own good. His new boss, son of his old boss, is embarrassed, but more interested in his new wire recorder, the latest technological toy. His own sons, brought up to believe they are the inheritors of a society in which anything is possible, are crushingly disillusioned by the discovery that it isn't. Home owners, car owners and just plain appliance owners find themselves "in a race with the junk-yard," yearning to possess just one item that is still functional by the time they've finished paying for it. Neighbourhoods that used to feel like open spaces are increasingly boxed in by high buildings. There's probably never been a time since its premiere in 1949 that Arthur Miller's Death of a Salesman hasn't seemed topical, but its pertinence at the moment is beyond painful.

Indeed, Soulpepper's revival, directed by Albert Schultz, is even more powerful now than when it was first presented two years ago. (Its remount, with just one major cast change, must count as a revival of a revival.) Some of this obviously has to do with the production playing itself in. Salesman is, above all else, a play about a family; actors and audiences respond to it fully and easily because we've all been there, caught in those same crosscurrents of love and resentment. On one level, it's the kind of identification that works for soap operas, but soaps don't give you the same sense of context, the same rich correlation between the characters' social and economic circumstances and their emotional conflicts. Willy Loman's role as a salesman doesn't determine his fate -- he'd find some way of screwing up his life, whatever his job was -- but it highlights and focuses it.

There's a scene at the end of the play's first act that takes off like a rocket, a scene in which we see the Loman family simultaneously pulling together and falling apart. Elder son Biff is back home in Brooklyn after years bumming around the countryside. In school, he'd been a football hero, with not just the ball but the whole world at his feet, or so everybody believed. (Only in America? I'm not sure.) But he dropped out, never tried for the college place that seemed guaranteed, and has become a bitter disappointment to everyone, including himself. He's briefly roused by the prospect of a new career selling sporting goods, egged on by younger brother Happy, who has a marketing scheme that seems to consist of re-enacting their boyhood. Willy, who a few moments earlier was all resentful accusation, is now all enthusiasm, his faith in the dream rekindled. Linda, his wife, is enthusiastic too, though she should probably know better -- but she's one person for whom love, family love, really does conquer all. But whenever she chimes in, Willy, who seems to think this is man talk, impatiently shushes her. He does it once too often, and Biff ( "Stop yelling at her!") explodes. Willy, punctured and momentarily ashamed, shuffles off. Linda tries to mend fences; Biff subsides. A whole domestic drama has unfolded before us in less than 10 minutes.

The key to it all is Willy's desperate volatility, his bounding from one feeling to another -- and from one opinion to its opposite -- in a second. He simply can't afford to look clearly at himself, or at anybody else. One way in which he differs from the traditional tragic hero is that it isn't the realization of truth that does him in, but the persistence of illusion. That may well be closer to the facts of life. And his is, in its way, quite a grand illusion. There is now a grandeur about Joseph Ziegler's performance that in no way obscures Willy's weakness or his occasional brutality; he always had Willy's chameleon quality down perfectly, but he now makes both the man's pride and his humiliation agonizingly real.

Nancy Palk, who has spent the last couple of years inhabiting several of the modern stage's major mothers, gets completely inside this one, a woman who can see through her husband and her sons while still protecting them; some of her finest moments are her angriest. The bond between her and Ari Cohen's Biff is very strongly portrayed; and he, in a play full of flashbacks, is equally convincing as golden boy and tarnished man. The moment at which he passes from one to the other, the moment when he loses all faith in his father and his world falls apart, is magnificently done. An abiding puzzle in the play is how much of this Willy has managed to blot out; much of the time he seems genuinely mystified about why Biff should have, as he says, given up on his life. A lesser mystery is why Happy, the less favoured son, should feel so un-resentful about it; his own life isn't going so well, and he lies about it in the family tradition, but it's for other reasons. Mike Ross, the production's newcomer, gets both Happy's brashness in company and his dependence at home. Michael Hanrahan and Gregory Prest are beautifully, quietly authoritative as the father and son next door who can help and support but, as the play's most moving scenes make clear, not save. William Webster, bathed in what seems to be his own private lighting design, is a haunting, taunting apparition as the rich Uncle Ben whom we only meet in Willy's tormented dreams. Lorenzo Savoini's set still seems cramped; it suggests that the Lomans live in a bungalow. Everything else is as good as before or better.
