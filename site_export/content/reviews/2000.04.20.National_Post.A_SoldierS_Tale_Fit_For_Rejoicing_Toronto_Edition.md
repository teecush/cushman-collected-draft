Gaudeamus runs till April 22

GAUDEAMUS

Elgin Theatre, Toronto

Let us rejoice. Gaudeamus, the much-travelled and much-lauded production brought to World Stage by the Maly Theatre of St. Petersburg, begins as amazingly as any show I have ever seen. A man dances onto a snow-covered stage, whirls happily around, then suddenly falls through a trapdoor.

Others follow him on, in accelerating succession, each cavorting in is own style and each meeting the same fate, each through a different trap. The stage becomes a maelstrom of prancing and disappearing bodies, a ballet performed on a minefield.

The men then pop back up like so many jack-rabbits, and we notice each is now in military uniform. One of them begins to recite, "To be or not to be -- in English. (His words are solemnly reproduced on the surtitles above the stage, also in English, though it might be a pleasantly surreal touch if they were to be shown in Russian.)

Those trapdoors might be foxholes, or they might be latrines. In the course of the evening -- two-and-a-half hours, no intermission - - they represent both. Gaudeamus is about the army. It's a kaleidoscopic play, worked up in rehearsal through collective improvisation, and based on a novel by Sergei Kaledin that has the distinction of being the only book to have been banned under Gorbachev.

One can see why: at least assuming the play, in style or substance, faithfully reflects its source. Scatologically disrespectful references to Lenin in the first dialogue scene suggest the work was moving with the times but was slightly ahead of them. It was also, and unforgivably, attacking from below.

The director is Lev Dodin, who is a legend. He has a company of 17, every one of whom seems able to act, sing, dance and play a musical instrument. The characters -- the male ones, anyway -- are members of a Construction Battalion, a division of the army used as a dumping-ground for ex-cons, physical misfits, ethnic minorities, and other undesirables, exploited as cheap labour with a few gestures toward military training. The period is the declining days of the Afghan campaign, with morale at an all-time low.

After the prelude, the infernal descent of the conscripts, we see a rookie recruit being hazed by a couple of old men," squaddies with a year's service behind them. Life, we gather, is a matter of survival: surviving the unofficial and generally vicious pecking order, surviving the work, surviving drink and drugs, surviving corruption.

The battalion commander promises three of the men --among them a Gypsy and a Jew -- an early discharge as a reward for cleaning the toilets; it turns out he wants money as well. Pay day is preceded by long and insanely repetitive lectures about taking care of your comrade when hes drunk.

For some practical indoctrination on Soviet policy in the Middle East, the men are divided into two groups -- Jews and Arabs -- the latter group unfeelingly including the one actual Jew in the brigade. Near the end of the play, there is a murder.

The weekly excursion into town, with the chance of picking up women, is of course all the men live for. Some of these encounters are unfeeling, some are actively brutal, a few are enchantingly lyrical. A junior officer brings his wife an unglamorous coat, a gift shyly proffered and rapturously accepted. A soldier suggests to a lady librarian she is frigid, and receives enthusiastic proof to the contrary. Their tryst takes place on top of a grand piano (flown on stage at regular intervals) and ends with the two of them picking out a tune with their bare toes.

Though individual characters persist and recur, there is no real story; there are 19 discrete scenes, some more dreamlike than others.

Dodin acknowledges a debt to the Russian experimental theatre of the 1920s -- the critical, surrealist kind murdered by Stalin in the name of "Socialist Realism" -- but the actual acting is earthy and colloquial in a way I dont associate with that tradition, or indeed with any other kind of Russian theatre.

Most of the exports I've encountered have been, for obvious reasons, classics, mainly Chekhov, or new plays performed as if they were classics -- with flexibility, certainly, and sometimes with humour, but always in a dignified, basso profundo, kind of way. (The men have generally looked and sounded like Boris Godunov.)

The actors here are as light on their feet and in their speech as the performers in any Western service comedy. The style connects in its rude subversiveness with the Central European tradition of The Good Soldier Schweik.

But it's also bound to remind us of Catch-22, especially in the day-tripper sexual encounters and in the mad futility of the army routine. A scene in which a soldier digs up an old field-telephone and finds that orders from the past have to be executed in the present is especially Heller-ish. Theres also a strong taste of Oh What a Lovely War, in the use of music and clowning

But then, Western culture, especially pop culture, runs right through the show -- final proof that it is, for better or worse, international.

Some of it seems added for our benefit, changing from town to town: at least, I doubt if a reference to Margaret Atwood played as well in Glasgow or New York, let alone St Petersburg, as it does in Toronto.

Others are plainly integral: the old Beatles song Girl, for example, cropping up at different times to yearning or malicious effect.

The actual text is, to judge from the surtitles, less rich than the staging or the basic idea -- there aren't many good jokes -- and thus probably takes its toll on the acting.

But talented and devoted as these performers are (many of them, as in the Vancouver production of The Overcoat, were students when the show began) it's hard to have any continuing involvement with these characters; there are too many of them, going on and off like lightbulbs. The show becomes relentless -- which, admittedly, suits the subject.

When they finally sing the title number -- Gaudeamus Igitur, the old school song about the glory of youth -- the irony seems very heavy. It's also, though, happily double-edged. The people on the stage are young, and in this production they do have something to glory in.
