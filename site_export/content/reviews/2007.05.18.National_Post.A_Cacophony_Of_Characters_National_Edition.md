THEATRE REVIEW

A MONTH IN THE COUNTRY

COURT HOUSE THEATRE,

NIAGARA-ON-THE-LAKE

- - -

Ivan Turgenev wrote a play called A Month in the Country, and so, 150 years later, did Brian Friel. Friels play is billed -- not just chronologically -- as "after Turgenev," and large sections of it do indeed follow very closely after. There are others in which it keeps a more radical distance.

One thing that Friel, Irelands most venerated living playwright, has surprisingly not done is to alter the original's time and place. Classic Russian plays have in the past been quite successfully transplanted to later periods in Ireland, but here we are still in the vast wasteland of provincial Russia in the 1840s. And though the dialogue can be racily contemporary, there is nothing about it -- not, at least, on the lips of the Shaw Festival's Canadian cast -- that sounds especially Irish.

What Friel has done, for the most part to great effect, is to sharpen both the absurdity and the heartbreak of the main plot bequeathed him by his predecessor. Here again we have Natalya Petrovna, young wife of a rich landowner, flirting inconclusively with the admirer, Rakitin, whom shes been keeping on a string for years (and whose self-lacerating, unconsummated passion is based on Turgenevs own feelings for a married opera singer). Here we have Belyayev, the household's new, young tutor suddenly arousing the interest both of Natalya and of her teenage ward Vera, of whom she becomes maliciously jealous. What in Turgenev is incipiently farcical becomes explicit in Friel, without in any way devaluing the emotional confrontations which, decorous in the original, come at us here with their white gloves off.

This is the only version I've seen of A Month in the Country to hold my attention throughout; usually Ive flagged somewhere in the middle as the characters seemed to be going endlessly around in the same circles. Tadeusz Bradeckis excellent production is ruthless but not heartless. It enables Fiona Byrne, in what must be her finest performance to date, to expose all the self-indulgence of Natalya, who believes the world owes her a romance, without skimping either on the pain she suffers or that which she causes. David Jansen's Rakitin, more puppy-dog than lapdog, is adequate while hardly probing the depths of the man's agony or of his ironic selfzcontempt, but Martin Happer is suitably guileless as Belyayev and Marla McLean a revelation as Vera, a character whom I have never known to register as more than a necessary ingenue but is here capable

of real feeling and real anger; her scenes with Natalya, who is trying simultaneously to befriend and exploit her, are among the strongest in the show.

There is an extraordinary tragifarcical performance by Blair Williams as Natalyas uncomprehending spouse, whose main interest is his threshing machine: Suddenly provoked into paroxysms of jealousy over the wrong man, he as quickly subsides into terrified acquiescence. He is the character most altered: Turgenev wrote him as stolid, but Friel, picking up on hints, makes him a volatile mothers boy, with Patricia Hamilton giving a grandly judicious account of the mother. She, too, has acquired a new dimension (in fact, a whole new backstory), and Friel has in general dealt more freely with the minor characters than with the major ones, with varying results. With the local doctor, who provides rich material in the first place, he's been completely successful. Ric Reid, emphasizing the man's peasant background and emotional dyspepsia, plays the doctor as a resentful hanger-on who despises himself and everyone else, especially those who laugh at his terrible jokes. Its a superb performance, as remote as possible from Reids fine gentle Inquisitor in Saint Joan (which is about bad things happening to good people because good people do them), and most delicately partnered by Sharry Flett as the spinster companion whose face speaks finely shaded volumes and with whom he eventually pairs off. He has a further fine foil in Michael Ball as an unprepossessing neighbour who admires Vera and pays the doctor to act as go- between: a nice enough fellow whose dramatic fate it is to bring out the worst -- the most selfish or the most desperate-- in everyone.

The role of Herr Schaaf, an older and Germanic tutor, has been much extended, given a more extended line in randiness and a wholly new one in malapropisms. David Schurmann plays him well, but he far outstays his welcome. Confession: I once played Schaaf, and was heard to complain that the brevity of the role proved that Turgenev was a novelist, not a playwright. Chekhov, I grumbled, would have known how to keep his small-part actors active throughout. I should have been careful for what I wished. Two servants who pursue an inconclusive courtship in the original have also had their relationship upgraded so that it becomes part subplot, part commentary on the self-inflicted torments of their employers. No complaints about the performances of Moya O'Connell and Thom Marriott but they become intrusive.

The show has too active a periphery. But its centre holds. - A Month in the Country is in repertoire through October 6. Call 1-800- 511- SHAW for tickets.
