TO LIFE

St. Lawrence Centre for the Arts, Toronto

The first act of To Life begins with the song of that name, from Fiddler on the Roof, and the second ends with it. Despite this symmetry, anyone trying to find an overall unity in this musical entertainment from the Harold Green Jewish Theatre Company risks turning their brain into a pretzel. Best to regard the evening as a double bill of two separate shows, linked only by a focus on the Jewish contribution to the American musical theatre, and by the presence in both of the same quartet of agile and melodious song-and-dancers.

Act One is concerned with four famous performers from the early decades of the 20th century: Eddie Cantor, Sophie Tucker, Fanny Brice and Al Jolson. Patrick Cook gets to approximate Cantor's appealing rhythmic bleat. Charlotte Moore, trailing the longest of red boas, does Tucker's red-hot basso mama routine, in accordance with the general image of Tucker as someone who can never have been young. Gabi Epstein gamely summons the ghost of Fanny Brice, while necessarily battling that of Barbra Streisand; her Second Hand Rose is actually faster and more economical than Streisand's. Shawn Wright brings weight and wit to a Jolson innocent of blackface and even, until near the end, of oratorical mannerisms. Each actor gets the establishing number for his or her character; afterwards, each legend's repertoire is up for grabs from the entire company. The performers and most of the material are good, so it's fun.

It might be even more fun if we were told more about, say, Jolson's egomania or Cantor's peppy liberalism, or about how their Jewishness informed their public personae. I guess it just isn't meant to be that kind of show. Cantor and Brice were comedians who sang, Tucker and Jolson were singers who clowned; so there's a welcome accent on comedy numbers, some of them done in welcome accents.

We also get the Spamalot number You Won't Succeed on Broadway (If You Don't Have Any Jews), done more enjoyably than in the original, with the cast gleefully pointing out that three-quarters of them are from the gentile percentile. In fact, Spamalot and other myths to the contrary, Jewish performers have not, after the heyday of Cantor and Co., run the American musical. (No, Ethel Merman wasn't Jewish and neither, obviously, was Mary Martin.)

Jewish writers are another matter, and the evening's second half is built around the songs they wrote for the book musicals of the '60s and early '70s: the last hurrah of the Rodgers and Hammerstein style. A few numbers, not all of them by Jerry Herman, show why the last hurrah was also the last gasp, but in general the standard is high. The songs have been arranged to tell a kind of story, and the idea works better than I would have expected.

It's a story of family, though not one particular family; or if it is, it involves a brother marrying a sister. The ambience is established in a fine sequence of songs from Jule Styne's London show Bar Mitzvah Boy. The general upliftingness sometimes makes you yearn for something mordant from Company or Follies, but the only real misstep is a group treatment of Maury Yeston's New Words, a song that loses its poignancy and point when it isn't done solo.

Conception and direction are by Avery Saltzman, and the excellent choreography is by Tim French. Mark Camilleri, the show's ace musical director, and Jeffrey Huard, who has been many other shows' ace musical director, provide two-piano accompaniment that ranges from admirable to dazzling.
