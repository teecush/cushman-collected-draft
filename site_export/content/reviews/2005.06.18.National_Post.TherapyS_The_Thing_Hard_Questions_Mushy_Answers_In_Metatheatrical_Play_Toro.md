Revolutions in Therapy closes tonight at the Artword Theatre, 416- 366-7723, ext. 290.; rcushman@lycos.com

There are exceptions, but new plays of the narrative kind are usually frustrating. Maybe its the hype that invariably precedes them, but they always seem to be holding out the promise of searing new insights into our lives and the society that shapes them. Not surprisingly, they fail to deliver. They also run out of steam as stories.

It isnt culturally respectable to say so, but you can generally get a better time at old plays. This doesn't have much to do with nostalgia, except of a purely esthetic kind; it can be pleasant to be taken back to times that were more confident about the theatre than ours is, and so produced more confidently entertaining plays. The reason is that we approach an old play, even when it's one that we havent seen before, without illusions. We have a good idea of what such a play has to offer. It comes from another time and the lives that it may have professed to change are long since over. We can enjoy watching these people swimming around in their natural habitat, and can learn something about what their world was like. And if they do turn out to have something to say to us directly, well, thats gravy.

A new play is unlikely to tell us anything about our own time that we don't already know. Except, that is, on a factual or anecdotal level, which explains why documentary drama, on stage or screen, is often more exciting than the made-up variety. There are stories around us more compelling than those any dramatist could invent. And maybe there is something self-defeating in the whole idea of narrative fiction. Look, as I devotedly have for four whole seasons and will again, at 24. It provides just enough character interest for us to keep watching without hating ourselves, but basically it's about generating suspense, an art that it may have refined to its purest condition yet. Every episode keeps us on the edge of our seats, and every episode is frustrating. The real-time component is part of this. We watch the minutes being counted off at the side of the screen, and at first we feel comforted; theres plenty of time before we have to return to the real world, and plenty of time for this weeks crisis to be defused. But even when it is, there has to be another one left unresolved, to keep us hungry for another week. This may be pleasurable, but the last instalment is always a letdown. This is partly because the climax of every series except the first has been weak in its own terms, but the chief reason is that, with no more cliffs to hang on to and nothing else from the experience worth preserving, we feel squeezed, empty.

Some of these thoughts went through my mind while watching Revolutions in Therapy, a new production from a three-person group who call themselves STO Union. This show (it is emphatically not a play, and the emphasis is theirs) is partly about finding alternative ways of describing and even alleviating personal angst, and partly about the relation between performers and audience. There's no automatic reason why these two things should be considered together, and by the end the proceedings have drifted off, but every so often one part brushes up against another with stimulating results.

Early in the proceedings one performer, Tracy Wright, asks another performer, Nadia Ross, about the pain she's suffering. Ross gives a fancy answer that Wright, rather refreshingly, says she doesnt understand. Ross starts telling a story instead about a man who underwent terrible bereavement. This hooks us, but she breaks off, and the show starts worrying aloud about the nature of narrative and in particular about the morality of suspense, about the fact that our desire to know what happens next makes us value a perpetually delayed future at the expense of the present moment, which, even when were not listening to stories, is no way to live our lives. She is in fact asking that most terrifying of all questions: Are we having fun yet?

STO Union is not offering documentary, and it certainly isn't in the classical revival business, but it is striving for something real." After Wright is through interrogating Ross, Ross interrogates Wright. (Both speak through microphones, which seem to give them protection by making them sound impersonal. When deprived of them, the performers act naked and scared.) It seems both have been through psychoanalysis, and are unimpressed. As a different form of therapy, and a different form of theatre, they offer "meditation, though, frankly, if they hadn't told me that thats what it was, I wouldnt have guessed.

However, they second-guess themselves. Just why," one of them says to another, "should they, meaning us, be interested in what you have to say?" I paraphrase, but not by much; anyway, it's a good question, and one that more companies should ask themselves. Later on they try to enlist us still further by walking among us, and trying to see things from our point of view. It's audience participation minus most of the embarrassment.

You want real? In her projection of pain and fear Ross, who is co- writer and co-director as well as performer, is as real as real can be; which means of course that she is acting, very well indeed. Jacob Wren, who is her co-co-writer/director and who makes a late appearance just as we are beginning to wonder if there was a mistake in the program, doesnt act at all and probably cant. But the fact that he seems to have no right to appear on a stage in front of a paying audience doesn't make him any more genuine. Wright, who occupies a less exalted position in the hierarchy (she's credited with "artistic collaboration") falls, in point of stage presence, somewhere between the other two. But the fact that one makes such comparisons proves that this is a show like any other. Acknowledging the existence of the audience makes no difference; neither does having one of the actors complain about the role shes been given.

Artifice doesnt cease to be artificial merely by drawing attention to itself. It might conceivably be different if they were making it up as they went along, but though the script grew out of improvisation in rehearsal, it is now just a script, as set in stone as any other. (Paradoxically, this method seems to work best when the aim is naturalism, as in the films and plays of Mike Leigh.) When Wren comes on, it's to take over Ross's role, she having apparently been overcome by the agony of it all. Thankfully, she reappears, but by now the metatheatrical stuff has killed off everything else.

Ross resumes her story but its no longer as compelling as it was before. Maybe thats the point. She still talks about pain, as do the others, but the urgency has gone out of it. Connections are made, or at least alluded to, between individual anguish and our condition as members of a capitalist society, and they sound convincing but not overwhelmingly so. Most of us, after all, know plenty of people who seem to be getting along all right and not having breakdowns. The particular condition the performers describe is not universal, and we don't really know if they have experienced it themselves or if they're just pretending. If its the former, then the assumption that they speak for all of us is presumptuous; if its the latter, then it's presumptuous and deceitful.

The production looks excellent, played out against a screen with an abstract design composed, as far as I could tell, of pieces of Kleenex. This periodically fades, to show video clips of the natural world, which are pretty, though their relevance is mysterious. What they mainly add is a layer of sentimentality. All right, the piece isn't a play, and in some ways thats a relief. But its still theatre, and therefore subject to the theatrical obligation to make interesting use of its audience's time. If we are to do without story, we need a good substitute. Revolutions in Therapy may have its subject confused with its method. Theatre often does function as therapy, both for its originators and for its recipients, but that isn't what it's for, and to claim otherwise is self-indulgence. This show, which is about to represent Canada at a festival in Stuttgart, asks some hard questions but it comes up with some mushy answers.
