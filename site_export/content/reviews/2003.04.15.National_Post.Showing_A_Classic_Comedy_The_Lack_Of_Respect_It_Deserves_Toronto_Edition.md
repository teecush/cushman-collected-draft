THE MAGNETISM OF THE HEART

Premiere Dance Theatre, Toronto

- - -

GAGARIN WAY

Buddies in Bad Times Theatre, Toronto

- - -

Deconstruction can be hard to appreciate when you don't know what the targeted work looked like when it was still constructed. The Magnetism of the Heart, brought to World Stage by the Polish company Teatr Rosmaitosci, is based on a 19th-century comedy called Maiden Vows by Aleksander Fredro. Fredro is apparently Poland's national comic playwright; on its home ground this production has been described as an assault on "the most holy of sacraments of the Polish stage," which could be either an accolade or a denunciation, depending on who's talking.

I had to make what acquaintance I could with the original, via Surtitles, while watching what had been done to it, and I couldn't see that anything very disrespectful was going on. The production certainly gets wild at times (though definitely not in the sense of being undisciplined) but it is also continuously wonderful. It struck me as an attempt to reclaim a play that must have suffered the fate of classic comedies around the world -- i.e., staged too often, with a kind of automatic, antiseptic jollity, and taken seriously, as a piece involving real people with real feelings. Laszlo Marton did the same thing, though far less elaborately, in his Soulpepper production of Moliere's School for Wives. Fredro is known indeed as the Polish Moliere, though on the evidence of this play, which is concerned with the duplicities and vulnerabilities of erotic love, he had more in common with Marivaux.

The show is directed by Grzegorz Jarzyna, an enfant terrible who stages each of his shows under a different name, presumably on the sound principle that it's harder to hit a moving target (for this play he's called himself, or herself, Sylwia Torsh). The main strategy here is to move the action steadily forward, from one era to another, as the evening progresses. Everything starts buttoned- up and fairly conventional. By the end, characters are chasing each other across the stage, shedding clothes as they run, then lying down and rutting vigorously to the accompanying strains of the Bee Gees' Stayin' Alive. Though since this 1970s music seems to be coming out of a 1930s radiogram, it does appear that the aural elements of the show have here streaked ahead of the visual.

The maiden vows of the play's original title are taken by a couple of sisters, Aniela and Klara. They make, as you might guess, a mutual promise to steer clear of men. Each, of course, has a suitor; Aniela is besieged by Gustaw, a cynically resourceful young rake, and Klara is being pursued by Albin, a dry-looking stick who is of indeterminate age but has apparently been hanging around for years (though probably not, as Gustaw disgustedly claims, for 50 of them.) Gustaw takes all their destinies in his hands and steers matters to the expected amatory conclusion. He does it by playing mind games, whose elegant cruelty is here played to the hilt. But so is the explosive joy that results. It's like Les Liaisons Dangereuses with a happy ending.

As the pace gets more hectic, the scenes get shorter. A few are limited to a single line. No 19th-century playwright is likely to have written this way, even in Poland, but then Jarzyna (or Torsh) is credited with adapting the text as well as directing it, and with the terms so plainly declared we can hardly complain of distortion. Anyway, it's not as if the director has indulged in the tedious contemporary sport of sneering at his author. The keynotes of the evening are lightness and grace, of a consistency and accomplishment whose likes we rarely get to see.

The sets, by Magdalena Maciejewska, are magic, and the music and soundscape (the latter incorporating a whole barnyard of country noises) inspired.

The performances are intensely, sometimes clownishly, physical, but never forced or grotesque, and the actors speak with similarly understated finesse; it's rare to find yourself laughing at the delivery of lines in a language you don't understand, but I did so here. Magdalena Cielecka (Klara), who is slimness itself, has an entrancing scene in which the fashion of the moment has her standing at a writing desk, her bustle undulating with indignant expectation. Maja Ostaszweska (Aniela) has the wittiest mouth movements I remember seeing, and is also very touching. Redbad Klynstra (Gustaw) makes a sublime Byronic entrance. And Cezary Kosinski (Albin) lopes magnificently. We last see him taking a shower, which, considering the enthusiasm with which the aroused Klara has just laid him, he probably needs.

The cast is completed by the girls' mother, Gustaw's uncle and a surly servant whose essential superiority to his arrogant employers reaches its conclusion when he takes centre position at the curtain call. I suppose this class consciousness might be considered revolutionary, but it's actually pretty standard in modern production of bourgeois comedy, and the text, as far as I could tell, gives plenty of pretext for it. The entire septet comes together, without benefit of words, for the most riotous breakfast scene since Hay Fever. Or, on the remote chance that it's the dramatist's inspiration rather than the director's, before Hay Fever.

Gagarin Way is a first play by a young Scottish dramatist, Gregory Burke, brought to World Stage by our very own Crow's Theatre. It premiered in Britain in August, 2001, just in time to avoid seeming terminally quaint. "We might," says one of the characters, "make political violence fashionable again." He now seems a little late, and the political violence that he actually pursues is almost endearingly old-fashioned. Still, not everybody can hijack a plane; you have to make the most of what you've got. The play centres around the kidnapping of a multinational upper manager by a pair of disaffected workers who propose to shoot him, as a gesture or an example. Or whatever: They aren't very clear themselves.

The play starts out very funny. It takes place in a computer factory in Dunfermline, Fife, and it kicks off with a discussion between Tom, a former student (politics and philosophy) working in security in the hope that something better will turn up, and Eddie, anarchist, autodidact, and -- we later learn -- psychotic. It's a laugh, though rather a snobbish one, to begin the play, bang, with Eddie the punk sounding off in the broadest and most profanely demotic Scots about Jean-Paul Sartre and someone else whose name sounds like "John Jenny" but actually turns out to be Jean Genet. It's even more of a gas because Eddie's contemptuous assessment of Genet's criminal chic and Sartre's glorification of it is right on the button. (He points out that Genet, celebrated for the time he spent in the clink, must therefore have been pretty ineffective at his chosen vocation of thief and concedes that Sartre certainly had a nose for a catchy title. Being and Nothingness: That's going to draw the crowds.)

We meet Gary, an old-style socialist whose grandfather fought in the Spanish Civil War. And with Gary comes, all trussed-up and temporarily unconscious, their chosen victim, Frank. When unwrapped and revived, Frank discomfits them by not being Japanese, and in fact being local, though since removed to the Home Counties. And hereabouts the play stops working properly.

It goes on being funny, if sometimes with excessive winsomeness: Eddie, berated for not bringing balaclavas to the party, protests, "You can only get woolly ones -- I'm allergic to woolly." There is also a fair amount of pungent political comment, though the most pungent comes from the captive Frank, who has been around and seen it all and is disgusted with capital and labour alike.

In fact, Frank -- at least as played, or spat out, by Hardee T. Lineham in an untraceable accent -- is the most menacing figure on stage, which may not have been the intention. Aidan Devine's Gary is like a cartoon shop steward, too sweet and worn down for his sanguinary intentions to be taken seriously.

Shawn Doyle's Eddie is more believably murderous. I called him an anarchist because it's a label that Gary pins on him and that he accepts, but politics don't really concern him except as farouche debating points; he's too anarchic to be an anarchist. Violence is what gives him the real buzz; he says so -- and rather too often, as if his author had run out of other things for him to say. I didn't lose belief in him, but I did lose interest.

Matthew MacFadzean, whose previous line of toughs might have made him obvious casting for Eddie, instead plays Tom, the fall guy, and does him very well.

All round, Jim Millan's production is better at getting laughs than at conjuring danger; it plays, in other words, to the script's strengths and fails to camouflage its weaknesses. When violence does come, it's surprisingly ineffective. Burke writes juicy dialogue, and is probably a coming man, though maybe not as preternatural in his accomplishments as his publicity might suggests. This play was produced in Britain at the National Theatre, and it's being claimed that Burke is the first playwright to have achieved this with a first play. Of course it was in 1967 -- pre-history -- but the National once gave a main-stage premiere to a play called Rosencrantz and Guildenstern Are Dead by an untried writer named Tom Stoppard. And there have been others since, such as Patrick Marber with Dealer's Choice.

In one way, though, Burke moves us on. Earlier left-wing British playwrights, like Edward Bond or Howard Brenton, anticipated this play's kidnap-the-capitalist or pinion-the-politician scenario, and though they usually gave the captive some decent arguments, if only to pass the time, they never left any doubt as to whose side they were on or who would ultimately win. In Burke's world such certainties, maybe even such hopes, are gone. Hence the mockery, hence the fury, hence the despair. The title derives, by the way, from a street name in an actual Scottish mining town. They wanted to name it after a Soviet hero, and the first astronaut (or, as Frank wearily corrects his captors, cosmonaut) was the most famous one to hand.

The Magnetism of the Heart, meanwhile, is the subtitle of the original Fredro play, and a very apposite one.
