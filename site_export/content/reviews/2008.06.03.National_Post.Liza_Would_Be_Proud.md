THEATRE REVIEW

Cabaret

Avon Theatre, Stratford

The curse of Cabaret is that its a musical designed to shock that now has to make its way in a theatrical world inured to shock. Directors have responded by upping the ante, most notoriously in Sam Mendes London and Broadway revival, with its trick ending in a concentration camp, just in case we hadn't got the message that Nazi Germany was horrific. It was undeniably exciting, but talk about overkill. In Amanda Dehnert's Stratford production, the curse is lifted. Its the most powerful version I have seen since Harold Princes 1960s original.

Prince's production worked by seduction. It lulled you into Christopher Isherwood's world of cheap Berlin lodging-houses and seedily glamorous Weimar nightclubs. Then it stunned you with an apparently innocuous folk song that turned into a Nazi anthem, a single stone through a Jewish greengrocers window and the Kit Kat Klubs celebrated Gorilla Song, an insidiously sweet-sounding number whose punchline, she wouldn't look Jewish at all, really was a punch: a sucker punch that woke you up and made you realize how far and how easily you had travelled. (It certainly upset audiences of the time, or at least those unable to tell the difference between anti-Semitism and the dramatic depiction of the same.)

That surprise effect is now gone beyond recall; Dehnert and her choreographer Kelly Devine downplay the number -- it seems for the longest time that there won't be a gorilla in the room at all, and when one does appear you might wish they hadnt bothered. But it doesnt matter. Tomorrow Belongs to Me has had its chilling double effect (far more so than in the Mendes version), and the production has come up with a blow-in-the-face first-act ending worthy of Hal Prince himself in Sweeney Todd mode.

The production has one unprecedented ace up its sleeve in the form of Sean Arbuckle's revelatory Clifford, the American equivalent of the Herr Issyvoo of Isherwood's stories and the play I Am a Camera. (If there is a musical with a more complex and still-developing genealogy than Cabaret, I dont know it.) Instead of the usual onlooking patsy, hes a passionate, ironic and troubled figure who centres the whole show. He is a camera observing, but he also participates. When he inaugurates the final valedictory chorus in which Wilkommen turns into Auf Wiedersehen, it really seems to be his song. He also gets one of his own: A previously unheard, musically adventurous ballad that John Kander and Fred Ebb added to their score for the Mendes production, but that was cut.

Trish Lindstrom's is an unusual Sally Bowles, compelling but not quite convincing, not so much charmingly feckless as bitterly uncaring. She's an androgynous figure who might well attract the bisexual Cliff ("If we could see her through his eyes ...") but is less likely to be saleable to a German club audience as "the toast of Mayfair." But nobody could claim that her cabaret numbers lack edge, much of it turned against herself.

Equally chilling, but less controversially so, are Cory OBriens Nazi organizer and Diana Coatsworth's Nazi sympathizer, both with a surface charm that corrodes. For warmth you go in this production to Frank Moore's frantically self-deluding Herr Schultz, the middle-aged wooer who presses his suit with fruit, and to Nora McLellans Fraulein Schneider, whose two Weillish songs -- the buoyant So What and the desperate What Would You Do -- wear the best of any in the score.

Their doomed romance, like that of Sally and Cliff, is watched from the side by Bruce Dows cabaret host, who plainly never expected else. It was, I think, a bad day for the show when the emcee became accepted as the star part. He's a commentator -- a show-stealing role to be sure -- but an actor can hardly steal a show that he already owns. By current orthodoxy, he controls the whole thing -- there's just too much of him.

Dehnerts production at least avoids trying to make him, and the club he inhabits, symbols of Fascism and victims of it at the same time. In fact, she quite subtly makes the point that gays were destined to be as much victims of the new era as Jews. The cabaret of Cabaret bears little relation to the club depicted in the stories, in which Sally sings American standards, badly. Our idea of Weimar cabaret has become a fantasy about a fantasy to be simultaneously indulged and deplored: a dream of an age in which anything went, especially sexually, and in production of this show its moved, not to the show's benefit, from metaphor to all-devouring reality.

Bruce Dow, mohawk-pated, seems to be having a good time going through the sleazy motions -- we've long gone past mere seediness -- but hes at his best in a straightforward despairing number like I Dont Care Much, which he sings as if he actually does, very much. To be honest, some of the emcee's numbers, especially Two Ladies, are feeble even as written, and the production doesn't do much for them. It's the real-life characters, about to be engulfed in terror, and they make it tremendous. - Cabaret runs until Oct. 24.
