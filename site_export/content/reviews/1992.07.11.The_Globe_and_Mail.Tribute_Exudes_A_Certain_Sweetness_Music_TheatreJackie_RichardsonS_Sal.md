Tribute exudes a certain sweetness MUSIC THEATRE"Jackie Richardson's salute to Manhattan cabaret singer Mabel Mercer at Top O' The Senator does credit to both ladies. Their qualities don't match, but they do touch

Saturday, July 11, 1992

ROBERT CUSHMAN

BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail IN the mid-1970s Mabel Mercer, the doyenne of Manhattan cabaret singers, played an extended season at the Playboy Club in London. Though born and raised in England, she had not set foot there in decades, but she refused to play the sentimental homecomer. I interviewed her for a BBC-TV special called Miss Mercer in Mayfair, and at one point suggested, with possibly exaggerated tact, that during the sixties she had gone into semi- retirement. "Nonsense," she said with a dismissive guffaw, "I was out of work."

Mercer's earthiness was one of her essential qualities, a counterweight to the image of the supersensitive interpreter of hothouse love lyrics, singing of heartbreak at daybreak. She came out of the music hall.

Jackie Richardson, who plays Mercer in Mabel!, a tribute directed by Richard Ouzounian at the Top O' The Senator, has her own earthiness, but it comes out of jazz and gospel.

Mercer had great rhythm but she didn't swing. Local hacks have been describing her as a jazz singer (even, God help us, a blues singer), but Richardson knows better and, being a good actress, subdues her own style to that of the lady she is impersonating. But here lies the nagging problem with a very pleasant show, as with all others of its kind: Is she impersonating Mabel or isn't she?

The answer, as usual, is that sometimes she does, sometimes she doesn't. The show is nominally set in the seventies, by which time Mercer, also in her 70s, was largely talking her songs. Sometimes Richardson does this too: "Jenny Rebecca, five days old," she exclaims in a wondering parlando that exactly matches Mercer's own. At others, having more voice and a different background, she strikes out on her own. But she cannot simply offer her own interpretations of songs that somebody else just happens to have sung before. For one thing, the show's format is modestly biographical, and for another Mercer's repertoire was very specialized. Drop in on her nearest male equivalent, Bobby Short, and you will hear a representative cross-section of the great American songbook. Mercer, however, chose only songs that reflected her own sedate, maternal, worldly-wise persona: a performing persona that related to but did not duplicate her off-stage self. In Funny Girl, Barbra Streisand created a fictitious character and called her Fanny Brice; but then she didn't sing any of Brice's songs. Richardson doesn't have that option.

The first half shows Mabel rehearsing, running through songs and reminiscing about her life to her accompanist Jimmy Lyon. Norman Amadio, who plays both the man and the piano, is nothing like the dapper Lyon but is very nice anyway, gravelly and self-effacing. Sometimes the patter is ingenious, sometimes it's painful as if taken from a book. ("It reminded me of Wales where I had spent my summers as a child." Nobody talks like that.) The relation of songs to life is most effective when most oblique. Being Green works surprisingly well as a song about being black, and Send in the Clowns unexpectedly illuminates Mabel's reunion with a neglectful mother. But The Last Time I Saw Paris is an obvious choice that's merely obvious, as is If Love Were All, Noel Coward's threnody about having a talent to amuse. I once asked Mercer if she identified with that song, and she pooh-poohed the idea, saying it was her job to sing to the audience's emotions, not her own. She always said that. She says it in this show. But the show doesn't believe her.

Act II is Mercer in performance. Richardson sits in a straight-backed chair, as Mabel did, and raises her hands eloquently to Heaven, as Mabel did. She also indulges in coy banter with the audience, which Mabel never did. And she sings, increasingly her own woman. She does a sublime Lazy Afternoon, a song Mercer featured but didn't own; Amadio, shedding cabaret conventions, supports her with some beautifully spare chording. On Isn't He Adorable, a Mercer specialty, she outshines the original. Occasionally, though, she sounds provincial rather than urbane; and Alec Wilder's enigmatic Did You Ever Cross Over to Sneeden's, Mercer's high-point as a tragedienne, eludes her.

Anyone approaching the show without my niggling preconceptions will find good songs well sung. More than that, the evening exudes a certain sweetness, which does credit to both ladies. Their qualities don't match, but they do touch.
