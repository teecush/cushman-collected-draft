The Canopy Theatre Company's Lysistrata - The Sex Strike (their subtitle) is the most satisfying production of an Aristophanes play I have ever seen. That isn't quite the sweeping statement it may sound. I don't have many Aristophanic experiences to choose from since, despite his textbook status as the father of comedy, his plays aren't done very often, certainly not in English. And there's a very good reason for that, which is that they don't usually work. That doesn't mean that his reputation is undeserved or that some of his jokes don't continue to resound down the millennia. It means that his and his contemporaries' conception of what makes a comedy is completely different from ours, in a way that certainly isn't true of Greek tragedy. By our definitions his plays are hardly plays at all; they're more like topical revues. And it isn't the topicality that does them in; the themes most of them deal with - in Lysistrata's case, war and sex - are always topical, because they're eternal. It's the form. Aristophanes' plays are largely indifferent to plot and character, the things that for us are the motors of any kind of comedy lasting more than 10 minutes. Situation comedy only came in with his Greek successors and their Roman successors. And it's been around ever since.

Aristophanes wrote comedies of ideas (like Shaw's, but lower on human interest) and the one that kicks off Lysistrata is a corker that has become a Greek myth in its own right, thereby earning its author a place at the very top of the class. The women of Athens, led by the title character, swear off sex with their men until the men put an end to the war with Sparta that is tearing both cities, and the whole of Greece, apart. (And eventually did.) They persuade the women of Sparta to do the same, though how the ladies of both towns were able to meet up for their conference is one of those pesky naturalistic details Aristophanes preferred to not to get into. The idea is a great one, like Christianity and western civilization, and though the actual women of Athens never put it into practice, the Canopy Company's program includes a page of instances in which similar plans have worked in other situations. It's a great dramatic idea, too. The problem is, once you've stated it, what do you do with it? For much of its length, Lysistrata does surprisingly little.

It does advance alternate, or maybe parallel, strategies. As do the women themselves. With remarkable ease, they take over Athens' public buildings, Acropolis included. Token explanation: all the able-bodied men are away fighting, leaving only some senile governors and a few easily intimidated thugs. And it's in the political confrontation of complacent men and angry, intelligent women that this adaptation comes on strongest. It's actually an adaptation of an adaptation: A writer called Phil Wilmott has worked over a version that Britain's National Theatre commissioned in the 1970s from no less than Germaine Greer, then in the full flush of The Female Eunuch. It must have seemed like a slam-dunk combination, though, for whatever reason, the National never produced it. But, whoever did what, the current version is excellent, very stylish and speakable and playable, preserving the original structure but making it more coherent while decorating it in ways that are contemporary but still thoroughly in keeping. (The National may have expected a more rampantly feminist interpretation, but as a literature teacher Greer has always, from what I know of her, been insistent on fidelity.)

The scene here is a bath house, cheekily adorned with a sign reading Male Members Only. As the theme of sexual frustration is advanced, those members will become increasingly prominent, so to speak. For the moment, though, Lysistrata and her cohorts say nothing about keeping out of men's beds. They scorchingly attack the male establishment for its conduct of the war, and its patronizing treatment of women in general; and they score all around the clock. The aged senators, furious at having the holiest of masculine sanctums invaded and clad only in towels, keep condemning themselves out of their own mouths. They are further humiliated by a chorus of cleaning women who, in a touch of class consciousness not so evident in the original, put brooms and mops into their hands and tell them to get on with it.

The sex strike only appears in earnest later on, though not before some attempted backsliding from the ladies, who are getting horny themselves. "Women!" complains their exasperated leader in a line sadly omitted in this version, "it's no wonder they write tragedies about us." But she rallies the troops, and there's a set-piece scene in which one young wife teases her husband, home on leave, to the point of desperation and beyond. From here it's a short skip to a summit conference of Greek leaders, each more protuberant than the last, and the joyful outbreak of peace.

The company, an offshoot of Hart House, performs in its own little amphitheatre on Philosopher's Walk: a perfect-sounding location for Greek drama, though most summers they do Shakespeare. Andrea Wasserman's production is lively and excellently pointed, with a few rough edges. In a cast that's at most semiprofessional the best, or at least the most contained, performances come ironically from men: from Thomas Gough as the most intransigent of the senators and from Coner Hefferon as the most conciliatory. Among the women, including Claire Acott in the title role, there's more enthusiasm than finesse. Music is confined to recordings of '50s rock 'n' roll hits, which is probably a wise decision. The ending, after all that foreplay, is something of an anticlimax - unavoidably, given the nature of the play. But we can at least applaud the feeling behind it, and wish that Aristophanes' dream had actually come to pass.
