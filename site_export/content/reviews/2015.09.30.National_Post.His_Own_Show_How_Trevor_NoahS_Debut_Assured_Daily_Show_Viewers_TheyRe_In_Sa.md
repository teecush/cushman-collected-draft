Judging by his 10-minute spot as bizarro host on The Colbert Report, carried off with aplomb and excellent comic timing, Barack Obama would have made a strong replacement for Jon Stewart on The Daily Show. Though the show would have had to go on hiatus until he was through with his current gig. But it was Trevor Noah who got the job, making his debut Monday night.

Like Obama, Noah projects charm and intelligence. Also like Obama, Noah - the South African son of a black mother and a white father - is of mixed race. He had a very funny bit about this on a TV special, remarking that Obama's similarly complicated heritage was held against him during his candidacy, but that when he won the presidency people began falling over themselves to acclaim him as "our black." Though "black" wasn't actually the word Noah used.

His first night as host of The Daily Show had two themes: his blackness and his newness. The production had fun with our fears and preconceptions. Would it be the same? Would it be different? It turned out to be both. There was the same-old voice-over opening: the date, Comedy Central, The Daily Show ... only the name at the end had changed, for obvious reasons. The font for the titles had changed, too, and so had the desk, but there still was a desk.

Noah's style emerged as cheeky self-deprecation. He told us how thrilled he was to have realized two of his wildest childhood dreams; the host position on a late-night talk show, and an indoor toilet. He paid the obligatory tribute to his predecessor, but far less fulsomely than is usual on such occasions: "Jon might find he has a new stepson and he's black, which is not ideal."

He admitted that the producers had tried to find a woman or an American for the position, but they had been turned down, "so once again a job Americans rejected is being done by an immigrant." He then proceeded, in traditional Daily Show style, to address a couple of news stories: the Pope's arrival and John Boehner's departure. The clips of both elicited something strongly resembling Stewart's patented reaction of coy disbelief.

The Boehner resignation provided the stronger material, though Marco Rubio's announcement of it, to thunderous applause from Florida Republicans, really made any satiric comment superfluous. Correspondent Jordan Klepper rattled the host with the news that people were saying "John, please come back." This was funny, but better yet was an item about the discovery of water on Mars, bringing a face even newer, and considerably darker, than the host's: Roy Wood Jr., Senior Mars Correspondent. Will people like us, Noah asked him, soon be going to Mars? "Black people can't catch a cab" was the brusque reply, "how are they going to Mars?" Wood could be the black Lewis Black.

The show's last segment, an interview with "rock-star comedian" Kevin Hart, was the weakest. But showbiz promotional chats were always the worst parts of Stewart's shows too - they felt like filler - while his political or literary interviews were among the best. They were actual inquiries and they had meat. We don't know yet whether Noah has the authority to pull this off, or whether he will be able to move us as Stewart increasingly did when he got angry and voiced it to camera - something that depended on the trust he had built up over the years.

Last Saturday, Noah was able to quickly earn that level of trust from an audience in Toronto, headlining the Just for Laughs festival at the Sony Centre. Considering he was only two days away from his premiere, one could easily deduce he has nerves of steel. Or maybe it was good therapy. Anyway, he was brilliant.

Stand-up comedy too often signals a jerky succession of one-liners. Noah took a topic, explored it in loving detail, before moving seamlessly on to the next.

He offered a meditation on the probability of Idris Elba being the next James Bond, and on the unlikelihood of a black Bond being able to carry out a secret agent's duty of blending unobtrusively into the background, especially in Scotland, a country from which, Noah informed us, he had just returned and in which, according to him, blackness is unknown. That's unlikely to be true, but nobody could quarrel with his vocal transformation from light South African to heavy Scottish. It was hilariously perfect, as was each of the many accents that followed it. He has a flawless ear and a twisting tongue. He plainly loves language.

Race obviously figures heavily in Noah's act, but he isn't heavy about it: just disarmingly sharp. He told us that he had to move from South Africa to America to discover that he was black and that, having become a connoisseur of racism, he thinks he prefers the overt to the subtle kind. He illustrated his points with impeccable mimicry of incomprehensible brothers and of hearty or genteel Lexington, Kentuckians.

Noah's virtuosity with accents is obviously going to come in handy - it already has - and his traditional comedic skills are greater than Stewart's. I've never seen Stewart do stand-up but I can't imagine him holding the Sony Centre in the palm of his hand for an hour or more. Unlike the comparably gifted Stephen Colbert, currently hamstrung by weak scripts and an unyielding, antiquated format, Noah has landed in an environment that suits him and that he should be able to tweak.

It looks as though The Daily Show is in safe hands.
