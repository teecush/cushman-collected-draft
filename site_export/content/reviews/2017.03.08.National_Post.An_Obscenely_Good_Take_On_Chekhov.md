THEATRE REVIEW

STUPID F-ING BIRD Pop-Up Toronto Theatre, 270 King Street West

Aaron Posner's Stupid F- ing Bird is described by its American author as sort of adapted from The Seagull. Id say it was sort of very closely adapted. The time has been changed from the 1890s to now, and the place from Russia to the United States. Two characters have gone missing and two others have been combined into one, but the story and the personal dynamics remain the same.

However, in addition to re-tracing Chekhovs narrative, Posner's piece offers a running commentary on it. His play has been accused of wanting to have its blini and eat it - which it does. What's important, though, is that it succeeds at both.

Fate has been on the Toronto productions side. In the first act of The Seagull, young Konstantin invites his friends and relations into his actress-mothers back yard to see an experimental play he has written. Conrad, the equivalent character here, does the same thing though he calls his piece "a site-specific performance event." Here, fortuitously and fortunately, he's part of a production that is itself sitespecific.

It was originally planned for the Storefront Theatre, where it would have been given a conventionally stationary setting. Events forced a move to a new venue, a spacious building right next door to the Royal Alex, in which the action moves from one room to another. The audience, pushing its own chairs, moves with it. An extra layer of selfreference is added to a show that's already loaded with it. This kind of thing can easily be irritating, but Stupid F-ing Bird gives self-consciousness a good name.

About that name: it seems intended to take Chekhovs play and its central symbol down a peg, and up to a point it does. When the lovelorn Masha, here called Mash (shes a chef), is asked the famous opening question about why she always wears black, she replies not that she's in mourning for her life, but, sarcastically, that black is slimming. She's still a mourner, though. She even sings a song about how awful everything is, a rather good one. We seem to be in for a Chekhov burlesque, but that joke wouldnt last a whole evening, and thankfully it isnt asked to. Or if it is, it gets more complex.

The profanity of the title extends, frequently, into the dialogue. Maybe it's overdone, but it does provide a window into how the generally tormented characters really feel, or how they would like to express themselves if gentility, social and theatrical, didn't get in the way. I wouldnt want to swap it full-time for Chekhovian subtlety, but as a one-off its a release, for the characters and for the audience.

As with words, so with sex. The play's an emotional daisy-chain. Mash is loved by the ineffectual Dev (formerly Medvedenko) while she pines for Conrad who adores Nina, the solo actress in his play. She seems to love him back until she meets Doyle Trigorin (not, I think, a happy combination of names), successful author and accredited lover of Conrad's mom. Trigorin seduces her, or maybe its the other way around. We see here how it happens.

Chekhovs Konstantin talks about finding new theatrical forms. Posner's Conrad does the same, and puts his talent where his mouth is by stepping out of character, or at least into the audience, and asking our opinion on the best way of getting Nina back. He listens too, though I can't say that on the first night we were very helpful.

Also like his predecessor, Conrad inveighs against the current state of the drama, everything from the bread of current Shakespeare productions to circuses. He wants theatre to matter, to change peoples lives. He invokes the standard jargon theatre people use to make themselves feel important. The best, most serious joke of all is that all this fourth-wall breaking in no way punctures the dramatic illusion. Were as involved with the characters as ever we were. So much for theories. When in the last act, Conrad, still following in Konstantin's footsteps, renounces his previous ideas, it means far more than it usually does because we've been on the journey with him.

Daniel Maslany plays Conrad. He has a background in improv which obviously helps with the audience-participation bits. He also presents an unsparing but not unsympathetic portrait of a young man who suffers even while being insufferable. But then all the performances are splendid. Vinetta Strombergs direction has everybody in the right space, physically and emotionally. Posners dialogue swings and so does his storytelling, even if the story is mostly somebody else's, and the actors feast on both.

Nina's trajectory, from ingenue to sexual awakening to apparent madness, is made piercingly clear by Karen Knox. In this version she gets to tell her own back-story rather than having it recited by somebody else. Her scenes with Craig Lauzons Trigorin, a magnetic shrug of a man, are electric. Most of the actors here are new to me; Sarah Orenstein and Richard Greenblatt, the two who arent, have never been better. As Emma Arkadina (another awkward combo of a name), famous actress and occasional mother, Orenstein benefits more than anyone from the modern setting. Traditional Arkadinas can be glamorously fuzzy but this star of three media, hanging on furiously to son and lover and career, is instantly and unsentimentally recognizable. Greenblatt plays the two-in-one role of Eugene Sorn, doctor and Emma's brother, also occasional narrator and master of ceremonies. He has the play's key speech, about how hard it is to be sure that we are really feeling anything. It isnt in Chekhov but it may be closer to Chekhov than anything else in the evening.

Rachel Cairns rages wittily and mopes powerfully as Mash. Brendan Hobin is delightful as Dev, the character who has strayed furthest from the original. His eventual marriage doesnt seem at all miserable, and he has a new identity as Conrad's best buddy and confidant, both inside and outside the action. In The Seagull the two have no relationship whatever.

I haven't mentioned the seagull itself. It does appear, but shrouded in a carrier bag, an image or lack of one that seems to reinforce the dismissive title. Posner's play is less an adaptation of Chekhov than a parallel text, "parallel lines who meet" as Stephen Sondheim wrote in Company, geometrically impossible but theatrically illuminating, in both directions.

This Toronto season has brought us John, Father Comes Home from the Wars, The Realistic Joneses and now this. American playwriting is back on a roll.
