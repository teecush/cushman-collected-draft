THEATRE REVIEW

JERSEY BOYS

Toronto Centre for the Arts

---

The truth about Jersey Boys is that it's a well-staged pop concert with a couple of good dramatic scenes. Make that one good dramatic scene and an epiphany. The latter occurs when the original Four Seasons, whose biography the show is, are reunited, years after having split up, at their induction into the Rock 'n' Roll Hall of Fame. They do a number, and in the middle of it each steps forward to tell us, with economy and dignity and even wit, what's been happening to them in the meantime.

The scene is the one that shows how it had all fallen apart. Tommy DeVito, the New Jersey kid with the talents of a musician and the instincts of a fixer, is now deep in debt to the mob and angry that his teammates don't give him enough credit for bringing -- and keeping -- the group together. (He exaggerates, but he has a point.) He's banished to Las Vegas. Nick Massi, the self-effacing bass player and vocal arranger, also takes his departure, on the grounds that he's tired of being one of four guys, with him as Ringo. (It might have been worse; he could have been George.) That leaves Bob Gaudio, the resident songwriter, and Frankie Valli, the lead singer whose stratospheric falsetto was the Seasons' signature.

It isn't just the group that falls apart at this point; it's the show, at least if you're judging it as any kind of drama. The four-way tensions that have sustained the piece melt away, and we start marking time in biography-land where stuff happens because stuff happened. There's a long sequence in which we hear about Gaudio trying to sell an "art song" that, from the buildup it's given, might be All the Things You Are and The Man I Love and the soliloquy from Carousel rolled into one, but that only turns out to be Can't Take My Eyes Off You. Jersey Boys is the most ambitious of jukebox musicals, and it may well be the best -- Ain't Misbehavin' was something different; a jukebox revue -- but it's still a jukebox musical. It isn't stupid like Mamma Mia!, but the numbers and the story bear no relation to one another, beyond the fact that the former was created by the people in the latter. There are a few half-hearted attempts to make the songs reflect character, but the lyrics are too generic for that to work. These are great pop songs, the early ones especially, but there's no way they're theatrical.

Well, they are in one sense: They foster a sense of occasion. The audience screams here when a song begins, because they recognize it and because they admire the actors for making it sound like the original. Once that nostalgic connection has been made, the songs fade into one another. Sergio Trujillo, the choreographer, has given them some stylish moves, probably better than the Seasons' own, but how many ways are there to stage four guys singing? And if I can indulge my own nostalgia: I was a teenager when those records came out, and what me and my mates loved about Sherry and Big Girls Don't Cry and (my own fave) Walk Like a Man, is that they were funny. Those bass voices solemnly confirming Valli's high-pitched exhortations sounded wonderfully absurd, we assumed on purpose. But maybe Americans hear it differently. It certainly doesn't happen here, where the contrasts are less extreme.

Which is not to take away from the four leads who are amazing, and who can also act. (They have a great sound system. You can tell who is singing what.) Each gets to tell a quarter of the story -- the show is divided into spring, summer, and you get the idea-- so there are hints of complexity, which the performers exploit. Andrew Rannells is open-faced and smart as Bob, Steve Gouveia pleasantly astringent as Nick. Jeremy Kushnier has Tommy's wise-guy desperation down perfectly; he gets, appropriately, to start the story off and he can never let go of the feeling that he owns it. Frankie himself, oddly enough, is the least distinctive as a character; the book (by Marshall Brickman and Rick Ellice) is at its best when setting up the boys and the Jersey background, but most of its jokes fall with a thud, its music-business scenes are Hollywood cliche, and it makes emotional demands regarding Frankie's wife, daughter and girlfriend that it never begins to justify. These domestic scenes are as bad as writing gets, and Joseph Leo Bwarie as Frankie has to bear the brunt of them. But he copes, and his vocals duly tingle the spine. The most thrilling musical moment in the show isn't a Seasons song at all; it's the young Frankie at a club segueing from I'm in the Mood for Love into its vocalese paraphrase ("there I go, there I go, there I go ...") You hear a star being born.

Nobody else makes an impression, except perhaps Jonathan Hadley as Bob Crewe, the swishy record exec. And thereby hangs a tale, or should. Crewe gets program credit for writing the lyrics, though according to the show itself Gaudio wrote both words and music for all the Seasons' songs. So was this another case of an industry guy cutting himself in? And wouldn't there at some point have been trouble about this? Either the show doesn't want to go there, or it hasn't been allowed to.

Des McAnuff's staging is fast, tight and tidy; it starts with a bang, before you can even catch your breath, and it keeps the coups coming, most notably the entry of a full brass section, strolling across a catwalk, instruments gleaming, in the middle of a number: the perfect visual correlative for what happens on the record. (Gaudio uses the phrase "objective correlative" in one scene; it reminds you that Brickman co-wrote Woody Allen movies.) Klara Zieglerova's set is unlovely -- this is Jersey -- but serviceable; though the Pop Art projections employed to lighten it up don't have much to do with anything. I persist in my eccentric belief that there's something unsatisfying in spending a whole evening watching talented performers expending most of their energies on trying to sound like other talented performers. I had fun, but now I'd like to see a musical. - Jersey Boys runs until Nov. 9. Call 416-872-01111 or visit dancaptickets.com for information.
