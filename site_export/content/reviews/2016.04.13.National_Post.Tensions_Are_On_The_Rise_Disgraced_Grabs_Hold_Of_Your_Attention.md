A Manhattan couple has invited another Manhattan couple over for food and chat, and the hostess brightly announces that she has bought pork tenderloin for dinner. This might seem tactless, since her husband is a Muslim, at least by heritage, and her male guest is Jewish, but it turns out that neither of them has any objections. The Jewish guest indeed admits to a ravenous appetite for pig meat; he's making up for lost time.

Disgraced, a Pulitzer-winning first play by an American Muslim writer, Ayad Akhtar, is about a group of people trying to shake off traditions and stereotypes, and finding that it isnt that simple. At its centre is Amir Kapoor, a hard-driving corporate lawyer of Pakistani descent. Hes hoping for a partnership in the Jewishowned firm that employs him, and from the sound of things, fully deserves one.

His bosses apparently think he's Indian, hence Hindu, and one of them has made him a present of a statue of Shiva which now stands proudly in a corner of the desirable apartment where Amir lives with his WASP wife Emily. She's an artist who has found inspiration in ancient Islamic culture: ironic, since her husband is scathing about Islam and has done everything possible to distance himself from it. Emilys work has attracted the attention of an art-dealer named Isaac. Hes the Jewish man who's come to dinner, accompanied by his wife Jory, who is black and who also happens to be a colleague of Amir's.

Amir is tense when the guests arrive. At the urging of his nephew, who was born Hussein but goes by the name of Abe, he has reluctantly appeared as a character witness for an imam accused of advocating terrorism. The newspapers have exaggerated his support, and hes now in trouble at work. When conversation turns to 9/11 and then to the Middle East, Amir and Isaac, despite their avowed secularism, get nationalistically heated, Amir especially. Masks slip, the more painfully because neither man suspected he was wearing a mask.

The characters are, rather obviously, representative figures assembled as counters in an argument. An unavoidable recent comparison is Yasmina Rezas God of Carnage, another play about an exploding dinner-party. Akhtar's play is shorter on psychological detail, but has a greater impact because its issues are more substantial. In fact they're blazingly urgent.

If anything, time has made them crueller. The play, which premiered in 2013, references al-Qaida and the Taliban but not ISIL. It recalls Bruce Norris Clybourne Park in the way it booby-traps its characters and their beliefs, but without its wit and surprise, or its sophisticated structure. Though, to be fair, that plays racial tensions were strictly black and white, while Disgraced covers nearly as many ethnic waterfronts as it has characters. It also throws in an adultery angle which is completely gratuitous, except that it provides a short-cut to an outbreak of physical violence. This, if you can stand another comparison, resembles the climax of David Mamet's Oleanna, but it's less effective because it doesnt feel inevitable, and we arent as invested in the people.

For all that, Disgraced doesn't just hold attention, it grabs it. Amir may not be the most fully realized person you will ever see on a stage, but his predicament is real, and forcefully presented. How much of him is conviction and how much accommodation? How much principle and how much spite? Raoul Bhaneja plays him unsparingly well. Michael Rubenfeld's Isaac, situated somewhere between amiability and creepiness, is an admirable sparring partner. The womens roles are thinner, but Birgitte Solem and Karen Glave mine what there is. And then theres Abe, vividly played by Ali Momen, who reappears at the end, having reverted to Hussein. His path doesn't so much counter his uncle's as complement it: hes on the way to being radicalized by being marginalized. This is the most straightforward scene in the play, and the most powerful. Robert Ross Parker directs a taut production on a set, by Sue LePage, of streamlined plush.

From a newish American play to an oldish British one, revived as a vehicle for Rupert Everett, and passing through on its way to New York. The play is David Hares The Judas Kiss, which presents two moments in the life of Oscar Wilde.

In the first he's in his favourite London hotel in 1895, immediately following the first of his three trials. His friends, or some of them, urge him to escape to France before the police come for him. We know he won't, but there is a paradoxical kind of suspense in waiting for something not to happen. Wilde may be acting, or failing to act, from lassitude or self-destructiveness or loyalty to his lover Lord Alfred Douglas, aka Bosie, who wants him to stay and face his accusers down.

This first act had me wondering what all the extravagant fuss over Everetts Oscar was about. Hes much better in the second which shows us Wilde after his prison term, broken in health but not in spirit, living with Bosie in serious poverty in Naples. He spends most of this act sitting down in one place; when he gets up it's a legitimate major moment. Everett here projects a mournful perceptiveness that turns acid when, at very long last, he realizes that Lord Alfred, who had indeed kissed him in London immediately before deserting him, is his Judas.

The trouble with this is that Bosie is as unspeakable a person here as he apparently was in real life. He even claims that he, not Oscar, was the real victim: he may have been spared Reading Gaol but he suffered guilt by association, was disowned by his family, and was denied his comforts as an aristocrat and his due as a poet. History suggests that, on the last count at least, he was fooling himself.

He also, under his friend's eyes, cavorts with a beautiful fisherman, and goes out drinking and dancing with him till all hours. Love may be blind, but Wildes myopia here strains belief, or at least patience. If, as the title suggests, the play is about Oscar and Bosie, then what is the rest of it doing?

Scenes with Robbie Ross, Wildes first male lover and more-or-less faithful friend, may be intended as a counterweight, but they're either too much or not enough. If the play is intended as a portrait of Wilde, it leaves out too much, notably its subject's fabled wit. Hares dialogue is pleasingly true to period, but I only laughed once. I was touched, though, by Oscars interactions with the hotel staff: loyal and admiring, and intrigued by the hints of their off-stage lives. Supporting performances are competent; Neil Armfields's production handles timeshifts imaginatively. Both these plays have been brought to Toronto by David Mirvish, and it strikes me that Disgraced might serve equally well as a title for The Judas Kiss. Indeed, for its own play, it seems rather vague. But vaguely menacing titles, plus timeliness, may be the recipe for pulling in Pulitzers.

Disgraced runs through April 24; The Judas Kiss through May 1.
