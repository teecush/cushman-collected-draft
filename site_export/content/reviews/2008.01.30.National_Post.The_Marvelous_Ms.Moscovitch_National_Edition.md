THEATRE REVIEW

THE RUSSIAN PLAY/ESSAY

The Factory Theatre, Toronto

---

Hannah Moscovitch's Essay and The Russian Play were first seen, in that order, at two successive Summerworks festivals. They obviously deserved further exposure, and having suggested in these pages that they should be revived as a double bill, I feel like basking in the results. It's a fact, though nobody seems to be making a fuss about it, that all the new plays to have opened on major Toronto stages in the past few weeks are by women. These two are the best of them. Both combine a biting wit with an unsentimental humanity. Both show a priceless quality to shift an argument, or an emotional state, on a dime.

Both plays have casts of one woman and two men. Comparing them, Ken Gass, the Factory's artistic director, asks us to query how much the "position of women in society ... has changed from Stalinist Russia to the halls of contemporary academe." Given that Pixie, the young lady in Essay, bursts into tears in her teacher's office and is offered Kleenex, while Sonya in The Russian Play is starved and tortured by the Soviet secret police for having displeased her influential lover, I'd say we've come a long way, baby. Essay is a campus play, set in a history department. Jeffrey, a teaching assistant, is querying Pixie, an undergraduate, on her choice of essay topic; asked to write on a military leader from 18th or 19th century Europe, Pixie wants to write on the obscure figure (well, I'd never heard of her) of Elisabeth Farnese, wife of Philip V of Spain, rather than on Napoleon like everybody else. Jeffrey suggests that she is letting her feminism-- of which he, as an enlightened young man, approves -- get in the way of her historical perspective. She rather dashes his preconceptions by saying that she isn't a feminist, though she later says, maybe for the sake of a quiet life, that, all right, she is. Otherwise, she sticks to her guns, and at the suggestion of the departmental head, a Professor Galbraith, ends up writing on Elisabeth and Napoleon.

The essay turns out badly. So Jeffrey thinks, and the professor seems to agree with him. He still advises -- again for the sake of a quiet life -- giving her a passing grade; after all, she's just one student, probably passing through on her way to what he, with cankered joviality, calls "women's studiesology"; and anyway, what can you expect of a girl named Pixie? He also suggests, with reference to the (I agree, appalling) concept of "herstory," that it might be possible to write about the historical significance of Napoleon's horse, and call the result "horsestory." Jeffrey is both incredulous and incensed by all this, and he calls on Pixie to rebut it and stand up for her principles. When she stays silent, he accuses her of behaving "like a lobotomy in a skirt," which is when she cries. At this moment, as at the climax of David Mamet's Oleanna, to which this play owes an acknowledged debt, we witness a teaching career imploding before our eyes: less violently than in Mamet, and less irretrievably, but still making the audience catch its breath and then review its reactions.

The play is really about Jeffrey whose political correctness happens to coincide with high academic and intellectual standards and a fatal lack of professional smarts. Jordan Pettle hits him off perfectly, as a study in impetuosity constantly reining itself in too late. (Like all Moscovitch's protagonists, he falls into traps that he really had no hope of avoiding.) He, like his two colleagues, is repeating his performance from Michael Rubenfeld's admirable original production. Richard Greenblatt is the professor who can never forgive Jeffrey for missing a conference at which he himself was a keynote speaker, but who seems genuinely pained by his juniors' missteps; he can behave decently for dubious reasons. He's also the vehicle for some hilariously barbed satire on campus politics: Why, he asks, should the cultural studies brigade act as if they want to take over his department ("they already have their own") and why is the sociology building so much nicer than his? With the boys fighting it out like this, Pixie wisely takes refuge in silence; or maybe she really doesn't have anything to say. Claire Jenkins' performance leaves the question appropriately open; what she does convey beyond question is a refusal to fit into anybody's ideological preconceptions. Like the play itself.

The Russian Play is a scintillating exercise in style (owing more to Chekhov's short stories than his plays, whatever the script says) which also manages to be a moving meditation on poverty, oppression and love. I have now seen Michelle Monteith's performance as Sonya three times, and I can't say that it has improved; it was perfect the first time and it still is, impossibly mature, both technically and emotionally, in its instant switches from sardonic narrator to trusting heroine and back again. That the two women are in fact one is only revealed at the end, though I can't see any point in keeping that secret. As before, Monteith gets strong support from Shawn Campbell and Aaron Willis as the two (married) men in her life, which is more than you can say of Sonya herself; one, a poor gravedigger, breaks her heart, the other, a rich factory-owner, breaks her body. Also as before, and also invaluable in their contributions to matters of atmosphere and tension, are Tom Howell's violin-playing and Kimberly Purtell's cruelly acute lighting. The play's scale, and its key, may be minor but the overall achievement, under Natasha Mytnowych's direction, is major. - The Russian Play/ Essay runs until Feb. 17.
