Ten years is a long time in the life of any theatre, and in the Toronto commercial theatre it's unprecedented.

The Phantom of the Opera has run even longer in London and New York than it has in Toronto, and it was a proven phenomenon in both places before it came here. So Garth Drabinsky's acumen in bidding for the Toronto rights is not especially remarkable; everybody must have been after them.

What is extraordinary, given that he was a virtual novice in live theatre at the time, is the ferocious pertinacity that it must have taken to drive the deal through. The show became of course the foundation stone of the Livent empire, the cash-cow that made it possible to raise money for other shows and other theatres.

With Livent's present existence cloudy and its future doubtful, it's salutary to remember that Phantom made possible a decade of theatre that was certainly uneven but whose peaks were like nothing the city had seen. There has been a lot of crowing in the press over Livent's misfortunes (and it seems to have been directed indiscriminately at both past and present regimes, depending on who provided the tastier target at a given moment) but I don't know anybody in the theatre who doesn't recognize that, with Livent's fall, a degree of flash and brilliance has gone out of Toronto and out of Canada.

When I worked for Livent, which was roughly during the era of Ragtime and Fosse and Parade, it was possible to forget for long periods that Phantom existed. This was ungrateful but human: There was other, and fresher, fish to fry. The media forgot it too, except when prodded into brief awareness by yet another anniversary.

But the public at large, I think, have always felt differently; for them Livent was in the business of megamusicals, and megamusicals primarily meant Phantom. And there is a good reason for this, though I may have to swallow hard before writing it: Phantom is a good show.

It's good musically: Music of the Night and -- especially -- All I Ask of You are the best tunes Andrew Lloyd Webber has written in his lush style; and if the rest is overblown it can be defended as operatic pastiche appropriate to the subject. It isn't good verbally -- the lyrics are characterless, in every sense, and there is virtually no book, but then people have never gone to operetta for the words. Dramatically, it isn't much either; it takes a good story (one assumes it's good, because it has been recycled so often) and hardly bothers to tell it. What it's really about is atmosphere -- lush, spooky, and melancholy. It's everybody's idea of a romantic musical, given spectacular flesh.

This is partly Webber's achievement, and very largely that of Hal Prince and his design team. As pure form, irrespective of content, it may be Mr. Prince's masterpiece of staging. I've never failed to gasp at the explosion of colour in the Masquerade number that opens the second act. (I once heard that song in concert -- just sung, no visuals -- and knew the meaning of disillusion.) It's a show devoted to raising goosebumps; the setting and music do much of the work, the heroine's threatened innocence provides a mild sexual charge, and the hero's self-pity does the rest: everybody loves an unhappy monster. With the possible exception of Les Miserables (it would have been interesting to see how that would have done in Toronto in an open-ended run; somehow I don't think it would have done as well) no other modern musical had tapped so deeply into the public's most sentimental impulses.

"Perhaps it was the chandeliers." That was Alan Jay Lerner, lyricist and librettist, musing whimsically on the success of his own earlier, similarly successful, and frankly much better romantic musical My Fair Lady. (Coincidental historical footnote: Mr. Webber originally wanted Mr. Lerner as his collaborator on Phantom. What a career-capper that would have been.) A lot of people, less whimsically, have attributed Phantom's success to its falling chandelier. I don't think so; it's a passing, though pleasing, moment.

The London producers were smart to choose the Phantom's mask, not the chandelier, for the show's logo. And if we have to choose among lighting fixtures, I'd go for the shimmering candles burning around the boat in the subterranean scenes. They really stand a second look.

So it became part of the landscape, backed up by shrewd and energetic marketing and educational campaigns -- which somehow never worked the same wonders for some of Livent's more ambitious and indeed more distinguished shows -- and by the best radio ads anyone had ever heard. It went some way towards brightening a dingy section of the downtown core, and must have perked up the local economy no end. (I wonder what will happen to the delicious Phantom dessert they've been serving over the road at the Senator for the last decade.)

And of course it wasn't just the show; it was the theatre. However Toronto ranks in the hierarchy of North American cities as regards theatre production, it is certainly the capital for theatre building and theatre restoration. The Pantages is a jewel restored, and the perfect setting for the show. (You could stage the Masquerade scene on the foyer staircase.) But one would still love to see other productions there.

Whatever happens -- or whoever happens -- to the Phantom and to Livent, the Pantages has to have a future. It would be tragic if the Pantages post-Phantom became just another ghost story.
