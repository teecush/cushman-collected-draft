When Harold Pinters Betrayal was first staged in London in 1978, the critics received it disdainfully. Since then, words have been feverishly eaten, and the play has become one of its authors most- admired and most-revived.

The new Soulpepper Theatre Company production, which launches the National Arts Centre's Studio Stage Series tomorrow, takes it a step further along the road to canonization.

One reason the play was scorned at its premiere was that it concerns English middle-class adultery, and everyone had had quite enough of that. Besides, Pinter was felt to have forsaken his roots. The man who had made his name writing about psychotic tramps and seaside boarding houses was devoting himself in this play to the emotional entanglements of the urban literati -- in other words, the world in which he himself now moved.

Worse yet, his characters weren't even real writers, just prosperous hangers-on: the people in Betrayal are Jerry, a literary agent; Robert, his best friend and a publisher; and Emma, wife of the latter and mistress of the former.

Also, audiences were tired -- justifiably -- of what had become known as the Pinter style: the icy-smooth, deliberate manner that Peter Hall, for many years Pinters regular director, seemed to employ as a form of elegant intimidation. Every word, every movement, came loaded with significance, even though nobody could be sure what they signified.

Betrayal, though, didnt even offer the consolations of obscurity. Its story happens to unfold in reverse chronological order, but apart from that, its development is straightforward -- or , at least, straightbackward. Things that seem puzzling in one scene are invariably clarified in the next.

The play's first scene shows Jerry and Emma meeting for a drink, some years after the end of the affair. We gather that Jerry would quite like to rekindle the relationship, and that Emma wouldn't. The last scene shows Jerry declaring his passion for the first time and Emma responding. The mood, which survives even the sudden appearance of the about-to-be-cuckolded Robert, is one of delicate tenderness. In hindsight, the beginning of their romance seems like its high point. Once consummated, it seems to have declined very quickly into a routine of lying, logistics and rendezvous in a rented flat in an unfashionable part of London.

Like all Pinters plays, the piece takes shape as a series of psychological power struggles. In any given scene, two of the characters will share some piece of knowledge of which the third is ignorant. So each of the trio is at once both victor and victim: It depends which way the light is shining.

From one viewpoint, Jerry seems to hold all the aces. He has the woman he desires, and none of the attendant responsibilities. But he still has to live with the fact that Robert has first claim on Emma, and he is in constant fear of losing her. Robert is the deceived husband, but he has been enjoying his own infidelities. Besides, about halfway through the action, Emma tells him about her affair, thereby strengthening the bond between them and making Jerry even more the outsider.

Emma, meanwhile, is pointedly -- even brutally -- excluded from the camaraderie of the two men, symbolized by their regular games of squash, though these seem to be more talked about than enjoyed. (As also, from what we gather, seems to be the case with sex.) Still, Emma has had both men, and has moved on to a third: The play has a fourth, unseen, character, a writer, Casey, whom Jerry represents and Robert publishes. They live off him but he walks off with their woman -- the authors revenge.

The relationships pulsate, unspoken words conveying invisible words. (The spoken words are, as usual, brilliant.) As in any good play, all the characters have areas of unknowability. Some, however, are more unknowable than others. And, as often with Pinter, the woman is little more than an indecipherable, much-coveted Sphinx.
