robertcushman@lycos.com; The Dora Awards are presented at the Winter Garden Theatre on Monday at 8 p.m.

Once again, in the brief interregnum between the announcement of the nominees for this year's Dora Awards and the prize-giving itself, we contemplate who deserves to win, who actually will win and who isn't even in the running but should be. As usual, the theatre awards are divided into two main sections, General and Independent. These correspond, more or less, to what used to be known as Mainstream and Alternative. Or perhaps -- since some of the theatres in the General section would still think of themselves as being Alternative, or at least as being alternative to other theatres in the same category -- we should simply call them Big and Small.

Big first then. There are five nominees for Outstanding New Play, and if critical response, general buzz and actual merit are any guides, John Mighton's Half Life should get it in a walk. The next- best contender, in my book, would be Claudia Dey's Trout Stanley, but if there is a threat it will be Rick Miller's Bigger than Jesus. At least, I think of it as being Miller's play (or his stand-up comedy routine), though it's in fact credited to him and his director, Daniel Brooks. I don't think it's a play, though I'm well aware the same has been said of many of the most notable events in theatrical history. The two remaining slots are filled by Keira Loughran's Little Dragon and David Young's No Great Mischief. I'd like to say that I can think of any number of overlooked candidates, but I can't. In fact, I can't think of one.

OUTSTANDING NEW MUSICAL This year the choice is down to two. (Remember, they have to be Canadian.) One is Aladdin, the Ross Petty pantomime, credited to David Finley. The other is Guy Mignault's Autour de Kurt Weill, which I didn't see but assume will win on cultural grounds.

OUTSTANDING PRODUCTION OF A PLAY Once again, the smart money is on Half Life, which was outstanding in text, performance, direction and design and has most (not quite all) of the nominations to prove it. And once again, Bigger than Jesus will be the competition. Take Me Out (a.k.a. "the baseball play") was a good production, let down by one major performance and the no-better-than-all-right quality of the text. Most puzzling nomination: The Leisure Society, on two counts -- it was a thoroughly overrated piece, but if people thought it was so good, why isn't it up there under New Play? And why did it get the nod in this category over two superior Factory Theatre all- rounders, Trout Stanley and the Suburban Motel double-header? The latter, one of the best-liked shows of the year (there were reservations in this column, but that's typical), hasn't been nominated for anything. Incredible.

OUTSTANDING PRODUCTION OF A MUSICAL Now we're talking. American musicals qualify for this one, so it's a straight fight between Hairspray and Urinetown, the latter having the edge, as it's the smaller, smarter and, all things considered, better show. Wicked hasn't been nominated at all, which is a pleasant surprise. The two Canadian shows mentioned above are in here as well, as is Ain't Misbehavin', but I can't believe any of them is a threat.

OUTSTANDING DIRECTION OF A PLAY Daniel Brooks is running against himself with Bigger than Jesus and Half Life. I hope he wins for the latter. Ken Gass is nominated for The Leisure Society, though I feel again that it should have been for End of Civilization, one of the Suburban Motel plays. I'm glad Joseph Ziegler got a nod for his Soulpepper Hamlet and sad that Ben Barnes didn't for his Soulpepper Translations. Also nominated are Eda Holmes for Trout Stanley and (yes, I do go to the odd French thing) Diana Leblanc for Le Bourgeois Gentilhomme.

OUTSTANDING PERFORMANCE BY A MALE IN A PRINCIPAL ROLE: PLAY This is the most perplexing category of all. Rick Miller is up there, of course, for Bigger than Jesus, as is Daniel MacIvor for his solo turn in Cul-de-Sac (which sounds like one of the trickier parts of a driving test). They were both excellent, but I think that Miller will probably win. However, I also think that one-person shows should be in a separate category, the requirements of acting on your own being so different from those of doing it with other people. Nominated in the latter, less flashy, section are Eric Peterson (my choice) in Half Life, Paul Soles in Trying and Stuart Hughes in Soulpepper's Pinter/Albee double-bill. All fine, but no more so than Dennis O'Connor in End of Civilization or than a whole bunch of Soulpepper actors: Michael Hanrahan, Hughes's opposite number, or William Hutt in Waiting for Godot, or Diego Matamoros, Gordon Rand and David Storch in Translations. Rand was also brilliant in Trout Stanley, but Matamoros beats him out as the year's most taken-for- granted talent since the plethora of nominations for Half Life includes none for his performance, which was, in its quiet way, almost the best male acting of the season.

OUTSTANDING PERFORMANCE BY A FEMALE IN A PRINCIPAL ROLE: PLAY "Male," "female" -- why so clinical? Whatever happened to those useful words "actor" and "actress"? Anyway, this one is clear-cut: Carolyn Hetherington must win, both for the sheer grace of her performance in Half Life, and for her status as a mature performer of whom few of us were properly aware. Also, there's not much competition, though Melody Johnson was pretty riveting in Trout Stanley. And what, pray, about the unlisted Martha Henry in Vigil?

OUTSTANDING PERFORMANCE BY A MALE IN A PRINCIPAL ROLE: MUSICAL This has to be a contest between Jay Brazeau, the mountainous mother in Hairspray, and David Keeley, the oleaginous cop in Urinetown. Brazeau's role does come with built-in natural advantages. I guess Tom Rooney's delightful performance as her hubby doesn't count as "principal." But who knew he could sing and dance?

OUTSTANDING PERFORMANCE BY A FEMALE IN A PRINCIPAL ROLE: MUSICAL Jennifer Waiser's Little Sally in Urinetown, no contest. The competition is thin: Divine Brown plucked from a cast of equals in Ain't Misbehavin', two by Mary Ann McDonald, and Vanessa Olivarez who, in the lead role of Hairspray, was close to being bad. Maybe they should have let Jay Brazeau qualify in this category.

OUTSTANDING PERFORMANCE IN A FEATURED ROLE Simple mathematics show this to be the most crowded pigeonhole of all. But they insist on lumping everybody together: male, female, play, musical. Of the half-dozen assembled I vote for Mike Shara in Take Me Out. I note that Fran Jaye got nominated for belting one of those indistinguishable supposed-to-be-soul numbers in Hairspray, while Jennifer Stewart, the life and real soul of that particular party, got nothing.

OUTSTANDING SET DESIGN Francis O'Connor for Translations. Some people claimed it overwhelmed the play; it didn't, and I'd be rooting for it even if it had. Costume Design Dunno. Maybe Sarah Balleux for her dual-focus duds (ancient below, modern above) in Le Bourgeois Gentilhomme. Lighting Design Kevin Lamotte's work on Translations glows in my memory. Sound Design Search me. I remember there being some terrifying noises in Cul-de-Sac, courtesy of Richard Feren.

MUSICAL DIRECTION Here, too, one takes excellence for granted, though Stephen Woodjetts (Urinetown) was noticeably good, perhaps because he appeared on stage. But I think I favour Marek Norman, who after his nominated work on the High Park As You Like It went on to compose a totally different score for the same play in Barrie, proving twice over that a Canadian can provide great Arden music without being Jann or even a Barenaked Lady. Choreography John Carrafa, Urinetown, exhilaratingly funny, just beats Jerry Mitchell, Hairspray, merely exhilarating.

I have to be less categorical about the Independent categories, because there was much I didn't see. Nonetheless, I shall be both shocked and furious if A Whistle in the Dark doesn't get Outstanding Production, as it was the best show of the season, period. Ditto for Joseph Ziegler as the play's patriarch, the performance of the year, any divison, any category. I am already incensed that there is no nomination for the director, Jason Byrne; do the judges think that those seven impeccable performances orchestrated themselves? This, considering the similar neglect of Ben Barnes, must be what is meant by the luck of the Irish.

Best actress in this division will, I hope, be Barbara Gordon in Beckett's Not I, with honourable mention for Jenny Young in The Anger of Ernest and Ernestine. Yanna McIntosh is also nominated for her Hedda Gabler, though it seems perverse to acclaim a fine actress for a performance so far below her best. Best new play in this sector: Gord Rand's Pond Life would be fine with me.

Awards are silly and unfair, and it's foolish for anyone, onlooker or participant, to get agitated about them. But the fact that we do is a sign that both we and the activity that's up for grabs are alive and somewhat kicking.
