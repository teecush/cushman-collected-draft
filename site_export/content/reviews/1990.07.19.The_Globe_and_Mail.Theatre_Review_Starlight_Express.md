THEATRE REVIEW STARLIGHT EXPRESS

Thursday, July 19, 1990

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Written by Andrew Lloyd Webber and Richard Stilgoe Directed by Arlene Phillips At O'Keefe Centre, Toronto The trouble with Starlight Express is that this musical on wheels is, at times, not spectacular enough Exciting, sometimes beautiful, but not always on the right track ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail STARLIGHT Express is one of those shows that nobody likes except the public. It is everlasting in London, ran respectably in New York, and is doing well on the road, if that is the right expression for a show ostensibly about the lives and loves of a set of trains. But critics everywhere have despised it, and the O'Keefe's pre-publicity material was confined to a single word, repeated over and over. The word was "spectacular" and all the critics had used it because they had to. It isn't a value judgment; it's a statement of fact.

One is supposed to have extreme reactions to Andrew Lloyd Webber. But I have never been able to regard him either as the messiah of the musical theatre or as the devil incarnate; and I have a soft spot for Starlight. When I saw it in London I could not comprehend its savage dismissal by the same journalists who had creamed over Cats, for the two shows have the same strengths and weaknesses. Both are environmental entertainments of a particular childlike kind: adventure playgrounds. Both are ensemble shows, and a lot of the good feeling they generate is company feeling. Neither is strong on individual character; and both are at their worst when trying to gouge sympathy for a protagonist, whether Grizabella the Glamor Cat or Rusty the Steam Engine.

I doubt if anyone unbriefed by the publicity or by what they could make out of the lyrics would realize that the 37 brightly-clothed roller-skated actors at the O'Keefe are actually meant to be trucks and engines. A train that stands up on its hind legs is a train in very bad trouble. In fact, Starlight is in large part an abstract entertainment in which color and movement are ends in themselves. Or rather an end in itself, since at the best moments the two fuse. When Arlene Phillips' choreography sends John Napier's stunning costume creations whizzing up the ramps and down the runways of the same designer's girdered set, the upshot is excitement and sometimes beauty.

What it is not, is narrative interest. Starlight Express is presented to us as a child's bedtime fantasy. There is an offstage narrator who calls himself Control and sounds like Christopher Robin gone beyond bears. He dreams up a Trans-America train race that attracts the locomotives of the world. In the end, the competition narrows down to three emblematic natives: the diesel Greaseball, the electric Electra, and Rusty. Steam is the underdog, and therefore the hero. Each engine, male, draws a single coach. Most are female, so we get a romantic subplot.

Rusty's precarious confidence is bolstered by his blues-singing Dad, and by some mysterious choo-choo deity called the Starlight Express which eventually materializes as a fierce drifting cloudscape framed by green lasers. It tells Rusty to believe in himself. It has probably seen too many musicals. Its wisdom is the wisdom of the ages, at least those between Carousel and Sunday in the Park with George.

The story, such as it is, is easier to follow in the North American production than in the London one, where the human trains went racing all over the auditorium, with the action monitored on video screens for those spectators who might have their backs to any of it. Here it all happens on a large but conventional stage, except for the races themselves which are on film. This last device is horrendous. First, it is an admission of failure: it is telling us that the climax of this stage musical cannot be staged. Second, nobody goes to the theatre to look at a screen. Third, while we may accept that these actor"dancers on stage are trains, film ineluctably reveals they are just people in warpaint being silly. Fourth, the sequences probably cost thousands to shoot but they still look cheap, grainy and unimaginative.

In fact, the trouble with Starlight Express is not that it is spectacular, but that it isn't, at key moments, spectacular enough. Theatre has every right to be visually extravagant; other things being equal it has a duty to be. (That a play like Love Letters gets praised for not having crowd scenes and falling chandeliers, as if this were a positive virtue, shows how confused our values have become.) Starlight Express is planned as a story musical but can't quite tell its story. Cats was a vaudeville that at desperate moments fell back on a fable at least as maudlin and sententious but mostly kept it out of sight. Which is preferable I don't know. But, though I am shouting with the minority, I prefer the music of Starlight. Webber has written a pop score, and an entertaining one: from the chugging and churning opening number Rolling Stock to the generic but rousing gospel closer Light at the End of the Tunnel, by way most notably of One Rock and Roll Too Many, the repentant lament of the losing locos. There are, admittedly, no hit ballads of the calibre of Memory. Thank God.

The touring production is crackerjack, with Jimmy Lockett's sonorous Poppa and Todd Lester's capering Red Caboose earning special credits in a troupe that brims with energy and, come to think of it, courage. The electronics are impeccable: as, of course, they have to be. That a show so dependent on high-tech should be so Luddite in its sympathies is a paradox. Though no more so than that a creative team from Britain, which still has a railway system worth the name, should have set a train musical in the United States, which hasn't. There is a sickness here somewhere. I think it's called nostalgia.
