THEATRE REVIEW

The Maids

Buddies in Bad Times Theatre, Toronto

Brendan Healys last-season production of Blasted, his opening shot as director of Buddies in Bad Times, left no doubt that hes got the goods. If any more proof were needed, it's available in his secondseason opener, the only production I have seen of Jean Genet's The Maids that comes close to making the play enjoyable. Make that the only production of any Genet play.

Like Blasted, The Maids has an overpoweringly effective setting, in this case a boudoir designed by Julie Fox in several shades of red and sumptuously lit by Kimberly Purtell. There may not actually be perfume in the air but the production definitely has an aroma. Also like Blasted, it has a cast of three: two who are onstage practically throughout, and a third who functions dramatically as an intruder, even though in this case its her house. After that, the tracks diverge.

The newcomer in Blasted brings violence graphically on to the stage; in The Maids, her arrival carries the threat of it but not, it turns out, the reality. In Blasted, Healys achievement was to present human behaviour in such detail that naturalism took on the quality of ritual. In The Maids, he takes a text that stakes everything on ritual and, in honouring that quality, makes it human.

The maids are Solange and Claire, middle-aged sisters locked in a love-hate relationship with the Madame who employs them and also, it seems, with one another. As the play begins, they are acting out a habitual charade, in which one of them puts on Madame's clothes and humiliates the other. This time it's Claire whos doing the dressing up, and just to make the game more interesting, shes addressing Solange as Claire. The logical end of the game would be that they kill Madame and that does seem to be the end toward which, in fantasy at least, they are working. Meanwhile, in what passes in Genet for the real world, the girls have framed Madame's lover on an embezzlement charge. Now they learn that he's out on bail, and likely to reveal everything; so the removal of Madame becomes an urgent necessity.

Genets drama is mesmeric or its nothing, and in the first long scene of this production, the spell is superbly cast. Diane D'Aquila's Solange, in particular, is a major achievement, limping around the stage with an aggressiveness born of long and painful habit and contemptuous of pity, kaleidoscopic in mood and cadence, but with a flinty undeviating economy of means. I hung on her every word even when I had ceased to care what those words might be.

The characters are female, but Genet was known to declare that he had written them for men (or boys) and indeed theyve been played that way. Healeys production breaks (as far as I know) new ground by splitting the difference, D'Aquila's Solange being partnered by the Claire of Ron Kennell, who does not seem to be doing a female impersonation; at least, he starts the play topless and he certainly doesnt have a womans breasts.

In his stage directions, Genet specified that the maids should be in their thirties and Madame even younger. I've yet to see a production realize that potentially interesting dynamic, but this one comes closer than most with Maria Ricossa playing Madame as a flighty chatelaine who obviously thinks she's young, and whose mindless patronizing of her domestics is as poisonously delightful as her declarations of undying loyalty to her fettered beloved.

Theres an element of suspense in her scene, though Genet was hardly a thriller writer. In fact, he was hardly a dramatist at all; his plays are essentially monologues, divided between a given number of mouthpieces. In this production the one idea - that social hierarchy is a matter of roleplaying, unjust but inescapable - is endlessly if fancifully reiterated. Which is why, even in a production as good as this one, using a translation as spry as Martin Crimps, it ends up boring. But still, on this occasion, hypnotic.
