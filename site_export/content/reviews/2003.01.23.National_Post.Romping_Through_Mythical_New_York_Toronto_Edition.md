LITTLE MERCYS FIRST MURDER

Tarragon Extra Space, Toronto

Melody Johnson is the actress of hard-boiled nerve endings. Her words fall out of the corner of her mouth in a belligerent squawk, shes full of sharp angles, and she has the air of an aggressive waif, hoping for the best but expecting the worst; her ideals have been trampled on, but they are always apt to reassert themselves.

In plays she always gives much the same performance (she showed greater versatility in a revue at Second City), but it's a performance I like, and she makes it seem right for every character she plays. For the central role in Little Mercy's First Murder its ideal.

This show is billed as a new musical, though its more a play with music; the songs punctuate rather than define the action. The book and lyrics are by rising playwright Morwyn Brebner. It has been very smartly directed by Eda Holmes. I enjoyed some of it, was impressed by more, yet found the whole thing crumbling in my mind before I had crossed the street from the theatre to the car park.

It's set in the well-known mythical city of New York, at an equally mythical date. The program swears it's 1942, but it seemed to me to bear a greater resemblance to the depressed 1930s. (There are references to the war, but I took them to refer to the coming conflict rather than the current one.)

Little Mercy is a young lady whose large mother has just expired with a knife stuck into her. So: Was it a Mercy-killing? (The script never actually makes that crack, but its implicit.) The cop on the spot thinks so, and he looks forward to seeing Little Mercy fry.

He nonetheless permits Mercy to visit the washroom down the hall, un-handcuffed and escorted only by a press photographer (this is not a realistic drama), and spends the rest of the show giving chase with Javert-like intensity. It takes him all night to catch up.

Mercy, meanwhile, has been on a tour of the citys infernal regions in the company of the cameraman, whose name is Weegee. He is apparently based on an actual person. I didn't know that, and presumably his voyeurism would have had more substance for those who did.

Anyway, he is professionally and personally attracted to disasters, and he never quite befriends Mercy, though he keeps looking as if he might. He also never buys her dinner, though he promised. Together they witness a fire, and see the rich people tramping into the opera. Mercy, who used to work in a library, comments sardonically on how the city houses its books far more handsomely than it does its people. Nevertheless, she was a great reader (the only one on the staff who was), and remarks on life's failure to measure up to the great novels.

She and Weegee wind up at a bar that boasts a variegated line-up of freaky performers. One of them, the headliner, we actually meet: Norma, a drag queen so smoothly convincing as to have the heterosexual barman completely in thrall. Mercy rebukes Weegee and the barman after theyve sung a misogynistic song (from the French). After that, its pretty much over bar the denouement.

It's a world-weary vision of a world worth wearying of, and some of it is delivered with style, a sort of downbeat swagger. But it ends up seeming pointless, and second-hand even beyond the dictates of pastiche, as if the author were trying to drape her own despondency in someone else's feathers. There isnt much story or much conflict, and the spoken lines have more the presumption of wit than its substance.

The music, by Jay Turvey and Paul Sportelli, helps a lot. It makes cunning use of Johnsons limited vocal range, cruelly pushing her highs and lows to make her seem more exposed than ever. The '30s -- or if you insist '40s -- come with built-in musical mordancy if you know where to look. This score takes in Broadway, Montmartre, Rio and Weimar (not much swing), with especial echoes of Marc Blitzsteins labour opera The Cradle Will Rock; the two pieces have a similar stations-of-the-cross structure, and there are at least two numbers that suggest either direct influence or a serious case of osmosis. One is a society ladys self-damning self-justification of her own patronizing attitude to the penniless, and the other is an ominous all-purpose protest song, improbably passed off as a radio hit of a time.

This latter contains Brebner's best efforts as a lyricist. Elsewhere she offers vague, sloppily rhymed words that don't match the music -- which, true to its imagined period, is the kind of music that absolutely demands precision. Theyre the kind of lyrics that sound impressive until you actually listen to them.

Maybe the most effective number is the first. The cast of six gaze cheekily out at us, singing no more words than la la la, as if to say, with more insolence than ingratiation: This is a musical, take it or leave it.

There has to be something to a script that can inspire its interpreters with this kind of confidence, and it persists throughout. Holmes staging, in sets by John Thompson, restores one's battered faith in what can be done in the smallest spaces. Even the projections behind a couple of scenes in a car are effective.

Peter Millard as Weegee fits comfortably into the shagged-out fedora and loosely-knotted tie that always signify either a hack or a private eye. Jeff Lillico is an unimpeachable Norma, bringing the house down with, or as, a little rumba numba. Tony Nappo, as her enslaved admirer, has everything it takes to make a musical comedy except a voice. Neil Barclay is a barrel-bellied cop, who has the best speech in the show, one in which he compares his own policeman's lot to that of his idyllic rural counterpart (probably as seen in the movies) and tells us, "my job is to protect people like you from people like you." Jane Johanson fills in neatly as everyone else. She and the director are also the co-choreographers, and they've done fine.
