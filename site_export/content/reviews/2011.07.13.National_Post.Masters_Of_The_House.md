THEATRE REVIEW

The Admirable Crichton Festival Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

The two newest productions at the Shaw Festival are both of British plays about failed social experiments. In George Bernard Shaw's On the Rocks, a prime minister tries to ram through what he takes to be a populist agenda and is defeated by an unholy alliance of vested interests. In J.M. Barrie's The Admirable Crichton, the established order of masters and servants is upended when its representatives find themselves in drastically altered surroundings, only to be restored when they return to what is conventionally known as civilization.

Barrie introduces us to the earl of Loam, who believes in equality: at least he believes in it once a month, when he hosts a tea party at which his family and friends wait on the army of domestics who make their comfortable lives possible. Everybody except the earl himself finds this embarrassing: his three daughters, their respective suitors, the servants themselves and most of all the butler, the supremely competent Crichton. He believes there has to be a hierarchy in every situation and, living in England, seems quite content that it should be based on birth rather than ability. He does allow that in a different situation, different criteria might apply. And of course the laws of drama ensure this will be put to the test.

Off on a tropical cruise the household -earl, daughters, daughters' admirers, butler and the lowly maid known as Tweeny -finds itself cast away on a desert island. And of course, after a brief attempt at maintaining the customary niceties, it transpires that the wholly admirable Crichton is the only one with the brains and the experience to get things done. If it wasn't for him, they'd all perish, but with him in charge a flourishing little society evolves, complete with improvised modern conveniences. Crichton is its unquestioned monarch; everybody else calls him "the guv" and happily worships him. Then they all get rescued and everything reverts to what it was.

This is all very predictable. But predictability can be dramatically satisfying, especially when the story is essentially a fable, and so it is here. Also Barrie was canny enough to complicate his situation, and the complication he introduces is sex. Back home, Lady Mary, the oldest and possibly (though it's a tough call) the most snobbish of the Loam sisters, wouldn't have given Crichton the time of day, though she might possibly have demanded it of him. On the island, seeing the man he really is, she falls heavily for him, and he for her, even though he had conditionally promised himself to the stilladoring Tweeny. Crichton and Mary, with her father's blessing, are just about to get married (one of their fellow castaways is, providentially, a parson) when the rescue ship arrives.

Back home, the entire party is subjected to a drawing-room interrogation by a suspicious dowager, and it seems for a moment that the two nearlyweds may crack under the strain and confess a passion that still obviously exists. But they don't, and everybody proceeds to breathe easily, notably the supposedly egalitarian earl. It all leaves a pleasantly bitter taste in the mouth.

My only previous acquaintance with the play was in a 1950s movie version, with Kenneth More as Crichton, in which he paired off happily with Tweeny just before the final credits, as if love among the lower orders conquered all and this constituted a happy ending. It's a relief to find that Barrie himself wasn't about to let his audience off so lightly.

Morris Panych's Niagara production, though, has its own compromises to make. First, as now seems to be the convention among directors of Barrie, he has his actors recite the author's stage directions, which, being equal parts sugar and vinegar, are admittedly irresistible. Trouble is, the performers who deliver them, in what sounds like imitation cockney, don't do it very well. Attired and masked as the wild animals who make desert-island life so interesting, they seem to have been cast for their prowess in the musical numbers.

No, Barrie didn't actually write or prescribe any such numbers, but Panych gives us Irving Berlin's great but puzzlingly irrelevant Shakin' the Blues Away; De Sylva, Brown and Henderson's not-greatbut-good It All Depends on You; and some others that even I couldn't identify. The songs, and their staging, come from the 1920s, though the play itself is from 1902 and depicts a world whose assumptions were to be seriously shaken by the First World War. My own guess is that Panych found the play too slight to sustain an evening on the Shaw Festival's main stage and wanted to give us a more substantial run for our money. Up to a point he succeeds. There's certainly fun to be had. But it comes at the cost of a creeping, and eventually contagious, suspicion that the director doesn't absolutely trust the play.

Both pleasure and doubt reach their climax at the curtain call, when we get a reprise of the Berlin tune with the entire cast Charlestoning like crazy, and with Steven Sutcliffe's Crichton shedding his sedateness to shake a festive leg, while apparently trying out both his possible partners. Which, I guess, may count as ironic epilogue. Anyway, it's delightful to see Sutcliffe reverting to his musical-theatre past.

Up until this point, the most spectacular physical activity he's been permitted is to shimmer into a room, Jeeves-like. Whether knowing his first place, basking in his second or, in his third, allowing the briefest glimpse of what looks suspiciously like a breaking heart, Sutcliffe's butler is as splendidly imperturbable as his Tory leader in On the Rocks is splendidly perturbable.

David Schuurman, by contrast, progresses from worldweary duke in that play to world-bettering earl while exuding an identical sweet crinkled reasonableness.

Ken MacDonald's sets are beautiful -if, by his standards, unspectacular -but the isle is fuller of noises than it is of danger.
