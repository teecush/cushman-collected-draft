THEATRE REVIEW SCENES FROM AN EXECUTION

Saturday, June 04, 1994

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Toronto ONT -- WRITTEN BY HOWARD BARKER Directed by Rodger Barton Presented by Equity Showcase Theatre At Harbourfront until June 11 Rating: Portrait of an artist This plain, lucid play shows a painter pitted against authority, and it can be applied to any number of contemporary issues BY ROBERT CUSHMAN The Globe and Mail Toronto WHAT gets executed in these scenes is not a person but a painting: an outsize depiction of the Battle of Lepanto, 1571, and defeat of the Turkish navy by a Christian coalition. The Doge of Venice commissions a commemorative canvas from his citys greatest artist, who happens to be a woman. This defiance of gender barriers is historically unlikely, but Howard Barker has never been a playwright to be inhibited by petty facts of life. Indeed he ignores them on principle. Scenes from an Execution is phantasmagoria, and a brilliant one.

Of the politically-minded - or bloody-minded - English playwrights who came to prominence in the 1970s, Barker is the intransigent lone wolf: the only one never to have collaborated on a play with any of the others when it was the fashion. Prolific though he is, he has never had a play presented on a large subsidized stage, but he has been a studio favourite and some actors rate him highly. There is even a company in Britain dedicated to performing his work and calling itself the Wrestling School.

The plays themselves are correspondingly strenuous, though less so than the polemical articles in which he and his disciples describe them. The literature surrounding this Toronto production proclaims the author's commitment to complexity and contradiction, and his loathing of easy options. You may have noticed," writes Barker in a tone befitting his name, "that we live in a laughing society . . . a theatre that takes itself seriously cannot any longer afford to be comic.

This is rubbish; and anyway Barker doesn't believe it himself. The alternative to what he calls diseased comedy" is healthy comedy, and Scenes from an Execution has the audience laughing almost from beginning to end. This may explain why director Rodger Barton has described it in an interview as the authors "most timid and accessible play. (Is he apologizing or what?) That may explain why I like it so much. The historical setting also helps. It puts us immediately in a free imaginative zone. The play shows the artist against authority, and we can appply its story to any number of contemporary issues.

Its like this. Galactia, the lady-genius, paints a horror-picture, and her patrons want a valour-picture. She herself calls it a noisy" painting; and, indeed, in the real world and a few centuries later, the same battle elicited from G. K. Chesterton an uncommonly noisy poem, the one in which Don John of Austria goes repeatedly clip-clopping to the war. The Venetian admiral, confronted by Galactia's depiction of him, proclaims that she has got him wrong, giving him the hands and face of a monster while he knows himself to be exceptionally nice. (Playwright and painter combine a witty and magisterial command of detail). Galactia - having offended church, state, and the military critical establishments - is sent to a dungeon, but her talent proves embarrassing. She is released and her work exhibited, as a demonstration of offical tolerance and commitment to art. Since Galactia is fiercely loyal to her own vision, but has no ideological beliefs beyond that, she seems half-willing to compromise with her enemies.

I can't quite see what is meant to be so complex about all this. Its a familar, even predictable story, right down to the ambivalent ending. What makes it live is the vigour with which Barker infuses Galactia herself - even her prison meditations rise above the stock modern vein of mock- Jacobean - and the urbane cartooning of her opponents, with all their scruples and cruelties firmly in place. "Your anger, says the Doge to Galactia is as much an act as my self-denigration, and more difficult to keep up." You can hear Barker, who plainly identifies with his heroine, saying a rueful amen to that.

There is a sexual story cutting across the political one. Barker writes extremely well about desire, and Galactia has a disabling weakness for a male married painter whose work (one Biblical subject endlessly recycled) she despises. He gets the job of replacing her, but isnt up to it. Having betrayed her, he falls more deeply in love with her. This gives the play added flavour, but no more than that.

The production, plain and lucid, does very right by the play. Wenna Shaw's Galactia is sometimes merely bossy where she should be arrogant, but she has no trouble driving the show. I also liked Michael Anderson's Doge, a picture of elegantly modulated pragmatism; Robert Dodds, who, as Galactias model as well as her lover, spends a lot of time naked; and Barrie Baldaro as her real inspiration, a damaged veteran with an arrow sticking out of his head. All these actors grow with the play, and all have first-rate timing. There are some weak performances but you can usually see what theyre getting at. "An honoured audience, says Barker, lecturing us on what's good for us, will quarrel with what it has seen, it will go home in a state of anger." I went home pleased and unquarrelsome. Incorrigible creature that I am I shall have, like Galactia, to live with dishonour.
