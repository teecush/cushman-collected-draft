E.Y. alias "Yip" Harburg, who wrote the lyrics for the 1939 movie of The Wizard of Oz and had a substantial if largely uncredited part in fashioning the screenplay, always used to claim that it was a political film. A lifelong leftist and a devout Roosevelt man, he said that when the Scarecrow, the Tin Woodman and the Cowardly Lion made their respective bids for a brain, a heart and "da noive," they were paying tribute to the man in the White House who had given America the qualities it needed to combat the Depression: intelligence, compassion and courage. It's uncertain whether he meant us to identify FDR with the Wizard himself who, you may remember, turns out to be something of a fraud.

Not altogether, though. It's he who, when pushed, teaches the three supplicants that they're at least as good as the poseurs who claim to have the virtues they themselves seek, and that all they really need is self-confidence. It's the same lesson that the Good Witch imparts to Dorothy about how to find her way home -- but only after she's learned it for herself. This message of self-reliance prompted the movie historian Ethan Mordden to call The Wizard of Oz the best right-wing musical ever made (and just in time for war, he noted). It's a judgment that would have flabbergasted Harburg, who gloried in having one of the fattest FBI files of anyone in show business; an investigator once asked him, quite seriously, if his Happiness Is a Thing Called Joe was a song about Stalin. But there is a cockeyed truth to it (and Yip always delighted in paradoxes, provided he was the one coining them). It proves, if nothing else, that extremes can meet.

It also suggests that The Wizard of Oz is a fable rich enough to be put to use in diverse ways. The film took liberties with the 40- year-old book it was based on; the Kansas dust bowl that Dorothy famously decides she isn't in any more was a different place in 1939 than it had been in 1900. L. Frank Baum's story had become the basis of a hugely successful, and thoroughly escapist, stage musical long before MGM acquired the movie rights; and, as we've seen, the movie isn't escapist, though it is about (temporary) escape.

Despite The Wiz and, please God, despite Wicked (which really is a fairy tale and not in a good way), the film still defines how everyone sees the story. The stage version now on view in Toronto hews very closely to the movie, including some material retrieved from the cutting-room floor. It's the work of an English actor, John Kane, and was first staged by the Royal Shakespeare Company, who gave it a mind-blowingly bounteous production. Our Young People's Theatre can hardly summon the same resources, but, on its own more modest terms, it's almost as effective. And it does put its own stamp on the story, while unobtrusively updating it.

The focus of Allen MacInnis's production is more psychological than sociological. In a program note, he lays great stress on Dorothy's trip to Oz being a dream; this, of course, is built into any version of the story, but the correspondences between the girl's real friends, foes and family, and her fantasy ones are sharper here than usual. I wish that Michael Gianfrancesco (sets) and Steve Lucas (lights) had found a way of reproducing the movie's alternation of sepia and full colour, but they have given the show a charming miniaturist look with an overarching theme. Once we get to Oz, we can see clear to the back wall of the stage, pulleys and lighting- grid in full view, and the theatre itself becomes a metaphor. The Munchkins are puppets, seen from the neck up; they look like relatives of Fozzie Bear, treated to a makeover in blue.

Most importantly, while the show treats the script, the lyrics and Harold Arlen's music with the utmost respect, nobody on stage acts as if they'd seen the movie. All the characters are freshly thought. When Saccha Dennis's Dorothy sings Over the Rainbow, it's an expression of hemmed-in teenage anger frustration rather than Judy Garland wistfulness, and a song you may never have wanted to hear again comes back to life. So does the girl herself. She's surrounded and supported by some of the best musical-theatre performers in Toronto, not to mention a dog who trots on and offstage with indomitable pertinacity. George Masswohl, an unassuming tower of strength as Uncle Henry, mutates into a very funny Emerald City Guard; Molly Atkinson, a salt-of-the-earth Auntie Em, has a nice sense of self-satire as the Good Witch. The three companions are Paul McQuillan, an enchantingly floppy Scarecrow; Thom Allison, a Tin Man in a Campbell's soup can who delivers some ringing vocal variations on If I Only Had a Heart; and Shawn Wright, an appealingly bashful Lion, though without -- inevitably -- the underweening majesty of Bert Lahr's King of the Forest. (This is the one role where comparisons can't help bearing down; it was written for Lahr, mainly by Harburg, as Dorothy was never written for Garland.) Sam Moses's Professor Marvel takes his sweet time, but he's a bumbling whiz of a Wizard; a shame that the available technology doesn't permit his exit by balloon. Sharron Matthews is more believably threatening as Miss Gulch than she is as the Wicked Witch, but, in the latter role, she does get to kick off The Jitterbug, a restored swing tune (Arlen at his rhythmic best), that becomes the most invigorating number in any current Toronto musical. Movie musicals slavishly transferred to stage are a plague, but this is something different: the latest chapter in the progress of a classic.
