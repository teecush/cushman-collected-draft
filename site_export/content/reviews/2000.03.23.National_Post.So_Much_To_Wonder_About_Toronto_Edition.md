ALICE

Young People's Theatre

A few years ago, the Stratford Festival mounted a version of Alice Through the Looking-Glass that seemed to have taken its cue from the famous judicial instructions issued by the King of Hearts in the high court of Wonderland: "Begin at the beginning, go on until you reach the end, and then stop." The Young People's Theatre, by contrast, gives us a new-look Alice that hops, or hip-hops, around both the Lewis Carroll masterpieces, throwing in fragments of The Hunting of the Snark for good measure and topping it off with a few familiar motifs from the author's life. This phantasmagoria (another Carroll title; it's a very funny poem about a ghost) is preferable to Stratford's prosaic literalness.

The adaptation, by Maristella Roca, is at least energetic in the way it hurls the elements together, and the production, by Gyllian Raby, has touches of imagination. Alice's fall down the rabbit hole, a notorious theatrical hazard, is nicely managed by a judicious application of modern technology. The show is, all the same, very sloppy.

It's also, I would think, in a no-win situation with the kids at whom it's directed. If they know the originals, they will resent, and be confused by, the liberties taken. If they don't know the originals (such childhood deprivation is unpleasant to contemplate but it undoubtedly exists), they'll be confused in another way as the allusions, which seem to be the point of the enterprise, come thick and fast and rarely stick around to explain themselves. The characters, filched and altered from Carroll, never come to life in their own right. The target audiences seem to be children who have been told about the books in some detail but haven't actually read them (a depressingly plausible scenario) and grown-ups who have the stories firmly under their belts and can get a mild thrill from seeing them mucked about with.

Here, as far as I can reconstruct it, is what happens. We begin with a nicely composed stage picture depicting a Victorian Alice, chaperoned by three tea-sipping older sisters and having her photograph taken by the real-life Carroll's real-life alter ego, the Reverend Charles Lutwidge Dodgson. He, far from being the child charmer of whom one has always read, is a short-tempered old party who really loses it when Alice makes an unauthorized grab at a tart that he was saving up for tea. (Look, it's the edible kind of tart; or does that make it sound worse?)

She, in her turn, is robbed of the cookie by a jive-talkin' bunny (honey, he ain't no white rabbit) and naturally she pursues him in a vertically downward direction. When she gets to Wonderland, or wherever it is, she learns that he has an unsavoury reputation as a ladies' rabbit; he supplies the Duchess with tarts, and probably other things. (She is not ugly in this version; indeed, she prides herself on her beauty.)

Alice meets the Jabberwocky, who recaps most of the known poetic data about himself, and advises her on how to ensnare and even kill the rabbit with the aid of a vorpal blade, or in this case vorpal carrot. (It looks like something out of a cut-rate tour of Star Wars.)

By the end of the show, though, the helpful Jabberwock has become the bad guy and the rabbit the good one. I don't know why, though I can tell you that the rabbit's name is Louis, presumably in reference to the original author's nice liberated side.

In his repressed and (ugh) Victorian aspect, he reappears as the Dormouse, linked to Dodgson by alliteration if nothing else. We also at various stages meet Tweedledum and Tweedledee, and a surprisingly svelte Queen of Hearts, still screaming for universal decapitation but a devotee of baseball rather than of croquet. This, presumably, is to make a 2000 North American audience feel at home.

One thing in the show's favour: Unlike most classic adaptations at YPT, it doesn't preach. This is emphatically true to Carroll; the stories are, among other things, ferocious satires on the moralizing literature to which Victorian children were routinely subjected.

But that's only a negative recommendation. The most vulnerable element in any dramatization of Alice is also the most important -- the humour. Either it gets ignored, by solemn psychological or sociological interpreters with higher things on their minds, or it gets trampled by guys who just wanna have fun. This show suffers from a little of the former affliction and a lot of the latter. Not much of Carroll's wit remains, and what there is is murdered. Instead we get a series of sidelong references to it -- self- conscious and intolerably self-satisfied.

This leaves the actors very little to act, and all the perfomances suffer from a debilitating lack of urgency. This is particularly true of Keira Loughran's Alice; deprived of the real girl's enchantingly demure bossiness, she can only be moonishly snippy. Her sole characteristic is a vaguely expressed desire to go home, as if the adaptor had got her source material confused with The Wizard of Oz. And I cringed at her repeated references to "Reverend Dodgson." It should be "Mr. Dodgson," and any properly brought-up English girl of the period would know that. An illiterate Alice is a contradiction in terms.

And so is an illogical Alice. I don't mind them messing with the storyline; that's an adaptor's prerogative. I do mind them missing the point. The books are built on the contrast between the implacable school-room reasoning that she brings with her and the equally impregnable upside-down arguments of the people in her dreams. This version, as far as I could make out through a fog of garbled speaking and inaudible singing, couldn't care less.
