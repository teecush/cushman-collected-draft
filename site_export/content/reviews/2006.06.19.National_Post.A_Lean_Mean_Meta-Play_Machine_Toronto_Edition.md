We have now had nearly a quarter of a century to decide whether The Real Thing, Tom Stoppards 1982 play about love among the playwrighting class, is the real thing. The jury, as far as I am concerned, is still out. The original London production and its New York remount left me disappointed that Stoppard should have settled for writing the kind of play that lesser dramatists write better. A revival in 1999, again in both cities, changed my mind completely; it was leaner, sexier, more passionate, far more moving and probably funnier. The new Soulpepper production, directed by Diana Leblanc, falls somewhere between the two. My admiration for the play remains largely intact, but some of my doubts have returned.

The play's central character is Henry, a playwright. Henry leaves his wife Charlotte, an actress, for Annie, another actress, currently married to Max, an actor. This is particularly messy because, when we first meet them all, Charlotte and Max are co- starring in Henry's latest West End play, the ironically titled House of Cards; I never thought to ask this before, but one thing Stoppard doesnt tell us is whether the double break-up meant that House of Cards had to fall early, or whether all concerned smiled gamely and went on taking the money. Anyway, Henry, who in the course of this play is dubbed "the last romantic," is devoted to Annie and to the idea -- no, the fact -- of being in love with her. She has equivalent feelings about him; but their ideas about what constitutes a trusting relationship, and especially about the importance or otherwise of sexual fidelity and the inevitability of jealousy, arent always in sync.

Henry has other passions. They include early '60s pop music, especially the Crystals and the Righteous Brothers, and the precise and rigorous use of the English language. He's enraged when people use it sloppily for political purposes. His pet example is a certain Private Brodie, a young Scots soldier arrested for trying to set fire to a monument as an anti-missile protest, and a favourite cause of Annies. While in jail Brodie writes a terrible play -- terrible by Henrys lights anyway -- and Henry, to please Annie, agrees to make it presentable. All these elements are juggled and cross- lighted to make an intriguing pattern.

We actually get to see a scene from House of Cards. But it isn't like a parody of your typical boulevard adultery play. It seems like a boulevard adultery play as it would be written by Tom Stoppard, and a very entertaining one, all badinage and reversals. The Real Thing itself isn't like that; theres only one joke that counts as vintage Stoppard. But, joking apart, all the characters talk as if theyre in a Stoppard play. They're unstoppably articulate, and that's a problem since this one, unlike his others, comes on as naturalistic.

Henrys later speeches about love as trust and dependence are very eloquent. Albert Schultz delivers them beautifully. Earlier on, his soft-spoken, soft-bearded Henry is less fluent and less convincing. That surface naturalism may have led him astray; in the plays most celebrated speech -- comparing an effective prose-style to a well-sprung cricket-bat -- one is conscious less of what the character is saying than of his effort to find the right persuasive words to say it. And there wouldn't be an effort; not on that subject, not for him.

Most damagingly, there is little chemistry between him and Megan Follows' Annie, and without that spark the play loses much of its emotional charge.

In a generally good cast, C. David Johnson is wonderful as Max, baffled and belligerent; dramatic geometry says he, as Henrys second wifes first husband, must loom large at the beginning and then disappear, and his absence seems a terrible deprivation.
