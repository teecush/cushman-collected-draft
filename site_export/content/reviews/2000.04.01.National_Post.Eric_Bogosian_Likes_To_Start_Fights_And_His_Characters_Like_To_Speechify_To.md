SUBURBIA

The Poor Alex Theatre

Eric Bogosian, best known as author and protagonist of Talk Radio, inspired elevated comparisons when his play SubUrbia was staged off-Broadway in 1995. The New York Times called it "Chekhov high on speed and Twinkies," and the description isn't quite as glib as it may sound. Like Chekhov's three sisters yearning for Moscow, Bogosian's characters are stuck in a faceless and stifling American town from which they dream of escaping to New York. Or L.A. Or probably anywhere.

They also, in their own fashion, philosophize and talk about art and wave guns around. They also drink and, as testified above, do drugs. Unlike Chekhov's people they are social underlings, even in their hometown, but their ranks do include three brothers, sort of - - three guys who grew up together, went through school together, and now, in their early 20s, hang out together.

There is Jeff, who has liberal ideas and vague ambitions of being a writer. There is Buff, who seems to be permanently zonked, or maybe just dumb, who has even vaguer ambitions of making videos, and no ideas at all, just fantasies and prejudices. And there is Tim, who was in the army for a time and has returned as a poisonous and surprisingly articulate racist, or maybe just an all-round hater.

There are also Suze, Jeff's increasingly disaffected girlfriend, who wants to be a performance artist, and Bee-Bee, who is going through detoxification and is dangerously out of things. This group spends most of its time outside the local 7 Eleven, to the great grief and occasional terror of the Pakistani brother and sister who own it.

The play's title is honest but disorienting, at least in terms of an audience's preconditioned responses. Mention "suburbia" and most of us think of families; of lives centred, however discordantly, around a dining-room table.

We think, at this post-Oscar moment, of American Beauty, a film that I enjoyed enough when I saw it to make me feel uncomfortable now about joining in the backlash. Still, I felt cheated even then when Kevin Spacey in his final voice-over started going on about the loneliness of it all; I couldn't see where, other than beyond the grave, he was coming from. Apparently we had been watching a feel- good movie all along, and Hollywood responded with prizes as Hollywood always does.

Meanwhile, in another, but related, realm, the far truer if ostensibly lighter-hearted Freaks and Geeks had its plug pulled. Maybe it was headed in too grisly a direction. SubUrbia is Freaks and Geeks -- the freaky part anyway -- a few years on and with the screws tightened. These ex-kids are all intelligent, or most of them are, but that just compounds the frustration.

Back into their lives comes one who got away: Pony, now a rock star (probably of lesser magnitude). He's been playing a concert at the local stadium; his former friends haven't got it together to attend but they are still excited about his arrival, and he seems unfeignedly and unselfconsciously glad to see them.

He tries gently to disillusion them about the supposed glamour of his new life but is very ready to offer them a ride in his limo. Buff is dazzled (though he proves a graceless guest); Suze, scenting an exit route, is interested; Jeff is puritanically, and jealously, disapproving; and Tim continues to spray venom. With Pony comes his publicist Erica, for whom two of the boys make a play, to which she responds with apparent enthusiasm.

The action proceeds through some savagely unexpected reversals to a physical and intellectual showdown between Jeff and Tim, residual but hopeless idealist against incurable nihilist. The male Pakistani, the same age as the rest but seeming older, upbraids the bunch of them for all the world like Doc in West Side Story telling the Jets and Sharks that they make the world dirty; or at least like one of the Sharks telling it to the Jets. In this world, though, there is no redemption.

The story holds, and so does the dialogue, if you accept Bogosian's habit of using the characters as mouthpieces for their ideas, or lack of them. He is almost too ready to start an argument. There seems to be no reason for the play not to seem more powerful than it does, except that we have met characters like these before and this production doesn't do much to give them new life.

Ed Gass-Donnelly's direction has pace and drive, but not much finesse. The actors can handle the more contemplative moments well enough, except when they're extended into prose arias, but for scenes of conflict (which means most of them) they go into automatic attack mode.

This means jerking out every line on an upward inflection without reference to its content or the person at whom it's directed.

Very likely, this comes down to experience and technique, or the lack of them; the penalty of casting a young play is raw acting (though the same director did much better with the same age group in Equity Showcase's Spring Awakening).

It's probably no accident that the most accomplished performance, by several miles, is given by the actor with the classiest resume. Matthew MacFadzean (Shaw Festival and Theatre Direct) gives the visiting Pony a sustained rhythm and a filled-out personality, as of a guy bending over backwards to be pleasant (which he is) and trying desperately to avoid being patronized (which he can't).

There is something glazed about him, but he never snaps. The other characters snap all the time, and the actors buckle under the strain, except perhaps for Noah Cappe as Buff, who goes through the piece in a grossed-out world of his own.

In the pivotal role of Jeff, Gregory Thomas is overtaxed, though he does give good voice to what seems to be the author's own dilemma: Jeff has all the right instincts and he can even put them into words, but even as he utters them he despairs of them ever doing any good.
