JEKYLL & HYDE

MUSICAL REVIEW

JEKYLL & HYDE

Saturday, May 10, 1997

Directed by Robin Phillips

Written by Leslie Bricusse, Frank Wildhorn


  


Starring Robert Cuccioli, Linda Eder


  


Rating: *

Jekyll lyrics plod through pop-cliche litany

IF it werent for the songs, the musical Jekyll & Hyde might not be too bad. Robin Phillips took over as director fairly late in the show's progress toward New York -- it has been on the road in America for ages -- and he has staged it as intelligently and adroitly as anyone could. He points up the drama and throws himself into the more risible passages with gusto. For the most lurid sequences, he bathes the stage in satanic red.

Phillips designed the sets, in collaboration with James Noone, and they give us rich pictures within a spare frame. We are taken on a neatly packaged tour of late-Victorian London as traditionally perceived by the musical theatre. As usual, the place is polarized between a comfortable, uncaring upper-class club land and a resentful, gin-sodden underworld that is populated almost exclusively by prostitutes, procurers and louche music-hall artistes. Dr. Jekyll's laboratory is an especial visual treat: a fairyland of bottles, flames and bubbling retorts.

Elsewhere, bubbling retorts are in rather short supply, Leslie Bricusses book being decidedly short on wit. But it adapts the Robert Louis Stevenson novella with perfect competence, drawing out themes for its own purposes. This Jekyll is a determined young idealist, bright-eyed and pony-tailed (no, honestly), who initially wants to explore mankinds divided consciousness by experimenting on inmates of a London mental hospital. The hospital board members, bishops, generals, society dragons and other uptight types, refuse permission. And when gentle Jekyll, having swallowed his own medicine, turns into horrible Mr. Hyde, he takes a grisly series of revenges on them, as part of a general war on what he calls "hypocrisy" in a society that's clearly rotten with it. (Shades, and they aren't the only ones, of Sweeney Todd .)

The show, faced with the problem of importing romantic interest into an original that doesnt have any, takes the same course as most previous adaptations and furnishes Jekyll with two contrasting female companions. There is Emma, the doctors adoring high-born fiancée, who spends the entire evening faithfully waiting for him in beautiful dresses and drawing rooms; and there is Lucy. Lucy is a loose woman -- you can tell, because she goes around in an off-the-shoulder shawl -- but pure and loving at heart. She works at a cabaret-cum-brothel known as The Red Rat, where we find her performing a number titled Good 'N' Evil . This might be a bit metaphysical for most of the Rats clientele, but it impresses the doctor, who is out slumming and naturally has these abstractions much on his mind. Befriended by Jekyll, Lucy is subsequently, and logically, brutalized by Hyde.

Bricusse as librettist can interest us in his tormented heros grapplings with the monster within, but Bricusse as lyricist thoroughly blows it. You might think that being English -- indeed, a Cambridge graduate -- he would find intelligent, stylish words that would suit the time, the place and the emotions. But he has been part of the mid-Atlantic showbiz circuit for more than 30 years now and obviously thinks and feels in its terms: Jekyll & Hyde adds the fatal extra ingredient of a Grand Subject. When Jekyll, after half a show's worth of preparation, is about to take his first draft of the fatal potion, he sings a big, big song called This Is the Moment . Need I go on? The lyric, perfectly matched by Frank Wildhorn's music, plods remorselessly through every once-in-a-lifetime, climb-every-mountain cliché in the pop litany, scrupulously avoiding any reference to the actual dramatic situation, since that might limit its chances outside.

(Songs like this may no longer make the pop charts, but they can still appeal to the less fastidious middle-of-the-road performers, or to any one of the Three Tenors. Or is that the same thing?)

I knew that it was not really the worst song I had ever heard in a musical. There are dozens like it, and when I switched on the radio that night I Dreamed a Dream from Les Misérables blared forth to confirm this. But that was no comfort at the time.

Other song titles include Once Upon a Dream (Emma remembering her courtship), Someone Like You (Lucy falling for her kind gentleman), and In His Eyes (the two of them independently expressing devotion toward the same man). Its a consistent score, all right. The collectors item is Murder, Murder , a weird piece of staging in which Hyde serially bumps off most of his enemies while the chorus stand a few inches away, fail to lift a finger and then have the nerve to complain about the killer's elusiveness.

As Hyde, Robert Cuccioli gets to do little more than cackle, but his Jekyll is a sympathetically tortured figure. He also has a powerful pair of lungs, though he is outclassed in this department by Linda Eder's Lucy, who cant be all amplification. Eder manages to make the stock musical character of a Cockney drab sympathetic, although in a musical so beholden to other musicals, it is difficult to banish the suspicion, when she turns up in the doctors posh rooms, that she has come to ask him for elocution lessons so that she can be a lady in a flower-shop. If her character is stock, the others are even stocker. Barrie Ingham, a British actor of excellent pedigree, is wasted as Jekyll's father-in-law-to-be, while George Merritt, described in his bio as "one of America's finest operatic baritones," acts stiffly but barely sings a note.

Jekyll & Hyde is at the Plymouth Theatre, New York, for an indefinite run.
