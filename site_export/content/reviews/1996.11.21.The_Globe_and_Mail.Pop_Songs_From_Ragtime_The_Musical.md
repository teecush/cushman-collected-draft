SONGS FROM RAGTIME THE MUSICAL

POP

SONGS FROM RAGTIME THE MUSICAL

Thursday, November 21, 1996

BMG

***½

Powerful songs superbly sung

A LOT will obviously depend on the book and the staging, but the songs on this advance CD make Ragtime sound like the most exciting big musical in nearly a decade. Lyricist Lynn Ahrens and composer Stephen Flaherty -- who wrote Once on This Island , My Favourite Year , and the very funny Lucky Stiff -- have spread their wings dramatically here. There seem to be two layers of inspiration. One is E. L. Doctorows novel, with its interlocking chain of personal dramas set against the panorama of American life at the beginning of the century. The other, more fundamental, is that society itself, and its music. Or, rather, its musics.

In the novel, ragtime is a metaphor for the black presence -- and by extension other new presences -- in urban America: an extremely beautiful new rhythm that can be soothing or disturbing. The score honours it with a stirring Joplinesque title tune that's also exemplary theatre music and with a breathtaking song called New Music that both states and justifies that music's emotional effect.

But ragtime was hardly the only sound around, and the score also takes in waltzing parlour ballads, pepped-up marches, vaudeville specialties, spirituals and political anthems. It taps into the whole tradition of American song (though surprisingly, considering the storys immigrant themes, theres nothing that sounds very Jewish) and the effect is liberating: after all the crabbed Europop scores of recent years, it's a tonic. Some pieces of the mosaic are more convincing than others, but there are no songs that are just pastiche; they all make a point or tell a story. The authors' evident commitment to the subject, their passion and exuberance, carry all before them.

The aim seems to be to get behind Doctorows dry and laconic narrative style to expose the thoughts and feelings the novel lets us guess at. Most of it rings true, and little is sentimental. Even a protest song is kept very specific to the situation. The score has its own modes of irony. There are five first-rate comedy numbers on the CD (The Crime of the Century , Henry Ford , What a Game! , Buffalo Nickel Photoplay Inc. , The Show Biz ) and four of them are double-edged. Another very powerful song, He Wanted To Say , is actually about subtext. The performers, most of whom are in the Toronto cast, are immaculate. The marketing term "concept album" is a misnomer. (Whats the concept, other than doing songs from the show?) It's a work-in-progress; and, for once, progress seems to be moving in the right direction.


  



  


Songs from Ragtime the Musical is available in stores. The official original cast recording is expected to be released late next year. Ragtime: The Musical has its world premiere at the Ford Centre for the Performing Arts, North York, Ont., on Dec. 8.
