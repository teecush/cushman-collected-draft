THE WRECKING BALL

Theatre Centre

- - -

The code of conduct at The Wrecking Ball is simple. Some half- dozen playwrights are commissioned to write, in the course of a week, plays based on events in the news. The resulting texts -- which tend, for obvious reasons, to be short -- are then given barely rehearsed readings for one night only by an intrepid group of actors. At last Sunday night's show, the third of the series, both writers and performers constituted a fair cross-section of Toronto's finest.

Ross Manson, speaking for those in charge, cheerfully announced in his opening address that one of the authors had broken the rules. This was Michael Healey, who under the title Generous offered a scene already written from a play already scheduled (at the Tarragon). A six-hander, it displayed more craft and polish in its writing, if not always in its performances, than anyone was likely to come up with when composing under the gun. Its topicality, though, could not be faulted, as it showed a minority trying to maintain itself in power through foul means (fair ones obviously not worth considering) and foul language. It was Healey back somewhere close to his Plan B turf but with more wicked humour; whether he can keep up this vein of cheerfully black comedy over a whole play -- or even whether he intends to -- we wait, eagerly, to discover.

Of those who played the game, the best was Claudia Dey. A brief like the Wrecking Ball's will bring out either the journalist in writers -- usually the cartoon journalist -- or, as in Dey's case, the poet. Oil shortages were her starting point; her Slick Says it was the Land of Plenty and the Plenty was Mine was a monologue, set in the foreseeable future, for a disempowered U.S. gas-pump attendant, lamenting a way of life that has gone from unsustainable to unsustained: "no more garage suicides," and people are actually walking. Dey might be the Randy Newman of Canadian playwrights, giving a voice to those whom progress, or at least progressive thought, has left behind. Gord Rand, who played the title role in Dey's Trout Stanley, gave the piece perfect voice.

Adam Pettle's The Bleachers was similarly oblique in structure if not in tone, drawing on the reappearance of an old news item. Two teenage girls, their speech rhythms perfectly captured by author and actresses (Patricia Fagan and Monica Dottor), complain and smoke and goof off from class, before going off to kill the mother of one of them. Slight, and apparently written in just one day, it still showed Pettle making a welcome break from his recent folksy domestic plays, in the general direction of his powerful Theatre Direct play, Misha.

In between playlets, Alon Nashman and Tanja Jacobs, both seen to advantage throughout the evening in more conventional roles, appeared as, respectively, Harold Pinter and Ann Coulter, reading from his Nobel acceptance speech and her book. They presented a powerful image of two people with no interest in listening to one another, ever. Pinter, naturally, was the more elegantly stylish, but Coulter, to my discomfiture, and probably against the organizers' intentions, got off the most effective line -- something about liberal discourse (liberal being her mysterious term for left- wing-of-all-persuasions) consisting of saying "Bush lied" to a college audience pre-programmed to cheer.

The two remaining Ball-Wreckers played with this temptation, if finally skirting it. Both took on Iraq. (So did Dey's play but more obliquely.) Alex Poch-Goldin's Doublespeak was a monologue for a Bush speechwriter, a Republican with doubts, in mental search of an America worth fighting for. That's interesting, but it never came to life; the too-easy satire of its opening hung over it throughout. Matthew Edison's Captive Audience tried to get everything in: Christian peacemakers, torture-planespotters, Islamist terrorism and CIA abduction, America blackmailing Canada (10% back on softwood lumber) and Canada playing, I do believe, hardball. The common thread seemed to be that everyone was in some sense a hostage or a hostage-taker. Edison may have the makings of an epic play here, but at the moment it's a mess, in shorthand.

So when Manson calls, as he did on Sunday, for more political plays on Toronto stages, my response is "yes, but." Not if they're diagrams or animated slogans; Political theatre can be as escapist as any other kind of theatre, but it depends what you want to escape from. Besides, everything is political; or so said singer- songwriter Claire Jenkins, halfway through a half-time set in which she also sparred in a kind of musical slapstick with her violinist partner, Tom Howlett. Jenkins' best number, The Band Played On, shares a title, obviously, with a famous song of the 1890s. It also, less obviously, has the identical subject and situation to She Didn't Say Yes, a Jerome Kern delight from 1929.
