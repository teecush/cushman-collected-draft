For the second time this week, I find myself writing about dirty, sexy money. Sexy money, anyway. According to the Galafilm documentary China's Sexual Revolution (airing tonight on CBC) there are more sex shops in China's biggest cities than in most cities in the West. And 75% of the worlds sex toys are made in China. But then, isnt 75% of everything?

They had a lot of catching up to do, and fast. It's happening" says the commentary "at warp speed. There was the cultural revolution, and then there was the economic revolution--pretty much its opposite -- and the sexual revolution is their child: a troublesome one as you might expect, coming from such incompatible parents.

The film, written and directed by Josh Freed and Miro Cernetig, takes us on a guided tour of China's new strip clubs, massage parlours and talk radio shows and liberated blogs that are not so much confessional as exultant. A young lady called Muzemei recorded the sounds of her own love-making and posted it on the Internet; it was so popular the server crashed for days. (Shes a revolutionary," says one observer. "Ten years ago she would have gone to jail.)

It all looks like a hyped-up version of our own world: the gospel according to Sex and the City, except that Sex and the City never took itself as gospel. The liberated sex presented here acts as if had something to prove. In that respect, its the cultural counterrevolution, as earnest as its opposite.

Some of the most compelling parts of the film--if only because they are, to us, the strangest -- concern that earlier time, when Red Guards patrolled towns and villages, and when immoral behaviour - -holding hands, for example-- would lead, at the very least, to a terrifying bout of enforced self-criticism.

The rejection of all this is quite explicit. I don't care what Chairman Mao thinks of my life" says one contemporary young woman. Well, he is dead. And, as the film delights in pointing out, when alive he was a hypocrite, a man of imperial tastes who thought a steady diet of virgins would keep him young.

But the old legacy still persists, and in a malign and unexpected way. China's "one-child policy produced a conflict between the imposed economy and the traditional peasant desire for a male heir. Science came to the rescue; female children were aborted. The result is that young men now outnumber young women by the millions.

No wonder the smart young females in the cities look so pleased; they have freedom and choice. The men, especially the poorer ones, end up frustrated. Theres a huge demand for prostitutes. And, though the statistical disproportion in the countryside is presumably the same as in the towns, the poor young women who have emigrated from the villages, end up supplying it.

China has the fastest-growing sex trade in the world. The film presents Chinas sexual revolution as primarily a woman's thing (maybe because of the Western tradition that women talking about sex make better copy); it also seems to be a rich person's thing.

As expected, there are many of the older people who deplore it. It may be all right for Europe, says one. There even homosexuality is normal." (This is the only time that subject is mentioned. Maybe it just doesnt happen in China. Like in Iran.) "But China doesnt want it.

So who," asks the commentary, in one of those bright summaries guaranteed to set the teeth on edge, "will win? The older generation, or generation sex? How far have they come from Mao to now?

There are clues apparently: It may be impossible to chain the new sexual dragon," (shot of a statue of a dragon, with a chain around its neck) "but parents are coming around. And indeed one mother does say, tolerantly or maybe just resignedly, that she looks at the world and it's just like television, a sigh that might echo round the world.

Where, the film inquires as if not expecting an answer, will it all lead: To a brave new world of sexuality or a Great Leap Backward?" Well, probably neither, but on the evidence provided the former's likelier than the latter, and certainly more likeable.

"This, says one contributing female expert, is one of the funniest periods in China for a long time." A male professor, who has some misgivings about the way things may be going, is still very clear about the alternative, one that he himself lived through: "If the cultural revolution comes again, he says, I will kill myself."

- China's Sexual Revolution airs tonight at 9 p.m. on CBC.
