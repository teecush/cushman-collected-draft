Moliere's The Sicilian isn't quite the oldest play in this year's Toronto Fringe Festival; there's a production of Aristophanes' Lysistrata that has it beat by several centuries and change. But as classics go, it's one of the rarest; it also turns out --and not just because of its scarcityvalue-- to be one of the freshest.

A one-act farce, The Sicilian is one of its author's comedie-ballets; a proto-musical that can accommodate as much song-and-dance as a production cares to give it. This production at the Factory Mainspace by a company seemingly without a name, cares a lot, with results that are eccentrically delightful. The story takes aim at one of the self-tormenting Moliere's favourite targets: the jealous husband or guardian whose efforts to keep his charge away from eligible younger men only succeeds in throwing her into their arms. In this case, the unfortunate man is the Sicilian of the title, Don Pedro (a Mafia don? In the 17th century?), so possibly jealous by nationality as well as nature. He's played by Brandon McGibbon, best known for roles as pale young men in new plays and musicals, here displaying vocal and psychological weight previously unexplored, plus a wonderfully comic ability to turn volcanic rages on and off. Trish Lindstrom plays his intended, rather too aggressively; Seann Gallagher, pleasantly loutish, is the successful wooer, French and hence an object of suspicion. But everyone's a foreigner, since the action's set in Spain. This enables play and production to draw on the tradition of native buskers hired to soften ladies on their balconies or in their boudoirs; the cast includes a pair of musicians (Isaac Haig, also the composer, and Sarah Joy Bennett) who play and sing with the kind of purposeful inaccuracy that requires high technical skill to bring off. Amy Wright's choreography, which does a lot to speed the show along, has been executed on the same principle. Joanna Yu contributes bright sets and costumes. Nicolas Billon wrote the neat translation/adaptation, and probably had the idea in the first place. The first-rate direction is by Lee Wilson.

Adam Brazier, one of our leading young actors, steps into directing with Nebraska (Frangipani Productions, Passe Muraille), which tells the story of Charlie Starkweather and Caril-Ann Fugate, a 1950s Bonnie and Clyde. The show has a country-rock score credited to four different composers, one of whom, Kevin McGarry, also wrote the book and presumably the lyrics, besides appearing as Charlie. It's a helpful score, atmospherically and to some extent psychologically, if you can forgive a line about "our killing and kissing spree" that might have fallen off a movie poster. The script, though, carries most of the dramatic weight, and it very effectively sketches the forces that draw its no-hope couple together: he a garbage collector scorned since childhood, she a standard victim of paternal abuse, asking of her new beloved that he buy her a Chinese panda bear to prove his love. He finds a toy one in a store ( "Made in China" he proudly points out) and kills the storekeeper to get it. And so it starts, the robbing coming in a poor second to the killing as a source of erotic excitement. Soon they're world-famous, at least in Nebraska. It's hardly a new insight that murder is the most effective way for America's dispossessed to get famous and get even, and this show runs the risk of monotony from about half-way through. It recovers itself, with an extended scene of a house-jacking in which tensions keep building and roles reversing. The end, though forecast in the beginning, is inconclusive; Caril-Ann peels off from Charlie and it hardly registers. For the most part, though, Brazier's direction is adroit and sensitive. McGarry and Lindsey Frazier give fine blanched performances as the gun-happy couple; a third castmember, Geoffrey Tyler, is only less effective because his role as narrator and all-purpose support has been none too clearly worked out.

I've been hanging with the heavy-hitters of this year's Fringe. However, the most advance-noticed production I've seen is also the most disappointing. This is Red Machine Part 1, presented by a cadre of up, coming and almost-there practitioners calling themselves The Room (Lower Ossington Theatre). It's supposedly a dramatization of the workings of different parts of the human brain; what we're offered as work-in-progress is a triple bill of plays "inspired by the hippocampus, the primary motor cortex and the temporal lobe." On the assumption that we're seeing them in that order, I'd say the hippocampus rules. The first piece, written by Brendan Gall and called First and Last is a set of Pinter/ Kafka mind-games, wittily written and well played by John Gilbert as a taunting old man and James Cade as a tortured young one. Gilbert is especially fine, proving yet again that a mature actor can take anything his juniors can write for him, often better than they can. The subsequent pieces, by Erin Shields (whose We Were Birds I greatly admired last year) and Michael Rubenfeld, left me agonizingly hostile. Shields' Missing Jesus Church is effectively a monologue, bound up (literally) in masochistic religious imagery; religion persists into the title at least of Rubenfeld's A Tribute to Joseph Smith, though if the founder of Mormonism is actually mentioned, I missed him. The show's technical standards are very high, but somehow that makes it worse.
