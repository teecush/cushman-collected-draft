Barry Manilow once recorded a CD of other people's theatre songs thats more palatable than the efforts of somewhat more pretentious singers with the same material, and he has to his credit at least one haunting tune in the great tradition, When October Goes.

So I would like to think he has a musical in him. But the new songs with which he pads out the story told by his famous old one are generic beyond the call of pastiche. They also sound consistently too late or too early for the mid-1940s period to which they are supposed to belong.

The lyrics (by Bruce Sussman and Jack Feldman) have a few neat rhyme schemes but no other ideas, and the book (by all three gentlemen) is brainless almost beyond belief -- its wisecracks, its narrative, and its attempts at period detail all in stiff competition for the booby prize.

We start with Stephen, a contemporary composer, trying to write the title song; we are privy to the eureka moment at which he realizes that "fashion" rhymes with passion (rather like the resident songwriter on the childrens TV show trying to figure out the right way to get to Sesame Street).

The rest of the song is strung across the evening as the show stretches out the lyric's three-minute storyline. We meet Lola ("she was a showgirl"), played by Darcie Roberts, who has come to town from the Midwest. After unsuccessfully auditioning for Broadway (Hello Mr. Lerner, hello Mr. Loewe), she gets luckier at the Copa, where she struts her stuff in front and on top of a white piano the size of a stretch limo, encouragingly accompanied by the lovestruck Tony (Franc D'Ambrosio), who seems anachronistically to be playing Dick Powell to her Ruby Keeler, though, for a hero, he has a remarkably sleazy singing voice.

In defiance of the rules of fantasy, Lola remains hayseed even after finding work in the Big City. Rico (he wore a diamond"), a racketeer club owner (Philip Hernandez), arrives and dances a bolero with her, at the end of which they are apparently in Havana, the hottest spot south of the Copa. It makes Guys and Dolls, in which the equivalent characters make the same trip by plane, look like ruthless realism. Meanwhile, "special guest star" Gavin MacLeod hangs around as a tough but kindly manager.

Stephen, the composer, at one point ambitiously describes the style of all this as being like an MGM movie; the stage is in fact an empty space populated by significant set pieces (staircases are favoured), occasional famous figures (Veronica Lake appears in a judiciously dim light) and characters in primary-coloured costumes that are sometimes striking.

Wayne Cilentos choreography is as ordinary as choreography gets. The cast perform with the heroic desperation customary in such circumstances; Beth McVey as the obligatory hard-boiled cigarette girl has my special condolences.
