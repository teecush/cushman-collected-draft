PINOCCHIO

Lorraine Kimsa Theatre for Young People, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - The Theatre for Young People has put the boat out for its new version of Pinocchio. We don't see the boat, the one on which the woodcarver Geppetto goes in search of his lost puppet boy, until near the end, but it's worth the wait. It undulates enchantingly on top of an ocean made out of blue cloth. The joy of David S. Craig's production is that it faces every narrative challenge issued by a fantastical story, and solves each one in terms of simple theatrical magic.

Sometimes it's the magic of homemade scenic devices (Glenn Davidson is the designer), sometimes of acting, and often of both together. The climax comes when Pinocchio and Geppetto, having been swallowed by a shark of leviathan proportions, escape from its belly by making it sneeze and getting themselves blown out on the explosion. The effect -- as lights flash, sound rumbles, and the performers hurtle toward us -- is worthy of a 3-D movie.

It's a very long time since I read the Carlo Collodi book -- or, to give you an impression of just how long, since it was read to me - - but Eleanor Albanese's script gives a strong impression of fidelity. There are a few oddities and a few glitches. Pinocchio's famous retractable nose is impressively managed, but it's only allowed to do its lie-detector stuff in one scene. For the rest of the play, the hero's constant struggle between the demands of truth and the attractions of falsehood is barely felt at all. Indeed, apart from the recurring temptation of truancy, his moral dilemmas are skated over. When he makes his crucial choice in favour of unselfishness, he doesn't seem to have much to overcome.

There is -- despite a promise in the press release -- no Cricket to counsel the hero, or even to urge him to give a little whistle. Either there's been a hefty last-minute cut, or the whole business was a Disney invention. (I never saw the movie, but it's in my conscience -- I mean my consciousness -- just as it's in everybody else's.) This Pinocchio does have a self-appointed guardian angel, the Blue Faerie, but she's a simpering ballerina, wafting unaccountably in and out of the action. The horror of the island on which delinquent boys are transformed into donkeys -- Victorian child-correction at its literary extreme -- is underplayed.

There's an engaging cheeriness to the adaptation, though, that feeds on the story's theatricality and also on its Italian origins. The latter is a joke that sometimes wears thin (how many times do you want to hear the entire company sing Santa Lucia?) but it occasions some ripe performances: from Tony De Santis as an endearing Geppetto, and from Robin Craig as a Puppet Master, alternately flamboyant and cringing, with a fixed loathing for his major competition, the Opera.

There is also rich work from Andrew Moodie and Christine Brubaker as the Dickensian con artists Fox and Cat, here claiming to be of English origin, and who knows if it's they or the actors who are lying? These, and many other transformations, are much assisted by Teresa Przybylski's costumes, and by the masks of Trish Leeper, who is also responsible for the design of the puppets.

This brings us to the presentation of Pinocchio himself, which is the most obviously stylized aspect of the show and also, given the circumstances, the most literal. He's a puppet, so he's played by a puppet: not one of your Italian marionettes (we get to meet a couple of those and they comment on the difference) but a stringless figure of the Japanese Bunraku type, complete with on-stage manipulator (Ingrid Rae Doucet) to move his limbs and speak his lines. This is ingenious and arresting, but it does detract from Pinocchio's solitude. There are always two of him, which makes him seem less vulnerable. It does allow, however, for a last heartening coup de theatre when the puppet becomes a real boy -- though, oddly enough, this wished-for transformation is not something on which the script has previously laid much stress. But overall this is a delightful show, the best at this address in a long time. Make haste to Front Street. And always let your conscience be your guide.
