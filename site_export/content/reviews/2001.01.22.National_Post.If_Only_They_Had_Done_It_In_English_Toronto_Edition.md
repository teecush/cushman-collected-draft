TORONTO - Ferdinand Bruckner, German playwright of the 1920s, was a card. Under the name, his real one, of Theodor Tagger, he was also a Berlin theatre manager; and this double identity afforded him the rare pleasure of turning down his own first play. (He thought it was too sexy.) This is the one that has been translated as Pains of Youth.

It received its world premiere in Hamburg in 1926, caused a stir, and launched a lengthy and productive career. Neither the play nor its author have ever been well known outside Germany and Austria. They have attracted occasional attention in the outside world at times when youthful pain has been news: in the 1950s -- when the play's tortured, cynical and hyper-articulate characters were compared to those of John Osborne's Look Back in Anger -- and now. This production, by the enterprising Theatre Voce company, is the North American premiere. It's a trial, since the actors aren't up to the job, but it's an interesting one.

Ed Roy's production comes in an Expressionist wrapping, which initially seems reasonable in a piece born in the Berlin of Grosz and Kokoschka. As a prologue we are given two musicians, traversing the stage while playing piercing Teutonic jazz on bass and sax. We then get a look at the set: a striking design (by David Wootton) depicting a bedroom in which the walls don't meet, the furnishings are composed of huge piled-up books, and everything is tilted and angular; through a screen we glimpse the adjoining room, in which sizeable parts of the action will later be played in silhouette. A character spends a long time scrubbing the floor, in silence. Then someone comes on and speaks; and from that moment style disappears. The actors, as always happens in productions that don't quite know what they're doing, automatically lunge as close to realism as they can get. The set remains, of course, and it still looks good; but it's also unhelpful. I suspect that a design for this play should be either naturalistically detailed or absolutely stark. Truthfully, I don't know what the right style for the piece would be, but I'm sure this production hasn't found it.

The characters are mostly medical students or actual doctors: the women supposedly in their late teens, the men in their twenties. Most of them -- it's never quite clear how many-- seem to live in the same boarding house; the script is very sparse on background detail. (The stage directions say that it's set in Vienna, like Shakespeare's Measure for Measure, which notoriously mentions the place only once. Bruckner's dialogue goes the Bard one better and never mentions it at all.)

Marie, whose room we are in, is a recent and ferociously hard- working graduate. Her neighbour, Desiree, is a dilettante of titled family, infuriatingly capable of sailing through her exams on a minimum of preparation. In the course of the play, the two become lovers, each having tired of a previous male partner. The man who got into Desiree's life was a career nihilist named Freder, whom we see seducing the maid of the house, apparently for the sheer pleasure of turning her into a prostitute. Freder's union with Desiree must have been a battle of control freaks, with the man the predestined winner; Desiree has lots of bravado but no conviction and the imbalance, assisted by drugs, leads her to despair.

Marie has lost her boyfriend Petrell, a would-be poet, to a fellow student called Irene whom she despises and whom, in a fairly spectacular scene, she ties to a hat stand, by her hair. She calls it scalping her. (Coupled with a later suicide, this scene suggests Hedda Gabler with the gloves off.) I should add that Marie is, if not the heroine of the play, then certainly its emotional centre.

All the characters are young in years, but self-consciously old in disillusion. They are the children of the generation that lost the First World War, and -- psychologically at least -- they are living in the ruins; why study or practise medicine, one of them asks, in a world best left to die? They are quick to describe and analyze themselves, and even quicker to describe and analyze one another; the author seems to think that this constitutes characterization, and even action, but the result is that we find the characters at one another's throats before we've even been introduced. That might be exciting, except that most of the actors here are both physically too old for their roles and temperamentally too young; they can manage neither the willed sophistication of these chic dead-end kids, nor their underlying terror. What with everybody wandering into the room as if they lived there, through a confusingly non-existent door in this case, it comes off like a rogue episode of Friends.

Every relationship in this play is a matter of sex and power, with the two amounting to much the same thing, and cruelty can be surprisingly hard to act. The best performance, by miles, comes from the maid; Erin MacKinnon is tremblingly real as Freder's victim. She is noticeably less good, not bad, just ordinary, when she decides she likes her new life, and meets the others on equal terms. Like them, she has to work at it.

Fiona Highet, sashaying around as Desiree, works the hardest and the least effectively; Anne Page manages a certain balance as the pivotal Marie; Christopher Morris as Freder stops just short of twirling a melodramatic moustache.

Linda Prystawska is plaintive but otherwise vacant as the scalped Irene, and I forgot Joel Hechter's performance of her literary lover even while I was watching it. David Jansen rounds out the cast as a doctor who has done time for mercy-killing: a fortuitously topical touch that, like much in the play, twitches at the nerves.

The cast is much hobbled by Daphne Moore's translation (first staged in London) which isn't in English: not with lines like "even in science it's nearly always the charlatan and not the worker who finally attains the laurels," which would leave any actor gasping for breath.

The equivalent lines in German may well sound very hip and hard- edged; here they kill style and belief at once.

But something remains: the anxiety seems genuine, and compassionately meant. I don't know what Bruckner's subsequent plays were like; he may well have gotten slicker but I wouldn't be surprised if this were his best, the one he really meant.
