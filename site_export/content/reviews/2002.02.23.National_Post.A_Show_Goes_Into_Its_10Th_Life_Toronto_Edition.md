CHRISTMAS AT THE IVANOVS

Poor Alex Theatre

- - -

Dancing into town, hot on the heels of The Piper, comes Cats, again. The Piper is largely about rats; Cats is entirely about cats - - which is its problem. (If there are any other fur-and-whiskery musicals, hovering and ready to pounce, could somebody please lock them up?)

Criticizing Cats at this stage is obviously like shooting kittens in a barrel. Here, however, goes.

Nobody will believe this, but when Cats opened in London -- way, way back in 1981 -- I enjoyed it. I even thought it was a good show. This had little to do with its position in the canon of Andrew Lloyd Webber, who had written better theatre music before (Evita) and would do so again (The Phantom of the Opera). It had a lot to do with Trevor Nunn, whose first commercial production this was.

Nunn was moonlighting from his job as director of the Royal Shakespeare Company for whom he had staged a sequence of wonderfully funny and/or moving productions, culminating in the epochal Nicholas Nickleby. There was a distinctive warmth about all of these shows (even Macbeth); they exuded a delighted trust in the resources of actors, audiences and the theatre itself.

Cats, an ensemble musical full of ragamuffin cheerfulness, was an extension of that tradition; unlike the later Les Miserables, which was a travesty of it.

Nunn plainly had a lot to do with that spirit. Though, since the show is not only through-sung but through-danced, much of the actual staging must have been the work of the choreographer, Gillian Lynne. But that was all right, too; in 1981, we were thrilled to see a British musical that could dance.

Cats, despite its cost and its spectacle, was originally an unpretentious show. When it opened in New York, it acquired a new layer of glitz, and lost some personality. (Also, some native colour. Cats is hardly community theatre but still, when a show's lyrics refer to Pall Mall and the Russell Hotel, it helps to have a cast with some idea of what and where they might be.)

Now, in the umpteenth touring version, restaged by Richard Stafford, we have tired glitz: a lethal combination. The current cast leap around a lot and seem very conscientious about it, but what they display is not so much energy as -- to borrow a distinction from Hal Prince, and one I cherish -- energeticness.

The dancing looks far less impressive than it once did. In fact, it looks startlingly ordinary: sub-Fosse routines only distinguished by the funny clothes the dancers are wearing and the length of time they keep going. (At the London premiere, the big Jellicle Ball number was greeted by wild applause and a piercing voice shouting Rubbish! Twenty years on, I have to acknowledge that the solitary heckler was right.)

One vital element that has gone missing is the original semi- environmental staging. John Napiers junkyard set and David Herseys lighting remain and are the most distinguished features of the show, but Napier's work is diminished by being stuck behind a proscenium. It's still a charming moment when a giant human boot descends from the flies to establish the physical scale of the proceedings; its even more delightful to notice that a discarded car licence plate bears the first three letters of the designers name; and the opening lightshow of cats' eyes gleaming in the dark to eerie electronic chords remains enticing. That, though, is before a single performer actually appears on the stage.

I said that the problem with Cats is that it actually is about cats. Well, all right, you and I, we should have such record- breakingly remunerative problems. But look here, the verses in Old Possum's Book of Practical Cats, the T. S. Eliot jeu desprit that supplied the shows inspiration and furnished its lyrics, are not about cats. They're about people, English types whimsically dressed up as cats, and it's their humanity that is the point. (Such as it is. The Eliot verses arent actually very good. They certainly arent very funny.)

In the show, all the effort goes into turning the humans back into cats. Satire has been sacrificed to cuteness. This has paid off commercially, to put it mildly. It's probably the ultimate tourist show. But it's hugely redundant.

People say that the trouble with Cats is it hasnt got a plot. Actually, the trouble is that it does have one. It isnt content to be a series of feline revue spots. It tries to link them together. So Macavity the Mystery Cat ("the Napoleon of crime") kidnaps Old Deuteronomy, who has to be miraculously brought back by the Magical Mr. Mistoffeles. The interim sequence in which the company moons about the stage, intoning we have to find Old Deuteronomy (a non- Eliot line) to an especially drab musical setting, has to be one of the most woeful moments in the history of the musical theatre. (It reminds you of the days when "English" meant amateur.)

More ambitiously, we have the reincarnation motif. One cat will ascend to kitty heaven on an outsized tire and come back after the show as somebody else. The choice falls on Grizabella, the scarcely seen Glamour Cat, who exists only to sing Memory and who we assume has had too many encounters with the wrong kind of Tom. (I don't, of course, mean Mr. Eliot). I would like to think that she is chosen so that she may return as somebody who won't keep singing that infernal song.

Its the scores low point, though Webber seems hamstrung throughout. He once declared that the poems' insistent rhythms practically dictated the tunes. He apparently thought this was a good thing; it isn't. This score should finally have silenced those who use "hummable" and memorable as terms of musical praise. I cant get the pounding melody of Mr. Mistoffeles out of my head. That doesnt mean I wouldn't like to.

- - -

Talk Is Free Theatre is a welcome new group, mainly composed of recent graduates from George Brown. Christmas at the Ivanovs, their initial production, is a Russian play from the late 1920s, banned at the rise of Stalin. It makes savage fun of the festive season, depicting a family (who are not called the Ivanovs) of the late Czarist era who go on being seasonably festive, even as each of them in succession drops dead.

Why attacks on Christmas under the old regime should have disturbed Stalin I don't know; I guess he was just a sentimental slob. But he certainly didnt like anarchy, and this play is determinedly anarchic, if that isnt a contradiction in terms (Even if it is, that never seems to have bothered the experimental theatre.)

The play also satirizes the police, the judiciary and the practice of locking accused people up in madhouses. The play was written by Alexander Vedensky. Additional material is credited by Daniil Kharms.

The Serbian-born director, Aleksandar Lukac, also adapted and, on top of all this, some of the actors are plainly making it up as they go along. In these circumstances it is difficult to assign praise and blame. Blame is due because the show's tendency to wander off into byways, and then into byways off those byways, can become more irritating than amusing. I don't want to upset anyone, but the characters have a related habit of disappearing up one anothers intestines, literally.

On the other hand, some bits, notably in the performances of Michelle Polak and Michaela Hayek, are genuinely chilling. Others are hilarious: There is one actor who looks, from certain fortunate angles, like Eric Idle, and -- with my mind thus set on Pythons -- I would not have been at all surprised to hear somebody say "hes not the Czar-king, he's just a very naughty boy." (Somebody very nearly does.)

Above all there is Kurt Smeaton, a young actor of stern authority and magisterial comic timing, who plays the Narrator. He gets to realize the dreams of actors and audiences everywhere by deconstructing the play and the other performances, while they are going on. He made me laugh uncontrollably. There ain't much fun to be had in Toronto at the moment, and this is worth your attention.
