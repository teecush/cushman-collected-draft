Colleen Murphy's The December Man is a play prompted by the 1989 Mont-real Massacre. It invents a story peripheral to the main event and tells it backwards. That may sound doubly, if not triply, perverse. In fact the choices turn out to be inspired and the execution, by both the author and her interpreters, virtually flawless. Micheline Chevrier's production, a collaboration between Canadian Stage and Edmonton's Citadel Theatre, was received on its Toronto first night with pin-drop concentration, a signal achievement for a play with no intermission and a minimal number of laughs.

It's grim but it isn't heavy. Much of it is familiar domestic drama raised to an unfamiliar level. We start with a middle-aged, working-class couple, Benoit and Kathleen, who seem to be preparing their home for a special occasion. John Ferguson's set is perfect, with just the right degree of respectable chintz, and with a quiet ceremonial air to it that turns out to be absolutely appropriate. We think for a moment that these are parents preparing a celebration of their son's graduation. It turns out that they are executing a suicide pact, and they want the place to look nice when they're found. It's one of the play's accomplishments that their concern, Kathleen's especially, for propriety comes to seem honourable rather than ridiculous. We learn that they did have a son, Jean, and that he has killed himself ahead of them.

Jean was one of the male engineering students present in the room at the Ecole Polytechnique that was invaded by Marc Lepine, and, like all the other men, he was ushered out of the room before the slaughter of the women began. His guilt never left him. It took various forms. He learned karate, so as to be more prepared were there ever a next time. He stopped going to classes by day and stayed out all night, haunting movie theatres. (He saw Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles II, twice.) The model building that was his course project, and whose specifics he had explained to his semi-comprehending but proud and fascinated father, didn't work out; he couldn't get it to balance properly. Finally, he hanged himself. I'm not giving any of this unfairly away here. The play's construction means that it is its own spoiler alert

We're taken back, step by step, through the months that followed Jean's death and then through the months that preceded it. Just about everything rings true. Benoit and Kathleen squabble with one another and with their son like any family, especially one in which the parents have invested all their pride and all their hope in an only child who's almost bound to seem unresponsive. Most people get through stuff like this; these people are thrown into a situation in which they can't. Benoit works on an assembly line whose management has waited decades to install air conditioning; Kathleen was a cleaner, though she gave that up after losing Jean, thereby -- at least in her husband's estimation -- making her state of mind even worse. He, meanwhile, drinks and smokes. Kathleen, a devout Catholic, is sure she'll be reunited with Jean in heaven. Benoit is far less sure; there's a weird sense, coinciding with his undoubted weariness, that he is going

through with their plan partly to oblige his wife. They bicker over many things, but they would never let one another down. Brian Dooley and Nicola Lipman superbly embody both the strengths and the stresses of the marital bond. He's a big, caring and impatient man with a load of justified resentments and the usual fears and inhibitions about communicating with his son. Where the father blusters, the mother nags; she's a worrier whose world collapses when she has no one to worry for. Mourning does not become her. Not every couple would end the story as they do, but we believe that this one would, ordinary though the partners seem. The play makes ineluctable tragedy out of the mundane, like prime Arthur Miller: Death of a Salesman and His Wife, perhaps. In its end is its beginning, and it works both ways.

This means that the dramatic climax has to be the story's start. The author lays out for herself the task of dramatizing the terrible day as it would have appeared to these people -- the casual turning on of the TV, the frantic telephoning that would be comic in another context, the overwhelming but temporary relief--and she brings it off with total conviction. A rather similar challenge confronts the actor who plays Jean, who only comes onstage halfway through, after what I can only describe as one hell of a retrospective buildup. This, too, is brilliantly met, with Jeff Irving conveying the essence of a walking nightmare, shifting between blankness and hysteria. There is a hint that one of the murdered women was his girlfriend, or at least that he would have liked her to be. (The hint is dropped twice: the only instance of the play's overplaying its hand.) The actual details of the massacre are sparingly mentioned, but they're omnipresent; we catch glimpses of them in Kathleen's bitter acknowledgment of how many lives were wrecked, even her irrational jealousy of how some other victims were buried. Christmas is a recurring image in the play. There could be many other ways of dramatizing that one horrific December day, but it's hard to believe that there could be one more effective. - In Toronto until May 17.
