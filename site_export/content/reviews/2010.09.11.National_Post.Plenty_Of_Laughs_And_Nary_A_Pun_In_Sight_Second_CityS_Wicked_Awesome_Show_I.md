Brace yourselves: The new revue at Second City doesn't have a single dud number. That's not all it doesn't have. It doesn't have a title that's a weak attempt at a topical pun. The show is called Something Wicked Awesome This Way Comes; and though that may be a jaw-breaker, it's an apt description of the show itself, which despite the implicit disclaimer, has more -- and more pungent -- political satire in it than any Second City show I remember. Not only that, it has a shape. It's standard practice at this house for characters from an early sketch to make surprise appearances in a later one, but I don't recall any show whose closing number managed to tie together as many previous themes as this one does. It has us surrounded.

Which is what the first number is about. It's a riff on the G20 summit that involves police corralling progressively smaller sections of the

audience until they're finally concentrating their attentions on just one man. (At least it was a man the night I went. His name was Doug. He was a sport.) Meanwhile they shout bizarre commands at one another, and at the public: "Please go back to the suburbs!" "Pay your mortgage!"

The show can cut surprisingly deep. An early number finds a couple, tired of the restrictions that come with being parents, attempting to unload their offspring on the folks next door, and getting a bleak response, not just about parenthood but about the whole family contract. ( "He's going to leave you, or die first -- he's a man.") Much later, two guys, who thought they were having a friendly discussion, find themselves arguing about the Middle East: a subject on which it's almost impossible to say anything without giving mortal offence to someone. Simultaneously invigorated and appalled by the taboos they're breaking, they chase one another around the auditorium -- and, at one point, into the street outside -- yelling "We're talking about Israel!" En route, as it happens, they get off some very handy capsule versions of the basic arguments on both sides. The fact that they do it at the top of their respective lungs seems thoroughly appropriate.

The most sustained piece is a press conference conducted by somebody ministerial with some kind of responsibility for the environment, speedily deflecting unwelcome inquiries by answering them in French, a language of which she's nimbly capable of professing ignorance when it's the one in which the question happens to be asked. This is a smashing spiralling scene in which the supporting cast keep switching between journalistic identities and all available parts of the theatre. (Helen Thomas, seeking new Canadian pastures, pops up in the balcony, peeved at her lack of access.) This is at once the sanest and the zaniest item--the saniest, perhaps -- of the evening. A subsequent, more sober kind of protracted craziness finds everybody forgetting there's a war on, including those who are fighting it.

On more domestic fronts, the obligatory dating sketch takes approach-avoidance to diabolical new levels of consistency and ingenuity. Another item turns relationships into a video game, while yet another finds kids packing for the cottage, i.e. cramming all their Nintendo games into a suitcase. (An impatient mother threatens to punish them by playing Diana Krall records.) There is indeed a lot of stuff involving gadgetry of one kind or another: Backseat driving takes on a new dimension when the person doing it is a grinning menace, trying out all the apps on his iPhone.

The show is anchored by two performing vets, Rob Baker and Adam Cawley, doing their most authoritative work yet. Baker's basic persona is that of the flabby bully, Cawley's is the skeptical, well-meaning everyman -- you see them at their purest as the Israel disputants -- but they find many variations within those parameters. The third male in the cast, Kris Siddiqi, has comparatively little to do, and the same goes for the female newcomer, Inessa Frantowski. The two longer-standing women are fine, Dale Boyer doing a bang-up job of outflanking opposition as the ministerial lady, and Caitlin Howden handling the one musical number, a song that begins as a wan, earnest Joni Mitchell parody and gradually expands into a just-about-everything parody. It's called Words, a title whose universal application is nicely, even beautifully, confirmed at the finale. Chris Earle, the director, has marshalled an evening with a brain, a heart, and--stop me before I get to Oz -- an identity. Some Second City revues may have had more individually hysterical moments, but overall this is the best since the halcyon shows of the '70s and '80s. And on those, since I only witnessed them as a tourist, my memories could be rose-coloured.
