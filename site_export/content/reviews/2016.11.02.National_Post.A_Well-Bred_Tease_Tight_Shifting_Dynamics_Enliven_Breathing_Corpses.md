THEATRE REVIEW

Breathing Corpses Coal Mine Theatre, Toronto

The Coal Mine Theatre seems to specialize in contemporary plays that practically scream for performance but, as far as Toronto is concerned, have hitherto screamed in vain. They may be American (Killer Joe); they may even be Canadian (Instructions to Any Future Socialist Government Wishing to Abolish Christmas). Now comes a British entry: Breathing Corpses by Laura Wade, first seen at the Royal Court Theatre in London in 2005, and a genuine seat-clutcher.

It's also a puzzle. In its first scene a hotel maid discovers a dead male body in the bed of the room shes servicing. In the last scene the same maid finds another male body in the same bed. In between we meet two other sets of characters, five people in all, one of them the man who was a corpse in the first scene. So were flashing back. The play has drawn comparisons to La Ronde, probably because of the way it circles back on itself, with the maid appearing at both ends of the evening but nowhere in the middle. But the comparison doesn't really hold because the structure doesn't give us one character from each scene moving on to the next. It more closely resembles

Harold Pinters Betrayal, which moved steadily back in time to explain, or at least illuminate, its initial situation. But even this parallel isnt exact because the movement of Breathing Corpses isn't strictly reverse linear. It's more crabwise.

Oh, not, not again says Amy, the maid, when she pulls back the sheets in the plays opening moments. It seems that the downmarket inn where she works is a favourite spot for suicides, and this is the second one she has personally uncovered. Its got so that the other employees call her "the Angel of Death": a joke that becomes darker as the action proceeds. For the moment, though, as she reads the suicide note, she's all blithe sympathy and concern; Erin Humphry plays her Irish, with a winsome earnestness and a hint of steel beneath.

The dead man's name, we learn, is Jim; and he had a wife called Elaine. We meet them in the second scene. Jim runs a storage business, and theres a strange and nasty smell coming from one of the boxes. When Jim investigates, he too finds a dead body and the discovery sends him into a deep and, as we already know, fatal depression. Before this happens, he and Elaine seem to be muddling through. Theyre empty nesters; and Elaine, picking up on the image that obsesses both her husband and the play, describes their home as a box. She's cheerful enough, though, to come by with a treat of "bacon sarnies" for Jim and his young assistant Ray. All the performances in David Ferry's production are excellent, but Richard Sheridan Willis Jim and Severn Thompsons Elaine are top of the crop. He plays with a corrugated brow and voice that take him with total conviction from stoicism through anomie to despair. She has an aweinspiring command of London speech and, inseparable from it, London attitude. (The play was written Yorkshire but the production moves it south, to admirable effect.) Simon Bracken gives them fine support as Ray, who is brighter and certainly more practical than his employers give him credit for. We see this trio again in a later scene; and this time it's later in time as well. Jim has retreated to his house, and he's taken all the doors off. Hes trying to dismantle the box.

Wedged between these two episodes, in the dead centre of the play, we find another, younger couple. Kate, who runs a business from home, is angry at her malfunctioning computer, and at many other things. She, too, is haunted by having found a body, female this time, in some bushes, while out walking the dog. She hates that dog; Ben, her partner, loves it. The discovery that Kate has viciously kicked the dog rouses Ben to unaccustomed fury; generally hes the passive one in what seems to be a semi-consensual sado-masochistic relationship; he has, and shows, the scars to prove it. Kim Nelson isn't totally in control of Kate, but her rages are truly frightening. Benjamin Sutherland's as Ben may be even more so, since theyre plainly out of his usual character but logical in the context of the scene.

Kate and Ben provide some clues as to what may be in Jims box, but they aren't conclusive The actual concluding scene is still less so; we can't even be sure of its place in the time scheme. It introduces a final character: a travelling salesman, given an uneasy charm by Jonathan Sousa, whose stock includes an extremely sharp knife. Its presence produces extraordinary suspense; we dont know who may do what to whom, or when, or if.

Its this sense of tight, shifting dynamics, in speech and action and interaction, that gives the play its distinction. Overall it hardly says more than that death is a universal threat that some can disregard and that paralyses others. But though none of the relationships can be developed at length, all are uncommonly believable, first amusingly, then horrifyingly, and - finally - teasingly. But the tease isn't gratuitous. It stays in your mind and breeds there.
