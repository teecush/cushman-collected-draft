Tickets to shows at Toronto's Summerworks Festival, previously $10 apiece, will this year cost $15. That doesnt sound like much, but if you want to take full advantage of a festival containing upwards of 50 shows, of which you can catch four or five on any given day, then it mounts up. The reason for the increase is that this year Summerworks has been deprived of federal funding. Nobody knows the reason for certain, but last year the Prime Ministers office had castigated the festival for producing a play called Homegrown, which, it said, glorified terrorism. (It didn't.) This year the subsidy was withdrawn. What a coincidence.

There is an argument for not subsidizing the arts at all. I don't agree with it, but its an argument. But as long as subsidy exists, it seems reasonable to ask that it be given or removed for reasons more substantial than governmental pique. The official word is they have a lot of arts events to choose from. Right. So it seems a good idea to take track record into account. Summerworks record is excellent. Many of its shows, apart from being fun at the time, have gone on to enjoy much wider exposure; for example, The Middle Place, which won the 2010-11 Toronto Theatre Critics Award for best production and was nominated for a Dora for best play, started out at Summerworks, was subsequently staged by two major Toronto theatres, and was then taken on tour.

The festival's greatest individual success story may well be Hannah Moscovitch, whose place among our best playwrights stems from her exposure at Summerworks. She's had several scripts done there, most memorably The Russian Play. This year, she and her director and leading actress from that show are reuniting for what amounts to a victory lap. By special invitation (as opposed to the usual process of juried selection) theyre offering Little One (Passe Muraille Mainspace), described as a stylish little lullabynightmare" about two mutually adoring, and mutually destructive, adoptive siblings from one of the nicer parts of Ottawa. That might turn out to have all kinds of ramifications, but on the surface it sounds more domestic, less political, than most of Moscovitch. On the other hand, shes an expert at blending the two. Natasha Mytnowych directs, and Michelle Monteith plays the sister, with Joe Cobden, new to the team but admirable elsewhere, as the brother. Which one is little remains to be revealed.

From Hannah's turn to Hannah's Turn (Lower Ossington Theatre), where the Hannah is Arendt (Jewish), and the relationship is the one she had with Martin Heidegger (Nazi). Fascinating subject, however they treat it. Richard Sanger "created (does that mean wrote?) and directs it, and Severn Thompson, previous adornment of Shaw, Shakespeare and elsewhere, is in the cast.

Every year seems to bring a greater number of established practitioners at Summerworks. In that department, though, few shows this year can compete with White Rabbit, Red Rabbit (The Theatre Centre), an Iranian play presented jointly by the prestigious Volcano company and the even more prestigious Necessary Angel, and described (by them) as wild" and "utterly original. Its a oneperson play, and at a given performance the person might be Eric Peterson or Yanna McIntosh or Gord Rand or Natasha Greenblatt or Kawa Ada or (a new name to me) Tara Grammy. Each of them will perform it cold," i.e, they wont have seen a script beforehand. You'll notice a wide range of age, gender and ethnicity there so I think we can take it the play isn't strictly representational. "It dissects the experience of a whole generation, the publicity says. But which generation? The author, Nassim Soleimanpour, is 29, and the producers note, acidly, that he crafted [the play] in such a way that it can travel throughout the world - unlike himself." Heres the catch, for those who may be particular fans of any of the above actors; it isnt announced which will be performing when. So the audience attends cold as well. So much for the star system.

There are star playwrights as well. Sarah Ruhl, the rising American, is represented by Eurydice (Passe Muraille Mainspace), "a retelling of the Orpheus myth from his dead bride's point of view. I imagine she had quite a lot to say about it. This is produced and directed by Kristina Nicoll, whom I know best as an actress, though, come to think of it, I haven't seen her at it in the last few years. Derrick Chua, well-known Toronto theatrical entrepreneur, is billed as godfather," I assume, affectionately.

The versatile Christopher Stanton, a coming man as actor and sound designer, is author and director of Elora Gorge (Theatre Centre), that promises to be Gothic-scary. The title is geographic.

The festival's most intriguing title though may well be The Physical Ramifications of Attempted Global Domination (Passe Muraille Mainspace), which examines the psychosomatic ailments typified by Hitler, Napoleon and Pol Pot - though I'm not sure the last aimed at global domination. Anyway, serves them right.

Time was when you could spend a Summerworks afternoon and evening happily shuttling between Passe Muraille and Factory Theatre, which are just around a couple of corners from one another. Now there are regular venues further afield, though everything, including a few "sitespecific" productions, is in the general area of Queen and King West. Enjoy. And keep cool.

? Summerworks runs until Aug. 14. For ticket and show information, visit summerworks.ca.
