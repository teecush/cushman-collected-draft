Until Nov. 1. at the Bluma Appel Theatre. Box office: 416-368-3110.

Some years ago I met an academic, high up in a U.S. theatre department, who was trying to show what a fearless rebel he was. "I think Peter Shaffer is a big load of nothing," he proclaimed heroically. It struck me that, in the circles in which he moved, he would be likelier to be torn to pieces for praising Shaffer, but to my shame I didn't mention it.

Part of the reason for my silence may have been that, though my own opinion of Shaffer's plays was higher than his, it wasn't that much higher. Amadeus (not his best play, incidentally) was still new at the time, and I thought it a plodding chronicle with a couple of incandescent moments. Despite all the tireless rewriting Shaffer has undertaken since the play's London premiere in 1979, that is still what I think.

But I also believe some of the attacks on the play to be misguided and in a peculiar way self-serving. Most of them, of course, hinge on Shaffer's portrayal of Mozart, the play's title character (Amadeus, "beloved of God," being the composer's significant middle name) but not its star role. That distinction goes to Antonio Salieri, Mozart's older contemporary, a minor talent tortured by the appearance on the scene -- his scene, since he's official composer at the court of the Emperor Joseph II -- of a major one. Mozart is presented as a childish, foul-mouthed buffoon, and no conceivable kind of artistic genius.

There are several answers to this. One is that some people seem to have naive and starry-eyed ideas about the personal behaviour of artists. Another is that Salieri is the play's narrator as well as its protagonist, so the Mozart we are offered is quite explicitly Salieri's Mozart; and Salieri, to put it mildly, has baggage. A third is that even this Mozart is no fool; on the admittedly rare occasions that he talks about music -- the only thing he really cares about -- he does so with sense, humour and point. I am no student of Mozartian biography, but I see nothing impossible in the idea of the man on that stage having written that music.

Salieri finds it all too possible; he also finds it unbearably unfair. Mozart's genius makes him, in his own estimation, a mediocrity, and he asks for the sympathy of us, his fellow mediocrities, in the audience. In his own way, Salieri is Mozart's greatest fan; and the play's best writing comes when he describes the effects on him of Mozart's successive compositions. This led John Simon, the critic of New York magazine, to pronounce the play (on its first Broadway production) absurd on the grounds that mediocrity, by definition, can never appreciate genius. The subtext here is, of course, "I, John Simon, appreciate Mozart; therefore I am no mediocrity."

But I'm afraid that if we accept Salieri's drastic either/or definitions, then Simon, since he is plainly not a genius, is a mediocrity, like most of us. If genius can be appreciated only by other geniuses, then genius is going to be playing to empty houses. Actually, the play shows, in its portrayal of a bitchy and improvident Mozart, it's perfectly possible to be a genius in one area of your life and mediocre or worse in all the others. Conversely, Salieri is an initially decent man who quite consciously turns himself into a villain. The play's real, burning subject is jealousy.

This comes to a head in a searing speech at the end of the first act, in which Salieri acknowledges his feelings, renounces his previous ascetic life and dedicates himself to the destruction of his rival -- which, in the second act, he accomplishes. But, tremendous as it is, this speech is a dead end; because it isn't just about jealousy, it's about God. Salieri, a religious man in a religious age (well, more religious than ours), has offered his musicianship to God, only to find God choosing to speak through Mozart, instead. Now, if this were merely a metaphor for any kind of artistic inspiration, it might wash; but Shaffer (and here, I think, it is Shaffer, not Salieri) seems to be taking it literally. He's making claims for Mozart he wouldn't make even for Bach or Shakespeare or Rembrandt. But if God speaks through any artist, he must be speaking through them all, including, come to think of it, the bad ones.

Anyway, it throws the play off track. "My quarrel is with God, not with Mozart," Salieri says, thereby entering the realms of the undramatizable. God isn't onstage, Mozart is; and we get a school- drama rendition of the rest of the composer's brief, unlucky life, with Salieri pushing the buttons. Much of this amounts to a tour of Mozart's greatest hits, each opera mined for possible biographical relevance. Sometimes this is effective, as when The Magic Flute leads to Mozart's rejection by the Freemasons, previously his only benefactors. Sometimes it's Hollywood kitsch, as when the ghostly Commander in Don Giovanni is identified with the composer's own father, of whom we have till now scarcely heard. Along with that you can take the earlier episode in which Mozart sits down at the harpsichord and transforms a humdrum march of Salieri's into what became the first-act finale of Figaro: the composer at work, just like in the movies.

Morris Panych's CanStage production is visually stunning; inventive up to the very last moment and a great improvement on the stodgy original. Ken MacDonald has set it in a bare, off-cream room into which are lowered a succession of flats, some ugly but all striking. Salieri, like the psychiatrist in Shaffer's Equus, is a role that any actor can play. I don't really mean that; it has to be an actor of great technical resource and personal command. But the personality has to be the actor's own, because the writing is faceless. David Storch, after an uncomfortably doddery beginning, eases into a performance of great economy, intelligence and, when called for, wit; though he doesn't have the transformative power for that first-act climax. Matthew Edison's Mozart starts excessively slapdash, but he, too, collects himself later. The two share a fine late scene -- a new addition, I think -- that does bring their relationship poignantly into focus.

Salieri has a couple of stooges whom he calls the Venticelli, professional gossips and a shameless narrative device. They make Salerio and Solanio of The Merchant of Venice look like live wires. Indeed, all the supporting characters in Amadeus must count as cruelty to actors, apart from Mozart's long-suffering wife, given an unfortunately lumpen performance by Shauna Black, and the Emperor Joseph, whom Robert Persichini makes vaguely benevolent and offhandedly funny.

The play's close finds Salieri claiming to have killed Mozart and nobody believing him; he's denied even that shot at immortality. The irony is compounded, of course, by the play itself having rehabilitated a previously forgotten composer, to the point where Cecilia Bartoli's record company can let her make an album of Salieri's Greatest Hits. They don't sound bad; if that's mediocrity, we should all be so lucky. The same, I think, is true of the play; and you are unlikely to see a better staging than this one.
