REVIEW

THEATRE

Bloodless: The Trial of Burke and Hare Panasonic Theatre, Toronto

Theatre 20 is a new company whose name derives from the number of accomplished performers who are its founding artists and whose mission is to revive interesting old musicals and to develop new ones. They've commenced operations with an example of the latter, which - good new musicals being even harder to come by than good new plays) - is a brave choice. It proves, on this occasion, a foolhardy one.

The foolhardiness stretches back to the actual conception of the piece. Bloodless, which began life a few years ago at the Winnipeg Fringe, concerns William Burke and William Hare, probably the most famous of all body-snatchers, and certainly the most innovative. The orthodox practice in their day was to dig up the recently deceased, present them to physicians in need of subjects for dissection, and pocket the reward.

Burke and Hare, Irish immigrants who flourished (if that's the word) in the Edinburgh of 1824, didn't sit around waiting for nature to take its course. They preferred to help it on its way. Hare and his wife kept a rock-bottom lodging house; Burke and his mistress, neighbours, persuaded them that they were sitting, in relative economic terms, on a gold-mine. First they dispatched a boarder who was in dire health anyway; then they moved on to prostitutes whom nobody would miss. Their preferred method was strangulation: hence the show's title.

Joseph Aragon, who wrote both book and score, is an avowed admirer of Stephen Sondheim, and it shows in his words and especially in his music. That being so, a set of gruesome murders among the underclass of 19th-century Britain was probably the last subject he should have chosen.

Comparisons to Sweeney Todd are inevitable, and he seems at times to have gone out of his way to invite them. His show starts, like Sweeney, with a jovially threatening chorus telling us about the horror of it all, and filling us in on the story. In this case the choristers are the lawyers and spectators at the killers' trial: hence the show's subtitle. But the framework disappears for long periods, and is never very effective when it's around.

Aragon's music has something of Sondheim's sinister, yearning plangency, and might be easier to appreciate in a less reminiscent context. But it's hard not to goggle at underscoring that sounds like a ghostly reprise from the murkiest depths of Sweeney's London.

Unfortunately, Bloodless fails to equal Sweeney's two main attractions: its exciting story (derived from Christopher Bond's extensive reworking of the original Victorian melodrama) and its characters. The real greatness in that show is the relationship between Sweeney, the possessed avenger, and Mrs. Lovett, the amoral opportunist: the symbiosis that makes atrocity possible. There are some faint echoes here, in a cheerful temptation song called The Bugger Is Better Off Dead, and in the attempted portrayal of Burke as a narcissistic tearaway and Hare as the disillusioned voice of caution, but the contrast is only fitfully pursued and the late addition of a sexual jealousy plot doesn't help much.

The Scots dramatist James Bridie wrote a play called The Anatomist whose main character was Dr. Knox, the surgeon and lecturer who bought the bodies, and who embodies medical-ethical problems more complex and absorbing than those of his suppliers. Bridie confined Burke and Hare themselves to supporting roles, and he was probably wise. At any rate, they never come to life here, and so neither does the show.

Knox and his students do figure, at one point singing a Gilbert and Sullivan parody based, like most Gilbert and Sullivan parodies, on the Major General's patter song from The Pirates of Penzance.

G&S's heyday was half a century later than that of B&H. The point, I suppose, is to paint the medics as opera-loving toffs, indifferent to the sufferings of those below. The show's main departure from the historical record is to make the villains' nemesis a crusading prostitute whose best friend was one of the victims. But the implicit arraignment of respectable society is nullified by the fact that once the murderers' guilt is known, nobody on stage cites the victims' status as a mitigating factor.

The populace ends up screaming for Burke's blood and ready to string up Hare by the hair, though their reasons may admittedly have little to do with social justice.

One positive thing the production reinforces is the extraordinary abundance (a fairly recent phenomenon, both here and abroad) of really good actors who can really sing. Evan Buliung makes a magnetic impression as Burke, a braggadocio in his own mind, and Trish Lindstrom, looking taller than ever before, an iron one as Mrs. Hare, the readiest to voice scruples and the most efficient at overcoming them.

Eddie Glen and Jan Alexandra Smith are their partners; theirs are more subdued roles, and in fact splitting the interest four ways may be one of the show's downfalls. With David Keeley as Knox, Jeff Irving as his most troubled assistant, and Carly Street as the avenging angel of the pavements, Theatre 20's company oozes talent.

Especially notable is Kaylee Harwood who plays two roles and gets sacrificed in both of them; in her first incarnation she shares a duet with Street so touching that we feel her loss as much on theatrical grounds as on moral ones. So the talent does extend to the writing; it's good to encounter a lyricist with some conception of what a rhyme is, though he does overdo them and some are dubious anyway. The tone wavers and so, fatally, does the story.

Bloodless runs until Oct. 28. For tickets, call 416-872-1212 or visit mirvish.com.
