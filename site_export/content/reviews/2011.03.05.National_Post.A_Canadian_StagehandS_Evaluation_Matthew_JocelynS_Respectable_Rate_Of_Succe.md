In its upcoming season, Canadian Stage will be shifting from plays with long titles to plays with short ones. I'm only half-kidding. Matthew Jocelyn, the company's artistic director, began his regime last fall with his own production of Fernando Krapp Wrote Me This Letter: An Attempt at the Truth, and his inaugural season is about to wind up with The Cosmonaut's Last Message to the Woman He Once Loved in the Former Soviet Union. In between we have had Studies in Motion: The Hauntings of Eadweard Muybridge and -just about making the verbiage cut -Saint Carmen of the Main. Next season reverts to previous CanStage practice by including Red.

So, I asked Jocelyn over a cup of tea this week, did this mark a major turn-around in his brave new policy? Red, a recent Broadway success, is in a tradition of American plays with monosyllabic titles -Wit, Proof, Doubt -and self-evidently "important" subjects that have arrived on the stage of the Bluma Appel bearing Pulitzers and/or Tonys. Red, whose principal character is the American painter Mark Rothko, is about Art (which as it happens was the title of another ancien-regime playchoice). So, will our seeing Red signify not just a reversion but a retreat?

Jocelyn says not. "It's a brilliant play, and there will be no reverting." He adds that the play, which is set in Rothko's studio, is a perfect fit for Kim Collier, the Vancouver-based artist who directs it and whose productions of Studies in Motion and No Exit have demonstrated a remarkable and sometimes stunning visual imagination if, I would say, a less impressive judgment in choice of texts. Collier joins Canadian Stage full time next season as an associate director, and she typifies the kind of artist on whom Jocelyn pins his hopes and his policy: outside the Toronto mainstream, aesthetically and geographically, and devoted to the breaking down of barriers between drama, music, dance and technology.

He's an outsider himself. Now aged 53, and Canadian born and bred, he was a darkhorse candidate for his current position, having spent most of his career directing drama and opera in Europe. He was hired, he says, "with a very clear mandate to create an arts centre, to show the very best of Canadian artists in the context of the performing arts of North America. We're looking at a range of great Canadians at work, and working out the best place for them. This is a very sophisticated, wealthy, international city. But it also represents both a crest and a trough of sophistication. There isn't a tradition of curiosity. People haven't been taught to be curious about the unknown." He defines his mission as being about creating "zones of discovery as opposed to comfort and reassurance."

Bearing in mind that one person's discovery can be another's reassurance (and, in the immortal words of Tom Stoppard, that the audience doesn't owe the theatre anything, I'd say that so far Jocelyn has succeeded about 50% of the time, which is a very respectable score. Counting productions that Canadian Stage has either originated or hosted, either at the Bluma or in the smaller premises at Berkeley Street, the successes would include Fernando Krapp (though it met with some surprising opposition from the theatrical establishment), Robert Lepage's The Andersen Project and the current offering The Middle Place. Studies in Motion and Saint Carmen seem to me, at best, triumphs of style over content.

The Lepage show did best at the box office, not surprisingly in view not only of its attractiveness but of its creator's reputation. It's hardly news that Lepage mixes media better than anyone, but it's still an event when his work reaches Toronto. Of the shows announced for next season, I'm especially interested by Martin Crimp's Cruel and Tender, because of its author and because it marks Atom Egoyan's virtual debut as a director of drama; by Jocelyn's own production of Marivaux's The Game of Love and Chance, because it's the most celebrated play of a perennially neglected classic playwright, and because it's been newly translated by Nicolas Billon, who two years ago did a bang-up job on Moliere's The Sicilian; and by a German play The Test, because it reunites director Jason Byrne, actor Eric Peterson, and Company Theatre, jointly responsible for the remarkable Festen a few years ago.

Company Theatre, who have a sparse but superlative track record and an indefinable identity, begin with this production a residency, or co-partnership, with Canadian Stage which, Jocelyn says, "could make the biggest difference with them; it could help them consolidate." He seems to be limiting himself to one production a year. "I'm not obsessive about directing. I love it. I'd be emotionally wizened if I didn't do it. But if I did it nine months of the year, it wouldn't be good. This theatre is not about me." It is, though, about his taste, and so far I'd trust that.
