The problem with plays about illness is that there's no one to blame. They can hardly help being moving, but they are also frustrating. The suffering is unrelated to the sufferer, as far as any kind of cause and effect is concerned. To become dramatic, they have to shift focus away from the disease itself and on to the relationships of those affected by it.

Diane Flacks Waiting Room is a good current illustration. The patient is a cancer stricken baby, and offstage. The play succeeds to the extent that it makes us care about the childs parents, and on how much it persuades us that their current crisis exposes tensions that were latent in their marriage anyway. It fails when it goes looking for a scapegoat, providing a far-fetched parallel narrative about a pediatric surgeon battling his own self diagnosed dementia. In the new epidemic of medical drama, Sharr White's The Other Place, a recent off-Broadway success now mounted by Canadian Stage, succeeds better than most. Coincidentally, its protagonist may suffer from one or other of the two conditions presented in Waiting Room: brain cancer or early-onset Alzheimer's. Both are presented as possibilities, and it would probably count as a spoiler to reveal which one is correct. Two things keep the play afloat. One is the fact that its constructed as a psychological thriller, with liberal use of false trails and flashbacks. This turns out to have its disadvantages (you may end up feeling youve been had) but it mostly fits very nicely together and its unfolding is compelling to watch.

The other thing the play has going for it is that it too is a powerful portrait of a marriage, in this case undiluted by sub-plots. The heroine, Juliana, is a neurologist who has switched from clinical practice to shilling for pharmaceuticals. She's at a conference, promoting the benefits of (irony of ironies) a new anti-dementia drug when she feels something snap in her brain. This is a scary experience and not, we learn, an isolated one. Her husband, Ian, is an oncologist, both loving and concerned. His feelings, though, often translate into anger and frustration. For one thing, Juliana keeps insisting that they are going through a divorce, though Ian is pretty sure that they aren't.

The major skeleton in their closet is their daughter, Laurel, who left home propelled by her mothers intolerance.

For once in a disease play, character may be destiny. Julianas paranoid proclivities, it seems, go back a long way; we get to see Juliana and Laurel, in present time, talking on the phone, re-enacting a classic mother-daughter impasse. Juliana is also oppressed by the memory of another young person whom she drove away, her assistant Richard, who may or may not have been involved with Laurel. Sorry to be so vague about all this, but it's that kind of play.

I might have liked it less, had I encountered it in a production less exciting than the present one by Daniel Brooks. It features a rock-hard, attack mode performance by Tamsin Kelsey as Juliana; neither the character nor the actress ever solicit our sympathy and in consequence both, especially in the play's concluding scenes, receive it. As Ian, Jim Mezon is magnificent, radiating rage while still signalling supportiveness; Mezon, always a powerful actor but usually a quiet one, here operates at his highest decibel level ever, and its rather refreshing. There are two others in the cast. Haley McGee plays all the subsidiary females, including Laurel and a young shrink whom Julianna suspects of carrying on with her husband, and makes them all different. Joe Cobden performs the equivalent male function and makes as much of it as anyone could; his roles vary from tenuous to almost invisible, and its a surprise that an actor last seen as the lead in An Enemy of the People should have taken them on.

I'm not sure why the production begins with projections of Old Masters (we seem to have wandered into the Louvre or the Sistine Chapel) but they're certainly good to look at. Judith Bowden's settings, appropriately cool, later prove good at transforming themselves, with the cast helpfully pitching in. One of the locations gives the play its title. The Other Place is how Ian and Juliana refer to their country home - if they were Canadian it and the play would be called The Cottage - from which Laurel disappeared and which they could not bring themselves to keep.

Juliana nerves herself to re-visit it, and has an encounter with the remarkably understanding new occupant, a scene that is gripping in overall effect if suspect on details. The title can have other connotations, and the author is finally unable to restrain himself from playing games with them. He gets even cuter at the end, which has Juliana referring back to the vision of a nubile girl in a yellow bikini whom she jealously saw - or thought she saw - in the audience at the lecture that began it all. We wind up, in fact, where we came in.

This kind of facile rhyming is typical American drama, whether Broadway or off. So is the compulsion to leave us, if not with hope, then at least on a note of peace. This time, though, it almost feels earned. There isn't too much mush.

The Other Place runs through Feb. 8 at the Bluma Appel Theatre.
