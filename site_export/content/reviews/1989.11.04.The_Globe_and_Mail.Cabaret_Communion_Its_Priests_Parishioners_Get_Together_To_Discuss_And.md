Cabaret communion Its priests, parishioners get together to discuss and celebrate the charms of supper club entertainment

Saturday, November 04, 1989

ROBERT CUSHMAN

New York NY -- BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail NEW YORK SOMEWHERE at home I have a boxed set of records entitled Songs of New York. It contains three LPs, so its trawl of Manhattan melodies is extensive. But it is far from exhaustive. Everybody knows that New York is a great place to write songs about.

It has also, traditionally, been a great place to listen to songs. The arrangement used to be that the Broadway stage furnished material, which then turned into popular song. This transmutation happened on records, in jazz clubs, and in diverse establishments where singers and pianists held sway. These joints were variously known as supper clubs, boites, upholstered sewers (a presumably affectionate description current among performers) and cabarets. The last of course suggests Brecht-and-Weill and Juliette Greco to cultural snobs and Liza Minnelli being divinely decadent to everyone else. It is a strange label to stick on the U.S. performance of U.S. songs. But stuck it has, and last month New York beheld its first Cabaret Convention.

Three lunchtimes of panel discussions at New York's Town Hall gave way to three evening concerts with a fourth gala concert lying in wait at the weekend. The convention was convened by the Mabel Mercer Foundation, named in memory of New York's figurehead cabaret singer. Born in Britain at the turn of the century, Mercer arrived in the United States during the Second World War and exemplified the conversational delivery of colloquial lyrics. (That, as far as I can define it, is the genius of the classic U.S. popular song: text and performance.) She had an excellent voice - her records of the fifties, when she was in her prime, are unfussily thrilling - but her style and authority survived its passing. To the end of her life, she could talk in tune. Among her early disciples were Frank Sinatra and Billie Holiday, and her influence has passed into jazz and pop singing. There are even a few rock singers who are Mercerites unawares.

So much for legend. Present reality is that, though there are plenty of fly-by-night rooms where apprentice singers (and experienced ones) can flex their vocal cords, few of them stay around and fewer still pay a living wage. The economics of the game are self-contradictory. When, exceptionally, a performer gets famous - as happened recently with Michael Feinstein - he moves into bigger spaces, Broadway theatres and even stadiums. Feinstein still sings the same repertoire, rooted in Gershwin and Berlin, and he can still, thanks to microphones, sing intimately. But his audience cannot respond intimately, which is half the equation and maybe the more important half. Occasionally - not often - cabaret has had me weeping with laughter and delight, or so touched that I closed my eyes to shut out everything but the voice and my memories. The theatre at its best can hardly do more.

I was in a small room with a drink in my hand. Fellow delegates to the convention deprecated my insistence on this last factor, but I was strengthened in it by the best new cabaret singer to appear in ages, a lady called Andrea Marcovicci. "The drink" she told me "is very important."

Marcovicci sang at the convention, but she also had her own act going in the Oak Room of the Algonquin Hotel, now the flagship venue of New York cabaret. Uncomfortably long and narrow, it still wins on history and ambience. I approached her with some mistrust, engendered by a rather doomy album and by a public image that suggested a thinking man's Madonna. Even her name seemed to promise an overpoweringly throbby romanticism: a veneer that can easily be applied to U.S. love songs but is at odds with their magical informality.

"We are going," she warned us at the start of her Algonquin program, "to wallow." An actress as well as a singer, she had just finished playing Saint Joan; being burned at the stake every night had given her, she said, a new insight into the meaning of the phrase "torch singer." She offered a madcap, self-mocking commentary - the wittiest I have heard in cabaret - between committed cameos of lost, longed-for or unrequited love. It was a Dorothy Parker mixture, and she is the only singer to render Parker's routine pop lyric to I Wished on the Moon, and make it sound as if Parker had actually written it. She never quite moved me, but she surprised, involved and delighted me.

This healthy blast of the new romanticism was balanced by a delightful whiff of the old. Elisabeth Welch is a singer whose New York career goes back a long way: to be precise, to Blackbirds of 1928, the show that gave us I Can't Give You Anything but Love. Shortly after that, she left for Europe, and since about 1933 she has been a much-loved fixture of the entertainment scene in Britain. Only in the last few years have her compatriots rediscovered her. So her career has followed the opposite trajectory to Mabel Mercer's. So has her image: Mercer was housewifeliness overlaid with glamor, Welch is the reverse. As a performer she is supremely likeable (as opposed to the suspect "loveable") and of course she has no need to re-interpret the old masters. Singing Porter, Gershwin or Berlin, she is authenticity personified. Having reached the age where any act must be autobiography, she simply talks to you, in song or anecdote. As well as lighting up the convention concerts, she had her own show at the Weill Recital Room in Carnegie Hall, a room whose elegant simplicity matches her own. The audience reacted to both with a huge collective smile.

Not all the convention performers were as warming as Welch or as bracing as Marcovicci. The curse of cabaret - one that it shares, though it would be horrified to admit as much, with pop and country music - is that it sees itself as the Last Bastion of Love. This can result in emotive stridency, in hard-sell vocal buccaneering masquerading as swing, and in displays of what I can only describe as aggressive vulnerability. All of these were well in evidence at Town Hall. But there were also glories. Jackie and Roy, a jazz duo of venerable youthfulness, pursued a sparkling musical courtship barely dimmed in 40 years. Julie Wilson, the reigning cabaret diva whom I often find too studied in dramatic songs, sang funny ones with regally eccentric force. Sylvia Syms, another veteran whose public persona has all the acerbity of a vat of chicken soup, becomes another person when she sings: toughly sensitive, sharply pointed, subtly rhythmic. (Both jazz and cabaret people love her, which is rare.) Barbara Lea, who started in the fifties and is now a consummate singer of popular songs - she acts from within and swings without sweat - stilled the house with a hushed, architecturally shaped Begin the Beguine (of all stereotyped familiar things). She plays Toronto next March. Weslia Whitfield, a younger, jazz-inflected lady from San Francisco, tried a little too hard but is plainly going to be exceptional. Barbara Carroll, a great unsung jazz pianist, proved also to be a good unsung singer. Two other musicians - Anne Hampton Calloway and Daryl Sherman - upheld the treasured image of the smart, funny woman sitting at the piano. An even funnier female named Julie Kurnitz sang newly minted sagas of misfortune and disgust. She also paid her respects to the classics. "Kurt Weill," she told us, "is one of the great popular composers of all time." She wasn't going to sing one of his songs. She just thought we should know. She was the discovery of the week, and manna from heaven.

It was ironic, of course, that all this cabaret talent could only be accommodated in a concert format that, by definition, is not cabaret. But it was close enough to proclaim the form, and to validate it as the best showcase for a repertoire that is still alive, and still being added to. It has two hang-ups. One is the conviction of the audience that the word "cabaret" signifies something too expensively rarefied for them, or too cheaply sleazy, or even both. The other is the reluctance of practitioners and promoters to accept that they are no longer in the mainstream of show business, with a clear shot at the really big money. There are enthusiastic claims about breakthroughs and changing tastes whenever a young face is spotted in the audience. There is a young audience for cabaret - there has to be, since there is a ceaseless stream of young aspirants to perform it - but it is a minority audience. That does not in itself spell cultural or financial disaster, but it can be hard to adjust to. (Black singers, once the backbone of the craft, are now conspicuously absent.) Mabel Mercer never made millions. But she survived. She still does.
