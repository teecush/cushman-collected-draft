The golden age of the American musical was the 1920s, when people had money to spend and no television or talking pictures or long- playing records to spend it on, when there were 50 shows running in New York at any given time, and when Florenz Ziegfeld, the master showman, was exciting the senses with his Follies and taking a flyer at narrative art with Show Boat.

The golden age of the American musical was the 1930s, when the Depression sent scores of the brightest talents out to Hollywood but also when those who remained, presumably feeling that they had nothing to lose, did some of their best work, and when the great songwriters -- Cole Porter, Rodgers and Hart, the Gershwins -- were at the height of their powers.

The golden age of the American musical was the 1940s and 1950s, when the era of great songs gave way to the era of great shows, with enough overlap to keep everybody happy; the time when Oklahoma! ushered in a long line of musicals with stories that made dramatic sense and numbers that were there to help tell them; the period that produced the dozen or so shows that constitute the Broadway musical repertoire.

Obviously, the above statements cancel one another out. Each, though, has something to be said for it, and last week they were all advanced with equal earnestness by Julie Andrews, in her capacity as narrator of the six-part series Broadway: The American Musical (PBS, and available on DVD).

Of course, the contradictions werent her fault; she was reading somebody elses script, instructing us in the basics of Broadway history as if she were running the little von Trapps through their scales. The cheerleading tone was preserved even into the series' last instalment, though if the Cameron Mackintosh/Andrew Lloyd Webber/Walt Disney musicals were presented to us as any kind of golden age, it was as a golden age of marketing.

The choice of Andrews as front woman for the American musical has been criticized on the grounds that she is and sounds anything but American. But the series offered us such a tourist's view of the musical that it seemed appropriate to have a brisk foreigner as guide. Or would you rather have had Carol Channing showing you around?

Nobody, given the amount of ground it had to cover and the amorphous audience it was addressing, can have expected this series to be anything but superficial. In the six hours, I counted just one provocative comment: Stephen Sondheims observation that West Side Story, despite its dead bodies, has a happy ending. But, accepting the limitations, they did a good job. They zeroed in on a selection of representative shows, and tried to relate each in its artistic and historical context. Newsreel and performance footage came movingly together in the treatment of On the Town, for example, a wartime show about sailors on leave grabbing 24 hours of happiness, watched by an audience for whom every goodbye might prove final.

Illustrative scenes came from all over, mostly from whatever might have strayed onto television. This often meant The Ed Sullivan Show (including, amusingly, the mild spoof on Sullivan himself in Bye Bye Birdie). A clip from the original Guys and Dolls revealed to me why Sam Levene is regarded by those who saw him as the irreplaceable Nathan Detroit. William Tabbert, standing at a piano singing Carefully Taught from South Pacific, flanked by Rodgers and Hammerstein themselves, showed how angry that song is. I realized for the first time how right Alfred Drake, with his Shakespearean sound, must have been for the cowboy hero of Oklahoma! (The 40s and '50s were the golden age; certainly the most confident one.)

When they showed movie clips they were refreshingly honest about it, and they generally stuck to films involving the stage performers, many of whom -- had anyone thought of recreating numbers in the TV studio? -- are now seriously unavailable.

That we never saw or heard a song complete was, given the program's documentary rhythm, forgivable. Numbers done full-length, with nobody but Sondheim or Hal Prince allowed to comment on them, would be another show, and one Id like to see; as I would a history of Broadway seen through the drawings of Al Hirschfeld, present here purely, and less interestingly, as Grand Old Man.

I would have liked more about performers generally; they started off beautifully with Bert Williams, Ziegfelds black star and plainly one of the great comic artists, and with Ethel Waters, but then lost interest. I would have liked more cross-referencing -- people talking about shows they weren't in. The series ran out of steam in the '80s, which -- considering most of the shows it was talking about -- was no coincidence.
