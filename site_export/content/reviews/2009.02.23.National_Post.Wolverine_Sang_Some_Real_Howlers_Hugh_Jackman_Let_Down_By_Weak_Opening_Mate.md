'This year's Oscars," some shill said before they started, "would be like no other." That wasn't true -- there's no way of changing the essential nature of this self-congratulatory schmoozefest, except perhaps by doing it without an audience -- but these were supposed to be different because of the recession. On the strength of the first hour, I can't say I'd have noticed. The stage did seem smaller than usual -- the performing area was narrowed to a blue-tinted circle, with a few ugly pillars behind it --but that seems to have been a design choice, probably no cheaper, at least in money terms, than any other.

Is it possibly more economical to hire a song-and-dance man as host, rather than a comedian? It seems unlikely, and any way it turned out to be a non-issue since Hugh Jackman turned out to be more valuable to the occasion as a chat-dispenser than as a musical performer. After being given the briefest of introductions -- maybe economy was at work here, or at least an attempt at good taste -- he launched into a spiel about actors stretching themselves -- i. e. performing as characters from countries other than their own. It doesn't sound much to get excited about -- doing accents is a useful acting skill, but hardly what it's all about -- but he was at least able to prove by enumeration that there's been a lot of it about this past year, most of it award-nominated. He then remarked that he, an Australian, had appeared this year as an Australian in a film called Australia, and all it had got him was the hosting gig. Next year, he said ins the spirit of downsizing, he was doing a film called New Zealand. All right, it's not that hilarious, but there was a certain point to it. And he was very assured doing it, and otherwise buttering up the audience, or at

least the stars in the front row. Maybe, if musicals give out on him, he can go into stand-up comedy, or its more fashionable stepchild, chat-show hosting.

His musical turn on the Oscars -- the reason, he imagines, for hiring him -- certainly let him down. Nothing wrong with the way he did it, but the material was awful. It was the old Billy Crystal routine of listing the nominated movies' names and doing bits round them, but without an ounce of inspiration.

A few points of interest: There were shout-outs to pictures like The Wrestler and The Dark Knight that weren't nominated but everyone thought should have been. There was a bit about not having seen The Reader, presumably because a song-and-dance routine referecning the Holocaust might strike even Hollywood as an uncomfortable stretch. And Anne Hathaway got to duet with Jackman in the Frost/Nixon bit, and proved she can sing, too. Someone should take advantage of that sometime.

Anyway, Jackman is one of those actors who can talk to the audience in his own person and still sound natural. Others who in the first stretch could do it turned out to be Whoopi Goldberg (because she's a comic), Goldie Hawn (ditto, and also because she's still an enchantress) and Tilda Swinton (I don't know; maybe because she's British and experimental).

Steve Martin and Tina Fey did it brilliantly, introducing the screenwriters awards. They even managed to undermine,, if not the whole idea of the awards, then at least their intolerable self-seriousness. Bring them back next year. Cohosts. I wouldn't even mind if Jackman made three. They have whole year to write him a good song.
