Theatre Reviews

Waiting for Lewis

Buddies in Bad Times Theatre, Toronto

The Other Side of the Closet

Young People's Theatre, Toronto

A good old playwrights' rule states that if a play has a message, the characters had better not know what it is. The urge to do plays that are ostentatiously about issues afflicts all levels of Canadian theatre but it is especially rife among those companies who do shows for kids. These productions often give the impression that the creators have mistaken their vocation and would have been happier as social workers.

The company known as Theatre Direct Canada are, from what I have seen, an exception. Their show The General (which I saw when they brought it to my own children's elementary school) might be said to have been "about" community involvement, but it took shape as an individual story about an eccentric crossing-guard and two reasonably complicated kids who came to his assistance when he was abruptly fired.

Their new play Waiting for Lewis, playing for two weeks at Buddies in Bad Times, is a more complex piece designed for older teens; and the company's publicity, which doesn't always do their work justice, suggests that it is "about" careers. But that word never crossed my mind while I was watching it and -- more significantly -- it never passed the lips of the actors either. The play, written by Fabrizio Filippo, concerns two households in emotional chaos; it deals in people, not abstractions, and thereby makes a profounder connection with its audience.

The first scene, hilariously well-observed, shows a family moving from the city to the sticks. Dad, an architect, wants to rediscover his vocation. Mom wants to keep everybody happy. Son Ben is doing the driving, and writhes uncomfortably when told how well he is doing. Younger sister Carey is in a mood of foul-mouthed resentment at her enforced departure from civilization.

Once arrived, in a small town that seems to be its very own Bible belt, father lets his dreams slide while his wife turns into the complete nest-maker -- to nobody's gratitude. In truth, they rather fade out while the play focuses on their children's relationships with one another and with their new schoolmate Lewis. Lewis lives with an alcoholic and smotheringly possessive mother. He is the local high school's star student but a loner nonetheless. He has some charm and distinction (he wears a suit, for heaven's sake, which is so uncool it's cool) and he is wildly unhappy. He plays on Ben's insecurities. (They first meet in the school washroom where Ben is having a self-conscious smoke. Lewis inquires whether he is trying to quit to which Ben replies that he is trying to start.) Sexually fearful himself, he tells Ben that Carey is sleeping around -- an allegation that, when she hears it, sends her into free fall.

The play hinges -- less firmly, I think, than the author would like -- on the visit to town of its one famous son, a rock star; on the enthusiastic reception he gets from a community that disapproves of everything he represents; and on the rejection by Lewis, himself groomed for success, of the hypocrisy involved. It isn't that much of a progression -- Lewis has seemed like a nay-sayer from the start -- and the choice of a pop-idol as focus seems rather obvious. What counts is the play's texture, the ebb and flow of its dialogue as it charts the simultaneous yearning of adolescence for privacy and for contact. Most unusually among plays in this area, it dares to be elliptical. It is staged by Joanna McIntyre with both sense and sensitivity, and finely acted, especially by the juniors: Shaun Benson, Gil Garratt and Maryke Hendrikse. I have seen plenty of plays for adults that were less grown-up.

The other side of the coin is The Other Side of the Closet, a previous season's success now revived at the Young People's Theatre. This, as the title amply indicates, is an upfront play about coming out. As didactic drama goes, Edward Roy (who has also written and directed a couple of plays for Theatre Direct) has written a superior example of the species. But even its complexities are simplistic. It starts with three boys yelling "faggot" at someone unseen, and from there you can count off the minutes before one of them is revealed as a closeted gay himself and his buddies start persecuting him.

It's only slightly less predictable that the most vicious of his attackers should be revealed as over-compensating for his own insecurities. This is a comforting thesis, and it certainly happens in real life, but maybe not as uniformly as playwrights would have us believe.

Meanwhile, there are two girls present: one of them thoughtlessly prejudiced and the other shiningly enlightened. The author does his sporting best to give her some flaws, but there is no escaping that she has all the answers, apart from a few that the hero has to learn later from fellow-students in a gay school program.

He himself is decently characterized, his experiences an updated reflection of what the author himself went through in school. But even with this role, the impression remains that this is a play in which the author has decided on an issue and then devised people to fit it.

They all seem to know they're in a play about homophobia, and their and the author's awareness makes the teen language -- however accurate it may be line-by-line -- sound very self-conscious.

The YPT's slogan is "theatre for a better world" but good theatre, in itself, makes a better world. (And that is something kids should be taught.) Plays like this seem to exist less for their own sake than for that of the discussion afterwards. Bigotry of any kind is a dead subject for a play unless you're prepared to enter into the minds and hearts of the bigots. And for that, pamphleteering plays like this have neither the time nor, seemingly, the inclination.

The performances, under the author's direction, are mostly good; and the social and educational intentions admirable. We are always told that plays should ask questions.

But this kind of theatre has already decided what the answers are, and the audiences are expected merely to agree with their instructors. Don't they get enough of that at school?
