THE LIFE

Thursday, May 8, 1997

Directed by Michael Blakemore

New York -- Written by Cy Coleman, Ira Gasman and David Newman


  


Starring Pamela Isaacs, Lillias White,


  


Sam Harris and Vernel Bagneris


  


Rating: ***

Life's hard to love, but worth respect

Special to The Globe and Mail

NOBODY could accuse The Life of being a glitzy musical. It is, by Broadway standards, a small musical in a small theatre. The life in question is that of prostitution; the play also finds generous space for the life of pimping, and some for those of drug-dealing and addiction. They are all, of course, intertwined.

After a brief introduction sardonically lamenting the cleaning up of midtown Manhattan, it gets down to the business of business. We are whisked back to 1980 or thereabouts, when vice around Times Square was at its peak. It is celebrated, but it is not glamorized.

Sam Harris, a lithe and accomplished actor-singer-mover with bundles of CDs to his credit, plays Jo-Jo, a small-time procurer and yes-man on his way to the top, or at least to survival. Jo-Jo is our guide and, tirelessly ingratiating though he is, he may be the least endearing front man a musical has ever had -- Pal Joey with the lovable bits left out, or better yet, Sportin' Life from Porgy and Bess , after the boat that was leavin' soon for New York had finally got there; a white Sportin' Life, although most of the other characters in The Life are black.

By contrast, the warmest character is Sonja, 10 years on the streets and past her prime at 26. Lillias White plays her, and when she sings a bitingly uproarious song called The Oldest Profession ,she brings down the house, not only with her gospel trills -- which theatre audiences always rise to, regardless of context -- but with her intelligence. (It's implied that the character is an early victim of AIDS.) Then there's Vernel Bagneris, the rubber-legged hoofer from the show One Mo' Time and the film Pennies from Heaven , easing up as a bartender with memories of show-business glory.

The Life features a compact and compelling corps of characters going through flavourful routines about being busted on the street and charged at the station; numbers with blunt titles such as My Body or Why Don't They Leave Us Alone , which Joey McKneely, the show's choreographer, has mounted with aggressive insouciance. Everyone on stage seems at ease with sleaze.

Michael Blakemore, an ace among British directors, has staged these scenes for maximum character value against Robin Wagner's bleakly unforgiving set.

So far, so impressive -- an aware, updated Guys and Dolls . There is also a plot, which remains subordinate in the first half of the show but gets dangerously assertive in the second. To be exact, there are two plots. One concerns Mary (Bellamy Young) an apparently virginal new arrival from the sticks whom one of the guys picks up to prove to another that any chick can be turned to profitable account.

She turns out to be a lot more knowledgeable than they reckoned: an example of the show getting its cynical wires crossed. It seems to be trying to prove simultaneously that there is no innocence and that innocence can be corrupted. Mary ends up with a promising future in porno movies, singing an uncomfortable duet with her producer called People Magazine . We know the scene is meant to be ironic, but it doesn't play that way, maybe because the authors are completely inside their musical idiom rather than at a sarcastic distance from it.

Then there's the more momentous story of Fleetwood and Queen. Queen -- played with a warm heart, a sharp tongue and an invigorating larynx by Pamela Isaacs -- loves and works for Fleetwood (Kevin Ramsey), a Vietnam vet with a damaged psyche and a coke habit. Memphis, the top man on the block -- toweringly embodied and toweringly sung by Chuck Cooper -- wants Queen as part of his own stable. This triangle gets pounded through scenes alternately brutal and maudlin, taking up a lot of time and trailing a lot of loose ends.

The power of the show is Cy Coleman. He co-produced it, wrote the book (in collaboration with David Newman and lyricist Ira Gasman), collaborated on the dance and vocal arrangements, and composed the music. He is one of Broadway's best composers, about the only one who still writes what we used to call songs, that is, shapely, autonomous melodies that please on first encounter, sound as if they would bear rehearing and attract pungent and bouncy lyrics -- songs, that is, that you do not despise yourself for liking.

There are plenty of them here, in those sections of the show that are strictly street-life; they are maybe the hottest, jazziest things Coleman has written since Sweet Charity in the mid-sixties. The Life is in some ways the coming-out version of that show: It doesn't call its hookers "hostesses," and it offers one song, Someday Is for Suckers , that might be the hard-driving answer to Charity's There's Gotta Be Something Better Than This .

However, when the melodramatic story takes over, Coleman starts writing the same kind of hollow, overblown dirges that everyone else turns out these days when emotion seems called for, and putting an overlay of soul on it doesn't make it sound any better. When Queen sings a fond song about her lover called He's No Good , it's like the hundredth retread of My Man . When she recalls We Had a Dream ,you start reflecting about what a happier musical theatre it would be if the word "dream', noun or verb, were banished from every lyricist's vocabulary.

When she and Sonja salute each other with My Friend ,it's time to leave the theatre; the show's running late anyway. But it's worth returning for the finale, which strikes the same bracingly sour note as the beginning, although, after all we've been through, more powerfully.

The Life is not an easy show to love, but a surprising amount of it is worthy of respect.

The Life is at the Ethel Barrymore Theatre, New York, for an indefinite run .
