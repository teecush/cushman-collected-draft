LAST ROMANTICS

Berkeley Street Theatre

- - -

Charles Ricketts had a great success in 1924 with his designs for the first London production of Bernard Shaw's Saint Joan. Before seeing Last Romantics, of which he is one of the two central characters, I knew little else about him. Of his fellow-artist and life-long companion, Charles Shannon, I knew absolutely nothing. Michael Lewis MacLennan's play has dispelled some of my ignorance, but not enough.

One thing I learned was that Ricketts only turned to stage design late in life as part of an increasingly frantic effort to keep his and his partner's bodies and souls together. The Saint Joan connection isn't specifically mentioned, and there's no pressing reason why it should be, but you can tell that Ricketts must have been the ideal man for the job.

Shaw's play is about the end of the Middle Ages, or at least it keeps saying so, and Ricketts's own artistic sympathies seem to have stopped short not much later. A collector and museum consultant as well as a practitioner, he loathed the Impressionists and shuddered at the mere mention of Van Gogh. He became very unfashionable and all-but-unemployable. He was not very nice about it.

Ricketts and Shannon met as students. Shannon had seen himself becoming a commercial artist, but Ricketts saw higher things in him, thought him, in fact, a new Michelangelo.

Shannon seems to have had doubts about this, and the play shows him going so far as to consider settling down with a lady model. Ricketts is incensed, but all bets, amatory or artistic, are off when Shannon falls off a ladder, hits his head and loses, at the very least, his memory. Ricketts devotes the rest of his life, and of his monetary and psychological resources, to caring for his friend.

So it's a love story, but of necessity a one-sided one. Shannon narrates it, presumably from some mental plane of his own, but he's a blank even before his mind gives way. Oliver Dennis wins our sympathy in the role but not really our interest.

Ricketts, by contrast, is full of qualities, few of them likable. He professes to despise worldly success but is viciously jealous of those who achieve it; his possessiveness is, if anything, intensified by Shannon's accident; his tastes are obscurantist. We can hardly afford to mock him for that last quality -- time makes philistines of most of us -- but it adds to the dossier, and he is peculiarly arrogant about it, as about much else.

The actor, Julian Richings, sniffs out the character's meannesses with ferret-like determination and displays them with a thoroughness that may be forensically admirable but is theatrically monotonous. The man is meant to be the centre of an informal salon, but you can't see why he would attract visitors; he doesn't have the glamour.

What Ricketts does have going for him is his devotion, both to his friend and to the art he does like. Retained as an adviser by the National Art Gallery in Ottawa (the play breaks out in a sudden, though historically legitimate, rash of Canadian content), he hatches a complicated scheme for buying an Italian painting before which he and Shannon had once stood in youthful awe.

He is convinced that, if en route the picture can be hung on their own wall, the sight of it will shock Shannon back to his senses. "We're on the cusp," he cries, at every scent of an upturn in their fortunes, medical or financial. Richings does find a piercing truth in moments like these, and then the play comes temporarily into focus.

Most of the time, though, it sprawls, especially in its first half, which takes us back to the Gay '90s -- at undue and ultimately irrelevant length since the play is not, in any historical or polemical way, about homosexuality. Oscar Wilde is on trial, again, and everybody is talking about him, mostly disapproving of his lack of discretion. I doubt that we need another play that invokes Wilde as a dominating, though largely unseen, presence -- or at least it can wait until after someone has brought us Stoppard's The Invention of Love -- though when Oscar does appear, in Reading Gaol, he gets one of the strongest scenes in the play -- one absolutely devoid of both mannerism and aphorism, and played with sad strength by Michael Hanrahan. (It's the centrepiece of a triptych of celebrity; the same actor also plays MacKenzie King and the French painter Puvis de Chavannes.)

In the light of Hanrahan's forthright performance, it's ironic that many of the other characters seem to have stepped out of an especially campy production of one of Wilde's lesser plays. Ricketts illustrated all of Wilde's books except one, the fateful exception being Salome, whose pictures made Aubrey Beardsley famous.

He is played by Steven McCarthy who looks appropriately exquisite but seems to have taken a celebrated declaration of Beardsley's -- "of course I'm affected; even my lungs are affected" -- and extended it to the vocal chords; I could hardly understand a word he said.

I was equally foxed by Jonathan Crombie as a Canadian cultural flunky (strangely enough, he was far more at ease as an Englishman).

More comprehensible but equally mannered are Barbara Gordon and Kate Hennig as a team of aunt-and-niece lesbians who write novels under the collective pseudonym of Michael Field. They seem to be doing Bloomsbury with a vengeance and strictly from the outside in. Interestingly, when given the chance later on to drop the nonsense and express genuine anger or genuine concern, they both take it.

Vickie Papavs goes in and out of credibility as the model but is certainly luscious.

The acting, in fact, is a confusion of styles; Richard Rose has directed the scenery more sensitively than he has the performers. Charlotte Dean, the designer, has provided a set of mobile walls between which the characters gracefully glide. They are covered with empty frames, within which we are to imagine the Ricketts-Shannon collection of old masters, lovingly assembled and painfully disposed of.

MacLennan wrote the admirable play Grace, seen in Toronto a few years ago, and there are hints of another fascinating piece buried somewhere here. But it hasn't got out.
