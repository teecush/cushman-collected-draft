KINGFISHER DAYS

Tarragon Theatre, Toronto

In Kingfisher Days, Susan Coyne plays Susan Coyne so confidently you'd swear shed been doing it all her life. She also wrote the script, which must obviously help. The play is, of course, based on her childhood memoir, which everyone except me has read. The dramatization brings her on in two guises: as an adult, doing the narration, and as a five-year-old child, doing the play.

My experience of watching grown-up actresses play little girls of five is rather limited, but I cannot imagine that many have done it more acutely or more completely than Coyne does here. Eyes shining and nose tip-tilted, she has the perfect attitude toward the grown- ups in her life: entreaty laced with blackmail. She is clear about everything, even the things shes puzzled by.

The story concerns one of several summers that she, a Toronto child, spent by the lake in Kenora with her family: a scholarly and abstracted father, a mother loaded with practical proverbs, and an assortment of siblings and half-siblings, right down to baby Andrew Coyne, here tagged, ornithology-fashion, as red-cheeks, tufted crest. Mother and father are the only family members who are portrayed on stage, but we get slides.

Susan has an additional father figure in the next cottage: a retired schoolteacher named Mr. Moir. Mr. Moir introduces her to literature -- to Lewis Carroll (Susan, like many well-brought-up and inquisitive girls, has a decided affinity with Alice) and, by subtle manoeuvre, to Shakespeare.

It goes like this: In a ruined fireplace, sole remnant of a cottage that once stood between the other two, Susan finds letters bearing the signature Nootsie-Tah. Nootsie-Tah is a fairy princess, banished for a time from her home in Peru and spending the interim (a thousand years) as lady-in-waiting to Queen Mab. Susan sends replies, of gradually increasing confidence and length. Nootsie-Tah is proudly illiterate but dictates her letters to a scribe, who of course is Mr. Moir. Susan dictates hers to her mother.

It could be cloying, and there are in fact a few letters too many; the situation is not in itself dynamic. But it holds us by working on two levels. We come to believe in Nootsie-Tah, her travels and travails, and we also enjoy keeping track for ourselves of the effect that composing her correspondence has on Mr. Moir. He's still a teacher, and a creative one with a flair for improvisation; he seizes, gently, the opportunity of opening a young mind and a young imagination.

He's given a jewel of a performance by Joseph Ziegler, very easy and crinkled but full of bottled-up gentlemans relish and meshing perfectly with Coynes implacable eagerness. We see two kinds of wonder feeding off one another, though I wish the play had found more room for the implicit running comparison, which occasionally breaks surface, to Alice and her creator or to Alice and her White Knight. Ziegler's Mr. Moir suggests what his Scrooge would have turned into, once the transformation had wholly set: a lovely creation alternated, rather than doubled, with the actor's drier account of Coyne senior, affable but aloof, and devoted to crosswords.

Martha Burns similarly combines the roles of mother (whose best scene, a cautious dip in the lake, is shown in silhouette) and of Nootsie-Tah, as whom she resembles a cross between Peter Pan and Tiger Lily, with a fair dose of the snootiness of Tinker Bell. Its a dashing performance, enlivened by entrances from and exits to all manner of unexpected places.

There is another theme running through the play, and no bones are made about it. Its Time, a subject that Coyne broaches in her own present-day person, right at the start of her introductory monologue. Most obviously it's the time that for a child, especially in summer, seems to stretch out for ever and for a parent rushes heartbreakingly fast. Hence, a call she receives on her cell-phone from her own daughter, in which the concerns of two generations crash together. Hence also the fact that she begins with a couple of false entrances, nervously wondering whether she's late, and appealing to the stage manager as the ultimate arbiter of all theatrical time.

Hence, yet again, her reference to the pleasure we must take in learning from the program that this play has no intermission, and will therefore be short enough for us to make it home in time to see the news. Here, I think, she confuses audiences with critics. Critics like plays to be short; watching them is our job, and the sooner we can clock off the better. Audiences like to get quantity for their money, though they will, all things being equal, settle for quality. As here.

By the end, Coyne has also mentioned geological time, the life expectancy of cloud forests and the accelerated chronology of adolescence. Nothing of this is any kind of revelation, but time, as experienced in regard to childhood, is an infallibly touching subject if linked to a believable situation, and so it is here.

Some of the script, especially the chat to the audience, is self- conscious. Correction: All of it is self-conscious. But thats rather the point, and Coyne makes most of it sound spontaneous and much of it funny. Ive only become conscious of her skill as a comedienne in the last year or so, and I'm charmed by it. I liked her reminiscence of her teenage shyness, and of how it defeated her mother's best advice ("Attack!") and eventually turned her into an actress. I even indulged the more blatant in-jokes, like her hesitation over the name of her director, Albert Schultz, who only happens to be her husband.

His production is sensitive and inventive, with a charming set by Ken Ganhum and magical lighting by Louise Guinand. And we learn -- or I did -- the true meaning of the phrase "halcyon days," for which you may look in the title.
