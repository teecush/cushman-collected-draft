THE CRIMSON VEIL

Factory Theatre, Toronto

For a musical -- or indeed for any kind of entertainment -- The Crimson Veil looks remarkably drab. Its essentially permanent set is dominated by a medium-sized slag heap, with some smaller slag heaps clustered around it. It seems that they are meant to be mountains, and the big one is identified for us, rather late on, as Mount Etna; the story unfolds in a timeless Sicily, a territory famous for folk tales.

The show is a fairy story.

There is of course no such thing as a new fairy story, and Allen Cole, composer and (with Glen Cairns) author of this one, has created his own variations on some familiar motifs. As Gilbert and Sullivan did in Iolanthe, Cole tells of a fairy who falls in love with a mortal, and of the price she pays for this violation of supernatural family values. Her name is Bellinda, and she has a mother; her beloved, who loves her right back, is an aspiring clarinettist named Bruno who lives wth his father in a cabin in the woods. Neither parent approves of the match, and they unwittingly collude in obstructing it. Bellinda is instructed by her mother to entertain her lover over three evenings, while wearing the crimson veil of the title, which Bruno is under strict orders not to remove. Bruno's father advises him not to stand for this, and to tear off the veil at the last encounter; he does so, and Bellinda not only loses her magic powers (a sacrifice that she, like the Little Mermaid, was prepared to make for love) but is carried off to a cave by a couple of very bad brother and sister fairies. A penitent Bruno rescues her by defeating the male fiend in hand-to-hand combat, and then by kissing Bellinda three times on the lips: a tougher quest than it may sound since she's been transformed into a poison-fanged monster. He breaks the spell, but she's still sore at his betrayal and sends him away.

End of Act One.

Bellinda repents during the intermission and subsequently sets off on her own quest. Bruno has gone into a trance, but has left a helpful note saying that he can only be awakened by someone who will first watch over him for a year a day, without sleeping. Bellinda goes for it, though she runs into last-minute trouble from the evil sister who, it is all of a sudden revealed, has her own designs on Bruno. What isn't revealed is how Bruno, who doesn't have supernatural status, was able to engineer this test. Or how Bellinda, who has lost hers, was able to pass it; black coffee can only do so much.

Fairy stories, even more than mortal stories, are supposed to lay out their ground rules at the start, and stick to them.

This one doesn't so much develop as lurch from one idea to another, and it seems very thin to fill a whole evening. You appreciate the wisdom of Stephen Sondheim and James Lapine in interweaving four separate plot-lines in their fairy-tale musical Into the Woods, and their inventiveness in keeping them going. (That's quite apart from how funny, moving and frightening they made them.) The new show has several features in common with the older one: a narrative that starts up again when it seems to be resolved, an emphasis on father-child relationships (which here, having taken up a lot of space in the first half, wither away in the second) and of course a forest setting. Cole's music also persistently echoes Sondheim's, not in whole songs but in dark lemon-flavoured chords and sharp melodic fragments, notably a recurring theme on flute. His tunes actually sound better in overture and entr'acte than when heard full-length in the action. They are sweet; but they don't have the dramatic weight to carry the action without benefit of dialogue. They are also hobbled by the composer's own lyrics, whose choice of detail often seems dictated by the necessity to make it to the next rhyme. ("Trust me, Bruno, I know of what I speak," sings his dad, when advocating forcible unveiling, "how will you ever see her cheek?") There are more elaborate trick-rhymes, even including some triples, which would be unlikely to impress Cole Porter but are certainly aiming high by more recent standards. Tim Rice has laid it down that the biggest musicals of the last 10 years (by now it's 20) would have been just as succesful had their lyrics been 10 times worse, or indeed 10 times better, than they actually are. (He should know.) In smaller theatres, though, words do count; and the wit with which Ann-Marie MacDonald fitted Cole's music in the recent Anything That Moves is here much missed.

The song that comes nearest to rousing the house is an increasingly frantic patter-number with which Bellinda takes us through her year-long vigil; it's wildly out of sync with the rest of the score, but that, in the circumstances, is a relief. (James Fagan Tait is credited with unspecified additional lyrics, so apportioning praise and blame is chancy ) This number also gives Naomi Emmerson, as Bellinda, the one solid opportunity afforded any of the performers, and she grabs it with both hands. She's also appealing in her earlier seductive scenes, sashaying in her yashmac.

Kevin Dennis, though, makes a ploddingly plaintive Bruno, while Julian Molnar and Michael McManus as the dysfunctional parents seem to be hanging around waiting for some motivation. The sinister siblings, who carry on like vampires but are never identified as such, are unmercifully camped up by Paul Braunstein and Kelly McIntosh in what reads like a desperate attempt to inject some life into the thing.

There is in fact no consistent tone. Jokiness and solemnity, in Leah Cherniak's production, flash on and off in alternating bursts, colliding in the weakest fight scene I have seen in years.

The staging in general is awkward; Cherniak needs a choreographer like Cole needs a lyricist. The cyclorama occasionally turns some striking colours, but that's not enough to lift a spectator's heart.
