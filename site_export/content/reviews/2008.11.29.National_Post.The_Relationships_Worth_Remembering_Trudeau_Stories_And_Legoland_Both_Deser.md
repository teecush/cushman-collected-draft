Theatre Passe Muraille's fall season of theatrical miniatures is now on its second leg. In one evening you can catch two shows, one in each of this theatres spaces.

On the main stage, theres Trudeau Stories, Brooke Johnson's unexpectedly engaging memoir of her relationship with the former prime minister, whom she met soon after he became former. It happened 20 years ago at the National Theatre School in Montreal; she was a final-year acting student, and he was a distinguished visitor at the school's fundraising ball. She, like most of her fellows, couldnt afford a ticket, but she got in as a student rep. Shed once asked Trudeau for an autograph, and he remembered. He asked her for a dance. It went well, though with some slight embarrassment on her part (she had toilet-paper stuffed inside her borrowed shoes) and they became friends. He was remarkably assiduous about returning her phone calls. They went to bars and restaurants together, discussed poetry, went on hikes. They did not become lovers, though there may have been a subterranean spark. She acknowledges, in the first of her many letters to him (so she kept copies?), that he has a reputation and she draws boundaries.

His attitude to her, meanwhile, emerges as not quite paternal, but paternally flavoured. He shows concern for her welfare and her career. He's intrigued by the theatre, even asks to attend a rehearsal, as if it were an area he would like to have cultivated, but for which he never had enough time. If they talked much about politics, we never get to hear of it. He just seems to have liked her. So do we.

For one thing, she's a credit to her old school. Her past professional work has made this abundantly clear, but its in especially sharp focus here. She talks to us, with a naturalness thats 90% complete, while also enacting a series of visual images of how she was -- and, sometimes, how he was -- at the time. It's like turning the pages of a live snapshot album; at the end of Allyson McMackon's production, as she recalls her reaction to the news of her old friends death, she replays them for us, at top speed, one after the other. What comes through, with a maximum of craft but a minimum of artifice, is a portrait of an especially attractive kind of young Canadian woman, forthright but reserved, humorously aware, as relayed by her older self. What comes through is not Trudeaus personality or even his fame, but their impact on someone who never expected to find herself exposed to them but kept her head and came to take them for granted. As history, it's just a footnote to a footnote. As autobiographical performance, it's delightful and, at just over an hour, doesnt outstay its welcome.

"This is a theatre. Youre only allowed to use the F-word if you're pretending to be poor." That observation, the sharpest piece of theatrical commentary I've heard all year, is proffered by Penny Lamb, teenager, to her younger brother, Ezra, who needs it. They are the two characters of Legoland, written by Jacob Richmond, co-directed by the author and Britt Small who is also ultra-democratically the stage manager, and originated by Atomic Vaudeville, from Victoria. The young Lambs grew up in a throwback hippie colony; Legoland is the name given by its inhabitants to everything outside their own walls, or whatever they have in place of walls. Penny and Ezra used to sneak out to visit it, and their escapades led to the fuzz (one anachronism deserves another) following them home and arresting everybody for growing stuff. Penny and Ezra were thrown into a Catholic boarding school, where Ezra, hyper-intelligent or at least hyper-articulate, developed AHDD while Penny was reviled, by the other girls and the staff, as a lesbian, which they use as a handy synonym for loser. She becomes a rabid fan of an especially sickly boy band; after theyve crashed and burned, shes revolted when their lead singer re-emerges as a particularly violent and misogynist white rapper. Funded by the cash they've made from peddling Ezra's prescription drugs, the pair set off for California so that Penny can give her former idol a piece of her mind. At the very least.

As they travel by Greyhound through the United States, they discover each state subject to an increasingly higher level of terror alert, each gradation named after a character from Sesame Street. (Elmo is the scariest.) Switching channels, Id say that Ezra and Penny are like a twisted version of Bart and Lisa, and that the smarty-pants Ezra also resembles the annoyingly omniscient baby from Family Guy, not least in his snooty quasi-British accent.

The Lambs tale is told to us by Penny herself, still in her school uniform, as part of her community service, in the hope (brainwashed, presumably) that having heard it we will depart to lead "fuller and lawfuller lives." Camille Steubel plays Penny; she has brilliant timing, and is very funny and occasionally touching. Amitai Marmorstein's Ezra looks like Little Lord Fauntleroy in specs and on anti-depressants, and he, too, is funny: not touching, but then I doubt if he's trying to be. He also operates puppets who impersonate additional characters including the menacing pop/rap star. Somewhere in the accompanying literature is a claim that Automatic Vaudeville want to offend. I dont think so: If you satirize everything, as they do, you offend no one, especially when your main focus is pop culture. Branding the everyday world as Legoland loses its sting, when youve already established that the people doing the branding are ridiculous. Both these shows were first seen in Toronto at Summerworks, though not by me. I'm glad to have caught up with them.

- Trudeau Stories plays at Theatre Passe Muraille, and Legoland at Theatre Passe Muraille Backspace, through Dec. 6. Call 416-504-7529 for more information.
