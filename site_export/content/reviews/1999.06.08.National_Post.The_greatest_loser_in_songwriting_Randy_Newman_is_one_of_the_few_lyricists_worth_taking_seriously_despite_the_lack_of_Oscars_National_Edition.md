A recurring feature of Academy Awards night these past two decades has been the unspectacular spectacle of Randy Newman not getting the Oscar for Best New Song.

Year after year, Newman -- the only singer/songwriter some of us can take seriously -- has sat hunched over a piano, sweetly croaking out some nominated composition of his own. Usually it has been something gently optimistic from a movie either for or about kids: I Love to See You Smile, from Parenthood, or You've Got a Friend in Me from Toy Story. Invariably, it has been the best song on offer.

Newman has always been the prototypical guy with the glasses, brainy and anxious. Both his work and his conversation suggest a civilized man with a huge awareness of life's unfairness and absurdities, sometimes angry about it, but more often resigned.

At the Oscars, he has looked and sounded even more resigned than he usual. This year, when he was nominated for That'll Do (from Babe: Pig in the City), and had Peter Gabriel to do the singing for him, he still managed to project wry fatalism from the piano bench (maybe contagiously, since Gabriel at the mike seemed to be projecting it as well).

He always seems to know in advance that his deceptively simple words and music are going to lose out to whatever overblown over- the-titles anthem is being bellowed loudest. He is too smart not to know. Over the years, Newman has been nominated, for best song or best score, a round dozen times.

He comes from a dynasty of film composers (his uncles Alfred and Lionel were both giants in the trade) and Los Angeles is his hometown. He was born there in 1943, grew up and raised his own family there, and has spent his career, apart from his live gigs, in the film and record studios. By his own count, he has personally attended seven or eight Oscarfests, and he has appeared on stage in four.

He points out that there is an especially daunting quality about an audience largely composed of nominees: "Eighty percent of them are losers. . . . So they're not happy."

Newman was in Toronto to promote Bad Love, his first assemblage of self-performed new songs in eleven years.

He has a reputation, partly self-fostered, for being lazy, so the discipline of movie schedules has been good for him. Producers, friends and even wives have prodded him into writing individual songs. "I haven't" he admits "made enough records in the time I've had." His first solo album was in 1968; Bad Love is his tenth.

Newman's songs occupy a fluid territory between rock, folk and traditional pop. His tunes have body, melody and shape; they even, on occasion, swing. His words have that most precious of lyrical qualities, poise: They sit comfortably on the music, convey their surface meaning without strain, and hang reverberating in the air.

He is a superb ironist, one for whom irony is a positive comic and dramatic quality, rather than a vague apology for meaning. This sets him apart from the wild and woolly supposed poets of his 1960s generation. (And, yes, that does include Bob Dylan.)

Bad Love is his first album for DreamWorks/Universal. He left his former label, Warner/Reprise, after Lenny Waronker, his producer and boyhood friend, had moved on. Newman found the move difficult since, "I couldn't find anyone at Warner to resign to." (He ended up leaving his name with the receptionist, which of course is how most people try to get into an organization.)

Warner has released a four-CD retrospective, entitled Guilty: 30 Years of Randy Newman, and going all the way back to his first song, Golden Gridiron Boy: a song about high school, recorded when he was 19. The chorus goes: "I'm too small to make the team/I can only play in the ba-a-and/But I'm big enough to have a dream/That one day she'll understand."

It inaugurated a long line of skewed love songs in which she doesn't. The persona has always reminded me of Piggy, the outcast schoolboy of Lord of the Flies, ridiculed because he knows too much and wears glasses.

The first Newman song I became aware of was Simon Smith and the Amazing Dancing Bear. I loved the subtle rise in confidence from the hopeful "they'll love us, won't they?" to the proud "they feed us, don't they?"; and the concluding "who needs money/when you're funny?" which reeks of tattered defiance, and the festive stride- piano accompaniment.

In the same vein, but cutting deeper, is Davey the Fat Boy, with its seedy showman's cry of "isn't he round"; no other lyricist would have found it, or made it wake such disturbing echoes. Or there is the promise of the slave-trader to his prey in the horrendously soothing Sail Away, that they'll no longer have to "run through the jungle and scuff up your feet . . . it's great to be an American."

"The tragedy America has visited on black people," says Newman, "will never be rectified," then adds: "I've written about it too many times."

But Newman ("I'm a liberal, very much so, I'm for throwing money at people") has specialized in writing about persecution from the point of view of the persecutor -- who usually turns out to be an underdog himself. He admits that song writing is "a strange field to try humour in," his kind anyway, and pictures some guy picking up one of his songs on the car radio and going "Oh, you mean he doesn't mean what he says."

On Bad Love, the "I" of the songs seems closer to Newman himself than on his previous records. The opener, My Country, beginning "let's go back to yesterday . . . the television turned on for the night," sounds initially like another of his ambiguous odes to Middle America. It turns out to be a confessional, "the most autobiographical I've ever written." He grew up glued to the family TV, and has remained so; now his children are grown but they still come around to visit, slightly to his surprise since they have their own TVs at home. A line about "a television large as all outdoors" made me laugh out loud. He sings, as ever, in an affectingly tortured mumble that alternately squeaks and growls.

It took him twelve weeks to write the songs for the album (he didn't have any stockpiled). There's a ballad on there that maybe doesn't belong (it was written for -- but was not used by --Michael Jackson), but most fit comfortably enough under the admittedly generous umbrella title.

There is Shame, a blues for a wealthy ageing man in thrall to a 19-year-old girl ("I've sunk pretty low this time"); Newman is now on his second marriage, to a woman much younger than himself. The One You Love is about being afraid of what your partner wants from you. ("My wife and I," he has said by way of commentary, "had a pretty turbulent courtship, and she scared me half the time.")

Better Off Dead is pretty much what its title suggests (unrequited love "makes you feel all dirty, nerdy"), and I Miss You is even more painfully romantic ("I miss you -- I'm sorry but I do"), something of a tribute to his first wife, to whom he was married for 18 years and whom he credits for bringing order to his life. These two songs set dark lyrics to lush, yearning, string- laden tunes: Newman functioning like a one-man Rodgers and Hart.

I Want Everyone to Like Me ("I'm really modest once you get to me") is the perfect closer, an ineffable compound of mordancy, modesty and cheek.

There are also overtly political songs. Great Nations of Europe is a jolly march, a movie-theme Marseillaise, which recapitulates colonial history. ("Hide your wives and daughters/hide your groceries too/the great nations of Europe are coming through.") Karl Marx is a great idiosyncratic song about liberal angst; the narrator, surveying the world from "my mansion on the hill" bewails the sight of beautiful young women appropriated by old rich husbands, "froggish men, unpleasant to see -- men much like me."

Newman, more than any of his contemporaries, thinks dramatically; he has written a musical, Faust. It was staged a few years ago in La Jolla, Calif., with partial success; he thought that the score "got too much credit" and the book (which he also wrote "with Goethe's help") not enough. It spawned an all-star concept album on which he played the Devil -- with, of course, the best tunes.

He wants the show to go on, though his views on musical theatre are mixed. "It's the lowest form of music, I'm more likely to cringe on Broadway than on any other street, and yet I'd like to get there."

Still, he admires Stephen Sondheim, invokes Lorenz Hart and Cole Porter as model lyricists, and wishes that it were still possible to earn a living "doing what Frank Loesser did."

He's still earning his in the movies. "They used to call me for comedies. But it's very difficult, and there's no point. If a picture's funny, it's already funny. Now I'm getting all these animated things. I'm doing Toy Story II."

Recall what I said about poise: When he writes songs for these pictures, Newman -- the smart, mournful kid -- puts his irony not so much on hold, as in suspension; he may not be challenging our intelligence but he still respects it.

At 55, he is wary of becoming old fashioned, of losing touch with newer composers, or as he puts it, "I watch myself for old crockery."

In fact, he doesn't need to worry; he crosses all the song writing traditions, all the time-lines.
