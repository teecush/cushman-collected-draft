SOUTH PACIFIC

The Four Seasons Centre for the Performing Arts, Toronto

---

Nellie Forbush, the heroine of Rodgers and Hammerstein's South Pacific, comes from Little Rock, Ark. Mary Martin, who created the role back in 1949 came, famously, from Texas. So does Carmen Cusack who, in the wonderful production that's just arrived in Toronto, gives a performance of Nellie that I doubt Martin herself could have equalled and on which I cannot believe that anybody could improve.

Nellie is a U.S. navy nurse, stationed in the south seas during the Second World War, who falls in love with a middle-aged French planter, Emile de Becque. She overcomes her initial qualms about the differences between them, only to recoil when she discovers that he has two children from an earlier marriage to a Polynesian woman, now deceased. She can accept that her beloved once killed a man, hence his exile; she can't, until sorely tested, overcome the racial indoctrination that comes with being a little girl from Little Rock. It seems prophetic that Nellie should hail from the town that, only a few years after the show's premiere, would be the site of one of the first battles over segregation. The triumph of Cusack's performance is how firmly it's rooted and how naturally it blossoms. Her accent, for a start, is impregnable, summoning a whole background and a whole cast of mind. She's both guarded and generous, cautious and headstrong, graceful and gawky, and when she runs up against her own limitations, she's as appalled by them as anyone; note how she has to bring herself to utter

the word "coloured." She makes you share her delight at washing that man right out of her hair and her deeper joy when she accepts him, just one song later, as a wonderful guy. You laugh with her, and cry both with and for her. South Pacific is, of course, a show designed to draw tears, but it doesn't win them cheaply. The show both begins and ends, especially in this production, with remarkable economy and tact. Jason Howard's Emile is rather stiffly acted, as this role written for an opera singer nearly always is, but he is honest

and earns his part in the finale.

Questions of race haunt the entire show, but there's only one song that directly confronts it: The self-hating You've Got to Be Carefully Taught, sung by the Philadelphia-born, Princeton-bred Lt. Joe Cable (Anderson Davis, strong and surprisingly complex) after refusing to marry the island girl Liat. In Bartlett Sher's production the message is as much in the staging as in the lyric. It's watched by six sailors, three black, three white, each trio on its own side of the stage. As the song proceeds, the three white guys peel off the stage in silent protest; they might be standing in for the audiences for whose benefit this song had to be cut when the show first toured in the South.

This is a touring version of a revival originally staged at New York's Lincoln Center, but nothing about it feels second-hand or second-rate. Every number here is not only beautifully sung but thoroughly acted; Cusack's blithe exploration of A Cockeyed Optimist sets the standard right at the start. The only disappointing number is the concert-party special Honeybun: significantly the only one that isn't part of the narrative. The revelation of Sher's direction is the quality of Hammerstein's and Joshua Logan's book--not only in the development and interaction of the love stories but in the itchy atmospherics of servicemen waiting on the edge of global conflict. There's an epic quality about the closing scenes in which they leave their island for actual combat. South Pacific is the great liberal musical, and this production honours its triple place in history: in its own time, in the time it depicts, and in ours.
