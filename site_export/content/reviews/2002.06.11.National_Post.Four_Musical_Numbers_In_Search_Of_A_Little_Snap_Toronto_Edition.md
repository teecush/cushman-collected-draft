SNAPPY TALES

Factory Theatre Studio, Toronto

In Snappy Tales four actor-singers rush around like mad things, performing in four miniature musicals, one of them so miniature it only lasts four minutes. The magic number further re-asserts itself in the matter of composers, a different one for each piece. There is, however, only one author, one lyricist and one director, and they are all the same man. He is Vincent de Tourdonnet, and he is a self-proclaimed satirist. He has taken four existing texts -- two literary, two dramatic -- musicalized and, in three out of the four cases, updated them. The results are interesting but, as you would guess, uneven.

We start with the ultra-brief one: The Doll's House, based on a story by Katherine Mansfield. (Id say the story if Id read it.) Two little girls of early 20th-century appearance peer over and into the lives of their dolls, imagine the mistress giving hell to the maid and reveal that they cannot wait to grow up and behave like that in real life. It's a satisfyingly mordant fragment, given music, by Stephen Eddins, to match.

Then comes The Good Person, based on (the program says "inspired by") Bertolt Brecht's play The Good Person of Szechuan. Brecht wrote a fable, set in a personalized China thats ancient and modern at once, in which three gods come down to Earth to give financial aid to someone deserving and discover -- not unexpectedly -- that the only worthy recipient of their alms is a prostitute. She tries to use the money they give her for charitable purposes and finds herself cheated and exploited left and right (i.e., screwed by clients and landlords alike). So she invents and impersonates a tough male cousin and, in his guise, finds herself acting as ruthlessly as everybody else.

De Tourdonnet pulls this story into present-day Toronto (the Good, he carefully reminds us), thereby giving it more teeth. The heroine uses her gods-given wealth to open a shelter for the homeless but soon, to her horror, finds herself operating a form of workfare. Some of Brechts pyramiding paradoxes have had to go, but the underlying point is not exactly hard to grasp, and it's a relief to be spared the original's Oriental whimsy; de Tourdonnets heroine is a more convincing hooker than Brechts ever was.

Before accepting her hospitality the gods are turned away from a restaurant; one hopes, for the sake of allusiveness, that the cuisine is Szechuan. The divinities themselves are an ecumenical, world-spanning trio: one token Confucius, one Santa Claus and one pre-Raphaelite fairy queen. Polemically this is the most powerful movement of the quartet and dramaturgically the neatest, but it's let down by J. Douglas Dodd's music, which has little character beyond cuteness.

The same goes for the score composed by Jim Kass for The Barnhouse Effect, which derives from a 1950 short story by Kurt Vonnegut. A scientist finds himself with the power to destroy any object -- any -- simply by looking at it and concentrating. This no- cost destructive capacity greatly endears him to the higher reaches of the U.S. military, though they are less charmed by his desire to use it to destroy destroyers, theirs included. The point has sharpened if anything in the intervening half-century, but it remains a single point. The story is awkwardly told here and is largely populated with caricatures left over from Dr. Strangelove.

These three pieces make up the first half of the evening. The second is occupied by Strange Medicine, which the program coyly describes as "based on an idea by Jules Romains." It is in fact a musicalization of Romains once-famous and now-forgotten comedy Dr. Knock. That play, subtitled The Triumph of Medicine, showed a GP descending on a new practice and revolutionizing it by persuading the local population that their bodies were merely an assortment of diseases waiting to happen and that they should dose themselves accordingly. The doctor gets rich, and so does the local pharmacist. The doctor is not a quack and he is not even primarily a profiteer; he is a fanatic.

Again, bringing this story into our own time sharpens its fangs, though its a weakness that we never really know what kind of medical system the protagonist is operating under, or just where in North America it's now meant to be happening. Besides this the musical, like the play it's based on, keeps cracking the same joke. Its a good and grim and pertinent joke but it does get monotonous. This habit of making one point over and over afflicts all the pieces on the bill -- all except the first, which only has the time to make one point, period.

Allen Coles music for Strange Medicine is by far the best in the show and maybe the best this prolific composer has composed for anything. An opening number in which the new physician is driven through the countryside by his predecessor and the predecessor's wife while they point out the beauties of the region is a particular delight, though a little long for its importance to the plot; it sways, as -- in the best staging of the evening -- do the three passengers, negotiating what seem to be hairpin bends on mountain passes. Grant Wenaus, the musical director, has designed some lovely vocal harmonies and also plays spiky, barbed piano throughout; the evening's overture, as he plays it, seems to promise better tunes than actually occur.

Barbara Barsky and Tracy Michailidis deal most efficiently with the various female roles; the men are Eddie Glen, a cheery, chubby fellow with a marked physical resemblance to the late Mel Torme, and Paul McQuillan, who at times seems far too nice a fellow to be credible as the passionate dispenser of Strange Medicine and at others seems just right. De Tourdonnet's staging manages for the first half to make a virtue of strict economic necessity, but the more ambitious last piece just looks starved.

This is an unromantic evening; the closest any of the components comes to a love song is a satiric tango and a besotted female patient who wants to be his very own hypochondriac. De Tourdonnet's lyrics, though, are not always tight or disciplined enough to fulfill his fierce intentions; these snappy tales snap less fiercely than seems to have been intended.
