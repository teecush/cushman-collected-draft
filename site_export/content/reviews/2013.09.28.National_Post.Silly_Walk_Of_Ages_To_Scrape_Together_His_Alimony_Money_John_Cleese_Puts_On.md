JohnCleese, currently embarked on what his title would suggest is a farewell tour of Canada, comes before us on an empty stage and a full video screen. When we enter the theatre, the screen is occupied by that title, Last Time to See Me Before I Die, spelled out in large letters. One of the letters is cheekily surmounted by the figure of the Grim Reaper, himself one of Cleese's own greatest roles. (See Monty Python and The Meaning of Life.) A few minutes before opening time, with the audience still fully lit and chattering, he wanders onstage, looks at his watch, gives an embarrassed grimace, and wanders off again. This means he has us laughing helplessly before his show even starts. I hope that isnt a spoiler.

When the lights do go down, the screen lights up to show us Cleese as Reaper moving menacingly through a montage of everybodys favourite clips from Monty Python and Fawlty Towers, all of them still jaw-breakingly hilarious. And then the man himself saunters back on, and furthers his disarmament program by copping to what he says we're all thinking, i.e. doesn't he look old? Actually, the shock isnt that he looks old onstage - in fact, hes 73 and well preserved, even if his socks are odd, his pants baggy and his waistline expanded-but that he looks so young onscreen. Admittedly, most of the scenes we're shown were shot in the 1960s and '70s, but even then Cleeses specialty was playing deranged authority figures. One remembers him as having always seemed middle-aged.

I cant recall him ever having done a solo turn on his TV shows. His scenes were always two-handers at the very least, and he isn't trying to reinvent himself as a standup comic now. What he's offering is an easygoing illustrated memoir, offhandedly and expertly delivered with the aid of a face-mic, a teleprompter, and a sense of timing thats fast, dry and unimpaired. Hes doing it, he tells us, to raise the money to pay an outstanding $2-million on a $22-million alimony settlement with his third wife, about whom his remarks are not charitable. He's also cutting, though rather more affectionately so, about his birthplace of Weston-super-Mare, described as a supernally dull English seaside town that got bombed during the war, despite the fact that there was nothing in it to destroy.Maybe, he muses, Goebbels had a relative there against whom he had a grudge. Or maybe the Germans were trying to prove that they did, so, have a sense of humour.

He tells us about his father, of whom he seems to have been fond, and about his mother, an undemanding person who wanted only one thing: "her way." She died at 101 having lived through all the upheavals of the 20th century without, he says, noticing any of them. (Well, he concedes, she did notice between 1939 and 1945 that there was a war on, started by somebody called Hilter.) She also suffered, it seems, from phobias both agora and claustro, making it very difficult for her to find a comfortable space. Thence to school, where he was bullied by boys smaller than himself. But then everybody in that school was shorter than the 12-year old Cleese, including the masters. His height, of course, was to prove a major factor in his comedy career - it fuelled his Silly Walk and Basil Fawlty's frenzies - though he does seem to have shrunk in more recent years. In time-honoured fashion he defended himself at school by making people laugh. He also made them laugh at university, becoming a member of the famous Cambridge Footlights, appearing in a show of theirs that, under the title of Cambridge Circus, transferred to London. (He downplays his contribution to this shows success; it was probably substantial.) After graduating, he was rescued from a career in law by a producer who sent Cambridge Circus to New Zealand (which, by his account, made Westonsuper-Mare seem up-to-date) and to New York. The show flopped on Broadway - thank you, New York Times - but Cleese was rescued by David Frost, a consistent benefactor, who engaged him as a writer/performer on his TV show The Frost Report on which he made a mark, and then gave him a TV showcase of his own, At Last the 1948 Show (also featuring the bugeyes Marty Feldman), which I could kick myself for having forgotten and of which I was very glad to be reminded. And from there to the shows and movies we all know about, the theatre audience torn between delight at the excerpts that are included and frustration over those that arent.

In the second half, Cleese talks about black humour, instancing the stage and TV shows that immediately preceded him: Beyond the Fringe, which he calls the best show he's ever seen (me, too) and That Was the Week That Was. I agree that their broaching of political and other subjects previously off limits were liberating, even intoxicating, but I don't know thatId call them black. He screens the limb-chopping scene from Monty Python and the Holy Grail which certainly is black, and hilarious, but also harmless and, in the Pythons own special sense, "silly": a sense that's its own kind of subversion. Maybe his own darkest joke is another, real-life, one about his mother: one that involves a murderous man from Fulham and that I'll leave you to discover.

There are illuminating memories of Peter Sellers, and sad and funny ones of Graham Chapman, who seems more and more the funniest of the Pythons, perhaps because he looked the most serious. He stresses that the Pythons were writers before they were performers and quarrelled over scripts, not over casting. He leaves out some things I wishhed included. Theres nothing from or about Life of Brian, which was the Pythons' furthest pushing of the envelope, and surely crucial to the points he's making.

Other omissions are the radio show Im Sorry Ill Read That Again, which was a bridge between Cambridge Circus and his TV career, and all of his post-comedy, though not post-humorous, activities. These range from making industrial training videos, and co-writing books with the psychiatrist Robin Skynner, books in which he's self cast as the curious everyman. There's a Cambridge tradition (exemplified by Jonathan Miller, Graeme Garden and Rob Buckman) of doctors turning into comedians; these books enabled Cleese to, in a sense, reverse that trajectory. They also underline the thoughtfulness that co-exists with the wildness and that has made his career, and this show about it, work.
