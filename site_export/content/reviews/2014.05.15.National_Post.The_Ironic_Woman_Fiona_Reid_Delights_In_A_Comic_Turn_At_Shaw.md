THEATRE REVIEW

The Charity That Began at Home

Court House Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont.

Arms and the Man

Royal George Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont.

Fiona Reid can divide a comic line into three parts, get a laugh on each without crowding the others, and still keep the sense and the rhythm unbroken. She can be equally hilarious, maybe more so, without saying a word. In The Charity That Began at Home she has a silent scene for the history books, a technical and imaginative triumph in itself and central to the play's meaning. She plays Lady Denison, an Edwardian chatelaine, who at this moment is having to deal with some serious misconduct among the help. She is later to admit that "I do so hate deciding things," and we watch as possible courses of action or inaction run through her mind and across her face, each of them leading her into an abyss of further doubt. I don't think I've ever seen such a superbly prolonged series of mental double-takes. You want it to go on forever; you also can't wait to find out how it will end. Don't worry: I'm not going to tell you.

St. John Hankin's play is sub-titled A Comedy for Philanthropists; it's also largely about them, Lady Denison's country house being the home in which the charity begins. Hankin, whose theatrical career lasted from 1903 to 1908 and whose ill-health drove him to suicide when he was still in his thirties, went in for mischievously sardonic titles. This play was published in a collection that he called Three Plays with Happy Endings; the Shaw Festival has already, and with great success, produced the other two. The Charity ... is the least known of them (not that the others are exactly standard items) but it may also be the best. It's irony made flesh.

Lady Denison and her daughter Margery are collectors of lame dogs. They open their house to decayed gentlefolk who are too dull or too impoverished or too disreputable to receive invitations anywhere else. (They do not, though, extend their hospitality to members of the lower orders. One must draw the line.) Their spiritual mentor is Basil Hylton, the youngish founder of what he calls "the Church of Humanity" which believes in forgiveness above all else. Lady Denison has occasional doubts about this ("if we don't punish the wicked people, it would be so discouraging to the good people") but then she could hardly be called the clearest thinker.

Hankin, a born playwright whose death was a major calamity for English drama, fills his stage with conflicting ideas and complementary actions. Christopher Newton, who has directed all the Shaw's Hankin revivals, orchestrates the sights, sounds, thoughts and feelings of this one magnificently. As costumed by Judith Bowden, some of the houseguests might have stepped out of the pages of Punch, the humorous magazine to which Hankin himself was a contributor: Sharry Flett, an etiolated witch of a German governess, or - a fine double cartoon - Neil Barclay, the last word in portliness, paired on first appearance with Jim Mezon as an unstoppable bore of an Indian Army general. The polite efforts of all concerned to find shelter from the General's name-dropping yarnspinning amount, as staged here, to a beautifully fluid social ballet. But his reminiscences are more than a joke; they serve a plot-function. This in turn leads to the General having, for the briefest moment, to face the truth about himself. As played by Mezon, it's almost moving.

That is the great thing about the play. It never lets you get comfortable about anyone. It is, to make proper use of an overused word, disturbing.

The play's final focus is on Margery and on the fiance whom she wants to reform and who, with the best will in the world, is uncertain whether he can measure up. Julia Course and Martin Happer give, barring the occasional casual slip out of period, fine performances, both probing and touching. The happiness of their ending is even more richly ambiguous than those of Hankin's other plays; there's more at stake, emotionally and intellectually. The servants, who have their own dramas going on, are excellently played by Edmund Stapleton, Andrew Bunker, and Darcy Gerhart who has a very expressive line in blubbing. This is the Shaw Festival at its very best.

Morris Panych's production of Shaw's own Arms and the Man isn't quite that, but it's excellent value. It starts with a chase through the auditorium; in the Balkan War of 1886 (eight years before the play's premiere) the victorious Bulgarians are pursuing the defeated Serbs, though the foe they have in their sights happens to be Swiss: the pragmatic mercenary Captain Bluntschli, the celebrated "chocolate cream soldier" who finds confectionary to be a more useful holster-filler than ammo. It makes an exciting prelude to the first scene proper, which finds Bluntschli taking refuge in the bedroom of Raina Petkoff, starry-eyed daughter of one of Bulgaria's first families. It also prompts Graeme Somerville to give a very convincing portrayal of the starved and exhausted fugitive, though at the expense of the crisp wit with which he punctures his protectress' illusions. By a similar token Kate Besworth's Raina glides over her last line, a surrender to romance, as if she hadn't noticed it were there. Vocally, this young actress has some way to go, but in mind and spirit she's already there; pert and dreamy, she excels at pointing up the false side of Raina's romanticism: the rapturous, hero-worshipping side.

As the hero in question, the peacocking Major Sergius Saranoff, Martin Happer is exactly right, vainglorious but vulnerable, stepping on and off his self-erected pedestal at a moment's notice. Louka, the maid who does the best job of tempting him off it, is voluptuously presented by Claire Jullien, though she seems less like Raina's rival and contemporary than, to put it as diplomatically as possible, her older sister. Norman Browning and Laurie Paton give conventional, meaning thoroughly appropriate, performances of the Petkoff parents. Ken MacDonald has set the play, or so says his director's program note, within a giant cuckoo clock, which seems fitting for a play with a Swiss protagonist; the cuckoo isn't too much in evidence but the machinery, certainly in the last scene, clicks, whirrs and rotates delightfully. So, more importantly, does the machinery of what, in traditional love-comedy terms, may be Shaw's most satisfying play. It's another play with a happy ending, but in this one there's no ambiguity about it. The good end happily, there are no bad to worry about, and we've been told some truths about war that we can live with. Talking of endings: the play's last line has been changed. Why, beats me.
