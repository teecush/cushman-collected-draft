DIANA OF DOBSON'S

Court House Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

Cicely Hamilton was an English actress, writer and suffragette; and she has been a footnote, if that, in the theatre history books. Diana of Dobson's was her first major play, originally staged in 1908 and a success then and for decades afterwards. It then fell into disuse, but it has been rediscovered in the last dozen years.

Now, at the Shaw Festival, it is receiving its first ever Canadian production. It's a find.

It is part angry satire, part fairy tale -- which is not, come to think of it, an uncommon combination. Dobson's is a drapery store in South London and, as far as its employees are concerned, maybe half- a-grade above a sweatshop. Diana, surname Massingberd, is one of the female sales staff. She shares an on-site dormitory with half-a- dozen of her fellows, all of them liable to have their subsistence wages docked still further if they break any of the establishment's numerous petty regulations.

(The Edwardian drapery business, at once exploitative and paternalistic, seems to have been a world unto itself. Contemporary writers -- H. G. Wells in Kipps and Harley Granville Barker in The Madras House -- use it when they want to portray capitalism in its purest form.)

Diana, daughter of a doctor who died and left her penniless, is by virtue of her middle-class upbringing more bitter, more outspoken, and hence more often in trouble, than her workmates. Suddenly, she comes into money, (ps)300 of it. For comparison purposes: Her shopgirl's wage is five shillings a week, while a young ex-guardsman can live, though not as comfortably as he'd like, on (ps)600 a year.

Diana decides to blow her windfall on a month of luxury in the Swiss Alps, and the Parisian gowns to go with it. Thus attired, and posing as a widow, she attracts the attention of the guardsman's managing aunt, who thinks her nephew's only hope is to marry money, and even of the bashful fellow himself.

He proposes; she reveals her true financial status; he is shocked. Stung, she challenges him rhetorically to try earning his own living, as she has had to do. Surprisingly, he takes her up on this; unsurprisingly, he can't cut it. What happens after that, you will have to attend the play to discover.

It's an oddly constructed piece, with a profligate habit of picking up characters and putting them right down as soon as they have served their turn. That the denizens of Dobson's should be dropped in this way is fair enough: They exist to make points about wage slavery and -- in the person of one who's about to become a bride on the grounds that anything would be better than her current life -- about marriage. The two themes are closely entwined, and this first scene prepares the ground.

But one wants to see more of the tycoon whom Diana encounters in Switzerland and for whom, it turns out, she once worked. He is not a nice man -- as a self-made success who's made his way from errand- boy to entrepreneur, he believes the weak deserve to go to the wall - - but he is an unapologetically vital one, and he recognizes the same quality in Diana.

In fact, he falls for her, thus giving the cue for a scene the author unhappily failed to write. They argue the relation of boss and worker very energetically, but are not allowed to come to grips with the allied question of the relation of husband and wife. It's true this is raised elsewhere, but what might be the sparkiest part of the puzzle is missing.

In terms of character and language, the play lacks texture. It plays, compared to other Shaw Festival discoveries, like hand- me- down St. John Hankin or Granville Barker. It's also very free with its coincidences. But it lives, because the issues it raises are firmly embedded in its action and because the same battles, or their descendants, are still being fought.

Then, too, it comes from a time when even serious playwrights were expected to tell stories; and this one keeps you agog to know what will happen next. There isn't a boring moment in it.

The director, Alisa Palmer, keeps the narrative going, though in other respects her production is rather shallow. The Dobson girls are allowed a surface quality apiece -- one chirpy, one sour, one pert, one peevish -- but then fall into an undifferentiated litany of complaint.

This unfortunately includes Severn Thompson's Diana. There is no special edge to her rebellion, no difference in pressure as she engages with her various companions. She has enough force and charm to carry the play, but she filters them through a fixed smile that lifts only in the very last scene -- an unusual mixture of pain and whimsy, set among the down-and-outs on the Thames Embankment.

Goldie Semple plays the mercenary matchmaker of the mountains, so dazzled by Diana that she starts humming the Merry Widow waltz. Hers is an elegant, seemingly self-generated, performance that might look very well in an Oscar Wilde revival but comes off here as an isolated turn. That she gets laughs is a good sign; that she also gets exit applause is not. Patricia Hamilton is agreeably agreeable as her confidante, or acquiescent acquaintance.

But the best performances in this female-driven play come from the men. Peter Hutt is hard and intelligent, with the right perverse streak of sensitivity, as the rag-trade king who has parlayed the rags into riches for himself and more rags for everyone else.

Evan Buliung plays the cashiered hero with remarkable delicacy. Outwardly a slice of well-bred, dunderheaded brawn, he has more feelings than anyone would believe and, with very few words to help him, he shows them first struggling for existence and then breaking through. More of a buffoon than he would like to be, he is yet less of one than everybody thinks. "Everybody," in this case, includes Diana, and when he accuses her of not having noticed what was going on inside him, he is right. We noticed, though, and the credit is the actor's.
