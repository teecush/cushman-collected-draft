Everybody, or so one hopes, wishes the new regime at the Factory Theatre well. Nigel Shawn Williams and Nina Lee Aquino, the joint artistic directors, were parachuted into their positions after the board's dismissal of their predecessor Ken Gass; but holding the way he was treated against the new duo would probably be unfair and would certainly be unproductive. It could result in there being no Factory at all. All that said, Every Letter Counts, the partnership's first offering, a play written by Aquino and directed by Williams, puts goodwill to a severe test.

Of the two partners, Williams comes off the better. His production sounds good, at least at the sound-effects level, and looks good, Anna Treusch having designed a handsome room of a set, on which Cameron Davis projects some striking images, of which the most important are a series of Scrabble tiles. These reinforce the play's main metaphor, driven home in its title. It's an autobiographical piece in which Aquino recounts how, when she was six, a kindly uncle helped her overcome her dyslexia by teaching her this most educational of board games. This may be a theatrical first; there are many plays that employ chess as a metaphor, but I don't know of any others that use Scrabble.

When not thus benevolently engaged, uncle Ninoy was a freedom-fighter in the Philippines, the most outspoken opponent of General Fernando Marcos, who imprisoned him, then released and let him leave the country, and finally - when he dared to return - had him shot as he got off the plane. Posthumously, he's a hero, with a museum in his honour in Manila. The museum is the play's ground zero. The grown-up Bunny (the author's surrogate name) visits it and gets to thinking about the four days she spent in her uncle's company in Texas, during his exile. She seems to feel guilty about letting him return, though what she could have done about it in such a short time and at her tender years is obscure.

Really it isn't, or wasn't, about her. But the play behaves as if it is, an effect compounded by having Aquino be not only the author and the heroine of the play but also its leading actor. Playing herself, she makes, for better or worse, no attempt to be a realistic six-year-old, except perhaps in the matter of sheer ill-tempered brattiness. This note, largely uninflected, infects her entire performance, and its monotony makes the chronology difficult to follow. There are flashbacks within the flashbacks: scenes of Ninoy's imprisonment, especially his meeting with a surprisingly suave and understanding General Marcos. Or perhaps not so surprising: Deceptively decent dictators are a theatrical staple. Earl Pastko seems to enjoy playing this one; you can understand why Ninoy might take the chance of returning, to reason with him. (Imelda and her shoes, alas, are referred to only in passing.)

Ninoy himself is played with dignity and charm by Jon de Leon. That he wasn't quite a saint is driven home to us by his brother and Bunny's father (Anthony Malarky), whose only apparent function is to balance the picture with some angry reminiscence. Not to devalue the heroine's dyslexia, or the efficacy of Scrabble in teaching her to arrange her thoughts as well as her words, but they don't seem very important compared to the fate of a country. Either this play is telling the wrong story or it's telling the right one in the wrong way.

An earlier theatrical essay in self-mythologizing has been revived by Heart in Hand Theatre. This one was a double. Seems that near the beginning of their respective careers Patti Smith, the singer, and Sam Shepard, the playwright, shacked up together. It was an ill-natured and short-lived alliance, but they reconciled enough to write a sort-of play, performed originally by themselves, in which they reconstructed their relationship as a series of quarrels and monologues. Slim (that's Sam) complains that Cavale (Patti) has stolen him away from his wife and children in a hopeless attempt to make him into a rock star. Cavale, who identifies with doomed poets, says that she's tired of telling stories about people killing themselves because they make her jealous: Roll that one around in your mind.

She also tells a poignant story about being cast as the Ugly Duckling in a school play, only to have another girl replace her for the transformation into a swan. This, the best speech in the play, is hauntingly delivered by Jessica Huras; the rest of her performance is loud but mopey. Slim, the amateur rocker, is played by a professional, Jason Collett (of Broken Social Scene), who brings to it the understated confidence often shown by musicians who turn actors; he's at home on the stage, if not quite in the play, and comes off better than she does because he doesn't seem to be trying so hard. The play has the kind of self-absorbed aimlessness, alleviated by flashes of lightning, that I associate with all but the best of Shepard's own plays; though in this case he seems to have written less well for his own role than Smith has for hers.

There is a third character, known as Lobster Man; he seems to be an unfortunate delivery boy whose employers make him dress up in a crustacean suit. Sam and Patti make fun of him, with explosive results that seem, in Esther Jun's production, both baffling and half-hearted. Once again, the best thing is the set: a cluttered, slummy mess of a room, designed by Akiva Romer-Segal.

Every Letter Counts runs through Feb. 24 at the Factory Theatre; Cowboy Mouth runs through Feb. 14 at Cameron House.
