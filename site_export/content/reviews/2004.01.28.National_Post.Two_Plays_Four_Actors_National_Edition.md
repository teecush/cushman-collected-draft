BLOOD

Theatre Passe Muraille Backspace

- - -

After a short break for renovations (new looks for lobby, box office and bar) Theatre Passe Muraille reopens its doors, both sets of them, with re-stagings of plays that have already been seen elsewhere in Toronto. Both are two-handers, depicting domestic partnerships in extremis.

Sean Reycraft's One Good Marriage was first seen at the Summerworks Festival. As often, a one-act play that must have knocked em dead in those crowded, uneven circumstances looks somewhat orphaned when asked to take up a whole evening on a main stage. Critics may not mind (we prefer short plays, especially in winter) but paying audiences may crave more quantity if not, on this occasion, quality.

That, though, is their affair. One Good Marriage is a well executed work. Its title is only partly sardonic. Stewart and Steph, its two characters, seem to be surviving, even though their wedding was the cue for a large-scale disaster. The exact nature of this catastrophe is not revealed until the end of the play, so I obviously have to shut up about it as well.

The two of them tell their story as if in front of some informal tribunal. They are colleagues as well as partners. Shes a teacher; he's the school librarian, though he has no taste for -- or presumably in -- books. ("It's not" he says of his job a book thing.) Grimly amused, they recall the washroom graffiti that were the kids acknowledgement of their relationship prior to tying the knot. They also tell about their honeymoon, a bad job (due to faulty booking arrangements) that they made the best of. Or, as they put it with bland expressions that come out barbed, "we got to know each other."

This talent seems to have stood them in good stead during their subsequent joint career as the innocent harbingers of apocalypse. Tragedy can kill a relationship, but it can also cement it. There are hints, in the ways Stew and Steph delicately but continually put one another down, that in other circumstances theirs could have been One Bad Marriage, maybe One Rapidly Failed Marriage. Now they seem to be in for the long haul, like those happily married couples in Strindberg or Albee.

Some of the writing is glib, as when a neighbour is described as kind of scary, in an anorexic-with-a-Honda kind of way. What kind of way would that be, exactly?

More damagingly, here is a built-in structural flaw. The plays vested interest in springing its final surprise keeps it at arm's length from the characters' interaction. We cant quite evaluate their reactions because we dont know what they're reacting to. The play's qualities as a thriller are at war with its qualities as psycholgical exploration, though both are real.

Both are excellently presented in Shari Holletts production, with fine performances by Jeff Miller (who looks like Kevin Spacey) and Mary Francis Moore; he is merry on the outisde and mean underneath, shes the reverse. Steve Lucas's set, one of those open- to-the-back-wall jobs they specialize in at Passe Muraille, isn't boring.

Ive been thinking about boredom, as people in my line of work naturally tend to. It may have got a worse rap than it deserves. It comes, after all, in varying intensities. There is the insupportable boredom that emanates from a really stupid, talentless play or musical, but we havent had one of those since ... well, for a few weeks. (There's no need to be gratuitously cruel.) There's the boredom tempered by grudging respect, as in Written on Water; something seems to be going on, even if its impossible to discern what. There is the boredom that may not actually exist but that we as an audience plead in self-defence, when a play thats deficient in all other respects stubbornly insists on holding our attention; think Restitution.

On the highest level, there's the boredom that periodically surfaces in a top-quality work like Ronnie Burkett's Provenance. This has passages that, by general agreement, dont work, but even those that do can exhaust our attention. Theres a long speech in Provenance that's moving, harrowing and superbly written and performed. Nevertheless, about two-thirds of the way through, my mind -- after duly acknowledging that it had been moved and harrowed -- switched off until the next thing came along. The scene went on being superb; it just did so without me.

And then there's the boredom of Blood. This seems to be due to a discrepancy between the intensity that the play itself keeps taking for granted, and the conviction of what actually happens on stage, not to mention the inherent limitations of the two-character play. Tom Walmsleys couple are brother and sister and -- as someone once said of the Jacobean sibling-drama Tis Pity She's a Whore -- the incest is sustained throughout.

Noelle, recently released after doing time for drugs, has an opportunity to make $500 if she can put up with an extra male for a threesome. The only partner she can come up with is her brother Chris, who has also been an addict but hasn't been in jail. He is, rather ostentatiously, trying to reform. (Once he wanted to go into the church.) Chris and Noelle have never had sex together, but it seems they both wanted to. The opportunities for dramatizing guilt, rage and tenderness are considerable, and are duly utilized.

This is one of those plays that will make most who see it feel they have led sheltered lives. There is therefore a considerable fallout of midddle-class guilt, which translates into a willingness to believe what were told. And yet, despite some high intensity in the writing, we dont.

Some of this may be due to the play's habit, endemic to duologues, of dropping the names of people we never meet. In One Good Marriage this doesn't matter; its a demonstration piece and we can be be given what information we need as we go along. Blood is naturalistic -- in structure, anyway -- and has to rely on hints.

The name that comes up most often is Martin, an evil genius (and former lover) in both characters lives. He's meant to inspire terror but, because we never get a chance to check him out, he doesn't. He doesnt work as an offstage Pinteresque menace either; were told too much about him, even if we're not told enough.

The play, which premiered 10 years ago at Factory, has a golden reputation, so it could be that Kate Lynch's production doesnt drive hard enough. Jane Spidell as Noelle starts it off with a terrific low-key monologue, but seems thereafter to be trying to be tough. When she answers the phone, she doesnt say "hello," she says "go." Spidell makes that sound terrifying, but she can't bring the same urgency to more extended passages; some recurrent technical flaws the night I saw her suggested she may not have made peace with the person she's playing.

Randy Hughson may be too easygoing and endearing for the circumstances. When he does occasionally snap into gear, he can be powerful. John Thompson's set -- high, deep and bare -- reminds again of the wonders that can be wrought in what seems, at first sight, one of Toronto's most limiting spaces.
