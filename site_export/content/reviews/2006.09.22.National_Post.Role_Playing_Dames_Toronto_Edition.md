FANNY KEMBLE

Studio Theatre, Stratford

- - -

Fanny Kemble, born in England and married in America, was a 19th- century actress and abolitionist. She came by the acting genealogically; the Kembles were the great theatrical dynasty of their day, and Fanny's aunt was the paradigm of tragediennes, Sarah Siddons. The origins of her anti-slavery sentiments are uncertain, but they were brought into the sharpest relief when she was on tour in the United States. While playing in Philadelphia, she was wooed and won by a Southern gentleman who, in the immemorial way of stage- door Johnnies, had fallen in love with her performance of Juliet. Shortly afterwards, he inherited a plantation in Georgia with all appurtenances, human and animal, attached. You could not make such a story up.

It has attracted the attention of more than one actress, who has seen in it the seeds of a one-woman show. The one who has actually brought it to the stage is Domini Blythe, herself a British-born performer (and from a theatrical family) who has made most of her career in North America -- especially at Stratford where she stands out even among a golden generation of actresses. To put the story into shape, she turned to a real writer and director in Peter Hinton, and he has come up with a real play, in a super-real production.

That doesnt mean that, in his text at least, hes solved all the problems inherent in both the narrative and the genre. His framework is a come-back performance by Fanny, long after she has put both her career and her marriage behind her. She looks back on both of them, and especially on the issues of freedom and servitude as they have manifested themselves in her personal and professional lives; the latter, it seems, was mainly devoted to Shakespeare, and it's with a Shakespearean recital that she's making her present come-back. It is, in both the good and bad senses, an obvious device. One grows tired of hearing the stage manager knocking on Fannys door with the five-minute call (its always five minutes; time seems to be standing still) and with her own reiterated question: "Who shall I enter as tonight?" The idea may be that Fanny has lived her life as a series of roles, but the woman we meet seems to have been very consistent; her circumstances may have changed but she hasn't. It's entertaining, though, to hear her reminisce about her relatives (her aunt Siddons "spoke in blank verse when instructing a servant" and never cracked a smile onstage or off), and stimulating to hear her reflect on the instances of slavery and subjection in the plays to which she devoted her talents. Even Juliet, she reflects, only does two things on her own initiative: fall in love and kill herself. Everything else is some mans idea. I dont think this is quite fair -- it's Juliet, not Romeo, who first proposes marriage, and it takes some guts for her to swallow that sleeping-potion -- but it's a provocative point. Dont get her started on The Taming of the Shrew.

Even before she discovered that her husband was a slave-owner on a large scale, Fanny knew that in her own marriage she had been, in a sense, sold. Or so the play tells us. We cant be sure whether this was her own insight or Hinton's; Kemble was herself an able and voluminous author, and the script makes much use of her writings, but the result sounds seamless. When read, though, the script seems very much the work of the man who wrote The Swanne; it has the same anchoring use of verse structure, the same relish for and freedom with historical detail, the same strange wind blowing through it.

It changes gears from the first act to the second. Before the intermission, Fanny's experiences on the plantation are a ghostly presence at the back of the plays mind; after it, they take over. In particular, Fannys black maid Psyche, merely mentioned in the first half, becomes the figure through whom Fanny discovers the physical and emotional facts of slavery. She challenges Fanny's right to record in her journals the stories that Psyche has told her. This seems to be a pre-emptive strike at what would later be called cultural appropriation -- of which the play itself is of course both an example and an effective defence.

Much of Psyche's story concerns a friend of hers, a slave called Jenny Hill who ran away and whose escape Psyche herself was too timid to join. This seems to be all Hintons work, derived from the merest hint in Kemble, and it unbalances the play; we have come, after all, to investigate not Psyches psyche but Fanny's. But it's powerful stuff in itself, and it inspires superb work from Blythe who, in impersonating Psyche, finds a majestic defiance even in her confession of her fears. Acting after all is appropriation, by definition; and this actress, whose primary role is another actress, is here its living justification.

In the rest of the play she is witty, graceful and quicksilver versatile, with her emotional changes and exchanges superbly placed and graded under Hinton's direction. He always has his performer in the right physical and mental place, and -- thanks to Robert Thomson -- the right light.

He also has, from Tamara Marie Kucheran, a superbly evocative dressing-room set; trunks, open or closed, erect or supine, evoke both the heroine's theatrical world and her marital one, and a black dressing table belongs triumphantly to both.
