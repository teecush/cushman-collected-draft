When Shakespeare wrote Richard III he accomplished something that was unprecedented, and something else that has remained unique. It was one of his earliest plays and, if not the first star vehicle in the English theatre, still the first to have attracted all the generations of subsequent actors. It is also the only viable play, ever, to have a recent historical monster at its centre. I don't know of any others that have done that, or even attempted it. If there are any successful plays with Hitler or Stalin as their protagonist, I haven't come across them.

Now of course there has long been debate as to whether the historical Richard was a monster, and it's been given new life by the discovery of his bones under the extremely unconsecrated ground of a Leicestershire parking lot. In at least one respect, the revisionists have now been vindicated. Richard, it appears, had a crooked spine, but was by no means the grotesque hunchback, born with a full set of teeth and a withered arm, so colourfully depicted by Shakespeare and the Tudor historians who were his sources. They wanted to make Richard look bad, and they took their assignment very literally.

Their motive, as has been often pointed out, was to make their own monarchs look good. Henry VII, first of the Tudors and grandfather of Shakespeare's own Queen Elizabeth, had come to the throne by defeating Richard in battle; his genealogical claim to the throne was tenuous, and he was no oil painting himself if actual paintings of him are to be trusted, but he could claim the moral high ground of having rescued England from the 30-year carnage of the Wars of the Roses, and that ground would be even higher if the king he had displaced were shown to be the devil's deputy on Earth. Henry's historians got right on it, the most eloquent of them being no less sanctified a figure than Sir Thomas More. One of the pleasures of Hilary Mantel's Wolf Hall novels has been seeing More taken down a few pegs from his position as the perfect Man for All Seasons. Mantel reminds us that this Catholic martyr had, in his day, been an enthusiastic prosecutor and persecutor of Protestant heretics. His book on Richard III shows him also to have been a disconcertingly brilliant purveyor of Tudor propaganda. Maybe it's to his credit that he never actually finished it.

The claims of Richard's detractors and of his defenders don't altogether cancel one another out; it's perfectly possible that Richard was a capable and even merciful governor of the north of England during the reign of his elder brother Edward IV and that, down south, he intrigued and murdered his way into being Edward's successor. It was a bloody period, where fighting at court could be as vicious if not as direct as on the battlefield. My own sympathies are with the pro-Richard party. I doubt, at the least, that he was much worse than his contemporaries, though I confess that, much as I would like to pin the murder of the Princes in the Tower on Henry VII, I fear the evidence points to Richard. I do believe that Shakespeare's immensely influential portrait of Richard traduces him. I still wouldn't wish it any different. (I admit to being tickled by the fact that the Canadian gentleman, "a 17th great-grand-nephew of Richard's older sister," whose DNA has been a crucial factor in identifying Richard's body, is a Mr. Ibsen. The namesake of a great dead playwright may have helped salvage a reputation that an even greater European playwright has lastingly savaged.)

It's a shame that Shakespeare can never have heard the word chutzpah, because his Richard embodies it. He does so, most famously, when the wins the love of his future wife, whose husband and father-in-law he had killed, right beside the coffin of the latter; this despite being, in his own words just one scene earlier, so ugly "that dogs bark at me as I halt by them." That's one of his rare moments of self-pity; more typical is his later description of himself, when off to seduce another princess: "To her go I, a jolly, thriving wooer." That, in its perfect mix of self-confidence and self-deprecation, is one of his very best lines.

Shakespeare wasn't short on chutzpah himself. He credits Richard with one murder - that of his other brother the Duke of Clarence - that even the historians hadn't thought of. (Clarence was popularly said to have been drowned in a butt of Malmsey wine. This seems merely to have been colourful slang for his having died drunk.) At the other extreme hedescribes the Princes in the Tower as the most beautiful babes that ever were born, here anticipating the style of modern tabloids (and not just of tabloids) who write as if the killing of children were not horrific enough in itself but must be bolstered with fulsome adjectives. The pouring-on of pathos when Richard is offstage, together with his gusto in villainy when he's on, have led Harold Bloom, writing recently in Newsweek, to suggest that Shakespeare was writing deliberate parody. Professor Bloom has an enviable confidence about what was going on in Shakespeare's mind, together with a less endearing habit of treating his own theories as if they were established facts, but I can only say that neither Richard nor the blood-soaked tragedy Titus Andronicus (another Bloom theory) feel like parody to me. I think Shakespeare meant them.

He might not have meant them at a later stage in his career, and even here he had to work his way up to it. Before Richard III come the three parts of Henry VI, which depict the Wars of the Roses in grisly detail. Richard appears about halfway through. At first he's just one bloodthirsty aristocrat among many, though wittier than most; gradually you feel him working his way, through that wit, to the forefront of Shakespeare's imagination. When all the plays are done in sequence he seems less a prodigy of evil, more the ultimate product of an age - which would also have made for good Tudor propaganda. In or out of order, Shakespeare never shortchanges Richard; he may end by offering his kingdom for a horse, but he doesn't do it so he can escape. He does it so he can continue to fight. And part of us - a large part - cheers him on. We know, morally, that he ought to lose. We just don't want him to.

It's the kind of ambivalence that drama thrives on, right down to The Wire. Or to The Sopranos, or Boardwalk Empire, or even Mad Men. Even more directly in the Richard line

is the British TV series of the 1990s, House of Cards, currently being aired in a new and inferior American adaptation. (I speak from prejudice. I haven't seen the U.S. version. But I bet I'm right.) Francis Urquhart, the English politician killing and blackmailing his way to the Premiership, is the Richard III of parliamentary politics, with his bland catchphrase - "you might very well think that; I couldn't possibly comment" - the latter-day equivalent of Richard's pious appeals to Holy Scripture. And both the British Urquhart, the late great Ian Richardson, and Kevin Spacey, his American successor, came to the role after playing Richard.

The significant difference is that Francis Urquhart (FU for short) is a fictional character. So are most of those other TV anti-heroes. Shakespeare himself never tried to do a Richard III again. True, there's Macbeth, who also murders his way to the throne; he was a historical figure. But he was a faraway and shadowy one, and though the play takes us, disturbingly, deep inside his head, it never asks us to enjoy, if only because he never gets to enjoy himself. And as I've said, modern monsters have gone undramatized.

Well, maybe that's not completely true: Bertolt Brecht wrote a Hitler play, The Resistible Rise of Arturo Ui, but it's a cartoon in which the Fuhrer is caricatured as a Chicago gangster who learns his oratorical skills from an old Shakespearean actor; he's Hitler and Al Capone and Richard III rolled into one. Last year in London there was Collaborators, which cut Stalin down to size by having him share the limelight with one of his victims, the writer Mikhail Bulgakov, and making him in effect a figure in Bulgakov's nightmares; that too was a cartoon. Portraying these men seriously on a stage may well be impossible; their crimes were too vast and too recent, their victims too numerous.

And Stalin died in his bed, which isn't satisfying, dramatically or otherwise. (There's no great play about Napoleon either.) Richard was a petty malefactor by comparison, his misdeeds affecting only his own country. With a world-class criminal, where do you start? Who, so to speak, owns the rights? The Elizabethans could both own and disown Richard. He was recent history, but a history they could tell themselves had been exorcised. We don't have that luxury. But, for as long as the play lasts, we're the beneficiaries of theirs.
