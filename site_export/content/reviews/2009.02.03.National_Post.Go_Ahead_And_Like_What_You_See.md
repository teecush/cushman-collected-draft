Maja Ardal is a veteran of three cold countries. Before she lived in Canada she lived in Scotland, and before that she lived in Iceland. You Fancy Yourself, her self-performed one-woman play, tells the story of a girl who came to Edinburgh from the even more frozen North in the early 1950s at the age of four, so I think we can take it that it's autobiographical. Our theatres, Passe Muraille especially, are currently so awash in reminiscent monologues that I approached this one suspiciously. My suspicions turned out to be unfounded. The show's a delight.

Ardal takes us on the journey of her surrogate figure, whom she calls Elsa, through her early years at home and at school. Elsa's father has arrived determined "to scale the citadels of Scottish learning," which I think means he's a teacher. (We never do find out how well he does at it.) Elsa is a resilient child, and it would be a stretch to say that she's persecuted, but she does bear the burden of being an immigrant in an age group that's especially xenophobic or-- since this is Scotland -- clannish. Socially, she's between classes. Her best friend Adelle lives in poverty with a sick chain-smoking mother, an aggressive brother and no one else. Elsa, who's a kind of Nordic Anne Shirley with a mind full of legends and fairy tales, fantasizes for Adelle's benefit about her father having met a glamorous end in the war. School life is dominated by a rich girl and her sycophants, who spend their breaks riding imaginary horses. Elsa can't help wanting to be accepted by the equestrian class who, knowing their power, alternate between excluding and admitting her. In dealing with them, Elsa comes close to betraying her friend. "You fancy yourself " is the taunt -- the dread accusation of getting above her position in the pecking order -- that the snobs throw at Elsa. The climax is her own triumphant realization that, yes, she does, in a way of which they can't conceive.

That's a bit too neat. Nor is the conflict of loyalties drawn as tight as it might be; we lose sight of Adelle, and especially of her family situation, for much of the play. (Having an interval is a mistake for this kind of show in this kind of space.) Adelle's folklore fantasies, and her newer dreams inspired by Hollywood, aren't that compelling either; if the show's meant to be about culture-clash, it doesn't make it. What count are the individual characters and vignettes. Under Mary Francis Moore's direction, Ardal begins the evening peeking shyly over the top of a trunk -- with Ubuntu at the Tarragon, Toronto has two shows playing whose main visual motif is luggage. A chunky presence in her own right, Ardal gets herself into the mind and body language of a small girl and shows us the world and its inhabitants as they appear to us all at that age: a mixture of documentary and horror-comic. She finds a distinct voice and stance for every character; most unusually in this kind of ventriloquial performance -- she doesn't rush the transitions.

The classroom provides the most drama, the most comedy, and -- whatever your background or gender -- the sharpest recognition. These primary-school kids seem to have the same teacher in every grade, a Miss Campbell, a sentimental battleaxe with a revisionist view of the Massacre of Glencoe, the 1692 atrocity in which, with encouragement from the English, the Campbell clan slaughtered the MacDonald clan in their beds. Literally, as the MacDonalds were their guests at the time. Miss Campbell, true to her family pride, believes that her ancestors have been much misjudged, and that the MacDonalds, always a shifty race, had it coming. My own observation is that the mutual suspicion may well persist to this day, between those who love soup and those who love burgers. As luck would have it, there is a David MacDonald in the class who, with his runny nose and attention deficit, reminds Elsa, who is nonetheless his only protector, of a troll. The show's finest scene is a singing contest at which the rich bitchette acquits herself atrociously, the despised David huskily but truly, Elsa herself outlandishly with windmill gestures and Adelle, tastefully disregarding all the direction Elsa has tried to give her, quite beautifully. Miss Campbell renders true judgment, thereby overcoming all her prejudices, except the unshakable one in favour of the poetry of Robert Burns. (Elsa's feelings about her friend's success are, not surprisingly, mixed, and drive her temporarily into the arms of the snobs.) Singing badly when you can sing well is really hard but Ardal -- without accompaniment, mind you -- makes all four vocal performances completely convincing and melts us with the last as surely as Adelle melts Miss Campbell. It's the crown of a virtuoso performance. She has every right to fancy herself. - You Fancy Yourself is at Theatre Passe Muraille Backspace until Feb. 14.
