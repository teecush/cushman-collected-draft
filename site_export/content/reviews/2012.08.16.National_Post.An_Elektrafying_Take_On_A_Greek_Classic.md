THEATRE REVIEW

Elektra Tom Patterson Theatre, Stratford

No more need to beware of Greeks bearing gifts, not when they are as gifted as director Thomas Moschopoulos and his cohorts in the staging of Sophokles's Elektra at Stratford.

Those harsh unfamiliar spellings are laid down by Anne Carson, the Canadianborn and U.S.-based poet and scholar whose superb, jagged translation is used here. It encompasses the exultant and the despondent, insults and laments, tirades and one liners. Its also on occasion funny in a way that Greek tragedy rarely is, at least not on purpose. This Elektra, its tone and impact defined by Yanna McIntoshs stark and thrilling performance of the title role is, well, elektrafying.

Moschopoulos has an impressive resume in Athens and elsewhere, but there is no sign here of a production having been simply brought in; it feels as if it as has been created with its actors, from the ground up. It might be described as a conventional staging, in that it makes crucial use of at least one of the bedrock conventions of the ancient Greek theatre. I refer not to masks or built-up buskins, but to music; vital portions of the text get sung.

The chorus do most of the vocalizing, but the principals, too, are liable to break into song. Some of them do it better than others, but the bridge holds: The bridge between Greek tragedy's stylized external forms and the intensely real emotions beneath, which are played with unflinching, and unflagging, naturalism. The result is not so much to make the old seem new as to make the insistently modern feel eternal.

The audience arrives to find the stage roped off. Behind the barrier is a long, illuminated table, covered in sheets. When the play starts, the sheets are pulled off, and the table splits into three, as does the giant figure that lays upon it. We are at the tomb of the late king Agamemnon, his body split in death, as it was in life by the hand of the usurper Aigisthos, egged on by his lover and his victim's wife, Clytemestra. (Yes, those are the names spellings here.)

To this memorial comes Orestes, Agamemnons longabsent son, whose return is dreaded by his mother, longed for by his sister Elektra, whose grief and vindictiveness surpass even her brother's. But then of course she, unlike him, has had to go on living with their mother.

As Elektra herself, McIntosh looks like the eternal student, or perhaps the eternal student drop-out. She wears the blacksweater uniform, complete with threatening dark-rimmed glasses that have signified the brainy malcontent since at least the Paris of the 1950s. An orphan in her own family's house, she brandishes her solitude like a weapon. She also brandishes an actual weapon, but she still has to wait for her brother to do the real slaughtering.

As his game plan includes spreading the false report of his own death, she has to move from faint hope to fullblown despair to incredulous recognition, the last being the nearest thing to happiness shes ever likely to experience. Its also the one moment that finds McIntosh fractionally behind the curve; otherwise she plays with a force and spontaneity unequalled in her work since Skylight, 12 years ago. Her Elektra is a killer of a performance: a symphony of rage and hurt.

She has one sparring partner and one outright antagonist. The first is her temporizing sister Chrysothemis, played with sympathy and subtlety by Laura Condlln. The antagonist of course is Clytemestra, whom Seana McKenna makes a glittering self-elected trophy wife. She does have a reasonable defence for having killed her husband; he sacrificed their other daughter, Iphigenia, so the Greek ships could sail for Troy.

That's the beautiful thing about Greek mythology; there's so much of it that any character can cite some of it to justify anything. Sophokles is generally accounted the sanest of the three great Greek tragedians, but he tells this story more mercilessly than either Aeschylus or Euripides did.

Ian Lakes Orestes is another arresting study in arrested development. He made me wish that Euripides extraordinary tragic farce Orestes, which Carson has also translated, could be done as a sequel.

As his silent partner Pylades, E.B. Smith is a grim, hulking presence, always available to block anyone's path to remorse or escape. Peter Hutt escorts the two of them as the tutor, at once peremptory and protective, who's deputed to tell the tale of Orestes death in a chariot race, a sports commentary so gripping that we forget hes made it all up; he raps out the words while rapping his staff.

The beat goes on in the choruses, which are for once exciting rather than embarrassing. Whether talking, singing or something in between, the seven women emerge as individuals, while also functioning as a collective sounding board. They don't so much comment on the action as take its pulse, whether they're sympathizing with Elektra, chiding her intransigence, or beating on the palace doors, caught up in the thrill of the chase. The final prey is Graham Abbey's Aigisthos, a smug thug who first struts, then totters the way to his doom.

It used to be academically fashionable to hold that character was a modern concept, unknown to the Greeks. This is belied by the story of the Athenian actor Polus, who is said to have played Elektra while cradling an urn containing the ashes of his own dead son, thus qualifying as the first recorded Method actor. It used, similarly, to be theatrically fashionable to prate about "ritual" which was taken to be the opposite of realism and far superior to it.

Urns, filled with ashes real or fake, play a recurring role in Elektra, and the breaking and scattering of one of them is one of major visual and psychological climaxes of this production. It has its share of ritual but it has a greater share of humanity, which includes inhumanity.

It's the best production of a Greek tragedy I have ever seen.
