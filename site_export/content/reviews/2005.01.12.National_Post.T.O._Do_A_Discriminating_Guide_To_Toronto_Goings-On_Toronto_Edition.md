CRITICS PICK

FILM

On the heels of Hotel Rwanda, Hollywood's take on Africa's troubles, comes a screening of a new documentary, Liberia: An Uncivil War, by Jonathan Stack and James Brabazon. Part of the ongoing Hot Docs series, Liberia looks at events in the summer of 2003, when rebels in the west African nation, already in control of 70% of the country, stormed Monrovia. Stack stayed in the capital while Brabazon travelled with the rebels, and what emerges is a balanced, terrifying and sad portrait of a once-proud nation in political tatters. Monrovia, settled by freed American slaves in the 1800s, has long considered itself the little brother of America, but since the end of the Cold War in 1989, relations cooled, and by 2003 little aid was forthcoming from the United States. Liberias president, Charles Taylor, promised to step down once peacekeepers arrived, but no one would send them before a ceasefire. The screening will be followed by a Q&A with Stack. At the Bloor Cinema, 506 Bloor St. W., 7 p.m. More information at 416-203-2155 or www.hotdocs.ca.

THEATRE

Christopher Morris, whose performance in Theatre by the Bays Much Ado About Nothing was on our honour roll of last year's notable performances, now gets to turn the tables on the director of that show, David Ferry. Morris is directing Chekhov's one-act farce The Bear, and Ferry is playing the title role, not a furry animal but a loudmouthed debt collector. The show, which opens this evening at the AGO (416-979-6608 ) and thereafter plays mostly matinees, marks the convergence of two initiatives: the gallerys to open its spaces (in this case, the Grange music room) to site-specific performances, and the Preface theatre companys to establish a tradition through mentorship; Morris' guru has been Serbian director Sasa Lukac, responsible a few seasons ago for the eye-opening Christmas at the Ivanovs. Robert Cushman

HOT TICKETS

STRATFORD EXPRESS

Tickets are available for the 21st Stratford Express, Stratford Festival of Canada's annual black-tie gala fundraiser aboard a private train, running May. 28. The journey begins in Toronto and guests travel to Stratford to see the final preview of Hello Dolly, while enjoying a gourmet dinner and silent auctions. The ride home features dessert and liqueurs. Tickets $500 in advance and $550 after Jan. 30. Tickets for the pre-show reception in Stratford, $75. Express tickets 416-363-1915, ext.206. Reception tickets 1-800-567- 1600.

CLASSICAL

THE TSO

Tickets are still available for the Toronto Symphony Orchestras presentations in the Mozart@249 festival. Conducted by Peter Oundjian, the concerts feature orchestra, piano, voice and French horn. The festival anticipates celebrating the 250th anniversary of Mozarts birthday next year. Mozart's Grace and Melody shows today and Thursday at 8 p.m. Angela Hewitt, piano and Karina Gauvin, soprano. $32 to $110; One...Two...Three Pianos! is on Saturday at 7:30 p.m. and Sunday at 3 p.m. and features Andre Laplante, Hewitt and Louis Lortie. $23 to $64; Dazzling Mozart runs Jan. 19 at 8 p.m. and Jan. 20 at 2 p.m. and 8 p.m. Evening $32 to $110 and matinee $25 to $65. Louis Lortie, piano and James Sommerville, horn. All at Roy Thomson Hall, 60 Simcoe St. 416-593-4828. www.tso.ca.

FUNDRAISERS

TSUNAMI FUND

CanStage will donate all proceeds from tomorrow's performance of Take Me Out to the Tsunami relief effort. The Tony Award winner for best play (2003) tells the story of a bi-racial gay baseball player who unexpectedly comes out during a press conference. Written by Richard Greenberg and directed by Morris Panych. Tickets $36-$8. 416- 368-3110. 27 Front St. East. www.canstage.com.

THE NFB

NFB Film Wednesdays in partnership with Omni Television presents a free showing of Courage and Compassion: The Story of Dr. Joseph Wong.
