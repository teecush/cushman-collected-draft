A CHRISTMAS CAROL

Premier Dance Theatre

- - -

Soulpepper's A Christmas Carol, back for a second run, is as joyous, touching and resourceful as it was last year: theatrical magic directed to a moral end, and therefore doubly satisfying.

As a family experience, its credentials now extend from the auditorium to the stage itself. Joseph Ziegler repeats his fine performance of Scrooge; his wife, Nancy Palk, has taken over as Mrs. Cratchit (she's the one newcomer) and their son, Henry Ziegler, remains a creditably unmawkish Tiny Tim. Tim is of course the youngest Cratchit, and when we learn at the end that the reformed Scrooge became a second father to the boy, the justice of it seems even more poetic than usual.

Scrooge, as we all know, is a creature of extremes. The senior Ziegler is devilishly effective at the beginning of his journey, and angelically so at the end. Much less desiccated than most stage or screen Scrooges, he presents a still-vigorous man of business whose misanthropy is as much a matter of position as of disposition: not merely uncharitable but anti-charitable.

This robs some of his flights -- like the one about Christmas well-wishers being brained with their own pudding and buried with sprigs of holly through their hearts -- of their baroque hilarity, but it does suggest the coherent philosophy of a man whose head is as hard as his heart.

Surprisingly, he has a vulnerable imagination: a mordant sense of humour that starts to betray him immediately the ghosts come knocking. It softens, and takes him over, as he revisits his own past Decembers: observing himself as a boy, reclaimed from a miserable boarding school by a repentant father (it was clever of Dickens to suggest that sudden changes of heart run in the family) and then as a shy young man at his employer's party, dancing with his sweetheart.

Ziegler, in a charming moment of impatient nostalgia, demonstrates the steps. He dances, too, through his redemption: kicking off his shoes in an ecstasy of impromptu choreography. He gets full measure from the dialogue with the boy who reassures him that it is indeed Christmas Day and whom he dispatches to buy a turkey as big as himself: one of the great self-hugging moments in literature.

I have never understood, by the way, why Scrooge, in his last dream, is so slow to realize that the man in the grave is himself; he has been given enough clues, and the one thing he is not -- as Ziegler consistently and brilliantly demonstrates -- is stupid.

Michael Shamata's production of his own adaptation is both snug and spacious. It is suggestively set, by John Ferguson, in a box with many doors. At first glance it looks like a grim Victorian hallway; it can, we discover, equally well be an unexpectedly bustling Victorian street. (A small cast doubles, trebles and quadruples; this may not be meant to heighten the dream-effect, but it does.) Or the doors simply mark the boundaries and passageways between the past and the present, the actual and the possible. There are some lightning bravura effects, like the sudden appearance of Ali Baba from the recesses of the Scrooge-child's imagination, and the stage is smoothly parcelled out among episodes. If a scene is cramped, it's for a reason.

Thus, the poverty of the Cratchits' Christmas is stressed by confining it to one tiny, crowded table. (And the festive goose is pitiful enough to be a gosling.) I missed the sharp Dickensian outline of Susan Coyne's Clara last year, but Palk gives a warmly harried account of the role, and the increasingly excellent Oliver Dennis is a practically perfect Bob. He also plays countless other roles, in at least one of which, a receiver of stolen goods, he is unrecognizable: Good to know that he can play nasty as well as nice.

Patrick Galligan also brings grace to virtue as Scrooge's nephew, who exemplifies a more elegant level of hospitable poverty; his guests include Palk who -- in one of the evening's swifter transformations -- is languidly witty, and Tanja Jacobs who, in this and other roles, is enjoyably outrageous.

Some of the young adults are weak, but John Jarvis does a magisterial job as an unassuming narrator whose real business is to play all four ghosts. (Imaginative costuming by Ferguson.) As Marley in chains, he clanks and yanks enthusiastically; he goes on to make a bonhomous Ghost of Christmas Past, a silently menacing Future and a Present who splits the difference by alternating twinkle with glower. In the latter aspect, he can be almost too thunderous; the story and its images are making their own point with little need of underlining.

As usual Dickens stirs our consciences; and Soulpepper (and sponsor, Clarica) do us a favour by letting us contribute to the Daily Bread Food Bank on our way out.

George Orwell famously wrote that Dickens was an author whom both Left and Right tried vainly to kidnap. A Christmas Carol, being a straight-out parable, demonstrates even more plainly than his other works that he would not have cared whether philanthropy was public or private as long as it got the job done.
