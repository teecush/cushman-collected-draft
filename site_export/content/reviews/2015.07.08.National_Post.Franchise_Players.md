Theatre Review

The Twelve-Pound Look Court House Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

This year's lunchtime show at the Shaw is a pointed pendant to the festival's production of Top Girls. A one-act piece by J. M. Barrie, first staged in 1910, it engages with relations between women and men, as Caryl Churchill's play obliquely does, and with notions of success and failure, as Churchill's play directly does. It's a jewel.

In its Niagara production, the jewel comes wrapped in tissue paper. The director, Lezlie Wade, has chosen to start the proceedings with an interpolated song. A portly butler (Neil Barclay) in a rich drawing room displays, disdainfully, a newspaper with a headline about Votes for Women. He goes into a number, originally from a Victor Herbert operetta, entitled "If Eve Had Left the Apple on the Bough," and including the eartickling couplet "Everything was simply rippin' /'Til the woman plucked the pippin."

While he tunefully pontificates, a maid (Harveen Sandhu) does the heavy lifting: literally and, from the look of it, painfully. It's all fun, and well performed, but it's heavy-handed compared to the play itself which makes little direct reference to the servant classes and none at all to The Vote. Maybe, though, both are implicit; it's worth noting that, as recorded in one of the festival's invaluable program notes, The Twelve Pound Look was a staple text for the Actresses' Franchise League.

The title refers to a woman's calculation of the amount of money she would need to save in order to leave a stifling marriage and strike out on her own. The play begins - just like Top Girls, come to think of it - with somebody celebrating an incipient promotion. Harry Sims, successful businessman, is to be knighted for, we gather, Services to Money. He's practicing the kneeling, scraping and backward exiting that will be required of him on the great day, and his apparently adoring wife is helping out with the rehearsal, standing - or rather sitting - in for the officiating monarch.

A professional typist has been sent for, to handle the thank-you notes that the almost Sir Harry is sending to his multitude of well-wishers. When she arrives she proves to be briskly efficient, with an impressive keyboard speed and an even more impressive ability to guess in advance what the letters will say. She also proves to be - and I am trying very hard not to be more specific - a figure from Harry's past. Wade's production, which is perfect, features excellent performances from Patrick Galligan and Kate Besworth as the knight and his lady, and a superlative one from Moya O'Connell as the newly hired help.

In her other great role this season, in The Lady from the Sea, O'Connell is obsessed; here, she's possessed. Selfpossessed, that is; her character's function in the play is to knock Harry off his perch, not the easiest task since his selfregard is practically undentable. But her knowledge and skills, both hard-earned, are up to the job.

He, once recovered from his initial shock, alternates between sneering at her and upbraiding her, professing pity at the life, so materially and spiritually impoverished,

that he fancies she must be leading, and contrasting it with his own existence, surrounded by the wealthy and powerful and rising steadily through their ranks. She hits him where it hurts by remarking on how boring his social circle must be, how paranoiacally preoccupied with who's in and who's out. Gently but firmly, she demolishes all his preconceptions, especially the ones about her. She even has some compassion for him, though not as much as she has for his wife, and the future wives of the two boys she learns he has fathered. The amount of cool, disillusioned wisdom, at once amused and sad, that O'Connell is able to inject into the simple repeated phrase "Oh, yes" is wonderful. This is an actress completely in control.

Galligan finds, along with the obligatory complacency and meanness, a certain degree of boyish eagerness in Harry; he isn't your standard mutton-chop plutocrat, but perhaps more slippery and more dangerous for that. Kate Besworth, unrecognizably transformed from her infant prodigy roles in Arcadia and Peter and the Starcatcher, shows the seeds of doubt planted in the mind of the future Lady Sims. Harry's response to the undermining of his world is to offer his wife another pearl necklace.

She seems, though, to have misgivings about accepting it. Instead she inquires after the price of one of those new typewriting machines. She too may be acquiring the twelve-pound look.

The production, like the play, is beautifully economical: everybody in the right place at the right time and hitting the right note. Barrie is of course best known as the author of Peter Pan, he's otherwise been famous for his craftsmanship and notorious for his sentimentality. This play confirms the first quality and makes mincemeat of the second.
