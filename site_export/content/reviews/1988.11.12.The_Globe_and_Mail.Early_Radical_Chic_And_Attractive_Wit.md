THE POLITICS OF PARADISE: A VINDICATION OF BYRON BY MICHAEL FOOT Collins, 424 pages, $34.95

Early radical chic and attractive wit

THE POLITICS OF PARADISE: A VINDICATION OF BYRON BY MICHAEL FOOT Collins, 424 pages, $34.95

Saturday, November 12, 1988

ROBERT CUSHMAN

REVIEW BY ROBERT CUSHMAN This, I have been informed with awe, could never happen here. A retiring Canadian political party leader might try to turn a quick buck with a volume of scabrous memoirs (his own), but would never squander his newfound leisure on composing a scholarly essay in literary biography.

I hate to admit it, but it doesnt often happen in Britain either. Michael Foot, former leader of the British Labor Party and still a liltingly powerful voice on the back benches, is no commonplace politician. He was generally reckoned to be too civilized, too intellectual, too vague, too nice to succeed as a leader of the opposition, and so in fact it proved. But he always had a second career going as a journalist and man of letters, and the boot in this study of the life, works and opinions of George Gordon Noel , Lord Byron is firmly on that other Foot.

If the phrase man of letters sounds old-fashioned, it was meant to. Foot is that good old left-wing thing, a self-trained scholar. His nearest equivalents on the British right are Paul Johnson, a self-educated pundit, and Bernard Levin, a self-made mandarin. They have the advantage of him in wit and stylistic grace; he beats them on enthusiasn. By modern LitCrit standards he is an amateur: certainly no computerized academic.

Consider Foot's footnotes: not curt acknowledgements of sources, but effusive tributes to all authors who have taught him anything about Byron, which seems to mean just about everyone who has ever written about him. They also give him a chance to vent some spleen held politely in check in the text itself. A description of Byron's ill-suited wife ("high moral earnestness in belligerent abundance, absolute assurance that she knew all the . . . answers . . . evangelical zeal to subdue others") reminds our author irresistibly of Margaret Thatcher. Or so he mischievously says.

This Byronic education, so conscientiously detailed, seems to be a fairly recent event. It has left him convinced that the poet, dismissed in the text books as an aristocratic cub high on charm, low on convictions and zero on morals, was actually a misunderstood and golden-hearted radical: an early nineteenth-century Michael Foot, in fact, but sexier.

The Politics Of Paradise is a celebration of this Byron rather than the vindication announced in the subtitle. The need for vindication arises in Foots mind because of William Hazlitt. Hazlitt, essayist andd critic, is a longer-established hero in Foots pantheon, his paradigm of the radical journalist. He was Byron's contemporary and should have been his ally. It seems, though, that Hazlitt disliked Byron and disapproved of him: the time-honored reaction of the ink-stained activist to the apparently careless dandy. Foot is grieved by this disobliging lapse in Hazlitt and sets out to explain it.

So his book has an odd shape. Its first chapter - a book in itself, really - is a detachable essay on Hazlitt that would be worth having if only for Foot's skill in quotation. The purpose of poetry, said Hazlitt, is to relieve the aching sense of pleasure by expressing it in the boldest manner. "Aching sense of pleasure" is wonderful, as applicable to the flesh as to the spirit, and without Foot I might never have known about it. We find Hazlitt attacking Shelley as a revolutionary without principle (He strives to overturn all established creeds and systems; but this is in him an effect of constitution), and going for Byron on much the same grounds: "He exists not by sympathy, but by antipathy. He scorns all things, even himself."

I have never been able to take Shelley seriously, perhaps because he was so solemn about himself that any gravity on my part seemed superfluous. But what Hazlitt objected to in Byron is dazzlingly attractive. Mockery, rather than scorn, directed at himself and at the world, a world that, as always, deserved it. Don Juan, his longest and greatest work, is an extraordinary feat of sustained humor, the funniest poem in the English language. Funny is not a word you are supposed to use about major poets (I was ticked off at school for applying it admiringly to Dryden), but Foot, operating outside the industry, comes near to calling Byron funny. And it is on Byrons funniness - his wit, if you prefer the respectable term - that Foots case really rests.

"I am like Adam," said Byron in a letter, "the first convict sentenced to transportation." Implicit in that throwaway line is a compassionate attack on orthodox religion and, beyond that, on the orthodox society that took the first opportunity of expelling him. It is sentimental to pretend that all humor is subversive (though reactionary jokes are funniest when there is not a reactionary governmennt), but the satirist, committed in the first place to attacking pomposity, usually finds himself attacking other complacent evils as well. Byron in the Prologue to Don Juan was hilarious about Wordsworth and the other lake poets not just because they wrote stodgy landscape verse, but because they had bounced from starry- eyed enthusiasm for the French Revolution to sycophantic distrust of any kind of change.

Byron himself was never starry-eyed. In his maiden speech in the House of Lords, he spoke on industrial riots, not so much defending the rioters as attacking their exploiters, while admitting that he himself as a nobleman had everything to lose from an overturn of society. This thread ran right through his life, culminating in his death in Greece, a practical fighter in a practical cause. There may of course have been an element of pose in this. So there is in most activities, especially writers' activities. The question is which pose you choose to adopt, and what the results are.

Foot skimps on the Greek Byron. The book is not a full biography, and he probably thinks - wrongly - that the end of the story is too well known to need retelling. On the other hand, he luxuriates in Byron's response to Italy, especially Venice, partly because it suits his political purpose but more because he enjoys it. This is not a very rigorous book, and Foot turns out not to need Hazlitt as a stalking-horse after all. Having set him up, Foot finds little use for him.

It is just possible that had Foot been their contemporary, he would have been as impatient with Byron as was Hazlitt. Radical chic backed up by aristocratic cool can be unbearble at close quarters. A century and more on, it can be irresistible. Foot obviously finds it so, and his enthusiasm has fed my own. Robert Cushman is former drama critic of the Observer.
