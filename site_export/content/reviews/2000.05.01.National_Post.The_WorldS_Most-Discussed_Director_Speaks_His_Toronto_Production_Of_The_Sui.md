Profile of Peter Brook.

Peter Brook has had -- is still having -- a unique career. He is now 75, and ever since he was 20 he has been the most-discussed, most-admired director, first in the English theatre, then in the English-speaking theatre, and now probably in the world. Last week, he was in Toronto with his production of The Suit, probably the most anticipated event of a World Stage festival crammed with brand-name international goodies. It lived up to expectation, in quality if not perhaps in quantity. It was a four-actor play that achieved a piercing emotional effect through charm, intimacy, and firm but gentle persuasion.

In this it resembled the man himself, five minutes of whose conversation are enough to turn anyone into an admiring disciple. Partly it's what he says and the affable firmness with which he says it -- as of a man who, even when he's questioning himself, is secure in the belief that questioning is the right thing to do. Partly, it's a shared unspoken awareness of his track record.

And to a large extent it's his eyes; never having had the opportunity to sit at close quarters with either Paul Newman or Frank Sinatra, I have to pronounce Brook's the clearest, most dazzling and most penetrating blue that I have ever beheld. They subdue and captivate interviewers; they must have been of immense persuasive assistance; and they constitute, I am sure, a vital part of his armoury in rehearsal.

The Suit, like nearly all his work since 1974, originated at the Theatre Bouffes-du-Nord, an old theatre in an unfashionable part of Paris that Brook and his colleagues converted from a state of genuine decay into one of cunningly simulated dilapidation. He's placed a bare, open stage within a traditional proscenium auditorium, so actors and audience share a space. Here he runs an international acting troupe: French, English, American, African, Japanese.

In a recent memoir, Threads of Time, he says that "for years, when asked the inevitable question 'Why did you move to Paris?' I would give the answer that seemed to suit the questioner, and the reporter would scribble it down in delight." One favourite answer, he told me last week in the lounge of the Sutton Place Hotel when I too asked the inevitable question, used to be "because of Margaret Thatcher." Here is some more of what I scribbled down:

"In 1968 Jean-Louis Barrault" -- the great French actor and director who at that time ran Paris' world stage season, the Theatre des Nations -- "asked me to do a Shakespeare workshop. I said I'd do The Tempest with an international group, and see what common elements there are between actors from different backgrounds working on the same text. It proved interesting and it led to putting together the international centre.

"So the work had started in Paris; and this wasn't entirely fortuitous. Paris has an entirely different artistic background. English work is always parochial -- meaning that we always use our own local themes. For example, Ted Hughes' poetry is always north- country." This certainly seemed to be the case in Hughes' translation of The Oresteia, which also came to World Stage from the British National Theatre; the lines and the rhythms drove the actors due north. "In Paris, the whole tradition is of a meeting of artistic currents from all over the world: Picasso, Modigliani."

He might also have mentioned innumerable American writers, painters (or would-be painters) and jazz musicians.

I suggested that he might have felt happier working with an international group in a city in which he himself was an expatriate - - it created a level playing field, so to speak -- but he didn't seem to think there was much in that; besides, Brook has always been a dedicated traveller and explorer -- one of nature's cosmopolitans - - and Paris had long been a second home.

In the three years before they founded the Bouffes-du-Nord, the actors were, in his words, "a nomad group" playing in Iran, in America and in Africa, where they would famously enter a village, unroll a carpet, and see how they could hold the attention of an audience by the acting-out of stories.

Some of this had its seeds in Brook's legendary English production of A Midsummer Night's Dream. This is famous for its stage magic -- its juggling, stilts and trapezes -- but Brook cites a single performance given in a Birmingham youth club where none of the trappings could be used. They had to re-invent the performance as they went along -- while using all the knowledge they had accumulated of the play itself -- and to trust one another and the audience. They found that in these circumstances an audience "can give you more."

It also got him out from under the proscenium arch: a magical frame in which he had been able to conjure anything he wished. He was a comparatively late convert to the doctrine of the open stage - - actors and audience "in one room" -- but now it governs his work. The Bouffes -- certainly when I used to visit it regularly in the '70s -- is a superb space, a crucible of energies. And always Brook himself seemed to be somewhere in evidence, dressed in a smock, like an unusually cultured concierge. He had gone discreetly native.

The troupe's membership is fluid; people come, go, and constantly return. As semi-permanent pillars, Brook cites a remarkable Japanese actor, Yoshi Oida, and Bruce Myers, a thoughtful and mischievous actor whom I once described, to his own delight, as "a Royal Shakespeare Company dropout."

Brook is skeptical about the idea of maintaining an absolutely permanent company. "In the '60s we all had this dream of 'making a family' and it always exploded in a short time; a self-declared family is an artificial one. We don't have to do our casting artificially, around a fixed group; people can leave us and go out into the ordinary show-business world: into an environment that's less comfortable than ours, but necessary."

Brook himself was once a vital part of that "ordinary show- business world," directing musicals and London farces as well as Shakespeare and opera, and arguably he remains so. His most successful Paris productions tend to re-appear across the world (often transmuted from French into English) and he remains a master showman and visual orchestrator, even when working on a tiny scale; in The Suit he created magically poignant images with a single orange sheet on a bed. And the commercial theatre on which he cut his teeth -- the enchantment-hungry theatre of a London still locked in postwar austerity -- was in many ways an exciting one.

"In England, they'd never heard of the idea of ensemble," he says. "And yet John Gielgud had essentially been working over and over with the same people -- he had 30 years of ensemble. I was happy to be in a milieu of highly talented people, where the watchword was quality at all costs -- even if it was at the cost of a certain kind of reality. I consciously went on from that; I could feel the changing winds."

His work now varies between free-flowing but meticulous explorations of classic plays (Shakespeare, Chekhov) and new works often built on old literary or mythological foundations; the most celebrated a nine-hour version (sometimes done as "a dusk-to-dawn mar-athon") of the Indian epic The Mahabharata. The Suit, being a new but pre-existing play, is an exception, though its African cast and setting obviously carry on from some of his earlier work.

He is appreciative of other people's epic accomplishments; a few years ago he visited Montreal with a production of Samuel Beckett's Happy Days, a virtual monologue featuring his actress-wife Natasha Parry. Here he broke a self-imposed rule by writing to the local papers, protesting against what he considered their churlish treatment of Robert Lepage's masterwork The Seven Streams of the River Ota. Lepage must have been delighted; it's difficult to think of a theatrical figure whose good opinion is better worth having.
