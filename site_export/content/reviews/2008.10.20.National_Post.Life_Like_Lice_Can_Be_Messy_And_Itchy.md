SCRATCH

Factory Theatre

Charlotte Corbeil-Coleman is the daughter of Layne Coleman, actor, director and former head of Theatre Passe Muraille, and Carole Corbeil, novelist, critic and plawright. Her play Scratch, begun eight years ago and reaching the Factory Theatre stage after many expansions and reworkings, started out as her reaction to two things: her mothers death from cancer and her own stubborn case of head lice. The names, and probably many of the circumstances, have been changed, but what occurs on the stage feels unshakably autobiographical, especially as the author herself appears in the central role. The other characters, each with their own take on the protagonist's double predicament, are her parents, her best friend, her mother's sister and a young man, known as the Poet, who is enlisted to care for the mother in the closing stages of her illness.

The first thing to be said about Corbeil-Coleman as a playwright is that her words swing. By that I mean that her dialogue, unlike that in 90% of purportedly realistic plays, never has you protesting that people dont actually talk like that. It isnt glib, fancy or stilted. But neither is it slavishly naturalistic. It has rhythm, wit and flavour. It also has a quick pulse. This is, obviously, a selfconscious play, and with a selfconscious shape. "If this were my story ..." says each of the characters, some of them repeatedly, as they watch Anna, the heroine, grappling with her twin afflictions. The one who comes closest to making it her own story is Madelyn, the friend, who watches, sympathetically aghast, as Anna seems to be withdrawing from her dying mother and refusing to visit her hospital bed, just when you would imagine the two of them might need each other most. At age 15, Anna's declared reaction to her imminent loss is that it makes her horny; she throws herself at the bemused Poet. (Nice man, comments her father, "terrible poet.") Madelyn, an aspiring painter, meanwhile wants nothing more than to be with Anna's mother, herself an artist who has always encouraged her. It isnt exactly Rashomon; nobody disagrees about the facts of the situation. Where they differ is over which facts, and especially which feelings, they want to stress.

The most questionable aspect of the production is the onstage presence of the author. Not that her performance is bad: Its actually very good, uncompromising in its head-tossing (and of course head-scratching) depiction of stubbornness as a form of evasion, or of innocence trying desperately hard to be experienced. It isn't that the author-actor tries to present herself in a good light; she's actually doubly hard on herself. It would be good to hear her voice mediated through someone elses. That could make it more sympathetic but not, I think, sentimental.

Apart from this, and from a habit of sending the actors walking around in circles (a device thats been patented by Scorched), ahdri zhina mandiela's direction is excellent. Monica Dottor gives a glowing, beautifully detailed account of Madelyn, while Mary Ann Mc-Donald's Mother pretty much defines the word gentle, even when being infuriating. Shes an unreconstructed spirit from the 60s or, to put it geographically rather than historically, she's the Annex made melting flesh. She denies her daughter the shampoo that might heal her hair because of the chemicals it contains. (We never do learn whether Anna used it at a later date. Or if not, why not.) She and her musician-husband (Kevin Bundy, very likeable especially when putting on brave faces) share, before tragedy strikes, a delectable variation on the traditional marital row over finances: "You are not" says Mother, striking an ambiguous blow for feminism, the only one not bringing enough money into the house. Ryan Hollyman does as much as is probably possible with the over-writing and underwritten Poet. Catherine Fitch plays his sharp-edged girlfriend, who is a bit of a caricature, plus several semi-official ladies who are rather more so; her most prominent role, though, is the Aunt, whom she eventually makes believable, even though her personality is primarily defined by her unwillingness to spend money on anything except taxis (for safety) and Kleenex (for just about everything). These are believable human quirks that somehow seem stagey when put on a stage. More importantly, one of the play's major climaxes falls into the same category of things that may very well have happened but still seem too good, or at least too neat, to be true.

There is something schematic about the play. Im not sure that it justifies its structure or even the metaphor thats emblazoned in its title: life equated with lice, messy with itchy. I don't know that it ends up making any particular statement about grief, loss or survival. But it does show them all happening -- believably, sometimes touchingly, and nearly always entertainingly. It's an impressive calling card. - Scratch runs through Nov. 2 at the Factory Theatre. Call 416-504-9971 for more information.
