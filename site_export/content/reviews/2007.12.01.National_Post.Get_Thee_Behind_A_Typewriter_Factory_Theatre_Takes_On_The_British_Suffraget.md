Linda Griffiths's Age of Arousal is a historical fantasia about the British suffragette movement. Its action unfolds over the single year of 1885, but the author has avowedly crammed into that time events and currents of thought from 1869 to 1914. The title is a pun; it refers to both intellectual and sexual arousal. Indeed it could be a multiple pun, since the actions and reactions of the five women in the cast are at least partially determined by how old they are.

At the senior end, there's Mary Barfoot, who's served time for militant activities and begins the play with a powerful account of how it felt to be force-fed, that is tortured. Now retired from front-line protest, she does her bit by running a secretarial school, giving women the tools to achieve the economic independence that has to precede all the other kinds. She owns a set of Remington typewriters that take on a practically totemic significance for all who behold them. Her admiring though increasingly mutinous assistant, with whom she has a physical as well as a professional relationship, is Rhoda Nunn, who is in her thirties. Rhoda re- encounters a trio of former acquaintances, the Madden sisters, who since the death of their father have been leading wretched lives of genteel poverty; they are, in order, Alice, Virginia and Monica. Monica, at 21, is the youngest person in the play and works as a shopgirl; Alice and Virginia, both middle-aged and more inhibited by their upbringing, sit at home and starve. Mary takes the sisters in and introduces them to the brave new world of secretarial training.

The one male in the play is Everard, Mary's cousin and a youngish ex-doctor. He falls in lust with Monica and in love with Rhoda, who fascinates him both as a woman and as a Woman; he is beginning to "get" feminism in the way one might get religion. Rhoda responds, warily but hungrily; she is the one character who might be said to cross a sexual bar line, Alice settling happily for celibacy and Virginia for dressing as a man with no emotional consequences, or at least none that are made very clear.

The play has a pattern rather than a story, and the dominant thread is how women relate to men when they feel theoretically obliged to break free of them. (Monica is the only one who doesn't feel that. She's a pre-post-feminist.) To a lesser extent, it's about how men relate to women in the same historical circumstances. However, this love-plot, for want of a better term, descends into melodrama. Fierier, if transient, sparks are struck by observations like Alice's when confronted by the Remingtons: "But men are secretaries." So they were, and it was technology that made the difference; there was a time when it was as revolutionary for women to get behind a boss's typewriter as it later became for them to escape from it. There's also a more elaborate, intellectually suggestive sequence in which the characters attend an avant-garde art exhibition and give us their impressions of Impressionism, experienced as both visual revelation and social threat. Such chronological cross-lighting effects, though, are surprisingly rare. We're shown little of what historically happened. It's theory divorced from practice.

This is all the more frustrating when you confront the published text. For this, Griffiths supplies a personal and historical essay that's far more compelling than anything that appears onstage. Among much else, it contains a nuts-and-bolts outline of the fight for women's suffrage and of how, as in all revolutionary movements, the moderates parted company from the militants. It may be too big a canvas for Griffiths, or anyone else, to encompass in a play but it could make a wonderful film--one that no one, as far as I know, has ever attempted.

The dialogue in Age of Arousal is elegant, sometimes witty, but it always seems to be floating above the ground, even when the people are supposed to be suffering. This extends to substantial passages in which the characters utter their thoughts, unheard by their companions; the author calls this "thought-speak" and says that it's different from the Shakespearean soliloquy or aside. She's right; those Elizabethan speeches are dynamic, the actors talk to us. Hers are static and unhelpfully flowery. But nothing is ever new, and her idea was given a thorough and more vigorous workout by Eugene O'Neill in his 1920s marathon Strange Interlude, itself memorably burlesqued by Groucho in the Marx Brothers' Animal Crackers.

Anyway, in Maja Ardal's production, the convention is difficult to discern; the texture is soggy, and the colours run. The set (by Julia Tribe) evokes nowhere in particular, and among the actors it's every man for herself. An English accent goes very well with Clare Coulter's customary air of abstracted hoity-toitiness; whether her magisterial eccentricity is what the role of Mary actually requires is another matter. Ellen-Ray Hennessy and Maggie Huculak do the elder sisters as comic turns, especially Hennessy whose Virginia is burdened by the plot with alcoholism and a trip to Berlin from which she brings back only red herrings. Gemma James-Smith has a more rewarding role as Monica and brings some freshness and energy to it. Sarah Dodd does the most powerful work as Rhoda, though, like most actors trapped in limbo, she sometimes pushes too hard to get out of it. Dylan Smith's main qualification for Everard seems to be that he's actually British; he seems too young and underpowered for the role. Maybe I'm being genderist (hey, I think I've invented a word) but the man's inner struggles strike me as the most interesting in the play. That, at least, is to the play's credit. It has the honesty to be conflicted.
