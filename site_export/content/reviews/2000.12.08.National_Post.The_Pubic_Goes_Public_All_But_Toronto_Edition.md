THE VAGINA MONOLOGUES

The Music Hall, Toronto

The production known as The Vagina Monologues started as a solo performance, given off-Broadway by Eve Ensler, an actress and playwright. To put it mildly, it caught on. The cast was expanded from one to three; the show became a metropolitan phenomenon and an international franchise. It now tours the world.

It is a highly entertaining show, enlivening and even enlightening. In format, it could hardly be simpler (except, presumably, in its one-person incarnation). The three performers sit on stools, at microphones, and read. I still think they should at least memorize their opening remarks, when they're welcoming us in their own persons. There is, though, a good excuse, at least for one of them. The team consists of two American full-timers and a local guest; the latter position is rotated weekly, and its incumbent would hardly have time to learn the lines.

The guest star this week is Gloria Reuben, TV star, actress and singer, and she is tremendous, superbly limber and relaxed, both vocally and physically. She has the most spectacular routine of the evening, in which she plays a lesbian dominatrix who has become a connoisseur, not only of other women's vaginas, but of other women's huge variety of pleasurable moans. She demonstrates several, getting especial mileage from the WASP moan, daintily surprised, and the triple-orgasm moan, for which she draws spectacularly on her professional vocal prowess. (If you attend a later performance you may find Shirley Douglas or Sonja Smits in the home-girl role; I wonder how they'll get on.)

The Yanks on Reuben's flanks are Starla Benford, a wry comedian with a crackling forte that she knows how to hold, and Sherri Parker Lee, who looks and sounds as Southern as her name and does world- weary extremely well.

Reading or even reciting material is not the easiest task for an actor; the usual tendency is to overstress but under-characterize. Here, though, all three consistently, and exhilaratingly, hit the right note. The words they deliver derive from interviews conducted by the author with hundreds of women. The subjects were asked what they thought their vaginas might like to wear (the replies seem to have been wearyingly arch) and what they might like to say (the replies are instructively hilarious, with "slow down" the most popular choice). Some of the responses were turned into longer speeches -- one wonders with how much editorial intervention.

Some of the stories are sad: One 70-year-old woman tells of coming in her first boyfriend's car and never wanting to have sex again. Some are terrible: A Bosnian woman gives a grim, detailed account of multiple rape. (This is somewhat sweetened -- unwisely, I think -- by some flowery poetic commentary, but the horror comes through, and it's a tribute to the show that it can co-exist with the surrounding humour without either element feeling forced or devalued.) Some are stoically amusing, even while professing anger, like a description of visits to the gynecologist.

And most are celebratory. A young girl, who'd been abused by a male family friend, tells of a subsequent more pleasurable initiation by a sophisticated older woman. One woman is even allowed to tell, a little shamefacedly, of being awakened by a man who loved vaginas, especially looking at them. Apparently he wasn't too prepossessing in other respects, but it's reassuring to hear him given that much credit.

The phrase "the myth of the vaginal orgasm" is never actually spoken, but it does sort of hover. I found myself wondering defensively if there might not even, somewhere, be some lesbians who were lousy lovers. The show does, magically, include a celebration of the clitoris, the only human organ, we are reminded, that functions solely as a source of pleasure. The puritanical downside of this, we are duly reminded, is genital mutilation.

The show take its title rather freely. Sometimes the vagina is treated strictly anatomically. Sometimes it becomes a metaphor. It is certainly an opportunist show. But among its qualities is that of being the most vivid dramatization of The Joy of Sex, in several varieties, that we are ever likely to encounter.
