A MONTH IN THE COUNTRY

Young Centre for the Performing Arts, Toronto

---

A wise man once said that the tragedy in an Ibsen play is that nothing proves to be what it seems, while the tragedy in a Chekhov play is that everything does. Chekhov's compatriot Ivan Turgenev was on to this before him, and Laszlo Marton's fascinating Soulpepper production of A Month in the Country, Turgenev's one major play, drives the point home in its final image. On one side of the stage Natalya Petrovna, bored wife of a landowner, reclining in a chair; on the other, an assortment of friends, relatives and dependants playing cards.

The play starts that way too, though there are some differences in personnel. By the end, as in Three Sisters or Uncle Vanya, various people have departed, taking some life and hope with them. Natalya's situation hasn't changed, but it's been stripped. When we first met her, she was frustrated; now she's drained and desperate. And, in a play that's as much comedy as tragedy, it's largely her own fault. She's been playing with people's lives.

The most important man in her own life has been not her affectionately oblivious husband but her long-standing platonic admirer Rakitin. Into this mix has come Belyayev, a new young tutor for Natalya's son, and still more or less a boy himself. He attracts every woman in the household, including Natalya's ward, Vera. Jealousy almost drives Natalya to marry the girl off to a grotesque suitor, and though she pulls back, damage has been done. In fact Belyayev, no Casanova, nurses what he considers a hopeless passion for Natalya herself, and the revelation throws everyone into confusion. Natalya makes her own, technically unstained bed, but everyone else's good intentions help arrange the bedding.

Turgenev was a greater novelist than playwright, and some things about the play are lax; minor characters flash in and out rather than, as in Chekhov, being held in suspension to reflect the central relationships, though that's clearly the intention. The relationships themselves are developed at leisure and resolved in haste, often without the satisfaction either of an explosion or a dying fall. But along with this goes a vaudevillean freedom unexpected in a drawing-room play; one scene climaxes in a song (scripted) and a bit of a dance (not). Marton's production uses a sparky new adaptation by himself and Susan Coyne, and a setting (by Andrei Both) that could be indoors or out or both at once. A similar ambiguity pervades the time-frame; the dialogue still denotes mid-19th-century Russia, but Rakitin is dressed like a 1930s boulevardier, while the props include a car, a radio and a skateboard, ridden effortlessly by Belyayev and very gingerly indeed by Rakitin -- an effect that in itself is enough to justify the anachronisms. The multiple focus brings us close to the characters pyschologically while still respecting the society from which they spring.

Once again in a Marton production, the characters are driven to physical extremes that are startling but completely appropriate. There is one of the director's trademark thunderstorms, and people get drenched in other ways too. If the production, like the play, remains fine but frustrating it's because it doesn't solve the problem of why we're supposed to care about the heroine. A few years ago, in a far freer adaptation at the Shaw Festival, Fiona Byrne gave a performance of the role that was both sympathetic and unsparing. This time she seems to be playing it scene by scene: honestly and attractively but without ever finding the key. Not at least until that last tableau, the production's own invention, that finds her catatonic, fanned by a wind-machine invented by her solicitous spouse. (In the original he was working on a thresher.)

Diego Matamoros, who opens the proceedings bending over Natalya in a pose at once predatory and protective, plays an urbane, self-mocking and self-torturing Rakitin; his observant spirit pervades the play, as it should -- Turgenev had been there himself. Joseph Ziegler is outrageously believable as the local doctor who exploits the gentry as bitterly and hilariously as they unthinkingly patronize him. One of the production's pleasures is that it allows these two actors, than whom we have none better, to square off against each other--for the first time, I think. Another is that it lets Ziegler and his wife Nancy Palk, as a beanpole ladies' companion yearning for escape, play a wonderfully unromantic proposal scene, that's here even more of a delight than usual. Jeff Lillico gets both the brashness and the shyness of Belyaev ringingly right, and Tal Gottfried's Vera grows rapidly and impressively from slip of a schoolgirl to disillusioned woman. As Natalya's husband, nobly overcoming his jealousy of the wrong man, David Storch is a full-blooded treat. It's even more of a treat, and a relief, to see Soulpepper tackling a big play again.
