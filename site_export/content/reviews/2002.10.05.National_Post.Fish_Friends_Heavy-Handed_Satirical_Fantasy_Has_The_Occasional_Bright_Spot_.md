Tarragon Theatre until Oct. 20. Box office 416-531-1827.

You want trends? I'll give you trends. The Tarragon Theatre seems bent on reinventing itself as an aquarium. Its main stage is currently occupied by Girl in a Goldfish Bowl, about a girl who fancies that the familys lodger is the reincarnation of her late wet pet. Now in the smaller room we have Fish/Wife, about a woman who is preparing a dish of sashimi for her guests and starts identifying with the raw material.

Rick Roberts, a good actor, here directs his own first play, a heavy-handed satirical fantasy with the occasional bright spot. Gwen is in her kitchen, with her thoughts divided between the menu and metaphysical speculation, when the dish shakes and water sprays up. Further experience reveals that the fish is alive, and that it reacts in this violent way whenever mention is made in its presence of death or dying or some cognate concept. Assisting Gwen in these discoveries is her lifelong friend Joanne.

Joanne has children; Gwen is married but childless. Her husband Rob is off in the next room, partying; Gwen has reason to believe that he is partying most enthusiastically with another old acquaintance named Sarah, whom she and Joanne have always resented. It is plain that they also, despite protestations of mutual devotion, resent one another. They also resent themselves, or the lives they are leading. I found the feeling contagious.

The two actresses come before us as clowns, in fright wigs and rouged cheeks. The play and production, in fact, signal their contempt for the characters from the first moment, leaving us wondering why we should be spending so much time in their company. (The play lasts only 50 minutes, but its more than enough.)

The raw fish dish is the only one that Gwen can cook, though her husband has uttered unkind thoughts as to whether it counts as cooking. Joanne is a strict vegetarian, and there are some fairly amusing exchanges on whether less strict vegetarians are transgressing their principles by eating fish. In their best moments, the two women recall the enjoyable bitching of Absolutely Fabulous, and Marjorie Campbell's performance as Joanne has indeed strong squawking echoes of the wondrous Joanna Lumley; bestowing gaseous condescension from a great height she commands attention to begin with but is exhausting by the end.

Lindsay Collins, as Gwen, is allowed to pace herself more, and so stays the course better.

She is helped by being the one to whom the really traumatic things happen; by the end of the play she is developing secondary fishy characteristics on her own account. But both performances nag.

There is some skill in the way that Roberts keeps us apprised of what's going on in Gwens marriage, and even what may have led her to this pass. But these suburban, or maybe just middle-class, tensions, have been explored a hundred times before. That doesnt make them irredeemable; Alan Ayckbourn regularly conjures high, sad comedy out of them, but then he bothers to understand his characters.

Roberts reaches back to the long-dead tradition of the Theatre of the Absurd, in which conventional people and conventional speech patterns are arraigned and invariably found wanting; he does with fish what Eugene Ionesco did with rhinos. We have seen over the last couple of years that some of Ionesco's plays still stand up (I doubt if all of them would) but there is a sense of wonder, even of poetry, in the way he approaches his subjects. He could also be very funny.

Roberts has some good lines, but the best of them -- something about recognizing the sound of mirthless laughter" -- rebounds all too easily on the play itself. It's fundamentally patronizing: an attack on undefended targets. (Or, of course, like shooting fish in a barrel.) And if the moral is that those who serve fish shall perish by fish -- or perish as fish -- then I don't think it's worth the trouble. There are some good macabre touches in the production, the best of them held in reserve until the very last second.
