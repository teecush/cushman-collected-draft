TWELFTH NIGHT

Festival Theatre, Stratford, Ont.

I loathe bouzouki music, and there is a great deal of it tinkling away in the new Stratford Twelfth Night. The rationale is that this is Illyria, lady, and that what was Illyria is now Yugoslavia. That is hardly an idyllic locale these days, but the trappings of this production are early-middle-20th century, when presumably things were nicer; the twins wear a dazzling match of cream suits. There is a lot of street life, far more than the play demands or needs, though I grudgingly admit that most of it is put to good dramatic use. You might call Antoni Cimolino's staging the Balkanization of Twelfth Night.

I wanted to get the demeaning trappings out of the way, because underneath them is the best all-round production of this wonderful but elusive play I have seen in decades. There is nothing revolutionary about it; its theme is the traditional one of the madness of love, a distemper successfully worked through by nearly everybody in the cast. The exceptions are Feste, who was not affected in the first place, and Malvolio, who as an incurable narcissist (sick of self-love, says Olivia) is doomed to end in an undiminished fury of self-righteousness, aggravated now by genuine psychological pain. When he storms out vowing revenge, he is, to quote Tom Stoppard on Hamlet, stark raving sane.

There are eight crucial characters in Twelfth Night, which is one reason its such a devil to get right, and they are all well played, some of them outstandingly.

Even better than their individual strengths are the way they relate and interlock. The confrontation of Malvolio and Sir Toby Belch is the explosion of an antagonism that has obviously been smouldering for years. The way in which Olivia chimes in on Festes jokes, knowing not only the lines but the timing, denotes a life- long rapport between jester and employer from which the puritan steward Malvolio, however invaluable and good at his job, is pointedly and forever excluded; he stands outside, simmering.

The connecting lines are even visible when one of the participants is offstage. When Sir Toby boozily proclaims his devotion to his niece, we can feel her version of the story hanging menacingly in the air. The Stratford platform, which has been known to tear a play into disconnected strips, can also be a potent crucible for the union of disparate elements. So it is here, on a set by Peter Hartwell that consists of the familiar structure plus some lattice work.

The dominating presence, as in Robin Phillips' production 20 years ago, is William Hutt's Feste, slightly frailer now (his songs are miked) but with the same enormous shrewdness, patience and dignity. There is also, I think, more cruelty in his baiting of Malvolio; their encounters bring two views of life into collision.

This Feste has either experienced everything or observed everything (we dont know which and he isnt telling) and he meets the other characters on ambiguous ground. He is their economic dependent and their spiritual superior, which makes them into patrons afraid to patronize; the exception is again Malvolio, who pays for it. He is also, as a jester, very professional; Feste's jokes ("Dost thou live by thy tabor?" No, sir, I live by the church) are commonly thought beyond saving, but Hutt with calm authority gets a laugh with every one of them. When that voice says something is funny, you believe it.

The voice begins the proceedings, in recorded form, with a snatch of the closing song about the wind and the rain. It heralds an actual storm, the one that shipwrecks Viola, but before that we see Sean Arbuckle's Duke Orsino, apparently ingesting the musical food of love while huddling under an umbrella down by the shore, and practising what looks like Zorbas dance to boot. When an attendant asks if hell go hunt, you're tempted to murmur, "What, in this weather?" It seems like strange behaviour but it makes increasing sense; this is a violently lovesick Orsino, whose insecurity causes him to shout as well as mope, and who is capable of the final threat to kill the thing he loves.

Equally startling and equally justifiable is Michelle Giroux's coltish Olivia, who, once lured out of mourning, never looks back, perhaps because she never loved her dead brother too much in the first place. Tara Roslings lucid, graceful Viola is the most enchantingly accomplished Stratford debut I remember. Her sisterly grief is real; its beautifully brought out by the gentle embarrassment of John Dolan's sea captain when he realizes that in gossiping about Olivia's loss he has stepped on Violas: an early sign that the production cares, and is on the right track. This Viola can snap out of grief and into love, and can relish chance and adventure, without ever losing track of her deepest feelings. She is also, a real test in this role, as comfortable in farce as in romance.

Of the licensed farceurs, James Blendicks familiar impersonation of a decayed English colonel comes in ideally for a Sir Toby with an infinite supply of hip flasks. Domini Blythe is a superb Maria, housekeeper rather than chambermaid, economically extrovert and in control of every scene she enters. Michael Therriault, who enters the scene in cap and goggles -- has he driven to Illyria or flown? - - is a Sir Andrew Aguecheek who swings metronomically between assertiveness and subservience; caught wearing yellow, Olivia's least favourite colour, he strips off the offending garments as if in some ceremony of self-flagellation and returns in sporty stripes, which do him no favours either. He can do nothing, though, about his hair, which remains obstinately flaxen.

Hair is an obsession of Peter Donaldson's Malvolio, who sports a rebellious toupee and -- we learn -- sticks curlers in it at night. This is the broadest performance of the evening, or at least the butt of the broadest direction. The tango into which he forces Olivia at the height of his delusion is indefensible, physically if not psychologically: Where is the music supposed to be coming from? But even when pushed to the brink, the actor never topples over. This is a bold, thoughtful account of a man who embraces humiliation but still keeps something unsullied; you believe he thinks nobly of the soul. You dont feel sorry for him, you may not even respect him, but you accept he has a place in the world and the right to fill it.

Its wrong for Sebastian to be seen apparently getting out of bed with Olivia, just after their first meeting and just before their marriage; she isn't that kind of girl and he, a truthful lad and Viola's true brother, says in the last scene he is still a virgin. It's even more wrong for the priest who unites them to raise his eyebrows and virtually wink at the audience. Much of the comic business -- the duel, for example -- is funny by any reckoning; some isn't, and striving to please the public, as Feste promises, should not mean pandering to it or to your own worst impulses. There is richness and subtlety here, and I hope they survive the season unscathed.
