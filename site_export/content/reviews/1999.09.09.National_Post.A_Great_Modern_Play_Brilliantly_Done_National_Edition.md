PLATONOV

du Maurier Theatre Centre, Toronto

Until September 17, (416) 973-4000

In and around Toronto, this has been a glorious theatrical summer. There has been an impressive average of enjoyable productions at Stratford (six out of ten), Niagara (six-and-a-half out of eleven), and Coronation Park, Oakville (one out of one). But the crown, beyond any question, goes to the still-new Soulpepper company, which, in five ambitious shows, has hardly put a foot wrong.

Soulpepper has had the stunningly simple idea of putting good actors in good plays and lacing both in the hands of good directors. It's that last component that separates it from some other well- intentioned actors' collectives; and its association this year with Hungarian director Laszlo Marton has proved inspirational. His production of The Play's the Thing was a beautifully sustained exercise in high comedy; his version of Chekhov's Platonov is that and much more.

Any staging of Platonov is a "version" since the original, written and discarded when its author was 20, would play uncut for about seven hours. It doesn't even have a canonical title; it has previously been billed in English as That Fellow Platonov, Don Juan (in the Russian Manner), and -- in a translation by Michael Frayn, heavy on the farce and amused by the sex -- Wild Honey. Some things of course remain constant. That Russian Don Juan fellow Platonov is a schoolmaster in his mid-30s passionately adored by all four of the principal women in the cast, one of them his own wife.

He reciprocates their devotion, at least up to a point; they are all more forceful than he is and there is a definite sense that he is going with a flow that he may once have started but is now unable to control.

He also drinks too much and berates himself for the loss of his former ideals --when, that is, he is not berating other people for losing theirs or, alternatively, for still having them. These bewildering philsophic swings recur in Chekhov's later plays but they are never so vividly concentrated in one character as they are here. He is described as "the hero of a great modern play that unfortunately hasn't been written yet"; that line sounds too good to be true, an adaptor's invention, but no, it's in the original. And it's true, except -- we learn -- that the play was written. Carrying an equal load of his author's sympathy and disapproval, he comes to a violent end.

He has already survived two attempts on his life, one of them his own. His wife meanwhile, though generally equable enough, has tried to throw herself under a train, onstage. (The original gives her a second suicide bid, cut here.) Another character gets lynched, an event greeted by the rest with a modicum of regret and a total lack of surprise. This is not one of those Chekhovian plays in which nothing happens. Actually none of his plays are, but this one is more not that than any other.

The adaptation here is by Marton himself and Susan Coyne; apparently, he chose and edited the scenes and she wrote the words, which are occasionally too self-consciously slangy but are full of life. About half the original has been cut, and what remains has been re-ordered; notably, all the older characters have gone. (There are moments when they seem to be kept forcibly offstage.)

The location of one scene is left frustratingly unclear, but otherwise everything is marvellously lucid: a tangled scenario made brilliantly clear. All the characters are absurd, and most of them know it; and their extravagance is rendered with absolute precision; Marton seems to have drawn his actors to their limits -- and they do seem to have been drawn, not pushed. The play takes place in summer, and you feel the heat, inside and out. The play is performed in timeless costumes on an accommodating brown-canvas set; only some very recognizable Toronto carrier-bags seemed gratuitously jarring.

All the actors stretch out, sometimes literally; there never was was such a play for characters lying full-length across the stage, exposing their souls and occasionally their bodies, being doused with water, covered with newspaper. Albert Schultz as Platonov starts with a charming, self-lacerating languor that stealthily expands until he takes on the status, not of a tragic hero but of something more winningly, if uncomfortably, recognizable: the nearest thing in drama to a modern Everyman.

His female nemeses include a chatelaine, who in the original is contending with the loss of her estate (not actually a cherry orchard but you get the idea). Deprived of this dimension Nancy Palk plays her as a rangy, glitter-eyed and impatient seductress. Susan Coyne, as a friend's wife whom our hero seduces, is a challengingly chill idealist-turned-avenging angel. Robyn Stevan is magnificently believable as Mrs. Platonov, simultaneously stolid and vulnerable. As a bookworm whom he teases with disastrous results, Liisa Repo- Martell is magic: eccentricity rendered with dazzling economy and absolute truth.

Among the men, Stuart Hughes is inalienably North American (Stanley Kowalski in a white suit) but he rises beautifully to his great scene as a farcically tearful cuckold. Chekhov, when he wrote the play, was an incipient doctor as well as an incipient author and he paints a prophetically bitter portrait of a physician who has to be sent almost at gun-point to a sickbed; he also gets to speak the play's valediction, which Diego Matamoros delivers with an extraordinary blend of anger, grotesquerie and pathos.

Mike Shara is stiff-backed and very funny as a priggish student, Platonov's most implacable enemy bar one. The exception is a murderous horse-thief, accurately played by Michael Hanrahan as the most moral and clear-sighted person on stage. There is also a quartet of servants, tiny roles faultlessly played.

It's moving to see each actor take a quick, discreet call at the end. I confess that up to a couple of years ago I would not have thought theatre of this quality existed in Canada. This is a show that can face the world.
