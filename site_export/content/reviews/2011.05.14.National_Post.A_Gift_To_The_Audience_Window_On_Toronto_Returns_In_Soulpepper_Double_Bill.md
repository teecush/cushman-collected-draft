I was so delighted by Soulpepper's Window on Toronto when I first saw it that I rather dreaded seeing it again. The show started life last year as part of the companys lab series, exploratory productions mounted for just a few performances on one of the Young Centres smallest stages. I was afraid familiarity, plus the move to the centre's second-largest space, might dilute the magic. I was thoroughly, jubilantly wrong. This is the funniest, most enchanting theatrical concoction since . well, I honestly can't think of a comparison.

The window through which we are invited to look is the service-portal of a hot-dog truck in Nathan Phillips Square. The stalls proprietor, whose name is Jason -and whos played by Jason Patrick Rothery -begins the proceedings by opening up for business and then retreating to the front row of the auditorium, from which he proceeds to watch the action. We, of course, watch it with him, through a small square brightly lit aperture surrounded by darkness. It's like a puppet stage, except that the figures who appear on it, mostly from the waist up, are teemingly flesh and blood. They are played by six members of last year's Soulpepper academy plus a couple of newcomers. They appear, disappear and reappear at a staggering rate and in an equally exhilarating multiplicity of guises. At first it seems that we are going to be watching a day in the life. By the time the show has run its 45-minute course, we will have watched a year, one that, before our very eyes and in a vivid succession of weathers, brings birth, love, marriage and near-death.

Jason himself is the mostly still centre of this maelstrom; and Rothery is superbly imperturbable. Jasons customers dont just come to him for sausages on buns. Many of them, including those who've never met him before, treat him as a friend and confidant. They use his counter, and the space behind it, as a drop-off and pick-up point. One (Brendan Wall) leaves a guitar in storage, and gets very threatening when he learns that someone else has picked it up. (All right, you've been lucky this time is his gracious response, more or less, when his instrument reappears.) Actors, using their real names, pick up scripts, and are dashed to find theyre for The Hobbit. (Jason says consolingly that it was always going to be a small role.) Tourists ask for the Vatican and are told to turn right at the Colosseum. A recent immigrant, in search of a girlfriend, is warned against seeking her in the back pages of Now, but finds romance (and more) anyway; Ins Chois ingratiating performance of this Romeo-in-waiting is probably the show's star turn, buttressed by his more sinister appearances in other hats and, especially, shirts. Gregory Prest comes by, belligerently, on a bike. Andre Sills looms, hugely, once as a cut-out of himself. People ask for stuff Jason doesn't stock, and occasionally congratulate him on the stuff that he does. An old lady (Trish Lindstrom) asks, more than once, if Jason knew there used to be a corner store on his site. Somebody else asks him if hes looked out recently. He replies that its all he does.

Among the things he sees is the rest of the cast travelling diagonally up and down as if on an escalator; I can't account for this naturalistically, but it's a wonderful effect. I started laughing almost as soon as the show began, and hardly stopped until the end. Even at the sad bits I smiled, not, I think, because Im particularly callous, but out of sheer joy at a production that feels like a perfect, loving gift from actors to audience, and of course of the quick-fire technical brilliance of it all. The most recent production, come to think of it, that does compare is the Shaw Festivals The President (to be revived this season), another high-speed firework display bursting with humanity, if of a more jaundiced kind. Window on Toronto is the best possible advertisement for the city. And for hot dogs.

It's directed, perhaps unexpectedly, by Laszlo Marton, who's scarcely a Toronto resident, though Im told hes done similar shows on his home turf. (Window on Budapest?) It shares a bill with (re)Birth: E.E. Cummings in Song, which doesn't name a director, though the first question I asked when I caught its trial airing at the last-but-one Global cabaret festival was "Who staged this?" It's a beautiful-looking and beautiful-sounding piece in which 10 actor-musicians (eight of them doubling from the evenings other half) appear in charming, intricate formations within a white marquee. (Theres an acknowledgment to Brendan Healy, the more-than-promising director who was part of the year's academy intake and now runs Buddies in Bad Times.) They perform musical settings of Cummings poems, to a constantly varying instrumental lineup, which at one point includes two accordions, which is a lot. There are a couple of drawbacks. One is that there is no discernible shape or progression; we are adjured to let it all just wash over us but when words are involved, as opposed to just music or dance, we can't help looking for sense. The other is the words themselves; I have great tolerance for whimsical wordplay, but Cummings' variety rubs me the wrong way. Also, the music makes them hard to follow, as is often the case with verse originally intended for neither music nor theatre. The best number is the chronicle of a courtship and marriage: It has a story; it's sung by musical director and multi-instrumentalist Mike Ross at his piquant and grainy best; and its visuals include a heart-stopping embrace between a fully lit actor and a silhouette. Cummings comes on first in the double bill, Window second. I saw them at a preview, but no allowances were needed.
