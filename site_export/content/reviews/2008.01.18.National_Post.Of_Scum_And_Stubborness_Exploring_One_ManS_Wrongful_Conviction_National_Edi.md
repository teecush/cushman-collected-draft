William Sampson can still laugh. That may be the most surprising - - though certainly not the most shocking -- revelation in Confessions of an Innocent Man, David Papermy's film about Sampson's three-year ordeal in a Saudi Arabian prison, tortured and condemned for a crime he plainly did not commit.

It's angry laughter, obviously, but it's also self-mocking and, at the same time, self-approving. He says that when he was finally released and arrived at Heathrow (after a flight spent half-fearing that the plane would land somewhere else and he'd be taken back) he looked in the mirror and saw "Charles Manson on a bad day." He qualifies that: "I didn't look as bad as I thought." He had reason to look bad: He had spent the last two and a half years of his imprisonment in solitary confinement, practising every form of non co-operation short of a hunger strike. He prowled his cell naked with his body covered in his own excrement. He is, by his own proclamation, a stubborn son of a bitch, and that's what got him through. He's justifiably proud. And honest. There's more than one kind of confession going on here.

The film is based on Sampson's book of the same title, and though it comes with a commentary spoken (in craggy, oracular tones) by Martin Sheen, it's Sampson himself in interviews who does most of the storytelling. The narrative is plainly laid out. We learn about Sampson's life as a business consultant in Riyadh, part of an expatriate British colony (he himself has dual U.K. and Canadian citizenship) boozing it up in an officially -- and rigorously --dry country.

Then, we learn about the day in 2000 when his life changed forever; a car-bomb killed a British engineer and the Saudi authorities, anxious to allay any suspicions of homegrown terrorism, pinned the crime on a Dutch friend of Sampson's who happened to be in the car behind. Under interrogation, he implicated Sampson and another expat (a Scot, as it happens, like Sampson himself ). Then followed confessions extracted under torture, a kangaroo trial, and death sentences that were never carried out, though Sampson was subjected to a mock execution. He was also further tortured, and named a fourth man. The tortures (including the notorious falanga, beating on the soles of the feet) are meticulously described and discreetly shown, in brief and shadowy but still disturbing dramatized scenes; so, almost as disturbingly, is Sampson's "dirty protest." He also tells about being raped.

About the only flaw in the film is how little it tells us about Sampson's three fellow victims. Two of them are interviewed, but we don't learn much about what they endured or how they reacted. (Raf Schyvens, the Dutchman, does speak of his remorse for having involved his colleagues, but nobody blames him for it. Nor should they.) We get the impression that Sampson was singled out for special treatment, but whether his bloody-mindedness was the cause of this or its result is never made clear.

Sampson says that his parents "had a very bad marriage ... They should never have been allowed to get married." But they did and he, as he says with typical mordancy, was the result. Nevertheless his father James, who lives in Vancouver and twice visited his son in his Saudi jail, emerges as the prime mover in his

son's eventual release (and possibly therefore in that of Sampson's companions). He got, as is well known, little help from his government, who were more concerned with diplomatic niceties.

Sampson senior speaks of "the scum that work for the Canadian Foreign Affairs Department -- the lowest scum on Earth. At least," he adds, fearful of encountering even worse, "I hope they are." His feelings are understandable, though the film itself suggests that Sampson's trial lawyer, for whom "oily" seems the appropriate word and who genially admits that Samspson may have suffered "some mental torture," might be a strong contender.

It's difficult, certainly, to stomach the evasiveness of Prime Minister Chretien (caught saying "it's not clear" Sampson was tortured) or the self-justifications of those lower down, claiming that firm protests would only have made matters worse. They are firmly debunked by Francine Dube, whose articles in the National Post did much to make the Sampson case a cause celebre, while in Britain, which hardly seems to have done a better job of defending its nationals, a reporter for the Guardian (some issues can make strange journalistic bedfellows) draws the moral that, if your government finds out about your misadventures abroad, then you are, as he caustically puts it, expendable.

A spokesman for Human Rights Watch makes the fairest summary: There may be a case for tactful diplomacy (meaning, not antagonizing countries with a lot of oil) but in this case it was inappropriate and probably self-defeating. By a crowning irony that Sampson himself might appreciate, he and his friends were finally released in exchange for prisoners from Guantanamo Bay. The film refrains from drawing the obvious moral that torture is indefensible, even when our side is doing it, but it's implicit. There are few surprises in this film, except in matters of detail including, as I began by saying, details of character. You switch on expecting to be angered and sickened, and you are. - Confessions of an Innocent Man airs tomorrow at 7 p.m. on CTV.
