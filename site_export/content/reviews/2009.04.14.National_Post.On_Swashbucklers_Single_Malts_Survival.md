David Mamet is characteristically sparing with the information he provides in Glengarry Glen Ross, but he takes care to give us the essentials. This selective economy is what makes the play so funny, so scary and so convincing. Look at the title. Once you've got past the natural assumption that it refers to a duel to the death between two rival brands of malt whisky, you realize that Glen Ross (which I think is only mentioned once) is the name of a stretch of dubious Florida properties that the play's Chicago real estate salesmen used to peddle in their glory days, while Glengarry (which gets three or four name-checks) denotes the even more dubious lands in the same state that they're reduced to offloading now. "Now," strictly speaking, would mean 1983, which is when the play was first staged; "then" could mean 1965, a year that one character invokes with a mixture of nostalgia and desperation. But it's easy to adjust the dates in our heads to the present, especially given that the play revolves around the stark certainty of layoffs. This has always been a cutthroat business, if only because its hucksters never had anything substantial to huck. But what was once a matter of competitive honour has now become one of survival.

There are four men, working for the same firm, engaged in a sales contest whose outcome will leave one of them with a Cadillac, one with a set of steak knives and the other two on the street. They've mostly been in the game for years. Effectively holding their fates in his hands is a young, and much resented, office manager, who in turn answers to two unseen proprietors. It's remarkable how complete a picture we get, mainly through dropped hints, of the hierarchy, how it was built up and how it operates, and of the pattern of the salesmen's lives: days drumming up trade on the phone, morose lunches, evenings spent charming and fooling gullible customers, nights in lonely hotels. There's a camaraderie bred of custom and shared apprehension, but since these fears are competitive by definition, any of these guys can turn on a dime. There is a kind of professional pride at work, and sometimes it gets spread around, but nobody ever misses a chance to take advantage. This could be the best workplace play ever written.

It has a fittingly deceptive structure. The first act, set in a Chinese restaurant where you can practically smell the MSG, is composed of duologues: salesman and boss, salesman and salesman, salesman and potential client. We only reach full ensemble when we get to the office, in turmoil because someone has broken in and stolen the "leads" -- the prized list of lucrative contacts. So there are two dramas going on, the robbery and the sales, the first providing a steady throbbing ground-bass to the ebb and flow of the second, until both explode in rapid succvession.

Mamet's celebrated profanity-driven dialogue, full of hesitations and false starts, can be taken two ways: to denote people at the top of their game or at the end of their tether. David Storch's Soulpepper production mostly opts for the latter; it isn't the funniest Glengarry I've seen but it could be the saddest. Its defining portrait is Eric Peterson's Shelley "the Machine" Levene: the one-time sales champ now reduced to begging -- and bribing -- for scraps. It's easy to feel sorry for him, but Peterson doesn't play for sympathy as Jack Lemmon did in the film; there's always a mean crack waiting to burst out of him, and even his moments of triumphant swagger have their underside. As Richard Roma, the up-and-comer who stands where Levene once did, Albert Schultz, hair spectacularly slicked, plays like a quiet cobra, hypnotising his prey with a philosophical aria about self-gratification that works its magic by sound as much as sense. Less satisfyingly, he sounds the same subdued note among his colleagues, and some of the flavour goes, both from his aggression and his moments of apparent sympathy. Kevin Bundy plays his civilian victim well, without getting the delicious tension between his awe of Roma and his fear of his own wife. (The guy practically apologizes for not letting himself get swindled.) Schultz's anger though, when his prize fruit goes pear-shaped, is electrifying.

Twin performances that make the Mamet pauses sing come from Peter Donaldson as the most abrasive member of the sales force, nursing grudges from way back, and William Webster, jowly and pouchy, as the most pathetic, facing the same defeat as Levene but without the same fight in him. Jordan Pettle gets the manager absolutely right, savouring his power and, beneath his collegiate veneer, as ruthless and venal a barracuda as the boys out in the field. Ken MacDonald's sets do well by the restaurant, less well by the office; the two are symbolically linked when the wall menu of one turns into the contest-scoreboard of the other. I'm not convinced that a play at once so full and so spare needs this kind of illustration, but I guess it serves for a play in which dog voraciously eats dog after first sharing a table with him. - Glengarry Glen Ross runs until May 9.
