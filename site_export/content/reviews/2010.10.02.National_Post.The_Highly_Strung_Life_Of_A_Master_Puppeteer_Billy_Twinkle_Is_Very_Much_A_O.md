Ronnie Burkett is one of our best actors. He's also a considerable playwright. He can sing too. And, oh yes, he also does puppets.

His new show Billy Twinkle (new to Toronto, that is; he's been touring it for the last two years) shows him in his finest form as a performer, but a little below that as a writer. It's full of brilliant scenes, but the thread that connects them frays a bit. It's the first Burkett show I've seen in which he himself, rather than one of his marionettes, plays the central role. There's probably some autobiography attached, though the story seems to owe more to its creator's worst nightmares than to his actual achievements. Billy Twinkle is a puppeteer, who when we meet him is clinging to a fairly debased rung in the professional hierarchy. He's working cruise ships, and Burkett starts the proceedings with a wonderfully smarmy rendition of his act. Technically, it's a good act -- it features a stripper-on-strings whose peeling is orchestrated with the maestro's usual breathtaking finesse -- but Billy isn't happy. He despises his audience, and treats us to an interior monologue about them. We then discover, as does he, that it wasn't as interior as he thought; he was overheard, and he's fired. He contemplates a conveniently located watery grave, quoting liberally from "to be or not to be" as he does so, but is dissuaded by the apparition of Sid Diamond, a former mentor whose stock-in-trade was puppet Shakespeare, and who finds Billy unworthy of his legacy. Sid, unusually for a Burkett character, is a glove puppet. He whisks Billy away to a miniature stage-upon-a-stage, on which unfolds the story of Billy's life. This means that we get to watch marionettes being operated by other marionettes: a split-level of virtuosity remarkable even for Burkett.

There are a few things wrong with this scenario. One is that there's too much, and too repetitive, cross-talk between Billy and the ghostly Sid. (The so-to-speak live, flashback Sid is another matter entirely.) Another is the matter of Billy's decline. The play's subtitle -- every play these days seems to have one--is Requiem for a Golden Boy. The implication is that Billy was a prodigy, who sold his soul to the cruise ships. He's signed up by a shady-looking-and-sounding manager, but the nature of the deal, and its implications, are skated over. It isn't clear what else Sid, or Burkett, or anybody else, expected him to do. Not everybody can be Ronnie Burkett, even if they're being played by Ronnie Burkett. Finally, though less importantly, the shipboard setting lovingly designed by Burkett himself -- he does that, too -- isn't used much as location or, up until the very end, as metaphor.

The biographical voyage itself, though, is treasure-stuffed. The scenes depicting Billy's Saskatchewan childhood are joys. He's shy but obsessed, with a busy mother and an indulgent father who confesses to ineptitude at building his son a toy theatre ( "I'm not like other dads ... I'm a pharmacist") but still enables his attendance at a puppet convention in Detroit. Here, and on subsequent visits, Billy meets several varieties of his fate. He introduces himself to Syd, who's impressed despite himself by the boy's cheek and determination. He encounters Benjy, a contemporary with avant-garde ambitions who comes to regard Billy as a marionettists' Messiah. He is seduced by an older man. The gay theme is almost as important as the puppetry one, starting with Billy's mom's friendship with a sales-rep colleague whom his dad describes as "bohemian." Back in Moose Jaw, Billy takes singing lessons from a Scots lady of mature years, who is at first shocked by his desire to do a red-hot mama number, but who throws herself enthusiastically into it on learning that the puppet, not the puppeteer, will be wearing the frock. The neat pastiche songwriting, here and elsewhere, is by John Alcorn.

As Billy Twinkle's Stars in Miniature expand in scope, so the shadows lengthen. Gawky, eager young figures give way to shuffling older ones, with the dolls representing Billy himself becoming progressively stiffer and more corpulent. Characters not currently in use sway visibly as they hang on their strings at the side: a fine, ghostly effect in itself. An evangelist puppeteer, with Jesus as her puppet, does an act that Billy finds at once ludicrous and disturbing, though his reaction might carry more weight if the show had touched any religious themes previously. (Billy does say that his family were Mennonites.) As far as secular religion goes, Sid's last hurrah is a beautiful Romeo and Juliet scene; Billy's lowest ebb, at least as Sid sees it, is a debased take on The Taming of the Shrew. Burkett adds Shakespearean verse-speaking to his accomplishments, culminating, as Billy returns to his nautical roots, in a reading of Prospero's epilogue totally different from, but bearing comparison with, that of Christopher Plummer -- echoes of whose tones I thought I caught in the portrayal of Sid. Burkett isn't a golden boy, but one of our theatre's golden men. His show may conceivably be some kind of an exorcism, but he's in no need of a requiem.
