Fresh from hunching over the copy of the season premiere of House sent to my home by a cooperative network or two (yes, they make House calls), I have to report that nothing much has changed in the hospitals of New Jersey. Not, at least, in the one that we're concerned with. Dr. Gregory House, he without whom no medical procedure can proceed, is told by his colleague Wilson, who wants to bail on him, you spread misery because you cant feel anything else." Havent people been telling him that ever since this show started?

Someone close to me recently remarked that the principal achievement of House has been to make a whole generation believe that Hugh Laurie is American. For those of us who remember Laurie as Bertie Wooster and other characters almost as ineffably English, that's quite an achievement. It would, after all, have been easy to make House a British genius imported by the North American medical system (I know one or two of those), but they chose the hard way, and it's paid off. Lauries House is believably American. What he isnt is believably human.

This isn't the actor's fault; he deals expertly with what hes given. I was at a social gathering last week at which there were quite a few doctors present. I took the opportunity to ask them what they thought of House, and of medical soaps generally. They seemed to find ER the most realistic in its depiction of hospital routine. None of them were very keen on House. The most important objection I heard was that, in an age of increasing medical specialization, Houses all-round expertise just isn't credible.

House's neuroticism and misanthropy are, of course, supposed to make this show more "serious and adult" than other hospital dramas. In fact, they just make it more pretentious, and therefore more offensive. Weve had shows about gangsters, cops, admen and vampires that have transcended their genres, to become authentically dramatic or authentically comic, sometimes both. But doctors and surgeons, who you would have thought worthy of at least equivalent treatment, are still stuck in the old routine.

Maybe its because their jobs, or public perception of their jobs, make it look too easy. Here are people facing life-and-death situations on a regular basis, so that becomes the formula: a new crisis every week, with the professionals trying to balance these responsibilities with their personal problems, sometimes their personal demons. With House, this has become especially formulaic, because he's all demons. Every show has him being foul to people for half the running time while the rest is taken up by a patient, horribly sick from this week's plot-device gimmick. Tonight, its feminism.

We start with a high-powered lady activist threatening to sue the pants off an all-male boardroom for their dismal record of harassment. Suddenly, her assistant collapses, clutching herself and her clothing while screaming, "I cant get 'em off me. Her boss inquires of the assembled hopeless males, You gonna call an ambulance, or you just gonna sit there and stare?" Somebody, apparently, does dial the right number because next thing we know the patient's in hospital, where shes discovered to be pregnant, and not hysterically. But that isnt half the story, hers or House's.

House, the misogynist's misogynist, takes the opportunity to let off a few zingers about yesterdays sluts being todays empowered women. But he can't give the patient too much face time, because he's trying to stop Wilson from leaving him; its that personal. Wilson is grieving the death of his beloved, for which he may or may not hold House partly responsible. House even refuses to tend to the patient, though of course everyone knows he has the magic bullet, until Wilson changes his mind, which his colleagues call unprofessional and I call unbelievable. I dont care how much the show has invested in his being a bastard. Nobody could say this show spares doctors their own share of suffering, what with Wilson's bereavement and House's terrible limp and their female colleague having been diagnosed with Huntingtons chorea. She confides as much to the patient, who confides right back that her boss is one of those ideologues who treats

her own staff as badly as she accuses her opponents of treating theirs, but that shes a force of nature nonetheless. And we're meant to make the connection that this is how House's staff regard him. Have all the principles you want, but bow down to genius. Its very neat.

Now, its true that House's colleagues talk right back to him, but they're still his stooges, just as the patients are. Hes the one who gets all the good lines, from a snappy, "Im not busy, it's just a euphemism for 'get the hell out of here, " to an extended comparison between grief and Newark as two places you need to drive right past. All right, that last one may not be good, but they clearly took a lot of trouble with it; its the show's idea of style. Maybe it's the scrubbed-clean setting that makes hospital dramas so undramatic; everythings antiseptic, right down to the music that they pipe in to persuade us that emotions are happening. (All network shows do it, but these ones are the worst.) Its insulting to the audience, and to the actors -- as if they weren't trusted to act. Instead they rattle off diagnostic jargon as if they understood it, which by now they very well may. But they can't seem like real physicians, because they can't seem like real people. Is there a doctor in the House?
