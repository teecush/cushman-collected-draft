CAPTURE ME

Tarragon Theatre, Toronto

The title of Judith Thompson's new play is mysterious, as is a fair amount else. At a guess, Capture Me is an unspoken invitation issued by Jerry Joy, the play's heroine and a junior kindergarten teacher, to Dodge, the older man to whom she was married for seven abuse-riddled years and who now seems set on reclaiming her. He has come back into her life with the threat, largely unspoken but understood by both of them, that he will at some point kill her.

Jerry is making other, and perhaps more conscious, claims on the possessive instincts of at least two more people. One is Aziz, an Arab refugee whose unseen daughter is one of her pupils. Dodge is not the kind of man who needs a pretext for jealousy, but if he were, the evolving relationship between Jerry and Aziz might provide one, though it remains platonic. Aziz, who is much shocked by Western manners and morals, would countenance nothing else. Jerry, adopted at birth, has also just discovered the identity of her biological mother, a doctor named Delphine, and has been making silent phone calls to her, leaving Delphine to fear that she too is being stalked. When Jerry finally finds the courage to declare herself, Delphine declares herself touched and pleased but unwilling to meet. She is in fact dying of cancer.

The remaining member of the play's group is Minkle (Thompson's characters have the most picturesque names), Jerry's friend and colleague, as outspoken, especially about sex, as Jerry is reserved, and the one person whose help and advice never has to be solicited. She is an unusually cheerful character for a Thompson play, though she has her own lurking demons. The others are fully paid up denizens of an inferno that seems made up, in equal measure, of Toronto now and the good old human condition.

Some of the writing has the power one expects from this writer, and some has a charm one doesn't, notably a scene in which Jerry instructs Aziz on the proper etiquette at a Canadian child's birthday party. About a third of the way through, the parts seem to be shaking into a coherent, and potentially fascinating, pattern. Jerry and Aziz seem to represent the innocence and fragility of human happiness, and Dodge, who explicitly compares himself to Iago, is everything that threatens it. But the moment passes, or rather it's prolonged without effective action to sustain it.

As Delphine, Nancy Palk has a fine first-act speech, brilliantly delivered, exploring her own conflicted reactions after hearing from her daughter. She's a soul in fear, hiding within an eroded shell of professional capability. But the second half finds her telling a story of childbirth and comparing it, at great length, to an exploding volcano. Note, she doesn't say it is a volcano; she says it's like a volcano. It was said long ago that metaphors are dramatic, similes aren't; and this one, a self-conscious slab of Fine Writing, just about eats the play.

The biggest flaw, though, is the failure to develop the relationship between Jerry and Dodge; it just keeps being restated. We do get to see how it started. Dodge was Jerry's professor, and she appears in flashback in his office, protesting that his teaching has offended her religious susceptibilities. Talk of the devil. She does, and it arouses him, which in turn fascinates her. Randi Helmers, all blond Nordic idealism, is even better at playing the young Jerry than she is at playing the sadder-but-wiser figure of the main action. Tom McCamus does glamorously Satanic very well but his role really consists of just two scenes, endlessly repeated. He is the sexy psychopath, repeatedly cropping up to terrorize Jerry, and he is the acknowledged sinner, lecturing a class of delinquents on the crimes for which they have been condemned and of which he knows himself to be guilty. These tracks run endlessly in parallel; they never converge.

Michael Dean Wint gives a sweet account of Aziz, though the character is irritatingly idealized, and even more irritatingly vague. We're never even told which country he comes from. Jerry isn't told either; indeed she says she never even asked. This is plain unbelievable. (Some of the romantic interludes between the teacher and the immigrant had me yearning irreverently for the comic treatment Zadie Smith gave to the equivalent relationship in White Teeth.) Aziz only develops complexities after he's left the scene, promising "to do what must be done." The more cynical characters suggest he's gone home to wreak vengeance on the men who killed his family. So maybe Jerry has fallen for two violent men in succession. Or maybe she hasn't; maybe they're opposites. There's a tableau in which Dodge and Aziz both appear silent at the back of the stage that could mean anything.

As Minkle, a Thelma in search of a Louise, Chick Reid grabs every opportunity she's given. But, good though the actors are, they all hit false notes on occasion, as when they break up their overwrought lines with the kind of melodramatic pauses that have nothing to do with real-life patterns of speech and little, I fancy, with real- life patterns of thought. What they do have to do with are memories of other actors' acting. A less indulgent director might have ironed this out, but here the author is the director. She also, in her twin capacities, presents at least three scenes that may or may not be dreams. This may be a teasing ambiguity; it may, as I think, be simply confusing.

The idea, perhaps, is to show that Jerry, in ironic contradiction of the title, has at least the potential to be mistress of her own fate. So probably she has, but in the play she remains a victim, not of other characters but of her author. There seems no reason other than dramatic convenience for the authorities to be unable to protect her, especially as she shows no inclination either to return to Dodge or to apologize for him. There seems no reason for her mother to be dying, or indeed to be making long speeches about volcanoes, except that both circumstances fill out what comes to seem like a willed vision of darkness. The play can sometimes evoke terror; it doesn't explore it.
