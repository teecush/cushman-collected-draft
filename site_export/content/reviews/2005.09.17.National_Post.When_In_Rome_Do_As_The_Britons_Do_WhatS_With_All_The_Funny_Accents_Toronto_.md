They still talk the same way in Capitol City, a.k.a. Forum Town, only more so. If the HBO series Rome is to be believed, every soldier and civilian in the time of Julius Caesar spoke with a British accent. The place used to be a bit more multicultural than that; indeed, in the 1950s, a peak period for movies set in the ancient world, Britons were rather a depressed species.

Jimmy Porter had something to say about that. He, you may remember, was the hero of John Osbornes Look Back in Anger, the breakthrough play for the new wave of English drama in 1956. Jimmy, like his author, won an undeserved reputation as a spokesman of political protest. In fact, his discontents were rather vague, and very self-centred, and it's a safe bet that he -- again like his author -- would have turned very reactionary in succeeding decades. Jimmy could, however, be an entertaining cultural critic, and the play's success was largely due to the appreciative laughter he drew from a like-minded audience who werent used to hearing their own feelings about, for instance, the stodginess of the posh Sunday papers given voice on the stage.

He struck a similar nerve when he turned his attention to the Hollywood epics whose lascivious ladies were invariably attended by husbands so weak and meek "theyd probably give some poor English actor the part. They were bound, Jimmy correctly theorized, to be cuckolded by the handsome gladiators, all of whom were American and most of whom were Victor Mature.

It was all a matter of class, in several senses of the word. Even in a progressive picture such as Stanley Kubrick's Spartacus, Kirk Douglas and Tony Curtis may have been the heroes but they were still slaves. For patrician villainy, Kubrick cast Laurence Olivier, for beauty Jean Simmons, and for weight, both physical and intellectual - - weight allied to wit -- Charles Laughton and Peter Ustinov. Ustinov actually played a slave dealer, probably not a profession for a gentleman but still many steps up the ladder from the slaves themselves.

Besides, he was a comic character, and in North America British actors have traditionally been reckoned good casting for clowns as well as kings. They are supposed to be good with words, funny words as well as noble ones. Indeed, in Canada the comic tradition, or its derivatives, may well have lasted longer. As far as I can determine, when Tyrone Guthrie started the Stratford Festival he insisted on his Canadian actors using their own accents, but there may have been some slippage over the years; at least, I suppose that the constant complaints about Stratford actors doing imitation English must have some basis in something. I can't say Ive ever heard it myself, or at least not until the current production of Edward II. In this the royals and barons are all impeccably, and very effectively, Canadian, but the jailers and murderers sound as if theyve come from the slums of London, and I don't mean London, Ontario. As it happens I think that making them lower class at all is a misreading of the text, but leaving that aside: What are 20th-century Cockney accents (they aren't 21st) doing in a play written 400 years ago and set 200 years before that?

In a show like Rome, of course, the problem shouldnt arise; the characters would have been speaking in Latin anyway, so any variety of English speech is an artifice, an American accent every bit as good as a British one. But this opinion does not seem to have been shared by the moguls in the United States, who used to star a Yank or two for box office and fill up with Brits for tone. The pattern persists, though nowadays your Gwyneth Paltrows and Matt Damons try their best to fit in vocally, and do so far more persuasively than Marlon Brando, say, peacocking his way through Mutiny on the Bounty.

When in Rome you do as the Britons do; and, just to be on the safe side, you had better be British. Perhaps this has to do with its being television; the mostly American directors have done time on Deadwood, The Sopranos, Six Feet Under and Sex and the City, and the general ambience seems like a stew of all of them, but the casting is strictly Masterpiece Theatre. Well, a bit of Shakespearean training never did any harm, especially when its allied to a talent for cheeky comedy, and most of the actors in the series are thoroughly qualified in both.

It's a shame that the writing doesn't give them more of a chance to show it. The scripts are trying desperately to be high-flown political drama and low-down steamy soap opera at the same time. The Romans, we are implicitly assured, may have had funny names and worn funny clothes (the assembled Senate looks, as ever, like the steering committee of a Turkish bath), but really they were just like us. We are briskly told that a wounded soldier is under the medics," but the medics themselves, mindful that this was all happening a long time ago, use words like "mayhap. Theres nothing wrong, of course, with mixing periods and styles; as with any other kind of writing, anachronism depends on having an ear. My memory tells me that I, Claudius, the progenitor of all these series, had it; I recall John Hurts Caligula bringing his horse into the Senate and murmuring his life has expanded so much since I made him a Consul." That's characterization, exposition and a good joke, all in one; that's wit.

Rome offers us only an invitation to "more lunch -- dormouse, perhaps? I suppose thats informative, but it does seem dragged in. Its not as if American TV writers can't be witty, even elegant, when dealing with their own time and place. Compare the unfailing mixture of brutality and formality in The Sopranos, or the new and highly promising Prison Break, in which one convict (the terrifying Peter Stormare) says to another, You're a mercurial man" -- now theres a good Latinate adjective -- and then, as if justifiably pleased with himself, repeats it.

The actors in Rome may have been cast for style, but they cant supply what's lacking in the script. Each is thrown back on what he or she knows best. The guys in the fictitious human-interest roles do all right, but really, who needs them? There are two stand-outs, however: Kenneth Cranham's Pompey, with a face as gnarled as a road map and a mind to match, is at the centre of the shows political scenes, which are its best. And the superb Lindsay Duncan, as an especially resilient matron, has just the mixture of fire and ice that the series seems to be aiming for, but has so far been given little opportunity to display it. She plays Attia, mother of the future Emperor Augustus and an all-round decadent; she has a high old time whipping slaves, bedding most available males and bullying her sullenly defiant daughter. She has to work terribly hard at it, as the population acknowledges; outside her villa, someone has scrawled "Attia fellat omnes. Its strange that the graffiti artists should be writing Latin while everyone else is talking in English, but maybe the producers felt they couldn't risk a translation. Mostly they reckon, correctly, that there's a special appeal to sex and violence when they come with cultural trappings. And I have heard stranger dialogue. I still treasure the memory of Robert Taylor in Quo Vadis who, while Nero fiddled and Rome burned, reassured a small boy, Don't worry, son, we'll get you out of this."
