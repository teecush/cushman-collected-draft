THEATRE REVIEW" SINNERS

Tuesday, February 26, 1991

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Toronto ONT -- WRITTEN BY NORM FOSTER Directed by Michael Ayoub Starring David Nairn, Araby Lockhart and Ted Follows At the Bayview Playhouse in Toronto In this part farce, part thriller, it's curtains for the clergyman Energetic Sinners hard to forgive BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Toronto THE first sinner on stage is Monica. She is spread out on a bed, languorous in her lingerie, awaiting the arrival of a man not her husband while doing mild calisthenics. Obviously a loose woman.

Just how loose I cannot tell you without delving into details of plot. This is generally regarded as unsporting when a play is either a farce or a thriller. Sinners is trying to be both, so giving away its secrets would be murder. But there I go.

It is fair, though, to examine the drama's moral outlook, which is that a woman with a hearty sexual appetite is capable of anything and deserves all she gets, as long as it's not what she wants.

Monica is married to a clergyman who returns unexpectedly (the sainted Kenneth Tynan once remarked that nobody in farce ever returns expectedly, but a good farceur is supposed to provide some rationale for the apparently irrational, a point by which playwright Norm Foster seems sublimely unbothered) and finds her with her lover. There is an embarrassing apology for a blackout; some actors get up and lie down visibly and risibly, and when it seems decent to look at the stage again, we find that there is a dead reverend on it.

Monica is prepared to rise above this. Her boyfriend, a furniture dealer named Peter, is not. We spend the rest of the evening watching him squirm. And when he looks out front, as he does with amazing frequency in Michael Ayoub's production, he can watch us doing the same.

I hate to admit it, but Sinners is not absolutely terrible. The author may care little for rhythm and nothing at all for plausibility, but he does provide some narrative energy and a certain amount of cheek; if his two-hour piece were boiled down to 30 minutes, it might make a bearable black sitcom, supposing that there were a network that would countenance such a thing.

The smoothest and the blackest twists arrive simultaneously; a police chief, who for reasons of his own wants a quiet life, connives at - no, engineers - the suppression of a criminal investigation. But there is no use pretending that Norm Foster is Canada's answer to Joe Orton. He may blunder into subversion for a giggle, but there is no answering sparkle in his dialogue. "Whenever I kill a minister, I get a little cranky" is as funny as it gets.

The acting gets a lot cranky. Ted Fellows' old cop, leading a life of quiet desperation, is an oasis of professional calm. Araby Lockhart, as his meddlesome wife, probably knows her business equally well, but her role is such a grinning caricature of censorious repression that I began to resent her for going about it so enthusiastically. (She is burdened with the final witless gag: one that perfectly encapsulates the nudge-wink tabloid mentality of the whole thing.) An oddity of both promotion and construction is that these two players, who don't appear until the second half, are billed as "starring," a tribute to their longevity and, I assume, popularity, but below the title.

The only actor above it is David Nairn, who plays Peter: Fair enough, he has to do most of the work. His performance, given the play's divided nature, is loyal to the point of schizophrenia. He acts at two speeds, careering through the farcical parts in high grimace, which he freezes every so often into a mask of horror, as if he had suddenly been informed of the reality of his situation.

It's a valiant and ingenious endeavour, but to bring it off, you maybe need to be Bob Hope at his cowardly comic peak, and preferably with access to Hope's team of writers.

There remain two actresses afflicted by their author's mindless misogyny. Michelle Hart, as the lewd Monica, gives in without a murmur but with plenty of noise. Glynis Davies, cast as a stock spinster despite the technicality of being married, puts up a spirited but unavailing fight.

Set and lighting design are credited to Rod Hillier; the set, looking like no home ever lived in by anyone, has walls that quiver under pressure, and the lighting goes on and off with startling abruptness or, to put it crudely, crudity.

The press kit informed me that this is an early Foster work and that he has moved on to "more sophisticated comedies of manners." They are probably masterpieces, but right now my pulse does not race. The same kit promises us "two hours' respite from wartime worries." The implication is that ordinarily you wouldn't bother with it, but there's a war on that makes the world safe for Sinners. That's the most terrifying explanation I've heard yet.
