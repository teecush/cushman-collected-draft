The Merchant of Venice runs in Withrow Park through Sept. 5. 647- 438-6742.; robertcushman@lycos.com

As is well known, there were officially no Jews in England in Shakespeare's time; they had been expelled in the 14th century and were not readmitted until later in the 17th. It is therefore unlikely that Shylock in The Merchant of Venice could have been drawn from life, at least as far as his Jewishness is concerned. A new book, of which more later, argues that he is in fact based on a race of usurers closer to home, the Puritan moneylenders of Elizabethan London. Thats quite persuasive; Shakespeare, like anybody working in the theatre, would have had every reason to attack the Protestant extremists who wanted to close him down, and Shylocks rabid dislike of music (hardly a Jewish characteristic) certainly marks him down as one of the killjoys.

Whatever. The play still calls him a Jew, and that is something with which we are stuck. Even the new production by Shakespeare in the Rough doesn't try to change that, though it has given the role to an actor who is not only gentile -- Native Canadian, in fact -- but a woman. I'm not sure if this counts as gender-blind casting; I would expect that to mean giving a woman a crack at playing a man (or vice versa), but here Shylock is turned female.

Its hard to judge the validity of this idea because in this performance it hardly gets to first base. Jani Lauzon has little of the fire, the wit and the depth of both hatred and suffering that make the character interesting.

All the same, if this is gender-blind casting, then the concept seems necessarily to entail tone-deaf casting. Men and women talk and behave differently in one anothers company than they do when left to themselves, and Shakespeare was quite smart enough to know this and to write accordingly. If you're going to perform a sex change on a major character, then you also need to do something far more radical to the text than just altering the pronouns.

(There are a few more ambitious rewrites. I was wondering how they'd cope with "I had it of Leah when I was a bachelor." Its become I had it of Nathan when I was a young woman. Call me reactionary, but I think the old line is sharper.)

It is fun to see Shylock turned into a Jewish mother, but he has after all only one short scene with his daughter, though its elongated here by having them sing a duet. This comes just after the speech about hating music.

Otherwise, Sanjay Talwar's production is lively and intelligent, with some sharp editing and an excellent use of Withrow Park's deep perspective to remind us of characters who are theoretically offstage. This troupe still has the inside track on open-air Shakespeare within the city, even if, compared to luxurious High Park, its a dirt track. Michael Blake does an excellent double as the Prince of Morocco and Gratiano, though I still wonder why no actor ever plays up the latter characters melancholic streak. The shifting moods that Carly Street uncovers in Portia, especially in the casket scene, put her on a different plane from the rest of the show, and her disguise at the trial is amazing.

But that's another thing: This is a play in which the heroine has to dress up as a man in order to be taken seriously as a lawyer. It's a sexist society as well as a racist one (an eclectic design that takes in crinolines and cellphones does nothing to change that), so its impossible to believe that this Shylocks enemies, so free with the anti-Semitism, wouldn't take hostile note of her womanhood as well.

Now for that book. It's Shadowplay: The Hidden Beliefs and Coded Politics of William Shakespeare, by Clare Asquith (HarperCollins, $34.95), and my friend John Fraser has prophesied elsewhere that it will create a stir equal to that occasioned by Stephen Greenblatts justly acclaimed Will in the World. Personally, I doubt it. Asquith takes the now-accepted view that Shakespeares parents were Catholics, forced to hide their religion in a Protestant England that was virtually a police state, and that he inherited their beliefs. She goes beyond it in declaring that the plays' real purpose is to fortify the old believers and preach the cause of tolerance to Elizabeth and James I, all the while evading the censors.

This raises some questions. Did Shakespeare have only one theme over some 40 plays? How did he manage to be both plain enough and subtle enough to get away with it all those years? How, come to that, have audiences ignorant of their real meaning managed to enjoy the plays for four centuries? Asquith has an answer: Shakespeare disguised his real concerns beneath "great universal dramas" that he was apparently able to conjure up at will, even though his mind was really on something else. At one point, she concedes, someone must have objected and he was forced to abandon his main preoccupation while writing, among other things, the two parts of Henry IV, featuring Falstaff. This would mean that he was able to do some of his very best work, including the creation of his greatest comic character, with one hand tied behind him.

Asquith, a Catholic herself, is fighting her own corner. She comes remarkably close to implying that England's conquest by Philip of Spain would have been a good thing, though even Elizabethan Catholics, mindful of how the Spanish had treated the Dutch, might have had second thoughts about that one. She also, at the last minute, suggests that most of Shakespeares best fellow-playwrights were playing the same game. Years ago I reviewed a production of John Fords 'Tis Pity She's a Whore, a tragedy whose characters include a Friar (mostly good) and a Cardinal (definitely bad), and I remarked then that there was a book to be written about the shifting attitudes of Renaissance dramatists toward the Catholic Church "treated both as venal superstition and moral authority, sometimes within the same play." This is not the book I had in mind. England had been Catholic for centuries; the Reformation was recent. So of course there were mixed feelings.

Asquith does, as noted above, have some pertinent comments on individual plays. She persuades me, more or less, that Jaques in As You Like It is a portrait of Ben Jonson, though I was shaken by her failure to notice that a brief scene in the same play to which she attaches great weight is in fact one of those perennial Elizabethan jokes about cuckolds horns. Her literary scholarship is altogether pretty shaky. She confidently asserts that the apocryphal Edward III is the only play Shakespeare failed to revise for publication, though half the plays werent published at all till after his death. She gets characters' names in The Tempest wrong, and can't decide whether the king Macbeth murdered was Duncan or Donald. John Fletcher, one Jacobean playwright she doesnt like (he was a Protestant), is accorded sole authorship of Henry VIII and The Two Noble Kinsmen, two plays that she also doesnt like; everyone else thinks they're collaborations. The Shakespearean scenes in Kinsmen are so like him at his latest and knottiest that I can't imagine the facile Fletcher having written them. As for Henry VIII, Asquith describes its opening scenes as masterly pastiche ... exactly in Shakespeares style. Heads, you win. Her magnanimity in allowing that in this play "Wolsey, Norfolk and Buckingham are all given noble farewell speeches" is flawed by the fact that Norfolk doesnt have a farewell speech -- because he isn't going anywhere. Nor do I see why the very moving death scene given to the impeccably Catholic Catherine of Aragon somehow doesn't count.

The only honest response to Asquiths claims is a simple one: We dont know. She, however, keeps advancing her theories as though they were proven facts, so the book comes to read like a gushy historical novel: Shakespeare v. the bad guys.

The contrast with Greenblatt's speculative biography is extreme. He gives us a Shakespeare at war with himself, and his pages are studded with perhapses and supposings and let-us-imagines. He never tries to pass conjectures off as facts, which of course makes them more convincing. He, too, lays great stress on Shakespeare's Catholic heritage, but is convinced that he settled for scepticism instead; he wasnt anybodys martyr. Greenblatt is especially good on the possible story behind the sonnets and on the unprecedented depth of characterization that started with Hamlet. On The Merchant he remarks that though Shakespeare may not have known about Jews, he certainly knew about Judaism; he heard the Old Testament every week in church. Asquith writes as if the question of anti-Semitism had never been raised. Greenblatt, too, of course, is fighting his own corner (which, so you'll be warned, happens to be much the same as mine), but he does it with more grace. To echo the truly Shakespearean pun of his title, his is the best Will in the World.
