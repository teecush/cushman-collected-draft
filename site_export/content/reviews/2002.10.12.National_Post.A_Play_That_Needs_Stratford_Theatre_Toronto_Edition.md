THE SWANNE: GEORGE III -- THE DEATH OF CUPID

Studio Theatre, Stratford

- - -

STRATFORD - The Swanne: George III -- The Death of Cupid, written by Peter Hinton, might be described, inadequately, as a biographical- historical-fantastical piece about the young Victoria, pre-Queen.

The play is huge: It has a cast of 21, lasts for 3 1/2 hours and at that is only the first part of a trilogy. Stratford may or may not need this play; by the end, I was persuaded that it does, though I certainly had my doubts on the way through.The play, though, with its cast playing multiple Shakespearean roles, certainly needs Stratford.

Victoria's older self narrates the play, or at least appears from time to time sounding oracular. We actually begin before her birth, at the deathbed of her grandfather, the supposedly mad George III, surrounded by squabbling relatives. It seems to be modelled on a similar scene near the beginning of Richard III; and the play turns out to be riddled with Shakespearean echoes. Some of these are obvious to the point of being crass: "To flee or not to flee, that is the question," says a princess about to leave the country. Some are outright arcane; a nobleman's doxy called Jaquenetta gives birth in the streets. Jaquenetta is the name of the wench left pregnant at the end of Love's Labour's Lost.

What's more, most of the play is written in verse. Maybe all of it is. Some passages sound iambic; a few indubitably rhyme. I suspect that Hinton is not the author destined to rescue English verse drama from the irrelevance in which it has languished, almost without interruption, for some 350 years, but he can certainly muster the odd vivid image. A boy in an orphanage, where sodomy and flagellation are prevailing practices, bares his bum to show what he calls "welts as high as hot-cakes."

Much of the time, though, the style is estrangingly archaic. I'm sure Hinton doesn't need me to tell him that phrases like "fie, fie" or "hie thee to somewhere-or-other" or even "I know not how to turn" were hardly colloquial speech circa 1820; he is obviously trying for Elizabethan sonority. It's the opposite of Elizabethan, though; those playwrights' genius was to make an artificial form sound like natural speech, which is why their plays have survived. Hinton's dense, artificial style is quite hard to follow, and his storytelling doesn't help. There is a lengthy section in the middle of the first act that, while you're watching it, is virtually incomprehensible; it's a flashback that incorporates its own flash- forward. In the second act its relevance to the rest of the action is, piece by piece, revealed; and there's a definite feeling of release. Whether the later satisfaction outweighs the earlier frustration must be a matter of personal taste, but my advice to audiences is, hang in there.

I had better try to explain the title, as far as I can. Young Victoria, a snippy and studious teenager oppressed by mother and governess, says she will call the play (and she may mean this play, or a play within it, or both) The Swanne, because that's what ugly ducklings grow into. She may be referring to herself; she may not.

But the play is full of swans. The unfortunate Jaquenetta is described in the cast list as "a Swanne-marked woman"; and the toff who marked her is billed as "Minister of the Royal Order of the Swanne." A dish of roast swan is produced at one point, and a poor woman refuses to eat it. This seems to be an ultimate faux pas ("You mean to say you don't like swan?") but considering what the gentry get up to, she's probably justified.

As for The Death of Cupid; the young god of love appears to Victoria at one point, but it's one of the play's obscurer interludes. The general message, though, appears to be that pre- Victorian society is in the business of destroying love, and that only a few people, mostly in the lower orders, are interested in preserving it. It's the rich what gets the pleasure, it's the poor what gets the blame. You must have heard that before; it's the same the whole world over.

So: An extravagantly jerry-built piece in a largely sterile style, set nearly 200 years ago in a faraway country of which we know little. Why should we care? Well, an audience cares about a play if the author does, or at least if he cares enough. Hinton obviously cares inordinately; the sheer scale of his enterprise is proof of that, besides being an attraction in itself.

He has obviously fallen in love with the period,or with his own free interpretation of it. He often forgets that we know less about it than he does (casual references to the Peterloo Massacre and the Cato Street Conspiracy will leave most Canadian spectators forgivably baffled) and he has given his fictitious characters mirthless quasi-Dickensian names like Bobber Squich (oppressed) and St. John Voranguish (oppressor). In the end, though, you happily join him in the labyrinth.

The cast, several of whom either extend or excel themselves, are there before you. The author is also the director; his staging is excellent and he seems to have put the actors, with one lamentable exception, at perfect ease with the text.

The most memorable character, logically in so consciously theatrical a piece, is an actress: an old, blind, bald tragedy- queen, known as The Scarecrow, who arrives late and then virtually takes the show over; Diane D'Aquila, having already played a commanding hairless monarch in Elizabeth Rex, is even better here for being allowed absurdity as well as authority.

The next-best scene is a dagger-sharp confrontation between a sedan-bound King George and his eternal Prince Regent son; Prinny is Bernard Hopkins, who has rarely been better, and his old man is Ian White, who has never been better. Lucy Peacock transforms herself unrecognizably as the cockney wet-nurse who is the play's moral touchstone; Domini Blythe's quizzical blend of economy and integrity shines through three separate roles and three separate accents.

The young Victoria is Sarah McVie, who is sedately inquisitive; the older one is Lally Cadeau, whom I hope will let up on the booming in future episodes.

Margot Dionne, a returned Stratford luminary, also pushes mannerism -- in her case, a twittering primness -- to excess in her major role, but contributes a hauntingly still cameo as Mrs. Fitzherbert, the most noted royal ex-floozie since Nell Gwyn.

There are others, too, whom we will doubtless get to know some of better later on; the story will probably continue to double back on itself. I look forward to tuning in next season.
