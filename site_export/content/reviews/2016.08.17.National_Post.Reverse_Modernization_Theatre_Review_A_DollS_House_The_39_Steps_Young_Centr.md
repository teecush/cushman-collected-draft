With some plays, the more you update them, the more they date.

When Henrik Ibsens A Dolls House first hit the stage in 1879, it did so with the force of a thunderclap. Its presentation of a woman walking out of a stifling and infantilizing marriage was denounced as blasphemous by some, hailed as liberating by others. Productions since then, set in or near the original period, continued to seem topical, not so much in what they show as in what they foreshadow. Audiences have the double satisfaction of weighing how much has changed against how much has not. A modern-dress revival forecloses on that. Which is what happens in Daniel Brooks' Soulpepper production. It tries to force relevance down our throats instead of letting us discover it for ourselves.

Ibsen's Nora Helmer is the wife of a banker, Torvald Helmer, who calls her his squirrel" and his "little fritter bird." The latter is a reference to her spending and shopping habits which he finds somewhere between endearing and exasperating. Their lifestyle accurately reflects that of a middle-class family of the time: a fragment of humanity, but large enough for theatrical and political traction. Today, even within that fragment, they would be an anomaly. The Helmers have two children, a maid and a full-time nanny. Nora has never had a job. Talk about rich peoples problems.

And they do have problems.

Years before, when Torvald was ill, Nora secretly borrowed money for his recuperative holiday, forging a signature to get her hands on the necessary 48,000. (Dollars? Kroner? Frank McGuinness adaptation keeps the Norwegian names but everything else about it sounds North American.) Now a blackmailer appears, threatening to tell if she doesn't persuade her husband to give him a job at the bank.

Since the man has a criminal record, and Torvald prides himself on his respectability, this is a long shot. Eventually, all is revealed, and Torvald, as uptight as he's upright, proposes a cover-up while reading Nora a lecture on how criminally irresponsible she has been and how she can never be allowed to contaminate their children. Nora realizes she herself is being treated like a child, just as she always has been. Despite his entreaties, she leaves, slamming the door: a sound-effect that shattered the audiences of the time and still reverberates today.

Which doesnt mean that all the narrative details hold up in a contemporary setting. To make them fit, desperate measures seem called for, and the two leading performances certainly supply them. Katherine Gauthier is unafraid to go to extremes, as she proved to great comedic effect in Tartuffe a couple of years ago. Watching her Nora and, to a lesser extent, Christopher Morris Torvald, I was torn between admiring their audacity and doubting their veracity.

Gauthier plays the early Nora by sliding between different notes of giggly hysteria, without a hint of the steel that enabled her to carry through what was, after all, a fairly daring scheme. I doubted that she would be able to carry off the transition to self-awareness. She does manage it, but only by doubling down on the hysterics.

Morris matches her; having presented Torvald as a cartoon of boyish smugness, he now goes on a crying jag of his own. He also feels her up a lot and even strips, as if to remind us, contrary to what we may have heard, 19th century people had sex. When we get to their final confrontation, there seems to be nothing real at stake. There's no indication Nora has any qualms about giving up her children even though this production, unlike

some, has brought them on stage.

We merely feel they'll be better off without her, as they seem to have a very competent nanny; Michelle Fisk, who plays her, is very competent too.

Good productions of A Dolls House keep you on edge with the realization these are flawed but caring people with things to gain and to lose. They tell a good story, which Brooks production fails to do. It makes no narrative sense of Dr. Rank, the friend who's dying of an unspecified disease. Diego Matamoros at least makes him effective as the messenger of death; on that level it's a fine performance.

Theres a superlative performance, however, from Damien Atkins as the blackmailer Krogstad, a once-decent man desperate to regain that decency (and beyond caring what it takes); ruthlessness and shame come beautifully, scarily, even movingly, together.

The production, on an ice-cold set by Lorenzo Savoini, has a certain visual style, though with plenty of oddities: some but not all the characters make their entrances in zombie-ish slow motion. Perhaps theyre meant to be things in Nora's dream, one that she only escapes when she makes her final exit through the auditorium. If that's the idea, it hardly adds anything.

Soulpeppers production of The 39 Steps is also overwrought, though in a different way. It has a lengthy pre-history. John Buchans spy-novel appeared in 1915. Alfred Hitchcock famously filmed it in 1935. Fifty years after that, there was a British stage version of the movie, in which four actors played all the roles; that must have been fun, a straight dramatization with the constant quick-changes supplying a comic edge. But then, courtesy of another British writer, Patrick Barlow, came an adaptation of the adaptation in which the edge moved to the centre. This unrelentingly farcical parody, a huge hit in London and New York, is the version we have here.

I think it's dire: a 10-minute spoof stretched over more than two hours. Once again four actors play, by the program's count, more than 150 characters. Or rather, three actors play more than 149 characters while the fourth confines himself to merely one. This is Kawa Ada who, as the stiff-lipped hero Richard Hannay, reaps all the benefits of being the straight man when everyone else is playing the fool. He was the only performer who made me laugh, or at least elicited some appreciative chuckles. He also smokes a pipe very well.

His male cohorts, Andrew Shaver and Anand Rajaram, are billed as Clowns 1 and 2 and, under Ravi Jains undeniably energetic direction, they knock themselves out. Rajaram tends to be all over the place, especially vocally, but Shaver has some real distinction, especially as Mr. Memory, a music-hall artiste whos the unconscious repository of the secrets everybody seeks. Raquel Duffy is painful as a Germanic femme fatale, quickly and mercifully dispatched, but redeems herself as a neglected crofter's wife - I mean a crofter's neglected wife - even managing to smuggle in some human feeling. Ada does the same, when obliged to deliver a political stump-speech on the heels of an endless sequence involving two doddering Scotsmen.

Most of the action takes place in Scotland, where the accents seem based on the idea that incomprehensibility is automatically funny. I have a pet theory: an audience, given the hint of a plot, will do their damnedest to follow it and will resent anything that gets in the way. Judging by the hilarity that surrounded me, Im wrong.

All the same, the bits that work best here are the ones in which the narrative manages to assert itself. Most of the time, the show is making fun of a story that - again - it cant be bothered to tell.

A Doll's House runs until Aug. 27, The 39 Steps until Sept. 3.
