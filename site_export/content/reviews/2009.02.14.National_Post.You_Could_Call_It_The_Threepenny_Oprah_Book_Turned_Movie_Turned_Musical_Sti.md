Look at it one way, and the musical of The Color Purple has four salient scenes. The first, immediately after curtain-rise, shows two small black girls, sisters, playing a clapping game; the date is 1904, the place is Georgia. The second occurs a few years later; Celie, one of the girls, has been virtually sold into marriage with a brutal farmer known as Mister. He really wanted Nettie, the other one, but their father judged her too pretty and clever to be sacrificed so he offered Celie, considered ugly but a hard worker, and threw in a cow as sweetener. Celie, by the way, was raped and twice pregnant by the time she was 14; her pa removed both of the babies at birth and disposed of them no-one-knows where. Now, Nettie arrives at the farm, but Mister attempts to force himself on her and Nettie runs off, promising to write.

She does too, every day for years, but Mister hides the letters from Celie. Eventually -- third vital scene -- another character finds them and tells Celie, thoughtfully withholding the information until the first-act curtain. This has the effect of making everything else that's happened so far seem irrelevant. Nettie has gone to Africa as a missionary, and a rather hurried sequence at the start of Act 2 shows her there. That's all we see of her until the last scene when, naturally, she comes home, bringing with her Celie's two children now, time having advanced to 1949, very fully grown. The music swells, and the show asks us to take as its emotional climax Celie's reunion with one character we barely know and two others we don't know at all. Judging by the musical's success on Broadway and elsewhere, this works for many people. I think it's a cheat, though--compared to some others in the genre--not a disgraceful one.

There is another way of regarding the show, and a more interesting one. This makes it the story of Celie's growth into self-reliance, both practical and emotional. No white characters appear, and few are mentioned. The show says, in tough terms, that oppression, especially of black women, didn't end when slavery did. Celie, for much of her life, might as well be Mister's slave. But after years of letting him exploit her domestic talents, she breaks free and goes into business for herself as a pants-designer. She has two mentors and role models. One is Sofia, the forceful woman who loves and marries Harpo, Mister's son by a former marriage, but leaves him; with the encouragement of his father and even of the as-yet-unliberated Celie, Harpo tries to beat her. (In all honesty Harpo, a mild if chirpy soul, didn't stand a chance.) The other is Shug Avery, a blues singer and old flame of Mister's, who comes back into his life and then leaves it again, taking Celie with her. Along with this goes Celie's spiritual progression. She starts as a conventional churchgoer; the first big number is a prolonged, jumping gospel sequence during which Celie looks, considering her social and physical condition, remarkably cheerful. The tragedies in her life threaten to destroy her faith, but she eventually arrives, prompted by Shug Avery, at a kind of pantheistic acceptance; at least, I think that's what the title song is about. The trouble with all this, though, is that it doesn't need the relationship of the sisters and makes it seem like a sentimental extra layer.

It could all be reconciled in the more spacious form of a novel, and the show is, of course, based on one, by Alice Walker. Steven Spielberg, by all accounts, made it work as a movie. And it makes a better musical than it would a play, music being a great lubricant. But it can't do everything, and Marsha Norman's book and Gary Griffin's direction have their work cut out imposing any kind of through-line. Themes and threads, notably Celie's lesbian relationship with Shug Avery, are forever being picked up and then put down again. More dialogue scenes might help, though the score (by Brenda Russell, Allee Willis and Stephen Bray, all theatrical first-timers) does an impressive job of dispensing information with flavour. ("If you think hard work been doggin' you before," sings the chorus when Mister takes Celie, "get ready for the Big Dog.") As usual, the music and lyrics are best when earthiest and most vernacular. When affirmation strikes, they go "contemporary," which is to say vacuous. You might call these numbers Threepenny Oprah, and indeed she's the principal producer. A second-act jive number alerts us that the 1940s have arrived; otherwise, two world wars and a depression pass by without anyone seeming to notice. And, as often happens in shows covering a lengthy time span, nobody seems to change physically until half an hour before the close at which point everybody ages very fast.

Otherwise, there are no quarrels with the show's look or with its performance. John Lee Beatty's sets are impressively framed by hanging Georgia moss. Donald Byrd's choreography is spirit-lifting and much enhanced by Paul Tazewell's costumes. Kenita Miller is a sweet, strong Celie; she even validates the convention that holding a single note very loud and very long is a sign of deep feeling. Angela Robinson similarly transcends type as a shimmering Shug, who's as warming when confiding in Celie as when stunning in a gold dress in a juke-joint. Stu James's Harpo switches charmingly between diffident and debonair, and Felicia P. Fields is a powerhouse Sofia. (A large lady, she gets the evening's biggest laughs when, pregnant, she arches her body backwards as an assault-weapon. Subtle it isn't.) Rufus Bonds Jr. is Mister, who reforms halfway through; he's good in both phases but two one-dimensional moods don't make one complex character. There's a trio of commenting church biddies who get rather irritating, and who turn up in the Africa number as part of a desperate attempt to yoke all the storylines together. Here, more than anywhere, the show buckles under its self-imposed strain. But it's an honest attempt. And given that "purple" usually denotes over-writing, over-acting or both, maybe the most impressive thing is how seldom it lives up to its title.
