THEATRE REVIEW POWER PLAY or RACE TO OBLIVION

Friday, September 25, 1992

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Toronto ONT -- WRITTEN BY MARTIN HUNTER Directed by Richard Monette Starring Peter Boretski, Albert Millaire and Goldie Semple At the Tarragon Theatre Rating: Intended as an excoriating satire on devious government, Power Play is presented by something calling itself Mulroney Productions. The title of course is a pun, the power both political and nuclear, the play both drama and game Flying blind to Rio BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Toronto TO begin with a detail: a Cabinet Minister glowers at his aide and asks, Whats this cable from our ambassador in Rio supposed to mean? Since the fellow has just handed him the wire, he hardly needs to be told who sent it. The information is included purely for our benefit, and it seems ungrateful to complain. Ungrateful but necessary: as conversation that line is unbelievable and hence unplayable.

Still, exposition is a problem that can baffle the best. It may be fairer to judge Martin Hunter's style by something less nakedly functional. A fey aging gentleman announces himself to a pair of eager youths as "the alcohol in the civilized drink they call modern society." You can tell the kind of decadent wag he is. If that isn't wit enough for you, step outside the play proper for a moment and observe that the piece, which is intended as an excoriating satire on devious government, is presented by something calling itself Mulroney Productions. The title of course is a pun, the power both political and nuclear, the play both drama and game. Lest we forget how deadly that game can be, the author throws in a but-seriously sub-title; though even here - and we are now on an altogether superior level of wordplay - oblivion may signify either annihilation or forgetfulness.

It is 10 years ago, bar an epilogue, and Canada is peddling reactors to Brazil. The minister doing the deal is Jean-Pierre Maclean, a name for all bases. He is consistently needled by a phenomenally belligerent TV reporter who runs a show called Dateline Ottawa; "that" she says, signing off with a sneer, "was the Honourable Jean-Pierre Maclean." We dont see too many interviewers like her on mainstream news, and were lucky. More soul-shaking flak comes from the minister's son, a God-haunted adolescent who switches from doing relief work in Rio de Janeiro to blowing up a statue of Christ just outside it: a form of protest incomprehensible to most of the other characters and at least one member of the audience. Everyone not already there goes flying down to Rio, and the plot manically thickens.

There is a widespread feeling in Canadian theatre that plays should be about Issues: the same issues you can read about in any newspaper or hear about on any newscast. This of course is bloody rubbish, and Hunter has got a bit beyond it; he makes his position on nuclear energy clear at the start (he's against it) and then writes a play. But he seems to think that black farce, his chosen form, permits an uptight seminarian to turn, with no seams showing, into a comic-opera Latin revolutionary, and a victim of police torture to groan one second and grin the next. It may be cranky to complain of inconsistency in characters who were never alive to begin with, but two wrongs dont make a right in art any more than in life. Not that the author is above wringing our withers in deadly earnest. Quite near the end of the play, a character reveals that he has cancer, and I bet that wiped the smile off your face. Having thus staked his claim to human depth, he drops the subject.

There is, as you might expect, carnival in Rio, and Richard Monettes production works in the odd swirl. He also moves locations fast, much helped by the most attractive feature of the evening, Martha Mann's cream panelled set. But he cannot take the curse off the scenes themselves, and at one maladroit moment, when somebody literally ducks out of a conversation, he actually intensifies it. The cast all seem to be playing the idea of being in a hard-hitting play. Peter Boretski has it easiest; as the alcohol in the civilized thingummy he at least gets a chance to be sly. Goldie Semple, as the media person, has it toughest; she bulldozes remorselessly, even when teetering in high heels and a wildly improbable backless dress. Albert Millaire as Jean-Pierre seems unwilling to commit himself to any positive quality whatever; this, given the dance his author leads him, is understandable but it is not satisfying. Power Play continues at the Tarragon until Oct. 10.
