YOUNG FRANKENSTEIN

Princess of Wales Theatre, Toronto

---

There's a light shining over at the Frankenstein place: another one. Like The Rocky Horror Show, Mel Brooks' Young Frankenstein is a manic, campy musical that takes off from the most revered of all sci-fihorror stories, holding the reverence but with a double-order of sex on the side. This time, though, the ambience and especially the music are more glitz than grunge, and the sex is strictly hetero.

A more obvious comparison of course is with Brooks' previous musical The Producers. They're both transformations and extensions of their creator's canonical movies. The Producers was a huge Broadway success; Young Frankenstein has been a mild Broadway failure. The newer show is in fact a fair amount of fun--just as well considering how hard it works at it -- but three things conspire against it. One is the inescapable deja-vuishness of the whole enterprise. Second is that Brooks' whole shtick is to reduce everything to showbiz: a natural fit for The Producers, which is about showbiz, but with no particular relevance here. Third, and closely allied to the second: The Producers had a dramatic pulse. Its protagonists desperately needed to put on their terrible show, and their drives drove the story. Young Frankenstein is half-over before it even has a story.

For those who don't know, the famous Dr. Frankenstein is dead, and his Transylvanian castle, with all its appurtenances, has been inherited by his grandson Frederick FrahnkenSteen, as he insists on being pronounced. Frederick is a celebrated brain-doctor working in New York at the Johns, Miriam and Anthony Hopkins Hospital. The time is the 1930s, a factor that determines the show's musical and choreographic style. Even when the show's action moves back to mythical Europe, its Art Deco heart belongs to Fred Astaire and Busby Berkeley.

As lyricist and composer, Brooks ladles out the same generic pastiche as in The Producers, but to far less purpose. I yield to none in my nostalgia for vintage musical comedy, and I confess to being a sucker for dialogue lines like "Pardon me, boy, is this the Transylvania choo-choo?," but Brooks isn't aiming here at sustained parody or satiric commentary; he's just writing the kind of tunes that other people have already written better. As if to prove the point, this show, like The Producers, brings over one song from its mainly non-musical original.

Instead of Springtime for Hitler, Brooks' own and relevant, we have Puttin' on the Ritz, Irving Berlin at his best, putting to shame everything else in the score.

Frederick at first wants no part of the Frankenstein legacy, but his ghostly granddad, accompanied by other ancestral spectres, prevails on him to change his mind: an idea borrowed from, or at least anticipated by, Gilbert and Sullivan. So he creates his own monster, a hulk with some charm but, owing to a grave error (actually a grave-robbing error) on the part of Frederick's humpbacked henchman Igor, of very little brain. The local yokels, NIMBYists to a person, go after the doctor and his creation with pitchforks. This being a Brooks show, the hero has his pick of available blonds. One, a local wench, closely resembles Ulla from The Producers, though with a lesser class of material; the kind she does have suggests that Brooks might have adapted Berlin still further and given her a number called Puttin' on the Tits. Frederick's other lady, a holdover from his New York days, is an untouchable tease, at least until she meets the monster of her dreams; she gives in fact a new meaning to the phrase "vamp till ready."

Susan Stroman's staging, and especially her choreography, put a high gloss on what is basically the old burlesque mix of leers and laughs. Some of the laughs, in Brooks' and Thomas Meehan's book, are good, and the sheer volume of them, good or bad, batters you into a sort of submission. Roger Bart's Frederick, the one remaining Broadway principal in this touring company, provides a valuable point of rest: His basic style is beamish and manic, but he can turn it on and off like a tap. He also resembles Danny Kaye, especially in a tongue-twisting introductory number about The Brain. His creation is played, most endearingly, by Shuler Hensley. Brad Oscar knocks himself out playing a blind hermit as Al Jolson in whiteface; Cory English, whose accent matches his name, is a cottage of strength as Igor; and I guess that Joanna Glushak is funny as the late Doktor's Dietrichian housekeeper; though I also guess that her catchphrase, "He Vas My Boyfriend," did better spoken in the movie than sung here.

Brooks' lyrics, indeed, are very disappointing, though there's a surprisingly pleasant ballad called Listen to Your Heart. The designers are Broadway royalty; Robin Wagner's sets aren't his best, but William Ivey Long's costumes take a witty trip into the world of lederhosen und Gewurtztraminer. The bodywork gleams. But the machine doesn't have an engine.
