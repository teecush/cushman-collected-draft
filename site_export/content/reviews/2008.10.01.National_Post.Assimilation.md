Season Two of Little Mosque of the Prairie had what its producers are pleased to describe as a cliffhanger ending: Would Rayann accept or reject a marriage proposal from JJ? Carlo Rota, who plays Rayann's dad Yasir, is also, of course, a familiar face on 24, a coincidence that throws into sharp relief Little Mosque's rather undemanding standards when it comes to hanging from cliffs.

Still, suspense is holy, and I've been requested not to defile any of it by giving stuff away. This makes discussion of tonight's Season Three premiere a little delicate, but I'll do my best. The episode has two comic mainsprings. One is the jealous impulse of Amaar, the young Imam, to make his friend and roommate JJ look bad. The other is the inability of Yasir, who will obviously never make a TV previewer, to keep his mouth shut about information that his wife Sarah would like to dispense for herself.

I hope I've been discreet enough.

In any event, both plot-threads could be charitably described as gentle and accurately described as tame. These qualities have their place, and though Little Mosque has never been much on laughs (there are none tonight), it is capable of summoning smiles. But they're built on a weak foundation. There's the name for a start. It's no coincidence that the funniest fictions on television -- Seinfeld, The Simpsons, Frasier, all the way back to The Burns and Allen Show -- have generally had the simplest declarative titles. Little Mosque's title rivals Californication in the cuteness stakes; it settles for nudging allusion rather than wholesale theft. Put your premise in your title, and you're signalling that you have an idea so outrageous that it will do the rest of the work for you. This is rash. Premises are merely promises. If they're to be kept, they have to be worked on. And the premise of Little Mosque has proved, partly for honourable reasons, more self-cancelling than most.

There is the idea, built into that title, that there is something inherently incongruous in the idea of a flourishing Muslim community in a small Canadian prairie town. Balancing that, there is the desire to show that Muslims are just like anyone else, and thus deserving of a sitcom that's just like any other. The latter impulse is the one that's won out. The comedy of culture-clash -- and, more subtly, of clashes within a culture -- hasn't quite vanished but it has faded, leaving generic storylines in which the women just happen to have their heads covered.

On that basic TV comedy level, it's low energy. If it has the Canadian virtue of restraint, it has the Canadian vice of pokeyness. There are two peaks in tonight's episode. One involves Sarah finding an unexpected new recruit for her book club: a nice moment for Sheila McCarthy, who does downbeat really well, unlike some of her colleagues who tend to beat you over the head with how subtle they're being. The other has Yasir intruding on the studio of a redneck radio host; it has a punchline -- actually a punch-visual -- that's predictable but still sort of satisfying. Also it's potentially fruitful to see a Muslim character interacting with someone who does not, in principle, like him. This is rare on a show in which, for example, church and mosque get on like a couple of holy houses on fire. That, to be sure, is an admirable sign of the times. But it isn't very dramatic, and therefore not very comic. Comedy has traditionally ended with reconciliation, but it's supposed to be a hard-won fight. Assimilation, when you think about it, is almost the opposite of funny. - Season Three of Little Mosque on the Prarie begins tonight at 8 p. m. on CBC.
