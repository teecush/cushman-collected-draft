You might expect a miniseries called The Tudors to be the most ambitious treatment of a dynasty since Dynasty. You would be wrong. This Irish-Canadian co-production on an English historical theme confines itself to just one member of the family, the fat one with the wives. Only that's not how he appears in this show, which takes a narrow view even of its one starring monarch. It's the young and agile Henry VIII whom we meet here, and only the first two of his half-dozen spouses figure in the credits.

"It's good to be king," announces the cover on the published version of the show's scripts, a tagline that nicely captures the tone -- nudgingly and unoriginally jovial -- of the texts themslves. Michael Hirst, who conceived and wrote the show, certainly doesn't think he's being unoriginal. "The story of young Henry VIII", he writes. "presented a rich and so-far untapped vein. He engineered the change in England from Catholicism to Protestantism, which was so profound that nobody had ever tackled it dramatically before." Nobody? Shakespeare didn't allude to it in his Henry VIII? Robert Bolt didn't go on and on about it in A Man for All Seasons? And as for Henry himself being an untapped vein -- it's only four years ago that there was a series actually called Henry VIII with Ray Winstone playing the king in ripe Cockney: a startling idea that worked because the actor invested it with such juice and vigour.

The Tudors, at least in its first episode, is the standard fancy- dress brew of sex and duplicity and duplicitous sex. It's easy on the violence, though: Somebody gets assassinated in the very first scene, but it doesn't even rate a viewers' warning. (I expect this to change when the king gets down to the business of snuffing out conspiracies and interrogating the past lovers of future wives.) The Henry we meet is already tired of his first wife, Katherine of Aragon, and especially of her failure to bear him a son. He consoles himself short-term with various ladies-in-waiting and long-term with investigating the possibilities of divorce. He also wants war with France; and he trusts his minister, Cardinal Wolsey, to provide him with "just cause." Wolsey seems to be playing Dick Cheney to Henry's George Bush, though what he is secretly intriguing for is peace. He's also angling to be Pope, and he's capable of throwing a foreign Bishop violently against a wall to make his ambitions clear. Sam Neill plays a very smooth and powerful Wolsey, waking velvet echoes of James Mason at his most sinister.

Jonathan Rhys Meyers's Henry, meanwhile, is nobody's fool, not even the Cardinal's. The late Margaret Webster, a distinguished Anglo-American theatre director who began her career working on local pageants, said that the experience had taught her that anyone - - man, woman or child -- could, with the aid of the famous Holbein makeup and stance, be made to look the living image of Henry VIII. Most Henries have made at least a gesture in that direction. Rhys Meyers, though, has none of it. A pre-credits sequence informs us that, before his elder brother's death, Henry was intended for the church, and he looks it here: Not only is he not fat to the point of emaciation, he looks both sallow and callow. Underneath, he's a calculator: rather like the revisionist view of Henry V -- Shakespeare's, not history's -- to whom he explicitly compares himself. (He's talking about the warrior Henry, but it's the long- sighted politician whom he really resembles.) "What," worries one of his advisors, "if he doesn't know what's in his best interests?" But he proves that he does, taking himself off from council chamber to bed chamber for a vigorous tumble with the nubile (and married) Lady Elizabeth Blount. Yes, it's good to be king. You might not think it possible for a nude scene to look anachronistic, but this one does. Henry also plays a fierce game of tennis: the "real" (i.e. royal) indoor variety, that's more like squash.

The show can't wait to break the news that the Tudors and their entourage were, if not just like us, then just like the people we read about in the tabloids. At some level they obviously were, but it might be more satisfying to make that discovery for ourselves than to be continually prodded into it. There's an art to anachromism, especially verbal; it has to have a style of its own, and Hirst's attempts aren't exactly Shakespeare, or even Shakespeare in Love. They aren't all jokey, either. "As a humanist," says Sir Thomas More, who seems to have been reading too many of his own biographies, "I have an abhorrence of war." He also speaks of "the principle of collective security and universal peace," and says that the treaty with France "encourages the creation of pan-European institutions." A man for all seasons, indeed, including one 400 years in the future. Nobody has yet promised to bring back "peace with honour" but Henry will do that when he gets back from the Field of Cloth of Gold, which is obviously about to be given sumptuous treatment.

- The Tudors debuts tonight at 9 p.m. on CBC.
