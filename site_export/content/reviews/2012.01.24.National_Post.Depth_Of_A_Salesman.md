THEATRE REVIEW

Kim's Convenience, Other People

Young Centre for the Performing Arts, Toronto

If you're going for realism, go for it. Ken MacKenzie's set for Kim's Convenience is a wonder of verisimilitude: a Regent Park variety store brought to the stage in such amazing detail that it seems to have been not so much designed as reassembled. Ins Choi's play, adopted by Soulpepper after a triumphant debut at last year's Toronto Fringe, has been given the best imaginable relaunching pad, and deserves it.

I didn't see the Fringe production, which Choi directed and in which he also acted, along with four colleagues of whom all but one have come along for the remount. There is now a new director, Weyni Mengesha, whose production makes a fine and fitting twin to her earlier staging of 'Da Kink in My Hair; both put a believable slice of multi-ethnic Toronto onstage and keep faith with it.

Kim's Convenience may be the better play; it's certainly the more cohesive. It makes a further pair with Soulpepper's own magically realistic Window on Toronto, a group creation in which Choi was one of the group, while also maintaining the company's Parfumerie tradition of doing heartwarming shows about retail. In a wider theatrical context, it's a pleasant coincidence that this production opened the night after The Golden Dragon, which is set in a fusion restaurant that's one-third Korean. The principal characters of Kim's Convenience, the family members who own and run and mind the store, are firstgeneration immigrants from that country, and their children. In the words, or almost, of I'm Still Here, we career from Korea to Korea.

Mr. Kim himself, patriarch and protagonist, has enforcedly careered from one job to another. In his birth country he was a teacher; and he runs his shop, and his family when they let him, with a fierce educator's pride. He's brought with him an encyclopedic knowledge of his national history and an ingrained hatred of the Japanese, one that leads him to call 911 to report illegal parking if the offending vehicle happens to be a Honda.

He and his wife have a grown son and daughter. The daughter, 30 and still living at home, is a photographer - not the profession her father would have chosen for her - who resents any encroachments on her independence and especially resents her father's suggestion that she should take over the store. Her brother is a graver disappointment, a brilliant student who got into drugs and then into prison; he now works for a car rental company, hating the fact that his clients can drive away while he has to take the TTC.

The neighbourhood is gentrifying, and Kim receives a generous offer to buy him out. He refuses; his customers, he says, depend on him and he couldn't desert them. Plain to us is that, domestically and professionally, he depends on them. The store is his identity.

In some ways the play is a lighter-hearted Death of a Salesman, one in which gulfs between the generations are wide but bridgeable. It also, as people come and go, has some elements of soap opera and some of sitcom; it could be accused of sacrificing truth to reconciliation. But the comedy is funny, and the emotions genuine, and at its best, the play combines the two.

There's a terrific scene in which father and daughter furiously tally up what, both emotionally and financially, they owe one another. A flashback in which Mr. and Mrs. Kim try to settle on a name for their new business that hasn't already been used on some other street corner is tender and poignant and handed me the biggest single laugh I've had in a theatre in ages.

All these elements are incarnate in Paul Sun-Hyung Lee's Kim, a massive creation, cunning and vulnerable. Stoutly built, he's a martial arts expert (who meets some serious competition) and a smart operator who is unshakably proud of his ability to recognize a shoplifter at 40 paces. He can vary the thickness of his accent according to whom he wants to mystify; he can also surrender, almost unconditionally, when his parental heart is touched. He made me laugh and he made me cry; and you can't ask for more than that.

Esther Jun, as his offspring and chief antagonist, is too unmodulated in her abrasiveness, but nicely melts when she finds love with a passing cop. Jean Yoon is so gently persuasive a wife and mother that I wish she had more to do and that more of it were in English. Choi has written himself the least rewarding role as the son, who tends to speak in set pieces, but he plays it very well. We meet four of Kim's customers, all of them black and all of them played by Cle Bennett, who transforms himself so completely that it was only at the third go-round that I realized he was the same actor.

A less hopeful but equally riveting slice of life in another city is provided in Christopher Shinn's Other People, an independent production under the same roof (in the small Tank House).

The principal characters are two aspiring artists and one who's seemingly made it, all sharing a house in New York's East Village around Christmas and New Year - not unlike the characters in Rent, a show one of them explicitly indicts for its sentimentality. I'm not sure the play can quite escape the same charge of putting its characters on view for the delectation of outsiders, but it certainly doesn't go easy on them, and it doesn't objectify them.

All three find a possible partner at some point; only one - a reformed junkie whose replacement addiction is God - is in that partnered state at the end, and you rather wish he wasn't. Under the direction of Aaron Willis, a cast that's mainly new to me (Tatiana Maslany, Ben Lewis, Indrit Kasapi, Mike McPhaden, Brendan McMurtryHowlett, Richard Lee) performs with flawless tonal and emotional accuracy.

This early Shinn play confirms my impression that he's the most interesting American playwright to appear since Stephen Adly Guirgis, himself the best since David Mamet.
