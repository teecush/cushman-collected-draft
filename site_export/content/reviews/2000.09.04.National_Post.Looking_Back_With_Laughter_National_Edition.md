LAUGHTER ON THE 23RD FLOOR

Leah Posluns Theatre, Toronto

TORONTO - Jaws drop and mouths water at the list of comedy writers who, in the early 1950s, stoked the fires of Your Show of Shows. This was the Saturday Night Live of its day, only funny: a network TV show that convulsed select portions of America on a weekly basis. Sid Caesar was the star, and his contracted scribes included Mel Brooks, Carl Reiner, Woody Allen, Larry Gelbart and Michael Stewart, the future librettist of Hello Dolly, no great jokester himself but good at collating those who were.

Also present, the junior member of a youthful team, was Neil Simon, who some 45 years later delved into his reminiscences of the time and turned them into a play. Laughter on the 23rd Floor -- named for the exalted level of the New York office building on which NBC deposited Caesar and his staff -- is therefore another of the autobiographical pieces that have recently taken up much of Simon's time.

It is by far the best of them. The constant stream of wisecracks that make up much of the dialogue seems less forced and grating than in the authors more usual domestic settings; this, its easy to believe, is how these characters would actually talk. (Comedians offstage are rarely funny; their writers have to be.) The attendant sentimentality is also easier to take: I was able to convince myself that it is not a sentimental play, merely an accurate depiction of a group of hard-shelled but soft-centred people.

This may be Simon's best play. It certainly shows him on his most appealing turf; and it seems to me the best excuse that the commercial theatre (or at least the non-musical commercial theatre) has offered for its existence in several years. So it's a shame that this, the plays Canadian premiere, is something less than a full- blooded commercial occasion. Zed Pitkins production is well intentioned but slow; and though his mostly non-Equity cast all seem to have the right ideas, they are not so good at putting them into practice.

The action, of which there is not a great deal, unfolds in the TV series' dying season. The network, sensing the emergence of a new demographic with little taste for the crazy or satiric humour favoured by this collection of mainly Jewish New Yorkers, is cutting both the show's budget and its running time.

The star comic in the play is called Max Prince, but he is recognisably Caesar in his pomp, his generosity, his alarming battery of neuroses and his awe-inspiring fits of fury. He is overwhelmingly, and overbearingly, childlike. Angelo Celeste, who plays him here, has a baby-faced look much like Caesars own, but is much shorter. This is unfortunate: Caesars height, most unusual in a comedian, contributed largely to the fear and adoration that he inspired in his employees, and Celeste, though he tries hard, doesn't have the force of personality to compensate.

Simon himself is clearly present as Lucas, the gangling young narrator (at least he gangles as Carter Siddall plays him). Of the identity of the others I am, despite my opening show of erudition, uncertain. I assume that Ira, the scarf-draped hypochondriac whose doctors' appointments make him two hours late for every meeting, is Woody Allen; he is excellently played by Craig Mason. The sweaty, belligerently philistine Milt (Lou Israel) could well be Mel Brooks; and Carl Reiner could conceivably be figured by the temporizing, level-headed Kenny (Ted Powers who, more than most of them, looks like he belongs).

But I am at a loss to identify Val (Austin Schatz, ripely appropriate) as the Russian-born-and-accented head writer; or the Irishman Brian (Shawn Devlin), the token Gentile; or Carole (Andrea McCulloch), the token woman. Of course, there doesnt have to be a one-to-one resemblance in every case; Simon is, after all, a dramatist.

Whatever, or whoever: Watching them interact to the intermission is bliss. In the second half one begins to wish that Simon would draw his themes a little tighter; would actually dramatize, rather than just allude to, the extent to which political repression (the blacklist) automatically selects comedy as a prime target. You actually want to witness Maxs fights with the top brass, rather than just hearing his wayward accounts of them. (He has a lovely way of recounting defeats as victories.)

You also wish that Simon hadn't, like most writers, flunked the main challenge of writing about creative people, which is showing them actually creating. The Caesarean writing sessions, at which half a dozen brilliant people battled one another with jokes, are part of show-business mythology. The nearest we get here is a read- through of a spoof on Julius Caesar (Simon's way of saying "Hail Caesar"?), which may or may not be authentic; either way, its likely to leave a Canadian audience reflecting how much better it was done by Wayne and Shuster.

The setting is surprisingly spacious and sanitary and free of smoke; we seem to be in a kitchen/dining-room left over from another play, conceivably Simons Brighton Beach Memoirs. But until the real thing comes along -- and there's no guarantee it will -- this version is, cautiously, recommended.
