ON THE MOON

Peter Cincotti (Concord)

- - -

Peter Cincotti, singer-pianist, was 18 when he made his New York debut in 2002 (at the Algonquine, no less); 19 when he cut his first CD for Concord, sounding pleasant but slight. He repeated four of the same songs, a lot more forcefully, on the Montreux Festival set Voices of Concord Jazz, an apparent attempt by a nice-great label to get some of the way back to its roots.

Cincottis upward trend continues on his new one, On the Moon, which has some very encouraging stuff. Neither his voice nor his piano are startling in themselves, but he applies them intelligently, the voice in particular. He wrote or co-wrote four of the songs, which range from the dreadful (He's Watching, a trip back to the land of I Believe) to the interesting (I'd Rather Be with You). He takes on Raise the Roof, from one of the two recent musicals of The Wild Party, and tries far too hard to make it live up to its title; that kind of full-bloodedness comes naturally or not at all.

If he doesnt have Broadway savvy, he does have street smarts; he kicks off with a cheekily aggressive St. Louis Blues (though what does he mean by "she wants for powder"?). Two other songs about places are from the department of Songs Everybody Knows But Hardly Anyone Sings: Bali Hai, which, with harmonica and multi-tracked vocal, sounds very sinister, and I Love Paris, given a take-it-or- leave-treatment, suggesting that if we hated Paris, he wouldn't be much bothered.

It may be my nostalgia kicking in, but the two best tracks are revivals of old pop hits: the Drifters' Up on the Roof, which is a delight to hear as a solo, and Eddy Arnold's sublimely self-pitying You Don't Know Me, which here completes a progress from country via Ray Charles soul to stark cabaret jazz.
