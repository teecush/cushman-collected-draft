Brothel #9 Factory Theatre, Toronto

The steady advance of Anusree Roy must rank among the most heartening forward marches of recent years. As actress and as playwright, she keeps doubling up on her own accomplishments. She first grabbed our attention with a delightful solo piece, Pyaasa.

Admittedly, she followed that up with a less delightful solo piece, Letters to My Grandma, but she compensated for it with a smart twohander, Roshni. And now comes Brothel #9, which is an even smarter four-hander, a fully fashioned full-length drama, complete with intermission. The bigger she gets, the better she gets. At this mathematical rate, she might, within a couple of years, give us an epic.

Brothel #9 is set in Calcutta, and as you might expect from its off-hand title, which combines the stark and the casual, it's about sexual trafficking. Rekha, a young vil-lage woman, arrives in the city thinking she has a job at a light-bulb factory, arranged for her by her brother-in-law. She finds instead that he has sold her as a prostitute. Her owner/employer is a man named Birbal. Jamuna, an indeterminately older woman, is the brothels senior attraction, cook, bottle-washer (fairly literally) and de facto madam.

Having pleaded with both of them, piteously and unavailingly, to release her, she turns with relief to a police officer, Salaudin, and finds -to the surprise of no one but herself -that hes one of the establishment's regulars, on the take and with first call on new merchandise. Salaudin's defloration of Rekha is unseen but very much heard; she emerges from it, staggering and apparently broken. She does not, however, fall apart. Determined to regain her freedom, she starts saving for it.

In addition to the four named characters, the cast includes a succession of anonymous men, skulking through the brothel door with varying degrees of furtiveness and bravado, and Rekhas door is, with increasing frequency, the one they make for.

The play tells a fairly simple, even melodramatic story, though its twists are both credible enough and adroit enough to keep us glued to the edges of our seats. It isnt often that a new play has us genuinely keyed up about what's going to happen next. There's an even more compelling set of interior actions, stemming from the fact that the power-relations within the quartet keep changing.

There is a solid sense, in Nigel Shawn Williams excellent production, of real lives being lived, real things being said: texture as well as text. The most obvious progression is Rekhas, from innocence to toughness, and Pamela Sinha makes both ends of the journey equally affecting. You could call her journey "empowering," but the play doesn't go in for words like that (or "journey," for that matter). It makes a big statement but, unlike the somewhat similar Ruined, which represents the Broadway idea of seriousness, it doesn't make big statements. Its also far better constructed, though like Ruined, it does leave you wondering whether the brothel is understaffed or the theatre is just underfunded.

Roy, who continues to write juicy roles for herself and to play them juicily, is Jamuna, whose whole existence is conflict. Shes capable of kindness, so long as she doesn't feel threatened, but she feels threatened most of the time. In the course of the play, Rekha gets pregnant, which makes Jamuna jealous on any number of levels. She fears loss of status; she also fears the loss of Salaudin, who's increasingly attracted to Rekha and whom she thinks to be, in a sense, hers.

The play, which manages to be unsentimentally hopeful, has a strong vein of equally unsentimental humour, and Roy mines most of it, which seems fair as she deposited it in the first place.

Sanjay Talwar, whose forte is playing weak authority figures, adds to their number as Salaudin, desperate to believe himself a good guy, and inclined to prove it by being generous with money; theres something pleasantly circular about how he effectively bribes the women whose other exploiters have bribed him. His developing feelings for Rekha seem genuine; but he is also, unsurprisingly, married.

The brutal Birbal, too, has a wife whos sick, in a village; his position on the totem-pole is compromised by the fact that most of what he earns goes to her.

Ash Knight's performance is the least defined of the four (also, to Western ears, the hardest initially to understand, though all four present us with that problem), but he fits aptly into the play's shifting cycle of dependencies.

There's a real dramatic world for the actors to fill; they are not forced, as actors so often are, to devote their energies to papering over the cracks. The people in this play are in flux, as people generally are, and the performers give us changes we can believe in.

Speaking of gifts, the Factory has already, with Eternal Hydra, made us a present of the best, almost-new play of the season. They now offer us the best really new one.

- Brothel #9 plays until March 27 at Toronto's Factory Theatre. For tickets call 416-504-9971.
