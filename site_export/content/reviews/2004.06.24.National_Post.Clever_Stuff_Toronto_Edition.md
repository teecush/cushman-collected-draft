NATHAN THE WISE

Harbourfront Centre Theatre

- - -

There is a character in Nathan the Wise known as the Great Patriarch. He seems to be the head of the Orthodox Church in 12th- century Jerusalem. He is very keen on burning Jews at the stake. He is also not above instructing a Christian knight to carry out a murderous ambush on Saladin, the famous Arab sultan to whom the knight just happens to owe his life. The Patrriarch stands out among the people in this play for his malevolence. The others are about as nice a bunch as you could hope to encounter in a serious drama, especially one set in the middle of the Second Crusade with the three relevant religions poised at one another's throats.

Strangely, all this benevolence does not add up to blandness. Nathan the Wise presents a conflict of good intentions. It's a comedy since the conflicts are, with conscious artificiality, resolved, but it could just as easily turn tragic.

It was the last work of Gotthold Ephraim Lessing, major 18th- century German novelist, philosopher, playwright and -- I am pleased to note -- critic. Lessing was the son of a pastor and an object of scandal in his time for his inquisitive tolerance of other faiths; one thinks of him as a born-again humanist. The title character of his play, who shares much of his own benevolent skepticism, is a rich and universally respected Jewish merchant, a sort of Shylock in reverse, complete with a much-loved only daughter. He comes home from a trip to find that she has been rescued from burning -- not at the stake -- by a passing Knight Templar, the very same ex-captive whose life was spared by Saladin.

Knight and maiden are naturally primed to fall in love, which, after some hesitation on the young man's part ("A Christian love a Jew? Well, why not, this is the Promised Land") they duly do, with Nathan's ecumenical blessing.

Nathan, meanwhile, has been summoned to the court of the Sultan who, as he well knows, wants to extort gold from him. Saladin is, however, an exceptionally civilized potentate. He puts a question to the Jew: Which religion -- Jewish, Christian or Muslim -- is the true one?

Nathan is far too canny and skeptical to give a direct answer. Besides, he doesn't have one. Instead he offers a parable, designed to show that though there may be a truth, it is unknowable. Saladin is enchanted, and proclaims Nathan his friend for life. Good old reliable Nathan, not called the Wise for nothing, offers him the money anyway.

Saladin, to get back to the other part of the plot, released the Templar because the young man reminded him of his own deceased brother. There are all sorts of fairy-tale tangles going on here, and they are resolved in a series of revelations and reunions that might have raised the eyebrows of Shakespeare or Moliere, not least because the ending they inspire, though happy, is far from conventional.

Lessing, in fact, turns romance against itself -- the point presumably being that though it would be lovely if everybody were as tolerant as his characters learn to be, it isn't likely. It certainly wouldn't have been so in Jerusalem then, and it seems achingly unreachable now, hence the impulse for this revival. Nathan, so kind and clever and urbane, is a historically improbable figure, a backward projection of Lessing's own Enlightenment hopes, and Saladin, glamorous figure though he was, can hardly have been that noble. The play's considerable achievement is to make us believe in them dramatically, using wit as a fence against sentimentality.

Tim Albery's Soulpepper production is good on argument but short on atmosphere. The clarity of the former is underlined by Sharin DiGenova's lighting, while the vagueness of the latter is reflected in Dany Lyne's set, which is overall sandy. This looks impressive at first, but once we have paid our respects to the fact that the Holy Land is basically desert, we remember that Jerusalem itself must, even then, have been a built-up area. There is little sense of a city's life being led, and a log across the middle of the stage, conceivably symbolizing a barrier between the races, serves mainly as an impediment to traffic.

Costumes are mixed, Nathan approximately modern, everyone else approximately medieval. But if the Jew doesn't inhabit the same period as everybody else, we lose the sense of how perilous his position is. (The play itself is hardly so familiar that we need to be jolted out of any pre-conceived notions about it.) Yes, he's an anachronism, but the point doesn't need underlining. What the production chiefly lacks is danger.

In which connection, the only flaw I could detect in William Webster's Nathan is an excess of affability. Nathan has been through things, and though Webster is very moving in the moments at which these memories explicitly surface, they should surely be a part of him throughout, sewn into his coat. In all other respects, he is very fine. He embodies Nathan's moral qualities in a way I suspect few other actors could, he radiates humour and, yes, wisdom, and he is in total command of a complex text. (Edward Kemp's translation is in colloquial, barely identifiable verse.) His telling of the long religious fable is superb, and it calls out an answering quality in Andrew Moodie's suave Saladin, who can turn on terror in an instant, but knows to use it sparingly.

The play's Achilles heel may be its attempt to make a protagonist of an ethically perfect figure, while the character who goes through the most twists and travails, and so should technically be the hero, is the Templar. It's a tricky role and Dusan Dukic, though occasionally caught a step behind his own dramatic progress, does well by it.

The daughter is a surprisingly insubstantial presence, to whom Cara Pifko brings little besides an innate perkiness. Karen Robinson is jarringly bourgeoise as the Sultan's scheming sister. (She sounds, if you'll pardon the stereotype, more Jewish than anyone else in the play.) Among the Christians, there are good neutral performances by Barbara Gordon as the daughter's duenna and by Derek Boyes as a conscience-torn friar, but the most striking is David Calderisi's Patriarch, political to his fingernails.

There is yet a fourth culture represented, in the person of a temporarily non-whirling Dervish, charmingly played by Vik Sahay, who crosses all lines; he partners Nathan at chess and works as the Sultan's treasurer, a post for which he has neither the heart nor the qualifications. He quits, disgusted, and sets off for India, telling Nathan, who hesitates to join him, "if you can't live your life in the instant, you'll always be the slave of others."

It may be the most resonant moment in the play, though it doesn't seem to lead anywhere, but maybe it's balanced by the last image, which is one of acceptance, and is very well staged. The show doesn't yield the same tingle of discovery as such earlier Soulpepper rarities as Don Carlos and Platonov, but it's a fascinating evening and a heartening one.

- In repertory until July 31. Box office: 416-973-4000.
