LITTLE SHOP OF HORRORS

Top of the Square

It's not much of a contest, but (bop-she-bop) Little Shop of Horrors is the best rock musical ever perpetrated. Based on a grade- B, or grade-Z, science-fiction movie directed by Roger Corman in a couple of weeks somewhere near the start of his career, it represents the triumphant mating of two species of junk. Howard Ashman wrote the book and lyrics, and Alan Menken the music, back in 1982, when they were young things looking for a break. They subsequently got one from the Disney organization, which swept them off to Hollywood; but nothing they wrote there, not even Beauty and the Beast, compares for wit and invention with this, their one successful stage-show. Ashman died tragically young; Menken teamed up with other lyricists -- Tim Rice, for example -- and progressed from mock-schlock to real schlock, which is not progress.

Little Shop of Horrors is set in New York: in a flower shop on a Skid Row sort of street, called Skid Row. This is no kind of address at which to run a floral business, which may explain why there isnt any: not at least until the store clerk Seymour brings in a strange- looking little green plant. At first resistant to its guardians tender ministrations ("I've given you sunshine/I've given you dirt/ Ive given you plant-food/Youve given me hurt!") the plant perks up remarkably when Seymour accidentally cuts his finger. Having once tasted blood, it demands more, and grows on this diet into a strange- looking big green plant, with insatiate jaws. It makes Seymour famous and the store prosperous; so prosperous that the owner, Mr. Mushnik, who took Seymour in as an orphan and has been exploiting him ever since, now adopts him rather than risk losing him. Seymour has a fervent crush on Audrey, his pneumatic co-worker, and he names the plant Audrey II in her honour.

For its musical inspiration, the show reaches back to the Brill Building sounds of the late '50s and early '60s. The opening number is called Downtown, but has nothing to do with Petula Clark; rather, it is a scene-setting reversal of Uptown, the 1962 song popularized by the Crystals. (Downtown, where the folks are broke/Downtown, where your lifes a joke.) It enables Seymour, the complete nebbish, to sing of his existential angst ("I keep asking God what Im for/And he says 'Gee, I'm not sure. ") The show comes with its own girl-group, one of whom is indeed called Crystal; the others are named Ronette and Chiffon. They sing narrative links while aggressively gyrating their bosoms, and they close the proceedings with a terrifying recitative: Eventually the plant achieved its purpose, which was to eat Cleveland.

Most rock musicals are intentionally solemn and unintentionally ludicrous, since the level of noise and hysteria onstage is invariably in excess of whatever is supposed to be happening in the story. Apart from The Rocky Horror Show, Little Shop is the only show I know to capitalize on this; it goes over the top with its tongue in its cheek. (Besides its subject, which is world- domination by omnivorous green things, surely justifies any amount of overwrought emotionalism.)

Audrey II finds a voice, a raucously powerful one, and uses it to sing "Feed me!" and other stomach-felt lyrics to the same effect. Forced to do its bidding, Seymour decides to eliminate Audrey Is singularly unsavoury boyfriend, whose career path was set when his mother noticed his penchant for torturing helpless animals: She said, son, be a de-entist, and he did. ("He's a dentist" echo the girls, and he'll never, never be any good.) "I know Seymours the greatest," confides Audrey, her affections torn, but Im dating a semi-sadist. She's kidding; there's nothing semi about him. Freed from his domination, not to mention the attendant medical bills, Audrey gratefully bestows her love on Seymour, who cant believe his luck. But its bootless: Things Have Gone Too Far.

Seen in the context of other musicals, the show, combining the attractions of cannibalism and retail trade, is a cross between She Loves Me and Sweeney Todd, with -- for those who like their cultural references cultural -- a strong whiff of Faust.

The current production is the inaugural venture of a new dinner theatre situated above the Hard Rock Cafe at Yonge and Dundas. The space has housed other eating-and-acting enterprises in the past, but last Thursday neither side of the operation had its act together. Both were especially short on speed of delivery; when Seymour yelled at the importunate Audrey II "don't expect dessert" he seemed to be speaking for the management.

The program is unusually quiet about the actors' previous experience, and I sometimes wondered whether they had any. Presumably they are professional, but they seem like amateurs. The crude distinction, which you take for granted until youre forcibly exposed to it, is one of pace. Professional actors, even bad ones, know how to pick up cues; amateurs dont, not with any consistency, and even the best tend to exist as islands on the stage.

The lack of give-and-take can be numbing, as it is here. The worst performance here is lamentably underdone, the next worst appallingly overdone. The best is given offstage, by Jamie Hofman as the soul-singing voice of Audrey II. (Am I allowed to say that when he came on stage for his curtain call I was surprised to find that he was white and looked harmless?) Michael Munrow's Seymour and Elizabeth Jones' Audrey I are somewhere in the middle, conscientious and well-sung -- she especially -- but short on cartooniness; if we start taking these characters seriously we're in trouble. The choreographer, Allison Plamondon, proves resourceful at manoeuvring her performers round a central pillar that is hell on sight-lines but is probably there to hold up the building. The conductor, Steve Lavoie, also designed the lighting. That's nothing: The producer, Mike Murley, also functions as director, designer and dentist. Sometimes versatility can be a curse.
