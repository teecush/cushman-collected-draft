Spectator Following Rapunzel and the gang into the enchanted Woods

Saturday, April 22, 1995

Robert Cushman

BY Robert Cushman THIS is not meant to be a riposte to theatre critic Kate Taylor's review of Into the Woods (When wishes come true, April 8), but I must answer her query about why the Canadian Stage Company should be doing this musical. They should be doing it because its a masterpiece. And its Canadian premiere is long overdue.

I love Into the Woods, and so do my children. (My wife does too.) This family agreement is especially pleasing, since the Stephen Sondheim-James Lapine show is partly about connections - sometimes made, more often missed - between the generations. It juxtaposes three familiar fairy-tales - Cinderella, Little Red Riding Hood and Jack and the Beanstalk - with a subsidiary role for Rapunzel and last-minute cameo appearances by Snow White and the Sleeping Beauty. Binding them all together in Lapines script is a newly invented fable: the story of the Baker and his Wife, and the Witch next door. The couple want a child, and the Witch promises them one, if certain quests are achieved. So into the woods they go, as do Jack, Cinders and Ms. Hood in pursuit of their own legendary goals. And their stories criss-cross, with the Baker slaying the Wolf with his bread- knife, and his wife helping Cinderella off with her shoe, and the witch revealed as Rapunzel's adoptive mother.

What I can only call the sheer story-ness of the show is the most captivating thing about it: the delight of never knowing who will come around the next corner, or lurk behind the next tree; the satisfaction of seeing how neatly the pieces interlock and how powerfully the separate tales flow into one in the show's darker second half, which is about what happens after happily ever after. It is, of course, this joy in narrative that unites children and adults (and, in my own case, has persisted through the multiple viewings of the Broadway-cast video that my own brood have wished upon me). Lapines book is one of the most satisfying ever written for a musical. Without denying us the pleasures of dialogue (as happens in the Lloyd Webberish pop operas), it merges seamlessly with Sondheims score. There are no jolts when one ends and the other begins, and in a musical that is the rarest kind of success.

It's partly because of the timeless world that the show inherits from its sources and then makes its own. Fairy-tale characters can burst into song with no questions asked. Also there's no question that in creating a classic it helps to start with another classic; or, as here, with a fistful of them. Myths reverberate; its their job. The Baker shares with his own estranged father a song of desperation called No More, in which he pleads, vainly, for escape from the world: "No more giants waging war/Cant we just pursue our lives/With our children and our wives . . .." I saw the show in London the night the Gulf War broke out, and the resonance was almost unbearable.

Then too the pastoral setting makes a perfect launching-pad. Anything, as the Prince Charming sings when he gets around to seducing the Baker's Wife, "can happen in the woods." Describing the appeal of his previous show Sunday in the Park with George (based on the Seurat painting A Sunday Afternoon on the Island of La Grande Jatte), Sondheim said, You just wanted to live in that park forever and ever. Personally I didn't, but I was very happy to stay in the woods; greenery, potent and deceptively soothing, can be the richest of dramatic environments. (The greatest play in the English language is A Midsummer Nights Dream; the most delightful is Loves Labour's Lost.) Canadian Stage has taken a puritanical attitude, cutting the trees down to projections on screens. They are lovely, and they go interestingly haywire when the action lurches out of control in Act Two, but they are overpowered by a surrounding array of steps, like something out of a 1950s Macbeth. This defoliation is one of my few cavils with the Toronto production.

Here I had better declare an interest. I auditioned - unsuccessfully - for the role of the Narrator in this production. (I'm not sure in which direction this biases me.) It was an interesting experience, not least because having to sing a section of No More heightened my already mountainous regard for Sondheims powers of musical and verbal characterization, in this case for the depiction, with piercing economy, of a mind that has gone to the end of its tether and back again.

I like most of his shows, some of them extravagantly, but none as much as this. I thought it was a near-masterpiece (Im a cautious soul) back in 1988 when I saw a preview of the Broadway production, which had an almost unbeatable cast. It was patronized by the New York critics who seemed to feel that a composite fairy-story was a letdown after the self-evidently Important Theme (The Plight of the Artist) of Sunday in the Park. It got far better reviews in London, where classic comedy and ensemble performance - every part in this show is worth performing - are part of the culture. The score, like the book, is a spider's web with ribs of steel: a cat's cradle of cunningly interwoven motifs, brief shafts of melody and lyric that grow richer at each repetition. For a taste, listen to the Bakers Wife as her princely fantasy develops through her scenes with her husband and Cinderella before erupting into a brief unsettling reality, with a truly shocking sequel. The tired old accusation that Sondheims music lacks emotion actually means that he offers crystalline intensity while most others today settle for deafening bloat.

"Children should listen," shrieks the Witch at the refractory Rapunzel. (An admonitory song while combing her daughter's hair had two mothers of my acquaintance asking the same question: How does he know?) "Children won't listen," she grieves after she has lost the girl for good. At the finale theres a final modulation as the company tell us that Children will listen. Its more a warning than a reassurance. "Careful the things you say . . .," your kids will pick up on them, not always as you would like. The music is healing, and the show over all is as compassionate as it's astringent, but the first time I saw it I wept. Admittedly, I was a soft touch. I had come through, just about, my own first year of fatherhood; and the show seemed to play out some of my fondest dreams and some of my worst nightmares. It still gets me.

It also makes me laugh a lot, never more than in the competing duet, Agony, of Cinderella's and Rapunzels royal swains, reprised with compound comic interest when they fall for a couple of other maidens. I must have her to wife, which is the ending first time round, mutates into "ah well, back to my wife." Their joint attitudinizing, like a couple of old theatrical prints of old theatrical princes, is deliciously handled at CSC: credit, I imagine, to the choreographer, Denise Clarke.

No family or romantic unit remains intact in this show, but the community just about survives. It might be a meditation on two essential texts: Philip Larkins imperishable they fuck you up, your mum and dad and Donne's "no man is an island."

I've left an awful lot out. Sondheims ability to penetrate and project the thoughts and emotions of his characters while retaining a recognizable personal voice, his range of subject matter and complete absorption in each new project, his wit and irony, his balancing of a faultless ear for human speech against the formal demands of verse and music, have long prompted the highest comparison: one at which Ive already hinted. Not all his shows could sustain it - but Into the Woods, showering sensual and intellectual delights while its themes and implications expand as effortlessly and implacably as Jack's beanstalk, is the most Shakespearean work of contemporary theatre that I know.

Robert Cushman is a freelance theatre critic based in Toronto. Into the Woods plays at Toronto's St. Lawrence Centre through May 20.
