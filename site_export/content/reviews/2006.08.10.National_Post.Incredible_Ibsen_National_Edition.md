ROSMERSHOLM

Court House Theatre, Niagara-on-the Lake, Ont.

THEATRE REVIEW

The Shaw Festival production of Rosmersholm is a revelation. I say this with feeling since, of all Henrik Ibsen's plays, this is the one I have always had the most trouble taking seriously. It concerns John Rosmer, a former pastor whose family has, for generations, been the most influential in the neighbourhood -- and also the most conservative. He has now forsaken both his religious and political beliefs, under the influence of Rebecca West, a young woman who moved into his house to help care for his sick wife, Beata, a troubled lady who drowned herself shortly before the play began.

Gradually, the play being one of the showpieces of Ibsen's cat- and-mouse dramaturgy, it's revealed that Rebecca was responsible for Beata's death. She had wanted Rosmer for herself and persuaded Beata that Rosmer loved her in return and that Beata herself stood between her husband and his happiness. Too late, Rebecca realizes that her own idealism, her belief in freedom and progress, has misled her. She is a murderess. She must atone.

Rebecca attributes her awakening, not to her own conscience, but to her exposure to the high moral tone of the Rosmer household. This is represented on stage by the massed portraits of the current Rosmer ancestors, gazing down on the present incumbent, each more unforgiving and censorious than the last. Or so, in the current production, we must imagine. In Peter Hartwell's economical design, the portraits are a series of empty frames, plus a single video still representing a couple of Rosmers past who look forbidding.

Ibsen wrote this play in the 1880s, and set it in his native Norway. Director Neil Munro has placed it "somewhere in Europe between the wars," though the time shift is hardly noticeable, and the shift in place not noticeable at all. No audience will sit through an Ibsen play believing that it could happen anywhere but Norway, and Munro virtually admits it by keeping the play's name intact, even while anglicizing it in the dialogue to "the House of Rosmer."

But though the generalized updating is unnecessary, some specific changes are very helpful, especially where the play's politics are concerned. It may come as a shock to hear Headmaster Kroll, Rosmer's brother-in-law and polemical antagonist, described as a leader of the Christian Right, but that is what he is after all, even if the term itself is anachronistic for both the 1880s and the 1930s. Here, for once, we discover what the "advanced ideas" in an Ibsen play actually are and why the characters are so passionately for or against them.

Rebecca is habitually played in the grand manner, as if she were a consolation prize for an actress who isn't getting to play Hedda Gabler this year. Waneta Storms plays her more intent, believing in her cause, biding her time but poleaxed by the revelation that her lover, in the years before she came to Rosmersholm, was also her biological father. The matter is confined to one scene but it tears Rebecca apart; it's the only point at which Storms storms, and it's enough. At the very moment when Patrick Galligan's Rosmer, the kind of open-faced paragon with whom any faction would want to burnish its image, admits he wants her physically, it becomes impossible for her to accede. Giving both participants a family curse may be adding incest to injury, but nobody could say that Ibsen starves his actors of motivation. Storms and Galligan play superbly together, and even better with Peter Hutt's magnificent Kroll, a role usually reduced to a pompous, bloodless caricature. Hutt, while never stinting on smugness and intolerance, grants him a point of view that's serious and sincerely-held, complicated by a deep personal involvement -- philosophical, familial, sexual -- with the people he's up against. He conveys, subtly and speedily, extraordinary shades of feeling.

This has been an up and down and in-between season at the Shaw, but the ups -- The Heiress, The Crucible, Design for Living, most of Too True to Be Good and now this -- have been spectacular.
