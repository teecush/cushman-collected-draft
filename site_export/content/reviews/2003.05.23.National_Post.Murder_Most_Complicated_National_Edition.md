BLOOD RELATIONS

Royal George Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont.

- - -

The classic theatrical treatment of the Lizzie Borden axe murders is now just half a century old. I refer of course to the finale number from the revue New Faces of 1952. ("Oh, you can't chop your momma up in Massachusetts / And then get dressed and go out for a walk. / No, you can't chop your momma up in Massachusetts; / Massachusetts is a far cry from New York!") But Sharon Pollock's play Blood Relations comes in a very honourable second. It was first staged in Edmonton in 1980, has been constantly revived ever since, and now gets a slap-up outing at the Shaw Festival, in the space where the cosy English thrillers used to be.

I would not dream of being frivolous, but I can't resist pointing out a bedrock similarity between the revue and the play. Both deal with their subject matter by putting it in an explicitly theatrical context. In the New Faces item, Miss Borden's trial and acquittal in her hometown of Fall River, Mass., are presented as part of the weekly hoedown, complete with square-dance calls. ("Cross your fingers, knock on wood / Local girl is making good.")

The Pollock piece gives us psychodrama. We meet Lizzie in 1902, 10 years after the bloody events, still living with her sister Emma in the house where their father and stepmother met their grisly ends. Lizzie is also conducting an affair with a Boston actress -- this is historically accurate -- who comes for tea every Sunday. On one of these occasions, The Actress -- that's how she's tagged throughout -- asks, presumably not for the first time, "Lizzie, did you do it?" and gets an evasive answer, also probably as usual. But this time Lizzie, playing to her lover's strengths, goes on to suggest a charade. The two of them will act out the events leading up to the killings. Lizzie will play Bridget, the maid, and The Actress will play Lizzie. And in this way perhaps the truth will come out.

There are serious problems with this framework, but what stands inside it is pitilessly gripping. Lizzie lost her mother when she was two. Her father, a rich skinflint, took another wife whom Lizzie detested. At 32, Lizzie is still unmarried, still at home, in defiance of all the rules laid down for her age, class and period. Her parents resent this, and she resents them right back. (Her sister, older and equally a spinster, doesn't seem to get the same flak. But then she doesn't give it either.)

Apart from striking Lizzie while trying to bully her into marrying a man she barely knows and doesn't like, her father is planning to sell the family farm right out from under her. To top it all off, he takes an axe to his daughter's pet pigeons, thereby giving her ideas. Strangely enough, she seems to blame her misfortunes more on her stepmother than on her father, whom she protests she still loves. But she takes a chopper to the both of them anyway; we see her raise the murder weapon on each occasion, before the merciful blackout.

That at least is the conclusion to which the experience of impersonating Lizzie drives The Actress, and it seems logical, indeed inexorable. The historical Lizzie got off, but her assumed guilt has passed into folklore. We hear a bit of her counsel's defence, and it's very unpersuasive. He says, basically, that no well-brought-up lady could possibly have done such things, which is the flip side of the stifling condescension that sent Lizzie bananas in the first place.

Eda Holmes' production reinforces, quietly and superbly, the enveloping sense of doom. Victims and killer alike go soberly about their business, wrapped in invisible shrouds.

Michael Ball and Nora McLellan, the latter's performance being the best I have ever seen her give, are gravely controlled as the parents, meaning well and acting mean. Laurie Paton plays The Actress, which effectively means she plays Lizzie, and she is sharp and solid as a rock. Jane Perry, who is so to speak the official Lizzie, suggests that the haunted woman finds considerable release in assuming the comparatively cheerful role of the maid. (Both ladies, in their different ways, grant Lizzie a corrosive sense of mischief.) Sharry Flett is mild and drawn as Emma, whose absence from home on the crucial day made, as they say at awards ceremonies, the whole thing possible.

Paton, Perry and Flett (they sound like a team of solicitors) are all performing striking doubles vis-a-vis their previous night's roles in Misalliance. They are also the only actors who appear in the play's outer action, Flett's Emma having graduated over the years to the role of nagging conscience, or perhaps just nagging voice of propriety.

Pollock was probably right to go for something more complicated than a bald murder story. But the hares she starts don't actually run. Why, for example, does Lizzie play Bridget rather than any other character? There's no indication that her performance as the maid influences the way in which The Actress interprets Lizzie. How do we account for the scenes in which neither of them appears?

The play ends with Lizzie denying she did it, then turning on The Actress and saying "you did." At least, that's what happens in this production; in the published script she turns on the audience. It makes no sense either way. We may empathize with Lizzie's plight, but that doesn't convict us of anything.

There's no suggestion that The Actress has twisted the facts, or come up with a new discovery. Everybody has always believed Lizzie was the killer, and it's not as if we have any way of checking out the play's veracity as we watch it; few of us have the facts of the Borden case at our fingertips.

Actually, Lizzie's guilt is what makes the story interesting. Certainly it's what makes it dramatically interesting. In recognizing this, The Actress at least shows that she knows her own business. And most of the time, when she's not splashing out of her depth, the playwright does too. Jump like a fish, jump like a porpoise; all join hands and habeas corpus.
