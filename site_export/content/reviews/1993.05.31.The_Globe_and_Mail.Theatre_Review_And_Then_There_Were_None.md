THEATRE REVIEW AND THEN THERE WERE NONE

Monday, May 31, 1993

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont. -- BY AGATHA CHRISTIE Directed by Jim Mezon Starring Michael Ball, Norman Browning, Peter Hutt and William Vickers At the Shaw Festival, Royal George Theatre Rating: Jim Mezon gives this Agatha Christie chestnut a stylish roasting A compelling process of elimination BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont. THE story is told of a London cab-driver who picked up some uncouth Americans on their way to see The Mousetrap. Aggrieved by their loudness, rudeness and refusal to tip, he shouted after them as they entered the theatre, "The detective did it."

It's just as well that there was nobody I disliked in the audience for And Then There Were None, because I saw the play in England years ago, so many years that it still bore its original title of Ten Little Niggers. In America, that had already been changed to Ten Little Indians. And then there was what we have now, another label for the same old Agatha Christie story.

It's the one about 10 assorted people summoned to a deserted house on a deserted island off the coast of Devon, on various pretences, by a host they have never met. When they are all assembled, a disembodied voice accuses each of them of having committed murder; and it turns out that in each of their pasts there has indeed been a death of which they are legally innocent but may have been morally guilty. Then one of them drops dead and, as the action progresses, more of them do: each in accordance with a stanza of the familiar rhyme about the 10 members of a visible minority. It dawns on the survivors that the killer is not merely among them but is one of them.

I, of course, knew which one. But though I knew who had done it, I had forgotten whom he had done, or at least how and in what order. So there was still suspense for me, not much affected by my superior knowledge. In fact, though I expected to have a mildly enjoyable time, I had a thoroughly good one. Jim Mezon's production makes it impossible for anyone, actor or spectator, to condescend to the material.

He gives it just as much style as it will take. Much of it derives from film: chilling music rising to a screech every time somebody hits the carpet, a sunset for the last character to walk into. It doesn't swamp or mock the play, and it allows the actors to play the characters for as much as they are worth, or - if we are being mean about it - considerably more.

Those assembled include a hanging judge, a boorish ex-copper, a dashingly sardonic young army officer, a nervous nerve-specialist formerly a surgeon (one of a long line of stage doctors who have bungled operations while drunk), a rigid frigid spinster with zero tolerance for "nudity and gramophone records" . . . shall I go on? It is tempting to describe them as a cross section of English society c. 1940. (Well, they start out cross. They end up hysterical.) But, of course, they are merely a selection of crime-fiction dummies, apprentice corpses. Their saving grace is that not even their author believes them to be anything else. She may just hint that it's a bad idea to drink or drive irresponsibly or for anyone to be judgmental, especially judges; but she doesn't push it, and neither would I. The play is totally without pretension. It's just good at its job.

Michael Ball has an endearingly sepulchral voice as his honour, coupled with what the text identifies as a typically judicial forward roll of the head. Norman Browning is all thug, Peter Hutt curls his lip romantically, and Roger Rowland is a retired general out of any British film you care to mention. (Playing Christie, it occurs to me, must be a lot like playing Brecht. In both, social role is all.) Wendy Thatcher may try too hard as the maiden lady - she pumps passion into what is by definition passionless - but it's a venial sin. What a good show. And how depressing if it turns out to the best thing in the Festival.
