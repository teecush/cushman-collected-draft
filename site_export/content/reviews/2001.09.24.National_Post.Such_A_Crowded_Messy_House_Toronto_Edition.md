Theatre Review

HABITAT

Bluma Appel Theatre, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - Judith Thompson's confused new play seems obsessed at its heart with mothers and children, especially daughters. We begin with a girl called Raine, unleashing an aria of selfish teenage resentment at the bedside of her mother, who happens to be dying of cancer. The lights change, the soundtrack crashes, mother sits up in bed, and we get to hear the story of how Raine as a baby nearly choked to death in her mother's arms.

Her mother dead, Raine takes herself off to a group home that has just opened up on Mapleview Lanes, a leafy street in Etobicoke. The local residents, despite nice liberal principles, are not pleased by the advent of a dozen or so deprived, unruly youngsters, and they launch a protest. Spearheading it are another mother and daughter, Margaret and Janet, who don't get along; Margaret is a widow who has sunk into careless inertia since her husband's death, and Janet is a lawyer who confesses in a monologue that she doesn't like her own children much either.

Lewis Chance, the ambiguous figure who runs the home, has an intense telephonic relationship with his own mother in New Brunswick and tells a story that parallels Raine's, concerning the death of his baby brother. The remaining character is Sparkle, another inmate, a housebreaker and congenital fantasist who at one point claims to have killed his parents; a fey, out-of-the-closet Peter Pan who nurses an erotic obsession with Lewis that may or may not be reciprocated.

Thompson might conceivably have written a decent play about any one of these people, but spreading the load as she has, she is compelled to keep stopping and starting with all of them, often picking them up at an inexplicable psychological distance from where she dropped them. They do not, whatever unifying themes they may theoretically embody, make much of a mosaic. The worst of all is that these fragmented characters have been dumped into a sociological problem drama that demands conventional linear treatment if it is to work at all. Current theatrical economics may be the culprit, but it is hugely damaging that we never meet most of the kids the neighbours are complaining about; we simply don't believe the home exists. There is probably meant to be a connecting metaphor (these are all our children, society is a neglectful parent) but as it plays, Habitat is a mess, subject and method hopelessly at odds.

There are a couple of good speeches: Raine's first one, and Lewis' insistently jovial address to the community he has invaded. There are also some deep purple ones, and they are not excused by having Lewis, a prime offender, turn around and dismiss them as sob stories for suckers; Thompson still goes on writing them. (To wrongfoot the audience? That's a frivolous intellectual game.)

Margaret and Janet pay a call on Lewis, and he comprehensively insults them, though it would be in his interest to conciliate them. Maybe he's self-destructive, but that needs exploring. Maybe the author couldn't resist a flashy scene. She seems to be making it up as she goes along. Margaret has a series of encounters with Raine, whom she likes, and this quasi-maternal relationship is probably the play's best shot at reconciling its public and private themes. But the scenes are fragmentary, and it is never clear whether Margaret's abrupt reversion to the role of spruce homebody is triggered by Raine or by the conflicting excitement of defending her property values against the chancy Mr. Chance.

Katherine Kaszas' production is geographically awkward and emotionally stiff. Shawn Kerwin's set has furniture but no walls; this is a play about houses, but we never feel them. (Nice trees, though.) Near the end the furniture has to be moved, ludicrously. For the forty millionth time: Theatre is not television. Use short scenes at your peril. If you use them, find a style.

The sound effects are loudly clashing symbols. The actors go defensively for realism. Their success rate varies. Stephen Ouimette as Lewis is more charismatic than convincing. Holly Lewis as Raine starts strong but is defeated by her character's bewildering changes in attitude and even in intelligence. Corinne Conley's Margaret is trapped within a stereotype, and Kristina Nicoll's Janet is a blank. Luke Kirby's posturing Sparkle is the most irritating performance I have seen all year, but he is probably keeping faith with the text.

Apparently, CanStage were so pleased to have snagged a famous playwright that they went into production with a piece that, in its present state, should not have been done. The play inaugurates a co- commissioning program with the Royal Exchange in Manchester, which suggests two important theatres asleep at the controls.
