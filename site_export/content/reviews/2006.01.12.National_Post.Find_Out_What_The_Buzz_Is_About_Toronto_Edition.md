HUMBLE BOY

Tarragon Theatre

- - -

Bumble bee, Humble Boy. Charlotte Jones' clever and charming play, five-years old and English, begins with a snatch of Rimsky- Korsakov's flute specialty, and follows up with the entrance of Felix Humble, an overweight young man who makes straight for the large hive in the corner of his parents' garden. The bees, however, have flown, or been evicted.

Felix is a research physicist working on String Theory, which is apparently an attempt to explain everything. (It tries to reconcile relativity and quantum mechanics, two theories that are incompatible.) As a friend of his mother's says, "If you have to research anything, everything would be the thing to research."

By that exacting standard, Jones' play doesn't quite add up. It doesn't find a connection between all of its elements, which include not just the habits of bees but the flowering of roses, the nature of grief and of love, a touch of class conflict and the plot of Hamlet. Felix's father, a biology teacher and amateur entomologist with a consuming desire to find and name a new bee species, has died, and his mother is about to re-marry, with a man of whom Felix violently disapproves. The Claudius to her Gertrude is the retired owner of a fleet of buses; he's also something of a Polonius, being the father of Felix's cast-off girlfriend. This Ophelia, though, has gone anything but mad; though she did get a bit angry, she has pulled herself together, borne a daughter, and become a nurse. There is also a gardener who wanders on and off, telling Felix things about Nature; I thought he might equal the First Gravedigger, but I turned out to be very wrong.

In more recent British terms, Humble Boy may be said to explore Tom Stoppard themes in Alan Ayckbourn territory. The cosmic concerns, though, don't always mesh with the domestic. You may say that Felix is trying to make sense of his own life as well as of the universe, but that's terribly vague. It's a gap in the writing, and also possibly in Richard Rose's production, that it never establishes Felix at the centre of things. He is a Hamlet without soliloquies, hence an unprivileged Hamlet, just one point-of-view among several. Dean Paul Gibson plays him delightfully, a self- tormenting brain in a shell both physically and emotionally gauche, but if he undergoes any kind of spiritual journey (as other characters keep hinting he does) the steps are far from clear.

Humble's mom is granted a recognizable epiphany -- a reversal of her Shakespearean prototype -- and Fiona Reid carries off the transformation triumphantly. Transformation is the word, since in the earlier part of the play she's been depicted, with relish, as both shallow and selfish, qualities on which Reid deploys her high- comedy skills, starting with an entrance in which she radiates impatient disdain through dark glasses atop a black funeral dress. You wonder why Felix should want to redeem her, but you're pleased when he does.

It's a fine actors' evening all round, with every character given both a solid place in the ensemble and at least one triumphant solo. Nicola Lipman all but stops the show as an exploited dowdy who, asked to say grace, revolts by loading it with any number of qualifying clauses; it doesn't take up much space on the page, but while she says it, time is suspended. Michael Ball is enthusiastically boorish as the unsuitable suitor who is allowed a mind, heart and wracking memories of his own; he also has a Glenn Miller fetish he's carried into the digital age. Sarah Dodd plays some of her moments of wit and wisdom rather too obviously as moments, but she's still immensely captivating, making sense and practicality sexy. She has a love scene with Felix that dissolves into farce but leaves you feeling, even as it does so, prepared to kill the author if she doesn't allow the two of them to get back together. Ian D. Clark rounds out the company as the gentle horticulturalist. The women, by the way, are named Flora and Rosie. In this pastoral setting it seems appropriate that even the director should be called Rose.

It's a show to be relished for its constantly shifting surfaces rather than for its dubious depths. It has wit, both structural and verbal, and a mastery of tone. The flute comes back at the end but there is no sting in the tail. Felix's, and the play's, last words are the very same with which Hamlet surrendered himself to fate: "Let be." Or, as the original spelling might very well have had it, "Let bee".
