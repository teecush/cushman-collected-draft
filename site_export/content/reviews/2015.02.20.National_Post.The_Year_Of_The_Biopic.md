Eight films are competing on Sunday for the Best Picture Academy Award. Four of them are biographical. That's a record.

Somewhere behind the movies', and the movie public's, fascination with true-life stories, is the belief that dramas are better for you if they are about people who actually existed. The reality factor makes a happy ending feel happier, a sad one sadder and both more ennobling. If the protagonist is heroic, and most are in some way, then we get to share vicariously in the heroism. Movies have always been about uplift.

We have The Imitation Game, with Benedict Cumberbatch as Alan Turing, famous for codecracking; and The Theory of Everything, with Eddie Redmayne as Stephen Hawking, famous for scientific genius combined with appalling physical disability; and Selma, with David Oyelowo as Martin Luther King, famous for freedom; and - though here the uplift is more controversial - American Sniper, with Bradley Cooper as Chris Kyle, famous for killing.

Even two of the other nominees might be described as biographically inflected; Birdman plays off the parallels between the fictitious career of its protagonist, formerly famous for acting superheroes, and the real career of Michael Keaton who plays him, while Boyhood is in part a meditation on the idea of biography, with its actors literally aging alongside the imaginary and not-at-all-famous people they portray.

Why the biopic should have crested this year is anybody's guess, but the genre itself is a venerable Hollywood tradition. The very first batch of Oscar nominees for Best Picture in 1929 included The Patriot, which, perhaps surprisingly, was not about George Washington or Abraham Lincoln, but about Paul I, the 18th-century Tsar of Russia who could hardly have been a household name, at least not in American households. Before long, however, both the studios and the academy were going more mainstream; nominated early talkies included Disraeli and The Private Life of Henry VIII, testifying to the industry's, and maybe the nation's, enduring belief that history is something that happens in England.

The success of the Turing and Hawking pictures certainly testifies to that; as also, in a bizarre way, does the fact that Selma, that most American of stories, has British actors playing both MLK and LBJ. Maybe it's some kind of nationalist backlash that has kept both Oyelowo and Tom Wilkinson from getting acting nominations, though both deserve them.

Both Cumberbatch and Redmayne are up for Best Actor, but of course they're Britons playing Britons. Cumberbatch is a biographical specialist. As well as playing Turing, he was Hawking (in a 2004 TV movie called Stephen Hawking) long before Redmayne was Hawking, and he was Julian Assange in the 2013 film The Fifth Estate. He's also played Vincent Van Gogh.

Two things can be guaranteed about any biographical or historical film: It will be attacked for getting facts wrong and defended on the grounds that drama necessitates the cutting of corners. The Imitation Game and The Theory of Everything have been especially vulnerable here, because they're both about scientists, and other scientists have been piling on, denouncing simplifications and falsifications. The simplifications are probably inevitable; science is a difficult movie subject because its practitioners must spend a lot of time talking about things the audience doesn't understand.

The experts have been especially scathing about The Imitation Game, even though - or perhaps because - on a lineby-line basis, it contains more technical dialogue than The Theory of Everything. It's also dialogue that should, on the face of it, be the more viscerally exciting. Hawking's discoveries about black holes weren't going to have any immediate effect on anybody's existence; Turing's breaking of the Nazis' war codes was literally a matter of life and death.

According to the film, he personally won the war. And yet it all rings false. It's written and shot like one of those stiff-upper British war movies that proliferated in the 1950s, only not as well. It grabs at cliches. It can't just present us with the moral dilemma of allowing Allied vessels to sink, so as not to tip off the enemy that they'd been deciphered; it has to give one of Turing's team a brother on one of the doomed ships and have him plead hysterically that it be spared. It has to drag in a historically unrelated spy plot and make a Blimpish caricature of Turing's unsympathetic superior.

It also, according to an article in The New York Review of Books, makes Turing himself far more of a socially clueless loner than he actually was, and more closeted a homosexual than he actually was. It's hard to believe that the latter subject could be considered taboo in a modern movie, so the alterations have to be put down to somebody's mistaken idea of what would seem dramatic. I don't think the filmmakers were trying to dumb anything down; I think they just couldn't help themselves. Even without the objections of the professionals, the science rings false because the character rings false.

The Theory of Everything starts with the advantage that the main character's life is as interesting as the science. That Hawking, who's now 71, has so triumphantly survived after being diagnosed with ALS and given two years to live, is phenomenal in itself.

Both Selma and The Imitation Game focus on specific crucial periods in their heroes' careers. That's the usual way of doing things, and a reasonable one. It goes back to The Story of Louis Pasteur and The Life of Emile Zola, formative back-to-back biopics (from 1936 and 1937 respectively), both starring Paul Muni, the Benedict Cumberbatch of his day, and both Oscar contenders. In '36, Muni won but the film didn't; in '37, it was the other way around. Between them, they set the image of the virtuous prestige picture. And both, despite their all-encompassing titles, concentrated on key moments: on Pasteur's triumph in persuading the world of the truth about microbes, on Zola's valiant advocacy in the Dreyfus case.

The Theory of Everything, though, gives us the whole life, because it's the endurance that's important; it belongs in the category of stuff that you couldn't make up, but that we know they didn't have to; therefore we accept it. Foreknowledge here is moving in itself; the film tears your heart out even in its lyrical earlier sequences when Hawking is courting his soon-to-be wife. It does follow the Muni movies in the sense of being a man-and-wife story, and in doing so, it skirts sentimentality. However, just when you think that Felicity Jones' radiantly sensible Jane Hawking is too self-sacrificing to be true, she develops wants and resentments. This is a love-and-marriage story that includes divorce.

The science is mostly pushed to one side (the movie's title is overreaching), but we believe what we're told about it because we believe the rest. There's a notorious tradition of actors getting prizes for playing characters with disabilities, the more severe the better, and by that yardstick, Redmayne should have it made, but he's remarkable anyway, communicating volumes through the smile around his eyes.

Were I an academy member, I might well vote for him. Though I might abstain altogether, to protest the fact that in this year of biopics, the best of them all has been shut out. Scientists may be hard subjects for the screen, but artists are impossible. Has there ever been a first-rate movie about a great author? (Shakespeare in Love doesn't count. It's a fantasy and it wears its artifice cheerfully on its sleeve.)

But there's one category of artist that's an exception: painters. And Mike Leigh's Mr. Turner was here to prove it. Watching a writer writing is about as exciting as - well, as watching paint dry. But you can watch a painter painting, especially if he applies the final strokes when the picture is actually being exhibited, as Turner does in the film and as, it's been attested, he did in life. You can show the finished works themselves, and reflect them in your own visual style, as Leigh does. You can recreate a society, and have it frame a central performance, like Timothy Spall's, that makes few concessions to loveability and none to victimhood. You can also sacrifice any chance of an Academy Award. I wouldn't say that Mr. Turner is too good for Hollywood, but I suspect that it's too honest.
