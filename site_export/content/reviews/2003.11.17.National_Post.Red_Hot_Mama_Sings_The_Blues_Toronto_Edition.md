COOKIN AT THE COOKERY

Bluma Appel Theatre, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - Alberta Hunter was 82 when she first appeared at the Cookery in Greenwich Village. She was resuming a career as a jazz, blues and cabaret performer, after a 20-year hiatus spent working as a nurse. At the time of her comeback, she was a wiry, bun-haired woman, wizened and wise-looking, who did not so much sing her songs as deliver them in a commanding rhythmic rasp.

In Cookin at the Cookery, subtitled The Music and Times of Alberta Hunter, the great survivor is portrayed by Jackie Richardson, who has a very powerful voice, is a considerable way short of 80, and could not reasonably be described as wiry; "ample" would be more like it. She is a warm, overflowing performer, while Hunter in her Cookery days, though certainly not ungenerous in her approach, was economical and kept her own counsel. Richardson might be a persuasive Bessie Smith or Pearl Bailey, maybe a Dinah Washington. But what she really does best, and does here, is Jackie Richardson.

Does this matter? I think it does, because Hunter is serious history. We think of Bessie Smith, the archetypal singer of classic blues, as a figure from some heroic age before the flood, but Alberta takes seniority even to her; she actually wrote Downhearted Blues, the song with which Bessie had her first hit record in 1923. In the '20s, Hunter was a part of the Chicago jazz scene, then of the wider New York entertainment scene; and she topped the decade off by going to Europe, playing opposite Paul Robeson in the first London production of Show Boat. She continued through the Second World War, and of course when she returned to singing in the 1970s, her age was a great part of her attraction.

All of these facts, except perhaps the last, are mentioned in Marion J. Caffey's script. (It would be straining courtesy to call it a play.) Caffey himself has successfully staged it around the United States, and he directs it here as well. Like most shows of its kind, it is jerry-built, awkwardly written and haphazardly researched. Most of it seems meant to represent Hunters opening night at the Cookery which, we are given to understand, she took as the cue for a full-scale bout of autobiography. Richardsons lack of resemblance to the aged Alberta is something of an issue, as is the failure of Dale F. Jordan's shiny split-level set to look anything like the actual Cookery, which was a cosy check-tablecloth sort of cafe. These objections would be pedantic if the script and production achieved firm compensating identities of their own, but they don't so they arent.

We flash back, a lot, to Albertas childhood in Memphis, where she was raised by a single mother whom Richardson -- somewhat confusingly -- plays herself. The young Alberta is done by Montego Glover, an American actress who is billed simply as Narrator but in fact is much more. We are told the two women remained close but the mother never once saw the daughter perform. Years later, during the Korean War while her daughter is off entertaining the troops, Mrs. Hunter dies. This sends Alberta into a breast-beating rendition of I've Got a Mind to Ramble, punctuated by cries of "these are really the blues." But this is cheating, since, for all the time mother and daughter have spent on stage together, we never get any real sense of their relationship. It's as if the song couldnt be trusted to make an impact on its own, but has to be artificially pumped up.

Albertas sexual abuse at the hands of a school friend's father occasions a melodramatic tete-a-tete but is then forgotten; her lesbianism is brought centre stage for two minutes and never referred to again. If these things aren't important to the drama, why bring them up in the first place? It feels like filler.

Much worse is the shows apparent ignorance of the music its supposed to be celebrating. When Alberta first arrives in Chicago (in 1917!) she lets off a couple of choruses of Swing Brother Swing, a number that dates, as its name suggests, from the 1930s; Billie Holiday sang it with Count Basie, who is even mentioned in the lyrics. Alberta then tells of singing a ballad, Two Cigarettes in the Dark, and getting booed by a jazz-hungry audience. Really they should have fallen down and worshiped her for her prophetic powers, for this song too dates from the '30s and sounds it. Hunter recorded it, at that much later period, in London. The show grabs at random items from her discography. But if the most impressive things about her were her longevity and versatility, then it's short-changing her by not keeping the times and genres distinct.

Hunter worked in Chicago with Louis Armstrong. That much is true. The show has the two of them performing the duet routine on When the Saints Go Marching In that Satch did with Danny Kaye in The Five Pennies, c. 1960. This has nothing to do with Alberta Hunter; its just generic showbiz, like most of the piece.

Here, though, critical schizophrenia sets in because, to tell the truth, the number is a riot. Glovers Louis is perfection -- the singing, the mugging, the spirit, everything -- and though she doesn't actually play the trumpet, we don't for a moment doubt that she could. With just a stoop and a cackle, she also becomes the living image of Barney Josephson, the venerable entrepreneur who brought Hunter back, and whose worshipful introductions of her and the other idols whom he booked into the Cookery often lasted nearly as long as the acts themselves. (In his younger days Josephson did more for racial integration than any other manager in the history of nightclubs. The show neglects to mention this, and it also -- more surprisingly --makes no reference to the film Remember My Name, to which Hunter contributed as both singer and composer and which did as much as the Cookery gig to re-establish her.) Glover doesnt have the strongest singing voice, but considering all her other talents it would be churlish to hold this against her. In the first half of the evening, she comes close to stealing the show.

The second half, however, finds Richardson taking such firm possession of St. Louis Blues that it hardly matters whom shes meant to be impersonating. Hunter at the Cookery was a suggestive imp; Richardson is a salacious steamroller. Nor can I imagine the real Alberta indulging her audience by having them clap along to Sweet Georgia Brown, though the theatre audience obviously enjoyed it.

Richardson is forced, by her own persona and the script's deficiencies, to play your stereotypical red hot mama, which Hunter wasn't. Indeed, for a production that makes a great show of decrying stereotypes, this one seems remarkably dependent on them. I wonder, for example, where it's documented that the supervisor who dismissed Hunter from her nursing job was both white and monstrously patronizing.

You get some of the authentic Alberta, more vinegar than syrup, in her appeal to her club audiences to remember to call their mothers. In general, though, I suspect that the less you know about the subject, the more you will like the show.
