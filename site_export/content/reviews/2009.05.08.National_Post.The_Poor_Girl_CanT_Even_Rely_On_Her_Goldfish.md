I, CLAUDIA

Young Centre for the Performing Arts

---

Re-encountering Kristen Thomson in I, Claudia is like welcoming back an old friend. Or a gaggle of old friends, as behind her masks Thomson impersonates four separate characters.

Claudia herself, the 12-and-a-half-year-old child of a recent divorce, is of course the centrepiece, and must by now be the best-loved character in Canadian drama; a perceptible sigh of affectionate recognition goes round the auditorium at the first glimpse of those preternaturally wide eyes that seem simultaneously to accuse and beseech. But we also get to meet Drachman, the Central European showman turned Canadian school janitor who acts as master of ceremonies; Doug, the father of Claudia's father; and Leslie, the woman who has supplanted Claudia's mother.

Drachman, from the imaginary country of Bulgonia, remains the least satisfactory character; it seems that Bulgonians speak with an impenetrable accent, and anyway I'm not persuaded that the play needs Drachman's toy theatre with its red traverse curtain in order to establish its performing convention -- though, given that it does exist, Chris Abraham's production makes the discreet most of it. The school basement is Claudia's refuge, her shelter from a bullying world; the implication that its custodian is the only person who really understands her, as one outsider responding to another, is touching but vague.

Doug, Thomson's other male impersonation, is more successful and seems to have grown richer down the years. (We first saw the play in 2001.) I certainly don't remember being as struck before by his battle with a recalcitrant piece of candy, a struggle that has him complaining "I never saw a wrapper so in love with its lozenge" -- one of the many lines in the piece that sends laughter crashing through the theatre in waves before subsiding into sadness. Doug seems to disapprove of his son's new choice, and he's fond of Claudia, but he can't do much to help her. His new daughter-in-law has more in common with him here than he might acknowledge. Leslie has two scenes, and the first of them, this time around, is a bit of a blur. She comes into her own in her second, being fitted impatiently for her wedding dress, fearful that her great day will be spoiled, anxious about what kind of impression she will make, what kind of wife, and what kind of stepmother. She is currently, as she fears and as Claudia herself makes clear, the person Claudia hates most in the world.

She once suggested that she and Claudia should be girlfriends, to which Claudia's unspoken response -- relayed to us with all the scorn of immediate pre-adolescence -- was "Leslie, just look at yourself." Her dad had first introduced Leslie as "a special friend," on which Claudia's comment is: "I hate to be precocious, but I know a euphemism when I see one." The most legendary of the play's many devastating lines, this both demonstrates and describes Thomson's ear for believable exaggeration, applied to an age group at which exaggeration is the norm.

A lot of things go wrong for Claudia, quite apart from her situation at home. She's picked on by the smart girls at school; her pet goldfish, objects of both her love and her scientific curiosity, die on her. Her parents are, with wonderful tact, kept offstage, but we get to know them, at least through her eyes. We share in her possessive pride over her prescribed outings with her dad, treats that make her feel both grown-up and sad; we're torn up when she speaks fiercely and tearfully of her mother as the nicest person in the whole world, and of the token that she placed under her mother's pillow in the hopes it would bring about a reconciliation. When she tells about her dream of how the new wedding day should have turned out, we're totally, as I think she'd say, swept away in it: more exhilaratingly than we were when the play was first done. Claudia emerges as a brave and even noble figure, as well as a sad and funny one. (And really there's no need to feel too terrible about her long-range prospects; we know that Thomson's own parents divorced, and she hasn't turned out too badly.)

Thomson has since proved with The Patient Hour that she can write for other actors as well as for herself, and that she isn't limited to monologues. We also know that she's a fine actress in more conventional circumstances. Her Claudia, though, is unlike anything else she has done; maybe unlike anything anyone has done. It's both carefully distanced and shatteringly immediate. To watch this play and this performance is to adopt Claudia as your own child; you feel protective and you feel proud. - I, Claudia runs until May 23.
