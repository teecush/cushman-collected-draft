A DELICATE BALANCE

Tom Patterson Theatre, Stratford, Ont.

---

The ambience could hardly be more different, but A Delicate Balance, directed by Diana Leblanc, is the Mary Poppins of productions: practically perfect in every way.

Edward Albee's 1966 play is the counterweight to his earlier and more celebrated Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf. The older play raises its curtain on a perpetually and bitterly battling married couple and shows by the end that, though nobody would describe theirs as a good marriage, they will survive together. A Delicate Balance begins with a couple who seem to have everything, and leads them, wittily and remorselessly, into despair. Of the two plays, I think it wears better. It's certainly the more economical.

Here are Tobias and Agnes, a prosperous pair of old-middle-aged WASPs. Tobias has inherited wealth (and some unseen servants), though he does seem troubled by taxes. Agnes runs his life for him. Agnes' younger sister Claire lives with them, and is an alcoholic by most people's definition, a mere drunk by her own. (Alcoholics, she says, "couldn't help it; I could, and wouldn't." Actually, nearly everyone in this play has a marked talent for putting it away; it seems to be a mark of caste.)

Julia, the couple's daughter, is on her fourth marriage and she now returns home in flight from it, just as she has done from the previous three. On this occasion, though, she is preceded, indeed pre-empted, by Harry and Edna, her parents' best friends, who have come down with a severe case of existential angst. Driven from their house by a nameless terror, they seek--practically demand -- refuge with Tobias and Agnes. They are not welcome, though it seems they cannot be dislodged.

Agnes especially dreads their presence. She begins the play with an elegant meditation on the possibility, which she seems to regard as a near certainty, that she will someday lose her mind. Her guests may be bringing that day closer. Their terror (Agnes calls it a plague) could be catching. And the two marriages, it seems, are very much alike; both have been marked by a progressive loss of intimacy. We gather that there was once a summer in which both husbands were unfaithful with Claire.

Skeletons get rattled, including that of Tobias's and Agnes's son who died in childhood; this is Albee's perpetual lost-boy motif (an adoptee himself, he even has the gall to name the kid Teddy) but it's more skillfully integrated than in Virginia Woolf, where it's a last-act device to keep the party going. If Agnes is abrasive, Tobias is seemingly placid, doing his best to be everybody's friend. Agnes goads him to take a stand, to stop withdrawing, which is what, in the literal sexual sense among others, he has been doing for years.

The six Stratford performers, individually and in battle formation, are about as good as anything gets. Leblanc orchestrates them on a drawing-room set by Astrid Janson that's complete and comfortable, save in one signficant detail: a carpet that juts off both sides of the platform sides, right into space. It's symmetrical, which is reassuring, but it's also disconcerting. We're not used to having the scenery exceed its prescribed boundaries in this way. It perfectly figures the precariously delicate balance that Agnes in particular is desperate to maintain.

The sensation of keeping chaos at bay is brilliantly evoked by Martha Henry, who weaves her way through the complex Henry Jamesian periods in which Agnes expresses herself with a patrician confidence terrified of letting its guard down. Close-cropped and as elegant as her surroundings, this Agnes never relaxes. Even when she descends to one-liners, her wit freezes. Fiona Reid's Claire, by contrast, crackles; she is the self-licensed jester, fastening on everybody's weaknesses and especially her own. Alone in this menage, she shows few signs of suffering, but she's a buccaneer of the wisecrack. Michelle Giroux' superbrat Julia is very much one of the family. She arrives to find that the intruders have taken over her room, so her fears and resentments get channelled into territorial warfare. The most strategic site in the room is, naturally, the handsomely pannelled bar. When the visitors offer to pour the drinks, both sides know it to be an act of conquest and when Julia, arms outstretched, rushes to prevent it, we have a scene of high tragic farce.

The central figure, though, the one on whom the pressures converge, is Tobias, whom David Fox keeps as amiable as possible for as long as possible. His mask slips quite early, in a self- lacerating speech about a pet cat whom he felt as a boy no longer loved him, and for whose death he now blames himself. Plainly, he feels the same loss, and the same guilt, in his marriage. But even this he delivers with a shambling grace, letting himself off the hook even as he baits it. Only at the end does Fox release the danger, shouting at the unwelcome Harry that he wants him to stay. Maybe he's doing this, as he protests (too much), from friendship; maybe it's to defy Agnes, who wants the opposite; maybe it's all furious sarcasm, geniality raised to the power of a threat. Or maybe Tobias, whose Biblical namesake gave shelter to an angel with gratifying results, wants to catch the plague -- he practically says "bring it on." It could be a blessing. Harry and Edna might be like the uninvited guests in a T. S. Eliot play, furies or guardians of mercy, depending on how you treat them.

James Blendick's Harry, describing the terror in slow, honey- sweet tones, leaves no doubt of its reality; Patricia Collins' Edna breaks down in pitiable tears. At times, just as believably, he's Tobias' good country club buddy, and she's a strict-but-fair godmother, slapping Julia for her own good. And at others, they treat the place as if they own it and have a right to. It's very funny, in every sense. They come and go and return and leave. It's as if they've held a mirror up to Agnes and Tobias, and the image has been rejected. Tobias returns to apologizing, Agnes to meditating on madness, and they and their realtives to their matching solitudes. If it's possible for a play and a production to be both lucid and mysterious, that is the achievement here. - A Delicate Balance is in repertoire until September 28.
