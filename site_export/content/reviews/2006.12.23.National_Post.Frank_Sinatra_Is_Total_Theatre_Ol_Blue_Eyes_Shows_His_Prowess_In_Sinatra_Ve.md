Duke Ellington once described Frank Sinatra as "total theatre. The truth of that is apparent in the new box set Sinatra Vegas (Reprise, $94.99), four CDs and a DVD of live performances at an assortment of desert hot spots between 1961 and 1987. I don't know if it's true to say that Sinatra was better" in front of an audience than he was in a recording studio; theres a level of perfectionism on his best records that was obviously unattainable when he was winging it. Nor did he necessarily inhabit songs -- the thing he did better than anyone else -- more completely when he was playing to a crowd. The truth is perhaps the other way around. The inwardness that distinguishes his studio ballad performances is recreated -- immediately, at the drop of a downbeat -- in the middle of what would normally be a clattery, rowdy room. Studio Sinatra is already intense drama; add visuals and live response, and you do indeed have theatre, exhibited within each song in purest, concentrated form.

You have that theatrical essential, contrast: not only between numbers but within them. Sinatra, increasingly, it seems, as he got older, breaks down the traditional saloon singers distinction between "ballads and swingers." The first song on the DVD (a performance at Caesars Palace in 1978) starts, conventionally enough, with All of Me, an up-tempo song he had recorded back in 1954, and that he continued to sing in the same Nelson Riddle arrangement. (Part of the interest in these discs is in noting how many songs that Sinatra never revisited in the studio nonetheless continued to slip in and out of his club and concert repertoire. One wonders how, with so much to choose from, he went about selecting them.) Just hearing him break, apparently out of nowhere, into that curt percussive opening phrase -- "All of me -- is exhilarating enough. Then you notice that on the second line -- can't you see I'm no good without you" -- though the pace remains the same, the tone has changed; its now sensitive going on vulnerable, a transition thats consummated by the time he gets to "your goodbye left me with eyes that cry near the end of the song. A plain, even maudlin lyric is not so much transformed as redeemed; the feelings behind it are laid bare but never lingered over; the overall mood remains driving and slangy.

The contrasts within the song mirror in microcosm the conflicts within the performer, between the impeccable singer and the extremely peccable talker. If, as a singer, Sinatra is a great actor, as a comedian and raconteur he's somewhere between mediocre and crass. His monologues here mainly consist of heavy-handed name- dropping, and to a passing but obviously heartfelt tribute to the great songwriters: I wonder what we would have done without Rodgers and Hart?"

Not much, apparently. These discs are loaded with R&H songs, including the perennial Lady Is a Tramp, a number that seems to be one of everybody's Sinatra favourites except mine. This is one song on which I find him too self- aggrandizingly flip, too out of touch with the original. Again, though, the DVD has the best of it; this time, when he changes the lyric to "thats why this chick is a champ, he injects some affection into the swagger. And when he gets to the really tender songs, the effect, visual as well as aural, can be extraordinary. George Harrisons Something is a song about not being able to find words; hence its instrumental bridge. When Sinatra sings it here, elaborating on what was already a majestic performance on record, his hands perform a fierce ballet of frustration while his voice crests. There's something in the way she moves but he can't describe it, and though that only makes him love her more, hes taking a beating from the struggle to articulate it.

Theres more than a hint of anger, one that surfaces again in his remarkable piano-accompanied performance of Send in the Clowns, anger turned inward: Losing my timing this late in my career." Some chance. Physically he seems, at the age of 68, in very good shape. The voice, by this date, is grainy and craggy, a weathered effect that, for the most part, he knows exactly how to exploit. And yet it can sometimes shed decades, as in Someone to Watch over Me, with its magically ingenuous verse. Every time, as soon as the music starts, the crudity of the Sinatra image falls away, and though we certainly remain aware of the man and his legend, he himself seems concerned only with the song.

His commitment to the individual value of each number may explain why he sang so few medleys: only two that I'm aware of in the whole of his recorded works. One of them turns up here, and it's a splicing together of two long existing Riddle arrangements: songs from disparate sources that fit together with seamless narrative logic. The Gal that Got Away -- the Judy Garland classic, sounding, with its gender reversed and its dynamics reduced, even more lost and harrowing than when she sang it -- is interrupted halfway through by a persuasive backstory in the form of It Never Entered My Mind: Rodgers and Hart again, and their masterpiece. Sinatras sensitivity on this date even extends to the inevitable My Way. "I hate this song, he mutters as he goes into it, a statement that cheered me enormously. He then proceeds to prove us both wrong, at least temporarily, by phrasing the thing with amazing subtlety. He actually understates it; bombast is left until the final stanza, and even then most of it is left to the orchestra.

Ive dwelt on the DVD because there is -- officially, at least -- very little visual evidence available of Sinatra in performance. The four CDs, though, all have their share of revelations. The several tunes that recur from one disc to another show Sinatra's claim never to have sung a song the same way twice to be true. Disc 1 (The Sands, 1961) gives us the singer in his absolute Kennedy-era prime, about a year into running his own record company. There are joyous versions of two numbers from his band- singing days with Tommy Dorsey: The One I Love Belongs to Somebody Else and Without a Song, the latter another example of his doing the lyricists a favour by uncovering the emotion, a kind of awed happiness, in whose direction they were rather vaguely pointing. It also includes Just One of Those Things, reclaimed for heartbreak; Billy May's uproarious arrangement of On the Road to Mandalay; Moonlight in Vermont, with some phrasing thats daring in concept and prodigious in execution; and You Make Me Feel so Young, which, when he sings it, is my favourite song.

Disc 2 (The Sands, 1966) is a kind of trial run for the canonical Sinatra at the Sands album. Hes with the same great band, Count Basie's, doing almost exactly the same program; One for My Baby has gone missing, and indeed is absent from the entire box, even though it's generally and justly regarded as the quintessential Sinatra saloon song, and therefore the quintessential Sinatra live performance. That apart, snobs of the alternate take, of which the disc is one long example, will seize on it and pronounce it superior to the authorized version: rightly, I think. A bonus track has Sinatra talking about working with Basie: It was like a juggernaut. When the orchestra started and it came at you, you knew you had to be part of that or you got lost. I tried to stay in the realm of what the orchestra was playing I hung back just a little bit, I guess thats the way to say it." Sinatra talking about his own music is always illuminating; a shame he did it so seldom.

Disc 3 (Caesars Palace, 1982) brings a band without strings, and rethought versions of some Sinatra standbys: I Cant Get Started, in which he manages to sound quizzical, world-weary and heartsore all in one line, and Night and Day sung to quintet arrangement with, one imagines, furrowed brow and certainly with furrowed voice. Yearning is what he does best; more and more in these performances, the ageing Sinatra seems to be singing about the unattainable. His voice goes astray on some long notes at the end of These Foolish Things, and it doesn't matter.

Disc 4 (The Golden Nugget, 1987) begins with I've Got the World on a String, the trumpet intro to Riddles 1953 arrangement still unmistakable, and takes in yet more R&H; a deep, fragile Spring Is Here, and My Heart Stood Still with a big climax that he takes full-throatedly, though admittedly its the orchestra that does the heaviest lifting. There's also Hoagy Carmichael's I Get Along without You Very Well, whose haunted first line should give you goosebumps and which he typically introduces as "a song about a big liar."

Much in the Vegas ethos and Sinatra's embracing of it, such as his stated distrust of people who didn't drink, sticks in the craw now. (It did then.) But when he sings, he transforms it and himself. There have been many fine popular singers but no other who has operated at, or maybe even aspired to, this level as an artist.
