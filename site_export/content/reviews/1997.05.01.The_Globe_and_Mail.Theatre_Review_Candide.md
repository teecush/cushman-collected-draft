CANDIDE

Theatre Review

CANDIDE

Thursday, May 1, 1997

Directed by Harold Prince

New York City -- Written by Leonard Bernstein, Hugh Wheeler and Richard Wilbur


  


Starring Jim Dale, Andrea Martin, Harolyn Blackwell and Jason Daniely


  


Rating: ***½

Plenty of cause for gratitude

THEATRE REVIEW / Inspired by Voltaires satire,

Leonard Bernstein first created this Broadway comic opera

in 1956. More than 40 years later, Harold Prince's revision

balances spectacle, humour and music.

Special to The Globe and Mail

LEONARD Bernstein composed Candide as a Broadway comic opera, and ever since the piece's premiere in 1956 the constituent parts of that definition have been slugging it out. No production is ever likely to get the balance of music, laughter and spectacle completely right, but Harold Princes new staging comes closer than any I have seen or heard of. It looks and sounds wonderful.

The shows source is of course the 18th-century satiric novella by Voltaire, the story of the high-born innocents Candide and Cunegonde who are told by their beloved tutor, Dr. Pangloss, that all is for the best in this best of all possible worlds and who go on believing him through earthquakes, wars, inquisitions, multitudinous ravishments and endless chicanery. Finally, experience gets the better of them and they settle for the more practical and limited philosophy that Voltaire summed up as cultivating one's own garden and that Bernstein hymns in the soaring finale anthem Make Our Garden Grow . The cast's massed voices, largely unaccompanied, ring gloriously here; and the stage looks like a sunburst.

Radiance is a feature of Clarke Durhams sets throughout, much enhanced by Ken Billingtons lighting and matched by Judith Dolan's richly frolicsome costumes. The designs -- which, having enveloped the stage as far as the orchestra pit, extend their arms to fondle part of the auditorium as well -- are a rich old storybook come to life, revelling in rococo, occasionally going for baroque. The dazzle persists through some of the story's darkest episodes, but it can also abruptly darken. An auto-da-fé sequence that starts as black knockabout comedy suddenly goes quietly and authentically grim.

The production -- the first Livent production to start life in the United States rather than in Canada -- is both an expansion and a refinement of one that Prince first staged on Broadway in 1973 and has been revising in opera houses ever since. The 1973 treatment turned Candide from a classic flop destime into a commercial hit. It jettisoned Lillian Hellmans script -- which hewed as far as possible to the structure of the mid-century American well-made play -- and commissioned a new book from Hugh Wheeler far closer in form to the picaresque original. It stressed informality and fun, seating the spectators amid the action in an audience-friendly staging of which traces still remain. It also shredded the score, leaving out whole numbers and drastically reducing the sumptuously witty orchestrations. Since then, Prince and his colleagues have been putting back the music, and now in New York we have virtually the original score, though not in the original order.

Wheeler's revision has itself been revised (by uncredited hands -- Wheeler himself is dead); but it is perhaps a judgment on the show that it can be so readily rearranged. "Picaresque" on the stage has a way of translating into episodic and the most common criticism of the piece has always been that it goes on making the same point in different locations. Prince's light-handed direction certainly reins in the didacticism but in doing so it goes to the opposite extreme. We frequently lose the moral compass altogether. In this tale of innocents abroad we get plenty of abroad -- as the story ranges from Germany to Lisbon to Buenos Aires to the Amazon jungle to Venice -- but not much innocence. Its missing in Candide, and in Cunegonde.

Well, not so much in Candide. Jason Danielys performance is the first I have seen to justify the show's title. He sings like an especially agreeable bird, plays charmingly with the audience, and is a picture of tousle-haired integrity. But the script increasingly takes his idealism for granted, and his conversion doesn't mean much because there is -- a really crucial lack in this text -- no intervening period of disillusion. Harolyn Blackwell as Cunegonde sings even more spectacularly, especially in Glitter and Be Gay , a spoof of the Jewel Song from Faust with an amazing cascade of trills. (Youve heard of coloratura, but this is ridiculous.) The number is, as it should be, a tour-de-force; but theres something wrong with it. It's supposed to be about the battle in Cunegonde, the self-consciously wronged heroine, between outraged virtue and gratified greed. Each side gets half the song, in strict alternation, but she keeps anticipating the transitions; finally she stops playing them altogether. Blackwell doesn't act as well as she sings but the script and direction dont help her; this Cunegonde isnt allowed to be virginal even when she's a virgin. The character's basic joke has gone.

Still, there is that voice; and if the shows horticultural moral can be paraphrased as "take what you can and be grateful," this show gives plenty of cause for gratitude. It is funny and, perhaps more important, consistently and contagiously good-humoured.

Jim Dale, narrating as Voltaire and playing Pangloss and numerous others, is a splendidly laid-back compère, with the art that conceals energy. Andrea Martin rampages effusively as Cunegondes abused but resilient duenna . She shares the ingratiatingly hilarious tango I Am Easily Assimilated (Bernstein's own lyrics) with a troupe of superannuated caballeros , who keep falling down before her charms and then inconsiderately getting up again. Patricia Birch has choreographed them delightfully, as she has done the whole show; there always seems to be something surprising going on. The score, one of the great achievements of American music, is buoyantly conducted by Eric Stern.

The original 1956 lyrics, pointed and urbane, were the work of the poet Richard Wilbur. They were supplemented in 1973 by Stephen Sondheim who has supplied extra material this time round. (Perhaps it's as well that we dont know which lyricist to hold responsible for rhyming hopeful with "Constantinople.") So the show marks the Broadway reunion, after 15 years, of Prince and Sondheim, one of the theatres great teams. For Prince, it's a golden production; the stage goes through a whole palette of tones -- when the actors arrive in a cart, drawn by pantomime horses, to start things off, it seems at once rowdy and autumnal but it's that final sunburst that seems emblematic. No other director of musicals marshals actors and design so richly; no other uses colour so well. In some ways this Candide is like the Masquerade number in The Phantom of the Opera ,expanded to the dimensions of a carnival -- and what's more, an intelligent carnival. This is Broadway's greatest showman at his zenith.
