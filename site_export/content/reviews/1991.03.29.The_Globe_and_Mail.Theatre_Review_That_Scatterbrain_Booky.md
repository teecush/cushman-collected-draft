THEATRE REVIEW" THAT SCATTERBRAIN BOOKY

Friday, March 29, 1991

ROBERT CUSHMAN

WRITTEN BY JOEY MILLER Directed by Peter Moss Starring Trish Lindstrom, Mary Ann MacDonald Young People's Theatre, Toronto A fine collection of 1930s songs and some nifty choreography are not enough to make this trip to Depression-era Toronto work Weak story line hurts nostalgic tale BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail That Scatterbrain Booky is a nostalgic trip back to the Toronto of the 1930s when things were really bad, though naturally the politicians tried to deny it. "Prime Minister Bennett," goes one ironic line, "says it's only a recession." Hollow laughter in the house, much of which, I fancy, will never have heard of former prime minister R. B. Bennett.

If the target audience of the YPT will have no memory of these events, neither will their parents. In fact "nostalgic" is quite the wrong word, and I was seduced into using it by Joey Miller's songs, which it would be churlish to describe as pastiche. They are, with a couple of folksy exceptions, as good and musicianly a bunch of songs of the thirties as anyone is likely to write in the nineties. Sprucely orchestrated and played by a band (musical director, Boko Suzuki) that sounds much bigger than it is, it also sets off some nifty choreography. For his first trick William Orlowski fashions a tap routine out of hopscotch - not such a stretch, perhaps, but still done with rousing precision - and if he never quite tops that, he still keeps the show, and his mainly juvenile cast, hopping. This is a kids' show - one that may teach its audiences a lot, both historically and emotionally - but it also demands to be judged as a full-grown musical.

Here it runs into trouble. Miller has written the book as well as the score, and his script does not have a central dynamic. The title refers to a 12-year-old girl, the third child of a poverty-stricken family. (A fourth is on the way as the show starts.) Father is unemployed but is too proud to tolerate his wife or children looking for work. Mother is a beleaguered rock. Elder sister has a Jewish sweetheart, whose range of interests - "books and boxing" - are oddly reminiscent of the Golden Boy of Clifford Odets. The bailiff is constantly battering at the door. Eventually they are evicted. They relocate, just managing to avoid the lowest depths. ("Cabbagetown - that's where the real down-and-outs end up.") So a fair amount happens. But none of it counts for very much. A lot of the blame probably lies with the source material. The show is based on a trilogy of books by Bernice Thurman Hunter, about her own childhood. Booky is her. At one point in the show she tangles with the school authorities, but she does little else to earn the adjective bestowed on her in the title. In fact she shows no positive qualities of any kind. She is not a person who makes things happen, which is the best qualification for being the centre of a show. She is not even the one they happen to, which is the second-best, though perilous. She is the one who watches things happen, and describes it all for us. In fact she is that great scourge of autobiographical drama: She Is A Camera.

To make it worse, she is a mini-camera. What I am about to say may constitute cruelty to children, but there is no help for it. Trish Lindstrom, the 11-year-old who plays Booky, cannot carry the weight. Her singing - of which she has to do a lot - is not clear or true enough; her timing and delivery are muddy. We rely on her to tell us what is going on, and what is going on is often obscure.

Even when we see or hear it for ourselves there is a curious lack of urgency about it. Family rows, fuelled by desperation and at one point ascending to violence, keep going off half-cocked. Neil Foster's Irish dad is a resolutely soft-grained underpowered performance. The staging, in Peter Moss's production, may not help. Leslie Frankish's set - a collection of front-stoops - is impeccably evocative of time and space but it is less helpful to action. Booky's clan seem to be taking all their meals alfresco, in a small square in the middle of the stage. It isn't spacious, but it isn't helpfully constricted either. It merely looks cramped and uncomfortable and so does the acting.

Mary Ann McDonald as Mother does get a chance to take off when she sings The Palais Royale, a glowing number about the dancehalls of her youth. But it's wrong. For one thing, she's singing about 1922 and the music remains resolutely 1930s; for another the song comes at the end of the first half after we have been conditioned to think of singing as something that is done by childhood. That seems a very useful convention - it sets them off in a world of their own - and breaking it is fatal.

There is a Halloween sequence which is well staged and designed without quite shaking off memories of Meet Me in St. Louis. The domestic tensions and the adolescent rites of passage never stop recalling Neil Simon's Brighton Beach Memoirs, dramatic memories of the same period which had the great advantage of not being based on a book. Writing music Joey Miller seems at home in the past but still his own man. Between songs he is thrashing about, looking for a story.
