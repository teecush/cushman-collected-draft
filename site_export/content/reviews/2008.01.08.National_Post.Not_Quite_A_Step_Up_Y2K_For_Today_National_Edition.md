You might guess that a jPod would be the next thing up from an iPod.

Guess again.

In Douglas Coupland's new TV series, based on his novel, jPod is the name given to a haphazard team of video-game designers all herded together during the Y2K computer panic (remember that?) because all their surnames began with the letter J. So we have a show whose premise is a pun, if that. It may have seemed delightfully inconsequential on the page. On the screen it's just silly.

I mean, by my simple arithmetic the show's co-workers, employees of a huge Vancouver company called Neotronic Arts, have been herded now for seven years, time enough for even the most intransigent organization to have acknowledged that its computer had bungled and to have done something to put it right.

The show, at least on the strength of tonight's first episode, makes nothing of its characters' shared initial, maybe because there's nothing that could be made. It just seems a too-cute way of getting together a variegated bunch of employees for another workplace comedy. At the centre of it is Evan Jarlewski (David Kopp), the team's "gore specialist," which involves finding new ways of putting "carnage and violence into video games while conforming to industry quality codes."

You might think that this job description would make him quite cutting edge but it seems that video violence in and of itself is, well, so 20th century. This at least is what I gleaned yesterday morning from hearing Coupland himself, who talks very engagingly about his own creations (as about most other things), discuss the matter in a radio interview with Shelagh Rogers. According to Coupland, Evan is the repository of traditional moral values in a world hell-bent on shedding and shredding them. And he does seem innocent, or at least charming, compared to his immediate colleagues: Casper Jesperson, self-renamed Cowboy, a Yalie obsessed with sex and death (especially from lung cancer) and obnoxiously proud of his physique; John Doe, who commits the serious crime of "bringing extra-buttery popcorn into the work environment"; Brianna Maria Jyang, known as Bree, who's a control freak; and Kaitlin Joyce, who seems to be a new arrival (once the computer's glitched, it stays glitched) and who wants out ASAP.

So there they are, rubbing along well enough as far as we can tell, when -- like every team in every TV office including The Office, not to mention 30 Rock -- they get saddled with a new supervisor who doesn't understand.

This one is more a Steve Carrell loser than an Alec Baldwin winner. He is Steve Lefkowitz, "VPinCharge of Vision," and without, as far as I can tell, a J to either of his names, which must prove that there's one rule for the bosses. At an initial departmental meeting (the episode's highlight), Steve proposes gentling up the department's latest product by incorporating an animal into it.

He suggests a turtle and the entire crew go on a turtle hunt. Steve also thinks the new game will benefit from having an X at the end of its name that will automatically "make it way cooler." Let's hear it for the author of Generation X, making sporting fun of the book that made him famous.

Let's also add this up: Evan, the gore specialist, is standing up for old-fashioned gentleness against a man who loves turtles. Well, who doesn't love a paradox?

Evan faces similar contradictions in his private life. His father is an ex-engineer turned actor whose new dream is to speak at least one line in a TV show. (He's played by Alan Thicke, who in his time has spoken many.) He's also conducting a halfhearted affair with a school contemporary of his son's whose repeated insistence that she was popular are the show's low point.

Mother, meanwhile, prides herself on being a middle-class lady who grows very good pot; and where on current TV have we heard something like that before. More excitingly, she kills a guy who looks like he's intruding on her operation; and she and an unwilling Evan spend much of the show trying to dispose of what the specialist in imaginary bloodletting calls " a real live dead body." He also says "if it was too easy to get rid of bodies, we'd run out of people; as a species, we're a disaster." I'm not denying that there are good lines.

But it's late in the day to make comic capital out of people acting cavalier over little things like murder. Black comedy has been around too long to be shocking, especially when the whole context is weightless. We are promised future instalments in which the characters are faced with "Chinese gangs ... sexual swingin' ... British royalty," but on the evidence so far this is another show acting cool over people who think they're acting cool.

Add it to the bulging list that we call Irony, a once noble concept that has come to mean what we have in place of humour. This particular specimen is amiable, but so far that's all. - jPod airs tonight at 9 p.m. on CBC.
