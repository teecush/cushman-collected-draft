TILLSONBURG

Berkeley Street Theatre

- - -

One of the attractive things about the new crop of Irish plays is that things happen in them: violent, exciting things. Sometimes, as in The Beauty Queen of Leenane, these incidents link up, pass through the characters and become a story; and the result is enthralling.

In Tillsonburg, a first play by Malachy McKenna, the events are scattered, rather than organic; there is liveliness but no real life. And it doesn't tell a story. It has three story-lines (not exactly the same thing) that have no reason other than geographical proximity to be occupying the same stage. Like a good sitcom, it is never quite boring, but never quite interesting either.

It has turned up in Toronto ahead of other highly-touted Irish (or English, or American) work for reasons of local pride; it is probably the first Irish play to be set in Canada. The author spent several summers as a tobacco-harvester in Southern Ontario, and those back-breaking, but companionable seasons on the farm were the inspiration for this play: that and a song called Tillsonburg by Stompin Tom Connors, which gives the author both a theme-tune and a theme.

McKenna seems to have split himself between two young Irishmen: Mac, who is a student, and Digger, who isnt. These two, pals since boyhood, come to work for Jon, who is in both personal and economic straits. Their only co-picker is Billy, who smokes a lot of stuff that isn't tobacco and is going crazy with it.

Mac and Digger get the lions' share of the stage-time. Both drink a lot, talk about girls a lot, phone their mothers and tell excellent jokes. They behave, in fact, like engaging young Irish buckos are expected to behave, at least on stage. (What with the rural setting and the ethnic humour, it occurred to me that the play might have been called The Paddy Fields.) They are also differentiated, to the extent that Mac is chunky and shy, while Digger is lanky and gregarious, though the physical differences may just be a matter of casting, and the pyschological ones have to be taken largely on trust.

In Act One we are told that this is their second joint visit to North America, the first having been a messy trip to New York. In Act Two we learn, far too late, about the traumatic events that befell them there; once again an author tries to give his characters a present by delving into their past. If he hasnt brought them alive first, this is self-defeating. The relationship is resolved in a burst of Boys Own Paper heroics.

Paul Essiembre gives a sturdy performance as Mac, and the author himself plays Digger; he has dealt himself some good lines and he deals them out again with full appreciation. There is, though, a disconcerting remoteness about his performance; he seems to go dead behind the eyes when he isn't talking.

Their employer is a man gamely defeated, and excellently played as such by David Ferry. (He says "eh?" with an authority that brings the Oldest Canadian Joke quivering back into life.) He has the beginning and the end of the play to himself, which suggests that it is all meant to be about him, but nothing that comes between really justifies it.

Jon is taking a battering from the weather, from the economy, and from his wife; she doesn't appear, having left him for a menacing figure known as Pete the Indian, whom we do meet and who seems to have stepped out of the pages of Mark Twain. Lawrence Bayne as Pete is called on to do little more than loom, which he does, effectively.

Pete also has a hold on Billy, whom he supplies with drugs and who fears and admires him in about equal proportions. Paul Fauteux is excellent value as Billy, whom he plays as a tousled, strung-out innocent, saying the first thing that comes into his head and wandering naked over to his co-workers bunks to discourse on the difference between the alluring graphics on a package of frozen burgers and the nasty grey stuff inside, and on the value of this as a metaphor for life.

That is about as deep as the play gets, though it does seem to have a submerged theme about fear of homosexuality, and a spasmodic one about territorial appropriation. Pete the Indian gets downright murderous about this, though it seems strange that he should choose a couple of Irish transients as his targets. But then there is nobody else available at the time. There is an attempt to tie all the narrative-threads together with melodrama, which is atavistically appealing; there may be only one Indian onstage, but the whites still get their wagons in a circle. At the end, there are the usual semi-regretful farewells.

The material is good enough for us to enjoy watching the actors acting, and Miles Potters production gives both text and performance plenty of rope. It also includes a terrific storm. Citizens of the real Tillsonburg are coming in to see the play on a gala weekend and this seems appropriate as well as admirable; the play gives the impression that the author selected a setting and then set about collecting incidents to fill it.
