Two shows in from the East Coast, both of a quality high enough to discourage Toronto from feeling smug about itself. On the other hand, Toronto may be entitled to ask why it always gets to see these things last. One of these two productions has already toured to Australia.

Revisited, which to date is going only as far as Germany, comes to us from the 2b theatre company of Halifax, as an indigenous contribution to the WorldStage season. Its audience (28 max) sits around a large table in a small room; the table is the stage though the actors, all two of them, mostly stand at its sides (and ours) and lean into it. The title is double-edged. The piece started out as a revisiting, or reduction, of Thornton Wilder's Our Town, itself a play about revisiting the past, but was prevented by Wilders estate from hewing too closely to the original material. So the troupe reworked their text (make that triple-edged) and supplemented it with references from other chroniclers and celebrants of small- town America: Sherwood Anderson, Willa Cather, Edgar Lee Masters (he of Spoon River Anthology) and, somewhat outclassing the others, Walt Whitman.

Its still very much Our Town. We still have a narrator guiding us through a day in the lives though this one, unlike Wilder's Stage Manager, gets to impersonate the people he describes. The focus is narrower. We concentrate on the young couple, here renamed Tom and Lucy, whom we follow from courtship to marriage to the bride's early death. We also have her wish to, yes, revisit just one day from her past, and her heartbreaking realization that we never value the stuff of our existence while its happening. There is some reference to the vast pitiless framework of time and space, and to the Great War after which the town and the country and the world will never be the same. There is a sterner concentration (because, in an hour- long show, so much else has to be left out) on the disturbing character of the towns lonely, alcoholic and ultimately suicidal music teacher. One element that isn't in Wilder is the revelation that Tom's mother writes stories; she gets short-listed for a prize from the Atlantic Quarterly but fears that her stay-at-home husband will never let her make the trip to the ceremony. We never are told how that turns out. I felt cheated.

Its all beautifully done, but I couldnt see the point. Revisited is hardly a re-interpretation; it doesn't tell us anything that isn't already there in Our Town. Still, the delicacy and inventiveness of Christian Barrys production may be their own justification. The director shares co-creation credit with the actors. Steven McCarthy, the narrator, carries a small sack of dirt that he scatters artistically across the tabletop to show how Main Street intersects with the railroad station. The contents of the same sack do duty for the burial mounds of the last section. McCarthys soft-voiced performance is searching, confiding and evocative. He brings a gentle and whimsical touch to the respectable ladies of the town and sends an uninhabited suit and shirt whistling down a clothesline the length of the table to represent one of the men. He plays, in fact, all the roles except one. That one is Lucy, to whom Michelle Monteith brings a combination of toughness and fragility to which the word "sensitive" hardly does justice. Her face is alight with each successive emotion. An actual spotlight, pitiless and sympathetic, epitomizes the staging; for the actors it's a gift and an opportunity, and they rise to both.

The Factory Theatre's main season is over but its staying up past its normal bedtime to host a bunch of guest-shows, under the umbrella title of Performance Spring. The first of these visitors is Tempting Providence which comes from Newfoundland and, unlike Revisited, is about where it comes from. It tells the real-life story of Myra Grimsley Bennett, who died a decade and a half ago at the age of 100, having arrived from Britain in 1921 with the official job description of nurse. In practice she was also doctor, surgeon, midwife and vet, the sole medical presence to a rugged stretch of country unlinked by roads. (Arriving in the summer, she hadnt quite realized what she was getting herself into.)Her initial contract expired after two years but, though racked with homesickness, she stayed on anyway, because she was needed.

It's to the credit of Robert Chafe, the author, that though the play tells a story that could clearly not have been invented, it doesn't feel like a documentary. Its first act, though, feels repetitive. In a rapid but protracted series of vignettes we see Nurse Bennett, the very sound and picture of a no- nonsense angel of mercy, working most hours, conquering local prejudice, local superstition and local hearts. A local heart of which she makes a particular conquest belongs to Angus, who manages to interrupt her professional ministrations just long enough to propose and be accepted. Probably it didnt happen quite like that, but its charming to pretend it did. Act 2 slows down and is shorter; it concentrates on an injury to Angus's brother and an epic sleigh ride to get him to proper treatment and avoid amputation. It's this exploit that seems to have sealed the Bennett legend. Honours followed, and even a real road. Jillian Keiley, a legendary Canadian director whose work has hardly been seen here, delivers an unobtrusively virtuoso production. Chairs and a table are arranged and re-arranged to suggest the various locations, but the focus is still firmly on the acting. Deidre Gillard-Rowlings finds exactly the right brisk rhythm for Myra and sticks to it; she also sticks to the same brisk pace and here we might have used some variation. Darryl Hopkins makes Angus the same sympathetic helpmate on stage as he must have been in life; and Robert Wyatt Thorne and Melanie Caines fill all the other roles to near-perfection. - Revisited runs through April 19 at the Brigantine Room, York Quay Centre, 416-973- 4000; Tempting Providence runs through April 22 at the Factory Theatre, 416-504-9971.
