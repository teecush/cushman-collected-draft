THEATRE REVIEW SIX DEGREES OF SEPARATION

Saturday, January 14, 1995

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Toronto ONT -- DIRECTED BY MARTI MARADEN Written by John Guare Starring Fiona Reid, Jim Mezon and Nigel Shawn Williams, At St Lawrence Centre through Feb. 4 Rating: In some important respects Marti Maraden's production measures up to the Fred Schepsi movie which playwright John Guare also scripted and which was terrific Retelling a great story BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to the Globe and Mail Toronto SOMETHING in me wishes that the plot of Six Degrees of Separation were the playwrights own invention. The most extraordinary thing about John Guares play is its narrative, a tall story that grabs the mind and the attention, and radiates in all sorts of directions. Guare - the most promising playwright in the U.S. for an uncomfortable number of years, and also author of the wonderful screenplay for Atlantic City -seized on a newspaper report that had lodged in his mind for years and turned it, in 1991, into his own masterpiece: the best American play since David Mamet's Glengarry Glen Ross (1984). As Shakespeare knew, the next best thing to telling a great story is retelling one.

There are Flan Kittredge, a very rich New York art-dealer, and his wife Ouisa. (Short for Flannery and Louisa - the diminutives tell you a lot about their style.) They narrate the story, and they also act it out. Into their apartment, on the night they are trying to stitch up a million- dollar-or-so transaction, there, literally, stumbles a black teen-ager called Paul. He is bleeding, and claims to have been mugged in Central Park. He also claims to be a college friend of his hosts' children, and the unacknowledged son of Sidney Poitier. Paul charms the Kittredges with his manners, his cooking, his unstoppable flow of casually erudite conversation, and, above all, with his promise of extras roles for them in his fathers forthcoming film of (wait for it) Cats. They take him in for the night. Next morning, they decide that it's they who have been taken in, when they discover Paul in bed with a boy of no social graces at all. They might, they scream, have been murdered or at least robbed. In fact, nothing is missing; though, God knows, they have a lot worth taking.

Later they discover that friends of theirs have been similarly stung. It turns out, thanks to the resentful detective work of the Kittredges' extremely sulky kids, that a former high-school nerd has given Paul the goods on them. He has also, in exchange for sex, taught him how to speak and conduct himself, and how to express gratitude for hospitality: with a basket of flowers in which nestles a small pot of expensive jam. Paul, meanwhile, has moved on to other prey: a young pair of would-be actors whom he fleeces, with tragic results. All the same, when he says that money is not his principal object, we believe him.

On one level, the play is an extremely funny satire on wealth-and- celebrity worship. If Paul wants in with the Kittredges, so do they with his supposed dad. The world may have room for just one more expose of radical chic, and Ouisa meditating on a possible visit to South Africa (she wouldnt want to see people who were just comparatively downtrodden," shed want the real thing) hilariously supplies it. It is also, rather portentously, about the ambiguity of people and things; Fran's most cherished possession is a two-sided painting by Kandinsky. Vitally, it is about loneliness, and personal and family failure.

The junior Kittredges are monsters; the boy, on first hearing about Paul, only breaks silence to scream, "You mean you gave him my pink shirt? " But it is pretty clear who has made them that way: clear too that Ouisa, even when she learns the truth about him, regards Paul as her second chance.

Their last telephone conversation is extraordinarily charged. Silly and snobbish as Ouisa seems, she can be awakened; and the play makes the process very moving. Its great triumph is that it slides from brittle to profound without any grinding change of gear. The title, like the play itself, derives from something someone once read; Ouisa has heard that everybody in the world is separated from everyone else by only six acquaintances. By my count, there are only four degress of separation between her and Paul but, as she says, it makes you think. It certainly makes you listen.

The play arrives in Toronto on the heels of the Fred Schepsi movie which Guare also scripted and which, apart from a new, sentimental coda, was terrific. It brilliantly expanded the play's narrative device, and it took us to places - the ceiling of the Sistine Chapel, for one - that the play can only describe or suggest. It was also perfectly cast. In some important respects, though, Marti Maradens production matches up. Phillip Silvers sets and lighting create a shimmering background in acryllic, and Christina Poddubiuk's costumes are stunning; Ouisa ends the evening in a black off-the-shoulder number for which Stephen Caras gets a special credit, and for which it is truly to die.

To put it another way, it is worthy of Fiona Reid's performance, which duplicates the plays own balancing-trick; always incipiently but never actually absurd, she looks sharp and weights nearly every line of Ouisas to perfection. Nobody else is really good enough, except perhaps Clive Cholerton in a wistful cameo as Paul's last victim. Jim Mezon's Flan, though capable, fades too easily into the woodwork; Michael Ball doubles without special distinction either way; and all the rest push too hard for a script that demands a perfect Manhattan balance of energy and poise. This is most evident with Paul himself: Nigel Shawn Williams has the hungry power of the predator but, finally, lacks the assurance of the consummate con man.
