A weekend in Niagara country that began with the waltzes and mazurkas of A Little Night Music continued with the breakneck farcical galop of The President. And after all that dancing came After the Dance.

The dancing here is occasionally literal but mainly metaphorical. The society depicted in Terence Rattigan's play seems to be suffering a collective hangover from one of Noel Coward's marvellous parties. Rattigan, Coward's successor as the boy-wonder playwright of the British theatre and forever linked with him in the critical imagination, wrote it soon after his establishing hit, the farcical French Without Tears. This one is English, with tears.

Set and written in 1939, under the imminent threat of the Second World War, it shows a generation still grappling with its guilt over having survived the First. David Scott-Fowler, a little too young to have perished at the Front, is doing a good job of drinking himself to death by way of atonement. Or maybe it's just that he's rich and weak-willed.

Anyway, he has the money to keep an open apartment for a group of like-minded friends, though unlike most of them he also has intellectual interests; in his soberer moments he works on a long-gestated and apparently unreadable book about an unmemorable European monarch.

David has a wife, Joan, who loves him more than either of them is disposed to admit. Into their lives comes Helen, a member of the next and more responsible generation, the one that knows -- though it's seldom explicitly mentioned -- that a war is about to begin.

Helen is the girlfriend of David's young relative Peter, whom David supports by employing him as an on-site proofreader for his literary endeavours, but it's really David on whom she has set her sights. She wants to reform him. She is prepared to sacrifice both his marriage and her own romance in the process.

The play failed with its first audience, who may have found its implicit warning of impending cataclysm too plausible to be necessary, and who may also have found the posturings of the previous decade's Bright Young Things somewhat beside the point. Or they might still have identified with that lost generation and resented seeing them used for moralizing purposes.

Interestingly, Coward-- supposedly the Brightest of them all -- was also one of those most earnestly inclined to wag a finger at their irresponsibility. He could have identified with both sides. Rattigan, one of the newer set, was to be lumped by posterity with the older, frivolous crowd; even his more successful plays fell out of favour in the '50s, so After the Dance stood hardly a chance.

But it's been rediscovered by an age that likes to unearth the bitterness in writers and worlds traditionally thought privileged and carefree. By that criterion, After the Dance ranks high among the 20th century's twilight-of-a-civilization plays.

Its effect depends on our finding the mad whirl of its characters' lives poisonous but seductive, and on the Scott-Fowlers and their circle being interesting both as symptoms and as individuals. A production eight years ago by the short-lived Montreal Young Company, for whom it was a surprising but rewarding choice, brought this off poignantly well.

Christopher Newton's production at Shaw, which should be thoroughly at home with the piece, does excellently with the collective aspect, more patchily with the personal. It boasts a fine clipped performance from Deborah Hay as Joan, loving and high-strung, pretending to be tough and carefree, clinging valiantly to an outworn code; and a fine flowing one from Neil Barclay as John, an amply-proportioned friend of the central couple, who seems permanently ensconced in their home and all but permanently on the sofa. He's as perceptive as he is parasitic, describing himself as able to change his life but unwilling to do so and David as just the reverse. He's right on both counts.

But that's a problem. John and, by extension, the play tell us more about the protagonist than they're dramatically able to show us. We have to take David's potential, for good and bad, on trust. What we actually see appears merely affable; and Patrick Galligan, a very sunny actor, cannot supply the missing elements of diabolism and waste. Helen, beavering away at him for his own supposed good, is similarly compelled to play on one note; Marla McLean starts her off with appropriate youthful urgency but turns into a nag. Ken James Stewart is good and fresh as her discarded intended, and among a pungent bevy of hangers-on there's a superbly pointed turn from Claire Jullien (understudying the official performer) as a soused survivor clinging to yesterday like there could never be a tomorrow. - After the Dance runs until Oct. 5.
