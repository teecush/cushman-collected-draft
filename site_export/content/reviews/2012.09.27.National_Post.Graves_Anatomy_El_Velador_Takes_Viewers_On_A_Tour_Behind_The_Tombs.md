One of the last things we hear on the soundtrack of PBSs new production of El Velador is a television announcement. It's saying, "unstable conditions will affect the region for at least 24 hours." It's a weather report, but it could equally well apply to living conditions in a part of Mexico effectively ruled by organized crime. Except that with the weather, theres a chance of things getting better once the 24 hours are up.

El Velador means The Night Watchman and Natalia Almadas calm, haunting documentary film revolves around Martin, who has that job in the northern Mexican town of Culiacan, identified in the film's publicity as "capital of Sinaloa and [of] Mexico's drug heartland." The particular site that Martin guards is the city cemetery; if Culiacan is the epicentre of the drug trade, then the graveyard is the epicentre of Culiacan.

In itself its a splendid structure, or rather series of structures; the graves and memorials run an architectural gamut from peasant simplicity to Byzantine grandeur. Theyre beautifully photographed: Almada, who comes from the territory she depicts, has shot a visual tone poem, a requiem about requiems.

There's little dialogue. Martin, middle-aged and grizzled, says a few sentences to the camera. He tells us that at night you can't walk around the cemetery; its where the gangs have their parties, and also their shoot-outs. Every dawn finds a fresh crop of bodies, frequently hacked and tortured. We owe this information to news bulletins coming off the radio belonging to the mausoleums coconut seller; he does a brisk trade what with the number of mourners around, and the fact that most of them have young children.

We get glimpses of gravestones, and do the math on the ages of the victims; one I noted was 22. Fatalities in the past six years - the term of office of President Felipe Calderon, who promised a war on the drug lords - have been estimated in the high 50,000s.

Most of the film's words issue from the TV or the radio. We hear an interview with a U.S. government drug czar, regretting the carnage and self-flagellating over how little attention his country pays to Mexico (described as its most important neighbour - "along with Canada") as compared to all the millions it's poured into Afghanistan. He does not, strangely enough, suggest neutralizing the illegal drug trade by legalizing the drugs. His interview is immediately followed by a commercial for mattresses, maybe an easy ironic point but more likely an acknowledgment that life goes on.

Toward the end of the film, the newscasts announce that one of the leading gangsters - the capo of capos - has been run to ground and killed, "along with seven gunmen." Martins buddies, discussing the event in the films longest sequence of overheard conversation, aren't impressed. The dude wasn't any good anymore. They got rid of him. Therell be someone else.

Its a climate of lament and acceptance. Just once, and in the distance, we hear a cry of agony: a woman wailing, "My son, my son." This in not, in short, the contemporary Hispanic version of Boardwalk Empire. (I have to confess there were times when I wished it were.) It couldn't be said that we get to know Martin himself, but the film takes its tone from his stoical pertinacity; he seems watchful by nature as well as by trade.

At least he's in a growth industry: he and the florists. We see new tombs being constructed, gorgeous new wreaths being hung. For at least one young woman, widowhood seems to have become a vocation.

Meanwhile, the children eagerly buy coconut water from the vendor, or have it bought for them, and play hopscotch on the gravestones. Life, as I said before, as the film keeps saying, goes on. And so does death.

El Velador (The Night Watchman) airs Sept. 27 at 10 p.m. on PBS.
