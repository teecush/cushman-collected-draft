TO THE WALL

Theatre Passe Muraille

Andy Jones, the cordial comic from Codco, has a plumply unassuming air but grandiose ambitions. To the Wall, his new one- man show, is about the ultimate problems of good and evil, especially evil.

He tells us of a psychological condition that afflicts him, a sort of future-conditional deja vu; if someone is late for an appointment, Jones will immediately begin to worry about all the terrible things that might have caused this. He calls this sensation futur possible, peut-etre horrible. Many of us will recognize it.

For Jones, it's emblematic of the state of the world, and he calls God to account for it. In fact, he invites God to attend a public meeting (a first, as he points out) timed to coincide with the closing moments of this show. It will surprise nobody that this situation comes with a built-in anti-climax.

It shouldnt surprise Jones himself, since hes an atheist. However, he grew up in an extreme Catholic community in St. John's, Nfld. ("we were Hassidic Catholics") and the old habits of feeling die hard, especially in times of crisis. His own theory, rather a persuasive one, is that God felt lonely, and threw all the elements of existence at random into a series of parallel universes from one of which humanity emerged.

Bound by that initial commitment to chance and non-intervention, God then had to stand by when evil emerged. In other words Jones posits a creator but not a merciful one, or at least not one prepared to act on merciful impulses.

So what does humanity do in the meantime? Samuel Beckett (another lapsed Papist) asked this question, and Jones conducts himself pretty much like Beckett's tramps -- though since theres only one of him he has to be more lucid.

He spends his time pondering metaphysical problems and bringing them down to his own level. He considers the peculiar sex appeal of the stage performer: Here he is, a tubby badly dressed fella without very much hair who can imitate other peoples voices and make us laugh, and what he is really doing with these talents is sending out messages to the ladies in the audience saying "mate with me." He recalls the hellfire preachers of his youth, less interested in praising God than in damning Protestants, and invites us to identify the precise moment at which they start going mad. On the evidence he provides, I don't see how you could tell.

Jones also talks about stories so sad they would make bunny- rabbits weep; about the number of buckets of sand you would have to empty on the ground before they formed a perfect map of Newfoundland (and Labrador), and how you could not hope to do it without virtually unlimited time and the services of three young and attractive assistants.

He talks also about the exploits of his Aunt Mary, a small-town switchboard operator who eloped with a diplomat (she had to elope; he was a Protestant) and who acquired a perfect upper-class English accent that so intimidated Hitler, when her husband was posted to pre-war Berlin, that the memory of it stopped him from invading England. This means, I think, that even the good stuff happens for terrifyingly arbitrary reasons.

He keeps all these elements running in parallel, and promises us they will all come together at the end. They do, too, sort of; though, for reasons already hinted at, the show does dribble away at its close. Up till then, though, it's fine, and Jones himself is worriedly lovable. Pushed to the wall, I would say I liked it.
