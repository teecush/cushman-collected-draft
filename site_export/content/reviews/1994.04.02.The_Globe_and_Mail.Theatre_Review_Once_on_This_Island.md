THEATRE REVIEW ONCE ON THIS ISLAND

Saturday, April 02, 1994

ROBERT CUSHMAN

LYRICS by LYNN AHRENS MUSIC by STEPHEN FLAHERTY Directed by Bob Baker Canadian Stage Company Rating: The Toronto production of Once On This Island may be lacking in natural hoofers, but there are impressive lyrics and some good musical numbers in an adult show that has some of the virtues of good children's theatre Musical love story ends up in tropical foliage BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail BRIEFLY: girl gets boy, girl loses boy, girl turns into tree. That is a tidier and more engaging story than most musicals have these days, and what is most impressive about Once On This Island is its single-minded dedication to telling it.

First seen in New York four years ago, the show is The Little Mermaid dried out and transposed to the Caribbean. Somewhere in the French Antilles poor Ti Moune falls for rich Daniel. She nurses him back to life after a car-crash, but neither he nor his family has any intention of letting this liaison be permanent. Daniel marries his childhood sweetheart; Ti Moune is cast out and dies.

No fairy story, this still has a supernatural superstructure. Ti Moune has attracted the attentions of four suitably jealous gods, three of them more or less benevolent but the fourth, Papa Ge, is the Demon of Death. A sinuously cavorting devil with patent Broadway connections (call him Sportin' Death), he makes a deal with Ti Moune: Daniel's life for her soul. Later on he reverses the offer: her soul for his death. It is one of the strengths of the show that Ti Moune really is mad enough to take a knife to her beloved; it is a further and greater strength that when one of the nicer deities intervenes we still believe that her final decision is her own. All the gods can do is transform her into foliage in which guise, says the synopsis in the program (and if they can give it away, why can't I?) "she sheltered peasants and grands hommes alike." Whether this has had any permanent effect on the island's class structure we are not told.

Oh, yes, I'm glad you asked. The grands hommes are the Beauxhommes, Daniel's mob, and they are not your ordinary sneering aristocrats. They are a special kind of sneering aristocrat, descendants of a French lord and a native girl. In other words, half the cast are half-caste: barred from France for being black, despised on the island for not being black enough. The Beauxhommes carry a burden of self-hatred that the show might probe more deeply than it does. But there are some stabbing moments. "I am Daniel's mistress," cries Ti Moune as the gates slam on her. She is also in love with him, but this he has never noticed. It's left to his washed- out fiancee to point it out - without prejudice to their marriage. Daniel's own attitude is expressed and exposed in a beautifully crafted song - light in its ironies, tight in its rhymes - called Some Girls. As in some you marry, some you love. Whose feelings is he trying to spare?

Lynn Ahrens's lyrics are impressive throughout. After a diet of Tim Rice and his lesser successors - the kind of lyrics that make one good joke and then lean back exhausted - it's a blessing to encounter a writer whose words, though short on obvious flash, always land in the right place. She also wrote the book, which is skeletal but firm. Again, it's a relief to find a show that has one; dialogue really is better than doggerel. Stephen Flaherty's music is more problematic; calypso and reggae, the obvious modes here, have severe dramatic limits, and when the score tries to step outside them it falls too often into the mode I think of as Euro-Broadway: the kind that tries to soar without first establishing a base. The big ballads are the weakest songs. But a musical scena called Rain is exciting and, as staged, a worthy addition to the long line of umbrella numbers.

I remember the New York production, staged by Graciela Daniele, as one long exhilarating dance. Few of the Toronto cast look like natural hoofers, and this version (directed by Bob Baker, choreographed by Mabel Robinson) is comparatively flat-footed. But it tells the story at least as well, and - most importantly - communicates a joy in doing so. This is an adult show with some of the virtues of good children's theatre. Leslie Frankish's set is routine cardboard cut-out in its basics, but some beguiling things pop out of it. The strongest singer in the cast, Salome Bey, is also the strongest presence; I only wish that as Ti Moune's mother she had more to do. Strength seems to run in the family with Ti Moune's dad (Jeff Jones) a haggard rock, and Cee-Cee Harshaw, the girl herself, assertive as well as sweet.
