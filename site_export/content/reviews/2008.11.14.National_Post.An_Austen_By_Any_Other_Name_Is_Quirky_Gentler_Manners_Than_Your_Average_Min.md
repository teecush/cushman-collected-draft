That indefatigable screenwriter Jane Austen is at it again, this time in semi-modern dress. Lost in Austen is based on Pride and Prejudice, but it isn't a straight adaptation in BBC classic-serial style. Nor is it a wholesale updating in the manner of the movie Clueless, which re-worked Austen's Emma as a story of Beverly Hills. It's something in-between and quirkier.

Its heroine, who has the very Austen-ish name of Amanda Price, is a contemporary West Londoner in her twenties who's hooked on Austen in general and Pride and Prejudice in particular. She pines for the gentler manners depicted in her idol's novels, and especially for their courtlier courtships: Understandably, as when her own live-in swain gets around to proposing marriage, his fond words are swallowed up in a beer-sodden burp. She gets small comfort from her mother who urges her to settle for the available male in a manner that may put you in mind -- and certainly puts Amanda in mind -- of the matchmaking Mrs. Bennet. So when the bewitching Elizabeth Bennet, everybody's favourite heroine, materializes in Amanda's bathroom, Amanda is primed, if incredulous. After a certain amount of backchat about the differences between fact and fiction, Amanda ventures through Elizabeth's magic portal. Elizabeth does not come with her. Amanda finds herself an uninvited guest in the Bennet household, regarded by its inhabitants with a mixture of fascination, suspicion and outright resentment. It shouldn't happen to a dog.

As a matter of fact, it has happened to a dog. There used to be--maybe still is --a highly literate hound on children's TV called Wishbone, who week after week insinuated himself into classic stories without any of the human actors noticing the difference. In one episode he played Mr. Darcy, the aloof charmer who eventually wins Elizabeth, or whom Elizabeth eventually wins. Lost in Austen is more ambitiously, self-consciously intertextual.

Amanda suspects at first that she has wandered into some elaborate TV set-up. Encountering a Darcy rather less charming

than the one she has envisioned, she muses: "He's not Colin Firth. But even Colin Firth wasn't Colin Firth. They had to change the shape of his head with makeup." Amanda has certainly read the book -- she has the well-thumbed Penguin Classic to prove it --but she's equally affected by seeing it on film and TV. Confronted by some of the realities of 18th-century English life, she is taken somewhat aback. She also discovers that, in those days, Alaska was known as "Russian America." I don't know if this was actually the case, or if it's just a serendipitously topical invention of the author's.

The larger joke depends, of course, on our knowledge of the original; and it's one that might not work with any other classic novel. Only Oliver Twist still has the same degree of popular currency -- it has the advantage of having been a hit musical, but its nightmare landscape can hardly compare with Austen's as a receptacle for romantic wish-fulfilment. Guy Andrews' script does an excellent job of simultaneously inflating and puncturing the dream. He has an excellent ear for both Austen's idiom and Amanda's, though he might perhaps have credited his heroine with more of his own sensitivity; for one so steeped in the books, she takes few pains to avoid shocking and confusing her companions.

The humour of incongruity holds up delightfully in the first episode. In the second, the show starts to dig deeper, with results whose satisfactoriness has yet to be determined. Amanda has come in at the start of the story and she knows how it is supposed to end. However, with her presence and Elizabeth's absence, things start to go differently. She falls ever so slightly for Mr. Bingley, the destined mate of Elizabeth's sister, Jane, and he for her. But one detour leads to another with at least one startling and distressing result. How are they going to work their way out of that one? Maybe they won't.

Jemima Rooper is a perfect Amanda, pert and puzzled. Hugh Bonneville makes Mr. Bennet, sardonically resigned to not being master in his own house, into the most amusing and engaging character around, and Alex Kingston expands on the traditional gushing portrayal of Elizabeth, giving her claws. Guy Henry is a greasy beanpole as the fawning parson Mr. Collins, even more unsavoury in the flesh, as Amanda observes, than on the page.

Amanda has impressed the impoverished gentry with her income of Pounds 27,000 a year, and by the fact that she "rents in Hammersmith." That area has changed since my day, but not as much obviously as it has since the Bennets', who, misconstruing Amanda's slang, refer to its "otter-strewn thoroughfares." Elizabeth, presumably, is still exploring them. It had better not turn out to be a dream. - Lost in Austen airs Sunday at 8 p. m. on VIVA.
