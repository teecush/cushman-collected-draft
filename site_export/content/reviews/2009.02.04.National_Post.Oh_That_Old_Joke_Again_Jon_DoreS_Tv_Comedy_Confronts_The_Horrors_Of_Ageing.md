When the funniest line in a comedy show is, "Why did the chicken cross the road?" there has to be desperation in the air. To be perfectly fair to tonight's episode of The Jon Dore Television Show ("Dore Gets Old"), we do actually get to see the road-crossing chicken, and that's pretty neat. (I've never thought about this before, but getting a chicken to cross on cue can't be easy.) Also, just to show how self-aware everyone is, we immediately get a flashback to the writers' room, where the star's team tries, not too successfully, to explain to him the chicken's relevance -- the show is about getting old, and it's an old joke.

More exactly, the show is about the chubby, bearded comic's fear of getting old. There he is, at the top of the show, scraping tunelessly away at a violin and proclaiming, "The guilt is killing me -- I shot JFK"; then, if one non-sequitur can lead to another, we find he's in a skate park, about to execute a daredevil leap ("rad to the bone") whose failure leaves him writhing on the ground in, let us hope, pain. That, though, isn't the worst of it. A group of kids see him lying there, and make fun of him for trying stuff he's too old for. Worse yet, a passing group of young women express their revulsion at the idea of such a palsied granddad hitting on them. Dore, who thinks of himself as young, is appalled. Not to say terrified.

He visits his psychotherapist (a fixture on the show), to whom he confesses his revulsion. "I can," he either boasts or confesses (it works both ways), "live with terminal cancer. What I can't take is kids telling me I'm a dinosaur." He's onto something there. However, since he's chronically unable to stay on track, he ends up giggling with his shrink over the word "stegosaurus." He also calls on an anti-ageing specialist, who himself has reached the age of 54. ("More than half a century," calculates Dore, who really has problems with this.) So, realizing that even the professionals can't beat old age, he decides to join it. He takes himself to a seniors centre to find how its inhabitants can support lives so obviously not worth living. The trio he interviews turn out to have a lot more vitality than he does, especially one wrinkled lady who sounds as if she grew up in the north of England. They even seem to have better sex lives than he does; and of course this, even more than skateboarding, is what's really on his mind. The show ends with our hero in bed with an old lady (the delightful veteran actress Araby Lockhart, if I read the credits right) about to make ravenous demands on his body.

So Dore moves from demonizing old age to worshipping it. Both extremes are of course equally crass, a fact about which the show makes no bones. There is an old and honourable history of making comedy out of crassness. The trouble with Dore's show, despite its alternation between interviews and acted scenes, is that there's nothing outside the crassness. The interviews recall those that used to be regular features on The Daily Show with Jon Stewart; Ed Helms or Rob Corddry would go out to meet people with extreme views and sit there slack-jawed, egging their subjects on to even greater outrageousness. The Stewart show seems largely to have dropped this idea, whether due to a shortage of intrepid interviewers or of gullible interviewees. The point, as in a somewhat different way with Borat or Ali G, is that something or someone gets satirized. On this show, the only person who gets exposed is Dore himself, and he's a dummy to begin with. (There is, presumably, a gulf between Dore the character and Dore the performer, but we can only respond to what we see.) The acted parts are a little like Curb Your Enthusiasm, but only a little. Larry David's character always has a plot to move around in -- this gives us a line on his obnoxiousness, and makes it richer. It doesn't soften it, or stop us from guiltily identifying with it, but it does suggest that there may be a world, and even other people, elsewhere. Dore's show is all Dore, and he isn't fascinating.

Dore's declared idol is David Letterman, whom I hold more responsible than any other performer for degrading the once-noble concept of irony into a synonym for "being bad on purpose, and smug about it." The real running gag of the Dore show -- it's emblazoned in the title --is "look at me, I'm on television," as if the idea of being a TV comic were so delicious in itself as to preclude any necessity of actually being funny. Dore encounters a genie (of the lamp persuasion) who admonishes him, when he can't decide on his three wishes, to "think of the theme of the episode." Comedic self-awareness also has a history, stretching back to Aristophanes, but it shouldn't be all there is. "Dore," say his writers, "this is hip humour; maybe you are getting old." In his nightmares. In their dreams. - The Jon Dore Television Show airs tonight at 10 p. m. on the Comedy network.
