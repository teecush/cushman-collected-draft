THE BALD SOPRANO THE LESSON

DuMaurier Theatre Centre

- - -

The plays of Eugene Ionesco -- Romanian by birth, French by adoption, stateless by temperament -- came violently into fashion in the 1950s and passed violently out again in the '60s. In the last few years they have been making a sly return.

Soulpepper's welcome double-billing of two Ionesco early one-act farces makes a fair case for the author's rehabilitation in its first half and an overwhelming one in its second.

The tag "Theatre of the Absurd" has done a lot more harm than good, but it applies to Ionesco if it applies to anyone. The Bald Soprano is a good entry point into his work; it was his first play (written in 1948) and derived, by his own account, from his experience of trying to learn English.

Amused and appalled by the stock phrases he found in his textbooks, he set about writing a play composed of just that kind of dialogue. Here are Mr. and Mrs. Smith in their living room complacently telling one another about the meal they have just shared, about their children and about their friends, the Bobby Watsons. (A large family, and every single one of them, male or female, is called Bobby Watson.)

Their friends the Martins pay a call and discover, in the course of an impeccably polite conversation, that they are married to one another. Ionesco may not have set out to satirize the expository techniques of the well-made play but he managed it anyway.

He also had a larger point to make about society. These people speak in dead sentences, therefore they are dead themselves. This is a rigged case; and even if we admit its partial truth (or regard it as a pleasant way of looking down on our neighbours), its point is rather quickly made.

Our continued interest is largely determined by how funny the author can make it, and his achievement here is uneven. At its best it is hilarious, especially in a fanatically logical sequence about a series of unattributable knocks at the front door.

Ionesco doesn't deal in non sequiturs; rather, he piles up the sequiturs until they cancel one another out. They need to be acted with a sort of deadpan panache, and of Soulpepper's central quartet, only Brenda Robins consistently has the hang of it, though Michael Hanrahan has a few red-faced moments.

Things perk up when a fire chief arrives (William Webster, looking alternately aglow and aghast) to tell anecdotes of virtuoso pointlessness, explain exactly whose fires he is and is not allowed to extinguish and generally supply a shoulder to be cried on. "A fireman," he explains, "is a confessor."

The play ends with language finally cracking apart, the cast screaming fractured platitudes at one another while -- a gratuitously frenetic touch in Jim Warren's production -- the stage revolves.

The show otherwise looks good, with its geometric moves on Glenn Davidson's sternly cosy set, though I wondered why, in a play that depends so much on references to a guidebook England that Ionesco at the time had never visited, nobody bothered with an accent.

The play leaves you thinking Ionesco had a great technique; but what could he do with it? Well, he learned, and fast, as is shown, appropriately enough, in The Lesson, written three years later.

This play, astutely directed by Chris Abraham, is a brilliant dramatization of the teacher-student relationship, written in terms of insouciant comedy and carried to the direst possible extremes.

A pupil comes to her professor's study for tuition towards her doctorate: a goal only modestly compromised by the fact that, though she can add one and one with perfect accuracy, subtraction utterly defeats her. Never mind: the professor will move on from arithmetic to philology. Gradually but also speedily (I'm sorry, the idiom is catching), the professor moves from patronizing pedagogy to bullying and from there to outright assault: anybody wanting a textbook study in schoolroom harassment need only see this play.

The professor's doubletalk is strongly reminiscent of Groucho Marx holding forth on the party of the second part, and there is more than a touch of Groucho about Tony Nardi's performance, forward stoop included; the part could probably be played with more demonic variety but what he does he does extremely well.

Meanwhile Liisa Repo-Martell, at her very best, takes the revolutionary step of playing the pupil as a real person with, at no cost to the humour, an unbroken and increasingly terrified emotional line.

The first play is a trifle, the second a masterpiece. Both offer the added attraction of Kristen Thomson playing a maid: In the first she's a pertly confident young thing who says her name is Sherlock Holmes, and in the second the professor's grumbling, motherly accomplice warning him that philology always leads to trouble. She is about 40 years too young for the latter role but she doesn't let it bother her and neither should you.
