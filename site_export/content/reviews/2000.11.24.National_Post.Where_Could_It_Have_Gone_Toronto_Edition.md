SMUDGE

Tarragon Extra Space, Toronto

There are certain plays you feel like a heel for disliking. Alex Bulmer's Smudge belongs in that category.

It is about a young woman who is diagnosed with retinitis pigmentosa, and goes gradually blind; and it is partially autobiographical. We are told that the character is not necessarily identical to that of the author, but that the experience is. We witness the protagonists anger, her rebellion, her humour -- which is very prominent -- and her, for want of a better word, acceptance.

The presentation in Alisa Palmers production is on a high level of professional skill, and the writing is at least adroit. It is also, though -- despite the personal commitment behind it -- generalized.

The author begins by plunging us, with admirable directness, right into the heart of the situation. Or so it seems: Sometimes says Freddie, our heroine and host, "I imagine myself a wild and famous silent screen star," one who has suddenly gone blind and who insists that nobody talk about it but that everything on the set be arranged to cater to her convenience.

And then you start wondering: Why a silent screen star? Why not a talking one? Or why not some other kind of prima donna altogether -- a queen, a politician or a rock star? Presumably the choice of image tells us something about the tastes and interests of Freddie herself; but it doesn't. Or the metaphor will be sustained throughout the play; but it isn't.

We move on to the scene in which Freddie receives the cruel diagnosis, and the even crueller prognosis. She ponders the vernacular habit of referring to a disease by its initials: RP, she says, like on baseball caps, or ski jackets. We cant be meant to believe that she actually said this, not at that moment and in the presence of authority, as represented by a doctor. Its the kind of bitter wisecrack that comes under the heading of "things-I-wish-I'd- thought-of-at-the-time."

The doctor then does what we all (in vain) long for doctors to do at such appalling moments; she displays some vulnerability of her own. She breaks down and sobs that she's in a terrible way herself, and that her husband or boyfriend has just left her. This is played with exaggerated sobs and strictly for laughs. After this double dose of the comedy of wish fulfillment, we are disinclined to believe anything.

Some things in what follows strike painfully home. Freddie talks about her diminishing vision, of how walking across the Second Cup to get some milk for her coffee becomes an agonizingly ludicrous experience. We watch strangers impatient when they dont realize her condition, and cringingly solicitous when they do. And we -- the sighted in the audience -- blush and laugh in recognition. We dont cringe ourselves, though, which is good; these scenes are tactfully short and sharp.

Slightly longer are scenes that show Freddie interacting with her therapist, or with her lesbian lover. Some of the tensions she has with the latter could arise in any relationship, and this, of course, is part of the point: Other emotions don't come to an end because of a physical disability. But we don't know enough about Freddie outside of (or indeed before) her blindness for these episodes to register much, and the play is awkwardly caught between being a case study and a full-length psychological profile.

Some of this may be due to Diane Flacks skilled but relentlessly life-of-the-party performance as Freddie. As a performer she is always on. This worked in Sibs, where she had good material and (in Richard Greenblatt) an equal partner; there she could be a counterpuncher. It lets her down badly in her TV series PR (she seems dogged by initials), where the material is atrocious and the focus, such as it is, is all on her and her energy.

That energy is a lot more controlled here. She is, after all, acting a real situation (by which I dont just mean biographically real) but it's one that's seen -- the word may be inapposite but I dont know a better one -- entirely from her perspective. She never steps back, never relaxes, and when she tries for vulnerability -- well, she can do it, but its like a stand-up comic saying, "but seriously, folks"; it comes too late.

Kate Lynch portrays a series of brisk but collapsible caregivers, personal and professional, without much variation. A third performer, Sherrylee Hunter, has little to do (little that's prominent, anyway) but does it well.

Carolyn M. Smith designed the sets and Andrea Lundy the lighting. The stage is divided by a translucent screen, behind which actions unfold in shadowy mime, obviously to give us a sense of Freddie's increasingly limited vision. (It may even relate to the initial silent-movie reference, though the visual styles are hardly similar.)

Its difficult to pay much attention, though, because theres always something going on in front. One moment, cuttingly written and played, shows Freddie, partially blind, shamed by an encounter with someone who is totally so. It jolts but, typically of the play, it goes nowhere. Maybe there's nowhere it could go.

Maybe this really is an unstageable -- or at least an undramatizeable -- subject.
