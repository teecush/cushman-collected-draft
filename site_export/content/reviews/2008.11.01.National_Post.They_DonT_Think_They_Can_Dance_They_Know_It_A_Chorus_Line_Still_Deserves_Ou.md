Ihave never believed that A Chorus Line was, in the advertising phrase recycled for the current revival: "The Best Musical. Ever." But it is an essential musical, one that both explores and embodies the drives, on both sides of the curtain, that make the form come alive. It's about energy, transmitted and received. Theoretically, it's about performers, but it's also about audiences. It could, and has often been known to, drive an audience crazy.

You know the drill. Zach, a director-choreographer, is holding auditions for a new Broadway show. He has chorus roles for eight performers and a shortlist of twice that number. On the thin pretext that his choristers will have characters to play as well as songs and dances to perform, he interrogates them about their past lives and especially about what made them want to dance. Some of the resultant confessions are merely spoken, others are musicalized. And they're done with such skill and conviction that by the end, we're rooting for every one of them -- even while knowing that half of them are going to be rejected. Catharsis is practically guaranteed.

A Chorus Line premiered in 1975, and ran longer than any Broadway production up to that time. Of all its major creators, only one -- Marvin Hamlisch, who wrote the mostly excellent music--is still alive. A new production, closely based on the original, opened in New York last year, and its touring version is now playing in Toronto. It's directed by Bob Avian, who 33 years ago was co-choreographer to Michael Bennett, the show's director and presiding genius. The job of recreating Bennett's choreography, though, has gone to Baayork Lee, who was in the original cast.

Some things are as exciting as I'd remembered from when the show was new. The opening sequence, in which Zach puts all the aspirants through an exhausting series of dance combinations, has always been my favourite part, and this time around some things seemed to come up with extra sharpness: the individualization of the characters before they've even been formally introduced, their fumbles and silent curses, their taskmaster's patience and his ruthlessness.

This number has the thrill of a documentary; even when -- something that's unlikely to happen at an actual audition -- the performers start singing, in unison, I Hope I Get It, you still buy it, the sentiment seems so urgent, so heartfelt and so right. (That simple line is probably the best single moment in Edward Kleban's lyrics). After it's over, the convention changes; the subject matter remains different from that of the average musical but the form becomes the same: People sing about their life experiences and how they feel about them. There's a long ensemble number, with mini-numbers inside it, whose title is Hello Twelve, Hello Thirteen, Hello Love, but it's the smaller, more individualized songs that are more effective.

Or they could be. It depends on the performers. Maybe standards have slipped since the '70s, or maybe Bennett's successors lack his flair for talent spotting and talent driving. The present corps are uniformly excellent as dancers, variable as singers and even more variable as actors. The best performance comes from Bryan Knowlton as Paul, the Hispanic boy whose traumatic moment came when his parents discovered him working in a drag show; he handles this monologue (he doesn't have a song) beautifully, even with a few too many sobs at the end. After hearing it you watch his dancing with a keener eye; he justifies the attention. Emily Fletcher does all right, but less well than is possible, by the infallible role of Sheila, the wisecracking den-mother (or considering her comparative youth, den-sister). Elsewhere, the cosmetic-surgery song Dance Ten Looks Three, a. k. a. Tits and Ass, is ruined by over-aggressiveness, while Nothing, the tale of the Puerto Rican student terrorized by a posturing acting-teacher, is spoiled by being squawked. Michael Gruber's Zach is forceful but wooden. By contrast, the proclamation by his ex-flame Cassie (Nikkie Snelson) that she'd be proud to be in this chorus comes from the heart of the show, and is genuinely uplifting.

Robin Wagner's bare mirrored set remains the same; so do Theoni V. Aldredge's costumes, including of course the gold suits and top hats in which the entire company executes the exhilarating, regimented, high-stepping finale. People used to argue about this number: Was it a celebration or a bitter comment on the relegation of individuals to cogs in a dancing machine? Now, it hardly matters. The number, and the show, come to us as reminders of a better time. This musical, unlike the TV "reality shows" of which it's the unwitting ancestor, celebrates not the cult of stardom but the quest for meaningful work.
