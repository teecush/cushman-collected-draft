DIARY OF ANNE FRANK AVON THEATRE, STRATFORD

The Stratford Festival production of The Diary of Anne Frank is, by any conventional standards, extremely well done. The question, to which I do not have an answer, is whether the dramatization of the most poignant and familiar of Holocaust documents is a case in which conventional standards should apply.

I am surprised to find myself harbouring these doubts. A few years ago I saw the play at the Toronto Young Peoples Theatre, in a production that was excellent but with no pretentions to greatness, and it effectively disposed of my qualms. If you were to put the Holocaust on stage at all, then this modest craftsman-like approach seemed the way to do it: far less offensive than turning it into a self-conscious piece of Art and asking to be congratulated on your sensitivity.

Al Waxmans Stratford production is not essentially different, but it has more of a gloss, the performances are generally more striking (though not necessarily better, and YPT certainly had a finer Anne) and, of course, since it's at Stratford, the stakes are higher. The show implicitly realizes this, and it tries very hard to be real. But that is, by definition, an impossible attempt.

Once on stage, the eight Jews hiding from the Nazis in an Amsterdam attic -- the four Franks, the three Van Daans, the solitary dentist Mr. Dussel, never leave it, not even during the intermission: Never, that is, until the end when they are betrayed and herded off to the death camps. We feel something of the tedium and frustration (the words seem inadequate, but that's part of my point) of being pent up for two years, unable to make a sound until night because men are working by day in the factory beneath, knowing all the time that the alternative is unimaginably worse.

A few years ago the familiar 1956 dramatization by Frances Goodrich and Albert Hackett was thoroughly overhauled by Wendy Kesselman. That version was staged in New York in 1997, and its the text used here. Goodrich and Hackett, an anonymously competent Broadway and Hollywood team, cast the material in the mould of an American well-made play; in effect, they wrote a Broadway domestic drama with a death sentence hanging over it. And the last thing they dwelt on -- in deference to the susceptibilities of audiences of both persuasions -- was the characters Jewishness.

Kesselman has roughened and loosened it up, going back to the originally published diary, and incorporating some material that has been revealed since. The characters are now less, shall we say, assimilated than they were; there is more racial and religious detail. There is also more sexual detail: more explicit emphasis on Anne Frank's pubescent yearnings.

Anne's constant friction with her mother -- excised for years by her father, the one survivor -- is now front and centre. All these tensions , of course, are what happen in any family; its just that here theyre happening in a pressure cooker.

The old Broadway mould is further broken by letting us hear symbolic sounds of the outside world: Hitler's speeches, the sound of the trains. (Neither are exactly original choices). The original naturalistic epilogue, in which Otto Frank was reunited with his Gentile protectors and told them what had happened to the others, is replaced by a monologue that he addresses against a projected background of barbed wire and crematoria.

Before that we have seen the SS actually arriving on stage to arrest the fugitives; in the old play, we only heard them arriving. The change is, I think, a mistake; it tries to show us the cruelty, but can't do it justice: Better to leave it to our imaginations. Some of the changes are clearly improvements, but the old version worked better because of its limitations. It was what it was.

The Stratford actors are excellent, though Maggie Blake cant lift Anne off the page (shes more convincing in her voice-over narration than in her live scenes) and her older sister Margot seems only to exist as her foil. But George A. Sperdakos has great quiet dignity as their father, and Diane D'Aquila gives a beautifully modulated account of his permanently anguished wife.

The more extrovert, perpetually squabbling Van Daans -- he ever- hungry, she ever-gushing -- are played to the hilt, though never over it by Robert King and Lally Cadeau with Nicholas Van Burek as their son Peter, a lanky, embarrassed presence between them. (Why, incidentally, were these characters' names changed? Historically they were called Van Pels. Was that name thought to be insufficiently catchy for Broadway in the Fifties? And if so, why hasnt this new more outspoken adaptation, restored their identity? It makes you wonder about the whole endeavour.)

The benefactors are good, too; one looks forward to their visits just as the prisoners do, with Deborah Hays servant girl an especially welcome presence. The performances mesh together admirably: a smooth cohesive ensemble, each picking up infallibly where the last one left off.

That's really good theatre. It isn't real life, where people stumble and overlap, and the timing is awkward. Maybe the whole thing should be improvised. Maybe a genius-director could incite his actors to the level of reality this subject seems to demand; maybe it isnt possible, or shouldnt even be attempted.

As it is, though, one of the most detailed and intelligent performances, that of the crotchety Dussel (real name, Pfeiffer) was the one I had most trouble believing, simply because the actor, Brian Tree, is one whose work I know (and like) especially well.

There is a danger with this play, in any version: one that it shares with pieces like The Miracle Worker. The audience, having shared vicariously in someone else's historical agony, leaves feeling drained, certainly, but also spuriously noble. Maybe only a historically traumatic production in Berlin in the Fifties managed to escape that. But if the Holocaust is not to be theatrically off- limits -- and I don't think it should be -- then probably this is the only way to approach it: Take a small number of individual tragedies and multiply by six million.
