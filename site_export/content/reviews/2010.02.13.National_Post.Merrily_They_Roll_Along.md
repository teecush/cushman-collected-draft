It looks at the start as if An Evening with Patti LuPone and Mandy Patinkin is going to consist of two Broadway stars singing Broadway songs, strung together to tell a single story. That turns out to be a false trail. What we get instead are groups of songs, each group telling a separate story, plus a few numbers that seem to be there for their own or their performers' sakes.

Its clear, looking back, that we were warned. They start off with Another Hundred People, Stephen Sondheims ode to urban angst. We then move into what seems to be the entire opening scene of South Pacific, which obviously isn't urban. LuPone slips right inside the skin of Nurse Nellie Forbush from Little Rock, Ark.; Patinkin gives a louring performance, both physically and vocally, of Emile de Becque (here described as "a cultured man" rather than "a cultured Frenchman," though it's hard to believe that Patinkin would have trouble with the accent); between them they get through A Cockeyed Optimist, the Twin Soliloquies, Some Enchanted Evening -- practically the whole bit. And since it ends with Emile proposing to Nellie, its not too much of a stretch to see them, though apparently with their names changed, whisked back to Sondheim-land for LuPone to give a flawlessly tongue-twisted account of the neurotic bride from Company who is so not getting married today. This is unexpectedly interspersed with Patinkin singing the super-ardent Loving You, from the same composers Passion. The two songs undercut one another beautifully: a case of Mutually Assured Deconstruction.

But then they go off on another tack altogether: back, it seems, to being singles. The songs continue to be artfully woven together; they're also good and enterprising choices in themselves. We get the only fast version of Baby It's Cold Outside Ive ever heard; a trio of first-rate Jerome Kerns; some prime cuts of Kander and Ebb; and Murray Grands neglected April in Fairbanks, which with the two frozen thesps propelling themselves around the stage on office chairs, is funny if not as hysterically so as the first-night audience seemed to think. (Ann Reinking did the show's amusingly sedate choreography.) There's also a large helping of Sondheims Merrily We Roll Along whose only fault is that it doesnt roll along long enough. I would happily have heard them do the entire score. As it is, the section finishes with Franklin Shepard Inc., a hysterically virtuoso number that finds Patinkin on his most compatible ground. He likes being virtuoso and he seems to like being hysterical.

I was getting to that. Both he and LuPone are known as mannered performers, but she seems to have put her mannerisms on hold, along with her sometimes mushy diction and her brassiness. Here she's precise and economical and, very often, achingly vulnerable: in Like It Was (incredible), In Buddy's Eyes and the aptly named A Quiet Thing. Patinkins fabled falsetto is matched by a lower register thats even more startling, but between the two, the actual songs -- their emotions and their thought-processes -- get mashed. He performs them to death. He switches speeds recklessly and, though he could obviously hit any note he chooses, underlines key points by speaking them. This is self-defeating; the glory of Sondheim's lyrics in particular is that they're impeccably colloquial within a strict musical form. Anyone can talk conversationally. Patinkin, an intelligent and sophisticated actor (he directed the show and, with pianist Paul Ford, put it together), insists on turning himself into a ham.

Just before and just after the intermission, the performers jump track and do Greatest Hits. LuPone, Broadways first Evita, sings Dont Cry for Me, Argentina. The fans scream, but it's a terrible song out of context. In context, it has a saving irony, set up as it is by it's anti-song Oh What a Circus, which Patinkin, Broadways first Che, could sing here but doesnt. LuPone's Everything's Coming Up Roses also plays over-intense, divorced from the revival of Gypsy that gave it birth. Patinkins finest Broadway hour was Sunday in the Park with George, but that shows Finishing the Hat was probably thought too rarified; instead he does Buddy's Blues, which he first assassinated in a concert performance of Follies and which he makes as agonizingly meaningless now as he did then.

But redemption awaits. The final sequence of the show returns to Rodgers and Hammerstein with the magnificent Bench Scene from Carousel. Lu-Pone, heart-stoppingly still and sure, transforms herself into the young Julie Jordan; though Patinkin, who's actually younger, looks and sounds more like her father than her lover --OK for South Pacific but creepy here. However the scene, one of the hottest in musicals, remains magic. Doing still more of Carousel counts, I think, as gilding the lily; it seems for a terrifying moment as if Patinkin might be embarking on the entire Soliloquy. But he doesnt; he does Youll Never Walk Alone, which usually reduces me to a blubbering wreck and did so again. Then there's the encore, bringing Irving Berlin's contrapuntal classic Youre Just in Love and Coffee in a Cardboard Cup, a Patinkin standby that sounds even better when the two of them are going at it in fast and furious unison. And then theres the encore to the encore when, with the house lights up and the mikes off, they sing a standard apiece. Tony Bennett does the unplugged thing at the end of his shows, but they go him one better by being not only unamplified but a capella. You've got to love that. And maybe they do it every performance but it felt spontaneous on Tuesday night.
