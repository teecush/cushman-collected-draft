Dracula

Avon Theatre, Stratford, Ont.

One of God's young Englishmen travels to Transylvania to meet a sinister nobleman. He imprudently reveals that he has a beautiful fiancee back home, and that his friend has another. His host metaphorically licks his lips, and takes off to meet the young ladies and suck their blood. He has his way with one, but she is posthumously rescued from his clutches by approved stake-driving methods. So he turns his attention to the other, whose fate is touch and go. (Will he touch? Will she go?) Clap your hands if you believe in vampires.

Its hard to tell the story of Dracula while keeping a straight face, and most recent dramatizations of the Bram Stoker novel have accordingly been campy frolics. The new Stratford musical however, written and directed by Richard Ouzounian, wants very much to be taken seriously. It gets its wish. The first-night audience was manifestly held, and though standing ovations on these occasions are de rigueur, this one seemed genuine.

My own response was that a high level of craftsmanship had been expended on a very thin story. For me, this is one horror-fantasy that doesnt resonate. We can all sympathize with Jekyll if not with Hyde, and with Frankenstein as well as with his monster. We know what a tough life the Phantom of the Opera has had. We don't have to believe in damnation in order to appreciate the dilemma of Faust.

Dracula, though, doesn't have a dilemma. Once bitten, he incessantly bites. The fact that he prefers to get his fangs into nubile young women gives his career a certain sexual charge, but for real erotic impact we would have to know something about the people (for want, in the Counts case, of a better word) who are involved.

We dont. Dracula, by the time we meet him, is mere malevolent menace, undeviating and uncomplicated. His victims, Mina and Lucy, are indistinguishable Victorian young ladies in white dresses, while their lovers and protectors are similarly cardboard honourable young men. The Unliving are pitted against the Undead.

Music is among the most effective of dramatic aids when it comes to keeping things creepy, and the narrative conventions of a musical help greatly in moving a story along. The unfolding of this one is measured but smooth.

There are other things that songs in a musical can do, but this show has to pass them up. Humour is outlawed, since its presence would undermine the entire enterprise. For exploration of character, see above. As for emotion: Well, it depends what you mean. I'm out of step with what passes for passion in most current musicals. The convention is that a character declares his or her devotion with lung-bursting fervour, ending on a sustained high note and with arms outstretched; the audience then falls down and renders homage. (Don't they expect singers to be able to sing?) Its noticeable that when the hero, at this shows crucial moment, proclaims his love for the heroine, he looks fixedly at us before sparing a glance for her.

Dracula is billed as a chamber musical, and it has only seven actors and five musicians. This enables the composer, Marek Norman, to provide his own spare and stylish orchestrations; the accompaniments, specializing in one-note figures that tantalize like water-drops on stone, are often more compelling than the actual melodies, which lack curves; they have the idea of romance but not its contours.

It's a politely lush sound, as if Andrew Lloyd Webber had stuck the score for Aspects of Love on to the story of The Phantom. It is the same story (except that this monster doesn't even offer his prey free singing lessons) and its much the same style, but the reduction in scale makes it seem less blatant.

Ouzounians lyrics have syllables that fit and rhymes that rhyme and they avoid obvious cliches. (Well, most of the time. Consider this quatrain: You and I are lovers / I've known it since we met / With so much to remember / And nothing to forget.) But after appreciating their negative virtues you begin looking for positive ones, and find only a pervasive literalness. The song-titles clue you in: I Will Shelter You, Let the Night Begin, The Blood Is the Life and that old suspenseful favourite Just One More Night.

"What," asked a friend afterward, do you call a number where half a dozen people sing different things at the same time?

"A first-act finale," I replied. Here, it's called The Last Light of the Sun. Theyre all Big Numbers for Big Moments, and its a relief when the authors try something more subtle and sidelong. This is a sinister faux folk-song called The Spider and the Fly, performed by a mental patient who, like a certain old lady, is eating the first as a chaser for the second. Benedict Campbell plays him, with cringing demonic effectiveness.

His is the only performance about which I can think of anything to say. The others are proficiently played, but you know, as in all such shows, they would come out the same whoever the actors were, as long as they could stand up straight and hit the notes. Juan Chioran is the lank-haired Count with the funny foreign voice, Michael Fletcher his learned and equally guttural nemesis, and Roger Honeywell, June Crowley, Amy Walsh and Shawn Wright the long- suffering lovers.

Visually, the production is a treat. Douglas Paraschuk's sets are framed by a glistening pair of mirrored walls, constantly parting and converging. The furnishings that spring up between them are spare but beautifully evocative; projections do the rest. Dracula is spectacular: not the kind of rhyme the show goes in for, but one that its staging at least deserves.
