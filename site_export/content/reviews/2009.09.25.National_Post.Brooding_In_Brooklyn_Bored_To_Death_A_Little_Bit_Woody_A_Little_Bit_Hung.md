There was a nifty bit of free association at the start of the first episode of Bored to Death. Jonathan, the protagonist, was in the graphic process of losing his girlfriend. A moving van was taking their bed away, and her with it. Despondent in his newly solitary Brooklyn apartment he spied, in a pile of books littering the floor, Raymond Chandler's Farewell My Lovely. Ouch, he doubtless thought, and we with him. But it didn't end there. Jonathan had apparently nursed a secret ambition to be Philip Marlowe, and next thing we knew he was at his computer, placing an ad on Craigslist, offering his services as a private detective: "unlicensed," he was honest enough to point out. Whether this admission made it legal on something about which his friends, and especially his friendly neighbourhood police officers, turn out to have their doubts.

New and desperate career paths seem to be the new staple of TV shows. We've had the hero of Hung, branching out as a male prostitute, and the two heroes of Flight of the Conchords. Bored to Death isn't as consistently hilarious as Flight of the Conchords, but it's still the funniest thing to come along since. Jonathan differs from his predecessors in that he isn't doing it for the money. He already has a job, working for a glossy magazine editor, played by Ted Danson. Beyond this, he's the author of one apparently successful novel. In this he resembles his author and first-and-last-namesake Jonathan Ames. Jason Schwartzman fits the part perfectly.

When Jonathan's beloved dumps him, the heavy labour is carried out by a company called Moishe's Moving. It's staffed by Israelis, and Jonathan expresses surprise at seeing them engaged in heavy manual labour. "What are you," they ask, "another self-hating New York Jew?" "Guess I am," Jonathan

says with one of the gentle (obviously not Gentile) sighs, that we will soon recognize as being his default mode of expression. Jonathan is in fact an updated Woody Allen, prone to self-diagnoses like "I'm not good with anger; I go straight to depression." He's less intellectually prickly than Woody, and less indiscriminately libidinous; in fact he seems to be a one-woman man. A running theme of the first three episodes, and presumably of those beyond, is his attempt to win back his ex.

Jonathan is contrasted with his unkempt and burly friend Ray (Zach Galifianakis), whose lady, a vegan environmentalist, hasn't left him but whom Ray seems to think may as well have done. She's withholding intimacy until he cleans up his slovenly act, which seems like one of the grand-daddies of all lost causes. Jonathan's scenes with Ray are among the joys of the show so far. When we finally meet her in the third episode, Ray's girlfriend, so overwhelmingly caring she freezes the blood, turns out to be very funny as well.

I haven't said much about Jonathan's private-eye activities, because I don't think they work very well. For the comedy, that is -- obviously they don't work well for Jonathan himself, as that's meant to be the point. He does get a surprising number of cases, but it's probably no coincidence that much the best of the initial trio is Episode 3, in which the detective activities are mentioned but not shown. This is also the episode that makes full use of Danson, playing a full-blown Manhattan neurotic who, immediately after describing an ex-wife as "the most orgasmic woman I've ever met," encounters her at a party on the arm of his successor. He's miffed, reasonably, since she looks like Glenn Close while the man who's dispossessed him looks, and behaves, like Oscar Levant. Danson also says --incredibly, since he himself looks taller and more impressively white-maned than ever -- that he envies Jim Jarmusch for his good hair. Jarmusch plays himself and gets to spend one entire scene biking around what seems to be his personal gym; he's interested in having Jonathan revise the script of his next movie and tells him, "You must really suffer from the dark clarity of your vision." (He's read Jonathan's book.) As if that wasn't enough, Jonathan has also to subject himself to a psychotherapist who's said to fancy himself "the Carl Jung of Brooklyn." The shrink dismisses every one of Jonathan's problems with a brisk hand-clap and tells him that, "People don't change. We simply become more compatible with our core misery." This is a patchy show, but the best patches are princely. - Bored to Death airs Sunday at 9:30 p. m. on HBO Canada.
