AWAKE AND SING

Equity Showcase Theatre, Toronto

Clifford Odets' Awake and Sing is a landmark play that still lands. It was written in 1935 and was the defining production of that fabled New York troupe the Group Theater. The Group transformed American drama and later infiltrated American film by combining the political with the aesthetic. There had been other champions of ensemble performance and of socially conscious plays, but the two concerns had never come together in this fashion. This being the Depression, there was a lot to be socially conscious about. There were also whole areas of American society that had hardly been put on stage before, except for laughs. Odets, an actor, a socialist and Jewish, just pushing 30 when he wrote the play, was, quite literally, the playwright the Group, and the times, had been looking for.

Awake and Sing is set in the apartment of the Berger family in the Bronx. Nominal head of the house is Myron, who once went to law school but has made nothing of it. Effective head is his wife Bessie, and she is very effective. She is especially keen on controlling the lives of her children. When daughter Hennie gets pregnant by a man who promptly disappears, Bessie makes sure that she marries a helpless admirer whom Hennie doesn't love and for whom she makes very few allowances. Son Ralph meanwhile has fallen in love with a girl whom we never meet, largely because Bessie has forbidden her from the house. The family has one comparatively prosperous member, the small-time capitalist Uncle Morty.

The political content, though, is more in the air than on the stage; we can link the oppression of the junior Bergers to what's going on in the world outside but only one character makes explicit connections, and much of his fire has gone out. This is Jacob, Bessie's father, an Eastern European of the old radical school, who still rails against the bosses and the politicians, but whose ideals are regarded by most of the others as a joke; he divides his time between grumbling, bestowing sad admonitions on his grandchildren, and retiring to his room to play Caruso records. The cast is completed by wiseguy Moe, a wooden-legged casualty of the First World War and now a bootlegger; we gather that he once pressed his attentions on Hennie, with some success, and that he would like to press them again.

You want the good news? The play fulfils its own Biblical injunction; it is awake and, nearly 60 years on, it still sings. There are is some melodramatic twists, which we happily swallow, and there are some lyrical flights, which we don't Odets has some very strange ideas, that may be ideologically mandated, about what constitutes a happy ending, and he overwrites like mad as if to convince himself that he believes in what he's saying.

Most of the time, though, he operates through humour used as a weapon. His Jewish-American dialogue was recognized at the time to be the best that Broadway had heard, and it has hardly been bettered since. It isn't Neil Simon (too serious) and it isn't Arthur Miller (too funny) but it probably made both of them possible. Only David Mamet, with his deadly crazy flights of fancy, has gone beyond it.

You want the bad news? One wants to scream the title at the current, horribly inadequate Toronto revival. It's mounted by an almost new organization calling themselves the Co., one of whose two moving spirits is Dean Gabourie, a young director with a respectable track record. For the first few minutes, which find most of the Bergers simmering at the kitchen table, it looks as if he will know how to orchestrate the play, and in particular how to adjust its volume controls. The promise is not fulfilled. This is a tin-eared production, with one unfortunate distinction; it gives us a Jewish family that apparently has never learned to shout.

It does operate under a number of handicaps. One is the theatre; the upstairs auditorium at Equity Showcase is an echoing church- hall. (There's a basement, which most other shows use; it's no treat to sit in, but it does allow for concentration.) The acoustical problems are compounded here by a topless setting of bits and pieces. The production seems very keen on re-creating the world just outside the action; we get a soundtrack of period news and period music, and lines of washing hang from the ceiling above us; for some reason the auditorium is drenched in gloom, even when the play is not in progress. We get to see grandpa Jacob poking about his room in silhouette. None of this extraneous stuff compensates for the lack of life at the centre.

Hardly any of the actors are up to their roles. Their indvidual inadequacies rebound on one another, so that the show always looks as well as sounds wrong; people are always hanging around the stage in awkward positions, or hugging the walls, or facing off as if they know they are supposed to be angry with one another but have no idea why. Hennie and Moe play their altercations as a series of unmodulated snarls; it's impossible to believe that there could ever have been any attraction between them.

Poor downtrodden Myron spends one whole scene with his head to one side and cupped in his hand, as if he had a toothache; this might work in a boldly stylized production (though I doubt that's what the play needs) but here it just looks strange.

A few performances have their fits and starts, but the only one to come consistently to life is the rebellious Ralphie of Aaron Willis. Conceivably he's helped by playing close to his own age, but there is a current running through him. It's easy to sympathize with his sufferings at the hands of his mother; here, indeed. it's all too easy. Bessie Berger, as written, is one of the great Jewish matriarchs; a monster, perhaps, but a force of nature. She's gone through a lot to keep this family together. Danielle Bourgon plays her, irritatingly and often inaudibly, as a mere whiner. She, or her director, or both, seem deaf to irony, deaf to anger, deaf to rhythm; when Bessie rounds on her son, reminding him of their poverty, and says "I bought a new dress every week? A lover I kept?" you would swear the actress believes that the woman really did these things. The question marks she couldn't hear? There have, I think, been cuts; I have seen one other production of this play and my principal memory of it is hearing Bessie mow down all dissenting voices with the repeated scornful phrase "another county heard from!" In this performance I heard that just once and even then I barely caught it. If we cannot both respect Bessie and resent her, the play crumbles. The best that can be said for this show is that makes you want to see someone else (Soulpepper possibly, the Shaw Festival definitely) tackle the play.
