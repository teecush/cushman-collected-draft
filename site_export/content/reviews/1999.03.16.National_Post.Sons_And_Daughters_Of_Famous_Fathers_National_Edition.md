THREE DAYS OF RAIN

Canadian Stage Theatre, Toronto

There have been plays before in which the same actors have played different generations of one family, and there have been plays in which the action has rolled backward from the present to the past. Richard Greenberg's Three Days of Rain, which comes to us from America modestly laurelled, may be the first play to combine these devices, and they set each other off rather well.

Act One finds us in 1995, in a grubby Manhattan apartment inhabited by Walker Janeway, son of a recently deceased and extremely eminent architect. Like many sons of famous fathers, Walker has gone into retreat, in his case quite literally; sometimes he vanishes for weeks on end. His married sister Nan comes looking for him so that they may attend the latest variant of that hallowed dramatic ritual known as the Reading of the Will.

Among their late father's creations was a splendiferous house on Long Island, built for his own parents, and he has bequeathed it, not to his children, but to Pip, the son of his late business partner, Theo.

Pip is an unremarkable but contentedly successful actor. ("I eat chocolate and never gain weight; life is good.") Nothing earth- shaking happens between these three, but in the course of some mildly entertaining ructions, three skeletons lurch out of their respective closets: from childhood, Pip has had to walk on eggshells so as not to upset the super-sensitive Walker; Pip believes that Walker was/is in love with him (no comment from Walker); and Pip and Nan were once lovers.

Like much upscale American playwriting these days, this section suggests an episode of Friends masquerading as drama: not quite serious, not quite funny, and not quite boring. It probably has more wit than is apparent, but in Rosemary Dunsmore's production the timing is off; often, you have to replay the lines in your mind to get the joke.

What does emerge is that the late Ned Janeway was a very chilly father and, probably, colleague. When Walker and Nan discover his journal, Walker, in one of his many fits of pique, destroys it. "Now we'll never know anything," moans Nan. Which leads us, with an anticipatory thump, into Act Two.

This, set in 1960, is another world, though it's the same room: at this date, the young Ned's living and working space. The actors who were previously the sons are now the fathers; the daughter turns into her own mother, by the name of Lina.

There are shocks. Ned, whom one had pictured as a monstrous authoritarian, a Yankee equivalent of Ibsen's tyrannical master builder, turns out to be a whole other kind of distant: gauche, shy, and with a stammer. Theo has more social graces but is haunted by doubts of his own abilities. Lina shifts sexual allegiance in a scene that has already been craftily pre-figured -- or, given the time scheme, post-echoed.

All the characters, in both age groups, are bursting with high or middlebrow cultural references, and in act one, Pip has described for us an archetypal Broadway matinee play of the Sunday in New York variety: The kind in which the girl comes out of the rain from the hero's apartment and gets into a robe and they "dance around one another." It turns out that Ned and Lina have performed this choreography in real life, and that their rapturous routine lasted for the three nights of the title, at the end of which Theo walked in.

So it's possible that it was guilt that prompted Ned to leave the house to Theo's son. One also notes that while Theo was crippled by the thought of being untalented, Pip can live with it easily.

By throwing away the journal Walker has, once again, walked away, this time from any chance of exchanging his chilly illusions about his parents for the somewhat warmer reality. The sins of the fathers are not visited upon the children; they're invented by them. The play has its share of glib ironies, but it winds up worthy of respect.

The acting, too, improves with age. As Walker, Michael McManus is your standard languid commentator, happier talking to the audience than to the other characters; as Ned, he blossoms, apparently liberated by his stutter and building a character out of it. Paul Eves, alternately wooden and amusing as Pip, puts on a good show of desperation as Theo.

Only Camilla Scott seems thoroughly out of her depth. As Nan, rebuked by her brother for her "suburban alacrity" (the adjective at least is right), she is drab and toneless; as Lina, she has rather more energy, but most of it goes into maintaining her accent, which is Southern and very deep-fried.
