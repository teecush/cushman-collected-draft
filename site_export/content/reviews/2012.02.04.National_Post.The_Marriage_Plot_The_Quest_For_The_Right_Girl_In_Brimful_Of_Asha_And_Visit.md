It's hard not to warm to a play whose author-director, who is also one half of the cast, greets you by name when you walk in and introduces you to his mother, who's the other half, and then proceeds to offer you a samosa. I'm not saying that any show that gives me this kind of welcome will automatically get a rave review. I'm just, as they say, saying.

A Brimful of Asha is about a family's desire to get their son married, preferably to someone of their selection, and about the son's obstinate preference for waiting until he's ready and has found someone for himself. The son, born 32 years ago in Canada to immigrant parents from India, is Ravi Jain, a professional theatrical here playing himself. His mother, similarly playing herself but making her first appearance on any stage, is Asha Jain. They have, in a small way, recreated their own kitchen in one corner of the Tarragon's Extra Space, or at least their own kitchen table. Asha sits at it; Ravi strolls or paces around it and, at one point, perches on it, for which impertinence his mother sternly rebukes him. She did this, when I saw the show, with such apparent spontaneity that I would like to believe it was a one-timeonly occurrence, but I have reason to believe that it wasn't.

While walking, Ravi talks. He tells us about a trip he took to India, some half-dozen years ago, principally to teach a theatre workshop in Calcutta, but also to travel around the country seeing new places, all his previous visits having been to his family's hometown of New Delhi. He was surprised, and more than a little disconcerted, to discover that his parents had planned their annual journey home to coincide with him. They had already, via the Internet, tracked down an eligible young lady in Bombay who would be just right for him. When he grudgingly agreed to meet her, he was alarmed to find his father insistent on travelling with him to perform the introductions. As it turned out, Ravi and his enforced date got on rather well, if only because she had no more interest in marrying him than he had in marrying her.

That, however, was not the end of it. It seems that, wherever he travelled, his extended family found means of throwing the two young people together. If it wasn't his family, it was hers. Often, it was both together: Ravi has stories to tell of gatherings in which tablesful of people discussed him and his career and marital prospects as if he wasn't there (and in a sense, since he could understand Hindi but not fluently speak it, he wasn't). And when his parents finally gave up on this particular girl, they found him another one of the same name.

Ravi recounts all this in a tone of aggrieved amusement. His co-star Asha meanwhile sits serenely and offers comments, most of them variations on the age-old question, one that seemingly crosses all cultural boundaries, "Why won't they listen to their mothers?" Her own marriage, she points out, was arranged, and it was also a happy one; still is, in fact. She makes a disarmingly good case, one to which even Ravi, as he tells us, was at one point inclined to give in. Or maybe he just felt beaten down; well actually, there was no maybe about it. In the end, though, they agree to differ. Or at least they agree enough to be here together, tonight and for a limited season.

It's a charming show, but it's also monotonous. Ravi's story necessarily consists of the same thing happening over and over again. More importantly, the dynamic between the two performers remains unchanged throughout. Asha's theatrical inexperience can also be a handicap, especially when it comes to audibility, though nobody could say she lacks for personality and, to be fair, raising her voice would go against the grain of her character. And she does prove capable of re-enacting a screaming phone conversation with her errant son, proving his own implicit claim that she is indeed a performing natural.

Visiting Mr. Green, presented by the Harold Green Jewish Theatre, touches on some similar themes and is also a two-hander for two generations, though in this case the representatives aren't relatives. Also in this case, the older performer is the furthest thing from a theatrical novice. He is, in fact, the 87-year-old Theodore Bikel, playing a character slightly younger than himself: an orthodox Jewish New Yorker, recently widowed and even more recently almost knocked down by a car while crossing the street. The errant motorist has been courtordered to spend every Thursday evening for the next six months calling on his near-victim and attending to his needs: a case of making the community service fit the crime. Bikel, as the visited Mr. Green, is a marvel to see and even more to hear, freshening the stockest of lines, situations and character traits. An arch-traditionalist who can be sharp or dense according to the author's requirements, he has barely cooked and never cleaned since his wife's death; their marriage was not an arranged one - they were both, like Ravi Jain, second-generation immigrants - but, at least by Mr. Green's own account, it was as happy as Asha's. Ross, the visitor, tidies up and brings kosher takeout. Mutual suspicion gradually gives way to mutual affection.

The play has two surprises to spring. The first, so unsurprising that it begs to be spoiled, is that Ross is gay. Since he is also Jewish, though secular, Mr. Green refuses to believe it (how long has he lived in New York?) and, again like Asha if for rather different reasons, tells him that he only has to find the right girl. Ross talks him through Sexual Tolerance 101, apparently for our benefit as much as Mr. Green's, though it's hard to conceive of any theatre audience that would need it, either now or in 1996 when Jeff Baron's play premiered. The date at least explains why Ross, a high-flyer with American Express, doesn't have a cellphone. Mr. Green's phone is rotary and disconnected, except that it does connect with the second surprise, which concerns Mr. Green's past and which I didn't see coming, though I kicked myself afterwards. The play then goes back to being predictable, up to and including the final curtain. For what it is, it's efficiently written; Aidan deSalaiz as Ross is good if a bit forced; and Bikel is as good and as unforced as any actor could possibly be. I have far more respect for A Brimful of Asha as a piece of theatre but I have to confess that I enjoyed Visiting Mr. Green more, or at least that it held my attention more easily. I am not proud of this.
