Some people believe that the fourth and final act of Chekhov's Three Sisters is the high-water mark of modern drama. And it might be if it weren't for the existence of the act that comes before it. Act 4 is a succession of personal Calvaries, shot through with laughter that's sometimes bitterly ironic and occasionally just plain funny. You could hardly ask for more. But in Act 3 you get it; you witness the ascent up the mountain, a process that involves hope and despair, depending on whom you're looking at; one person's triumph involves another's defeat and both, you know at the time, are going to be transitory.

That at least is how it goes in Martha Henry's amazing new production at Stratford, a triumph of what I can best call personal interlocking. It's the scene of the town fire. The house of the three Prozorov sisters has become an impromptu centre for disaster relief. Everybody they know in their provincial town drifts in and then out, among them the drunken old army doctor Chebutykin, confessing as so often before that he's forgotten all the medicine he ever knew. This time, though, it isn't a joke; he's killed a patient. James Blendick is an actor who can seem trapped in his own orotund delivery, and there are moments in this show when he's too much the one-man chorus. But he rises superbly to this, his great moment of truth-telling, and especially to the twice-repeated allegation that Natasha, the shrewish wife of the sisters' feckless brother is -- in Susan Coyne's live-wire adaptation -- "having it off with Protopopov," the unseen town councillor who seems to control everyone's destiny, including that of the man he's cuckolding.

The doctor's audience includes the schoolteacher Kuligin, who, in Peter Hutt's performance, is visibly but ambiguously shocked: Perhaps it's because he is easily shockable, perhaps it's because he's come to suspect that his own wife, the middle sister, Masha, is having it off with someone, or soon will be. And present, too, is the very someone, the philandering and philosophizing Colonel Vershinin. When Masha enters, there ensues the most extraordinary seduction scene. Tom McCamus has played Vershinin as a man whose word-spinning, though it may originally have been born of conviction, also comes in very handy as an erotic weapon. He now uses it quite consciously as such, drawing off his boots as he talks about mankind's terrible present and glorious feature; Lucy Peacock's Masha, taking a conscious decision to be hypnotized, removes her own shoes and then her shawl. That's as far as it goes, but it's enough to detonate Masha's admission to her sisters -- who don't want to hear it -- that she loves Vershinin and her headlong departure, we assume, to his bed.

As in any first-rate Chekhov production, everything has roots, everything has consequences. The aftermath for Masha and Vershinin is an enforced farewell that McCamus plays as the superficial mugged by the tragic, and Peacock, who these last few years has become a great actress, plays with a feral despair so abandoned -- in both senses -- that you can hardly believe you're watching it. She played Masha at Stratford 20 years ago, so she's strictly too old for it, but she makes that work for her; Masha, with her rage and her boredom and her lightning changes of mood, is a drama queen who knows it: Hedda Gabler with a human face. (She always wears black, and everyone knows what that means in Chekhov.)

Irene Poole's Olga is her own kind of ageless, with an unusual edge to her long-sufferingness; when Hutt's Kuligin, indomitably well-meaning, says that he should have married her, she responds with a wince that could mean "I wish" or "horrors" but probably the latter. Dalal Badr has fewer technical resources than her fellows, but her Irina is a very honest account of an anti-ingenue. She isn't "radiant" as Olga says, and she too knows it. She's a study in settling for progressively less; her longing for Moscow less heartache than toothache. Outside the sisterhood, too, everyone has a developing biography. Kelli Fox's Natasha progresses by easy stages from outsider to virago, never suspecting her own conduct; Juan Chioran's Solyony, desperate for acceptance, wins some sympathy for his sinister solitude before it turns murderous. Two male studies in failure wring the heart: Gordon S. Miller's waster Andrei, delivering his visions of the future to a baby that probably isn't his, and Sean Arbuckle's Baron, adjusting the cushions on his chair before embarking on an oration, and desperately seeking a word of love from a fiancee who can't give it to him. I've never wished more devoutly for a supernatural rewrite that might avert his fate. Then there are the small-part seniors: Joyce Campion's octogenarian nurse (the only character who gets a happy ending -- maybe Chekhov is just telling us to wait) and Robert King's uncomprehending Ferapont who at one point, through a delicious invention it would be unfair to reveal, gets the stage to himself.

Performances apart, Henry's direction is superb, alternating huddled scenes with lonely spacious ones and making a tour de force of the abortive second-act revelry; the intimate Tom Patterson stage, of course, increased the impact sixfold. There have been other productions of Three Sisters that I've hugely admired, but none that has involved me as deeply with the characters as this one. It's one of the great evenings in the Canadian theatre. And I expect it will get richer.
