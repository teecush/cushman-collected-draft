THEATRE REVIEW

Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf ?

Young Centre for the Performing Arts

It's common knowledge, verging on folklore, that Edward Albee's Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf ? begins with George and Martha, history professor and wife, surname unknown, returning home in the small hours from a faculty party; and with Martha surveying their living room and exclaiming "what a dump!" She's quoting Bette Davis from a movie and in most productions, including the tremendous new one from Soulpepper, she does a Davis imitation. Nancy Palk's Martha, however, doesn't leave her mimicry there. She is a fountain of quick-change voices -- taunting, teasing, commanding -- until she finds her own voice, the one that underlies them all, which is lost and terrified.

This vocal virtuosity proves liberating for the play, the character and for the actress who gives the performance of her career. Make that the loud performance of her career; she's been very fine in quiet, caring roles such as the mother in Our Town. In more extrovert or domineering roles, she's sometimes seemed effortful. But here everything flows as freely as the booze the characters never stop downing, and at full throttle. Palk, playing one of American drama's great wisecrackers, is more confidently funny than ever before:Whiplash sarcastic at one moment, lankily languid -- sometimes self-mockingly,

sometimes not -- the next. Her bite and buoyancy help make her attractive; for once, when immersed in Albee's extended marital bloodbath, one sides, at least initially, with Martha rather than George, who usually attracts sympathy just for the amount he has to put up with. Their reciprocal baiting reaches its height, or its depths, when Martha ostentatiously makes out with Nick, the young chemistry professor who's been invited over with his wife, Honey, for an early morning of after-party partying. George's reaction is to bury his nose in a book, his imperturbability carefully calibrated, in Diego Matamoros' performance, to inflict maximum hurt.

The turning point, at the start of the third and last act, is a soliloquy for Martha that's not so much post-coital as noncoital, Nick having proved a disappointment. This is her cue to begin a harrowing downward spiral, for her and George (a team, whatever else) to reform ranks, and for him to take over. Up till now, Matamoros has been the irresistibly cool counter-puncher to her red-hot hammer-blows; he's also been the picture of a defeated man, "one of the biggest bogs in the history department" whose elegantly wielded words are his only remaining weapon. He now becomes both his wife's scourge and her salvation. The issue here is his demolition of a lie: This childless couple's imaginary son. I have always found both this fantasy and its destruction too cut-and-dried to be convincing but here it is, at least, very powerful. Albee subtitled this act The Exorcism, and for once it does have that ceremonial quality: a necessary rite that in Matamoros' hands starts cruel and finishes compassionate. One of its effects is to drive the intruders from the house (another exorcism). George and Martha are left together in an ending that can seem sentimental ( "Who's afraid of Virginia Woolf ?" "I am, I am.") but is here very moving, with the two actors -- old colleagues after all, but seldom teamed before--attaining a rare degree of communion.

The play juxtaposes, and exposes, two barren marriages. While George and Martha delight in stripping their marital scars in company, Nick and Honey have theirs dragged, cajoled or threatened out of them -- and they're at least equally grievous. As Nick, envied and mocked by George as the wave of the scientific future, Tim Campbell is perfect: broad-shouldered, self-satisfied, his limited sympathies subsiding as his insecurities increase. The recurring description of Diana Donnelly's Honey as "slim-hipped" here seems like just another of the characters' games but she beautifully registers the trophy bride's progress from delicious shock to shock absolute. Fine as all four performances are individually, the real prize of Diana Leblanc's production is the phenomenal degree of contact between them. There is never a moment at which either action or reaction goes slack, and both the pacing and the placing are impeccable. Astrid Janson's gaunt, precipitous set is a suitably gladiatorial arena, with a couple of oddities. The books are mostly painted, suggesting that even the household's literacy is suspect, and the shelves are adorned with pictures of George and Martha's namesakes, the Washingtons: Is this the production getting cute or are the characters? I still have doubts as to whether the play signifies much beyond its very enjoyable self. But that self is substantial: dialogue that stings, action that builds, characters who've become archetypes and -- it now appears -- compassion. In its 11 years, Soulpepper has given us any number of good productions and a handful of great ones. This is one of the greats. - Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf ? is in repertory through Oct. 24 at the Young Centre.
