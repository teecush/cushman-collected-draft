THE LEAGUE OF NATHANS

Factory Theatre, Toronto

Whatever its other flaws, Jason Sherman's play The League of Nathans, 11 years old and now revived under its author's direction, hasn't dated. Its characters are Canadian Jews, and the focus of its attention is the Middle East. It deals, that is, with feelings and situations that have worsened, grown harder and more acute, but have not essentially changed.

There are three men who were boys together in Toronto in the 1970s: Nathan Isaacs, known as Izzy; Nathan Glass, known as Glass; and Nathan Abramowitz, known -- uniquely -- as Nathan. Conceivably Izzy gets nickname-status because he's a joker and something of a joke, Glass gets surname recognition because he's sombre and tough, and Nathan stays Nathan because he's too vaguely characterized for people to have any other kind of handle on him. Their friendship is sealed in a field; the three of them gather there one night in 1973 to pledge reciprocal allegiance in the kind of parody religious ceremony only possible to those who, at some level, believe. At their ages, 13 and each on the verge of bar mitzvah, well-brought- up Jewish boys really don't have any other option, however much they may resent it.

They talk, with teenaged flippancy and teenaged pugnacity, about Israel, or at least about the things they've heard from parents and teachers; about how Jews and Arabs treat, or might treat, one another, and about who has the right. Attitudes are beginning to take shape. Glass floats militant Zionism as the only safeguard against another Holocaust; Abramowitz says those who have been persecuted should be the last to persecute. This seminal scene is fluently written and staged, the boys' minds, bodies and personalities bouncing off one another, and merging closely enough to create a group identity. Most of the play's first half is lively; it switches constantly between past and present (which for these purposes is 1993) in a counterpoint that's usually bracing and never confusing.

The league of Nathans splinters, as far as we can tell, when Glass visits Israel and falls apart when he gets back. He is involved in a deadly attack on an Arab attendant at a Canadian gas station. He was, or so he tells Abramowitz before escaping back to Israel, a witness but not a participant. Now, 17 years later, all three Nathans find themselves summoned, from east and west, to a reunion to be held at a disused synagogue, of medieval origin, in Toledo, Spain.

The play then goes the way of the friendship, right off the rails. It seems unlikely that all three men would agree to keep this date; and belief is strained even further when we learn, as a surprise, who set the whole thing up. All this matters less, though, than the falsity of the supposed moral showdown between Abramowitz and Glass. When Nathan G. told Nathan A. about the gas station killing, he had made out that he was inside the car, watching two buddies actually commit the murder and scared to intervene; almost his first words were "it wasn't my fault, Nathan, I didn't do it." Now, hardened by years on the West Bank, he glories in the deed, claiming that it had rid the world of another potential Jew-killer. Such a transition is certainly not impossible, even if you reject the implicit assertion that all Israeli settlers subscribe to the ethical standards of Rabbi Meir Kahane. The problem is that neither of the Nathans seems to recognize there has been a transition. Nor, we must assume, does the author, unless he was hoping nobody would notice.

Glass never actually goes back on his story; what he does, in alternating blasts, is a) blame Abramowitz for not having offered him moral support at the time, though from the scene as we saw it, Glass was in too much of a hurry to have had time to accept any; and b) taunt him for not having lived up to his humanist principles by turning Glass in. (Why should he, if he believed Glass' protestations of innocence? He isn't a cop.) After pausing to sneer at his friend's "goyische wife"(ex-friend probably, ex-wife certainly) Glass asks him what, with all his high morality, he has achieved in his life. Nathan-who-is-called-Nathan collapses in a corner, whimpering in agony "I haven't done anything."

Michael Schultz, playing the beleaguered one, makes this moment very effective, though as his tormentor Rami Posner, required to be constantly on the attack, has been getting increasingly hoarse and forced. But the effectiveness is just that: an effect, and isolated. We certainly haven't seen Nathan the Good do anything, but who knows what he might have been up to between the short, sharp scenes? It's hard to make inaction register as a dramatic fact. The play leaves gaps in its development, and then blames the characters for them.

Abramowitz does do one thing: he writes, re-workings of Biblical stories. He inherited this habit, along with his sense of Jewish moral obligation, from his grandfather who used to tuck him up with mildly humorous elaborations of tales from the Old Testament. These tended to be supposedly redemptive fables with a destructive edge: Lot in Sodom, Noah's Ark. Nathan, progressing, has been trying his hand at a narrative of pure reconciliation: Joseph and his brothers. The end of the play comes when he finds an end to the story, thus occasioning the kind of hopeful finale with which outwardly cynical playwrights like to show that they really have hearts. (David Hare, in England, pulls this trick all the time.) The play's final statement seems to be: Reaching out to people is better than killing them. Few are going to quarrel with that, but I these characters don't fill it out much.

But there is, of course, a third Nathan, and he's the best thing in the play, Izzy is. Certainly, he seems so as played here by Ben Carlson, for several years the best youngish actor at the Shaw Festival and now -- after Swollen Tongues and this -- possibly the best in the entire Toronto catchment-area. Carlson is both solid and mercurial, an invaluable combination; he establishes his characters so firmly and so economically they can fly off in any direction and take you with them. Izzy is the conventional member of the league. He got married, went to run his father-in-law's factory in Winnipeg because it was the easy and expected thing to do, became a pillar of his community and bought bonds for Israel for much the same reason. If there's a Yiddish equivalent for homme moyen sensuel, he's it. He's shallow but he's also sincere, what he feels he really feels, if only for a moment. He can be equally moved by the picture of a maimed Palestinian girl and by the glimpse of a concentration-camp tattoo on the wrist of a man in the Spanish synagogue; the latter sight prompts an access of religious feeling that sends him, in the play's most resonant moment, searching for skullcaps for his and his friends' previously uncovered heads.

It's hard to believe Glass -- who refers to him bitingly as "that very funny man, Mr. Isaacs" -- can ever have had much time for him, but you can understand Izzy would have clung to both the other guys. Of all the characters in the half-dozen Sherman plays I've seen, Isaacs comes closest to be being a flesh-and-blood person. No, that's mean; he doesn't come close, he is.

A fourth actor, Ken James, doubles, rather too effortfully, as the grandfather and as another older Jewish man whom the Nathans encounter both on the plane to Spain and in the shul when they get there. He interrogates them about their places in the world and seems, like the similarly Strange Passenger of Ibsen's Peer Gynt, to function as a free-floating conscience.

The play may not have been overtaken by events, but it has been overshadowed by more recent plays, all of them written by Adam Pettle: Zadie's Shoes presents a fuller portrait of the wandering Jew as comic sage; Misha offers a more chilling picture of a violent Israeli vet; and Sunday Father has more amusing Biblical paraphrases that also form a more revealing dramatic arc (though, to be fair, you get a more detailed discussion in Job, the Hip-Hop Musical.)

Most of those plays are also funnier. Sherman's has the veneer of humour, but only two good jokes, one of which the script chivalrously attributes to Jackie Mason. (The other one is about the flight into Egypt, and it's really good.) Astrid Janson's set of textured walls stands around for the first act looking gaunt and imposing, and really comes into its own as the abandoned synagogue of the second.
