I have no wish to poach on any music critics preserve, but I would like to state that, considered purely as theatre, the Canadian Opera Company's new production of Gotterdammerung is as stunning a feat of staging as I have seen in Toronto. One image alone is enough to make the history books: that of the scheming Hagen standing at the back of the stage, the outline of his bald-eagle head piercing the shadows, while Brunnhilde, alone downstage, unveils her plans for self-immolation. I don't know how one divides the credit for this between direction (Tim Albery), design (Michael Levine) and lighting (David Finn); the collaboration is seamless and inspired.

The production is especially rich in what must, I suppose, be one of the primary Wagnerian skills, that of holding a single picture for a very long time. The palace scenes in this production are set in a high-tech corporate office, around a boardroom table that gets larger, scene by scene, as the power games get nastier. There is a matching power in the way that characters sit impassive in their designer chairs, listening to their rivals emote. When Hagen dreams of his Nibelung father, the two communicate by computer, the screens glowing red in the greyness. A battery of lights burns with a matching red for any scenes involving fire. The horizon is crisscrossed by electronic cables, doubling as the threads of destiny spun by the Fates in the opening scene. Theres even some legitimate humour when the Rhinemaidens turn up as a coy trio of bathing belles.

Not everything works. A truly frightening chorus of men in suits (the Harry Rosen choir, my companion called them) lose some of their impact when they start pounding the table with spears. Staging, synopsis and surtitles taken all together cannot make clear what happens at the triple apocalypse -- one flood, two fires -- of the operas grand finale. But the whole cycle has been an international-sized triumph of which Richard Bradshaw, conducting as dynamically from his general director's office as from the pit, must feel immensely proud. I look forward to the apotheosis in the new opera house with Das Rheingold, the remaining instalment of what, thinking in megamusical terms, we might call The Ring of the Lords.

At the opposite physical extreme of musical theatre the Acting Up company, which made its debut last year with Tick, Tick ... Boom, takes firm steps forward with John and Jen, a sturdier piece in a much superior production. One of the attractions of this two-person American show is that it ought to be terrible but isn't. In the first half, set in the 60s, John and Jen are brother and sister; she, the older, goes off to study in New York and becomes a counterculture warrior, he goes off to fight in Vietnam and becomes dead.

In the second, set in the 80s, Jen and John are mother and son, she having named him after the sibling whose end has left her with a load of unrealized obligation; the permissive teen has become an over-protective adult and her boy, of course, resents it. They fight; in the end they reconcile.

It sounds schematic and it sounds sentimental. In the first act, it seems outrageously neat that one family should provide such an exact microcosm of the split within America. There's even a duet titled You Don't Understand that spells it all out. The catalyst, a vivid offstage presence, is a borderline abusive father against whom Jen revolts while Jon takes him as a model of manhood. (Theres also a mother, but we learn almost nothing about her.) In the second, the positive ending seems, in retrospect, inevitable. But it doesnt seem so at the time. There's a battle going on, a commonplace one certainly, but real, and moment to moment the show rings true. It can't avoid -- doesnt want to avoid -- the cuteness endemic to American musicals about families or the uplift endemic to American musicals about anything, but it sails through them. We dont, after all, see many musicals that focus closely on brothers and sisters or on mothers and sons, and this does both and does it honestly. The first relationship moves from bonding to conflict, the second the other way, and that grand scheme is very effective.

This is almost a through-composed musical, so at moments of high emotion, the characters stop singing and start talking, rather than the traditional other way around. Tom Greenwald's lyrics are unflashy but attentive, with the occasional spark of wry wit; the first Jon complains that though his sister advised him to metaphorically hit a home run, she was never there to see him literally do it. (Her never coming home throughout her years at college is about the hardest thing in the show to believe.) Andrew Lippa's music isnt as melodically rich as his later score for The Wild Party, where he has all the styles of the 1920s to play with, but its a cunning feat of tension and release

The performances, under Michael Jones's direction, are musically and dramatically first-rate. Stephanie Roth is both concerned and abrasive as a woman in whom the two qualities run together, the more so the older she gets. Kyle Blair, the superb jack of last year's Stratford Into the Woods, now plays boyhood again, twice, and he has great fun emphasizing the two Jons different varieties of disgruntlement. He also has a great capacity for playing anger, excitement and hurt; he can be soaring and searing at the same time.

Were often told that the problem with doing satire is that the status quo is a satire in itself. I think it's more that oppositional comedy is an industry, and we're running out of shock. Torontos Second City regularly pays tribute to topicality, generally about the United States, with some kind of punning title, and then proceeds to domestic business as usual. Things are the same back source. In Chicago, the first Second City recently unveiled its 92nd revue, Iraqtile Dysfunction, which has even more merits than the current Toronto show but has nothing to say about Iraq.

Well, almost nothing. I liked a number in which a janitor called Abdul is thoughtlessly branded a terrorist by everyone at his office, except for one kind lady who offers him Girl Scout cookies, and is gratefully told "I like you. Dont come to work on Tuesday." Next thing we know, he's being interrogated.

But that's another scene, largely inhabited by characters from still others. Everyones a running gag. One of them is an ex-CIA agent who subsequently shows up on a PTA committee, and finds that another mother has told her son the date of the Spring Fair, which was supposed to be a secret. (We have two choices. We could re- schedule, but that would be difficult. Well have to shoot him.) A candidate for the priesthood is subjected to a word-association test to see if he's gay. I think that's political; its certainly funny. The show is slick and inventive, with a blessed touch of madness. Even a routine about a married couple forced into conversation by the breakdown of their TV comes up fresh. ("Do you want a drink?" Im an alcoholic. "Is that a yes or a no?")

One performer, a dangerously cherubic naif named Brian Gallivan, is a likely candidate for star-alumnus status. His finest moment comes when, in a wheedling Hispanic accent, he goes through the bag of a member of the audience commenting incredulously on what he finds there. This was a genuine improvization. Believe me -- I stayed for the second show to check.
