NO GREAT MISCHIEF

Tarragon Theatre, Toronto

- - -

Alistair Macleod's No Great Mischief is a novel in the form of an imaginary memoir. This makes it one of the trickiest kinds of novel to adapt for the stage, since such attempts are liable to result in one character doing most of the talking. The problem is exacerbated when, as often happens, the narrator turns out not to be the crucial character -- when he is stuck telling what is essentially someone elses story. The Tarragons treatment of Macleod's book, crafted by David S. Young with the help of the cast, suffers severely from this displaced focus and pays heavily for it.

R.H. Thomson, who does the introspective monologist better than most, appears here as Alexander, fictitious present-day scion of the historical clan Macdonald who moved from Scotland to Cape Breton in the late-18th century. He's quizzical and observant, but that isnt enough to make a protagonist. As a child, Alexander lost both his parents to an accident on the ice; most of his family, including his elder brother Calum, last of a long line of Calum Macdonalds, went down the mines; but Alexander opted for dentistry and now lives and works in Windsor. Calum, who was told at 15 that he was now the man of the house and tried to be, lives in a doss-house in Toronto, the victim of an uncontrollable temper and a violent disposition. He is played by David Fox who, not for the first time, veers between being genuinely powerful and just overpowering. The framing action starts with Alexander paying him a regulation visit, and end with the two of them, at Calums instigation, taking a last fateful drive back to Nova Scotia. Calum is the dynamo of the action, but it's Alexander who gets the stage time. Even when Calum is present he's likely to be acting out what his brother says about him. This happens, most damagingly, at what proves to be the fateful moment in Calums career. In other words, the story is illustrated, but its not dramatized.

And then there's the Curse of Canadian Content, here present in a peculiarly virulent and self-defeating form. If the purpose of Canadian drama is, in the old phrase, to allow us to tell our stories to one another, then the trouble with this story is that it is told rather than shown. Sometimes there's both, but when there is, the telling outweighs the showing.

If one gets brownie points for setting a play in a region of Canada other than that in which its being performed, then its held to be especially virtuous if that region is Cape Breton, because of the musical opportunities. Every member of this cast can play some kind of instrument (Fox plays the spoons) and there is dancing even at the curtain-call, but the all the fiddling and capering do nothing to speed the play along.

Richard Rose's production adheres to the traditional rules for folksy ensemble theatre, with the actors loyally miming livestock and voicing sound-effects. Nancy Palk, the only woman present, has a sharp few moments near the beginning and a sustained vignette near the end, but is wasted in between. Geoffrey Pounsett is charming as somebody referred to, even more delightfully, as "serious grandfather;" and Jody Richardson shakes things up a bit as a shady American relative who makes a surprise appearance, which is good, and an abrupt disappearance which in a play, as opposed to a novel, is simply frustrating.

I started feeling unexpectedly tender toward A Couple of Blaguards, the strictly commercial Irish piece to which I was rude a couple of weeks ago, and which also assumes that we will be fascinated by a family whom it does nothing to bring to life. No Great Mischief may conceivably be more intelligent, and it is unquestionably more serious, but A Couple of Blaguards is, from time to time, funny. And it doesn't behave as if it thinks it's doing us good.
