Arriving with the recondite recommendation of being one of the best Mexican plays of all time," Sabina Bermans Moliere, translated by Shelley Tepperman, is actually set in France, and 17th-century France at that. It's about the rivalry between its title figure, the greatest French comic playwright, and his tragedian counterpart and sometime protege, Jean Racine. Racine narrates the play as an obituary tribute to the deceased Moliere, who famously died onstage while performing in, of all things, The Imaginary Invalid. Racine, obviously one of nature's revisionists, disputes this story, and otherwise seems torn between tearing his competitor down and crying him up. Its like Amadeus fought between equals.

Arriving in Paris as a gauche and prim unknown, Racine has every reason to be jealous of Moliere, whos already disgustingly successful as a dramatist, actor and impresario. Racine despises comedy as a frivolous and probably immoral genre, which puts him fully in agreement with the oddly named, and even more oddly titled, Monseigneur Prefixe, Archbishop of France. (You can be archbishop of a city, not a country.) Historically, Moliere was persecuted by the Church, who took his portrayal of a hypocrite in Tartuffe as an attack on religion and who got the play banned. That's bad enough, but this play's Archbishop makes it personal. It turns out the seduction scene in Tartuffe is actually a re-enactment of the Monseigneurs own attempt to debauch Racines wife, an actress in Moliere's troupe. Besides, the Archbishop disapproves of comedy as taking the mind of the impressionable King Louis XIV off its proper business of making war; I've heard some philistine arguments in my time but this one takes the gateau. He reckons that the royal sinews will be more effectively stiffened by a good dose of Racinian tragic verse and, incredibly enough, hes right. Early on, the Archbishop makes a wager that he can wipe the infuriating smile off of Molieres face by destroying his happiness, which is certainly fragile, given that he's being cuckolded by his young also-actress wife. (He makes a play out of it, of course.) Later, His Grace raises the stakes and goes after Moliere's life, enlisting Racines help to do it. The Churchs view seems to be that if tragedy is the lesser of two evils, it's still an evil, and Racine gets neutered by being given a civil-service job-- though not till after he's succeeded in writing Phedre.

The play switches at impressive speed between past and present, onstage and offstage, pratfalls and heartache, springing constant surprises as it does. It gets a superb production from Richard Rose -- conceivably his best ever. Charlotte Dean designed the revolving and resourceful sets and witty costumes, Rebecca Picherak did the lighting, and there isnt a moment at which the stage isnt both visually and aurally alive. I can't remember a time when the Toronto theatre was so full of good big casts, and this 13-strong company is one of the best, as alive when reacting in groups as when squaring off one-on-one. So it seems churlish to observe that, despite all the fireworks, what's supposed to be the central conflict doesnt catch fire.

Moliere is the title character but Racine is the central one. (Just as Amadeus, named after Mozart, is really about Salieri.) Rick Robertss Racine is the one involved in the play's crises of conscience, in its three-way battles of Church, state and art. Richard McMillan's Moliere is their victim but stands outside them. He has the best lines and the best attitude ("I know, he tells Racine, its more prestigious to make the audience suffer"), but his bedraggled life and triumphant career seem like a self-contained play going on inside the other one. Both actors give superlative performances, but their contest, the plays announced theme, is more spoken than felt.

The intrigues don't quite work either, since the most problematic role, the Archbishop, gets the most problematic performance. As often as Julian Richings extends his robed arms, like a malignant black bat, he remains petty bureaucrat rather than grand conspirator. The rest are a catalogue of excellence: Kyle Horton's inscrutable king, Nancy Palk doubling as his rambling mother and Molieres mother-in-law whos also his virtual first wife, Cara Pifko as the present and provocative Mme Moliere, Richard Binsley as the fabulist La Fontaine, Mike Ross, the production's musical director, periwigged at the harpsichord as the great Lully, Moliere's own MD, and flashing a time-serving grin when required. Just as performance, the evening is a cupful of coups. And, whatever my reservations about the script, the production couldnt be there without it.

I will, though, voice a couple of extra qualms. The translation has its off-moments; why keep calling Moliere a "comic rather than the more authentic and resonant comedian?" And why end with a song celebrating the cutting off of the kings head? Historically, this wasn't to happen until the Louis after next; also historically, this Louis did far more to promote and protect Moliere than the play allows. In theory, writers are free to take what liberties they please with the facts; in practice, truth has an uncomfortable habit of being not only stranger than fiction but more dramatic.
