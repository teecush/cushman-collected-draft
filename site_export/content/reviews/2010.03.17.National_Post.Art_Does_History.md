Joel Greenberg's Studio 180 is our foremost practitioner of what is generally known as "documentary theatre." Its two best productions have been The Laramie Project, which was pretty much a documentary, reconstructing the murder of Matthew Shepard and its aftermath through the recorded words of the actual participants; and Stuff Happens, in which most of the words were made up, but in which the characters -- American and British politicians in the run-up to the Iraq war -- were both actual and persuasive. Studio 180's new show, The Overwhelming, is also a run-up piece, and the events it runs up to, the Rwandan massacres, are even more appalling; but this time the characters are fictitious. And one sits through it asking a question that may be unfairly prompted by the memory of Studio 180's previous shows, but is inescapable nonetheless: Why am I supposed to care about these made-up people when a real and recent atrocity, taking countless real lives, is just waiting to happen?

It might be easier if the characters in J.T. Rogers' play, written by an American but first staged by the National Theatre in London, came across as people, rather than representative mouthpieces and well-intentionedreactors. Chiefamong the latter is Jack Exley, a U.S. political science academic who comes to the Rwandan capital Kigali in 1994 to research a book he hopes will gain him

tenure. Specifically, he wants to meet up with Joseph Gasana, an old Rwandan friend, now working as a doctor. Joseph is a Tutsi, and he's gone missing. Jack's attempts to track him down -- via the police, the UN, his own embassy -- are met with stonewalling or outright hostility. With him are Linda, his second wife and a writer, and Geoffrey, his teenaged son from his first marriage. There's some stepson-stepmom tension between these two (Geoffrey all but says "you're not the boss of me") that contributes nothing to the play, either personally or politically, but is emblematic of the soap-opera level on which the playwright feels obliged to operate.

It isn't all as bad as that. All three members of the Exley family seem remarkably naive, but their explorations of the society in which they find themselves means they're always asking questions, and sometimes getting interesting answers. Linda, while remaining sympathetic to the plight of her husband's friend, lends an increasingly sympathetic ear to some smooth Hutu propaganda. She wants a story; Geoffrey wants sex, which he finds courtesy of a friendly Tutsi prostitute. His reluctance to use a condom in a disease-ridden country is set up as a plot point, but never pursued. The relationship culminates in the first (offstage) outbreak of full-out horror, but it's questionable whether we need a whole laborious subplot to get us there. It's as if the reactions of these innocents abroad were more important than the events themselves.

Sometimes, the play finds a pithy and powerful way of relaying the news. A diplomat asks Jack the blunt rhetorical question of whether any country, in the history of the world, has based its foreign policy on "the right thing to do." It's a moment that, on the first night, stirred the audience to uneasy laughter. Foreign or indigenous, the parties on the ground engage in an elaborate routine of buck-passing. It's clear that, given a shift in dynamics, the Hutu might as easily have been victims and the Tutsi killers; and that their tribal differences were invented and exploited by their Belgian conquerors. The French, the Americans, the beleaguered UN peacekeepers. All have their plausible reasons for refusing to intervene. Nobody's to blame or, to put it another way, everybody is. But didn't we know that already?

Greenberg's production is swift and efficient, though he hasn't found a distinctive visual style here, as he did for Stuff Happens, with its serried rows of mobile chairs. He settles here for everyone democratically moving the furniture around, as demonstrated in just about every play we see. David Storch holds the centre as Jack, though his exaggerated nervousness and bizarre all-purpose academic accent can strain both patience and belief. Some other performances are muddy, swallowed up in the bare brick-backed expanses of Michael Gianfrancesco's tandoori-red set. In the service of appearing hard-bitten, Hardee T. Lineham as an American resident gnashes and mashes most of what he says, including some important information. Brendan McMurtry-Howlett is suitably fresh as young Geoff. Nigel Shawn Williams makes, as usual, a weighty and sympathetic impression as Joseph, whom we first meet through his idealistic letters to Jack but who, when he finally appears, turns out to have his own share of ambiguities. He also plays various non-speaking servants, a telling way of linking Joseph's plight to those of compatriots less articulate than he is.

Eventually Jack, who has spent the play trying always to say and do the right thing, finds his principles tested when the conflict comes literally to his own door. It's nerve-rending, as it should be. But everything leading up to it is underwhelming.
