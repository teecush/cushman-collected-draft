ZADIES SHOES

Factory Theatre, Toronto

The new theatrical year gets off to a fine start with Zadies Shoes, a first full-length play by the 27-year-old Adam Pettle. It begins in cracking form, everybody racing ahead but under firm control, there is some bunching together at the rails somewhere around the middle, but the author streaks ahead at the end.

From which you may gather that this is a play concerned with horse racing. More precisely it's concerned with gambling, to which its hero, a young Jewish man named Benjamin, is addicted. When he first meet him he is watching a televised football game, on which he's had a minor and, as it turns out, unsuccessful flutter.

Its the track, though, thats his main preoccupation; betting big Gs on the gee-gees is both his downfall and, or so he devoutly hopes, his salvation. To fund his habit he has taken money from his Gentile girlfriend Ruth, a cancer patient who has despaired of conventional treatment and needs the cash to fly to Mexico for alternative therapy.

By this point Benjamin, too, is willing to try anything. He even, for the first time since his bar mitzvah, wanders into a synagogue where, meeting with an old religious man, he receives a hot tip plus some good moral advice. He neglects the advice but takes the tip. It's a longshot, but if it pays off, his financial problems, and conceivably his personal ones too, will be solved. Intermission hits with the ponies under starters' orders, making for the best cliff- hanging curtain in recent memory.

Its more than a matter of momentary suspense. Were involved with Benjamin, both for his failings and his good intentions, and also of course with Ruth. Both of them are in a position where the best that they can do is pray, as a musical-comedy hero in a somewhat similar situation once pointed out when asking luck to be his lady.

They share this condition with several of the other characters: with Ruth's elder sister, a curling champion wholly wrapped up in the outcome of her next and vital game, and with her younger sister, an eclectic cultist newly pregnant and equally wrapped up in that.

If that sounds schematic, it is -- and it's where the play is weakest. A disastrous meeting between the three girls goes the way the dramatist needs it to, rather than any way it plausibly would; nor are the two ugly sisters any more convincing when they change from monsters of self-absorption to angels of mercy. In these roles Torri Higginson and Juno Mills-Cockell hardly muster an ounce of conviction between them, and though Jackie Maxwells direction accurately shows off whats best in the play, it can find no mitigating nuance in what isn't.

This extends to some of the scenes involving Benjamin, whose gambling is apparently a family curse. The play's title refers to his granddad who came barefoot to Canada, having lost his shoes in a poker game. Benjys father, a successful doctor, is unable to bale his son out because of being similarly hooked; occasional flashbacks to their early relationship are painfully awkward, and also doggedly unrevealing.

Its more important, though, that Benjamin lives so vividly in the present; Jordan Pettle (the author's brother -- the evening is a riot of sibling revelry) makes his compulsive wrigglings between addiction and affection both painful and sympathetic -- painful because sympathetic -- and uncomfortably believable. (Could a nice guy be so selfish? Yes.) Kelli Fox as Ruth is chunkily enduring and good-humoured until she cracks, at which point her anger scalds. One character calls her "a rock," and it suits.

I fear that I haven't conveyed how funny the play is. The synagogue scenes have the weight of ages behind them, irresistible when both the timing and the anecdotes are as rich as those deployed by Paul Soles as a rabbinical horse-player, a combination of wise man and wise guy.

But, as the Levys rye posters used to concede, you dont have to be Jewish. Even richer, and certainly less predictable, comedy comes from Benjamin's racetrack friend and confidant, known as Bear, a recuperating gambler and junkie and alcoholic, and pretty together, considering. Randy Hughson's rollickingly sodden performance arouses a couple of seconds of misgivings (as in, hasnt he done this before?) followed by minutes on end of total bliss, culminating in as richly disorienting a final exit as I can remember. He has the script totally in his corner: A scene in which he desperately waits for a telephone not to ring is what should be known as a tour-de- farce, the fruit of scrupulously clever preparation, and all the more hilarious for having deadly serious underpinnings.

The character also functions as a moral force, an unexpected task that Hughson performs superbly, and is further folded into the plot by being Ruths sister's baby's father. (We also meet the curling sister's boyfriend, though symmetry seems the only reason for this.)

The set, designed by Sue LePage, is centrally composed of racetrack-stands. The thematic relevance is obvious but it means the indoor scenes, which is most of them, inevitably get shoved to the side. They survive, though, and the play, though composed of episodes, is not episodic. It wins by far more than a nose.
