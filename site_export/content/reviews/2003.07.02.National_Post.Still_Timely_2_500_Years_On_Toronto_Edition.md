THE HOUSE OF ATREUS: AGAMEMNON, ELECTRA, THE FLIES

Studio Theatre, Stratford, Ont.

- - -

Over the last few decades, theatre companies have mounted mar- athon productions that stitched together some of the extant Greek tragedies to tell a continuous story (John Barton's The Greeks) or that re-crossed the same ancient territory in a giant succession of new scripts (John Barton's Tantalus). Stratford's The House of Atreus has elements of both these approaches, but is its own absorbing animal.

It starts with Aeschylus' Agamemnon, which is the first part of the only surviving Greek trilogy, The Oresteia. Agamemnon, King of Argos, son of Atreus, returns home from his 10-year generalship at the siege of Troy only to be murdered by his wife, Clytemnestra, and her lover Aegisthus. It's a revenge killing, with a multiplicity of motives: Clytemnestra hates her husband for having sacrificed their daughter Iphigenia so that the gods would send the Greek fleet a favouring wind for Troy; Aegisthus' family has been at war with Agamemnon's for two blood-soaked generations. The vendetta is pursued through Aeschylus' two subsequent instalments, in which Agamemnon's surviving children, Electra and Orestes, avenge their father and are pursued for their pains by the Furies, goddesses of the ancient unrelenting code for which our own civilization's metaphor is "an eye for an eye."

The Stratford venture, however, steps aside from Oresteia II and III, and instead offers us two dramatizations of the myth from 20th- century France: Jean Giraudoux's Electra and Jean-Paul Sartre's The Flies. The first was written under the approaching shadow of the Second World War, the second during the German Occupation. Each reflects the preoccupations of its time and, in these productions, is costumed accordingly. The Agamemnon designs, meanwhile, are as conventionally classical as you're going to get these days. Each of the plays has a different director, but the actors are the same. One of the enterprise's pleasures is to watch them going through stylistic hoops, not to mention ageing, while preserving their identities.

In Agamemnon, most of the cast are in the chorus, the most talkative in any surviving Greek play; it accounts for at least half the text. David Latham's production, which uses Ted Hughes' magnificent mailed-fist translation, accepts the chorus as the play's principal character, or in this case collection of characters; this is a resolutely un-stylized presentation, with no masks or massed speaking. It works most of the time. There is one moment when the chorus is explicitly, and rather comically, divided against itself, wondering what to do as the king is noisily dispatched off stage. Dividing up the speeches, for clarity and colour, is one thing; setting up artificial conflicts between the choristers is another, a drain on the actors' energy and the audience's concentration. It also highlights the uneven level of accomplishment within the ranks. The older ones do best; Walter Borden dominates, as a sedentary brooder perched on the edge of a two-stepped dais that's the central design feature of all three plays; but Maria Vacratsis and Ian White are strong too. Their juniors feature underdeveloped voices and a couple of speech impediments.

Part of the chorus' role is to fill us in on the backstory of the Trojan War, and to act as a sounding board for the principals, especially Clytemnestra, a monster with a lot of right on her side. Karen Robinson in the role is sharp and glittering as a newly honed dagger, but she seems to find the long speeches a trial. She dives into them with tremendous energy, but is then reduced to bobbing about. If the lady protests too much, it's true of the actress rather than the character.

Flaws acknowledged, this is still one of the best realizations of a Greek tragedy I have seen. Its strength is to present a single inevitable action while summoning the infinity of shadows that surround it. The Greek and Trojan armies, like this play's avengers, were caught up in an endless futile round of murderous self- justification. They knew it, but couldn't stop it. The play is amazingly timely, and has been for 2,500 years.

It seems more urgently topical than its two younger companions. These too draw on the audience's knowledge of the background, but they're hampered by telling stories they don't actually believe in. Giraudoux, in Electra, holds the problem pretty much at bay by sheer force of wit, and the great strength of Leon Rubin's production is its unwavering sense of serious playfulness.

The play has two commentator-figures, both deliciously played. Sean Arbuckle's beggar is a figure of barbed whimsy who asks the higher-ups all the impertinent questions that the deferential Greek chorus never got round to. The other is a gardener, to whom the troublesome Electra is about to be married off; Rami Posner spends the first half of the play standing expectantly on the edge of the action with a posy in his hand. Dismissed at the start of the second half, he delivers an anti-tragic manifesto on the virtues of cultivating one's garden rather than populating graveyards. Borden and Topham crop up again as his relatives. He, as a pompous judge, spoils his effects by swallowing his words, but she, nothing if not versatile, is a delight as his perpetually randy wife. Promising for the past two seasons, she has reached the arrival stage. The onlookers are completed by a trio of malignant schoolgirls (Julia Donovan, Julie Tepperman, Randi Helmers) who, we discover, are the Furies. They age before us in time with Electra's growing appetite for revenge.

She, played by Sarah Dodd with exemplary implacability, begins the play like Hamlet, unaware her father was murdered but knowing something is rotten in her mother's marriage. Mother and daughter share a mutual loathing; Robinson's Clytemnestra burns all the clearer for being relieved of her tragedy-queen responsibilities. Orestes, banished, returns, and the oft-dramatized reconciliation of brother and sister is played out again; they sleep together and she wakes up with knowledge and a motive, after which they're no stopping her. And then there is a twist. The usurper Aegisthus asks Electra to join him in saving the city.

Giraudoux was writing in the age of appeasement, and he ended up serving in the Vichy government. From this perspective his drift looks shabby. But the fit isn't precise; coming in the wake of Agamemnon, we still think of the story as Greek. Unfashionable for decades, the playwright's time may be coming again if he can be done with this degree of style.

Sartre's The Flies, by contrast, is stuck in its own time. Narrative continuity goes by the board, since the play re-enacts much of the action of Electra, but we still feel we're seeing another facet of the same prism. The flies are the latest incarnation of the Furies, welcomed by a guilty Aegsithus as a means of intimidating his subjects. In Peter Lichtenfels' production they're gas-masked figures whose fingers perform an unremitting hand- jive.

A graver problem is that Sartre's characters do little but talk, and not nearly as entertainingly as Giraudoux's. This time it's Orestes the avenger who gets away from Electra. Dodd's performance is a good as before, bar a few moments of sentimentality in which it's her turn to protest too much. Johnstone, a cipher in the preceding play, comes to life here as Orestes takes on the task of the "necessary murder": a killer, and unrepentantly so, let the Furies -- whose role at the end is ambiguous, if not downright confusing -- pursue him as they may. He is, in fact, the existentialist hero of the Resistance, and one might accept him more happily if Sartre didn't let him proclaim himself at such length. Aeschylus' Furies were eventually absorbed into a humane judicial system; Sartre's just go on buzzing. We've had a theatrical crash course in the descent of man.

I have left the dominant performance till last: Scott Wentworth's Aegisthus, who in each play presents a radically different aspect of the same unmistakable man. He gives the closing moments of Agamemnon an electric shock, when he appears out of nowhere, undressed to the nines as killer and gigolo. In Electra he's the consummate pinstriped politician, Claudius in the play's Hamlet pattern. In The Flies, he's a variant of the same, but with a death wish. Witty, poised, and effortlessly powerful, the actor is a national resource. He also, in the unity he finds in diversity, incarnates the achievement of these productions as a whole -- an audacious concept, excitingly fulfilled.
