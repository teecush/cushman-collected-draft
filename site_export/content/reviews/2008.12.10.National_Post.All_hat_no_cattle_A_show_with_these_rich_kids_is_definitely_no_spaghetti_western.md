The emblematic moment in Rich Kids: Cattle Drive comes early, when Kourtney tries for the first time to get on a horse. (I think it's Kourtney. The cast also includes a Courtenay. It's very confusing.) All the other nine spoiled darlings who have signed up for a stint on a Colorado ranch have managed it, but she's having problems. Joshua, the trail boss, is unsympathetic. He blames it on her jeans. "You've got to wear ranch jeans -- not Gucci jeans."

Kourtney is incensed -- as much as anything by the insult to her fashion sense: "First of all, who wears Gucci jeans?" Then she gets defiant; these are her jeans, and she's not going to change them: "If you don't like it, give me a stepping stool." Later on she muses on the indignity of it all: "I thought that comment about Gucci jeans was really mean and uncalled-for." And she reaches for the ultimate grievance: "It's like stereotyping. It's a racist comment in a way."

No question: Kourtney, or Courtenay, and company have been set up to be stereotyped. "There's more to me," says one, "than just being a spoiled brat." And though you know to laugh, you're not quite sure how. (She does say she has outside interests, like going to lunch.) Rich Kids: Cattle Drive has taken an understandably long time to reach our screens; it aired in the U. S. in 2005 under the title Filthy Rich:Cattle Drive, which sort of gives it away. After all, if you're going to do a show about a bunch of greenhorns learning to be cowboys and cowgirls, there's no intrinsic reason for them to be wealthy. The idea is to play into our desire to see the privileged ones make fools of themselves and suck it up. And they certainly play along: how consciously, is hard to tell.

At least two of them should know what they're getting into, being as they're actors and the children of actors. One of them is the son of Anthony Quinn, no less; the other's father is Robert Blake, known not just for his film and TV work, but for what his son Noah coyly refers to as "that little murder trial." Of all the philosophical gems with which these kids, in hallowed reality show fashion, introduce themselves, I think Noah's is my favourite. "I definitely allow the pendulum of life to take me on its journey," he says.

This particular swing of the pendulum has its participants given two days of training at Saddleback Ranch before being divided into two gangs and sent off to herd 100 head of cattle per group (one gets the red steers, the other the black) with, of course, a running competition going on between them, and trail boss Joshua pronouncing judgment at dinner as to who's done the best. The winning team gets massages, the losing team gets to do the dishes. If this

all seems too gritty a prospect, there are words of comfort: "Don't think they'll be leaving the world of gossip behind." Nor do they: "There's just the gossip on Britney," says one of the preliminary teasers, and you might think at that moment they mean Spears. But no, they mean one of their own, who spells it Brittny (her dad's a footballer) and who, even as they all fly from Los Angeles to Colorado in a luxury jet, is getting close with Alex, the junior Quinn. He, however, is "definitely keeping my options open," meaning that he's playing her off against Kourtney or Courtenay. Much of the first episode is devoted to everybody talking about this. The word is that in later instalments, Fabian ("son," so help me Wikipedia, "of an Ecuadorian businessman") emerges as the really obnoxious one. So far, though, the easiest one to distinguish is Alexander (not Alex) Clifford, because he's English, "eldest son of the Lord Clifford of Chudleigh." It's unclear why he chose to come, since he's never wanted to go to America, whose culture has in his mind consisted of McDonald's and -- I think I heard this right --Salem. But his presence, and a few pained remarks he makes about the others' lack of equestrian prowess, does put us in touch with a vanished age when the wealthy were expected to be able to ride.

This show, obviously, is the reality show eating its own tail, the foul mismating of at least two sub-genres. It comes with all the trimmings: sex (theirs), schadenfreude (ours), envy (ours and theirs), built-in opportunities for slapstick and a gloating narrator saying things like, "If they think they had it rough before, they're in for a rude awakening." It may also make you wonder what became of the dear old Western, including the comic variety in which Bob Hope or even Kenneth More (anyone else remember The Sheriff of Fractured Jaw?) would go incongruously riding on the range, but eventually come up trumps, which would include getting the girl, who was usually pneumatic and either Jane (Russell) or Jayne (Mansfield.) It also suggests that Dirty Sexy Money (I miss that show) was more of a documentary than we might have guessed. We can't, though, dismiss these young things quite out of hand, even if they do seem to regard allowances of US$20,000 a month as mere pittances. ("Do you realize," says one of the more aware, "that $2,000 a month is a lot for some people?") It isn't as if any of us would do better as impromptu cowhands, though it's also not as if anyone would ask us. They're doing it, in case you wondered, for charity -- and, however crummy, the show is a few steps up from Oprah's Big Give. They are being asked to do something real, and even interesting. They may also be getting, from the long-suffering Joshua, lessons in eloquence. Waking them up at 5 a. m. --a time few of them may have known existed, at least not as part of the morning -- he says "we're burning daylight here." That's Shakespearean. Like, literally. - Rich Kids: Cattle Drive airs tonight at 10 p. m. ET on Slice.
