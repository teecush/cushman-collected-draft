ALL THINGS CONSIDERED BY BERNARD LEVIN Random House, 305 pages, $29.95

The devourable Bernard Levin

ALL THINGS CONSIDERED BY BERNARD LEVIN Random House, 305 pages, $29.95

Saturday, July 01, 1989

ROBERT CUSHMAN

REVIEW BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Here is a collection - the fifth - of articles written for the Times (of London) by Bernard Levin in his role as Personality Columnist. Levin is the most celebrated columnist - maybe the most celebrated journalist - in the British press, and quite deservedly so. He is opinionated, unquenchable and writes elegant English, which seems colloquial even when it isn't. (Nobody could talk that well that fast, though in his TV appearances Levin often gives the impression that he might; oh yes, he is also very fond of parentheses and has seduced many into the erroneous belief that they can get away with it as well as he does.) His long, cascading sentences appear to pour on to the page of their own volition, though they must really be the result of immense and painful effort. At least I hope they are.

Most of all he is extremely funny. He once supplied the Times with a hilarious fantasy on the social life of the lobster, though he weakened its impact by serving it up in other guises on subsequent occasions. He seems to have given up now on writing set-piece funnies, but his sense of humor, which is fundamentally a sense of paradox, still informs nearly everything he writes. In this, as in much else, he has learned from Shaw.

Levin started out as a political commentator, and politics is still very much his beat. He has moved over the years from centre Left to centre Right. I might have said extreme Right were it not that the preface to this volume reveals that he is finally having doubts about Thatcherism; he still seems impervious to its essential meanness, but at least he worries about its threats to free speech.

As well as getting conservatism he also got religion, though of no recognized brand. Or if it is, he isnt letting on. A born but non- practicing Jew, he is now a fascinated observer of Christianity. "I would like," he says of Christ to be written down a fan. He might also, I think, be written down a mystical moralist. Theres nothing cloudy about the morality, but its source remains myterious.

The central piece here is called The Evil That Men Do. It deals with the Cambodian nightmare, or rather with a book about it, or - rather still - with a review of that book by Salman Rushdie. Rushdie refers to atrocities performed, and boasted about, by a certain Comrade Tek, and says, "How can men become like Comrade Tek? . . . The terrible lesson of our century is that it isn't difficult. I could be Comrade Tek. And so could you."

In jumps Levin to say, with no desire to exculpate the century, that no, we couldn't. We - he and Rushdie and their readers - have all doubtless done shameful things in our time and under pressure might do far worse, but still there is a gulf. Comrade Tek and a handful more are beyond it, writes Levin; the rest of us would never go to the Tekian extreme of tearing out the liver of captured prisoners and then demonstrating the process on live monkeys.

I hope hes right. I cant help thinking, though, that Nazi Germany was able to staff a great number of concentration camps with men who proved capable of sustained inhumanity. They cannot all have been monsters from the outset. Some of them must initially have been responding to pressure - propagandist pressure, peer pressure, fear pressure.

What interests me, though, is Levin's next point. "Some people are evil; are evil, not are made evil." Don't blame heredity or environment because some people have a propensity to criminal behavior by reason of what they wish to be, not of what has been done to them. Why do they wish it? Because, like Comrade Tek, given the choice between good and evil, they choose evil. But this simply moves the question back a stage. Why do they choose evil? A "propensity" (Levins word) is something youre born with. And something you're born with, like something you're born into, is not your fault.

Now, according to Levin, the cry its not our fault is the sickness, and may prove the destruction, of our age. But what his argument really seems to point to is that nothing is really anybodys fault. And, though it makes me nervous to say so, I have to say that it's a conclusion I find inescapable. Nobody is fundamentally to blame for anything. Of course we cannot live our lives on that basis; we have to judge, and if Comrade Tek were in my power I should have no compunction about consigning him to any punishment that was available. But perhaps we should think of justice merely as one of society's modes of self-preservation, and stop being hypocritical about it. Nothing is more nauseating (and here I suspect that Levin would agree) than the judge who, having passed sentence, complacently lectures his victim.

If, as is probable, Levin is thinking of a morality that transcends human judiciaries, he still raises intractable problems: questions that 700 years ago came near to fazing Dante. Having depicted the damned in his Inferno, he could not avoid asking how anybody could deserve such unending torment, not merely permitted but decreed by a beneficent God. He could only still his own questionings by invoking a doctrine of predestination that even in the Middle Ages required a supreme act of faith to accept. Most of us today find it hideous, but we have yet to come up with an alternative.

Levin and I were colleagues for a time; at least we were both theatre critics for leading Sunday newspapers. We didn't fraternize much, coming from different generations and different traditions, but I do have pleasant memories of being goosed in the National Theatre and turning to find a beaming Bernard chortling, "Aha! The opposition!"

I enjoyed reading him when he was flaying something pretentious, but I found the Higher Levin difficult to take. Any play that could be construed, however fancifully, as having reference to God or art seemed assured of a rave; actually, for Levin, God and art were identical, and before Peter Shaffer's Amadeus, which invoked both, he practically prostrated himself. To judge from his tribute in the current book to a short-lived West End piece called J. J. Farr (about a rationalist who sees the error of his ways), he is still at it.

But prostrate prose does not become him. He is insufferable on music where soprano-and-conductor worship has supplanted any pretence at analysis or even description, better on books than on plays and on things than on books. I can take or leave his reviews, but my first action on picking up a copy of The Times is to see if there is a Levin column. If there is, I devour it immediately. So, I imagine, does everybody else. Robert Cushman is former theatre critic of The Observer.
