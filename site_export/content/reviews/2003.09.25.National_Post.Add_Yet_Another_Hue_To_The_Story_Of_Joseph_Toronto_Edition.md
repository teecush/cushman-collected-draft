REMNANTS

Tarragon Theatre, Toronto

- - -

The Old Testament tale of Joseph and his brothers has inspired modern authors from Thomas Mann to Tim Rice and Andrew Lloyd Webber. Now, in what is effectively the opening production of the new Tarragon regime, we have Jason Sherman's Remnants, an extremely clever and ultimately moving attempt to wed the old story to the Jewish experience in the first half of the 20th century.

The title is, at the least, double-edged. It recalls the old Jewish idea of the saving remnant; those few who survive the worst catastrophes, to bear witness and to carry on. More prosaically, it can mean the scraps in a tailors shop that may, perhaps, be stitched together to make a new garment. That is how this plays Joseph gets his multi-coloured coat. Jacob, his father, is a prosperous Jewish tailor in 1920s Poland. Ten of his sons help in the business; the other two, born to Jacob's second and better- loved wife, get more generous treatment. Young Benjamin, a mute, stays home as his father's pet; Joseph is a yeshiva student.

The older brothers traditional jealousy of the favourite son, with his pesky dreams, is cursorily treated. I never thought I would write such a thing, but I found myself missing Rices wit. What is interesting is more specific. The brothers are Communists, planning to unionize the store; they suspect Joseph of being their father's spy. (Their shifting political commitments are deftly handled throughout.) They plan to kill him, but, as in the original, Reuben the eldest stays their hands; he proposes packing Joseph on board a boat to New York. By some mischance it docks at Halifax instead, and Joseph, having got past the immigration authorities, begins a Canadian odyssey.

He works for four years in a Toronto sweatshop but is fired when the owner, a Mr. Pottinger, tries unsuccessfully to involve him in precisely the kind of union-busting activity of which his brothers had accused him back home. Pottinger, of course, is Potiphar, but he has no wife; he tries to seduce Joseph -- physically as well as politically -- on his own account. This may score high as dramatic economy, but considering everything else that's going on, its a bit much. After more adventures Joe finds himself, thanks to his prowess at interpreting dreams, in the service of Mackenzie King, currently (1932) between premierships; history may not be able to offer Sherman a Pharaoh, but it has done him the favour of dealing him a King. And in an even snugger fit, the dream of the fat and lean kine turns out to signify seven years of Depression followed by seven more of wartime resurgence.

Comes the war, and Joseph is appointed the PMs representative on immigration; which of course makes him complicit in Canada's infamous anti-Semitic quota system. As you'd expect, his brothers, desperate to escape from Poland, unknowingly become his clients, and he plays out with them the same cat-and-mice game that was adumbrated in the Bible, played for even higher stakes: agonizingly high, in fact.

Sometimes the play has to spend too much energy connecting the Biblical dots, getting from one incident to another. But even these scenes travel fast. The strength of the play, much the best of Shermans that I have seen, is that its built like a fable, almost like a cartoon, more concerned with story and its implications than with character. (Sherman's previous scriptural update, Patience, based on the less-tractable Book of Job, died on its feet, endlessly probing a protagonist who had never come to life in the first place.) In an ideal world one would like to know more about Joseph and especially about the corrosive effect on him of either concealing or denying his Jewishness, but one settles for him here as a providential enigma. Most of his brothers, probably for budgetary reasons, spend the evening in the wings; they are represented on stage by Reuben, kind but weak, and Judah, bullying but effective. As they move between those poles, and as history itself changes them from predators to victims, they take on not individual complexity but collective texture. This is more than enough; the long-delayed reunion and forgiveness ring true on all sides.

Line by line, the play is cleanly, piercingly written, with occasional funny lines that are more than funny. Told that his father must have been a nice man, Joseph says that he wasn't, except to him, which tells you something about Jacob and more about Joseph. "Dont you find that power corrupts?" Joseph is asked, and replies "absolutely"; which would be the glibbest of gags if he himself were not becoming, at the very least, compromised.

Richard Roses production, his first as Tarragon's boss, is also his best in ages: gaunt, speedy and very well acted. Graeme Thomson's designs, built of trunks and suitcases, rescue the oldest of refugee images; and his lighting is amazing, especially in its pinpoint use of a downstage scrim shaped like an outsize portrait frame. Dmitry Chepotevskys Joseph is best in his days of quietness and comparative innocence; called on for complexity, and especially for implacability, he stonewalls. Alex Poch-Goldin and Alon Nashman, though, are superb as the principal brothers, offering essences but not caricatures, and as many other characters as well; Poch-Goldin is especially delectable as an English butler, condemned and reprieved on strict Genesis principles. There is a great double by Victor Ertmannis as a sardonic King (rather a flattering portrait, I suspect) and a Polish peasant, first Jacobs employee and then his protector, and Jerry Franken does fine work as a variety of elders, from Jacob himself to the Canadian mandarin in charge of keeping Jews out. The play ends at Jacob's deathbed, the patriarch looking backward and forward -- to today's Middle East -- with equal bitterness. Then the brothers and the righteous Pole join hands in hope; the moment courts sentimentality but avoids it. The old story and the new make a handsome couple.
