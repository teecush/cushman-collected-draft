I seem to remember that when How Now Mrs. Brown Cow played here last year Brendan O'Carroll - its star, author, director and reason for being - told us in an uproarious curtain speech that he and his gang would be back this February with another instalment of what he cheekily described as their "five-part trilogy. He said he knew this because David Mirvish had promised him that it would be a cold day in February before he was ever allowed on a Mirvish stage again. Hes back again now with the threatened sequel, which is called Good Mourning Mrs. Brown. And hes behind his promised time, although in fairness this March certainly feels like February. Anyway, I hardly have the right to complain as I was late on the first night myself, for which I too blame the weather.

I wasn't all that late. I was in time for the play proper, if proper is the word. But I did miss the opening announcement in which O'Carroll told us just what to do with our cellphones and which last year had disposed me to love the show even before it got started. Id be very surprised if there wasnt such a prelude this year as well, since the format repeats that of its predecessor in every other respect. Maybe, if I had been thus softened up, I would have enjoyed myself as much this time as last. As it is, I have to profess myself disappointed and, in view of my previous enthusiasm, more than a little shamefaced.

If I, too, may repeat myself: O'Carroll plays Mrs. Agnes Brown, a Dublin widow who rules her family with a rod of iron and a tongue of salt. It's all a bit hard to keep track of, but she has an older unmarried daughter and two sons, one gay and partnered, one married and straight: sexually anyway. All these, plus the married sons wife, live under her roof. Theres another daughter, married, who fades out of this particular play and whose only function when in it seems to be to permit her mother to refer to her in-laws out in Donegal as sheepshaggers." Another of her favourite words is "feck; it, or its derivatives or their stronger Anglo-Saxon equivalents, crop up in just about every other line she utters. (Here she is admittedly in good cultural company; Canadian Stage's next season includes a compilation entitled Beckett, Feck It!) She's 9/10 prurient, 1/10 puritanical: a not-unheard-of combination, but one that seems to operate here according to which aspect is going to get the next laugh.

Also on the premises is Mrs. Browns nonagenerian father-in-law, on whom she inflicts unceasing verbal abuse and sometimes, in a slapstick sort of way, physical abuse as well. There was an odd resonant moment in the Brown Cow play when Grandad (Dermot ONeill in a flaxen floppy wig that must have been abandoned the day that the circus left town) turned to a family member and simply said, Help me." It went nowhere, but there were hopes, dashed, that this time it might. The family decides to stage a mock funeral so that the old man, who hasn't heard a nice word said to or about him in decades, may hear his own eulogy. This is the main storyline. At least it's what the title is about.

There are other bits of plot alternating through the evening. Theres the tribulations of Father Quinn, who is losing his vocation on account of his flock being lured away by evangelicals. This leads to the unprecedented spectacle of two Jehovahs Witnesses (well, actually they're Mormons) who, once they've met Mrs. Brown, cant wait to get their feet outside the door. There are the tribulations of brainy daughter Cathy, a psychologist, whos dating a copper. There's the saga of daughter-in-law Maria who may or may not be having twins. There's her husband Brendan, a former safe-cracker, being lured back to his life of crime by a loony confederate.

At one point, when Mrs. Brown is uncharacteristically offstage, theres a rather touching little scene in which Grandad gives Brendan some good advice. Then the matriarch barges back in and spoils it. At this point I decided that I didnt really like Mrs. Brown. And my annoyance at her habit of hogging every conversation spilled over onto her creator's habit -well, it's really a cross between a professional obligation and a compulsion -of hogging the stage, good at it though he undoubtedly is. Ironically, part of the trouble may be that, underneath all the gags, Mrs. Brown is quite a believable character, though not a very attractive one. When Cathy (Jennifer Gibney, whos Mrs. OCarroll in private life) accuses her mom of ruining her life, it's a textbook mother-daughter conflict.

There was a scene just like it in the previous play. Like all sitcoms, this one repeats and recycles, in technique as well as subject matter. Once again, in a deadeningly overamplified production, a supporting actor gets rebuked when her mic goes off. Once again O'Carroll suggests doing an instant recap when someone else gets a line wrong. Once again -many times again -the other actors break up at the stars supposed ad-libs. I almost believed in this last time, but its now impossible not to notice that it's always the actors playing the gay couple who get hysterics and that they always regain their composure with miraculous ease. The supporting acting does seem, with one terrible exception, to have improved, but the material has deteriorated, though O'Carrolls timing of the odd good lines remains impressive. (He has less physical comedy than before, though he still makes innumerable cups of tea.) Maybe this lightning just cannot strike twice. Even with three parts still to come, it seems safe to say that when youve seen one Mrs. Brown play you've seen them all. It's probably worth seeing one though.

Good Mourning Mrs. Brown runs at the Princess of Wales Theatre through March 19. Call 416-872-1212 or visit mirvish.com for ticket information.
