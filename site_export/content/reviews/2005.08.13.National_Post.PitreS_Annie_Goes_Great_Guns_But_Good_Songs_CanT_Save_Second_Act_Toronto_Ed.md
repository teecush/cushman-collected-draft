robertcushman@lycos.com

Louise Pitre in Annie Get Your Gun is, to mangle a song title, as wonderful as falling in love. At least the experience hit me that way because, though I have admired Pitre on occasion, I have never before seen her in a show that I liked. As Irving Berlin's Annie Oakley, she sings as well as any leading lady I have seen in Toronto. In between her numbers, and during them as well, she acts, bringing out all the facets of the character she plays.

Make that both the facets. Annie is brash, and she's naive. Any complexities in her role result from the rubbing together of those two elements. Her first appearance, certainly in Pitre's performance, is a delight; she's in hot perplexed pursuit of a bird she's just shot off some lady's hat. She's a phenomenal backwoods sharpshooter who takes that ability enchantingly for granted but has no idea that it's something the outside world would call a talent and therefore marketable. But she learns, fast, through challenge contests and world tours with Buffalo Bill's Wild West Show and an instant requited passion for the show's own champion hotshot, Frank Butler. Both career and romance go wrong, but only for as long as it takes to give the musical a story.

Pitre plays both the joy and the heartache full out, but tactfully; this isn't a crying show. The defining moment of her performance is the rueful glance she bestows on her trusty rifle before launching into You Can't Get a Man with a Gun. The song itself is a tour de force that finds neat and funny rhymes for almost every known variety of firearm. She makes it even better by linking it fully to the personality and predicament of the woman who sings it.

She is equally persuasive in the gentler numbers, in the incredibly bewitching Moonshine Lullaby or in I Got Lost in His Arms, a song that musically and lyrically is an object lesson in the arts of tension and release. This show has a great set of songs; if they don't quite add up to a great score, that's because their placement is often arbitrary. I Got the Sun in the Morning is a joyous swinger, and I wouldn't be without it, but there's no dramatic reason for it to appear at one point in the show rather than another, or indeed in this show rather than any other.

But then the show itself is no great piece of architecture. The book (by Herbert and Dorothy Fields, revised on this occasion by Don Carrier) doesn't have a second act. After the intermission it simply stands around finding lame excuses for Annie and Frank, who have split arbitrarily up, not to get together again until the last possible moment.

The two leads sing, between them, all but one of the songs, so it's unfortunate that this production is virtually Frankless. Pitre has the warmth and colour to sing Annie without belting like Ethel Merman. (Actually, to judge from the cast album, Merman in this role didn't belt like Merman, at least not all the time.)

Frank has fewer specifics to fall back on; he's a Broadway baritone or nothing. Billy Ray Cyrus wears a cowboy hat to the manner born, and acts rather sweetly from underneath it, but his soft-grained country voice doesn't have the tone, range or rhythm for his songs. We know, as their big duet tells us, that anything he can do she can do better, but it shouldn't be quite such a foregone conclusion.

The production at Massey Hall is in semi-concert form. That is, the actors, while wearing their costumes and knowing their lines, have to thread their way around the orchestra, which takes up most of the stage and accounts for a good half of the vaunted "company of 50." Still, it's good to hear a big band playing a big-band score. And Donna Feore's direction is very resourceful, even working in some choreography, nearly all of it good.

The supporting cast has virtually nothing to play, a fact that Sandra Caldwell's vibrant presence in the especially thankless role of Dolly Tate makes particularly hard to bear. The others do their best, but Buffalo Bill remains an avuncular cipher and his manager, Charlie, a grinning stooge, while Chief Sitting Bull would surely be denounced as a comic travesty if only his jokes were funnier. The ways of political correctness are strange. This version retains the poisonous ending, in which Annie gets her man by letting him think he's the better shot, but deletes I'm an Indian Too, a harmless number whose absence saps some badly needed energy from the end of the first act. In its place we get the cast pacing hopefully around the stage in yet another encore of There's No Business Like Show Business. In its proper place that much-misunderstood song is splendidly done, with Pitre's Annie visibly seduced into embracing the travelling life. It isn't an explosion of theatrical pomp; it actually dwells on the seamier side. "The towel you've taken from the last hotel ... the roustabouts who move the show at dawn ... the closing when the customers won't come." Mind you, this Annie has already had her run extended, and justly; the sheriff won't be escorting her out of town just yet.

- - -

The roustabouts who move the Cirque du Soleil presumably work unionized hours, and don't have to do anything at dawn, or not very often. When the Cirque hits town, it settles here for a while. Corteo, its latest presentation, purports to have a theme, as most of them do. This time it's a requiem for a dying clown who looks back on past circus experiences (probably a metaphor for life, but don't think about it). However, unless I'm getting blase, this edition lacks something, both in beauty and in daring. The skills are awesome, of course, but life and limb go, for the most part, remarkably unimperilled. The distinguishing characteristic is the frequent appearance of what might be children but are probably very small adults. One gives the show its most delightful sequence as she floats out over the audience, attached by a harness to five balloons, while her daddy-clown asks for her to be given back. This time around, though, the Cirque, with its long intermission for selling the maximum amount of merchandise, seems more business than show.

- - -

At the smaller end of town this year's SummerWorks festival has come up with at least one real winner. This is Essay (Theatre Passe Muraille), by Hannah Moscovitch, in which a first-year history student provokes her teacher by handing in a paper about a woman he deems unsuitable. She isn't, though, making a feminist protest (as she memorably says, "It's only an elective"); in fact it's he, one short generation older, who berates her for being insufficiently feminist and gets in trouble for so doing. The twists are endless, and are both aggravated and manipulated by an apparently gleeful professor who has the two of them in his power. Or so it seems. The situation could never be resolved, but it could be further complicated, and I gather the author plans to extend it. In its present state it's tautly directed (by Michael Rubenfeld) and acted (by Jordan Pettle, Claire Jenkins and Richard Greenblatt). Chekhov said that if you bring a revolver onstage, you have sooner or later to use it. This production proves the same to be true of a box of Kleenex. It's quite a debut.

The File, by Greg Nelson (Factory Studio), is a timely piece about ethical principle and government surveillance and the proper or permissible limits of both. It holds as argument, but never transcends it. Two excellent actors (Michael Spencer-Davis and Sarah Dodd) play four characters, courtesy of instant flashback. They spell out the issues, whereas Essay lets us do our own spelling. Democrats Abroad (Passe Muraille Backspace) is a monologue by Chris Earle, a vision that I think and hope is exaggerated of a repressive U.S. flooding Canada with disillusioned soldiers and dissident celebrities ("every city got at least one Baldwin"). If the premise seems glib, Earle's writing can be amusing and heartening, and his performing is spot-on.

In Kathryn: Starring as Herself at Last (Factory), an octogenarian performer, Kathryn Albertson, proves that having appeared on Broadway and being a recovered alcoholic do not, in themselves, make you Elaine Stritch. I think she might be happier talking to us off-the-cuff than acting out a script that her life story fails to justify. Your Palace in the Sky (Palmerston Library) suffers from the delusion that some banal fictitious stories will take on significance if you paste them on to the 1985 Air-India crash. The result is inept and, considering the real suffering involved, insulting.

- Annie Get Your Gun runs through Sept. 3 at Massey Hall, 416- 872-4255. Corteo runs through Sept. 11 at Ontario Place, 1-800-450- 1480. SummerWorks closes tomorrow.
