WRINKLES

Winter Garden Theatre, Toronto

TORONTO - Breezing, not to say wheezing, into town with the least attractive title and logo in recent memory, Wrinkles turns out to be surprisingly not bad. Bits of it are even good.

The show (which I saw at a preview) is a revue, on the old-fashioned model: a collection of songs and sketches from a variety of pens, delivered by an interweaving ensemble of half-a-dozen hard-working performers.

Unlike many revues of the past, it has a unifying theme. Or, to put it another way, all the jokes are on the same subject. The theme, of course, is age and ageing -- though how old you have to be to qualify for the show's attention is left uncertain.

The evening begins with the redoubtable Douglas Chamberlain welcoming us, in what turns out to be the first of a series of recurring monologues, to "seniors boot camp." From now on, he tells us, we are geezers, and our role in life is to be annoying. (Later on, he will issue specific instructions on how best to carry this out, whether in the car or at the supermarket checkout.)

The message seems clear: This is a show about being 65 or over. But then the entire cast come on to do a bouncy musical number, and though some of them are initially supported by canes or walkers, they quickly toss them away. The songs title, probably grimmer than intended, is We're Still Alive, but its most insistently repeated phrase is "nobody on this stage is under 50," which lowers the bar considerably.

Basically, then, this is another entertainment for and about Baby Boomers -- who, somebody has realized, maybe from reading it in the papers, are no longer babies. These are people greatly exercised by the realization that, to quote the title of one of the show's better sketches, Paul McCartneys Fifty-Eight. (Serves him right, I say.)

If the Baby Boomers have discovered getting old, then getting old must really exist. Myself, Im just a little too old to be a Boomer, though I'm not quite as old as Paul McCartney, and our generation -- mine and Paul's -- doesnt have a name, which I find a relief. It means I can be exempt from all this ageist angst, and in fact I came out of Wrinkles feeling younger than I have in years. (If they want to quote me on that, I cant stop them, but it's not the intention.)

In practice, of course, the show finds its material where it can. The actors are mature but in excellent shape, and the people they play go up and down the scale. When the characters hit 70, the performers put on special voices. Anything to do with fading memory, fading strength or -- a great favourite -- fading libido that turns out not to have faded at all, is regarded as fair game.

This leads, as I may have mentioned before, to a certain amount of repetition. One sketch about Viagra may be OK; one sketch, with song attached, about a man's loss of erection may be OK; but having both seems excessively deflating. One writer, Frank Peppiatt, supplies a routine about a middle-aged woman disturbed by her elders continuing sex lives. He follows it with a routine whose punchline depicts a middle-aged woman being shocked by her elders continuing sex lives.

Some of the spoken humour is wretched, but the music comes to the rescue. There's something odd about it, though, a kind of temporal disjunction.

When the characters reminisce about the music of their youth, they are likely to mention Joni Mitchell, Eric Clapton or Blood Sweat and Tears. Boomer stuff, in fact. But the songs they actually sing are in an earlier style: mainstream American theatre music that suggests, at the very latest, the early 1960s.

At one point they go even further and beseech us to Bring Back Swing, apparently oblivious to the fact that somebody already has. (One of these performers actually appeared in Swingstep, for God's sake.) Swing, of course, is now just instant shorthand for nostalgia, but if these characters are meant to be remembering the real 30s and 40s thing, then they're a lot older than anyone else in the show. Most of the time, though, it's very pleasant to hear musicianly tunes that support lyrics you can actually follow and the actors can actually work with. One or two of them are rather solemn (I never knew playwright David French wrote lyrics) but many are funny. Especially well crafted are a clutch of numbers by Stan Daniels, who once wrote a cult Broadway flop called So Long, 174th Street. Two songs by other hands tell the old story of the man who trades in his wife for a younger model and gets taken. Both are good. The first is the more elaborate, the second (by Nancy White), the more effective -- or would be if its predecessor hadnt spiked its guns.

Its noticeably well delivered by Judy Marshak, the coolest person on stage. Her female colleagues are Denise Fergusson, whose persona is that of a maiden aunt who's been at the martinis, and Corrine Conley, whose hoity-toity demeanour wafts us back to the long-lost world of Elizabeth Arden. Two of the men specialize in suavity going to seed: Gary Krawford has the more genially harried manner, Brian McKay the more sonorous voice.

Heinar Pillar, who conceived the show (with Joyce Gordon) also directs it, smoothly enough, against a functional background of flats and obelisks.
