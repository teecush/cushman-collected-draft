SYNCOPATION

Winter Garden Theatre, Toronto

DESDEMONA: A PLAY ABOUT A HANDKERCHIEF

Artword Alternative Theatre, Toronto

I ought to like Syncopation. It is, in its way, a pioneering work: an example of a genre that has never existed before but that I have always thought should have.

Always is maybe pitching it a bit high; let's just say since 1978. That was the year in which Michael Bennett, the creator of A Chorus Line, mounted his follow-up production, a musical called Ballroom, whose main fault was that it was a musical. That is to say it employed the three elements of dialogue, song and dance to tell a story for which only two of them were necessary.

It was about unglamorous people finding a new vitality on the dance floor, and when they danced the show itself came gloriously to life. Unfortunately, they also sang, and not only were their songs lacklustre (though a couple of them have mysteriously survived), they were also unnecessary. Ballroom should have been something new: a play with dances.

Syncopation, by American playwright Allan Knee, is that something, and I wish I could be more excited about it. First performed in New Haven, Conn., in 1999, it is a two-hander, set on the Lower East Side of New York before the First World War.

Henry Ribolow, a 34-year-old meat packer and part-time dish- washer, has placed a newspaper ad for a dancing partner, someone with whom he might conquer the world. Anna Bianchi, who sews beads on dresses for a living, presents herself at Henrys studio, a bleak rented room in a tenement.

Change is in the air -- social, political and musical. The undulating sounds of ragtime emerge from Henrys portable Victrola, inspiring a whole menagerie (think foxtrot, think grizzly bear) of new steps. Oddly enough, the word "ragtime" is never mentioned in Syncopation, but then neither is the word syncopation.

Great subtlety perhaps, but what is in the play is thin and predictable. From the moment Anna, timidly and with misgivings, crosses Henry's threshold, you know the two of them will get together. Of course there are obstacles -- Anna is engaged to another man, Henry still lives with his mother -- but really the only suspense lies in wondering what mood the author will settle for at the end of the play. Will he soothe us with a happy ending or tease us with a bittersweet one? My lips, of course, are sealed; though I cannot forbear remarking that this, following The Graduate, is the second Mirvish presentation in quick succession whose denouement involves a fractured wedding.

The play's angle is that Henry, who believes dance will set you free and who dabbles in radical politics on the side, is actually more inhibited than Anna, so their relationship is syncopated, too.

Anna may be awfully concerned for her reputation; she is bashful when Henry asks her to take his arm, and when he suggests she raise her skirt a fraction to facilitate movement her shocked reply is, "Ive had enough freedom."

But it turns out she knows more about freedom than he does. She is fascinated, and eventually influenced, by a group of advanced women who congregate at a railroad station and are called the odd ladies; she is even more enthralled by an associated odd gentleman.

This, as the author rightly judges, was an explosive time in an exploding place, but very little of that ferment seeps into his play. We get a mild narrative (both characters are diarists, which helps with the exposition) but no sparks, either around or between them, not even when they dance.

Both actors, Cynthia Dale and Geordie Johnson, in fact dance very well. But dancing is not, after all, what they do, and they are unable to sweep one another off their feet or us off ours. Yet the play seems implicitly to demand that they should, and, of course -- audiences being programmed for romance -- we want them to. Maybe this play, unlike Ballroom, should have been a musical; it needs an extra lift.

Dales performance seems to want it to be; she delivers her emotions in tidy little packages, dispatching them swiftly, as if they were preludes to unwritten numbers. She seems constantly about to break into something, if not out in something. Johnson makes heavy weather of his Jewish-American accent and hence of his role.

Both performances, under Kelly Robinson's direction, are well- oiled but glib, signalling what the end of an episode will be before it has arrived. Of course, sizeable elements of this story have already been a musical. They called it Ragtime.

Comparisons of Paula Vogel's Desdemona: A Play About a Handkerchief (also American, vintage 1993) to Tom Stoppards Rosencrantz and Guildenstern Are Dead seem to me inept. Stoppard played absolutely fair with his Shakespearean source, figure- skating with the plot and characters of Hamlet while keeping them scrupulously intact; Vogel takes the three female characters of Othello and changes everything except their names and elements of their dramatic situation.

Before somebody points out that Shakespeare was just as high- handed with his own sources: His plays stand up in their own right, while Vogels absolutely depends on our knowledge of the original.

The result is tawdry and rather confusing. It also, by leaving all the men offstage, never engages with its real conflict.

The big joke is that this Desdemona seems to have slept with every man in Venice and Cyprus except Cassio. She also stands in, or lies down, for the prostitute Bianca on busy nights. Does this mean, the play indignantly asks -- or the publicity tells us that it asks - - that she therefore deserved to be murdered? (No, it doesn't. Next question.) Meanwhile, Emilia has been downgraded from Desdemona's trusty servant to her exploited laundress.

The play does make the occasional neat point about class, if not about sex, and Sue Miners production is as good as may be, given the painful inadequacy of two-thirds of the cast. Sarah Nevilles cockney Bianca, whose dialogue seems to traverse the erotic slang of three centuries, is marginally less excruciating than Anita La Selva's odious English deb of a Desdemona. (The author calls for the accents, but one hopes she didn't want them done this crudely.)

Brooke Johnson plays Emilia Irish and is far better at it -- and at everything else. But then Emilia always was the best role. Shakespeare's Desdemona, though, was a far more independent character than the one we get here, and her willow scene makes a far stronger feminist -- or just human -- statement than anything this play can come up with.

Still, Vogel went on to write the far superior How I Learned to Drive, so there can be such a thing as progress.
