THE TEMPEST

Festival Theatre, Stratford

---

Stratford's new production of The Tempest is, for its first 40 minutes, terrific. After a shipwreck scene that's both exciting and comprehensible, Christopher Plummer takes the stage as Prospero to tell his daughter Miranda, and us, the backstory. He does it superbly, switching back and forth with quicksilver brilliance between present tenderness and past pain, sometimes merging them so that the past itself seems present. He relives every detail, and makes us live it too; when he speaks of the brother who betrayed and banished him, both his anger and his anguish are searing hot.

The subsequent scene with an uncanny, and uncannily effective, Ariel maintains both the pressure and the delight; the parallel encounter with a nondescript Caliban lowers them without, at this point, doing serious damage. Then comes the love-at-first-sight meeting of Miranda and the castaway Ferdinand: very nice, until Ferdinand attempts to draw his sword, only to find it not, as in most productions, glued to its scabbard but waving helplessly in the air on invisible strings. And it's at this point that things start to come apart. Instead of a fearsome magician imposing himself by force of will as much as art, Prospero becomes a children's conjuror, playing twinkling party tricks. Worse, both Ferdinand and Miranda seem to be in on the joke, both here and later; they say they're scared of him but it's never believable. Bang goes one of the prime sources of tension in a play that needs all it can get.

The Tempest is a play that's indisputably great without being, by most conventional criteria, good. Its protagonist is all-important but he's mostly absent during the middle section; then, when he returns, he hardly lets anybody else get a word in. His supernatural powers give him complete external control; the only suspense lies within -- is he going to opt for forgiveness or revenge? Plummer plays the moment of choice with the expected authority; he holds a magisterial, agonized pause. But for the decision to reverberate beyond the moment, we have to believe in the world in which it's made and the people who are the objects of it. The work of establishing those characters has to be done in the play's first half; in the second, they don't stand a chance. In this production, most of them are lay-figures. The evening becomes less and less a play, and more and more the Christopher Plummer show. As such, it's well worth seeing, and hearing. Plummer has virtuoso command of the verse, and Prospero's farewell to his art is a vocal symphony. He can also be endearingly self-deprecating.

Des McAnuff's production certainly keeps him hopping. This Prospero is liable to appear unexpectedly in any corner of his island; in the mock-banquet scene he arises suddenly through a trap-door though, as at this point he's supposed to be invisible, I'm not sure whom he's trying to impress. There's much about McAnuff's direction that I liked; he makes line-by-line sense of the text, something for which he sometimes gets insufficient credit, and he knits the plot threads together by showing us Prospero's victims groping their way around the isle, though perhaps they should look more in acute distress and less as if they were on a nice hike.

Robert Brill's mostly bare set incorporates a revolve that's well used, and a boat that's delightfully used, not least when Julyana Soelistya's tiny Ariel, an apparent refugee from the Blue Man Group, pops up in the corner of it. In fact, nearly everything in, and around, this performance is magical, including her singing of Michael Roth's lovely songs. She's pert and truly unearthly, and yet with a yearning connection to humanity. Her "do you love me master, no?" is moving, and movingly reciprocated. In fact this new father-daughter relationship rather displaces the play's official one.

Trish Lindstrom's homeschooled Miranda has perfect rapport with Plummer in their first scene, but neither she nor Gareth Potter's Ferdinand, doing some phallic business with an impossibly long log, seem able to regard the play's and Prospero's emphasis on chastity as anything more than a giggle. Perhaps we can't play these scenes anymore, but the joke could be on us. Besides, Prospero has seen his daughter almost raped; he's entitled to be neurotic about it.

Prospero and Ariel apart, the stand-out performance is Geraint Wyn Davies' Stephano, which starts with the sozzled Welsh charm of his Bottom and builds into a grotesquely frightening mock-king. He carries the clown scenes almost single-handedly, since Bruce Dow's Trinculo is wearisome camp and Dion Johnstone's Caliban, apart from a nice reading of "the isle is full of noises," almost nonexistent. Caliban may be a noble savage or a savage savage or, and preferably, both, but he shouldn't be bland. He and Prospero have a "moment" at the end, and Prospero has another when he and his brother exchange baleful looks; both are correct, but neither comes out of anything. The courtiers are in Jacobean dress, which makes a nice change, but they never emerge from their elegant shells. Alonso's torment and repentance, Sebastian's vacillation, Antonio's unregeneracy: the actors go through the motions, but never bring them to life. And unless I was nodding off, Gonzalo's benediction, a rare last-act opportunity to see the story from someone else's perspective, has been cut. Also surprisingly truncated is the wedding masque: Three built-up goddesses who display minimal interest in what they're saying, and no dancing. Not much of an engagement present, considering Prospero's, and Stratford's, resources. It also takes some of the edge off what may be Plummer's finest, silent moment: the metaphysical headache, as if his brain were about to split, that precedes the tempered bitterness of his "revels now are ended" speech. This is telepathic acting, and very impressive. But it isn't part of a complete whole.
