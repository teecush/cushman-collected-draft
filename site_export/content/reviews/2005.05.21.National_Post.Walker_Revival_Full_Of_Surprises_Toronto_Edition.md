Adult Entertainment plunges mordantly into sex and violence

Revivals, revivals. A 35-year-old theatre and a 20-year-old company are each celebrating their anniversaries by bringing back one of their greatest hits.

In the case of Factory Theatre (b. 1970), that should perhaps be two of their greatest hits. Or, alternatively, one-third of one of their greatest hits. In 1997 and 1998, the Factory presented Suburban Motel, a cycle of six plays by George F. Walker, all of them set in the one room suggested by that title. They are now reviving two of them. First off, Adult Entertainment.

Walker's Suburban Motel is, to understate matters, a long way from Neil Simon's Plaza Suite, which had a similar linking idea. It could be on the edge of any Canadian city -- indeed, in the current instalment, only a single reference to "the Crown" clues us in that we are even in Canada. That would be the Crown in its legal aspect. Our four characters here are all legals or involved with legals. There are Max and Donny, who are cops; Pam, Donny's estranged wife; and Jayne, Max's girlfriend, who is here for a tryst. She is also a lawyer, and she has an angle. She has a client who is technically guilty but whom she would like to get off, by incriminating the client's notably vicious boyfriend. She asks Max to lean on him. Max agrees. And if you think this makes Max seem bent, you should meet Donny. He's way over the edge.

This, of course, is the world Walker subsequently explored, and is still exploring, in his TV series, This Is Wonderland. Or rather it's a complement to it. I don't remember seeing many cops in Wonderland, crooked or otherwise, and though the lawyers who are its stars have their quirks, they don't seem to have compromised themselves as Jayne has. On the other hand, the TV show lets us see the clients, the wrecked or endangered humanity that is only an off- stage presence in the play.

I have to confess that I prefer This Is Wonderland. I like its humour, which is often uproarious, its sense of the city, its variousness. It has texture. Its alternating storylines engage me more than does the concentrated portrait of four souls in purgatory that we get here. It feels like life, while Suburban Motel, or at least this part of it separated from its original siblings, feels like a very thin sliver of it.

I say this rather nervously because, as a piece of writing for the stage, I can think of absolutely nothing to say against Adult Entertainment. It's taut, it's funny, it's mordant, it's surprising, it's full of sex and violence. It manages the considerable feat of presenting a stageful of self-loathing characters without either patronizing or endorsing them. Its vision isn't theirs. The events are those of a nightmare (literally, in one scene) but the play itself stays sane. The language doesn't sing, but it snaps. And there is one recurring line for Jayne, one to which she clings for assurance when things begin to fall apart, that is every bit as good as it thinks it is.

I have similarly mixed feelings about Ken Gass's production, which is good but doesn't feel ideal. Its best sequence comes at the very beginning and is, strictly speaking, extraneous. Or so I judge from the printed text, whose first stage direction begins "Jayne Johnson and Max Malone are having sex." Then a phone rings and the play starts, and the abruptness of this sets the tone for what follows. Here, though, instead of the characters being discovered, we see them coming into the room, careering around it, pulling off clothes, arriving on the bed. It's well and wittily choreographed, and it puts the "adult" into adult entertainment; we become thinking voyeurs, noting that Jayne is in two minds, and Max very much in one. It's a flaw, though, in the production and maybe in the play, that this isn't followed through. We may be able to work out intellectually how much Max is driven by desire, and how Jayne is split between lust and calculation, but we seldom feel it.

The performers are fine, but they hold us as actors rather than as characters. Jane Spidell, playing her near-namesake, is flamingly talented, and she looks right, but she has to work at the hard- boiledness and also, more curiously, at the vulnerability. Ron White as Max is a solid centre to the action, but he sucks emotion in rather than letting it out; he may be meant to be strong and silent but there must be more going on inside him than the actor lets on. David Ferry as Donny, the former vice-squad man who has been warped by hanging out with hookers, is coiled and cute, in a nasty way, but not really dangerous, and maybe physically wrong; the script says he's large, and Ferry is medium. Linda Prystawska seems initially out of her depth as Pam, but she improves, and makes the most of the play's best line, in which diurnal life incongruously intrudes. She may be the vessel -- not the mouthpiece -- for the play's ultimate message, which is that, though you can't touch pitch without being defiled, pitch will touch you anyway, so deal with it. Next up, The End of Civilization, in which Max and Donny ride again.

The kind of play Adult Entertainment is has worn better than the kind of play The Anger in Ernest and Ernestine is. But genres aren't shows, and the latter piece, which the Theatre Columbus troupe have brought back for their 20th birthday, gave me a better time. What the suburban-motel piece is for its opening few minutes, this one is for its entire length: a danced duet of approach and avoidance, consummation and revulsion. The show was written in 1987 by Leah Cherniak, Robert Morgan and Martha Ross. Cherniak directed it, and does so again now, with Rick Roberts and Jenny Young replacing her co-authors as the actors.

Ernest and Ernestine are a shy pair, doomed to become one. He lives in an anal-retentive world of his own; she has what he calls "pizazz," though it seems more like she's sort of flaky. (She thinks it's smart to leave teabags in the pot, which I think would be grounds for divorce.) But they get married, as they would, and set up house in -- apparently -- a basement with a furnace in the living room. Their breakfasts show where they're headed. Ernest takes his time, counting every cornflake; Ernestine, rushing off to work, performs a ballet, more frantically scrambled at each successive rendition, of chewing, swallowing, dressing, cleaning and farewelling.

There are a good number of reasons for being prejudiced against this show, starting with the title. (Do Ernests marry Ernestines? If so, they deserve all they get.) There is the revelation in the program that Theatre Columbus's founding figures -- its Christopher and Christabel Columbuses -- studied in Paris at the fabled feet of Jacques LeCoq; we have suffered too much over the years in the names of mime and clowning for this to seem anything but a threat. There is a sequence in which the couple try to deal with their unruly furnace by strict adherence to the rulebook, which they read aloud; this seems like one of Eugene Ionesco's gratuitous illustrations of the absurdity of words, people and everything else. There is the fact that everything is predictable; it's a given that these lovebirds are going to end up hating one another. And so they do, without ever quite ceasing -- which is also predictable -- to love one another.

You don't have to have been in a committed relationship for all this to strike home; just having shared a space with someone will usually do the trick. But this piece makes it fresh and makes it clever. It also makes great technical demands on its performers, demands that Roberts and Young meet with almost insouciant assurance. She incorporates a heel-clicking Highland fling (or is it Latin?) and even a touch of ventriloquism without turning a hair; he shows how every man, when he thinks himself alone, can be his own Bruce Springsteen. One especially nice touch: Ernest and Ernestine are never more united than when they can join their anger and focus it outward, against all the forces that would punish them for having driven the wrong way up a one-way street. Maybe that's meant to be another metaphor for marriage.
