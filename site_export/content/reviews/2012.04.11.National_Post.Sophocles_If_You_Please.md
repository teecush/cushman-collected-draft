THEATRE REVIEWS

Ajax & Little Iliad

Enwave Theatre, Toronto

Was Spring

Tarragon Theatre, Toronto

Of the seven surviving tragedies of Sophocles, two deal with the Trojan War: Ajax, one of his earliest plays, and Philoctetes, one of his last. Both are newly pertinent today, at least according to, of all unexpected organizations, the U.S. Army. It has pressed both plays into service to help its soldiers deal with post-traumatic stress disorder. This has caught the attention of two Canadian theatre creators, Evan Webber and Frank Cox-OConnell, and their linked double bill, Ajax & Little Iliad, is the stimulating result. I saw it in Toronto, as part of World Stage last week; it's now going on to Vancouver and Banff, Alta.

We begin with the Little Iliad, the name given to a supposed lost Homeric sequel that covers the parts of the story left untold by the big Iliad, notably the bit about the Trojan horse. Leading up to this, and dramatized by Sophocles, is the tale of Philoctetes, a PTSD sufferer if ever there was one. He was a champion archer who on the approach to Troy was bitten by a serpent, leaving him with a suppurating wound that revolted all who came near him. His Grecian comrades abandoned him on the island of Lemnos.

Ten years later, with Troy still untaken, a prophecy revealed that they needed the power of his bow if ever they were to get the job done. So they sent the crafty Odysseus, who had counselled the marooning of Philoctetes in the first place, to repatriate the bow, with or without its owner. Also along is Neoptolemus, the idealistic son of the recently deceased Achilles, who, repudiating Odyssean trickery, makes an appeal to Philoctetes' better nature, or at least his patriotism. Philoctetes, understandably consumed with anger and bitterness, will have none of it, and it takes a last-minute appearance by the god Heracles (who promises him that if he goes to Troy hell be cured) to change his mind. This is a tragedy with a happy ending.

In the current version we meet Evan (Webber, naturally), a Toronto theatre person whos made Skype contact with Tom (Cox-O'Connell, obviously), an old buddy whom he hasn't seen in years and who is now in the Canadian military, about to leave for Afghanistan. Evan leads him through an impromptu re-enactment of the play, designed to question Toms willingness to go to war, as opposed to Philoctetes determination to stay out of it.

The shifting, sliding relationship between the two guys is cleverly written and delightfully played, and the technical realization is superb. We never see Tom in the flesh: just his face, a tiny image projected on to a doll (antique if not Greek) on Evan's desk. We still feel we've met him; and we get closer to both men, or to the contours of their conversation, by listening to them through headphones.

Our reactions to them - mine, anyway - are not what the authors may have intended, or indeed what I would have expected. Its the patriotic Tom who emerges as honest if confused and the pacifist Evan as manipulative: not so much of his friend as of his source. I have to confess to feeling protective of this material (for sentimental reasons: I once played Philoctetes in school), and though I concede that its up for grabs by anyone who cares to do the grabbing, I detected some fancy footwork here.

For example: When Evan, under pressure, agrees to let Odysseus state his case, it's a different case to the one Sophocles gave him. That case was based on realpolitik; this one is based on camaraderie, which actually makes it seem less honest. Odysseus, by the way, appears here under his more familiar Latin name of Ulysses, and Neoptolemus under his alternate label of Pyrrhus, which apart from rolling more easily off the tongue reminds us that this nice young man is also the merciless sacker of Troy whose acts are horrifically chronicled in the Player's speech in Hamlet. The show obviously wants us to make the connection; indeed Evan draws Toms attention to it.

Greek mythology comes with character complexity built in; its a tapestry with an unlimited number of panels, and each author - from Sophocles to these, his latest descendants - can choose which he wishes to highlight, and how.

Sophocles had a military career as well as a theatrical one, though I think our hosts may be exaggerating both his importance as a general and his incompetence. In their second half, Ajax, they appear, through a splendid piece of trompe l'oeil staging, as a couple of battleweary Athenian vets, attending the theatre to see what their commander has to say about war. We are told something, but hear virtually nothing, of the play itself, whose title character goes mad and kills first some sheep and then himself when his rival Odysseus, still duplicitous, is awarded the coveted armour of Achilles.

Contrary to what's claimed here, theres no tradition that this happened after the fall of Troy, or that Ajax himself was ever in the horse. Still, again, liberty of interpretation and all that. The shows point of view, however, is lost in the (frequently funny) cross-talk and in one piece of deconstruction too many. The real, i.e. paying or freeloading, audience are handed masks so that we can pretend to be the chorus, which also seems pointless. The principals are represented by midget puppets, moved around by the sound designer, Chris Stanton, who is also an accomplished actor and makes a surprise personal appearance. This could be a scholarly joke: Sophocles is celebrated for introducing a third actor to the Greek stage where his predecessors had never dared employ more than two. Anyway, Ajax is the only one of his plays that I had never seen in any form, so I am glad to have had this chance of collecting it, kind of.

The first 20 minutes or so of Danel MacIvor's Was Spring are wonderful. Clare Coulter appears as an elderly woman confined to some kind of institution; white-haired and imperious, radiating fear and assurance in equal proportions, she waxes nostalgic for the days when you could smoke in hospitals and expresses her profound disapproval of everybody and everything in her current existence. One thing she does not seem to be is sorry for herself.

Unfortunately her perfect monologue has to end, and she is joined by one middle-aged woman and one young one. The three are called Kitty, Kath and Kit. One clings desperately to the hope that they might be grandmother, mother and daughter, or maybe just three members of a namesake association, but as they serially reminisce about their suspiciously similar life experiences, the awful truth has to be faced: They are three aspects of the same person, and the play is the anglophone, though mercifully shorter, equivalent of Michel Tremblay's Albertine in Five Times.

A character split several ways inevitably arouses expectations; she is implicitly offered as Everywoman. KKK, though, seems to have had a rather specialized biography, chiefly consisting of an abusive boyfriend and an abusing daughter. Theres hardly enough of her to go around.

As each of her upbraids the others for what she was or will become, Coulter maintains the edge, devastatingly disapproving even when silent. Caroline Gillis gives a reading, competent but featureless; Jessica Moss, superb in her own play Modern Love, seems constrained in this one. There are some good lines, including a real heart-stopper about shy not being called painful for nothing. But mainly its another evening of people sitting around being lyrical.
