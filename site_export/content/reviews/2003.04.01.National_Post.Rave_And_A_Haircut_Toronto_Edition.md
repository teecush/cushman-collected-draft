SWEENEY TODD

Bluma Appel Theatre, Toronto

"Mrs Lovett," sings George Masswohl's Sweeney Todd, escaped convict and ascetic avenger, to Fiona Reid's cheerfully practical purveyor of meat pies, "how I've lived without you all these years I'll never know!" Sweeney the barber has slit the throat of a man who threatened to reveal his secret and deliver him to the law; she, desperately short of raw materials, has pointed out what a sin it would be to let a juicy, newly dead body go to waste.

Sweeney has just been balked of killing the man he was really after, the judge who once had him transported on a trumped-up charge so as to have easier access to the beauteous and virtuous Mrs. Todd, and the disappointment has sent him over the edge, into universal and murderous misanthropy. In Mrs. Lovett, who fancies him, he has found the ideal partner. The deranged visionary meets the businesslike amoral functionary. It's the combination that makes atrocities possible. In bringing them together, and showing just how and why each needs the other, Stephen Sondheim's musical touches greatness.

Sweeney Todd derives from a play by English dramatist Christopher Bond, who took the archetypal melodramatic figures of the demon barber and his culinary partner and supplied them with a back- story. It was Bond, taking a hint from The Count of Monte Cristo, who made Sweeney the victim of a judicial frame-up that he learns, on his return, to have had consequences that outrun his most feverish nightmares: The judge raped Todd's wife, who went mad and took poison. Joanna, the couple's daughter is now the judge's pent- up ward whom he, pushing his passions forward a generation, now plans to marry.

Bond's play, closely followed in Hugh Wheeler's spare musical book, springs at least one superb narrative surprise; Sondheim's music, which is virtually continuous, carries, colours and deepens the story, driving through it like the "perfect machine" to which the chorus compares the hero's compulsively turning mind.

Sweeney's obsession is of tragic proportions (there are very few musical protagonists of whom that could be said) and his murderous fury spends itself as relentlessly and as self-destructively as Macbeth's.

Morris Panych's CanStage production is imperfect but the size, emotional rather than physical, is there. Visually it lacks some of the expected Panych panache, and Ken MacDonald's set is a disappointingly conventional collection of scaffolding, steps and catwalks. Sweeney's tonsorial parlour, where much of the most crucial (and bloodiest) action takes place, is remote from us; it can, as it turns out, be trundled closer to us but the effect, when it comes, is laborious and belated.

Panych staged the wonderful mime drama The Overcoat (which is returning to Toronto as part of this year's WorldStage season prior to opening in London) and he is his own choreographer here. He keeps the commenting crowd in ceaseless motion, sometimes fussily and occasionally to the point of coyness.

Some scenes, though, he stages brilliantly. The second-act opening, in which the citizens of London, unknowing cannibals, thump their tables and demand "more hot pies," is more uproarious and more chilling than I have ever known it before. (The song is called God, That's Good.) The madhouse outbreak, which empties a covey of lunatics into a town already sufficiently crazed, is superbly done. The "city's on fire" song that accompanies it inaugurates an almost unbroken and superbly resourceful musical sequence that carries the show right through to its final scene. Here Sondheim fully justifies his own claims about music as an ideal engine of suspense. I was on the edge of my seat throughout.

Musically, the performance is very strong. Bruce Kellett's musicians play what I assume are cut-down versions of Jonathan Tunick's original orchestrations, with the vital parts intact. The opening Ballad of Sweeney Todd, with its seductive rhythm and its eerie chords that seem to have risen from the murkiest depths of the Thames, is as potent as ever, more interesting and more haunting than Mack the Knife, to which it bears a scene-setting resemblance. It recurs as narrative punctuation, and you listen avidly every time. (And as for lyrics, when you hear the early expository couplet "And what if none of their souls were saved / They went to their Maker impeccably shaved," you know you're safe with the show.)

The company does full justice to the choral writing, which is the finest in any musical in decades. Among the solos and duets, Pretty Women is as yearning a ballad as Sondheim has written, perversely lovelier for being sung in duet by the bereaved Sweeney and the arch- enemy he is about to dispatch. The crashing, discordant Epiphany carries Sweeney persuasively over the brink of madness before the famous duet A Little Priest in which he and Mrs. Lovett celebrate their new mass-catering system and spell out the ingredients. I seem to be the only person who doesn't find this one of Sondheim's best lyrics, but the rollicking waltz tune carries it, making it the outrageously effective first-act finale it is designed to be.

For all the heartless jollity, this is a moral show. It is a dark show (as opposed to a merely cynical one like Chicago, whose movie audience is swallowing it quite happily as escapist entertainment) but though it sympathizes with Sweeney, and gives him energy, it isn't on his side. The verdict on him is pronounced very simply and effectively by the simple-witted apprentice Tobias: "You shouldn't hurt people, Mr. Todd."

It's capped by another violent narrative twist (which some strange cuts make less powerful than it should be), but it isn't contradicted. The hero's passion, in approved tragic form, spends itself, leaving the title song to make a sombre summing-up -- not, as it's sometimes misinterpreted, "we are all guilty," but "psychopaths are more common than you think." Panych's production, which mercifully refrains from poking its fingers at the audience, makes the point very well.

The two lead performances are admirably balanced. Masswohl, principally a singer, keeps a brooding grip on the role, occasionally and effectively breaking into a kind of speaking-on- pitch. His face is a bland mask that only cracks at the last, posthumous moment, but this is surprisingly effective; the man is essentially unknowable. Fiona Reid makes Mrs. Lovett's spoken lines as flavourful, and funnier, than I have ever known them, radiating a merry, conscienceless practicality; she anybody's cockney mum. In what is effectively her musical debut, she does astonishingly well in some very tricky songs, rarely faltering in pitch and never in rhythm, and never missing a lyrical point. The only number that defeats her is By the Sea, Mrs. Lovett's dream of a coastal retirement, perhaps because it's the only song in the score that is a number, to be performed rather than acted. (A campy staging doesn't help her, though it's presumably meant to. )

There are especially good supporting performances from Shaun Amyot as Toby and Mary Ann McDonald as a mysterious, premonitory Beggar Woman. Damien Atkins, bent double and speaking in a kind of piping sneer, is too grotesque for the beadle but sings his parlour ballads very amusingly. Regan Thiel chirrups sweetly enough as Johanna who, after spending two acts playing the simpleton, turns out to be a determined chip off her dad's block, and Tyley Ross is very pleasant as her sailor lover. This is a self-described "musical thriller" that fills both halves of that prescription to the limit and beyond.
