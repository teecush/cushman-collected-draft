Both of the operas to have arrived in the Canadian Opera Company's repertoire over the past two weeks are comedies. Rossini's Cinderella (La Cenerentola) is a re-telling, in largely farcical terms, of the most famous fairy tale of them all. Richard Strauss's Ariadne auf Naxos is actually about comedy, and its intersection with tragedy. Both provide interesting sidelights on the theatrical world beyond the opera house: We do musical and nonmusical plays now, especially the classics, and on the whole tradition of the backstage play, and especially the backstage musical.

The COC's Cinderella impressed me as maybe the first comic opera I've ever seen that is actually funny. (It's also a musical feast, from the sumptuous overture onwards.) The production is one of those that has been bought in from abroad. Four other opera houses are involved but the prime mover is a Catalan director, Joan Font, who heads a multi-disciplinary company -a clown super-troupe, to judge by description, called Els Comediants. Members of this group are responsible for the show's design and choreography, both of which are delightful. The production's most obvious innovation is the addition to the cast of halfa-dozen mice. At least I, conditioned by Disney, took them to be mice; a rival faction holds that they are rats. Anyway the six long-tailed rodents act as Cinderella's sympathetic attendants, and also as all-purpose scene-shifters. They are most beguilingly danced, and they never (well, hardly ever) obscure the main action. It's worth a visit just to see them lined up in a row on the floor, their chins cradled in their paws, caught up in the conversation between the heroine and the opera's equivalent of her fairy godmother, who happens to be neither fairy nor female. Easier to miss, but lovely, is the mouse (or rat) who curls up and goes to sleep on a pillow thrown on the stage by Cinders' tyrannical impoverished stepfather, roused from slumber.

This is another gender reversal from the story we've grown up on. We expect a wicked stepmother. Rossini and his librettist go back to the original tale as told by Charles Perrault, a version not as grim as Grimm but still plenty tough enough. The ugly sisters could hardly be more snobbish or unpleasant, or their father, for a sign of whose affection Cinderella still movingly yearns, a more unscrupulous or more unfeeling social climber. The production has been criticized for making his scenes with Dandini, the prince's valet disguised as the prince, too slapstick, but I can't see how else they could or should be done. Brett Polegato's Dandini, revelling in the brief opportunity to be his boss's boss, is a clever valet who beats any Figaro I've seen, Rossini's or Mozart's.

Equally amusing is the staging of the huge chorus, the prince's retinue improbably invading the baron's house, and all nodding their heads in obsequious bureaucratic unison. They make a good satiric point, as does the staging of a ball in which the rats (or mice) are the waiters and Cinderella's family the only visible guests. The laughs throw into sharper relief the technical and emotional heights scaled by Elizabeth DeShong's coloratura Cinderella and the ambiguous ardour of Lawrence Brownlee's game-playing prince.

Only the suggestion at the end that the whole thing has been Cinderella's chimney-corner dream seems a bitter twist too far. We already know from Dandini's rueful admission of his real impoverished status that fairy tales don't usually come true; we don't need to have it rubbed in. There's no coach-and-pumpkin scene, but the set performs its own transformation -airy, magical and economical -from kitchen to palace. We've been hearing a lot recently about "European style" in the theatre; this clean-lined production is a triumphant example of it. Opera is often the crucible of theatrical production, and the COC veers regularly between old-fashioned and cutting-edge. Cinderella is the latter.

Ariadne auf Naxos falls between the two, as may befit a work that promises more stylistically than it delivers. There is a prologue, in which a rich capricious patron decrees that a tragic opera and a burlesque commedia shall be performed simultaneously. The opera people are appalled, the comedians cheerfully grit their teeth; the show goes on. The preceding proceedings, especially on this production's realistic backstage set, prefigure a long line of American musicals. Zerbinetta, the soubrette, realistic about sex while yearning for just one serious admirer, recalls the girl in Show Boat who sings about Life Upon the Wicked Stage, while the whole set-up, intermingling life and performance, suggests last year's dazzling Stratford production of Kiss Me Kate. And I've a suspicion that Cole Porter did both the romantic and the deflationary part better.

The opera proper concerns an abandoned heroine, Ariadne, determined on suicide but deflected by the arrival of the god Bacchus. Or, as Zerbinetta sardonically comments, by just another man. I'm not sure that she's doing more than underlining the obvious; after all, that would still have been the story even without the comedians' intrusions. Vulgarian that I am, I wanted the conflict between them to be more direct, and Zerbinetta's triumph to be more emphatic. I also wanted the opera's self-important young composer, who in the prologue seems quite smitten with Zerbinetta, to come back and admit defeat or perhaps -which would surely be a tragiccomic point -a shared half-victory.

The production's skimpy secondact set suggests that the invisible patron is a skinflint as well as a prankster. I'm not sure what to make of the fact that Ariadne's three Wagnerian attendants are funnier than the actual clowns, skilful though their performances are. Jane Archibald's Zerbinetta is more than skilful, physically as well as vocally; if she ever wants a change from opera, she could be an ace musical-comedy performer. She's one of a trio of superb Canadian female singers in this production, the others being Alice Coote, delightfully pompous in the travesti role of the composer, and Adrianne Pieczonka, who has now resumed the role of Ariadne; she was ill at the premiere but her understudy, Amber Wagner, performed with increasing confidence and ended in triumph.

The idea of the piece is wonderful, the execution patchy, though the librettist, Hugo von Hofmansthal, was a searching comic playwright in his own right, one whose plays are well worth investigating. I'd recommend The Deep Man.
