Maybe it takes foreign visitors to make us really look at our city. The confluence of the Luminato and New World Stage festivals has brought us two companies from abroad who have mounted site- specific productions in parts of Toronto to which your average theatre-goer, or indeed your average theatre, rarely travels. One of them even brought along a Toronto play.

This was Risk Everything, originally seen as the last component of George F. Walker's Suburban Motel cycle, and now presented by a Polish troupe, TR Warszawa, at Mc-Bride's Bicycle Shop, way out west on Dundas. The play centres on Carol, whose philosophy is that of the title, and who has exercised it by stealing $68,000 from a thug called Steamboat. She is now holed up, terrified but unrepentant, with her comparatively practical daughter, Denise, and Denise's timid husband, R.J. They receive one visitor, on whom Carol avidly and lubriciously fastens; he's Miki, a porn-film director known in this production, though not in Walker's original, as Miki Fucky. Walker's work is not exactly short on sex and violence, but here both are way upfront.

The play is performed against big store windows giving on to the street, so part of the fun for the official audience comes from watching the reactions of the unofficial audience outside. The people on Dundas West greeted with commendable sang-froid the sight of Carol sitting on the toilet or of Miki attacking her, equipped with a huge strap-on. The street life, in turn, contributed to the play; when a police car drove by, sirens sounding, the whole cast hit the floor as if by prior arrangement. Sometimes the two worlds met; when characters, especially Carol, went out into the street and accosted passersby, or when, twice, a white SUV roared to a halt disgorging from its trunk the bloodied figures first of R.J., then of Miki, both of them having rashly tried to intercede with Steamboat. His response is to deliver them both back home with dynamite stuck to their chests. This is one effect that worked better in the original when it was done deadpan.

The Polish production is announced as a mixed-media show. In practice, this means that there's a functioning TV on the set. But the script calls for that, anyway; R.J. is addicted to the tube, and the play is partly about his efforts to wean himself from it. There are surtitles, though the cast frequently attempt the English text, or approximations of it, and this is rather charming. Essentially, we are getting Walker's none-too-subtle original with the volume turned up and the result, though prolonged, is both impressive and invigorating. Carol's risk-everything code, a mixture of greed and disrespect for the lives of others, has always struck me as a playwright's confection, but it benefits from the full-out performance of Aleksandra Konieczna, who is shamelessness personified. Agnieszka Podsiadlik's Denise is a good sober counterweight, and Jan Drawnel's R.J. a delight, always on the verge of protest without ever quite getting there.

From Australia, Urban Theatre Projects takes us a little closer to home (home, in Toronto theatre circles, being approximately Queen and Bathurst) with a piece that in fact is called Back Home and is presented in "an undisclosed Toronto backyard," which turns out to adjoin the Centre for Addiction and Mental Health on Queen West. The dramatic setting, though, remains Australian. Four youngish men, bearing the names of their actors (Aaron Fa'Aoso, Buddy Dannoun, Leo Tanoi, Shannon Williams), gather for a reunion barbecue. One of them is Middle Eastern, one Caribbean, one Black Australian, one Native Australian. All, by mainstream accounting, are minority; all feel themselves to be marginalized; they alternate between sharing their grievances and taking them out on one another. Buddy, the host, is an aspiring DJ, and here too amplified music punctuates the action. Here, though, it's pivotal rather than decorative, in a production (by Alicia Talbot) that stakes as much on naturalism as the Poles do on style, though the results are oddly similar: overlong but powerful. This is also a magical-mystery-tour production; you board a bus at York Quay to get to the action, and en route you get a lecture on Toronto's native heritage

- Risk Everything closes tonight at McBride's Bicycle Shop. Back Home also closes tonight; board the bus at Harbourfront Centre, 7:15 p.m.
