THE LETTERS OF NOEL COWARD

Edited by Barry Day

Knopf

800 pp., $47

---

How, on a given day, did Noel Coward decide which of his talents to use? The acting, singing, directing and producing would obviously be scheduled in advance, but how about the writing choices? What prompted him to compose a song, a play, a story, a novel, a poem, a diary entry or -- something he seems to have done at some point on most days --a letter?

Probably it wasn't a problem. Coward was a single-minded multi- talent, an enviable combination. One of his charms seems to have been that, while always ferociously busy, he had time for people; for friends, colleagues and family. Though in his case "family," at least in the blood-relative sense, boiled down to one person. The same man who created Mrs. Worthington, that song in which the stage- struck mother and her awful progeny are forever immolated, owed his own career to Mrs. Violet Coward's maternal determination to put him on the stage, at the age of 11. He never forgot it, and neither did she. The theatre was his passport out of genteel suburban poverty, and it was hers as well: literally -- her son was able to take very good care of her, and incidentally of his father and brother, for whom he had little personal warmth -- but also vicariously. She bathed in his glow, and he loved the fact that she did. Even before she changed his life by securing him a role in a London Christmas show, she was his confidante. When sent away to stay with relatives, he wrote her longing letters beginning "Dear Darling old Mummy- snooks," or "mother duckie."

Homesickness, Barry Day observes, was "a condition that was to apply for many years to come when he was separated from his mother." Day, who has previously edited a handsome and formidably complete collection of Coward's lyrics, has not so much annotated the letters (he explicitly rejects footnotes) as contextualized them. He has in fact given us a Coward biography, with the letters -- including some written to the subject rather than by him -- as the chief exhibit. What becomes clear is that you could tell most of the Coward story, at least up until 1954, when Violet died, by his letters to her, so concerned was he that she be made to feel part of it. The filial tone changes somewhat as he grows older, or perhaps just as he grows more successful. The salutation is shortened to "Darling," and the signature is "Snoop." Hers, to him, is "Snig." These exchanges were probably more fun for them to write than they are for us to read. (Though they are restraint itself compared to the way Coward habitually greeted Alexander Woollcott, the self-anointed wit of the Algonquin Round Table; he was "Dearest Acky-wacky-weesa." It may have suited him. He certainly doesn't seem to have been very grown- up.)

Regular and affectionate though the letters to Violet may be, they are informative rather than revealing. He has, after all, a duty to put a good face on everything. Troubles with temperamental actors are treated as jokes, and any personal unhappiness goes unmentioned. His love life is strictly out of bounds, and not only when writing to Mum. Probably the love of Coward's life was his American manager and producer, Jack Wilson, but as their relationship cools it's Wilson's professional rather than his personal conduct that comes in for censure. Livid though Coward obviously was at Wilson's grievous mismanagement of his finances, he cannot bring himself to be rude to him. He tries to make allowances. And this is consistent with all of his friendships. Coward, by many people's testimony, was a very kind man. He certainly seems to have found it hard to be explicitly unkind. You feel that he swallowed a lot of bile.

It seems to be the flip side of his refusal to admit emotional vulnerability. "To hell with God-damned L'Amour," he writes to Marlene Dietrich, who's been sending sorrowful epistles about her (mostly) unrequited passion for Yul Brynner; "it always causes far more trouble than it's worth." He liked to pretend that he himself was above it, though we know from other sources that he wasn't. If he disparaged romance, he exalted friendship, particularly in that of theatrical peers such as the Lunts and of the small circle of helpers and associates whom he came to think of as his "family." One of them was theatrical designer Gladys Calthrop, whom he addressed as "Cock" and to whom he wrote nearly as often as he did to his mother, and far less guardedly. From Jerusalem: "I flew here yesterday and it's a fair f--ker except for the Arabs and Jews fighting each other and me having to faire the gentil promenade."

This was 1938, and the prelude to his career, still too little known or appreciated, as a wartime agent. He shared with Winston Churchill (here "my dear Winston," though they admired rather than liked one another) the distinction of being among the few Englishmen to have been consistently right about Hitler. He was sent to Paris to help set up a Bureau of Propaganda, an assignment that found him at first enthusiastic and then disillusioned, before setting off as a roving observer-entertainer through the United States, Australia and most of the Asian Front. Thence home to write, direct and star in In Which We Serve (and he didn't even like movies).

He did his greatest work before the war, became a figurehead during it and fell from favour after it. Notoriously, he attacked the new young playwrights of the 1950s, just as he himself had been attacked in the '20s for writing such radical plays as The Vortex. That was in print; in person the story was, again, something else. One of the most cheering entries in his Diaries is the realization that, against all his instinctive prejudices, he is "on [Harold] Pinter's wavelength." He expands on this here in letters to the playwright: "You cheerfully break every rule of the theatre that I was brought up to believe in, except the cardinal one of never boring for a split-second. I love your choice of words, your resolute refusal to explain anything ..." It's one of the best concentrated pieces of Pinter criticism I've read.

He also maintained mutually admiring correspondences with John Osborne, Edward Albee and, despite grave philosophical differences, Arnold Wesker. This section, a record of talent saluting talent, is the most valuable and revealing in the book.

Almost the last letter printed is from his 70th year, three years before his death. His doctors, to his fury, had told him to exercise and give up smoking. He writes, "I suppose I could make very old bones indeed -- look at Mum -- but I'm not at all sure I want that. I've never wanted to be the last to leave any party." The letter was written to Gladys Calthrop. It was signed Yours, LAZARUS.

FROM 'NOELIE' WITH LOVE - To his secretary, Lorn Loraine, about actress Tallulah Bankhead, who had given him an Augustus John painting only to demand it back, Coward wrote, "Tallulah, apart from being a conceited slut, is a black liar. She gave me the bloody picture.Why in hell should I wish to be lent an Augustus John portrait of a pear drop? Send it back!" A year or two later, American theatre director Jack Wilson was considering casting the actress. Coward wrote again to Lorn, "The play he has for Tallulah is based on a Henry James novel. It is very gentle and subtle and if Miss Bankhead can discipline herself to refrain from farting to get a laugh or bouncing ping-pong balls off her tits, it might be a success." - When he was 64, Coward wrote to Laurence Olivier ("Larryboy"), "It is being slowly and painfully beaten into my skull that I am no longer a precocious boy of 19, although when I look in the mirror at this lovely little heart-shaped face with all those pretty little jowls hanging from it, I find it hard to believe." - The next year he wrote to his friends the acting team Alfred Lunt and Lynn Fontanne, "I have had several chins taken away in London by a brilliant plastic surgeon ... I now look an old 12."
