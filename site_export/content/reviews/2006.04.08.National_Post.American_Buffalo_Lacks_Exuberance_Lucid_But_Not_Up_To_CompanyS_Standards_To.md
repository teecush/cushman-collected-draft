When its animal, an American buffalo is a member of a threatened and depleted species. When it's mineral, it's a coin that has a similar rarity value, though the price it will fetch is largely determined by its physical condition; which, come to think of it, must be true of the bovine definition as well. David Mamets play American Buffalo embraces both meanings, the first as metaphor, the second as plot device, though theres a certain amount of crossover between the two.

Mamet's three characters see themselves as businessmen, robber barons, rugged individualists of the type that made America America. That was a long time ago. Now -- in 1977, to be exact -- it's tough to get a break, especially if youre trying to scrape a living on the back streets of Chicago, within earshot of the El. Donny, the most grounded of the three, runs a second-hand store. Bobby, a young addict, is Donnys gofer. (A junkie in a junk shop. Ha.) Teach, as Damon Runyon might have put it, does the best he can, a profession as overcrowded in Mamet's Chicago as it was on Runyon's Broadway. He is always angry. The first thing he says when he walks into the store is F---in Ruthie, which he repeats five, or it might be six, times. What this Ruthie may or may not have done to him is immaterial. The point is that, in Teachs mind, someone is always doing something.

As in Mamet's next play, Glengarry Glen Ross (which is both a refinement and an expansion of the same territory), the characters spend much of their time planning a break-in. Donny sold a buffalo nickel to a collector for what he now considers a ridiculously low price. So he wants to get it back and take the rest of the man's hoard while hes at it. He enlists Bobby to help him with the job, and Teach angles himself in, too. But none of the three wholly trusts the others, and things fall apart.

Mamets dialogue no longer has the thrill of novelty, but it still has both music and muscle. In this play, he depends less than usual on the fast overlap and more on the built-up aria. He famously makes poetry out of profanity; he also shows his characters, who have imaginative minds but limited horizons, striving toward a philosophy. Commonplace words become witty through artful repetition; concepts compress and crash together. Teach insists on packing a gun in case "God forbid, something inevitable happens."

Stuart Hughes's production for Soulpepper is lucid (something not to be taken for granted in handling Mamet) and reasonably entertaining but, by the standards of both play and company, disappointing. It lacks both terror and exuberance. Victoria Wallace's set, piled high with bric-a-brac and deep and wide as well, is breathtaking at first glance, but inappropriately charming as the action proceeds. (Her costumes, though, are excellent).

Ted Dykstras Teach plays things very close to the chest. Hes a hunched ball of resentment from his first appearance to his last, his accent sounds right and his obscenely slicked-down hair tells us exactly who he thinks he is. Dykstra gets to the soul of the character, but he misses the equally important outward flourishes. Teach's paranoia should be baroque in its excess; this is a man who can say of someone he has never met and of whom he has barely heard, "guys like that, I like to f--- their wives." And at the end, both performance and production literally pull their punches. Teach administers a savage beating, or so he has done in previous stagings; here it's down to a single blow. Its followed by a fit of destructive rage to which neither his heart nor his hands seem committed. If its any consolation, he's a lot better in the role than Al Pacino.

Jeff Lillico's Bobby is appealing but unsuitably healthy, even wholesome. Michael Hanrahan comes off best with an understated, compassionate performance as Donny. Donny is compassionate, up to a point. He looks out for Bobby, again up to a point, and dislikes violence. But it is he who sets the plot in motion, out of a mixture of greed and pique, planning a crime to avenge an imagined slight. A saint he aint, but Hanrahan shows us little of his underside. Think of Donny as Tony Soprano to Teachs Paulie, with Bobby as a cross between Christopher and Anthony Jr. I don't know if Mamet was a direct influence on The Sopranos, but he certainly helped make that show possible.

A higher-toned American classic from the previous decade, Edward Albee's Whos Afraid of Virginia Woolf? is a play that, like American Buffalo, retains its stylistic zing long after its shock effect has worn off. As ostentatiously long as Buffalo is disconcertingly short, it sets its sights not just on America, but on the whole civilization of which its a part. At least, it can't be for nothing that George, the history professor, brings down the second-act curtain with a reading from The Decline of the West. And of course he, an ageing history professor, spends some of the play sparring with Nick, a new appointment to his college's biology faculty.

Not that I think any of this means much; were all much more concerned with the bloody feuding between George and his wife, Martha, and with how they bait Nick and his wife, Honey, who step so unwarily into their marital lair. And with how much they all drink; thats something that had disappeared by Mamet's time. George and Martha, America's first first couple; the names must be significant, or maybe theyre just their authors little joke.

In other words, the play's intellectual superstructure is a pretentious irrelevance, but the wit-combats work like a charm. They were very well orchestrated in Ron Ulrich's just-closed production at the Neptune Theatre in Halifax, which was better than almost anything weve seen in Toronto this year. Nigel Bennetts dry, suffering George made the running in the first 1 1/2 acts, as Georges tend to. George, for whom defence is the best offence, has the choicest wisecracks.

Sad, flamboyant Martha, of course, has her share, and it was no surprise that a flaming-haired and flaming-mouthed Fiona Reid made the most of them. What was revelatory was the way she, with Bennett's collaboration, transformed the last act, practically drawing a map of the character's sickness and despair. It concerns, you may recall, the couples fantasy of having a son, and Georges methodical destruction of it. I have never believed either in the fantasy or in the self-aware cut-and-dried games the two of them play with it, but I believed this time in the results, watching Reid, as if on an operating table, unravelling and, just possibly, reravelling again.

Geoffrey Pounsett was a good, pugnacious Nick, and Mary Ashton a perfect mouse of a Honey. Both couples are childless; that must portend something, if portentousness is your thing. George and Martha, for better and worse, have stayed together; one doubts if their juniors will have the same luck, or lack of it. As in American Buffalo, the characters -- the two principals, anyway -- end by reclaiming something of their best selves. That looks more sentimental in Albee than it does in Mamet, but this production, by never letting go of the bleakness, made it touching.
