THEATRE REVIEW THE CEZANNE SYNDROME

Friday, June 15, 1990

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Written by Normand Canac-Marquis Translated by Louison Danis Directed by Jim Millan Starring John Jarvis, Martha Burns and George Merner At the Brigantine Room, Toronto Onstage car repairs notwithstanding, some things are just plain mystifying Now, is that Cezanne or Suzanne? BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail THE Cezanne Syndrome opened at the World Stage festival at Torontos Harborfront on Wednesday, three years after receiving its world premiere in Montreal. In the meantime, it has been seen in Ottawa, Chicago, Vancouver and New York. In each city, it received reviews that the Toronto producers have been happy to flourish in their handouts. It is a horrible thought, but maybe there should be a theatrical Meech Lake, charged to expedite the progress of important Canadian plays to the city that fancies itself Canada's theatrical capital.

I make that point out of a sense of duty, since personally I could have lived without The Cezanne Syndrome for another three years if not for ever. It reaches us as a piece of fancy cultural bullying. At an early point, a police officer wanders on - at least, that's what he is for most of the evening, but at this moment hes moonlighting as an art critic - to tell us that Cezanne in his painting ignored realism in the interests of a deeper reality. Due warning is also given by the program, which tells us, more or less, that we are to see a series of disjointed flashbacks in the mind of the central character.

This suggests the need for a highly theatrical symbol-crashing production, such as the play apparently received at earlier points in its odyssey. However, Jim Millans talent, at least as demonstrated in Unidentified Human Remains and the True Nature of Love, is for lucid storytelling lubricated by wit. Normand Canac-Marquis's text has few pretentions as narrative, and is not, one hopes, trying to be funny, though some of its shortcomings here may be attributed to Louison Danis's stilted translation. Millan accordingly comes up with doggedness tempered by oddity. Everything seems to be going on in a stodgy, interminable present tense.

There are, admittedly, strange things. The hero, Gilbert, is a motor mechanic and he appears to be fixing a car in his bedroom; at least, he and his live-in lady make love on top of it. Other times they quarrel, and the author has a keen eye, if not ear, for the architecture of domestic warfare. This, though, is something for which most people hardly need to go to the theatre, and if they do they generally have several plays a season from which to choose.

The woman, identified in the cast list as Her, conducts a tape-recorded correspondence with an idealized friend who apparently has everything she herself lacks. The name of this friend is Suzanne, so the title is a highly significant joke. As if that wasnt enough, it turns out at the end that Suzanne is really Hers own name. This is not a satisfying discovery. Indeed, it reeks of old-fashioned mystification.

Now, Gilbert's love in fact left him; she drove off, taking their child with her, and both of them perished in a grisly crash. So Gilbert has plenty of reason for feeling disoriented, and for taking a blowtorch to the car, literally, and to his memories, figuratively. In retrospect I sympathize with him. At the time I was exasperated; I couldn't tell what was going on, and had little interest in finding out. The play's doughy quality enervated me.

It was greatly assisted in this by the lifelessly literary performance of Martha Burns, which must have got stuck during rehearsal, just after the line-learning stage. By comparison, John Jarvis as Gilbert quivers with humanity, perhaps too tremulously; most patrons will wish that they could entrust their own auto repairs to so sensitive a soul. George Merner completes the cast as the cop whose name is given to us as Thomas Wancicovski, which certainly puts him ahead of Gilbert and Her in the identity stakes. He has a lot less to say, but contributes generously to the pervasive portentousness.

And those of us who, what with Robert Lepage and Denys Arcand, have been inclined to prostrate ourselves before the dramatic talents of French Canada have learned a chastening but probably useful lesson.
