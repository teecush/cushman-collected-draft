There's never a boring moment in God of Carnage, but there are few really arresting ones, either. The characters are Alan and Annette and Michael and Veronica, two American married couples. In Yasmina Rezas original script they were French, like the play itself, and so they were in Christopher Hamptons English translation when it was first presented in London. They then underwent a transatlantic transplant for ease of North American consumption. This kind of parochial patronage isn't precisely what the play itself is satirizing but it is in the same, how you say, ballpark.

Alan and Annette, the more upscale of the pairs, have a son who got in a fight with the son of Michael and Veronica, and knocked out two of his teeth. (Both the unseen boys are 11.)

The victim's parents have invited the assailants parents over, so that a civilized apology may be drafted. At first all seems to be going politely, but then rifts appear, not just between the marriages but within them. What first tips things over are the calls Alan keeps getting on his cellphone, which he can never resist answering, in full hearing of the others. They and we learn that he is a lawyer counselling a pharmaceutical company on how not to accept liability for a potentially lethal drug. Veronica, who writes well-intentioned books about the worlds worst countries, is appalled by this. So is Annette, though not for moral reasons; she resents her husband's absorption in his work, of which his phone is an umbilical symbol. Michael, who runs an appliance business, is less affronted by Alan's ethics than by his superior airs. As the evening proceeds, the two men are revealed as, essentially, allies. In the plays title speech Alan proclaims his belief, exemplified by both his own and his sons generation, in a murderously aggressive spirit that, despite all the comforting lies we may tell ourselves, rules our thoughts and actions. Michael echoes him by copping to the inner Neanderthal he has been repressing in resentful deference to his wife's liberal values.

Linked to this is the assertion that good deeds - Veronica's for example - are motivated by the desire to think well of oneself. This is convincing if not especially disturbing; after all, its the results rather than the causes that count. It also has an ironic flip-side; even Alan is shamed at one point into doing a decent thing, and it hardly seems to matter whether hes impelled by conscience or by the knowledge that the others' eyes and ears are upon him. Anyway, that's about all the play has to say. It spins out variations, mostly verbal but sometimes ingeniously physical, on a single theme. Annette, after too much drink and dessert, throws graphically up over Veronicas cherished volume of Kokoschka illustrations, thereby putting her hostess into an un-pretty pickle of aggrieved indignation and hospitable compassion.

God of Carnage is an off-Mirvish production by Joel Greenbergs Studio 180 who recently brought us Clybourne Park, another conversation piece in which conciliation gave way to violence, but one in which both the issues and the construction were considerably more complex and far-reaching. That play was witty to the bone; this one is merely funny at the fingertips. The acting is good, though John Bourgeois' Alan gets too easily ruffled too often. Tony Nappo, with a strong physical and vocal resemblance to the late James Gandolfini who played the role on Broadway, slips easily into Michael's rages and resentments. The women, though, have the best of it, Sarah Orensteins damaged Annette falling elegantly apart and Linda Kashs Veronica trying and explosively failing to keep it together. Since it's her house that's the setting, John Thompson has honoured her taste with a striking design in red and black.

For social satire more biting and locally pertinent, travel a couple of blocks south on Yonge to The Little Mermaid. This, the latest and possibly the best of Ross Pettys holiday pantomimes, bears little resemblance to the Disney movie or the Hans Andersen story; as is gleefully acknowledged in an opening number hymning the freedoms and delights of The Public Domain. This mermaid is a social activist who resides on the bottom of Torontos harbour and uses her day at the surface to protest, and subvert, a scheme to turn her home into a site for "three casinos, a large airport, and 14 condos designed by David Mirvish." Given the way civic policy is going, I'd say she was swimming against the tide, but pantos are allowed, indeed required, to have happy endings. Besides, nobody could resist a heroine who looks and sounds like Chilina Kennedy, a super singer, actress, comedienne and dancer. She backs up every word and note she utters, and is even delightful when struck temporarily dumb. In Tracey Flye's production, choreographed by Marc Kimmelman, marine creatures travel on skates - quite a trick when you also have a tail - and Kennedys mermaid skates like an Angel, which happens to be her name.

Her chief antagonist is, of course, Petty himself as Ogopogo, a sea-vandal who also appears in an earthly entrepreneurial disguise so complete that I initially failed to penetrate it. The kids in the audience were far sharper; they booed him on sight. He is, as usual, excellent though he seems to be relinquishing star comic status to Dan Chameroy, in his accustomed drag as Angels Auntie Plumbum, who confuses gender still further by being less mermaid than Merman, of the Ethel variety; wherever she goes, themes from Gypsy go with her. He/she has mastered/mistressed being vulgar and delicate at the same time. There is equally reliable clown backup from Eddie Glen and Reid Janisse, the latter also billed, rather low down, as Writer; he's done a good job. Jordan Clark, a terrific dancer, plays Ogopogo's seductress-assistant. Angel has three flirtatious friends, one of whom is, like, a valley mermaid. She also has a love-interest (Marc Devigne) whom she meets rather late, but extremely cute; what first attracts him is the way she harmonizes, which shows him to be a young man of taste. Even the four hardworking male supers have personality while Lana Carillo, as a Shrimp whos the stern principal of her school of fish, gives possibly the most terrifying performance on any Toronto stage. Erika Connors costumes are a stylish delight, and Michael Gianfrancesco's sets, lavish within their means, are very attractive. The storyline forbids the inclusion of anything resembling a village square scene, which is a great relief. Petty's pantos have shaken free of most of the old conventions, even while establishing their own.
