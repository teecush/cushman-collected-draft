If we are to judge by this years Dora nominations -- and for once we can -- the 2006-'07 season was the one in which the kids beat the grown-ups at their own game. Or rather games. Three productions from the Lorraine Kimsa Theatre for Young People have between them been nominated in so many categories in the main, or General Theatre, division that the company has hardly any shows left over for the Theatre for Young Audiences section, which is where they usually go.

Of those three shows, two -- I Think I Can and Danny, King of the Basement -- really were among the best things of the year. The third, Seussical, really wasn't, though some of its individual participants were. I Think I Can, the Florence Gibson-Shawn Byfield piece about empowerment through tap-dancing, is up for outstanding new musical (the Doras shrink from the word "best), and I dont see how it can lose, unless someone feels churlish about the fact that it doesnt have any songs. But then, its two competitors, Bird Flu over the Cuckoo's Nest, the best Second City revue in years, wasn't notable for its songs either, and neither was CanStages The Story of My Life, which had a lot of them. I think I Think I Can can, and will. It should also get outstanding production of a musical, given that the competition is Story of My Life, We Will Rock You (which may win but shouldnt), Seussical and what we apparently have to refer to as Richard O'Brien's The Rocky Horror Show. (Who does Mr. OBrien think he is -- Monty Python?) No mention here, or anywhere else, for Songs for a New World, though it was a better show than most of them, and very stylishly staged. That brings us (oh these subtle distinctions) to outstanding direction of a musical, a category that this year has found room for Seussical, as well as the atrociously directed (ah, but by Ben Elton, whos famous) We Will Rock You. Here, too, I Think I Can's Conrad Alexandowicz should waltz, or hop, off with it; though he might have serious competition from Christopher Bond and Hilton Battle over at Evil Dead: the Musical where staging and performance prevail over (mostly) terrible material. Battle could also be a winner for best choreography.

Seussical comes into its own in the musical performance categories. George Masswohl's Horton the Elephant should win by a trunk over Yvan Pedneault (We Will Rock You) and Rick Ward (Evil Dead). Id cheer, too, if Corinne Koslos Gertrude McFuzz flew off victorious; I might do the same for Sarah Cornell (Evil Dead), Melody Johnson (I Think I Can) or Erica Peck (We Will Rock You) but the bird has it, if only by a tail feather.

On to more solemn stuff. The big surprise in the new-play category is the omission, apparently on a technicality, of Wajdi Mouawad's ambitious Scorched. I wasn't one of its big boosters, but it obviously deserved to be there, especially as most of the pieces that are feel like barrel-scraping, including Michael Hollingsworths The Saskatchewan Rebellion, which isnt even new. So the field should be clear for Michael Healey's generous Generous. Though last time Healey won, I'd prophesied hed lose: Apologies in advance, Michael. Scorched does make it into outstanding production of a play (Im confused, too) and will very likely win. What should win is Orpheus Descending but it wasn't even nominated; as a joint product of the Mirvishes (too commercial) and Stratford (too Stratford) it didn't stand a chance, given the Doras notorious reverse snobbery. Its even more scandalous that Miles Potter's direction didn't get a nod, though Ill admit that the actual best direction nominees are all worthy and hard to choose between; put a gun to my head and Ill squawk for Chris Abraham (Insomnia). Seana McKenna's transcendent Orpheus performance has been recognized, and should be a sure thing, which doesn't mean it is. Fiona Reid (Homebody/Kabul and Generous) should be the runner-up but isnt there; sometimes one despairs. Outstanding male performer will probably be Kenneth Welsh in Leaving Home; this Canadian classic" must be Soulpeppers most-nominated production ever, which, given the Doras' past neglect of the company's magnificent Ibsens and Chekhovs, suggests that there might just be a little parochialism at work here. And its certainly astonishing that The Threepenny Opera was completely ignored in all the musical categories.

Most ludicrous category: featured role (either gender) in a play or musical. There is, of course, enough talent here in any season to furnish four awards. With them all squeezed together, Id plump for Jane Spidell in Leaving Home. The Ensemble (i.e., everyone) in Danny, King of the Basement also got nominated; this is a cop-out, though they were all great, especially Jordan Pettle (who should have been named for Generous, anyway). The set, costume, lighting, sound and musical direction categories are embarrassments of (mostly) riches, though I'm perplexed that The Rocky Horror Show didn't get a costume nomination for sheer wit while Seussical did.

Down in the Independent Theatre division, I note that Hannah Moscovitchs The Russian Play didnt get an outstanding new play nomination, though it's far superior to anything that did. Michelle Monteith (female performance) mercifully did, and deserves to win, despite stiff competition from Sarah Dodd (Marion Bridge, glad they singled her out) and Deena Aziz (Homebody/Kabul). Male performance should go, please, to Thom Allison, who in Elegies: A Song Cycle was star-quality personified; though Ryan Kelly was superb in Will the Real J.T. LeRoy Please Stand Up, which should also have been among the best new plays. Poaching on to the Opera division: I pause on the verge of asserting that Lady Macbeth of Mtsensk should win everything to recall that Das Rheingold was wonderful, too, both in itself and as the capstone of the COC's Ring Cycle. Also, though Nicola Beller Carbones Lady MofMwas amazing, so, too, were the duelling divas of Elektra, Susan Bullock and Ewa Podles. Operatic candidates also infiltrate the General division, notably in musical direction, though how you measure Richard Bradshaws big band against, say, Rick Fox's (We Will Rock You) is beyond me.

As for Monday's awards show itself: It will be better than last year's. It has to be.
