THE PIPER

Factory Theatre, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - Colleen Murphy's new play takes up the old and frightening story of the Pied Piper, who lured a townful of rats into a mass watery grave and a townful of children into the intestines of a mountain. The Necessary Angel company and the Factory Theatre have pooled resources to employ 20 actors -- all of them good, and some of them among the best in town. Richard Rose has turned in a smartly drilled production, and Teresa Przybylski has designed mordantly mischievous sets and costumes. Don Horsburgh has composed a good if abbreviated musical score. So much talent can seldom have been so profligately abused.

To blame the author is also to sympathize with her, since she is one of her own principal victims. She has had a piercing insight into the original legend and what it might mean to us now, and has gone and buried it under a load of words -- long, elephantine words that seem intended to serve as social satire. (Some of the heavy- handedness may be the directors fault. Either he lacks a sense of humour or I do. Id say that on the first night the audience was on my side; there was little laughter, and what there was sounded scattered and self-conscious.) At the play's centre, panting for life, is a fable of institutionalized child abuse: A pampered society professes to love its children but actually neglects them and deserves to lose them.

It begins well, with an encounter between representatives of the play's two principal classes of victim. Tod, young son of the Deputy Mayor of Hamelin, meets Kingsley, described in the program (which tends, in pantomime style, to be more amusing than the play) as an Introspective King Rat." Kingsley, who sounds as if he once nested in the London School of Economics, knows intellectually about the depravity of the world but can never quite believe it when it happens, especially when it happens to him. When he quotes Nietzsche (which he does, repeatedly) you believe not only that he has read him but that he could, in a pinch, spell him; which is more than you could say for anyone else on stage, with or without a tail.

This is painfully demonstrated by the subsequent arrival of the Town Councillors, a round dozen of them. Their first appearance, rising from the ground to peer en masse over the edges of their conference table, is something of a coup, but everything else about them is agonizingly predictable, and they sink the play.

They are all corrupt, incompetent and competitively ambitious; and they equate polysyllables with wit. We know within five minutes that their author loathes them, but she has to keep them around in order to tell her story. So there they are, striking the same attitudes over and over: a cross between Friedrich Durrenmatt and Toytown.

The Mayor, humorously christened Pops Poppenheimer, is plainly meant to make us think of Mel with a dash of Mike; he presides over an administration that has sold off all the towns essential services, making itself vulnerable to rats and pipers alike.

To show the plays open-handedness there are a couple of hard- line feminists, belonging to a group calling itself Mothers Against Fathers, and an equally hidebound Russian lady who goes weak-kneed and dewy-eyed at the mention of Stalin. There must be more timely ways of satirizing the Left.

In the Robert Browning poem, from which most of us know the story, the Mayor and Corporation are the Pied Piper's undifferentiated antagonists. Corporation of course means something different to us than it did to the Victorians, and there is a clumsy song here to push the point.

In this version the corporate in-fighting takes up so much time that the evening is half over before the traditional action has started. The rats here are not an unwanted plague; they are an exploited underclass who come in useful as workers and, sometimes, as scapegoats. So when young Tod disappears, his father and mother - - who have been too busy with their own affairs, private and public, to take any notice of him -- find it convenient to blame the rats. They rise in revolt, and extermination is decreed. There is a horrible clashing of gears here, as between the old and new stories - - a conflict exemplified by the play's indecision as to whether its rats are people or rodents; it wants them both ways.

Anyway, this zany-looking young guy has wandered on stage, as unobtrusively as may be considering he wears a funny suit and plays a funny flute. (He also has a thing about insects.) Pink, the Mayors understandably bratty daughter, falls for him. Pops is furious but allows the match when the Piper, shyly and under duress, offers the traditional rat-disposal deal. He delivers, the furry guys drown, the town welshes, the piper takes the kids. And here, finally, the play begins to make emotional and intellectual sense.

When you read the poem, the disappearance of a townful of children registers as a pleasantly chilling cautionary tale. It is quite another thing when you have met a few of them and can imagine them vanishing just off stage; when you wonder whether Pink, the only one youve got to know, will escape being her own lover's victim; when you can actually see and hear the bereaved parents, however nasty they may be as individuals.

We remember that, in the council meetings, their main preoccupation was finding baby-sitters; they never wanted to spend the time themselves. They deserve what they get. And it shouldn't happen to anyone. This is provocative; and a second encounter between Tod and Kingsley, both of whom have endured a lot, rounds it symmetrically off. But you have to wade through a lot of facetious rubbish en route.

As for the actors, they are in there, wading with you. Julian Richings dominates as Kingsley, tirelessly reasonable even in the direst of extremities. This rat seems to have nearly as many lives as a cat, and needs them. Mostly we are glad to see him, though there are limits; at about the fourth resurrection I thought I detected a groan, suppressed but unanimous, all around the auditorium.

Melody Johnson is toughly interesting as Pink, Adam Kelly pleasantly spineless as the Piper (a Wandering Minstrel he, according to the program), and Rick Roberts and Maggie Huculak fight very fiercely as the selfish parents, who sometimes seem to have stepped out of another play.

Victor Ertmanis is better than I have ever seen him as the Mayor, a sadistic bumbler but with a few more qualms than his colleagues about the exercise of arbitrary power. The funniest of the town- hall bunch, with an unsteady walk and a crazy gleam in his eye, is Ross Manson as a sozzled preacher firing off tirades against the media.

Maria Vacratsis is criminally misused as the Stalinist, Richard McMillan scarcely less so as a nondescript functionary who always wanted children and finally gets to adopt a dozen of them; but his gently agonized expression as they, too, dance into oblivion finally justifies his presence.

The play is also a sort of musical, and Horsburghs music is stronger than Murphys words. The rats get a marching song that is dead-on Brecht-Weill. There is a love song for Pink that is icily melodic; and the Piper's tune turns out to be genuinely haunting.
