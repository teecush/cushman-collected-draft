Two small Toronto theatre groups are mounting plays by modern European masters whose work is more often read about than seen. The more recondite of the two is Max Frisch whose plays were very big circa 1960 but have not, at least in their English translations, been staged much since. He was a German-Swiss playwright and novelist and like his contemporary and compatriot Friedrich Durrenmatt, he wrote taut cockeyed political parables, situated somewhere between Bertolt Brecht and Eugene Ionesco. Fireraisers, which isn't his most ambitious play but may be his neatest and most approachable, is being staged by the Red Light District company or, as they roguishly put it, by their Fire Safety Initiative.

The plays original title was Biedermann and the Fire Raisers, and that title isnt the only thing that's now been tweaked and compressed. It's a cautionary tale about complacency and psychological blackmail. In an unnamed city thats experiencing a wave of arson, two confidence tricksters - one rough, one smooth - strong-arm and sweet-talk their way into a prosperous bourgeois home, Gottlieb Bierdermann proprietor.

In the current version, updated and transplanted to the time and place of smoke detectors and chrysotile asbestos, Gottlieb has become Gillian Biedermann, a svelte female CEO with chrysotile connections, who recently fired (pun unintended but apposite) a creative employee who subsequently committed suicide. Schmitz, the orphaned circus strongman who intimidates Biedermann into giving him food, wine and a bed for the night, has also changed gender and is a female wrestler, not only foster-homed but homeless. Schmitzs buddy Eisenring remains much the same, blithely vouchsafing that he has just got out of prison. The pair take up residence in the Biedermann garage, filling it with flammable material, secure in the knowledge that, having once admitted them, their host will go to any lengths not to see what's in front of her face. The original Biedermann had an onstage wife even more readily guilttripped than himself, the new one has an offstage husband named Robert of, we are repeatedly told, a nervous disposition. Many of the wife's former lines have been appropriated by a maid hired to help care for Robert. At least thats what the program says; Im glad it does because otherwise I'd never have guessed.

Frisch, grappling with Switzerland's ambiguous wartime record, seems to have intended his play as a commentary on the resistible rise of Nazi Germany. It plays here as a generalized and rather protracted satire on human gullibility. There is a chorus of firemen, who utter fateful warnings; they keep saying "woe, woe," prompting Herr Beidermann as he then was to request them, in a line unhappily cut in the current version, to stop being so defeatist. Like their Greek antecedents, they know whats going to happen but are powerless to stop it. In this production theyre reduced to two who appear, rather wittily, within an alcove that may represent a TV screen or just the outside world. In addition to their original text, they spout antincendiary warnings taken from contemporary public announcements. Obviously we're meant to think of Canada now, but it's unclear whether were being warned against too much surveillance or too little.

The shows best things come straight from Frisch's play-as-written: the cat-and-mouse game between Biedermann and his/her self-invited guests. But much of this has been dulled in Lauren Gillis' production, which hardly matches up to the dynamic re-working of The Misanthrope that was my previous Red Light experience. Marcel Dragonieri, who was superb in that, is similarly imposing here as half the chorus, plus the plaintive voice of Robert, and Justin Miller hits the right audacious note as the cool ex-con. Briana Templeton holds the centre pretty well as Gillian; you can sense her defences crumbling. But the overall effect is both slow and slapdash.

Adam Seeligs Like the First Time is announced as "modelled on a play by Nobel prize winner Luigi Pirandello"; how closely modelled I couldnt say, but the characters all have Italian names and the script certainly exhibits the Pirandellian obsession with the fluidity of identity. We are, his plays assert, whatever other people take us to be, which means we can be trapped in several personas simultaneously. This play gives us a woman - known variously as Fulvia, Flora and Francesca, according to which of the men in her life is in it at the time - who, after a long time running wild, returns to the bosom of her family, posing as her husband's second wife, unrecognized by her now-teenage daughter, which is plausible, and also by her mother, which isn't. At least it isnt in Seeligs production, which takes most of the play with plodding earnestness, making it seem like screechy melodrama.

Unlike Red Light, Seelig's One Little Goat company employs Equity actors, but the performances here are even more plodding and tentative than those in Fireraisers. The one actor with spark is Dov Mickelson, who starts the play off as an ex-lover of the heroine's, prepared to leave and maybe even kill his own wife to be with her. So locked is he in his obsession, that he fails to notice that his beloved has suspended herself with a scarf round her neck and may be attempting suicide.

If the other actors reached his level of wit and intensity, the play might make sense. As it was, it left me wishing again that some Canadian company would take on Pirandello's Right You Are If You Think So, a more scintillating examination of the madonnawhore complex that was recently staged in London under the unanswerable title Absolutely Perhaps. Pirandello's admirers always claim that he was a great but untranslatable comic writer. That production disproved the second part of that assessment while triumphantly proving the first.
