Politics is show business. That's the conclusion, indisputable if hardly a revelation, reached by Frost/Nixon at its own conclusion, and dropped neatly in our laps. The play makes a decent case for it, not least by being an example of it.

Decent, but thin. Peter Morgan's play centres on the 1977 television interviews in which David Frost elicited from Richard Nixon a first-time-ever admission that he had indeed been at fault over Watergate, both the burglary itself and the cover-up. It draws a parallel between the two participants. On the one hand, the disgraced president, saved from impeachment only by the pardon so opportunely bestowed by his successor, and obsessed with restoring his prestige, or at least with doing it no further harm; on the other, the gadfly entrepreneur, in search of a coup that might redeem a career in danger of fizzling on three continents. So the two men need one another, but for incompatible reasons.

Historically, Frost won. Theatrically, Nixon does. This is partly for technical reasons. To put it crudely, Nixon was a historical figure, Frost was a media one; which means (even more crudely) that the former asks to be acted and the latter to be impersonated. Actor Len Cariou's resume includes such self-destructive heroes as Coriolanus and Sweeney Todd, and some of that stature gets into his Nixon, whom he presents as wily but never weaselly. Nixon agrees to four interviews, of which only the last is to concern Watergate, and he gets off handily in the first three. So it's awesome to see his face crumble, as Cariou's graphically does, when he tumbles into giving an unguarded reply to an unexpected question; and it's impressive when (against advice) he walks back into the ring and confesses that he let himself and America down. Maybe he was just cutting his losses, but the play offers the tragic option and the actor takes it. A coda shows him wryly envying his adversary's ability to work a room and enjoy it. This, considering how high Nixon actually climbed, is guff, but you swallow it at the time.

One reason, other than a million-dollar fee, that Nixon agrees to the interviews is that he's persuaded that his opponent is an apolitical lightweight who's never even bothered to vote. This certainly squares with my own impression of Frost as a man of no particular convictions, or even performing talents, who happened to be the right age, and the right personality, for the British "satire boom" of the early '60s. It's why he's a less substantial figure than Jon Stewart, whose Daily Show is the contemporary equivalent of Frost's That Was the Week That Was, but who means what he says and is also (or therefore) funnier. The play's Frost takes a terrific professional and financial gamble that only pays off, or so we're told, because his assistant Jim Reston, who functions as the play's part-time skeptical narrator, unearths a crucial and hitherto neglected piece of evidence. All Frost has to do is spring it; there isn't even any suggestion that he's been purposely lulling his quarry into a sense of false security. (Well, he suggests it, but his staff aren't buying it and neither do we.) David Storch's quiz-kid Frost spends most of the evening running around like a self-absorbed squeaky-voiced chicken, in increasing fear of having its head chopped off. On this level it's a perfectly good performance -- actually quite an interesting one -- but it bears, even when he's supposed to be on camera, little relation to the twang-toned, unshakably confident (some would say complacent) inquisitor we know from the box -- a medium of which he was one of the first real masters.

The actor isn't helped by the play's efforts to give him a life outside the box. Morgan, the screenwriter of The Queen (a more substantial work on a more substantial subject) seems to be a documentarian rather than a dramatist; the script's most compelling scenes are transcripts. The made-up or guessed-at bits are plodding, and the Frost camp has most of them. There's even an attempt at love interest with the peripatetic Frost meeting his future fiancee sitting across the aisle in first class, a scene that neither the writing nor the performance make credible and that nothing could make relevant. There are also scenes involving Nixon superagent (in the 10% sense) Swifty Lazar, whose underlining of the politics-performance equation doesn't excuse their lameness.

Two actor-playwrights, Damien Atkins and Michael Healey, step persuasively outside their usual comfort zones as Frost aides, while Tom Mc-Beath seems ruggedly inside his as point man to Nixon. Ari Cohen's Reston is too much the shoulder-shrugger to carry any weight. Ted Dykstra's direction gives the play flash, which it needs, but it can't fill in the holes.

The story holds you. It just doesn't seem as significant as everyone thinks it is. Nixon's capitulation may have been cathartic for him but it can't have told Americans anything they didn't already know. It made no difference, except to David Frost's career which is only important if you're David Frost. And the stakes seem grotesquely disproportionate: a man jeopardizing his job against one who cashiered his country. The real drama was in Watergate itself. The interviews weren't even an epilogue: just a belated sideshow. - Frost/Nixon runs until Nov. 8. For tickets and information call 416-368-3110.
