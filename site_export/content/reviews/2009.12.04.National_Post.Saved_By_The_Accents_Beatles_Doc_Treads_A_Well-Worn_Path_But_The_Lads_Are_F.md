They looked so innoc ent back in 1962. Well, maybe not innocent, but fresh and fly and unspoiled. They sat politely around tables, drank cups of tea, smoked cigarettes (everyone did then) and -- at least when posing for the press -- wore jackets and ties. So did George Martin, their studio producer, the universally acknowledged fifth Beatle. Martin was still wearing a tie seven years later at the time of Abbey Road, (the album not the studio), while John, Paul, Ringo and the other George had progressed through the fashions of Carnaby Street, Haight-Ashbury, India and showbiz plutocracy.

Martin remained the school teacher, in appearance and in role, teased and respected by the smart boys in the class. "I thought they were nice boys" he says guardedly of his first encounters with them."

"I don't think he was that convinced about our musical ability" says one of the nice boys, probably John, "but he liked the way we had about us." Didn't we all.

These quotes are taken from The Beatles on Record, a documentary that purports to be "an intimate glimpse of the creative process behind their musical masterpieces" but is in fact a glorified promo for the new re-mastered versions of the original albums. It's "narrated entirely by John Lennon, Paul McCartney, George Harrison, Ringo Starr and Sir George Martin." This turns out to mean that bits from pre-existing interviews have been cobbled together to make a commentary track. Occasionally -- very occasionally -- someone talks to camera. John Lennon even gets name-checked by a caption. Otherwise, you're on your own as to which of them is talking and, especially early on, it can get very confusing. According to the publicity, the narrative "features never-before-heard outtakes of studio chat from the band's first recording sessions at the infamous Abbey Road studios." I'm not sure what those studios have done to deserve their infamy; most likely, this is yet another example of someone thinking the word means the precise opposite of its actual meaning. As for the studio chat ( "George is tuning up!") one tries one's best to pretend that it's scintillating or revealing or both, but if it wasn't for the Liverpool accents, it would be neither.

Visually, the show consists mainly of footage of the boys in studio, or purportedly in studio, much of it stills. And we do get to hear snatches of most of the songs, from Love Me Do all the way through to The End.

Still, there's some charm and there's nostalgia value. Where it comes will depend on your age and your perspective. For me -- more now than at the time -- the magical Beatle years were the first ones. The first records came out when I was in my last years at high school in London, when we were delighted to hear a pop group who sang as well as played. After Please Please Me, their second single and first monster hit, and the LP that was named after it, theirs became the only records you ever heard at parties, displacing those of Buddy Holly.

I always felt the Beatles' finest moment was John's remark when they played at the Royal Variety Performance: "Those of you upstairs, applaud; the rest, rattle your jewellery." It wasn't exactly revolutionary, but it was subversive: of the canons of showbusiness as much as of society. It's hard to imagine an American performer saying anything like that in the White House, or in the even more sacred precincts of the Academy Awards. It was in fact pure music-hall.

The Beatles' initial musical energy may have been borrowed American, but their joint personality was pure British. No wonder they went on to conquer America; the Yanks could never have heard a combination at once so refreshingly familiar and so devastatingly strange. (And still, at that stage, cuddly.)

Back home, once the first patriotic thrill had worn off, we began to feel that we'd lost them. Still, as the '60s progressed and became "the '60s," it was impossible, however much or little of a fan you were, not to feel simply glad the Beatles existed. And their breakup, however much it may have been heralded, was a real shock. They seemed, as an entity, so providentially well-matched and well-rounded. You knew that, individually, they were never going to amount to anything as good. And you were right.

The Beatles on Record can't help capturing some of all that, except for the last part; it all but ignores internecine tensions and it stops when the group does. It does contain some nuggets, some less familiar than others. There's what amounts to an early music video for I Feel Fine: John, Paul and George on guitars and Ringo on exercise bike. There's John, soon after that, saying that "We'd never heard of anything in rock 'n' roll lasting more than two years" and George expressing a modest hope that they'd survive for four. There's Paul saying they quit touring because, "We can't develop where no one can hear us." There's John saying, mercifully, that I Am the Walrus was "not that serious."

And most of all there's the unflappable Martin, who's really this program's central figure, patiently insisting on re-takes while admitting that by Revolver " They'd started telling me what they'd want." In the interests of musical communication he learned to play the guitar while they learned piano, and "they learned to play the piano better than I got to play the guitar." After what he plainly regards as the disastrous self-indulgence of Let It Be, he was surprised to get a call from Paul saying, "We're going to do another record -- do you want to produce it?" Martin said, "Only if you let me produce it." Which they did; and so all five came back, literally and figuratively, to Abbey Road. It made a nice ending. It still does.
