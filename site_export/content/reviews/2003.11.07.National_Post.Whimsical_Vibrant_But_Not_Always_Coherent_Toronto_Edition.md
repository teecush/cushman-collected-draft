THE ADVENTURES OF A BLACK GIRL IN SEARCH OF GOD

Harbourfront Centre Theatre

- - -

The commercial restaging, for an extended run, of last year's inaugural production of the Obsidian Theatre Company is a breakthrough for black theatre in Toronto. The first-night reception was understandably tumultuous. It hurts, in the circumstances, to say that the play isnt very good.

The production is. Djanet Sears, who wrote The Adventures of a Black Girl in Search of God, also directs it, and (with Alejandra Nunez), composed it. The latter is very important. The show employs a vibrant singing-dancing chorus who are said to represent the heroines ancestors, though I can't see how this could be clear to anybody who hadn't read the program. When I reviewed the shows first production, I wrote the chorus seemed "increasingly a diversion from the drama rather than an illumination of it." This is still true; what seems additionally evident at a second viewing is that the chorus seems designed to give an air of size and unity to what is actually a play of bits and pieces.

The most interesting piece, and potentially the largest, is a matter of old and new history, but this too is something on which the program is rather more informative than the play. The action unfolds around Negro Creek, a corner of Southern Ontario named for the black soldiers who were settled there after the War of 1812. In 1995, a white council, presumably for reasons of misplaced political correctness, decided to rename it but were opposed by members of the black community, furious at the denial of their history. This protest is the ground bass of the play, (and its success is theoretically the dramatic climax) but its so submerged as to be almost out of sight. This is partly because the actor charged with relaying it to us has, despite many admirable qualities, a tendency to gabble; but it's never really dramatized anyway.

It's rather loosely tied to the guerrilla campaign of some Negro Creek seniors to repossess certain black historical artifacts -- some demeaning, some not -- from white properties. Their culminating raid, presented with a wealth of comic detail, is conducted on a museum and involves the participants posing as cleaners. It proves too much for the health of the ringleader, a retired judge who has been living on borrowed time. This is a fact with which his daughter, a former obstetrician, has enormous difficulty in coming to terms. Her own child died at the age of eight, and the mother has since veered between blaming herself and blaming God; she is the black girl of the title, which has been creatively pinched from Bernard Shaw. She asks the eternal question of how a divine authority can permit so much suffering; its a rhetorical question (and the rhetoric is powerful), and she gets as little answer as she expects.

She has a surrogate on hand to pose it to; her husband -- estranged, though they still love one another -- is a minister, though his vocation seems to be social rather than spiritual. Challenged to justify the ways of God to man, or to woman, he replies: "Im in sales, not marketing." It's the best line in a play not short of good lines.

The play also has a generous spirit. What it lacks is coherence. It strays off into whimsical sidebars, and the heroine, whose quest should unify the play, is frequently displaced. Karen Robinson, the revival's principal newcomer, gives her more passion and presence than she had before, but she cannot help ceding pride of place to her activist dad, who has the action, the glamour and the jokes. Walter Borden plays him, as before, with crafty wit, while David Collins, also as before, plays Gods sales rep with fire and a twinkle.

Also held over and still looking good are the sets (Astrid Janson), the lighting (Paul Mathiesen), and the choreography (Vivine Scarlett). The last, along with the singing, retains its power but arouses doubts. Its rooted in black church music, which is as stirring here as it usually is; but it seems unambiguously celebratory for a play whose final gesture seems almost secular. There is space for argument here, and a Greek tragic chorus, to which this play's ensemble has been compared, might have paused to provide one; so, indeed, might a Greek comic chorus. But that doesn't happen here. The music seems to exist for its own sweet sake (and for some uncommonly nifty scene shifting).

None of which is to deny that the production's rebirth, with Mirvish Productions acting as midwife and upping the size of the cast from 21 to a phenomenal 23, is an event, and it deserves to be seen.
