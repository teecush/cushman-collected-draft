NOTHING SACRED

Festival Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont.

- - -

In the opening moments of George F. Walker's Nothing Sacred, a 19th-century Russian bailiff is enthusiastically beating a peasant. (The emancipation of the serfs is only a couple of years away, so the bailiff is enjoying his privileges while they last. He claims, sounding as though he believes it, that the peasant is enjoying them as well.) Onto the scene come two young men. One of them is Arkady Kirsanov, the kind-hearted son of the landowner on whose estate this is happening. The other is his pal Bazarov, self-described nihilist. Arkady asks Bazarov, whom he reveres, if he can't put a stop to this distressing scene. "Yes, I can," says Bazarov, in a cool tone that makes no promises as to whether or not he actually will.

As it turns out, and in the most immediate sense, he does. Bazarov is one tough customer, and pretty soon he is beating up the bailiff. But he probably means more than that. He means that he knows how to destroy the political system that allows such things to happen. The questions then become, whether he will get the chance to put his theories into practice, and how they will work out if he does. The play doesn't answer them and there's no reason why it should, but it puts them, trenchantly and suggestively, in motion.

Nothing Sacred, which was first staged in 1988 and is being given a brave and brilliant revival on the main stage of the Shaw Festival, is a reordering of the Ivan Turgenev novel generally known in English as Fathers and Sons. (On my bookshelf it's Fathers and Children, which the translator swears is more accurate, but it's a fact that the children whom the novel shows in relation to their fathers are uniformly male.)

It's a good play, maybe its author's best, and some of its merits derive from its source. The two writers have widely different sensibilities; to put it very crudely, Turgenev was a landscape painter while Walker is a cartoonist. Walker, as is his right, changes Turgenev a lot, but it can't be a coincidence that his characters here, however much he buffets them about, have stronger legs and generally warmer hearts, than those in his original, contemporary work. The play is also very funny; by quickening the pace and sharpening the angles, Walker brings out the element of farce that inhabits most of the great works of Russian realism.

There is, though, a price to be paid. If you take over another writer's characters and situations and then change some of his narrative but not all, there are liable to be inconsistencies. Where the Kirsanov household is concerned, the play stays close to the book. There is Arkady and there is his father, Nikolai, who runs the estate, conscientiously but reluctantly, since it's his inheritance from his own father, and who wants to be a nice man, and often succeeds. There is Nikolai's housekeeper, by whom he has a child -- another son. There is Nikolai's brother Pavel, who, like him, once professed liberal principles, but has now most emphatically abandoned them. He takes pride in adhering to the old values, though they aren't necessarily the old Russian values; his favourite society seems to be England where the rich know their place, its obligations as well as its privileges. He and Bazarov naturally get on one another, and take great pleasure in doing so. Eventually they fight a duel, as they do in the novel, though here it has a very different outcome.

Bazarov's story differs greatly; and so, as a result, does his character. His relationship with the beautiful chatelaine Anna Odintsov is different (and so is she). His relationship with his own skills as a gifted, and unsentimentally practical, medical student is different, or at least less thoroughly explored.

Most crucially, his relationship with his own parents (much humbler than the Kirsanovs) has disappeared, taking with it half of what, if his title is to be trusted, Turgenev meant his novel to be about. The Bazarov of the book ends up as a doctor in his father's village, where he has been able to put at least some ideals into practice. Walker's Bazarov finishes, still a cuckoo in his friend's family's nest, where he is a figure of acute tragi-comedy. The Niagara program note claims that Walker's Bazarov is "a good deal more likeable than Turgenev's."

This may be true at the beginning of their respective stories, where the stage nihilist, as fanatical as his literary counterpart, still has the emotional advantage of being, literally, a figure of flesh and blood, and also benefits from being given some of his author's best jokes. It isn't true at the end, when Turgenev's Bazarov has simply been through more than Walker's, and has grown up because of it, and is by so much the more sympathetic.

One of the great attractions of the play is the way it organizes its people and events into evolving groups, affected by distinctions of class and age but not governed by them. This geometric quality is beautifully reflected in Morris Panych's production, which is remarkable for its wit and clarity. (At least it would be remarkable from most directors; from this one it's par for the course.) Ken MacDonald's set, wide open at sides and top, is enhanced by sudden bursts of colour and dominated by shifting, projecting patterns of lines, circles and arrows.

This being nominally Russia, I thought of 1920s Constructivism, though the program, more sophisticated in such matters than I, cites the equally Russian, but exiled, Wassily Kandinsky. There are also, when the patterns start popping, reminiscences of Saul Bass, the great designer of Hollywood title sequences, so it's perfectly logical that the evening should start with a rolling of credits.

Beneath and in front of these scenic fireworks there are plenty of human ones. As Bazarov, Mike Shara seems to have taken his cue from an instruction that's issued, somewhat incongruously, to another character altogether, but that happens to suit him perfectly: "Your attitude should be one of muted insouciance." He is the revolutionary who knows all the answers, sees no need to shout them out, and is sourly amused by all who disagree with him. He doesn't hate, but then he doesn't love either; and he does visibly grow up -- not politically (the play doesn't give him time) but emotionally.

Arkady, the central consciousness of the book, is here stuck with being his friend's progressively puzzled shadow, but Jeff Meadows, with a round and open face, makes him credibly decent. As a third companion, an eager sycophant who apparently lives to be rebuffed, Dylan Trowbridge gives an exceedingly bold performance; bold because it takes the chance of being unsympathetically ridiculous and features a whinnying laugh that would be unbearable if it weren't so truthful.

Jim Mezon starts off his Kirsanov pere with a bluff authority that must be second nature to him, but deepens it with a loss and loneliness that seem wholly specific. I wished Benedict Campbell might take Pavel's self-willed (and self-wounding) foppery to more extravagant lengths, seeking out the dignity in absurdity, but on his own more compact terms he is excellent. The scene in which the two brothers exchange nocturnal confidences is the evening's finest.

It's a treat to see Tara Rosling, one of our premiere actresses of waifs and strays, getting as Anna the chance to play elegant; she makes an especially striking entrance in a blue dress that perfectly sets off the colouring of the surrounding chairs and tables: This is especially cunning as it isn't actually her house, but then the production makes no pretence to naturalism. She has trouble, though, filling in the role, which has been ruthlessly transformed into that of a bomb-throwing revolutionary, an invention abrupt in itself and having nothing no visible connection to the rest of her.

Finally there are the servants, all admirably acted. Trish Lindstrom, as her master's mistress, plays sola. Four male attendants are established separately, and then, in one of the play's most satisfying strokes, come together -- first in a search party, then at a deathbed -- as a selection of varied individuals with a common interest. They are the whip-wielding bailiff (Patrick Galligan); his victim (Andrew Bunker), subsequently transformed first to a robber, then to Bazarov's gauchest but most important disciple; a superstitious bodyguard (Micheal Querin); and the Kirsanov family's senior retainer (Richard Farrell).

The last, who has seen everything, is especially impressive; he is the one who is commanded to be mutedly insouciant, and the funny thing is that he already is. His insouciance is in fact so muted that his superiors never notice it, which is their loss. The joke is that the oldest character in the play is also, inside himself, the most radical, and though he himself may not do anything with it, somebody will, sometime.
