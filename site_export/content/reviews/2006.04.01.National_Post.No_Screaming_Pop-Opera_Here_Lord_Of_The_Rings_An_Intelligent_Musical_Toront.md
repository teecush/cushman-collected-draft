I see that Time has described The Lord of the Rings as a definitive megamusical. I have some problems with that. Im not sure that it's grammatically possible to be A definitive anything (please be more definite with your articles, Time) and anyway, "megamusical" is an exceptionally ugly word of which I had hoped we'd seen the last. Ive just discovered that my spellcheck doesnt recognize it. Good ol' spellcheck. Its standards are so self- effacingly high, it doesn't recognize the word spellcheck, either.

Also, if we have to use it, the term megamusical has certain specific connotations that The Lord of the Rings, big and expensive and musical though it undoubtedly is, doesnt quite match. Megamusicals, also known as pop-operas, are a recent, though extinct, breed, in which meaning, story and character get drowned in non-stop helpings of vocal and instrumental soup. They dont have spoken dialogue to help them explore, expound or be funny. They are, almost by definition, dumb.

This doesn't stop them being, for those who like them, very effective. It may even help. Shows like Les Miserables or Miss Saigon seem to deliver emotionally in a way that The Lord of the Rings doesn't. So, for that matter, do dumb shows of a later, more modest stripe such as Wicked or Mamma Mia!. They scream their audiences into submission. The Lord of the Rings is blessed, or cursed, with intelligence.

Note that I said "intelligence," not intellect. Theres nothing deep or complex about the Tolkien trilogy on which the show is based. Its an invigorating adventure story, with good and evil clearly marked out, even when individual characters slide between them. The source's appeal, as I noted in my initial review, is closely bound up with how much there is of it; with the way it creates a world, using sheer narrative energy to build a new mythology, practically from scratch. I was one of those who doubted whether it could be effectively adapted for the stage. It had, after all, taxed the resources of three long films.

Well, there are things missing that are definitely missed, and some that are there but rushed. All the same, the adaptation is remarkably successful and clever. It respects its original's shape; like the books, it starts in the Shire and ends there. It craftily combines two of the originals battles, and deals in brilliant shorthand with the complicated sequence of Frodos betrayal by Gollum to the spider Shelob, his capture by the orcs and his rescue by Sam. I actually prefer the show to the movie. The latter lost me during its second instalment, when it turned into an endless series of fights, prolonged for their own sake. The show, for all its compression, gives more breathing space to the characters, and even as spectacle it's more exciting. Live theatre, when it works, always is, precisely because of the odds against it, especially now, when we know that a movie's vaunted "cast of thousands" has been computer- generated. The staged warfare isnt altogether free of self- indulgence. It occurred to me that one reason the orcs keep losing is that they spend too much time leaping up and down on their sprung snowshoes and not enough smiting the enemy. But the battles are thrillingly choreographed, in a style that draws both on Japanese martial arts and British productions of Shakespeares histories, and you can actually follow them. And just to see the stage bathed alternately in gold and blood-red is a stirring experience.

The show does have an identity problem. We were constantly assured before its opening that it wasn't going to be a musical, but it seems uncertain what kind of musical it doesn't want to be. I cant really judge the lyrics, which tend to be tongue-twisters, Elvish chants or otherwise indecipherable, though Im sure they're better than the jolly ditties with which Tolkien peppered his own text. Most of the songs are used as a kind of vocal underscoring, a new convention and an effective one, more notable for isolated haunting phrases than as whole melodies. There are times, though, when the score harks back to old ways.

One is the hoedown at the Prancing Pony inn, which could be the best ethnic dancing (if hobbits count as an ethnicity) in a musical since Fiddler on the Roof. There is a difference, though. When the Jews and Cossacks do their challenge-dance in Fiddler's inn, it tells us about their relationship, which is a large part of what the shows about. The face off between hobbits and men tells us nothing and goes nowhere. But its so much fun one would hesitate to cut it. The same cannot be said of the periodic retreats into pop-opera conventions that are the only boring things in the show: the love duets that fail, just like the film did, to persuade us that this story has any interest in women; and worse yet, the five minutes in which the elf-queen Galadriel sings at us that we're in Lothlorien and that it's a nice place to be, both of which we already knew. It also obscures what is important about the scene -- Galadriels temptation by the power of the ring, and her victory over it.

The temptation of power is the storys emotional core. It's there in the show, most centrally in James Loye's Frodo, but not yet as clearly or as strongly as it should be. At the moment the most conflicted character on stage, Brent Carvers Gandalf, is the one whose inner struggles are supposed to lie in the past. The production needs some rewriting and redirecting to clarify who some of the characters are, what they want and why they talk so funny. The four hobbits, though, are already funny in the right way -- as Tolkien clearly wanted them to be, but better than he managed, since his own sense of humour was heavy-handed; the word "donnish" might have been invented for his jokes. The master-servant relationship in his book is remarkably similar to the one in Pickwick Papers. I wonder if Tolkien would have acknowledged how much Sam Gamgee owes to Sam Weller. Probably not. He disapproved of all English literature later than Beowulf. He also denied that his ring had anything in common with Wagners; they're both round is as far as he'd go. Well, of course. Both convey unlimited powers, both corrupt their wearers and all who covet them, both cause brother to kill brother and both are discovered floating in a stream. Otherwise, nothing in common. Still, some of the staging in Lord of the Rings is Wagnerian in its grandeur, or what one imagines "Wagnerian" would have been like when Wagner himself still had a say in it. Its also, in its own way, as imaginative as the magnificent new-style Ring at the COC. To have combined this with the human element is an accomplishment.

- - -

Friday night is music night at a Toronto vegetarian restaurant, Le Commensal at Bay and Dundas, where I recently discovered a singer. (Doubly serendipitous; I wasnt looking and I'm not a vegetarian.) Her name is Beverly Taft; she was singing jazz-tinged standards and making them sound fresh without tricks. I especially liked her transformation of The Way You Look Tonight into waltz time, bring out all out of what Alan Jay Lerner, in another song, called its "tender fun." Even better was a relaxed and rapturous Blue Skies, lightly swinging, one of those occasions on which, as the song itself more or less says, everything suddenly goes right. She was singing with just piano. She has a CD with ensemble accompaniment and a broader repertoire, titled How Ya Gonna Keep 'Em Down on the Farm, so called because it was recorded near one. It doesnt, alas, include that song, which is surely ripe for rediscovery by a respectable singer, but it does have Im an Old Cowhand, complete with verse and wittily phrased. I recommend the lady and the venue; together when they coincide, separately when they don't. A subsequent trip to Le Commensal yielded pianist Norman Amadio, one of Toronto's most unassuming treasures.
