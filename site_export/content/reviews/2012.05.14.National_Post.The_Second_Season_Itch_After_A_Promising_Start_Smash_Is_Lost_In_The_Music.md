Well, we all make mistakes. A couple of months ago I was going into modified raptures over two then-new TV series, Smash and Luck, and anticipating increasingly good times for both. I was wrong in both cases, though for different reasons.

Luck ran out of it, and will not be back, though its first and only season just kept getting better. Its last episode drew all its threads together, while keeping some menacing options open for a sequel. It had more narrative clarity, and indeed more narrative, than all the previous instalments combined. I have to admit in previous weeks I was often content to let the dialogue and atmosphere wash over me. But the real emotional touchstones of this race-track story were the horses; and the second season has been cancelled on the humane grounds that too many of them were getting fatally injured. It may also be, of course, that the show was halted because not enough people liked it, while a sizable number actively hated it. The elusive (and allusive) storytelling apparently baffled them. Personally I cant see why anyone, or anyone interested in acting, would not want a weekly TV drama starring Dustin Hoffman.

Smash will be coming back, though apparently without its creator and showrunner Theresa Rebeck, which may be unfair. The show's first four episodes were its best, and Rebeck wrote the first three of them, though admittedly it was the fourth that was the best of all. It was in the wake of that opening quartet that I wrote my enthusiastic report. In those weeks Smash promised to be what it was advertised to be: a reasonably truthful and very entertaining story about the creation of a Broadway musical. It's been downhill since. The show is still watchable, but it doesnt seem to know whether its a show about a musical, or a musical in its own right.

It's a failure of style, or rather a failure to establish a style. Every episode will contain two or three numbers. There'll be something from the musical about Marilyn Monroe - at first simply called Marilyn, now rashly retitled Bombshell, which should really give the New York critics something to feast on - that the characters are supposedly putting together. Weve typically seen these songs staged in a bare rehearsal studio, which at some point magically transforms into a fully set and-costumed theatre. Fair enough; thats fantasy, but it's consistent fantasy. Do it often enough and you've got yourself a convention.

But there are also songs not from the show - pre-existent pop songs, mostly - that characters get up and sing because theyve been asked to perform at a party, or because theyre auditioning for something. And there are others that they sing to themselves, into a mirror usually, as an expression of their inner feelings but with full accompaniment, which is a different, non-naturalistic convention entirely.

There have been even less believable occasions on which whole ensembles have gone into impromptu song-and dance routines in a bowling alley, or the entire cast appeared in a grotesque Bollywood spectacle because in genue Karen (Katharine McPhee) was in an Indian restaurant and having hallucinations.

Two weeks ago Bombshell, as it now is, left New York for its Boston tryout. Composer Tom (Christian Borle) got into the spirit of the thing by sitting down at his piano and singing Cole Porter's apparently to impress his new boyfriend. Nice; but then suddenly the whole company were assembling on the Grand Central concourse (wrong station for the Boston train, but possibly chosen for the identification factor) cavorting around their suitcases like - well, like characters in a musical.

Now, it's true that in the golden age of Hollywood musicals, all these ways of doing a number could turn up in the same picture without anyone being bothered. But musicals in those days were an accepted genre whose practitioners had the time and the experience to play around a bit. Smash is trying to invent the TV musical, and to do it afresh every week with only 60 minutes minus commercials to do it in. Two songs in one hour do not make a musical, and in Smash they all seem to come out of nowhere. The guiding principle seems to be to try everything and hope something sticks.

That seems to be the narrative approach as well. Previously on Smash: I said that it wouldnt necessarily have been a better show if it had been done for cable. Wrong: done to cable standards, it might have been a musical Slings and Arrows, with the offstage narrative reflecting but subordinate to the business of putting on, not just a show, but one particular show. We still have no idea what these fictitious creators are trying to say about Monroe, and the occasional moments in which the aspirant actresses ask themselves, "What would Marilyn do?" are pathetic.

Apparently terrified of confusing their audience, the writers have settled for multipronged soap opera. One appreciates that the participants in a production will sometimes find themselves romantically entangled, but all of them, all the time? In fact, the parts of Smash that work are those most directly, even realistically, concerned with the theatre. I believe in Derek (Jack Davenport), the shoulder-chipped British director, when hes being a talented bastard at rehearsals; I couldn't care less about his relationship with Ivy (Megan Hilty), the bitchier and more insecure of the two standby Marilyns.

Tom and Julia (Debra Messing) are a thoroughly credible songwriting partnership, and I look fondly back on the episodes when they were actually shown writing songs; I also sympathize with Messing for having to act Julia's adulterous spree with a thoroughly boring actor, whose re-engagement in the role of Joe DiMaggio has demonstrated a remarkably cavalier attitude on the shows part to little things like authors rights and actors' contracts. The actual Bombshell songs have been well staged, and (by Marc Shaiman and Scott Wittman) cleverly written, though it's hard to see how theyd fit together. This score seems to consist of pastiche production numbers linked by no conceivable book. I used to think that Id like to see the finished product onstage. I now just think that the numbers placed end to end might make a terrific DVD.

Last week's episode was one of the better ones. It showed us Bombshell in previews, which meant it had largely to stick to business. I've grown to like Anjelica Huston as the producer, and it was touching to hear her talk-sing her way through September Song, which her grandfather Walter talkedsung definitively in 1938.

Rebecca (Uma Thurman), the star brought in to play Monroe and a well-observed character in her own right, prudently bowed out. We wait for tonights season finale to reveal whether Karen or Ivy will replace her; though Ive heard it suggested that Ivy's monster diva of a mother might be enlisted, which, since she's played by Bernadette Peters, sounds like a swell idea that would serve her daughter right. The crazy thing is, I don't really care, but I still want to know.

Smash's season finale airs May 14 at 10 p.m. on CTV and NBC.
