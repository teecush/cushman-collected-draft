Last year I raved about The Double, a crazily inventive Dostoevsky adaptation for two actors and a talking bassist, at the Factory Studio. It's now been re-mounted, apparently with some alterations, at the Tarragon Extra Space, and I hope to get back to it. In the meantime, highly recommended. While I'm in laudatory mood, Ben Heppner's mad scene in the wonderful Canadian Opera Company production of Peter Grimes is the greatest experience available on a Toronto stage: at least until tonight, when it closes. In its hushed intensity it's a joint triumph of composition, staging and performance. Earlier Heppner, even when he's not singing, even when he's just sitting brooding and mending his fishing nets, has extraordinary presence.

A happy memory from earlier this year is of a speech in John Murrell's Taking Shakespeare, at Stratford. A young man, newly turned on to Shakespeare in general and Othello in particular, pours out his enthusiasm for both. To create that play and its central character, he says, Shakespeare must have been to Venice. And Cyprus. And he must have been a soldier. And black. That reductio ad absurdum constitutes a brilliant demolition of the silly, recurring Shakespearean "authorship debate". It was good to hear it from a real playwright, as opposed to somebody with starry-eyed ideas about how playwrights actually work. Murrell, one of Canada's finest if less fashionable dramatists, has had a comeback year, what with a new play at Stratford, an adaptation at Shaw, and a revival by Soulpepper of one of his earlier successes, the 1982 Farther West.

This proves disappointing. It concerns May Buchanan, a real-life Ontario woman from the late-19th century who became a prostitute at 14 and a successful madam later on. We're told that her father, amused or appalled or both, told her to move "farther west" and that she took his words on both a literal level, moving and plying her trades in Calgary and Vancouver, and a symbolic one, refusing to stay still and let anybody, especially any man, tie her down. The play, though, never tells us anything about May other than that she existed, that she was attractive and efficient, and that men were obsessed with her. The locations may change, but the characters and situations stay the same.

It starts well, with Tara Nicodemo's May recounting her early life while disengaging herself from a somnolent customer and getting dressed. (This has been the year, with La Ronde and now this production, that nudity came to Soulpepper.) Nicodemo does well throughout, though not well enough to lift the character out of her repetitive rut. Of her two principal pursuers one, played with credible passion by Matthew MacFadzean, is an initially shy and respectable hanger-on who gets as far as marrying her but finds that doesn't count for much, with dire results. The other is a puritanical Bible-spouting policeman whose revulsion from May's profession masks a deep desire for her person; a pattern familiar in fiction from Somerset Maugham's besotted Reverend in Rain to the character Michael Shannon plays on Boardwalk Empire. (There's a minor case, come to think of it, in Peter Grimes.) As played by Dan Lett, this play's constable seems constantly on the verge of unintended comedy; though in Diana Leblanc's production life isn't made easy for him by a scene in which he's required to harangue May while apparently oblivious of the fact that one of his men and one of hers are locked in potentially mortal combat. It ends with them falling into a pool of water; leaving space for this is one of the problems that bedevils Astrid Janson's set, an all-purpose split-level construction on which nothing looks comfortable.

A strong-on-paper cast is largely wasted; an extreme example is Evan Buliung, held in reserve until the second act and then appearing to play, nicely, a gentle Englishman, yet another of May's admirers, whom the play hardly needs.Her female employees, of whom she's admirably protective, signal their appearances in the first act with much boisterous laughing and singing, always the same song. Sarah Berthiaume's Yukonstyle is a great improvement on her preceding play The Flood Thereafter, in which a supposed mythological overlay obscured a couple of good domestic scenes. Here, the personal relationships are all with any greater resonance being contemporary and emanating from the characters' TV screens. The overriding theme seems to be family responsibility, as exemplified in Japanese and native Canadian cultures but rejected by representatives of both who are taught, painfully, not to do it again. The Japanese, female, is Yuko, now a chef at a restaurant in Whitehorse where the temperature is "between cold and death." The native, male, is Garin, her dishwasher and uneasily platonic roommate. They take in Kate, a feckless, pregnant 17-year-old just off the bus, who says she wants an abortion but has come to the wrong place, and is unwilling to get back on the bus to Vancouver to obtain one; she shies off from more primitive means as well. The Robert Pickton trial is in progress and on the news, and Garin comes to believe that his mother, who vanished when he was two, may have been one of Pickton's victims. Under pressure, Garin's French-Canadian father, who has drunk himself into cirrhosis, tells the truth about their relationship. That they should never even have attempted to have this conversation before is one of the play's several implausibilities. But Berthiaume (translated by Nadine Desrochers) does demonstrate a knack for bringing her characters to the boil, even if she can't always deal with them once they've boiled over.

The same strengths and weaknesses are evident in her three younger actors (Grace Lynn Kung, Ryan Cunningham, Kate Corbett); papa (Louis Klanfer) is the only one who stays on a convincing keel throughout. He's also the only one not required to switch performance modes; the others all function periodically as narrators, either of their own stories or somebody else's. There are also awkward flashbacks and gratuitous symbolism, and the director, Ted Witzel, hasn't found a style to blend it all together. With his set and lighting designers (Gillian Gallow, Bonnie Beecher) he seems to be aiming at stark and arriving at dingy. But the scenes mostly hold, even if not all the speeches do. I don't know what the song at the beginning and end is meant to convey, but it gets things off to a jaunty start and finish.
