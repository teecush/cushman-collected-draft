Our subject is Gross hypocrisy. I mean that in the nicest way. In Slings and Arrows, Paul Gross played a decent and talented artist haunted by the suspicion that he was a fraud. In The Trojan Horse, the new two-part thriller on CBC, he's a politician whose idealism masks unscrupulous ambition: the same persona turned inside out.

The Trojan Horse is the successor to H20, which saw Gross as Tom Mc-Laughlin, Canadian prime minister whose good deeds included donating his country's water supply to keep the folks in the United States from dying of thirst and whose bad included getting rid of his own father. Part one of this sequel showed Canada opting, by a 2% referendum majority, to become part of the U.S., and Gross vowing to take vengeance on an unspecified "them" for allowing this to happen. His plan is to become American president, and he has the backing of a limitlessly wealthy media tycoon (Kenneth Welsh) and of the all-powerful heads of the three major European secret services. He also sets about re-wooing his ex-wife, and he gets himself shot, non-fatally, so that he can stage a resurrection, thereby endearing himself to everyone who enjoys a good story.

That is where we left Tom last week. In part two, which airs on Sunday, the plot thickens. One thing that makes The Trojan Horse more fun than a series such as The Border is that it keeps several storylines going at once. There are the spy chiefs who have got hold of a computer progam guaranteed to fix any election and are prepared to put it at Tom's service. The MI5 man on this team of ruthless smoothies is played by the late William Hutt, visibly frail but still capable of knocking you to attention with an authoritative snap. The schemers have to eliminate everybody else who knows about it, incurring a lawyer whose long-lost journalist mother (Greta Scacchi) finds out about it, and starts investigating. She is our heroine, and the one uncompromised character in a show whose principal message is a plague on all their houses, especially the White one

This is currently occupied by Tom Skerritt, who's very good as a pious, spineless Republican who doesn't look or behave in the least like George Bush. He does have a secretary of state (Saul Rubinek) who's Kissinger without the accent and a security advisor (Rachael Crawford) who might be confused with Condi Rice. This week the Pres has to deal with an exceptionally ugly Saudi hostage situation, arranged for his benefit by his own staff. Unfortunately it also gives a leg-up to his Canadian rival.

Gross himself and John Krizanc are the writers, Charles Biname the director, and the thing moves like the wind. After his deathbed recovery, Tom and his bride "retreat to the dessert." So says the press release, but I regret to inform you that it's a typo. I challenge anyone, by the way, to make sense of the following sentence from the same document: "Biname shot to the desert of Saudi Arabia north of Toronto and the Badlands of Alberta substituted for the Sierra Dablo Mountains, the White House at the White House set in the Kleinberg Studios north of Toronto and was able to shoot for location for a day in London, England." It might have been nice, too, to make some mention of Hutt's death and to have added The Wire to the bio of Clark Johnson, who appears here as a killer with a conscience.

There are flaws. The story repeats itself; Scacchi gets attacked in hotel rooms on two continents. And it doesn't make good on all its pretentions. Though it professes to have a satiric side, it isn't very witty; there is a neat scene in which Rubinek and Ziegler, swapping scandals, trade a gay affair against an abortion, and Guy Nadon gets to say, "for two weeks I was the prime minister of Canada, so you know I'm harmless," but that's about all. The business of Canada surrendering itself isn't explored. Similarly, the contradictions in Gross' character are mentioned, but we'd have to see him actually in power for them to mean anything. When he finally has a showdown with Scacchi, she accuses him of some of the few things he hasn't done, and I'm not sure the show notices. And though the show mostly gets more exciting as its story gets more outrageous, the final twist lost me. I stopped believing, not in the action, but in the motivation. This isn't drama on the level of The Englishman's Boy, but it beats most of CBC as entertainment.

- The Trojan Horse continues Sunday at 10 p.m. on CBC.
