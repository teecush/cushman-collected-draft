THE ANDERSEN PROJECT

Bluma Appel Theatre, Toronto

---

Robert Lepage, the French-Canadian theatrical magician, was commissioned by the Danish government to create a show about their country's favourite literary son, Hans Christian Andersen. Uncertain of how to approach the subject, he hit on building it around the character of a French-Canadian artist, commissioned to create a show about the selfsame writer, and uncertain of how to approach it. The result is a consummate piece of stagecraft, whose only flaw is that it never quite connects with its ostensible subject. Rather, it dances around it. But it's an intoxicating dance.

The joy of Lepage's work is that it manages to be both high-tech and actor-based. In terms of performance, The Andersen Project is a one man show, the one man being Yves Jacques, who plays three principal roles, and is as impressive at embodying each of them as he is dazzling at switching between them. Like his director, he projects a kind of insouciant shyness, daring you to believe what he is bringing off and never breaking a sweat while doing it. All three of his characters are in some way beleagured. Frederic, the commissioned writer, is a rock lyricist who's never sure why he was given the job in the first place. The finished piece is to be presented at the Paris Opera, and he comes to the city of lights to work on it. There's an odd similarity here to the hysterically unhistorical Danny Kaye movie in which the storyteller -- he's Hans Christian Andersen; Andersen, that's he -- comes to wonderful Copenhagen and gets his heart broken. There's a more intentional link to one of Andersen's own lesser-known stories, The Dryad, in which a tree-nymph sacrifices all for one day in Paris. This is the story on which Frederic, to the bemusement of his patrons, chooses to base his own homage. The dryad's tale is spread out across the evening, told to us in charming, wistful increments, as a cross between a puppet show and an animated cartoon. But much of the time it's forgotten about, and it never seems all that relevant when it's remembered.

Lepage seems more interested in Andersen's own biography, and especially in his sexuality or lack of it. He seems to have been, for practical purposes, asexual; but that doesn't mean that he doesn't have pangs. These are reflected in the play's own characters, at least two of whom are having trouble with their love lives. Frederic has broken up with his girlfriend back in Montreal, and obviously regrets it. He spends a lot of time on the phone with her, and finally has a rude if not unexpected awakening. At the same time, he's being double-talked and double-crossed by Arnaud, the Parisian opera director, who has his own betrayal problems. A flat-sharing arrangement has deposited Frederic in an apartment above a peep-show emporium, and when Arnaud calls on him for meetings, he finds the location reawakening instincts that he has struggled to suppress and that are derailing his marriage. We also meet, after a fashion, the peep show's janitor, a Moroccan immigrant who has to mop up after people like Arnaud. Though we aren't privy to his inner life, it's a fair bet that he, too, has his frustrations; and we see him expressing them through his secret life as a subway graffiti artist. (Enchantingly staged, by the way.) The nearest thing we see to a loving relationship, though it too seems somewhat one-sided, is between Frederic and the bitch -- I mean that literally -- whom he has inherited along with the apartment. At one point he takes her to a dog psychiatrist, though she seems very free from hang-ups. As Frederic rather bitterly says, how come he never gets laid when she does all the time?

Jacques plays all his roles, silent or voluble, with equal virtuosity; Arnaud, when he gets going, is a nonstop talker; he starts the show hilariously by apologizing for a cancelled performance of La boheme (it's been struck by a strike) and later delivers a breakneck broken-English oration whose impact may even have been increased by the fact that I couldn't understand a word of it. Around him, Lepage builds an extraordinary -- but never overpowering -- production, in which the actor becomes part of the desiogn without ever being swamped by it. No director uses projections this inventively or -- pardoxically -- this unobtrusively, to the point where you can hardly tell where they leave off and the more solid scenery starts. He can whisk us, magically but also very simply, on a subway train (he's always been great at transportation); he can take delight the in the old-fashioned drapes of an opera house; he can find magic in the grim line of cubicles at a porno theatre. Intellectually, The Andersen Project may not add up. Theatrically, all its parts make a perfect fit.
