PURPLE HEART

Theatre Passe Muraille Backspace, Toronto

Plays about solitude are getting so plentiful, you can't move for them.

Purple Heart is a one-character piece about a young woman named Constance who rarely leaves her Toronto apartment. When she does, to hear her tell it, bad things happen to her.

Once she ventured onto the subway and it broke down between stations, forcing her to descend to the track and walk terrified to safety, in the discouraging company of other passengers who seemed disconcertingly cheerful about it. So she took to cycling, and on one shopping excursion she met the love of her life. That sounds more hopeful, but, of course, every relationship comes with a price.

So she stays home, surrounded by fairy-lights, the pride and joy of her life. They trail along the floor, over the sofa, and, most spectacularly, in an arch that spans the stage, giving the effect of a false proscenium. (Our first sight of it is pretty amazing.)

She also munches her way through myriad boxes of Cracker Jack, and arranges the empty cartons to form what is by now a very high wall. She watches old movies on TV, obsessively it seems, her faves being Fred Astaire musicals and anything with Audrey Hepburn. Logically then, her ultimate picture would be Funny Face, which has Astaire and Hepburn, but I dont remember hearing it mentioned.

The top of her pantheon is Breakfast at Tiffanys, and her adoration of it has inspired her to write copious letters to the real Tiffany's with ideas for window displays. All her suggestions have been spurned, and the sight of the meagre jewelry store that actually faces her building is not much consolation. She is also very keen on The Little Mermaid whose heroine she compares to Hepburn's Holly Golightly. (Holly moved to New York; the Little Mermaid moved to Denmark; its the same, only Denmarks cleaner.)

I can't think what else to tell you. Purple Heart is the work of a visibly talented author, Gail Packwood, and actress, Kirsten Johnson, who have, like their protagonist, painted themselves into a corner. The title refers to Constance's belief that medals should be awarded not just for deeds of heroism, like killing people, but for long endurance -- for, if you like, constancy. She speaks of the pressure she feels "to be successful, to be happy, to be thin," and adds, I cant do it any more.

I doubt if she ever could, or would. We seem to be listening to a heavily decorated -- and perpetual -- hard-luck story. That doesnt make Constance's problems unworthy of our attention, but it does place a heavy responsibility on her interpreters to keep us interested. Johnson is very good at knitting her character's thoughts and emotions together but her range is pinched. Dismissive and defeated from the start, she has nowhere to go.

There is no point in calling Packwoods writing contrived (every play is a contrivance, unless its the work of an idiot savant) and she does build up her own pleasing Toronto mythology: a crazy woman blessing the traffic (one direction only), "that fancy Loblaws down in the Beaches."

Too often, though, listening to the play is like watching the shuffling of an over-familiar pack of cards. Now it's time for another letter to Tiffany's, now for a movie-reference, now for a joke about TVO.

Every so often Constance tells a story we would like to see, and hear, for ourselves: her subway misadventure (I can see there might be technical problems here), her crucial encounter with her lover.

Packwood can obviously write dialogue and it would be good to get it straight, rather than refracted. Solo shows have obvious economic advantages, and a fluent, competent writer can turn them out with comparative ease. But they ought to function only as a court of last appeal: a way of telling stories that can't be told any other way. This story as we see it probably couldn't be; the more interesting story-behind-the-story assuredly could.
