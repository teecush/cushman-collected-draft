THEATRE REVIEW TRUDEAU AND THE FLQ

Monday, May 06, 1996

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Written and directed by Michael Hollingsworth Starring John Dolan and Henry Tarvainen At Theatre Centre West until May 21 Rating: Hollingsworth take on FLQ crisis long on detail, short on depth BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail THE art of the cartoon is to reduce characters to the sum of their obsessions. Usually it's a very simple sum: one driving force per person. Even when the passion might seem to be an honourable one - a thirst for peace or justice or equality - by the time the cartoonist is through with it, it will look like just another lowest common denominator.

This is fine with most of us; we are habitually cynical about public figures of all periods and all persuasions. We lend a willing eye and ear to the Royal Canadian Air Farce and This Hour Has 22 Minutes and (on a far higher level of vitriolic inventiveness) the British series Spitting Image . And we are benevolently curious when Michael Hollingsworth tries to make this short-bite TV technique work for the duration of a 2-1/2-hour stage play.

Or a cycle of stage plays. Or two cycles. Having taken on most of early Canada in The History of the Village of the Small Huts, Hollingsworth is creeping up on the way we live now in a new collection called The Global Village. Trudeau and the FLQ is this second serial's second instalment. It deals, as you would expect, with the attempts of some Quebeckers to get in on the international protest-and-terrorism scene in the late sixties and with the reaction of that other favoured Quebecker who happened to be in charge at the time.

As author, and especially as director, Hollingsworth does his thing superbly. He calls his outfit VideoCabaret, and he comes as close to television as you can while working on a stage with actors who are patently and exhilaratingly live. This may seem a pointless exercise but the paradox turns out to be its own justification. The stage is a black box crammed with bold colour: the joint achievement of set and lighting designer Jim Plaxton, costume designer Astrid Janson, and prop designers Shadowland. (I've never encountered this last credit before, but they've certainly earned it.) The performing style, which never wavers, is similarly witty and similarly emphatic. Although the action covers eight years no character ever gets a change of clothes, though the individual actors, playing six or seven roles apiece, change a great deal. There are more than 100 scenes; and not the least impressive aspect of the evening is the ability of everybody and everything to be in the right place at the right time. The ambience is summed up in the presentation of the FLQ's letter-bomb campaign. A postal worker does a merry little dance to the recording of the Beatles' Please, Mr. Postman. He approaches his box. Bang. Blackout. The show is not selling compassion.

The revolutionaries, dressed in identical shabby, plaid jackets, are led by a squat malcontent called Maurice (played with salivating bravura by Henry Tarvainen), whose credo is that he wants to "kill all the bourgeoisie" he defines the bourgeoisie as "anyone with more money than me." After being locked up, he orchestrates the kidnap campaign as a bargaining counter for his own release. A female comrade called Suzanne is delegated to represent various stages of the sexual revolution: from "read the Kama Sutra or get lost" to "I'm not a woman, I'm a dyke."

Their escalation is counterpointed with that of Trudeau: coming man in Act One, Prime Minister in Act Two. At the very end, though mercifully not before, he meets Maggie, a voluminous flower child: watch, presumably, this space. He is given a superbly languid performance by John Dolan, who does not so much turn a caricature into a human being as persuade you that a caricature is more interesting.

There are, predictably, problems. Though the script is on its own terms well organized, the endless succession of minuscule scenes becomes exhausting. You begin to crave depth. I'm split on this; I think it's quite likely that the FLQ actually were the egotistical goons presented here, but it might make for more interesting drama if we pretended they weren't. At least, it might flatter my humanistic literary prejudices.

Despite all the blood and guts, the evening is short on conflict. The title is a pointer. Sure enough Trudeau takes up some of the stage time and the FLQ much of the rest. Unfortunately history dictates that they never meet. Nor do they have any subtler impact on one another. Though Trudeau keeps protesting his horror of nationalist fanatics, this has nothing to do with the strong measures he eventually takes; any head of state would have done the same. Still, if Hollingsworth merely means us to have a good sustained giggle at the recent past, he has triumphantly succeeded.
