SPRING AWAKENING

Studio Theatre,

York Quay Centre, Harbourfront

Any child caught up in the German school system at the end of the last century was lucky to get out of it alive. That at least is what the writers tell us, from the moderately conservative Thomas Mann to the unquenchably radical Frank Wedekind. In Mann's great novel Buddenbrooks, a well- meaning, not-too-bright boy is crushed between family expectations and academic cruelty.

Spring Awakening, which is Wedekind's first and probably best play, shows us a whole stageful of broken, stunted or cancelled teenage lives.

Their teachers are self-satisfied sadists and their parents, even the best-intentioned, not much better. Still, most kids survive and the title is not ironic. Spring Awakening is a surprisingly cheerful and even charming play. Spring not only awakens, it ripens to a sticky, pastoral summer.It is also, even by our complacently enlightened standards, both amazingly outspoken and alarmingly close to home.

Here is the roll call: Melchior Gabor, prize student, is sent to a reformatory for knowing too much about sex. He has got a 14-year- old girl pregnant and has written a basic biological paper, later found among the effects of a suicidal friend who had requested the information and been unable to find it in the encyclopaedias.

The friend, Moritz Stiefel, has shot himself, his desperation engendered partly by pubescent angst but mainly by an insanely competitive exam regime. (His surname, by the way, is misprounced throughout this production, perhaps they were trying for a pun on "stifle.") The girl, Wendla Bergmann, dies at the hands of an abortionist, procured for her by a mother who would have preferred her to go on believing in the stork.

Minor characters include a girl whose parents regularly beat her for their own amusement and a boy who has a long lyrical speech consigning to the flames the latest in a long series of classical nudes that have been his sources of erotic stimulation.

You use whatever is at hand, so to speak.

The play is full of these introspective outbursts, like Dawson's Creek with a classical education. Other scenes, especially those involving the grown-ups, are contrastingly brief and brutish.

Ed Gass-Donnelly's production has its flaws, mainly arising from a compulsion to underline the obvious. But at its best it is excellent: the best Equity Showcase show in a long time.

It is especially successful with the two principal boys whose scenes together have a remarkably easy flow. Both are, in their different ways, somewhat knowing, somewhat naive, and the balance here is just right.

Moritz ends by emerging from the grave to extol the virtues of death. Life, he says in a striking phrase, "is a sin of omission".

Melchior, momentarily tempted, resists and strides off to glory.

Philip Riccio's Moritz has a touching air of foredoomed defeat, ruefully acknowledged. Aaron Poole's Melchior, a touch less accomplished (and, as written, a touch less interesting), nevertheless drives the play forcefully and intelligently along. Both deliver their taxing texts with exemplary lucidity, as does Dylan Trowbridge as the incendiary art-fancier.

The girls are less convincing: They are inescapably contemporary Canadian kids who, in this context, know too much. Holly Lewis works hard at Wendla's innocence, but she seems inappropriately tough.

The big dividing-line, though, is between the children and the adults. Colleen Williams as Wendla's blushing mother seems in her earlier scenes to share her daughter's reluctance to believe that these people could possibly be real. She is moving, though, when tragedy finally strikes.

The other elders vary from well-meaning ineffectiveness to an apparent ignorance of what they're supposed to be talking about.

Though his staging is economically effective, the director is also prone to bouts of fashionable self-indulgence: there are two explicit masturbation scenes, which is one more than necessary, while Melchior proceeds from kissing Wendla to penetrating her with unlikely speed. (The ecnounter is not supposed to be a rape, though the production seems to think it is.) Most grotesque is the lip- licking staging of Melchior's interrogation by his teachers.

The satire here is admittedly heavy-handed as written, but it's not improved by having the principal excite himself to orgasm at the prospect of expelling the boy or by equipping the staff with goose- steps and Hitler salutes. The director obviously disapproves of Fascism and of sexual repression, but doesn't everybody? Whatever happened to the good old theory of playing even monsters from their own point of view?

Still, these are lapses of judgment rather than of skill or imagination. The all-purpose Germanness, by the way, extends to the background music which is Brecht and Weill. We hear a song from Mahagonny, and are sent home to the strains of Brecht himself warbling Mack the Knife.

The implication, a true one, is that Wedekind paved the way for Brecht, though I'm not at all sure that Brecht ever wrote anything as good or as enduringly pertinent as Spring Awakening. And if you like your drama to be socially useful, then this is the best sex- education play ever written.

- Spring Awakening plays to Dec. 5 at the York Quay Centre. Call 416-533-6100.
