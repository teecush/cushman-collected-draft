THEATRE REVIEW

Ride the Cyclone Theatre Passe Muraille, Toronto

No question that for verve, imagination and an unflaggingly high level of performance, Ride the Cyclone, a small-sized musical, puts bigger shows to shame.

It also represents an interesting confluence of forces. It's the creation of the Atomic Vaudeville company from Victoria; in Toronto it was the hit of last years Summerworks festival; and its now returned as a co-production by Theatre Passe Muraille, one of the city's oldest established alternative theatres with no particular track record in musicals, and Acting Up Stage, who do nothing but musicals, usually off-the-wall ones that originated in the United States. So it's a step forward for everyone concerned.

That said, I have my reservations, which are about the presentation being superior to the material. Jacob Richmond, who wrote the script, was the author of Atomic Vaudevilles Legoland, a satire that was often crazily funny but had a rickety structure. The same is true of Ride the Cyclone.

Set in the fictitious town of Uranium, Sask., it concerns six teenage members of the high school glee club who went for a ride on the eponymous local roller coaster and were killed when it malfunctioned. When we meet them, theyre holding a posthumous convocation to reflect on their so-called lives. Each contributes a monologue followed by a song, which evolves into a production number in which all the others enthusiastically, if mostly sardonically, join. It's like Glee with claws.

Their self-appointed leader, in death as in life, is Ocean O'Connell Rosenberg (Rielle Braid), half-Catholic, half Jewish, who channelled her mixed feelings about her mixed parentage into becoming a take-no-prisoners debating champ. Then there are Noel (Kholby Wardell), who has/had fantasies of being a Parisian sex-object; Ricky (Elliott Loran), a blank nerd whose secret life centred on being a superhero; Misha (Matthew Coulson), an immigrant from Ukraine whose old hometown was devastated by the uranium that gave his new hometown its name; and Jane Doe (Sarah Pelzer), so named because nobody knew who she was or, since her head was cut off and no one can find it, ever will. Last, and maybe least in the others estimation, is Constance (Kelly Hudson), a bespectacled girl who is embarrassed at being the only one who actually loves Uranium, a ghost town the others couldnt wait to leave.

Their set-up speeches are, without exception, hilarious. The lyrics, on which Richmond collaborated with Brooke Maxwell, are less good, though some of this may be blamed on an indistinct sound system and on accompaniment that is sparse but loud.

The show also has a complicated set-up. Jane Doe, whose plot line is implausible even in this context, is thrust to the front too early, so we wonder if she belongs in some other story. We've already met a super-narrator, The Amazing Karnak, a mechanical fairground fortune teller who, naturally, knew what was going to happen but didn't say and now feels guilty.

Karnak, though played by a presumably pre-recorded voice, is a delight, so much that I felt rather cheated that the show wasnt actually about him. He does however provide some justification for the title, which is otherwise catchy but irrelevant.

All the performances are smashing but Hudsons is the standout, as riotous as the others but poignant in a more grounded way. She has the best song and she rocks the house with it. She also has the last laugh, since it turns out that just before the fatal ride she lost her virginity, which is more than any of her more glamorous colleagues seem to have achieved.

The direction, by Richmond and Brett Small, gets the show resoundingly across. I saw it from the end of the front row, which, sightlines being what they are, may have skewed my perspective and soured my reaction. Anyway, it's already a super hit and on its way to becoming a cult. Or maybe vice versa.

- Ride the Cyclone runs until Dec. 3 (for now). For tickets, visit passemuraille.on.ca.
