Some people can do self-deprecation. Some can't. Some can do ironic self-praise, and keep it ironic and therefore charming. Some merely come off smug, or at best clueless. Steve Carell and Cameron Diaz, presenting this year's Oscar for best animated picture, told us how beautiful they were; he made it seem funny, she didn't. But then he has the comedy background; all she had was the fact that she actually is beautiful. Anyway she wasn't unduly strained by announcing the winning title, which was Up.

Oscar-giving actors have always come in pairs and they have, with rare exceptions, proved awful at playing themselves in public. (The flatulent material they're generally given doesn't help, of course.) Oscar hosts have generally been solo acts, but this year the powers that be decided to go for a double. Steve Martin, who's experienced at this kind of thing, was partnered with Alec Baldwin, who isn't.

Well, it was an idea. It seemed planned as a homage to great double-acts past: Hope and Crosby, Astaire and Rogers, Burton and Taylor. What? No, straight up, Dick and Liz were among those hymned in an opening number, alternately star-struck and smutty, delivered, somewhat off-key, by Neil Patrick Harris. He did so, with dancing girls, against a blue Art Deco set. It was the kind of number that really needed Cole Porter to write the lyrics, but he was unfortunately unavailable. It also pointed up the double bind in which the Academy Awards increasingly find themselves. They're torn between making fun of the whole event and the knowledge that if they go too far, there won't be an event.

"I wrote that speech for him," said Martin, presumably off the cuff, after an especially tear-drenched acceptance speech for best adapted screenplay. Earlier on, surveying the audience, he'd said "over here is the Inglourious Basterds section, and over here are the guys who made the movie." It may not read like much, but it was funny when he said it. That's his gift, abetted by the fact that he's never quite been a major movie star, and so can plausibly pose as a WASP-ish outsider. His delivery was weightless but priceless. Baldwin, by contrast, was heavy. We were rooting for him; we wanted him to make a transition comparable to the one he made in 30 Rock. (He did, in fact, pay tribute to Tina Fey for revitalizing his career. She was part of a fascinating duo with Robert Downey Jr., and was comedically on top of things. With Downey, you can never tell.) Baldwin, on present evidence, can do sitcom but not stand-up But once past the embarrassing song-and-dance at the top, the show looked like settling down. The clips were longer than usual and this, oddly enough, speeded things along. They actually told you something about the movies.
