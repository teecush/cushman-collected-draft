THE RIDE DOWN MT. MORGAN

Berkeley Street Theatre, Toronto

The climax of Arthur Miller's Death of a Salesman comes when the protagonist, Willy Loman, commits suicide by crashing his car. The same author's The Ride Down Mt. Morgan, premiered a full 42 years later in 1991, begins where the older play stopped, sort of. Its hero, Lyman Felt, is revealed to us in a hospital bed, his presence there being the result of his having driven full-tilt down the icy hill referred to in the title. Its at least possible he was trying to kill himself.

From Loman to Lyman is a long haul, and not just chronologically. Suicide was the only thing at which Willy L. ever succeeded. It seems to be the only thing at which Mr L. Felt has ever failed. In his youth he was a promising poet, published in The New Yorker, and a friend of James Baldwin; he then switched to selling insurance, a field in which he is now a mogul. (The world may be full of poets- turned-insurance-men, but there can be few who were that good at both.) Willy had an extramarital affair on the road, but it wasnt really crucial to his downfall. Lyman may owe his present prone position to the fact that he has had two wives, simultaneously.

"That's bigamy! said a shocked Margaret Dumont to some leering proposition of Groucho Marx, to which Groucho replied well, its big o' me, too. Lets be big for a change." Lyman thinks, at least some of the time; he sees himself, in his less guilt-wracked moments, as an explorer, an adventurer and a man who really loves women. And though the play has some moral and intellectual quarrels with this position, emotionally its on his side. It can hardly help this, because it's written from his point of view.

Miller's original title for Salesman was The Inside of His Head, and he might well have resurrected it as at least a subtitle for this play. Receiving the news that both his wives have been called to his bedside, Lyman gloomily imagines the course of their inevitable meeting. We see his imaginings played out, and they seem to be 100% accurate.

From their home in New York -- probably an East Side townhouse -- comes Lymans legal partner, Theo, svelte and businesslike and attended by divorced daughter Bessie, who takes the situation even worse than her mother does. Bessie rages; Theo just faints, though she has some bitter things to say when she comes to. From the immediate neighbourhood comes Leah, also prominent in the insurance business but with a very different style. Theo puts her down as a kind of leftover hippie, the kind, in Theos estimation, who never bothers to wash her underwear. She's earth-motherly, and a literal mother as well, having born Lyman a young son whom we never see.

Subsequent encounters between the parties seem to be in real time; Lyman, however, reserves the right to disappear through a hole in the bed when the discussions get too heated, then to reappear at the side of the stage and lose himself in reminiscence. In other words, there are flashbacks. Technically there is nothing very challenging about this, but the mood and style are very difficult to hit off. The present production is both the play's Canadian premiere and the first offering of a new company called Birdland Theatre; it has had a nightmarish preparatory period -- the word "jinxed" hardly seems adequate -- that has included the deaths of two of the principal people involved, one of them the director, Cameron Wright. Ones heart goes out to all involved, and it seems cruel, however necessary, to point out that the element most missing from the show is playfulness.

Part of this is the plays fault. Its structure is comic -- the first scene between the wives borders on farce, and is framed as such -- and its texture tries to be, but it lacks both the verbal and the narrative inventiveness to keep its ideas afloat. Lyman declares that, after a lifetime of subterfuge, he wants to tell the truth, and the truths he tells are comic ones, of the painful variety; of how he has been bored in both his marriages, and used each to alleviate the other, to the benefit of all concerned. (There is one brief, funny scene that shows this system in action. It seems to work, at least in the short term.) He talks a lot, but -- intentionally or not -- his words form a smokescreen; we never get a real sense of what either union was actually like.

You start to wonder, impertinently and perhaps irrelevantly, what it all means to Miller himself, thrice-married though only sequentially. There is little of the social resonance you get in his earlier plays (from All My Sons, via Salesman and The Crucible, to A View from the Bridge). Like Harold Pinter, whom he otherwise little resembles, Miller has tended to write about rich people since becoming one himself, and the air in his later work seems less nourishing. I know there have been suggestions that Lyman's behaviour is a comment on the ethos of the 1980s, but I've never believed that selfishness, greed or hedonism were inventions of that decade. Besides, those qualities in Lyman are the most attractive ones in the play. He has a lively mind, which is just as well considering the amount of the play that takes place there.

The role calls for the wit, the style, and the passion, both intellectual and sexual, of a Christopher Plummer; indeed its difficult to think of another Canadian with the weight and range to play it. Ron White manages the elegant ironies very well as long as hes sitting up in bed, but when he starts getting up and burrowing into his past, he cannot find a full man there.

The production has apparently been staged by the actors themselves, in accordance with their director's presumed intentions, but the results are ragged. Pace slackens; exits and entrances seem drained of energy or purpose; the set takes economy to the point of drabness; crystals hanging from the ceiling cast flickering, distracting reflections throughout. Kate Trotter as Theo has some memorably angry moments, and a provocative semi-mad scene near the end, but Zorana Kydd does little to suggest what Lyman's second wife may have had that his first didnt.

There is good, impeccably dressed support from Peter Van Wart as Lymans lawyer, the voice of conventional decency (i.e., he wonders why his client didn't just have an affair the second time around) and really excellent support from Marium Carvell as a nurse who, if she were free, might well elicit Lyman's third proposal. Melissa Kramer plays the thoroughly ungrateful role of the thoroughly ungrateful daughter, who admittedly has much to be ungrateful about.

No Miller play is dismissable, and this one has more substance buried in it than anything by Edward Albee, on whose territory it seems sometimes to be trespassing, but it needs more, from everybody.
