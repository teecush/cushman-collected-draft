The Greeks didnt only invent the Western theatre. They also invented the Western open-air theatre. Philosophers Walk, by the University of Toronto law faculty, may not quite be the Theatre of Dionysus, but it certainly has an appropriately evocative name. For the past three summers, the Canopy Theatre Company has challenged the city's Shakespearean monopoly with an alfresco Attic tragedy. This year it's Oedipus Rex, the big one.

The Canopy company is a barely professional outfit, mainly composed of recent theatre-school graduates, and this production (which I saw at its one and only preview) has no chance of qualifying as a transcendental experience. Not that Ive ever seen a production of Oedipus that did. This one is the story delivered pure and simple, with a few unfortunate flourishes. But once youve accepted that, it becomes rewarding and even engrossing.

To dispose of some of the oddities first: The set consists of a rostrum, approached by several shallow steps and, right at the top, a very steep one. The actors, who after all are mainly young and agile, take the latter obstacle in their stride, but it has to be an ungainly stride. It seems especially hard on Oedipus himself in the throes of his final humiliation. He's blinded himself, and has to be helped around the stage. He's taken the blame for everything, like a good tragic hero. Hasnt he suffered enough? Surely the least he deserves is a reasonably level playing field on which to take his medicine.

There are five Doric columns on top of the platform, and another three scattered around below. Theres also a sacrificial flame, burning all on its own, and a few lampposts, which obviously come with the property but blend in surprisingly well. The total effect is all-purpose classical, a style that also extends to the costumes, and especially to the white sheets worn by members of the chorus.

The chorus is, as always, a problem. The solution attempted by Andrea Wasserman's production is to naturalize and colloquialize them as much as possible. It doesn't look like that at the start of the show; when we first see them the actors are wearing flimsy white masks. But it turns out that the chorus is doubling as the plague- stricken population of Thebes (usually theyre Elders, whose status seems to entitle them to some kind of pestilence-immunity package), and theyre dragging themselves, or one another, to the royal palace, groaning. Their condition once established, the masks come off, though the complaints and lamentations go on. Timing is everything, and it obviously isn't the production's fault that the sequence looks like the "bring out your dead" number in Spamalot, minus the laughs. Well, almost minus the laughs.

Even in the main body of the play, this chorus has no intention of staying choral, not if that means staying on the sidelines. Sophocles play is, among other things, the first detective drama to have come down to us, and it may well be the best, with very superior twists. Oedipus relentlessly probes the facts surrounding the late King Laiuss murder and the remarriage of his widow, Jocasta, and discovers that he himself has done it; he unwittingly killed his father and, as all the world knows, went on to unwittingly marry his mother. Though he didn't have a complex about it. And jokes about how much Oedipus loved his mother (does anyone remember Never on Sunday?) are invariably as ignorant as they are cheap. Oedipus didn't know.

Now the same foul temper that led Oedipus to regicide also leads him, in the course of his investigations, to level paranoid accusations at those he feels are against him, chiefly his brother- in-law, Creon, and the prophet Tiresias. And in this production, the chorus joins in the mudslinging, sometimes appropriating some of the kings own lines to do it. At best theyre sycophants, at worst they're a lynch mob. The intention, obviously, is to turn them from commentators to participants: to, a dreaded word in this context, humanize them. But there's something amateurish in the sound of all that jeering, something very lets-get-in-touch-with-our-feelings- and-make-it-up-as-we-go-along. It would take far more experienced actors to make all this ad-lib chattiness convincing, though I doubt that more experienced actors could easily be persuaded to try.

The soloists, who would seem to offer severer technical challenges, actually fare far better. Paul Kits Oedipus is a brash, impatient young man with an incorruptible conscience and a helpfully loud voice; this may not amount to great tragic acting, but it covers the basics. Joel Grothe is similarly vigorous as Creon, though it's a shame that the two are much the same age; Creon should surely be older. Some maturer actors do helpful work: John Illingworth as a de facto chorus leader (a.k.a. Priest of Zeus), Albert Masters as an amusingly bumptious Messenger from Corinth, Peter Higginson doubling a piteous old Shepherd with a powerful Tiresias. That's the most accomplished performance of the evening.

Lada Darewych has trouble finding a tone and personality for Jocasta, but thats understandable. Mother? Wife? How old is she supposed to be? Maybe one shouldnt ask such questions, but in a production that makes gestures to realism, they're inescapable. And even as written, her reactions -- she guesses the truth before anyone, and counsels against pursuing it further -- are the most accessible of any in the play. She's tired of living in a myth, governed by fates and oracles, but she cant escape it. The men embrace it, but it doesnt spare them, either. Greek tragedy presents the ineluctable tendency of things to go wrong, which is why we both need it and resist it, and this one does it more starkly than any. Everyone involved has good intentions, and it avails them nothing. That, at least, comes through.

- Oedipus Rex runs through Aug. 5 at Philosopher's Stage on Philosopher's Walk.
