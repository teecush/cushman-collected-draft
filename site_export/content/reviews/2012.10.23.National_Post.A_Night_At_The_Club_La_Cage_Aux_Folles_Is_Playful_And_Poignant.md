Time has been kind to La Cage aux Folles. When the musical premiered in 1983, it was an old-fashioned show with a newfangled premise. Its principal characters, Georges and Albin, are a middle-aged gay couple, married in all but name, running a transvestite cabaret club in St. Tropez; Georges is the manager and compere, Albin the drag queen and star. Putting them at the centre of a traditional show was a kind of coming out for the Broadway musical. It was also an artistic coming out for the composer-lyricist, Jerry Herman, whose previous big hits had been Hello Dolly and Mame. The heroines of those shows had always seemed, especially when doing their iconic numbers, like drag queens in disguise. When Albin, in La Cage's key number, sings about the release he feels in putting a little more mascara on, he's eliminating the middlewoman.

He's also going out on a limb, as is the musical itself. The thing that's always bothered me about Dolly and Mame, the latter especially, is that they're thoroughly conventional shows preaching about being unconventional. Mame even tells us, in roughly so many words, that the fellow we ought to be is unconventional. She's the ultimate safe rebel. But Albin, and Georges, too, do have something to fight against. And they don't, by and large, preach.

Georges has a son, Jean-Marie, the result of a one-night heterosexual fling. Georges and Albin have brought the boy up, and he now wants to get married -- to a girl. Unfortunately, Anne, his beloved, is the daughter of the most aggressively homophobic politician on the Riviera. Jean-Marie is bringing his future in-laws home to meet the folks, and he wants the swishy Albin to make himself scarce: something that embarrasses Georges and hurts Albin deeply. Harvey Fierstein's book, based on a French play, sets this situation up very well in its first act. The second, in which the visitors arrive, takes a nose-dive into uninspired farce, but redeems itself when Albin makes a surprise appearance that's a veritable coup and that I, not having seen the show since its first production, had forgotten.

That's another example of time's kindness. But the highly enjoyable revival that's touched down at the Royal Alex is its beneficiary in less trivial ways. In the 1983 production, the crusading politico and his wife were crude caricatures; they still are, but they carry more weight. It's a paradox: Gay marriage was barely an issue when the show was new; 30 years later, it's empowered a new generation of reactionary polemicists.

The current production is directed by Terry Johnson, a leading British playwright who has moved from staging his own work to staging other people's. Johnson started at a small theatre in London and eventually progressed to Broadway. That's the same trail that was blazed by the famous Sam Mendes production of Cabaret, and this La Cage takes a similar conceptual approach, emphasizing the tawdriness of the clubland setting and presenting the whole show as an extended club routine. There's some playful, but painless, interaction with the audience. The blurring of fantasy and reality makes for some very rich effects. When Albin, in everyday female garb, dances socially with Georges, they look like an old married couple. And then you realize that that is indeed what they are, but that Albin isn't really a woman, although that is how he feels most at home. The ambiguities are positively Shakespearean.

The glory of the show is Christopher Sieber's wonderful Albin, who can slide instantly from petulant complaint to deep pain, and also into hilarious anger, transmitted by steep vocal descents into gravelly basso profundo. He's also impeccably skilled in pure musical-comedy terms. He transforms The Best of Times, a Herman cheer-up song with a French accent, into genuine joy, for instance.

Albin has always been the star role of this show. Georges is just, pardon the expression, the straight man, though it must be possible to do more with him than is accomplished here by George Hamilton who, cast for road-company celebrity value, acquiesces by obeying the Woody Allen dictum that success is mainly a matter of just showing up. He deals with song and dance mainly by avoiding them; his acting is confined to some amusing raised-eyebrow moments when his plans fall apart and then come together again. If he does too little, Jeigh Madjus as butler-maid of the menage does much too much. Katie Donohue, though, makes all that can be made of the underwritten Anne, and the six Cagelles, given appropriately seedy choreography by Lynne Page, are a riot.
