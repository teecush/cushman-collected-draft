THEATRE REVIEW

Hosanna Soulpepper

Soulpepper's revival of Hosanna, Michel Tremblays famous study of a Montreal drag-queen, includes wonderful performances, but still comes across as dated.

The apartment Hosanna (a.k.a. Claude) shares with his biker-lover Cuirette (a.k.a. Raymond) is suddenly revealed, just before the intermission, to include a four-panelled floor-to-ceiling mirror. No matter how selfadmiring Hosanna may be, this can hardly be meant to be realistic. It paves the way, however, for the second halfs shift into formality: Hosanna tells the story of his humiliation at the party to which he had gone proudly dressed as Elizabeth Taylor dressed as Cleopatra while Cuirette, who was partly responsible, antiphonally apologizes.

Hosanna's long narration, shifting between shame and anger and self-recognition, is the spellbinding crown of Damien Atkins's superb performance: his third this year. From his and the plays first moments, hes been fully alive in every flounce, every sneer, every drag - if you'll pardon the expression - on his cigarette.

That's even before he starts talking. When he does, his vocal range and emotional control are extraordinary. Jason Cadieux Cuirette gives him sympathetic support, even when his character doesnt. Their duet, under Gregory Prest's direction, is beautifully orchestrated.

This is the best production of Hosanna I've seen.

It cant, however prevent the play, now 40 years old, from seeming technically old-fashioned. It withholds information so that it can have a second act while devoting most of its first to having the characters tell each other what they already know, with a shamelessness that would have raised Ibsens bushy eyebrows. Sentimentality is kept at bay, largely due to Atkins's performance, until the end when Hosanna proclaims/admits that he's a man. At which point the director has Cuirette pick him up and carry him to bed, like a groom carrying a bride across the threshold. Is this meant to be irony? Or just a nice stage-picture to finish with?

Hosanna runs until October 15 at the Young Theatre in Toronto.
