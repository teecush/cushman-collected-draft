A WOMAN OF NO IMPORTANCE

Court House Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

NIAGARA-ON-THE-LAKE - A cracking first week at the Shaw Festival ended with the least-known of Oscar Wilde's society comedies. It proved a fascinating evening, and an improving one as well: a steady upward climb. I cannot recall a production, as one of its characters might have said in an off moment, that began so badly and ended so well.

Like the other Wilde plays that led up to The Importance of Being Earnest, A Woman of No Importance alternates scenes of studiedly flippant commentary with others of solemn, palpitating melodrama. Once upon a time in the West End, the latter was regarded as the price you paid for the former; the staid characters had their scenes cut to ribbons while the beribboned ones were dressed to the nines. Surely, producers said, a writer as sophisticated as Wilde could not have meant his more overripe and overwrought exchanges to be taken seriously.

We have since realized that he probably did, and that, even if he didn't, we would be well advised to play them as if he did; there are just too many of them to be sent up. Besides, they concern real situations and real emotions, and good actors can get at the truth that lies beneath the purple. That is the approach taken in Susan Ferley's production, and it works superbly.

What works less well are the jokes, which is a shame, since they seem to be among the best in Wilde. (Or maybe it's just that they haven't been overexposed.) They are delivered by an assortment of peers and peeresses, languidly passing the time over a country- house weekend. Some of the lines relate to the individuals who speak them; others, the author seems to have distributed around the cast with magnificent impartiality.

There is one epigram here that has made the dictionaries of quotations: a description of fox-hunting as "the unspeakable in full pursuit of the uneatable." There are many about marriage, usually seen from the woman's point of view; one especially world-weary lady describes her husband as "a sort of promissory note; I'm tired of meeting him."

The female ensemble here is ragged; the only actress in command of the text is Jennifer Phipps, as a fearsomely benevolent dragon. She often seems to be woolgathering (metaphorically and literally; she's very attached to her knitting), but just when you think she's drifted off, she drifts on again, bringing down the house with another perfectly pitched punchline.

Nobody else has her gift of phrasing. Sharry Flett, as the hostess, has an air, but the benevolent vagueness of her characterization seems also to have infected her technique. And in what would once have been the star role, the glittering kind in which English high comediennes used to swan around in dresses by Cecil Beaton, Brigitte Robinson practically fades into the wallpaper -- or would, if the small, open stage at Niagara's Court House had any walls.

Wilde's paradoxes, so compulsively spun, tend to cancel one another out; but this chorus of wives, dowagers and divorcees is presumably meant to provide a background, tempting but unsavoury, against which the play's good characters can shine all the purer. That doesn't sound much like Wilde the dandy and aesthete, but it does seem to be the play he wrote. As it unfolds, the chorus recedes and the plot takes over, which, in this production, is a good thing.

The story concerns Mrs. Arbuthnot, who was once seduced and abandoned by Lord Illingworth. She bore a son, Gerald, who has grown up and is now, to his mother's horror, about to accept a position as his father's secretary. Neither father nor son knows of their relationship; when Mrs. A. in desperation reveals it to Lord I., it merely makes him more anxious to forward the boy's future.

Gerald, meanwhile, is unshaken by the revelation that his father once ruined an unidentified young woman. (Wasn't it, he inquires, the girl's fault, too? -- a question to which his mother has no answer.) But he is revolted when he finds him making advances to a young American woman whom he himself admires. All three make common cause against Lord Illingworth, who retires from the field, defeated but unbowed.

Pleading for her son, Mrs. Arbuthnot says things like "leave me the little vineyard of my life ... the ewe-lamb God sent me, in pity or in wrath"; she describes herself as "a woman who drags a chain ... like a thing that is a leper." Mary Haney, in a magnificent performance, cuts beneath the rhetoric to the angry, agonized woman beneath. Some of the morality may seem alien to us, but Wilde's loathing of the double standard comes through with scorching clarity.

Not all her colleagues can make the material work so well. The American girl (Severn Thompson) is a Henry Jamesian innocent abroad whom the decadent English deride as a prig and a prude, and one cannot help agreeing with them.

The one character who speaks in both the play's voices is Lord Illingworth, by turns a cynical wit and a man who both inflicts and suffers pain. He loves his son, somewhat ambiguously (their first scene together, as employer and protege, plays like a homosexual seduction), fights to keep him, and loses. Jim Mezon, pudgy and soft- spoken, is not the most obvious casting for an Edwardian roue, but he excels at playing men divided, men with layers, and his scenes with Haney reach a very high level of tension and involvement.

His resemblance to his author is clearly intended: Wilde may condemn the things that Illingworth does, but he clearly enjoys and endorses much of what he says. In his next play, An Ideal Husband, he presents an almost identical character as the voice of charity and reason. He was dealing, of course, with a social class that he had reason both to fear and to envy. Only in his last play did he get them in perspective; in The Importance, which opens tomorrow at Stratford, all the characters are monsters, but nothing -- unless, of course, it involves muffins or cucumber sandwiches -- is a matter of life and death.
