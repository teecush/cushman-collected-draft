There's an extraordinary scene in the second act of Oil and Water - extraordinary in a good way. A woman is washing down the body of a man who seems at the moment more dead than alive. The date is 1942; the place is the coast of Newfoundland, in the wake of a ferocious storm. The woman is Violet Pike, the unlettered wife of a miner. The man is Lanier Phillips, a sailor in the U.S. Navy, whose ship has just been wrecked. Violet and her neighbours have already cleaned off the oil-soaked bodies of Laniers fellow survivors, but his remains obstinately discoloured, or so they think. His first sign of life comes when he opens his eyes and his mouth, and gently informs them that it wont come off: he's black. He is the first black man they have ever seen. They are the first white people, either in the service or in his hometown of Atlanta, to treat him like a human being.

It's a true story. More importantly, it feels true - in this scene, anyway. Some of its impact comes from the patience, the determination, the quietness, with which Violet - a superbly gnarled and business-like performance by Petrina Bromley - carries out her task. The theatrical impact of careful, honest, determined work (as championed in the theories, if not the practice, of Bertolt Brecht), has seldom been more powerfully demonstrated. The quietness has a lot do with it; up till now, Jillian Keileys production has been very noisy, and not always helpfully so, and this scene gains immeasurably from the contrast. The unfussy intensity of Keileys staging and Bromley's performance casts a spell on the audience and also, it seems, on everyone else onstage - on her helpers, on the recipient of her ministrations, and even on those who aren't strictly part of the scene but are there as onlookers. At the back are two of Laniers fellow sailors, one drowned, one saved; to the side is his future self, telling the story to his young daughter.

Subsequent scenes are almost equally affecting, and for similar reasons. We see Lanier in contact with Violet, her husband, her nephew and a friend; theres a shy ease about the encounter, along with some spiky intimations of domestic conflict, that gain enormously from the feeling that nobody is trying to batter us into anything. The late sections of this show restore confidence in Keiley's high reputation, and in that of her St. John's company, Artistic Fraud, which presents it. They also raise high hopes for her new appointment as director of the English-language company at the National Arts Centre.

And not before time, because the first act of Robert Chafes play, at least as presented here, is a mess - a sincereandwell-intentionedmess,but thats not much consolation. Even presenting it in two acts seems unnecessary, since the total running time is only 90 minutes, and the intermission arrives so abruptly that what must have been meant as a cliff-hanger registers only as a question mark.

The structure is not so much complex as complicated. We have Lanier's life on board crosscut with the miners' and their families lives on land and underneath it: two kinds of exploitation plus the pouring of oil into already troubled waters. I could buy those as ideas, but they arent fleshed out and we don't get close to the people who are supposed to be embodying them. Part of the reason for that is that it's hard to hear them. The Newfie accents, or my provincial lack of exposure to them, may be partly to blame, but whats really troublesome is the music that the actors have to act against. The production claims to have found much common ground between Newfoundland folk tunes and American spirituals, and it may well be right because Andrew Lambs score, drawing on both, comes to feel like a constant homogenous wash, drowning everybody out long before we get to the actual drowning. The music isn't in itself bad, and it's very well sung. Indeed, when the healing process is capped by an a capella rendition of Balm in Gilead, its very stirring indeed, partly because it comes at the end of a long stretch of fine and unmediated dialogue.

There are still further layers. Young Lanier is haunted by the warning voice of his great-grandmother, an exslave, warning him against the dangers of land and of white people in general. The historical Lanier, transformed by his wartime experience, became a civil rights pioneer, and marched with Martin Luther King. When we meet him with his daughter, shes being bussed into a newly integrated school and is meeting the kind of racist violence we associate with, say, Little Rock in 1957. Only this is Boston in 1974, which is a shock. The connection is made, though, in the program rather than in the play. Indeed, it's only late in the action that it's made clear that the man at the side of the stage is recalling the events that are happening in the middle of it. So maybe its meant as a surprise. But I think youre better off knowing it. Otherwise, it's just confusing. Even with the knowledge, the introduction of the older Lanier does nothing to make the story more moving. It merely turns him into a plaster saint.

None of the other acting is on the level of Bromley's Violet, but Neema Bickersteths singing is powerful, Jeremiah Sparks patriarchal Lanier is dignified, and Ryan Allen as his younger self is very touching, especially after his rescue. Violet's menfolk (Jody Richardson and Mark Power) likewise come into their own when they're given characters to act and the space to act them in. On a set by Shawn Kerwin mainly composed of ladders, Keiley does surprisingly little to summon up the ship in its initial state, but the wreck itself is skilfully evoked. Probably you have to have the storm before you can appreciate the calm, but the balance here is out of whack.
