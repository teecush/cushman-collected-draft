Despite being housed on the Comedy Channel -- or conceivably because of being housed on the Comedy Channel -- Improv Heaven & Hell is promoted less as a source of laughter than as a bloodsport. Its presiding spirits, Andrew Currie and Albert Howell, gained their notoriety on Citytv's Speaker's Corner where they put on horns and dark glasses, insulted people, and called themselves the Devil's Advocates.

Now here they are, still in the gear, puffing on cigars while making their entrance through simulated flames and proceeding to a couple of neat little Satanic thrones. I'm surprised that the Devil permits a couple of mere mouthpieces to have thrones; but it seems that hierarchy, even in hell, is not what it was.

They look as if they're killing time in Seville while waiting to carry off Don Juan, and in the meantime are looking for some fun on the side. Actually, they're improvisational comedians (former Second City) who are being denied the chance to improvise themselves; which sounds pretty diabolical in itself.

In the meantime, they have four other comics whom they can make jump through hoops. They throw them themes, ideas, on which to make up scenes, and if ever the performers threaten to get too comfortable with the material, they change the rules on them.

The idea is, of course, similar to that of the British-born, American-adopted Whose Line Is It Anyway? Many of the routines are similar, and Colin Mochrie, a veteran of that show, is also a regular on this, and quite the best thing on it. (He's billed as a guest star, but he seems to be the kind of guest who never leaves.) The difference is that Improv H&H claims to be the dirtier, and therefore purer, version. Whose Line is heavily edited, and the viewers only get to see the sequences that the producers think work. Improv is also pre-taped, but it makes a point of leaving everything in.

There is an unsurprising rule about improvised comedy, and that is that very little of it is actually funny. Sometimes it can take off and be inspired, but not often. It may be corrupt and commercial but Whose Line is a lot more entertaining because of being selective. (It's also more overall talented, though it's a better thing in its British incarnation, with Clive Anderson in charge, than in its American, with Drew Carey.)

All comics with any life in them improvise to some extent, but improvisation is the tool rather than the product. I have had a couple of the most hilarious nights of my life at Second City (this was a long time ago) but the laughs from sketches which, even if they had never been written down, had been carefully honed. The genuinely improvised bits were thrown in at the end of the evening - - free of charge, really -- by which time the actors were really flying; even so, one admired their guts and good humour rather than their actual accomplishment.

Also, it helped to be there. Just watching it at home, you feel removed. Improv is not, I would guess, as unedited as all that; the transitions between items seem unnaturally swift. Maybe this shouldn't matter (who wants to see people just changing position?) but it does.

It makes the show a package, to be judged like any other. It proceeds on the dispiriting principle that nothing much is going to be funny, and that we can laugh at that. This makes the hosts into downmarket Lettermen. They are there to make their victims (their word) suffer.

Now of course this isn't a game show; the performers are pros, and you don't have to feel sorry for them. In this season's first two episodes, Mochrie is quizzically commanding, fat Jack Mosshammer and bald Paul O'Sullivan are amiable punchbags, and Teresa Pavlinek, though often seeming out of it, blossoms when she has to make up songs.

But the set-up is inescapable; they are programmed to fail. Currie and Howell, meanwhile, are programmed to sneer and either they're not very good at playing devils (as opposed to advocates) or they've given themselves bad material. As stars, they are singularly unappealing; you can't even love to hate them, you just sort of resent them.

To be fair, they have expressed some discomfort themselves about being kept on the sidelines. Just once in each show, they are allowed to show off. They riff on a given subject; in the first show, it was toothpaste, in the second bumblebees, (for whose lazy habits they expressed amusing disgust).

The thing is that they improvise in unison. They've worked together a long time, and they've presumably developed a telepathic rapport; they also probably have some practised routines that they can adapt as necessary.

All the same, it's quite a trick; and though one must take his cue from the other, it's impossible to tell, however closely you monitor their lips, which of them is leading. It's impressive, though it's only funny when it breaks down. But the humour of things is worked overtime in this show.

It wouldn't matter -- it's a modest production after all -- except that it does make implicit claims for itself: claims to be mean and hard-hitting; our two fiends keep telling us how nasty they are, rather than just being. The comedy never has a chance to be about anything. It's too busy just trying to exist.

There's better news on the comedy front. This just in: The Simpsons were funny last Sunday. Funnier than they've been in nearly three years. Not as funny as they still are in all those infinitely re-watchable reruns, but comparably, with some of the old richness of character, structural complexity, and insouciant toughness. Pray that it wasn't a fluke.
