THEATRE REVIEW

Incident at Vichy Soulpepper Theatre

All My Sons Stratford Festival

Its one year late for his centenary, but this is proving to be a golden time for Arthur Miller. New York has seen acclaimed revivals of The Crucible and A View from the Bridge. Now, up here, we have Miller productions from Soulpepper and Stratford, both of them outstanding.

Soulpeppers choice is a rare one: Incident at Vichy, a 1964 play that was gripping when new and mesmerizing now. Alan Dilworth's production starts with the sound of a train: a terrifying noise because it's 1942 and were in the supposedly free section of occupied France. Ten men sit in a line on a bench in a featureless room, all of them brought there by police working, it seems, in perfect concord. None of them knows why they have been caught in this dragnet; they try to tell themselves that its a random checkup but they can't help fearing that they have been picked up because of the way they look, the way they sound. Most of them are Jewish. A few have heard of what is going on in the rest of Europe. They know what the trains are carrying, and where they're going.

The play is unashamedly schematic. Its first part is a discussion between those who know or guess the worst and those who are in denial, trying to believe that they will be all right if their papers are in order. The audience of course knows the terrible truth, but this hardly lessens the suspense. In fact it increases it; we want to know which if any of the detainees will be allowed to leave. Greater even than that is the overarching tension that comes from waiting for an axe to fall.

Some of the characters believe that such atrocities could never happen in a civilized country on a civilized continent; others know only too well that they could. The positions are familiar and baldly stated, and some of the cast find it hard to put conversational flesh on the bones. They speechify rather than talk. But the texture deepens as the play proceeds.

As an army major with qualms, Oliver Dennis reclaims the stereotype of "the good German," resisting his Gestapo controllers, then surrendering to them, then trying to salvage a fragment of self-respect. Kawa Ada brings a nervous bravado to an actor trying to convince himself, against all experience, that his enemies respect for culture will save him. Its a recurring theme of the play: men can love literature, love music, and still be killers.

Finally, we reach the two men at the end of the line: Stuart Hughes as an ex-army doctor who came out of hiding to get medicine for his wife, and Diego Matamoros

as an Austrian prince apparently arrested because of his accent and the one prisoner who seems certain to be released. There is nothing as simple as a conflict between realism and idealism.

The doctor certainly has no illusions, and Hughes' bitter practicality drives the play. The prince, having lived through the annexation of his homeland, also knows about the Nazis; indeed he believes that the world may have entered a new age of barbarism, spurred by demagogues - on whom his thoughts seem, in 2016, chillingly apposite. But he has yet to take in the full scope and immediacy of the horror; he is still, with the best and most benevolent of intentions, above it all' "Are you all Jews?" he asks his fellows, in the nicest possible way. Matamoros, courtly and gentle throughout, lets us see, feel, the princes mind expanding, from awareness of pathos to awareness of tragedy. The interplay of these two actors is superb; they restore Soulpepper to a level of performance that, once upon a time, we took for granted.

Millers best plays are exciting stories plus. All My Sons, his first success, is a real breath-suspender. Inspired by actual events it tells of Joe Keller who, during the war, allowed faulty airplane parts to leave his Ohio factory, and let his partner take the rap when twenty-one American pilots met their deaths.

Joe's own son Larry, also a pilot, may not have been one of the 21, but he, too, died in action. Now, in 1946, the Keller family awaits the arrival of Ann Deever, the jailed partner's daughter; she was Larrys fiancee and is now being courted by his brother Chris, a beefy idealist. Joe is cautiously in favour of the match; his wife Kate is passionately against it. She cannot bring herself to believe that Larry is dead. Gradually it becomes clear that for her the possibility of his death is bound up with the probability of Joes guilt. She's trying, in her own way, to protect her husband. People think her disturbed, but she may be the clearest-sighted person around.

Martha Henry's Stratford production is dazzlingly acted. Its also dazzlingly designed by Douglas Paraschuk, in the round and on a greensward setting with an especially fetching gazebo for the use of Joes family and friends. Though the court exonerated Joe, a shadow hangs over him, and he's at great pains to dispel it, from the minds of others and his own: playing unofficial scoutmaster with the neighbouring kids, the concerned husband, the all-American dad. Joseph Ziegler gets all the swagger and the neediness, his hands rapping his knees as he makes his points or waits for others to make theirs. Cornered, his geniality fades and desperation descends as he makes his excuses: he wanted to make money for his family, everybody was doing it, it was too late to call a halt.

It's maybe the plays fault that Joes final despair, unlike Willy Loman's in Miller's next play Death of a Salesman, doesnt appear inevitable; you feel he would have weaseled out of it somehow. If theres a tragic figure here it's Chris, whose insistence that his father face the truth blows up in his face. Tim Campbell, reprising the father-son relationship he played with Ziegler in Soulpepper's Salesman, is visually

and emotionally perfect. So is Lucy Peacock as Kate, by turns the haunted mother and the desperately practical hostess and wife.

The Deever family in this production are black, as are the Kellers neighbours. This, given the plays immediate postwar setting, is implausible in some ways but suggestive in others. It casts new light on why Joe got off while his partner went to jail. It also provides for an excellent performance from E.B. Smith as the doctor next door and an electric one from Michael Blake as Ann's brother who returns from visiting his imprisoned father and jolts the whole play into higher gear. As Ann herself, Sarah Afful is less satisfying, her emotions float somewhere above the given situation. The group scenes in this production are a revelation: all manner of sparks flying on the surface and angry currents crossing thrillingly underneath.

Incident at Vichy is at the Young Centre, Toronto, through June 25; All My Sons is at Tom Patterson Theatre, Stratford, through September 25.
