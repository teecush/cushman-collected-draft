Christopher Plummer in Barrymore doesn't put a foot wrong. That's the literal as well as the metaphorical truth. As Plummer portrays him, in what is virtually a one-man show, John Barrymore, the great American actor and lush, was also no end of a twinkle toes. He also sang very well, and though he was 59 in 1942, the year in which William Luce's play is set, he kept right up to date with the popular songs of the day. He comes before us hauling a drinks trolley while giving out with I Got a Gal in Kalamazoo, and moving about as gracefully and roguishly as a man in that position possibly could. Later on there will be more singing, all unaccompanied and impeccable, and even some dancing, likewise delightful.

There's also quite a lot of Shakespeare. The year 1942 was also the year of Barrymore's death, and the conceit of the play is that Jack, as he was commonly known, is making a last-minute attempt to reclaim his reputation as a classical actor, tarnished after long years spent degrading it in the movies while succumbing to the ravages of alcohol and alimony. His past includes legendary performances in two Shakespearean roles, Hamlet and Richard III, and he's making a desperate bid to revive the second of these, though the evening also includes numerous quotations from the first. (Plummer, who himself has played both of the roles in his time, strikes an especially fine yearning note on Hamlet's "what a piece of work is a man.")

Barrymore has rented a theatre, just like the one we're in, in order to go over Richard's lines, and hired a prompter to get him through them. In the first act his talent for digression, fuelled by his own rampant insecurities, prevents him getting much further than "now is the winter of our discontent," and he needs help even with that. After the intermission he jumps, unexplained, straight to the battle of Bosworth and "my kingdom for a horse." Having done the first half in civvies, he has now acquired a crown, a hump, a makeshift throne and a very tatty Plantagenet costume. I don't remember these accoutrements from when Plummer in Barrymore first came to town, 10 years ago, but whatever, they're a welcome change of visual pace, and Plummer plays the seedy comedy of them to the self-satirizing hilt.

There are various conventions available to first-person plays, especially first-person plays about entertainers, and this one switches unblushingly between them. Barrymore

talks to the prompter, who's kept in the wings throughout; he talks to himself; and he sometimes seems to be talking directly to us. (He even mentions us.) The main problem with Luce's script, though, is not that it's shameless but that it's shapeless. It has one moment that ought to be pivotal: Barrymore says piteously that he was once a good Richard, to which the prompter (the invisibly admirable John Plumpis) sternly and indeed promptly replies, "No, you were a great Richard." This would seem to be the cue for Jack to jack it up, and recover his former glories, or alternatively to jack it in, and surrender to desolation. In fact he just goes on favouring us with his reminiscences. The point, perhaps, is that he's unsalvageable, but he's no more or less so at the evening's end than he was at its beginning, which is dramatically unsatisfying. He doesn't learn or acknowledge anything that he doesn't already know and neither, anecdotes apart, do we.

That's not to deny that the anecdotes can be very entertaining, especially as retailed by Plummer with infallible wit, cheek and timing. He recites limericks, about drink or women. He tells showbiz stories, some of which -- like the famous Sam Goldwyn one about turning lesbians into Americans -- have been dragged in from the common store. He boasts about his alcoholic capacity and his marital incapacity: four wives, all aspiring actresses, all the occasion of barbed one-liners (though Elaine Barrie, the last and most notorious Mrs. Barrymore, gets off remarkably unscathed). He talks about his comparatively late awakening to serious theatrical ambition, about his daunting actor father and his daunting actor siblings: Lionel, who revelled in ham and Hollywood, and Ethel, who stuck sternly to the theatre.

Plummer knits the whole together into a portrait of rueful bravado. There's a powerful extra ingredient in the contrast between the subject, self-wasted at 59, and his impersonator, some 20 years older and in his prime, in fact probably better than ever. It was all the more poignant for those of us who came directly to it from the memorial celebration of Peter Donaldson, who was Plummer's natural successor among Canadian Shakespeareans and who died even younger than Barrymore; clips of Plummer and Donaldson together in Stratford's Caesar and Cleopatra took on extraordinary resonance.

What Plummer offers us in Barrymore isn't great acting; the material doesn't allow it. But it's the irresistible work of a star who's also a craftsman. He pays implicit tribute to Barrymore as both man and legend, makes us like him, lets us see flickers of past genius amid a roaring fire of congeniality. He's super-enjoyable.
