Some thinkers in the 18th century conceived of the universe as a gigantic timepiece, and God as the mechanic in charge. In his play The Clockmaker, Stephen Massicotte takes off from this idea, to produce something that starts out irritating and ends up impressive.

Perhaps in homage to his philosophical heritage, Massicotte has given his play a Germanic setting and ambience. His central character is called Heinrich Mann, though with no apparent reference to Thomas Mann's equally smart brother, and is, as advertised, a timid but expert maker and refurbisher of clocks. When we first meet him he's been summoned to a government office for a meeting that it would be nice, but impossible, to avoid describing as Kafkaesque. His interviewer goes by the name of Monsieur Pierre, which makes the continental waters even muddier, and he starts out modestly enough, wrongfooting Heinrich over how he feels about the chair he's sitting in and having him fill in apparently meaningless forms that are duly consigned to the filing cabinet. That's just at the first appointment. At the second, he escalates the treatment, informing our hero that he is under surveillance for crimes he has not yet committed but might in the future. He even, with unusual liberality, suggests what these crimes might be. One is murder. Another is adultery.

These strike a couple of fearful chords with Heinrich, since he's recently encountered a new customer: from all appearances, his only customer. This is Frieda, a young woman with a badly damaged clock that should be either repaired or abandoned, and a marriage answering to the same description. Between the virginal Heinrich and the worldlier but terrorized Frieda, an understanding begins to take shape.

So far, so average. The interrogation scenes seem derivative, the incipiently romantic ones sweet but also -- to use the appropriate timepiece term -- second-hand. Then the play starts to gain traction. We follow Frieda home and behold her abusive husband, Adolphus, who alternates between beating physically up on her and mentally up on himself, the two kinds of violence being equally abrupt and almost equally alarming. There's even a scene in which Heinrich, empowered by love, confronts Adolphus, only to learn the sad truth that, whatever the story books may say, when you stand up to bullies, they do not invariably run away. Meanwhile--and I do mean meanwhile--there has been a separate series of scenes between Heinrich and Frieda, that seem to be taking place either in dreams or in an alternate universe. In these, Frieda prefers to be called Anna and is on her way home bearing a loaf of bread. (Heinrich, however, has not turned into a baker.) They show no awareness of their other encounters, and besides, the weather's different. But they are recognizably the same people, with much that was unspoken between them in their workaday lives coming joyfully into the open. They even make their way to the site of Heinrich's bureaucratic torments; this enables the play's designer to pull off a minor coup, and the playwright a major one. The office turns out to be housed in a clock tower, which, of course, finds Heinrich on his home ground. The play approaches its climax amidst a joyous clanging of symbols.

The double time scheme, or whatever it is, passes from being baffling-going-on-infuriating to being satisfying and even enlightening. Time, for a moment, stands still, and the play becomes a meditation on the joys and terrors of remembering things. (Most "time" plays are in fact "memory" plays.) Both Heinrich and Frieda have to decide how much future torment is worth the memory of past happiness, and we share their dilemma because we have come to care about them. I don't want to give away the ending, if only because I'm not sure I understand it, but on the way we have a slant on the afterlife, which may owe something to Bernard Shaw (the Don Juan in Hell scene in Man and Superman) and Thornton Wilder (the graveyard scene in Our Town) but is still quirky and individual. The moral might be that no one has the right to play God, including God.

The play was first staged at the Alberta PlayRites festival, and has been remounted here, very well, by its Calgary director, Bob White, who has given it weight while -- mostly -- avoiding heaviness. Christian Goutsis repeats his original role as Heinrich; he's initially charming and ultimately touching, but he does keep hitting the same piping note between times. The other actors are Toronto regulars. Damien Atkins is a suave functionary, at his best when admitting to unaccustomed discomfiture. The remaining two performances are stellar. Kevin Bundy, not an actor I usually associate with menace, has tremendous presence as Adolphus, whether letting himself go or holding himself in, as he does in a long sequence of silent motionless bewilderment at the end. As Frieda/ Anna, Claire Calnan is magnificently strong and clear in every moment, authoritative as an actress even when her character is jelly. Scott Reid's set is dominated by an imposing archway that gives it the air of a vintage railway station; but it's also remarkably versatile. A good start to the Tarragon's new season (especially with Through the Leaves playing concurrently in the Extra Space) and to Toronto's.
