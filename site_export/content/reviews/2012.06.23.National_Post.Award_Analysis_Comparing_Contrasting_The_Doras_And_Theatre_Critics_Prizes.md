Its prize-giving time in Toronto theatre again, and even if it's not twice as exciting as it used to be, at least it's twice as populous. On Monday, the long-established Dora awards will be announced and presented. But this is also the second successive season for which the citys theatre critics have organized their own awards, whose recipients were released to a breathlessly expectant world at the end of last month. Our choices - five of us hacks argued it out, three from the dailies, two from weeklies - overlapped somewhat with those of the Dora nominators, but not as much as you might expect. Some of their choices make me roll my eyes, but I daresay some of ours make them - whoever they are - do the same. Anyway, some thoughts and forecasts on the Dora hopefuls in what, by general consent, has been an unusually strong Toronto season.

It hasnt been a crowded time for great new plays, but then no time is. The Doras hem themselves in by confining their outstanding new play category to plays that have received their world premieres in Toronto. The equivalent critical prize is for best Canadian play, which at least meant we could give serious consideration to Carole Frechette's The Small Room at the Top of the Stairs (an import from Quebec) even though we eventually lighted on Ins Choi's very Torontonian Kims Convenience. This is also on the Dora list, and the smart money must be on it to win, as its local without being parochial; it puts the everyday city recognizably onstage while being funny and touching in its own right. Among its competitors, Pamela Mala Sinha's Crash, while distinguished, is limited by being a monologue (maybe that should be a separate category - there are certainly enough of them every year); Ajax and Little Iliad was an oddball double bill, only half of which worked; and, going to the opposite extreme of scale, the choice of Luminato's One Thousand and One Nights is baffling. An international rather than a homegrown show, and more notable as a staging than as a script, it might have been expected to turn up, if anywhere, among the best touring productions. That it doesnt, while the very Canadian Ride the Cyclone does, suggests the rules are getting tangled. One notable feature of this years Dora picks for new play: not one of them is by an established playwright. Cyclone is the work of comparative newcomers as well, which is encouraging.

The Cyclone's regional origins also exclude it from the Dora lists for outstanding new musical for which, if admitted, it would surely have been a shoo-in; although lumping musicals and operas together, as the Doras do in all their musical categories, does muddy the waters somewhat. I wasn't even one of Cyclones greatest fans, but I cant see that it had any serious competition, given the inadmissability of the American Caroline or Change. The latter, though, is up for outstanding musical production (concentrate hard; there is a difference) and I can't see any defensible way it could lose. It won the equivalent critics' award; we also handed out prizes to two of its cast, Arlene Duncan (best actress in a musical) and Deborah Hay (best supporting actress in same.) Our enthusiasm, though, is nothing to that of the Doras jurors, who have handed four out of five nominations for outstanding female performance in a musical to ladies from that cast and two out of five for outstanding male performance to its gentlemen. One of the latter, Michael Levinson, is a very young gentleman indeed, and if he wins, he should be cheered for more than sentimental reasons; his was simply, singing included, one of the best child performances Ive ever seen.

Paul Sun-Hyung Lee, playing Kim of convenience fame, will likely take the principal male in a play award for the same good reasons that the play itself will win. His main competitors, I'd guess, are Nigel Shawn Williams and Kevin Hanchard, both for performances in Topdog Underdog that were fine when they gave them at the Shaw Festival and, by all accounts, even finer when they gave them again in Toronto. Also nominated are another Shavian, Jim Mezon, a wonderful actor who in Red gave, to my mind, one of his less wonderful performances; and Michael Healey, who in Clybourne Park, gave possibly his most wonderful performance - it certainly extended him. Anyway, it's good to see that show recognized for something; its the best American play to reach us in years, but the Doras have no mechanism for acknowledging plays from away. The critics introduced one this year; it went to The Golden Dragon by everyones favourite German, Roland Schimmelpfennig.

As for outstanding principal female: The critics' choice was Nicole Underhay (in The Small Room), and I'd also root for her among the Dora gals, despite competition from Pamela Sinha (but see my earlier caveat regarding monologues) and Maev Beaty. The other nominations I dont understand; one of them is for a performance that came close to being downright bad. The most shameful omission from the list is Megan Follows, who was amazing in The Penelopiad. Now, you might claim that she, along with all the other ladies in that cast, has been recognized, because theres a category labelled outstanding performance in a supporting role/ensemble, and the ensembles for both The Penelopiad and The Golden Dragon have been listed there. It's a cop-out. Since ensemble" means the entire cast, just whom are they meant to be supporting? A separate "ensemble" category might make sense; the current arrangement just puts an additional squeeze on a box that already has to accommodate performances straight and musical, male and female. The three individuals who made it this year are Fiona Reid (White Biting Dog), Jeff Lillico (The Glass Menagerie), and Ryan Kelly (The Normal Heart): all deserving cases, but there are so many others.

Finally, there's outstanding production of a play or musical (one award) and theres outstanding direction (two awards, one for each genre). As you might expect, theres a lot of overlap. In fact, the only production not nominated for direction is War Horse. The original director, Marianne Elliott, didn't travel with it, but it was obviously still her staging. And some staging it is, getting additional nods in the sound, costume and choreography departments. It may very well gallop home with all of them. It depends on how tender towards the commercial theatre (it's a Mirvish show here) the judges feel this year. Theyve been known to swing both ways. There are also lighting and sound nominations for The Small Room at the Top of the Stairs, as well there should be. It should also be up for best production and best direction, but it isnt. The director was Weyni Mengesha, also responsible for Kim's Convenience. You'd think the Doras, which love a multiple nominee, would have noticed.
