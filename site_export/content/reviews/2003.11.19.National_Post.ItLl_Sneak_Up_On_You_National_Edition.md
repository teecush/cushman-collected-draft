TEQUILA VAMPIRE MATINEE

Theatre Passe Muraille, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - There are a lot of elements jostling rudely for attention in the title Tequila Vampire Matinee, and so there are -- too many, in fact -- in the work itself, which I need hardly say is a musical. It also stands heavily indebted to some more celebrated tuners: Some of the debts are built into the structure and explicitly acknowledged (I Pagliacci) and others are not (The Rocky Horror Show). Confused and derivative though it is, it still manages to have its own flavour. It is an original, and to be welcomed.

Its written -- book, music, lyrics, kit and caboodle -- by Kevin Quain, who is also in it. Quain is best known (though frankly not by me) as a kind of underground troubadour, operating mainly out of the Cameron House on Queen Street. This is his theatrical debut piece, and I think he means it as a myth.

At any rate, its impossible to work out when the story is meant to be happening. Most of it unfolds in a saloon, in a Western town that everybody calls San Something-or-other, and is probably somewhere near the Mexican border. (That's where the Tequila comes in.) The setting has all the trappings of a dime novel from the 19th century. But most of the characters belong to a vagabond theatrical troupe, one of whom has her starry eyes set on the bright lights of Las Vegas, which didn't have any lights until the 1950s.

Go figure; the show leaves you plenty of time. It starts with a puppet prologue, performed by Punch and Judy. I dont know if these figures, originally Italian, have ever been as popular in Canada as they were on the English beaches of my childhood, but they must have some currency, or Quain wouldnt have used them. So you'll probably remember that Punch has a big nose and a bigger stick, the latter of which he uses to belabour Judy, who loves him, until she's senseless. Lest you should think Punch a mere misogynist, I must point out that he administers the same treatment to everyone else, chortling, in the authorized version, thats the way to do it" as he does it. Here, though, we only get Punch and wife. The excuse for their presence is that the chief of the itinerant actors, known as Big Daddy, is a puppeteer, among other things, and that Punch is his favourite glove (though he actually has all the debonair charm of Ed the Sock). In fact, Big Daddy identifies.

And the reason for this is that he is in love with, or at least very possessive of, Sugar Plum, the blond ingenue who dreams of Vegas. He is tormented by the thought that she might leave him. All this is revealed in a roadside episode that takes place before a front cloth, and very long and very cramped the scene is. But then the cloth disappears, the bar is revealed, and with a full stage at its disposal the show begins to go, if not with a swing, then with an attractively crooked lope.

The saloon is owned and run by a square-jawed young man called Ramon, and both he and it are on their uppers. Ramon sings, very well, about leading a dogs life, and anyone old and lucky enough to remember a hit song -- or monologue to music -- called Life Gits Tee'jus, Don' It? will recognize the mood, and the idiom, immediately. Big Daddy and company descend on the place (I mean that pretty much literally), and Big Daddy, who seems to have gone to the same theatrical school as the King and the Duke who caused such problems to Huck Finn, sweet-and-fast-talks Ramon into letting him and his strollers use the place as their theatre.

Also in the company are Lulu, who is a lesbian, and Twitch, who is a pyromaniac, though neither of these attributes play as significant a part in the action as we are led to believe they will. Its more important that Ramon falls for Sugar Plum, leading her to realize that there may be more to life than getting beaten up by her old man. Big Daddy, getting wind of this, suffers all the tortures of jealousy. After singing a lament of his own, to a tune strongly resembling Help Me Make It Through the Night, he puts on a wind-up record of Veste la giubba to emphasize the point.

Unfortunately, Big Daddy has been presented as such a louse that there is no future, on even the most farcical level, in having us root for him as a broken-hearted clown. Its no use trying to persuade us that Mr. Punch has feelings. This reduces the impact of the set-piece scene.

The show goes on (that's where the Matinee comes in) and it proves not to be the expected burlesque bill but a rather expert pastiche of classic bedroom farce, part Moliere, part commedia dell'arte. It also parallels the real-life situations of the characters performing it. Its rich and its clever, though not as rich or clever as it would obviously like, and indeed needs, to be.

It is something, though, and it's funny, as the show frequently is, often at the times you least expect it, which of course are the best times. Quain has written a kind of Brechtian country score, down-home music that suggests home is about to be repossessed by the bank. And if his lyrics have no special distinction, plus the usual quotient of bum rhymes, at least they don't sound as if theyre trying to be clever.

Ted Dykstras direction is lethargic, but that, once we're past the preparatory stuff, doesn't matter, and could even be intentional. A laid-back musical may sound like an oxymoron, but this could be one of a kind. The choreographer, Dawn Naser, hasnt come up with any notable steps but shes arranged some entertaining groupings.

The cast are excellent. J.D. Nicholsen's Big Daddy is powerful if, as already indicated, irremediably unsympathetic. Amy Rutherford's Sugar Plum is a delight, popping her eyes and fluttering her lashes like one of Fiona Reids demurer heroines stripped of her sophistication. Brendan Walls Ramon has looks, humour and a voice. As Lulu, Shelley Simester reveals herself a mistress of the lost art of deafening an audience in song without artificial aids, and as Twitch, a shaven-headed buffoon accurately described as "the reason children are afraid of clowns, Stephen Sparks is sensationally deft, bodily, facially and vocally. That's the way to do it.

Quain himself appears as Maestro, a pianist, and Dragoslav Tanaskovic is Vladimir, a bass player. Both have vestigial parts in the action, which they discharge with the inimitably out- of-it quality peculiar to acting musicians. Otherwise, they're the band. As to where the Vampires come in; they don't, hardly, and when they do it's ridiculous. But not to worry: As Big Daddy and Co. would doubtless say to an unruly audience: What do you want? Blood?"
