THEATRE REVIEW

ORSONS SHADOW

Theatre Passe Muraille, Toronto

In 1960, Laurence Olivier appeared at the Royal Court Theatre in London in Rhinoceros, a play by Eugene Ionesco, directed by Orson Welles. Hows that for a lineup. Add to it that Olivier's leading lady was Joan Plowright, his imminently future wife. This confluence of personalities is the subject of Orson's Shadow, a play by Austin Pendleton who is, in rough order of distinction, an American actor, director and playwright.

Ive an interest to declare. I could be the only person in this plays Toronto audience who saw that production of Rhinoceros, though not at the Court; I caught the subsequent West End run, with Plowright's role taken over by the then-lesser-known Maggie Smith. It was the first time I ever saw Olivier onstage, and the main thing I remember of his performance is its beguiling and, I later discovered, atypical unassumingness; he was playing a shy man and didn't care who knew it. Of Welles production I remember little, though I recall that it was widely criticized for being tricksy and heavy-handed. So I have my own reasons for finding Orsons Shadow fascinating. I imagine, though, that my fascination will be shared by any who have heard of and care about these people, though even they will have to make allowances. This is a play by the stage-struck for the stagestruck, and there are some odd things about it, starting with the title.

I don't know what it means. Is Orson's shadow Welles himself, who at this stage was widely held to be the mere ghost of the boy-genius who directed Citizen Kane? Not corporeally of course; when we first meet this plays Welles, hes just come offstage from playing Falstaff, without padding. Is everyone in the play meant to be in Orson's shadow? That doesn't make sense, since he isnt the central figure; nobody is. Maybe its meant to refer to the remaining leading character, who is Kenneth Tynan, British theatre critic, master stylist and hero-worshipper. Probably his two greatest heroes were Welles and Olivier, and this play presents Tynan as the catalyst who made this production possible. In the first scene, he visits Welles in a Dublin theatre and nervously castigates him for wasting his talents on a cobbled version of Shakespeare. (It's Chimes at Midnight, the compressed Henry IV that afterward became Welles' last near-masterpiece of a movie.) Tynan mentions that Olivier is looking for a director for Rhinoceros, and offers to suggest Welles. Welles, who has issues with Olivier, grudgingly agrees. So off hops Tynan to London, to make the proposition which Olivier, equally grudgingly and insecurely, accepts, with some prompting from the soon-to-be Lady Olivier. Tynan has his own agenda; he wants to be Oliviers dramaturg when Olivier becomes director of the new National Theatre.

Little of this holds water historically. In 1959, when these conversations are set, the National was four years in the future; nobody knew who its director would be or even if it would happen. Tynan did indeed buck for, and obtained, the National Theatre job but not until 1962, immediately after giving Olivier a notoriously bad review. He would have had nothing to do with setting up Rhinceros, a play he disliked; he already knew Olivier and his previous crazy-but-beautiful wife Vivien Leigh quite well, though the play presents him as a stuttering stranger to them; and he certainly wouldnt have hung around rehearsals as he does here. If he had done that, he wouldn't have reviewed the show, which he later did; like me, he saw the transfer. He defended Welles, said Olivier was great but wasting his time, and panned the play, condemning its satire on all varieties of conformity for not being politically specific enough. Tynan was the greatest of theatre critics, but sometimes he wore blinkers.

Does all this matter? It does because the play depends on celebrity appeal; it works in quotes and anecdotes from all over its characters' lives, but even at the level of gossip it ought to get its facts right. Its not as if theres any universal meaning here. I suppose the play may be said to be about self-destructiveness, with Welles eating and Tynan smoking

their respective ways to premature death. But that doesn't wash for Olivier, who may eventually have been laid low by his workload at the National but is here presented as a survivor, feigning humility but afraid only of his not-quite-ex wife.

Pendleton knows, though, what makes theatre and theatricals tick; and if his two set-up scenes drag a bit, the rehearsal that makes up the second act has a persuasive swing and crackle that Rona Waddington's production captures very well. The performances start from accurate impersonation and move outwards. Steve Ross Welles is physically and vocally massive, with a quicksilver turn of wit. As Tynan, Christopher Stanton hints at the brilliance while capturing the insecurity that can nag at a man in the presence of those he most admires. Paul Eves Olivier is maybe the cleverest, an obsequious pussycat in the face of his genius director but knowing exactly how to work any room. (He tells Tynan he can have the dramaturg job but only on the undertstanding that every recommendation he makes will be disregarded. Legend has it that that is what actually happened.) Janet Porter, though she makes no attempt at Plowright's throaty north-country accent, makes her a warm and supportive presence, and Geoff Scovell is perky as Welles' apocryphal Irish assistant who surprisingly gets away with calling Olivier Larry. The only false note comes from Camilla Scott, who plays Vivien Leigh as just any old West Ender isnstead of what she was: an exotically unplaceable wits ender.
