I have two abiding memories of the 2000 production of Medea at the Stratford Festival. One is of the believability of Seana McKennas Medea and Scott Wentworths Jason as a couple in or after the last stages of marital breakdown, competing in blame and resentment. The other is of the way in which the play, otherwise done as naturalistically as Athenian tragedy will allow, began and ended with fragments of the original Greek and with the actors in the traditional tragic masks. We were taken on a journey from then to now and back again.

Restaged in Toronto with the same two leading actors, Miles Potter's production retains this double focus. We get both immediacy and immensity, both hit and myth. Within this giant pendulum swing, there are the personal oscillations, emotional and technical, of McKenna's Medea, by turns wronged refugee wife, silky sorceress, sardonic commentator and unappeasable fury. Medea, a kings daughter, avenges herself on the husband whos dumped her in favour of a younger princess, by having her supplanter and her father burned alive, and then by killing her own and Jason's two sons. She exults in the first deed; feels compunction but no guilt over the second. And certainly in Robinson Jeffers' domesticated but still resonant adaptation of Euripides original, she enjoys telling us, via the chorus, just how angry, clever and desperate she is and how appalling and contemptible are all men. It sometimes feels that its the actress as much as the character who's doing the enjoying, but she's so adept at changing gears, at stopping on the comic brink and plunging instantly back into the inferno, that it doesnt matter. Wentworths Jason, his voice a ringing sneer, hardly whitewashes Jason, but he makes his case as forcefully as she makes hers. (And Medea must have been hell to live with.) At the end, with his mask clapped on, he's the one who's trapped in tragedy. She gets away.

Michael Spencer-Davis and Nigel Bennett give affable support as, respectively, a confessedly weak king and one whos not quite strong enough. As the nurse who graduates from home help to atrocity reporter, a black-clad Patricia Connolly bites into every word, whether watchful or appalled, that she answers. If the production is slightly less powerful in a big house than it was at the Tom Patterson eight years ago, thats partly due to the upping of the chorus from three ladies to five. Then, they could be distinct people who sometimes merged; now, despite distinguished personal resumes (from Shaw, Stratford and elsewhere) they're the usual uncertain bunch, not quite individuals, not quite a collective. Until, that is, near the end, which is when the production shows its hand and clutches them within it. They beat on the doors of Peter Hartwell's imposing new set, behind which Medea is committing double filicide. They feel inexorable tragedy sweeping down, and they make us feel it. Flames spring up from altars to bar Jasons way, and to tell him and us that magic is at work and, beyond that, fate. Michael Beckers music and Peter McBoyle's sound design, both thrilling, deliver the aural coup de grace. The verbal comes from McKenna, ineluctable and unreachable. You already know the end.

It's worth, though, going beyond the end. Medea escaped to Athens, courtesy of King Aegeus to whom she'd promised release from sterility. She came through and he had a son, Theseus, whose second wife Phaedra became a tragic heroine on her own account, falling in love with her stepson and causing both his death and her own. And Phaedra is a role that Connolly played at Stratford two decades ago and that McKenna is to play there this year. Myth is continuous. And inescapable. - Medea runs through Feb. 9 at the Canon Theatre. For more information, call 416-872-1212.
