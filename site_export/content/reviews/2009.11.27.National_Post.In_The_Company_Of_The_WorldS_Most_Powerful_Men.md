STUFF HAPPENS

Royal Alexandra Theatre, Toronto

---

Studio 180's production of Stuff Happens survives transfer to the big house with impact intact, force and lucidity undiminished. There are two major cast changes, both with a clarifying effect. Michael Healey plays George W. Bush, going easy on the impersonator mannerisms while supplying a few of his own, mainly in the sphere of dangerous thick-browed affability. Healey's Bush, infuriatingly if courteously opaque to all who have to deal with him, emerges here as shrewder and more controlling than his predecessor's: an expert at letting himself be talked into courses on which he's already decided while neutering opposition. Whatever else he may be, this Dubya's no fool.

Karen Robinson's Condoleeza Rice is a complementary study in deviousness, hard-shelled as well as hard-centred, putting up walls where her boss prefers to smile deceptively through windows. Robinson has a megaphonic delivery that can sometimes be disconcerting, but her impersonality -- a kind of loud self-effacement -- explains why her opponents and questioners never stand a chance. She is her job; and they can't get through that.

David Hare's play traces the build-up to the Iraq War, from 9/11 to the parachuted president's premature announcement of victory, with some pungent last words on how wrong things have gone since. The focus is mainly on the U.S. inner circle, with side-trips to the UN and to a harried Tony Blair and team in London. I'm one of those who believes Hare to be more naturally a journalist than a playwright, but a journalist with a strong sense of theatrical effect. So this play, in which history supplies the plot and he supplies (most of ) the dialogue, finds him on his strongest ground. He supplies an immense amount of information, at high but digestible speed, while shaping a gripping and persuasive main action that's mainly a series of cabinet battles, official and otherwise. Joel Greenberg's staging is a shifting parade of suits in chairs. The chairs are the office kind, on wheels. They can be arrayed in triple formation to suggest a press briefing at the White House, then turned in an instant to face across the Atlantic for question time in the House of Commons.

The play's good guy, or its nearest equivalent, is Colin Powell, who goes through the action feeling the ground slipping beneath his feet while trying to persuade himself that it isn't. The sense of his arguments sometimes get swamped, in Nigel Shawn Williams' performance, by undirected emotion. But he presents a powerful, sympathetic figure whose final volte-face is almost comprehensible; he's been ground down. Andrew Gillies offers an equally winning, and more controlled, Tony Blair, a blend of career politician and career pedant. (As vocal impersonation, his performance is rather flattering.) I'd remembered the attack dogs, Donald Rumsfeld and Dick Cheney, as having more stage time than they actually do: a tribute to them that they make such vivid impressions so quickly. David Fox's Rumsfeld moves and speaks in snake-like parabolas, pulling out paradoxes like a triumphant professor. Hardee T. Lineham's Cheney, by contrast, is a blunt instrument, leading with his bullet head. The supporting roles include a memorably imperturbable Hans Blix from Guy Bannerman.

As before, I was much struck by the last speech, spoken by an Iraqi exile (Sam Kalileh), which has the effect of turning the rest of the play on its head. His is virtually the only voice we hear from the land that everyone else has merely been talking about, and what he offers is a lament for a country crucified (his word) both by its rulers and by the invaders. It balances an introductory tirade from an American journalist (Paul Essiembre) who says, if I may recycle my earlier review, that a dictator was removed, and that arguing about the character and motives of those who removed him -- which is what the play itself proceeds to do -- is moralizing self-indulgence. These two speeches, from opposite political perspectives, cast an ironic light on what goes between them that must have been intended, but not perhaps in quite the way that it actually plays out. There's a sense in which the play ends just when it's getting really interesting; and events since last March when this production first appeared, let alone since 2004 when the play opened in London, have distanced it still further. It's a history play now in more ways than one. But the history's exciting, and its consequences aren't going away.
