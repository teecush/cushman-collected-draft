THEATRE REVIEW

The Realistic Joneses Tarragon Theatre

In Will Eno's The Realistic Joneses, American family drama meets, explosively, that other dear old thing, the Theatre of the Absurd. In Eugene Ionesco's The Bald Soprano, the primary absurdist play, we met two married couples who spoke exclusively in polite cliches to illustrate the futility of language and hence of what we used to call bourgeois existence. One of those couples was named Smith, as if to say "how average can you get?" Eno's play does Ionesco one better. It also gives us two couples, but both of them are named Jones. It may be worth noting that, once this point has been established in the play's first scene, it is never mentioned again.

Bob and Jennifer Jones are a 40-ish couple, living in a small town with a nice view of the mountains. John and Pony Jones are a very slightly younger couple who have just moved into a neighbouring house, and who kick the play off by paying a neighbourly visit. "We know that house," says Bob, adding as one conversationally might, "someone else used to live there." "Wow" says John, "who knew the place had such an interesting history." It does not seem at this point that he is being sarcastic, though he certainly will be later.

Language in this play is very slippery. What distinguishes Eno from Ionesco is that his characters are in on the joke, while dizzyingly aware that the joke is on them. John has found a website that offers transcripts of any audio-book, prompting Jennifer, the most grounded of the four, to ask "wouldn't that just be the book?" John also says "We communicate pretty well, even without words" to which skeptical Bob replies "Yeah?" Failure of communication is a staple of modern drama, but Eno takes it to a more immediately recognizable plane than Beckett or even Pinter. Compared to their characters, his Joneses are indeed realistic. They come with biographies. They also come with medical conditions, the men anyway. Bob and John both suffer from a (fictitious) neurological disease. It's why they're neighbours; they've moved to be near the doctor who specializes in their complaint and who gave his name to it. It apparently causes seizures, blackouts, temporary blindness and forgetting words. Bob mostly accepts his situation, even exploits it, while wanting to know as few of the details as possible; he suggests that Jennifer keep his doctor's appointment for him and bring him back a report. John is nobody's stoic; he rages and plays word-and-mind games. Jennifer seems to be a fine caregiver, though she chafes in the role; Pony, who shrieks at the sight of a dead squirrel, could hardly be less suited to it.

There is some flirtation across the marriage lines but it's inconclusive. This isn't one of those American plays in which family members yell at one another while pulling skeletons out of cupboards, the plays (The Humans is the current exhibit) that win awards and have profitable Broadway runs and that turn up in Canada some five years later, making us wonder what all the fuss was about. The Realistic Joneses did play on Broadway but its honesty about relationships, coupled with its paucity of plot, must have made audiences uncomfortable.

The night I saw it, there were walkouts. That production, with a starry cast, was good, but the Tarragon one, directed by Richard Rose, is better. It's certainly much funnier, the four actors feast on the wrong-footing and secondguessing with which these Joneses strive to keep up with one another. Susan Coyne as Jennifer begins with good humour and common sense; the latter remains a constant but, increasingly, she burns underneath. Tom Barnett as Bob shows, with alarming conviction, a man declining into a slug. Jenny Young is a fine neurotic Pony. Patrick McManus as John is the play's dynamo, insecurity fuelling aggression. All four discharge their parts with crack-shot timing but McManus has the hottest ammunition.

Charlotte Dean's set revolves elegantly between one couple's deck and the other's kitchen, with a side-trip to the local grocery store. Thomas Ryder Payne has created a soundscape that alternately lulls and frightens, while Graeme S. Thomson's lighting rises to virtuosity in a backyard encounter involving the two men and some unsettling blackouts. The best scenes, though, are the first and last, where all four Joneses get to interact. The first finds them uneasily sounding one another out; in the last they're replete and dangerously calm. "I don't think anything good is going to happen to us," says Bob. "But, you know, what are you going to do."
