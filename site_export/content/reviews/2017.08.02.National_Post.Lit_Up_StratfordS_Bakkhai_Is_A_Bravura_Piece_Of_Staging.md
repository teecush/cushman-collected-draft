THEATRE REVIEW

Bakkhai Tom

Patterson Theatre, Stratford

For the past few Stratford seasons, Jillian Keiley has seemed like a director in search of a project.

This year, she has found the right one. Her production of Bakkhai - the Greek tragedy whose title is more usually transliterated as Bacchae - is a bravura piece of staging, lit up by some exceptional performances.

Euripides'play, by common consent his greatest, was also his last (or last but one). It premiered in old Athens in 406 BC, following the death of its aged, exiled author. It won him a posthumous prize at the Theatre of Dionysus - an ironic achievement considering that Dionysus himself, aka Bacchus, god of wine and other things, is the plays dominating character, and not exactly a sympathetic one. Begotten by Zeus and a mortal woman, whom Zeuswife promptly dispatched with one of her husband's thunderbolts, he is only half a god - or, as this translation by the Canadian poet Anne Carson repeatedly and suggestively insists, a "daimon."

He is still powerful enough to wreak vengeance on the people of Thebes, his mother's hometown, who have denied his divinity.

He arrives there, in mortal disguise, attended by a troop of female devotees from Asia, who function both as the plays chorus and as its collective title character. They intoxicate - literally in this production - the women of the city and have them heading, again literally, for the hills. The Theban women too have become the daimons disciples.

The Theban men mostly haven't, least of all their authoritarian king, Pentheus.

He tries to lock up the supposed prophet of Dionysus, who is in fact Dionysus himself, but the prison walls fall in. I am a god, Dionysus announces. "I'll show him," and does.

Trading on Pentheusprurience, he persuades him to put on female clothing, in order to get a close-up view of what his countrywomen may be up to in the mountains. Once Pentheus is there, Dionysus alerts the women to his presence, and they tear him to pieces. Leading the pack is Pentheusown mother, Agave.

Many years ago in London, there was a top-class amateur production of The Bacchae that remains the most powerful staging I have seen, of this or any Greek tragedy.

As Pentheus went to his doom, I can still see and hear the chorus giving out with a percussive chant of Let ... justice ... walk the earth, pounding out the beat with their feet. The scene doesn't have the same vocal or visual impact in Keiley's more elaborate production, but hers is the best version of the play Ive seen since. It falls only slightly short of Stratfords thrilling Elektra (another Carson translation) of a few seasons ago. Like its chosen text, it's a canny blend of the earthy and the unearthly.

Shawn Kerwin's costumes are modern where it counts: in establishing the hierarchy and identities of Pentheus and his subordinates. Dionysus looks timeless, as gods and hippies are timeless: ageless too.

The production is also a triumph of imaginative casting. Mac Fyfe, a mainstay of VideoCabaret, but a virtual newcomer to Stratford, plays Dionysus; Pentheus is Gordon S. Miller, a still-young Festival favourite, previously confined to subordinate and mainly comic roles. Fyfe makes a glass-smooth, playful, but still implacable demigod, whose sexual ambiguity is a taunt in itself, especially potent when he tempts and transforms Pentheus from uptight king to drag queen. Two-and-a-half millennia old, this is one of the most extraordinary scenes in world drama, brilliantly done here. Before his cross-dressing, undertaken with a hypnotic blend of fear and fascination, Millers Pentheus has been one of natures bureaucrats, young and raw and rigid. He is the perfect victim perfectly performed, except on the few occasions when he shouts.

And then there's Lucy Peacock's Agave, a late arrival in Euripides but here given a silent establishing scene in which, tipsy on divine wine, she happily lets herself be whisked away to Mount Cithaeron (a fatal place whatever tragedy youre in).

When she returns shes still happy, cradling what she believes to be the head of a lion but is actually that of her son. Peacock, dreamy and smiling for the agonizingly long time her delusion endures, is superb. When she is forced awake she has to fall back on standardized emoting, but it may well be that there is a point at which the unendurable becomes un-actable.

Nigel Bennett gives sympathetic support as her father Cadmus. Earlier he shares a sad-funny scene with Graham Abbey's Tiresias, the prophet whose role in play after play is to tell things to kings that they don't want to hear. The virtuoso bearer of bad tidings is Andre Morin as the servant who, horror-struck but lucid, moved but not involved, narrates Pentheusgruesome fate to the chorus, here reverting to their traditional function of asking what the news is and tut-tutting when its bad - as of course it always is.

Earlier on, these seven ladies against Thebes have been more active, singing and dancing and performing one routine that's explicitly orgasmic if not orgiastic.

As it happens, and if I may trust translations, the ode they're chanting at the time makes no mention of sexual action, lesbian or otherwise. Pentheus suspects, wrongly, that the Theban women are off in "hidden nooks ... serving the lusts of men," but the chorus are not those women, though its admittedly easy to get them confused.

In the program Keiley says that she started by trying to make the play into a feminist sex-positive statement but that Carson persuaded her she was wrong. I dont see how you'd make any kind of positive statement from a play whose climax is a mother dismembering her son; anyway Keiley settled, rightly, for the play being openended or multi-faceted. This might get her into a fight with another program note that claims "this is the most political play you will see this year" (or conceivably any other year) and that it's all about men trying, and failing, to control womens bodies.

In fact the central tension of the play isnt between men and women; it's between gods and humans. Dionysus takes his revenge on all the Thebans, men and women alike.

Olympian theology has always seemed to me to make a lot of sense. Its gods are ourselves writ large; they may do occasional good turns for mortals they favour, but they have all the mortal vices while being spared the mortal consequences - which is why, to answer the old religious question, bad things continue to happen to good people.

Or just to people. This production, and these performances, get that right.

Bakkhai runs in repertory through September 23.
