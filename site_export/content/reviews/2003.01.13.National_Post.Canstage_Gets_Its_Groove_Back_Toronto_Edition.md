SUNDAY FATHER

Berkeley Street Theatre

- - -

Just a week ago, in my summary of last year's theatre, I castigated CanStage for its terrible record with new Canadian plays. 2003 has barely dawned, and already the company makes a monkey out of me. From our first sight of the stage, which has been divided into three glistening compartments, Sunday Father, the new unburnt offering from Adam Pettle, is irresistible.

The tripartite set accommodates an action that moves swiftly through a variety of rooms. It also happens to match the plays cast list.There are three onstage characters: brothers Jed and Alan and Amy, Jeds wife. Others of course are mentioned without being seen: The most important of these are Jed and Amy's young son Daniel, and the brothers' father, a very successful lawyer.

Jed and Alan are the children of divorce. They spent weekends with their dad, and rounded off each visit by recording a tape, riffing on the activities of the day. These spoof radio-shows, brimming with hardly suppressed sibling rivalry, are recreated for us by the two grown-up actors as counterpoint to, and commentary on, the present-day action. Their catch-phrase introduction is "Hello, ladies and Gentiles."

The North American Jewishness that was spray-painted over Pettles earlier play, Zadies Shoes, is here applied with more surgical precision. Jed and Alan are, of course, Jewish. Amy isn't, a circumstance that breeds some tension between her and Jed, mostly expressed through uneasy jokes. It is not, though, the immediate cause of their break-up. Break up they do, and Jed finds himself on track for his father's fate of becoming a Sunday father. It probably suited his father. He doesnt believe it will suit him.

Jed and Alan have maintained a bantering relationship, centred on drinks and sports. Alan, unmarried and with a penchant for younger women, is a perpetual jock. So its ironic it should be Jed who's made a career in sports, at least to the extent of writing about them.

Alan, with apparent incongruity, has followed his father into the law; indeed he's followed his father into his fathers office. This was a painful course, because his father has always liked to make him squirm. Jed was the favourite; Alan resented it then and he resents it now, probably even more. His preferred Biblical excerpt is Cain and Abel.

At one point Alan tells this story, uncle-fashion, to an unseen Daniel, recasting it, to bitterly hilarious effect, in terms of contemporary Forest Hill.

At another, Jed, who is as much into Greek mythology as Hebrew, tells him in similar vein about the birth of Pallas Athene. This is also entertaining though not bitter. At a third, Amy does the bedtime routine with the legend of Pandoras box; one doesn't want to sound racist or sexist -- and the play is neither of those things -- but her goyish update sounds pretty pallid compared to the free- wheeling deconstructions offered by the two guys.

Not that that makes her a bad mother: But it's undeniable that Amy -- a psychiatrist with the physicians proverbial inability to cure herself -- gets less of a crack dramatically than the boys do. Even so, the play deals alarmingly well with the bitterness of divorce between two loving but incompatible people.

Especially cunning is a scene in which Jed and Amy recall their first meeting, so smoothly affectingly as to make the audience, sentimental slobs that we are, sniff a reconciliation; the possibility is then withdrawn and we -- acknowledging the truth -- take it without resentment.

A more serious glitch is one common to plays in which a major participant is glimpsed only through other peoples perceptions and other people's memories. The play, or its title, may link Jed to dad, but we never get a clear sense of what the old man is like. Clearly, he's overpowering -- he even dies in a lively way -- but that isnt enough. His invisibility may connect to Alans identification of him with God -- Abel's God -- but the fact remains that they can say anything, and we just have to sit there and take it. Why is he so terrible to Alan? Yes, parents have been known to play favourites, but this one's partiality strikes from beyond the grave. I dont want to give anything away, and its not an exact analogy, but I was reminded of one of my favorite Jewish jokes, concerning the Reading of the Rich Man's Will. "And to my no-good brother-in-law Louis, who said I'd never mention him in my will: Hello, Louis".

A last weakness is that the stage-right portion of the set goes unused until late in the evening. Otherwise the design (by Britta Gerecke, new to me and to Toronto) focuses attention admirably, apart from being smart and striking itself. Ive heard complaints that its too clinical and that it inhibits the actors; since the play has feeling to spare, and since the performers had no trouble making me laugh and -- towards the end -- cry, I'd be inclined to discount them.

David Storch's direction, especially of the actors, looked flawless to me. There are very few forced notes.

The plays themes interlock, but if you break then down you can say that it deals -- in ascending order of effectiveness -- with relationships parental, marital, and fraternal. The dialogue in Pettles previous plays has always flowed easily but has sometimes sounded glib; this time, given the characters and their circumstances, all doubts are squashed.

The brotherly scenes are duets as supple and uproarious as any I remember in a Canadian play. Ari Cohen's Alan is a wonderful sweaty- nerved lunk, a breathing compound of resentment, obnoxiousness, vulnerability and generosity.

Jed is Jordan Pettle, the author's own brother, who was also in Zadie's Shoes. His role there, an irresponsible charmer, was theoretically more complex than his role here, which is that of an emotional lightweight who is nonetheless honest and doing his best; the acting Pettle, more focused and economical than before, keeps him likeable without ever usurping the moral high ground.

Liisa Repo-Martell, whose previous appearances have nearly all been as physical or emotional orphans, seizes gratefully and rewardingly on her first chance to appear before a paying public in a well-tailored suit; she has a firm new vocal register to go with it and is, in the best sense, unrecognizable.

For once actors in a new play get to relish their roles rather than rescue them.
