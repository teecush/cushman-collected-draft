THE CD COLLECTOR There's more of the golden oldies to come BROADWAY MUSICALS

Saturday, February 13, 1993

Robert Cushman

Robert Cushman Robert Cushman continues his survey of the Sony Broadway series of reissued cast albums. I LOOKED forward to hearing The Rothschilds (Sony SK 30337), but this Jerry Bock-Sheldon Harnick score - inevitably tagged as the rich man's Fiddler on the Roof -is an attempted epic that comes off as inflated- domestic. By contrast the normally grandiose team of John Kander and Fred Ebb attempted their smallest subject in 70, GIRLS, 70 (SK 30589) and emerged with their most charming score. It flopped but is deservedly a cult. The renowned character actress Mildred Natwick as the leader of a gang of senior-citizen fur-thieves sounds radiantly sensible, and The Elephant Song, her cheerful homily on death, is one of the great neglected numbers. The score contains too many coy songs about old age, but it also boasts one of Broadways few palatable anthems of affirmation, unabashedly entitled Yes.

The disc of Over Here (SK 32961) was a guilty pleasure for me. The first musical I ever saw on Broadway for reviewing purposes, it showed the two surviving Andrews Sisters building morale on the Second World War home-front: just like real life, in fact, though broaching a few subjects that were left untouched in the forties. Who then could have imagined that Patty Andrews, the dynamo of the group, would one day be entertaining the troops with something called the VD Polka. The show was dimly lit, had an impenetrable story, and was generally ridiculous, but it is periodically ignited with a big number. If the dancing is missing on disc, the big-band sounds are still there, and the songs (by the Sherman brothers, Disneys men of choice in the Mary Poppins era) are as good or bad as the real thing.

Mr. President (SK 48212) was Irving Berlin's last show and the only tame score he ever wrote: the blandest of Valentines to the White House, though today, that might give it curiosity value. 1776 (SK 48215) digs rather deeper into constitutional history, being a straight-faced resume of the signing of the Declaration of Independence. Pop songwriter Sherman Edwards, whose cherished project this was, wrote a rather stirring score, compromised by the lamest lyrics this side of Blood Brothers.

1776 might have been called All American (SK 48216) if that title had not been pre-empted by an early sixties campus frolic that differed from previous college musicals in focusing on faculty rather than students. No wonder people worried about the musical becoming a middle-aged form. Mel Brooks wrote the book for this one, and it was apparently a worthy precursor of Springtime for Hitler. The score, though, by Charles Strouse and Lee Adams has been maligned; it's tuneful and even funny. Ray Bolger is said to have been miscast but actually comes across very well; as does Eileen Herlie, a noted English tragedienne who found an incongruous second career on Broadway with a voice that sounds wavery but hits the notes anyway.

Hallelujah Baby! (SK 48218) was an ambitious 1967 attempt to musicalize the history of black Americans; its one of those shows in which time marches on but the characters, representative figures, stay the same age. Its good intentions were overtaken by actual events; like The Rothschilds it suffered from the convention - not overturned until the next decade - that every musical had to be a love story. The recording conveys little sense of dramatic unity but there are individually effective songs (by Betty Comden, Adolph Green and Jule Styne), most of them put over by Leslie Uggams, who became a star in a role originally meant for Lena Horne.

The Broadway ethos takes a heavier toll on Dear World (SK 48220). Jerry Herman followed his huge successes with Hello Dolly and Mame by turning Jean Giraudouxs Madwoman of Chaillot (which Arthur Miller once called the most subversive play he knew) into yet another thoroughly conventional ode to non-conformity: a sequence of up-tunes and lukewarm ballads with an accordion written into the orchestration to prove that we're in Paris. He was obviously trying for something more, and Angela Lansbury, cast in the lead, could probably have given it to him. Anyway, she's in great voice.

In Out of This World (SK 48223), another composer-lyricist also offered the mixture as before, but since he was Cole Porter its worth savouring. The setting is ancient-and-modern Greek: the Amphitryon legend turned into standard U.S. sex-farce. The score, Porters first after the triumph of Kiss Me Kate, was inevitably underrated at the time and has been just as inevitably overrated since. But it does offer the king of the gods' complaisant self-introduction I Jupiter I Rex ("am positively teeming with sex"), the delectable duel of compliments Cherry Pies Ought To Be You, and for Charlotte Greenwood as Juno the indignantly rueful Nobody's Chasing Me (nobody wants to own me and I objectnobody wants to phone me, even collect.") Completing the list: A Tree Grows in Brooklyn (SK 48014) with wonderful songs by Arthur Schwartz and Dorothy Fields and a honey of a performance by the late Shirley Booth; Miss Liberty (SK 48015), middling Irving Berlin and the earliest show in the batch; Candide (SK 48017), the first and best reading of Bernstein's greatest score; Dames at Sea (SK 48214); a spot-on spoof of the Busby Berkeley era that introduced Bernadette Peters; and Goldilocks (SK 48222), another Hollywood lampoon, partly written by critic Walter Kerr and offering Elaine Stritch some good material ravenously accepted.

And there is already much more where they came from.
