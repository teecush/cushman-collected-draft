In yesterday's review of The Perfect Pie, I mistakenly credited two excellent actresses, Liisa Repo-Martell and Tara Rosling, with one another's performances. My apologies. Building Jerusalem runs until Jan. 29. Box office 504-9971

BUILDING JERUSALEM

Factory Theatre

There's a kind of play, increasingly popular over the past 25 years, that throws together a number of historical figures from roughly the same period and has them bounce off one another. The prototype is Tom Stoppard's Travesties, which corrals James Joyce, Tristan Tzara, and Lenin, all of whom were on the loose in Zurich during the First World War, and has them going to bat for their various revolutions.

There's another, more venerable kind of play whose strategy is to gather its characters in an inaccessible house, at the behest of some mysterious non-appearing host, and let them stew. Usually they are there to be murdered, as in the Agatha Christie artwork latterly known as And Then There Were None.

Building Jerusalem, from a company calling itself Volcano, has bits of both. Of its five characters four are historical; they are not famous, but they all make good ideological figureheads, and all were alive and of the same age and in Toronto on the cusp of the 19th and 20th centuries. None of them are about to let their swords sleep in their hands. They are assembled on New Year's Eve, 1899; they can't get out since they are bound by polite convention and the weather is what you'd call seasonal. Nobody wants to kill them, but some absent mastermind has decided to rub them together for the sake of the sparks. They pass the time playing party games and otherwise creating their own entertainment, because these are the old days and that's what people used to do in them. The production is described as a multi-disciplinary event, which seems to be a fancy handle for a show in which the performers talk, sing, and dance; in other words, it's what is known in the trade as a musical.

The four prisoners are: the Reverend Silas Rand, a Baptist minister with a mission to the Mi'kmaq; Karl Pearson, an English eugenicist popular in Germany, with schemes for breeding inferior races to serve the ones he likes; Adelaide Hoodless, who thinks women need instruction in domestic science more than they need the vote; and Augusta Stowe-Gullen, who disagrees. Stowe-Gullen is a doctor, the first woman to graduate from a Canadian medical school. She is much exercised with the threatened closure and last-minute reprieve of Toronto's Women's College Hospital -- this is a hundred years ago, right -- and if that doesn't put her on the side of the angels, one would like to know what could.

Pearson is bad, but as everybody else on stage is against him, he does acquire a certain dignity, and he sticks to his guns; at one appealing moment he appends to a spirited chorus of D'ye ken John Peel? a new stanza of his own about the hell of being locked up with people you hate. (Add Jean-Paul Sartre's Huis Clos to the list of possible influences.) Augusta is good, and she has a very powerful speech about the horrors of force-feeding currently being inflicted on a suffragette in England. She is, though, inclined to hector. Adelaide seems to have, by the play's lights, the wrong ideas but the right feelings. The Reverend Silas seems to suffer from a more complicated version of the same complaint; he is agonizingly sincere, but his efforts to interpret a Mi'kmaq legend in Biblical terms are generally mocked as patronising and misguided. (I'm not sure why. There are common elements in most mythologies. Why not use them?) All in all, the characters are treated even-handedly. There are some arch moments early on, when the Victorians are apparently being mocked for their foolishness in not being us, but the show soon outgrows these.

The fifth character is a young girl, who draws her elders out and orchestrates their conflicts. She is called Alice, though the apparent homage seems misplaced; Lewis Carroll's heroine surveyed her elders with a child's clear eye, but she didn't act superior to them and she didn't have -- as this one seems to -- magical powers. Her choice of parlour-games is admirably calculated to unsettle the guests and get them quarrelling. (For the overall structure, credit Ross Manson, the show's conceiver and director).

The songs are prettily sung and tellingly chosen. (Arranger and composer, Bill Brennan.) The dances are festive but disciplined knees-ups with an increasing touch of frenzy. (Choreographer, Claudia Moore.)

Finally, though, this is a play of ideas, dependent on text. (Writer, Michael Redhill.) The performers, chosen largely for other skills, often supply readings that are awkward or toneless or both. Martin Julien's Silas, with his horrified acceptance of a world in which "God will be dead," is an exception; so is Ross Manson as the happy pioneer of genocide, when Alice shows them all the coming century and he cries out, "That is not what I meant." Singing apart, the ladies -- Claudia Moore, Kim Renders, Sarah Chase -- look better than they sound. Teresa Przybylzski's set, a receding series of curtained wings, has an appropriate air of an old puppet-theatre, and is helpfully lit by J. P. Robichaud.

Good though it is, the show seems rather slight to have been in development for three-and-a-half years, but you can't legislate these things.
