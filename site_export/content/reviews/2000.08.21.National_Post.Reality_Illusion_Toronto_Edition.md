THE MATCHMAKER; SIX CHARACTERS IN SEARCH OF AN AUTHOR

Shaw Festival, Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont.

The Shaw Festival rounds off its repertoire with two plays obsessed by other plays. For Thornton Wilder, writing The Matchmaker, the existence of a long line of theatrical predecessors was a stimulant and a delight. For Luigi Pirandello, at work on Six Characters in Search of an Author, the whole idea of drama and its physical representation on a stage, was a stimulant and a torment.

The Matchmaker's central scene takes place in a moderately fancy restaurant, and the play itself might punningly be described as exceptionally richly-sourced. Wilder wrote it in 1954, basing it on an earlier comedy of his own, The Merchant of Yonkers. This in turn was an Americanization of a 19th-century Viennese piece by Johann Nestroy, Einen Jux will es sich Machen, which means, sort of, He the Town Red Will Be Painting.

Nestroy wrote in an urban patois, supposedly impenetrable even to other Austrians. He pinched his basic idea from a brief English farce by one John Oxenford, titled A Day Well Spent. Thats as far back as its possible to spool, though it's a safe bet Oxenford, too, had his sources, if only in folklore. Fast-forward, though, and we find The Matchmaker itself turned into a musical: a little thing called Hello Dolly! whose success has turned the play -- any of the plays -- into an endangered species. (I spent several weeks in the 1960s convinced that the song Matchmaker, Matchmaker must logically belong to this show, and was much dashed to find that it comes from Fiddler on the Roof.)

Even that's not all: 20 years ago, Tom Stoppard, ignorant of Wilders version, made his own, exceptionally hilarious, adaptation from Nestroy and called it On the Razzle. A Day Well Spent has proved to be A Play Well Spent, even if its original author has never been in a position to collect royalties.

The notion that has proved so attractive is the eternal one of the country mouse on the loose in the city. Or the industrious apprentice who gets tired of being industrious, and grants himself just one day in London, or Vienna, or in this case, New York. In The Matchmaker, as in all its companion pieces, there are two of these lads; they live, this time around, in the 1880s and are called Cornelius Hackl and Barnaby Tucker. They slave for a tight-fisted hay and feed magnate named Horace Vandergelder, of Yonkers, N.Y. The play retains the lineaments of farce. So naturally, when the pair head for the bright lights, almost the first person they run into is their employer, who thinks theyre home, minding the store. They spend much of the play trying to elude him. Cornelius spends another important part of it falling in love with a charming widowed milliner named Mrs. Molloy. Mr. Vandergelder also has a niece, who may or not be eloping with a young artist of whom her uncle, it goes without saying, disapproves.

Wilder added one important element to those he inherited, and it's emblazoned in his title.

Vandergelder's wife died years ago, and he is now looking for a replacement.

So, to help him search, he calls in Dolly: Mrs. Dolly Gallagher Levi, who is poor and bossy and multi-talented and herself bereft of a spouse. (Of The Matchmakers four central characters, three start the play widowed, which may be a record.) Of course Dolly, slowly and artfully, manoeuvres Horace into marrying her. The play proceeds on the assumption that Mrs. Levi is controlling the entire action, but it never quite dramatizes this. So a lot depends on the actress taking us in. The Mrs. Levi of the play is not quite the Dolly of the musical, and its nice to see the character back where she belongs. Which is to say, scrabbling for a living rather than descending a staircase in a red dress, with waiters falling inexplicably prostrate before her. This Dolly is philosophic and philanthropic, but even unmusicalized she is a force of nature.

Goldie Semple does the reflective moments (Wilder's specialty) very well, but the active parts find her anxiously characterizing -- in an Irish accent, that seems to absorb much of her energy -- rather than simply being. Intended to be irresistible she is, at most, sly.

Michael Ball, as her prey, does an initially heroic job of being tyrannical, but soon relaxes into his accustomed mode, gruff-but- likeable. Vandergelder may have a soft underbelly (another Wilder specialty) but it shouldn't protrude. Both performances have their pleasures, but I felt at ease with neither of them. And I suspect that the lady, at least, shared my discomfort. The central battle never gets fought.

So the production has to fall back on charm, which the play already lays on pretty thick. Wilder was an omnivorous and affectionate student of old theatrical forms, and part of what he loved was their artifice, unashamed and unself-consious His own adaptation is equally unashamed, but it is very self-conscious, and he delights in having his characters step out of the frame to favour us with little homilies. ("There," you hear the author trimphantly chuckling, goes the fourth wall.)

These speeches are often surprisingly sensible and are generally witty (as is much of the dialogue) but there has to be some tough love in the background if the general benevolence is not to seem cloying. And its not the plays fault, but many of the lines now sound like cues for song. This may testify to the inherent musicality of Wilder's prose, but it is a problem. "Put on your Sunday clothes," says Cornelius to Barnaby. We'll be wearing ribbons down our back, says Mrs Molloy, predicting next seasonsfashion. "Hello, Dolly," says -- well, nobody says it, but you can sense that theyre all thinking it. The awareness hangs especially heavy during the scene breaks: Actors perform little character-dances across the stage while the sets are changed, and at one point the front of a train reverses itself and turns neatly into a clothes-closet.

Christopher Newton stages this kind of thing better than most directors, but he is not at his most inspired. And though William Schmuck's costumes, and his Yonkers store setting, are treats, his Harmonia Gardens restaurant is disappointingly table d'hote.

The play and production are both at their best where the story is at its most Nestroyan (unless the word is Nestructive), which is to say with the young or youngish characters. Corinne Koslo, who was superbly stern in The Apple Cart, is now wonderfully roguish as the melting Mrs. Molloy. Dylan Trowbridge, another recurringly impressive actor, is equally enchanting as the bashful Barnaby, and Kevin Bundy does fine as the maturer scamp, Cornelius. (Thirty- three, actually, and never been kissed: His is the desperation that every farce requires.)

Good minor work is done by Craig Erickson as a surprisingly forceful artist and Fiona Byrne as his tear-gushing fountain of a beloved; by George Dawson as a shady manservant who matches the matchmaker for versatility of employment; by Lisa Norton as an assistant milliner; and by Peter Millard, one of the Shaw ensembles cornerstone actors, exemplifying its overall strength by playing the minimal role of a barber.

The final act is presided over by an elderly aunt who was jilted in her youth, an experience that has turned her into a sort of Miss. Havisham in reverse, dispatching young people to wedded bliss, whether they want to go or not. Patricia Hamilton gives her authority, but not the lachrymose zaniness for which she entreats.

Pirandellos Six Characters in Search of an Author begins by showing us a company of actors rehearsing, with signal lack of enthusiasm, another play by the same dramatist. Suddenly, there materialize among them a sepulchral, haunted sextet claiming to be figures from an unfinished play.

So they are in limbo and, after the fashion of theatrical ghosts, they seek revenge, not this time on the man who killed them, but on the man who gave them life but refused its completion. They want the actors to accord them this closure by performing their play.

There are problems here. First, there is no script. Second, when the actors do have a go, their efforts inevitably strike the characters as agonizingly unreal. Third, the characters squabble among themselves as to whose story this really is and how it should be interpreted, though they seem agreed on the basic facts, as follows: The Father and the Mother have a Son. Years ago, the Father encouraged the Mother to take a lover, with whom she subsequently left town. The results of this union were the Stepdaughter, the Boy, and the Little Girl. The lover died, leaving his family destitute, and the Stepdaughter went to work in a brothel, where one of her clients turned out to be the Father. The Mother burst in, just in time, and the Father took the whole clan back to live -- in misery, recrimination, and ultimate tragedy -- with him and the Son.

This is a lot of information to get across, which may explain why the characters' author gave up on them. Pirandello himself, a notorious sluggard at exposition, is here able to solve the problem by having the characters simply relate it to the actors. It still takes some following.

Various overlapping things, about life and about the theatre, are being said. One is that fictitious characters have a special and more intense form of life, being forever frozen in the moment of time that consitutes their story: Keats said it earlier in Ode to a Grecian Urn, and Shakespeare in Shall I compare thee to a summer's day, and Dorothy Fields was to say it again in The Way You Look Tonight.

Another is that a real person can be similarly hung on one shameful moment in his life -- as happened to the almost-incestuous Father -- and that being defined like this is a kind of Hell.

A third is that a playwright never gets the performance he wants, since actors and directors have their own ideas and personalities, and that the characters, if they could see the show themselves, would share their creators pain. (Living subjects of TV and movie biographies presumably do.)

A fourth is that every one of us goes through life as the hero of his or her own melodrama, impatient of anyone elses interpretation.

Yet another is that characters get away from their writers, even from those who persevere to the final curtain. None of this is news, and Pirandello often seems the foremost pactitioner of what Basil Fawlty would recognize as the Sybilline School of Dramatic Revelation. ("We should put you on Mastermind, Sybil. Sybil Fawlty from Torquay: special subject, the Bleedin' Obvious.")

On the other hand, it's all true, and Pirandello found the ideal crucible for it, by confronting working professional players with a family drama thats as extreme and believable as the tabloids. Just occasionally, a staging comes along that releases the agony and the dynamism beneath the mannerisms and the long-windedness. Tadeusz Bardeckis is one of those rare productions: a superb crown on a good Shaw season

It has its oddities. The play was written in 1922; the production updates it, reasonably enough, to now and -- presumably -- here. Barry MacGregor gives us and himself a very good time as a British director presiding over a North American troupe -- at, as it might be, the Shaw Festival. (Though one may doubt the Shaw's Chris Newton is the jaded dictator this man seems to be.)

The actors -- with Ben Carlson an earnest, floral-shirted leading man and Jillian Cook a super-cool stage-manager -- do a terrific job of playing themselves or people they know, though it remains true that nothing on stage seems quite as artificial as actors acting actors acting. (I'm not sure whether this proves Pirandellos point or contradicts it.) The characters remain in their original period and locale, and its odd that the actors -- who think them interesting lunatics -- never comment on how funny they're dressed or ask how they got here from Italy. Nor am I clear how it is that, when the Stepdaughter breaks into a song from the ancient musical Chu Chin Chow, the entire company is able to join in and turn it into a first-rate production number. (The show was in their recent repertoire? Now, there's a coincidence.)

Domenico Pietropaolos translation has punched up the periphery of the play. He might have been similarly aggressive with its centre.

Not that Im complaining much. Bradecki's choreography, with or without music, is magnificent. The production is full of bold, casual, heart-stopping groupings. The actors, occupying the front row of the auditorium to watch the characters, seem to blend into us, the audience, which is beautifully unsettling. The use of the silent, doomed, perfectly played Little Girl (Zoe Roycroft) is downright inspired: She carries a pool of unease around with her. The Boy (Devon Tulloch), more actively troubled, is similarly silent and similarly magnetic. Joel Hechter is fine as the sullen Son who wants no part of the others, on or off stage, and as the Mother, Sharry Flett is a bodeful presence, though rather upscale for the illiterate woman she's supposed to represent.

The performers of the two principal Characters surpass themselves. Norman Browning is superbly pained and contained as the guilt-ridden philosophizing Father who, every time he excuses himself, accuses himself. Kelli Fox hits barely a false note as the sulphurously vengeful Stepdaughter: The only unembarrassing account of this role I have ever seen.

"Reality! Illusion! Who cares?" snaps the enraged Director, or words to that effect. Whichever it is, the characters apparently crude story ends up moving us -- which may be Pirandellos special joke. He thought up a drama too sensational to be taken seriously, and found a way of putting it over. It's Six Characters and an Author in Search of an Audience, and finding one. The title, actually, is a wonderful come-on. Children hearing it are attracted by the idea, though they would probably be baffled by its realization.

The play has become Pirandello's flagship. We hardly see any of his 42 others, though enthusiasts claim many of them are masterpieces. They're probably lying, but one would like to find out, especially if Bradecki were directing.

I would like, for a start, to cross Right You Are (If You Think So) off my list.
