Until June 14. Box office: 212-239-6200.

SALOME

Barrymore Theatre, New York

Al Pacino, Marisa Tomei, Dianne Wiest and David Strathairn headline, under the direction of Estelle Parsons, in what is billed as Oscar Wilde's Salome: The Reading. In other words, your money (were talking Broadway prices) buys you an impressive quantity of star power, even if the stars havent learned their lines. The bargain is a fair one, as the stars are blazing. Indeed, even the downside has an upside, since actors who appear with scripts are automatically excused from getting into period costume or strutting about in front of period sets. When, as in the case of Salome, the period in question is Biblical, this can save a lot of embarrassment.

Salome, the play, is a curiosity of curiosities. Wilde wrote it in 1893, in French; it was then translated into English by an unknown hand. (The translator credited in the published text is Lord Alfred Douglas, but authorities doubt the truth of this, and in fact Douglas denied it himself. He said if he had written the translation, it would have been much better. Maybe Bosie's indignation at being held responsible for what he considered inferior goods does something to explain his subsequent bad behaviour.)

Wilde's play inspired Richard Strauss opera, whose success, as is often the case with musicals, has eclipsed that of the original. The play is hardly ever revived, which is unfortunate because, though lush and eccentric, it is very good. It tells the familiar Biblical story: The prophet Jokanaan (John the Baptist) is imprisoned in a cistern in the palace courtyard for railing at Herod the Tetrarch of Judea, and especially at his marriage to his brothers widow, Herodias. Salome, Herodias' daughter and the object of her stepfather's unrequited lust, is fascinated by Jokanaan, who angrily declines to have any bodily contact with her. Herod begs Salome to dance for him, promising any reward she fancies, even to the half of my kingdom. She does her show-stopping number, the one with the veils, and asks for the prophets head on the famous silver charger. Herod reluctantly gives her what she wants; she bestows on the mouth of the dead Jokanaan the kisses that the live one had refused. Herod, in a presumed mixture of jealousy and disgust, orders her killed.

All this is economically dramatized in one continuous and concentrated scene that yet finds space for a telling accumulation of sidelights; as well as text, we get context. We hear the squabbling Jewish sects, forever arguing points of doctrine. We get some insight into the new Roman Empire, to which an admiring Herod owes his authority (hes very impressed that the current Caesar should have found time to write a satire attacking the Stoics). Above all, we are made aware, through the thundering admonitions of the Baptist, of the new cult that is advancing on Jerusalem from Galilee, and that represents a threat to just about everyone onstage. Jokanaan advises Salome to seek out Jesus (whose actual name is never mentioned -- he's simply the Man) and ask forgiveness for her sins. She shows no sign of having heard what he's saying. Herod, who takes a livelier interest in the world around him and is something of a humanitarian in his spare time, approves of some of the miracles whose report has reached him: "I hold it," he says, a good deed to heal a leper. But there are limits. He knows when his power is really being challenged. "I allow," he insists, no man to raise the dead.

Wilde seems to have been inspired by the swift barbarity of the story and by the contrasting luxuriance of the atmosphere in which it unfolds. His style massages the palate like a bunch of ripe grapes, slowly crushed and savoured one after another; it lingers on detail. Much of the text comes in list form. It begins with Herodias page describing the strange look of the moon ("she is like a woman rising from a tomb") while his friend the Young Syrian, who later kills himself rather than watch Salome throw herself at Jokanaan, rhapsodizes on the Princess extraordinary beauty. It's difficult to keep track of who is celebrating which. Salome professes her adoration, first of Jokanaan's body, then of his hair, before finally settling on his mouth -- she has an atlas worth of topographical similes to bestow on each feature. Herod, who seems to owe his literary colouring as much to the Arabian Nights as to the New Testament, has an astonishing list of consolation prizes -- peacocks, pearls, crystals and emeralds with miraculous properties - - to offer Salome in exchange for her requested delicacy of tete du Baptiste sur le plat.

The scripts calculated excess seems to call out for rich, ripe, oratorical acting -- the kind for which English actors, raised on Shakespeare, used to be recruited in order to bestow a semblance of class to the supporting roles in Hollywood epics. That, in the current production -- an Actors Studio project that has been percolating in private for years before braving the New York masses - - is just what it doesn't get. The actors go as far as is humanly possible for the demotic, the naturalistic and the conversational, in fact for the unabashedly American, and once we get over the initial shock to our expectations, it works. This is where Parsons' informal staging proves its cunning. The pretence of reading is by now just that (Pacino, in particular, negotiates great verbal parabolas without once glancing at the text), but it helps keep convention at bay. A large, and very uneven, supporting cast sits on nondescript benches and rostra, chiming in as required. Meanwhile, the four leads share, roughly speaking, a couple of styles between them.

The more solidly traditional work comes from Strathairn, who makes Jokanaans imprecations lucid and imposing without ever sliding into gratuitous rhetoric, and from Wiest, a splendidly forthright Herodias, whose disdain for symbols and omens (the moon is like the moon, that is all) stands in refreshing contrast to the mental habits of virtually everybody else onstage. These are timeless performances.

Tomeis Salome, by contrast, is violently, sulkily contemporary. If she's a princess, she's a Jewish-American one, and her dance is little more than a series of sulky gyrations. Herod, one feels, is rather easily aroused. But then Salome is the archetypal Hollywood brat, and Tomei unerringly conveys the headlong selfishness of a child trained to get everything she desires and to acknowledge no other horizon.

The driving force, though -- onstage and apparently behind it -- is Pacino, who has been experimenting with Herod for ages. My companion mused aloud on the reasons for the actors long fascination with the role. The best reason I can think of is that it allows him to show off.

But he shows off very creatively. The only other time Ive seen him live was in a production of American Buffalo in which he was like a parody of Method acting, all mumble and mannerism. Here, though, he really communicates, though certainly in an offbeat way. He proves at the end that he is fully capable of the grand manner; for the most part, though, he offers us insecurity enthroned.

His Herod is as egotistical as Salome, but brainier. He knows what he wants: He wants her, as once he wanted her mother. (That passion has long faded, as Wiest's Herodias, steeled in contempt, bitterly acknowledges -- not, one suspects, that she fancies him much either.) But he is restrained by fear of just about everything. He fears Caesar and he fears the supernatural, so he does his best to ingratiate himself with both. He speaks, repeatedly, of an enemy, the King of Cappadocia, on whom he tries, childlike, to unload all his fears. It is that king, not he, who tells lies, and who will be summoned to Rome and crucified, and who will be eaten of worms as the prophet has foretold. When he hears of the rumoured new Messiah, he reaches heights of sublime petulance. ("This Man must be found and told that I forbid Him to raise the dead.") The catalogue of luxury goods with which he tries to buy Salome off is hilarious and frightening.

His wheedling and whining becomes, undeniably, a little monotonous, but then so, I suspect, would any other treatment of the role. He has certainly found its heart. Reading the play after seeing Pacino perform it, I found myself unable to imagine it done in any other way.
