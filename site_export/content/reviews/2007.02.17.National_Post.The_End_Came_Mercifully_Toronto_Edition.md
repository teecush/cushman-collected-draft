There's a new offering from the company known as Mammalian Diving Reflex, a name that may have some meaning for the people behind it but has always seemed to me like three vaguely imposing words pulled at random from the dictionary. I have similar trouble parsing the title of this production, which is Diplomatic Immunities: The End. I'll have a wild guess. It means that the world is a dangerous place in which no one, regardless of nationality, is secure. So tear up your passports. This is certainly the burden of the show itself, a mix of videotaped interviews and live improvisation with just a smidgen of script that no one, least of all its creators, would call a play. There are eight credited "research artists," four of each sex, whom it would be equally inaccurate to call a cast; nevertheless, they are the people who are on stage throughout.

Near the end of The End, there's a video section in which a classroom ful of kids are asked what song they would most like to hear at, or just prior to, the end of the world. The majority vote goes to We Are the Champions, and one of the (ahem) cast proceeds to sing it live, on stage and off-key. With its built-in complacency, and its dismissive reference to "losers," that song strikes me as a cheesy choice for a global epiphany. Maybe the bad singing is meant to be a comment on that, or maybe it's meant to be touching in its honesty. You never know.

That same inquiry, about ideal requests to be forwarded to some universal DJ, has been echoing through the evening. The Mammalian research artists have been out on the streets of Toronto asking people questions, including that one, and they line up a few members of the theatre audience and ask them as well. We know that when the victims of 9/11 were facing their own version of the end of the world, music was not what was on their minds; they wanted, insofar as they had a choice, to get in touch with their families. That's probably how most of us would react. But none of this show's respondents, live or taped, mentioned such a thing, and only one said that, on the whole, he would prefer silence. (Myself, if forced to select music at the close, I wouldn't go for a song at all. I'd choose Bach, if only as some kind of atonement for not having listened to him enough when I was alive.)

Still, if the answers to this and other queries were mostly mushy and banal, the questioned were only taking their cues from the questioners. When one of the live subjects on the first night demurred at assessing his feelings about the end of the world, he was asked how he would feel if only part of it were about to end. Partial apocalypse sounds like a contradiction in terms; it certainly lowers the tone. Nor is it ever very clear what the show has in mind when it talks about the world ending. It could be ecological catastrophe, or nuclear war, or some kind of Biblical Armageddon.

We are given some preliminary information about the taped subjects, but it goes by too fast, and there are too many of them, for us to keep track. Only two make a vivid impression, both, as it happens, in different branches of the same business. One is the proprietor of a Queen Street sex store, who blames everything on greed and capitalism; he's not the only person in the show to make this case, but he's the most lucid. The other is a middle-aged, crack-addicted prostitute who presents the authentic face and voice of hopelessness. Asked how she would like to die -- another of the show's favourite questions -- she says "nobody dies in their sleep anymore." That's a line more haunting than most playwrights could devise.

It may also mean that we're concerned here not with some curse peculiar to our own age but with our old friend the Human Condition, that is, we all die. The show isn't sure and it doesn't pretend to be. Darren O'Donnell, who conceived and directed it, has said more than once that most conventional theatre, with scripts and actors, is boring. I wouldn't argue with that. But it doesn't follow that unconventional theatre is necessarily any less boring. Sheer performing skill, though not an end in itself, can be useful in holding boredom at bay. Of the current Mammalian Diving octet, only O'Donnell himself has any significant credentials in this respect, and his energy level here is unusually low. When he and his colleagues are left to talk among themselves, the results are painful, stilted trying to pass as casual. One of the four men (I'm not sure which; their names went by very fast) is a possible exception; he presides over the live interviews and does so with authority and nice comic timing. Two of those grilled on the first night -- by fellow audience members as well as from the stage -- responded with good-natured dignity; nobody, as the show says, is forced to participate, but it doesn't seem to have occurred to the team that having one person interrogated by a group automatically smacks of bullying. A third victim was an outright volunteer, with cheek to spare; quizzed on his claim that he gave 20% of his income to charity, he said that he included under that heading money spent on taking out women who didn't end up sleeping with him. Well, it's a point of view.

The things we do right are as much on the show's mind as the things we do wrong. At the end we're all asked to stand and recite a credo of good intentions, beginning with the declaration that each one of us is the best person in the room. As I didn't feel I could make that claim for myself, I stopped lip-synching fairly early on I'm all for happy endings but this is ridiculous.

- Diplomatic Immunities: The End runs through Feb. 25 at Buddies in Bad Times Theatre, 416-975-8555.
