robertcushman@lycos.com; Hair plays through June 17 at CanStage, 416- 368-3110.

The best scene in the CanStage production of Hair involves a character called Margaret Mead, who is played by an actor called Matthew Boden. (You get the drift.) Margaret is one half of a safari- suited couple who, on some cost-conscious combination of honeymoon and anthropological survey, wander into the precincts of The Tribe. The Tribe are the 1968 New York hippies who are the show's collective protagonist. Margaret is fascinated by them and asks them all sorts of questions about their beliefs, their style preferences (especially that all-important Hair) and their sex lives. She is, of course, especially intrigued by the last item. Margaret looks tousled and rangy, like Elaine Stritch, and she talks as Stritch might have done had she allowed her long residence in England to affect her accent. Some of that style and crackle carries through when she eventually breaks into song, making her, in this company, just about unique.

The trick casting means that Margaret ends up, like all the other outsiders in the show, as a joke. (The insiders are treated, as they treat themselves, with stultifying solemnity.) The strange thing is, though, that the show places its audience in exactly the same position as it places Margaret and her partner, Hubert. It even has them entering from the auditorium to prove the point. It addresses us as if we, too, were tourists. When the show was new, it paraded its characters as if they were on a reserve; now that its old, it exhibits them as if they were in a museum. Its nostalgic, all right. When did you last hear the word psychedelic"?

The numbers, which take up most of the show, tick off the prescribed attitudes as briskly and impersonally as if they were items in a catalogue. We get, in rapid succession, songs about astrology (Aquarius, obviously), drugs (Hashish), sexual variation (Sodomy), race (Colored Spade) and somewhat displaced Beatlemania (Manchester England). These are all reasonable subjects for songs (except perhaps the first, which isn't a reasonable subject, period), but they could be sung by anybody. Later on we get Flower Power Peace March, Hare Krishna and, by way of a love song, Claude Is My Acid.

Claude is the show's nearest approach to a hero. Hes just received his Vietnam draft card, and cant quite bring himself to burn it. That, God knows, was a real dilemma for a lot of people, but Claude isn't real. He's a cipher, torn -- or so the show would have us believe -- between the value systems of his friends and of his family. Since the former are an amorphous sentimentalized mass and the latter are revue-sketch caricatures, it isnt a very exciting contest. The show keeps forgetting about it, anyway, and then has the nerve to try and milk it for pathos. Its suggested a couple of times that Claude's best course would be to escape to Canada, and it's never made clear why he doesnt. Still, the idea does net the show some extra laughs since, as we all know, audiences who are Canadian always find it funny to hear their country mentioned by people who arent.

Robert A. Prior's production has come in for a hefty collection of brickbats, most of them deserved, but the real fault lies in the material. Even Galt MacDermot's music, much the best thing about it, is catchy but thin. James Rado, who collaborated with the late Gerome Ragni on the book and lyrics (the latter mostly clumsy, occasionally perceptive), has apparently reworked the script to make the narrative firmer. It is indeed clearer than it was in its original Broadway production, which I saw in its London incarnation and remember as being an energetic bore, but it isnt any stronger.

Apparently, it once was. William Goldman, in his invaluable study of late 60s Broadway, The Season, describes the contrast between the show's origins at New York's Public Theatre and the commercial transformation that made it a hit. When it started, Goldman says, its informality felt genuine and its anti-war message heartfelt. (Also imaginative; it involved toy tanks.) Uptown, it became a matter of making the right noises, as loudly as possible. And so it has remained. Act Two presents a kind of minstrel show involving the assassination of a black Abe Lincoln (you cant accuse the show of missing any bases) that somehow develops into a battlefield phantasmagoria. Were undoubtedly meant to open our eyes and think of Iraq, but the whole thing feels now, as it did then, like cashing in. It also invites mortifying comparisons with Twyla Tharp's Movin' Out.

Priors program note describes the show as celebrating the 60s attempt "to alter the course of human evolution. (Wow. And you thought mere political change was a challenge.) He then comes down to Earth and tells us how many thousands of times the songs got covered. He reinforces his own staging with a nonstop light show that's at its best when it's abstract and at its worst when its specific. If two guys are singing a song about The Flag, and actually holding on to one while they do it, showing us a projection of the thing as well seems kind of superfluous. Stephen Huess choreography can be pleasing; the highlight is Good Morning Starshine, which has The Tribe swaying in rhythm while sitting entwined on a giant bed. Their singing; though, is mostly screechy and often toneless. The black women are especially bad in this respect, which may be an attempt to defuse a stereotype, but I wouldn't want to bet on it.

The acting doesn't have much more character than the singing. Jamie McKnight as Claude seems like a nice young man. Craig Burtanowski as Berger, The Tribes head honcho and main mouthpiece, does have a personality, but it isnt an attractive one. And so ends a CanStage season that has sometimes offered interesting plays, and sometimes satisfying productions, but never both at the same time. I see that the management has abandoned Passion," its slogan for the last few years, in favour of "Humanity," which ought to cover everything. Though I also note that next season's opening title is Of Mice and Men, which, apart from its dismaying cautionary connotations, would seem to get us at best only halfway there.
