TERRACOTTA WARRIORS

Elgin Theatre, Toronto

- - -

The principal character of Terracotta Warriors is Qin, First Emperor of China, a position he attained in 221 BC by the time- honoured method of doing in his nearest rival, who would undoubtedly, had things turned out differently, have done likewise to him.

Qin begins this "action-musical" in fairly spectacular fashion, by defeating his enemy in battle. It may be the nicest thing he does all evening. Once he has been coronated (I quote the word employed in the program synopsis, which I take to mean the same thing as being crowned, though possibly, given the mores of the time, more painful) he settles down to the usual imperial pursuits.

He ruins his mother's love life by having her lover torn apart by five strategically placed horses. He roots out conspirators, actual or imagined. He collects concubines, whose ultimate fate is to be buried alive when he himself has snuffed it; during his lifetime, he has inflicted the same penalty on the authors of displeasing books. The forbidden texts themselves he has cast into the flames, in what the program calls "the world's first cultural revolution"; it might also have credited him with the world's first, and possibly most effective, gun-control registry, since he has similarly incinerated a pile of equally forbidden weapons.

Among Qin's more positive achievements was the building of China's Great Wall, though it's a safe bet the labour practices on this project weren't what we would call enlightened either. All told, the First Emperor, to give him his contractual billing, is not the most endearing choice imaginable as hero of a musical.

This, though, may be to apply alien criteria to a form that advertises itself as "an entirely new genre and a new brand." (The italics are mine, and they denote dread.) Dennis K. Law, MD -- writer, producer, director and originator of the action-musical -- has now created two of these shows. The first, Of Heaven & Earth, played only in Vancouver, which is also where Terracotta Warriors originated. Law, abruptly dropping the mission statements about introducing traditional Chinese performing arts to a wide Western audience, says that his invention "was inspired by the need for something new on the Broadway scene." It seems a little premature to make that claim for a form that has yet to travel south of Toronto. It is also suicidally irrelevant, since the best chance of doing something new "on the Broadway scene" has always been to forget about Broadway. Do what you want to do, for the audience you have, and if it's alluring enough the big boys will come begging. Law's notes must, I feel, have strayed into the program by mistake; they read more like a pitch for investors than an aid for audiences.

When it stays close to its proclaimed roots -- as a display of Chinese music, dancing, acrobatics and martial arts, strung on a narrative thread -- Terracotta Warriors is quite impressive. Its costumes are gorgeous. Its performers, of whom there are going on 100, have energy, discipline and skill, though they never make you feel, as Chinese opera performers have been known to do, that what you are watching is not physically possible. The curtain call is the most enjoyable part of the show, but there are occasional moments en route that strike home dramatically. The sight of the doomed authors, trying pathetically to pull their writings out of the fire, is affecting, and a scene of self-immolation by hanging manages to be delicate and brutal at once. Mostly, though, this is a series of set pieces, and therefore specially subject to the law of diminishing returns. It's possible, if you keep one eye rigorously trained on the synopsis, to see how the scenes fit into the story. It is not too easy to care.

Now it's true that action-musicals are being puffed as "more amazing than heart-warming, more visual than emotionally appealing." That may be. There are a couple of gymnastic Asian dancers in this year's musicals at Stratford, and what they do is far more exhilarating than anything that happens here, partly because there's less of it. It's still possible for audiences familiar with even the less sophisticated Western entertainments -- cheerleading, perhaps, or Radio City Music Hall -- to feel that they've seen some of this stuff before: A waving banner is a waving banner, whatever angle it's being held at. And even old Broadway may have got ahead of this show; there are American musicals that have used Oriental theatre techniques far more imaginatively than anything that's on offer here, musicals from Pacific Overtures all the way back to The King and I. The latter show, in fact, has bequeathed a romantic subplot to Terracotta Warriors. Here, too, the monarch's favourite girl enters on a doomed romance in defiance of her lord and master; here, too, the young lovers kiss in a shadow and meet sticky ends.

Indeed, despite its avowed indifference to "comedic dialogue, tuneful songs and light-hearted romance" (all those things we're all so bored with), the show seems as bound to Broadway aesthetically as it would like to be commercially. Though it has live traditional instrumentalists, it also uses a pre-recorded score that suggests, at its most adventurous, Leonard Bernstein ready to rumble. Its stage-stealing role is that of the Emperor's chief eunuch, who starts as his pander and confidant but ends up despising him. Purse- lipped and in a permanent hunch, he's like a cross between Rigoletto and the emcee in Cabaret. His thoughts are relayed to us from the side boxes by a soprano who does the only singing -- indeed has the only words -- in the show. She performs half-a-dozen songs, two of which sound like the theme from Love Story. It's obviously not the show's fault that I don't understand the language, but surely a translation would have been in order, or at least surtitles. It might have been our one chance of learning something not only about the eunuch but about the Emperor himself, of whose thoughts and feelings we know as little when we go out as when we came in. A tyrant as protagonist is problematical enough; a tyrant who's also a cipher is impossible. It's Springtime for Hitler without the laughs.

There is a basic throne-room set, in heavy red with fat encrypted pillars, that can hardly help looking like one of the more grandiose restaurants at Queen and Spadina. It's interspersed with some etched backdrops that are far more pleasing. One of them depicts the afterlife, in which the ghostly warriors of the title are present en masse. "After death all men are equal and the Emperor is mocked and the lovers triumph in a ghostly celebration." So concludes the synopsis, or almost. "What an incredible journey of the mind!" it adds, prudently furnishing its own rave review. Under the circumstances, I need hardly apologize for quoting so liberally from the program. If it's going to encroach on my territory, the best I can do is encroach back. Besides, there are times when the publicity for a show can be more entertaining than the show. It can certainly be more revealing.
