THEATRE REVIEW

Noble Parasites

Theatre Passe Muraille, Toronto

Mike McPhaden, author of the greatly overpraised Poochwater, has come up trumps in spades, giving us a pair of futuristic fables that amount to a double dose of dystopia. Or, coming right on top of We Will Rock You, a triple dose. The new deal, though, comes minus the happy ending.

The overall title Noble Parasites applies most obviously to the double bill's first half, subtitled The Bookworm. (It applies to the second piece by strained metaphor or not at all.) We are, says the program, "deep underground, centuries from today." Society, though presumably it still reproduces itself, has gone monastic; its members dress up in the starkest form of ecclesiastical couture and devote themselves to mortification of the flesh. They do not, though, have to do the mortifying themselves; their bodies have been invaded by multitudes of bugs whom they both welcome and worship. Top of the heap is the mighty tapeworm, who practically has hymns sung to him.

Somewhere offstage is someone or something very powerful called the Witch, though I couldnt make out if the characters were in thrall to her or at war with her -- or both. Food is scarce, and the citizens regulation greeting is happy in my hunger. Literacy in the world of The Bookworm has become very circumscribed (they've probably banned rock music as well). Education consists of learning sets of rhymed verses praising the goodness and rightness of this limited world. Young people parrot them in oral exams designed to gauge their fitness for adulthood. What happens to them if they fail is not spelled out, but it obviously isn't nice.

We meet Lindy, whos about to take the test, and her cousin and instructor Fran, whos both stimulated and spooked by Lindy, having acquired forbidden knowledge of how things used to be. McPhaden tells a good suspenseful story, laced with insouciant black humour. He dallies somewhat in the section establishing the young heroines, not much helped in Rebeca Brown's production by Kate Hewlett's Lindy drifting in and out of conviction and Amy Rutherfords Fran never getting there in the first place. Things perk up with Julian Richings arrival as the examining Minister, looking like a cadaver in a cassock. He smells a rat (which, in this play's climate, must be very easy to do) and puts Fran in the exquisitely ironic position of not knowing whether she wants her protege to succeed or fail. And the examiner shows enough intellectual ambivalence for the screws to turn even tighter.

The second play, Sea Change -- "a city park, decades from today" - - is a kind of long-range prequel to the first. The two pieces are in fact linked by a single object, a copy of Reader's Digest. (Im not telling how.) Things are materially better than they will later become, but the rot has already set in; technology is omnipresent, individuality is eroding and life is cheap. Ironically, the person who feels least at home in a world where people have computers literally in the palm of their hands is a former video-game designer. Hes been overtaken. He has billion-dollar election- rigging secrets in his briefcase, but he's no match for two young women: a peddler of uplifting pamphlets who can change identities in an instant and a gun-toting adventuress. There are any number of twists and turns. This spy-spoof is less imaginative satire than its companion piece but brisker entertainment. Richings manages to be taut and bemused at the same time, and the two women are all kinds of tough. I blame both plays, or the worlds of both plays, on global warming, as I am surely meant to do. Warm is the new cool. - Until April 29. Call 416-504- 7529 for ticket information.
