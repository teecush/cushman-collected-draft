Erin Shields' Soliciting Temptation is full of surprises. It unfolds in a series of narrative and character reversals. That makes it compelling. It doesn't make it believable.

It also makes it impossible to review without giving stuff away: stuff you would undoubtedly enjoy more if I hadn't told you about it. So you have been warned. For those still with us: Soliciting Temptation is a 90-minute two-hander whose characters are identified only as Man and Girl and whose setting is similarly unspecified. All we know is that it's a poor, hot country where prostitution is rife and where it's advisable not to drink the water; what with that, and the actress playing Girl being Hispanic, we might be as close to home as Mexico.

Man, whom we discover to be Canadian and even Torontonian (he has a 416 phone number), is a businessman whom we see, in rapid succession, crawling out of his mosquito-netted hotel bed, stubbing his toe, and phoning front desk to complain about the broken air conditioning. He asks to be moved to another room, but puts that request on hold when he hears a knock on the door. It's the Girl he ordered.

She looks young, younger it seems that he had expected. He's disconcerted, as he also is by the businesslike way in which she starts undressing and, most of all, by her failure to say a word. Maybe she doesn't speak his language. Then, suddenly, she does talk: in English. This is the play's first and most effective shock. It's also, when it happens, a relief. Derek Boyes has been playing the Man splendidly, and continues to do so; all the same, one didn't want the whole play to be his monologue.

One may have second thoughts about that. He certainly does. It seems that he's the victim of a sting operation, about to be locked up for procuring sex with a minor, or at

least with someone he believed to be a minor. In the meantime, she lectures him on the evils of the sex trade, and especially of sex tourism, and of his own complicity in same. He protests - convincingly at first, less so later on - that he doesn't make a habit of this kind of thing, and anyway that he's allowing sex workers (he's careful to use the term; she scoffs at it) a chance to improve their lot. She replies in terms that might - as he notes when he stops quivering - have come straight out of a pamphlet. The audience is put in the, I suppose, stimulating position of endorsing much of what she says and hating the way she says it.

Dramatically, it's hardly a fair fight. Boyes, in his many performances with Soulpepper, has specialized in playing goodnatured weak-willed men; that's also his starting-point here, but he advances from it to explore degrees of anger, authority, humiliation and fear to which he

has never had visible access before. His antagonist, Miriam Fernandes, doesn't have the same resources of technique or experience, and has to say things like "how very heteronormative of you." The fact that the play is inclined to laugh at her for saying them doesn't make the actress' task any easier. The author has to employ some fairly desperate plot devices (one in particular) to endow her with any complexity, and it still isn't enough to stop her from sounding shrill. The tension remains, and is stretched to the max in Andrea Donaldson's production. Ken Mackenzie's set offers a persuasive rundown hotel room, with an especially intriguing occasional glimpse of a green passage just beyond the door, a suitably mysterious zone from which the Girl emerges and into which she disappears; the costume plan had me wondering why, given the heat indoors and out, the Man was wearing an undershirt.

Shields' best-known play is If We Were Birds, a powerful reworking of the Greek myth of Philomela. Some of that power, and of Shields' literary skill, is evident here but maybe she's happier when working with somebody else's story. There is some kind of rapprochement between the characters here, but it isn't very persuasive. The play could be the kinder, gentler Canadian version of such American duels-to-the-sexual-death as Oleanna and Venus in Fur; it's less good than the former, better than the latter. It could even be the season's best new Canadian play. This season, though, that isn't much of an accolade.

Michael Hollingsworth's VideoCabaret, on its second comic-book trip through The History of the Village of the Small Huts (i.e. Canada), has reached Trudeau and the FLQ, one of the best of the cycle and, along with The Great War, the most emotionally affecting. The quick-change satirical cameos are as dazzling, and as dazzlingly lit, as ever. But the fact that the action zeros in on a couple of hostage-takings, one of them fatal, precludes sneering; and in Prime Minister Pierre himself, brilliantly portrayed by Mac Fyfe with all mannerisms fastidiously on display, the series may even have found itself a hero; at least it never shows him doing anything wrong and seems to applaud him doing everything ruthlessly right. (Mind you, there is a second Trudeau instalment in store, so the results aren't finally in. And that one will probably have more about Maggie, who is here only a teaser.) Fyfe is matched by Jacob James' Maurice Duplessis, earnestly studying revolutionary tactics through multiple viewings of The Battle of Algiers. The downside of VideoCab's innumerable short sharp scenes has always been a rhythmic monotony; to put it another way, the shows, however great, always feel long. This one, though, as cells divide and sub-divide, works up a real head of narrative steam. It's also, of course, in a retrospective way, very topical.

Soliciting Temptation runs through May 4 at the Tarragon Extraspace; Trudeau and the FLQ runs through May 8 at the Young Centre.
