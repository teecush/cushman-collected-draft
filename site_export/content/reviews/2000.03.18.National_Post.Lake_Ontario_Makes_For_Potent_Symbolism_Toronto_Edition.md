THE WATER CRAWLERS

Buddies in Bad Times

The best thing about The Water Crawlers is its visual poetry. It takes place on the edge of Lake Ontario, or of the tiny portion of it that lies just behind the Etobicoke motel strip.

The front part of the broad, sunken stage is filled with water, and though we are repeatedly told how poisonously filthy the lake is, how full of needles and condoms and human waste, it has a kind of magic.

Potent enough in the daytime, it's even more so at night, with the lights shining down more intensely and the reflections more acute.

Stephan Droege, who designed both the set and the lighting, has also supplied a properly dilapidated motel-sign (Tourist Court, it says), some nondescript frontage and, very importantly, a couple of swings.

Completing the picture -- burly, sorrowful and moonfaced -- is David Fox in the role of Billy Musgrave, 63 years old, former champion marathon swimmer, and now -- O time! O manners! -- owner of the Marathon Motel. Now he shivers at the side of the water, trying to muster the will and courage to go in again.

I wish that he and his three acting colleagues had more to do, or at least more varied things to do. Geoff Kavanagh, the author, has taken an admittedly appealing image and stretched it beyond its natural resonance.

Billy, for reasons of both pride and finance, wants to enter one more race, but is both physically and psychologically fearful. (And, rather naturally at his age, out of practice.)

His wife, herself a former Olympic athlete, alternately doubts and spurs him on. Their daughter, Marilyn, ambiguously proud of the two of them, sits and stews at the motel reservation-desk, since her parents cannot afford to hire a clerk.

Marilyn was named for a 16-year old girl who was the first to swim across Lake Ontario -- a feat that is her fathers Holy Grail - - and she even gives lecture-demonstrations about her namesake to visitors. (The play begins with one of these and it is, at the time and for some time after, confusing.)

Finally, there is Martin, whose lover has recently died of AIDS and has resigned himself to following.

Four beached characters, all needing to take the plunge.

Billy actually succeeds in persuading Martin to enter the water with him as his pacer and guide. (Billy was once known as Billy the Baptist -- "he brought thousands into the water.")

Even more surprisingly, Billy triumphs in a qualifying race, though nobody seems to notice. (A 63-year old man wins a swimming race says Mrs M, vainly turning the pages of her newspaper, and nobody even mentions it").

Somewhere in the background there seems to be a national metaphor; Lake Ontario is referred to as "the toilet of Canada."

The play takes place in 1989, and the implication is that what was bad then is worse now. It doesn't mean much. There is immediate gratification though in the sight of the two men actually swimming while the two women swing on the swings and cheer them on.

All the characters get wet at some point; we don't see many plays in which so many real people spend so much time in so much real water, and its a genuine attraction.

When theyre not splashing, though, they're talking, usually about the past, in dialogue that is neither as witty nor as trenchant as it needs to be. There's a nice, charitable feel to the play, and Dean Gabouries direction gives it visual definition, but its inert.

Fox, who excels at presenting men who beat out of time with their fellows, does very well here, exuding a dangerous cheeriness, but he can't help running out of steam. Elizabeth Shepherd has elected to play his wife with a north of England heartiness that is not, I think, her natural mode; but the technique and her intelligence are impeccable.

Catherine McNally plays Catherine, who flourishes the scars of a convent education, on alternating notes of enthusiasm and derision, which seems fair enough. And Greg Kramer very skillfully navigates Martin's dramatic problem, which is that he talks too much and too introspectively for his own good as a person or ours as an audience.

The play ends, for all parties, on the standard tragi-comic note of hope deferred. It's a pleasingly elegiac conclusion; the trouble is that the whole play has been elegiac.
