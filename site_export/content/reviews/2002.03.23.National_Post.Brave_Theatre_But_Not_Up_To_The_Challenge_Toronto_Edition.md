Until March 31. (416) 504-9971

GYNTY

Factory Theatre Studio

- - -

The heroine of Educating Rita, asked how she would solve the problems of staging Ibsen's Peer Gynt, cracked back with improbable smartness, "Do it on the radio."

The Theatre Columbus company have refined on this idea. They do it on the stage, and pretend they're doing it on the radio.

Five actors play between them the 50 characters of Ibsen's mammoth essay in the satirical-metaphysical-picaresque. They come before us in a detailed mockup of a broadcasting studio, standing at microphones, reading from scripts, and obeying the instructions of an unseen producer.

The director, Leah Cherniak, quotes as her inspiration the famous 1930s radio productions of Orson Welles. (Does she think that radio drama died out after that? Only in America.) A better reference- point might be Welles' stage version of Moby Dick, in which he sidestepped any realistic depiction of a whale or an ocean by presenting the whole thing as a rehearsal.

There is some ingenuity in Cherniak's plan, and some fascination in the way she works it out, but it destroys the play. And since the play is a great one -- the greatest after Shakespeare, I think -- that we hardly ever see, that is serious.

Peer Gynt is the archetypal fantasist; he fancies himself the hero of the folk tales told to him in his 19th-century Norwegian childhood -- he also just fancies himself -- and sometimes, thanks to his looks and charm, he gets away with it. He is also the uncommitted centre of his own universe.

In his middle years, he prospers as a slave-trader, and then goes adventuring in Africa and Egypt, but loses nearly everything. In his old age he comes home and faces death. He discovers that he, the great exponent of selfishness, the great wriggler-out of things, never had a real identity to lose; that he has proved unworthy of Heaven or Hell; and that he will be melted down -- by God's emissary, the Button-Moulder -- in a casting ladle. All that saves him -- provisionally -- is his continued love for the virtuous Solveig, who has waited for him for decades. This is a comedy: Peer ends up getting the girl, albeit in peculiar circumstances.

The original is in rhymed verse, and I suspect that it will never really work in English until some poet-translator does for it what Richard Wilbur has so wittily done for Moliere. The present version, unrhymed but with verse-sounding rhythms, is by the Irish dramatist Frank McGuinness: hence the new title Gynty, which seems rather silly here since the production drops the Irish accents half-way through.

Ibsen originally intended Peer Gynt to be read rather than acted; but it's in dialogue, and it has characters, and he was a playwright, and it's a play. Certainly it has to be one when it's on a stage. The relationships need to be realized, spatially and emotionally. It's hard to do this when the actors are bunched together, reciting. The convention eventually loosens up -- there must be a crucial moment when they drop their scripts, though I confess I failed to register it -- but not before it has done irreparable damage. And the disappearance of most of the studio trappings -- the mikes remain -- is confusing.

Worst of all, three separate actors share the role of Peer. This has been done before, on the insufficient grounds that performers of different ages are needed to represent his different stages. On radio, of course, this wouldn't apply; so the production is even false to its own concept.

Marcel Jeannin plays the young Peer on a pleasant but unvaried note of twinkling roguishness. David Jansen does the middle age, holding himself so far distant from the role that it virtually disappears. (This is especially true of his scenes as the would-be roue, abasing himself before the desert pin-up Anitra, she of the dance.) Oliver Dennis is the old man and much the best of the trio; his bleak refusal to let others have a happy family homecoming when none awaits him is the only moment of sustained feeling in the show.

Peer -- a character who combines the attractions of Hamlet, Falstaff and Lear -- is the greatest star role ever written, and should be played and received as such. (Step forward, Albert Schultz. Or Randy Hughson. Or one of the young lions of Shaw or Stratford.) Also, an audience needs a recognizable voice and body to unite a sprawling play that is always in danger of escaping beyond our grasp. Anyone who didn't know the piece would probably be lost here for much of the time. Peer may be Everyman, but even Everyman should have a face.

I know the trendy reasoning: that since Peer has no sustained identity, it makes sense to split him up. But that isn't what Ibsen was on about. Peer, despite his agonizings, does have a self, and a consistent one. But it's a limited self, a selfish self. He lives by the code of the trolls -- "be yourself and yourself alone " -- or, in the more helpful words of the translation I grew up on, " troll, to thyself be -- enough." Add to this the fatal advice of the Great Boyg to "go roundabout". It takes Solveig to break the deadlock, and it takes her forever.

All three Peers, when off-duty, do good work in minor roles: mostly male, though there is some gender crossing. Of the official women, Emma Campbell is a wondrously good and unforced Solveig, and makes instant and enjoyable switches into the various temptresses. (Her troll princess thpeakth with a lithp.) Martha Ross, though, is too trim and too bland for Peer's loving, scolding peasant mother; the richest mother-son relationship in world drama goes for practically nothing.

Indeed, too much is glided over, or simply cut, including some vital echoes and repetitions. This may sound masochistic but at three hours the play is too short. I admit to enjoying, shamelessly, the moments in which the actors stepped out of character and played actors.

For all I have said, the show is no disgrace. It is, obviously, a brave enterprise. But it is very frustrating.
