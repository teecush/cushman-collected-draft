Authors latest play covers wide social, emotional ground

Michael Healeys latest play is called Generous, and it lives up to its title. It's generous in the social and emotional ground it covers, in the quality and quantity of barbed laughter it provokes, in both the sharpness and the compassion of its insights into relationships, and in the spirit that animates all these. It's generous, too, in the opportunities it offers its actors, a superb bunch who reply in kind. It returns its author, after the blur of Rune Arlidge and the fiasco of The Innocent Eye Test, to his rightful place at the top of the heap.

Its also a generous, or a least a complicated, play structurally, though I think Healey has been making it sound more complex than it is. He describes it as being four two-act plays, each with its own title, with their first acts grouped in succession on the near side of the single intermission, and their second acts.

In fact, its just one play in a number of scenes. It's true that these scenes take place across a wide expanse of time, and that each of the earlier ones seems to feature a discrete group of characters. But these people, or some of them, converge as the evening proceeds, and as its theme -- the costs and rewards of virtue -- comes into focus. Compared to the technical games played in many of Alan Ayckbourn's plays, or in Caryl Churchills Top Girls (which Soulpepper has blessedly announced for revival next year), the tricks of Generous are kids stuff. Still, it makes sense to approach the play scene by scene, applying to each of them the title, or sub-title, the author intended.

We begin, then, with PMO," set 15 years ago in Ottawa, with a minority government on the verge of collapse. Inspired by an apercu attributed to Brian Mulroney when discussing a member of his cabinet ("slit her throat), this shows us a loyal junior minister (Heritage) behaving like the knights who murdered Thomas a Becket; that is, taking a remark of her leader somewhat too literally and stabbing a rival MP in the neck, fatally wounding herself in the process.

She bleeds to death right in front of us and her colleagues but only one of them notices, let alone cares. The rest are far too concerned with fending off an imminent vote of non-confidence; the air, if you can believe this of our rulers, is thick with profanity.

The mode, which persists for this episode only, is frank cartoon, with most of the casting cross-gender. The scene was given a showing of its own last year at a political-theatre evening, and I remember it as being more substantial and, frankly, funnier than it is here. Daryl Cloran's direction, in stark contrast to his handling of the rest of the play, is frantic in the wrong way, and never manages to focus our attention on the character who, as it turns out, matters most. Still, Fiona Reid's grey-haired PM is a sight to see, looking terrific in trousers, dispensing high-octane ruthlessness as his world falls apart and finally asking how will this country survive without us?" He means it, too.

We move on to "The Death of the Alberta Report, set in Calgary at roughly the same time. A female oil executive, in between telephonically juggling husband and lover and agreeing to shop for her son at that great leveller, Canadian Tire (where retail goes to puke its guts out"), grants an interview to a phenomenally persistent journalist, and takes his breath away by refusing even to pretend to care about the environment. Were charmed by her frankness, even if he isnt.

Actually, he is charmed, as she proves by seducing him. She's a creature of iron impulse, fuelled by a fear of approaching death. Yanna McIntosh returns in this role to something like her best form, her wit and elegance blazing and her anger almost keeping pace with them. She looks great in trousers, too. Tom Barnett as her prey does a fine job of, essentially, keeping his own counsel. This scene establishes what the play proves best at: balancing power-play with sexual tension.

And now it's now, as "One Party Rule, set in the present, shows another erotic encounter, or the aftermath of one, where the worldly authority rests with the woman. Shes a widowed judge; hes a clerk in her office, her junior in every sense. They have just had what she is determined will remain a one-night stand. He wants to stay. And not just because he wants to help himself; he also wants to help her. Reid as the judge, a commanding woman in an unaccustomed state of fragility, and Jordan Pettle as the clerk, infuriatingly perky and endearingly sensitive, play this scene magnificently, with Cloran's direction exactly charting every physical and emotional move. The scene also has a wonderful delayed curtain-line.

It isn't the end, though, because next comes Lily," a wholly mimed scene in which a young couple take delivery of a tub of KFC and ferociously fight over it, because he wants to eat it -- all of it -- and she, who is Lily, doesnt think he should.

Meanwhile, the delivery-guy (McIntosh, in the single funniest performance of the evening) gawps in silent amazement and waits to be paid. The scene also affords us a momentary glimpse of Pettles clerk; it plays like a Monty Python gag but signals that the pieces, or some of them, may eventually fit together.

And so they do in the second half, whose arrival signifies that I should stop giving everything away. Anyway: the continuation of "Lily reveals that she, the judge and the clerk are variously linked and that at least two of them have the urge to do good for people; the clerk tags himself, not at all sheepishly, as the I- love-you guy." (He's talking about real relationships, not bumper stickers.) Lily herself persists into the renewed "Death of the Alberta Report, in a brief appearance that makes Michelle Monteith's edgy performance click into place.

McIntoshs exec is still alive, and wants to go into politics: to do good, if you believe her, and I think you should.

She is still ruthless, though, as hinted in the subtitle; she wants to take her ex-fling (who in Barnetts performance has subtly but inexorably matured) with her.

The last scene disposes simultaneously of PMO" and "One Party Rule. The conscientious objector from the first scene (Ari Cohen, a little too emphatically distraught) reappears in the judge's chambers, having long quit politics for the law. The clerk, who seems increasingly to speak for the author, suggests he should return. Asked why aren't politicians revered?" he answers, "Because we give them an impossible task. Govern us, we say, take our money and govern us, and we will hate you for it.... They become the nexus of our self-loathing."

The implication is that both parties (and, I suppose, all Parties) should get over it. He has a proposition for the judge, too, which she answers in a speech so perfectly balanced by both writer and actress that I found myself simultaneously hoping that it would be the end, since I couldnt see how anything could follow it, and that it wouldnt, because I didn't want the play to stop.

There is no grand finale, no final reunion of all the characters; nor should there be. Some ends are left rightly loose. (Some wrongly, too. There's a part of the judges back-story of which I cannot make sense.) We are shown people trying to do the right thing, personally or socially or both, and are left to judge whether it matters that they may be doing good in order to feel good.

It seems that in most cases, though maybe not all, it doesnt. That may not be profound, but it is acute, certainly when delivered with as much wit and surprise, both verbal and visual, as it is here.

Yannik Larivee's set is a bit cumbrous for all the changes required of it, but the pieces look good when they arrive.

Generous is a good play about politics and an even better play about love. It's also the first play of the new Toronto season. If it yields a better one, we will have been insanely lucky.

- Generous runs through Oct. 29 at the Tarragon Theatre, 416-531- 1827.
