THEATRE REVIEW

A Christmas Carol

Young Centre for the Performing Arts

Dickens makes it all right to cry. There is no shortage of books, films and even plays that know how to jerk the tears; we yield to most of them while despising ourselves for doing so. The difference with Dickens may have been best summed up in George Orwell's phrase about him; that he was generously angry, though I would add that he was also imaginatively angry. He doesnt present us with scenes of deprived or even dying children merely for emotional effect; he links them to the society around them without ever colluding into dehumanizing the victims or turning them into statistics. And hes also, of course, generously and almost continuously funny; his unique potency -- possibly even stronger when he's transferred from page to stage -- is his ability not so much to make us laugh till we cry as to keep laughter and tears in play simultaneously. And nowhere is this more evident than in that concentrated essence of things Dickensian, A Christmas Carol.

In Soulpepper's production, now on view for the third time, the joy is captured in its purest form when Joseph Zieglers magnificent Scrooge awakens from his dream to find that its still Christmas Day and that he has time to put his repentance into practice. He overflows with pleasure; as he addresses the "remarkable, intelligent boy" who has the good fortune to be standing underneath his window at the crucial moment, it's as if he's hugging himself and most of the human race and we, as its representatives, are returning the compliment. Zieglers ecstatic caperings in this scene are on a level with those of Alastair Sims sublime movie miser, and though he can't compete with the elongated and emaciated Sim in the matter of resembling a Victorian illustration come to life, he is a rather more believable businessman. This is a fleshy flinty Scrooge with years of life still before him, to be used for better or worse; so his reformation means something in human terms as well as mythical. His misanthropy is of course as extravagant as his later benevolence; Edgar Johnson, Dickens' biographer, pointed out that a man who suggests that any idiot with "Merry Christmas" on his lips should be laid to rest with a twig of holly through his heart, must have an active sense of humour thats bound to make him a soft touch for the spirits. Zieglers Scrooge puts up a good fight but caves early; from the moment he catches a glimpse of his own lonely schoolboy self, he's a goner. Looking on at the happiness he's missed out on, the wrong paths hes taken, this Ebenezer is very moved and very moving. Watch him, a spectral onlooker, joining in the dance at the Fezziwigs party, a moment of gratification that falls back into yearning. The character is inhibited, the actor is restrained; so the final release of both is overwhelming.

He's finely supported by other veterans of this show's previous outings: Oliver Dennis prince of Cratchits, John Jarvis as all four ghosts (Jacob Marley and the Christmas trio), Patrick Galligan as Scrooges nephew and a bevy of others. Jarvis sometimes thunders his warnings more than is necessary, and Galligan occasionally generalizes his messages of good cheer, but these are venial sins. Michael Shamata's adaptation remains ideal; it gets everything in, without strain. His production has changed somewhat; we have seen it before on a proscenium stage but now it's done in the round. This works well when Scrooge is being jostled, physically and emotionally, by the merrymakers on the streets of London -- we get a stereoscopic view of his isolation. Its less happy when those same citizens appear standing in a ring with a plan of the city pinned somewhere around their chests. Some scene changes, too, are cumbersome; the Fezziwig bash takes forever to get started, with surprisingly laboured performances from Kevin Bundy and Maggie Huculak as the founders of the feast. But the soul of the piece remains, more vibrant than ever. Dickens, as has often been pointed out, didnt exploit the Christmas spirit; he invented it. In every sense, he makes us feel good. And, as in previous years, a collection at the doors for the Daily Bread Food Bank allows us to put those feelings to some practical use. Which, in its way, is one of the best and most fitting ideas Soulpepper has ever had.
