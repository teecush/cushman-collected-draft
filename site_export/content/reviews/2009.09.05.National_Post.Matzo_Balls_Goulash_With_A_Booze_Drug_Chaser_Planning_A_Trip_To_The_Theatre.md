Sholem Aleichem. That isn't meant as a greeting but as an acknowledgment of one of few observable patterns in Toronto theatre this quarter. The Harold Green Jewish Theatre is bringing us, for one week only, Sholem Aleichem: Laughter Through Tears, a one-man show about the great Yiddish storyteller, featuring the actor and folk-singer Theodore Bikel. These days, anyone with a career thats lasted a decade gets to be called a veteran, but Bikel deserves the term if anyone does. Back in 1959 on Broadway, he was the original Captain Von Trapp in The Sound of Music. And 10 years before that, he was starring in London in Peter Ustinovs The Love of Four Colonels; he was the Russian colonel. He's also played the King of Siam in The King and I. He must be the Anglophone theatre's leading cosmopolitan. Hard on his heels, and more elaborately, comes Fiddler on the Roof (at the Canon, courtesy Mirvish Productions), the classic musicalization of Aleichems greatest hits. This is one of those farewell tours, bringing back Topol in the role he played in the movie and, before that, in London. Topol, who is of course Israeli, first saw the show in his home country, where he was heard to say, "If it works here, the least Jewish country in the world, itll work anywhere."

From matzo ball soup to goulash. Soulpepper, whose very name has a flavour of paprika, has long had a Hungarian connection, courtesy of their regular guest director Laszlo Marton, and this year they've doubled it. Hot on the heels of Marton's production of The Guardsman, they bring us a Hungarian play that for once isnt by Ferenc Molnar. Another Laszlo, Miklos Laszlo, wrote Parfumerie, which is known over here through a number of variously free adaptations: the movies The Shop Around the Corner and Youve Got Mail, the movie musical In the Good Old Summertime, the beloved stage musical She Loves Me. But who among us has ever seen the play? Before this, Soulpepper makes its first venture into Greek tragedy, or at least into the vicinity of Greek tragedy. In place of a scheduled homegrown adaptation of Sophocles' Antigone, they're presenting Jean Anouilhs Second World War play on the same theme, in which the heroines resistance is code for France's. Since Anouilh hewed close to Sophocles' plot and characters, the cast, headed by Liisa Repo-Martell and R. H. Thomson, remains the same.

Among more recent imported plays, the most eagerly awaited must be Tom Stoppards Rock n' Roll (co-production by CanStage and the Edmonton Citadel) in which, with vintage Stoppardian eclecticism, Pink Floyd meets the Velvet Revolution. Also highly rated from Britain, though less glossy, CanStage in collaboration with Nightwood, brings us That Face by Polly Stenham, about a mother named Martha, hooked on booze and drugs, which puts her one addiction ahead of her namesake in Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf. From New York and, before that, Chicago, comes Tracy Letts very long August: Osage County, toting the raves and prizes that settle around one Broadway play per season. A touring cast is headed by Estelle Parsons, who at 81 is going very strong. The Mirvishes biggest gun, though, has to be The Boys in the Photograph, an atypically gritty Andrew Lloyd Webber musical formerly known as The Beautiful Game, i. e. soccer.

A presumably more modest musical is Mimi or A Poisoners' Comedy, composed by Allen Cole, directed by Alisa Palmer, and staged at the Tarragon, the same combination that gave us Anything That Moves, which, whatever people may tell you about The Drowsy Chaperone, was the best Canadian musical in years. For non-musical stuff, the Tarragon is currently enlisting shows from other cities. From the lively Edmonton Fringe, via Calgary's Alberta Theatre Projects, theres the Bent Out of Shape company in The Drowning Girls. From the RCA Theatre Company, St. Johns, N. L., but directed by the Tarragon's own Richard Rose, comes Rocking the Cradle, freely adapted from Federico Garcia Lorca's Yerma, a play that seems to invite adaptation: anything to stop it being done straight. The Factory, home of the Canadian playwright, offers accommodation to two of them: Brad Fraser directs his own True Love Lies, already seen in the U. K. and advertised as "a searing comedy exposing a modern family and their long-held secrets" (bet youve never seen one of those before); Michel-Marc Bouchard is represented by The Madonna Painter, an exquisite parable about the 1918 Spanish flu epidemic, "comic and cataclysmic." Well, it would be. Theatre Passe Muraille keeps a lower profile with, for one week apiece, Bash, a gay hip-hop musical, and a remount of Letters to My Grandma, by and with the gifted Anusree Roy, and with its script, one hopes, beefed up. Canadian classic department: CanStage revives Morris Panychs 7 Stories, about a suicidal man (Peter Anderson) standing atop a building with seven storeys. Add it all up, and there's not one new non-musical Canadian play that hasn't already been seen somewhere else. Are we turning into New York?

In Ottawa, the NACs English theatre, under Peter Hinton, announces a surprisingly conventional program: The Drowsy Chaperone, Jacob Two-Two, A Christmas Carol. The last, however, has the great Stephen Ouimette as Scrooge and a stunning supportingcast (DianeDAquila, Geordie Johnson, Tanja Jacobs and on and on) drawn from the theatre's new Acting Company. With this in mind, the season's highlight may well be The Ark, a one-night investigation of ancient Greek theatre that should bear later fruit. Back in Metroland, the Canadian Opera Company has two new productions with imposing staging credits: Madama Butterfly is directed by Brian Macdonald and designed by Susan Benson, legendary collaborators at Stratford on another Japanese extravaganza The Mikado. The Nightingale and Other Short Fables, a multiple bill, marks the return to the COC of Robert Lepage: a mouth-watering event.
