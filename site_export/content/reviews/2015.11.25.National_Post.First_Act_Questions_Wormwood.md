There is a wonderful image towards the end of Andrew Kushnirs new play Wormwood, beautifully realized in Richard Rose's production. A young man in a garden begs a young woman to come away with him while her father, previously opposed to their union, fervently advances a counter-proposition. Meanwhile, from a window above, the family next door - another father and daughter, plus a mother - look down in mingled fury and despair, beseeching the boy to take their girl instead. Out of the window they dangle two bedsheets tied together: an improvised ladder. This is how the young man, like the prince in a fairy tale, got into the garden in the first place. Now they want him to use it to get out again.

We are in Ukraine, in 2004: the time of the Orange Revolution. The young man is Ivan, a Canadian of Ukrainian heritage; he is one of two brothers who have come over to monitor what are supposed to be the country's first democratic elections. The other brother moves out (and fades out) while Ivan remains billeted with the trio in the window: a volubly irascible man known to us as the Professor, his housekeeper who may or may not be his wife and who doesnt talk much, and their daughter who talks a lot though not in English.

As for the neighbours, father and daughter: the Professor claims to hate them for being survivors of Chernobyl, and thus presumably vessels of contagion. But he has other reasons, though the play takes its time to reveal them.

The survivor-father is identified as "the Doctor", and indeed he is one. His daughter is called Artemisia, a name that linguists and herbalists (Im neither, but I can Google) may identify as the Latin term for the bitter herb of the play's title. There's yet a third significance to wormwood, one the play holds in reserve until near the end when its electric. Earlier, though, Artemisia the character has wafted around like somebody symbolic of something: maybe of the broken state, or broken heart, of Ukraine itself.

The entire first act, though it contains some lovely writing, leaves you wondering what the author (Canadian-Ukrainian himself ) has in mind. We get that Ivan is well-intentioned, probably out of his depth, enamoured of his ancestral country and of its attractive herbally equipped embodiment in the next house, but there is little sense of where either he or the play are going. Political details are scanted; we can just about tell that the contest is between reformers and oligarchs, but to fill that out you have to read the very helpful program. It names historical names, which the play never does. (Its not too generous with fictitious ones either, as you may have noted.)

The story has a storyteller: the Kobzar, a blind Cossack bard, modern in dress but with the glint of ages, who admits his own imperfections. "You can commit to memory", he tells us, but memory will not always return the compliment. Witty though this is, we still wonder why he's there. Again, we have to wait till after the intermission to find out.

It's worth the wait. The second half explodes, beginning with a dream sequence thats both nightmarish and hilarious, often at the same time. Ivans private dilemma becomes a convincing metaphor for a larger public one: whether to stay and help, or leave and help. Political specifics are still avoided, though it's a shock when the Doctor reveals which way he has voted; it seems that, against all expectation, he prefers the devil he knows.

Meanwhile he delivers a searing account of just how it felt to be caught up in the Chernobyl disaster, powerful on its own account and haunting as a token of a national tragedy, brutally exploited. The play, like most plays, reaches us after a development period of several years, and it takes no account of more recent developments in Ukraine. But we can still infer them. In that light, the characters and their situation seem even more desperate.

Rose's production has a spare, versatile set by Camellia Koo, and a top-notch cast giving top-notch performances. Luke Humphrey is a fine conflicted Ivan, both earnest and feckless. As Artemisia, Chala Hunter brings charm to a role thats overwritten in one sense, underwritten in another. Ben Campbell captures the Professors sound and fury and frustration, as he plays a long and cunning and finally desperate game. As his opposite number, the Doctor (a bee-keeper on the side), Scott Wentworth is superb, with a sober and commanding bedside manner, and a tragic trove of memories. Memory, it seems, has committed to him, with bitter results; so it makes sense - more than sense, eventually -that he also plays the Kobzar. As the Housekeeper, a scary female Cyclops, Nancy Palk holds most of her powers in reserve, until she unleashes them in a phantasmagoric speech about dentistry. As her daughter, Amy Keating communicates sad and funny volumes while speaking almost entirely in Russian; she also wields a hair-dryer like a benevolently intentioned weapon; later she lends a whole new meaning to the idea of buttering somebody up. Ken James Stewart has the disposable role of Ivan's brother, but his real value to the show is musical; he plays the blind Kobzar's Cossack guide (unlike the Kobzar, hes dressed for the part) and also supplies a vocal complement to Victor Mishalow, an Australian-Ukrainian-Canadian who provides invaluable atmospheric accompaniment on a zither-like instrument called the bandura.

This, after Nicolas Billons Butcher, is the second play to reach us recently on what Timothy Snyder's book calls the Bloodlands. Billon's play is the tighter and the more viscerally exciting, but Wormwood feels the more authentic, and not just because of the authors lineage. This is Kushnirs first major independent play, and it's a notable debut.
