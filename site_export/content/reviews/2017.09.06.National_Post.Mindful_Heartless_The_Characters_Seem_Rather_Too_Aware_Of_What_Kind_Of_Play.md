THEATRE REVIEW

Middletown

Jackie Maxwell Studio, Niagara-on-the-Lake

"I do that a lot" says John, a handyman on the perpetual edge of a mental breakdown, "use humour to distance myself from the pain." To which Mary, a pregnant housewife new in town, replies "I was using humour to try to be funny." The town she is new in is called Middletown, as is the play she's in, and it's both a reality and a metaphor.

I like Will Eno's style enormously. He uses humour in John's way and in Mary's, both at the same time and both successfully. He shows us people not just talking past one another but living past one another. Middletown is his tribute to, and rebuttal of, Thornton Wilder's Our Town, and this Shaw Festival production, the play's Canadian premiere, can hardly help standing in the same relation to the Shaw's revival last year of the Wilder play (which I didn't see).

Both are celebrations, or at least commemorations, of the American small town. One difference between them is that Our Town, even when new, was set in a remembered past while Middletown unfolds in an evanescent present. Another is that Our Town waits until its third act, set in a graveyard, to remind us that in the midst of life we are in death. In Middletown, which has only two acts, the memento mori is emblazoned from the start. Middletown also departs from its predecessor in being as much concerned with birth as it is with death. It ends with the two events happening virtually simultaneously. And its message is that whoever we are, wherever we are, we are constantly in the middle, caught between those two mortal poles. That is undeniably true. But I don't know that it's very profound.

Probably Eno isn't claiming that it is. His delectably quotable dialogue largely consists of playful variations on his theme. When Mary applies for a library card, the friendly librarian, letting her tongue run away with her, says, "Good for you, dear. I think a lot of people figure, why bother? I'm just going to die anyway." The play even contains a built-in critique of its own sensibility. A tourist says that inspecting Middletown's vestigial local history makes him feel "sad and beautiful but not THAT sad and beautiful." His wife chips in with "There's a long history of death in both our families."

I love it. And yet, as much as I relish Eno's way with words and as little fault as I could find with Meg Roe's production (none, really), I ended up disappointed, unable to shake the suspicion that the play's idea is a gigantic platitude. Its characters are sketched in but not fleshed out - which may be intentional but is still less satisfying than Eno's The Realistic Joneses or than Annie Baker's comparably oddball and allusive John.

Middletown's most celebrated citizen is an astronaut, and one scene shows him in his extraterrestrial element, remarking on humanity's insignificance when viewed from space and also on its paradoxical grandeur. Beautifully designed by Camellia Koo (set) and Kevin Lamotte (lighting), the episode still seems both pretentious and defensive. So does a meta-dramatic interlude in which actors playing at being audience members discuss the Story So Far, mirroring the real audience's expectation of a romantic connection between Mary, whose husband is never around, and John, who comes to fix her sink.

There's a Chekhovian feeling here, if not of passion thwarted, then at least of friendship frustrated. It's sort of touching but not really moving, maybe because the characters seem rather too aware of what kind of play they're in. And yet (another and yet) that hardly accounts for the excellence of Gray Powell's John, putting a brave face on despair, or for the magnificence of Moya O'Connell's Mary, a performance that without doing anything obviously spectacular, is wonderfully real and alive.

The most complex, or at least complicated, character is the local Cop, who guides us through Middletown, rather like Our Town's Stage Manager, and is incisively played by Benedict Campbell who was Wilder's narratorfigure last year. The Cop would like to think well of himself, and have others think the same, and he is capable of poignant insights ("You think you catch some kind of non-suicidal gleam in their eye. You didn't"), including one that probably sums up the play: "People come, people go. Crying, by the way, in both directions." He's also a bully, whom we first see half-choking a local derelict while exhorting him to utter all the sentiments that the play is half-satirizing, half-endorsing: "Say, my life's a mystery to me. Be a good human. Be filled with wonder and humanity and awe. Awe!" The homeless object of his attentions is called the Mechanic, for no discernible reason, and goes around expressing thwarted hopes that the play already has well covered in the character of John. That's no reflection on Jeff Meadows' performance. Distinguished supporting females include Claire Jullien and Sara Topham, two former Stratford Juliets playing well-observed medics in a well-observed hospital; Fiona Byrne, as another of the same; and, best of the bunch, Tara Rosling, serenely well-intentioned behind her library desk.

Like her customers, I have my reservations. All the same: recommended.

Middletown runs until Sept. 10.
