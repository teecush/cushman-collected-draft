THEATRE REVIEW

It's a Wonderful Life, Bluma Appel Theatre, Toronto CIBC Radio I think I heard that right "presents the Cabbagetown Players in It's a Wonderful Life." Thus begins Soulpeppers stage version of the 1946 Frank Capra movie, the perennial hearttugger presented as if it was being done as a Christmas radio play, also in the postwar 1940s.

It isnt a homegrown Soulpepper version. It's an existing adaptation by an American writer, Philip Grecian, that has been seen before in Toronto. CanadianStage presented it in 2008 in the very same theatre. The two productions share a contributor: John Gzowski, billed as sound and foley designer.

The latter part of that job description is anachronistic for the '40s and indeed for radio. But it chimes with the determination of Albert Schultzs Soulpepper production to make a supersized meal out of the conventions of broadcasting. Everybody, not just the actors playing studio techies but the actors playing actors, participates enthusiastically in the simulation of sound effects. Lorenzo Savoinis set lovingly evokes the period and the season and the medium, though the snowflecked windows struck me as excessive. Every radio drama studio I've been in was subterranean.

Anyway, here they are to tell the story of George Bailey, the smalltown boy who gave up his dreams of college to take over his family's savingsandloan business, running it so generously and humanely as to earn the undying gratitude of his fellowcitizens and the enmity of Mr. Potter, the tycoon who owns most of the town and wants all of it. I described him last time around as "Montgomery Burns without the nuance."

Georges take is that Potter "hates everybody who has anything he cant have." A run of bad luck, plus Potter's knavery, brings George to the brink of ruin and suicide. An apprentice angel named Clarence has to be dispatched from heaven to rescue him, and to earn his wings in the process, having waited for them nearly 300 years. Clarence shows George what life would have been like had George never been born.

Schultz, in an interview in this paper, has pointed out the resemblances between this story and A Christmas Carol (whose deservedly immortal Soulpepper dramatization is back again at the Young Centre). The protagonist of each has his destiny altered by supernatural visitation, timed for Christmas Eve. But there are important differences, and the advantage is all Dickens'.

We learn about Scrooges past as he himself relives it, courtesy of the spirits. We learn about Georges through an extended flashback, as a superior angel brings Clarence up to speed on his case history. This takes up much of the play, and it's clumsy. Even more crucially, we watch Scrooge change. George doesn't change at all; he was always a nice guy and he remains one. A Christmas Carol is both moving and delightful. Its a Wonderful Life, certainly in this incarnation, is sentimental. It gets to you, especially in scenes involving George and his children, but you may hate yourself afterwards.

Sentimentality also infects the staging. Lights are dimmed, even blacked out, at what are deemed to be affecting moments. But were supposed to be in a radio studio where the actors have to see to read their scripts, and where the effects would anyway be wasted on the listeners.

The production betrays its own convention in other ways. In commercial radio tradition, the action periodically stops for some words from our sponsors who in this case include Telus and, as noted, CIBC. There are also plugs for Soulpepper's other current holiday shows. But we're supposed to be in the 1940s. This kind of thing can be great in a Ross Petty pantomime, where anachronism is part of the fun, but here it seems smug. Also, the spoken puffs arent as witty as Pettys video clips. In fact they aren't witty at all.

Still, in the concluding stages you forget the trappings and get caught up in the story, which I guess is the intention. The acting, mainly from Soulpepper trusties, is fine. Gregory Prest has all the shy urgency of George. He doesn't do a James Stewart impersonation, but theres something about the set of his mouth and his chin when he squares up to opposition that does recall the original. As his loving wife, Raquel Duffy brings 1940s womanhood to life.

Michelle Monteith gives the evenings most creative performance as the presumptive bad girl: first sly, then tremulous. Diego Matamoros captures the commanding boom of an oldtime radio announcer, more menacing if anything than his subsequent sneering Mr Potter. One of the script's weaknesses is that he and George don't have a final showdown. Up in the celestial realms, Oliver Dennis plays Clarence and William Webster is God; or, as the program would have it, The Boss.

Its A Wonderful Life runs until Dec. 31 at the Bluma Appel Theatre in Toronto.
