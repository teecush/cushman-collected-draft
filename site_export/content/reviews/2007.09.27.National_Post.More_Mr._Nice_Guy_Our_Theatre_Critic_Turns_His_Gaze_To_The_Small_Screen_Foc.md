When we left The Office -- taking care, I hope, to switch off all the lights, because the Dunder Mifflin paper company doesnt look capable of withstanding any significant amount of extravagance -- Ryan was about to ascend from the depths of Scranton to the heights of Corporate. Four years ago, at the beginning of the shows first season, Ryan was a mere intern, so this is Irony. Michael, his boss, who had been confidently salivating for the Corporate gig, chose instead to return to his old perch and to the arms of his former girlfriend, the currently unemployed Jan. Or, more accurately, to her new and spectacular cleavage. More accurately still, he didn't have a choice; New York didn't want him.

Nor did they want Jim or Karen, Michaels subordinates, who were likewise condemned to return to base. Jim, like Michael, was all set to burn himself on an old flame, in his case the receptionist Pam. Meanwhile Dwight, Michaels second-in-command, is still entangled with Angela, Ms. Uptight from the Christian Right, a nervous relationship because they're two very nervous people. When Michael, with a degree of neurotic complacency excessive even for him, bequeathed his old position to Dwight before himself being interviewed for his new one, Dwight set about establishing his own reign of petty terror. It included lectures on the origins of paper and the appointment of a secret deputy. It must have left his staff nostalgic for Michael's rule of benign neglect. Which is just as well, as theyve now got it back.

So, at the political level, The Office returns tonight without much in the way of suspense, unless of course Ryan screws up in the Big Apple and is ignominiously returned to base. But that doesnt seem to be his style. On the personal level, though, much is uncertain, since the various romances have always been rocky and will doubtless continue to be. Trouble is that, funny though The Office undoubtedly is -- very funny at times -- I don't much care. And this perplexes me, because in the past I have become invested in the love affairs of TV characters who were far less skillfully depicted than the ones here. You might say, I suppose, that I'm not meant to care: that these are self-centred people, satirically conceived, whom we are expected to observe with ironic detachment. But that isnt the way the show has worked out. Indeed, the main thing that bothers me about it is how it has progressively broken faith with its sardonic origins.

There was a revealing moment in the last season finale. Jim, undergoing his own New York interview, was asked what he thought of Michael. He replied, hes a very nice guy. There was a "but" attached, and Jim may have been hypocritically saying what he thought management wanted to hear. But in fact he was right. Michael, as Steve Carell now plays him, is a nice guy. He wasn't three years ago, and Jim wouldn't have said he was. Back then the show was closely modelled on the BBC series that inspired it. Ricky Gervais David, the British Michael, was a monster, oozing smarmy incompetence. Carells Michael is also incompetent but, at least as we have him now, he's well-meaning. When he spouts political correctness he seems to believe it, for reasons that are at least partly humane. The British Office was also more believable as an office, and more geographically rooted. Instead of NBC's generic Scranton it was set in Slough, the appropriately despondent outer London township that inspired the normally kindly John Betjeman to write a poem beginning Come, friendly bombs, and fall on Slough.

American TV has a habit of softening up British comedy, most notoriously in the Fawlty Towers travesty that tried to persuade us that Basil and Sybil really loved one another. Even All in the Family was, to my mind, a pale reflection of Till Death Us Do Part. Here in Canada, too, we can do good misanthropy; at its best, Ken Finklemans The Newsroom had the insouciant savagery (and the workplace credibility) that The Office misses, or that I miss in The Office. The U.S. networks let it through just once -- in, ironically, the biggest comic success theyve ever had, Seinfeld. But then I don't think they ever noticed: at least, not until that show's much underrated final instalment rubbed their noses in it. Curb Your Enthusiasm, which makes it manifest, is strictly a cable creation.

North American fans of The Office tend to believe that the series has improved over the years, citing its greater depth of human interest. I dont dispute that -- the longer a series goes on, the more varied characters it is likely to generate -- but I dont think that's quite the point. Much probably has to do with the talents of Carell himself: He can do self-absorbed and self-pitying, but he wasn't born to be unlikeable. This may have been why the show has imported a later adornment of The Daily Show, Ed Helms, to portray the uncrackable insensitivity that Carrell lacks. (Helms Daily Show shtick was to cheerfully insult people, and it carries over.) Thats not to deny either Carell or the show itself their bouts of lunatic inspiration. Nobody is going to forget the bus ride to last season's beach party, with Michael encountering opposition from his black colleague Stanley and telling him to "go to the back of the bus" and then, realizing what he'd said, amending it to the front of the bus. Or the middle of the bus. Or drive the bus. Id class with that the Benihana Christmas episode in which Michael found himself unable to remember which of two Japanese waitresses he was attempting to score with. Stevie Wonder, he pointed out, is married; did anyone think that he loved his wife less, just because he couldnt tell what she looked like?

Tonight's season opener is called "Fun Run," and has Michael organizing a charity run in order to ward off a supposed curse on the office. Think of it as Exercise to Exorcise. - Season Four of The Office premieres tonight at 9 p.m. on Global and NBC.
