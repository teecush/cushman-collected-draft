Tripping through Anne country

Spectator

Tripping through Anne country

Friday, August 30, 1996

A WEEK or so ago, I was in Charlottetown, where the summers cultural attractions include a dinner-theatre show wholly devoted to spoofing Anne of Green Gables . It's called Annekenstein but, despite that, I wish I'd seen it. (Its dates and mine didnt match.) Certainly, its publicity hits the right note. "Know the book?" it asks. Seen the musical? Read the license-plate? That catches the mood of the place exactly: not just the town but the whole province. If Prince Edward Island is most famous for two things -- Lucy Maud Montgomery and the potato -- there can be no doubt that, in the battle for the spotlight and the tourist dollar, no one is putting money on the spud.

A gift shop on the island advertises itself as "celebrating the life and times of Anne of Green Gables." There cant be many fictional characters with lives and times. Usually these are reserved for their authors. The various Stratfords are devoted to Shakespeare, not to Hamlet. If you visit the village of Haworth in Yorkshire, you may take in a Brontë museum and nibble Brontë cakes, but even if you go for a walk on the Brontë moors you are unlikely to bump into Jane Eyre or Heathcliff. (Just as well in the case of the latter.) Anne is different. Anne manifests herself.

Like Santa Claus, she can subdivide. You may glimpse her on the streets of Charlottetown, decked out in the approved uniform -- green smock, white apron, Red Riding Hood basket, pigtails and a barrelful of freckles -- pointing out the sights to visitors and handing out leaflets for the local restaurants. But at the very same moment she may be in Cavendish, strolling with her friend Diana and delivering carefully scheduled impromptu performances, derived from the book and making generous use of the children in the audience. In defiance of the authorized pecking-order, it was Diana who emerged as the leader. She was the one with the merrier laugh. She was the one you could hear.

Cavendish is the heart, or the main cash terminal, of Anne country; here fact and fiction mate. You can stay at the Anne Shirley Motel and gaze across the street to the graveyard where Lucy Maud Montgomery lies buried, a circumstance emphatically announced on the cemetery gate. She actually died, as she had for many years lived, in Ontario; but she did grow up in Cavendish, which was the acknowledged model for Avonlea. Piety may have dictated the choice of resting-place, but commerce has certainly made the most of it. There are the Lucy-lived-here sites, there are the Anne-would-have-lived-here-if-she'd-existed sites, there are the beaches (once celebrated by Montgomery herself and as gloriously silky now as then), and there are the strip malls and theme parks.

There is also, and this I was in time for, the Lucy Maud Montgomery Festival, which included culinary events based on famous incidents from the books. There was an Old-Fashioned Ice Cream Social: not as old-fashioned as all that, since the ice cream was the island's dominant commercial brand selling more expensively than at the convenience store down the road. More agreeably, there were Old-Fashioned Games With Raspberry Cordial, which tasted delicious. Anne and Diana dropped by both events, though they didnt seem to eat or drink much. (Perhaps they were being careful. In the book, Diana gets famously drunk on cordial: a scene, if I may anticipate, thats particularly badly done in the musical.) Best of all, there was a barn dance, with a country string band that rocked the car park. The Avonlea connection seemed minimal, but the strain of Maritime culture that was being tapped into was arguably richer.

Was Montgomery a good enough writer to sustain a whole provincial economy? Probably not, if you're talking poetic rather than commercial justice. On the other hand, her tartness and narrative zest make her too good to deserve all the things that are done in her name. (And whatever Calvin Trillin may have said in The New Yorker, it isn't all being kept going just for the Japanese.) The Charlottetown Festival has of course survived on Anne of Green Gables -- The Musical for the 32 years of its existence. This year, notoriously, the main festival stage has been reduced to doing Anne and nothing else. The current production is enjoyable if coarse-grained. Its idea of comedy is shrieking caricature, and the heroines chums are a wearisomely energetic bunch of ballet-school dropouts. On the other hand its well sung, especially by Giulio Kukurugya as the painfully shy old Matthew. It's nice to have him sing that which he cannot say; it's desperately wrong to put him through cute little dance routines, just because hes in a musical. The show is at its worst when it clings to a 30-year-old idea of imitation Broadway, as in the obligatory anonymous love ballad. Its at its best when it remains its own native animal: as when Tracy Michailidis, a piercingly effective Anne, makes her enforced apology to the pompous Mrs. Lynde, rhyming her name with excruciating earnestness at the end of every overwrought line.

Which reminds me. There are an awful lot of words that rhyme with nation: for youthful patriotic euphoria you have generation, information, dedication and celebration. As a prophet of doom, you intone words like inflation, humiliation, damnation and colonization. I quote, all too literally, from Spirit of a Nation , which is being offered lunchtimes, free, by the Festival's Young Company. In a show that looks and sounds like a slightly dramatized aerobics class, they tell us what a great country this. They extend a compassionate welcome to the persecuted people of other lands. They demonstrate their multicultural diversity by doing folk dances. (This bit isn't bad.) And they warn us of the dangers of global warming, national disunity, credit-card overspending, and all-out nuclear war. For a happy ending they tell us, throbbingly, that they "want to be Canadian" though we thought they already were.

National pride can take some nasty knocks in Charlottetown. Outside the provincial parliament building, people dress up in frock coats and re-enact the deliberations of the Fathers of Confederation: an earlier and more consequential case of wanting to be Canadian. An initially respectful audience drifts inexorably away.

Ironic, and more than sad, because if ever a place were naturally calculated to be a source of pride, its Prince Edward Island, especially with the sterner scenic glories of Nova Scotia just across the water. It deserves better than its giving. And it's not all base tourism. If cooking's an art, then its being practiced with great invention, country-house richness, and impeccably local ingredients at the Inn at Bay Fortune, at the eastern end of the island. Here -- its probably true of the western end as well -- you can feel the imposed local mythology gradually slipping away. As you drive you see fewer and fewer Marilla's Antique Stores, Matthew's Ice Creameries, or Green Gables Anythings. I suspect that Lucy Maud would like it that way.
