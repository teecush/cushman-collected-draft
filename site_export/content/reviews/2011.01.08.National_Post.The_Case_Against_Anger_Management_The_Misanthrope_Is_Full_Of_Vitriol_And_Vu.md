Charlotte Dean, who consistently designs the most elegant sets and costumes around, has outdone herself in Tarragon's production of The Misanthrope. The action takes place in a seductively curved white and cerise apartment, the col-ours of the walls and furnishings finding echoes in the actors' clothes. In the last scene they sport periwigs the colour of pink grapefruit, each shaped slightly differently from the others. This is an updated production of Moliere's comedy but the finale finds the characters partying in fancy dress, in knowing homage to their 17th-century prototypes. Martin Crimp's 1996 translation, like the people who inhabit it, is nothing if not knowing.

It actually calls itself a "version." It keeps the original's shape and action, and much of its dialogue, but it changes the locale to London, and diverges in several other respects. Alceste, the title character and the only one to retain his former name, is now a permanently disgruntled dramatist. "Surely as a playwright you're aware," says his friend John, formerly Philinte, "of sounding like something straight out of Moliere;" to which Alceste aptly replies, "Jesus Christ, you think you're so clever." Celimene, the fashion-conscious coquette to whom he is agonizingly enslaved, is Jennifer, an American movie star; it's in her hotel suite that the action unfolds. The marquesses who attend her are now her agent and a blue-blooded and terminally vain English actor. Moliere's Oronte, the influential courtier who unwisely solicits Alceste's opinion of his poem, is now a theatre critic with delusions of creativity.

Crimp's couplets aren't equal to those of Richard Wilbur (whose translation is being done at Stratford this year) or of Tony Harrison (whose translation was done in Toronto a couple of years back in an arresting production by Bertolt Brecht's granddaughter). At one point he even rhymes "paracetamol" with "acceptable," which certainly isn't acceptable, though it does provoke a derisive reply from Alceste, who's more critical than any critic. "Nothing's more effete," he says, "than the moral contortions of the self-proclaimed elite," and that certainly strikes home, not least because, in some ways, it can apply to him as well. Crimp's is a racy, punchy text that succeeds superbly in creating a world. Admittedly, it's a more trivial world than Moliere himself created; he, as actor-playwright-manager, was as much a showbiz creature as anyone, but as a satirist he took on bigger targets. His Alceste antagonized a man who might have had him sent to the Bastille; Crimp's risks only gossip and bad reviews. But the trivialization is itself a part of the point, and Richard Rose's production gets it dazzlingly across.

He has a magnificent Alceste in Stuart Hughes who gives the performance of his life: surly and magnetic not by turns but simultaneously. He's a 1950s rebel -- John Osborne's Jimmy Porter or Kingsley Amis' Jim Dixon--thrown into a glib 1990s society that likes to think of itself as "post-modern": a provincial among the metropolitans (accents and manners throughout are spot-on) and angrier than ever. What distinguishes him from some others is that his anger isn't a pose; it's also, for the most part, justified, even if out of proportion to most of its targets. And of course when it comes to the woman he loves, he can't follow through; he rages but he relents. When he seems to be treating her the same way he treats his enemies, monotony momentarily threatens, but by the end we can see that his romantic obsession is just another side of the same unalterable man. Hughes keeps the vitriol and the vulnerability going full-strength; he's impossible, and underneath it all a good and even likeable man. You can see why Patrick Galligan's pliable, affable (and admirably acted) John wants to leave the party and talk him around, though in this version he's soon dissuaded.

The production doesn't go to the cynical extremes of some others. Even so, its revelation -- Hughes apart -- is the radical transformation of Alceste's other confidante, who loves him but isn't going to wait around forever, into a reporter for whom no confidences are sacred, and whose revelations precipitate the denouement. This gives the play something it's never had before, narrative suspense; it also gives Michelle Giroux, whom Stratford was mad to lose, the opportunity for a devastatingly observed and delivered performance of an iPad tapping scribe, nasally languid behind her big black specs. David Storch is equally delightful as a critic in tight pants, insinuating himself obsequiously through doorways and leaping beseechingly over couches -- the latter a habit that Alceste amusingly picks up -- before turning nasty. (He's called Covington, a name that's a hybrid of two current London reviewers. I obviously got out just in time.) Brandon McGibbon and Julian Richings are suitably vulpine hangers-on, and Maria Ricossa makes something, though nothing definite enough, of a role that never seems to come out right: a jealous prude who has here been transformed into Jennifer's jealous acting teacher. Andrea Runge plays Jennifer, and seems, visually and verbally, to be trying much too hard. The score (Mike Ross and Nicole Bellamy) gives 20th-century tunes a 17th-century gloss: as in, Don't Knock Baroque. There's an equivalent visual joke in the way the actors treat the fourth wall. At first you think they're addressing the audience, old comedy style; then you realize they're preening in front of an invisible mirror: all of them, Alceste included. Everyone is sick of self-love, misanthropes included.
