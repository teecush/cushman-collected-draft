FANNY'S FIRST PLAY

Court House Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

- - -

NIAGARA-ON-THE-LAKE - So commercial success is not everything. Fanny's First Play, originally produced in 1911, earned Bernard Shaw his longest West End run. It is now one of his least-performed works and is, in fact, one of his lesser pieces, a light reprise of themes -- among them the compromises of marriage, the iniquities of imprisonment, and the uncomprehending gulf between parents and children -- to which he gave fuller expression elsewhere. But it is deftly done, though the current lamentable production seems to be doing its best to disguise the fact.

It is a play-within-a-play. The outer shell introduces us to Count ODowda, whose distaste for modern life has induced him to withdraw from Britain to Venice, where he can dress in Mozartian regalia and, as he says, "shut out out the nineteenth century.

You mean," somebody objects, "the twentieth century.

To me," the count grandly replies, "the century I shut out will always be the nineteenth century.

Even in the twenty-first, that is still a good joke, and I reproduce it here to compensate for the Shaw Festival having cut it.

The count has sent his daughter Fanny to Cambridge, and as a graduation present he gives her a performance of an anonymous new play of her own choice, to be presented by professional actors and judged by professional critics (who, as the theatrical manager sagely remarks, come cheap").

In fact Fanny herself has written the play, and she has done it, as any forward-thinking young lady might, to shock her father.

The play itself is about children shocking their parents. Margaret Knox and Bobby Gilbey, whose fathers are business partners, have been unofficially engaged since childhood, though neither likes the situation much. In the real world, suffragettes are being sent down and locked up, and on one mad night the same fate independently befalls both Margaret and Bobby, though not for political reasons; each, in an excess of holiday spirits, has assaulted a policeman. As if this were not enough to bring their families to shame, each has made an undesirable acquaintance: Bobby has taken up with what an omniscient footman calls "a daughter of joy" and Margaret with a Frenchman.

We are told that rehearsals of Fannys play went badly to begin with because the director hadn't read it. Todd Hammond's Niagara production suggests not so much that he hasnt read it as that he hasnt listened to it. What Shaw said of Shakespeare is also true of his own work: The ear is the key to him. Both wrote in a heightened style that we have to accept as normal communication.

Shaw was often a verbal cartoonist, but the essence of a good cartoon is economy of line. What we get here, especially from the older characters, is gross and graceless caricature; if this is meant to be the fault of the fictitious actors then the joke wears very thin very fast.

The senior Gilbeys, in particular, seem to have stepped from some especially snobbish British B-movie of the fifties about people in trade. Peter Millard orates on a carpet, frothing and writhing; Nora McLlellan, with her trademark elongation of final consonants, seems unwilling to deliver a single line as if it might conceivably be addressed to some other sentient being.

Roger Rowland's Mr Knox, perhaps in compensation, is a mumbling nonentity. There is far more humanity, even some quiet passion, in Donna Belleville's account of his pious wife (Shaw always took religion seriously) but integrating this approach with the performances of her peers is, understandably, beyond her. The super- flunky is a role in which only perfection will do, and though Peter Krantz has the right impassive countenance he falls far short in vocal poise. He also muffs the plays best comic climax.

Severn Thompson, who plays Fanny herself, doubles as her heroine Margaret, though strangely, neither Fannys father nor the assembled critics seem to notice this. She plays both parts with the excessive flashing of eyes and teeth that are supposed to denote charm, but she does have some of the real stuff as well, and she comes noticeably to life in her scenes with Matthew Edison's Bobby, the neatest and best performance of the evening.

Simon Bradbury is fun as Margaret's champion Lieutenant Duvallet, especially in his long speech at the end, exalting the freedom of English family life over the constrictions of French. Its a surprisingly fluent oration, considering the characters earlier difficulties with the language, but after all, this is Fanny's first play.

Margaret declines to become the French lieutenant's woman, but Bobby does end up with his darling Dora, the Cockney lily, whom Caroline Cave plays her with more enthusiasm than accuracy.

In the play-without-the-play David Schurmann, who exudes weary urbanity, is disastrously miscast as the unworldly Count. William Vickers pipes and flutes absurdly as the senior critic, but the others are still very recognizable, especially the eternal brash young populist incarnated by Todd Witham. The way in which the whole tribe fall on the refreshments when their host's back is turned is definitely drawn from life. That, though, is a minor compensation. In a year when the standards of Shakespeare at Stratford have rocketed, it seems a shame that Shaw at Shaw should have plummeted.
