robertcushman@lycos.com; Past Perfect runs through April 2 at the Tarragon, 416-531-1827; The Rochdale Project runs through March 19 at Theatre Passe Muraille, 416-504-7529); Kafka and Son runs through March 18 at the Al Green Theatre, 416-504-7529.

In Past Perfect, Michel Tremblay's most recent play, four people take turns telling Albertine, a young Montreal woman, how impossibly self-centred and self-dramatizing she is. She doesn't deny it. This simplicity, as of a Greek tragedy minus the earth-shattering themes, is characteristic of veteran playwrights who have already done narrative structure. And as the play proceeds, Tremblay's embracing of monotony does to some extent pay off. To put it another way, the later scenes are less boring than the earlier ones.

This, at the Tarragon, may have as much to do with performance as with script. The central character has figured in many previous Tremblay works, most elaborately in Albertine in Five Times, in which five different versions of her, aged from 30 to 70, held the stage simultaneously. Past Perfect rounds off those decades by going further back. The year is 1930, and the heroine is 20, working in a restaurant and in a deadly sulk about that and everything else, especially about having lost her boyfriend to her own sister. Her first conversation is with her mother, who does the janitor's work in their building. (The janitor whose work she does is her husband.) Albertine mocks her mama for wearing her good straw hat while working and mama chides Albertine for wearing her best Sunday dress on a Thursday. She then realizes that Albertine is actually wearing her sister's best Sunday dress and deduces correctly that this is all part of a hopeless scheme to win her beau back. As Nancy Beatty plays her, Albertine's mom is so subdued you expect her to sink through one of her own scrubbed floors.

She's dynamite, though, compared to sister Madeleine, who is portrayed by the hitherto-promising Claire Calnan as beyond dowdy. Not only has she been robbed of her one nice frock by the woman she's sharing the stage with, but she delivers her lines, many of which are perfectly sensible, in a mixture of shout and squawk.

She may have been forced into operatic mode by Leah Cherniak's staging, which has the two girls chasing and haranguing one another all around their living room. It isn't very effective and it isn't very believable. The most devastating conversations between two people usually occur when the participants are sitting down; if they are on their feet, it's for some practical reason, not because a director thinks it will look striking. There are some pretty stage- pictures here, composed from cigarette smoke and the changing positions of the furniture on Yannik Larivee's revolving set, but they're offset by obtrusively brutal lighting and constant back- projections of tearful early movie queens, from whom Albertine is said to have derived much of her world view. Having made its point in about two minutes, this footage keeps coming back, not just to haunt the leading lady, who is having a tough enough time as it is, but to upstage her.

As Albertine, Caroline Cave is required to act in a state of continual climax; she hits a few peaks cleanly and powerfully but in between she is simply strident. The Greek comparison may be apposite; she's Electra, right down to her adoration of her (absentee) father. More prosaically, she's a very recognizable type of neurotic narcissist, and the production does her and us no favours in taking her at her own inflated valuation. Something is wrong when a talented actress spends so much energy acting badly.

Admittedly there are things in the text, at least in this translation, that would defy any actor's attempts to act natural. The exposition is shamelessly naked, and period atmosphere consists of people saying repeatedly that they're "in the middle of a Depression." The play only perks up when we meet the two last arrivals, who have identifiable attitudes and the specific details to back them up.

One, the only family member with a sense of humour, is Albertine's fat, gay brother (Jeffrey R. Smith), who gets a frosty response when he asks her to forget her own problems for a moment and think about his. The other, delightfully played by Brendan Gall, is the sisters' Gentleman Caller, who, when Albertine protests her undying love for him, says in effect that if that is love he doesn't want it; it feels too much like being smothered. She, of course, declaims that she'll never get over him, though, on the evidence of the five later Albertines in the earlier play, she actually bounced right back.

The Rochdale Project is a collective enterprise memorializing another collective enterprise: the alternative college, squat and drug- exchange that is part of 1960s Toronto mythology. As a play, it's hopelessly split between story and scrapbook. There are six principal characters of whom five are hippie stereotypes; an amateur dealer, an airhead, an earth-mother, an autodidact and a designer of geodesic domes. The sixth (Melissa Good, whose performance lives up to her name) is an ex-Mennonite dragging around a personal secret which -- in a classic example of a dramatic no-win situation -- she refuses to divulge until long after we have guessed it. She is also the character who takes the firmest stand to keep Rochdale open, as it succumbs to debt and disorder, though it isn't clear why she bothers.

The cast, under Simon Heath's direction, performs cheerfully and efficiently. They seem to belong less to the age of Hair than to that of Rent, especially in the matter of non- payment. They even start the second act Rent-style, all lined up, though they don't sing at us.

- - -

Kafka and Son is a thrilling adaptation of an unmailed letter from the novelist to his overbearing father, whom he blames or credits for making him the incredibly influential wreck he was. "You're a true Kafka," he tells his dad, "in loudness and in presence of mind"; the term "Kafkaesque" obviously meant different things back then. Its more familiar connotations are brilliantly realized in Mark Cassidy's production, which situates young Franz, menacingly lit and mostly in a fetal position, in one makeshift cage after another, with a slanting overhead grille completing the effect. Alon Nashman's performance of both roles is flawless, vocally, physically and imaginatively: what, in light of Kafka Junior's tortured timidity, you might call a tour de faiblesse.
