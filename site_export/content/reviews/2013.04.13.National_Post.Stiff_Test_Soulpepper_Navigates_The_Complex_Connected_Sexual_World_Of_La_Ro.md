The only thing wrong with Soulpepper's production of La Ronde is the billing. Its announced as a play by Arthur Schnitzler, adapted by Jason Sherman. In fact, its a play by Sherman, based on an idea (or two) by Schnitzler.

Schnitzler's Viennese La Ronde (Reigen in the original) is famous as a victim of censorship. It would be more accurate to call it a victim of self-censorship. The play, a series of sexual encounters, was brought up on a charge of obscenity at its first production in 1920, some years after it was actually written. The case was thrown out but Schnitzler was so upset at being labelled a pornographer that he forbade any future performances. His heirs honoured his wishes, and the play was hardly seen again until it came out of copyright in the 1980s. This occasioned a flood of productions that has hardly abated since.

Some of these versions have been freer than others, but they've all adhered to Schnitzlers daisy-chain structure. One partner from each episode goes forward into the next; the final scene shows the last character getting together with the first. Theres one respect, though, in which no contemporary production has felt able to honour the original. Halfway through each scene in Schnitzler's text there is row of asterisks, denoting that a curtain is drawn or the lights lowered while the man and the woman (it's all strictly hetero) actually get on with it. This of course strikes modern sensibilities as unbearably square, so we get nine or 10 episodes (as written, one of Schnitzlers scenes is strictly post-coital) of simulated sex. Apart from being a stiff test of a productions inventiveness, this undermines what Schnitzler's play is about. Its subject isn't the sexual act; its the things people say, the lies they tell, to themselves and to one another, before and after committing it. Of course, all adaptations try to get that in as well, but theyve compromised themselves. In fact, they're realizing Schnitzler's own worst fears. They turn the audience into voyeurs, voyeurism being the only sexual activity the theatre is actually equipped to present.

It can, of course, suggest others. Shermans version, which is set in modern Toronto, takes in cunnilingus, sexual therapy (these two are in the same scene) and at least an attempt at insemination by donor. Truth in advertising apart, thats his right, and he handles them very well. His play isn't as good as his model's; Schnitzler was a great playwright, Sherman obviously isnt; and theres nothing here to compare in mordancy or subtlety with the original's one marital scene, in which a husband tenderly patronizes his supposedly innocent wife, not knowing that we've just seen her having it off with a young student; in the next scene of course the husband himself will execute a particularly cynical seduction of a young girl. In its own right, though, Shermans La Ronde is racy and acute and occasionally very funny.

It also takes some structural liberties, some more successful than others. In Schnitzlers Vienna, there is no continuity; no character alludes to anything he or she has done before, even though we've seen them doing it, and no character, once vanished, is ever referred to again. Sherman's Toronto is a smaller world, and it keeps subplots going; a prostitute from central Europe (the second in the theatre this year) desperate to buy her way out, an African maid whose dreams are invaded by the "peacekeepers" who raped her. (We see them, too; a mistake, I think.) There are gains and losses here, but the gains are solid ones. More radically, Sherman gives us more than duologues. Here he has my sympathy. Fine though the originals individual scenes are, Ive never seen a production of La Ronde that didn't, somewhere in its second half, get monotonous. Sherman tries to vary and speed things up by turning two of the duologues into threesomes, dramaturgically speaking. One is set in a dance club, and is itself prolonged to the point of dreariness; the other though, the therapy episode, is one of the evening's highlights.

Schnitzlers bookend characters were a streetwalker and an aristocrat, and his play is as much about class as about sex. Shermans substitutes money for class (similar but not identical) and is also concerned with the supposed dehumanization of sex through hook-ups, both physical and technological. This a cliche, but it's vigorously pursued and gives the play a through-line. There's also a new last scene, though its one that echoes Schnitzlers first and is similarly set on a lonely bridge. It may be meant as a flashback, or as a signal that the cycle is about to start all over again (though, in a couple of cases, that would be biologically impossible); either way, it's haunting.

If it's outside time its also, in Alan Dilworths very fine production, outside space: the only point at which the actors step out of their frame. Lorenzo Savoini's set, possibly his best ever, is otherwise enclosed by a big white false proscenium, which lends the action a chilly elegance at the furthest extreme from Viennese plush; it's especially apt for a scene in a biology lab. Theres one large and extremely versatile bed, and a pair of sliding glass doors; behind them, projections (also designed by Savoini) beautifully evoke the city. Dilworths staging is physically ingenious, emotionally and atmospherically superb; it's also, for all the explicitness, remarkably tasteful and tactful. There continue to be weaknesses in the company's junior ranks, though Leah Doz and Adrian Morningstar are impressive arrivals. As often, though, its the senior officer corps who do best in supposedly dangerous territory. Brenda Robins is remarkable as an actress turned sex worker (her Schnitzlerian equivalent was an actress fulltime), a thorough professional whether being solicitous or sadistic. There are other excellent performances from Maev Beaty, going from lab coat to kimono; Mike Ross, though the rewrites give him too little to do; and Stuart Hughes, as a soldier tough outside and troubled within. (In Soulpeppers and Sherman's previous kick at this can, 10 years ago, his character was transformed into an athlete, on the odd grounds that Canada wasn't at war at the time. Its much better to have him back in uniform.) Theres an oddly uneasy performance from Brandon McGibbon, and half a good one from Paul Sun-Hyung Lee as a masochistic financier; he's overtaxed by a long speech in his first scene, very effective in his second. This is Soulpepper's most adventurous production in years. And, just to end with, it has one of the nicest curtain calls I've ever seen.

La Ronde is in repertory at the Young Centre for the Performing Arts. For tickets and information, call 416-866-8666 or visit youngcentre.ca.
