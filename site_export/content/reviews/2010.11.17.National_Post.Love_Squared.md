WIDE AWAKE HEARTS

Tarragon Extra Space, Toronto

---

OK, let's get alphabetical. Meet A, B, C and D. A is a screenwriter. B is A's wife, an actress who's appearing in his latest independent movie. C is A's oldest friend, an actor who's also in the movie, in which he gets to play steamy seduction scenes with B. D is a film editor, and an old flame of C's, a flame that may or may not still be burning. The four of them are the characters in Brendan Gall's new play Wide Awake Hearts. They are played by --reading from A to D--Gord Rand, Lesley Faulkner, Raoul Bhaneja and Maev Beaty, all of whose performances are alive and quivering.

The highest quiver quotient is scored by Rand, who starts the evening with a long speech about a child actor who messed up on every rehearsal but was perfect on the take. It's an entertaining way of filling us in on what the characters do for a living, but its broader thematic relevance is not immediately apparent. (Nor, as things turn out, is it ultimately apparent.) Rand and Faulkner have invited Bhaneja over for dinner, and they now invite him to stay: a step up from the chain motel on which he has previously been billeted. Next thing we know, Bhaneja and Faulkner are getting it on together. Only it turns out they aren't really; they're merely performing a scene from Rand's script. But then why should Rand have put them in that position, given that he's obviously insecure, both personally and professionally, and seems to have his real-life suspicions in the first place? It's hard to care about the characters as individuals, what with them having letters as well as names, so maybe the play is making a general statement about all writers being manipulative masochists. Well, I've heard less persuasive things.

Tom Stoppard's The Real Thing also begins with an infidelity scene that turns out to be an extract from a play written by one of the characters in the main action. After that trick opening, however, Stoppard dropped the device and got down to business. Gall stays on the life vs. art, reality vs. illusion kick, which proves, yet again, to be not nearly as fascinating as its author thinks it is. Also, Stoppard's players-within-the-play turned out to be in a situation at stimulating right angles to their factual existence, whereas in Gall's play the fantasy is a mere mirror of the reality, even if a distorting one. Wife and putative lover go on misbehaving, and the author never quite gives up on the possibility that it's actually happening. Neither do the two other characters, who are jealous and who treat the possibly guilty couple to angry pieces of their own minds. Rand is especially prone to upbraiding Bhaneja for betraying their friendship; Bhaneja also gets it from both Faulkner and Beaty for being a compulsive, immature womanizer, something he doesn't feel inclined to dispute.

Some of these exchanges are elegantly funny -- those good performances didn't come from nowhere -- and some are acute. There's an especially thoughtful bit about whether we feel ourselves to be "in love" only because it's something we've seen on the screen. (Or read about in books. There's a school of thought that believes romantic love to be something that was invented by the troubadours in the Middle Ages.) The characters all talk movie-smart, and when one of them tells another off for doing this, he does so in words that are equally movie-smart. This attempt to disarm by double bluff is the moment at which it becomes impossible to take the play seriously. And once that happens, it turns tiresome.

Each of the performers gets a monologue. My favourite was Bhaneja's, apparently delivered in a confessional booth, in which he pleads cheerfully guilty to having broken all the major Commandments, plus a few others. ( "I have eaten meat on Sunday -- and enjoyed it; I have worked on Sunday -- and received overtime pay.") Rand, having got the play off to a flying start, continues to inject it with nervous adrenalin, though he does end up repeating some of his effects. He is unexpectedly absent from the play's last section, but maybe his early departure is meant to balance Beaty's late arrival, which has the combined impact of a cold shower and an electric shock. Poised and furious, she blasts Bhaneja for betraying her, Faulkner for letting him, and Rand for hiring her so that she'd find out. She is masterful too in the play's last soliloquy, in which she ( "a mere cutter") lectures on the practical problems and responsibilities of her craft, not to mention its metaphorical significance. She's disappointingly conventional, though, in the jealousy scenes that come in between.

Gina Wilkinson delivers a fast production with one unfortunate side effect; the play's habit of having the first line of a scene echo or contradict the last line of the one before becomes an irritating mannerism. For all his pretensions, what we hear and see of Rand's movie script resembles a weepie from the 1940s; Lorenzo Savoini's set and Bonnie Beecher's lighting are moodily helpful in establishing this. The play is a step back from Gall's previous Tarragon piece, Alias Godot, which was also tricksy and allusive, but with a wider range and more verbal and structural wit. And its characters weren't theatricals. Wide Awake Hearts reinforces my suspicion that, except for musicals and really funny farces, plays about plays, in which I include plays about movies, are a snare and a delusion.
