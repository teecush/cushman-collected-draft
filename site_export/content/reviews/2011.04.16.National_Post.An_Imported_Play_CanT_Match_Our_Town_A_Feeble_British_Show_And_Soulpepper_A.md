A play can be the victim of its own publicity. Or just of its own title. Some first-night audience members at Ghost Stories sounded like they didnt know whether to laugh or scream. They tried doing both, sometimes simultaneously, but seemed very selfconscious about it. As audiences in such circumstances invariably do: see any production of The Rocky Horror Show. This though was worse. There was nothing about the evening at the Panasonic to justify any reaction more violent than the occasional sigh or giggle at the feebleness of it all, plus perhaps the odd murmur of wonderment at what might have happened to a show that crossed the Atlantic with glowing credentials.

Ghost Stories is the joint product of two respected British companies (Lyric Hammersmith, in West London, and the combined Everyman and Playhouse theatres in Liverpool) and it got excellent notices on its own ground. It has a script by two successful British writers, Jeremy Dyson and Andy Nyman, who have co-directed it with Sean Holmes, whose record includes some good work with the Royal Shakespeare Company.

This is the set-up. A man announcing himself as Philip Goodman, professor of parapsychology, gives an illustrated lecture on supra-normal phenomena and the people ("percipients") who claim to have witnessed or experienced or been terrified by them. The professor professes himself skeptical but open to conviction. He replays interviews with three of these percipients, whose stories are acted out in front of us, all of them involving ghoulies and ghosties and (especially) things that go bump in the night. There is a night watchman, spooked by mannequins at the deserted factory he guards and especially by one resembling the brain-damaged daughter whom hes never visited. There's a teenager, out driving his parents' car without a licence, who runs someone over and is, naturally, and perhaps supernaturally, traumatized. And theres an ostentatiously prosperous businessman haunted by his wife who died in childbirth. These peoples guilt-problems, however, have nothing on those experienced, and long repressed, by Prof. Goodman himself, who turns out, in a series of selfcancelling twists, to be the effective subject of his own experiment.

The script has been riddled, for export purposes, with artificial Canadian references patronizing to both the actors (can't they do British accents?) and the audience (are we that insular?). The awkwardness infects the performances, especially that of Jason Blicker, who plays the professor with the air of a man who'd rather be anywhere else. Admittedly, the guy has things to hide, but the actors unease seems way beyond the call of character. Jack Langedijk and David Reale arent bad as his first two exhibits, and Darrin Baker as the yuppie third has moments of being positively good. Each of their anecdotes concludes with something materializing with a loud plop, though without remaining long enough for us to feel anything except frustrated. It took three directors to stage this? Our homegrown cycle The Mill managed to be far scarier with fewer resources, and threw in some substance as well. In short, Ghost Stories is nothing to lose sleep over.

Thornton Wilder's Our Town is also something of a ghost story, with its last act set in a graveyard whose inhabitants either pity or are indifferent to those left behind, and whose most recent arrival is allowed to relive a day from her past. She speedily flees back to the cemetery, not so much a ghost as aghast, at how precious every moment of life seems when you know how briefly it lasts.

The implication, that this knowledge would be intolerable if the living actually had to live with it, has always seemed flimsy to me in the past, but now in the fourth run of Joseph Ziegler's much-loved Soulpepper production, it fully strikes home. Some of this may be due to the arrival of Krystin Pellerin in the role of Emily, the wife who died young and whose adolescence, engagement and wedding we have already witnessed. Radiant, she has already established Emily as someone for whom every thought, word and feeling has a value, so her posthumous devastation is devastating.

The chorus of the longer dead is also polyphonically finer than ever: Nancy Palk serene though hurting, William Webster bitter through hurting, Brenda Robins, Diego Matamoros, a near-invisible Kevin Bundy. The more often you see this play, knowing whats to come, the more moving it is, especially when most of the faces are so familiar. The gentle Palk and astringent Jane Spidell have refined the contrast between their neighbour-mothers, Jeff Lillico plays by my count his third enchanting courtship of Pellerin, and among a fine corps of children, Dominique Matamoros exhibits a presumably hereditary gift of presence and timing. Albert Schultz now practically is the Stage Manager.

What Ive previously described as Soulpepper's feel-good factor can have its sentimental downside, but so far this year -in The Fantasticks, The Time of Your Life and here -it's been at its humane and welcoming best. Two footnotes: In the smaller space next door, the George Brown school is presenting Wilders other major play, The Skin of Our Teeth: a rich educational opportunity (and also, perhaps, a missed marketing opportunity). The Polish holocaust play Our Class has, perhaps surprisingly, more in common with Our Town than a possessive pronoun: Theyre both microcosmic plays, reaching outwards. And a correction: Contrary to what I wrote in my review, Our Class has been produced in Poland (at Teatr Na Woli in Warsaw, where it's still running) though after its world premiere at the National Theatre in London.

- Ghost Stories runs through May 29 at the Panasonic Theatre; Our Town is in repertory at the Young Centre for the Performing Arts, 416-866-0666.
