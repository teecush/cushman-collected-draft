According to a recent article in The New York Times, the apostrophe is an endangered species. Its probably true, but I don't know that I care very much. Or: Its probably true, but I dont know that I care very much. However, as long as apostrophes are around, they should be used right. You can probably see where I'm going with this one. Im going towards its" and "its, and the appalling tendency -- though naturally not by respectable newspapers -- to confuse them. It's appalling not because it's an affront to style, but because its an affront to sense and logic. (Though of course its on sense and logic that good style is based.) The apostrophe, as we all know, signifies an elision; it's" stands in for "it is. There's no need for an apostrophe, and therefore no place, when its" is possessive.

Confusingly, and yes illogically, we do use the apostrophe in other possessive cases. We speak or write of "Henrys hat. That is an elision; its short for Henry his hat," which is what our comparatively remote ancestors would have written. And said, too, probably, but you can bet that they stopped saying it before they stopped writing it. Writing fell into line to reflect speech, which is how things should be. But inserting an apostrophe into "its has no bearing on how anyone pronounces anything. The word sounds the same either way. The admitted inconsistency between the two possessives, its" and "Henry's, may be a good reason for letting the apostrophe die unmourned. Bernard Shaw, starting more than 100 years ago, made a point of doing without those jumped-up commas; he would certainly have written Henrys hat" -- probably did in Pygmalion -- and his prose was superb. Shaw wanted to kill the apostrophe by ignoring it; we, paradoxically, may be hastening its demise by shoving it where it doesn't belong. My guess is that the practice started with blithe ignorance and spread through earnest imitation. Thats how bad style gets around.

Even more infamous things have been done to English in this decade. One of them is the use of the word "infamous. It used to be the opposite of famous"; now it increasingly turns up as something between a synonym and a superlative. So a word we need is turned into one we dont. And people don't know they're doing it; they merely assume that a slightly longer word will be more impressive. The adjective for that is "pretentious.

Another might be cloth-eared." That, anyway, is what comes to my mind when, in another example, I hear people say that they could care less. The phrase means of course that they do care, but that isnt what they mean it to mean. They believe, in defiance of anything they may have been taught about negatives and double negatives, that its equivalent to saying they couldn't care less; which is plainly ridiculous. I've heard this usage defended as being ironic, like saying "I should worry to mean that you dont. The problem with that is that when people say I should worry" they accompany it with a shrug, physical or vocal. Ive never heard anyone say "I could care less ironically. They always say it aggressively. It merely proves that you can be as pretentious by dropping a syllable as by adding one.

And what gets into all the otherwise irreproachable people who tell you that something either beautiful or terrible is happening to my partner and I?" They would never dream of saying that it's "happening to I. But you don't change an object to a subject simply by sticking a few more words in front of it. In theory, its no worse than using me" as a subject -- as in "me and my gang can beat up you and your gang -- but that older solecism is at least forceful. The new one is just flabby.

Yes, I know, language changes, and should. And slang enriches, more often than it debases. That last sentence is the fourth in this article that Ive begun with the word and," something that my schoolteachers forbade me to do even once, and about which I remain completely unrepentant. Elisions, too, other than in dialogue, would have been barred from serious print 50 years ago; now, as you'll have noticed, I can't do without them. But the usages Ive been attacking arent slangy; they're shabby-genteel. Grammar exists for clarity, not the other way about, but these aren't creative attacks on grammar; theyre misguided attempts to seem more grammatical, a linguistic equivalent of political correctness. They dont subvert syntax; they merely misunderstand it.

We're becoming mealy-mouthed. Some blame it on the digital revolution -- all that blogging and texting and twittering, though I'm more inclined to believe the opposing argument, which is that people are writing again, maybe more than ever before, and that that's a good thing. Of course there's more bad writing than good; there always has been. The only difference is that both bad and good are now more visible. And sometimes the bad fades away. Twenty years ago, we were rightly alarmed at the constant misuse of the word "hopefully." Now it seems to have disappeared. Its over; hopefully.
