HEDWIG AND THE ANGRY INCH

Bathurst Street Theatre, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - A short distance into Hedwig and The Angry Inch, I was shocked. This was not for any of the reasons that you or the creators of the show might expect.

It was not because of Hedwig, who is a German-born American- based former rock star reduced to playing a seedy dive in Toronto (impersonated, very convincingly, by the Bathurst Street Theatre) while her former lover headlines at a huge downtown stadium that Hedwig refers to as the SkyLab; all this I could take or leave. It was not because of The Angry Inch who is/are Hedwig's band, named after a small fleshy protuberance of which Hedwig, who was once Helmut, found herself in unwilling possession after undergoing a slightly incomplete sex-change operation. I am notoriously squeamish, but this job has hardened me and I bore the anatomical details, as detailed in speech and song, without so much as an inward shudder. It wasn't even because a show that has aroused terrific enthusiasm in New York and elsewhere now looks and sounds terrible; these things happen, often. No, what got me in the marrow of my bones was Hedwig's repeated complaints about her agent, whose name, she had the gall to announce, was Phyllis Stein.

Do you remember Phyllis? She is a splendid Toronto lady, well- bred and broad-minded, whose opinion I have sometimes solicited regarding some of the loucher entertainments around town. I was consulting her only last month about Closer, after which I felt she deserved a rest. But, after hearing her name bandied about from a public stage, I had no choice but to put in another call.

This is how it went.

ME (tentatively): Hello, Phyllis. Is it ... true?

PHYLLIS (suspiciously cheery): Hello, darling. Is what true?

ME: Don't bother. You've answered my question. You never called me darling before, and certainly not in italics. Phyllis, I don't want to sound like someone in a rock musical but you've betrayed me. You've gone over to the other side. You're in the show business. And you never told me.

PHYLLIS (a light laugh): A girl has to live. And I've always fancied the glamour. I thought some of it might rub off on me. Though mind you, when I met Hedwig, I thought it might be better if some of mine rubbed off on her. But I like to do a good turn when I can. And Hedwig needed one.

ME: You can say that again. But let's get this, if you'll pardon the paradox, straight. The person we see on stage is not Hedwig. The person we see on stage is Ted Dykstra, noted Canadian actor. And in New York, I gather, it was someone else.

PHYLLIS (nodding -- or so I assume): John Cameron Mitchell. He wrote the script. And Stephen Trask -- he wrote the songs -- was in the band. Such talented boys. They asked if they could use my name, and how could I refuse? They said it was just right for an agent.

ME: That sounds like their sense of fun. Just remember I saw you first. In Toronto, anyway. So you liked the show in New York?

PHYLLIS: Darling, I loved it. Such flash. And did you see the quote from Rolling Stone: "The first rock musical that truly rocks!"

ME: Well, they should know. The trouble is that rocking is the only movement it has. When Ted Dykstra sings his songs -- and he sings all of them -- all he does is prowl from one side of the stage to the other and back again. Like an actor impersonating a rock singer. And flash? The only visual element apart from Hedwig's drag - - and to be fair he does look about 14 feet tall when he first comes on -- is a series of slides depicting childhood drawings that Hedwig did back when she was Helmut. He was not, it seems, a visually gifted child. And they persist long after he's grown up, changed gender, changed country. Look, you've quoted Rolling Stone at me. Can I quote Stephen Sondheim to you?

PHYLLIS: If you have to.

ME: "Rock music is fine for rock concerts. Whether they're rock concerts called rock concerts or rock concerts called Hair or Jesus Christ Superstar ..." This was some time ago, you understand, but nothing has changed. Hedwig is a rock concert with unusually long monologues between the songs and I suppose it deserves credit for not pretending to be anything else. Except it is in a theatre, which arouses expectations. Now we're always being told that rock concerts have all the visual and visceral excitement that the theatre has lost. If that's true, I can't see that the answer is for the theatre to imitate what another medium is already doing better. But anyway, nobody seems to have told Jim Millan, who directed this one; it's drab. And meanwhile, as always, the score destroys any chance of developing story or character. And have you noticed -- unless they're outright camp like The Rocky Horror Show -- all rock musicals, however flippantly they start out, invariably end up with the hero taking the sins of the world on his shoulders? Think of Tommy. It's no accident that the first rock opera was JC Superstar. To those of us who never thought of rock stars as messiahs in the first place, it's pretty ludicrous. Especially since the volume of the music is always far in excess of any conceivable level of emotion in the drama.

PHYLLIS: But you're missing the real pain here ... Hedwig's divided body and divided nature, symbolizing the schism in all of us. It's no accident that she comes from Berlin ...

ME: And compares herself to the Wall. I know. Childish and pretentious, as usual. And yes, to save you the trouble of saying so, it's not my kind of music. But that's not the point. And in fact I've heard the New York cast CD, and I liked some of it. It has variety, and an occasional country sound -- Hedwig goes through a period as trailer trash -- and country tends to work dramatically, however limited it may be musically. And you can hear the words, or at least read them. (Which is the big rock cop-out, but we won't go into that.) And Mitchell as a singer has lyricism. Dykstra just bashes through everything, helped or hindered by the sound system. He's better when he talks -- he's a good actor -- but he doesn't have the real drag-queen glitter that the script presumes. But then neither does the script. There's one funny line.

PHYLLIS: Which I suppose you're going to be shameless enough to quote?

ME: You bet. Hedwig comes on wearing a fur, and says that some woman had asked her, "What poor creature had to die for you to wear that?" and that she replied, "My aunt Trudi." By the way, isn't Hedwig a Scandinavian name rather than a German one?

PHYLLIS: Don't stick your neck out. It might get chopped off.

ME: Ouch. Hedwig mentions other people but of course they never appear, and her patter doesn't bring them to life. Not her -- er, his -- mother, nor the American who seduced him in New York and procured the operation so he could marry her, nor Tommy Gnosis, her protege and nemesis -- he's the one who's supposed to be appearing at SkyDome. You'd think it might be fun to let us hear one of his numbers, so we could compare, but not a chance. Which reminds me, Phyllis, I have to ask. Remember, I didn't see the New York production. Were you Hedwig's agent there, too, or were you just dropped in here as another dab of local colour?

PHYLLIS: Darling, my lips are sealed.
