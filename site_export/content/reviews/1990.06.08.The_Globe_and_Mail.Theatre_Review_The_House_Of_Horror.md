THEATRE REVIEW THE HOUSE OF HORROR

Friday, June 08, 1990

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Written, directed and performed by Paul Zaloom At the du Maurier World Stage in Toronto A comic-strip view of the human race destroying itself BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail PAUL ZALOOM is a name with possibilities. You can imagine it - the Zaloom part, anyway - spattered across a strip-cartoon panel, as one of the sounds of mayhem. It goes well with his form of theatre, which is a comic- strip view of the human race destroying itself.

Zaloom functions as actor, puppeteer and stand-up comedian. There can be no question of his energy. Nor is there any doubt about his moral credentials: He opposes nothing of which anyone in his audience would conceivably be in favor. According to his publicity, he was against pollution long before most people sensed that there was anything funny in the air. Now that they know, he can hardly help seeming a bit conventional.

The show he has brought from New York to Toronto's Harbourfront (where it opened Wednesday as part of the du Maurier World Stage) is in three parts, of which the second is by far the best. It is also much the shortest. Let us save it till last, so as to end on an up beat.

First, anyway, comes the title-piece. The House of Horror turns out to be a doll house: necessarily, since it is inhabited by puppets. They are glove-puppets, manipulated by a fully visible Zaloom. He enters in protective clothing: a mask and what look like yellow oilskins but are likely something far more chemically sinister. He needs his armor, because the house is a deathtrap.

A Mr. and Mrs. Smith are being shown over it by a Bermuda-shirted real- estate salesman. They take it on an afternoons trial, and proceed to do incautious things like breathing and sitting on the furniture. Mrs. Smiths hair falls out, her already horrible twins - they look like two of the three little pigs - develop rashes (identical, as they disgustedly note), the baby disappears, and grandma sprouts a split personality. This last symptom is niftily represented by having her turned upside-down and inside-out, to reveal a totally different face, dress and manner.

There is a peculiar rushed tempo that afflicts puppeteers and ventriloquists when they change voices. It is as if they can hardly trust themselves to get plausibly from one characterization to another, and hope that the quickness of the tongue may deceive the ear. (Impressionists have the same problem.) This is bearable when conversation is just something that happens between one man and his dummy. But when the jerky rhythm is imposed on an entire sitcom it becomes intolerably scrappy.

Zaloom likes to draw attention to his own shortcomings. Calls himself a puppeteer, complains one of his creations. "You can see his lips move when I talk." (You bet.) This kind of self-deprecation is funny, but not really disarming, maybe because it's all that is funny. At one point the doorbell plays O Canada and Zaloom says: Canadian content. It's the law. This got the loudest laugh of the evening. Its nice of him to try to make us feel at home, especially when hes the visitor, but even ingratiation has its limits. Meanwhile, his satire suffers; usually a puppeteer's characters take on independent life, but these remain puppets.

The last segment, entitled Yikes! is in a form that Zaloom seems to have invented: the Theatre of the Found Object. He mounts three little parables - on Peace, Justice and Religion in America - and peoples them with things. An egg carton packed full of eggs represents a progressively intimidated jury. The President of the United States, knuckling under to the military, is a shrub. These are neat visual puns, but neither they nor the stories they fill out are bold enough to justify the complacent aren't-we-being-irreverent vibes that shuttle between stage and auditorium.

Only in the religious section do things perk up. The situation is an ecumenical conference with the Archbishop of Canterbury played by a feather, a Cardinal by a piggybank, and a homburg giving a richly ethnic performance as a rabbi. This has some good jokes, and is fast as opposed to frenetic.

The brief, effective middle piece is called Phood, and is announced as a world premiere. Here Zaloom takes the stage as the glad-handing host at a manufacturers convention for the promotion of what he calls "food-like substances." His foils here are blow-ups of genuine promo literature, and hilariously horrendous they are. (How do you fancy Its Not Lobster, cooked in Not Butter?) Zaloom discovered, assembled and now underlines them. This is an accomplishment, as well as a public service, but the essential comic work has, of course, been done for him by his targets.

The late Jim Henson was apparently a great admirer of Zaloom's, so there must be more in him than I can see. But Sesame Streeters will know what I mean when I say that though this may indeed be Theatre of Trash, it hardly merits an Oscar. Zaloom!
