Some plays begin by assailing the audiences eyes. A few make their first appeal through the ear. David Frenchs Leaving Home is among the select few to make their initial impact by way of the nose. The pleasant aroma of cooking fish assaults our nostrils as we take our places for Soulpepper's revival, the first Toronto production, surprisingly, that this Canadian textbook classic has received since its premiere in 1972. The cooking is done in a fully equipped kitchen, stage right; the rest is living- cum-dining room. The phrase kitchen sink drama is always taken to be synonymous with squalor, economic and possibly moral, but in fact when sinks, stoves and fridges do appear on a stage, it's a sign that were dealing, at the very least, with the respectable working class, and very likely with the aspiring lower middle. The people in Leaving Home have elements of both.

It was the first of Frenchs cycle of plays concerning the Mercer family who came from Newfoundland to live in Toronto but never really felt comfortable there. Jacob, the father, resents nearly everyone, and especially his two sons, Ben and Billy, the former of whom has driven the wedge in further by going to university. Mary, Jacob's wife, is a peacemaker but by no means a doormat. Young Billy has got his girlfriend pregnant (they've played Vatican roulette and lost), and the pair are set to marry on the day of the play. Joining them for a pre-nuptial lunch is the brides mother, another transported Newfoundlander and an old flame of Jacobs; she brings along her latest boyfriend, an undertaker whom she nicknames Lazarus because "at night he rises from the dead." (She talks that way a lot.) The elders in this play are masters and mistresses of the fine parental art of making their children want to sink through the floor.

When it was first produced, Leaving Home was hailed as a beacon of the new Canadian drama. Anywhere but in Canada it would have seemed fearfully old-fashioned: a box-set family drama working its way to a neat resolution. Compare what Thomas Murphy, another first- timer, was doing in Ireland with family tensions in A Whistle in the Dark a decade before. To its credit, French's play doesn't pretend that its most serious problems are soluble, but it still manages a final epiphany that seems unconnected to whats preceded it. But it also has the old-fashioned virtues of involvement and suspense. Once past the necessary lumps of exposition, it sinks its hooks in and never lets go.

I was tempted to say it has the virtues of a well-made play, but in fact its rather oddly made. The title refers to Ben's decision to move out of his parents' house, a plan that he divulges to his mother early on and that she accurately predicts will drive his father wild. Never mind that Dad himself has often suggested that its time for his son to act like a man and be independent; as far as hes concerned, anything that Ben says or does must be wrong, and he's perfectly capable of yelling that any of his previous remarks on the subject shouldn't be dredged up and held against him. This kind of compulsive wrong-footing within families is painfully familiar (and hardly confined to fathers and sons), but Im not convinced that the peculiarly nasty example of it that French presents here is really, as he seems to want us to think, a mask for deep, inexpressible affection. Whatever: This relationship is the heart of the play, and the rest of it begins to seem like an elaborate irrelevance. In theory, one can see that the plight of Billy and his bride, who have grave misgivings about their shotgun marriage, is a variation on the theme; they, too, have urgent reasons for leaving home. But their relationship is only sketched in and is abandoned just when it seems that it might get interesting.

Ted Dykstras production, on a dream of a realistic set by Patrick Clark, is cunningly staged and finely orchestrated. There is a surprising amount of humour, and some inspirational casting. Kenneth Welsh's paterfamilias is magnificent, or it is if you overlook the trifling fact of some three-quarters of his lines being, at least to my immigrant ears, incomprehensible. But from underneath all the gabbling and the slurring (which, among other things, obscures what seems to be a major theme about the hostility between Protestants and Catholics), there emerges a man in whom anger and bewilderment have overridden anything else. Welsh finds a rhythm of resentment into which everything fits: spasms of bonhomie; brief excursions on the dance floor; ghastly, distorted chop-logic; bouts of physical violence. It's impossible to approve of him, almost impossible to like him, but we get a very clear sense of the barriers hes beating against; Welsh practically erects them onstage. His chief antagonists labour under the disadvantage of being as consistently right as he is wrong: Diane DAquila occasionally hits a discordantly plaintive note but her Mary is otherwise suitably rocklike, while Jeff Lillico does a very good job of stating the case for flight while wishing that he didn't have to.

As the mother-in-law-to-be, Jane Spidell is a revelation; she has always been a fastidious actress, and she still is, but her inner poise here enables her to present a late-blooming hedonist whose demonstrativeness embarrasses everyone around her while delighting us. Her mortician beloved, Harold (a.k.a. the formaldehyde kid), is the indispensable Oliver Dennis; he never gets to speak a word but in his black-clad lanky silence, a drink never out of his hand, he suggests that a great comic creation has waited 35 years to find proper expression. "I think," says Jacob at a moment of uproar, "that we should all take an example from Harold," and it's to Denniss credit that its the most explosively funny line in the play. Anthony Johnston and Martha MacIsaac do their best for the underwritten not-so-happy couple; it isn't MacIsaac's fault (though it may, on consideration, be the productions) that the scene in which she proves herself her mothers daughter by running her hand up the imperturbable Harold's leg seems to belong to some other character in some other play. Sex, in this play, is pretty much beneath the surface, and it should probably stay there.
