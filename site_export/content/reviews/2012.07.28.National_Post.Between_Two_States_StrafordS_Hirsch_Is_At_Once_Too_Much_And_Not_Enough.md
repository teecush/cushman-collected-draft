Alon Nashman, an actor I greatly admire, is appearing at Stratford in a one-man play about John Hirsch, a director whose memory I revere. I wish I liked the results more than I do.

There can be no quarrel with Nashman's actual performance of Hirsch, which is nicely poised between creation and impersonation. He has some moments of being moving, and more of being very funny. The undoubted highlight of Hirsch comes when the director, rehearsing The Tempest during his tumultuous reign as head of the festival, puts the actor playing Caliban through his paces, and also through hell, when trying to get his first entrance right. He isn't showing enough anger at Prospero. He isn't showing enough lust towards Miranda. He isn't showing that, perversely, he really loves both of them. And as if all that weren't enough, the director can hardly hear him. (Do you realize, Hirsch asks parenthetically, just how much it costs to rehearse you? Do you know what we're paying Len Cariou?) One feels for the unfortunate actor, and there's an added piquancy if you happen to know that he was Miles Potter, himself to become a prominent Stratford director in later years. The point is made that Hirsch was a tyrant, could be a bully, but was a tyrant and a bully with insights and an inspirational fervour; by the end of the scene he has got some results, and expresses himself moderately pleased with them. You get the feeling that his rehearsals must have been great fun if you weren't the one being shot at, and maybe even if you were. In retrospect, anyway.

Hirsch, as is well known, was a Hungarian orphan of the Holocaust who emigrated to Canada when still a boy, and was both haunted and driven ever after by the sense of what, both personally and culturally, he had lost and yearned in some way to restore. In another of this show's best scenes, we find the stateless hero, standing at one consulate after another, answering the same questions, taking the same blood tests. It's pure chance that he was standing in line for Canada, which wasn't high on his list, when there happened to be a vacancy. Given a choice of destinations, he opted for Winnipeg because it seemed to be in the centre geographically and therefore safe. Growing up there, he was able to put it in the cultural centre as well. The Manitoba Theatre Centre, which he founded, became the benchmark for regional theatres all over Canada.

You can glean that from this show. But the piece itself - devised by Nashman and Paul Thompson, and directed by Thompson - is both scattershot and cumbersome. It leaves a lot out, maybe because it is trying to get too much in. There's a whole outer layer in which Nashman appears as himself, wondering how to set about doing justice to his subject. Halfway through he decides he hasn't managed it, and tries a new tack. This involves him half-reciting, half-chanting, a verse narrative, full of amusingly forced rhymes. (Hirsch, we are told, went wandering the earsch.) In terms of what we actually learn, though, there's very little difference. If anything, the narrative gets scrappier. Earlier on, Nashman has interrupted the story to broach the question of how, and when, a Jew can tell his children about the Holocaust. It's a good question, but it has little to do with Hirsch, who didn't have any children. This entire superstructure (or second-guessing second level, call it what you like) is either too much or not enough.

The staging further slows things down. In an effort to link Hirsch's life with his work, the production brings on Mother Courage's cart, referencing both a play he directed and the memories he was fated to drag behind him. Nashman is attended by a couple of stage-hands who bring him props and changes of costume; they loom so large that they seem on the verge of taking out Equity membership.

There's proper tribute to Hirsch's production of the Yiddish classic The Dybbuk, which inspired the youthful Nashman and so may be have said to have lit the long fuse that led to this performance. Dybbuk certainly ranks among the finest things I've ever seen in the Canadian theatre; so do Hirsch's later Stratford productions of Tartuffe and As You Like It, which go unmentioned here. You can't have everything, but Hirsch's own love-hate relation with the festival is under-explored. We do get snapshots of his time at the CBC, and a very entertaining scene in a New York diner, drawn from Hirsch's time working at Lincoln Center and enabling Nashman to create three characters while actually playing only one of them.

Towards the end we find Hirsch in Israel writing to someone, previously unmentioned, called "Brian." We have to wait for a still later scene to discover that Brian was actually Hirsch's lover, and we don't get much insight into the relationship even then. It's even more surprising that we are told literally nothing about the family with whom he was billeted in Winnipeg in a trial arrangement that, emotionally, turned out to last a lifetime; his surrogate mother was arguably the most important person in his life.

There are glimpses of his biological family in Europe that are first charming and then, for obvious reasons, wrenching. It's a miracle that he himself escaped; and, for Canada and its theatre, a blessing. To date, only Robert Lepage has challenged him as the greatest director ever to hold a Canadian passport. He's in danger of being undervalued, perhaps even forgotten, and it's good that he is receiving this tribute. It's heartfelt, and thoughtful, and not at all sentimental. But, not having a shape or a structure, it remains anecdotal.

Hirsch is in repertory through Sept. 14 at the Studio Theatre, Stratford. Call 1-800-567-1600 or visit stratfordfestival.ca for ticket information.
