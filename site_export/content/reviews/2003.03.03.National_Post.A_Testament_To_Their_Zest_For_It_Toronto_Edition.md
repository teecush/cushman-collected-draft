JOB: THE HIP-HOP MUSICAL

Tarragon Extra Space, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - It gets harder and harder for a conscientious critic to find shows to which he can take his teenage children; the theatre may not be changing but they are. But Job, surnamed The Hip-Hop Musical, seemed a likely bet. I was told in advance that it contained no sex and no bad language, but I took them anyway.

They liked it. So did I. But I have to record that on the way out they remarked to me that, after an hour of listening to rap, they wanted desperately to hear some other kind of music -- any other kind of music. This, considering the sounds that pound through our house for much of the day and night, came as a surprise. (Driving home, I took them at their word, and subjected them to a standard- issue 1950s musical of no special distinction called By the Beautiful Sea. That, I thought, would learn 'em.)

As well as being an all-but-all-rap musical -- the first ever to cross my sheltered path -- Job, based on the Book of the same name, is a two-actor musical, the second to hit this town in as many weeks. The first was the comparatively conventional This Could Be Love, with two actors playing a character apiece in somebody else's script. In this case, the performers, Jerome Saibil and Eli Batalion, are also, as they would probably have to be, the authors; they wrote the lyrics and collaborated with Paul Bercovitch on the, well, music. Between them they play a multiplicity of roles, several of which they share. On at least one occasion a character passes from the care of one to that of the other, right in the middle of a scene.

All of which is very high-energy, high-pace and high-definition. Saibil and Batalion are not, at least according to the stereotypes that dwell in my head, your everyday rappers. They are white Jewish boys (they're both 22) from Montreal, and former scriptural and philosophy students to boot. Their lyrics throw in insouciant references to Hegel's Phenomenology and Immanuel Kant; and their subject matter, as already stated, comes from the Old Testament. In this very contemporary version, the prosperous and then inordinately long-suffering patriarch becomes Job Low, a rising record-company executive who abruptly stops rising. Jehovah is his boss, J. Hoover, of Hoover Records. Satan, a.k.a. Lucifer, is Lou Safire, VP in charge of finance, who snakily persuades the old man to test Job's company loyalty by stripping him of perks, shares, dental plan and most of his salary: everything in fact that makes the corporate life worth living.

This Job's tormentors stop short of giving him boils, though chances are they would if they could. But their incapacity in this regard does suggest a slight disconnect between the old story and the new setting. Nobody, whatever their opinion of the corporate culture and our dependence on it, thinks that it's in the business of dispensing divine justice. We may naively expect our employers to treat us fairly, but that's as far as it goes. It isn't even mildly surprising to find a music-business czar acting like a capricious, conceited and sadistic monster.

There are some who would say that the exceptionally mysterious ways pursued by the God of the Book of Job could well expose him to the same charges. But it's also true that most people find that idea worrying, and try to find some explanation for it. If you believe in God, you would like to think that the universe makes some kind of sense. Belief in music moguls who have turned their entire catalogue over to hip hop has no such corollary.

Batalion and Saibil seem somewhat aware of this, and even play with it a bit. Their primary personae in the show are a couple of squabbling narrators, known as MC Cain and MC Abel. The end of the show finds them arguing intensely (and just out of sight, which for some reason makes it funnier) about the moral, if any, of the story they have just told. Finally, they give up and revert to fratricidal type. This conclusion, which seems irritatingly abrupt at the time, makes more sense the more you think about it.

As it happens, Saibil and Batalion, or Cain and Abel, are often at their most endearing when they step out of their neo-Biblical roles and simply bitch at one another. Each is especially critical of the quality of the other's rhymes. What, one of them wants to know are "knishes" doing in there? (I think they go with "wishes" -- it's hard to keep track -- but I agree, it's far-fetched.) I suspect, though, that they and I would have our differences over what constitutes a good rhyme. To crib from the lyrical extracts from their score that were helpfully reproduced in last Tuesday's National Post: "Orwellian Twist" and "Machiavellian Twist" make an excellent rhyme; "launch" and "hunch" make a terrible rhyme. You can hardly deny the guys' zest and ingenuity in keeping the jingles coming, or at the musical sampling (Beatles to Beethoven) with which they vary them, but even at its best, the style gets oppressive.

The rhymes, the puns, the omnipresent short lines leave very little air, which is what my kids were complaining about. By the time the show got round to Job's comforters -- three sycophantic execs -- it had lost us, or at least me, though an earlier, and even oilier, board-meeting scene works very well. Characterization varies. The show can hardly to be said to make the best of a good Job; in fact, you never feel an atom of sympathy for him. But his wife -- a headscarved Hamptonian whose rise to academic tenure counterpoints his fall -- is a creation, and so is Hoover, who as befits his name is a vacuum, and goes around like an ambulant Tower of Pisa. Both these characters are alive, whichever of the dynamic duo is playing them.

The piece, charmingly unpretentious as it is, may turn out to be the high-water mark of the rap musical, just as that less intellectual Biblical treat Joseph and the Amazing Technicolour Dreamcoat was of the rock musical. (Joseph made fun of other pop- music forms. Job makes fun of its own. This may be preferable. It certainly seems more honest.) Saibil and Batalion are already at work on Job II, though they seem to have exhausted the original story. Maybe the theatre is changing.
