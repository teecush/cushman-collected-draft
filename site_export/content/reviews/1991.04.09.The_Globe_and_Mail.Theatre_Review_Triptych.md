THEATRE REVIEW" TRIPTYCH

Tuesday, April 09, 1991

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Toronto ONT -- Presented by Crow's Theatre At the Poor Alex Theatre, Toronto Half a dozen actors gather together to play all the roles in three short plays which, unfortunately, run far too long Childishness portrayed in three too many stages BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Toronto THIS is indeed a cryptic Triptych. Crow's Theatre has assembled three short pieces - each runs just under an hour - and handed them over to the same half-dozen actors to play back-to-back-to-back. No common theme is declared, though one that suggests itself is childishness.

We lead off with "6": six the title, six the number of actors, six the age they play at being. The six characters have found eight authors, who have pooled their memories, their observations, and their inventiveness in order to re-create the world of first-grade. First, as adults, they reminisce about that age of innocence, or at any rate of unselfconsciousness: the days when, as one of them memorably says, "I never broke eye-contact." Then, having fought their way out from underneath a large tarpaulin, they start in on impersonation, and on a lightning series of group vignettes. It is reasonably well done, but it never progresses beyond agreeable cuteness. It arouses impatience, but not hostility.

Having rampaged around as children, the actors move on in the second piece to playing grown-ups. This they do with far less conviction. There is not a moment of weight, style, or even simple skill in the entire performance: doubly unfortunate since they are supposed to be playing a tankful of media barracudas. We may be dealing here with an ensemble of total deadheads, but that is always unlikely (and their work in the surrounding playlets does not, on the whole, suggest it.) Maybe it is the fault of the director Blake Brooker, who fails to create anything in the way of atmosphere or tension, and who constantly leaves his actors hanging around in unhappy poses. Nor are they much helped by the set, a wall of silver foil inset with television screens.

Then there is the play. It is called Prince, and Jim Millan, Crow's Artistic Director, wrote it. He posits a young TV magnate appointed by a reactionary senator to the chairmanship of the CBC with explicit instructions to destroy it. The author can hardly have foreseen the elevation of Professor Crispo but events could hardly have conspired to make his theme more pertinent. In fact he neglects it completely. Prince, the whizz-kid mogul, sits ensconced in his office playing mind-games (and occasional body-games) with everyone who strays within his orbit. Here we are again with the memoirs of a maleficent megalomaniac, being told nothing that we have not heard a hundred times before.

Anything to praise? A sequence in which Prince is interviewed by the National shows a fair command of obsequious telespeak; and Tom Nardi, who plays him, has the interesting look of a young slicked-down Dustin Hoffman. I could call the thing half-baked, but I'm in no mood for compliments.

In the last segment, Brownie From Hell, Millan reverts to directing and does a respectable if ultimately aimless job. This piece, like both the others, starts with a possibly bright idea that it fails to develop. The baking Brownie is of the Girl Scout variety, and met an early death on a guided tour of a hot dog factory. Now she sits and ponders what might have happened to her had she been allowed to grow up. From what she can see, a woman's life is still not much, the whole sex having been conditioned by their childhood reading to settle for domesticity and dependence. Both in books and (therefore) out of them, boys have all the fun.

She is very likely right, though personally I would back Jane Eyre to beat up Oliver Twist any day. I cite these books since they are among the few she doesn't. Some selectivity might not hurt. Nor is there any particular reason for the case to be made by a brownie - though, to return to where we came in, it helps the cuteness-quotient.

Carol Sinclair, who wrote the piece, also plays the lead and she is good at being wickedly gleeful. Her acting colleagues climb out of various holes to offer support. Either they are heckling demons or illustrative figments from stories, movies or TV series. The latter catagories are there to bring the cultural argument up to date; though if you are going to make What Ever Happened to Baby Jane a prime exhibit, it might help to draft an actress who can impersonate Bette Davis. The woods must be full of them.

Not a very fulfiling evening. As Cockney cabdrivers used to say when they strayed into Hollywood movies, Cor, lumme guv'nor, stone the flippin' Crows.
