CABARET

The New Yorker Theatre, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - According to the program, this production of Cabaret was "inspired by" the one directed by Sam Mendes in 1998 in New York. To be honest, there isn't much inspiration on offer at the New Yorker but the lineage is certainly recognizable. Cabaret has been on a downward slope ever since Hal Prince's brilliant original staging in 1966. Prince's production was about the rise of Nazism in 1930 Berlin; Mendes' was about how deliciously naughty we are; Jordan Allison's is about how deliciously naughty we would like to be and how heavy-handedly we go about it.

Allison is a 20-year-old who has raised nearly half-a-million dollars to get this show on and to direct it himself. Hats are off to him for his enterprise, but maybe even that much money isn't enough to get this show looking right in even a small commercial house. The costumes are schlumpy beyond the call of the Depression (just what is that dress Fraulein Schneider wears to her engagement party?) and the conceptual conceit that the whole show is taking place within the confines of the Kit Kat Klub -- that every character is a cabaret-turn and every scene a number -- never stands a chance, for the stage is just a neutral space. Merely putting the musicians on stage doesn't cut it; lots of musicals do that. So the scenes in Fraulein Schneider's boarding-house, which we have no alternative but to take literally, are more than usually confusing. You never know whether you're in a room or a passage.

Nor has the budget been stretched to employ actors who can do it. People have always complained about this show that the performers cast for Sally Bowles and the Master of Ceremonies -- even those who are actors rather than musical-theatre people -- have far too much star-quality to have ever been working at a dive like the Kit Kat. Well, not this time. Neither here has a spark of wit, and both galumph through their roles. Barbara-Lynn Redpath's characterization stops short at a horsey English accent. A smudged carbon of innumerable previous Sallies, she does just one thing that's original -- she sings the title-song tremulous and choked-up rather than defiant, as if really torn at the prospect of parting from Clifford. It doesn't work, but it was worth a try. Stephen Sheffer also shines in one song, his soft, defeated crooning of I Don't Care Much. Otherwise, his emcee is epicene by numbers.

Brian Wigg's Cliff is intelligent if colourless; though he would be wise to talk his way through his four lines of song. (They are all that remain to him in this latest re-ordering of the John Kander- Fred Ebb score. Credit Allison at least with cutting Mein Herr and Maybe This Time, two songs from the movie that have been creeping into theatre editions and have no business there.) Allan Price, though his accent is more French than German, has a decent go at Herr Schultz, but Julia DeSotto is a bossy Fraulein Schneider, devoid of soft spots. The more she bellows about her vulnerability, the less we believe her. None of the principals has much of a voice; more importantly, none is very adept at putting over a number.

I don't know why the production has given star-billing to two unknowns (Sheffer and Redpath) in a cast of other unknowns. This is supposed to be an ensemble piece. It was a bad day for Cabaret when the emcee became the star role. If the show is about any individual characters, it's about Sally and Cliff. The emcee isn't a character at all; he's the compere who steals the show but -- as I pointed out last time this musical hit town -- you can't steal a show that's already been handed to you.

Dancing in the club numbers consists of simulating sexual intercourse in various positions: A narrowing-down of options that must, so to speak, have taken a lot of heat off the choreographer, Monica Morrow. She does vary the pattern at the beginning of the second act by having the chorus adopt a swastika formation, like Springtime for Hitler without the laughs. (Well, unfortunately, not without the laughs.) The staging of the famous, or perhaps notorious, Gorilla song -- "if you could see her through my eyes/ She wouldn't look Jewish at all" -- is crass. It's done as a fast tap number. The lulling seductiveness of evil -- which is what the song is about, what the show is about -- flies out the window.

Which raises the question: What is all the giggling and squealing for?As Prince staged it, the cabaret was meant to be a lure, a symbol of the forgetfulness that made fascism possible. The idea was moralistic, in the American way, but it had its own logic and followed it through. Since then, what the movie famously called "divine decadence" has become an end in itself; something with which to titillate, and then to shock. Recent productions come close to blaming Weimar nightlife for the Nazis, which is absurd. The flamboyantly gay emcee is identified with the forces that, as he must have known, would destroy him. As they do: In a closing image that was invented by Mendes and looks like it's becoming standard, our host comes before us as a concentration-camp victim. In that earlier, slicker production, this seemed like a desperate attempt to top all the evening's previous shock-effects. In this more cloddish version it seems, oddly, more honest.

And the production has developed some power in its second half; the single stone through the window of Herr Schultz, the Jewish greengrocer, remains more frightening than any of the painted menaces in the club routines. (At this moment, it's possibly more frightening than it's ever been.) It's a tribute to Kander and Ebb and to their librettist, Joe Masteroff, that their material, even in these reduced circumstances, still seems immensely strong. And there, since I can feel my own candour ebbing and since most of those concerned are very young, I shall stop.
