AN EVENING WITH HERSHEY FELDER

George Weston Recital Hall, Toronto

A most peculiar night at the lovely George Weston. Even its title was indeterminate. The program called it An Evening with Hershey Felder, but Felder didn't arrive until halfway through the proceedings. The marquee insisted the name of the attraction was Noahs Ark, but the musicians didnt even come in two by two.

Noah's Ark was in fact the centrepiece of the evening. It is a very earnest-sounding musical on which Felder and his partner, Kim Campbell, who has had some high-profile day jobs, have been at work since 1997. They presented us with some excerpts from it, he conducting and she narrating. It is a retelling of the Old Testament story that seems to lay heavy stress on the fate of the overwhelming percentage of the world's population that was left behind to drown, and on the general unfairness of it all. Noah also has cause to meditate on the unworthiness of his son Ham, who always seems to be the villain in any telling of this tale and who is here caught trying to seduce his brother Shems wife; he gets on board, just by having the right family connections.

The animals also seem perturbed by the fact that most members of their various species are about to be wiped out; they sang two songs but, survivors or victims, they were all drowned, by the orchestra. Felder wrote some advanced nursery-rhyme music for them, which sounded effective. His songs for the humans were less happy; James Barbour as Noah bellowed undramatically for ages in the very accents of Andrew Lloyd Webber: the anguished Webber, unfortunately, of the Phantom of the Opera rather than the sprightly one of Joseph and the Amazing Technicolor Dreamcoat, which remains the No. 1 Genesis musical. Christianne Tisdale, as the beleaguered Mrs. Shem, was handed an especially insipid song lamenting the fate of her neighbours: "As I shall live and they shall die/I have no words to say goodbye."

The best thing to be said of Campbells rhymes is that they do truly rhyme, but they do so very predictably. There are one or two powerful images of catastrophe, but this whole skeptical approach to the biblical story is not as new as the authors seem to think. I don't know how the show might stage; its characters seem to do an awful lot of standing around emoting, but there would obviously be opportunities for spectacle. It could be more titanic than Titanic.

Felder, a dark-haired, compact and unforgivably youthful multi- talent, also gave us extracts from his recent Broadway show (he acts, too), George Gershwin Alone. I had not much warmed to what I had read of this, but now I would like to see it. It appears to be a biography of Gershwin fleshed out not only with his music, but with his own, or presumably his own, spoken and written words. Felder gave an excited commentary on Bess You Is My Woman Now, proudly describing how the music supports the text, while the song itself was patiently performed by Barbour and Tisdale. (Is this how it was done on Broadway? I thought it was a one-man show.) Felder had the very feel of Gershwin, innocently egocentric, both in his speech and his show-off playing of Rhapsody in Blue. He also led us in a singalong.

Before this, the same two singers demonstrated some of the pitfalls of performing Broadway songs out of context in a concert hall. Nobody ever knows how, or how much, to act them. While listening to his partner sing her half of I Loved You, from Carousel, Barbour stood with his hands in his pockets, to illustrate Billy Bigelow's feigned indifference; he has played the part onstage and I suspect the stance was carried over. In the theatre, though, he would have the chance to move around a bit. But in the wake of the National Theatre production of Carousel, this whole stolid approach to Rodgers and Hammerstein seems very outdated. Barbour's performance of Make Believe, from Show Boat, was extremely strange; some of his vowel sounds were grotesque, and at times he seemed to have only a glancing acquaintance with the melody. Tisdale sang sweetly in both selections.

The first half of the evening (without Hershey Felder) was devoted to Leonard Bernstein. The National Academy Orchestra, dynamically conducted by Boris Brott, played the most succulent of musical comedy lollipops, the overture to Candide, and really extended themselves in a suite of dances from West Side Story, which includes nearly all the best tunes. They even got the finger clicks and the shouts of "Mambo!" right, and I think I enjoyed the music more than I ever have in the theatre. Between these orchestral encounters, Glenn White, a superb young lyric baritone, sang Maria, a song I don't even like very much, and made some of the most beautiful sounds I have ever heard.
