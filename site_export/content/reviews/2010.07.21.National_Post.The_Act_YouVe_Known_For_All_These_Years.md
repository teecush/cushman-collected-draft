RAIN

Canon Theatre, Toronto

---

The band called Rain, who perform the show called Rain, started out in southern California in the 1970s playing Beatles covers, which is what they still do. Of course, few groups, musical or other, retain the same personnel over 30 years or more, so the guys currently playing the Canon Theatre in Toronto are a cover band of a cover band.

What they offer is basically a concert, but they're playing in a theatre and there are some gestures towards drama, or at least narrative, in the show they put on. They start with a video montage illustrating the cultural scene pre-Beatles, roughly from Bill Haley and Rock Around the Clock to the Kennedy assassination. It's strictly the American cultural scene: England, and the culture from which the Beatles actually sprang, apparently didn't exist until the lads waved their way down the aircraft steps on their first trans-Atlantic trip. Then we're into their appearance on The Ed Sullivan Show with Ed himself, amiably grotesque as he sways on his heels, looking and sounding as if he wasn't sure what was about to hit him but thought it could probably be contained. And then, finally, the lights go up on the stage itself to reveal our four Beatles simulacra, dressed, wigged and positioned exactly like their models in their early-mid-'60s incarnation, and giving out with a succession of their first and most immediately likeable hits.

They do a decent imitation, especially of the harmonies, and they look, at least from the middle distance, very convincing. At least three of them do; Steve Landes' John looks as if his moptop has to be kidding. In compensation later on in the show, when there's some talking, he sounds the most believably Liverpudlian of the bunch. Joey Curatolo, who as Paul gets to do most of the emcee-ing when it's called for, comes off as a mid-American chat-show host. Ralph Castelli as Ringo thrashes around enthusiastically, probably with more fireworks than the original, and Joe Bithorn as George fades into the middle, as George himself tended to do. None of them has much bite or personality as an individual singer, so once the first wave of nostalgia has subsided, the songs stop sounding very interesting.

I've never understood the appeal of watching one performer spend an entire evening imitating the repertoire, sound and mannerisms of another performer when the originals are freely, if now only mechanically, available. There is, I concede, a little more point to the exercise when it's celebrating a collective rather than an individual, but only a little. It would be different if the show were providing some kind of context or commentary but Rain barely attempts this; it makes Jersey Boys look like high art. It's also disconcerting that, though they have made every effort to look and sound like the Beatles, they sometimes step out of character, speaking of them in a reverential third person. It may sound like honesty, but it comes off more like damage-control. Even more confusingly, they're still using their Beatle speaking voices and personalities even when talking as themselves. What they talk about, mainly, is how happy they are to be back in Toronto; they also beg for audience participation, rather than simply earning it. It's middle-of-the-road pandering, and one hopes that it would have made The Beatles themselves cringe.

The show proceeds on its vaguely chronological way, reaching Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band by intermission. For this section, Rain are togged out in carnival costumes from the album sleeve. The Beatles themselves, having already given up live performance, never had to look this silly, and it doesn't do much for the songs. Nor does the necessity of reproducing the studio sound in live conditions, something else the originals were spared. The mix here is bleary, drowning out the words without providing a compensating ambience; A Day in the Life sounds toothless. Act Two starts with a peace-and-love sequence that lacks conviction but can't bring itself to be ironic either. It's just nostalgia. And then the story's more or less over, since this show certainly isn't going to deal with the band's break-up. All the rest is singalong, with Hey Jude a rather uninspiring climax. Some of the best Lennon-McCartney songs have been overlooked on the way through; the Revolver album is a particular casualty, represented only by Eleanor Rigby; For No One and Here, There and Everywhere -- their two best, I think -- are nowhere to be heard. The side-show visuals continue, but more as distraction than illustration; the graphics are imitation Terry Gilliam without the sidestepping wit; they go, always, for the obvious. Americans seem never to have understood the Beatles' humour. They owed as much to British music-hall as to American rhythm and blues. Rain is more like seaside variety.
