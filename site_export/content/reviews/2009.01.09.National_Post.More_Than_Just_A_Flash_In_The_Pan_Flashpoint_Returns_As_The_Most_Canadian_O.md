Iassume that cops and allied trades really do say "copy that," meaning "OK" or "I've got your message and will carry it out" as frequently as TV would have us believe. As far as I'm concerned, the phrase, however authentic it may be, belongs to Mary Lynn Rajskub, who has used it to such effect as Chloe on 24 that the makers of Little Miss Sunshine, in which she had a cameo role, employed it as a gag to herald her appearance. Any other character who uses it is copying her. Copy that.

Few shows use it as promiscuously as Flashpoint, which has a lot of characters to spread it around. Flashpoint has some other fleeting resemblances to 24, in that it depicts a team of expert law enforcers dealing with crises on a weekly basis. It also has some structural similarities to another American show that's just starting up again, Damages. Both begin with flash-forwards to lure us into the main action, though where Damages takes us beyond the end of the main story, Flashpoint merely lures us in with tense moments from the episode we're about to see. These episodes are basically self-contained. This is how television drama series have traditionally unfolded, but after all the recent shows that have demonstrated the power of the ongoing narrative, the old formulas are getting to look, well, formulaic.

There is some internal development of course. The crew of Flashpoint's Strategic Reserve Unit have their personal problems, which are carried over from week to week. I didn't keep up with all of the first season, but my impression on returning for the second is that things haven't progressed much. Team captain Ed (Enrico Colantoni) is still uneasily balancing work and home; Greg (Hugh Dillon) is still the troubled loner. Both are still very well acted. Two of their younger henchmen and henchwomen are nurturing an off-again, on-again love interest. But their stories are inevitably subordinated to the short-term problem of the week.

This season's opener gives us Colm Feore, whom we discover in the teaser yelling at his SRU protectors "Where the hell's my wife?" (This could be his signature line of the year, given that he's also playing the president's husband in 24 and, at Stratford, Macbeth.)He's a billionaire tycoon turned philanthropist (another overlap with Damages -- the pool of possible plots is limited) hosting a gathering at the Royal York Hotel; one of the pleasures of this show is seeing Toronto cast as Toronto. He turns his back for a moment and his wife is abducted. She's held hostage as payment for misdeeds he committed while building his fortune in South America. ("You don't," he admits, "become what I am by being nice to everybody.") It's an unusual situation because, though SRU has no trouble finding the victim, they can't touch her; she's been fitted with a necklace bomb. Feore and Wendy Crewson, she especially, give noble performances as husband and wife.

Ed functions here as their personal bodyguard, and they take to him so much that he gets offered a paid-for suite (corporate rate) to share with his wife, who we imagine gets very few treats. ("When you're rich," says Feore, "you get things cheap." A truth for the ages.) But the events of

the day sort of take the shine off that. Personal feelings come more vividly into play in the next episode where, instead of swarming over a hotel, the team members get to intone "copy that" while laying siege to a house. Inside it a woman (Kristin Booth, very intense), driven hysterical by infertility, holds a knife to the throat of another woman whom she suspects of being her husband's mistress. Do they try to talk her down, or do they break in? There are good arguments on both sides, all professional, all coloured by personal experience.

If Flashpoint is the kinder, gentler, more contemplative, more Canadian cop show, Howie Do It proves, from its title on, that we can be as crass as anyone else and maybe crasser. This is Howie Mandel ("The best part of my job is you cheer just because I show up and work") resuscitating what he calls "the hidden camera prank show" -- as in Candid Camera, for those with long memories, and People Are Funny, for those with elephantine memories. He may even be parodying those shows, though such a thing may not be possible; indeed, they were probably more ingenious and sophisticated. The trick is that Mandel puts on a black wig and pretends to be a producer auditioning his victims for some other TV show; then, after they've humiliated themselves enough, he reveals himself in all his baldness, and they fall down and worship -- or at least in one case -- still look hurt. But presumably they all signed releases. Howie sprays water or blows gale force winds at guys auditioning to be weather reporters. He sends a gullible young man to pick up girls with ill-advised pick-up lines. ("You have nice legs. What time do they open?") He has a fellow dress up in a bunny suit and sing greeting telegrams at a funeral. He has one man watch while someone else makes love to his wife. He ends each segment with by screeching "This is Howie Do It!" Each of the embarrassments is single-track; it's impressed on us that all the participants in a scene except the one unctuously identified as "the mark" are professional actors, or otherwise in on the joke. This reminds me of an old comedian's maxim: A civilian audience will laugh at a comic dressed up as an old lady hurtling towards a brick wall, but for an audience of comics, it has to be a real old lady. Howie Do It is definitely comedy for civilians. There's a whole studio of them, convulsing themselves, even though all they're looking at is film clips. I watched my review copy right through to the end. I'm a saint. Don't copy that.
