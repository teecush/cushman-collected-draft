THEATRE REVIEW OKLAHOMA!

Thursday, July 05, 1990

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Written by Richard Rodgers and Oscar Hammerstein Directed by John Davidson Starring John Davidson and Kim Crosby The latest version of Oklahoma! to hit Toronto should introduce some emotional delicacy in its treatment of characters Makeshift revival needs reworking ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail IT has often been pointed out that an old-fashioned show about Oklahoma (you think Oklahoma! is an old-fashioned show? You don't know from old- fashioned) would have started with that song. Wise after the event, we can all see that it would not have had a tenth of the impact that it has at the musical's climax. This is not just because of the architectural advantage of a slow build. It is because, at the beginning of the show there is no Oklahoma, so the song would make no sense: not that that would deter some producers, then or now. When the show starts the characters are living in an unnamed area of the American West (I believe it was called Indian Territory but the show ain't lettin' on); when it ends, statehood is looming. A mere three weeks have elapsed, but that of course is an eternity in politics.

Oscar Hammerstein, who wrote the book and lyrics for Oklahoma! in 1943, had done the same for Show Boat 16 years earlier, so he presumably knew a big subject when he found one. Show Boat knew all along that it was an epic; Ol' Man River winds through the action to prove it. But in the years between Hammerstein had had flop after flop so perhaps he felt cautious. He keeps Oklahoma! domestic all through the first act. Then, without warning, he takes up the second act curtain on a hoedown number called The Farmer and the Cowman, all about how territory folks should stick together, not to mention what will happen when this territory joins the union just like all the others. The show's perspective deepens, and stays deep. The hero, Curly, is a cowboy, and the heroine, Laurey, owns her own ranch. So their eventual union mirrors the bigger one. That is what the title song is about. But it is very abrupt, and I wish that a few hints could have been dropped earlier.

Similarly with the love story itself. Everybody says how audacious it was (the less charitable say, how childish) to build an entire long first act on nothing more momentous than the question of who is to take Laurey to the box social. This is rubbish. A subject is important to an audience if it is important to the characters, and everyone on stage knows that Laurey's choice of escort is vitally connected to her eventual choice of a husband. Which is something that she and everyone else in her close-knit community might be excused for finding of absorbing interest.

The problem is that while Laurey's pattern of advance and retreat from Curly is well documented, her attraction towards his rival, the unsavory hired hand, Jud Fry, is never explored.

Jud himself is a terrifically powerful figure; he makes Curly so jealous that the cowboy tries to tempt him to suicide. The resultant song, Pore Jud Is Daid, is brilliant black comedy, the kind of thing the wholesome Hammerstein is supposed to have been incapable of writing. He was obviously so disturbed by Jud that he felt compelled to reject him, falling back on a boy-scout great-outdoors morality that only the unfailing lilt and vigor of Richard Rodgers's music can make palatable. Jud is a creature of dark corners, meditating revenge and gloating over pornographic photos; Hammerstein cannot really have believed that the emerging United States would render him obsolete.

A misfit dramatically as well as psychologically, Jud adds immeasurably to the strength and range of Oklahoma! and there is no point in tidying him up. But a first-rate revival, which the show needs and deserves, might well amplify the libretto a little. It should certainly re-think the ballet. And it should balance an overall spaciousness with a comic and emotional delicacy in its treatment of characters who spend their time hiding from themselves and one another.

I offer these thoughts to get myself off the hook of considering in detail the makeshift revival at O'Keefe. John Davidson directs and plays a smily Curly; he means well but the attitudes he strikes are singer, not cowboy: more country than western. Kim Crosby's Laurey fought, on Tuesday night, a losing battle with her personal amplification system, which had arranged for her to sing all her songs to buzzsaw accompaniment.

Natural sound might also make the orchestra screech less horrendously. The tunes are admittedly indestructible, (12 songs, 11 famous) though one does not so much listen to them as pleasantly register their re- appearance. I enjoyed the eccentric male dancing in the Kansas City number, led by Christopher Wells as Will Parker, and the horseplay generally. The comedy of the subplot - another case of a girl and two suitors - may have whiskers but they are very presentably combed, and Andrew Boyer as Ali Hakim the skirt-chasing marriage-fleeing peddler is neat. ) Darrell Sandeen's Jud hulks, skulks, and dominates, an old Beast forever denied his Beauty.
