Johnny Cash led, to put it mildly, an eventful life. Very little of it gets in The Man in Black, the self-proclaimed "tribute" show that's just rolled into town starring Shawn Barker, who's making a career out of it. When he sings Folsom Prison Blues (the second song in) and I Got Stripes (two songs later) you would imagine there might be some theatrical mileage in mentioning Cash's own frequent brushes with the law -- even, if Wikipedia be my witness, he never did real time himself. At the least, there might be some reference to the iconic status of his Folsom and San Quentin concerts within his own career. He wasn't just playing another couple of gigs. He had reasons, passionately felt, for being there.

The Man in Black is in fact just another concert playing at being a theatre piece. Its director, Kurt Brown, also gets a credit as writer, though it's hard to believe he stayed up late many nights to come up with this script. There's also the perennial puzzler of why we should want to see somebody spending an entire evening singing somebody else's songs, without putting them into any kind of context, when the originals are still readily available. That said, the show is better than many of its kind, much better certainly than the Beatles show Rain that paid us a visit last year; though it does suffer from the same uncertainty over who we are meant to believe is actually singing to us.

For most of the evening, Barker insists that he's Cash; toward the end, after he's offered the ritual thanks to his back-up musicians, one of them, without any change in staging conventions, asks for a round of applause for him under his own name. That's not the only example of having it both ways. The show's conceit is that we're seeing Cash in his prime, but in a program that includes some songs from the very end of his career. Well, all right, we'll suspend disbelief. But we're also told that his two lady back-up singers will be standing in for his wife and sometime duet partner June Carter who can't be there on account of she's dead. In fact she died only a short time before he did. What does that do to the chronology?

Barker is a performer of considerable authority, and physically and vocally he makes a very convincing Cash. (But what, one wonders, is he going to do for an encore?) He might make an even more convincing Elvis; in fact it seems that he was a Presley imitator at the time he was tapped to become a Cash imitator. The one aspect of Cash he doesn't even try to capture is the man in black's cavalier attitude toward singing in tune. Even on A Boy Named Sue he remains obstinately on key. But then singing off it, on purpose, is one of the hardest things to do. He chats the audience up between songs with reasonably charming self-deprecation, and he does manage to sneak some of the man's demons in through the show's back door.

The script confines biographical detail to descriptions of his first and last recording sessions, but something of his feeling for, and championing of, the dispossessed comes through in the prison songs. There's even more in The Ballad of Ira Hayes, the bitter commemoration of a native American war hero, and there's the most of all in The Man in Black itself. We get a sense of what makes Cash, even to a country-music agnostic like me, one of the most powerful and attractive figures in popular song.

There's also the fact that, unlike so many Nashville heroes and heroines, he never whined, either in his songs or in how he sung them. One of the best sections here, and another that subliminally tells you something about the man, is a rip-roaring gospel medley, in which the back-up ladies (Michelle Berting and Blair Irwin, both locally recruited) also do themselves proud; as they also do when duetting with the star on Jackson and If I Were a Carpenter. The latter, which I've never associated with Cash, sounds much more attractive here than it ever did as a solo.

The show never tells us anything about its protagonist's scarred upbringing, and it keeps very quiet about his drug busts, though it does end, in a rather coy fashion, with Cocaine Blues. (They try to pass it off as an impromptu encore, though in fact it's listed right there in the program.) The musicians, led by guitarist Mike Burns, are scorchers. For what it is, this show is all right. It also leaves all the room in the world for a real musical written about Cash, one that links the man to the music and that would belong in a theatre, not -- as this production spiritually does -- in a bar.
