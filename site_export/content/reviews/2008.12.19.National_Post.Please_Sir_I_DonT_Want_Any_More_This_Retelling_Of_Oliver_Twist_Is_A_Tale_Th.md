Did you know that Oliver Twist ends with the young hero standing before a holly-decked fireside and wishing everyone a Merry Christmas, straight to camera? Probably not, because it isn't in the book. But the producers of the latest BBC/PBS dramatization (currently airing in two parts on TVO) obviously had an eye to the time of year at which their production was likely to land in the schedules.

That isnt all thats wrong with it, but as a piece of clumsy opportunism, it's symptomatic. We all know that Charles Dickens invented the modern idea of Christmas as a time when people get festive and behave decently to one another; he wrote a joyous chapter to that effect in his first novel Pickwick Papers long before he institutionalized the idea by inventing Scrooge. That doesn't mean it has to be shoehorned into every adaptation of every story. It seems especially superfluous now, arriving on the heels of the updated Christmas Carol which we saw a week ago and which is bound to become a staple for at least the next few winters.

That version moves Dickens story to the present, with Scrooge as a London debt-collector repossessing pensioners TV sets when they need them most and stuck on a pretty nurse who rejects him for having the wrong values. Eventually, of course, and with some new and subtle variations, he acquires the right ones. The modernization does highlight some awkward questions: If Scrooge is abandoning his career grinding the faces of the poor, how is he going to subsidize his newfound philanthropy? But the new version plays an enjoyable game with our awareness of the old one. It can take whatever liberties it pleases: It still makes us laugh and cry as Dickens should. It's a genuine reinvention.

The new Oliver Twist, by contrast, stays in period and comes to us as a conventional adaptation. It makes all kinds of changes but hardly any of them work. It isn't a kaleidoscopic but essentially faithful rearrangement of the original, like the brilliant Andrew Davies Bleak House of a few seasons back. It does juggle and rework the plot, working hard from the first to provide a backstory for Olivers birth in the workhouse; Dickens himself, like a TV writer today, seems only to have given any thought to this about halfway through. Here Sally Phelps adaptation resembles Alan Bleasdale's fine version (which, at six years old, was hardly ready to be retired) but without any of the panache. It gives the skulking villain Monks, Oliver's half-brother, an overtly murderous role and amorous designs on the super-virtuous heroine Rose. This doesnt make either Monks or Rose less tedious than they are in the book. What does happen is that the sentimentality that in the novel is hived off within a few skippable chapters now engulfs the whole piece, which is what often happens when you fancy yourself tougher than a great writer: You make him soft.

One reason for this is that the adaptation is almost completely without humour. And one reason for that is that it retains, as far as I could tell, just one line of Dickens dialogue: I'm sure you can guess which one. Dickens' Oliver is a plucky little number; his TV equivalent (whose accent seems to fluctuate between coarse and posh according to the company hes keeping) improves on that by being an outspoken preteen advocate for social justice. After hes asked for more and has been dragged before the workhouse board, who are unsubtly seated around their own groaning board, he tells them just what he thinks of them and their dietary regulations. Which is admirable of him, but less cutting, less moving and less economic than the scene Dickens actually wrote.

Similarly, when Oliver gets to London and is brought to trial on a false pickpocketing charge, the ferociously ignorant magistrate of the original becomes a pantomime villain whose clerk informs him that he's sentenced a mere 22 people to death this week, "and it's only Tuesday." Incredibly, the same Mr. Fang later presides at Fagins trial for murder. And hereby hangs (operative word) a particularly appalling tale. Id feared that political correctness might rob Fagin of his Jewishness, but no: Timothy Spall, who seems perplexed as to whether he's playing villain or victim, affects a singsong Central European delivery, as if that would help with lines like never trust a goyim; we hate them as we hate bitter herbs. The judge, who has suddenly acquired a jury, instructs them in the case of "Fagin the Jew" who is accused of murdering a Christian child. Early Victorian England may have been both barbarous and bigoted, but it wasn't the Middle Ages. Nor was it The Merchant of Venice, but this Fagin is offered the chance of mercy if he will go down on his knees and "take Christ as your saviour." I wont spoil the ending for you.

I will reveal, though, that Bill Sikes (Tom Hardy) is not the usual hulking ruffian but a smooth operator out of a Guy Ritchie movie who never really meant to kill Nancy, just to rough her up a bit. The horror of that murder, and of Sikes subsequent flight, goes for nothing, which must be a first. He meets his fate, in a literal upending of the original, not on a rooftop but in a sewer. The Artful Dodger works very hard, shouldering the duties of three characters, which I suppose is clever. Further rewrites give Sophie Okonedo's Nancy more face time with Oliver than is customary; her contemporary cockney twang makes the character more immediate and more appealing than usual. Other mitigating female performances are Sarah Lancashire's surprisingly sexy Mrs. Corney (her partner Mr. Bumble has the pouchy look of Bert Lahr's Cowardly Lion) and Anna Massey, the best undamed actress of her British generation, making a sharp and benevolent mountain out of a textual molehill as the housekeeper Mrs. Bedwin. Oh yes, and the Artful Dodger and Bill Sikes' dog lived happily ever after. - Oliver Twist concludes this Sunday on TVO at 9 p. m.
