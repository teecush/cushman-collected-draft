AFTER THE FALL

Equity Showcase Theatre

For anyone in Toronto with an obsessive interest in the life and loves of Arthur Miller, now is the time. At Berkeley Street, we have the Canadian premiere of The Ride Down Mt. Morgan (1991), which takes us inside the mind of a high-flying bigamist. Miller himself has been married three times, most famously to Marilyn Monroe, and its all been perfectly legal, but still one senses a resonance.

That play has now been joined by Equity Showcase's revival of After the Fall (1964), which is far more blatantly, not to say notoriously, autobiographical. Or perhaps the word is self- referential; or even self-indulgent. Actually there's no even about it. After the Fall may well be the most self-indulgent play ever staged.

It lasts three hours, and it contains some stunning scenes, all of them about marriage. You have to wait for them, though, and you spend most of the wait listening to the protagonist talking. Sometimes he talks to other characters, but for much of the play he harangues an unseen auditor who may, according to his authors preface, be either God or his shrink. (The heros shrink, not the playwright's or the deity's.)

My own impression was that he was addressing a meeting of Angst Anonymous. To the portmanteau words invoked above, I would add "self- flagellating" and, especially, self-justifying. I would stop short, though, of "self-serving." Millers unremitting uprightness may get on your nerves -- it does on mine -- but this is a work of agonizingly honest intent. You respect it, even while glancing furtively at your watch.

Distancing himself, Miller has made his surrogate a lawyer, name of Quentin. He too is thrice-spliced. His first wife is, well, his first wife -- acquired, one imagines, before he got successful. His second is Maggie, the Marilyn figure, here transformed from an actress to a singer, though working as a switchboard operator when first he encounters her. His third, who hovers on the plays margins radiating wisdom and understanding, presumably parallels Inge Morath, the Swedish photographer who became Miller's last, and lasting, spouse. Here she is named Holga and is German. When Quentin meets her she is a tour guide at a concentration camp. He was too callow for his first marriage, and unable to cope with his second (who could?); now he hopes to prove worthy of his third.

My people died in this place, says Quentin at the camp. "They also built it." He is referring, one imagines, not to the Jewish race but to the human one. Miller's gloss on his title is that the expulsion from Eden endowed humanity with the power of moral choice, and that humanity has been trying to wriggle out of the responsibility ever since. Cains first instinct when charged by God was to deny responsibility. Miller quotes that primal evasion in the introduction to this play, and goes back to it in the actual text of The Ride Down Mt. Morgan.

Quentin feels guilty about everything, including his attempts not to feel guilty. His conscience casts a frustratingly wide net. His father went bust in the 1929 crash; his mother blamed his father at the time and has gone on doing so ever since. Some of this has rubbed off on Quentin himself, but its hard to see exactly what.

He also seems to feel bad about his brother, though this relationship is very sketchily drawn. (Perhaps Miller trusted us to take it for granted. His plays are full of fraternal tensions. No wonder he keeps harking back to Cain and Abel.)

Later, Quentin, like Miller, goes through McCarthyism. His old professor, summoned before the committee, commits suicide. Quentin has stood by him, personally and professionally, but still feels inexplicably responsible. Another friend is subpoenaed, and opts to be a friendly witness. (The original production of After the Fall was directed by that most celebrated of name-namers, Elia Kazan. Those rehearsals must have been fun.)

It's practically impossible to write boringly about the blacklist, and these scenes are duly gripping. But the fog that surrounds them, in the shape of Quentin's musings after and even during the event, is almost impenetrable. I have seen two productions of After the Fall, and at each of them my main response to the actor playing the lead was one of amazement that he could have remembered all those lines, followed by admiration that he should have made sense of them.

In this production, Joseph Gallaccios memory is impeccable and his phrasing mostly lucid, though he sometimes confuses natural with casual. As for characterization, there is none, nor is any possible.

Anyway, were waiting for Marilyn, or Maggie. There's an earlier character, who in this production looks more Monroevian than Maggie herself does, and may be said to function, both psychologically and dramaturgically, as a pneumatic tease.

But when the real Maggie finally appears, near the end of the first act, she gives the play an electric shock. When Quentin first meets her, she's just a girl on a park bench, nervous and eager, arousing his protectiveness ahead of his libido. (This is a far cry from Millers first encounter with Monroe, which occurred when she was an official Hollywood sex goddess.)

In London a few years ago, Maggie was played by a mercurial black actress, suggesting that we were watching a play about what might have happened if Arthur Miller had married Billie Holiday. In this production shes a stocky gamine, so it's more as if Miller had hooked up with Judy Garland; Lesley Faulkner, a find, is erotic vulnerability personified, a dream when she's nobody in particular, and a nightmare when she supposedly has the whole world at her feet.

Like Marilyn -- or Billie or Judy -- Maggie is drug-dependent and impossibly demanding and impossibly needy. Her scenes with Quentin are riveting, the circumstances are extravagant, but the dynamic, the ebb and flow, seem to be those of any couple, magnified. Even Quentins habit of lecturing her, as he does everybody else, rather than talking to her is believable; he would, and its his only line of defence.

The play has been accused of exploiting Monroe, but I don't agree; I think it makes her live. The mastery of domestic tension stretches back into Quentin's first marriage, especially when his first wife gets jealous and begins bristling at every ring of the phone. Mind you, however heavy the authors guilt trip, its still hard not to hear him crowing, somewhere behind it all, "I've been to bed with Marilyn Monroe."

Rod Ceballos' staging is excellent, especially in a final sequence in which Quentin silently confronts everybody from his past, present and future. The production is especially commendable, considering the special circumstances of Equity Showcase. Many of the large cast are inexperienced, some dont have union cards, and there are several performances by actors who are either too young for their roles or just not good enough.

But it doesnt matter. One adjusts. One adjusts better, in fact, than at The Ride Down Mt. Morgan, which has a cast of seasoned but largely miscast professionals, a kind of soft-centredness to which actor-driven companies like the new Birdland Theatre are especially prone. (One of Soulpepper's great achievements is that, whatever traps they may have fallen into, they've avoided that one. They've never seemed outclassed by their plays.) In other respects, After the Fall made me feel better about Mt. Morgan, just as the author himself seems to have done. In the later play, he leaves off blaming himself and lets cheerfulness break in. It's a great relief all around.
