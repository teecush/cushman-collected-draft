CHERI

Tarragon Theatre, Toronto

Up until now, the closest I had come to the novels of Colette was seeing the movie musical of Gigi, a picture I have reason to believe softened and sentimentalized its original. Nobody would accuse Paul Bettis' Cheri of that -- not the softening part anyway.

The play is an adaptation of two novels (Cheri and The Last of Cheri) written in Paris in the 1920s and set, mais naturellement, in that city's and that decade's legendary demi-monde. At the centre of this more or less glittering hemisphere is Lea, an ageing courtesan, obviously very good at her job but now semi-retired. For the last few years, Lea has devoted herself to the care and schooling of Cheri, a young man whose mother, an old friend and colleague, had once ever-so-gently flung him at her.

Now, however, the time has come for Cheri to move on. Lea knows the rules -- she has lived by them, after all -- and briskly, if regretfully, she sends him on his way, which means into marriage to a girl his own age with a fortune to match. The girl, however, rapidly turns into a woman -- an efficient businesswoman at that. Cheri, who has spent his life in the hands of women more competent than himself, finds himself in a situation where even his youth is no defence. He begins to despair.

The relationship of Cheri and Lea -- in its pure state, so to speak -- is excellently presented. For its first scene, which finds the couple in and out of bed, this seems to be a smart, sexy play. You see and feel why they are together, what they see in one another, and what they have tacitly agreed to overlook.

Donna Goodhand's Lea is not what I would call a raving beauty, but she has charm and sensuality and a sensible, worldly kind of compassion. She may tell her boy-toy that there are plenty more where he came from, but it's plain that she doesn't believe it.

Her performance reaches its peak at the play's half-way mark: Cheri has bolted and come back to her -- and for a moment it seems as if this will work. Then she considers the practicalities and, for the second and last time, sends him home. After which she quietly and discreetly goes to pieces, and so, less quietly and discreetly, does the play.

One grows tired of following Cheri's disintegration because, absent from Lea, there is not much to him. He expects other people to do his living and feeling for him: "I know how you should behave," he tells Lea; or again, "I don't mind not being your first lover, but it would have been fitting to be your last." I'm not saying that isn't acute characterization, merely that there's a limit to how often you have to hear it.

Michael McMurtry's performance might be described as uncompromising; he presents a rat-like youth who seems always to be pouting -- not just with his mouth but with his eyes and nose. To be asked to feel sorry for him -- or to feel anything for him -- is a definite imposition.

Soo Garay does a properly competent job on his betrothed, and the rest of the cast consists of Barbara Gordon and Jackie Burroughs, playing an assortment of hags. Gordon gobbles up the part of Cheri's mama without properly digesting it; she is actually difficult to understand, a matter of phrasing rather than volume. Burroughs, improbably billed as the "Pal" and sporting an alarmingly frizzy wig, plays everybody's confidante, plus Lea's maid, who has a scene- stealing limp.

The play's comic touch is very unsure; there was sporadic laughter on the first night, but it was scattered and half-hearted. The production invokes the elements of elegance -- gauze curtains and piano music and potted plants -- and then tries to undercut a style that it hasn't created; it wants to move us, but gives us no reason to care. On that count, it's more sentimental than Gigi.
