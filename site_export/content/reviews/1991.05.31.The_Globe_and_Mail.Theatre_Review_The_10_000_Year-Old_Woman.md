THEATRE REVIEW THE 10,000 YEAR-OLD WOMAN

Friday, May 31, 1991

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Toronto ONT -- WRITTEN AND PERFORMED BY CHERYL CASHMAN At the Theatre Centre, Toronto Cheryl Cashman's one-woman show has half a dozen clowns, one gifted and ebullient comic sketch, and plenty of self-indulgence Sincerity is no excuse for embarrassing the audience BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Toronto ACTUALLY, Cheryl Cashman confides at the beginning of her one-woman show, she isn't 10,000 years old at all. She's only 580 but the management bumped her age up so that they might more easily take our money. (This isn't quite as funny as the show gets, but for a long time it seems uncomfortably likely that it will be.) In terms of arithmetic, I suppose Cashman has a right to detain us five times as long as Mel Brooks's 2,000-year-old stripling, but I question her tact in doing so. Besides, Brooks had a first-rate foil in Carl Reiner, while Cashman has only half a dozen painfully grimacing red-nosed acolytes who minister to her needs when she is on stage and move things aimlessly about during the few moments when she isn't.

They are billed, low down the program, as "clowns," and the show adheres to the fashionable belief that this gives it and them a special claim upon us. A clown, by this reckoning, is no mere entertainer but a licenced holy fool. Cashman herself is the arch-clown and we are privileged to have her attention for three hours.

She adopts seven personae in this time, and one of them I liked. This is Sonya, a self-proclaimed cheerleader from the Yukon (I think she said Yukon; she sounds more like the Ukraine) recruiting for the emotional Olympics, mainly from among the audience. We are urged to converse entirely in vowels; consonants, she convincingly explains, are inhibiting. She all but blames them for the state of the nation.

Sonya is the only one of her creations whom Cashman doesn't patronize. The others are winsome or downtrodden or both, but Sonya shows her to be a gifted, ebullient standup comic with a firm command of the house. She even managed a perfectly timed put-down of some jerk in the congregation who had muttered an obscenity at her 10 minutes earlier when she approached him in one of her previous incarnations.

I was the guilty man, and I fear that if I had my time over I would do it all again. The lady was impersonating a sweetly smiling male half-wit at the time. Before this she had given us her frustrated housewife (is there, in this kind of comedy, any other kind of housewife?) and, some kind of summit in pointlessness, a failed performer ("I played the streetcar in Streetcar Named Desire") who adopted a second career as a vampire.

There is also the recurring title figure, who speaks in a bad-tempered wheeze and wears a moldering wedding dress like Miss Havisham. Everything else about her is borrowed from the fatuously enduring heroine of Beckett's Happy Days, including her name, Winnie.

She has not, however, inherited Beckett's wit or his poetry. Cashman the performer is done in by Cashman the writer, who is mercilessly self- indulgent. (There is no director and it shows.) It made me angry; and that was just the first act.

In the second, she comes before us in her own person and talks about her life, as if she were bestowing some kind of gift. If she wants to give an inspirational discourse on how she beat cancer, that I guess is her privilege. It made me queasy, but that could be my hangup. But she also talks about her friends: about the playwright George Ryga, with whom she apparently had a relationship and who died on her, and about others, teachers or colleagues, who committed suicide or died of AIDS. At one point - Happy Days is here again - she arraigns God, whom she imagines saying, "Let's get the artists; let's invent AIDS" as if nobody who was not an artist had died of it and as if even artists do not, eventually and invariably, die of something.

She sings, very well, some excruciating songs of her own composition; and she reads, beautifully, some intimate poems written to her by Ryga. She uses all her skills. The effect is way beyond embarrassing.

It is also very instructive. In shows that there are, after all, some barriers between life and art that must not be breached. She is asking to be applauded for her grief. She is unquestionably sincere, and almost inconceivably cheap.
