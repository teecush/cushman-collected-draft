The hit of last year's Summerworks festival was If We Were Birds, Erin Shields' dramatization of the myth of Philomel. Shes followed it this year with another play based in legend, The Epic of Gilgamesh (up until the part where Enkidu dies), which roguish title may presuppose a more intimate knowledge of Sumerian epic than many of us are likely to possess. Also, the fact that it is an epic -- meaning, one damn thing after another -- makes the source less amenable to drama than the Philomel story, which has a natural arc plus a gift of a metaphor. Gilgamesh is a hero king, very conscious of both his heroism and his kingship. He demands a lot of his people, whom he treats as his personal press gang, whether in battle or in the building of a tall and shaky tower to the citys, and his own, greater glory. He has a terrible attitude towards women, on whom he routinely exercises whatever is the Sumerian for droit de seigneur, and he's very bad for the environment.

This is no modern imposition. The original is ambiguous about its protagonist, whom it presents as a force of nature, noble but worrying. Shields takes this built-in irony and tilts it towards satire. Her writing has both wit and weight --colloquial one moment, incantatory the next -- and it always holds the stage. The story, though, doesn't, and the contemporary parallels are too obvious to be interesting. One of Gilgameshs angrier ladies journeys to the forest, where she seduces Enkidu, the wild man, whom she brings back to town hoping that hell topple the tyrant. But the two warriors, after the obligatory trial of strength, get on like a palace on fire; despite some minor differences --Gilgamesh wants to show Enkidu his music system, while Enkidu would prefer a brisk run around the city walls -- they become best buddies. Enkidu emerges as a moderating influence, but static characters having adventures aren't enough to make a play. Gideon Arthurs' production is very impressive; Frank OConnells Gilgamesh, with the eyes of a fanatic (specifically a fitness fanatic) and hitting just the right balance between spoiled boy and dangerous man, heads an excellent cast.

Shields also figures in Summerworks as co-author and co-performer of Montparnasse, in which she displays a roguish stage presence and a delectable body. The latter is worth mentioning, indeed cannot be ignored, since she delivers the play's first speech while reclining on a sofa and totally nude, a state to which she frequently returns. There's nothing gratuitous about this; she plays Margaret, an ex-pat model in the Paris of the 1920s where, it seems, a girl could hardly move without meeting and being propositioned by an artist (usually French) or a writer (usually American). Soutine, Modigliani, Hemingway, Joyce, Man Ray and Sylvia Beach all figure, and are briefly impersonated in these memoirs of two young ladies, of whom the other, Amelia, has come to town in order to paint on her own account but takes to modelling on her own account. So she strips, too, but later and more selfconsciously. She still proves successful enough, in a more specialized way, to arouse her friends jealousy, and when Amelia paints Margarets portrait, the possessive sparks really fly: which of them owns it, aesthetically and otherwise? This question has been posed before (in two musicals, for a start: Sunday in the Park with George and, less obviously, City of Angels) but it's done here sharply, amusingly, and even poignantly. Shields does her vocal quick changes well, but, as a performer rather than an actress, skates over them. Her partner, Maev Beaty, not only grabs immediate hold of each character, but nails and holds every one, while in her principal role she's astonishing, suffusing the theatre with her shyness, confidence, happiness and pain. Andrea Donaldsons direction is terrific, and I had the best time.

I had another good one at The Middle Place, which has been assembled by Andrew Kushnir from videoed interviews at an Etobicoke homeless shelter. Interestingly, the five actors read the transcripts but didnt see the footage, so they had to create rather than impersonate. The results, under Alan Dilworth's direction, are remarkable, both imaginative and respectful: factually-based but more than dramatized social work. Paul Dunn takes Kushnir's place as interviewer, and is thus an actor playing an actor; from the others we have more instant role-changing. Akosua Amo-Adem has massive, though quite different, presence, as both administrator and inmate; Kevin Walker is scarcely less compelling as a thin, shaky, prospective father. The whole is disturbing but not depressing.

For a topping-off Summerworks experience, I recommend Impromptu Splendour, at which three Second City alumni exercise their improvisational chops on parodying a given playwright, whose name is pulled from a hat by a previous audience. At the show I saw, the subject was David Mamet, and though they had time to prepare the essentials -- an office setting, much double-crossing, even more profanity -- they still had to include motifs suggested on the night. Made up or prepared, the results were a scream, with an accuracy that at times approached the poetic. Matt Baram and Ron Pedersen were perfect, and if Naomi Snieckus seemed less so -- well, nobody said that Mamet was any place for women. - Summerworks closes tomorrow.
