ROCK AND ROLL

The Dream in High Park, Toronto

John Gray's Rock and Roll is another of those Canadian theatre classics that make you wonder where the audiences of 20 years ago got their standards. The combined charms of an open-air setting and a cheerful and dedicated cast make this revival moderately entertaining. All the same . . .

The show depicts the 1980 reunion, in an archetypal small Nova Scotia town called Mushaboom, of a four-man band called the Monarchs who 20 years before had torn up the Maritimes. They were adolescents then; now they're confronting the frustrations of early middle-age. Manny (Paul Braunstein) has inherited a retail empire that owns half the town but his wealth, as he repeatedly explains, has not made him happy.

Chink (Randy Kempf) was, and has remained, a drifter. Brent (Jonathan Whitaker) has a career in insurance which bores him, and is married to Shirley (Mary Ellen Mahoney) who used to hang out with the group, hoping vainly to be given a chance to sing.

The fourth man, Parker, is the only one to have stayed in the music business where he now makes an unglamorous living as an all- purpose entertainer. Here life and art begin to converge. The Monarchs are based on an actual band called the Lincolns, one of whose members, Frank MacKay, created the role of Parker in Rock and Roll's original 1981 production. He plays it again now, giving us nostalgia in two dimensions.

The format of the show, as you would expect, is that the regrouped group reminisce about their former life and start acting it out before our eyes. It's made clear -- and indeed we can hear it for ourselves -- that until Parker joined them, the Monarchs were terrible. When he auditions with a song called The Fat Boy (everybody has a problem, and that's his) the temperature rises vertically. MacKay sings and cavorts with raucous attractiveness, and propels the group through the inevitable final sequence: the triumphant reunion concert itself.

Even this, though, doesn't exactly lift the audience out of its seats: and for a fairly obvious reason. We have seen plenty of shows recently in which a biography culminated in a final unmediated sequence of show-stopping songs. There was Jolson and, before that, Buddy. I recall a one-man opus about Hank Williams, and a long- running British show titled John, Paul, George, Ringo . . . and Bert, which structurally was like Rock and Roll but had the benefit of being about a rather more interesting group. All these, of course, had their audience's memories ready primed; they concerned real performers who sang songs that we knew before we came. John Gray has to create his own nostalgia as he goes along: a fearsome task and one that neither his words nor his music are up to fulfilling.

The book and score of a musical are supposed to be mutually enhancing; here they seem to be mutually accusing. Each expects the other to do the work. Parker, on the verge of leaving both the band and the town, sings a song about his parents: Two people whom we haven't met and of whom, up to this point, we haven't even heard. There is a long sequence showing how Brent and Shirley, like any other teenagers, used to make out in his car. Big deal. Shirley periodically reiterates her wish to perform; she sings songs about wanting to sing songs. (I don't know how they kept her out; she looks like she could eat them for breakfast.) There's nothing else to her. In fact, all the characters are static.

Then again, no sooner has the group achieved success by taking on Parker than it begins to fall apart; we hardly see the band in its glory so we have no real reason to root for them.

There is one other character: a role-model rock-and-roller called Screamin' John McGee (Michael Blake) who teaches the boys the rudiments of their craft, and hangs around afterwards, commenting at great length -- in arch rhyming couplets and worse prose -- on the travails of youth: first love, first hangover. Even his early death doesn't free us from him, since much of the second act takes place (shades of Our Town) in the local graveyard.

There are a couple of sweet tunes, and the occasional neat line of lyric or dialogue. The basic themes -- the persistence and the erosion of youthful dreams, the importance of music to people who have no particular aptitude for it -- remain both potent and poignant; though they were far more elegantly handled in the recent Two Pianos -- Four Hands.

Katherine Kaszas' direction is not, to put it kindly, over- inventive, but it does convey some affection for the material, and the actors seem happy to be there, all of which helps.

- Rock and Roll runs until Aug. 22.
