ANYTHING THAT MOVES

Tarragon Theatre, Toronto

Any way you take it, Anything That Moves is a very neat show. When it had its tryout last year (some tryout -- it won a Dora), I pronounced it, somewhat guardedly, the best new Canadian musical I had seen. I can now drop the guard: It is the best.

The show's shape and premise remain the same, but Ann-Marie Macdonald's book and lyrics, Allen Cole's music, and Alisa Palmer's direction have all been extensively revised and almost uniformly improved. The six actors -- two returners, four newcomers -- are all exemplary. Tim Howar is charming and euphonious as Joel, a shy and impoverished young florist who meets and falls for Jinny, a research scientist with a romantic backlog of men who are drunk or married or both.

Glynis Ranney, who acts as well as she sings, is splendidly edgy and hurting in this role. She even manages to surmount the proverbial hurdle of sharing a stage with something furry and four- legged, in this case a laboratory rat named Pat (one of the author's simpler rhymes) to whom she sings her first number, which is naturally drowned out by enthusiastic gurgles from the audience.

Jinny first sees Joel in the company of Tyrone, his flamboyantly gay best friend, and jumps to the conclusion they are lovers. Tyrone has all the show's best lines, or at least Juan Chioran makes them sound that way. The apex of saturnine, as fastidious in some respects as he is promiscuous in others, he sports an outstandingly twirlable moustache that he never actually touches. Judy Marshak is Jinny's mother, Fleur, addicted to alcohol and men but celebrating 15 years of abstinence from the former. She stops the show in the second act with a song called Menopausal Mama, far better integrated this time around than it was before. She now sings it to the other characters rather than to us. (Both parties enthusiastically respond.)

If Jinny has issues with her mother, so does Joel with his father, a military historian in a kilt played with sympathetic uptightness by George Masswohl. Usually remote from his son, geographically as well as emotionally, he is in town to bury a dead comrade. The officiating funeral director is a high-energy black lesbian named Alberta, who starts out, in Sandra Caldwell's performance, as the most overpowering of undertakers, every line a production number. This grows on you, and she later quietens down effectively. She also happens to be Joel's best customer -- an example of what a Dickensian character, speaking of the dramatic Unities, called "a kind of universal dovetailedness."

There is a lot of this. Each of the six onstage characters is dogged (or, as at least one of them would say, bitched) by the memory of another person, living or dead, who has to be escaped or accommodated. The musical shows our chosen half-dozen regrouping and at least potentially pairing-off. There are more question marks hanging over their destinies this year than there were last, when an epilogue tied up everything too neatly. One central problem remains: Jinny's belief that Joel is gay seems contrived, his failure to enlighten her even more so. Plenty of reasons are given -- she likes him very much but for the moment she wants a soul mate rather than a lover -- but they all seem like protesting too much. The theme of mistaken (sexual) identity is now, though, more central than it was: The show becomes a comedy about lying, and so successful as such that in the second act, when everybody convenes at a dinner party to which some are invited and others not, it almost seems to forget it's a musical.

Usually, though, it remembers in time and with enjoyable results. The subject matter may suggest the Sondheim of Company, but the show doesn't hit that level of acerbity or indeed of passion. It's a nice piece about nice people who deserve nice relationships, in a tone and style that recall the smaller U.S. shows of the '60s.

This means that as a lyricist, MacDonald is placing herself in the lineage of Sheldon Harnick, if not quite of Cole Porter. So one holds her to high standards. She meets them , with bright observation, lilting conversational rhythms and rhymes that surprise, usually in the right way. There are lapses, marring a couple of otherwise irresistible songs: "Diva" is not a good fit with "Mona Lisa," nor does "sucky" chime as she would like it to. (I won't quote the actual rhyme for fear of asterisks.) Otherwise she writes wonderful patter: A breakneck duet for Joel and Tyrone is an especial treat.

The ballads are less adroit. Cole's tunes are pretty enough but they tell us things we already know so they stop the show in its tracks rather than just stopping it. And while it may be funny to have Jinny ask despairingly "why can't a straight man be more like a fag?" it's foolhardy to make this the basis for an entire My Fair Lady parody. Inconsistent, too, since there are no other specific spoofs in the show. (Both shows feature flower sellers: Maybe that's the connection.)

Astrid Janson has designed a simple, pleasing and useful set, and the staging has improved immeasurably, up to the standard of Palmer's knife-edge work on Sibs. The choreographer, Valerie Moore, has obviously been invaluable: There are no big dance numbers, but this time around, Anything That Moves really moves.
