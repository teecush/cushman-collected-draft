KAFKA IN LOVE

Hart House Swimming Pool, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - The last production of what may be the last World Stage Festival was mounted at the weekend for four performances only. Kafka in Love was a Canadian production from a group called Autumn Leaf Performance. Billed as a water-opera, it was a mixture of live aquashow, pre-recorded music and film. Presented in the uncommon theatrical surroundings of the university pool at Hart House, and an unlikely bet for touring, it may be remembered by those who saw it as a super- curiosity.

The premise is that we are in Berlin in 1936, year of the Olympics. A German aquastar, Gerti Wasner, has prepared a piece of performance art that's been banned by the Ministry of Propaganda, since it purports to describe her affair, 23 years earlier, with Franz Kafka, a writer of whom the Nazis would have been unlikely to approve even had he not been Jewish. (In one of the few spoken passages in the work, Kafka is officially described as a bug; presumably Goebbels had read Metamorphosis.) Gerti, played here by Kirsten Johnson, takes the plunge anyway, abetted by six chlorinated- water nymphs drawn from the ranks of the Toronto Synchronized Swimming Club.

Projected on a screen above the pool is a film about the Gerti- Franz romance, though its unclear whether this movie is meant to be understood as part of Gertis own project or of the Canadian director Thom Sokolowski's project about her project. The movie shows us a Kafka disillusioned with his fiancee back home, and ripe for a fling. Those are not terms one would usually apply to the tortured author of The Trial and The Castle, but this work suggests that, like some other writers who have presented themselves as sexual incompetents (Philip Larkin comes to mind), Kafka was holding out on us. He seems, apart from anything else, to have been quite the looker back in 1913; there's a nice moment early on when his screen-image is reflected in the waters beneath.

Of course the film could be romanticizing; its Kafka after all as an actor (Avi Phillips, playing opposite the young Gerti of Kelci Archibald). He recites pieces of anguished prose in voice-over, and he does a fair amount of walking down passages that are, well, Kafkaesque, and that look as if they were photographed in another part of Hart House. But really there is not much to distinguish him from any sensitive young man, and even though he and Gerti do some happy splashing of their own (they met at an Italian spa), the twin spectacles dont do much to illuminate one another.

The live one, though, is more fun to watch. Patricia Edwards is credited with swimsuit design, and she has come up with a dashing set of ensembles in primary colours. The matching cloaks that they discard before getting wet are especially elegant. The "synchronized choreography" that Debbie Sands has devised is not especially inventive; I guess options in these circumstances are fairly limited, but there must be ways of deploying the chorines besides gathering them into Busby Berkeley starfish formations, a trick the show falls back on twice.

Still, they do it very well. A couple of them turn up at one point wearing tall cylindrical hats decorated with wrap-around photos of Kafka himself: presumably to signify the depth, or height, of Gerti's infatuation.

Other, non-human things, float upon the waters -- cushions bearing relevant artifacts: a bowler hat, an open book, a pen and paper. And, when the two lovers succumb to passion on-screen, the pool is filled with lighted tapers, an effect that can be counted on to be magical, even on dry land. John Denniston's lighting is very moody around here, and the music, though I couldnt tell you what piece was playing at the time, is haunting. Here the piece does, appropriately, come together.

The score, opera or not, is about the last thing in the evening one notices. Its a tapestry of pre-existing compositions that blend into a continuous plangent blur suggestive of exposed nerve endings.

At the end of the show the present-tense Gerti, having executed her gesture of defiance, announces that she accepts the new order, and dives in one last time. Whether she really meant that from now on she would stop hugging her memories of decadent Czech writers, or whether she was executing a coded gesture of resistance, I couldn't say. The piece seems to be riding on its audience's built-in responses to a famous author and an infamous epoch; it has nothing decipherable to tell us about Kafka or love or Nazism or any combination of, or antagonism between these things. The idea of a play in a pool has real appeal, and so up to a point does the reality, but such pleasures as this one provides are abstract, which in this case is a polite word for kitsch.

One would have liked World Stage to end with a grand finale rather than a hollow bonbon. It would have been more in keeping with a festival that this year has been remarkably impressive.

There have been lapses of course. There was Horse Country, for example, an American two-hander that followed in Beckettian tradition by presenting a comic double act and hijacking it for higher purposes. It ended up making a valid enough point about the national tendency to interpret the pursuit of happiness as the right to be entertained. But this insight hardly justified the penitential unfunniness of the endlessly circling dialogue that preceded it. Its usually a bad sign when ones main response to the actors is admiration for their feat in remembering their lines.

Otherwise, the most disappointing shows were British (100, Outlying Islands), Canadian (Tamara) or a mixture of the two (Gagarin Way). I grieve for both my countries.

Canada's honour, though, was redeemed by The Overcoat -- at least, I imagine it was. I didn't see it this time round but, on the assumption that it was as good now as it was when it played at Canadian Stage three years ago, it brought the seasons score of masterpieces to five, the others being The Notebook/The Proof (Belgium), The Mysteries (South Africa), Glengarry Glen Ross (U.S.A.) and The Magnetism of the Heart (Poland). Thats a feast in just one month. We shall miss World Stage, though I hope against hope we shan't have to.
