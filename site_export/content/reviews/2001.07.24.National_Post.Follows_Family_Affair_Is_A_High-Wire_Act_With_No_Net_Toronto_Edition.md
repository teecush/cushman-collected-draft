HAY FEVER

Gravenhurst Opera House

Think of this summer's three full-length Noel Coward revivals as a pyramid. At its apex sits Stratford's Private Lives -- absolutely secure in its understanding of the play and of the techniques needed to get it across. At its base, balancing one another, are Soulpepper's Present Laughter and the production of Hay Fever at the Opera House in Gravenhurst. The former is thoroughly wrong-headed in its design choices, in its central characterization and in its rhythm; but at least it has a rhythm, and cleaves to it with considerable skill. The latter commits no egregious gaffes of interpretation, and it certainly doesn't try the fatal course of updating the play, but it proceeds, under Ted Follows' inert and muddled direction, in a series of badly timed fits and starts.

The director also appears as David Bliss, an apparently successful author of 1920s schlock novels married to a temporarily retired star actress, Judith Bliss, who grievously misses her public and compensates by putting on a non-stop performance for everybody else. This role is played by the director's own former wife, Dawn Greenhalgh; their offspring, and the companions of their offspring, account for the two junior Blisses, son and daughter, and for the quartet of outsiders, each one of them invited by a different family member to spend the same weekend at the family's riverside retreat (which they have somehow resisted calling "the Bower of Bliss) just outside London.

Whether the hosts have serious sexual designs on their guests is never too clear; somehow it's difficult to conceive of any member of the household concentrating long enough actually to get into bed with anyone. Besides, they don't seem to have any bodies. But they are all accomplished flirts, and by the end of the first evening they have contrived, almost without noticing, to change partners; each is necking with somebody else's former prey.

The visitors are greatly unnerved by this, especially as their morale has already been shattered by their having been forced to participate in a series of highly theatrical party games for which none of them possesses any aptitude. (Would you like to play the adverb game, in which you have to perform a designated action "in the manner of the word," up against a real-life West End star and her cubs? I thought not.) So, the very next morning, they all creep away together leaving their hosts, who have otherwise forgotten their existence, musing at the extraordinary way in which some people behave.

Coward himself once remarked that the play has long been a favourite with amateurs; it has a small cast and only one set "which must lead them, poor dears, to imagine that it is easy to act." He then added that he, as a humble professional, thought it one of the hardest he, or anybody else, had ever written.

He is of course right; the play demands huge technical accomplishment because it offers nothing to hang on to. It has no plot, no characters of any substance and no real subject; it says far less about sex than does Private Lives and far less about theatricality, on and off stage, than does Present Laughter. It consists entirely of behaviour, which can be delicious but has to be both precisely defined and seamlessly orchestrated.

The extended Follows clan behave their socks off, some of them more effectively than others, but each of them exists in his or her own cocoon; being related in real life doesn't seem to help them relate on stage. Getting them all on the same stage at the same time is obviously an administrative coup, but it is not the automatic artistic triumph that everybody concerned seems to think it is.

There is a certain kick in seeing the former Mr. and Mrs. Follows hurl marital insults at one another (Judith and David are probably going to stay together but they enjoy fighting), but that's as far as the chemistry goes. Dawn Greenhalgh's Judith has a brisk presence, but she is very bunched-up, a suburban leading lady rather than a metropolitan one.

Judith lives her life as if it were one of the rosy vehicles on which she rode to fame, plays with titles like Love's Whirlwind ("Is this a game? Yes, and a game that must be played to the finish.") Greenhalgh never suggests the abandon of a woman who can slip in and out of melodrama without even noticing. Nor does she have much truck with subtlety: "Men don't grow old like women -- as you'll discover to your cost, Myra, in a year or two" is a line that loses its effectiveness if it's hissed directly into the victim's face.

Ted Follows' portrayal of David as a bumbling fossil makes a change from the more usual crisp and suave interpretation, but it doesn't seem to suit the play as well. Samantha Follows' daughter is spirited but not much else; Laurence Follows' interpretation of son Simon as a languorous aesthete is a reasonable idea taken way over the top.

Among the guests, Edwina Follows barely registers as a terrorized flapper. Judith's sporty admirer has been turned into an American for the benefit of Sean O'Bryan (Samantha Follows' husband) and the transformation wears well; his is the only performance that comfortably inhabits the skin of its character. Stuart Hughes (Megan Follows' partner) offers an elaborate cameo of a stodgy diplomat -- all guffaws and unbendings -- but one that makes no connection to the rest of the play.

Worst of all -- certainly most disappointing, considering her track record -- is Megan Follows' account of that Myra Arundel who, according to Judith, "goes around using sex as a sort of shrimping net," She draws a cartoon of a drawling '20s vamp and goes through the play without modifying it by an inch. Just what possessed her to play the morning-after scene as if she were immune to the psychological hangover that afflicts all her companions? Smiling through it all, she leaves herself with nothing to act.

There is also a housekeeper, cast from outside the tribe. She was Judith's dresser, which Myra says is the only excuse for her. There can be little excuse, though, for Robin Craig's ostentatiously rude performance, and none whatever for her interpolated full-throttle rendition of Tea for Two in the last act -- the kind of nonsensical turn that begs for applause and gets it while sensitive souls shrink in their seats.

Applause for Dennis Horn's beaming set, though, is permissible; and the costumes, "co-ordinated" by Hilary Corbett, are bang-on.
