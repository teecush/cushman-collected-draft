The new Second City revue Dreams Really Do Come True! (And Other Lies) is not one of the best. It is, for example, nowhere near as good as its immediate predecessor. Still, it has a few things going for it with which few of its forerunners have bothered. One of them is a title that actually has some relevance to the show. The opening number features the six performers writhing nightmarishly about in the half-light and, yes, telling one another about their impossible dreams. There's the one, for example, who wants to be a star but is told to forget it because this is Canada. Snappy, that.

The show also has a shape. At least, it keeps referring back to its initial idea and staging, though the linking bits that keep nudging us in this direction are painfully lame. The closing number reprises key moments from what has gone before, something that's been done in the past at Second City but never this thoroughly. Half of these supposed highlights I was quite happy to be reminded of; the other half I'd rather forget.

I'll start, as the show itself does, with the good stuff. First up after the prelude is the dating sketch. Every Second City revue has one, but this breaks new ground by being about a good date. A man and a woman, matched by eHarmony, complain about their previous partners having lacked the intelligence for which they had advertised and then find that not only can they both name-drop Nietzsche, but they both sound as if they've actually read him. It's like when Harry met Sally, only better because they both explosively feel it and neither of them is faking.

Then there's the technology sketch, this one being a laughterof-painful-recognition item about a mother grovelling to her blackmailing eight-year-old for help with the Facebook photo and the VCR. Elsewhere in the evening Nigel Downer, who plays the kid, is more impressive at physical comedy than verbal, and in this scene he does indeed do some spectacular rolling-around. But it's his unbelieving cries of "Mom, it's so EASY" (the last word extended to some 20 syllables) that really eat into the grown-up soul.

Best of all, both subtle and savage, is the Stephen Harper sketch. This finds the tall and very funny Alastair Forbes as the PM, reflexively congratulating the short and very funny Jason DeRosse on his heroism as a disabled Afghanistan vet, the two of them remaining at devastating crosspurposes over what it's all supposed to be for. (Most persuasive rationale: "It's to help Tim Hortons gain a foothold in a new market.")

All these come before the intermission, as does the one in which a suburban married couple is dismayed by the news that their two hip downtown lesbian friends are about to tie their own knot. Not, of course, that they themselves have anything against gay marriage: just against marriage. (And the suburbs.) This, too, is familiar territory, but at least it's covered with a fair amount of zing. In its second half, though, the show goes into a steep decline, with only a couple of treasures. One, very nicely staged, is about mounted police representing the kinder, gentler face of law enforcement because, after all, it's hard to arrest someone when you're on a horse. The other is about breastfeeding in a public place and boasts that rarest of rarities, a punchline.

Otherwise, the show relies, to a crutch-like extent, on audience participation. On the first night, one intrepid conscript proved fully capable of upstaging the professionals when pressed into service as a grunt in Vietnam. This number is, or was, quite good fun though you may question why the show is digging into ancient history, and ancient American history at that. Don't they know there's a war or two on now? But perhaps one number on Afghanistan was thought to be enough. Not much fun at all, though the cast tried hard to behave otherwise, was a long bit about a TV station whose ownership kept changing, according to suggestions from the house. So all right, those who pay the piper get to call the tune, but at this rate shouldn't they be paid them-selves? And couldn't they call better ones, or ones that yielded funnier results? The responses here proved that it's possible to be improvisational and mechanical at the same time. But then it can't be easy to come up with good instant jokes about the Abraham Lincoln channel.

Two-thirds of the cast are new to the main troupe: all three of the men, plus Ashley Cormeau who's been lumbered with the interlocutor's role but is otherwise clever. The female recidivists are Carly Heffernan, who's likewise agreeable, and Inessa Frantowski who, playing a far-too-available lady with an accent that may possibly be Russian, gets to importune a gentleman spectator in a scene of which the show seems to be unreasonably proud. At least, they keep reminding us of it. The direction, both the good and the selfindulgent, is by Kerry Griffin.

- Dreams Really Do Come True runs indefinitely at The Second City, 416-343-0011.
