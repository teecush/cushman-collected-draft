Theatre review

Dame Edna's Glorious Goodbye: The Farewell Tour

Princess of Wales Theatre, Toronto

On the first night of her valedictory sojourn in Toronto Dame Edna Everage, self-anointed gigastar, devoted a considerable portion of her precious time and inexhaustible energy to affectionately browbeating an audience member named Elaine. Well, she claimed she was doing it affectionately. Or at least for Elaine's own good. After lengthy interrogation, she elicited the information that Elaine lives in a detached house, meaning, the Dame helpfully translated, that you can walk all around it. She then raised her eyes to the patrons in the balcony and said "that's an impossible dream for you."

So she's at it again, playing off the folks in the expensive seats down front against those in the cheaper ones way up above, and hilariously insulting both groups. Dame Edna made her first public appearance back in 1955, when she was just an Australian suburban housewife; just how old that makes her I'm not sure, but Barry Humphries, the man behind her or perhaps inside her, admits to 82. But the razor-sharpness of both the character and her creator remains unblunted. Once again we marvel at, to use a phrase that might earn her censorious rebuke, the number of balls Edna that can keep in the air; having staked out, early in the proceedings, the five or six people on whom she is going to pick she keeps returning to them, making them the objects of both her criticisms and her confidences. I'm impressed that she can remember all their names, let alone their salient characteristics, and knocked out by the insouciant unexpectedness with which she brings each of them back into what, if it weren't so egomaniacal, you might call the conversation.

Her named victims from the orchestra seats are always women, helpfully lectured on their dress sense or lack of it ("saved up for that, did you?"); they don't have the Dame's advantages and wouldn't know what to do with them if they did. Those aloft she greets, as always, as "paupers," though this time she also, more compassionately, calls them "les miserables" (or "mizzies"). One of the great joys of the show is the recurrent upward tilt of the head that signifies that she is about to address these unfortunates, meaning that she has thought of a new way of insulting them. She also has a few kind words for those in the side-boxes ("the ashtrays") and an interestingly macabre explanation, that I would not dream of giving away, for the few empty seats. As always, Dame Edna gives new meaning to the phrase "target audience."

We don't have to feel sorry for the people she singles out. The routine is well-known by now, and I have it on good authority that ticket-buyers actually compete for the seats where they are likeliest to attract her attention. They're prepared, and they can give as good as they get. This is even, or especially, the case when they're brought physically onstage. It certainly was on the Toronto first night when a man and woman determined by the Dame to have matching DNA were ceremonially married by her, despite each having a spouse already, one being present in the house, the other reached by phone after the ceremony and apparently cool with it. Shy at first, the newly happy couple rapidly warmed up, and came close to stealing the star's thunder. Maybe what she says is really true; she isn't bullying them, she's empowering them. "I don't think of you as an audience" she says, "but as a focus group."

There's a sustaining tension in the Edna phenomenon between Humphries, the brilliantly intelligent standup comedian, and the impenetrably stupid woman who is his mouthpiece, He's endowed her with his own quickwittedness and her own closed-mindedness. There's more of course. With her purple hair, her amazing spectacles (in both senses), her glittering bangles and no less glittering frocks, and her complete confidence in her own superiority, she's the ultimate satire on celebrity culture. But she's also a great individual creation, the most sacred of monsters; and indeed part - slightly too long a part - of her second act concerns her spiritual awakening at an ashram, with the Dalai Lama himself, Dal for short. (She got pally with the Dali. You're welcome.) She also, game thing that she is, sings and dances with a supporting quartet, the tackiness of whose routines isn't quite excused by their being that way on purpose. More accomplished and enjoyable are a couple of video sequences. One is a ruthless expose of the sordid truth behind the Everage myth, including a very funny guest cameo by Hugh Jackman, who tells us about taking Edna's acting classes Down Under, with Mel and Cate and the rest of the gang. The other shows Edna's many apotheoses, encountering the rich and famous, even hobnobbing with Charles and Camilla in the Royal Box before excusing herself because she's got a better seat.

And there's a coda, that's a complete departure from all previous Edna shows. For one thing, it's moving. And it convinces you that this Farewell Tour really is Goodbye and that it really is Glorious.
