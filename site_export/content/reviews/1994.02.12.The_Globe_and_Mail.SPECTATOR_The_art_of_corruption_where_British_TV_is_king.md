SPECTATOR The art of corruption, where British TV is king

Saturday, February 12, 1994

ROBERT CUSHMAN

BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to the Globe and Mail FOR some months now my most compulsive TV date has been with the British drama series Inside the Line, about crooked cops. Over the last four weeks, though, it has had to share my favours with the British drama series To Play the King, about one spectacularly crooked politician.

A lot of the attraction of both shows has lain in the acting - not just its quality but its familiarity. These are the actors I grew up on. I watched Ian Richardson, the lizardly Francis Urquhart of To Play the King, over some 16 years of work with the Royal Shakespeare Company, working his way up from fops and minor villains to tragic monarchs, high comic lovers and major villains. (Most of that experience must have come in useful for Urquhart: a ruler, a killer, a sexual magnet and a fastidiously natty dresser.) Richardson, maybe the last major British actor in the ringingly precise John Gielgud tradition, became a Shakespearean virtuoso, exhilarating to watch and listen to because he was in such total control of his means. He still is. Nobody else's voice flashes such effortless steel, while to find a precedent for his chillingly cozy conspiratorial asides to camera you have to go back to Olivier's Richard III. On TV the only precedent for such convention-shattering intimacy is George Burns in the halcyon days of the Burns and Allen show.

Soliloquy ought to be a natural for television, but it's hardly ever tried. Its appearance on To Play the King and its predecessor House of Cards derives, like so much in British television, from the theatre. Actually the best British TV surpasses - in wit, trenchancy and narrative skills - most of what's being offered on the English stage. But, in terms of tradition, the theatre contributes hugely: The writers and directors are theatre-influenced, the actors are - overwhelmingly - theatre-trained.

The writing and performance of To Play the King, with its obsessive references to the archetypal usurpers Richard and Macbeth, are obviously joke-classical, and the joke is all the richer for being played by people who could do it straight. Inside the Line, like other cop series before it, comes out of the tradition of naturalistic protest associated for nearly 40 years with London's Royal Court Theatre - though there is no rigid divide between classic and modern and the same people move between the two sectors to the benefit of both. Nearly every instalment of Inside the Line features some old sweat from the National Theatre as a senior policeman who before the hour is out will have been revealed accepting bribes, beating up suspects or suppressing evidence. All these guys are actors of weight; some have been my favourites for so long - one or two of them are friends - that I find myself instinctively rooting for them, but it seems the more respectable your lineage the guiltier you inevitably are. Neil Pearson, playing the younger officer from Complaints Division whose job it is to expose them, is so right in this role that I find it hard to imagine him in any other. His Superintendent Clark is an abrasive, philandering cynic whose saving grace is that he is dedicated to his job and does it honestly and compared to most of what's going on around him - humanely.

If British TV drama comes out of theatre, American comes, naturally, out of cinema. But the results look inevitably like cut-rate movies, than which nothing is more deadening. The background music ends up doing most of the acting, and does it garishly, sucking out the life. American TV has never been much on live actors; its best shows are Sesame Street and The Simpsons. NYPD Blue, the U.S. equivalent of Inside the Line, is incorrigibly glossy, however hard it tries to be gritty.

The bent coppers of Inside the Line, protected by official apathy or official complicity, get away with it more often than not. This is in step with the despair that has understandably infected political theatre in Britain since Thatcherism came in, and that has trickled down to films and TV. Still, Clark and his team survive to fight another week so the conventions of the genre are not altogether breached. The Francis Urquhart saga has ditched them completely. This was not so evident in House of Cards, where F. U. was the outsider intriguing for the premiership and as such had our imaginative sympathy if not our approval. But in To Play the King he has been the man in power; we have been shown the tyrant triumphant. This is unprecedented: even Marlowe's invincible Tamburlaine had finally to give in to Death. Upping the ante, To Play the King gave us not only the victory of the villain but the defeat of the hero, Hamlet going down without Claudius.

House of Cards didn't have a hero, only dupes and victims. It didn't really have politics either; Urquhart's foils were wets and softies so we assumed he was a hard-liner, but little was made of it. In the sequel he stood revealed as an authoritarian inching toward fascism, and the only man apparently capable of stopping him was the monarch. Michael Kitchen's puzzled, caring king transcended the obvious (and delicious) impersonation of Prince Charles to become a genuine serio-comic creation; he got and deserved equal billing with Richardson. Why royalists objected I cannot imagine; this was the best publicity the throne has had in years. All our hopes were pinned on him; up to the last moment the plot and all our expectations of poetic justice pointed to his victory; and then he lost. It was, unquestionably, more like real life than most drama; and it was very hard to take.

Andrew Davies, who wrote the screenplay, is about as good a playwright as Britain has, in any medium. His double series A Very Peculiar Practice surpassed anything by the acknowledged TV masters, Dennis Potter or Alan Bleasdale, and his adaptations - the bulk of his work - are the best there are. He has a version of Middlemarch that's currently a hit on the BBC, and is presumably en route for Masterpiece Theatre. I worry though whether, as is rumoured, he could go a third round with F. U. If Urquhart remains victorious he could become as predictable as Perry Mason in reverse, but to bring him down now might seem like cheating. (Will the icy Mrs. U. anticipate her inevitable elevation to Lady U. by taking up sleep- walking? I can't see it.) But, if and when it happens, I'll be there. Meanwhile I shall have to get my fix from the cops. What a dull world it would be without corruption.
