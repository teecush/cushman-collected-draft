Some years ago, William Finn, composer-lyricist of March of the Falsettos and other shows, underwent emergency brain-surgery. He then wrote a musical about it. When people start talking about unlikely subjects for musicals, they always say how crazy and brave it was for Andrew Lloyd Webber to write a show about cats. Personally, I always thought it was, especially in a culture as anthropomorphically-inclined as Britain, a bloody obvious idea, practically a no-brainer. Song-and-dance about a frontal lobe operation, though, is something else. You might call it a new brainer. Which is very nearly what Finn did call it.

A New Brain has its snags, but it's largely delightful, sometimes moving and has Finn's best score. James Lapine wrote the book, which, in terms of actual dialogue, is very sparing; we're someway into a fairly brief evening before we realize that the show is not, in fact, composed. The autobiography is not exact but is fairly blatant. The hero Gordon is, like Finn, a songwriter, though operating in less prestigious territory. He writes for a kids' show that's hosted by a human-sized talking and singing frog, and he despises the show, its star and his own contributions to it. He is lunching with his agent to discuss this situation when he comes over all funny and has to be rushed to hospital. The only other specific things we learn about Gordon is that, again like Finn, he is both gay (we meet his boyfriend) and Jewish (we meet his mother).

Acting Up Stage, who specialize in bringing offbeat American musicals to Toronto, have done this one very well, with the director, Daryl Cloran, delivering a faultless production that moves smoothly, sensitively and inventively from one mood to the next. The same can be said of Steven Gallagher's Gordon, a supple and touching performance that establishes a direct rapport with the audience, even though the writing gives him more predicament than personality. The show's trajectory is that a young man with issues finds that they matter less than the fact of being alive, especially when you've just had a narrow escape from being dead. The show's last rejoicing number is called I Feel so Much Spring. The cast, led by Gallagher, sing it beautifully, and we can appreciate the weight of feeling behind it, but it may leave you reflecting that off-Broadway uplift is not so very different from Broadway uplift. Frankly, any character could sing it, in almost any show

Finn has always had a gift for the plangent, at its best here in a song called I'd Rather Be Sailing that both aches and soars, especially as sung by Thom Allison. He plays Gordon's lover, whose passion for boats sometimes seems to eclipse his passion for Gordon (or that's how Gordon, a grumpy landlubber, tends to see it) but who still keeps faithful, anxious vigil at his bedside. What Finn is less good at is being clever: Barbara Barsky as Gordon's mother is handed a number called Throw It Out in which she visits her son's apartment and gets rid of his books, which she seems to be blaming for something. She enumerates books and titles, but they seem to be there for the rhymes, rather than for any specific light they shed on either character.

There's also a choric bag lady (Paula Wolfson), the painful reality of whose situation is eclipsed by her function as a metaphorical messenger, first of warning, then of goodwill. Better are the scenes inside the hospital, in which the terrors engulfing Gordon and his support-group are played off against the businesslike concerns of the medical staff: from a cheerily insensitive surgeon (Jonathan Tan) to a male nurse who swings between sympathy and resentment and who sums himself up in song as Poor, Unsuccessful and Fat. He's splendidly embodied by Steve Ross. The two elements come together in a hallucinatory sequence, most inventively choreographed by Marc Kimmelman, in which Gordon's memories of his father's racetrack gambling habit are played out by a ghostly, mocking array of doctors, nurses and friends.

Musical director Wayne Gwillim musters his forces impeccably; this is as well-sung a show as you'll find in Toronto. Remaining performers include Parris Greaves as a minister, Patricia Zentilli as a surprisingly soft-boiled agent and Allie Hughes, delightful as a waitress still smitten with childish adoration of Mr. Bungee, the frog-star employer who haunts Gordon's nightmares. He's played by Juan Chioran, who must be the tallest amphibian in captivity, and who seems happiest when he's allowed to take his head off and give Gordon a pep talk. His function seems to be to help our hero back to health by persuading him that writing cheerful songs for children can be a noble pursuit, as indeed it is. The really affecting journey in the show is a musician's one. Gordon's initial attempt to write a number called Frogs Have So Much Spring, in which neither he nor anyone else can believe, finally evolves into I Feel so Much Spring and, just before that, to the show's real climax Time and Music. Lindsay Anne Black designed the ingenious set; musical arrangements, carried over from the New York original, are by another fine composer (and finer lyricist), Jason Robert Brown. - A New Brain is at the Berkeley Street Theatre until Sunday.
