The first production at Torontos new opera house is the work of a first-time director. Its easy to lose sight of this in light of the more obvious excitements of the occasion: the Canadian Opera Company's attainment of its own welcoming and acoustically perfect home and its audacity at starting operations with the complete four- part cycle of the Ring of the Nibelungs. Also, Das Rheingold, chronologically the first opera of the tetralogy but in this case the last to reach the stage (the three subsequent instalments had made their bows, one by one, in the company's old quarters at the Hummingbird Centre), is directed by the same man who has designed all four, so the visual continuity is impeccable. There is, in fact, an air of inevitability about the whole process.

The man is Michael Levine, a national treasure whose work isnt seen in Canada nearly often enough, though he was born and grew up in Toronto and recently moved back here. Along with Richard Bradshaw, the COCs artistic director and the cycle's conductor, he must be considered one of the major architects of Canada's Ring. (Of course in the real world, or even the Wagnerian world, rings dont have architects. They have forgers. But that word has an unfortunate, well, ring.) In Levines words, asking him to direct was one of Richard's wild, unexpected ideas." But it had, from the start, its own logic.

Levine joined the more experienced, and very varied, team of Atom Egoyan, Francois Girard and Tim Albery. He helped create, and interconnect, their worlds. Finally, he got to round off the process himself or, Rheingold being the beginning of the story, to show how it started.

There's also a career logic to this. Levine, when I talked to him this week in his studio on the seventh floor of a converted garment- district building at King and Spadina, told me that he had trained in London at "one of the best courses around, the Central School of Art and Design. This was in 1979; the Thatcher government had just taken office in the United Kingdom but had yet to have much effect, and I felt like I was living in the Victorian period; I got a flat for (ps)7 a week. London still felt like a lot of little villages." He had "three excellent years at Central, where one of his occasional instructors was Philip Prowse, head of design at what was then, and may be still, Britains most adventurous repertory theatre, the Citizens in Glasgow.

I'd written letters to all the theatres -- Glasgow was one of two that replied. Philip came into the Central's studio one day, I showed him my work, and I got an assistant designers job at the Citz. I was so excited, I arrived two weeks early, without even a place to stay, went to the theatre and said I'll do anything, I'll sweep the floors. And I was sweeping the floor when Giles [Havergal, the Citizens artistic director] walked in and asked 'Who are you?' I said, Im the new designer.' "

As a London critic at the time, I used to find myself flying to Glasgow about once every two weeks, since the Citz had a style and a repertoire unlike any other. It would stage, with unblushing sang- froid, classic plays that one would otherwise know only from textbooks, bring them to outrageous life and then immediately move on to something else. Levine and I were both surprised to find that I had seen the first play he had ever designed on the stage -- at, he recalls, a budget of (ps)200. The play was Torquato Tasso by Goethe, and I remember it as first-rate Citz; one feared it would be boring, but was riveted. Levine also designed an obscure Jacobean comedy, The Custom of the Country, which the production set in Hollywood: not the place, the myth. Levine now expresses doubts about this idea, but I remember falling helplessly apart when one of the characters appeared as Mickey Mouse, complete with tail.

Levine was only there for a year but the experience was formative. "There was a fantastic team of people, working for nothing -- nothing being something when you're straight out of college. More specifically, Philip Prowses work was a huge influence on me; he had a way of working that was stylized, but off the cuff. He had an incredible rapport with the people he worked with, a wonderful way of getting results. The overlapping of direction and design made an impression on me; I never felt anything -- direction, design, acting -- should take the first role. Philip had a specific design rule: He felt that things should feel permanent on stage. He wasnt big on scene changes. I like scene changes. But I have a certain respect for Philip's ideology. He taught me that if you create something on stage, you have to have a good reason for destroying it."

The burning of Valhalla at the end of Gotterdammerung presumably counts as such a reason, especially as we have now seen in Rheingold how that palace was built. We see it as an architects' model with, just behind it, a great door representing a part of that model in its fully realized state -- though Levine admits that not everyone has got the connection, and I have to count myself among those who missed it.

Prowse took the overlapping of direction and design quite literally. He became a director in his own right, in demand for plays and operas way outside Glasgow. And so Levine has been following, if belatedly, in his mentors footsteps. He has found the experience "glorious -- I enjoyed every minute of it. Even as a designer, he has always made a point of attending as many rehearsals as possible, so he can be there to help solve problems as they come up. Not that directing hasnt also been daunting. Technical rehearsals, in his dual role, were like being in the eye of a tornado."

"And even in the rehearsal room it can be a terrible blind roller coaster ride; you don't know what you're doing. The singers come with the emotions of the piece already inside them, but not always the intentions of it. Most of the discussions we have had were about finding those intentions. You have people who may have sung the role in six or seven productions; you have to borrow some things and reject others. Its a difficult minuet; you have to earn their trust. All of my singers, though, have been really amenable.

One doesnt necessarily expect a designer to be an actors' or singers' director, but Levine seems to me to have drawn first-rate vocal and even physical characterizations from his Alberich (Richard Paul Fink), first wheedling, then snarling; from Loge (Richard Berkeley-Steele), the sardonic outsider among the gods; and from the majestic scold, Fricka (Judit Nemeth). His conception of the two giants is to have each represented by a team of actors, carrying the seated singer about.

Singers dont necessarily take to sitting down (though some do their finest work in the boardroom setting Levine and Albery have designed for the villains in Gotterdam-merung), still, Levine seems to have persuaded them. And perhaps the most moving in Rheingold comes when the gentler giant Fasolt (Robert Pomakov) calls for more gold to cover the eyes of the beautiful goddess Freya, whom he is about to lose.

On his home ground, so to speak, Levine, with much billowing drapery, has designed wonderful settings for the Rhine itself, whose maidens toss about fluffy white pillows; for the approach to Valhalla; and for Alberichs industrial-revolution smithy, where junior Nibelungs toil and where the master himself turns magically into a dragon. He goes from Toronto to New York where a Levine- designed production of Madama Butterfly opens the new season at the Met.

Looking in the opposite direction -- backward -- he won this year's Dora design award, in the opera/musical category, for a revived production of Wozzeck that he designed years ago. His acceptance speech was charming -- in an evening for the most part painfully short on charm or any good qualities -- but bemused. A noted British director once described Levine to me as a great designer and a very nice man." On the strength of one meeting and years of observation, I'd go with that.
