CUL-DE-SAC

Buddies in Bad Times Theatre

- - -

Daniel MacIvor is at the peak of his performing form in Cul-de- Sac. It's a solo show that he also wrote and which is directed, very well, by his old colleague Daniel Brooks. MacIvor, without ever leaving the stage or changing a costume, plays some half-dozen roles, but his pivotal role is that of Leonard, a gay man who lives at the end of a dead-end street. (That is a non-judgmental statement of fact. Leonard does at one point toy with the idea of it being a metaphor, but he wisely and rapidly discards it. Unkind critics may end up having similar thoughts about the title, but this one is inclined to stifle them. More or less.) Some of his neighbours would like to exchange the dead-end designation for the one in the play's title, which they think more seemly, but Leonard, who knows what the French actually means, has his doubts about that.

Leonard is a magnificent creation, a gushing fountain of witty self-deprecation, unable to get to the end of a sentence without restarting or qualifying it three times. He's a character built out of speech rhythms. (These, he points out, are not Australian, even if they sometimes sound it, thereby echoing in reverse a line of the late Peter Allen's: "For those of you who are wondering, is he or isn't he? Yes, I am Australian.") He also worries about whether his life would have been different in the unlikely event he got into hockey and about his inability to sing. Leonard's relationship recently ended, and his search for consolation proves fatal. He is, even when we meet him, dead, and the play tells us, among other things, how this happened.

It begins with a terrifying onslaught of sound and light that suggests the mother of all thunderstorms, but which signifies the approach of death. The sound makes its way right down the street and back up again. The technical effects in Brooks's production are stupendous; Richard Feren designed the sound and Kimberly Purtell the lighting, which includes an amazing red wash that encircles Leonard at crucial moments, lending an especially sinister shade to his fingernails.

With all this going on around him, I felt I could happily have listened to Leonard all night. Maybe this was an overenthusiastic response -- anyway there turned out to be no chance to put it to the test. Though Leonard keeps returning as our narrator and guide, the focus shifts to his neighbours, testifying (to the audience? the police? God?) as to how they felt about him in life, and how they feel about him now he's gone. They also air several of their own hang-ups. Unfortunately they have little interesting to say about the protagonist, and their author has little interesting to say about them. (Leonard's own remarks about them are almost unfailingly amusing, but that doesn't amount to dramatization.)

The performance level, too, slips from brilliant to merely clever. A married couple, needling one another about their sex life or the lack of it, inspire a back-and-forth routine of vocal quick- changes; it's like watching a Ronnie Burkett show without the puppets -- and also without the humanity.

Some of the other locals, thankfully, are not at home, but we do get to meet: a cranky veterinarian, forced by the pressures of retirement to get belligerent about his lawn; an opera queen whose enthusiasms run to nudism and Gilbert and Sullivan (The Pirates of Penzance is a leitmotif in the play, though I don't think the reasons go beyond playfulness); and, briefly, a lawyer.

There is also the lawyer's teenage daughter who set out to write a novel but, in every sense, lost it. She refers to stuffy and unsympathetic adults -- which in her experience seems to mean all adults except Leonard -- as "knobs." She is much the most engaging of the bunch; all the same , we've been spoiled in recent years for piquant studies of female adolescence, and this one comes in a distant third to I, Claudia and Girl in a Goldfish Bowl.

The group portrait amounts to no more than another chorus of "all the lonely people," and there's no particular irony or power when none of them realize the full horror of what happened to Leonard in the third hour of his last morning, that none of them, in fact, can put a name to the noise.

We discover it, and the realization entails a second astonishing performance from MacIvor, as physically tight as the first is endearingly floppy. The son et lumiere complement him by going into scary overdrive. But that's all. MacIvor is extravagantly talented, but all that talent is employed to such little effect. Correct that: His work is usually plenty effective, and never more than here. Say instead that it's effective to so little point. As the presentation of one man's fate, Cul-de-Sac is self-sufficiently powerful. What surrounds it is padding.
