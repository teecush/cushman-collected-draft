TIME AND THE CONWAYS

Royal George Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

J. B. Priestley's Time and the Conways is a strong and bitter family drama, on a familiar English model, with a few metaphysical musings attached. First staged in 1937, the play takes a middle- class family in the North of England (Priestley's perennial stamping ground) from the end of the First World War to the brink of the Second, and -- here's the twist -- back again.

Act One, 1919: the widowed Mrs Conway, her two sons, four daughters and assorted neighbours have a party. They are celebrating the 21st birthday of one of the young Miss Conways, and they all anticipate bright years ahead. Act Two, 1938: the same young lady's 40th. The bright years have not materialized. Marriages, investments and ideals have all soured, and there is much recrimination. Act Three, 1919 again. We pick up exactly where we first left off. The characters resume their hopeful attitudes, even more intensely than before. This time, though, we know what they don't. The prevailing sound is what a colleague once described as a right crash of old irony.

To be more specific: Mrs. Conway gives herself airs (she also sings them) and seems jealous of her daughters. When we first meet them, Kay, the birthday girl, wants to be a novelist; Madge is a visionary socialist; Hazel is fashion-conscious; and Carol is simply and enthusiastically young.

They have a bony friend called Joan, who is inarticulately loved by their shy brother, Alan, but is swept up by their very un-shy other brother, Robin, a returning airman who's his mother's favourite. In the first act, the women are organizing a game of charades, into which all the male characters are, with various degrees of unwilligness, dragooned. (The word they're acting out is "pussyfoot" which must have thematic significance, though I couldn't say what.)

At the Shaw, Neil Munro stages all this with a great sense of swirl, but not much else. By the end of Act One I still hadn't worked out who the characters were, and when one of them failed to turn up for Act Two, I didn't notice.

The atmosphere is Little Women crossed with Chekhov: Three Sisters, of course, but also -- and increasingly, as the action turns to financial ruin and threatened family property -- The Cherry Orchard. The middle act is a variant of that fine old standby, the Reading of the Will. The family solicitor gives the Conways a bleak assessment of their financial position, and all hell breaks loose.

The only man who could save them is an uppity little entrepreneur who has married Hazel and seems to be treating her appallingly, and he refuses. (It's another old theme: the revenge of the nouveau- riche on the snobbish petty-genteel.) Mrs C. slaps his face: A moment brilliantly staged here with the other characters, their backs to us, gathered round like ringside spectators. He says she'll be sorry.

The time games aren't just a matter of flashback and flash- forward. When sensitive Kay asks kindly Alan for comfort, he says that we shouldn't get hung up on chronology; that one moment of time is as important as any other.

This makes sense, especially in the theatre, where first scenes are rehearsed as carefully as last. (Generally more, schedules being what they are.) But the play itself contradicts the theory. The last act's only interest lies in our awareness of what is going to happen later, and our withers are shamelessly wrung; Priestley even has a girl who is going to die proclaim how intensely she is going to live.

The middle-act quarrelling, though, is the goods: a rivetingly constructed display of all-around meanness. It doesn't, however, reverberate much beyond the personal level. Nobody would accuse Priestley of lacking social consciousness, and he plainly meant the Conways to reflect all the national upheavals of the Twenties and Thirties. But in practice, we are hardly more conscious here of the depression and the coming war than we were in She Loves Me the night before.

The individual performances find it hard to recover from the hustle of the first act. They never put down roots. Nora McLellan as Mrs. Conway certainly cuts through more incisively than anyone else, and her scream at her ineffectual son ("don't be so forgiving!") is superb; but she plays up the woman's affectations and neuroses so insistently that you never get to see the character beneath.

Jenny L. Wright cannot get a grip on Kay, who is the nearest thing to a central figure; while the other women (Laurie Paton, Jan Alexandra Smith, Jane Perry) make up an anonymous mass of suffering and complaint, relieved by the occasional moment of genuine pain.

Simon Bradbury plays the thrusting northern upstart, as he must have done in 20 other plays, but it's still effective. Peter Krantz shuffles sympathetically as Alan; Bruce Davies walks through the caddishly hollow Robin with no conviction whatever. Brian Perchaluk's drawing-room set shrinks suggestively from an overstuffed past, fit to serve as the frontispiece of a Samuel French acting edition, to a melancholy, shell-like present.
