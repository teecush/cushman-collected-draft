robertcushman@lycos.com; Last Will and Videotape runs indefinitely at Mysteriously Yours, 416-468-7469; Movin' Out runs through Dec. 31 at the Canon Theatre, 416-872-1212.

The corpse that provides the pretext for the latest interactive mystery from Mysteriously Yours is that of a multimillionaire (nobody says he's multi, but anything less would cut very little ice these days) by the name of William Randolph. He seems, however, to have little in common, richness and deadness apart, with the late Mr. Hearst. Nor, more surprisingly perhaps, does he bear any notable resemblance to Hearst's most celebrated fictional equivalent. This show is not a spoof of Citizen Kane. It isn't really a spoof of anything, except perhaps itself.

That's a pity. Mysteriously Yours has always been far more concerned with being funny than with being thrilling, and it has thrived in the past on parodying such highly recognizable sources as Casablanca and The Godfather. (The two shows in question are each being given occasional performances during the holidays.) When left to its own devices, it's left with its own devices and nothing more. This dinner theatre still provides both dinner and theatre of a higher quality than that combination would generally suggest, but in the new show, Last Will and Videotape, it's coasting.

Anyway. The centenarian Randolph has left behind a baby-doll wife whose protuberances might themselves be murder weapons and who is greedy, a daughter who is grasping, her videographer boyfriend who is covetous, unctuous Uncle Shecky, who is after every nickel he can get, and the family lawyer. All expected handsome handouts in the will of the deceased, so all had a motive to decease him. There's also a pianist, though he's there to finger rather than be fingered. This is, I believe, the first Mysteriously Yours production to feature a musical number: a spirited company rendition of Well Did You Evah, with new lyrics that would probably not have amused Cole Porter.

And, most important, there is Jean Daigle, the troupe's protean resident sleuth, appearing this time as a Newfie cop. Daigle's zest and his quick-thinking authority never cease to impress me, and this time he gets to play another role as well; he appears, in screened flashback, as the murdered man, of whom one can only say that he had it coming, no matter whence it came. We see him about to make another will, which can only mean trouble. I wonder if some day some higher-minded theatre group might kidnap Daigle and force him, for more glory but probably lower wages, to play Moliere or Feydeau. They could give him back later, and everybody would win. I should add that his solving of the case seemed to me, on this occasion, exceptionally clear. I actually understood who had done it, and how, and why. I always guess wrong, but this time I felt like kicking myself.

I saw the alternate company for Movin' Out and they were as impressive as the A-team celebrated in the Post by my colleague Michael Crabb. I cannot in fact imagine any improvement on James Fox (no, not that one, though he is also English), who played piano and sang the Billy Joel songs. That includes Joel himself; Fox is grittier. The musical highlight of the evening was his rendition, in a screaming growl, of We Didn't Start the Fire, a ferocious catalogue of American icons from the end of the Second World War to the Kennedy assassination. ("JFK blown away, what else do I have to say?")

It wasn't, for me, the choreographic highlight. The evocation of the Vietnam War that Twyla Tharp had arranged on the main stage looked to me pretty generic, whether it was going for the pity or the terror. Movin' Out is by current standards an unusually good show, but I would question the general assumption that the music and the staging are always in perfect sync. Quite often one outpaces the other. Tharp, for example, gets a grave and lovely romantic duet out of the unpromising Just the Way You Are: "I don't want clever conversation" ... a sensible decision when you're about to rhyme "hard" with "are."

Or maybe that line's a sly prophetic comment on the show's lack of any kind of conversation. It isn't quite true that it has no spoken words, but for the most part song and dance have to carry not just one another but the characters and story. It reinforced my doubts about using pre-existing songs in a narrative musical (as opposed to a revue). The show never plumbs the stupid depths of Mamma Mia! and its songs are far better in the first place. They are used as commentary, and the show is strict enough about this concept to deny Fox, or whoever, to sing Joel's Piano Man -- because that would, inescapably, be a song about him. Tharp had great success with a ballet based on Frank Sinatra songs, but there was no story and they danced to records. This one, with the music present, is close enough in feeling to a conventional musical for it to become frustrating that the characters don't have a chance to sing their own songs.

It also hurts that the characters don't have much character, only names: Brenda and Eddie and Tony and James and Judy, all chosen because they crop up in Joel songs -- that arbitrary. The three boys go off to war, and one is killed. So that's why Vietnam was fought: for the sake of romantic symmetry. Back home in Long Island, the remaining quartet drift and decline, but love and redemption somehow prevail, and all ends sappy, I mean happy, with the dead one giving his ghostly blessing.

Joel's songs really are dramatic, in their lyrical detail and in their dramatic sweep, and I'd far rather hear an original musical from him than from Elton John. Tharp's choreography, thrillingly emphatic in its mix of flashing lights and flashing legs, similarly matches yearning and aggression. At its best, the show has the same kind of excitement as the great dance musicals West Side Story and A Chorus Line. But its form prevents it from taking that extra, how you say, step.
