This year the American musical lost two men from its last great generation of writers, the group that first made its mark in the late 1950s and early '60s. That doesn't mean that no talented lyricists and composers have emerged since; but Fred Ebb, who wrote words, and Cy Coleman, who wrote music, each managed the kind of consistent, productive career that has been denied to their successors. Both started when costs were still just about containable, and a writer was allowed a speedy second chance.

Ebb's first Broadway show was Flora the Red Menace (1965), which introduced Liza Minnelli; Coleman's was Wildcat (1960), Lucille Ball's first musical and also, to no one's surprise, her last. Both flopped, but each was followed within two years by what turned out to be its writers' masterpiece; Ebb, with his composer John Kander, wrote Cabaret; Coleman, with his lyricist Carolyn Leigh, Little Me.

Coleman and Ebb never collaborated, though it was talked of, and they might have made a good team; each was known for writing smart, hard and witty songs. Coleman, with whom I'm concerned here and who was my second-favourite contemporary composer, died a month ago of a heart attack, and that death, at the age of 75, was a shock. He fell sick, suddenly and dramatically, at a first-night party (though not for one of his own shows, that would have been too much). He had always seemed, in his life and his work, to be boyishly buoyant (he had one of the most distinctive, pealing laughs I have ever heard), and he had half-a-dozen projects on the boil. There had been a late- life marriage, a daughter and a rejuvenating return -- with a glossy New York club booking -- to his early career as a jazz-flavoured singer-pianist.

There was something very satisfying about that, because it was in the Manhattan cabaret scene of the '50s that Coleman had found himself as a composer. A concert prodigy (he played at Carnegie Hall when he was seven), he had switched to jazz, formed his own trio and started writing songs. These immediately became favourites with late- night singers, including the two cult monarchs, the matronly Mabel Mercer and the effervescing Bobby Short. To this day, in what remains of New York cabaret, Coleman's songs are probably sung more often than those of any other composer later than Cole Porter. Meanwhile, his tunes had been taken up by performers with a far wider constituency: Frank Sinatra, for whom he composed Witchcraft, and especially by Peggy Lee (who herself wrote some songs with him) and Tony Bennett, both of whom recorded an album's worth of Coleman material.

These songs included It Amazes Me, which is about sophistication caught off-guard; The Rules of the Road, which is about sophistication getting dumped and drowning its sorrows in a kind of stoical bravado; and The Best Is Yet to Come, which is about sophistication facing a glorious erotic future. The last, written in 1959, somewhat belied its title by turning out to be not only the best song of Coleman's career but the best non-theatre song to be written by anyone after the end of the Second World War. Introduced by Bennett, but recorded definitively by Sinatra with Count Basie, it makes stunningly resourceful use of one simple, muscular melodic idea and a rhythm that, through all its undulations, never lets up. It's a song that's supremely confident but never behaves like a bully. It also has a Carolyn Leigh lyric ("you think you've flown before but you ain't left the ground") as snappy and driving as the music. Leigh, whose qualities included an endless joy in and flair for rhyming, matched her collaborator not only in worldliness but in occasional unexpected vulnerability. In On Second Thought, about regrets and trying not to phone, she finds a sung equivalent for the spirit of Dorothy Parker -- something, incidentally, that Parker herself, in her few songwriting efforts, signally failed to do.

Leigh is usually considered Broadway's second-best female lyricist, first place going to Dorothy Fields, who belonged to the really great generation, the pre-war one. I think I prefer Leigh, certainly as a partner for Coleman. He split up with Leigh after their first two shows (because of her insecurities, or so it's usually claimed) and teamed up with Fields. Their first show, Sweet Charity (1966), is probably Coleman's best-known; it's scheduled for a revival next year. It's terrific entertainment, and its score is probably the last from a traditional Broadway musical to have entered the cultural bloodstream. But there is more energy than inventiveness; some of the wit has gone, from the music as well as the words.

Here's a paradox. The theatre was lucky to have Coleman; in his group of Broadway composers, he ranks second only to Stephen Sondheim. And yet it's not certain that the theatre is where he belonged. Little Me is his only great show, though it was never a great hit. A merciless spoof of show-business autobiographies, its durability seems threatened, not by the writing -- nearly everything in the score and in Neil Simon's book is hilarious -- but by the impossibility of finding a comedian capable of taking the right cartoon approach to the show's multiple leading roles. (They were written -- all seven of them -- for Sid Caesar.) Constructed of episodes, sketches really, it allowed Coleman and Leigh to write in their discrete pop/cabaret style, which was their best style. And indeed at least three of the songs have become extractable standards.

That's not to say that his later work is negligible. He wrote an intentionally overripe comic-opera score for the art-deco farce On the Twentieth Century; a jazz -- well, jazz-pop-Hollywood --score for City of Angels, a staged film noir with its own built-in commentary; a soul-sleaze-gospel score for The Life (1997), which is about Times Square prostitution and contains a song called The Oldest Profession that's about the only full-throated roof-raising diva number of the last three decades that doesn't insult its audience's intelligence. These are all substantial shows, even though none of them is quite satisfying. Coleman, as you'd expect from his background, was a stylistic jackdaw, but his essays in style went beyond pastiche; there was always something of himself in them. He used to have a trademark: the Coleman march-tune (Hey Look Me Over, Pass Me By, It's Not Where You Start) that sounded like a generic rouser but always turned out to have flair, spring, amusement. One show, the circus musical Barnum (1980), was largely made up of tunes like this but they all turned out to be worth paying attention to; indeed, they were the only things in the show that were.

There were also bolts of sheer melodic sweetness. Barnum has a song for Jenny Lind that you can imagine the legendary nightingale having actually sung. City of Angels boasts what may be the last great torch song. Coleman was the heir of George Gershwin, who made jazz work in the theatre, and Harold Arlen, who didn't quite but wrote magnificent songs anyway. That makes him unique among his peers, and with no apparent successors.

He was also, to the end, a matchless performer of his own songs, as demonstrated on a recent CD, It Started with a Dream, and on a 30- year-old LP that should be reissued. I once asked him about the apparent cult of self-laceration among cabaret performers. He didn't want any part of it. "No," he said, with that laugh, "I've done my suffering. I love what I do, and I'm lucky." And that, in everything he did, comes across.
