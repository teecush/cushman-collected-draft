For as long as I can remember, Second City's revues have taken place on inoffensive sets mainly composed of doors. The new one does away with all that. Second City for Mayor demonstrates its commitment to urban renewal by erecting a new multi-levelled set composed of fire-escapes and catwalks. The design, by Camellia Koo, gives new opportunities for inventive staging, of which Melody A. Johnson's direction takes full advantage. Especially good are the transitions from one sketch to the next, with Steve Del Balso's lighting bathing the stage each time in something rich and fairly strange. This may be the most stylish Second City show ever.

It is not, however, the funniest. Some things are very good, and there's an unaccustomed consistency of tone, but overall standards of both writing and performance are down from the last few shows. What gives it unity, apart from its visual excellence, is a mordancy about Toronto that has been an intermittent theme before but that here takes over. Even the title has a purposeful air about it. These comic candidates call their constituency "city town," and it even seems to have its own superhero, the Blue Falcon, who looks not unlike Adam Giambrone, with skeletons falling out of wherever it is he keeps his cape. (He more or less says that he has better things to do than save his fellow-citizens from themselves. They more or less say that they have better things to do than to be saved.) There's a scene in a hard-core gym, terrifyingly calling itself the lifelike name Extreme Fitness, where clients are given new names that combine natural disasters with elements from the periodic table. (Hello, Iron Famine.) The idea is repeated rather than developed, but visually, with all those levels coming into their own as things to hang bodies from, it's a scream.

The design reflects the fact that most of the show is set, if not outdoors, then in public spaces, even when the subjects treated are basically domestic. Befuddled, sleep-deprived new fathers huddle for mutual commiseration in -- where else? -- Shoppers Drug Mart. Even the mandatory dating-sketch has a new spin on it: An operative from eHarmony has been sent to discover firsthand why one of their less successful clients ( "we call you the black hole") scares off all the men they fix her up with; it turns out to have something to do with the falcon that perches on her wrist, and that we are told about but, alas, never see. There is one family-values item, but it, too, is a little off the beaten track; mom and dad are shocked to find their daughter sexting, and even more shocked by how mild it is compared with what they used to get up to. But this again is just one joke -- all right, one-and-a-half jokes--repeated.

Two numbers do break new ground. One of them is an encounter between an ex-Guantanamo inmate, now running a Coffee Time, and one of his former tormentors. This may be the blackest revue sketch since Beyond the Fringe took on capital punishment; and unlike that one, it manages to work as comedy, superficially bland but dangerous. The other is an old-time hootenanny, on the theme of countries you probably wouldn't want to live in: titled It Could Be Worse. As to which countries those might be, Reid Janisse takes suggestions from the audience, some of them less expected than others. He probably has some lyrics prepared, but, spontaneous or not, you have to respect a man who, asked to sing about Iran, can come up in perfect rhyme and metre with a line about a place whose name is the past tense of "I run." This is an historic occasion -- the first time I have ever laughed out loud at a Second City song. Music has not generally been this organization's strongest suit.

Janisse, the most seasoned and familiar of the performers, is admirably crisp and exact throughout: not so much the evening's star (it doesn't have one) as its tent-pole, holding the whole edifice up. Adam Cawley is lanky, fresh-faced and underneath it all (he's the Falcon), lethal. Caitlin Howden has a virtuoso bit, especially well staged, glorifying the human hand, and a dead-on portrayal of a kindergarten teacher dedicated to squashing her students' dreams just as her own were squashed. Rob Baker, playing politicians and other loudmouths, meets them rather too enthusiastically on their own terms; he's more restrained and more effective, oddly enough, in the complimentary improv section that follows the main performance -- at least, he was the night I went. (Funnily enough, the improv in general seemed more structured than some of the rehearsed stuff that preceded it. Could it be...? Oh no, perish the thought.) Dale Boyer and Kris Siddiqi complete the company. In Second City tradition, motifs from earlier scenes recur in later ones, but this time there's even an attempt to tie them all together at the finale. One perpetual failing remains: Johnson certainly knows how, visually, to frame a sketch's ending but nobody, it seems, knows how to write it. I blame television.
