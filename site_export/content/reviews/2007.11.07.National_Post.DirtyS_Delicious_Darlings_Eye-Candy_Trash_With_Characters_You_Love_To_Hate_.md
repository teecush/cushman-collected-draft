'Iwant," said one of the spoiled-rotten young things on Dirty Sexy Money ,"to be a human being." This was in the first episode, which aired six weeks ago, and it struck me at the time that she'd come to the wrong show. Now I'm not so sure. Nobody is going to give DSM, as it shall henceforth be known, marks for ruthless realism. But it is succeeding on all levels, and to do that it has to create its own kind of truth.

It's enjoyable social-calendar, eye-candy trash. It has a mystery plot as its baseline, not nail-bitingly tense, but compelling in a relaxed way that's rather refreshing. It has the apparently effortless wit that TV shows so often find it easier to attain when they are not structured or marketed as comedies. It's very well acted.

Besides, how would most of us know whether it was real? DSM centres on the Darling family, who are darling in the way that's only possible when the head of the house is a millionaire, probably multi. (Not a billionaire. There is one of those on the show, and Papa Darling describes him as such, as if aware of the gulf between them. Not that he sounds very awestruck about it. He seems to loathe the guy. Where the right lies, if it lies anywhere, is yet to be revealed.) The younger Darlings carry on as if they too were millionaires, or at least have splendiferous allowances; Trickle- down economics can work if your circumstances are rarefied enough.

Set against all this corruption (we assume) and decadence (we know) is our hero Nick, a New York lawyer primarily engaged in representing charities pro bono; That is, he works like a dog and gets thrown a bone. Nick's late father, known as Dutch, devoted his life to being the Darlings' consigliere, and Nick has been offered the chance to go the same route.

Despite initial skepticism, that borders on repulsion, Nick accepts: for a variety of reasons that include discovering who, if anyone, killed his dad. Peter Krause plays Nick and he brings to the role, and to the antics that surround him, an expanded version of the amused bemusement that distinguished his performance in Six Feet Under (which I heretically think was a less good show).

Krause has marvellous chemistry with Donald Sutherland, who plays Tripp, the Darling-in-chief; The two are partners in a Faustian bargain of which both are well aware and that neither cares to bring to the surface. What goes on between them is not just a battle of wits, but a battle of wit, and both performances quietly gleam.

An enemy of Tripp's warns Nick that "He's not what you think he is." But Nick doesn't know what he thinks Tripp is, and neither do we. The show's richest men are very nice to Nick -- suspiciously so - - and Nick is very nice to everyone, in a tolerantly judgmental sort of way. He behaves like everybody's uncle -- perhaps, in view of his ancestry, their Dutch uncle.

Tripp Darling is cultured. He gives his chauffeur (who doubles as personal airplane-pilot, and trebles as fall-guy every time a family member commits a traffic offence) a copy of Xenophon and tells him to keep at it; The rewards will justify the effort.

Sutherland does this kind of thing beautifully, with a great paternalistic purr. He has more trouble keeping his immediate family in line. He's recently discovered -- or did he always know? -- that his wife Letitia (a majestic Jill Clayburgh) was carrying on for forty years with his late friend and lawyer. As a result, one of Tripp's children isn't his -- so Letitia has been getting in Dutch, as well as vice versa -- but we have been left guessing which. They're a fine collection, all raised to believe that money can buy anything and anybody.

There's Patrick, a senate hopeful, who's played, very well, by Willliam Baldwin. He's being groomed as a future president of the U.S. and he does seem to have a few ideals. However his chances of even making it to senator are jeopardized by an affair with a transsexual hooker. (That's what the exasperated Nick calls her. Patrick denies she's a hooker.)

There's Karen, on the brink of her fourth marriage, but still with her hooks into Nick,

her childhood sweetheart: "He deflowered me," she informs her current fiance, who seems commendably unfazed. It may be an index of how deeply the show has its hooks into me that I've come to detest Karen for her transparent campaign to snatch Nick from his nice normal wife Lisa. It would be cool if she turned out to be the bastard, making her Nick's half-sister, but I doubt the show would go there.

On the other hand, it has given us the Darling twins, Jeremy and Juliet, in whom the sense of entitlement (and resentment over it) reaches delirious depths, and who revel in their "twintuition" which they carry to the verge of being twincestuous.

The remaining sibling is Brian, a priest with an illegitimate son whom he's trying to pass off as a Swedish orphan. At first I thought Brian was a Catholic priest, but it turns out he's married; I'm not sure whether that makes his situation more or less shocking. He started out as the most obnoxious character, and he's still the sharpest-tongued, but as life has closed in on him he's become rather endearing.

The show also features guest celebrities. Some are kept waiting, unheard and unseen, on the ends of phones ("Hey, Condi!"), some appear as themselves (Dan Rather covers a Darling party), some even act (Peter Bogdanovich, perhaps the world's best cameo performer, put in a delightful appearance in the first week, though I have no idea who he was meant to be).

For all its mix of schadenfreude and voyeurism, DSM avoids the airless camp of Dynasty or Dallas, still the definitive plutocratic soaps. (Which reminds me: Juliet, told by Jeremy that Pluto's not a planet, replied ineffably, "It will always be a planet to me.") It's kept grounded by being viewed through the quizzical eyes of Nick, whose status as narrator must mean that he'll be alive at the end, unless of course they're going to pull a Sunset Boulevard on us.

I hope, anyway, that everything will be wrapped up in a single series. That would be so much classier. Though with all that Dirty Sexy Money at stake, I wouldn't want to bet on it.

- Dirty Sexy Money airs tonight at 10 p.m. on A-Channel.
