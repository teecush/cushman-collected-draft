Summerworks is a juried festival, which makes it surprising that something as awful as Fabuloso (Tarragon Mainspace) should turn up there. Not that juries aren't fallible, but at alternative theatrefests their mistakes are likelier to lie in the direction of earnestness and pretension.

Fabuloso, by the American John Kolvenbach, is blatantly commercial in every respect except its awkward 75-minute length. The show has received an unprecedented amount of hype due to the presence in the cast, after a long time away from the stage, of Nicholas Campbell. He has a few quiet moments here that are rather affecting, but most of the time he is shouting or gabbling or both. He plays one half of a frantically fractious engaged couple seen invading the home of another couple, married, whose relationship has reached an advanced state of stagnation. Or so we are told; we're asked to take an extraordinary amount on trust.

The husband and the male intruder are boyhood friends who haven't seen each other in years. The hosts, as the play all but comes out and says, accept their unruly guests as substitutes for the children whom, for reasons unexplored, they have never had. But there is no reality to either pair, and the play simply lurches from one scene to the next. Surest sign of a failed piece of theatre: The audience starts clapping just before the final scene, in the kind hope that it's all over.

Summerworks has another cuckoo-in-the-nest play, In Full Light, by young playwright Daniel Karasik.

A teenage girl is knocked down, injured but not killed, by a motorist who, oozing contrition, goes to the victim's mild-mannered father, proffers $6,000 in recompense, and will not be denied. Later he claims to have lost his job and his home and moves in with the family, eventually making off with the wife's jewels and (ambiguously) her affections.

Meanwhile, the daughter throws herself, violently, at the nervous bird-watching young man in the house opposite. It's even hinted that she too is a trickster, having invited the accident to attract his attention. This part of the play is as contrived and overwrought as its heroine. The other half, though, is excellent.

The intruder might be either a con man or an acute study in psychological need; the script keeps both options open and so, insidiously, does David Ferry's performance.

The best Summerworks play I saw was Paul Dunn's Offensive Shadows (Tarragon Extra Space), advertised as a sequel to A Midsummer Night's Dream.

It's also a prequel; we learn, in their own past-tense voices, what happened to Shakespeare's four lovers before he introduced us to them: Demetrius, a nerd, was engaged to Hermia before she met Lysander, a jock; Helena was always the wallflower. We then get a speedy run-down of their adventures in the woods, as they would have remembered them; then their adventures on a reunion picnic, five years after the whirlwind weddings with whose results they are now predictably but not hopelessly disillusioned.

Many productions of the Dream have discovered how close these characters are to modern teenagers; Dunn's very funny, and sometimes probing, script keeps that idiom going while cunningly recalling lines and themes from the original. Performances (Jessica Greenberg, Mark McGrinder, Jason Mitchell, Kimwun Perehinec) and direction (Michael Shamata) are pitch-perfect.

There's more past-tense narration (stuff is catching) in Hannah Moscovitch's Mexico City (Tarragon Extra Space), in which a married couple from 1960 tell us of their adventures in the titular town, finally breaking down and talking to each other when the local customs get to them. Brendan Gall (the birdman from In Full Light) and Molly Atkinson, who could be louder, are the actors.

It's a squib but expert and entertaining, especially by comparison with Yogyakarta, with which it shares a double-bill and which concerns revolution, hostage-taking and idealistic Canadian ignorance in Indonesia. Written by Alan Dilworth (in better form as director of Mexico City), it never gets beyond stating familiar positions. Its best feature is a slanting frame of a set, designed by Camellia Koo.

Another, slightly better, hostage-play, Martin Julien's Home Free (Factory Mainspace), also has a few ideas and no place to take them. "Inspired by, but not based on" last year's abduction of Christian pacifists in Iraq, it shows one captive's gay partner asking the authorities back in Canada to let him, and their relationship, play a more visible role in the rescue effort. The actors, especially Hume Baugh, do their best to personalize the characters, but they remain debating points --obscure ones.

I suppose these plays could be described as "political." But if political drama is to amount to anything, beyond making its perpetrators feel important, it has to offer more than the repackaging of stuff we can read in the papers.

It also has to mean more than making jokes about Conrad Black, though that's mostly what we got at Summerworks edition of The Wrecking Ball (Theatre Passe Muraille), whose formula is plays written on topical topics at a week's notice. The closing item, by Linda Griffiths, was titled Simply Black. But it turned out to be good: an imagining of its protagonist's reapplication for Canadian citizenship that worked as literary parody, as performance (David Ferry again, with Griffiths herself dark-glassed and silent as his lady) and as drama of some depth and ambiguity If it was a hatchet job, the hatchet was double-edged. Which is what political theatre, like any other kind of theatre, is supposed to be.
