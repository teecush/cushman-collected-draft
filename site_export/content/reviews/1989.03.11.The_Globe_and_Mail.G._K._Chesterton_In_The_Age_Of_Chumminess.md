G. K. Chesterton in the age of chumminess

GILBERT: THE MAN WHO WAS G. K. CHESTERTON BY MICHAEL COREN Random House, 270 pages, $29.95

Saturday, March 11, 1989

ROBERT CUSHMAN

REVIEW BY ROBERT CUSHMAN An age of chumminess is upon us. Michael Coren, a British journalist now living in Canada, is far too young ever to have known G. K. Chesterton. But here he is, in his title and throughout his text, positively on first- name terms with the chap. This is impudent and grating, and sometimes confusing. Corens very first paragraph contains a reference to a more familiar Gilbert, the one who wrote with Sullivan. You would think he might have noticed.

After a few pages of Coren's slipshod prose you realize that he wouldn't. Pondering his heros sexuality - it is possible, though Coren judges it unlikely, that Chestertons long and happy marriage was unconsummated - he announces: "In the early 1900s, the pre-permissive decades, faithfulness within the marriage bed, even if that bed was used only for sleeping upon, was considered standard." If that sentence is unravelled, it seems to mean that Edwardian husbands never brought their mistresses home, for fear of waking up their wives. That is what it seemsto mean. What it probablymeans is that Edwardian husbands rarely took mistresses at all. Which is not true.

It is mystifying that a man can write so clumsily after immersing himself in prose as felicitous as Chesterton's; sad, too, since Coren is intelligent and has a respectable supply of insights. By his account, Chesterton had not at all a bad life. At his death in 1936 he was one of London's pre-eminent literary journalists, and a respected biographer, essayist and versifier. As for his fiction, he had written some striking satirical fantasies (The Napoleon Of Notting Hill, The Man Who Was Thursday) and had created Father Brown, the Catholic priest as detective, or, as I like to think of him, the Bloodhound of Heaven.

Chesterton was probably the most famous British Catholic writer of the twentieth century, at least until the advent (is that the word?) of Graham Greene. The contrast between the two is instructive, a matter not just of temperament but of era. Greenes religion, at any rate as it appears in his fiction, is an instrument of torture. For Chesterton, a late but predestined convert, it was an emanation of well-being; one would call it a source of consolation, except that there didnt seem to be much he needed to be consoled about.

In his youth he was exploited - overworked and underpaid - by publishers and editors, but he liked working and was unconcerned about money. When, later, he needed it, he made it. There was not even much trauma in his childhood. His father was a rationalist who nevertheless sent his children to nonconformist chapel, thereby giving his son two things to rebel against but apparently nothing much to resent. At school, an outsize shambling figure, Chesterton became, says Coren, a mascot, accepted as the archetypal eccentric and tolerated as such. He made good there as part of a self-created elite, helping to found a magazine and a debating society, two entities that, in one form or another, followed him through his life. He needed not just friendship but fellowship, companions to write, talk, eat and drink with.

That eating and drinking, as was more or less said of Sir Toby Belch, nearly undid him. He was obese by 30, and at 40 almost succumbed to a combination of gout and heart trouble, though he rallied and lived another 20 years. He was also very tall. In the dust-jacket photograph he looms at you, a rumpled, squinting figure in an untidily curling collar and layers of coat. He looks a slightly Frenchified (which would have pleased him) Victorian sage, just out of his time.

Like those Victorians, he invariably knew he was right, or wrote as if he did. In this he greatly resembles his contemporary and sparring partner, Bernard Shaw. Philosophically they had nothing in common, spiritually they had everything. ("I am the only person who understands him," wrote GKC of GBS, and I do not agree with him.) Chesterton's prose reeks of the saloon bar and Shaw's of the health farm, but essentially both are saying the same thing: "How simple life would be if people would only listen to me."

The paradox, Chestertons famous weapon, was Shaws as well. Both were masters of the verbal rapier, but used it so remorselessly that it might as well have been a bludgeon.

Chesterton could toss off an aside as enchanting as this on Prohibition: Well-to-do Americans were only too delighted to discuss it over the nuts and wine. They were even willing, if necessary, to dispense with the nuts. He could also write verse as unbearably back-slapping as his address to F. E. Smith, a politician who had had the temerity to express an opinion on religion: "Talk about the pews and steeples and the cash that goes therewith! But the souls of Christian peoples . . . Chuck it, Smith!"

This cocksureness also led Chesterton to espouse the cause of Distributism - or every man his own smallholding - as an alternative to what he saw as the doomed creeds of capitalism and communism. Coren, a little severely, sees this as a mild forerunner of fascism. He never engages, though, with the criticism George Orwell made of Chesterton: that though a true friend of democracy according to his lights, during his last 20 years he "suppressed his sensibility and intellectual honesty in the service of Roman Catholic propaganda." Indeed, Coren never mentions Orwell at all.

He writes at length, however, of the greatest stain on Chesterton's reputation: his alleged anti-Semitism. He acquits him of any vicious prejudice; some of his best friends truly wereJews. Unfortunately some of his best friends were anything but; they included his brother Cecil and - notoriously - Hilaire Belloc. Belloc wrote a jolly poem that ended: Now and then we persecute these curly-headed men. It is unlikely that Chesterton would ever have made jokes about persecution, but over a glass of beer he might well have laughed agreeably at Belloc's. Chesterton, says Coren, "gave in to the meagre temptations of a vile fashion."

Oddly enough, he had himself been the victim of a bizarre form of anti- Semitism; an editor had turned down one of his early articles because he thought the handwriting too Jewish. When he came to write on the Jewish question - it is significant that he thought there was one - his attitude was paternalistic. He was prepared to judge tolerantly, but he reserved the right to judge. But it is doubtful that he did much harm, and more important that, long before it became fashionable, he was an unflinching anti-Nazi. "I am quite ready to believe now that Belloc and I will die defending the last Jew in Europe."

He may even have been right about Belloc. After all, Ezra Pound was sobered by the revelations of the death camps. Chesterton died too early for that experience. It would doubtless have made him very tactful - for a time. A permanently tactful Chesterton would be difficult to imagine, and impossible to enjoy.

In accordance with the canons of pop biography, Coren has written his book as a flashback, devoting his first chapter to his subjects death and funeral. It is difficult to produce tears over the fate of a man to whom your readers have not yet been properly introduced, but Coren does his best. "Around 10:15 on the morning of 14th June, 1936, Gilberts soul passed gently from this earth." There's pathos for you. Chuck it, Mike. Robert Cushman is former drama critic of The Observer of London.
