QUE SERA

Artword Theatre, Toronto

This staged biography of Doris Day begins with the central character behind the mike and in front of the band. There's a medley going on, and the musicians -- they're just a trio -- seem to be playing cut-down versions of swing-era charts, the kind of things that first put Day on top in the 1940s. The songs, though, sound strangely unfamiliar, and it takes a moment to realize that they are actually the title tunes from Day's light-comedy films of the '60s - - Lover Come Back, Pillow Talk, Teacher's Pet -- given a new sound. Or, more precisely, an older sound that seems new. This collapsing of two periods of the subject's career, as in a dream, is an ingenious idea. It turns out to be the last idea of the evening.

Morgonn Ewen, who plays Doris, manages to look not unlike her, especially after donning a full blond wig later in the show. Her singing, though it lacks the original's secure swing and strong lower register, is most of the time a fair facsimile in tone and phrasing. A singer rather than an actress (she may be the first leading lady to earn additional billing as "assistant musical director"), she manages to inject a tremulous appeal into her spoken lines. And that exhausts the litany of permissible compliment for what is otherwise an appallingly inept show.

Written by Claudette Williams and directed by Rebecca Cann, it seems to fancy itself as hard-hitting. It seizes on the obvious contrast between the sunny songs its subject sang and the troubled life she actually led -- and hits us relentlessly over the head with it, without ever exploring either the myth or the reality. The opening sequence concludes with Doris going into a genuine oldie, Lullaby of Broadway, and breaking up on it. She is then berated by the five other members of the cast for screwing up the lyric (to my chagrin, I hadn't noticed) and for self-indulgent behaviour generally. Clearly they represent her demons, mental and corporeal; and they hang around for the rest of the night, commenting and doing impersonations.

We flash back to the subject's childhood; she overhears her father having adulterous sex and she suffers a car accident that puts an end to hopes of a dancing career. In ironic counterpoint to these and other early traumas, she and her colleagues partly sing, partly intone, the optimistically romantic lyrics of When I Fall in Love. She grows up and goes through three marriages (and three sarcastically mimed wedding ceremonies): Her first husband abuses her, causing the miscarriage of her second baby; her second neglects her; and her third, her manager after she achieves stardom, all but ruins her. At some point in this progression she pauses to sing It's Magic.

Meanwhile, she's having a career. Still bearing her baptismal name of Doris Kappelhoff, she auditions for Les Brown -- a top and extremely durable big-band leader, here portrayed as an amiable hick -- and with minimal effort wins a job. Her audition song is Day by Day; her new employers feel she should change her name, and we sit there patiently -- or not -- waiting for the penny to drop. Later she gets to Hollywood and meets director Michael Curtiz, who demonstrates a great gift for helpful exposition: "I want you to come by the studio tomorrow" he tells her "and do a screen test for my new picture, Romance on the High Seas." So then come the movie musicals, and then the non-musical comedies opposite Cary Grant and Rock Hudson, and a TV series, and a retirement devoted to running hotels for dogs.

There was a war on when Day was a band singer and it contributed to her success, especially with her big hit Sentimental Journey. The show, though, hasn't noticed. Nor does it seem at all concerned with either the period or the content of her films. "She just floated along with whatever came up," a musician once said about her, but the show goes further and presents her as a total zombie.

In her movies, though, she appears not only squeaky clean, for which she's notorious, but capable, and the paradox might at least be worth mentioning. We never learn then what her fans saw in her, or why she has since been revalued as an unlikely feminist heroine. (She was an excellent singer, but that of course is the last thing anybody cares about.)

The only whiff of history, or even of drama, comes when we reach the '60s; we are reminded that the Charles Manson gang intended to kill Day's son Terry, who had let his house to Sharon Tate. This information is relayed to us by a couple of recurring ladies who keep gossiping about "our Doris" and who read about the murders -- for the first time, apparently -- in the centre pages of a supermarket paper; seems the story couldn't even make the front of the tabloids.

The worst of Day's assembly-line musicals can never have been as baldly or badly written as this; indeed, her own stab at a biopic -- Love Me or Leave Me, based on the life of '30s torcher Ruth Etting - - is by comparison a masterpiece of atmosphere or accuracy. Its title song, as it happens, occasions Ewen's best singing here; her worst comes in a closing rendition of 'S Wonderful (yes, there's an upbeat ending), done in a twittering style that suggests some confusion with Day's lesser alliterative contemporaries, Patti Page or the terminally syrupy Joni James.

Nobody else in the cast seems able to sing, though they're all required to. Their acting is unhappy, too; they all, whether slamming it across with desperate bravado or mumbling it like a criminal confession, radiate intense distrust of their material. Since one of them is also the author, this is really serious. The physical production looks poverty-stricken: financially, which is of course forgivable, and imaginatively, which isn't.
