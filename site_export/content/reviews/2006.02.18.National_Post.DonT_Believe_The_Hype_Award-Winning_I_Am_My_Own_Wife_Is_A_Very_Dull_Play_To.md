An East German man named Lothar von Beerfelde spent much of his life, and certainly the most fulfilling parts of it, dressed as a woman and going under the name of Charlotte von Mahlsdorf. Lothar/ Charlotte, whose biography presents anyone writing it with a pretty problem in pronouns, spent his childhood under the Nazis and much of her adulthood under the Communists. Lothar's father was Nazi to the core, and showed it in the way he treated his family. Charlotte devoted her life to preserving parts of her country's nobler past, turning her Berlin home into a museum notable for its collection of furniture and -- her special passion -- old gramophone records. Being homosexual made her an easy target for official blackmail, and the Stasi pressured her into serving as an informer. But she survived the regime and ended up honoured for her cultural activities, if pilloried for her snitching.

It's an intriguing story, or sounds as if it should be. An American writer, Doug Wright, has turned it into a very dull play, which he has called I Am My Own Wife. Those words, or so the play assures us, were Lothar's own answer to the question, posed by his mother while she was hanging out the laundry, of when he was going to settle down and get married. It's a catchy label; also a catch- penny one, since Charlotte's sexuality is one of the many things about which the play has almost nothing to say. It comes to us from New York loaded with awards: a Tony, a Pulitzer, an Obie (since it cleaned up Off-Broadway before cleaning up On) and God knows what else. However bad things get for new Canadian plays -- and this season has been dire -- we can at least comfort ourselves that we don't dole out prizes with such reckless abandon as they do down south. Not, of course, that we have as many prizes to dole.

And not that they have many plays to choose from, or not many that make it to Times Square. Those that do make it seem, increasingly, to be live movies-of-the-week, dedicated to shining a sympathetic spotlight on someone who is ill, unbalanced or in a sexual or intellectual minority. Everybody gets to feel compassionate, and the plays invariably make their way to CanStage. (I'm not knocking the management for this. It's a public service, since we've heard these plays talked about and want to see them. But they don't add much lustre to the resume.) I Am My Own Wife has an added cachet: It's a one-man show (though it might be a test case for gender-blind casting) and almost bound to occasion a virtuoso performance.

Stephen Ouimette is the actor in the Toronto production and he delivers, outlining Charlotte with delicacy and dignity. His first appearance raises the highest hopes; she enters impressively through a pair of double doors in the centre of the back wall, defuses the moment by realizing she has forgotten something, exits apologetically and returns bearing an ancient horned phonograph of the kind that plays cylinders and hypnotizes dogs. (Charlotte is devoted to that old HMV or RCA logo -- depending on where you grew up -- and it could be the single most endearing thing about her.) She has, she tells us, "funfzehn thousand records." Unfortunately, though, Ouimette cannot devote all his energies to playing Charlotte. He has, without changing his dress, to play a load of other characters, including the author himself.

This is what really wrecks the evening. Wright is determined not just to tell us about Charlotte but to tell us about his fascination with her, rather than showing us by writing a real play about her. Nor do we even learn much about what that fascination might have been, beyond the fact that Wright is gay himself. It's somewhat reminiscent of Richard Greenblatt's recent show about Tom Lehrer, but Letters from Lehrer, whatever its flaws, was at least up-front about its real subject being Greenblatt himself, and it provided backup: Greenblatt told us in detail why Lehrer's songs were so important to him. (And of course the fact that he was performing the show himself made it more immediate.) Wright, who first heard about Charlotte from an American reporter-friend in Berlin, gets bogged down in the minutiae of his own trips to and from Germany. He seems to have had a lot more co-operation from Charlotte than Greenblatt did from Lehrer; he got interviews and had access to letters.

One that we hear is from a friend and colleague of Charlotte's, a gay antique dealer whom she shopped to the Stasi. He wrote from prison, with no recriminations. But for all the weight and time this episode is given, it remains an anecdote, diverting our attention to a subsidiary character who, since he never comes to life, either, isn't worth the trouble. Years before this, the young Lothar killed his father, to stop him from persecuting, and probably killing, the entire family. He went to prison for it. Charlotte tells us this, and we are given no reason not to believe it, but we are given no clues as to how these events actually affected her, and little sense of what it was like to grow up a non-Nazi in Nazi Germany. Stuff happens, that's all.

We're told toward the end that there is considerable doubt as to how effective an informer Charlotte actually was. We are also told that we will probably never know the truth. That indeed seems to be the point of the play and of the structure the author has chosen: that we can never know about Charlotte or Wright, himself or anyone. And I believe it; it's old news. I still think it would have been better if Wright had gone ahead and guessed, and come up with either a sustained monologue for Charlotte or -- probably less effective -- a conventional play with characters and a story.

The piece that we have is directed by Robin Phillips, of whom, especially now when we see so little of his work, it seems a waste. He gives it an elegant and, as far as I could tell, a sensitive production, with a few unnecessary touches. The sound system turns up the echo when Charlotte has something particularly poignant or harrowing to say; the play may need the help but the actor doesn't. Hirsham Ali's set is austerely handsome; museum clutter, or something resembling it, is occasionally visible behind the scrim but, once past the first sighting, it's more frustrating than evocative. Louise Guinand's lighting is alternately discreet and striking. Wright says that Charlotte is a transvestite without being a drag queen; she goes through the play, as she apparently did through life, in a plain black dress. Susan Benson is the impeccable designer, though, as she's another great neglected talent, it seems a waste of her, too. Still, Charlotte's status as the least spectacular cross-dresser in theatrical history points to something; she was, as the play tells us, a survivor. And popular culture has taught us that in Germany, the arch-survivors are women: Mother Courage, Fraulein Schneider, anyone played by Marlene Dietrich. In wanting to be one of them, Lothar was joining hands with history, and Charlotte was being practical.
