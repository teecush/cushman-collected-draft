The Hats of Mr. Zenobe plays until May 14, $10-$24. Call 416-504- 9971.

THE HATS OF MR. ZENOBE

Factory Theatre

David Astle, writer and performer, has based his solo play, The Hats of Mr. Zenobe, on the life of Vahan Poladian, who in his 80 years on earth came up against some of the worst horrors of the 20th century.

Poladian was an Armenian whose family managed to flee the country during the Turkish massacres. He was refused entry to the U.S. but gained admission to France. He married another Armenian expatriate and they had a daughter, but they were separated when World War Two broke out and Poladian was conscripted. He was captured and sent to a German prisoner of war camp. When he was released and returned to France, his wife and child had disappeared. He spent his last 20 years in the Armenian Home in the French town of St. Raphael where he seems to have committed a slow form of suicide. During this time, though, he blossomed as a performance artist; allowed one franc a day, he accumulated a collection of old clothes, especially hats.

Thus costumed, he gave twice-daily shows on the streets of St. Raphael, designed to remind the spectators of their own past and of his.

Astle has renamed his protagonist Monsieur Zenobe, and seems to have lengthened his life somewhat. Near the beginning of the show we find him mentally cataloguing his stamp-collection.

He seems to specialize in countries that have been sites of atrocity: Armenia, Germany, Yugoslavia, Rwanda -- the last two only qualified some time after the real Poladian's death, which occurred in 1982. Astle makes Zenobe into a walking catalogue of modern inhumanity.

Astle, a teacher and performer based in Montreal and with a background in clowning and mime, seems to have been attracted by two aspects of the Poladian story. First, there is the mans tragic personal history. The most haunting sequence in the show has Zenobe wearing a miners hat, with its light the only illumination on a darkened stage, searching for his wife and daughter and repeatedly calling out their names, over and over.

This glancing approach to the biography, though, isn't always as effective; and might well be impossible to follow without the information supplied in the program.

Most of Astle's energies go into re-imagining his hero as a performer. This Zenobe is in the concluding stages of a hunger- strike, and he imagines himself escaping from the home, locking himself inside one more welcoming home (which, he doesnt fail to tell us, is where you hang your hat) and giving one last show, an omnibus retrospective.

Its a technique that's become familiar in stage biographies of celebrated actors and singers: cf. Barrymore and Hank Williams: The Show He Never Gave. Zenobe calls his genre the Theatre of Forgetting, though what it wants us to do is remember. He wears a patchwork quilt of a coat, and round his neck he wears an empty picture-frame: empty, that is, until he recalls his lost family, at which point their portrait appears within it. He warns against the authority-figures of the earth --queens and generals and especially popes -- and lampoons them in sequences that are well-meant but laboured.

He wants to equip us for the 21st century. Meanwhile he constantly invokes the 20th, though I doubt that anyone who had actually suffered through so much of it would talk about it so much in the abstract. But then, as I said, he keeps his actual experiences mostly at arm's length. Unfortunately, they shame his chosen style; he speaks largely in a vein of melancholy whimsy that comes close to being unbearable.

There are recurrent loud knocks at the door: the recurrent nightmare of the lifelong refugee Sometimes he ignores them; sometimes he opens it and speaks to his unseen visitors. One of them is the district nurse, whose title he playfully alters to dirty news; the particular dirty news is the threat of an injection ("theyll put chicken in my arm" ) if he doesnt eat. We in the audience keep hoping against hope that there will be someone there, and that the someone will appear on stage.

But no, this is remorselessly a one-man show, and an elaborate one, complete with intermission. (Even Joe Bologna and Renee Taylor, celebrating themselves at the Winter Garden, manage without one of those.) Astle is a clever if lightweight performer, but he hasn't written himself a script inventive enough to sustain his bundle of themes. Would it be unforgivably flip to suggest that the star is wearing too many hats?
