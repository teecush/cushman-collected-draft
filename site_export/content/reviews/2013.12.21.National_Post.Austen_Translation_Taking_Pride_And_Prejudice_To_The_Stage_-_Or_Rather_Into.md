It is a truth universally acknowledged that Jane Austen's Pride and Prejudice has one of the most perfectly balanced opening sentences in English literature. Its words arent spoken in the adaptation of the novel at Campbell House, which doesnt have a narrator. It does nevertheless convey something of its author's amused but merciless voice.

The last time I visited Campbell House for theatrical or indeed any other purposes, it was to see a twoactor dramatization of The Turn of the Screw, which proved to be clever and ultimately scary. Pride and Prejudice, a novel that's theatrically harder to handle, or at least longer, has been staged, all over the stately house, by Burt & Werneburg, a company consisting of two actresses, Hallie Burt and Kate Werneburg, who take responsibility for the adaptation and for playing all the roles. They call their version Elizabeth - Darcy; and, as youd expect, even without that title, it focuses on the most popular courtship in English fiction, that between the proud Mr. Fitzwilliam Darcy and the prejudiced (against him, anyway) Miss Elizabeth Bennet. The results are, again as you might expect, uneven; but theyre ultimately enjoyable.

This is a site-specific production in a site that pretty much imposes its own rules. As with The Turn of the Screw we assemble in the hall, and are then led up and down the main staircase into a succession of dining rooms, sitting rooms and a subterranean kitchen. Unlike The Turn of the Screw, which is very much the story of one particular house, Pride and Prejudice moves between dwellings, so we have to imagine ourselves in some half-dozen establishments in four different English counties. The adaptation does a nifty job of letting us know where we're presumed to be and just who the actors, who don't have time for any but the most basic costume changes, are impersonating at any given moment. The direction, by Tom Arthur Davis, is especially neat in the manner of surprise entrances; you never know through which door somebody is going to enter or who theyre going to be when they get there. All the same, though I understand the desire to make use of every available space, I doubt that even so smug and insensitive a wooer as the Reverend Mr. Collins would have invaded Elizabeths bedroom in order to propose to her. Not in good British society in 1813.

Both performers appear as a multitude of characters, but they naturally take one title role apiece. Burt's Elizabeth is generally delightful, whether pert or hurt, as attractive and intelligent, and with as intuitive a sense of her own dignity, as Austen's readers have always taken her to be. Werneburgs Darcy doesnt initially get beyond a varnish of prim hauteur, inexpertly applied; she does though improve considerably when the character reveals his more vulnerable side.

As for their other assumptions: Burt's wit comes in handy for Lizzie's acerbic father, of whom one would like to see more than this version has time for. His most famous utterance, about Lizzies having to choose between being abandoned by her mother if she refuses Mr. Collins and by her father if she accepts him, is given its due, spoken through a door so as to spare the actress the necessity of talking to herself. Even better is the scene in which the reclusive Mr. Bennet accepts responsibility for neglecting his family, and asks to be allowed to indulge his guilt because - and here I paraphrase - it wont last long. Burt is, however, thoroughly off key as younger sister Lydia, the cause of the incipient family disgrace, whom she plays with a kittenish vulgarity that's out of style and out of period. Both Lydia and her seducer Wickham have been shortshifted by the adaptation, but that's probably unavoidable.

The same fault that hobbles Werneburgs Darcy - an insensitivity to phrasing, so that everything comes out squashed and hasty - also causes her to overplay both Mr. Collins and his rival in tactlessness Mrs. Bennet; the latter, the archetype of managing mothers, is great fun when you hear her cannonading from a distance but most uncomfortable to share a room with. Surprisingly, Werneburg plays the snobbish Lady Catherine de Bourgh, a role that might seem an invitation to Gorgonhood, with considerable and very effective restraint. She is similarly graceful with Elizabeths friend Charlotte, acceding with sad clear eyes to a future as Mrs. Collins, and Jane, eldest of the Bennet sisters; I never realized before how much Pride and Prejudice, with its harried and fractious parents and five marriageable daughters has in common with Fiddler on the Roof. Jane and her noble suitor Mr. Bingley are still not the liveliest company, but then neither are they in the book.

The show was first seen on the Toronto Fringe where, says the director in a program note, critics objected to his ending it with Mozart's Requiem. The irony, he says, is justified, because at least two of the story's marriages are obviously doomed. That's all very well, but the bride descending the staircase as the music plays is Elizabeth herself, with Darcy waiting for her; and the production doesn't seem to want to be ironic about them. Their union is a hard-won victory, in the play as in the book. The same program makes amends, though, by concluding the long customary list of acknowledgments with the name of Jane Austen, without whom.
