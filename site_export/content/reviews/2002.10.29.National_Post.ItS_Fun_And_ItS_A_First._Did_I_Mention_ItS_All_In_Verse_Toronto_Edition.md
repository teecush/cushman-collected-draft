SWOLLEN TONGUES

Factory Studio, Toronto

In days when rhyming couplets were the rage,

They weren't much used upon the comic stage.

John Dryden and his Restoration friends

Found couplets ideal for satiric ends,

In lampoons written for a readers gaze;

And when the call went out for tragic plays,

Heroick Couplets were the style they cherished

For showing warriors who loved, then perished.

But as for comedies, when penning those,

Dryden and Co. did their best stuff in prose.

So Kathleen Oliver, poet, of Vancouver

Executes an unparalleled manoeuvre

When, in a play called Swollen Tongues -- her first --

She gives us comedy thats rhyming-versed.

(Translations from the French, however free,

Don't count; and nor, if you are asking me,

Does Caryl Churchill's tone-deaf Serious Money;

While Steven Berkoffs plays -- no, its not funny.)

I have one disappointment, I confess:

I'd hoped for comedy in modern dress

To match the modern words the author uses,

Some cutting comment on our time's abuses.

But were in period-comedy penitentiary

With costumes bought in from the 17th century.

Or maybe from the 18th. But, whatever,

The settings somewhere between now and never.

The themes that power Oliver's two hours' traffic

Are language, lust and love: some straight, some Sapphic.

Catherine wants Sonja, and woos her with verse;

Thomas the same, but Thomas verse is worse.

Thomas is Catherines brother; they've a tutor,

A Doctor Wise, who's Catherines secret suitor --

Though this last twist is wheeled into position

A bare two minutes before intermission.

A twist and a half, to no ones great surprise

The Doc's a woman, in a man's disguise.

(Were glad she sheds it; frankly she looked weird

In gown, specs, moustache and the briefest beard.)

One thinks of Candide, though more apropos

Would be the artifice of Marivaux.

(His gender-bending, though, is somewhat tearful.

While Oliver scorns bitter-sweet; shes cheerful.)

The English Restoration's undertext

Is here explored: Its sex farce is bisexed.

Shakespeare, as usual, also haunts our minds:

The Bacchae, too, because young Thomas finds

A new dimension in himself when set up

(You have to be there) in an Amazon's get-up.

In fact, all dramas here, as in a mirror. No,

I havent finished; did I mention Cyrano?

Lovers face off in poetry-contest fashion,

As if facility were a gauge of passion.

I find this dubious; but the mood's ironic,

And overall the author's tones a tonic.

Some flaws: Rhymes that are best described as "nearly,

Sentence inversion where the author clearly

Around the words has turned to meet the rhyme,

And padding -- Lord, she does this all the time.

Well, some of it: Her first line ends in work,"

And on its tail, gratuitously, comes "shirk.

What recommends her most to our attention

Is: She gets laughs -- usually by declension.

That is, shell step from staid and pedagogical

Down to the slangy or the scatological.

It's a tough life; and if she isn't Pope

Shes still a cardinal talent, a white hope.

The production is by Necessary Angel:

A venerated troupe on whom a changell

Quickly be visited, since Richard Rose,

Their founder and director, shortly goes

To the equivalent post at Tarragon.

In fact, he's there already. (He's a paragon.)

But in this period of overlap

Hes staged the present play, with all the snap

It merits. David Boechlers set's effective,

Especially in matters of perspective.

Among the cast, Ben Carlson's very fine,

Hitting the mark with Thomas every line.

He comes across as delicate, yet blubbery,

Even in hulking drag, out in the shrubbery.

None of the others is a perfect fit,

Though far more often than they miss, they hit.

Melody Johnsons cool can be sublime,

But she might vary it from time to time.

She's Sonja, self-proclaimed a happy slut.

Catherine's Karen Hines, bubbling but

A little over-eager. Nancy Palk,

As hinted earlier, talks much happier talk

And smiles more winning smiles, when Dr W.

Says, in effect, Let it no longer trouble you:

Im female." The play gains a firmer touch;

That choice of ambiguities was too much.

They end by welcoming us to "Lesbania;

I cant see why they don't say Lesbiania."

It would scan better and make better sense.

And, at the risk of sitting on the fence,

I liked the language, relished the best rhymes

(And eat your hearts out, Buddies in Bad Times)

And yet I left, aware of something lacking:

The play is weightless, less a feast than snacking

But it's a rich snack: rollicking and chic,

And -- at this point in history -- unique.
