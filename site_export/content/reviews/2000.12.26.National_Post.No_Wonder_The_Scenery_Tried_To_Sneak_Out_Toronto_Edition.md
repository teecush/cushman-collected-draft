THE MERRY WIDOW

Jane Mallett Theatre

Franz Lehar, the composer of The Merry Widow was Hungarian, and the show itself was first produced in Vienna. Most of its characters, meanwhile, are citizens of some mythical principality in the very mittel of Mitteleuropa, but all of them are spending their time in Paris, for reasons either of business or pleasure. Pleasure predominates, since this is an operetta and in operetta life, given half a chance, is naturlich and naturellement just one mad whirl. Act one is a party at an embassy, act two a party at a private mansion and act three a party at, where else, Maxim's.

The gentlemen go through the entire entertainment in evening dress; the ladies in gowns of matching elegance except for interludes in which they either bustle around as roguishly upscale Parisian call girls or don milmiad frocks and pigtailed tresses to perform the peasant dances of their native land, looking inevitably like Valkyries working up to horned helmets.

So it all looks defiantly timeless: decoratively unrealistic in 1905, the year of its birth, and decoratively unrealistic now. Unrealistic, however, does not mean unreal; nor is it a licence to commit mayhem upon an unsuspecting script.

According to this new production, the librettists of The Merry Widow worked out topical references to Jurassic Park, the American election and even Mel Lastman (Toronto's profile must be rising if Central European diplomats are going around making jokes about our politicians). That's just in the first act; there are similar gags in the remainder, but I was too disheartened to note them. Now these interpolations may not contradict the show's costume scheme, but they certainly violate its spirit. They reduce a classic comic opera to the level of a Ross Petty Christmas pantomime, with the crucial difference that Mr. Petty knows what he's doing. Guillermo Siva- Marin, who directed this production and also takes credit for additional dialogue and -- God forgive him, for I never shall -- lyrics, clearly doesn't; at least I don't imagine that he actually set out to insult the intelligence of his actors, who delivered this stuff with the kind of desperate bravado that denotes deep embarrassment, or of his audience, who confirmed the performers' worst suspicions by stubbornly refusing to laugh.

It's possible that the Toronto Operetta Theatre caught this long- incubating disease from the Stratford Festival, who used to subject Gilbert and Sullivan to just this kind of stupidity; at least TOT has the excuse of being a small-scale outfit mounting its shows on a shoestring and for limited runs.

The piece itself the director seems to regard, equally patronizingly, as a silly charade interrupted by some pretty tunes. Lehar's tunes are of course more than pretty: They comprise a cavalcade of all that's best -- certainly all that's most familiar - - in European operetta, peaking in the untouchable Vilja. There is also the famous waltz, which in this uncredited translation is called Love Unspoken, and that rousing march of mystified misogynists whose refrain is "Girls, Girls, Girls" but whose printed title is Oh the Women, and which comes across as a sort of male can- can, or perhaps a can't-can't.

Lovely though they are, the melodies can sound stodgy or skittish if they're sung and played with flair, and especially if they're not not firmly anchored to the surrounding dialogue, of which there is a great deal.

The Merry Widow is based on the generally infallible Benedick- and-Beatrice or Fred-and-Ginger conceit of the couple who despise one another so much and so noisily that they have to be in love. She, Anna Glawari, is the free-spirited woman, left rich and happy by the timely death of her husband; he, Count Danilo, is the accredited rake who crossed her path once before. Both hail from somewhere very small and very broke called Pontevedro, and their union is earnestly desired by Baron Zeta, their country's ambassador to France; the widow is the only Pontevedrian with any money, and the baron doesn't want to see her throwing it away on some smooth- tongued foreigner. But he has other problems: He suspects, correctly, that his wife is deceiving him and he very nearly blunders into having his suspicions confirmed. These twin actions are laboriously set up but neatly resolved, the principal couple acting as good angels in the affairs of the secondary menage a trois.

So we need to believe in Anna and Danilo as strong personalities, and we need to sense electricity -- both positive and negative -- flowing between them. Fred Love as Danilo sings strongly and has the right air of decent worldliness in his acting, but his relationship to Barbara Hannigan's Anna is almost non-existent; she has no especial radiance about her -- she seems in fact rather prim, and it's very hard to tell what, at any given moment, she is either speaking or singing about; she trills, remorselessly.

As the straying baroness, Elizabeth Beeler is somewhat more credible, though Craig Ashton as her lover makes a wildly unlikely Parisian rake. Ian Simpson has an efficiently light touch as the Baron's all-purpose factotum, and Gregory Cross puts in some conscientious clowning as his employer, though he never once suggests a man whose world -- personal and professional -- is about to cave in on him. But then I don't suppose his director wants him to.

The story ends with an energetic double flourish of the double standard: The men in the cast upbraid the women for their indiscretions; the women, disguised in masks and decolletage, catch the men in the act, or as close to it as they're likely to get; and we end in the assurance that both sexes are frail, but women are smarter. (This counts as traditional comic wisdom, as preached by Europeans.) Something of this comes over, but never at an adult level.

The costumes are smart, and the sets look at first quite happy in their cut-rate state. Gradually, though, they develop their own kind of desperation. The luckless singers of one ardent duet are thoroughly upstaged by shadowy but very visible supers who take their places behind hitherto isolated bits of greenery and start moving them about. It looks in the half-light as if the scenery is trying to sneak away from the show without telling anybody; on top of this, the transformation, once accomplished, turns out to have been totally unnecessary.

The whole production, obviously intended to be elegantly naughty, is stalled at pretty and giggly. It does, though, make you want to see a rigorously opulent staging of the work directed by, say, Robin Phillips at, say, Stratford (on the assumption that the Festival has outgrown the urge to deface light opera): a production that might take the piece by the scruff of its merry widow and shake it out for real sex, real wit, real pain, and real romance. They're all there, waiting.
