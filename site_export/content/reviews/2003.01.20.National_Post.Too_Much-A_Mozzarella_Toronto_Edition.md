MAMBO ITALIANO

Elgin Theatre, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - One of the moments I liked in Mambo Italiano -- one of the two, if we're going to be exact -- has a young Montreal woman of Italian parentage reflecting on what she found most frightening when she first ventured into the world beyond her own family. She discovered that out there, when people shout at you, its because they dont like you: They're really shouting at you. In Italian households shouting is the normal method of communication, and may even signify deep affection.

I can believe it, just as I can of Jewish households (personal experience) and Greek ones (a reliable informant). But put this behaviour on a stage, try and make a whole play out of it, and the results are coarse as well as deafening. In the case of Mambo Italiano they are even worse, since this one tries, not only to bludgeon us into laughter but also, mamma mia, to touch our hearts and even our minds. Since it doesn't have a thought in its own head, this last presumption is the crowning insult.

Steve Galluccio wrote the play, which has been a huge hit in Montreal in French and English. He gives us a wife, a husband, and their two grown-up children. The loudest of the tribe, is of course the matriarch who, almost equally of course, is called Maria. She bellows practically non-stop. Her husband, Gino, who is very rotund, can put up a token roar himself, but he mostly relies on self-pity and self-deprecation. Their daughter Anna, who is in her thirties, still lives at home, and is a mean screamer herself. Their son Angelo is a mute by comparison, but then hes artistic; like his author, he has a tidy career writing television sitcoms, though his success doesnt prevent his parents from telling him he should get a real job. He is also gay.

Egged on by his sister, who wants him to do her rebelling for her, Angelo comes out to his family. They are of course appalled. (I'm sorry to keep saying "of course" but it's that kind of play.)

Nino, Angelos boyfriend, is also upset, since he was very happy in the closet; the two of them used to enjoy themselves looking down from their window and making fun of Gay Pride parades. Nino meets Pina who, with no supporting evidence that I could see, regards herself as terminally unattractive but is desperate to get a man -- and she throws herself at him. (Pina may be the most demeaning, and least credible, piece of characterization in the play.) Before you know it Nino and Pina are engaged, to the relieved delight of Ninos mother, Lina. (I don't know why so many of the play's characters have rhyming names; maybe its practising to be a musical.) Since Pina, of course, is fiercely competitive with Angelos parents, they have to pretend to be actively proud of their son's sexuality. This is probably preferable to the cliched development we were all dreading, which would have involved them clutching Angelo and his lover to their bosoms, but the scene of competitive parental boasting has to be seen to be disbelieved. Anyway, it heralds an all- round family reconciliation. This occurs in adjacent confessional booths, for maximum cuteness.

The gay plot line concludes with one former partner further out than ever and marching in the parade (mercifully without his parents joining in) and the other going straight and getting married: Something, in other words, for everyone. To be fair, Nino's future heterosexuality is left in doubt; the suggestion, since its silent and economical, is the other moment in the play that I liked.

Did I say the play? There are three of them, going on by turns: The ear-splitting family cartoon, the sensitive homosexual piece and, raising its head at odd moments, the valentine to Montreal itself. They are not compatible. Nobody really objects to stereotypes; they are, after all, only watered-down archetypes, and archetypes always have a respectful press. There is nothing wrong with caricature -- it can, in the theatre as in the visual arts, be a very good thing. But it has to know what its good for, and that does not include tugging at our heartstrings. Mambo Italiano is that most moronic of dramatic oxymorons: A sentimental farce. You may be able to get away with this combination for 30 minutes on TV, but not for two and half hours on a stage. (I'm being unfair to sitcoms, the best of which keep sentimentality at arm's length. Seinfeld always avoided it, and then -- in its much-maligned final episode -- executed a mocking dance on its grave.) Like a sitcom, the play wants us to feel warm toward the four characters around the family dinner table, and cool toward all the others; and, as in a sitcom, this division of sympathies is purely arbitrary, unjustified by anything we are shown.

The nadir here is a prolonged bit of mystification about a dead aunt who may or may not have committed suicide. You might call it a mystery except nobody in the audience is interested in learning the solution. It seems Aunt Yolanda was a baffled life-force who wanted to teach her relatives to dance the mambo. You cant help feeling that her main motive for this was to provide her author with a catchy if barely relevant title.

But then Mambo Italiano takes a baffling approach to both history and language. Its older characters talk like Chico Marx. The play is set, according to the program, in the present. However, when Angelo, by way of providing a first-act climax, arraigns his elders for their petty-mindedness, he says they came to Canada from Italy "because of the war." His parents, though, look to be in their sixties at most. Maybe it was their parents who emigrated. But if so, why havent Gino and Maria learned to speak proper English? And, that being the case, why when they're alone together, are they speaking in broken English rather than in Italian? And how did this conceit work out in the French version? The answer to all these questions, except perhaps the last, seems to be: For the sake of cheap laughs. The cheapness might not matter if the laughs happened. But the humour is woefully predictable. When Poppa mentions the existence of a "homosexuale [NB that is not a typo] village," it's a foregone conclusion Mamma will ask how he comes to know so much about it.

The actors throw themselves into the style with what looks suspiciously like enthusiasm. I suppose Mary Long (Maria) deserves to be congratulated on the strength of her lungs, though shes overdoing things even by prevailing standards, and that Michel Perron (Gino) also warrants commendation for the expressive variety of his shrugs. There is matchingly shameless work from Ellen David as Anna, who compensates for nagging others by beating up on herself; from Penny Mancuso as Ninos beanpole ma, who mysteriously envies the slimness of a woman with far more meat on her bones than she has herself; and from Suzanne Le Nir as the predatory Pina, a pitiful victim of dramaturgical machismo: Her author exploits her for his own purposes, then heartlessly casts her aside.

As Angelo and Nino, Andreas Apergis and Joseph Gallaccio prove that quieter acting need not necessarily mean less simplified acting. The director is Gordon McCall, of Montreal's not-for-profit Centaur Theatre, where the show originated. The production's unsubtlety may be the result of a conscious decision to sell out to the forces of commercialism, but I suspect it happened naturally.
