Soulpepper's ongoing voyage through the plays of David French has now arrived at Jitters, the 1979 piece in which the author poked fun at the burgeoning Toronto independent theatre movement of which he himself was an important part. The title refers to the different varieties of nervous terror experienced by everyone in the business of putting on a play (and in most other businesses). In a Toronto theatre not unlike French's old stamping ground, the Tarragon, a new play is being mounted under all the usual pressures, plus some additional ones caused by the promised presence of a Broadway producer.

The play is part farce, part satirical commentary on the Canadian theatre and its insecurities, and its misfortune is that both halves of its split personality have been eclipsed by later events. It opened a few years before Michael Frayn's Noises Off, the most inventive of backstage farces, and one that never feels like an in-joke. The comic anatomy of Canadian theatre was performed more expansively, more incisively and more affectionately by Slings and Arrows. In Jitters, the farcical part is the better, or so it seems in Ted Dykstra's Soulpepper production. The second and best of its three acts takes us to just before curtain-up on opening night. It's an occasion plagued by actors who go missing, arrive late and drunk, or get locked in the bathroom. Much of the burden of this falls on the author, who, since he knows all the lines and was once an actor, may have to go on himself. A nervous creature at the best of times, he is now petrified, although there is obviously a part of him that would really love to do it. He's presumably a French self-portrait, though Mike Ross's performance sports both the spectacles and the mannerisms of a Canadian actor-dramatist of more recent vintage. Whoever he is, Ross builds him up beautifully in all his fretful, accommodating passive-aggressiveness, finishing him off in a sustained cadenza of physical comedy as he throws himself in and out of an ill-fitting costume, according to the fluctuating condition of the man he's supposed to be replacing.

Unfortunately, when we come back after the intermission, all this exciting stuff might just as well not have happened. The play switches gears and goes back to what really seems to be on its mind, which is the Canadian theatre's psychological dependence on the approval of America. We have a leading lady who has returned to Toronto after achieving some measure of success in New York, and who is responsible for Broadway taking an interest. We have a leading man who proudly parades his indifference to such temptations but is obviously a prey to bitter doubts. "Where but in Canada" he asks in the play's most widely quoted line "can you be a top-notch actor all your life and still die broke and anonymous?" (Answer: A lot of places. Including America.) C. David Johnson's performance is admirably huffy; Diane D'Aquila is more effective in the diva's vulnerability than in her imperiousness. Maybe it's because we've just seen Kiss Me Kate; maybe it's because Johnson and D'Aquila were once husband and wife; but there seems to be more history between these characters than the play ever gets round to exploring. Their hostility fuels much of the action, but it's hard to believe that it's just a matter of differing world views. In the weak third act, they, like the play, seem to be repeating themselves. There's a line about all Canadian actors measuring themselves against Christopher Plummer that gets as big and as rueful a laugh now as it presumably did 30 years ago, so the national complex about making it, or not making it, somewhere else still persists. But it isn't that pervasive, and this play really runs it into the ground.

As self-satire, it's pretty skimpy. The play that's being rehearsed bears the blessed title of The Care and Treatment of Roses, and it seems to be in the vein of French's own Newfoundland family dramas; but we only see one rudimentary scene from it, twice. He does, though, offer a wicked parody of a really starchy theatre review. The director is seriously under-characterized, and Kevin Bundy doesn't manage to give him much personality. Jordan Pettle's prissy dictator of a stage manager has a lot of personality. Oliver Dennis is very funny as a hypochondriacal actor, though this is one of his reliable performances rather than one of his inspired ones; Sarah Wilson is a delight as an ASM. After a sluggish start, and before stasis sets in, there is a steady stream of theatrical disaster-jokes, so the evening is pleasant. But it's fuzzy.
