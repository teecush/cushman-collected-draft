(From the National Post, Friday, January 4, 2002) In Robert Cushman's roundup of the best theatre of 2001, the production of Zadie's Shoes was miscredited. Factory Theatre produced of Adam Pettle's "invigorating" play. *****

The most gratifying Canadian theatre event of the year occurred in England, where Robert Lepage won the Best Play Award for his solo piece The Far Side of the Moon. It was gratifying because when this delightful work -- a triumph for Lepage as writer, director and performer -- was seen the previous year in Toronto, it was denounced by every critic, except this one, which is why I bring it up. Even in Toronto, most real people had loved it.

Also in London, The Royal Family, a revival of a whiskery American comedy about the Barrymores, sold out almost before it opened because it starred Judi Dench. She has been Britain's favourite actress virtually since her debut in 1957 (as Ophelia to John Neville's Hamlet) but it took movies and Oscars to make her this bankable. It couldn't have happened to a greater Dame.

In New York, a similar thing happened on a much larger scale when The Producers became the biggest show -- in terms of box-office takings, awards and impossibility to get into -- in the history of the world. This doesn't mean it has more to offer its audience than any previous blockbuster, though obviously its provision of laughs and showbiz and laughing at showbiz hit a nerve. It means the machine gets ever more efficient at exploiting the kind of success that nothing succeeds like. It also helped that it was a pre-sold title, though to nothing like the same extent as the Disney monsters that were ruling Broadway before it came along.

In Toronto, Adam Pettle and Kristen Thomson made auspicious playwrighting debuts. The year began invigoratingly at Passe Muraille, with Pettle's gambling comedy, Zadie's Shoes, followed in quick succession by a couple of mesmerizing monologues, Thomson's I Claudia and Morris Panych's Earshot (the latter a visual feast as well). Both were at Tarragon and both exhilaratingly performed, by Thomson herself and Randy Hughson, both of them blessedly ubiquitous during the remainder of the year.

After these three, though, things declined precipitously (apart from This Hotel, an intriguing play in a wonderful production), with established writers contributing some stinkers. Two welcome if belated imports: Skylight, with a superb performance by Yanna McIntosh; and Steve Martin's Picasso at the Lapin Agile, a treat as staged by American Randall Arney with a fine local cast.

Soulpepper, still Toronto's best hope, tripped over Feydeau and Coward, pulled themselves together with Schnitzler, Chekhov (especially), Ionesco and Dickens. Outside their loop, there was one brilliant revival, Kate Lynch's Amazonian Midsummer Night's Dream.

Stratford had its strongest season in years, gratifyingly rich in Shakespeare, and the Shaw Festival its weakest, redeemed at the end by The Return of the Prodigal. Two formerly promising Canadian musicals -- Anything that Moves and The Drowsy Chaperone -- kept their promises.

Big musicals, usually in tacky touring versions, were deadly, though The Full Monty at least gave us a chance to see Kaye Ballard. The generally moribund commercial theatre did, however, do well with plays. There were two dazzling imported two-handers, Stones in His Pockets and The Island.

There was Fully Committed, a hilarious solo stand by the voice of them all, Mark McKinney. I have often railed against one-person shows, but I confess this has been a great year for them. Add to the list the little-seen but compelling The Vindication of Senyora Clito Mestres, a Catalan performed here in English by an outstanding Yugoslav actress, Dragana Varagic.

This was also the year in which, at the behest of financial benefactors, new names were slapped onto two Toronto theatres: the Pantages became the Canon, and the plain old Young Peoples' Theatre became the jaw-breaking Lorraine Kimsa Theatre for Young People. The generous donors might have shown even more generosity if they had allowed the previous titles -- one historic, the other usefully descriptive -- to remain. They would certainly have shown more class.

To end where I begun: The Canadian Opera Company's revival of the Lepage-directed double bill Bluebeard's Castle and Erwartung was the most majestic piece of theatre seen on a Toronto stage all year.
