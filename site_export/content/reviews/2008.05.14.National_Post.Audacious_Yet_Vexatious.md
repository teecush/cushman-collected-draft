Trevor Nunn had two inspired ideas for his production of My Fair Lady. One concerns the placing of the intermission. Traditionally the first act curtain has fallen, anticlimactically, in the midst of the Embassy Ball to which Professor Henry Higgins escorts Eliza Doolittle to test whether her transformation from Cockney flower-girl to posh-speaking lady is complete enough to convince the swellest of the swells. Nunn pauses instead on the preceding scene, Eliza about to set off for her ordeal, radiant but terrified: Cinderella on her way to the ball, which as pantomime writers have always known is by far the more uplifting and suspenseful way of doing things.

The other, smaller innovation also concerns the first act. Every time the action wanders outdoors we see newsboys hawking papers announcing that the king -- Edward VII, presumably -- is sick, dying or, finally, dead. So when we reach the Ascot Gavotte, the fashionable world has gone into mourning, and a number that everyone remembers as being clothed, audaciously, all in white, is now in black: Call it the audacity of surprise.

These two effects were stunning when the production opened at Britain's National Theatre in 2001, and they have a residual impact in the American touring production, "redirected by Shaun Kerrison," that re-opens the Toronto (formerly the North York) Centre for the Performing Arts after 10 wasted waste-land years. But they mean far less, because they no longer have a dramatic context. The acting strength that has always been the hallmark of Nunn's best work, in plays or in musicals, has diminished almost to vanishing point. Meanwhile, the tricksiness and over-elaboration that were mild blemishes on the show in London have become debilitating eyesores.

There is one life-saving performance, the Eliza of Lisa O'Haire who has a singing voice that lights up both the wistfulness of Wouldn't It Be Loverly and the rapture of I Could Have Danced All Night and who, British herself, finds the right spoken sound for both halves of her character's progress. (Some of the supporting accents in this show about accents are atrocious.) Her acting too is well up to conveying Eliza's native confidence,

hurt affections, and resilience. Her last two numbers are spoiled by the production's compulsion to illustrate and underline everything, but she splendidly holds up her end in the final, nearly wordless scene in which she comes back to the professor, here beautifully staged half as jest, half as challenge.

She doesn't, though, get much of a response from 'er 'Enry. Christopher Cazenove does reasonably when asked to play Higgins' passion for the English language and his casual lack of passion for anything or anyone else; he's all right, in other words, in the dialogue scenes that are taken verbatim from Pygmalion, either stage play or screenplay. But if he can cope with Shaw, he's at sea with Lerner and Loewe, and with the romantic patina -- romantic even in denial -- that their songs cast over the original. Higgins is a role famously written for a non-singer. But there are non-singers and non-singers, and Cazenove's Sprechstimme is numbingly defective in pitch, colour and even rhythm. (William D. Brohn's exacting new orchestration for Why Can't a Woman Be More Like a Man completely did him in.) Out with all those things go wit, charm and any reason for Eliza to fancy him. I've Grown Accustomed to Her Face, that paragon of anti-love songs, goes for nearly nothing.

It isn't completely his fault. I remember my relief when Alex Jennings, the fine Higgins I saw in London, was after all the busy-ness granted an empty stage on which to sing

that song; and my dismay as it started filling up with phantom flower girls to remind us of what he was missing, as if the song itself weren't already telling us. Eliza's Show Me is similarly disrupted by a parade of suffragettes with placards reading "deeds, not words": a sop, presumably, to someone's social conscience, like the presentation of Eliza's dustman dad (Tim Jerome, not so much horny-handed as heavy-handed) as short on benignity and long on neglect. Both his songs are staged as extended knees-up routines, though only one needs to be; British directors seem to regard American musicals as so many boxes of chocolates, and cannot bear to leave a single flavour untasted. Matthew Bourne's choreography is dreadful, alternately -- sometimes simultaneously -- cute and overblown. The initial visual impact of the Ascot scene is destroyed when the assembled toffs start high-stepping as if they themselves were horses, when the whole point is meant to be their unmoved stateliness. With a Little Bit of Luck involves a prolonged prance on dustbin-lids, recalling a routine in that great neglected movie musical It's Always Fair Weather. In fact, the staging keeps prompting memories of Gene Kelly and MGM: a lamppost or a sofa (Singin' in the Rain), a strap-hanging subway ride (On the Town).

O'Haire apart, the best things about the show are the ironic presence of Marni Nixon, the celebrated vocal ghost who dubbed Audrey Hepburn in the Fair Lady film, in the non-singing role of Mrs. Higgins, and the un-ironic excellence with which she plays it. Walter Charles has his moments as Colonel Pickering, though he's too little the gentleman, too much the buffoon. Distressingly further down the same road is Justin Bohon's Freddy, so exaggerated a twit that, though he has the voice to sing On the Street Where You Live, he completely forfeits the right. Anthony Ward's sets make an evocative job of Covent Garden (where the production has its most charming moments) and an imposing one of Higgins' monumentally book-lined study. His establishment is, as usual, impossibly large for a mere professor of phonetics: I counted four maids, two footmen and a housekeeper. Broadway has always been more starry-eyed about the British class system than Britain itself was. Which, though I'm not complaining, is the difference in a nutshell between the incurably romantic My Fair Lady and its impenitently satirical source. - My Fair Lady plays until May 31.
