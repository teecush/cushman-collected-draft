THEATRE REVIEW

Chitty Chitty Bang Bang

Canon Theatre, Toronto

Boxing Night at Chitty Chitty Bang Bang was distinguished by the show's starting half an hour late. This is an especially cruel occurrence when the audience is full of children, though I have to say that this house took its enforced exile to the concession stands in the lobby with exceptionally good grace. Unspecified "mechanical difficulties" were cited for the delay, but we all had our suspicions. Finally, Steve Wilson, cast in the leading role of Caractacus Potts, appeared to confirm, admittedly with considerable charm, our darkest fears. The flying car, the production's title character and raison d'etre, wouldn't fly. It could still do all kinds of other things (e. g. "move laterally") and, honestly, we'd hardly notice, but we could have our money back if we liked.

But this patience was rewarded, because at the very end of the show, the car did in fact levitate, just as it was supposed to. (Well, I assume as it was supposed to: Maybe on a good night it belches fire and tap-dances as well.) I don't know how this happened. In the storyline proper, CCBB will only do complicated things, like letting her headlights light, if the person in the driver's seat says please. So I can only imagine that, during the intermission last Friday, someone talked to her very, very nicely.

The attraction at the Canon is the touring version of the London and Broadway stage musical of the Hollywood film musical of the Ian Fleming book. I missed all those previous incarnations, so I'm going purely on present evidence when I declare Chitty Chitty Bang Bang to be, in the face of enormous competition, the worst-constructed musical I have ever seen. It doesn't have enough of a story to fill its space, though it seems to think it does, and it stops and starts and sputters worse than CCBB herself could have done before Caractacus gave her a facelift.

For the benefit of my few fellow-ignorami, I had better explain. The show is set in England, depicted as so often in American musicals as an Edwardian never-never land. Caractacus is a young widower whose two children develop an attachment to a broken-down old crock that they prevail upon their dad to buy for them. Father is an inventor, with a special interest in sweets, and he tries to sell one of his creations to a confectionery magnate: a bit of action whose only uphsot is that Caractacus gets together with the tycoon's daughter to have a love affair. Retreating behind his garage doors, Caractacus performs on his new plaything (the car, not the lady) what seems to be a full wash and service. He does not however appear in any way responsible for her newly revealed supermechanical powers: a serious break in fairy-tale logic, especially given that these powers are said to be crucial to the rounding-up of the baddies at the end. Note I said "said to be." It happens offstage, which is where our newly shiny heroine spends most of the second act. The Magic of the Theatre has its limits.

Oh yes, the baddies. They're Vulgarians, from Vulgaria. Thick accents and muddy sound made me think at first that they were Bulgarians, but it turns out Fleming wasn't being xenophobic, just snobbish. Figures. Vulgaria is ruled, from the side of the stage, by a Baron and Baroness who mysteriously covet the car even before it's revealed to be a) road-worthy, b) amphibious, and c) gravity-defying; and who will stop at nothing to get it. The Baron giggles and likes toys; the Baroness cackles and hates children. (Presumably the Baroness is barren, and resents it, but like the rest of the show she isn't much on motivation.) She employs a Childcatcher to lure kiddies with sweets, catch them in nets, and then do God knows what to them; given a bit more stage time she could be seriously disturbing.

Just after his humiliation at the sweet factory, Caractacus leads an extended knees-up called Me Ol' Bamboo whose only rationale seems to be to reprise the Step in Time number that was so popular in Mary Poppins. Wilson does this, and everything else that's asked of him, with appealing shy chirpiness, though for someone in his line of work he's short on eccentricity. His offspring (Jeremy Lipton and Camille Mancuso on my night) are perfectly fine, while George Dvorsky and Elizabeth Ward Land are an enjoyably campy villain and villainess. Some of their backchat went, one hopes, over the heads of the youngest, but it's nothing compared to the badinage foisted on their comic spies (tall Dirk Lumbard and short Scott Cote), which may have you pining for the suave sophistication of Ross Petty's Cinderella. One might have expected more wit from a Fleming spy-spoof; but on second thoughts, perhaps not. There's also a whole pack of dogs, whose leader is a real professional.

Next up at the Canon is Medea, another play that ends --at least in the original Greek -- with its protagonist making a getaway in a heavenly car. Sweet are the uses of diversity: Bang Bang Chitty Chitty Bang Bang, we may still need you. - Chitty Chitty Bang Bang runs until Jan. 4. For tickets, call 416-872-1212 or visit mirvish.com.
