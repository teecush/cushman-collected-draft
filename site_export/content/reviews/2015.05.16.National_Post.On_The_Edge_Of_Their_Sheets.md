According to the Soulpepper program, Alan Ayckbourn has written, and on his home turf directed, 77 full-length plays. Few people can have seen all of them, so few are in a position to say which is the best. My own score comes in at under a third. But of the ones I know, the best is Bedroom Farce, the one that Soulpepper is currently presenting. It's a perfect play.

The title is a tease, but also the literal truth. The entire action takes place over one night in three bedrooms, which share the stage throughout. The sleeping-chamber at stage left belongs to Ernest and Delia, an elderly couple who are about to celebrate their wedding anniversary in a favourite restaurant.

("We're regulars. We go there every year.") Centre stage is occupied by Malcolm and Kate, newlyweds who are throwing a party. Over on the right we find Jan, who is attending the party, and her husband Nick, who isn't as he is confined to bed with an injured back about which he is vocally grumpy. There's a fourth couple, Trevor and Susannah, whom we never see at home as they are too busy invading the spaces of the other characters and laying them waste. Trevor is the son of Ernest and Delia and a former boyfriend of Jan. Trevor and Susannah are made for one another, only in the sense that they are both totally self-absorbed. The condition takes them differently. Trevor's default setting is a cheerful unconsciousness of anybody else's feelings. Susannah's is hysteria.

They too attend Malcolm and Kate's party, but separately, having quarrelled before leaving home. They keep it up after their arrival, thereby ruining everybody's evening. The proximate cause of their row is Trevor's having a nostalgic cuddle with Jan, in on which Susannah fatally walks. This leads to Susannah's running to her in-laws (who don't much like her) for comfort and counsel, and to Trevor's going round to Nick and Jan to Explain. Neither visit is much welcomed by its recipients, who by now are trying to get some rest.

This is something on which their erstwhile hosts have given up. Malcolm uses the unforeseen spare time to do some rather precarious DIY, ostensibly as a present for Kate who, delectable in her nightdress, looks neglected. This, then, is a bedroom farce in which nobody gets any sex and hardly anybody gets any sleep. The play's superbly constructed; without any apparent strain it tells its story within the scenic limits it's set itself; we hear just as much as we need of the world elsewhere, without ever needing to see it for ourselves. Voyages between the rooms are always strictly accounted for and superbly synchronized. Meanwhile the four marriages are, politely but remorselessly, stripped bare. The production in general is excellent, physically inventive and very funny. It's anchored by the performances of its senior couple. Derek Boyes' Ernest lacks something in asperity, but has an owlish geniality, as befits a man whose favourite bedside riding is that 19th-century Boys' Own classic Tom Brown's Schooldays. ("Always worth a re-read.") Delia is a blissfully brisk Corinne Koslo, contemplating her compliant husband, her unsatisfactory daughter-in-law, and her own curl-papered image in the mirror, with equally dispassionate efficiency. Malcolm and Kate are Gordon Hecht and Katherine Gauthier, who were the hilariously self-indulgent lovers in Soulpepper's Tartuffe and are even better here.

Nick and Jan are less convincing. Jan, as her husband complacently states, is a woman notable for her commonsense equability; it, too, can be strained on occasion but Caitlin Driscoll plays her as if anger were her natural state, and there's quite enough of that going around already. Nick is a self-styled high-flyer laid humiliatingly low; Alex McCooeye is immensely tall, and to see his length stretched out upon the bed is a comic treat in itself, even before misfortunes like a dropped book force him into elaborate contortions. He's less adept vocally, which is a particular shame as Nick has the most trenchant speeches in the play, most of them directed at Trevor, whose blithe insensitivity is precisely captured by Ron Pederson. He's partnered with equal conviction (which in her case means total lack of it) by Amy Matysio. These two, cuckoos in every nest, are the only characters who get to grab some sleep in one another's arms. Needless to say, it's on somebody else's bed.
