THE GOOD LIFE

Tarragon Theatre

- - -

Daniel Brooks's play The Good Life contains an amusing scene in which an actor gives an interview about a show he is currently rehearsing. This actor is also a playwright, though he hasnt written the play that hes puffing. This makes him the opposite number to Brooks himself, who has also been known to act but here functions only as author and director. (Directing is what he is best known for.) To add to the fun, the fictitious actor/playwright is called Dan.

Dan makes some pertinent remarks about the directions in which an audience's mind might wander while they're watching him on-stage. They might, for example, start thinking about the theatres splendid new washrooms: a line that went down especially well with the knowing first-night audience at the Tarragon, a theatre that has itself recently undergone just such a facelift.

You will have realized by now that The Good Life is a self- conscious play. Dan even tells his interviewer that nothing he says should be taken too seriously since he is just playing an acknowledged game, undertaken for publicity purposes only. Its decent of Brooks to put it that way, since he himself has been very articulate in interviews (and, to a lesser extent, in the program) about his intentions in this play. It's nice to know we don't have to take his pronouncements seriously.

Its always tempting to review plays in terms of their advance buildup, and I really should stop doing it, but some shows seem to ask for it. The Good Life is a play about love, or at least a play in which the characters make a lot of speeches about love. This is not a subject on which definite conclusions are to be expected -- though some distinction between "love" and being in love might be appreciated -- and Brooks has described his play as unfinished: structurally and intellectually a work-in-progress. This, he is honest enough to admit, is true of most plays; all artistic practitioners (this even includes reviewers) are likely to have further thoughts and to go on having them. Its the way things are.

That being the case, what's the big deal about this particular play, and why should an audience care? Audiences, skeptical but incorrigible, will have noticed by now that most plays have loose ends and frustrating final curtains; the most they expect is something that will be lively and engaging enough to keep their minds off those new washrooms. The audience, always and inevitably, is part of the theatrical transaction, but they aren't the ones who are there to do the work, and demands that they should pull their socks up and participate more always strike me as pretty dumb. The moment an audience tries to like or understand something, you have lost it.

The Good Life is part entertaining, part boring; the entertainment (there are good jokes) counts for a lot while youre watching, but the boredom is what you resentfully remember when its over. That passion fades, or that spouses stray, is not a revelation; we need to know about the people.

Dan is married to Gina, herself an actress. They sometimes seem about to take centre stage in an interesting play, one that is regularly derailed by the need to counterpoint their relationship with that of their also-married friends, Chris and Mary. One of these marriages looks happy, the other keeps erupting into fights, often in public; and, wouldn't you know, it's the miserable pair who stay together and the contented pair who split. There are also two younger characters; each arrives to chat up Dan for media purposes, and each serves as an erotic catalyst.

Dan is an articulate charmer in whom romantic love serves as a camouflage for selfishness. (I would like to add "or vice versa" but there is little evidence for it.) He even gets the play started -- very well -- with an acknowledgment, disarming in context though not thereafter, of the controlling role of his own ego. He is played by Guillermo Verdecchia; this makes a vital contribution to the general hall-of-mirrors effect, since Verdecchia was Brookss writing and performing partner for The Noam Chomsky Lectures, and other things.

He is beguiling at the outset, quietly insolent and self- possessed, and increasingly irritating later on when hes still quietly insolent and self-possessed. This may be because Dan emerges not just as a bastard but as a shallow bastard; he says things like our children don't belong to us, they belong to Disney, which sounds smart but isn't. (How many kids do you know who are truly owned -- not influenced, owned -- by pop culture?)

It may also be because Verdecchias range is limited; he can talk, he can even get angry, but he cant suffer, and the play does seem to want us to feel for him. Maybe he's a performer, not an actor.

The same may well be true of Bob Martin (the lanky comedian who plays Chris and whom I remember as the narrator in The Drowsy Chaperone), but for him it works. In the first place, his timing gleams; and in the second the man he plays is as wrapped up in his own ideas as was the Chaperone chap in his records of old musicals. The two performances are remarkably similar but both are truthful. Martin, in good, wry, stand up fashion, even rises to the challenge of a final oration in which he details the stages by which a physical love may be transformed into the love of an ideal: something of which he approves. His wife furiously dismisses this as plagiarized Plato, which seems about right.

Actually the whole play, according to its author, is modelled on a Platonic dialogue. (Not "platonic" in the vulgar sense, except that if you're talking about sex you probably arent doing it, at least not at that moment.) He also acknowledges many other sources, from Jane Austen to Ingmar Bergman. One he doesnt list is Tom Stoppard, whose The Real Thing much resembles The Good Life in its musings on love both licensed and adulterous, in its juxtaposition of two marriages, and in its autobiographical allusions; it also plays, though more whole-heartedly, with the relationship between life and theatre.

I am not accusing Brooks of pillaging Stoppard, merely pointing out that Stoppard had already covered much of the same territory, while managing to tell a story (The Good Life has just enough of one to be frustrating) and to depict characters who are more than mouthpieces. I should add, in fairness to Brooks's advocacy of ongoing creation, that I disliked The Real Thing when it first appeared and only appreciated it the second time around, though I would attribute this to a superior production; I don't think the text changed.

Brookss own production here is both nifty and stately, with scenes suggestively superimposed on other scenes and a satisfying use of microphones for the big speeches, one by each major character. Tamsin Kelsey (Gina) and Tracy Wright (Mary) as the wives have more physical presence than their partners but are starved for material, Wright especially. She is allowed to shout but Kelsey gets to smoulder and be decisive. Waneta Storms has little substantial to do as the lady interviewer, whose role in the action is ambiguous going on confusing; as her male counterpart, Luke Kirby has even less.

He does, however, get to deliver the evenings best line, one in which he takes aim at Dan's own writing and -- by an implication that the audience laps up -- at the play in which he figures. (That Daniel Brooks: He covers every base.) I also liked Dan's observation that it isn't subtext most plays lack, it's supertext. Too right.
