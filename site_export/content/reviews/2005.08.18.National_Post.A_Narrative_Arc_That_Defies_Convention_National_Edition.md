THE LARK

Festival Theatre, Stratford

- - -

The Stratford production of The Lark, Jean Anouilh's Joan of Arc play, is a fine one, with a consistent approach, rigorously maintained. It is more intense and less ebullient than most shows on the main Festival stage -- and its worth noting that the Stratford audience, belying the bad rap that it so often gets, was palpably hooked. So was I. But I doubt that either the style or the tone is right for this play.

The director, Michael Lindsay-Hogg, has updated the setting by 500 years, from the 1430s and the last stages of the Hundred Years War to the 1940s and the German occupation of France. Its true that Anouilh, writing the play soon after the Second World War, can hardly have helped drawing on the experience of occupation, collaboration and resistance for his dramatization of Joan, liberating her country from foreign invasion and getting burned alive for it. But that doesn't mean that he was assuming an exact correspondence between one well-defined historical period and another. Costume plays can be helpfully punched up by non-specific modern dress -- but here there are specifics, and they don't match. Charles the Dauphin, the snivelling symbol of French legitimacy, could hardly be less like Charles de Gaulle. Theres no modern European equivalent for the ecclesiastical court by which Joan was tried and condemned, and I doubt that Vichy collaborators, lay or clerical, would have been as scrupulous in their proceedings as Anouilhs priests appear to be. We also have to cope with an enemy commander whom both history and the play say is English -- the Earl of Warwick, in fact -- but whose bearing and insignia declare him every inch a Nazi.

The courtroom of Rouen, as directed by Lindsay-Hogg and designed by Eugene Lee, might be a Gestapo interrogation-centre. The lighting is cold and spare, and emotions are battened down. This hardly accords with Anouilh's playful, almost fairy-tale, view of history, in which Joan's career before her capture is brought to us through a series of flashbacks.

The play, done here in a Cold-War adaptation by Lillian Hellman that is a compact of Brodway efficiency and beleaguered Broadway liberalism, is an odd experience for audiences who grew up on Shaws Saint Joan. The two plays naturally cover the same historical ground and there are whole scenes in the French play that paraphrase, parody or parallel the English one. Then suddenly it strikes out on its own. Robert de Beaudricourt (Brian Tree), Joans first helper, is as puzzled and blustery as in Shaw, but he is given a butter- fingered sexual interest in the Maid that she, intriguingly, knows just how to parry; in Shaw, such thoughts would never have entered anyone's head. Anouilh's Dauphin starts out as an echo of Shaws, a grown-up child needing to be chivvied into manhood, then acquires an idenity of his own, less petulant, more selfish, more worldly. Steven Sutcliffe develops him brilliantly.

Anouilhs Warwick is a domesticated version of Shaw's ruthlessly polite politician, more genial and less cynical in his humour. That at least is what I get from the script, which I first encountered in a less businesslike translation by Christopher Fry. Graham Abbey's Warwick seems to me a virtuoso exercise in playing against the text; the character, who does indeed speak of the power of propaganda, becomes a club-footed Goebbels magnified to the scale of Richard III. Its a mordant, compelling piece of acting, consummately world- weary and therefore dangerous, but there isnt a laugh in it.

In this production the Inquisitor is a woman, a prospect that doesn't seem much likelier in the 20th century than in the 14th, but that does enable Martha Henry to draw all eyes while sitting motionless at the tip of the stage, dark-suited and completely silent, for the entire first act. Nobody can have expected this Spanish Inquisition, and when she does finally speak (she does the accent) her words burn. They do not, however, convince; their brand of misanthropy, much reiterated as the play proceeds, seems abstract, something made up out of the dramatist's own head.

The story of Joan of Arc, says the wise fool Dauphin in the plays last line, is a story that ends happily. At least thats what Anouilh wrote; Fry transferred the line to Cauchon, and Hellman cuts it entirely.

Anouilh, more sentimental, gives us one last flashback, to the Dauphin's coronation, supposedly Joan's finest hour, and leaves it at that. Its the kind of spectacle that would normally set the Stratford stage ablaze, but here its staged like a child's birthday party, as if downbeat were the point. This set the seal on my belief that the production was on the wrong track. But it's as fascinating as many right ones.
