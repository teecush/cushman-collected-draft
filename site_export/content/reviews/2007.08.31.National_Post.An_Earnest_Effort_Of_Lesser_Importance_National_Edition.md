Oscar Wilde's lesser comedies -- meaning all those that aren't The Importance of Being Earnest -- are tricky animals: curious combinations of epigrammatic satire and high-flown society melodrama. Even in An Ideal Husband, the best of the second-best, the two elements rarely coalesce; they come at us in alternating swatches. Wilde resolved the discord in The Importance, his last play, by making his text -- with its ceaseless cascade of genteel insults -- into verbal farce, and his action into situation-farce. The guilty secrets that haunt the protagonists of the earlier plays are still there, rattling around in their closets: babies, left abandoned in handbags, grow up to lead double lives that necessitate their being called Ernest in town and Jack in the country. Plot and dialogue are saying the same ominous thing: Transgress the rules of society and you will never be forgiven.

The plots and jokes of the earlier plays say it too; however, being melodramas, they provide their heroes and heroines with escape clauses, generally taking the form of the love of a good woman. This, as Wilde himself must have feared and was anyway soon to discover, makes them far less realistic than the apparently fantastical Importance. If An Ideal Husband is the most interesting of the experimental hybrids, that's partly because the scandal it exhumes is a good, solid political one. In his youth, Sir Robert Chiltern, a high-flying upcoming statesman, profited from a spot of insider trading. If this is revealed, it will spell the end both of his career and of the piece of high-minded legislation he is currently sponsoring. Enter Mrs. Cheveley, who is a woman with her own past -- sexual naturally -- and a blackmailing adventuress. (It's a rule in society drama of this period that any Mrs. will be at least one of these things. To be a lady, you have to be a Lady.) She threatens to dish the dirt on Sir Robert, and though she's eventually thwarted -- I don't think that's giving much away -- she manages en route to shake the marital confidence of the trusting and virtuous Lady Chiltern almost to its foundations: to bring her, indeed, to the brink of destroying her own reputation.

Richard Monette's production is the final attraction both of this Stratford season and of his undervalued fourteen-year reign as artistic director. So I wish that it were better. It fails to harmonize the play's two voices (I'm not sure it even tries) and it's sometimes plain to the point of being dull; Michael Gianfranesco's sets are nondescript, Francois St-Aubin's frocks and frock-coats mostly unbecoming. Monette does succeed, though, in making us take the serious bits seriously. When Tom McCamus and Brigit Wilson, as the embattled and embittered Chilterns, start going for one another at full decibel level, the hammers resound, the tongs blaze and the actors cut through the stilted words to the perfectly valid emotions beneath. Elsewhere, he locates the beating heart behind the stuffed shirt, and even finds some self- deprecating humour; she doesn't have that luxury and is stuck with being indignantly virtuous.

The good Lady Chiltern and the bad Mrs. Cheveley were at school together, and the latter has never stopped being jealous of the former. Dixie Seatle is excellent in the few moments at which she plays this grudge for real, but most of the time she settles for being a cut-rate Mata Hari, with an arrantly artificial laugh. Self- congratulatory laughter is rather a feature of this show. The play contains a bevy of society dames, delivering withering witticisms that sound like Lady Bracknell's leftovers, even though they obviously aren't. These attendant harpies are presumably meant to suggest the social pressures that could bring Sir Robert crashing down, but Wilde doesn't give them much of a context, and neither does the production. A succession of toothy or just mouthy ladies lines up to deliver unsupported specialty turns. Sometimes they get exit applause, an almost infallible sign that bad acting has just occurred. I said almost infallible: Brian Tree, as the elder statesman Earl of Caversham, gets a hand too, and he deserves it.

Not, I admit, that his happily harrumphing performance does much to establish the play's political ambience. Where Tree scores is as the heavy father to the dashing Lord Goring, who is the play's nearest approach to a unifying force. Goring figures as compassionate commentator, self-satisfied man about town, cheerful comic wooer and resourceful saviour of his friends the Chilterns. That's a lot to combine; David Snelgrove dishes out the elements one by one without reconciling them. He's best when playing with his romantic partner, whom Sara Topham, after an uncertain start, makes both sensible and delectable. Their union is part of a surprisingly satisfying last act, one in which, by a rich accumulation of plot twists and petardhoistings, realists and idealists alike are revealed to have feet of clay and to be all the better for it. Here Wilde's wit works, not as a collection of one-liners but structurally. The production rises to it, and fends off anti- climax.

In a broader sense it doesn't. An Ideal Husband is the final entry in a Stratford week that was otherwise an unbroken -- and, in my experience, unprecedented -- series of triumphs: The Odyssey, A Delicate Balance, Pentecost. In that context it's a letdown. But the successes count for more than the disappointment, in quantity, quality, and in sheer variety. This regime is going out in glory. - In repertoire until Oct. 27.
