THEATRE REVIEW

ROCK N' ROLL

Bluma Appel Theatre, Toronto

Fiona Reid has two magnificent moments in Rock 'n Roll, and shes a different character in each. Tom Stoppard's play covers a lot of ground, moving in time from 1968 to 1990, and shifting in space between an English professor's Cambridge house and a Czech dissidents Prague apartment. In Act One, Reid, as the professors wife, cries out that her body, ravaged by cancer, still houses the same spirit, the same desires, the same capacity to love and need to be loved. It's a heart-stopping sequence. In Act Two she's her former characters grown-up daughter, responding to an old admirers overdue overtures almost before he can make them. The wit and wisdom, simplicity and generosity, are overwhelming. Please: Stratford, Soulpepper, somebody cast our greatest comedienne as a Restoration heroine or as Beatrice in Much Ado About Nothing before time runs out on all of us.

The two scenes exemplify what the play is meant to be about. It's the plea of its author, himself an Anglo-Czech, for politics, not just socialism, with a human face. And the symbol for that, played out rather frivolously in England and with mortal seriousness in what was then called Czechoslovakia, is the music that gives the play its title and its linking soundtrack. As usual with Stoppard, the argument is both quizzical and passionate. There are people who think this his best play; I'm not among them, though I agree that second-rate Stoppard is better than first-rate almost anyone else. The recurring complaint that his plays are all mind, no heart, has always seemed to me to be rubbish. Paradoxically, though, the plays of which it seems most nearly true are the two, this one and The Real Thing, in which hes ostensibly closest to wearing his heart on this sleeve: in this case, a record-sleeve. What usually makes the disparate elements cohere in Stoppard is his humour, but Rock n' Roll is his least funny play. The parts never came together in Trevor Nunn's London-New York production, and theyre even further apart in this Toronto-Edmonton co-production, directed by Donna Feore.

Both of Reids characters --Eleanor, a classics teacher trying passionately and impatiently to impart the truth of Sappho's love-poetry, and Esme, her ex-hippie offspring -- are sideline observers. One thing Esme observes, peering over the garden wall, is an apparition that might be the god Pan, or might also be Syd Barrrett, troubled hero of Pink Floyd who, after the band cast him out, did indeed retire to a reclusive existence in Cambridge (history has always had a way of playing into Stoppard's hands). Esme passes on her enthusiasm, plus a few albums,

to Jan, an Anglophile rock-loving Czech who returns home for the short-lived Prague spring and whose heroes are the Plastic People of the Universe, a home-grown group persecuted despite being apolitical. Or persecuted because theyre apolitical, long hair representing an existential threat that can be cut but not tamed. Shaun Smyth is very fine as Jan, especially in his later, soberer years when, forcibly cropped himself, he comes to realize that engagement has a point.

Sometimes, though not often, he succumbs to this productions fatal weakness: acting so intent on demonstrating the characters' passions that it never makes clear what they're being passionate about. This unfocused emoting runs riot in the supporting cast who range, with the exception of Cyrus Lane as Jans more committed friend, from the anonymous to the atrocious. It also affects the third leading actor, Kenneth Welsh, who plays Eleanors husband Max, an old-school Communist who never left the party. Welsh looks, moves and sounds like the right kind of jaundiced English academic, but he hardly connects with the man's thoughts nor, accordingly, with his feelings. Max, who starts by saying that he's "kicked in the guts by nine-tenths of anything you can tell me about Soviet Russia" is probably, in isolation, the richest character in the piece; Stoppard, his political opponent, has written him with great magnanimity, as a man wrong about communism but right about nearly everything else. Welsh gets his anguished love for his wife, but his political pain stops short at irritability, and his ruminations on the decline of his own country lack weight. This is partly the fault of the play, which is sketchier in Cambridge than it is in Prague, accentuated by the production which places some crucial debates far back and out of reach.

Michael Gianfrescos sets give us two lots of painted bookshelves, especially unhelpful in Prague where theyre supposed to house Jan's LPs; these remain undisturbed even after the secret police have paid a supposedly devastating visit. The scenes of crackdown are altogether lacking in threat. That leaves the music which, even to a rock skeptic like me, is invigorating, as well as cunningly selected for thematic relevance (some wag has suggested a soundtrack album, which is a very good idea) though it does play down rock's more aggressive side. Its peace and lover, brother, even now. The play ends with the Rolling Stones playing a breakthrough concert in the Czech capital. Jan and Esme are present. Max isnt, though his opinions on the validity of a visiting millionaire pop group as a symbol of liberation might be very interesting. - Rock 'n' Roll is at the Bluma Appel Theatre in Toronto through Oct. 24.
