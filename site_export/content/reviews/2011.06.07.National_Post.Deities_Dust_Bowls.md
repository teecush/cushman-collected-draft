THEATRE REVIEW

Jesus Christ Superstar & The Grapes of Wrath Avon Theatre, Stratford, Ont.

Regarding Jesus Christ Superstar: I don't know how to love it. But I can certainly admire what Des McAnuff has done with it, which is to bring shape, lucidity and narrative drive to what is at root a rock oratorio. Theres an explosive but disciplined theatricality to his direction here that taps right into the pulse of Andrew Lloyd Webbers music which in this, his first full-scale work, was powerfully theatrical, theatrical not being necessarily the same thing as dramatic.

Robert Brill has designed an apparently simple set, metallic and split-levelled, that proves very versatile, and eventually furnishes an amazing crucifixion. Paul Tazewell's costumes make the odd Biblical nod but mainly summon the 1960s youth movement that spawned the show and the contemporary Middle East. Howell Binkley's lighting is superb and Rick Foxs musical direction is powerhouse. The cast is across-theboard ideal. Paul Nolans Jesus played opening night with a bronchial infection that robbed him of notes, but the challenge may even have helped his intensity; anyway, he was flawlessly charismatic and unreadable. Chilina Kennedy is a touchingly devoted Mary Magdalene and Josh Young an electrically angry and baffled Judas. And so on and on, with special mention for Bruce Dow's Herod who, more Roman than the Romans, seems to be playing all the decadent emperors rolled into one.

It's interesting that this number, the closest thing in the score to a traditional show tune, should always go down so well. Apart from its entertainment value as a vaudeville strut, it gives Tim Rices lyrics a chance to stretch out and be funny. Elsewhere, the clenched musical rhythms enforce clenched, earnest words, lyrics that are prosaic without being conversational; the exchanges in the high priests scenes are especially painful. Banality, musical and verbal, of a higher-flown kind comes in the big ballad: the detachable hit (generically I Don't Know How to Cry for His Memory, Argentina) that is the worst thing in every early Webber score. Mary M's variant of it, apparently stemming from her prostitute past, is that shes baffled by Jesus refusal to fancy her. This keys into the show's binding idea, which is that everybody sees in the superstar whatever they want to see. It's a valid point, though it has the disadvantage of reducing the protagonist to a blank slate; even when he throws the money-changers out of the temple hes a strangely passive figure. It also means that the other figures are static, though Pilate is a possible exception.

Judas ought to be, especially if you accept Rices contention that he, Jesus and Mary make a romantic triangle; but that has the effect of cheapening the story without illuminating it. Judas' motivations may be more tangled than usual, but they're no more accessible, however hard the music pounds them. That goes for most of the show. Some bits seem to be there simply because theyre too famous to be left out. Maybe the Gospel story should only be told by someone whos passionate about it, whether for or against or torn between: like Stephen Adly Guirgis' The Last Days of Judas Iscariot, the work of a Catholic, however lapsed. For all the sound and the fury, the bells and the whistles, the agnostic superstar leaves the enigmatic story exactly where it found it. Unlike the more satisfying Camelot: the other musical being given a probably-definitive staging at Stratford this year.

Stratford can also boast two Biblically-soaked epics, the other being a stage version of John Steinbeck's Depression classic The Grapes of Wrath. This, too, is a mightily impressive production of a problematic text. Two scenes in particular burn themselves on the mind. In one, a character billed as Man Going Back (Robert King) gives a bitter account of just why hes returning to the dust bowl of Oklahoma from the supposed promised land of California, where the fruitpickin work certainly exists but where there are 10 hungry applicants for every piece of it. The other has a labour organizer (Tyrone Savage) passionately describing what this does to wages and how the law and the bosses react to any attempt to resist it. Both these actors, who do equally well in various other roles, are standouts in an already outstanding cast.

Their characters, though, are peripheral, and there's the rub with Frank Galati's adaptation; the commentary is more powerful than the main action. This concerns the Joad family, sharecroppers who, having lost their Oklahoma home, make that California trip, getting kicked on Route 66. They journey, some dozen of them to begin with, in an overloaded weather-beaten truck that itself makes a great visual statement. Antoni Cimolinos production is in fact full of remarkable scenes, culminating in the frantic building of trenches to stem a flood; though, given the sightlines at the Avon, its cruel to the audi-ence to have so many scenes of actors sitting on the floor.

Evan Buliung is riveting as Tom Joad, the hero in spite of himself, doggedly cresting wave after wave of disaster; Tom McCamus no less so as his de facto mentor, a selfdefrocked pastor who ends up preaching what the authorities take to be red revolution -i.e. social justice -though still in sacerdotal cadences.

There are lovely performances from Steve Ross as a simple-minded brother and Randy Hughson as an agonized uncle, drinking to anesthetize. But, as often in dramatized novels, the focus gets dispersed, not helped, by occasional inconsistent recourse to onstage narrators and by the all-too-consistent use of a folk-country trio who, by underlining the obvious, make the Depression more depressing than necessary.

Unlike the film, the play doesn' t end with Tom's freedom-fighting cri de coeur, but goes on to further miseries. This is morally admirable but dramatically risky; it means the most vital character leaves before the end. The actual end is a kind of pieta, finely and stoically acted by Wright and Kennedy and, as a harrowingly ageless and hopeless man, Dylan Trowbridge. Its moving, but not completely so, perhaps because the final gesture is Rose of Sharons and, for all that Kennedy does for her, we just haven't previously seen enough of her to really know her. That said, for a big production to end on a note of bleak endurance is rare indeed, and truthful. Even hopelessness, in the right context, can be hopeful
