The first impression given by Sexual Practices of the Japanese is that they do it with dummies. The stage is lined with mannequins: not womannequins, since what we see is a line of empty grey suits, waiting to be inhabited, as in due course some of them will be. It's a striking opening to a Vancouver-born show that inaugurates the Factory Theatre's performing spring season, and that proves to be visually impressive throughout. Atmospheric, too, and intelligent and well performed for the most part, though some of its constituent parts outstay their welcome.

There are three in the cast: one man, Raugi Yu, and two women, Manami Hara and Maiko Bae Yamomoto. Hara and Yamomoto also co-wrote the show with Hiro Kanagawa and James Long, and Long and Yamomoto co-directed it. The first verbal section gives us the two women actors dressed as that old Japanese fantasy, two little maids from school. They quiz (male) members of the audience on their notions of Japanese women. As induced audience participation goes, it's quite disarming. Then they entice another guy on stage -- a very game specimen, on the first night -- and ask him searching questions about baseball, e. g., "in what city do the Seattle Mariners play during a home game?" (Seattle was one of the show's previous touring dates.) The relevance of this turns out to be the sex-symbol status of a Japanese Mariner, Ichiro Suzuki. Later on, in the show proper, one of the female characters will use him as prime fantasy material.

This is one of three narrative streams that run through the show, and if that terminology calls to mind Robert Lepage's masterpiece, The Seven Streams of the River Ota, the comparison is not inapt; apart from the Japanese setting, this production has some of Lepage's fluidity and pictorial panache. And his play, too, interwove its stories, though they were both more numerous and more complex. The authors of this show seem to have conceived of it as three separate plays, juggled together rather than placed end to end, but the audience is likely to experience them as one, and they do converge. One concerns the experiences of a young woman travelling to work on the Tokyo subway. (I don't think anybody actually says it's Tokyo, but that was my notion.) Jammed up against her fellow passengers she gets remorselessly and, of course, anonymously, groped. She doesn't find it erotic. Strung out as it is across the evening, this has considerable visceral impact (the lighting and sound are superb) but less as story. The emotional outcome is inevitable, yes, but also predictable. The text makes great ominous play with the reiterated subway announcement "Be Aware" but it may be raising our consciousness less than it thinks.

In another part of the urban jungle -- or, put another way, at the commuter's destination -- the CEO of a sex-toy company, one of those walking suits, plots new ways of heightening female pleasure and corporate profits. His female employees respectfully point out that he may be ignoring the human element, a statement he finds interesting. His is a hierarchical world; his wife respectfully packs him a lunch every day, even though he never eats it (and, he admits, he'd probably miss it if she didn't). Meanwhile, one of his subordinates instructs the other on how to cater -- psychologically -- to the men in authority above them. This is the only section in which characters interact, or perhaps one should just say co-exist. When the boss invites one of his assistants to a "love hotel," he finds himself unable, despite her charms, to take his mind off his work. And, meanwhile, the baseball fantasies rise to a climax; point made, I would have thought, quite early on, but perhaps you really have to care about baseball. However, this does inspire the funniest of the show's collision points: a duel in which the offensive and defensive weapons are ball player's bat and businessman's briefcase.

We have, then, a fantasia on Western views of Asian sexuality, whose upshot seems to be that the Japanese are having less fun than we've been led to believe but they're working on it. And since this fantasia is itself the product of a divided view -- the creators are Japanese-Canadians -- it has an extra level of wryness. They are complicit in what they're satirizing, and they feel the pain while keeping a certain distance from it. It's a tantalizing show, promising more than it delivers. Which I guess is another stereotype.

On the subject of good-looking shows: the Canadian Opera Company's production of Debussy's Pelleas et Melisande (director Nicholas Muni, designer Dany Lyne) ranks with the Ring and Lady Macbeth of Mtsensk among the best things to happen in the new house. A dual-levelled set, with a third layer occasionally dropped in for people to die on, conjures up both a feudal court in which the rulers prefer to keep the starving ruled down below and out of sight, and the shifts between dream and reality in a beautiful, mysterious work. Maybe the mystery turned off the first-night audience, whose response was phenomenally tepid by operatic standards. But even if diva-worship (and divoworship) is your main thing, you should be amply rewarded by Russell Braun's Pelleas, Isabel Bayrakdarian's Melisande, and above all -- certainly in acting terms -- by the great Pavlo Hunka as the jealous Golaud, working wonders with an anguished pianissimo. - Sexual Practices of the Japanese runs through May 18 at the Factory Theatre.
