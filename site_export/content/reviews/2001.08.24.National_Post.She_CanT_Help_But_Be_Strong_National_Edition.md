GOOD MOTHER

Tom Patterson Theatre, Stratford, Ont.

Damien Atkins' new play begins with a quietly terrifying scene in which a woman in a kitchen clutches her head, lets slip the phone on which she was presumably dialling 911, and falls unconscious to the ground. The lights go down, to come up again on a hospital where the woman's husband waits for news of his wife's condition, desperately trying to control his anxiety so as not to alarm the young son who waits with him and whose own worry is able to co-exist with his craving for potato chips.

The balance of tension here is accurately observed and economically conveyed. A young doctor passes through, though only a junior member of the team, and a researcher rather than a physician, he reluctantly lets himself be drawn into providing some information.

The patient has had a stroke and is in a coma; she will not be the same woman when she comes out of it, not ever. Much of the scene's focus, though, is on the doctor's embarrassment, which is played -- by the author, as it happens -- for gentle but obvious laughs. He seems, as both player and playwright, to be offering comic relief that hasn't yet been called for. And since we are never to see this character again, we don't need to get that engaged with him; there is such a thing as too much human interest.

That is how it goes with Good Mother: scenes of genuinely piercing emotion alternating with others that are forced if not actually false. The spine of the play is the clinical development of the stricken Anne Driver, who up to now has lived up to her married name and been the driving force of her family. They have sometimes resented this but they have basically depended on it. Her husband, son and 18-year-old daughter now have to live without the sexy, intelligent woman they knew but with a helpless and initially incoherent person who disturbingly resembles her.

Anne, in fact, makes remarkable progress in the course of the play, but this only underscores the gulf between her and the woman she used to be. There are a couple of flashbacks to reinforce this point, but they aren't really needed. (The second, which comes in the form of a dream, is especially superfluous.) More helpful, really, is the casting of Seana McKenna, who usually plays strong, vibrant women and cannot help bringing those associations with her. She is the contrast, right there, and it is heartbreaking; that she is a fine and fiercely controlled actress in her own right doesn't hurt either, or at least it hurts in the right way. One may have wondered why she wanted to take on this part so soon after playing the cancer victim in Wit, but in fact the two roles turn out to be polar opposites. The pain of Wit lay in the protagonist being in full command of her faculties, almost until the end; that of Good Mother lies in her having lost them from the beginning.

It's moving almost beyond the call of drama, by which I mean it would take a lout of a playwright not to squeeze some legitimate pathos from the situation. There are, of course, plenty of louts out there, and I don't want to undervalue the skill, tact and promise Atkins demonstrates. But he is stuck with the familiar problem that attends plays about illness. There is only so far you can go in writing about the patient. Most of the emphasis has to fall on the actions and reactions of those around her, who in this case are very much around. The danger, of course, is that you end up writing an ordinary family-crisis play with a secret weapon up its sleeve, and that is often the case here.

Not always: The scenes involving the boy (Andrew Dodd) go on as truthfully, in writing and performance, as they began. Those with the father, played with affecting twitchy authenticity by Wayne Best, are fine, too, though underdeveloped. He becomes increasingly drawn to his wife's younger sister, a divorcee who is constantly around, ever-helpful and adoring (of both Drivers); this relationship naturally arouses the resentment of the two children. Most of what we learn about the sister she has to tell us herself; Jane Spidell copes very well in the role, considering. (She seems condemned this season to underwritten characters; in The Trials of Ezra Pound, she fared even worse.)

You would expect the husband-wife relationship, with its ramifications, to be the centre of the play, but it gets increasingly elbowed out by the problems of their daughter. Unfortunately, neither the writing nor the performance (by Michele Graff) of this role can raise it above the stock figure of the sullen teenager. Sure, she is at an age when daughters resent and fight with their mothers. Yes, she is now in a situation of uncommon stress, aggravated by the loss of her boyfriend (Shawn Mathieson), a relationship to which the play devotes a lot of time without ever bringing it truly alive. But wouldn't she, in the circumstances, make some effort? Would she blame everyone for everything all the time? The play eventually zeroes in on the relationship, cruelly misshapen but still oddly unaltered, between mother and daughter; but though it avoids sentimentality when dealing with Anne herself, it embraces it when presenting the two together.

There are two other characters. One is Anne's bouncy and largely unqualified nurse (Lisa Horner). Well-intentioned and full of earthly wisdom, she is a type but an enjoyable one; she too tells us more about herself than, dramatically, we need to know. The same goes, more damagingly, for a clinical psychiatrist (Kim Horsman) mainly distinguished by her briskness and lack of empathy; qualities that can certainly exist in doctors but here have no effect on the rest of the play and are therefore not worth the time they take to delineate. It's meaningless character writing, television-style.

We could, in the meantime, do with more information about the people who do matter. What business, for example, is Anne's husband supposed to be in? Miles Potter's direction is lucid scene by scene, but cannot prevent the play as a whole from straggling.

This, the last production of the Stratford season, is the only one of the four new plays on the bill that is truly, wholly new. The others have been an adaptation of a novel (Tempest-Tost), a semi- documentary (Ezra Pound), and the latest sliver of a continuing soap (Wingfield on Ice). It is also the most conventional: the one that could most easily have turned up somewhere else. This, surprisingly, is a hopeful sign: If Stratford is going to plunge seriously into producing new plays, as portended by the opening of the Studio next year, then it cannot wait around for special events, worthy to share a stage with Shakespeare and Chekhov. It has to be more eclectic than that, perhaps more modest, and it has to keep them coming. Good Mother may point the way to some good rowdy children.
