Ihated Without You. This was not because of its standard of performance, which is high. Some 15 years ago, Anthony Rapp created the role of Mark, the videographer-narrator of Rent, and his performance was the best thing in the show. His talents are still on display in the current offering, which is in part a memento of what apparently remains his finest hour. All alone, if you don't count his five back-up musicians, he looks back on his experiences auditioning for Rent, appearing in the workshop, rehearsing for off-Broadway. And then of course the catastrophe that has helped make the musical a legend: the death from an aortic aneurysm and just before the first preview, of Jonathan Larson, the show's author and composer.

Without You is partly Rapp's memorial to Larson. It's also, and more regrettably, a memorial to Rapp's mother, who died of cancer shortly after Rent's opening. (She had been ill before, but had had a lengthy period of remission.) Basing a public performance on private grief is a dodgy proposition, worsened in this case by the fact that Rapp has nothing to say about either person that can make them matter to anyone but him.

Larson makes the better subject of the two, partly because the audience is likely to have some knowledge of and interest in him before entering the theatre, and partly because Rapp can pay tribute to him -- and to his own past -- by singing his songs. Rapp still has an urgent, wiry voice, and it's stirring as well as nostalgic to hear him giving out with Rent's opening number, the one about being "in America, at the end of the millennium." His intensity and the surge of the music carry the lyric, which seemed vaguely pretentious (or pretentiously vague) even at the time. He recounts his initial, and understandable, dismay at the idea of a "rock opera" and how surprised and impressed he was to find that its subject matter included gayness and AIDS. "This" he asked himself, "is a musical?": a strange question, surely, in a New York that had already seen Falsettoland. But all we learn about Larson himself is that he was sort of a cool guy; well, that and the fact that his being cut off in, or quite possibly before, his creative prime was a cruel deprivation, but we already knew that. There's nothing about Larson's personal life, not even a mention of his other musical Tick Tick Boom: a better score than Rent and a show that, being itself self-referential, might have helped.

That Larson's death occurs quite early in Without You is chronology's fault. Still, it leaves Rapp's feeling about his mother to occupy the bulk of the evening. Of course he loved her; of course he was devastated by her illness and her death. We take that for granted. We're also quite willing to believe that she was a very admirable woman. She was, after an initial period of denial, accepting of his sexuality, which is a credit to her but, dramatically speaking, deprives the story of a possible conflict: so much so that we wonder why he bothers to mention it.

The only link between the show's two subjects -- other than that they're things that happened to him, or at least how they're presented -- is that appearing in Rent, with its themes of loss, helped Rapp to deal with his own. The connection involves him in honouring his mother by singing Seasons of Love, but since that's another song with stirring music but portentously sentimental words (never trust a song in which the word "love" has three syllables) the effect is mush. So are the various nostrums proffered by the grief counsellor first brought in to help the Rent company deal with the death of Larson and then consulted by Rapp to deal with his own loss. And it does come across as just his. We're told that he has a brother and sister; presumably they had feelings too, but we're never told what they were -- not even when his brother, also living in New York, travels back with him to the family home in the midwest.

It all seems to be another chapter in the history of the sanctimonious narcissism that's been with us since the 1960s: the belief that the intensity of someone's feelings automatically entitles them to be expressed in public without the mediation of focus and structure: In a word, of art. Rapp achieves this just once, though to be fair it is at his show's climax. He has the late Larson's help, but that's no crime; in fact it's the point. He sings the song from Rent that's his own show's title song, and it does become a moving statement about loss and survival: A summary of his own condition that we can also relate to ours. Not all the songs he sings are from his greatest hit; some are his own compositions, including one called Wild Bill about his mother's tumour that would probably be a low point if the words were audible. Towards the end he tells us about his mother's memorial service, and the show is probably meant to be an extended variation on that event. At a memorial, though, the audience doesn't pay to get in and nobody expects applause.
