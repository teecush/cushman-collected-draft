THEATRE REVIEW" FLESH AND BLOOD

Tuesday, April 09, 1991

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Toronto ONT -- WRITTEN BY COLIN THOMAS Directed by JoAnn McIntyre Starring Mark Saunders, David Orth, Rob Osborne and Amanda Stepto At The Stage Downstairs, Toronto Passive suffering is not very interesting dramatically, and a call to action merely leads us outside the theatre Morality not simple in moral Theatre Direct play ufbyBY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Toronto FLESH and Blood is a good play trying to pass itself off as a social tract. It is being presented by Theatre Direct, which tours plays to schools. With the play comes a glossy informative brochure about AIDS and how to avoid it. Flesh and Blood, though, is not about AIDS but - it is not a very subtle difference - about people living with or dying from it. Its subjects are the eternal ones: sex, friendship, family and death.

Acquired immune deficiency syndrome, let us pray, is not an eternal subject though it is disturbing enough to be mistaken for one. The first wave of AIDS plays made that mistake, putting the disease on stage and saying, in effect, "Isn't this terrible? Something must be done." Both utterances were undeniable, but passive suffering is not very interesting dramatically, and a call to action outside the theatre merely leads us outside the theatre.

Colin Thomas, a Vancouver playwright, starts by introducing us to two brothers, one straight, one gay, both the victims of a guilt-dispensing offstage mother with a virtuoso line in intimidation. (She laments that she lives in Saskatchewan where abortion is illegal; otherwise, she might have spared herself all the pain of parenthood.) She has discovered Allan, her youngest, more or less in flagrante with his girlfriend, Sherri, and he has left home in consequence. He now moves in with big brother, Jim, and Jim's lover, Ralph, at Jim's insistence, moves out.

Jim has AIDS, but not until halfway through the play does Allan find out. Jim has always seen himself as the protector in their relationship, and he is not about to have the roles reversed. Nor, protest though he may, does Allan find the strains and responsibilities easy.

Both brothers are self-destructive. Allan drinks; Jim invited his condition. Having been cautious since the epidemic broke, he succumbed one night to unsafe sex. Allan, similarly insisting on one night of unprotected passion, provokes the breakup of his own affair with Sherri. Indeed, the playwright manages to make the humble condom a potent (you should pardon the expression) dramatic symbol. Allan's mother retrieved the one he used at home and waited, vengefully, to confront him with it. No wonder he left.

The play's later developments are controlled, as all plays involving AIDS tend to be, by Jim's deterioration. The details are helpfully and economically filled in by his increasingly frail testimonies at an encounter group, but the progress toward the hospital bed is inevitable in more ways than one.

Here, Flesh and Blood does become "an AIDS play." For all its compassion, it leaves you wanting to blame someone. Drama works either toward anger or acceptance. There is no point in being angry with a medical phenomenon; you end up railing at life itself.

But acceptance seems inhuman. It could be that the only satisfactory play about AIDS could be written from a standpoint that none of us could tolerate: a fundamentalist one, like that of the mother in this play, that sees death as the wages of sin. Given the scale on which those wages are being paid, it could be our equivalent of a Greek curse.

Flesh and Blood comes to a liberal accommodation with tragedy. Jim (Allan, too) is not punished for sin but for carelessness or, from another point of view, for love. Passion breaks the rules. The play is moral all right, but its morality is not simple, JoAnn McIntyre's production spreads the action across a wide space, and in doing so loses visual and aural focus. (The theatre has an awkward echoing acoustic.) But she gets good performances from Mark Saunders, grey with pent-up suffering as Jim, and David Orth, wild and flailing as Allan. Rob Osborne's Ralph is a neutral performance, perhaps because the script seems unwilling to characterize him as a camp queen, but is unable to come up with an alternative. His role in the structure is to keep returning loyally to the vortex of sickness, while Sherri has to get away from it. Amanda Stepto starts her as a pert caricature and gets steadily deeper.
