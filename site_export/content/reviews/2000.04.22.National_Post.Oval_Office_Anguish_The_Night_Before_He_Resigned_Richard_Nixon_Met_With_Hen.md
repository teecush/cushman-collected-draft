NIXON'S NIXON

Jane Mallet Theatre

It wouldn't be easy, but perhaps the only thing left for a playwright to do with the character of Richard Nixon would be to make us feel sorry for him.

There is one moment in Nixon's Nixon that comes near to doing this. The president, with Watergate unravelled and impeachment beckoning, protests the most hurtful thing about the situation is what it will do to his daughter, Julie. He mentally flashes back to his celebrated Checkers speech, and to how his little girl had melted in his arms after she had seen the broadcast. "Daddy" she apparently said "you mentioned Checkers on television."

That TV appearance, of course, is the one moment at which Nixon successfully twisted the heartstrings of the American people. It's notorious; and in invoking it, playwright Russell Lees simultaneously discredits it. That is pretty much his approach to Nixon throughout. The man was in a tough place, but he deserved it. I agree; but I don't necessarily want to hear it re-stated for 90 minutes.

Nixon's antagonist in this two-character piece (an Anglo- American contribution to World Stage) is a tuxedoed Henry Kissinger who is paying a reluctant evening call on his fallen chief in what is presumably the Oval Office.

Whatever it is, it's nostalgically rendered in Charles Towers' production by an economic assemblage of lampshades, rotary phones and radiograms.

Each man has his own agenda. Nixon's is the more complicated, as he alternately craves vindication, consolation and someone to join in his memory-gams. Kissinger merely wants an assurance that the incoming Gerald Ford will keep him on as Secretary of State.

Only so, he insists, can Nixon's diplomatic legacy be preserved; and the doggedness with which he keeps repeating it can be mildly amusing. Of course it's also, in the circumstances, somewhat unfeeling, and the play's title may be double-edged. It may refer to the president, trying to rewrite history according to his own prescription; or it may refer to Kissinger, ratting on his boss as his boss ratted on the country.

One can believe that in his desperation Nixon would keep re- playing his triumphant encounters with other heads of state, and that he would get Kissinger to listen to him. It's harder to credit that they would, at the commander-in-chief's insistence, systematically act out all the roles. ("I'll be Brezhnev; you be me.")

At first embarrassed, Kissinger gradually gets into the spirit of the thing; and at what you might call the Julie moment he takes on that part too; which gives it some of its zing.

The play is moderately well-written. It builds through a mind- bending rationale about the bombing of Cambodia as a way of disengaging from Vietnam ("They'll think I'm really off my rocker.") to a final fantasy scenario about engineering a new global conflict as a way to distract the attention of the American voter. (Well, we may be meant to think, it worked for Clinton; though one doubts that he ever contemplated unleashing a Third World War.) It climaxes with Nixon, momentarily appalled, computing the number of deaths engineered by his administration, with Kissinger meticulously doing the numbers. But it never takes off, unless you consider a cross- talk between two actual politicians to be hysterically funny in itself.

The acting is good. Tim Donoghue gets the look of Kissinger uncannily right (probably it's the glasses, and perhaps the hair), and if he often sounds like Dr Strangelove, well, who in this role wouldn't? Only once, though -- when he remembers his boyhood as a refugee from the Nazis -- does he seem like a human being; otherwise he's an automaton, and though this may be the intention, the effect is still dull.

Keith Jochim's Nixon is less of a lookalike: Lacking the oily civil-servant air that contributed to his downfall, and in fact seeming somewhat open-faced and rugged; but he gets the bluster and the hollowness very well. (Did you see Fail Safe the other week? And did you find yourself wondering what recent U.S. president could have behaved with the dignity and compassion of the one played by Richard Dreyfuss?)

Both characters are naturally preoccupied with those damning audio recordings which, at the time of the action, have yet to be released. In the context of this World Stage season as a whole, and given Nixon's notoriously earthy vocabulary, the play might be regarded as the real-life version of Krapp's Last Tape.
