Johnny Mercer was the prince of America lyricists, and to celebrate him is to salute the whole mid-century era of popular music he wrote with nearly every major composer, from Kern to Mancini. He was sung by literally every singer, and he worked in every available setting -- jazz, Broadway, Hollywood, big bands, Tin Pan Alley, and radio.

He was also one of the great American vocalists, perhaps the only singer-songwriter to justify this hypen. He sang rhythm and comedy songs with whimsical finesse and in his later years he was a uniquely wistful balladeer, singing less about love than about evanescence.

His personal style, impish and melancholy, has little in common with the chunky, cheerful approach of Scott Walker, CBC radio host and singer. Walker can hit deep notes that Mercer could only have reached in his dreams, but his idea of swing is to increase the tempo and self-consciously shift a few accents. Mercer was a Southerner who even some black listeners thought was black; Walker is definitely white-bread, looking and sounding like the front-man for a Glenn Miller revival band.

Not sounding like Mercer is no disqualification for singing his songs. But in Too Marvelous for Words (the New Year's Eve attraction at the Limelight Dinner Theatre, with dates lined up elsewhere in Ontario), Walker is actually playing Mercer. The premise is that it's 1965, and he's running through his massive catalogue in order to cut a retrospective album for Capitol Records, the company that he had co-founded 20 years earlier. One of the many unexplored ironies left lying about is that by this date, Capitol was re- making itself as "home of the Beatles." The new owners wouldn't have given Mercer the time of day.

Much of the script derives from an autobiographical "Lyrics and Lyricists" evening that Mercer gave in New York in the early '70s. It's diversified, though, with ancedotes drawn from all over, not all about Mercer.

Indeed, given the huge bulk of available material, the show goes off at a surprising number of tangents. It includes one song, Baby It's Cold Outside, that Mercer recorded but didn't write; an agonizingly protracted sequence about Sadie Vimmerstedt, the Ohio housewife who sent him the opening lines for I Wanna Be Around; and appearances by a character called "the Kid", an unnamed Mercer protege who seems to be based on Margaret Whiting but who also sings songs associated with Connee Boswell and Billie Holiday. This last device seems to promise some kind of pay-off, but it never comes.

Susan Henley plays all the women -- including Johnny's wife, Ginger -- and the two duet with great charm and spirit on Mercer's first published song, Out of Breath and Scared to Death of You, full of cute '30s wordplay. There are some other enterprising selections; I was delighted by the inclusion of The Matrimonial Stomp from Li'l Abner, though it lost its funniest lines.

If most of the other songs are familiar they're still worth hearing, and Walker communicates his own enthusiasm for them; he does a touching skylark. The musicians -- pianist David Warrack and bassist Bob Price -- are excellent. The commentary makes some interesting points -- Mercer's lack of success on Broadway, his long association with Fred Astaire, another small-town boy made good who was also a great part-time singer -- but except on the most obvious level of "and them I wrote", it never connects the man to the work. And the show doesn't end; it stops.

Missing are Mercer's destructive passion for Judy Garland, his increasing pessimism about America and the music business, and his drinking. Plainly it wasn't meant to be that kind of show; it's a small, unpretentious entertainment with no thought of disturbing anyone.

And Mercer is a difficult theatrical subject since -- as he himself readily admitted -- he wasn't an obviously theatrical writer. He knew as much as anyone about capturing speech rhythms in song but he didn't create or illuminate characters. He spent a lifetime writing about romantic love but -- unlike Cole Porter or Lorenz Hart -- he had nothing individual to say on the subject; he merely rearranged the conventional symbols.

He did it, though, with endless verbal resource; and it is his delight in the magical, unexpected but wholly appropriate world that gives his work its extraordinary distinction. The most famous example -- the "huckleberry friend" of Moon River -- may also be the best, drenched as it is in yearning for an unreachable past. Mercer was the most lyrical of lyricists -- a rural writer in an urban world -- and the themes of childhood and loss are the most personal in his work, increasingly so as his own death drew nearer. I wonder if that's a coincidence.

A couple of seasons ago on Broadway there was a full-scale Mercer revue called Dream, which seemed dimly aware of this but rang away from its own insights; it presented Moon River as an eleven o'clock star solo rather than as the ultimate home-town song. (It also managed, with awe-inspiring consistency, to misread such dramatic clues as do exist in the lyrics.) Mercer still awaits a worthy stage tribute. So do many of his peers. For any of them, the limelight is a jewel of a cabaret space.
