THE DUMB WAITER / THE ZOO STORY

Harbourfront Studio Theatre

- - -

I will, any day, take minor Harold Pinter, which The Dumb Waiter is, over major Edward Albee, which The Zoo Story is. Soulpepper's new double bill gives us the opportunity to take them together. Both go down smoothly, and both go down very well, especially the Pinter, which is practically impeccable.

There are two of them in every Pinter play, or at least in many, especially the early ones: Two wide boys, two wise guys, engaged in nefarious and often mysterious pursuits, threatening others while feeling threatened themselves. They are the Irishman and the Jew who abduct the unfortunate hero of The Birthday Party, the two servants who stand guard over the alcoholic novelist in No Man's Land. They are, in an especially subtle variation, the two brothers in The Caretaker, who are barely on stage together but palpably bonded nonetheless. In their purest form they are Ben and Gus, who have The Dumb Waiter to themselves.

Ben and Gus, as we not too gradually learn, are gunmen, employed on a regular basis by an unspecified organization; it might be criminal or it might, to judge from the resources it apparently has at its disposal, be quasi-governmental. They are clearly Londoners, though when we meet them they are in Birmingham, in the basement of a big house awaiting the arrival of their latest victim. It's the usual arrangement, though Gus, who seems unhealthily given to questioning and complaining, thinks the sleeping and catering arrangements represent a decline from what they're used to. Both men are nervous. Ben, the senior partner, tries to relieve his feelings by reading and re-reading the newspaper; Gus would like to make some tea, but is frustrated by the facilities.

Around 1960, when this play was first produced (it seems to have been written in 1958, maybe as a trial run for the far more complex Birthday Party) Pinter acknowledged three influences on his work: Beckett, Kafka, and American gangster films. All are in evidence here, but he seems to have left another off the list: The BBC Light Entertainment Department. He is writing in the style of such radio and TV classics as The Goon Show, Hancock's Half Hour and Steptoe and Son; barbed cross-talk routines in which Cockney-influenced banter climbs into absurdity before boiling over into exasperation. Or maybe it isn't an influence; maybe it's a bred-in-the-bone affinity. Ben and Gus squabble over the problems that surround common phrases concerning the boiling of kettles (or, to be as precise as they might like to be, the boiling of water in kettles). Their patience is further tested by the activity of the dumb waiter itself, a miniature elevator device used in big old houses to transmit orders to kitchens below. This one starts making demands on them ("Scampi!") that they are very ill-equipped to meet. Someone, it seems, is playing games with them, and cruel ones.

Ben and Gus are played, respectively, by Michael Hanrahan and Stuart Hughes, the same actors who played the equivalent roles in Soulpepper's No Man's Land. Part of the point of this production is to let us see what was then a subsidiary couple, operating in its own right. The compliment, and the promotion, are justified. They have a few problems with the accents but none with the rhythms, which are far more important. We begin with Hughes doing some modest calisthenics, mainly involving the putting-on of shoes; Hanrahan, lying on his bed, watches with disgust. There are shades of Laurel and Hardy, especially on Hughes' side of the equation. Already we have two modes of humour, two modes of tension; and Ted Dykstra's production subsequently plays both to the hilt. The two men sitting back to back, in complementary perplexity, are a rich sight. Steve Lucas' set exudes genteel damp, and John Lott's soundscape is first- rate, from the off-stage roar of traffic kicking itself and the play into gear to the minatory creaking of the dumb-waiter itself. The only possible quarrel with Dykstra's direction might be that the characters' interaction is so detailed, the surprise ending is not much of a surprise -- but that seems a small price to pay.

The same two actors supply the other half of the evening's raison d'etre by appearing in The Zoo Story, another duologue and one that starts with bird-song instead of with motorbikes. If The Dumb Waiter was among Pinter's first plays, The Zoo Story was Albee's very first or at least the first to be admitted into his canon. It's a more ambitious piece. The Dumb Waiter is pretty much a self-defining mechanism (and so more enjoyable to us than to its original audiences, who wondered what it meant); The Zoo Story undeniably opens on to wider vistas, and not just because it takes place in the open air. Jerry, the urban outcast who keeps the respectable publisher Peter glued to his Central Park bench with stories of his lonely childhood and lonelier adulthood and of the landlady whom he hates and of her dog who hates him, has a statement to make about love. His question as to why, if his feelings have no value, the word "love" was invented in the first place, is a resonant one, and it crowns a brilliantly-witty monologue. It does not, for me, erase sickly sentimentality that suffuses this as it does so many plays by Albee (and that you never, repeat never, get in Pinter), or the air of warmed-over Kerouac that Jerry brings with him. It's a play very much of its period, and Albee was unwise to update it, as he recently has. Peter might just have sat still on his bench in innocent old 1959 but at any later date he would have turned and ran in two minutes. And the religious undertones in the characters' names (Jerry for Jesus) and in the climactic action are very suspect.

What is exciting, as often in Albee, is the play's aggressive humour, along with -- very well staged in Diana Leblanc's production -- the battle for the bench. We're always being told that Pinter's plays are about territorial battles; this one of Albee's really is. Hanrahan, with his pained and bashful smile, gives a beautifully complete account of Peter; Albee has now written a prequel dealing with Peter's marriage, but in light of what Hanrahan implies here, with his smile and the nervous crossing of his legs, it hardly seems necessary. Hughes takes longer to get inside Jerry's skin; he seems at first to be paying unconscious though certainly timely tribute to the young Marlon Brando. But he has found his voice by the big speech, and it's a hypnotic one.
