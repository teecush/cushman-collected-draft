Theres a heart-stopping moment early in South Pacific when Carmen Cusack, as Ensign Nellie Forbush, sings one of the Twin Soliloquies in which she and the middle-aged planter Emile de Becque guardedly express their awakening feelings for one another. He's a cultured Frenchman, Nellie sings. "I'm a little hick." In the preceding dialogue, shes already laughingly used the word hick to describe herself; now it comes back to haunt her, and her hesitation before singing it and the determined emphasis with which she eventually does, speak subtle volumes.

Cusacks performance as Knucklehead (and also, in this production, Touslehead) Nellie, is one of the best I've seen in a musical: forthright, puzzled -and to an amazing extent in this genre -quiet. Nellie is the Second World War army nurse from Little Rock, Ark., who can cope with differences in age and education, and even with her lover's admission that hes killed a man, but not with the discovery that the two enchanting Polynesian children in his house are actually his. Her deliberate (as in slow and thoughtful) Southern accent helps a lot; you feel the battle between her inherited prejudices and her shame at making what she knows to be the wrong decision. It helps, too, that the shows book is tactful enough to excuse her from making any kind of reasoned declaration; she would have been too embarrassed. In another mood, her full-out singing and goofy dancing when she proclaims her love for a wonderful guy are wonderful themselves; anyone would melt. And her own melting, when she meets a girl from the other part of the plot who's in a state analogous to her own but worse, is another example of tact in writing and performance producing something powerfully and legitimately moving.

Bartlett Sher's very fine production is now on its second tour of duty in Toronto. Visually and musically, its as good as ever; dramatically, its even better. The part of Emile, previously played by Jason Howard, who sang well but acted stiffly, has been assumed (until March 8 when Howard returns) by David Pittsinger, who is swell in both departments. He can't quite compensate for the sketchy writing in Emile's plea of justifiable homicide but hes persuasive when uttering his outsiders credo to the U.S. Forces ("I know what you're against -what are you for?") and he even manages to make something heartfelt out of This Nearly Was Mine, an outrageously redundant song with all the earmarks of having been written to honour the leading man's contract.

Conviction and determination stamp nearly all the performances, notably Jodi Kimuras Bloody Mary, who rises far above her characters traditional role of exotic comic relief. She's a crafty chancer, fighting for her own and her child's survival. Early on in the show the marines teach her the phrase stingy bastard and she uses it constantly thereafter; it escalates from a joke to a bargaining tool to a heartbroken angry rebuke hurled at the faithless Lieutenant Cable (another guilty victim of his own prejudice). Earlier on weve seen her trying to seduce him on her daughters behalf even while knowing that he's slipping away. You may never have thought of Happy Talk as a song of desperation, but once you've heard this performance you may never think of it any other way. Luther Billis, Marys American counterpart, is given a new shine by Timothy Gulan, his eye constantly cocked and with a sensitive streak, traceable to his being hopelessly in love with Nellie. Hes the comic and she's the leading lady; romantic barriers don't get more insuperable than that.

Aaron Ramey, the new Joe Cable, is ardent and tortured enough in his love scenes but disappointingly wooden at marine HQ. These are the least convincing scenes in the show; some of the fault may be in the writing, but these are the only spots in the show where Shers actors seem content to play the stereotypes rather than to burrow beneath them. The Thanksgiving Follies sequence, though, seems to have improved, not only in the cheerfully tacky numbers themselves but in the tension of what goes on around and behind the stage, as everyones lives unravel in a contrast that's almost Chekhovian.

Despite all the obvious simplifications, there is a sense in this show of what life in the real Pacific theatre might have been like. It's always been my least favourite of the big four Richard Rodgers-Oscar Hammerstein musicals, but this revival makes a hell of a case for it. The sheer assurance of the piece, and the way it justifies that assurance, makes much of subsequent Broadway look like a joke. Note how it paints a broad canvas but begins and ends in domesticity. It comes from a time when liberal pieties could have the force of a manifesto. Another writer, a victim of McCarthyism whom Hammerstein befriended, said at his death, "The man was my friend; I liked the world a lot better when he was in it." Too right.
