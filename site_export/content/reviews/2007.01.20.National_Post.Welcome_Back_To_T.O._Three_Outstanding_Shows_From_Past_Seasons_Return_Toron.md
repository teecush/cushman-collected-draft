This is the colour to wear this week as part of the annual Windfall Clothing fundraiser Serendipity is sweet. A fortuitous festival of Canadian theatre is going on in Toronto at the moment, with the metropolitan transfer of Stratford's Orpheus Descending joined by the return to the city of three outstanding shows from past seasons.

Leading the pack is Half Life, which now displaces The Drawer Boy as the best combination of Canadian writing, acting and direction that I have seen. It was first seen in 2005; its toured in the interim, here and abroad, with a couple of cast changes but the original actors are now all back in place. Watching John Mightons play a second time, I found myself wondering how many drafts it must have gone through before reaching its canonical form. Its themes and actions dovetail in a seemingly casual, effortless pattern that must actually have taken much hard labour to work out.

Patrick and Clara are two old people in a nursing home. Patrick is spry, aware and sardonic; Clara is sweet, confused and wheelchairbound. They fall in love, convinced that they are resuming an inconclusive romance that flared for a brief period during the Second World War. The first time I saw the play, I believed that this had indeed been the case; this time I doubted it. Donald, Clara's son, is sure that it couldn't have happened; he speaks with theoretical authority, as a psychologist specializing in the limitations of the human memory. He believes these limits to be a good thing, speculating on how unbearable existence would be if we lacked the capacity to forget, and pointing out that even the most capacious computers have built-in limits. He also, in what he believes to be in his mothers best interests, thwarts her and Patricks stated desire to get married. It's an age-reversed Romeo- and-Juliet situation, with an additional bittersweet twist; we are programmed initially to expect the divorced Donald to get together with Patrick's daughter Anna. Antagonism over their elders puts paid to that, though they seem initially to complement one another: shes an artist, hes a scientist, and they're both professionally frustrated.

In the play's first scene, Donald propounds a theory about limited attention spans, drawn from his experience of trying to tell a story at a party, and the theme echoes through the play. The action is full of conversations that are broken or simply lost, and the play is accordingly made up of short scenes which, however, are never sketchy. Relationships are touched lightly but fully, notably that between Donald and Clara; he visits her daily but can think of little to say; she still thinks of him as her brilliant boy. Some of the best writing in the play concerns the ability of children to feel an intensity of happiness -- and, by unspoken corollary, of unhappiness -- unattainable to adults, which is one thing that makes cruelty to children so uniquely abhorrent.

The play packs a lot in. It gives us, as well as the criss- crossing connections at its core, a sense of the life of the home; its hard, come the final curtain, to believe that we have only met two members of staff and one semi-official visitor. Its discretion makes the occasional shock all the more powerful, as when Patrick is discovered at the start of a scene, tied to a chair. It also enjoys itself with recurring motifs: telephone numbers and the ability to recall them are significant, as are entry codes; Patrick was a code-breaker in the war and he still has the knack. One of the key scenes is set on Remembrance Day. Its extraordinary, too, how a play dealing in tiny encounters turns out to have cosmic range. Half life can be a measure of geological time as well as of the shrunken existences experienced by some of the plays' characters -- by all of them and all of us, if we are to believe Donald. He thinks it a blessing that time swallows us all up; the local vicar finds it an unbearable idea for which belief in God is the only salve.

Randy Hughson's performance of the latter role is the most improved since the first opening. Before, he seemed to function chiefly as comic relief; he is still funny, funnier if anything, but he can make us sense the dormant despair that his compulsive cheeriness keeps at bay. (He does like, when chatting with the old folks, to dwell on all the terrible things they have come through; the play lets us hear some hilariously harrowing snatches from one of his sermons.) Barbara Gordon shifts brilliantly between ages as a dyspeptic resident (who says when hearing of a deceased inmate "at least she wont have to do any more crafts") and a brisk late- arriving nurse; and Maggie Huculak is equally fine as the underpaid caregiver she replaces, one in whom an uncommon sympathy and efficiency co-exist with a tendency to pilfer from the patients. Diego Matamoross Donald has lost some of the deprecatory wit that used to light up his first scene, but his intellect and his weary good nature are still splendidly in place. Laura de Carteret sometimes goes for easy options as Anna, but I suspect hers is the least developed role. The two central seniors are beyond praise, with Carolyn Hetherington's supremely gentle Clara radiating a grace that outlives lucidity and Eric Peterson's Patrick showing cantankerousness giving birth to courtesy. Daniel Brookss production is scenically, sonically, emotionally and intellectually immaculate.

In the early years of the 20th century, there was an American playwright named Clyde Fitch who was so successful and prolific that he once had two plays open against each other on the same night. Daniel Brooks notched up a similar record last Wednesday, which saw the openings, or re-openings, both of Half Life and of Monster, which Brooks and Daniel MacIvor created together in 1999, which Brooks directs and which MacIvor, all on his own, performs. If Monster (which, for obvious reasons, I saw at a preview) had been my first encounter with MacIvor solo -- I missed it the first time around -- I would undoubtedly have been knocked out. As it was, I was still mightily impressed, but could not shake a sense of being served the mixture-as-before. Like the later Cul-de-sac, it evokes a community with something nasty happening behind every window; like the earlier Here Lies Henry, it has a narrator-protagonist -- though this one does more narrating than protagonizing -- who projects his own violent discontents on to the rest of the world. At one moment hes ingenuous, at the next diabolical, and the transitions, as usual with MacIvor, are both instant and awesome. The blood- freezing tale he unfolds begins with a young couple next door, whose altercations Mac- Ivor voices in a quick-change register and rhythm that suggest Ronnie Burkett without his puppets. She wants a child, he doesn't; and he could be right because we shift to a young boy's axe-murder of his father, which in turn is fondly, or at least obsessively, remembered by another kid who lives next door. The crime inspired a movie; we meet its director, a recovering alcoholic, and the more prosperous producer who claims the film was his in the first place. There is wit as well as horror in the writing and, of course, in the acting: MacIvors ability to people a stage while standing virtually immobile is phenomenal, and Brooks gives him the cool, cruel atmospheric backup to which he and we are accustomed. The upshot -- that there is a monster in all of us, perpetrators or scavengers -- is not that profound, or even that involving, but in performance and presentation, this is theatre at a very high technical level.

Monster is part of the farewell season of MacIvors production company, da da kamera. Brooks now runs the Necessary Angel company, which gave birth to Half Life. His predecessor, Richard Rose, now runs the Tarragon where, if you're still with me, Half Life was first staged, though not as a Tarragon production. That theatre's own best play of 2006, Rosa Labordes Leo, about two young men and one young woman of variously revolutionary dispositions growing up hopefully in Allendes Chile and drastically in Pinochet's, is now back where it started, and standing up to a second viewing less well than I had hoped. It once looked deceptively modest, with ramifications; it now seems to have more to be modest about, though stylishly written and interestingly fluid in construction. Some of the edge has gone off Rose's direction with its central conceit of a triangular stage for a triangular relationship. The plays jump- cuts from the personal to the political and back are now less jumpy and less cutting than I remembered them, and without this shifting focus both levels of concern seem pro forma. Its still worth seeing, and the performances are excellent. Leo himself, accidental hero and central consciousness, in love with both his companions, mopes and twitches beautifully. Cara Pifko is sharp and glittering as the shared girlfriend and muse, with a sideline (mysterious or maybe just underwritten) in kleptomania. Sergio Di Zio is best of all as the most committed of all, taking an engagingly single- minded character and making him flesh and blood.
