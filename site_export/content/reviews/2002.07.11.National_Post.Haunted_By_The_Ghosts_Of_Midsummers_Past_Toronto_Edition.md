A MIDSUMMER NIGHTS DREAM

High Park, Toronto

In theory A Midsummer Nights Dream is my favourite play. My affection is obviously shared by a great many other people, since I have yet to get through a year in this job without being required to attend a new production. If it isn't a Stratford spectacular, it's a bare-bones Equity showcase; if it isnt Kate Lynch trying out an all- female cast at Theatre Passe Muraille, its the Dream in High Park celebrating its 20th anniversary by bringing back the play that gave it both birth and a name. And if it gets increasingly difficult for me to see the play fresh and whole, it seems equally hard for directors and actors to convince me that they have. We're all haunted by the ghosts of past productions.

The ghosts can be laid; the Lynch ladies last year had me listening intently and delightedly to every line. The new Dream is conventionally shred-and-patchy, despite its publicized attractions of being both gender-bent and colour-blind. The first category of liberation consists solely of the casting of Alison Sealy-Smith as Pita (formerly Peter) Quince. Spelling apart, there's nothing about her performance, or even her costume, to suggest that she isnt sportingly playing the man as a man. (But do the clown scenes work as well with an actress in this role as they might with an actor? They dont. The dynamic between the characters is spoiled. Men behave differently when a woman is around, even when they outnumber her five to one.)

Racially mixed the show certainly is, though in a play whose characters are ethnically neutral to begin with (they're either fairies or Elizabethans with ancient Greek names stuck on) this hardly impinges. When it does -- when the actors translate occasional lines into what one assumes are their native dialects -- the device falls on its face. (It isn't like the French characters in Stratfords Henry VI periodically going into French, that makes a dramatic point.)

Musically, the show features a lot of homemade percussion, and this works better. When Bottom calls for the tongs and the bones, he gets the pots and the pans. Scenically, it affects a simplicity that turns out to be rather cumbersome. The multi-tiered permanent set looks like a Jungle Gym, and its very handy when the lovers start chasing one another. Things that look like hollow tree trunks are lowered from aloft, to provide hiding places for whatever immortal schemer happens to be standing underneath. Characters are painstakingly strapped into flying harnesses. It all seems to be a revolt against a realistic tradition of staging the Dream that hasn't existed in decades. Tricks are tricks, even if the audience can see how they're played, and these just slow up the action. Besides, we are watching this pastoral play in a park, surrounded by genuine trees. You can hardly get more naturalistic than that.

Richard Greenblatts production is full of ideas, but few of them go anywhere. We start with intimations of a police state. Theseus and Hippolyta make their opening nuptial speeches from an upper level and over a P.A. system, as if their wedding were a political charade. Threatening guards hold back the populace who are down on the ground floor as befits the lower classes. But they show no signs of being oppressed when we actually meet them, while the happy couple exhibit no signs of incompatibility whatsoever. Thats in performance; who knows what tension there may have been at rehearsal when the groom discovered that his bride was going to appropriate many of his lines, chiefly (do I hear a feminist axe grinding?) the more benevolent ones. This robs Theseus (Sanjay Talwar), never a complex figure at the best of times, of his one chance of development, from impassive figurehead to merciful prince. It does, though, rescue Hippolyta from her usual fate of standing around looking either adoring or resentful, so there may be a cosmic fairness about it.

Actually these two characters get their best breaks in a scene from which they are usually absent. It generally escapes notice that, in their initial slanging match, Oberon and Titania each accuse the other of having it off with their mortal opposites. In this production we see Theseus and Hippolyta, both presumably having had a last pre-marital night in the woods, drugged -- or maybe just exhausted -- in the trains of their fairy lovers. It makes an impression; and it pays off in the benediction sequence at the end of the play, when Oberon and Titania single out their old flames for special blessing.

That first fairy quarrel is the best scene of the evening, Oberon perched at the back of the auditorium in gold mask and cloak, looking and sounding like Darth Vader with a makeover. Walter Borden goes on to play the rest of the role in the same vein. Wake when some vile thing is near, hissed amiably into Titania's ear, is about as friendly as he gets, and it's a wonder that so malevolent a figure should take such a kindly interest in the fates of the lovers. He does show momentary signs of lightening up when he gets to the lyrical passage that begins "we are spirits of another sort" but then pulls back as if saying this isnt me. The production obliges him by cutting the rest of the speech. Maggie Huculaks Titania, initially promising, fades rather literally into the background, only recovering to register both shock and inhuman amusement at the thought of her dalliance with a mortal.

A boy, Adrian Morningstar, is a lively if unmodulated Puck. The mechanicals are very unfunny; one of them has a stutter, which I think tells all. Robert Perschini, with his buttery solemnity, should have been an excellent Bottom, and he has been given a fine ass's head (with tail extra). It deserves to make its first entrance from the centre rather than being hustled on from the side. He only comes to life when he acts Pyramus at the end, doing it with deadly and rather touching seriousness; as, even more effectively, does Matt Bois as his tragical Thisbe.

There are some nice touches in the lovers' scenes, especially the arrival in the forest of the lost Lysander and Hermia, scanning a map and wearing Mountain Co-Op knapsacks. Michelle Polaks Hermia is a very determined girl who at bedtime banishes her lover firmly to the far extremity of a very wide stage, and who is also good at karate. You never saw a maiden in less need of protection in the woods. Helenas startlingly modern line "she was a vixen when she went to school" rings very true. The fourfold brawl, as usual, goes on for too long with too much slapstick. But there's a treat at the end of it once the onset of darkness coincides exactly the fall of night in the real world. The Shakespearean foresight is staggering.
