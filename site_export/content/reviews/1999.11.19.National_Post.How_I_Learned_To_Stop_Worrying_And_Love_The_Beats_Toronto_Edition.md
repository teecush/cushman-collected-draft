HOWL

Tallulahs Cabaret, Buddies in Bad Times Theatre, Toronto

You learn something every day. I had always assumed that the Beat Generation were so called because of their affinity with jazz. It turns out that Jack Kerouac, who coined the phrase, was thinking of beat as in deadbeat, broke, freedom just another name for nothing left to lose. (I never thought Id want to quote that line.)

Kerouac also used beat as an abbreviation for beatific; he thought of himself and his friends as exemplifying, in the program's phrase, the secret holiness of the downtrodden. (I got this information from the program note to Howl, which, as often these days, is a lot more informative than the play.)

There are four beats in Howl: Kerouac, Neal Cassady, William Burroughs and Allen Ginsberg, after whose well-known poem the show is named. As the downtrodden go, they seem rather perky; and though the show does seem to regard them as holy, we gather this more from the devout light that it shines on them than from anything they do, say or write.

The text is drawn from their verse and prose, and it begins with Ginsberg's celebrated "I have seen the best minds of my generation destroyed by madness...". That is a sonorous line, but it is much too rhetorical to be specific (i.e., its undramatic), and it doesnt seem relevant to the characters; their minds were good but they weren't the best, and they weren't mad.

Nor were they destroyed, or no more than anybody else; they were actually, Ginsberg and Burroughs certainly, survivors. If the madness that destroyed them is meant to be societys (it probably is), then the point isnt theatrically proven.

The idea of the Beats turns out to be more fun than listening to their actual writings, which are windy. The most attractive style belongs to Cassady (Hume Baugh), the non-professional scribe who limits himself to writing letters. The show proper begins with an anecdote about meeting, and nearly making it with, his dream girl on a Greyhound bus. The others greet this tall-sounding story with an assortment of whoops and giggles, but they all seem to believe him; they're just the thinkers, but Cassady is their action man.

This is another literary evening masquerading as theatre, and proceeding on the questionable assumption that slabs of non- dramatic text, lifted from the page and delivered by one actor in the general direction of another actor, constitute dialogue.

The extracts were apparently chosen by the director, Mark Cassidy, in consultation with the actors, and they vary. Burroughs' stuff comes over as a total loss, and Patrick Conner in an undertakers suit makes him seem an exceptionally nasty piece of work, superciliously sadistic.

Jim Jones Kerouac, on the road of course, is engaging, partly because he seems to regard himself as something of a fraud, perhaps because he keys into everybody's favourite myths about America as a place to be endlessly travelled through. His love of jazz is another bonus, occasioning some of the liveliest descriptive writing in the show, prose that gets entertainingly lost as it attempts to mimic the music. (There is an attendant saxophonist, Jon Stewart, whose mournful, fevered sound does more to conjure up the '50s than anything else in the show.)

Alon Nashman makes the finest impact as Ginsberg: not the hearty bearded prophet of later years but a nervous, spectacled sophomore - - one of Natures Woody Allens, or even one of her young Arthur Millers -- who suffers eloquently and movingly when he falls in love. I dont know why we're supposed to care so much about the foursome's love lives, but Nashman is the only one who makes it seem worthwhile.

The fact that the Beats had sex with each other is surely not such a big deal but we seem to spend an awful lot of time watching them doing it. The end of one coupling is accompanied by a back- projection of a mushroom cloud: a strangely ambiguous use of an image that elsewhere is used as a symbol of everything the Beats were rebelling against, along with footage of phenomena such as Nixon.

These pictures constitute the shows only gestures toward historical context, and as such they are extraordinarily lazy. Movements, after all, can often be best defined through their enemies, and the Beats had some lulus; Howl sells its heroes short by taking them entirely on their own terms. A subject and a conflict have gone missing.

The shows last words, like its first, go to Ginsberg; the complete quartet gather on a staircase to declaim his most exalted, or at least most excited, thoughts. This is where the holiness comes in: In the eyes of the master, holy are -- among many other things, and in ascending order of dubiousness -- the body, the typewriter and the bomb. (I wasn't kidding about the ambiguity on this last item; it seems Ginsberg and co. didn't need Dr. Strangelove to stop them worrying.) This is the kind of guff whose time came round again with New Age (if you'll pardon the expression) thinking, which is probably why it's in the theatre now.
