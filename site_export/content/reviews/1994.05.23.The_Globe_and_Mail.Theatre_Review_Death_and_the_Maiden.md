THEATRE REVIEW DEATH AND THE MAIDEN

Monday, May 23, 1994

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Toronto ONT -- WRITTEN BY ARIEL DORFMAN Directed by Jonas Jurasas Starring Robert Haley, Fiona Reid and R. H. Thomson The Canadian Stage Company Rating: This production of a worthy, gripping story banishes an essential doubt Death and the Maiden revisited BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Toronto THE story-line of Ariel Dorfman's Death and the Maiden must by now be fairly well-known. Fifteen years ago, a woman named Paulina was tortured and raped by the secret police of a South American country, probably the author's own Chile. She is married to Gerardo, a lawyer about to head an inquiry into the judicial crimes of what is now, mercifully, the former regime. Into their beach house comes Roberto, whom Paulina recognizes - by his voice, since she was blindfolded through her ordeal - as the doctor who officiated at her interrogation. She proceeds to interrogate him -with a gun at his head and with her husband's reluctant co-operation.

Paulina doesn't just want revenge. She wants exorcism. She wants, among other things, to be able to listen once more to a piece of music that she once loved - music that was destroyed for her when it was played as background during her ordeal. The piece is the Schubert quartet that gives the play its name. It's a somewhat laborious device, but it does provide an image of concord, fragile but precious, needing desperately to be restored.

The play has two overlapping themes. One is the horror of torture, including the anguish that remains with the victims long after the event. The other is the agony of a country trying in a still-compromised present to come to terms with its past, the danger of imitating your persecutors while trying to bring them to justice. The physical pain inflicted on Roberto is nothing to what Paulina went through, but the psychological pressures are alarmingly similar. The doctor gives in and signs a confession. Afterward, he protests that it was all a lie; he did it only to save his life. The play leaves us almost convinced of his guilt, but not quite; and that nagging doubt is essential to the effect. Without it, the piece becomes a tract: worthy of course, and gripping, because the issues are vital and the story good, but obvious in its attack on an evil we would all condemn anyway.

Here I have to confess a bias. I saw the play four years ago in London in a rigorous and unsentimental production, distinguished above all by the performance of Juliet Stevenson as Paulina. She had two great qualities, strength and speed; she was possessed and her motor ran dangerously fast, but she was terrifyingly sane. Fiona Reid's Pauline is bird-like and brittle: not just damaged but unhinged, even before her tormentor appears. There is little warmth between her and her husband, and we are robbed of one of the play's most striking qualities: its assertion that even the worst of traumas can be absorbed into the cycle of blame and forgiveness that constitutes the average marriage. R. H. Thomson's Geraldo offers her bluff tolerance shading into exasperation, but not much sympathy. The one person who must surely believe in Roberto's innocence is the actor who plays him, but Robert Haley with his oily smile, short-back-and-sides haircut, and gleaming Gestapo glasses is the Inquisition personified. His final broken recantation shifts the balance, but too late. All three performances are alive, but only as people in a play.

Jonas Jurasas's direction is tricksy. The shanty-like set by Sue LePage has no solid walls, but finds space for a moored boat into which people retreat at improbable moments. Paulina trails a tape of the Schubert; periodically she pulls it taut and notes emerge. When she tells her story, the houselights go up and she wanders among us. When Roberto tells his, he remains onstage, his face bathed in a demonic glow. In fact, the production plays its own power games. If these are the author's instructions, the British production did him a favour by disregarding them.

And then there is the epilogue. Gerardo's official inquiry is over. Roberto is at large. The three characters meet again at a performance of guess which string quartet. In London this was a brief and simple scene, a chilling question mark. In Toronto we are ushered onstage for a proper performance by four live musicians. While they play, Roberto acts smooth and sinister, Paulina distressed and Gerardo concerned. All doubts about the doctor's guilt are banished. Then, having had its fun, the production stops being environmental, and the actors climb onto the podium to take their bows. We have heard some great music beautifully played. Everything else is 100-per-cent phony.
