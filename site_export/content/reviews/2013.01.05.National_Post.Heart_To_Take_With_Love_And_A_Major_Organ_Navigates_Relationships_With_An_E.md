Julia Lederer, author of With Love and a Major Organ, has a neat way with a digital simile. So does the young woman she herself plays, in a production at the Next Stage Theatre Festival. Having fallen heavily for a man with whom she briefly interacts on her subway commute every morning, she tells him, "You back me up better than any hard drive." Well, she doesnt exactly tell him; she confides the thought, along with many others of the same kind, to a cassette recorder and, one day, during one of their brief strap-hanging or pole-clutching encounters, slips the tape into his pocket. His response is minimal at best, so she tries harder. The next time around, she gives him her heart as well. I mean that literally. She detaches the beating organ (how, we are aren't told) and makes him a present of it. Physically, she suffers surprisingly few ill effects. Psychologically, of course, she's a mess. But then she always was.

Obviously, its all meant to be a kind of metaphor. But in what we actually see and hear, it functions as a piece of plot that everyone takes as a matter of course. The plays third character opens the show by reciting a fable about a girl who was born with a paper heart, but that doesn't solve any problems. Nor does the storyteller, an older, now-single lady, retain her narrator role in the rest of the show. Instead, we see her acting out a series of vignettes illustrating the hazards and frustrations of speed-dating. She also complains about her 29-year-old son, who's disappeared. The initially separate storylines eventually converge, in a manner more predictable than believable. Then again, literal credibility doesnt seem to be the point. But it isnt clear what is. I guess the experiences of both generations are meant to illustrate the perils and opportunities of relationships in the computer age. The older woman subscribes to online counselling. The younger constructs her life by Facebook, blog and GPS. The man in the middle is cowed and fazed by it all, and who can blame him?

Unable to establish a workable convention for itself, the play becomes a protracted exercise in whimsy. It's salvaged from ultimate tiresomeness by a reasonable supply of funny lines and by the acting. The author and Martha Ross give decent performances, and Robin Archer gives rather more. He's a tall, shy-seeming man, most of whose professional experience, according to the program, has been as a stand-up comic. He stands up a lot here too, many of his appearances taking place at rush-hour, but he talks far less than I would imagine he does when flying solo. That in itself makes him endearing, at least in this context. He conveys that his character has issues as well. With one woman trying to idealize and the other to infantilize him, he still comes across as the most grown-up person onstage.

Memorial, another three-hander at Next Stage, is another play, one of whose characters goes in for making mix-tapes. Tapes? In 2012 - sorry, 2013? Lederers heroine, insistently up-to-date in all other matters, defends this one antiquarian pursuit as being sort of nostalgic and romantic. Steven Gallagher, author of Memorial, has constructed his play largely in flashbacks; so when Dylan, his protagonist, offers a tape to his new boyfriend, Trevor, its part of the past. In the present, Dylan is about to get married to Trevor. He's also about to die of brain cancer. He's opted to remain at home, tended by Trevor and, to a lesser extent, by his older sister Ruth, the first person to whom he came out and his fractious confidante ever since.

The play begins with Ruths tribute at Dylans funeral and/ or memorial. Then Dylan interrupts her. It turns out this isn't fantasy; it's a rehearsal. Dylan is planning his own obsequies; and - attempting a feat attained by few people in life and maybe only by Tom Sawyer in fiction - he wants them to happen while he can still be around to enjoy them. (This latter point is slow to be revealed, but its stressed in the plays publicity, so it seems fair to mention it.) Dylan, you will gather, is mischievous, self-centred and a control freak; charges he mainly wouldn't deny but that he objects to having Ruth bluntly describe in what will shortly be public.

The play is somewhat awkwardly split between being a clinical study of the experience of dying, both doing it and watching it, and being a character study of Dylan himself. It is, though, magnetically well written on both levels and, under D. Jeremy Smith's direction, very tightly and fluently performed. Mark Crawford makes, at our chronologically earliest sighting of Dylan, a very convincing 16-year-old. He goes on being a very convincing 16-year-old even when Dylan has theoretically grown up and become a casting director, throwing adolescent tantrums and exerting infuriating adolescent charm. (A lifelong Muppets devotee, he still wears a Grover T-shirt. Though according to Kate, his real favourite was Miss Piggy which, she says, should have given them an early clue.) Pierre Simpson is as caring and supportive a stage-partner as his character is said to be a life-partner. His bio states thats hes appeared in John Osborne's Look Back in Anger; I don't know which role he played, but Memorial, with its self-pitying but charismatic protagonist and its enforcedly constricted setting, reminded me of that play but with its social and sexual tensions compressed and reordered. As in Osborne, the woman gets dealt the weakest hand: Kate is something of a dramatic convenience, but Mary Francis Moore brings her to jagged if partial life.

The Next Stage Theatre Festival is at the Factory Theatre through January 13. (416-966-1062)
