Memphis runs to Aug. 13 at the Robert Gill Theatre, 214 College St., Tuesday to Sunday at 8 p.m., with 2:30 p.m. matinees on Saturday and Sunday. Tickets are $20 except for Friday and Saturday evenings ($25) and the Sunday matinee (pwyc). Call (416) 978-7986.

Toward the end of Memphis there is a scene in which Martin Luther King, on the eve of his assassination, is badgered by a trio of reporters, asking a long series of hard and intrusive questions. (They have probably been stitched together from a whole series of such confrontations that happened over the years.) King loses his temper and shouts. I treasure this moment since it is the only point in the evening at which King, an inspirational leader and orator, evinces any passion, or even raises his voice. It's nice to know that the actor, Dwayne Morgan, has it in him. The rest of the time he performs in a apologetic mumble.

Subtitled The Death of Martin Luther King, Memphis is numbingly terrible. It switches back and forth between King's last day and events from his earlier life on the apparent assumption that they will automatically illuminate one another. The dramatist, John P. Moore, explicitly acknowledges three sources: the memoirs of King's wife, Coretta, and of two of King's closest associates, all of them characters in the play. His script suggests that he has put the language of these and other books on stage, without bothering to turn it into conversation. There are a couple of especially awkward telephone sequences. In one King fills in Coretta, long-distance, on the appalling working conditions of the striking black garbage workers whom he has come to Tennessee to champion. It's interesting stuff but a) she would have known some of it already and b) people don't talk, especially to loved ones, in crisp unmediated sentences filled with helpful facts and figures.

The dialogue is -- almost literally -- unspeakable. I say "almost" because really skilled actors can make sense out of anything that connects, however distantly, with human truth. The half-dozen performers here are, according to their biographies, experienced at many things but professional acting does not seem to be among them. Courtney Cunningham has some bright moments as an admiring Memphis minister, inviting King to a mouth-watering dinner that he will not live to attend, and Tamla Matthews brings intelligence, though not much technical backup, to the role of Georgia Powers who, after making history as a black female senator, found herself working as a stenographer for the civil-rights movement, and was also King's occasional mistress. Most of the time, though, the players don't stand a chance.

When King makes his first pass at the senator, she can't believe her ears. "Can we run that scene again?" she says; and, would you believe it, they do. This is dramatic straw-clutching -- throw in a theatrical device to prove this is a play -- and there's a lot of it. It culminates in the choral repetition of the casual words exchanged by King and his friends just before the fatal shot. Our awareness of the death is used throughout as a way of exacting attention for everything else. We hear at the beginning of King as a child being alternately terrorized and comforted by his family but these presumably traumatic experiences are never referred to again. The effectiveness or otherwise of non-violent protest is mentioned but never explored. King's womanizing is given a lot of space (and his wife's acceptance of it is given a little), perhaps to stress his humanity, perhaps because sex is easier to dramatize than politics, especially if you're doing vignettes. The hero's friend, Ralph Abernathy, asks him -- in a line that probably doesn't come from a book but that doesn't sound colloquial either -- if he ever met a black woman he couldn't charm. King responds that his first love was white, and that he almost married her, but was prevented by the prejudices of both families. That makes for a mildly effective scene, but again it serves no discernible dramatic purpose; it happens because, apparently, it happened. This is school-pageant stuff. Certainly you come out admiring and respecting King, but no more than you presumably did when you went in. Colin Taylor's self- designed production looks drab and feels drastically under- rehearsed.
