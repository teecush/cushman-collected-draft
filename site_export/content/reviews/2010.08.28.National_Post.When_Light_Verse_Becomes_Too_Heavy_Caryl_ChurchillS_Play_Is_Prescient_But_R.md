Iused to hate Serious Money. I mean the play. I'm not in love with it now, though my objections have nothing to do with what it has to say. Caryl Churchill's play, first staged in London in 1987 but only now receiving its professional Canadian premiere, is billed as "a city comedy," and it deals with financial trading, insider and otherwise, in the ferocious wake of Britain's Big Bang, less explosively known as deregulation. Topical in its day, it seems positively prophetic now. It concludes with its banker-protagonist, an ex-pat American, looking forward to "five more glorious years" of Thatcherism, which he adds are "handy though not essential, because it would take far more than Labour to stop us." He also says "sure, this is a dangerous system and it could crash any minute", though he adds that he confines his intimations of Armageddon to "just before breakfast." No, the problem isn't the message, it's the medium.

The play is mostly in verse, and when I first saw it I growled that it was an attempt to write in rhyming couplets by someone who hadn't a clue. That was unfair. Couplets indeed predominate, but Churchill varies them with quatrains, unrhymed verse and the occasional chunk of prose -- all adroitly done. The rhymes themselves are good, even in the two-liners; "pay for it" goes very smartly with "favourite", though it might go even better if it didn't crop up twice in as many minutes. It's the lengths to which she has to go to get to the rhymes that torture the ears. I mean "lengths" literally. She uses uneven lines of the kind that Ogden Nash made intentionally funny and William McGonagall made unintentionally hilarious. She also pads shamelessly. Somebody asks to be treated not only with "respect," which is where the line should end, but with "bonhomie" (rhymes with "economy"). Rhyming couplets fastidiously used can be wonderfully sharp comic weapons, as witness the Moliere translations of Richard Wilbur and Tony Harrison. Carelessly, they become point-blunting doggerel. Which is what happens here. A play that fancies itself as wielding a scalpel is actually brandishing a blunderbuss.

There is, to be fair, more to it; Churchill says she used verse to capture the energy of getting indescribably rich unimaginably quick. And here she does succeed. She tells a tangled tale that's centred in Britain but also takes in North and South America. At its centre is a young dealer who dies when about to be investigated; it could be suicide, it could be murder. He's a son of old money who's been making a fortune in new; and the play partly depicts a greedy, corrupt old-boy network displaced by a greedy, corrupt new-boy-and-girl network. There are no heroes here, and few visible victims, though we do occasionally hear of lives being ruined on a massive scale as the deals go through. There's no emotional investment for the audience. Everything depends on our being caught up in the story.

Churchill doesn't make that easy. We take for granted that the financial dealings will be impenetrable; it's almost part of their charm. I couldn't work out what the one character who ends up going to jail had actually done, but maybe that, too, is the point. The timeline is equally confusing; just when you think you're safely in the present, they throw another flashback at you. Style and story together need expert handling. In Eda Holmes' production, they come over loud but not clear. The Studio Theatre's acoustics seem largely to blame; The Entertainer, last year, had upholstery to soak up the sound, but this time the words get swallowed. The older players, predictably, cope best: Anthony Bekenn as a Brummie whistle-blower of mysterious motivation, Steven Sutcliffe in a variety of roles including an industrialist who's targeted by a take-over and, typical of the play, is as great a weasel as those who want to swallow him. Less distinct but still distinctive are David Schurmann as an old-school stockbroker and Graeme Somerville as a snarling corporate raider. Apart from Ali Momen, who supplies an oddly likeable centre as the guy from the Yank bank, the younger ones simply don't have the chops, at least not as individuals. As a group, they contribute energy, notably in two songs that conclude each of the acts, with lyrics (by Ian Dury) that really know about verse-rhythms. The first, depicting the activity on a trading floor, consists mainly of four-letter words. If the Shaw's intention was to outrage the bourgeoisie, they may have succeeded. Some rows of seats were conspicuously empty after the first-night interval.

The Age of Arousal, Linda Griffiths' fantasia about feminism around the turn of the last century, gets a more stylish production (from Jackie Maxwell) than it received at the Factory a few years ago. There's an especially beguiling ballet for typewriters: both the newfangled machines themselves and the women who use them to assault a previously male secretarial preserve. If the play runs out of steam in its second half, it's because its characters are less people in a story than figures in a pattern. Two of them, though, get exceptional performances: from Kelli Fox, again demonstrating her capacity for turning individual grievance into torrential emotional force, and from Sharry Flett as her sister who finds a comparable fervour in shocked shyness. As the veteran suffragette who takes them in and oversees the arousal, educational and sexual, Donna Belleville lacks the eccentric authority Claire Coulter brought to the role, while Jenny Young as her rebellious protegee spells out her part rather than embodying it.

- Serious Money plays through Sept. 12 at the Studio Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake; Age of Arousal at the Court House Theatre (1-800-511-SHAW).
