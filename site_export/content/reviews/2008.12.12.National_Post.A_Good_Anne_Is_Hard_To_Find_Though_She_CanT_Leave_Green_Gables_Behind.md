Arriving in good time to spoil everyone's holidays, Anne of Green Gables: A New Beginning is Kevin Sullivan's latest addition to the saga of the orphan girl with red hair and a funny way of talking. The subtitle derives from a presumption that the Old Beginning -- Lucy Maud Montgomery's -- wasn't good enough. The company responsible calls itself Anne Prequel Productions Inc., proving that second-guessing is now an industry.

You don't have to be a Montgomery fanatic to observe that, while her version was tart and taut, this one is aimless and endless. That sub-title is also a pun. We are here to witness "a new beginning" for the grown-up Anne Shirley of 1945, a successful novelist stricken with writers' block while trying to deliver a play, and consumed with worry for her only son who has yet to return from the war. So she retreats to the old place on P. E. I., where a rummage in the attic reveals things previously unknown about her childhood. It seems that, before coming into the care of the Cuthberts of Green Gables, Anne had done time in both a workhouse and a Great House, experiences that left an inexplicable lack of scars on the girl we met in the books. It seems that her father vanished, after causing her mother's death with his careless driving (of a horse and cart). Anne's path and her dad's occasionally re-crossed in her pre-Gables days, but it's been years since she heard of him and she now becomes obsessed with tracking him down. This gives her the stimulus to finish her play, though it's not clear how. Meantime she goes into prolonged flashback. We alternate between the present Anne and the past one.

The first thing we notice is that, carrot tops apart, they have absolutely nothing in common. Older Anne (Barbara Hershey) is a capable if dull woman who thinks and speaks just like everybody else. The younger one notoriously expresses herself in high-flown phrases drawn from early exposure to 19th-century romantic poetry ("My life," she observes early on, "does not have to be a graveyard of buried hopes") and it's impossible to believe that so compulsive a verbal tic would just disappear, whatever the passage of years. It gets little semi-orphan Annie into endless trouble, and viewers, too, may want to wring her neck: No reflection on the child actress, Hannah Endiott-Douglas, who's doing what's required, but she seems, for so supposedly smart a kid, pathologically incapable of knowing when to shut up.

What I called a workhouse is officially named the Bolingbroke County Poorhouse, and it seems to have been synthesized from the author-director's reading of Oliver Twist, Jane Eyre and The Count of Monte Cristo. Here Anne suffers much persecution, due to her criminal parentage and exotic vocabulary: "God punishes the disobedient," says the matron, adding, as an apparent afterthought, "with eternal damnation." (And Anne thought she was going to get off with standing in a corner.) Delivered, due to the sudden benevolence of authorities who must have had every reason to close the place years ago, Anne finds eventual refuge in the house of the rich and imperious Mrs. Thomas, unexpectedly played by Shirley MacLaine, who after all these years is still one of nature's gamines and ill-suited to imperiousness, or indeed to period drama. Not that it isn't nice to see her again, and she visibly brightens when allowed to deliver one of the script's two funny lines. At first she dispatches Anne to clean the stables, but eventually she takes to her, as one eccentric redhead to another. Mrs. Thomas owns the local lumber mill, and is having serious troubles with banks and unions. Anne gets embroiled in these, and it's all very confusing. Nor, in the eye of eternity or even of basic character development, does it get us anywhere.

There's some constancy in what I suppose we have to call the main plot, in the shape of Anne's summer-stock director (Ron Lea) who's always hanging around as if on the verge of proposing but never quite does. Anyway, no causal connection is ever established between the two timelines. This is a production without style, originality or imagination. It's aimed, obviously, at those who feel there can never be too much Anne, but even they may find that there can. - Anne of Green Gables: A New Beginning airs Sunday at 7 p. m. on CTV.
