THE MAN WHO CAME TO DINNER

Festival Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

- - -

NIAGARA-ON-THE-LAKE - There is a scene in The Man Who Came to Dinner that involves penguins. They arrive in a crate, onstage, and then, offstage, they escape and start waddling about. Everybody loves penguins, and audiences throughout this play's 60-year stage history have felt frustrated at having to hear about them rather than see them. There are, though, worse things than frustration, as is being demonstrated at the Shaw Festival, where the new production does let us see the penguins. They are not, of course and alas, real penguins. They are children dressed up as penguins, and they are not convincing.

They aren't funny, either, in part because they aren't convincing. However, the production, which is extravagantly misguided throughout, stakes a huge amount on the belief that they are. Our first glimpse of one of them, through a glass more or less darkly, is actually rather charming, but then they start appearing in full view, dogging the entrance of nearly every character in an extremely busy play. The theory seems to be that a failed joke becomes a successful joke if it is repeated 87 times. I can't recall the last occasion on which I saw a single gag hammered quite so remorselessly into the ground.

The play's protagonist is Sheridan Whiteside, an American literary and broadcasting personality of the 1930s, whom the authors, Moss Hart and George S. Kaufman, modelled extremely closely on their friend Alexander Woollcott, a critic and pundit who built a national reputation on a disgustingly ornate prose style and a huge capacity for being rude to people.

In the play he is entertainingly rude to almost everybody, but his principal targets are a middle-class family in the fictitious town of Mesalia, Ohio, to whose house he is confined one Christmas after breaking his hip on a patch of ice on their doorstep. He takes over the premises, exchanging cables and international phone calls with the rich and famous, receiving tribute from them (the penguins are a gift from Admiral Byrd), and occasionally welcoming them in person. He insults them, too, but in a fawning, friendly fashion.

The main action concerns Whiteside's unscrupulous attempts to thwart a romance between his secretary, whom he cannot afford to lose, and a local journalist. Later -- much later -- he repents, but this is only a necessity of plot; his championing of the artistic and romantic ambitions of his hosts' son and daughter similarly registers as one more method of spiting their parents.

For practical purposes, Whiteside is a horror, and it's a great indictment of all our moral characters that we enjoy him so thoroughly. At least, it should be. Michael Ball, who plays him here, has an incurably cuddly persona, and when he says that he is a cheery and gracious person by nature, it seems all too believable. He hardly has a bark, let alone a bite, so the play lacks a dynamic.

Still, he is an intelligent and accomplished actor, and it is a pleasure to watch him slump deep into his wheelchair, weighed down by misanthropy and smoking jackets. His shortcomings are, on the whole, the least of this show's problems.

Neil Munro, who directs, was responsible a couple of seasons ago for an enchanting production of the same authors' You Can't Take It with You, a whimsical comedy about a houseful of lovable eccentrics, Conceivably, he is trying to treat this very different play the same way. The Man Who Came to Dinner is a hard-as-nails extravaganza whose obsessively topical humour should have faded but hasn't; its sacred monsters are drawn, with libellous exactness, from life, and their professional craziness needs a realistic background to set it off.

Whiteside's unwilling host is not, as he is played here, a high- pitched maniac; he is an ordinary businessman, driven apoplectic. He has a sister who is a maniac, but her appearances do not need to be accompanied by spooky music; the actress, Mary Haney, can certainly do without it.

Noel Coward turns up, bearing the nom d'etage of Beverly Carlton, and singing his latest composition, a barbed pastiche actually composed by Cole Porter. Patrick R. Brown does a conscientious Coward impression, but conscience, as I have a horrible feeling I've said before, cannot make Cowards of us all, and he doesn't have the necessary heedless audacity. Anyway, his scene is ruined -- actually the whole play is -- by a sudden excursion into fantasy; he leaves the piano, which nevertheless goes on playing, and launches into a full-scale song-and-dance routine. Moments like this, and there are many of them even before we get to the penguins, suggest a director with no sense of humour desperately trying to prove the opposite.

Banjo, a.k.a. Harpo Marx, is more spiritedly done by Simon Bradbury (he's the only actor I've seen who doesn't try to squeeze in bits of Groucho on the side) but he goes on doing his shtick long after it's ceased to be necessary; we know who he's meant to be, and he should be speeding the action up, not slowing it down. (Did I mention the play runs three hours?)

Jane Perry is self-defeatingly effortful as a bitch movie star, but Laurie Paton is excellent as the secretary and Kevin Bundy surprisingly lively as her beau. Nora McLellan atones for her mother in Fanny's First Play with a more composed portrait of a matron not dissimilarly situated, and there are good lightning sketches from Richard Farrell as a doctor, Robert Benson as a radio producer, George Dawson as an entomologist, Al Kozlik as a manservant, and Jillian Cook as a cook.
