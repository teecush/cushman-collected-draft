ANYTHING GOES

Avon Theatre, Stratford

- - -

I don't approve of the Stratford Festival's presenting two big musicals in one season. However, if theyre going to do it, they could hardly have chosen two better shows. I expected Anything Goes, the second, to be a letdown after Guys and Dolls. I was wrong.

The two make an instructive couple, being similarly flavoured products of different theatrical eras. If Guys and Dolls (1950) is the musical-comedy masterpiece, it got that way partly by paying attention to the Rodgers and Hammerstein revolution of the 1940s, with its emphasis on story and character. However, for the kind of world it represents, it bypassed the backwoods primness favoured by R&H and went back to the unreformed metropolitan musicals of the previous two decades, a raffish breed of which Anything Goes (1934), with music and lyrics by Cole Porter, may be the prime example. The gangsters and showgirls and even the missionaries who populate Frank Loessers musical are the staple figures of Porter's. Indeed, the earlier show directly anticipates the later in making one of its central figures a female evangelist. It's typical, though, of Porter that his lady does her soul-saving as part of a cabaret act.

Like Guys and Dolls, Anything Goes is a New York show. Its setting is a luxury liner chugging its way across the Atlantic, but thats a technicality; the passengers and even the crew bring Manhattan with them. Its there in every word they speak, and especially in every note they sing.

It's true those notes don't have the cumulative theatrical effectiveness of the ones in Guys and Dolls. Porter was probably a better composer than Loesser (lyrically, Id say they come out even) but each of his songs has to stand alone; they are prime numbers all right, but they dont add up. You're the Top is exhilaratingly clever but it could be sung by anyone. Adelaide's Lament is laugh- out-loud funny because it could only be sung by Adelaide. Porters Blow, Gabriel, Blow may be a more rousing gospel spoof than Loessers Sit Down, You're Rockin' the Boat but its never going to bring down the house in the same way, however hard the performers work at it; we dont have the same attachment either to the people singing it or to the situation they sing it in.

That's not to say that Anything Goes has a bad book. It has a good one, though it's hard to know whom to credit. Even in 1934, the first script, by P.G. Wodehouse and Guy Bolton, had to be revised, by Howard Lindsay and Russel Crouse. The current text, by Timothy Crouse (son of Russel) and John Weidman, is a further adaptation, prepared for a Lincoln Centre revival in 1987 and since accepted as standard.

The antiquarian in me would like to see the original given a try, but the realist -- who once tried to read it and gave up -- admits we are better off without; its fault is not that its frivolous but that it works its jokes to death. The new version whistles along, winking at the spirit of screwball and, remarkably often, recapturing it.

The basic plot remains the same. Billy Crocker, millionaires runner and bon vivant, has stowed away to woo Hope Harcourt, heiress, who is engaged to Lord Evelyn Oakleigh, Englishman. Billy has two allies: Reno Sweeney, old flame and nightclub evangelist aforesaid; and Moonface Martin, criminal. Moonface, who spends much of the show disguised as a minister, is surprisingly harmless considering that he ranks as Public Enemy No. 13. (In the original he was superstitious and desperate to improve his rating, a gag they should have kept.)

That's all you need to know, except that at one point Billy is mistaken for Public Enemy No. 1, and feted by all the celebrity snobs on board. So you might say the show, unlike Guys and Dolls, has a satiric point to make as well as being a celebration of the permissive society that was trying to surmount or ignore the Depression. The title song is about both these things.

The sound of that tune in the overture, closely followed by a briskly syncopated You're the Top, is enough to raise the spirits, and they stay raised throughout. The original score, which contains five standards, has been augmented by other Porter songs, though I cant help noting that the only one that really works (Easy to Love) was written for the show in the first place and then cut. Porters songs aren't quite as interchangeable as he (or his estate) might like to think. Even if they can't be said to advance the action, they do rise from it with surprising ease -- far more naturally than those in Pal Joey.

Anne Allans staging and Patrick Clarks set are both, in different ways, built of steps, and they show one another off to great effect. The title number, somewhat cramped for space, never reaches the delirious heights of the one in the current, highly praised London version, but in other respects I prefer the Stratford production. It's lighter and wittier, it carries the story better and it is, by and large, better acted.

The major flaw is the casting of the incurably ladylike Cynthia Dale in the powerhouse role of Reno, whose first line, which is also her first joke, depends on her not being a lady and whose subsequent material, both spoken and sung, is fashioned accordingly -- for Ethel Merman, which is probably the best indication of how wrong Dale is. The best of her here, though it still isn't right, is her rangy red-haired visual line. (She looks strikingly like the late and stylish jazz singer Marian Montgomery, who also played this role once upon a time, even less happily.)

Michael Gruber is a smooth if somewhat squat leading man. Understudy Dayna Tekatch was, at the performance I saw, a delightful Hope, if strained by one of her songs. And Jimmy Spadola is about as ebulliently lunar as a Moonface could be. (Well, he might grin a little less.) As Moons moll, Sheila McCarthy sports another terrific profile -- she seems to be chewing gum with every inch of her body -- and is on top of every opportunity shes given; a shame there aren't more.

Douglas Chamberlain earns his passage with the way he says, "Crocker, I didn't know you were Chinese" (its a long story), and as a duo whom we always knew were Chinese, the brothers Jason and Julius Sermonia not only dance like devils -- as do a large percentage of the cast -- but act like them as well.

I doubt the ships captain and purser should look so much as if they've been promoted from the chorus. It should be more of a surprise when they join in the fun. And though it may well be necessary to turn every number into a showstopper (what other identity do they have?), it makes the last couple of songs feel exhausting.

One isn't sorry to see the show end, unlike Guys and Dolls, where one resents the last scene simply for being the last scene. But while it lasts, it's joyful, and that joy is in itself a statement.
