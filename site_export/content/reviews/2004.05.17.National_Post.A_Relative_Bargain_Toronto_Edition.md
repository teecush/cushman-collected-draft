CITY FOR SALE

Cameron House, Toronto

- - -

INVASION FREE SINCE 1812

Tim Sims Playhouse, Toronto

- - -

Two ventures in topical satire, one of them disappointing and the other downright dismal.

VideoCabaret have paused in their cartoon-epic re-enactment of Canadian history to zero in on a slice of life both more recent and more geographically confined. City for Sale, written and directed by Deanne Taylor, is set, say the handouts, "in a lakeside metropolis whose leading citizens have sold out the public trust and public assets." Much of the action is concerned with said citizens' attempts to dispose of a couple of bits of town that have escaped their favourite developers: a patch of waterfront and an island. A bridge and airport are mentioned.

There are, as they say, no prizes. However, one thing that differentiates this show from the Village of the Small Huts history cycle is that those plays name names, real ones. City for Sale is comparatively coy with characters who are, theoretically, imaginary. They include Oscar Price, a media mogul who has the whole town in his hands; Nick Wolfe, a skilled and lascivious lobbyist who has done a lot to put it there; and a Mayor Hamwell, formerly in retail, and bringing to high office a chronic condition of foot-in-mouth and truly horrendous hair.

The VideoCabaret style remains the same as ever: innumerable short scenes, played on a small square box of a stage; harsh, fierce lighting; eight actors making dazzling quick changes between equally dazzling costumes and wigs. (Lights by Andy Moro; togs by Shadowland.) But the structure, and the roguish pretence of fiction do not go well together. Taking at least some responsibility for her characters, Taylor has supplied them with elaborate plots and counterplots; in fact, she has written a play rather than the usual VideoCab revue. A lot of it concerns Nick Wolfes love life, if love is the word; he has three influential or ambitious women on a string, which customarily contributes to his success, and eventually proves his downfall. It only takes one worm to turn.

I dont know how much of this is true but, though I'm always willing to believe the worst of people much richer than I am, the sex here gets in the way of the politics, and indeed of the satire. I wouldn't say that VideoCabaret have gone soft and opted for, horror of horrors, human interest, but they are asking us to take a certain interest in their characters feelings while denying us any sustained acquaintance with them. Of course they try to be as heartless as possible, but the cuteness of the concept has dulled their edge.

Theres something else that undoes them. This story has a happy ending. The good guys win. It isn't in VideoCabaret's nature to admit that there are good guys, and they go through some of the motions of defaming their own side: the chorus of islanders (John Alcorn has written them a couple of neat songs) who stand up against the forces of Price Media and its cynically branded Greenworld project are a bunch of floppy idealists, and probably vegans; Mac Mackenzie, the left-wing councillor who wins as mayor when scandal catches up with his opponents, has to get into bed -- metaphorically, you understand -- with some dubious fellows. But it looks like window dressing. Rather more effectively, the forces of greed are allowed to assert, calmly, that though theyve lost the battle, they hasnt lost the war; all politicians may not be the same but all politics are. The play has one deathless plutocratic line: I like to do business with governments; they own all the best stuff.

Seven members of the hard-working, hoop-jumping cast are absolutely fine: Janet Burke, Patrick Conner, Beverley Cooper, Greg Campbell, Shari Hollett, Ruth Madoc-Jones and Stephen Sparks. The eighth, Anand Rajaram, verges at times on the sublime; it would be tempting to say that as Mayor Hamwell he hams well, but in fact he is gentle and lost, and both speaks and looks the very poetry of haplessness.

Second City is never likely to betray its heritage and go in for storytelling. Invasion Free Since 1812, the troupe's "55th Sketch Comedy Revue," has at any rate the series' best title in recent memory. It suggests that the show will be more sardonically political than usual, and the director, Bob Martin, says himself that the show will be a little louder and a little angrier than its predecessors. Alas, the best title hides the worst show.

Being Canadian here means, as it usually does, having an attitude about Americans. You wouldnt think that playing that for comedy, now, would be all that hard. The shows polemical set piece gives us a couple of rabble-rousing young Republicans (and they do look very good in their bow ties and red suspenders) blaming every one of the world's evils, and every one of their government's policies, on Osama bin Laden. The trouble is, this isnt just their punchline, its the show's; the writers' thinking is every bit as lazy as that of the people theyre lampooning. (And like them, theyre relying on their audience agreeing with them before they've started.) The number touches, heavily, of course on Iraq; but there is only one line about Abu Ghraib, the scandal that, whatever your thinking, has changed the whole profile of the war. These are supposed to be improv comedians, able to adapt to breaking news rather than go with what they had in rehearsal.

The show also digs into more distant U.S. history for a poison- quill exchange between Thomas Jefferson and Benjamin Franklin that may mark a new low in pointlessness. It says nothing about them or us. (You want to lock the performers in a room with the CD of Stan Freberg's History of America, so they may learn how these things should be done.) Its matched by the very next number, the free- association ramblings of an old woman in church. I have sometimes lamented Second Citys inability to think up punchlines, but here, where what has gone before is so feeble, you hardly notice.

There is some better material, though the bad stuff tends to crowd it out. There is an endearing running gag about Paul Martin opening up the first marijuana counter at Shoppers (an experience that takes him, in succeeding scenes, back to the '60s), and a sharp one involving Paul Constable as a radio DJ whose station format keeps changing while his patter remains the same. There are some sketches about marriage that may make you wince as well as laugh -- in fact, wince rather than laugh. There's one about a gay man applying for maternity leave, from a boss who has a virtuoso line in office psychobabble-with-gestures; Naomi Snieckus hand-jives (and word-jives) her way through this splendidly. A tribute to Rochester ("the Winnipeg of America," and did I also hear it referred to as the "home of Jack Benny"?) makes a delightful closer. The performers -- the others are Sandy Jobin-Beavans, Jamillah Ross and Matt Baram (a cross between Woody Allen and Harpo Marx) -- are fine. They are also the writers, however, so one's congratulations have to be muted.
