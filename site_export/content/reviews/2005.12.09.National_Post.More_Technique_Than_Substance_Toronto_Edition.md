JIMMY

Buddies in Bad Times Theatre,

Toronto

- - -

Playwrights have been saying for centuries that life's a dream, but few have been as earnest about it as Marie Brassard, author, director and sole performer of Jimmy. Jimmy is a self-described "homosexual hairdresser" who came into being 50 years ago as a thing in someone's dream. The person who dreamed him was a U.S. army general who was due to ship out to Korea the next day but never made it on account of he woke up dead.

Jimmy, whose imaginary existence had been cut off at a moment of unconsummated passion, was forced to spend the next half-century in whatever limbo unemployed dream-figures inhabit, waiting to be dreamed up by somebody else. Eventually somebody obliged, that somebody being an actress.

I've heard this story before, or one very like it. I'm thinking of Pirandello's Six Characters in Search of an Author, in which figures from an abandoned play wander into a theatre and ask the director and his troupe to put them out of their agony by completing their drama.

The difference is that Pirandello's play really is about the theatre; it makes some true, if obvious, points about the ways in which life's raw material is inevitably distorted and simplified when it gets into the hands of a playwright (and let's not even talk about actors).

Brassard's is not really about dreams; it captures the textures and rhythms of dreaming, but its central device is not so much playful as whimsical. We may well invent people in our dreams, but those people don't then hang about and crop up, with full biographies attached, in the dreams of others we haven't even met.

It all seems to be a pretext for a play about love, one that's periodically poignant but overwhelmingly sentimental. In the general's dream Jimmy falls in reciprocated love with a young soldier -- chances are the general identified with the soldier and died of shame at the thought.

The actress wants to make Jimmy over as her lover, and he naturally recoils, quite graphically. Eventually, Jimmy is reunited with his G.I. courtesy, I guess, of the suddenly generous actress if she's still the one doing the dreaming. Moral: Happiness is only possible in sleep, and at that somebody else has to do it for you.

Remember that an actress is actually playing Jimmy. Here we move from text to performance, which is on a higher plane. Literally: The whole show unfolds on a ledge perched in the right-hand corner of the stage.

Our first sight is of Brassard, inscrutably androgynous, with her bare back toward us and a braid of thick black hair running down it. There's a mirror behind her and a screen beside her, both supplying reflections and refractions.

When she turns toward us she's Jimmy, in a bulging suit with a flapping shirt, a loose-hung tie and a smartly folded pocket handkerchief. Later Jimmy will use this napkin to wipe himself after masturbating, having been aroused at the thought of a fish. (He again had his back turned, but he will still apologize for having done such a thing in front of us. If the actress is still an actress, the audience is still an audience. Keep up.)

When Brassard speaks, it's with the voice of Jimmy, which comes to us, through a distorting mike, as a cracked baritone that bears little resemblance to the sound of a homosexual hairdresser or of any other human being, but is undeniably striking.

Later she adds the voice of the actress, then alternates between them; and so quick are the transitions that at moments you would swear that she is speaking with two tongues at once. It becomes apparent that Brassard is not only playing the actress, she is that actress; which may explain, if not legitimize, some bewilderingly irrelevant interludes about her relationship with her mother.

The identification is clinched when, suddenly, the technology fails and Brassard apologizes to us in her own unmediated voice. It's a startling moment, as such breaks in convention always are, and you even wonder whether it might not be genuine. In fact it's woven so closely into the text that it had to be planned. (Maybe that's why the audience was kept waiting for ages before being allowed into the theatre; we were being softened up for the idea of technical glitches.)

So this is really another play about the theatre and like most such, more notable for technique than for substance. In a funny way, though, it backfires: Jimmy emerges as an interesting and affecting character, Brassard doesn't. Whether that constitutes a victory or a defeat, I wouldn't presume to judge.
