SOUTH PACIFIC

Avon Theatre, Stratford

- - -

I find that, for a musical I don't much like, I like South Pacific quite a lot. From a distance, when Im listening to a cast album or remembering the movie or contemplating individual songs, it strikes me as the stodgiest and least amusing of the big four Rodgers and Hammerstein shows. But on the rare occasions when I get to see it on the stage, I am conquered by its craft and awed by its self- belief. Oscar Hammerstein and Joshua Logan, his collaborator on the book, welded three James Michener short stories into a seamless narrative about sailors and civilians waiting for their chapter of the Second World War to get started, and it sweeps you along. Richard Rodgers wrote his most ostentatiously romantic score - - every number is some kind of love song, or at least some kind of sex song -- and it wraps you up in the tapestry.

The shows particular glow was best described by the director and critic Harold Clurman in his review of the original 1949 production; he wrote that when Mary Martin as Nurse Nellie Forbush sang that she was in love with a wonderful guy, one murmured not "who cares?" but congratulations, congratulations, congratulations, to you both. Cynthia Dale, whose Nellie is the best thing she has done at Stratford, induces a similar response, getting right inside the poundingly happy tune without forcing a note. Nellie's role is unusually constructed, in that her two biggest numbers come practically back to back; A Wonderful Guy appears hot on the heels of the equally delightful I'm Gonna Wash That Man Right Outta My Hair, and acts as a counterweight to it. First comes the heroines declaration of independence to her friends in the showers; then, after shes dried off, her ecstatic recantation. We go on a small journey with her, and neither stage of it would work half as well without the other.

That trip is itself part of a longer one that Nellie takes in a show that's also a simplistic but powerful parable about culture shock and tolerance. Dale's role in last years Cat on a Hot Tin Roof may have been a stretch for her, but it bequeathed to her a pert Southern accent that sits beautifully on this musicals little girl from Little Rock, floored by the revelation that her French lover was formerly married to a Polynesian -- and has two small children to prove it. Nellie gets to sing other charming songs, like the sweetly raucous Honey Bun and the surprisingly tricky Cockeyed Optimist, but the emotional peak of Dale's performance isn't musical at all. It comes when she meets Liat, the bewildered native sweetheart of Joe Cable, the young lieutenant who has gone to his death with his "carefully taught" racism still unresolved, and takes the heartbroken girl in her arms. Its the moment in which Nellies own dam of prejudice finally bursts. In this production it's rather clumsily staged, but my throat still lumped.

If Nellie's numbers find Rogers and Hammerstein at their sprightliest, the solos for Emile de Becque, her middle-aged Gallic flame, find the team dusting off their funeral attire; the lament This Nearly Was Mine is stately, and the supposedly joyous Some Enchanted Evening positively elephantine. Out of context these songs bore me, but within it they work. Theodore Baerg sings Emile excellently, and if his acting tends to the stolid, its never offensively so. Laird Mackintosh, as Joe, also sings better than he talks, but since his big song is Younger than Springtime, which sits beautifully in the middle of the shows great divide, that's all right, too. Grace Chan is a good Bloody Mary, though she never reconciles the character's twin aspects of heartless bawd and hearty comic relief. Ive seen this musical described in reputable print as the teams funniest, but I find that baffling; the show's comedy is formulaic. It's a tribute to Bruce Dow that he finds as much in the role of Luther Billis, a Sergeant Bilko with a soft centre, as he does. Douglas Paraschuk supplies bamboo sets that are evocative, though less of the actual South Pacific than of the Hawaiian room at a theme hotel.

Both of this years Stratford musicals have been staged by director-choreographers, though neither show had even a choreographer-choreographer in its credits when it was new. Michael Lichtefeld keeps the action of South Pacific crisp and the steps plausible; when he stages jokey routines for the second act camp concert, they are such things a cast of imaginative amateurs might conceivably come up with. Did I say "camp" concert? When the male chorus sing Theres Nothin' Like a Dame, it's hard to take their itchy frustration seriously; after all, theyve got each other. Theres a larger problem, though not unrelated; except for a few moments of urgency from Brian McKay's tetchy Captain Brackett, there is little sense that there's a war on, a phoney war until half-way through Act Two, but still a war. Some film footage of planes taking off is too meagre, too late. Admittedly the writers dont give much to go on; admittedly, too, a recent London production that tried hard to fill in the gaps seemed, by most accounts, merely to be trying hard. There are one or two lines -- about the doubtful chances of war bringing a better world -- that ring uncomfortable bells, but theyre quickly forgotten. The show is a product of post- war confidence, though not of post-war complacency, and that is connected with a confidence about how to write musicals. It makes love better than it makes war, and its love story is honestly moving. In its time South Pacific was considered a daring show. It isn't that now, but it's still surprising.
