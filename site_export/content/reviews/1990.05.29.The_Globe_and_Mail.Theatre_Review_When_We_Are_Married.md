THEATRE REVIEW When We Are Married

Tuesday, May 29, 1990

ROBERT CUSHMAN

NIAGARA-ON-THE-LAKE, Ont. -- y J. B. Priestley Directed by Tony van Bridge Starring George Dawson, Irene Hogan, Jack Medley and Tony van Bridge At the Shaw Festival With Priestley, the meek inherit the mirth BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail NIAGARA-ON-THE-LAKE, Ont. Putting down the mighty while exalting the humble and meek is a venerable tradition: admirable in theory and a neat if extremely uncommon trick in practice. It also makes very good theatre, and has done at least since the Middle Ages, when it was given ritual form. They called it the Feast of Fools then, and it was the occasion for the lord of the manor to step down from his seat and minister to his meanest dependents. Mind you, it was only allowed to happen once a year.

In subsequent centuries, it developed into a classic formula: the Comedy of the Turning Worm. J. B. Priestley stumbled on it in 1938, when he wrote When We Are Married, and in so doing created his most enduring play. Its appearance at the Royal George Theatre on Saturday brought the Shaw Festivals opening week to a fittingly festive finish.

The play is literally festive. Set in West Yorkshire in the prosperous days of 1908, it depicts the silver wedding celebration of three prominent couples, who have grown up together and were all married at the same ceremony.

The men, in particular, are much on their dignity and well aware of hierarchy: Alderman Joseph Helliwell, Councillor Albert Parker, Mr. Herbert Soppitt. They are now informed that there has been a terrible 25- year-old mistake, and that they were never legally spliced at all.

This doesn't half put the cat among the pigeons. Or rather, it temporarily de-claws the cats while arousing the pigeons to the possibilities of independent flight. Among the six parties affected, two are pompous, two are rather sweet and two are downright horrible. The sweeties, of course, have been matrimonially shackled to the horrors. Aroused by alcohol and freedom, they begin to assert themselves.

In Britain, the play has always been a favorite: at worst an amiable repertory romp, at best a rich, sly human comedy. Tony van Bridge's production, which clocks in somewhere between these extremes, is most dignified by his own performance. Van Bridge plays Henry Ormonroyd, a photographer from the Yorkshire Argus, an old man so resigned to his own habitual drunkenness that when somebody falsely tells him he has come to the wrong house, he turns unprotesting on his way as if it were just what he had expected. He acts as raisonneur, lecturing the estranged couples on the pitfalls and consolations of marriage, and also as deus ex machina; it is he who resolves their problems by the kind of outrageous coincidence that we accept as being not only necessary but uplifting. Van Bridge shares some cherishable scenes with an uncannily pert teen-age domestic played by Sherry Smith. She lacks his seasoned timing but, with her mob- cap permanently over her eyes, she too conveys the sense of a life going its own unalterable way.

Other performances vary from the proficient to the rowdily amateur. After all, the discovery that three couples are not actually married is in itself pretty tame, as is the further discovery that they actually are. What counts is how they wriggle in between, and to do this to maximum effect they have to be rounded and real. In this production, they are superficial: not caricatures, but types. Irene Hogans uncompromising harridan does best in this vein, with Jack Medleys spongy alderman a fair likeness of your average complacently sensual man. Graham Harley's councillor is a hand-wringing Dickensian hypocrite, effective enough until his partner (Barbara Worthy) tells him what he is. There is more craft and humanity in this scene than either player shows us. George Dawson takes the Waking Up of Herbert Soppitt in one great gulp; it might be more delightful as a series of exploratory sips.

To vary the marital pattern Priestley brings on a mature good-time girl, adorned here with the largest, most extravagantly ribboned and pom- pommed hat to appear on a stage since the last chorus girl paraded out of the Gay Nineties. Mary Haney, underneath it, sounds uncertain which end of England she has wandered in from.

The set makes the stage look as easy and comfortable as in Night Must Fall it seemed crabbed and constricting; perplexingly the same man, John Dinning, designed both.

The title song that the actors warble in improbable unison as the play ends cheapens the evening; the endless recorded reprise that chases us out of the theatre nearly ruins it. The crazy thing is that Priestley took his title from an actual parlor ballad of the period; if they had to sing, they could have sung that. Anyway the real music is in the play's dialogue, which, like its cunning construction, seems artless while coming closer to real theatrical poetry than its author ever achieved when obviously trying.
