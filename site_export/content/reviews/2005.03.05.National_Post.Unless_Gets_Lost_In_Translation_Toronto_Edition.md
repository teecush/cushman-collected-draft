Most of Unless gets in the way. I am referring to the new play at the Bluma Appel, not to the Carol Shields novel on which it is based and which I haven't read. In a novel, strands of story and groups of characters can be laid out in parallel, so to speak, and can make a satisfying pattern. In a play, you want to know what the main narrative is, and how everything else contributes to it. This applies even to Chekhov, whose symphonic structures are built around what are essentially thriller plots: Will they save the cherry orchard? How many excuses can they come up with for not going to Moscow? Whom will Uncle Vanya shoot and whom will he miss? When lesser dramatists try the same kind of juggling act, they are doomed. Most of what's on stage at the Bluma seems to be there purely because it was once on the page somewhere else.

The adaptation of Unless was begun by Shields herself, and completed after her death by her daughter Sara Cassidy. It is, fittingly enough, about a mother and daughter -- at least, that's the only part of the play that grabs you. There is Reta Winters, a fairly successful writer of what she calls "light fiction," who lives with her family about an hour outside Toronto. And there is Norah, the eldest of her three girls and a university student. Without warning, Norah drops out and joins the homeless. Reta, naturally, is devastated.

Reta, at her first appearance, speaks pregnantly of undergoing the "useful monotony" of happiness. This, it seems, is not enough for Norah, who takes up daily residence on a downtown street corner, wearing a sign inscribed with the single word "Goodness." It's an intriguing situation, with built-in emotional pull. We feel sorry for Reta and curious about Norah. But our questions remain unanswered; we learn virtually nothing about Norah, even in the rare scenes in which she actually appears.

Mostly we watch Reta accommodating other things and other people. The play may be telling us a harsh truth: that, even for a virtually bereaved parent, life goes on. But it feels more like padding. We witness Reta at work on her new book -- scenes and characters take shape before our eyes. We hear her accept compliments from her new agent (in New York, yet) and see her fend off his attempts to rewrite her. She deals, in turn, with an inept interviewer, a kaffeeklatsch of friends, her other children, who are given very little dramatic presence, and her doctor husband, who has almost none.

She also writes letters to editors, complaining about the persistent marginalization of women. And somewhere in all this we are presumably meant to piece together the sources of her daughter's desperation. The mother observes and expresses; the daughter internalizes and explodes.

But a character has to exist in her own right before she can illuminate something or somebody else, and Reta doesn't. Her scenes with other people are just sketches, overlong and under-amusing. Most of the time, though, she addresses us directly, which is even more enervating.

The title is a literary conceit about the significance of seemingly innocuous words, conjunctions and such. In a similar way, that old novelistic touchstone, the authorial consciousness, has been transported to the theatre unmediated, and has, as usual, wilted there. Nicola Cavendish, the actress charged with embodying it, is capable but no miracle worker.

Roy Surette's production itself enjoys switching between levels: upper and lower, darker and lighter, illusion and reality. The overall flavour, characteristic of new plays at this address, is of CanStage doing CanLit. (Such shows tend to come from somewhere else. This one is a co-production with the Arts Club company of Vancouver.)

It seems somehow typical that the most ambitious dialogue sequence is also the most frustrating. It involves Reta's mother-in- law, previously incapable of making conversation, suddenly finding her voice when left alone with the agent. The staging frantically signals that it's meant to be funny and touching, but one just wants it to stop, since, somewhere else, the story of Reta and Norah, which is all we're interested in, is finally showing signs of coming to a head. Celine Stubel brings some intensity to Norah. Phillip Clarkson's costumes are excellent; they have more colour than anything else in the show.

Norah's chosen piece of pavement is at Bloor and Bathurst, which places her kitty-corner from the Bathurst Street Theatre, the home of Bat Boy: The Musical, at which she might have a slightly better time, as did I. This off-Broadway import derives from stories published in the Weekly World News about a creature found in a cave in West Virginia who was indeed half bat and half boy. (I only know what I'm told.) If, as seems likely, the WWN is the campiest of tabloids, then it stands to what you might call reason that its creepiest scoop was just asking to be turned into a spoof musical. I'm not sure anyone else was asking. Just about every musical you see today is a spoof musical, and they aren't all The Producers or Urinetown, not by a long shot. Those shows have internal consistency: The Producers is about the theatre, and Urinetown is a sustained riff on a certain theatrical tradition. (It's a satire on satires, something that shouldn't work but does.)

Bat Boy, by contrast, just lurches. It starts with a chorale intoning "hold me, Bat Boy, touch me, Bat Boy," an amusing take on all the sickly Messianic musicals from Jesus Christ Superstar to -- the real prototype -- Tommy. By the end, when the Bat in Bat Boy has triumphed over the Boy and he's reverted to sucking blood, including his girlfriend's, in quite a big way, the ambience is more that of The Phantom of the Opera, with appropriate crashing chords in the score. Somewhere en route, Bat Boy has been taken in by a local family and taught to speak BBC English, with the help of BBC tapes.

This is so we can be reminded of My Fair Lady, though the relevant song seems to have strayed out of some other, and thoroughly undistinguished, musical altogether.

The low point, of the writing and particularly of the staging, comes when the lovers take to the woods and are surrounded by papier mache animals, as if that in itself constituted a parody of The Lion King, or indeed as if a parody of The Lion King were self-evidently a good thing.

For the most part, Laurence O'Keefe's music and (especially) his lyrics are neat, if inevitably cloying. He does seem to like the musical as a form. The book writers (Keythe Farley and Brian Flemming) give the impression that they despise it but would quite like to cash in on it. They are cynically irresponsible -- when all else fails, they hint that they're being serious about the way we treat outsiders. (An evening of parody is difficult to sustain, especially if you neither like nor understand your target.)

Bat Boy's adoptive family are turned into arbitrary monsters, especially dad, who is a veterinarian, and our second underwritten medical man this week. I wondered if he might be suffering from some kind of post-Vietnam, or maybe post-Gulf trauma. That could make him two kinds of vet. The surrounding gap-toothed gap-brained rustics don't pass muster even as caricature.

Michael McGinn's production is hearty (not to mention overmiked) rather than inventive. The cast all pitch enthusiastically in, with Jayne Lewis and Laura Caswell doing especially well as -- again -- mother and daughter. Jay T. Schramek swoops and flutters well in the title role, but he can't help sounding silly -- not wrong, just silly -- when he does the accent.

The Bathurst Street was formerly one of my Bad Karma Theatres, but for now I think I'd give it a suspended sentence.
