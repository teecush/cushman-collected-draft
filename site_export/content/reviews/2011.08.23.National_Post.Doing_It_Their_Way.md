THEATRE REVIEW

Come Fly Away

Four Seasons Centre for the Performing Arts, Toronto

Frank Sinatra never sang the words "come fly away." He did of course sing the words "come fly with me," at the beginning of the song of that name, closely followed by "come fly, let's fly away." But "come fly away," no. Which makes it odd that Twyla Tharp should have made that the title of her dance piece set to Sinatra songs. Maybe there's some arcane copyright reason. It's even odder in that Come Fly with Me itself is not one of the show's chosen songs. It points to a certain spirit of approximation, or compromise, or opportunism that does in fact permeate the entire entertainment. It's as if - excuse me while I mix some metaphors - the show were trying to hang itself from Sinatra's hatband without having earned hanging rights.

Fourteen dancers, comprising eight principals and an ensemble, perform Tharp's choreography to 24 Sinatra recordings, plus three instrumental ringers. The vocals have been surgically detached from their orchestral accompaniments; instead, and I guess preferably, a live band plays the original arrangements. The dancers and musicians are all excellent, and the singing, obviously, is magnificent. (Well, most of it is. I may be the only Sinatra-phile who has never much cared for his version of The Way You Look Tonight. And it's my favourite song.) It's Tharp's own contribution that's the letdown. Her staging amounts to 80 minutes of second-best Broadway.

She's also credited with "concept and book." The former may be the idea of setting dances to the songs. The latter is a bemusing term for a show with no spoken words, but perhaps it refers to the story, which is paper-thin. Four couples meet in a bar, and fall in and out of love or lust for the duration, their moods theoretically reflecting, or dictated by, whatever music happens to be playing. As a prologue we have Sinatra singing the verse to Stardust, setting a nostalgic bittersweet mood to which the show never returns. (There's no matching epilogue.)

Then the full lights go up and the company assembles in the watering hole, for a generic athletic opening number to the strains of Luck Be a Lady. That song, you may remember, was originally about gambling. There's no hint of that here; here, insofar as it's about anything beyond being an opening number, it's about getting lucky with a lady. Which, considered on its own, may be a legitimate stretch. It seems a shame, though, to take one of the few songs of the evening that isn't some kind of love song and turn it into one.

And that really is the problem, and it may explain why Tharp's work here is so far below the level of her previous dance-musical, Movin' Out. The musical material there, Billy Joel songs, was far less distinguished, but it did have wider-reaching subject matter; it gave her something to dramatize. Sinatra, most of the time, just sang love songs: happy love songs and sad love songs. He found marvellous distinguishing nuances in their lyrics, but it's hard to provide physical equivalents for them. The difference here between the subtlety of the sounds and the blatancy of the moves can be painful. Actually, it's worse than that.

I said that Sinatra sang sad love songs; and in fact, invigorating though his up-tempos can be, it was in the heartbreak ballads, sometimes called "suicide songs," that he did his greatest work. Come Fly Away includes just one of them, the iconic One for My Baby. Well, how do you illustrate that? Not the song itself - it started life, after all, as a driven specialty for Fred Astaire - but Sinatra's broken-voiced assumption of the solitary drinker, pouring his heart out to an unresponsive bartender. Tharp's solution is to have one of her couples do a slo-mo recap of the happier times the singer is presumably recalling. Meanwhile, on the other side of the stage, a waiter clears up for the night. The latter touch is appropriate and effective. All the same, it's tempting just to close your eyes and listen.

It may be telling - or maybe it's just carelessness - that some of the brighter Sinatra numbers, Witchcraft or You Make Me Feel So Young, are heard not in the singer's definitive 1950s performances but in his more blatant, less delicate remakes from the following decade. There is nothing spooky or teasing about the dance to Witchcraft; it's just another strenuously suggestive duo, in sub-Bob Fosse style. Humour is out, too; Sinatra's and Neal Hefti's Pick Yourself Up, with its cheeky delayed ending, loses its impact by being cut in half. Numbers such as Yes, Sir, That's My Baby and Let's Face the Music and Dance have little relation to what their lyrics are saying and less to how they're saying it. Even eroticism can pall.

That's hardly the performers' fault. Ashley Blair Fitzgerald, in particular, is endlessly sexy with gorgeous auburn hair, a body that seems to thrive on being thrown about Apache-style, and limber legs that wrap themselves provocatively, and often, around her partner. On Fly Me to the Moon, she really does seem about to take off into space. She and Anthony Burrell also shine on That's Life, not one of the Voice's finest hours but one in which Tharp finds genuine punchy inspiration. The corps en masse are also well served on Here's to the Losers, with its witty Marty Paich arrangement that keeps promising to turn into I Wish I Were in Love Again. (Now there's a song they might have included.) It's significant though that the strongest, most inventive dances are done to the non-vocal tracks, and that the best of them is to Dave Brubeck's Take Five, a song with no Sinatra connections whatever.

As if to compensate, the evening ends with the Chairman's two late-life hits, My Way, done at grim full length though it's a song loathed by every Sinatra fan I've ever known and also apparently by the man himself, and New York New York. These are presumably thrown in for easy identification as neither of them has any relevance to the story the show has purported to be telling. At the very end, courtesy of Donald Holder's fine lighting design, an outline of Sinatra's face appears in the empyrean, as if the show has been about him. It hasn't.
