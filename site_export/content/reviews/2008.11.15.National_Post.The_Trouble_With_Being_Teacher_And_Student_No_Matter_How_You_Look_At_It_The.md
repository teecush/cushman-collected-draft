The title character of Bashir Lazhar is a substitute teacher. He's standing in for at least two people, both of them women. One is his immediate predecessor in a Montreal classroom, who met a violent end by which her Grade 6 students are still, as both he and we assume, traumatized. The other is the wife whom he, a refugee, left behind with their own children in Algeria. She was a teacher, while he worked in a cafe. Her influence on him, his inheritance from her, are among several things that this 75-minute one-man play leaves unexplored or undeveloped. But what we do have is written with such sympathy and charm, and performed with such grace and power, that even our frustration rebounds to the plays credit. Were involved.

Evelyne de la Cheneliere's play begins with Bashir practising his first words to his class, trying out introductions stern, jovial, friendly or outright ingratiating. Just telling them his name is a challenge. Finally, he just chalks it up on the board and also, you feel, chalks it up to experience.

Under beautifully modulated direction from Jennifer Tarver, Raoul Bhaneja conveys the paradox of a man who feels himself in some sense a fraud but also realizes that he is in the one situation he was born for. The air of confidence he wears in class ceases to be a mask; talking to these children he knows who he is, even if his expectations of them seem unrealistically high. ("I'm going to read you a paragraph from Balzacs The Wild Asss Skin-- I'm sure you all know it.) But he wins them over, one of them at least, a girl who delivers an outstanding assignment on the meaning of violence, a subject that resonates on both sides of the desk. One of the outstanding achievements of the performance and the production is their ability to conjure up the presence of unseen characters; watching Bhaneja, you really feel as if you are in that classroom.

You are almost equally convinced by his dealings with the refugee board, to whom he presents a case beyond tears and soaked in angry irony, and with an increasingly unsympathetic (and, one imagines, stony-faced) school principal. The more he arouses his pupils' feelings, and his own, the more suspicion he arouses in staff and parents. He reaches out, clumsily, to a female colleague who he momentarily thought was reaching out to him. He chides his classs aspirations to study Spanish, suggesting that before learning a second language they should master their first. But he himself is teaching in, and therefore, of necessity, learning, a second language, and this gives a formality, and an extra layer of meaning, to everything he says.

The plays time-scheme isn't altogether clear. Some of it clearly flashes back to before the start of Bashir's teaching career, but its hard to work out how much. Are his worst stresses past or present? We have a character whos totally engaging from moment to moment, but in a cloudy context. Even the vaunted theme of violence and the response to it hangs somewhat in the air, undefined. But Bhaneja's body language -- the way he can suddenly spin around; the way he moves a chair in the principal's office, pretending to an assurance he cannot feel -- is as meaningful as his words. These can sometimes be very funny, intentionally or otherwise. Jokes," he sternly tells his class, "arent an excuse to stop working; and in Morwyn Brebners first-rate translation -- her second this year, following her sublime version of The President at the Shaw Festival -- they do a lot of work. The play is also a moving essay in what may be the most moving of themes: the potential, so often wasted or abused, for joy in childhood. This play centres on, and is performed by, a man, but its author, translator and director are all women. I don't know how much to read into that, but in addition to carrying an unbearable personal burden, Bashir can never shake the feeling of being a substitute's substitute. Hearing at the end of the play of another, female, teacher, he remarks with a kind of crushed approval that shell be maternal." Theres a lot going on in this play, not all of it realized, but the theme of what children and their various guardians need and deserve comes through beautifully.
