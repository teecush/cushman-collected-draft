Every production of Hay Fever has to have a staircase. Much of the action in Noel Coward's play about the worst weekend in the history of country house comedy consists of guests and residents going back and forth between the sleeping quarters upstairs and the eating and playing quarters at ground level. On the set that Douglas Paraschuk has designed for the new Stratford production, one of the stairs is missing. Or maybe it's just rotten; from a seat in the stalls, it's impossible to tell. We do, though, get a very good view of the characters negotiating this obstacle. The Bliss family, whose summer place it is, hop daintily or swing insouciantly over it. Their visitors are less knowledgeable, less adroit, and loaded down with suitcases. Naturally they keep tripping. And they never learn. Alisa Palmer's production has locked itself into a single running gag that isn't too hilarious to begin with and gets more relentlessly unfunny with each repetition. If it were cut, though, there might not be any production left.

Hay Fever is famously short on plot but what it has is strong, simple and symmetrical. Judith Bliss, star of the 1920s London stage, currently in rustic retirement, has invited an admirer down for the weekend. Similar invitations have been issued by her husband, David, who writes terrible romantic novels, and by their children Sorel and Simon. None of them has bothered to inform the others about this until immediately before their guests' arrival. When they do arrive, they are first ignored and then humiliated, forced to participate in charade-like games for which they possess neither aptitude nor interest. Games over, the players pair off for flirting purposes, the joke being that none of the hosts is partnered with his or her original intended - though how serious the intentions may ever have been is very much open to question.

Judith, who made her name in plays with titles like Love's Whirlwind, acts off-stage in

much the same way she used to act on, and the other Blisses can hardly help playing along. Come the end of the evening, their victims are flabbergasted to find that they have become engaged to a younger Bliss or have precipitated the divorce of an elder. By next morning, of course, the family have forgotten all about it - indeed seem unaware that they ever had any visitors at all. The civilians, cowed and sleepless (their rooms were uniformly horrible), make as speedy an exit as they can manage, thereby provoking the Blisses to deplore the extraordinary way in which some people behave.

Coward himself remarked on Hay Fever's popularity with amateur groups: "It has a small cast and only one set which must lead them, poor dears, to imagine that it is easy to act." He added that, since this "species of delusion" was universal among amateurs, he had no intention of disabusing them except to suggest, mildly, that "from the professional point of view, Hay Fever is one of the most difficult plays to perform that I have ever encountered." The Stratford program quotes that last sentence and proceeds, bizarrely, to theorize that the difficulty is a philosophical one, born of the play's "utter commitment to unconventionality." Actually half the characters are thoroughly conventional; that's where the fun is.

Coward's point, which he went on to make perfectly clear, was a purely technical one; the comedy, short on action, depends on behaviour: on the delivery, feather-light and rapier-sharp, of lines that look innocuous on the page but can be devastating in context, and on an overall rhythm that should bind the cast together, even while the people they're playing are locked in mutual incomprehension. It's a play for an ensemble, an ensemble of specialists.

There's another weird statement in the program, this one from the director herself, who says that the play is really about mothers and daughters. In her actual production, though, the relationship of Lucy Peacock (Judith) and Ruby Joy (Sorel) bulks no larger than any of the others in the criss-cross, and it's hard to see how it could.

What we actually get is a disconnected set of performances that never build into a show. Peacock, technically expert, covers all Judith's selfindulgent bases without quite taking possession of them - or, perhaps, letting them possess her; her natural quality is earthy rather than flighty. (It's hard to believe she would need to practise the names of the flowers in her garden.) Her husband, normally and I think correctly played as tweedily absent-minded, comes over in Kevin Bundy's performance as a fretful young man with a beard stuck on. Tyrone Savage's Simon starts off as a painfully exaggerated idea of a male Bright Young Thing, but relaxes and improves thereafter.

In general, the flies in the web come off better than the spiders. That, to be fair, is how they're written, though you wouldn't know it from Cynthia Dale's ineffectual stab at the vamp Myra Arundel, the only one who really fights back. The others are endearingly relatable, as they grapple with unthinkable things like the possibility of the Bliss family not serving tea.

Gareth Potter is splendid as the young boxer who finds himself going separate rounds with each of the ladies of the house; Sanjay Talwar is even better, pretty much perfect in fact, as the diplomatic diplomat who here seems to have taken the English garb of the English sahib, and internalized it. At least I think that's the idea; with non-traditional casting, you can never be sure whether or not you're supposed to notice. His initial scene with Ijeoma Emesowum's terrified flapper, the two of them making the smallest of talk while wondering whether they've come to the right place, is the best in the play, as Coward himself realized. Even here, though, the lines are drawn less tight than they should be. Sarah Orenstein's chain-smoking dresser turned maid is amusingly surly until the production either pushes her over the top or stops her from going there. The set is an applause-getter and the costumes are swank. But the overall laughter-level is painfully low. The comedy sinks like a fleet of lead balloons.
