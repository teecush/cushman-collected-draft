Tim Carroll, the newly named artistic director of the Shaw Festival, has never directed a play by George Bernard Shaw. This may make him seem an unlikely choice for his new post, which he takes up in 2017. However, he does have what he himself calls a "shadow CV" - the mental list, common to all directors, of plays they would have done with huge success if only things had worked out as planned, and this includes a lot of Shaw. He has a longstanding enthusiasm for Shaw's plays, something he attributes in part to his mother - like GBS himself, "an Irish Protestant from Dublin."

He's British, but he has a Canadian profile, one connected not with the Niagara-on-the-Lake festival but with the other one: the Stratford one. He's staged two Shakespeares along with a production of Peter Pan. The last would probably be classified as "family entertainment" and he's returning to Stratford next year to direct another show in the same category: The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe. So he's going to have a bifurcated Canadian year in 2016: staging his own production at one festival while spending most of his time at the other, shadowing Jackie Maxwell, the outgoing director.

He's best known for his Shakespeare work, and especially for two productions, of Twelfth Night and Richard III, that went to Broadway with Mark Rylance as their star. These, like his Stratford Shakespeares, were productions that tried to re-create at least some elements of Elizabethan stagecraft. The "Original Practices" tag is one that has stuck to him, though when I talked to him on the phone last Thursday, just after his appointment was announced, he insisted that it only accounted for four of the 30 Shakespeares he has directed. "The notion has grown up," he said. "Actually, the majority of my Shakespeare productions have been in modern dress. But people love to put you in boxes."

What most attracts Carroll to Shaw's plays, and by extension plays in his tradition, is that they have "real intellectual meat. All Shaw's plays confront us with our prejudices - especially our comfortable, liberal ones." He takes that tradition to include musicals, which have become important to the festival, financially and otherwise. He himself directed Stephen Sondheim's A Little Night Music a few years ago; and Sondheim has "real literary juices."

He doesn't think intelligent theatre has to be dutiful or forbidding. "It shouldn't be like going to the dentist. It should be a great visceral experience." He's also open to extending the repertoire backwards: something that hasn't been done before. "If there

was an overwhelming reason to do an Aristophanes, we'd do it." That's interesting: Aristophanes, the great comic playwright of ancient Greece, was in some ways the GBS of his time, and some of Shaw's plays owe him an explicit debt - though, unlike Shaw, this most explicit of comedians never wrote Plays for Puritans.

Carroll inherits a performing ensemble that Jackie Maxwell herself inherited, in its spirit and largely in its personnel, from her predecessor Christopher Newton, who really created it. All three directors incidentally are British born, though both Newton and Maxwell were already established features of the Canadian theatre when they took over. Carroll admits, "I know there's a sense of nervousness when the new guy comes in. I don't want to lose the sense of ensemble. I want to make the ensemble the star."

Canada, where film and TV opportunities are limited, is an enviable place for actors, and for lovers of theatre acting. Carroll confirms that "in the U.S., where I've worked a lot, the reputation of Canadian actors is very high - especially for the classics, which in America of course means anything more than five years old."

Certainly classically inclined theatres from Chicago to San Francisco regularly borrow actors from Shaw and Stratford. As for the U.K., whose two major subsidized theatres created wonderful companies in the '60s and '70s: "The National Theatre doesn't have an ensemble, the Royal Shakespeare Company doesn't, though not necessarily because it doesn't want to - they just can't hold on to people."

Sounding rather like the late Robin Phillips, talking about "semaphore acting" when he moved here from the U.K. to take over Stratford, Carroll says, "A lot of theatre in Britain is living on past glories. There's a lot of demonstrative, shallow, fake acting there."

His substantial CV is remarkably varied, from formal - he's done a lot of opera - to decidedly informal. Building on his work at London's Elizabethan Globe theatre - "where every performance was different, because it was in the open air" - he started his own London company, the Factory, where he did a Hamlet "where we chose the cast by lottery before each show" and a dramatization of Homer's Odyssey where "each of the 24 books was performed in a different style chosen by the audience." How much of

this free-and-easiness can be applied at the Shaw I'm not certain, and neither I imagine is he, but it's intriguing.

The board that selected him started with a list of 30 candidates, whittled it down to twelve, then to six, then to four. Carroll was the unanimous choice, partly due to his philosophy about the business side. This, inevitably, includes reaching younger people; like most theatres, the festival is concerned about the age of its audience.

Shaw has begun to move into the family entertainment business, with The Wind in the Willows and Huckleberry Finn as future projects. Peter Jewett, the head of the Shaw's search committee describes his new employee as "very engaging, somewhat self-effacing." And one other thing: the actors to whom I've spoken have loved the experience of working with him.
