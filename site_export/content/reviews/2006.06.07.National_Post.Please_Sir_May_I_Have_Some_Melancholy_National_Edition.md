OLIVER!

Festival Theatre, Stratford

- - -

It's tempting to imagine a really dark production of Oliver!, one that would put the twist back into Oliver Twist. Dickens novel is, after all, one of the most horrific in the language, a non-stop chronicle of crime and child-abuse, and Lionel Barts epic crowd- pleaser of a musical can't altogether prevent cheerlessness from breaking in; at times it even seems to welcome it. Not for long, admittedly, and even the most determinedly misanthropic director would probably be defeated by the change of gear halfway through the first act, when the show goes merrily ahead into streets-of-London- type overdrive. But we can still continue to dream and, up until that point and in a few places later, Donna Feore's Stratford production allows us our nightmares.

We certainly believe in the starving workhouse children of the opening, even if an inappropriately festive bout of ring around the rosey interrupts their otherwise-unimpeachable yearning for Food, Glorious Food. Olivers fateful asking for more is accorded full dramatic value, as is his grotesque enslavement to the local undertaker; though its a shame, when a funeral procession makes a spectral appearance at the corners of the stage, that it isn't made clear that it's a childs coffin theyre following.

Terror can co-exist with comedy; it generally does in Dickens. Bruce Dow's Mr. Bumble and Mary Ellen Mahoney's Widow Corney, complacent and commanding, respectively, are splendidly matched, first in song, then in marriage. Equally effective, on a smaller scale, is Kyle Blair as Noah Claypole, the undertakers sneak who may be the nastiest piece of work in the entire story. Oliver himself is splendidly played, and soaringly sung, by Tyler Pearse, a nine-year-old who seems completely unfazed by the instant transitions hes required to make from terror to defiance to all- around wholesomeness. The designer, Santo Loquasto, sets the mood with a giant, threatening drop-curtain and reinforces it with a giant, threatening and very mobile bridge.

Around, above and underneath it the director mounts an admirably stage-filling production, though her choreography as the show goes into cockney knees-up mode and declines into a stock succession of kicks and twirls. The optimistic choral reprise of Oliver's Who Will Buy is devalued when the entire urban population reappears in gleaming white so that we'll know how happy theyre all feeling.

The song itself, though, is an especially felicitous example of Barts capacity for speaking showbiz like a native; he makes us believe in simplified emotions by believing them himself. He can give Bill Sikes a stock "I am a villain" song that still offers the actor the chance of something more: A chance here seized upon by Brad Rudy who, in taking it quietly, makes it sound genuinely psychopathic. He can give Nancy (here clad, as in all productions, in hot traditional harlot's red) the infectious It's a Fine Life, which seduces us with its swing and snap, and then throw it back at us, an act later, as a surprisingly grim and complex trio. He can also write Nancy what must count, I suppose, as one of the great bad songs, As Long As He Needs Me. It really is bad: One musical phrase endlessly repeated, though fitted out with a different lyrical cliche every time. But it always stops the show, and Blythe Wilson gives it the appropriate, lung-bursting treatment. She makes nothing of her dialogue, though, and even in her songs falls into the classic error of talking lines she could be singing, as if this made them funnier or more affecting. Outside of patter songs, it never does.

Barts greatest liberty, of course, was to turn Fagin, the crafty, cringing devil whom Dickens habitually dubs the Jew and who in the novel prompts Sikes to murder, into a lovable old rogue who wouldnt hurt a fly. It's sentimental, it's shameless and its wonderful. And it is so because its true to the book -- if not to what Dickens put into it, then to what generations of readers have taken out. We all screen out Fagin's monstrosity and remember his charm and his humour. It's like My Fair Lady reclaiming the romance in Pygmalion. And Bart, East End Jewish himself, connects with his own roots by making the comedy ethnically idiomatic ("so at my time of life I should start turning over new leaves?") as the original never did. Through that, he achieves a comic richness both of characterization and, to coin a word, performability. Colm Feore revels in both, whether lurking at the edge of the crowd, leaping into its centre or leaving it behind to explode all over the stage, physically and vocally, in his showpiece Reviewing the Situation. Like most Fagins nowadays, he plays down the racial aspect, at least until his last moment, when he plonks his hat on his head, turns upstage and exits into the sunset. Feore's Coriolanus, the evening before, had ended on a funeral pyre. That makes two nights in a row that this actor has gone out in a blaze of glory.
