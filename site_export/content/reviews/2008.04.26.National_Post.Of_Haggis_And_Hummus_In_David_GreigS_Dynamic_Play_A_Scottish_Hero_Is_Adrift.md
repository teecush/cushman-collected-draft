The World Stage season brings to Harbourfront a Scottish play set in the Middle East: a double slice of exotica.

Damascus comes from the justly fabled Traverse Theatre in Edinburgh, which has maintained a reputation for literary experiments for over 45 years. Its the work of David Greig, a scarily prolific playwright who's also the dramaturge for the National Theatre of Scotland. Some of it has a rewarding thickness of text and texture beyond our usual expectations. Some more of it -- it's a long play by current standards -- feels like filler: intelligently selected but oddly placed.

Our protagonist is Paul, like his author -- who has taught play-writing workshops there -- a Scottish writer on a cultural mission to the Syrian capital. Paul is trying to sell English-as-a-second-language storybooks. We find him in the lobby of a somewhat grand hotel, jet-lagged from the flight in and impatient for the flight out; its Valentines Day and he'd like to be home with his wife. (It seems, all things considered, a badly planned trip.) His departure plans are frustrated by an airport bomb in Beirut. Meanwhile, he negotiates, professionally and personally, with Muna, a junior academic charged with vetting his volume.

Their longest encounter is a richly rewarding duologue that mingles the political, the linguistic and the erotic. Material that Paul had thought innocuous turns out to be highly charged; between the fundamentalists and the modernizers (the majority, according to Muna) he's crossing a minefield. Some of his preconceptions about the Arab world are challenged; though when she accuses the West of using Syria as an offshore torture-chamber, you may feel like shouting back that it couldnt happen without some enthusiastic Syrian cooperation. There is much talk of tenses: of the difficulty of transmitting into Arabic the stiff but subtle English conventions of present, future and past, perfect and imperfect. And this in turn becomes a vehicle of flirtation: Muna, who is younger than Paul, takes on the air of a perpetually postponed Future Indefinite. I cant place it, but I'm sure that somewhere in the Past Perfect I've met this device before, but no matter; Greig and his actors make it their own, Present Dynamic.

Ewen Bremner, playing Paul, is so deeply inside the character that the actual performance sometimes fails to get out; this is especially the case at the beginning when his muted rendition of a phone call home, mostly with his back to us, results in important information going missing. He still catches the essence of the pedantic, conscientious man cut loose from his moorings: the born Mr. Unlucky. Long before the end of the play, you begin to doubt whether hell get out of Damascus alive. (And Im not saying he doesn't, and I'm not saying he does.) Nathalie Armin complements him, almost perfectly, as Muna, feeling the pull, weighing her options. When the two come close to connecting, its like watching a British play that we saw last year at Stratford, David Edgars Pentecost, with its priorities reversed: In this dramatized clash of cultures, the personal trumps the public, in effectiveness if not in intention.

In fact, what goes on around this relationship is something of a blur. This is partly a matter of performance: Philip Howard's production feels like a decent piece of work that's been shipped into a space not so much too large for it as too awkwardly proportioned. But its also in the writing. Syria as a place in itself -- as opposed to part of some generic Middle East -- comes sharply into focus when Muna confronts Paul. But for much of the time, this could be just another story of a European losing and/or finding himself in a mysterious city.

For reasons of dramatic economy, theatrical economics or both, the hotel is strikingly short of both guests and staff. It seems to be run single-handedly by a young receptionist-barman named Zacharias, whose twin ambitions, for both of which he enlists Pauls assistance, are to bed an English-speaking girl and to sell the movie script of his life to Hollywood. He's winningly played by Khalid Laith, but in the end (he gets, surprisingly, the final curtain) he comes off less as a person than as a device. Wasim, Muna's superior (and ex-teacher, and ex-lover), a radical gone tame, might tell us more about the country but he remains, in Alex Elliotts performance, doggedly uninteresting; he and Muna converse in Arabic, which we hear as English, so we know when theyre being rude about Paul but he doesn't. It's similar to a device used in Translations, but it wears thin much quicker.

This may be the only play that recalls both Brian Friels opus and Casablanca (plus a good dozen other movies); its presided over -- from atop a spiral staircase -- by a lady pianist (played with excessive doominess by Dolya Gavanski) who seems, like Zacharias, to work a 24-hour day, supplying music appropriate to every mood and in every style. I'd hoped she might stretch to "we're off on the road to Damascus" -- Hope and Crosby meet St. Paul -- but no joy. As a conception she is, pardon my oxymoron, pure kitsch. Her background is, she tells us repeatedly, as eclectic as her repertoire: ex-Marxist, ex-Christian and transsexual. Presumably, she personifies Damascus, with the implication being that Damascus has something for everyone--but what city haven't you heard that about? Damascus the play has something for some people, but it requires patience.
