DRINKING ALONE

Ford Centre Studio Theatre

Kenneth Tynan, whose great qualities as a critic included a substantial fund of common sense, once defined serious theatre as that which presents a view of life "from which the pain is not left out."

Theatre does not have to be serious to be good; nobody wants pain in Guys and Dolls. Trouble comes when a play flirts with pain without carrying through. You get semi-serious theatre, of which Drinking Alone is an example.

It belongs to an endangered species, in this town anyway. Norm Foster, who wrote it, is one of Canada's most successful playwrights; his plays, though, are hardly ever seen in Toronto, which notoriusly lacks a middle ground between shows with pretentions and shows with songs. We hardly get to see play in which the tone is comic, the settings middle-class and the problems soluble. The people who write them don't believe in them in the way they used to. So they try, conscientiously, to extend the boundaries. One character in Drinking Alone has cancer; that would have been at least unmentionable in a domestic comedy 20 years ago. The form, though, won't accommodate the content

Drinking Alone is actually more worthwhile than some of the more self-important stuff on our more self-important stages. It isn't especially funny (I think I laughed once), but it isn't boring either. But it has not found its own comfort-level; even structurally it seems to be two plays. We are in the home of Joe, a bachelor in his early thirties, and the frustrated proprietor of a modest dry-cleaning business. His sister, Carrie, a successful TV newsreader, is unhappily married and an alcoholic. Their mother was also an alcoholic, and this caused their father, Ivan, successful owner of a chain of bookstores, to leave home and set up house with another woman, name of Phyllis. The two generations have lived bitterly apart for years, but today, Ivan's birthday, they all gather in Joe's living room and reciminate.

Thus far, we have a predictable family drama. Father will tell son that he hasn't made anything of himself; son will reply he never had the chance; sister, in between kitchen trips to grab another beer, will accuse everyone of ruining her life. Stepmother will try to keep the peace; and we can be sure that, by the end, some kind of reconciliation will have occurred. All this does in fact come to pass.

There is, however, a fifth character, Renee, hired by Joe from an escort agency to pose as his fiancee and thus impress his father. (Why remains obscure.) Renee is, to use a suitably antique expression, a kook; told to talk as little as possible, she starts quacking away to her putative in-laws about her two-year engagement. Nobody is more surprised by her volubility than she is; her self- discovery through role-playing takes some swallowing but is entertaining. It does not, though, mesh with the familial squabbles; plainly meant to be a catalyst, she is at most an irritant. She is also the love interest: a single mother new to escorting who wants to be a hairdresser.

A play advanced enough to involve drunkenness, single motherhood and quasi-prostitution is not going to end in an old-fashioned love scene but the intention is the same. Joe and Renee begin and end the play; unfortunately, though, their growing attraction is a blank for the rest of it. Joe seems to be the metephorical solitary drinker of the title (as his sister is the literal one) but the idea is never developed. Other characters aren't much more substantial. None has a believable job.

A more accomplished production might have papered over the cracks, which could have been amusing, or exposed them, which might have been powerful. This one, directed by Eli Lukawitz, is the initial offering by a company called Tribal Productions which has taken up residence in the Ford Not bad, but unsteady.

The performances all lunge in the general direction of emotion. Lisa Messinger strikes out ahead as Renee. None manages to keep patness or cuteness entirely at bay. Life, in the end, goes on but it generally extorts a higher cost than this play seems able or willing to pay.
