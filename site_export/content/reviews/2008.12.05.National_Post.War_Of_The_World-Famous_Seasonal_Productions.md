THEATRE REVIEWS

IT'S A WONDERFUL LIFE

Bluma Appel Theatre

A Christmas Carol

Young Centre for the Performing Arts

---

George Bailey, meet Ebenezer Scrooge. I'm sure you two will have a lot to talk about. After all, you're both known for having received life-changing supernatural visitations that hit you right in the festive season.

But first --and in deference to the fact that George's story, It's a Wonderful Life, is currently being told in Toronto as a staged radio play (yes, I saw your eyebrows ascend) -- a few words from our critic.

It's a Wonderful Life is brought to you by the Canadian Stage Company who got it from an American playwright, Philip Grecian, who of course got it from the 1946 Frank Capra movie that has come to epitomize the concept known as Capracorn. George is the bashful but determined do-gooder who functions in his archetypal American small town as a kind of one-man Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac, and whose benevolent stewardship of the family savings and loan company earns him the undying enmity of the bigwig plutocrat Mr. Potter, who resembles -- how can I put this? -- Montgomery Burns without the nuance. Due partly to the villainy of Mr. Potter, partly to the carelessness of his drunken uncle (George has always been good to his family) and partly to the value of his insurance policy, our boy is tempted to suicide and despair, not in that order. But he is saved by Clarence, an apprentice angel with an Irish accent, sent down from heaven to show George what he has accomplished in his life by bringing hope and happiness to his fellow citizens. By way of subplot, Clarence's successful execution of this mission wins him a promotion. Every time a bell rings, we are told, an angel gets his wings. Clap your hands if you believe in this.

George seems terribly slow on the uptake; Clarence makes it perfectly clear that he is giving him a counterfactual demonstration of what the town would have been like had he never been born, but he still keeps wondering why nobody recognizes him. Compare this with A Christmas Carol, which Soulpepper has brought back, in Michael Shamata's exemplary adaptation and production, for a fourth season: Scrooge catches on very early to what the three Christmas ghosts have in store for him, so we can concentrate on his reactions to their revelations. (All right, he does seem reluctant to realize that he himself is the unmourned corpse at Christmas Yet to Come, but who wouldn't be?)Compare, more importantly, that George is the innocent victim of one rich scoundrel, while Scrooge is used to indict a whole system in which he himself is a major participant. One is a good guy throughout; the other, far more dramatically, a bad one who changes. Of course Dickens' tale isn't realistic either; but it doesn't pretend to be. (Capra's does.) But, with the wit and imagination of a great writer behind it, it's real. (Capra's isn't.)

Whichmay explain why Soulpepper tells their story as if they believed in it, while CanStage tells theirs as if they were apologizing for it. It's a very elaborate apology, with a lot of talent behind it. As stated, the Grecian adaptation's conceit is that the show is being broadcast live from a 1940s radio studio. This is lovingly recreated in Donna Feore's production, which is an impressive feat of visual orchestration. We watch the stage actors playing radio actors, flirting and joshing and doing their knitting when they're not actually on mic and making a great show of reading from scripts when they are. A lady pianist supplies mood music, as if this were a silent movie. (If American radio drama was really like this, then American radio drama deserved to die.) In a party scene, the cast break into a brief Charleston; real radio actors wouldn't exert themselves like that, but it's fun to watch. So is John Gzowski, billed anachronistically as "Foley artist," doing the sound effects before our eyes. There are commercial breaks, mostly sung and gorgeously in period. But it all distracts from what's meant to be the play.

That it takes hold at all is principally due to Mike Shara, who gets better and better. Without any kind of Jimmy Stewart impersonation, he has George's aw-shucksiness shiningly to rights; this is a performance of unblinking excellence. Marla McLean is his de-serving marital partner, Patrick McKenna charms as Clarence and others more earthbound, and Juan Chioran gets to play two kinds of cad, the evil Potter and the bottom-pinching thesp who impersonates him. A strong supporting cast includes Douglas E. Hughes (playing God) and Leslie Arden (playing the piano), with Blair Williams as announcer and man-of-many-voices, three of them sequentially at one point. But it still all feels like cashing in on a famous title. The program reveals that the same adaptor has done another, straightforwardly theatrical, version of the same material, seen last year in Vancouver. As the old joke has it, what's a Grecian earn? At Christmas, I should imagine, quite a bit.

A Christmas Carol is worth the price just to see Joseph Ziegler's Ebenezer kicking off his slippers in an ecstasy of joyous reformation; his final rapprochement with Oliver Dennis's crown prince of Cratchits is a heart-warmimg bonus. Who knew being good could be so much fun? Ziegler's Scrooge is beautifully of a piece; the seeds of humour, if not of good humour, are sown in his early extravagant misanthropy, while his encroaching pain at the sight of opportunities past is very moving. Other welcome returning veterans are John Jarvis's quartet of spirits and Patrick Galligan's incorrigibly cheerful nephew, with Maggie Huculak as an assortment of wenches and crones. In terms of overall ensemble, CanStage cannot on this occasion be faulted. But Soulpepper has the soul. - It's a Wonderful Life runs until Dec. 20. Call 416-368-3110 for tickets and information. A Christmas Carol runs until Dec. 24. Call 416-866-8666.
