PATIENCE

Avon Theatre, Stratford

The Stratford Festival has revived Gilbert and Sullivan's Patience (or Bunthornes Bride) as part of its Oscar Wilde centenary celebration. The opera, first staged in 1881, is a satire on Wilde, who at that time had yet to come into his own as an author, but was already considered a social and artistic portent. As the figurehead of an Aesthetic Movement, dedicated to what Lewis Carroll called reeling, writhing, and fainting in coils, he was both mocked and celebrated. Patience does mockery.

The Stratford production is a concert performance; one of those semi-staged, book-in-hand affairs that have become popular in London and New York as cost-effective methods of acquainting us with risky or unfamiliar old musicals. (A similar outfit existed recently in Toronto but seems to have gone into abeyance.)

This Patience is an unusually elaborate example of the genre, with a full orchestra and a carefully worked-out staging. The conceit devised by the director, Brian Hill, is that we are some kind of Victorian garden party, whose host hands out bound scripts and scores to his guests and conjures them to get on with it. He also sits periodically at the piano (he may even, for all I could tell, play it); reads the stage directions, which are among the juiciest parts of the text; ushers the other actors on and off; and generally fusses.

There is much merriment when things go intentionally wrong, and some embarrassment when they do it by mistake. This, in fact, is a concert performance that makes fun of concert performances.

It works very nicely, though it strikes me as strictly a one-off solution. If Stratford is to persevere with this format (which seems a not-bad idea) it cant go on apologizing for it. Even during the season, it tempts the curse of built-in obsolescence. Most concert- performances are put on for two or three nights. This one is sticking around for three months; and I can't help fearing that by the end of that period most of the cast will know their lines. The words, try as they may to resist them, will have forced their way into the performers' skulls. And what will the concept do then, poor thing?

Musically, the performance, under the baton of Berthold Carriere, is well up to snuff. Sullivans score, which I had never heard before, jog-trots along for its first half hour or so, but takes off halfway through the first act with a couple of sweetly yearning ballads, reinforced in the second by some top-notch comedy tunes. (One of them -- Then Go to Him and Say to Him -- I seem to have been humming for years without knowing it, like Molieres bourgeois gentleman speaking prose.)

Dramatically, the show leaves more to be desired. And at this point you will please excuse me while I attempt to summarize the plot.

The opera's Wilde-man is called Reginald Bunthorne. Personally, he has no use for poetry ("What's the use of yearning for Elysian fields when you know you cant get em and would only let 'em out on building leases if you had 'em?") but he thrives on adoration and, aestheticism being the going thing, he gets plenty of it.

All the high-born young ladies in the neighbourhood pine for him, in chorus, but he fancies Patience, the local milkmaid, who has never known love and is not impressed by what shes heard about it. She is persuaded that she is missing out, but somehow gets the idea that since love is unselfish, she can only love someone she doesnt like. (She's a proto-modern heroine, Patience is.) On this basis, she agrees to marry Bunthorne.

There then appears Archibald Gros-venor, a rival and apparently dishier poet, to whom all the girls immediately transfer their affections. Meanwhile, a troop of dragoons, all experienced ladykillers, hover around aghast at no longer being flavours of the month.

At the end, everything is worked out and everyone is paired off, except that nobody is Bunthorne's bride. He seems rather happy with this arrangement. Maybe Gilbert knew something.

There is a gulf, though, between the neat wit and elegant construction of the script when read, and the fruitily emphatic manner in which it gets deposited on stage. This is a constant problem with G&S, and it probably has much to do with trained singers not being always the niftiest dispensers of spoken humour (or even, sometimes, of sung humour).

The ornateness of Gilberts Victorian style is deceptive; he was out to attack pomposity with its own weapons, but too many productions seem to be on the side of the enemy. The traditional G&S performing style comes between the audience and the jokes, and its compounded in Patience by the affectation that the show is supposed to be satirizing.

The dragoons here are especially butterfingered. (And for them the concert format is a snare and a delusion; it's probably a mistake to embark on a G&S patter song if you're not sure of the words.) The lovesick maidens are rather better; Barbara Barsky is languorously statuesque as the ring-leading Lady Angela, and Barbara Fulton is Lady Ella, who doesnt say much but is delectable to look at. Theresa Tova, though, as the formidable Lady Jane (not pretty she admits cheerfully, "massive") seems keen to laugh at her character before the audience gets a chance to.

Three performers carry the show. Bruce Dow, a silently chortling master of ceremonies, goes on to play Bunthorne himself, whom he impales like a sulky butterfly; talking or singing, he is consistently and economically funny. George Masswohl, as his rival, is heavier and less precise, but knows fundamentally what hes about. (The two are dressed in identical bottle-green smoking- jackets; I don't know if this was actually de rigueur for fashionable versifiers of the pre-pre-Raphaelite era, but it certainly makes a helpful uniform on stage.) Christina Gordon in the title role is ideal: disconcertingly innocent, even more disconcertingly practical, all-over pert, and singing like a dream.

The production at least attempts to meet the piece on its own terms; it's far preferable to the camped-up, haphazardly updated G&S revivals with which Stratford used to tempt the tourists.
