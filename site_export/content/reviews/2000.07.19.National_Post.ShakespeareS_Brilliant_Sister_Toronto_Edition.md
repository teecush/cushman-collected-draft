A ROOM OF ONES OWN

Court House Theatre, Niagara on the Lake, Shaw Festival

Pamela Rabe, a tall and deep-toned actress who was born in Canada but has spent most of her career in Australia, made her Shaw Festival debut last week as Orinthia, the king's mistress, in The Apple Cart. Orinthia fancies herself, or Shaw seems to have fancied her, as an enchantress; but she emerged here, for all the physical and vocal exertions that the actress lavished upon her -- or maybe because of them -- as energetically repellent. A couple of nights later the same actress appeared at the same festival, as the gaunt, intense Virginia Woolf, delivering a lecture to a long-ago audience of Cambridge undergraduates, played by us. And this time she was dazzling: dazzlingly attractive too.

A Room of One's Own is adapted (not, I suspect, a very arduous task) by the English director Patrick Garland from a celebrated address given by Woolf in 1928, and subsequently published as a book. Its the talk in which she lays it down that in order to succeed as a writer, or indeed as anything else beyond wife- and motherhood, a woman needs financial independence -- she reckons this as (ps)500 a year, 1920s money -- and a place in which to work. She adds to this famous demand, the equally powerful image of "Shakespeares sister, an imaginary figure as talented as her brother who, Woolf speculates, would have come to a very bad end, had she had the temerity to follow in his footsteps.

The talk was given at Girton, Cambridge's first grudging attempt to provide a college for women, separate from but not equal to the many that already existed for men. Mordantly and wittily, she expounds on the difference in the amenities offered to the two student genders, notably the food. (Prunes, even mitigated by custard, are an unforgiving vegetable"; then, as if anticipating our objections, she delivers a perfect put-down of a coda: "Fruit, they are NOT.) According to her, the official feeling was that, if women had to be given a higher education, then both the prospect and the experience should be made as forbidding for them as possible.

She was undoubtedly right; indeed, the same ethos persisted as late as the '60s, when I was an undergraduate. Not that the cuisine at the mens colleges was anything to write home about (well, not in that sense anyway) but at least we were in town. Girtons geographical position seemed to have been chosen expressly to discourage the girls from leaving the premises and to deter any visitors, especially male ones. You wouldn't believe how long it took to get there, even by bus. Woolf herself, en route to her lecture, seems to have walked it. (She was a hero.) Brilliantly, she defines the male position; it's not so much that men want women to feel inferior, as that they want to go on feeling superior themselves.

Ive seen it objected that A Room of Ones Own isn't a real play." This, I think, is to its credit. Real plays about famous writers are usually terrible. They make unjustified demands for sympathy, based on the assumption that the audience will have read the books (or will feel intimidated if they haven't). Some years ago Stratford mounted a real play about this very writer, a play called Virginia; and even though it starred Maggie Smith in what was generally reckoned one of her greatest performances, it bored me, though to indifference rather than to tears. It wanted tears, for the protagonists sufferings; the present show wants respect, for her mind, and it presents the evidence.

Admittedly, I hadnt read any Woolf when I saw Virginia; since then I've read quite a lot, and having been awed by her style, I'm far more inclined to take an interest. As it happens I prefer the essays to the novels, whose style is so concentratedly dazzling as to cause headaches. The non-fiction is tight and argumentative and graceful and wonderfully witty: qualities that Pamela Rabe not only captures but embodies. Her phrasing is almost flawless; long, Augustan sentences twist and turn elegantly before us, and arrive always with their tails poised and stinging. She uses the style to get at the ideas, and the ideas to get at the woman. (She was probably trying the same with Orinthia, who was full of ideas; none of them, unfortunately, much worth listening to.)

It helps, obviously, that she looks like Virginia, spare and long- boned; she has also designed a physical vocabulary for her. In Micheline Chevriers production she roams, not too restlessly, around the stage, suggesting that, when confronting an audience, her body is nervous but her mind is absolutely at ease. Im tempted to call this solo show the best ensemble performance of the Shaw season.

The ideas are dramatic and still challenging; Woolf can move from amused indignation at the barriers erected against her sex on university lawns and in university libraries, to a combination of lament and celebration for the lives and achievements of the great Victorian woman novelists, to deliver a stark challenge to her hearers: "You haven't written Shakespeare's plays. (You might feel like responding that not many men have either.) The woman speaking the ideas is real and embattled, and the result is drama; the term lecture theatre" seems absolutely apposite.

You dont have to know anything about Virginia Woolf when you go in; though its certain that you will want to know all about her by the time you come out.
