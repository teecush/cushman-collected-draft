(From the National Post, Tuesday, January 16, 2001) I made some large mistakes in yesterday's review of Stones in His Pockets (Winter Garden). I attributed the superb performances of the two actors, Conleth Hill and Sean Campion, to their standbys, Declan Mooney and Stevie Ray Dallimore, whose names are given almost equal prominence in the program. (Hill was on the left in the picture that accompanied my review, Campion on the right.) I also, for reasons I cannot explain, misremembered the names of a couple of minor characters whose names are not in the program at all: Lewis is actually called Clem, and Victor should really be Simon. And the name I misheard as Ashley is really Aisling. My apologies to everyone, and -- to Messrs. Hill and Campion -- my renewed (and this time, I hope, accurate) congratulations. --R.C. *****

STONES IN HIS POCKETS

Winter Garden Theatre, Toronto

---

TORONTO - Hard on the heels of David Mamet's film State and Main comes, from Belfast via London, a play rooted in a very similar situation. A Hollywood crew descends on a small community and, to put the matter as neutrally as possible, uses it. Mamet's film is set in New England, Marie Jones' play in old Ireland. More importantly, Mamet tells his story from the point of the invaders, Jones tells hers from that of the residents.

The result is that, appallingly though Mamet's characters may behave, we want their picture to get made. With Jones, we don't much care whether it is or not; we don't bear the movie people any particular ill-will, but it's the locals who count; whatever they want is fine with us.

Jones, on the evidence of her bio an extraordinarily prolific playwright and actress, has furnished a lively script, but there is no doubt that the immense appeal of the play lies in its format and its performance. There are many characters but only two actors: Stevie Ray Dallimore, who is gaunt and fairly tall, and Declan Mooney, who is shorter and rounder -- a classic duo.

Most of the time they play, respectively, Jake and Charlie, two youngish inhabitants of County Kerry who, along with 338 of their neighbours, are working as extras on what seems to be a period- piece manor-house romance with occasional gestures toward social conscience. The extras play "the dispossessed," which is something they seem to know about.

Both Jake and Charlie have a great fund of wit, as well as dead- end prospects. Jake has been to America and returned empty-handed; Charlie, former owner of a failed video store, has written a movie script that he hopes somebody on the set may look at. Both are angry and frustrated, though Jake's anger is much closer to the surface.

At any moment, though, either of them may change into somebody else. Mooney may start swanning around (he really does swan) as Caroline Giovanni, the fey and velvet-voiced American star of the picture, anxious to perfect her Irish accent by close (if brief) contact with some man who has one, and probably as well-intentioned as a person so cocooned in privilege can be. Or he may become Victor, the assistant director in charge of crowd control, who is Irish himself but tries to disguise it. Or Lewis, the terminally relaxed (or maybe just languidly exhausted) real director, who is English and who only manifests himself to the extras on very special occasions, and on whom, we realize, Victor is modelling himself. Or, with a terrifying rolling gait, one of the unit's security heavies.

Dallimore gets his chance across the gender-gap as Ashley, the continuity-person, gangling, vague and, in voice at least, fashionably London. He also plays Mickey, a stooped and cunning senior, revered as "one of the few remaining extras of The Quiet Man."

Mooney seems to be slightly the more virtuosic of the two, but in Ian McElhinney's production all the transformations are effected with dazzling grace, completeness and economy. There is pleasure in wondering who will morph next, and when, and into whom, and even in the bare-faced excuses a character will offer so that he may leave the stage and return immediately as somebody else. Sometimes he doesn't even bother to leave -- an actor may simply turn on his heel and transform himself in turning. Don't leave before the curtain- call, in which everybody we have met gets to take a bow in his or her own style.

Another character is Sean, a local boy who can't even get a job as an extra, though he's been obsessed by movies, or their glamour, since childhood. He's turned to drugs and, one night, having been thrown out of his own town's pub on to his own town's street for daring to approach the leading lady, he puts stones in his pockets and drowns himself.

Jake claims this as an extreme case of exploitation: the uncaring world of media and capital exploiting the rural Irish, materially and psychologically. (If there has been an Irish economic miracle, it doesn't seem to have reached this community.) When the film company, intent on wrapping up on schedule, seem about to refuse the extras time off to attend the funeral, battle is almost joined. Things are patched up and most faces saved, but scars have been exposed and will continue to fester.

Dramatically, this focus on the dead Sean is not too satisfying, because we have hardly met him. (I can only remember that Dallimore plays him because I recall him having a scene with Caroline, who's definitely played by Mooney.) So there has to be a lot of posthumous filling-in to make us care, and even that doesn't really work.

Indeed, despite some sublime comic moments, parts of the show are surprisingly heavy going; the actors, especially early on, have to do some of their pirouetting in a vacuum. There are certainly real issues here, the same ones touched on more subtly in such other recent Irish plays as The Weir and The Beauty Queen of Leenane: a rage to get out coupled with an even deeper rage that this should be necessary. Where the play really bogs down is in Jake's and Charlie's climactic decision to transcend their situation by "making their own movie." It's not so much wishful thinking as wishful writing -- the characters trapped in the playwright's metaphor.

What we will remember is the antics Jake and Charlie are forced into when the cameras are rolling, when they have to render homage to the heroine ("we have to look at her looking at us looking dispossessed") or witness an eviction without uttering a peep of protest, and we watch their heads bobbing from side to side, reacting to two principals who are still in their trailers.

Later, in a glorious set-piece, we watch the two of them going gamely and agonizingly through their own portions of a massed country-dance with, of course, largely invisible partners. (It isn't clear just when, or indeed if, this is supposed to have been rehearsed; maybe the producers just believe the Irish naturally got rhythm.)

At the end they corral Lewis, the director, and outline to him their ideas for a film about the deceased Sean. It seems surprisingly easy, considering the difficulties Charlie has earlier had unloading his script. Lewis listens politely and even makes a few suggestions. He finds the story too downbeat, and he thinks that Stones in His Pockets, which is what they want to call it, will be a real turn-off of a title.

Judging from the international success of the play, the laugh is on Lewis, but in fairness to him it should be pointed out that the dramatist, though keeping both the title and the suicide, has arranged things so that we go away cheerful, and that the actors have kept us happy almost throughout.
