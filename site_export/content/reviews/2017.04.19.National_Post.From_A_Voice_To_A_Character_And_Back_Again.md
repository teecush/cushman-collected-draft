THEATRE REVIEW

Orphans

Coal Mine Theatre, Toronto

From the U.K. comes Orphans, a play about urban fear that is gripping and clever, but ultimately hollow.

The literal orphans of Dennis Kelly's play are Helen and Liam, a brother and sister in their 20s who lost their parents in a fire when they were children. Helen is now married to Danny, who unlike his wife and brother-in-law speaks with a BBC accent, and so may be taken to embody middle-class liberal values. We are told Danny has a good job though not what it is; I reckon he's a teacher.

Helen and Liam are still close, so close that Liam has a key to Helen and Danny's house, and is likely to turn up unexpectedly. Which is what he does at the beginning of the play, interrupting a nice celebratory dinner for two. Liam is nervous and covered in blood. He explains, sometimes haltingly, sometimes in a rush, that he had found a man - or, he says, " a boy" - lying in the street, beaten and bleeding, and had stopped to help him. It seems that the victim had finally got up and staggered away.

Once Liam has been given sympathy and a clean T-shirt, his hosts start to feel doubts. It comes out that the wounded boy was from a racial minority. Liam has always been a loose cannon and he speaks affectionately of a friend, Ian, who's a neo-Nazi. Liam claims that it's all in fun and that he himself simply likes the memorabilia, but Danny in particular is suspicious. Gradually Liam's story breaks down, and so does he. Helen and Danny have to decide whether to call the police. The longer they wait, the deeper in they are.

Leora Morris, directing the Coal Mine production, writes in her program note that "setting our production in 2009 in Luton, UK - a hotbed of unrest around immigration and inclusion - offers a way to consider our present moment's genesis." I don't think the play makes any specific mention of Luton, a town on the outermost skirts of London with its own history of racial unrest, but 2009 is certainly when it was written and first staged. As she says it's a pre-Brexit play that has only gained in timeliness.

The tensions that it expertly manipulates are of class as well as race. Helen has, as people used to say, married up, and is very conscious of it. Loyal to Liam, she also resents him: just how much, and why, is one of the play's most brutal revelations. She is also both possessive of Danny and impatient with him. The strongest-willed person in the play, she is also the most desperate; it's a dramatically fruitful combination of qualities and Diana Bentley, in a sharp-edged fiercely immediate performance, takes full advantage of it.

As Liam, Tim Dowler-Coltman has the full look, sound and feel of a confused skinhead. He talks a lot about how much he loves his sister and of how he admires the world that she, Danny and their four-year old son have created for themselves. By the same token, of course, he envies it. Meaning to be disarming, he's actually alarming. He gets around to verbalizing his jealousy in the play's last section, saying repeatedly and with relish that it's as if he'd brought "a dead cat" into their house and trailed it all over their lovely John Lewis sofa. At this point, though, the writing has fallen to pieces; Liam is talking like a character in a play.

Most damagingly, Liam's disintegration as a voice coincides with Danny's as a character. We're verging on spoiler territory here, but the play is out to prove that a nicely spoken sheltered person like Danny can, under pressure, become a sadistic thug. I don't doubt it (see Dustin Hoffman in Straw Dogs) but I doubt that the pressure in this particular situation is enough. Danny has other options; he doesn't have to behave in the way his author needs him to behave.

In this he resembles, come to think of it, the inconsistently magical heroine of Matilda The Musical, for which Kelly wrote the book.

None of that is a reflection on David Patrick Flemming's performance, which is on the same high level of technique and intelligence as those of his two colleagues. There is a fourth character: Shane, Helen's and Danny's fouryear-old son, whose main purpose in the play is to keep us terrified that something awful is going to happen to him.

There's good and bad suspense. This play has both.

Runs until April 30
