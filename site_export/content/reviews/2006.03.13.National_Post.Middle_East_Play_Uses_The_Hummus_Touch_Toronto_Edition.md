THE ARAB-ISRAELI COOKBOOK

Berkely Street Theatre Upstairs

- - -

Here is an uncontroversial compliment. The Arab-Israeli Cookbook is the most mouth-watering play in Toronto. It tantalizes the tastebuds more than any play since Saturday, Sunday, Monday, Eduardo de Filippo's celebration of the bedrock Neapolitan values of work, home and pasta.

Both plays devote a lot of stage time to food: cooking it, eating it, talking about it. The current one derives from a research trip to the Middle East, undertaken in 2003 by Robin Soans. Soans, an English actor with an uncanny vocal resemblance to the great Alec McCowen, interviewed scores of Israelis and Palestinians: Jewish, Muslim, Christian. He discovered that, whatever their differences, they had one thing in common: a consuming interest in consuming.

So not only are they like one another, with virtually interchangeable cuisines, they are like everybody else, in or out of war zones. We all cling to simple pleasures, in the face of all the pressures that assail us. That's a humane observation, but it isn't necessarily enough to make a play.

The script that Soans assembled from his interviews supposedly allows each person to speak in his or her unmediated voice. This suggests that all of his subjects spoke English. If a translator was involved, that immediately introduces another level; and there are some speeches, which, translated or not, are too neatly turned to pass as verbatim reproduction. One dish is described as having "a stiff whiff of antimacassars and mothballs." That sounds like a phrase that's been tidied up. In a conventional play, that might not matter, but it does in a piece such as this, which stakes everything on authenticity. The script is notably short on stops and starts. Everything sounds like a soundbite.

You notice this more because the play is, considering its setting, remarkably short on conflict. It is so intent on demonstrating its characters' common humanity it neglects the things that divide them. Jews and Arabs only appear together in one scene, set in a hummus store, where the sight of an unguarded bag sends everyone into terror. It's the most dramatic scene, though it violates the evening's premise. (Was Soans himself present at this incident? Did one of his subjects tell him about it? If so, who? And how can that person guarantee the accuracy of everyone else's dialogue?)

Most of the rest is monologue, a form that is even more limiting than usual here because for the most part the speakers talk only of their own communities. We hear that the two sides live in a state of mutual fear and resentment, but only in passing. We are mainly told how each group feels about its own friends and neighbours.

Surprisingly, given the alternative theatre's reputation for leftiness, we are made more aware of the Israelis' sufferings than of the Palestinians'. Maybe Jews talk more. More likely, it's a matter of suicide bombings lending themselves more easily to exciting set-piece narrative than harassment at checkpoints. Whatever you think of it politically, it's a weakness dramatically. The underlying grievances on both sides are barely mentioned, let alone discussed. The play does, though, perform a valuable service in giving a voice to the region's Christians, consigned to the sidelines on a good day, caught in the crossfire on a bad.

One of them comes closest to being the play's voice. She has a compassionate heart and an austere outlook, as well as being one of the best cooks, and she doubts any of the grassroots attempts at understanding -- inter-cultural sports, protesting mothers, etc. -- will bear fruit. She might be passing ironic judgment on the play.

She's played with dignity by Barbara Gordon, one of seven performers giving multiple accomplished performances. But that too is a problem. Sometimes when an actor you thought was still playing an Israeli is now an Arab, it makes a point; other times, it's confusing. Beyond that, the actors are in the same bind as the script; they try hard to be conversational and end up falling into familiar stage rhythms. Victor Ertmannis, whose roles include the hummus-maker and a bus driver on what's virtually a suicide route, is the most persuasive. The others are David Fox, whose familiar massive presence accommodates surprising versatility, Mark McGrinder, Jeff Miller, Kimwun Perehinec, and Maria Ricossa as a widow who could afford to live anywhere but stays in Israel because it's where she feels humanly connected. The play is directed, with great attention to culinary detail, by Joel Greenberg, who three years ago staged The Laramie Project, a venture in the same documentary style that concentrated, more successfully, on one sensational story in one town and let it ramnify. Here the ingredients remain obstinately scattered. They don't, in fact, cook.
