Jon Stewart, columnist. Solemn music played as Oscar's statue revolved, untouched and unclaimable. Then we cut to the stage, on which stood three silver columns. One of them rose, and out from under it stood our host. He seemed slightly nervous, slightly cool, which is what we expected of him.

It was a very short monologue. He stood at the front of the stage, apparently unfazed by the absence of a desk (which you cant always say for him on The Daily Show) and possibly moving less than any host in Oscar history. Its not just that he isn't Billy Crystal; he isn't even David Letterman, whom I remember as roaming around the stage in apparent agonies of embarrassment. Im not knocking him. I liked Letterman as an Oscar host. I respected his out-of-placeness. Its the only time I've ever found him funny.

Stewart takes to it more naturally, while seemingly aware that he still has a reputation as an iconoclast to take care of. The stars in the theatre audience probably share his ambivalence. They'd like to make fun of the spectacle and what it represents, but theyre part of it. Jack Nicholson sat in the front row, as usual. At one point, he seemed to be threatening Stewart not so much through his teeth as with them.

The monologue fell into two parts. The first was about the writers strike and how grateful everybody was that it was over. It was surprisingly tame, hardly worth calling the writers back for. Then, by way of reflections on the self-congratulation about to run rampant ("We're going to spend the next four to five hours giving each other golden statues") we proceeded to consideration of the films themselves, or at least of the five nominees for best picture, all but one of them about psychopaths. (Thank God for teen pregnancy.)

Stewart's amiable double bind became apparent as he started listing and commenting on the nominees. Every title was greeted with fervent applause, by its clique of employees and supporters in the house, or -- who knows? -- by people who genuinely liked it. They seemed genuinely to think that Stewart agreed with them, though his purpose at this juncture was to make fun of them. "Thank God for Atonement," he said, and then after the cheering had died down, at last a film that does justice to the passion and raw sexuality of Yom Kippur. Some members of my family saw the punchline coming, others didnt, but we all found it funny. Personally, Ive been waiting for someone to make that joke ever since the novel came out. I also relished his words to Diablo Cody on her progression from exotic dancer to screenwriter for Juno ("I hope you're enjoying the pay cut"), and most of all to his Bushian admonition to keep movies about the Iraq war running in theatres for 100 years, despite their dismal commercial record: "Withdrawing the Iraqi movies will only embolden the audience -- YOU CAN'T LET THE AUDIENCE WIN."

Let him come back next year. Unless Stephen Colbert wants to do it. In character.
