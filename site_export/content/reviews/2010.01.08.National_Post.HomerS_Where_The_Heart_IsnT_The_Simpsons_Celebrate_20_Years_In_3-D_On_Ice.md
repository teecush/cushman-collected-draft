This is the 20th year of The Simpsons so some celebration is called for, something perhaps on the order of the recent multi-part epic commemorating Monty Python.

But what we actually get is The Simpsons 20th Anniversary Special: In 3-D! On Ice!, a mingy single program, whose "executive producer, writer and director" Morgan Spurlock is also its presenter, and who strolls through it in the apparent belief that it's about him.

There aren't many clips, although those there are--mainly drawn from The Simpsons' earlier, greater years -- are joyful. Otherwise the prevailing note is unfocused sycophancy; the program takes The Simpsons' greatness as a given without ever, except in the most superficial way, asking what it consists of. It's way behind the show itself, which has always been as good at mocking itself as at mocking everything else. It marked a previous anniversary with its own retrospective, presided over by the oleaginous omnipresent actor Troy ( "You may remember me from such previous infomercials as ...") McClure. He asked, rhetorically, "Who knows what further wacky antics these characters will get up to until the show becomes unprofitable?"

That day, the special makes very clear, has yet to dawn. Merchandising tie-ins alone would keep The Simpsons in profit. This fact has also been unblushingly acknowledged in the show itself, with Bart in particular always ready to comment on the extreme tackiness of the products involved.

There are interviews with writers, actors and executives; Matt Groening, who started it all, comes over as furry and friendly. A reel of outtakes, enclosed with the press screener, reveals however that some of the best material wasn't used. One is the declaration of Nancy Cartwright, voice of Bart, that -- what with short hours, great material, good company and (one assumes) never-ending residuals -- she has "the best job in America." All the actors emphasize the importance of the animation being fitted to their voice-tracks rather than the other way around: a vital fact about the comic strength of The Simpsons, and one that the finished film doesn't mention, let alone explore. Dan Castellanata, who as Homer is the greatest voice of all, appears in both the accepted and rejected footage hidden behind dark glasses, as if to deny himself any independent existence.

Castellanata is also the voice of Mayor Quimby and Krusty the Klown who, though it's a tough call, may be the most venal characters in Springfield. We learn from Groening that Krusty is based on Rusty Nails, a real clown from his own childhood who wasn't venal at all.

That's nice. Spurlock gets to travel to Scotland to investigate whether Groundskeeper Willie hails from Glasgow or Aberdeen. Some of the best characters, though, go completely unmentioned: the divinely incompetent Police Chief Wiggum, for example, not to mention those magnificently haughty extra-terrestrials Kang and Kodos. The Halloween episodes, which are their preserve, aren't mentioned either. So with the show's superb musical sequences: Moby chooses Homer's Mr. Plow song as his favourite Simpsons number, which, given the existence of the "Oh Streetcar" episode, is pathetic.

The Simpsons is subversive, like all good comedy, but it isn't anarchic. It has structure. This distinguishes it from its imitators which start from the premise that there aren't any rules and so deny themselves the fun of breaking them.

There is some pretense at showing opposition to The Simpsons, but it's very tame: some risible protests from the Catholic League, and the well-worn sight of the first president Bush enjoining American families to be more like The Waltons and less like The Simpsons. A forlorn hope, George: All families are more like The Simpsons than they are like The Waltons. More amusingly, the head of the Nuclear Energy Institute complains, in a resigned sort of way, that "most people learn about nuclear energy from The Simpsons." (He's probably right.)

The one really damning criticism gets laughed off; I refer to the fact that, in the second of its two decades if not for longer, the show has gone terribly downhill.

The Simpsons took about two years to find its legs --and, especially in Homer's case, its voice. Its next four or five years were perfect, infinitely quotable and limitlessly rewatchable. Anything after that is depressing. The show used to pack every minute with jokes, verbal and visual, all of them pertinent, all of them inspired. Also, it never let up on its narrative. Now it's flabby.

What's billed as "the landmark 450th episode" also airs on Sunday, and it's a case in point. It starts well enough; the pre-credit sequence is up to legendary standards, and the main plot-line seems promising. Krusty's sponsors insist on him hiring a co-host, a sugary Julie Andrews update called Princess Penelope. The little girls love her, everyone else hates her, especially Krusty himself, until he has a change of heart, from which point things get soggy.

As for the Simpsons themselves: Marge is practically invisible, Bart and Lisa go through motions and Homer is confined to a limp sub-plot, about being lured away to a rival nuclear plant, which has no satiric point and is wrapped up with insulting casualness. The two plots barely nudge each other's elbows, a terrible decline for a show that was once an ongoing triumph of architectural elegance. If we're meant to be celebrating a Simpsons milestone, shouldn't the Simpsons be at the centre of it? For all the teeming life of Springfield, the show's strength has always been that it's a family entertainment. That and the fact that Homer -- as every man will guiltily admit --is Everyman.
