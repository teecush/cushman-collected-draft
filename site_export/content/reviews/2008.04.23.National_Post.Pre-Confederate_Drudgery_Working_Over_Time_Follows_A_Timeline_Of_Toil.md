Each episode of Working Over Time begins with Jeff Douglas telling us about Donald Alexander Smith. He was the railroad financier and Hudson's Bay Company director who got to drive in the last celebratory spike on the Canadian Pacific Railroad. We get a momentary shot of Douglas impersonating Smith, looking every inch the Victorian worthy. Then we get a shot of him, looking not too impressed by the ceremony, as one of "the guys who drove all the other spikes." The series is about that guy, and about all those who came before and after him, doing all the impossible backbreaking jobs that created this country. It is in fact an alternative history of Canada.

It's a lot more gleeful than that sounds, largely due to the personality and the methods of Douglas himself. He takes us across the continent and down the centuries, finding out the things people had to do, doing them himself and conscientiously dressing for each part. He's delighfully informative and self-deprecating. Being taught by experts -- either those who are still obliged to live in the old ways or who are doing it as some kind of project -- he has repeated shots at doing as they do. Most of the time, he's not very good at it. But he isn't disgracefully bad either, and there's nothing demeaning about the process, either for him or for us. When he laughs at himself, as he frequently does -- either at the time or in retrospective commentary -- the laughter is warming, sometimes even healing.

Tonight's first episode, "Staying Alive," begins briskly with the injunction that "13,000 years ago, man's first job was to survive." Douglas is in moccassins -- maybe we've moved on a couple of millennia -- as he says that the ability to make fire in the most primitive circumstances was "the difference between life and death," and adds, "I guess I would have died." The next job was "to make a weapon out of a rock --hunting large animals before the arrow was invented." Cue shots of animals on cave walls, and Douglas being handed an Aztec weapon which might, he muses, have been used to hunt a mammoth. And then there was the business of skinning it.

That's just in comparatively temperate zones. We move to the Arctic, where "it's not too easy to find something to eat" and "I may get the chance to kill a baby seal--not sure how I feel about that." He learns to build a shelter, and then, shifting ground again to Queen Charlotte's Island on the West Coast, to take a giant tree and turn it into an ocean-going canoe. "They used these canoes to travel far distances; even" -- here's a jaw-dropper -- "as far as Japan." But trade and exploration cut both ways and, over on the other side of the country, Canada got its first European visitors. A thousand years ago the Vikings arrived, and "there goes the neighbourhood." Even the Vikings get some sympathy, though, as they'd come here in search of "low-grade bog ore," which you'd find just where you'd expect. Skipping a few more centuries, we also get to identify with the Jesuits who branched out from Quebec as missionaries to the First Nations but found that the tribes had a lot to teach them--about snowshoes, for example. We learn that, sleeping in log-houses, fifty people to a dorm, there was so much smoke you might go blind or die of a respiratory disease. There were other intruders with worldlier aims -- the Hudson's Bay company, for example. "It looks like the white guys are planning on staying, and that can't be good."

Tomorrow's episode shows that those white guys had at least to earn their keep. We backtrack to 1497, with John Cabot arriving, along with "18 to 20 guys from Bristol who did the actual work" but who found it preferable to staying in jail. Flash forward to 1837, and I mean "flash" because that is when somebody had the literally bright idea of building a lighthouse to cut down on the ships that were being wrecked on the coast of "New-found-land" (Douglas does deliver the province's name like that). We've always known that lighthouse-keeping was a lonely life, but did you realize that the light had to be re-charged every two hours, so someone in the family had to stay up all night? There were more companionable jobs. You could be a pioneer, learn how to plough and to flail ("Flail the grain," says Douglas' kindly instructor, "don't flail yourself"). It seems the only job tougher than the pioneer's was that of the pioneer's wife, keeping house and slaving over a hot log fire. A helpful lady (from a present-day pioneer village?) fills in a few more details. "Most of the time you're pregnant. It's a very difficult job." And kids at a later stage of development are naturally rambunctious. "Don't pull on Mommy's skirt," says Douglas, who at this point is wearing one, along with apron and mob cap, "mommy's trying to work here."

Not surprisingly, Douglas decides that what he always wanted to be was a lumberjack. He hadn't realized that this involved getting from the lower part of the tree to the higher, literally on a springboard. It's scary. It's also, like the whole show, a little exhilarating to watch, and a little shaming. And there's better or worse to come. This is Douglas's promise for the rest of the week: "We're gonna build towns [and] cities and come up with some of the messiest, smelliest, dirtiest jobs ever devised by the mind of man. I'm looking forward to it." - Working Over Time airs on History Television at 2 p.mand 9 p.m. until Friday.
