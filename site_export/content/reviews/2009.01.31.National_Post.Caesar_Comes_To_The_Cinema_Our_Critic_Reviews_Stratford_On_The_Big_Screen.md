Last years Stratford Festival production of Caesar and Cleopatra (author Bernard Shaw, on a season pass from Niagara-on-the-Lake) began with a neat little coup-de- theatre. The audience arrived in the Festival Theatre to find itself confronted by the immobile figure of a beaked Egyptian deity, presumably with an actor inside. We waited, those of us who already knew the play, to hear him deliver the long prologue with which Shaw brings us up to speed on the Roman-Egyptian political situation c. 50 BC. But he fooled us: He just stood there until the lights went down, and then slid gracefully out of our sight via a trap door. Way to make your audience feel winked at.

In the filmed version of this production (see it in a Cineplex theatre at 1 p. m. today, or wait for it to hit TV) only the last beat of this faux-overture is retained, since sitting in front of a screen, watching nothing happening, would be a lousy way of initiating a dramatic experience. Thus truncated, it doesn't make much sense, but we do still get the transformation that follows: straight into the first desert encounter between the aged Julius Caesar and the teenaged queen, he haranguing the Sphinx as his spiritual equal, she hiding terrified between its paws. The sets for this show were always spectacular and I enjoyed being reminded of them. As to whether they'd make the same impression on a viewer who hadnt seen them onstage, I have my doubts.

The production was filmed onstage, before an audience, from an array of strategically-based cameras; so we only see the sets in undefined sections. Economics apart, this is probably the best way to do it, short of a complete studio rethink; which, for a show as detailed in its theatricality as this one, would probably be counter-productive anyway. We get this one complete with spectators who are enthusiastically audible and intermittently visible.

All the same, the best of this version is in close-ups and two-shots. The film emphasizes both the strengths and the weaknesses of Des McAnuffs original production. The strengths are in the two lead performances and in the way they bounce off one another. Shaw idealized Caesar by humanizing him, making him a mouthpiece for all the right political and ethical ideas (the Shavian ones, that is) while granting him a modicum of vanity and a superabundance of schoolboy impetuosity. He needs an actor equally adept at striking poses and deflating them. Christopher Plummer is superb at both. In his seventies, he may well be at the peak of his powers, vocally, intellectually, and maybe even physically. (Though we do notice, more than onstage, that a double has been slyly edged into place for his plunge into the harbour of Alexandria.) Whether in rhetoric or cross-talk, he never lets either the meaning or the rhythm of a line slip by him. Fifty years his junior, Nikki James can hardly have the same technical command, but she, too, captures every mood. Cleopatra, unlike Caesar, develops, and you can follow her here from frightened, credulous girl to a queen whose capricious authority Caesar can applaud if not approve. By reducing their relationship to a chaste interplay of teacher and student, Shaw sanitized history outrageously; but he had his agenda, and Plummer and James carry it out, almost to the letter. Theirs is, among other things, a contest in roguishness.

And along with it goes an embodiment of the play's central debate, between a virtuous, superhuman politics and a vindictive, expedient one: the former winning all the debating points and the latter, as always, carrying off the game. What should be surrounding and strengthening it, though, goes too often by default. Shaw wrote two alternative prologues; this production, wanting to get to the stars as soon as possible, spurns them both. This turns out to be symptomatic. When Rome and Egypt confront one another in the play's second act, the conflict is played out in the broadest terms; theres no cut-and-thrust, everyone -- especially the Egyptians, nearly all cast against type, which in Shaw is generally fatal -- shouts as though they were at a public meeting. Later, when Caesar and Cleopatras philosophies come into direct conflict, and they appeal to their friends and supporters for judgment, some small but significant cuts weaken the issues and reduce everyone's stature; it matters not just that Britannus, Caesar's ultra-respectable slave, casts his vote against his master but why he does.

These cuts were in the stage production, too. But cuts can sometimes make a play seem longer and, with the spectacle inevitably reduced, this play-on-film feels long in a way it didn't in the theatre. But the lead performances make it worthwhile. And there is a prologue of sorts. Plummer, in his own person, sits on the edge of the stage and welcomes us, and he could hardly do it more charmingly. We don't quite get the best of both worlds, but we do get a pleasing taste of both. - For more information on participating theatres, visit cineplex.com.Weekend Post
