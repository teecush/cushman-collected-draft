Wadji Mouawads Scorched, as translated by Linda Gaboriau, contains one line that, in the 18 months since the play was first seen at the Tarragon, has become justifiably famous. A French-Canadian lawyer, who has trouble with English idioms, talks about being caught "between the devil and the Blue Danube. He says a lot of things like that. In a modest spirit of emulation, I'd like to say that the revival of this extravagantly praised play leaves me feeling, more than ever, like a skeleton on the beach.

It is, I readily admit, a play of greater size and assurance than we're accustomed to. It erects an elaborate structure, with building blocks ranging from Greek myth to todays headlines, in order to tell a story about genocidal warfare and its effects on countries and families. It moves nimbly around time and space: from contemporary Canada to both the past and present of an unnamed ravaged country in the Middle East. The strongest resemblance is to Mouawads own birth country of Lebanon; one of the pivotal offstage events is a massacre in a refugee camp. But there are also ingredients that suggest Afghanistan, and places even farther afield such as Sudan or Somalia. An atrocity is an atrocity wherever it happens, true. But a generalized Everycountry does not necessarily bring it more forcefully home than an identifiable place from which we might extrapolate for ourselves. It also leads to generalized characters. The problem with Scorched is that its impressive machinery is operated by puppets.

There is one exception, one case in which a real insight is embodied in a real person. This is a torturer (Alex Poch-Goldin) who once had dreams of fame as a musician -- in a preparatory career as a sniper, he amused himself by playing air-rifle -- and regards his current vocation as another branch of show business; his victims are there to entertain him, i. e., to stop him from being bored. But even this revelation is weakened by having its subject be all too aware of it; he makes his avowal, flourishing, a red nose that's also a plot-point, before the tribunal that's trying him. The other characters are lay-figures jumping through predetermined hoops.

It starts hopefully enough. The Montreal lawyer summons a brother and sister, both young adults, to the reading of their late mothers will. Mother was an immigrant from the plays other country," and she and her children hadn't spoken in years. The air is thick with resentment, full of sullen silences; there are flashier scenes in Richard Rose's production, but this is the best. The siblings are dispatched by posthumous maternal fiat to find the father they thought dead, and a brother they never knew they had. The couple are very well played by Sophie Goulet and Sergio Di Zio; she is a mathematician, for symbolic reasons, and he is a boxer, for no discernible reason at all. Other questions arise. Why does the lawyer (delightfully played by Alon Nashman) get his English proverbs wrong but nothing else? It isnt very plausible or, after the first few times, very funny. It seems like a cut-price way of supplying "human interest," something the play otherwise lacks.

Denying it to us may be a reason for having Nawal the mother, whose flashback story unfolds in counterpoint with her childrens present-tense one, played by three different actresses: Janick Hebert, Sarah Orenstein and Nicola Lipman, in ascending order of age. This is despite the fact that she has a friend and contemporary who's played by one actress throughout; and the probability that, though all three performers are good, Orenstein as the middle one could play the entire woman herself. She's impressively flinty in what she does, but if she were allowed some variation, she might even persuade us that Nawal is one real woman instead of a series of poses. As it is, her only consistency is as a vessel of misfortune: robbed of a child when young, raped and tortured when older. Her positive attributes, like teaching herself to read in a savagely sexist culture, are painted on. Her decisive action, which comes out of nowhere, is to kill, offstage, a guerrilla leader of whom we have only just heard.

Orenstein, replacing Kelli Fox, is one of two changes from the original cast. The territory has indeed been purged of Foxes, since David Fox has been succeeded by Jerry Franken, his old sparring-partner from The Drawer Boy and elsewhere. A less idiosyncratic actor than his predecessor, Franken makes his constant reappearances as various elders seem less like a running gag, but he throws away one of the plays best speeches in which a long train of reprisals and counter-reprisals is traced back to end precisely nowhere.

That is the plays pithiest point. A woollier one is that civil war is a kind of incest. Cue the Oedipus legend, scrambled and repackaged. But Oedipus wasn't just a victim; he was an actor, who dedicated himself to discovering the truth. Nawal, from beyond the grave, sends her children to discover it, and it proves hideous. She could just as well have told them while she was alive, but maybe she thought sleuthing would be better for their souls. Or maybe it's just so there could be a play. Either way, the generations arrive at a posthumous reconciliation thats purest mush The first-night audience started applauding before this final scene: at best, a sign that the timing is off and at worst, that the audience not only thinks but hopes its over. Still, there was a standing ovation at the real close. I stayed in my seat. I'd hoped that a second viewing would make me like the play more. But it made me like it less.
