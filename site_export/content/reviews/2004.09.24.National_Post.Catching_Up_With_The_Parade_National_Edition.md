BROADWAY: THE GOLDEN AGE

- - -

There's a Rodgers and Hammerstein musical titled Me and Juliet that contains a song called Intermission Talk. It's sung by the ensemble, who are divided between cynics sneering that "the theatre is dying" and enthusiasts proclaiming that "the theatre is living." The latter, of course, get the last ringing word. This was 1953, part of the era in which R&H themselves were the kings of Broadway, though most of the shows they mention in the song are musicals, not plays. The period 1945-60 was a prosperous and creative time for musicals; it was also the era of Arthur Miller, Tennessee Williams and the late Eugene O'Neill.

This, roughly, is the period commemorated in Rick McKay's documentary Broadway: The Golden Age. It begins with a clip of Carol Channing, in her prime, singing Before the Parade Passes By from Hello Dolly, which is scary, though not as alarming as the sight and sound of Channing herself in interview, 40 years on. Her face is almost entirely masked by dark brown shades while her voice, which once ranged from squawk to croak, is now just croak. She's talking about what a unique experience the live theatre is, and she seems to imply -- or maybe it's the context that does it for her -- that Broadway and the theatre are the same thing.

Two things about the film are already clear: It's hung up on divas, and it takes a scattershot approach to its material. There are at least two movies here -- one about the shows themselves, and the other about the offstage lives of the people involved in them -- and they're recklessly intermingled.

McKay himself came to New York as a stage-struck youth from Beech Grove, Ind., and found -- like everybody before him, no matter when they arrived -- that it wasn't what it was, that the parade had in fact passed by. So he set out to talk to and record as many as he could find of the people he'd read about in newspapers, magazines and history books. He corralled an impressive number of celebrated interviewees, most of them heroically averse to fancy Hollywood notions such as facelifts. (They tend to be shot in unflattering and unvarying close-up.) He could not, understandably, bear to sacrifice a single one of them. They are of various vintages, so it never becomes clear just what the boundaries of Broadway's golden age might be.

Some speakers look back -- as spectators if not as participants - -as far as the 1930s, though it's a fact that even then Broadway types were fondly remembering the wildly productive days before the talkies and before the crash. Most would zero in on the '40s and '50s, though some would extend the glory days into the '60s. Why else begin with Hello Dolly? But then some might pinpoint that show as the start of the decadence. Stephen Sondheim analyzes the phenomenon of the Compulsory Standing Ovation (he's about the only one who analyzes anything) and it's arguable that Dolly was the show that began it.

Broadway still produces new musicals; it no longer produces new plays. There is at least as much here -- perhaps surprisingly, given the film's sensibility -- about drama as about musicals. Everybody, musical or legit, names as their favourite performer Laurette Taylor, who created the mother in The Glass Menagerie. She never made a talkie, but McKay has dug up a screen test that shows her to have been as breathtakingly natural as her admirers claim.

The same can't be said for those who are held up as her peers. Kim Stanley, caught in an onstage clip from Bus Stop, looks and sounds Hollywood sincere, while Marlon Brando, also described as a guy who might have wandered in off the street, has always seemed to me stylized as hell. But maybe I've seen too many Brando imitations.

As this suggests, much of the film's content has been determined by what film and TV clips are available to back up the interviews. There can be no other reason for devoting a whole segment to Robert Goulet and his insecurities about being in Camelot. Another self- contained section re-tells the familiar story of Shirley MacLaine's discovery as an understudy in The Pajama Game. This time, though, MacLaine gets to do much of the telling, and she's charming.

Carol Burnett tells about sharing an apartment -- and sharing a dress -- with four other aspirant actresses when she first came to New York. (That kind of struggle still goes on, of course, but it's only interesting when the people doing it have become famous.) Most contributors talk about how much more camaraderie there was then than there is now, and it's probably true; there were more shows, therefore there was more of a community. One gets a sense of everything being less desperate, less pretentious, largely because going to the shows was so affordable. We hear how people shuddered when top price rose to an unheard-of twenty dollars. No wonder Broadway was part of the culture then, while it's just a tourist attraction now.

Other reasons are offered for the decline. West Side Story is rightly singled out as a capstone of the period and given extensive coverage. On the other hand, its abolition of the distinction between singing and dancing ensembles is also cited as another nail in the coffin. The use of mics, which crept in in the '50s and is now universal, is unanimously denounced; I wouldn't argue, though I can't help suspecting that in Shakespeare's time, when roofed-in theatres first appeared, there were Elizabethan barnstormers who looked back to the good old days and swore that all this new- fangled candlelight was killing the drama.

McKay ends up acknowledging that today's young audiences and performers may in time look back upon this as a golden age. I don't think he really believes this, and neither frankly do I; but anything's possible. Perhaps, in 40 years' time, people will think back fondly to the great days of 2004 when Broadway had theatres.

Rating Three
