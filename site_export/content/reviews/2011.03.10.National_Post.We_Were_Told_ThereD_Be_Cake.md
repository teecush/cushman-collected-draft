If I were looking for some kind of unifying theme in Second City's latest revue, I'd say that most of the sketches concern dysfunctional relationships. But then most comedy is about dysfunctional relationships. So I won't look. Anyway This Party's a Riot! doesn't quite live up to its name, and is less good than its immediate predecessor, partly because it's not as striking visually, partly because its horizons are more limited.

Something Wicked Awesome This Way Comes, the previous go-round, recognized that politics might be good for something more than an irrelevant show title. (Although, admittedly, it's hard not to sympathize with the current team's quoted opinion that jokes about Toronto Mayor Rob Ford would be too easy and therefore not funny.) This one gets us firmly back in the living room, the bedroom, the office, the family car and Starbucks. It should appeal to anyone who has ever wanted to shoot a barista. It's a very good night out for other reasons, too. For instance:

The car scene A complacent boomer reminisces with infuriating smugness about the '60s while his son bitterly complains about dad's failure to die, or at least retire, thereby freeing the younger generation to get a job and pay off its student loan. Turns out they're on their way to the old people's home to pick up grandpa whom they've both treated appallingly, and who in a mumbling but humbling sort of way (shades of The Simpsons) lets them know it.

This scene comes early in the show and is probably the best thing in it. In its casual way, it draws blood. It's also a great showcase for the three men in the cast: Bob Baker (a big baby who can really boom) outrageously preening, Adam Cawley indignantly smouldering and Kris Siddiqi furiously muttering.

The office scene Might also be called the suggestions box scene. Several funny things happen, but none funnier than Dale Boyer standing pained and completely silent at the side of the stage while everybody else is sitting down. The relevant suggestion is that the office needs more chairs. It's nixed.

The other office scene This is dominated by Inessa Frantowski, a newcomer, lamenting the fact that she has to come to work every day. Also that she has to decide which weather-appropriate clothes to wear. We hear you, sister, and your existential angst.

This is one of two numbers in the evening that have something resembling a good punchline. The other one I won't spoil for you, except to say that it rounds off not only its own first scene, but the entire first half.

The club scene Two adolescent dudes jerk their carcasses about, more or less to the music, while boasting about all the chicks they expect to pick up here at the hub of the nightlife universe. ("It was worth the drive from Woodbridge.")

Cawley and Siddiqi are hilariously clueless here, especially the tall, thin Cawley showing off his post-workout physique. I suppose this may be the title number.

The hospital scene Carly Heffernan, as an oblivious nurse, tells her troubles to a patient who is comatose and wishes, to judge from his electronically amplified thoughts, that she were, too. This one gets quite dark.

The divorce scene A bewildered teenager (Cawley again; he's really the star this time), already traumatized by his parents' split, has to cope with the fact that his stepdad, with whom he's just decided to bond, is about to become his ex-stepdad. There are further complications, reminding me at least of the old calypso about "shame and scandal in the family."

The gesture at world affairs scene, doubling as the computer scene Actually, this isn't all that great, but the idea carries it. Middle Eastern revolutionaries, empowered by the Internet, are too glued to their screens to get up and take part in anything.

The school scene This touches on the multiculturalism debate, and occasions the best (and most truly political) line in the show: "I have no clue what it means to be a Canadian but I'm pissed off when people aren't."

There are also the zombie scene, the threesome scene, the compulsive-hoarder scene and a very good mistaken pickup scene. And there's the fact that the show doesn't know how to conclude, and decides to end it all with an improvisation that will (one hopes) vary from night to night but refused to take at the premiere. Of course, they could improvise still further and ditch the idea. In an earlier attempt to involve the audience, they also pulled CBC's Jian Ghomeshi up onstage, but I don't suppose that he'll be there every night. The show was directed by Bruce Pirrie.
