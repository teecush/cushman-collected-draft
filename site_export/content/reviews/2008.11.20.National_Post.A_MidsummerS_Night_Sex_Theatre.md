A program note for this new Canadian adaptation of Miss Julie reminds us that in 1888, when August Strindberg's original play was first published, it was described as "filthy," "debauched" and "repulsive." (And this in what we think of as permissive Sweden. But maybe things were different back then.) The same note goes on to remark, quite accurately, that in performance the play never turns out to be as much fun as those adjectives suggest. This production sets out to restore some of the excitement. Despite all kinds of deficiencies, it succeeds.

Miss Julie: Sheh'mah takes its new suffix from a First Nations word meaning "white person." The Canadian Miss Julie, like her Nordic namesake, is a rich man's daughter surrounded by servants. In this case the servants (in her word and theirs) are Indians. We meet two of them: the absent master's valet, here named Jonny, and his fiancee, Christie Ann the cook, in whose kitchen the action takes place. Just outside there's a party going on, and it's got Miss Julie very hot. During the course of the night -- in Strindberg's it's Midsummer's Eve -- she and Jonny, each of them with a barrelful of issues, have sex while ChristieAnn slumbers next door. It's her first time; it certainly isn't his, as he takes brutal pleasure in informing her. They make frantic plans to run away together, maybe open a hotel. The plans are dashed when the lady reveals that she has no money of her own. He slinks back into service; she, having passed through disillusion and degradation (in her own mind, at least) takes the traditional heroic way out.

The play has been re-set in the 1920s, so we are not, despite the attendant rhetoric, getting a full update. Kick Theatre, the new company responsible, announces itself in another note as "devoted to producing catalytic, dynamic theatre that reintroduces audiences to the cathartic power of the stage." That's a lot to take on. It also presumes a catharsis-starved audience, desperate to get its kicks from Kick. That sounds patronizing, as does the assumption that the audience would be unable to appreciate the tensions underlying Miss Julie unless those tensions are dug up and deposited in their own backyard. We've all read books, seen plays and watched movies depicting masters and servants. We have historical imaginations. We know about class. We can relate.

It may be splitting hairs, but I suspect that what happens in transplants like this one is that the actors relate to something immediate to them, and the audience picks up on that. Kick is right about one thing: Most translations of Strindberg are awful. Tara Beagan, credited as "play-re-wright" (a new word on me, and one that I'm not crazy to meet again) and herself part-Native, has come up with a text that has awkwardnesses of its own, but is mostly direct and dynamic. Part of this is certainly due to the added bite of making the social distinctions racial as well: something that illuminates Miss Julie's masochistic fascination as well as Jonny and Christie Ann's well-voiced resentments. But more of it comes from making the sex more explicit, verbally and visually, than Strindberg would have dared, though probably not more than he would have liked. Artistic choices merge with budgetary necessities. The play unfolds in real time; in the original merrymaking extras burst onto the stage while the lovers are presumed to be going at it in the wings. Here the party animals remain out of sight, while Jonny and Julie couple in plain view, violently and convincingly. It makes sense of a lot of things.

Melee Hutton's production has trouble with matters like getting people persuasively in and out of doors, but it keeps a firm grip on emotional temperature. This is despite some alarming unevenness in the acting. Christine Horne obviously knows what the pale and driven Miss Julie, in all her quivering self-destructive delicacy, is about, but in a play full of long speeches, she is embarrassingly unable to give shape or variety to any lasting more than a couple of lines. (She also does some strange physical things, like nearly strangling herself with a shawl that seems to be doubling as a blanket, and sliding off a bed with a pronounced bump.) Darrell Dennis has more technical command, as well as a sullen bravado that's right for Jonny; one thing that neither the original nor this adaptation does is to glorify the oppressed. But he swallows many of his words and, on the first night, stumbled over more. (The whole show, in fact, seemed under-rehearsed.) Gail Maurice infuses Christie Ann, who knows very well what the other two are up to, with an appealing air of tolerant disdain in her first scene, but she too fails to sustain. Teresa Przybylski's wood-lined set has a touch of setting to it, as if paying homage to the original Scandinavian location as well as the new British Columbian one.

And I have to confess: I have seen Miss Julie done by the top classical companies in Britain and Canada, in productions that were technically superior to this one; none of them held my attention as this one did. - Miss Julie: Sheh'mah is at The Theatre Centre until Nov. 29.
