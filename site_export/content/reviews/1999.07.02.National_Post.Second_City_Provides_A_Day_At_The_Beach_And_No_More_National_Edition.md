NUDE BEACH WEAR: 100% OFF

The Second City, Toronto

It was some time during the Trudeau era, when I was a mere visitor to Toronto, that I first took in a Second City revue. I was impressed, to the point of hysteria. A couple of years later I returned, and the magic had flown; perhaps I had gone sour but I think it's likelier that they had gone off.

Revisiting the organization for their 46th show, I find that equilibrium has been restored. There are no signs of comic genius on show, but there is a good level of competence and invention. The six writer-performers are all agreeable, and seem to enjoy one anothers company: just as well, since there are very few solos. Kevin Dorffs direction shows them off with unobtrusive efficiency.

The title Nude Beach Wear: 100% Off (or, Hanlan's Don't Point) is fairly funny for a start (though the bit in brackets isnt). Its even relevant, since the closing song is actually about Toronto's nude beach. It's the only extended musical sequence in the show. Its also about the worst thing in it. Its hard of course to cut a title song and leave your show without a hook, but maybe they should go ahead and do it. Nobody would care; improvised revue -- Second City do not, alas, improvise in performance but presumably they still do in rehearsal -- has always thrived on inconsequentiality.

The first-half closer, on the other hand, is a high point: a Ms. Millennium competition played out between Miss America, who is brassy and supremely self-confident and refuses to let the compere finish his sentences, Miss Rest of the World, who has two invisible children at her breast and who builds onstage an equally invisible hut with accommodation for eight families, and Miss Canada, who lollops into view, is chronically self-deprecatory and waves her hand with supreme listlessness but who takes a vicious mile as soon as someone gives her a generous inch. (Now, is this display of worm- turning acute self-satire or just poignant wish-fulfillment?)

This sketch has the texture that comes from functioning effectively on two levels: as broad political satire (Miss C gives thanks to Petro Canada-- whoever thought that an oil company could regulate a whole government?) and as narrow entertainment lampoon, with the oleaginous host flanked by a pair of overflowingly celebratory show dancers billed as the Dancing Homosexuals: a coming- out that was long overdue.

Not much else in the show qualifies as political, though there is a token jab at the Harris government ("things will be hard -- then they'll just be difficult") and I guess you could count an episode in which each member of the cast murders another, all of them hoping to secure the reversion of the others' welfare cheques.

But then, as that scene reminds us, the personal is political and this show is plenty personal. Its very much into relationships. These can be marital. In the shows opening number, one of its best, a husband is dismayed to find that Carol, his wife's old friend from nursing college, is actually a man by that name, just back from a tour of duty in Somalia that seems to have been rewarding financially and in other ways (you give them their inoculations and their faces just light up).

They can be extra-marital, as in a masochistic blues that includes the memorable simile "love is like a sausage -- you don't want to see it being made." They can even be fraternal: In one scene, -- the best-written in the evening -- a teenager beseeches her gay, and somewhat shocked, older brother for helpful hints on how to administer fellatio. (Never blow, he advises urgently, "it's a misnomer.") Or they can be a matter of group dynamics; three couples go into ecstasies of competitiveness over a new parlour game.

The dominating presence in the cast is Lee Smart, who has a smooth, rotund demeanour and very emphatic teeth, which he flashes to particular effect as the Pope, communicating with his Canadian flock through minimalist and somewhat esoteric mime.

Balancing his expressive placidity, we have the equally expressive edginess of Melody Johnson, who excels at playing women (and one cat) who are either repressed or aggressive. (As Miss Canada, she gets to be both.) The other women are Tracy Dawson, who is usefully exuberant, and Mary Pat Farrell, who is usefully controlled. The other men are Gavin Crawford, who is economically wry, and Doug Morency, whose specialty is a homespun smirk.

Many of the numbers have trouble finding a punchline: a condition common in revues, and also in reviews.
