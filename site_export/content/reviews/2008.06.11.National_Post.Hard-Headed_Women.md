Four of our finest actresses appear in The Trojan Women, giving performances that are predictably memorable. (I know that phrase confounds past with future, but we are dealing with myth here.) What goes on around them is not so great.

This is despite the perennial topicality of the play. It may even be because of it. Greek tragedy is full of refugee dramas, but Euripides' play is the one with which it is easiest for us to identify, and that fact may lull us, artistically speaking, into a sense of false security. The setting is the end of the Trojan war. Troy has been sacked, the men have been killed and their widows and daughters, the former queen Hecuba at their head, huddle on the plains in what might be a primitive tent city, awaiting deportation and slavery.

Euripides has the reputation of being a more naturalistic writer than his predecessors Aeschylus and Sophocles, and to some extent this is true. But he still wrote within the same conventions, for performance in huge amphitheatres, for actors in masks, for a singing chorus and with a maximum limit of three speaking actors in any one scene. This solved some problems for him, but creates many for us. Marti Maraden's production treats them as though they don't exist.

For example: The play contains the first, and maybe still the greatest, atrocity scene in world drama, great because it's presented as a breach in nature, something previously unimaginable. It represents the birth of realpolitik. Hector, the Trojan warrior, left a young son; the Greek council has decided that the child must be killed, lest he too grow up to be a hero. Soldiers appear on stage to tear him from the arms of his mother, Andromache; he is to be thrown from a cliff, his head dashed on rocks. This is all explained in the silent boy's presence. How is he supposed to react? How are his elders supposed to react to him? Euripides didn't have to bother about this, but we do, unless we're going in for extreme stylization. I've no idea what the answer is, but it hasn't been found at Stratford,

where they just say the lines and hope for the best.

Actually the best, in this scene, does happen in the form of Seana McKenna's Andromache, whose personal grief moves by astonishing gradation into universal agony. For Andromache, as for most Greek sufferers, it's all about her but that's all right because she's Everywoman. Martha Henry's hard-bitten Hecuba watches her intently, watching other people -- ironically, compassionately, angrily -- being something that Henry may do better than any other actor on Earth. And on Earth, curled up on it, is where her performance begins. She rouses herself to face one calamity after another.

Hecuba, who's already lost a home, a husband and innumerable sons, is further deprived of a grandson and two daughters. One of the latter is the prophetess Cassandra, who's been given as concubine to Greek general Agamemnon. This is the cue for Kelli Fox to enter in a Woodstock wedding dress that does her no favours. (John Pennoyer's costumes, mixing periods in the cause of timelessness, are generally unfortunate.) The quartet is completed by Yanna Mc-Intosh, creaselessly beguiling as Helen, the putative cause of all the trouble, begging and charming her way out of death by stoning, just as the vengeful Hecuba knew and dreaded she would.

These four performances have a rigour that the rest of the show lacks. Nicholas Rudall's translation strikes a reasonable balance between stark and casual, but it has some awful lapses. Sean Arbuckle, as the humane herald lumbered with bringing all the bad news, should not have to bear the additional burden of beginning a bulletin with "I'm not good at this." For one thing, he is good at it. And then there is the eternal problem of the chorus, composed of captives. The venerable Joyce Campion is the standout; her very presence testifying to a catastrophe that crosses generations. But here too the production gestures towards formality then backs away from it. The chorines are best when singing (music by Marc Desormeaux), second best when speaking in unison and weakest when trying to make individual lines sound both ominous and sensible.

That leaves the gods, who speak a prologue that's bland to them, but bitterly ironic for everyone else. Greek tragedy draws on a vast mythological corpus: The writers could light up parts of the canvas as they chose, confident that their audience could fill gaps and draw conclusions. It's not so much subtext as ubertext. Here we have Poseidon and Athena, miffed that the Greeks haven't been making them the proper sacrifices, capriciously plotting their downfall, which will happen after the play ends. Poseidon, god of the ocean, is in naval uniform, and the two of them appear to be hanging out on the gangway of some beached battleship. I find the Greek view of divinity, Euripides' anyway, very plausible. It's the only theology that answers that old question about bad things happening to good people. The gods have the power but, with all that immortality to play with, they long ago got bored with us. In another view, they are us magnified, mainly for the worse. The Trojan Women is maybe the prime example of a play that's great without being, in any conventional sense, good. It has no plot, only episodes. It ends with Troy in flames: the only possible climax but halfheartedly depicted here. If you want to know what burning Troy was really like then, in a Stratford season full of cross-references, stroll along the Avon and catch the First Player's speech in Hamlet. - The Trojan Women runs until Oct. 5.
