Apparently I am part of a conspiracy. In his column in Wednesdays Globe and Mail, John McLachlan Gray accused me, along with Christie Blatchford and an anonymous front-page headline writer, of being suborned by the National Post to take part in what he termed a Tokyo Rose attack on all things Canadian. My own contribution to this remarkably offensive offensive was that I had written a less-than-enthusiastic review of Diana Krall's recent concert at the Hummingbird Centre in Toronto.

Let me get the ugliest part of this out of the way first. I have worked as a critic for the Post for three years, and during that time no editor, publisher or proprietor has told me either what to say or how to say it. I don't imagine that things are any different at the Globe, and I wonder why Mr. Gray finds it so hard to credit that writers on other papers might enjoy the same independence he does.

Now, it is true that early in my review I described Krall as "Canadas local girl"; in a Canadian paper, it seemed reasonable to acknowledge her Canadianness. It is rather a jump, though, to assume from that that any criticisms I made of her are caused by her being Canadian. Or rather -- this seems to be Mr. Grays main point -- that they are the result of her being Canadian and successful.

We've heard this tune before. We are always being told that Canadians have a national inferiority complex that makes them resent any of their compatriots who get ahead of the pack. (We hear it, amusingly enough, from both the left and the right, though usually in different contexts.) I don't see it. It seems to me that when a Canadian team wins an international game, or when a Canadian performer makes it in Hollywood, the enthusiasm across the country is deafening. It is true that success can inspire a backlash; but that has to do with imperfect human nature. There is nothing specifically Canadian about it. It has been known to happen in America. It certainly happens in Britain, which (I confess) is where Im from. Its weird, but the only evidence that Canadians have such a complex is their apparent compulsion to believe that they do.

This myth can obviously be very comforting to Canadians who are criticized; they can blame bad reviews on their compatriots' mean- spirited masochism. For my own part, I can see no way of dispelling it other than by subscribing to a sort of critical George Bush doctrine -- if I'm not with them, Im against them -- and giving all Canadian artists good reviews all the time. Perhaps this is what Mr. Gray wants; though I find it strange that, in his capacity as a playwright, he would rather be praised for his nationality than for his talent.

Actually, I find it hard to see what he wants through the contortions to which he subjects my review. A neutral reference to the orchestras fiddles and horns is construed as a sneer. I am told that I would have preferred synthesizers to a live band (I wouldn't, and never said so) and that I "saved [my] most withering contempt for Krall's fans," whom I never actually mentioned but whom I plainly felt to be dupes of a "commercial machine," a phrase he puts in quotes, although I never used it.

Here he seems to be joining hands with Kralls managers, one of whom disinterestedly wrote to the Post pointing out that his client had received standing ovations. Well, he has a job to do, but Mr. Gray must surely know -- as another correspondent has pointed out -- that standing ovations in big halls have long ceased to be a reliable index of quality either way. If an event is perceived to be large enough, audiences will practically enter applauding. This doesnt make them fools; it doesn't make them infallible, either.

I liked some things Krall did and didn't like others; and I dont think it takes a genius to note that, unlike Keith Jarretts, Krall's appearance is part of her package. But I don't think Mr. Gray was looking for nuance.

If I have any agenda regarding her, its this: Every so often a performer comes along and is hailed for rediscovering the classic American popular song. Those of us who love this music know that in fact it never went away and that dozens of singers, old and young, have been out there performing and preserving it for years, some of them extraordinarily well. Occasionally one of them catches the lightning, and it seems arbitrary which one. (It isnt just looks; you should see some of the sexy covers on the obscurer CDs in my collection.)

Before Krall there was Harry Connick Jr., and since her there has been Jane Monheit -- both American and, to my ears, her inferiors. One singer whom I prefer to any of them is Jeri Brown, who is Canadian. I did make a disparaging reference to a Joni Mitchell song in Krall's repertoire, but I try to spread my xenophobia around: The Krall arrangements that I described as soporific, at least when heard in bulk, are by Claus Ogerman, who is German; and her conductor, whom in other contexts I greatly admire, is Alan Broadbent, a New Zealander. Mr. Gray does give himself an out clause by suggesting it's not so much Kralls Canadianness as her not being from Toronto that I object to, but that doesnt wash: I like Robert Lepage.

He concludes his column by asking what Christie Blatchford and I would find to write about if we couldn't be anti-Canadian. I can't answer for Christie (except to say that I find it difficult to imagine her running out of subject matter), but I have been writing for this paper about theatre, mainly in and around Toronto, for the last three years and -- obviously -- 90% of the authors, actors and directors I have reviewed have been Canadian. I have subjected them to raves, pans and lots of things in between. And as long as I keep on doing the job, that is how I expect it to remain.
