HOMAGE

Buddies in Bad Times Theatre

Is everybody these days writing the same play? I have now seen three in a row that feature family conflicts both within and across generations, cross-cutting between past and present, and a house that's about to be sold from right underneath its occupant. There was Proof, there was El Paso and now there is James Harkness's Homage.

I confess: The three plays don't have much else in common, and El Paso, which is extremely busy, stands at the furthest possible remove from Homage, which is distinguished by its reverential stillness. There are scenes when almost everybody in it seems to be asleep. We start with a woman puttering around in a kitchen. In a living room, a step down on a gently split-levelled set, two men sit in the half-light; one appears to be snoozing, the other to be reading. The two groups are under the same roof, but at different times.

The house, we learn, is a Southern Ontario farmhouse; and the woman, we see, is baking a pie. We watch her preparing it and putting it in the oven, with the kind of tranced respect we have been trained to accord to actors carrying out domestic tasks in realistic detail.

She is of course the farmer's wife, and her name is Anne. The farmer, Wilfred, whom we meet a little later, suffers from a cough whose seriousness he refuses to acknowledge. It seems likely it was caused by nearby industrial pollution, but the victim will have nothing to do with this idea or with the protest that some of his neighbours are organizing. His spouse, meanwhile, writes letters to her son Jim, who split for the city when he was still in his teens and has not been home since.

He's back now, though, now being a little bit later than then. He is the slumped young man and, following the death of both his parents, he has taken possesion of the farm. Both he and it are in dire financial straits. Nick, the man doing the reading, is actually an accountant, and is going through the books. Having done so, he pronounces that Jim has no choice but to sell up. But it's difficult to get Jim, who drinks a lot, to take this or anything else seriously.

The title may conceivably be a triple pun: home, homage, homosexual. Jim is gay, and mordantly flippant. He fled home because he felt stifled by farm and family, and craved creativity. Nick, his boyhood friend, stayed behind, despite having a cosmpolitan background (well, his parents were Italian) that Jim had always envied. This being Buddies in Bad Times, one assumes Jim and Nick had once had a sexual relationship, but it's never explicitly stated. Anyway, Nick now has a wife and kids, a circumstance Jim seems to regard with mild derision.

He also, after Jim's departure, did his best to look after Anne and Wilfred. That cough killed Wilfred, and Anne had gone rapidly downhill after his death. Jim, though, has found Anne's journal, and is marvelling at how well it was written. He realizes that the artistic expressiveness he left home to find was there on the farm all along. And I didn't realize until I started this sentence how very Wizard of Oz that sounds.

David Oiye's direction goes for a style best described as narcoleptic lyricism. This has its effective moments; the scenic overlapping means Jim and Nick can exchange memories and recriminations while Anne and Wilfred slumber on the sofa right alongside them. But for much of the time, it is just somnolent. Anne Anglim as Anne bestirs herself in her less trance-like moments, but there are too few of them. Bob Nasmith is excessively hoarse and subdued, even for someone in Wilfred's condition.

Andy Velasquez captures Nick's amiable stolidity but hardly varies it. Tom Barnett finds the appropriate quivers of self-hatred and self-pity for Jim, but doesn't seem quite to the manner, or mannerisms, born. (Barnett was also, more convincingly, the artsy intruder in another rural drama, The Drawer Boy. To adapt the old song: How Ya Gonna Keep Him Down on the Farm, After He's Seen the Farm? Twice.)

In theory, Harkness's decision to keep issues (big business, pollution, etc.) in the background and concentrate on the people is to be applauded. In practice, the politics sound like they might be more absorbing than the family dramas, which are thin.

The play does connect with the guilt many people feel over their parents, the regret for what has been missed, and this can be poignant. The play is well crafted, too, in terms of information revealed or withheld. But much of the time, the characters are going over old ground, slowly. David Fraser's set is of an unusual solidity for this address, and works very well.
