REVIEW IN BRIEF THEATRE / More Divine

Saturday, October 15, 1994

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Written and directed by Sky Gilbert. At Buddies in Bad Times Theatre until until Oct. 30. Rating: -1/2 No plot but lots of poetry BY ROBERT CUSHMAN 'IT is advisable, if a play has neither plot nor characters that it have poetry." That is a line from Sky Gilbert's latest. The self-reference is blatant, and as charming as it is obviously intended to be. More Divine has no plot, and at most one character, but it does have poetry.

Some of this poetry is visual. The show begins in Paris, done as a black-and-white etching from a stage-size comic book. A bevy of schoolboys - in berets, short pants, satchels and umbrellas - coyly burlesque every Parisian rain-dance that ever cluttered up a movie musical. They also arouse the attentions of the late critics and philosophers Roland Barthes and Michel Foucault. Barthes is first seen, in a room the size of a phone- booth, actually waiting in stoical agony for the phone to ring. Foucault is first seen aggressively masturbating. They are rivals for the same boy and, naturally, macho Michel wins him from reclusive Roland.

Barthes, however, is soon off on the road to Morocco which differs from Paris --most effectively - in being a bare wide stage with a Sky-blue backdrop. Here he finds happiness, or anyway three available youths. But he is tracked down by Foucault who tells him a) that he should come out; b) that he is exploiting his young Arabs; and c) that AIDS will not only unite but exalt gays everywhere. That's about it for dialectical conflict, though Foucault seems to score a posthumous victory when Barthes, back in Paris, is lured out to a fancy-dress disco peopled by the guests from Buddies in Bad Times' own reopening party; everybody parties while the play gracefully self-destructs.

Earlier the two gurus visit a plush Parisian theatre (another minor coup for designer Steve Lucas) to see a revival of a society melodrama which they have chosen over a possible Pinter on the wildly improbable grounds that it's shorter. What they see is a strident burlesque, in which the promiscuous heroine is really a man. It's mostly feeble, a parody of a non-existent form, but occasionally inspiration descends. An actor detaches himself to lament his plight as a heterosexual forced to comply with the (artistic) whims of his - I quote from memory - "camp fag drag- queen director." The is-he-or-isn't-he questions that this sends buzzing round the audience, and then reflects back on to them, knock Pirandello out of the ballpark.

Gilbert probably couldn't tell a story or conduct an argument for nuts, but at his best he writes with real wit and real rhythm, and as a director he communicates these qualities to his actors. Randall Lanthier's unassuming balding Barthes is far more winning than anybody would guess from reading his logic-chopping prose, and though this Foucault is a caricature intellectual (thin-rimmed glasses and a thin-rimmed mouth) Mark Christmann animates the skeleton to perfection. Everybody else is very good. So is the new theatre: Buddies in Bad Times, they've seen it all and, my dear, they're still here.
