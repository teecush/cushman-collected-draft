The first image on the opening credits of The No. 1 Ladies Detective Agency is of a brown finger pointing to a spot on a map of Africa. Up pops a title to tell us that it's Botswana, which is where our scene is set. This is informative, and also cute.

The same might be said of the pilot episode for this new series, based on a popular set of novels by Alexander McCall Smith. Darker themes occasionally peep through, but as detection series go, this one is as sunny as the climate. It's a co-production of HBO, the BBC and the Weinsteins of Miramax: I'm not sure what the last part of that partnership might be expected to bring to the table, but for the rest this is far more sly and cozy mainstream-British than tough and gritty cable-American. This first instalment has the special cachet of being cowritten and directed by the late Anthony Minghella and produced by the late Sydney Pollack, both of whom died before the rest of the season went into production; and it shows them both in uncommonly relaxed mood.

It revolves around Precious Ramotswe, a charming and ample lady from the bush, played winningly and amusingly if without great emotional variation by Jill Scott, best known as a singer-songwriter. Precious's beloved father died, leaving her his van and 180 cows; these turn out to be cash-cows, since she sells them and drives off to town (Gabarone) in the van to start a new life and a new business. The van breaks down en route, but is salvaged by a widowed auto mechanic (Lucian Msamati, who is brilliant) who becomes Precious's precious friend and, at last sighting, the nearest thing to a love-interest -- best to call it, for the moment, a like-interest. Precious also has a married past; she is divorced from a man who beat her, so savagely that her only child miscarried. This man, and his

shady lawyer, now want some part of her fortune; encountering them at her father's funeral, she remarks that it's the first funeral at which she's seen him since her baby's. This is one of those acrid moments that the show doesn't develop, at least not this week, but that lingers in the mind.

Precious sets up shop, on the irresistibly named Zebra Drive, as Botswana's Best (and, as she freely admits, only) Lady Detective. ("The bad men," says her car-fixing guy, already smitten, "had better watch out.") At first, business is slow to develop, as is mercilessly noted by Precious's sole employee Grace, a secretary just out of college with near-perfect scores and a very brisk and bespectacled manner. She's played by Anika Noni Rose (Dreamgirls), whose fast, clipped voice, superlative comic timing and general air of loyal disenchantment are irresistible; she raises the show's level from pleasant diversion to essential entertainment. She's a joy, whether comparing her own employability to that of less qualified but more pliable ladies ("nine times out of 10, short skirt gets the job") or opening a file in which the agency can record, as she punctiliously puts it, "every 'unch and every 'int." (Office equipment consists of two typewriters, each with missing keys; neither boasts a functioning "h.")

Grace also tangles with the male proprietor of next door's Last Chance Hairdressing Salon, who tells her "your hair and my scissors should spend some quality time together." "That man," says Grace severely, "is very much like a woman." Try getting away with that line in a European setting. Minghella's coauthor is Richard Curtis, of Blackadder and other things, and I suspect him of some of Grace's material. She may well, if 24 doesn't buck its ideas up a bit, displace Chloe O'Brian in my affections.

Business improves, and by the end of this double-length episode the firm has taken on, and solved, five whole cases. Precious's success-rate is attributed to her traditional "woman's intuition" which translates as an ability to make connections: to take a chance remark from one context and apply it constructively to another. She is as often asked to investigate misdemeanours as crimes. In time-honoured private-eye fashion, she's engaged by a woman digging for dirt on her husband, a scene that occasions a delectable triple-play between Scott, Rose, and Nikki Amukia Bird as the bristling wife, wanting to know "is my husband a philanderer of the first water?" ("Adulterer," snaps Grace unforgivingly from the outer office. It's the rule on this show that all men are guilty until proved innocent.) Cracking this one leads Precious into an ill-advised attempt to play Miss Marple and Mata Hari simultaneously, a process that leads to a highly foreseeable but still enjoyable reversal. At the other extreme of seriousness we have a child-abduction ring, involving an updated witch-doctor operation. Presiding over it is Idris Elba, Stringer Bell from The Wire, playing a totally different but equally chilling super-thug, and managing the African voice as persuasively as he did the American. (His native tongue is cockney, or what's now called estuary English.) This could be disturbing stuff but it's kept at a distance, just as the abuse in Precious' life is kept in the past. So far, this is a law-and-order series in which the good is always victorious; which makes it, certainly by HBO standards, rather tame. It also reverts to the old-fashioned formula of a different story, or stories, every week. Botswana has a happier history than most African countries, but life there can't really be that neat. It isn't that neat anywhere. - The No. 1 Ladies Detective Agency premiers this Sunday at 9 p. m. on HBO Canada.
