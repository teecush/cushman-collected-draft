Theatre Review

WILD ABANDON AND SEE BOB RUN

Factory Studio Theatre, Toronto

Few people really warm to the prospect of monologues in the theatre -- except, of course, those who write and/or perform them. For them the one-person show is a dream of a workout. These two plays, written in the late 1980s by Daniel MacIvor, are superior examples of the genre: studies of individual desperation (naturally) in contrasting styles (mercifully). There are longueurs but for the most part the audience gets to share in the dream.

The first play, Wild Abandon, presents a young man of 25 at the end of his tether: literally, since the stage's few furnishings include a chair and a noose, and he has to make up his mind whether or not to use them. Life, sex and religion (he's a lapsed Catholic) aren't doing much for him, and his childhood memories amount mainly to resentment that his parents wouldn't let him paint his room black. (Which of us, he asks rhetorically, didn't want a black room as a child? There I go, out of step again.)

He has another more specific youthful recollection; when he was 11, an unknown woman whom he decided was his real mother approached him and, as he stood there waiting for her embrace, stuck a fork into him. She was taken away but the experience has, understandably, scarred him.

The piece unfolds as a kind of scrambled lecture, a fantasia on interlocked themes whose titles ("Birth Mother," "L-O-V-E") are flashed on a screen at the back of the stage. They amount to a generalized account of contemporary angst, making the speaker a character with whom everyone can identify but in whom nobody can quite believe.

There is plenty of spring in the writing -- some arresting images, some funny jokes -- and the actor, Jason Cadieux, delivers with magnificently casual accuracy. The program discloses that his character is called Steve, though I don't recall the matter being raised in public.

The protagonist of the second piece is, by contrast, extremely insistent about her name. She is called Bob, short for Roberta, and she is a terrified teenager, hitching a series of rides across the country, making for what she refers to as "the water." The play is called See Bob Run.

Bob is running from the memory of paternal abuse and also, perversely, from the two people who seem actually to have loved her: her boyfriend and her mother. Some of this information is relayed to us, poignantly and economically, through a fable that she recounts to us in between bending the ears of her various lifts. In this story she is a princess, and this connects neatly with her real life; her lover is actually a singer, an artist currently known as Prince. (It may even be his real name.) She is also fleeing an especially bloody scene at home; its nature, and her gradual acknowledgment of it, are slowly revealed to us over the course of the play. It is about one story and one conversation too long, but it holds.

There are two kinds of solo play. The first, typified by Wild Abandon, is the one in which the character talks to an indeterminate someone who may be himself or the audience; its pleasures are those of freewheeeling phantasmagoria, its perils are born of the belief that anything goes.

See Bob Run belongs, most of the time, to the second brand, in which a character talks unstoppably to another, unseen and improbably silent, character. It is difficult to bring this off convincingly (just as no playwright has ever managed plausibly to represent one half of a telephone conversation) but MacIvor manages surprisingly well. You may occasionally wonder why none of Bob's couriers push her out of the car (one does but not just for talking too much), but the dialogue is surprisingly free of the standard repetitions of what the other character has supposedly just said. Bob is a compulsive, overriding talker, and we come to understand why.

Tara Rosling, perhaps because she is older than the character, drives too hard and eagerly at it to begin with, but she settles down as things get grimmer, and by the end she is haunting. She is able to reveal a more shy, ingratiating persona in a little animal story that the two actors recount together before the evening's main business gets underway. (It's a woolly-sheep story, meaning a shaggy- dog story that happens to be about sheep.)

They both do this charmingly; and it's a treat, to be savoured while possible, to see two whole people on a stage, interacting. Ed Gass Donnelly's direction is, as far as you can tell with monologues, perfect.
