SLIGHTLY BIGGER CITIES

du Maurier Theatre Centre

I hesitate to describe Bruce McCulloch, sometime Kid in the Hall, as a stand-up comedian. For one thing, he does a lot of his current stage act sitting down, in a low-lying red armchair that one can only hope has seen better days. For another, every solo comic that I have seen -- good, bad or indifferent -- has had a fluent command of natural speech rhythms and has addressed the audience accordingly. McCulloch, who writes his own stuff, sounds as if he's reciting it, with a limited range of inflection.

He comes from Calgary, found fame of a kind in Toronto and is now based in Los Angeles. According to the advertising, this progression underlies the shows title, though the phrase itself only occurs once, in a reference to young women serving at McDonalds who dream of doing the same work "in slightly bigger cities." A not-bad observation, it is clearly meant to resonate plangently in our memories, and McCulloch apparently sees himself as a lyric poet of the world inside and around him.

Some of his phrases do recur, such as Is there anything sadder than a pair of empty shoes after they've gone? This baffling inquiry (is it the shoes themselves who've departed or their owner? - - in either case the answer is yes, in the real world there are many things much sadder, and anyone who needs to ask should get out more) makes its first appearance at the very start of the evening. It sets a tone -- one of winsome complaint -- for much of what follows. Repeating and elaborating the question at a later point, though, does not automatically qualify as dramatic structure.

Some lines are good; one or two are even funny. Just before winding up, he intones a look-at-all-the-lonely-people litany that includes a heart-warming reference to the hapless guy whose nearest approach to his beloved will be to sniff her bed-linen when he helps her move.

Earlier he has told us that you really get to know people not by loving them but by breaking up with them. This is a dubious theory, but he illustrates it engagingly by talking about a past flame who left him, it seems, to get in touch with her inner pain, though she already had a good pain thing going, being the kind of woman who, when served with the wrong wine, would describe the situation as "Kafkaesque," this despite, or more likely because of, the fact that she had never read Kafka. (I think Ive met her: repeatedly.)

I would not, though, want to push McCulloch too enthusiastically as an oracle on inter-gender relationships: not after hearing him go on for what felt like ages about his erotic prowess in a manner that is plainly meant to be self-deprecatory in its self-glorification and to reflect on the whole male sex rather than just him but is embarrassing anyway. And though I concede that the experience of watching a porn film with your father might give rise to some really lacerating comedy, it hasnt done so yet.

Passing, as one so easily does, from sex to religion, the unsupported statement that the latter activity gives stupid people something to do besides scratching lottery tickets, which in a way is the same thing, merely suggests that our commentator is in over his head.

Some topics he attacks at greater length. There is a flippant fantasia about a bun-fight between "the Clinique Ladies and the Body Shop Girls," and a solemn fantasia about a vigil for the late Kurt Cobain, and it's hard to say which is the more ineffectual. Occasionally, when his feelings become too overpowering for words, McCulloch gets up to do a pounding dance, his arms flailing and his legs rubbery.

In repose he looks tousled and sounds petulant. He is riding a depressing wave of men getting up alone on stages to tell us about their hang-ups -- real, imaginary or projected -- and passing off the result as a) social commentary and b) theatre. (The soiloquiser generally shares the stage with a musician; in this case it's a rock guitarist, Brian Connelly.) I speak of this plague as it afflicts Toronto, but I imagine that it's also raging, maybe even more intensively, in slightly smaller cities.
