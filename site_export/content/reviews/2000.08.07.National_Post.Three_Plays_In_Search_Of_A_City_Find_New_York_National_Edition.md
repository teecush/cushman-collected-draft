SPINNING INTO BUTTER, YOU CANT TAKE IT WITH YOU, THE REAL THING

The American tradition of social drama -- well made and well meaning -- has been asleep in recent years, but it has never actually perished. A late example called Spinning Into Butter was recently given an enthusiastic welcome at the Goodman, the prestigious Chicago theatre David Mamet, among others, calls home. It has now been re-mounted in New York at the Mitzi E. Newhouse, the smaller of the two dramatic stages at the Lincoln Centre.

The elusive title (elusive meaning hard to remember, though not as hard as Mitzi E. Newhouse) derives from the nursery tale of Little Black Sambo. This isnt a story that surfaces much these days in polite society, but let me jog your memory: Little Black Sambo went for a walk in the forest, and was progressively stripped of his clothes by a succession of tigers, who then chased one another round a tree for the right of full possession. They ran so furiously they all melted away into butter. Little Black Sambo picked up his clothes and the butter, went home, spread the butter on a heap of pancakes, and wolfed them down, a conclusion that made me feel queasy when I was a child, and still does.

In Rebecca Gilman's play, a black student at a mostly WASP Vermont college receives a barrage of racial hate mail. Faculty and students go into various kinds of shock. When the truth emerges (it's predictable but had better not be revealed), one of the senior deans invokes the Sambo story to describe the unseen student who, he says, has been watching his seniors run themselves ragged and relishing the spectacle. That, of course, is just his interpretation and we arent encouraged to trust him. His reaction to the crisis has been to call a series of student meetings, where he can parade his own tolerance at -- we gather -- embarrassing length.

The college seems to be run by this man, three other deans, an absentee president and a security guard. The protagonist is Sarah Daniels, who is the dean of students (i.e., shes the one who has to talk to them). She is white, but previously worked at a black Chicago college, and seems to have been hired on that basis.

When the crisis strikes, Sarah reacts more directly and humanely than anyone else. But she has a guilty secret, and under pressure she reveals it: Sarah, despite having started her career with the best radical intentions, is a racist.

She makes this confession in a 20-minute monologue that is both well written and (by Hope Davis, a much-touted young actress who can be both tart and touching, and can gauge the distance between neurosis and dementia) and well delivered. In Chicago, Sarah found that her dislike of her more belligerent students outweighed her affection for the more agreeable ones. She also found herself cowering away from the scruffier black passengers on her commuter train.

This speech apparently shook the play's Chicago audience to its core. New York, not having the same local stake in the matter, seems to have taken it in stride.

It certainly gives rise to a lot of questions. The colleague who hears it (he also happens to be Sarah's ex-lover, a relationship that takes up either too much dramatic space or not enough) first reacts with stereotyped shock, then later admits hes quite careful about where he sits on a train as well.

He doesnt point out that Sarah would feel just as uncomfortable in the presence of threatening white travellers, or that no teacher is obliged to like all her students equally. We seem to be listening to a vastly inflated display of liberal guilt -- and one, by the way, that has little direct bearing on the main action. There is race in both, but that's not enough for dramatic coherence.

If I had to point out the author's moral for her, I would say Sarah should lighten up. She is the only person on stage, students included, who tries to treat people as people, rather than as issues. They all seem pretty buttery, even before they start spinning. (I except the security man, who seems to have all the compassion of the traditional working stiff, plus most of the answers. Frustratingly, he never lets on what they are.)

The storytelling holds attention, though, and on that basis one would once have prophesied a big-time transfer and a movie deal. The play is the Tea and Sympathy of its day. Broadway, for better or worse, now craves stronger stimulants.

Also obviously courting a commercial transfer is the latest production of the Roundabout Theater which, in 20 years of existence, has become the most successful producing organization off- Broadway. The Roundabouters mostly devote themselves, as off- Broadway always used to do, to smart revivals. (Cabaret is one of theirs.)

They now have a new home, the Selwyn (an old theatre on 42nd Street reconstituted, at great expense, to look like an old theatre on 42nd Street), which they have opened with The Man Who Came to Dinner, the 1939 comedy about a monumentally cantankerous celebrity writer, transparently modelled on Alexander Woollcott, the famous Round Table critic and wit -- though in his case I have always thought both those nouns to be complimentary to the point of sycophancy.

Moss Hart and George S. Kaufman, both Woollcott buddies, also wrote You Cant Take It with You, which the Shaw Festival staged so enjoyably last year. This play is that one in reverse: Instead of a Bohemian family invaded by Wall Street conservatives, we have a solidly prosperous Midwestern household exhaustively disrupted by the eponymous Sheridan Whiteside, a visiting exotic who came unwillingly to dine and stayed even more reluctantly, having broken his leg while saying goodbye.

The play belongs to an age when authors could happily write parts for 30 grown-up actors, and throw in half a dozen choirboys at the end of Act Two. All this abundance produces energy, as does the authors contagious delight in their material. The play is an in- joke preposterously expanded; but somehow, even if you don't get the references, you still get the point. (It's also, for all its apparent free-wheelingness, devilishly well constructed.) It, too, would be a natural for the Shaw, except I dont see how they would cast the lead.

Its a problem in New York, too. Nathan Lane seems to enjoy being wicked in his wheelchair, but he holds back too much. Perhaps in an attempt to inject variety, he soft-pedals some of the insults, but the point about Whiteside is that -- apart from a few moments of sentimentality dictated by the plot -- he is unrelentingly, exorbitantly rude. Even when paying a compliment, he's rude.

Jean Smart, playing a predatory actress, is similarly timid. She tiptoes around her character's monstrosity rather than stepping inside and taking possession.

Jerry Zaks production, though, has a good supply of fizz, and it takes full advantage of a couple of characters who are still cultural icons. One is called Beverly Carlton, but is transparently, and consummately, Noel Coward. Its madly gay. Another is an uncontrolled zany named Banjo, who has two off-stage brothers named Wacko and Sloppo. He is actually based on Harpo Marx, a real-life chum of Woollcott's, but since he talks, most actors play him as Groucho. Lewis J. Stadlen, an experienced Marxist fellow-traveller, plays him as both (adding a dash of Chico, I shouldn't wonder) and tops the mixture off with a heavy sauce of Jimmy Durante, who took the part in the film. Its madly wonderful.

In my very first column for the Post, I disparaged The Real Thing as "the Tom Stoppard play for people who dont like Tom Stoppard." I have now been conclusively contradicted. A revival that started in London last year has come to New York (the Barrymore Theater) and more than lives up to its title. Better than real, it is the best Thing in town.

What has changed ? David Leveaux's production, which originated in a studio space, is more scenically fluid and more economic than the original staging, and above all more intimate, in both the theatrical and the sexual sense. All these qualities have survived the transition to a larger house.

The play concerns a playwright who -- after writing glibly about love, jealousy and adultery -- has to discover what, in his second major relationship, he really feels about these things. His partner is an actress. Both their previous spouses are actors, whom we see in Scene One performing a scene from one of the hero's plays. Only in Scene Two do we realize that what we were watching was art, not life.

Stephen Dillane and Jennifer Ehle are the leads (both won Tonys, as did the production) and one believes in them, separately and together. Dillane is especially magnetic, a tousled compulsive thinker, from whom the authors epigrams and tirades flow as an expression of feeling, rather than as some kind of tortured display of cleverness, jokey appendages on an otherwise conventional play.

With the characters in focus, the plays structural and intellectual conceits no longer seem like minor Stoppard. In fact, they are dazzling. The Pirandellian comedy of the second scene is not only hilarious in itself, but offers a series of windows into the speakers' souls.

Toward the end, the talk threatens to outdistance the amount of relationship we have actually been shown, but the threat is averted. The play runs till Aug. 13. It's worth a trip or, if you already have one planned, worth a siege of the box office.
