JAZZ"Festival gets under way for its 10-day summer run Downtown Jazz hits the streets

Monday, June 25, 1990

ROBERT CUSHMAN

BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail JOE Williams is famous for singing blues, and he also sings ballads. According to critical legend he has an artistically split personality. In fact he is a seamless stylist with a large repertoire. Drawing on two main sources, he splits the difference between them, bringing urbanity to the blues and soul to standards. The proportions may vary from gig to gig, but the recipe remains constant.

Opening the du Maurier Ltd. Downtown Jazz festival at Roy Thomson Hall on Friday, he naturally had his Count Basie side uppermost. Rob McConnell's Boss Brass, which has its own Basie aspect - an overhanging sense of power held pleasantly in reserve, waiting to spring - seemed more than happy to co-operate. Williams was a mellow singer even when young, and in his enduring prime (old seems quite the wrong word for him, even if he has turned 70) he radiates sensual assurance. Grey-haired and white- suited he is a smiling jazz aristocrat: positively Ducal.

The voice rasps a little more than it used to, and the falsetto is a bit less thrilling. But the instrument still rings at both ends of the register; there is a tautness about it that stops it from being cavernous (like Billy Eckstine) or overly smooth (Nat King Cole). A couple of years ago I heard him still a New York club with a wonderfully modulated and, it seemed, deeply-felt How Deep Is the Ocean. It's on his latest record. That night it took everybody by surprise, maybe including him.

There was nothing like that on Friday. Maybe the occasion and the hall forbade it. His program was relentlessly up; even the contemplative moments had a Vegas tinge. Alec Wilder's A Child Is Born, which craves simplicity, was pumped up with two wedges of recitation. He opened and closed with one of those philosophical autobiographical numbers by which all mature singers get visited sooner or later - without exemption. Sure enough, he did it his way.

There were no classics and only one warhorse: a welcome retread of Alright O.K. You Win. There were also homages to his Basie predecessor Jimmy Rushing: I Want a Little Girl, the most satisfying number in the set. It was all expert and enjoyable, a master in repose. But one waited in vain for lightning to strike.

Williams has probably the least bruised voice of any accredited blues singer. He saves his vulnerability for his ballads, when he sings them, but his blues are rhythmically jovial assertions of male supremacy. Operating at the other end of the system - in gender, attitude, age and, on this occasion, place on the bill - is the evening's opening act: Holly Cole , dynamiting the American popular song.

Cole is a cunning subversive;, she dresses like a nightclub siren, with the off-the-shoulder air of a hipster's Madonna, and she has the traditional musical virtues as well. She swings mightily, phrases with point, and has a powerful way with dynamics. She began with that most standard of standards, The Man I Love , and served notice of her agenda from the second line which came out as "he won't be big and strong." This might have been a sad acceptance of unavodiable reality but it sounded more like a statement of principle. According to Ira Gershwin "he'll build a little home" a mere meliorist might change "he'll" to "we'll" but Cole went all the way to "I'll." I want to hear her sing Ol' Man River, toting dat barge, lifting dat bale, getting more than a little drunk, and landing, at a defiant up tempo, in jail.

This kind of vocal deconstruction is stimulating for a couple of numbers but self-defeating in the long stretch. Love for Sale doesn't need an extra coating of savage irony; it's already there in what Cole Porter wrote. The contrastingly regretful irony of Johnny Mercer's Talk to Me, Baby ("tell me lies") needs a filigree touch.

Her centrepiece - and the title-cut of her first CD - is Girl Talk, a mid-tempo mid-60s ambler about women chattering among themslves. It was composed by Basie's ace arranger Neal Hefti; and Joe Williams might have sung it to paternalist perfection. Cole tried valiantly to turn it against itself, but it kept asserting its own identity. Maybe it should have been left to die of disuse.

Cole's voice filled the hall more effectively than her presentation. She allows her two sidemen plenty of musical space, and rightly; pianist Aaron Davis is an especial wizard, conjuring a whole melody through a few driving fragments. But she has to do either more or less while they're playing; she can't just stand there and undulate.

In between the voices the Boss Brass, crammed with Toronto's best, had a joyous reunion that went from a restfully rocking Smoke Gets in Your Eyes to McConnell's own Traditional Piece, a solid piece of three-story masonry. Its name notwithstanding, the heart of this band is in its rhythm: Don Thompson thoughtful on piano, Ed Bickert incomparably relaxed on guitar, and Jerry Fuller suspending the whole mighty operation securely between his drumsticks from first beat to last.
