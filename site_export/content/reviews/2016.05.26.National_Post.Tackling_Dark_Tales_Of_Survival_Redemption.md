THEATRE REVIEW

The Model Apartment (Harold Green) The Testament of Mary (Soulpepper)

So, how to dramatize the Holocaust? In The Model Apartment, the American playwright Donald Margulies does it by hereditary displacement. He gives us a now-elderly couple, Max and Lola, who met and married just after the war when both were survivors, she of a camp, he of the woods where he had been a partisan. They moved to America and had a daughter, Debby, who is more visibly traumatized by their experiences than they themselves appear to be. She has grown up mentally disturbed and physically obese.

Max and Lola have followed the post-retirement trail from Brooklyn to Florida. It seems their condo isn't ready, so they are offered a temporary apartment that looks good but in which nothing works: the TV is a hollow shell, theres nothing in the fridge and there isnt even a proper bed. They left New York without telling Debby where they were going, but she found out anyway and she follows them there. And she in turn is followed by her boyfriend Neil who seems only marginally more stable than she is, and whom she took in when he was homeless. The play happens during a long first night in the apartment. Max, whom we are told can sleep anywhere, gets some fitful rest on a pull-out couch, periodically rousing himself to join the others in their ongoing screamingmatch.

The "model apartment" to which Max and Lola are consigned may be intended to remind us of Theresienstadt, the model camp to which Jews and others were sent, before being transferred to hellholes less decorously disguised. The unseen "nice landlord" to whom they refer may even be an allusion to the soothing guides who escorted victims into the gas chambers. The play certainly isn't shy of waking more obvious echoes. Lola shares with Neil - and therefore with us - stories of her friendship in Belsen with Anne Frank: memories that are almost certainly fantasies, and that Debby has heard so often that she can recite them in unison.

Max says comparatively little about his own experiences, though he does occasionally let off complaints on the order of I came out of the woods for this? It turns out he named his daughter after a Deborah who was his own first (murdered) love.

Debby bears the scars of her parents' experiences. She screams incessantly, claims to see Nazis in every Howard Johnsons, and embarrasses her elders with her sexual explicitness. Its a possible situation. But having established it, the play can do little but repeat it. It's efficiently written, but it can't avoid the taint of exploitation, of tapping into available responses, even if at one remove.

The Harold Green theatre has given it a good production, directed by Tanja Jacobs. A tetchy Eric Peterson and a practically unrecognizable Clare Coulter provide a sold base-line for Lisa Nortons rather spectacular performance as Debby, fearlessly padded and even more courageously emoted. Tony Ofori completes the cast as Neil, and has the most effective moment of what I can only call camp about the camps, when he sees Lolas prisoner-number and cheerfully volunteers that he has tattoos too. It's the kind of jolting serio-comic shock for which the whole play may be aiming but which it cannot sustain.

Soulpepper also has a play about a Jewish family: The Testament of Mary, a monologue for the mother of Jesus. Nancy Palk doesn't play her as noticeably Jewish, though remembering her wonderful rabbi in Angels in America there were moments when I rather wished she would. Instead, she offers an accomplished and sensitive account of a highly intelligent woman forced to watch her son suffer unimaginable pain after reaching heights of, to her, unaccountable celebrity. Yes, she was at the marriage feast at Cana; yes, she heard about the walking on the water. And yes, she was there for the raising of Lazarus, but all she could think of, encountering him postresurrection, was that this was a man who was now condemned to die twice. Its the most haunting passage of Colm Toibins play, adapted from his own novella. We meet Mary in hiding, plagued, she tells us, by visits from a couple of visitors - presumably two of the evangelists - who want to hear her story as long as it's in accord with their own. They certainly don't want to hear that she fled in fear from the scene of the crucifixion, or to hear her skepticism about her sons resurrection. It seems she never cared much anyway for the men he hung out with.

Its hard not to be reminded of the skeptical mother of Monty Python's Brian ("he isn't the Messiah ...") but Toibins script, though not without a vein of sardonic humour, isnt written or played for laughs. It suffers from the curse that afflicts nearly all one-person plays: it has to tell rather than show. Though I felt, perversely, that there were things, especially the politics of the times, about which I would like to have been told more.

Aaron Willis's production injects some intelligent tension, with threatening sound effects (Richard Feren) and even more alarming light-changes (Lorenzo Savoini). Palk keeps her performance lucid and level, carefully husbanding her occasional outbreaks, one of which is actually about her husband. The most devastating is her cry that it - meaning, really, Christianity - "wasn't worth it": not worth the agony it caused a mother.

The Model Apartment runs through May 29 at the Toronto Centre for the Arts; The Testament of Mary is in repertory through June 18 at the Young Centre.
