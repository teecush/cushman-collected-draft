COME GOOD RAIN

THEATRE REVIEW

COME GOOD RAIN

Friday, March 21, 1997

Written, directed, performed

by George Seremba


  


Rating: **½

Out of the heart of darkness

When Ugandan actor and activist takes centre stage,

his role requires him to be as theatrically

true as he is factually believable.

Special to The Globe and Mail


  


GEORGE Seremba survived torture and attempted execution in his native Uganda, and this one-man show is his story. He is also a professional actor and writer. His writing, on this evidence, is lively and evocative, at its best when swift and humorous. It can also be elaborately, and unconvincingly, literary. His acting has a sympathetic openness that carries him a long way, but rhythmically he is monotonous. Blessings and curses, memories and forebodings, all go to the same tune. And already I feel guilty.

He is at his best when at his most observant. He offers a humorous memoir of schooldays in the early days of Ugandan independence. He was asked to inform on fellow students heard speaking in African dialects; on one level this is a wry hangover from colonialism, on another a chilling forecast of the pervasive espionage of future years. He goes on to evoke the whispered barroom conversations on deaths and disappearances ("Did you hear about so and so?" "Yes, how sad indeed.") that characterized the successive terrors of Milton Obote and Idi Amin. He remembers cheering the first appearance of Amin as a reassuring father-figure ("the gentle giant") and remembers a choice malapropism in the General's professed wish to "undress the queen" on Ugandan soil. Seremba himself survived Amin, though he had to take refuge in Kenya. On his return, though, he was arrested by the security forces of the reinstated Obote. They beat him, humiliated him, took him to a forest -- a notorious killing site under two regimes -- shot him and left him for dead. The rain helped to stanch his multitude of wounds; friendly hands rescued him and smuggled him out of the country. Some of the helpers were his relatives. In one of the play's more resonant lines, he thanks God "for the African extended family."

His words ring less effective when he recreates his thought processes on the way to and from the place of execution. These thoughts were amazingly magnanimous (to use his own bitingly satirical word about Amin, just prior to his expulsion of Uganda's Asians); Seremba seems to have been as much concerned with the fate of his friends and his country as with his own. I don't doubt him, and I honour him, but I do find it hard to believe that the words in his head were as eloquently organized as the ones that come out on the stage.

This is a story that should be told. The theatre -- the most immediate of all narrative forms -- is an excellent place to tell it; and Seremba, perhaps uniquely among victims, has the skills to do it in his own person. But those skills impose their own standards. A non-actor getting up on stage and recounting his experiences would be a different matter; an actor impersonating Seremba would be yet another. Because he's playing himself -- something actors, with very good reason, rarely do, however much they may draw on their own personalities -- the demands on his credibility are extreme. He has to be not just factually believable (no problems there), but theatrically true. And, however much I might tell myself that I, who had never been through anything remotely comparable, had no right to judge, he lost me.

He also leaves a lot out. He seems to have been a fairly apolitical drama student at Makerere University. Suddenly he's an activist and a refugee; what were the stages between? The things he tells us about the world around him are fascinating; and one wants more facts, less lyricism. There is an obvious comparison with George Tabori's My Mother's Courage ,that other survivor's story about which I raved earlier this week. If anything the Tabori play has too many devices but in the end, by distancing its subject, it brings us closer to it. Both plays, by the way, begin with the protagonist entering, bearing a candle. Seremba gets excellent support from the drumming, alternately healing and threatening, of Emman Mutema.


  


Come Good Rain is at Factory Theatre Studio Space until April 6.
