If you are a man named Ernest, you may be best advised not to marry a woman named Ernestine. And vice, of course, versa. It's probably advisable too to get to know your partner a bit before tying the knot and moving into a basement flat with no windows, a temperamental furnace, and only a drawbridge to connect you to the outside world. Oh, you may start out thinking you adore your home and your partner and calling the latter sweetie all the time, but your delight will dissipate, to be replaced by what was always there, bubbling beneath the surface: The Anger in Ernest and Ernestine.

Which is the title of the two-character play by Leah Cherniak, Robert Morgan and Martha Ross, first produced by Theatre Columbus in 1987 and since established, if not as a Canadian classic, then certainly as a Canadian standby. Its now been revived by Soulpepper as a studio (i.e. small-scale) production with Cherniak directing. It stands up well. Unfolding as much in action as in words, in accordance with the Columbus esthetic, it traces a funny and persuasively slippery slope. The downhill ride starts at breakfast, or over three successive breakfasts. Ernest, who at this stage doesnt have a job, lovingly counts out his cornflakes (in German, which is cute) while Ernestine, who has not merely a job but a career, ties herself into literal and increasingly frantic knots, trying to get ready, get dressed, get fed and get out. Raquel Duffy, struggling to fit into her heels while spraying cereal in all directions including her own, is a scream. Gregory Prest matches her in technical finesse, and is more intrinsically and endearingly comic: a matter partly of his own personality as a performer, and partly of the advantages the script hands him. He arrives catastrophically late for a marital date-night, due to the fascinating people and animals he encounters on the way home from work. (He has a job by this point, though hardly a good one.) Ernestine finds this behaviour unforgivable, and she's probably right, but the audience gets to hear Ernest's own description of the events and its charming. Anyhow, the way their argument develops, their common determination to make the worst of a bad situation is most acutely written and performed.

Theres one scene in which the two unite, to write an exaggeratedly angry letter in the wake of a traffic accident. One can see the point - that they're channelling their mutual resentment into an attack on a third party - but it's the wrong kind of grotesque and it feels like a roadblock. So, even more, does the presence of an intermission, stopping the flow and making the second half feel repetitious. Still, its an entertaining and uncomfortably believable tale, expertly told. Shannon Lea Doyles set is excellent, a room you can't wait to get out of.

Gregory Prest might someday make a good Cyrano de Bergerac: an idea prompted

by Soulpepper's other new studio show, Cowboy Versus Samurai. This is a 10-year-old play by an American writer, Michael Golamco, avowedly based on the classic tale of the long-nosed cavalier-poet, hopelessly in love with a beautiful woman and finding painful solace in furnishing a handsome rival with the words to win her heart. This new version takes us to the town of Breakneck, Wyoming: initial population 1,000, initial Asian population two. The two are Travis, an English teacher of Korean heritage, and Chester, whose adoptive parents never bothered to ask just where he was from and who therefore casts himself as an all-purpose Asian and very militant about it. A newcomer arrives in town and at school: Veronica, another teacher, also Korean but with a history of dating only white men. Travis, attracted but feeling racially as opposed to nasally disqualified, compensates by ghostwriting flowery letters for the man she really fancies, a parttime PE teacher named Del. This does not convince. In the original Cyrano, the lovemaking never got much beyond serenading under balconies, so the subterfuge was credible. But, this being the 21st century, Veronica and Del actually move in together, so its impossible she wouldnt notice. (And why would he be writing her all those letters anyway? They see each other every day.) Beyond this, the play is fatally split between being a study of a self-defeating love and a piece about race; sometimes the subjects seem to synchronize, but mostly they swim past one another. The race part yields the best or at least the funniest writing: Chester the firebrand is an all-purpose revolutionary, appropriating tropes equally from black power and the KKK (substitute yellow for white, he says of the latter, and it's the same speech). He has a very nice line in chastising Travis for his accommodating tendencies. ("You're like a Chinese waiter offering them a fork".) Miqelon Rodriguez makes the most of these opportunities, though he remains a cartoon figure in a play that aspires to be real.

Ins Chois production also boasts a first-rate performance from TJ Riley as Del, the sheepish tough guy, as far removed from a real cowboy as Travis from a real samurai. (The title is a real reach.) Jonathan Tan, charged as Travis with tying the threads together, makes a sympathetic job of it, best when watchfully reacting to everyone elses extravagances. The evening's most significant lack is that it can't make us care who Veronica (Rosie Simon) ends up with or why. That's a shame: the Cyrano story is the heartbreaker and/or tear-jerker of all time, and anyone who sees it must have yearned to see it furnished. somehow, with a happy ending. Here it's all attitudes struck, not feelings felt.
