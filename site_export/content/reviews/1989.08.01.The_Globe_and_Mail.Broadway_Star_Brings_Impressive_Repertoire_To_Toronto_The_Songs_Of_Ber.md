Broadway star brings impressive repertoire to Toronto The songs of Bernadette

Tuesday, August 01, 1989

ROBERT CUSHMAN

BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Bernadette Peters may be the last of Broadways great leading ladies. She is about to play Toronto as half of a two-star package at Roy Thomson Hall, opposite Peter Allen. Opposite may not be quite the right word. "We wanted," she says, to work really together. So they start off the evening with some joint numbers; she does some stuff, then he does; and the last segment reunites them.

Following which, presumably, they live happily ever after.

The Allen section includes some songs from Legs Diamond, last season's superflop musical which he both wrote and starred in. Musicals flop easily on Broadway these days, so it is quite possible that out of context these numbers will have much to say for themselves. But show-fanciers may be more intrigued by the Peters repertoire. They should certainly be impressed by the Peters track record. I estimate that in the last five years, six successful new musicals have opened on Broadway. She has been in three of them. They were Stephen Sondheim's Sunday in the Park with George, Andrew Lloyd Webbers Song and Dance, and Sondheims Into the Woods. For each of the first two she won the Tony Award as best actress in a musical, surprising nobody. Actually her performance in Sunday in the Park ranks with Angela Lansbury's in Gypsy: simply the best I have seen in any musical.

Peters' Broadway career is actually on its second lap. Born in 1948, she started performing informally at the age of five ("I sang in front of the TV set"), joined the actors union at nine (I was in a play directed by Otto Preminger - it closed out of town) and went on the road at 13.

She started studying acting at 17 and decided, "Hey, this is cool, this is for me." Cooler still was her appearance, shortly thereafter, at an off-off-Broadway coffee house called the Cafe Cino in a spoof of the thirties Busby Berkeley movie musicals entitled Dames at Sea; she was Ruby Keeler.

From there she went into summer stock, and then into a disastrous musical with Bea Arthur called A Mothers Kisses from which she was fired just as the show was hitting the road. For this, she had turned down the off-Broadway transfer of Dames. When she called the Dames producers to say that she was now available, they told her politely that they had hired somebody else. Oh, she said. "That's great." And she meant it. (I was 17. I had such ethics.) The somebody else was Sandy Duncan, who promptly landed a role in a bigger show - one that ran, this time - and Peters found herself back at the centre of a sizeable cult hit. "People would say, 'Youre the hottest thing in town, and I would say, 'I am?' But I certainly knew more people all of a sudden."

She finally made it to Broadway playing the lead in a musical version of La Strada; it ran for one night. "Suddenly I wasnt so hot any more," she says. She stayed warm, though. The highlight of a string of near- misses was Mack and Mabel, a musical with a terrific score, a problem book, and a great posthumous reputation. It cemented Bernadette Peters performing image as that of a resilient waif: a potential legend waiting for the right setting.

The memory lingered during a 10-year absence from the theatre, spent mainly in California. On television, she did comedies, dramas and game shows. She made movies: she was superb in Pennies from Heaven, and wasted in the film version of Annie. She was playing the Fairmont Hotel in San Francisco when the offer came from writer-director James Lapine to take part in the workshop of Sunday in the Park.

Her specialty in her early roles had been wide-eyed appeal. She patented it as the imaginary movie queen in Dames at Sea, and developed it in Mack and Mabel, playing a real-life movie star who came to a bad end. (The bad end was one of the show's problems.) The innocence began to be edged with toughness, and the kind of worldly wisdom that is bred by necessity, out of self-defence. This appeared most poignantly in Pennies from Heaven; in her recent shows the image has matured and deepened.

In Sunday in the Park she was Dot, mistress and model of the pointillist painter Georges Seurat, by whom she was bewitched, bothered and explosively bewildered. Frizzy-haired above a square-cut gamine face, she was impossibly attractive, with a personality that lit rockets. She had a song called Everybody Loves Louis which she sang on leaving her artist for a less demanding baker; the mood was bravado with severe misgivings. Words and music bristled with quickfire jokes and tell-tale changes of mood. She made every point with coruscating precision, but the song never fragmented. It remained a shimmering, immaculate whole, and so did she. She embodied, in a new mould for the 1980s, a fundamental truth about the musical: wit, especially when combined with vulnerability, is very sexy.

The attack is that of a traditional Broadway soubrette, but the sound is new: modern, bruised, post-rock. It also adapts well to a Cockney accent. At least it did in Song and Dance, whose song component was virtually a one-woman show for her: the saga of an accident-prone English girl at large in the United States. She switched from nervously reassuring letters home (very funny) to stabbing fury at errant lovers to yearning arias of regret like Tell Me on a Sunday. Sunday seems to be her day.

The partnership with Peter Allen stems from the 1982 Oscar show when they both appeared in an Irving Berlin medley.
