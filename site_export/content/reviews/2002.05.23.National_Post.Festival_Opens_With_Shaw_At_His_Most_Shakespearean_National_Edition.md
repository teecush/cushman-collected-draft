In repertory until Oct. 27. Box office: 1-800-511-SHAW.

CAESAR AND CLEOPATRA

Festival Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

Your contemporary actress can look fetching, all done up in traditional temptress garb as Cleopatra, but your contemporary actor tends to look hopeless strutting around in a tunic. Though less frivolous reasons are put forward in the program, this must surely be one of the considerations that has prompted Christopher Newton, opening his last season as director of the Shaw Festival, to set his production of Caesar and Cleopatra not in its historical period but at the time in which Shaw wrote it.

That would be the late 1890s, and for a time it looks as if it's going to work. The curtain rises on a silent stageful of actors in khaki, playing cards, giving and receiving orders. They, we eventually deduce, are the Roman army of occupation in Egypt. While they putter about in their puttees, the voice of Newton himself fills us in on the historical background, in lines taken from one of the play's alternate prologues, supposedly spoken by the Egyptian god Ra.

The soldiers look as if they might be about to go into the other prologue, which is a group scene, but they never do. (I suspect a last-minute cut, since some of the characters turn up in the play's last scene without having been introduced. Anyway, it's a loss.)

William Schmuck has designed a multi-stepped and many-levelled set, which takes us from the Sphinx in the desert to an Alexandrian lighthouse to the roof of the queen's palace with beguiling ease, and even seems (through trompe l'oeil rather than hydraulics.) to go up and down. Newton deploys his large cast around and across it with his customary skill, and is particularly adept at positioning Caesar and Cleopatra themselves according to the shifting tides of their relationship. In Shaw's determinedly (and unhistorically) asexual reading, this is largely a matter of master and pupil.

Caesar is the closest Shaw came to depicting his ideal man: a political and military genius who also happens to be a convinced pacifist and humanitarian; since nobody else shares his principles, he has to fight everyone anyway. Basically, he is Shaw singing If I Ruled the World.

Cleopatra is the scared, superstitious schoolgirl whom Caesar teaches to be a queen but whom he cannot stop from developing into a tyrant; she is the everyday world at its sexiest. She cannot tempt him; he cannot convert her. When he sails back to Rome the political implications are cataclysmic but the personal cost amounts, for both of them, to no more than rueful regret. This is not tragedy, or even erotic comedy, but then Shaw thought both those genres frivolous.

Of course, both leads -- Caesar especially -- think and behave like Edwardians from the zenith of the British Empire, with historical names and in fancy dress. So do the other characters, colonized and colonizers alike. It seems to make sense to costume them accordingly, or so Newton and his literary advisor Ronald Bryden argue in their program notes. But the play is already full of jokey anachronisms, most notoriously in the figure of Britannus, Caesar's secretary from the land of the Druids, who behaves like the most censoriously correct of English civil servants. Putting him into a starched blue suit merely kills the joke by underlining it.

Nor does the modern dress do much for the more fustian passages, except bland them out. When people in this play are not exchanging Shavian witticisms they are saying things like "what ho" and "what say you to Mithridates of Pergamos?" You may want to believe that is Shaw making fun of the pompous chronicle plays of his contemporaries, but the truth is that it's a pseudo-Shakespearean style into which he fell quite naturally when confronted by history. (He did the same thing in Saint Joan).

Bryden may be right in claiming that previous productions of this play have drowned the text in spectacle, but this one is just as grandiose, only with different clothes on. It is juiceless; there are surprisingly few laughs. Maybe the play needed to be done, irrespective of period, on one of the Shaw's smaller stages. Then the characters could get at one another, and the ideas could get at us.

The program claims that this, Shaw's eighth full-length play, was his best to date. It was his most ambitious, certainly, possibly his most pretentious, but I doubt it could ever make as satisfying an evening in the theatre as Arms and the Man or You Never Can Tell, both of which preceded it. Still, it could be engaging and, in its own odd way, moving. As it is, when Caesar sails away and Cleopatra equivocally weeps (rather too sentimentally here), we don't know why it's meant to matter.

The lighting (by Kevin Lamotte) gives us a terrific blood-red sky for Cleopatra's disposal of her political adversary Pothinus, but is otherwise murky rather than moody. The low key that it sets for the first meeting of Caesar and Cleopatra at the Sphinx ("a dear little baby sphinx," she insists, not the great one) seems to infect Jim Mezon's whole performance. He is quiet -- very quiet -- and gentle and believably visionary, especially when he stares sad-eyed at somebody who has failed to meet his standards, but he lacks crispness and iron and the sense of boyish adventure, which is actually what keeps this conqueror going. He should be, in his own high-minded way, the most ruthless person on stage.

The more-than-promising Caroline Cave is splendidly in the moment for each stage of Cleopatra's progress, but sometimes seems to be getting them in the wrong order.

The rest are competent but unexciting. Apollodorus (Patrick R. Brown), the chivalrous carpet-fancier, works rather well as a Noel Coward aesthete either hugely or slightly ahead of his time, depending on which calendar you're using. (The wind-up gramophone that plays Jealousy is the nicest update in the show). Rufio (Guy Bannerman), Caesar's aide, is a kilted Caledonian, affecting in his last scene but underpowered earlier on. Britannus (Norman Browning) is as effective as circumstances permit; likewise Pothinus (Neil Barclay), less vulpine in a fez than he might be without. Ftatateeta (Sarah Orenstein), Cleo's murderously possessive nurse, seems to be trying excessively hard, in a thick, unplaceable accent shared by nobody else on stage.
