THE VENTRILOQUIST

Factory Studio Theatre

- - -

Larry Tremblay's The Ventriloquist is not about ventriloquism -- or not in any literal sense. True, it does begin with a man who has a doll on his knee, a doll played by a live actress. The doll represents a girl who has just celebrated her 16th birthday, her form of celebration being to cut off her blonde braids. For her present, she asked her mother for a Parker pen. What she actually received was a frustrating set of Chinese boxes. By the time she got to the last one, she needed a magnifying glass to view the contents, a glass she usually uses to view her stamp collection. The girl seems angry. The ventriloquist seems intimidated. So ventriloquism is a metaphor, and the play is really about adult control and childish resentment. Or vice versa.

No, it's really about literary ambition, especially among the young. The doll morphs into Gaby, a young woman writing in her room, behind a locked door. She now has her Parker pen and she uses it to write, effortlessly, the most beautiful stories and the most beautiful novel the world has ever seen. She wants to be better than Balzac, and she succeeds.

No, it's really about the power of fiction, and life's tendency to imitate art. The things, some of them atrocious, that Gaby writes about tend to come true.

No, it's really about psychiatry as abuse. Though apparently in her own room, Gaby is simultaneously in the consulting chamber of Dr. Limestone, a therapist with unorthodox methods and a controlling personality, who is identical to the ventriloquist, and who's treating her for writers' block. He has a prospectus, which apparently guarantees a cure if the patient obeys its demands. He gets Gaby to tell her story, and her stories, while stripped to her underwear. He has a compensating story of his own, about how his mother once stripped him to his underwear and forced him to eat onion soup. He hates onions. Gaby has already composed a story about a girl who fell in love with a beautiful young man who was actually an onion. Ah, the power of disguise.

No, it's really about sibling rivalry in its cruellest form. Gaby had an adored brother called Edgar, also a writer -- an acclaimed poet. He met a horrible end. Gaby wrote about it. But before this, or maybe in a parallel universe, Gaby took him her story; and he read it, admired it, made off with it and was furiously jealous of it.

No, it's really -- well, intermittently -- about the outside world trying to get inside the heads, and the rooms, of children. While Gaby sits creating fictions and/or interacting with Dr. Limestone, her door is knocked on by her father, her mother, who subsequently dies of shock at her son Edgar's fate, her sickeningly pious friend Lea whose birthday gift to Gaby was -- yes -- her prized stamp collection and who got called foul names for her pains, and a Dr. Mortimer who comes with parental recommendation but who sounds every bit as predatory as Dr. Limestone. She's also called on by Balzac, who isn't pleased to hear that Gaby has gone into competition with him and calls her the same names she called Lea. This seems to be Harold Bloom's theory of the anxiety of influence turned inside out; it's the older writer who's anxious, and how.

No, it's really about Africa, and the Europeans' fascination with it. Dr. Limestone takes over Gaby's story and makes it about Edgar, who takes the manuscript of the novel to an African village and reads it to a riveted audience. While he reads it, his skin turns black and heavily-tattooed, to the fascination of a woman called Flybelly, who seems to be Gaby's alter ego and who, towards the end of the reading, cuts off her braids. Edgar is played by the same actor as Dr. Limestone so what we took to be colour-blind casting isn't. In fact Edgar is Doctor Limestone and he and Gaby end by hurling insults at one another, sexual and literary.

No, it's really about ...well, get this. An epilogue finds Dr. Limestone back with his doll on his knee (it's now her 27th birthday), while his mother shouts at him through the door, telling him it isn't right to shut himself up like that and he should get a life. So the whole thing has been a man's projection of his own hang- ups onto an imaginary woman, which is maybe what art is, or maybe just what men are. And the play itself has been a set of Chinese boxes. And the ventriloquist's name is Parker.

It's clever, to the point where cleverness becomes silliness. Everything adds up, or at least everything in the play has an echo somewhere else, but everything cancels out. It has its compelling moments, and its witty ones, including Gaby's declaration that she doesn't "want to be like those stupid girls who think the world doesn't revolve around them." But Gaby and Dr. Limestone and their various appurtenances are never more than their author's own dummies. And the fact that this may be the point doesn't make it any less frustrating. Keith Turnbull's production of his own translation is kept afloat by Meg Roe, an actress new to Toronto, as the gabby Gaby. Nigel Shawn Williams is fine at the play's top and tail, which put him in shy mode, but he doesn't have the fluency or the authority for the shrink in the middle. French-Canadian drama seems to be going through an aridly self-conscious phase, at least judging from the examples that reach Toronto of which this is the latest

There is, as it happens, a corker of a play going around about the interaction of truth with storytelling, one that tells a really good story itself. It's The Pillowman, by Irish playwright Martin McDonagh, whose The Beauty Queen of Leenane knocked us out a few years ago, and whose The Lieutentant of Inishmore is a hit in New York. It's been done all over Canada, even by students, but the Toronto theatre has yet to stage it. This may not be Toronto's fault -- the rights to major plays are held up for years by the prospect of first-rate tours that never materialize -- but it's the Toronto audience's misfortune.
