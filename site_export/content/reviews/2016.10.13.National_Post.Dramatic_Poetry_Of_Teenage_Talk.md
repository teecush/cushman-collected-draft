THEATRE REVIEW

Concord Floral CanStage Bluma Appel Theatre

Jordan Tannahill's Concord Floral is a magnetic piece of theatre. It has two poles, ancient and modern. The old one is Giovanni Boccaccio's The Decameron, the Italian counterpart of The Canterbury Tales, in which ten young people, taking shelter while fleeing plague-stricken medieval Florence, beguile the time by telling stories.

The present-day inspiration, the one that gives the play its colourful title, is in Tannahill's own programnote description, "a million-square foot abandoned greenhouse in Vaughan."

He attended a party there five years ago, and what with "the piles of old cigarette butts, broken beer bottles, underwear, used condoms, and graffiti," it reminded him of the places where he and his friends used to hang out when they were teenagers.

Tannahill was reading The Decameron at the time of the party. He put the two pieces together, making his characters 10 Grade 12 students who are reading the book as a class project (they must have an enlightened teacher), and whose favourite gathering place, other than the school cafeteria, is their very own Concord Floral. And from that place they let loose their very own plague.

It's a deceptive piece in some ways, full of surprises. We assume, from the Italian connection, that the dozen teens are all going to tell their own tales, plain or fanciful. As it turns out, there's only one story and it involves the kids themselves. There are a few personal monologues operating at a tangent to that story but we are mostly caught up in a single, driving narrative.

Two girls, named Nearly Wild and Rosa Mundi, make a night-trip to Concord Floral; Rosa drops her cellphone through one of the many holes in the floor; Nearly descends to retrieve it, and finds a dead female body. They flee the scene, in horror and terror. That night Nearly gets a call, purporting to come from the dead girl using the missing phone.

Tannahill has written a bravura script that makes dramatic poetry of teenage talk. We respond to the writing, though, less for its own sake than as one element in a tightly-wound theatrical experience. The production, directed by Erin Brubacher and Cara Spooner, has been in development for three years and has been staged, with varying casts, in Toronto, Ottawa and Whitehorse. It has always been performed by real teenagers, some of them aspiring professional actors, some not. Its first full Toronto run puts the audience up with the actors. They perform on a platform at one end of the Bluma Appel stage; we sit on risers, facing them.

The Bluma audi torium yawns beside and beneath us. Concord Floral is thrillingly concentrated, a continuous symphony of speech, movement, lighting (Kimberley Purtell) and especially sound (Christopher Willes).

The actors are microphoned which, given the confined space, doesn' t bode too well for the future of Canadian acting. Still, their voices an immediacy that comes out well in the mix. Jovana Miladinovic is fresh and unsparing as Nearly who starts out sympathetic and then reveals unsettling complexities; there is ear-catching work too from Jessica Munk as her ghostly caller, and from Franco Pang and Theo Gallaro. Some of the young people get to double as wildlife; a fox and a bobolink help tell the story and have crucial roles in it. So do inanimate objects; a couch is given a voice and so is the greenhouse itself.

There's a built-in puzzle to the story, one that gets resolved in a way that's quite unexpected and perfectly satisfying. Emotionally it lets the audience off a hook, but it doesn't do the same for the characters.

The narrative involves shaming, ganging-up and teenage groupthink; the things that it's fashionable to blame on "youth today" and social media but that the play is honest enough to admit has always been around, even if technology has made it faster and easier.

The greenhouse, Concord Floral itself, has the last word, a quotation from Susan Sontag that's also printed in the program: "10 per cent of any population is cruel, no matter what, and 10 per cent is merciful, no matter what, and the remaining 80 per cent can be moved in either direction."

It then suggests that the 80 may be moving toward mercy. It's a nice thought, but on the play's own previous showing, a pious hope.
