THEATRE REVIEW

Caroline, or Change Berkeley Street Theatre, Toronto

I have lost count of the American shows that someone or other has hailed as "the first great musical of the 21st century." They include - and please don't laugh - Spring Awakening, The Producers, Avenue Q, American Idiot, Next to Normal and Caroline, or Change. The last - book and lyrics by Tony Kushner, music by Jeanine Tesori - is the one with a serious claim: the most exciting, the most moving, simply the most.

So it certainly seems in the show's Canadian premiere, a triumphant joint production by Acting Up Stage and Obsidian. Caroline is a black maid, working for a white Jewish family in Louisiana in 1963. "Change" refers both to the political transformation that's about to overtake the country, and to the coins that Noah, the eight-year-old son of the household, has an incorrigible habit of leaving in his pants pocket.

Noah is traumatized by his mother's death from cancer and by his father's second marriage; the stepmother is a New Yorker named Rose, who is often flummoxed by her new surroundings but wants to do right by them. To teach Noah providence and tidiness, she tells Caroline, who has always been scrupulous about returning his coins when she finds them in the laundry, that from now on she may keep them. Caroline resents the implied charity, but working a six-day week for $30 and with three children to feed, clothe and medicate, she's hardly in a position to refuse.

Out of their individual deprivations, Noah and Caroline have formed a bond; at least, she lets him light her cigarettes, which is a big thrill.

Somewhere in the first act, the president is assassinated. This galvanizes everyone, or at least makes them think. Caroline muses, in remarkably nuanced song, about JFK having at least had good intentions. On civil rights, as on most other things, she's an angry stoic. She has an older son in Vietnam, and her vision of the future stretches to being able to read a map so she can find out just where that is.

Arlene Duncan, playing her, manages at this point as at many others to make limited horizons seem radiant. Her teenage daughter is far more militant, though, as a Martin Luther King supporter, not militant enough to suit Rose's father, who comes to visit for Hanukkah. He's an unreconstructed 1930s radical who might have been one of the good guys in a Marc Blitzstein agitprop musical.

Granddad also performs the narrative function of making Noah a present of a $20 bill, which he leaves in the laundry. This occasions a delicious musical sequence in which he counts off the hours till he can get home from school and rescue his riches. More significantly, it precipitates a bitter battle between Noah and Caroline in which both say unforgivable things.

The show keeps the personal, the political and the grey areas in between in almost perfect balance. There's very little dialogue; the score carries the narrative and the ideas, and the sophisticated results make all the bloated humourless pop operas look sick.

I haven't been the greatest fan of Kushner's plays; the two-part Angels in America struck me as a good small piece about Roy Cohn unmercifully distended. But his actual writing gifts have never been in doubt, and he turns out to be a very good lyricist. He knows what rhymes are, and what they're good for; he's conversational without being banal; he writes to character, action and themes; and he's off-handedly funny, notably in a couplet about lighting the Menorah, though it isn't in the Torah.

There's blues and there's gospel. Supposedly soulful roof-raising anthems have been ruining the musical ever since Jennifer Holliday told the world she wasn't going at the first-act curtain of Dreamgirls; Duncan, who superbly conveys both Caroline's pride and her hurt, redeems their honour and their relevance here. And she does it unplugged, and to invisible accompaniment. Reza Jacobs' musical direction is immaculate, suggesting an awesome and justified degree of trust between his offstage five-piece band and the cast.

There are some flaws, though. A commentative trio modelled on the Supremes is a device that has been done to death in other shows and that this one can't make fresh. It's impossible to tell that the other anonymous singers who appear in the basement are meant to be the voices of the appliances or that the messenger of Kennedy's death is meant to be a bus rather than its driver.

Otherwise Robert McQueen, who directed Acting Up Stage's beautiful Light in the Piazza, moves masterfully between the various levels, physical and thematic, with crucial assistance from Tim French's choreography and Kimberly Purtell's lighting.

Michael Levinson's Noah is a phenomenal child performance and Deborah Hay's Rose a dazzling grown-up one, a devastatingly well-observed portrayal of a woman striving for brisk cheerfulness while actually at her wits' end. There is also blazing work from Alana Hibbert as Caroline's skeptical friend and from Sabryn Rock as the activist daughter whose closing number is the show's nearest approach to traditional musicaltheatre affirmation. It's also a significant departure from tradition since you would expect the mother, the central figure, to have the last word.

The double title may be more than a cute excuse for a pun. It may be about choice.
