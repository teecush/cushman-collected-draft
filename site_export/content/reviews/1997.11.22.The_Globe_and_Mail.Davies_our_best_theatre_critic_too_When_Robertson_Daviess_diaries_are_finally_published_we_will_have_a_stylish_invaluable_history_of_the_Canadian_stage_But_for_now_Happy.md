Davies our best theatre critic, too

When Robertson Davies's diaries are finally published, we will have a stylish, invaluable history of the Canadian stage. But for now, Happy Alchemy is a blend of the inspired, the urbane, the pedantic and the querulous. HAPPY ALCHEMY: Writings on the Theatre and Other Lively Arts

Saturday, November 22, 1997

By Robertson Davies

Edited by Jennifer Surridge and Brenda Davies


  


McClelland & Stewart, 384 pages, $32.50

ROBERTSON Davies was an actor when young and wrote plays and opera libretti when older. He also wrote prolifically about the theatre, most notably in a trilogy of books chronicling the first years of the Stratford Festival, from 1953 to 1955: Renown at Stratford ,followed by Twice Have the Trumpets Sounded and, a little desperately, Thrice the Brinded Cat Hath Mewed .He and his publishers then called a halt, perhaps because they were unable to find a Shakespearean line about something happening four times.

It's also possible that as the festival grew and became established, Davies felt that it was time for celebration to give way to criticism. "If you can't spray the acid around," he wrote, "you are no use as a critic" that was a task which -- at least in public -- he felt unable to perform. A bad review from Davies would have been especially hard to stomach, because he so obviously knew what he was talking about.

In fact, the three Stratford books, especially the second, are enough to establish Davies as the finest of Canadian theatre critics. He was able to seize a national moment and place it in an international context. He conducted acute and probing meditations on the plays themselves. (He called The Taming of the Shrew a great play; this is demonstrably true -- the play always works -- but it was a statement as critically eccentric 40 years ago as it would be politically incendiary today.) He could describe and evaluate production and performance, and could estimate the contribution of each to the overall experience.

I associate some of his achievement with his having been involved himself. I don't mean that he knew in detail how actors and technicians go about their work; he may have done, but that kind of specialized expertise has little bearing on the broad task of writing a review. (Though since theatre is a collaborative art, it does help to have some idea of who does what before handing round the praise and blame. I have noticed, for example, that literary-minded critics have an exaggerated belief in the power of the director.) What I have in mind is not so much experience as temperament: The flair for recreating a performance on the page is likely to co-exist with the urge to join in. It hardly matters how well or badly; Davies never claimed to have been an especially good actor, though he said that he had his moments. But the power of evoking the event, of matching its rhythms with his own, was one that Davies possessed in spades.

In later life he did it in a kind of shorthand. From 1957 to 1995, he set down notes on every production he saw. These are nearly as illuminating as his full-length pieces, and often more trenchant. (On Barry MacGregor as a pox-ridden pimp in Pericles :"Despite all this elaborate decay he continued to look like a hero trying to be a wreck.") With no immediate readership in mind, he was able to spray the acid around, and with deadly aim. Of the four actors who have played Richard III at Stratford, "Colm Feore is the dismal forty-fourth" the whole production proves that the festival (this was in 1988) "needs shaking up from top to bottom" "Sheila Nadler as Mrs. Quickly [in Verdi's Falstaff ]has a fine voice and presence but is too pleased with herself as a comic." I never saw the performance, but I get exactly what he means, just as I can when he assesses Christopher Plummer's long-ago Cyrano: "Plummer's style is dry and ironic and he cannot suggest adoration . . .but in all that was grotesque, humorous and daring he was admirable." My admiration remains when I read him on shows I have seen myself. There is one recent Stratford production to which he seems to me absurdly indulgent, but on individual performances -- a whole raft of them, for example, in the Shaw Festival production of The Silver King --he is almost unchallengeable.

Happy Alchemy -- a phrase adopted by Davies to denote the transmutation of good theatre into great -- is a collection of essays and speeches to which a few extracts from the theatre diaries have been affixed as headnotes. In time we should have the diaries in full, but that time, in deference to the late author's wishes, is not yet. When they are published we will have a stylish history of the Canadian stage, or at least the classical and operatic end of it, over a period of 40 years. It should be invaluable.

So much for the wonderful book we don't have. The one we do have is uneven: a blend of the inspired, the urbane, the pedantic and the querulous. Davies is capable of writing a zestful, measured panegyric on the classical Athenians, fastidiously distinguishing their notions of democracy from ours: "The Greeks would have found the trust we place in elected persons utterly naive; they assumed, not quite that all men are crooks, but that all men are the better for watching." He then cheapens the whole thing, or his editors do it for him, by tacking on a schoolmasterly playlet about the Oedipus family, in which the wit gives way to smug jokiness.

The speeches are written with jaunty affability that can slide into condescension: It's one thing to buttonhole your audience; it's another to pat them on their darling little heads. "I am sure that some of you are from the United States, and perhaps you are wondering: Surely we too are a witty people? Of course you are." It may have been charming in person, but it grates on the page, and the manner infects the essays as well, nagging at their innate elegance. The editors have exacerbated it by retaining repetition from one piece to another. We don't need, though in their preface they protest that we do, to be told three times that Donizetti's Lucia di Lammermoor is a travesty of Walter Scott's novel, or twice that Vaughan Williams, being English, made a better job of musicalizing The Merry Wives of Windsor than Verdi, who couldn't help turning it into an Italian sex farce. (I don't dispute either opinion, by the way).

I have especially in mind a curious essay entitled Opera and Humour. It's curious because most of it is given over to a historical analysis of the things that make us laugh, a survey that, in typical Davies fashion, manages to be both concise and expansive. The section on opera arrives late and never confronts a basic point: Music, except at the level of rude noises, can only be funny if it is supporting funny words or parodying other music.

Davies's view of culture might be called maverick-aristocratic. He manages, in lamenting the decay in Canada of humane learning, to play the pieties of right and left alike. Sometimes, though, he betrays himself. He refers in passing to a Stratford production of Love's Labour's Lost ,and quotes a speech in which the curate Sir Nathaniel excoriates the ignorance of Costard the clown. "Sir," the speech begins, "he hath never fed of the dainties that are bred in a book. He hath not eat paper, as it were; he hath not drunk ink." Right on, says Davies, and please tell our philistine legislators.

Unfortunately, it isn't that simple. In the play, the words -- a series of amusingly strained metaphors -- are delivered by a sycophantic pedant to a pompous pedant. The clown, described by Davies as "a stupid oaf," proves, like so many Shakespearean fools, to be smarter than either of them. The play is actually about the triumph of experience over book-learning. It exalts, though not in terms any politician would be likely to understand, the virtues of common sense. If Davies had been responding to the play rather than simply using it, he would have enjoyed proclaiming this fact, especially if an actor worth celebrating were playing Costard.

Celebration is the key. Defining himself as a passionate playgoer rather than a critic, he says, "Critics often do not like the theatre; I have liked nothing better." This is unfair polemic -- most theatre critics do like the theatre whatever you might suspect from their writing -- but unimpeachable autobiography. Discussing Bernard Shaw's criticism, Davies writes of "a high-spirited enthusiasm which is always rare and which we seek with an unappeasable hunger." He might have been describing himself. He probably was.


  


Robert Cushman is a cultural and theatre critic.
