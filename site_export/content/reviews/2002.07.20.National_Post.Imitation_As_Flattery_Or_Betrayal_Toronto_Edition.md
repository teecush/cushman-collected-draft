Until Aug. 4. 416-872-1111

FRANK -- A LIFE IN SONG

Bluma Appel Theatre

- - -

I have never seen the point of having a performer devote a whole evening to imitating another performer, especially when the one imitated is a singer whose authentic work is still easily available. Tom Burlinson's Frank -- A Life in Song does nothing to shake my skepticism.

Burlinson is an Australian (though born in Canada) who dubbed the singing voice of the younger Frank Sinatra in the biographical TV series that aired in 1991. Burlinson's voice was certainly a very close fit.

He has now expanded this impersonation into a full-length stage show in which he sings cornerstones of the Sinatra repertoire, from All or Nothing at All to New York, New York, and makes with the gestures as well. He captures the elusive Sinatra timbre unusually well, and he carefully copies as much as he can of the phrasing; his mimicry is at its most confident at the beginning of a song; but he tends to lose both tone and conviction at the end of a phrase: a problem that the original never had.

At best, though, it's a party-trick, good for perhaps two or three songs. The show is presumably aimed at Sinatra fans (who else would care?) but this fan, at least, wants none of it.

There are three levels to the Sinatra repertoire. First, and most importantly, there are the masterpieces of American popular song, songs that would have been classics even if Sinatra had never touched them, but are unquestionably the better for his attention. Burlinson gives us several of them. But these numbers -- I've Got the World on a String, I've Got You Under My Skin, Night and Day and (to a lesser extent) You Make Me Feel So Young -- offer infinite possibilities for interpretation; why would a singer want to limit himself by reproducing what another singer, however gifted, has already done with them?

Then there are the songs -- mostly written for Sinatra by his on- call team of Sammy Cahn and Jimmy Van Heusen -- that are good but only survive by virtue of his association with them: The Tender Trap, Come Fly with Me, All the Way. These, by and large, are the ones Burlinson does best, maybe because they will always sound like an imitation Sinatra, whoever does them.

Finally, there are the songs that nobody, including Sinatra, should ever have sung in the first place. My evening was shadowed from the start by the certain knowledge that, at some point in it, Burlinson was going to sing My Way. In fact, he saves it till last: a valedictory position in which it sounds even phonier than usual. Burlinson has not been doing it his way, he has made a point of doing it someone else's way. But he cannot claim to be doing the song in character either. This show is being mounted in a theatre, but it is almost totally lacking in dramatic content.

When Burlinson talks to us between songs, he does it solely in his own person. He seems an agreeable enough young man (or youngish - - he's 45) but the script he has given himself is atrocious. Purporting to be a casual barebones biography of Sinatra (whom he insists, gratingly, on referring to as Frank), it sounds like a bad magazine digest with no relation to normal human speech.

It is also stuffed with familiar anecdotes that the knowledgeable will be able to see coming a long way off. (Fair's fair; he does come up with a charming Sinatra quote about Johnny Mercer that I'd never heard before.) There are only two sequences of relief. One is a quick enumeration of Sinatra's good and bad personal qualities; the other, very near the end, comes when Burlinson talks, unpretentiously and with a modicum of wit, about Burlinson, or rather about his own fascination with his subject. In both of these there is some personality and the evening begins, belatedly, to make some sense.

His choice of songs is depressingly obvious. Sinatra's greatest achievement was his exploration and elevation, through album after album, of the American songbook, a legacy that extended far beyond what are accepted as his signature songs.

Burlinson, oddly, steers away from the few Sinatra recordings that might be considered directly autobiographical (Nancy, I'm a Fool to Want You, the entire September of My Years collection) but he sings only one song that would not immediately be tagged as "a Sinatra song"; it's Nevertheless, quite effectively linked to the breakup with Ava Gardner, and sung winsomely and well.

Indeed, Burlinson seems at his best on ballads; the contemplative tempo forces the performer to think through the lyrics, even if the thoughts he comes up with are another man's. One for My Baby remains moving, even at second hand.

In the up numbers, though, he is going, rather too enthusiastically, through somebody else's motions, physical and vocal, and so comes off like any other Vegas clone. When the supreme style and confidence of the master are lacking, the swingin' style becomes rather repellent.

Burlinson has good assistance from a pair of dancers, Aaron Cash and (especially) Mandy Carnie; their jitterbug routine is a treat. Even more of one is the sound of the original Sinatra arrangements (sans strings) played by an onstage big band.

Indeed, the evening becomes less a tribute to Sinatra than to Billy May, Quincy Jones and, above all, Nelson Riddle; their work, after all, was meant to be relayed to us by other people. Sinatra once said that he wished somebody else could learn to do what he did, so that his art didn't die with him. I doubt that he meant he wanted others to copy him; rather that he wanted them to sing popular songs as inquiringly as he did but on their own, equally independent, terms. In this context imitation, like other forms of flattery, is a betrayal.
