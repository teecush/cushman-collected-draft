SCARAMOUCHE JONES

Winter Garden Theatre, Toronto

All the world, or so Cole Porter once said, loves a clown, a proposition thats put to a hefty test in Scaramouche Jones.

This is a one-man play performed by Pete Postlethwaite, the British actor whom Steven Spielberg is said to have described as the best in the world. Postlethwaite has greeted this accolade with amused self-deprecation; and I suspect that Spielberg, if he said anything, was inadvertently paying tribute to a breed of actors who dont exist in the United States, and who may be an endangered species even in the United Kingdom. They cut their teeth in regional repertory (Postlethwaite is recognizably a northerner), progressed into the national classical companies and became character actors in film and on television. A few (Bob Hoskins, Patrick Stewart, Alan Rickman, Brenda Blethyn) hit the right role and became stars; Postlethwaite's break was In the Name of the Father.

Since the early 1970s, which is when he came to London and when I first began watching him, he has never to my knowledge given a bad performance. He is a remarkable blend of tough and sensitive, capable of putting his vulnerability on hold when he plays a sneak or a villain. This particular abnegation is not required of him in Scaramouche Jones, in which he is all gentleness, reacting with resilient amazement to a brutal world. His Scaramouche is a circus funnyman on the tip of his 100th birthday, though looking wonderful for his age. Postlethwaite, phenomenally unpushy for a solo performer, is soft-edged without ever being soft-centred.

The play he's in, written by Justin Butcher, is another thing: little more than a contrivance. We meet Scaramouche in the last hour or so of 1999. When the millennium expires, he expects and intends to die with it. This will be satisfyingly symmetrical since he was born on the stroke of midnight, as 1899 yielded to 1900. He was the son of a gypsy prostitute in Trinidad; his fathers name, as he repeatedly informs us, was Legion (for we are many") but his mother told him that daddy was an Englishman and he hugs this information to himself with pride. He will in fact end up in England, but he doesnt get there until both he and the century are half done.

Before that he was orphaned, kidnapped and sold into slavery; his master is a North African snake charmer with whom he is reasonably happy, and from whom he learns many things, including classical Latin. In his thirties, moving eastward across Europe, he connects with his gypsy roots, an experience that gains him a brace of cruel beatings, both from his own folk and from the authorities plus a mockery of a marriage.

The Second World War finds him in Italy; he is sent to a concentration camp at Split in Croatia, where he is put to work as a gravedigger. Here he discovers his talent for comedy; he makes funny faces to amuse the children who are about to be machine-gunned and tossed into the pit.

His path here recalls, but varies from, that pursued by Roberto Benigni in Life Is Beautiful; the latter's clown character came into a camp and put his known comic talent to use, whereas Scaramouche only finds his vocation in extremis. Postlethwaite, mercifully, is less benign than Benigni; he doesn't ask to be loved, and the show, by virtue of that, stops short of being offensive. But it is very suspect.

The idea seems to be to give us a clowns eye view of the last century; Scaramouche, though he has plenty of troubles of his own, is initially shielded by geography from the worst public events -- he occasionally refers to them as distant rumours -- but is then brought right up against the periods defining atrocity. But we all know that the 20th century was a chamber of horrors, and a dramatist who wants to exploit them has to earn the privilege. Butcher's account of the camp at Spalato (a.k.a. Split) seems to be a Holocaust compendium; it has gas chambers and execution pits. In reality, the one, at Auschwitz and Treblinka, replaced the other, which had flourished, if that's the word, on the Russian front. Maybe Ive been misinformed, but its news to me that there were gas chambers in the Western camps; they were confined to the extermination centres of Poland. In the West there were prisons and transit camps; they weren't pleasant, but neither were they slaughterhouses. The designated victims were moved on. The play seems to be taking a free ride on history, and on our pre- conditioned reactions to it.

After the war, Scaramouche moves on to England, having acquired a surname, courtesy of a friendly Welsh customs officer who -- rather surprisingly -- recommends Jones as a good old English name, rather than fiercely reserving it for the Welsh. Maybe he's being ironic. Here our hero becomes a clown, apparently a very successful one. We arent told the details. The second 50 years are skated over; what we thought would be the intermission turns out to be the final curtain. We do see Scaramouche, before embarking on his autobiography, shaking his head over the news from East Timor, in implicit recognition that humanity hasnt improved much. What is ducked completely is the really interesting question: How could a man with his experiences embark on a career as a children's entertainer? How did he use his memories? How, in fact, did it all work? There is a running image of Scaramouche, as one trauma succeeds another in his life, acquiring layers of white faces, till he finally graduates to the professional white of clownage. I didn't find it very illuminating. What is more subtle and less pretentious than the rest, is the theme of the colonial child hanging on to his Englishness, as England itself shrinks and the Empire disappears.

Otherwise the script is journeyman work; it tells its story, isnt boring and includes a couple of good jokes. And it provides a vehicle for a masterful actor, one who neither misses nor belabours a beat and whose body is as controlled and expansive as his mind and voice. In Canadian terms,Id say he combines the earthiness of Randy Hughson with the fastidiousness of Diego Matamoros. (He has also become the movies' best imitation Canadian, courtesy of performances in The Shipping News and Between Strangers.) It's great to have him among us. I wish he were here in a play.
