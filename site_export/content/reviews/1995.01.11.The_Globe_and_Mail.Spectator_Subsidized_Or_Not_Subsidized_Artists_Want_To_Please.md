SPECTATOR Subsidized or not subsidized, artists want to please

Wednesday, January 11, 1995

ROBERT CUSHMAN

BY ROBERT CUSHMAN THE cultural free market, or what likes to think of itself as such, has been running riot in The Globe and Mail the past two Saturdays. First Andrew Coyne, then William Thorsell.

It was, I confess, cheering to find Coyne, in his suave diatribe against subsidy, insisting on the importance of the audience to the various transactions that we lump together under the heading of art. As Coyne says, in most discussions about art the audience gets left out. Artists lured into public debate talk usually about themselves, occasionally about the few other artists of whom they approve, frequently and with understandable hostility about the critics who are a kind of audience but hardly a representative one. Coyne queries "where the relationship between the artist and his audience begins." It's a good question. But I don't get much out of his answers because, when he talks about the audience, I don't know who or what he means.

Does he think of the audience as one, undifferentiated mass whose taste can easily be gauged in advance? Does he think of it as somehow reducible to a single person? That suggests very tough times indeed, though it's the only context in which his suggestion that "the willing paying audience is present at the creation" makes any kind of sense. A painter might conceivably find room in the studio for a solitary spectator, and they might have some intense conversations about the brushwork; but it doesn't seem a very comfortable arrangement, even given the most cultivated of onlookers. Up the numbers and it becomes self-evidently absurd.

With performed art, there is an important sense in which a play or a piece of music only comes into existence in the presence of an audience. But many of the irrevocable decisions have already been taken before that happens. The show has been rehearsed, and an audience (whose function is to be an outside factor) has no place at rehearsals. Maybe Coyne sees the consulting process as taking place even earlier, with the artist or artists approaching prospective customers and saying, "Look, we have this idea for a comedy or a sculpture or a nude mime of a black mass, does it sound like something you'd be willing to pay money to see?" This does admittedly bear some resemblance to the way in which a commercial production solicits investors, but that has nothing to do with asking the audience what they want. It's asking other people what they think the audience will want.

When we see the finished product, we may hate it. In which case it is our right and maybe our duty to leave, and vow never to spend time or money on any such entertainment again, and advise our friends to go, or rather not go, and do likewise. (Of course their tastes may differ from ours, but subtleties like these don't seem to be Coyne's concern.) It's called voting with our feet; it's a good old custom, and at the more rational points of his article Coyne seems in favour of it; Thorsell too. But they have got it into their heads that this is an audience prerogative that arts subsidy has eliminated. Coyne sees "unconditional granting agencies" indulging the whims of a bunch of pampered esthetes, oblivious of what the public may actually turn out to enjoy. Thorsell - well, if I read him rightly, he believes that country music is a brand-new popular form, called into existence by the Republican majority in the U.S. Congress.

I don't know what world they're living in, but I've never been there. Arts councils are actually cash-strapped committees agonizing between rival claimants; and no subsidized organization gets enough. Do you think the Stratford Festival would have announced such a miserably diminished season for 1995 if they could afford to be independent of the box office? (I'm sorry, but the theatre is the form of which I have most experience, so my terms and examples tend to come from there.) But even if they could, even if they were granted so much money that they could play with impunity to totally empty houses, no company would want to; and not only because their paymasters would get nervous. Artists hate being ignored. A work of art, any work of art, is a statement begging for a response; there is nothing more frustrating than the sound of no hands clapping.

Artists - like industrialists, politicians and journalists, especially columnists - are driven largely by vanity. It comes with the territory. So why do we indulge them in their self-importance? Because they make us feel important, too. They don't do it for all of us, and it's perfectly possible to lead a good, useful and intelligent life without being interested in any of the arts. But most of us have some kind of pleasurable response to some kind of music, drama, literature or visual representation.

Well, all right, but why should the government get involved? Coyne muddies his own waters by referring back to the golden eras of patronage, when Renaissance princes commissioned what masterpieces they wanted and took the consequences. So they did, but the resultant works were then available to all sorts of people who had not placed an order and who should therefore, by Coyne's logic, have been debarred from taking any meaningful pleasure in them. (And the descendants of these peasants actually have the gall to go and gawk at the stuff in galleries, some of it which until recently didn't even have the grace to charge admission.) He also tells us that such things as opera "began as popular art forms." Again true, but tastes have changed and costs have risen.

Few contemporary opera lovers could afford the prices that an unsubsidized opera house would have to charge. That is what subsidy is for: to help audiences, present and future. If it keeps artists from starving that's a benevolent side-effect, but it's not the main point. Why should those who don't like opera help pay for the fun of those who do? Well, why shouldn't they? We all pay taxes for some things we don't use or even like. (Pace Thorsell, it's not only cultural spending that would be questioned if our tax-dollars were itemized.) At this point in the argument it's traditional to invoke a comparison with schools or hospitals, but chances are that Coyne, who makes most Globe editorial writers seem like bleeding-heart liberals, doesn't approve of spending public money on them either. So how about public parks? Not everybody walks in them, but most people would admit their desirability.

Like Coyne I'm embarrassed by some of the claims that are made for subsidized, or indeed non-subsidized, art. I don't think that art has, or should have, much to do with "establishing a national identity" - except in the very important sense that the availability of enjoyable art, from any source, makes a country a nicer place to live in. Like Thorsell, I have my doubts about "Canadian cultural nationalism," though I can't see what this has to do with the attempts of the commercial monopolies who supply cable television to squeeze as much money as possible out of their subscribers. He seems to be using the admirable public revolt against this as a convoluted way of mounting yet another attack on the CBC.

As it happens, I grew up with British television, where subsidized and commercial networks had a virtually equal share of the action. It's obvious that the superiority of British to U.S. television is due to the British commercial companies having had the BBC to compete with and to emulate. Unlike Thorsell I wouldn't call that an elitist situation (isn't it fascinating the way that left and right have joined hands over the use of that word), but if it is, then the trouble with CBC-TV is that it's not elitist enough.

I also went a lot to the theatre, subsidized and other. I was the same person, with the same potential relation to what happened on stage, whether subsidized or wholly commercial. Many in the audience were probably unaware of which sector they were attending. Writers, actors and directors went with equal alacrity from one system to the other, and were as hard-working and as eager to please in either situation.

I don't know precisely what Thorsell's artistic tastes are, but I suspect that if he had to rely entirely on the free market to satisfy them, he might end up frustrated. Coyne disclaims any "neo-conservative ideology," but his theories are so wildly at variance with the facts that it's difficult to see what else could be driving him.
