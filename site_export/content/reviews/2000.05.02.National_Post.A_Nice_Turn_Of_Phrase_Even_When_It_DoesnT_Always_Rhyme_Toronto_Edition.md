WORLD STAGE WRAP-UP

Anything That Moves (Canadian Stage Theatre Upstairs); Dali (Tarragon Extra Space), Toronto

Watching and listening to Anything That Moves, I was all set to salute Ann-Marie MacDonald as that rare bird, a playwright who can write lyrics: This at a time when even most lyricists can't write lyrics.

Six songs into Act One, however, she shook my confidence severely by having one of her characters launch full-throated into the astounding declaration "Monogamy! Monotony! There's a reason why they rhyme!"

There are far better reasons why they don't rhyme: hard, factual reasons having to do with the sound of words and the stressing of syllables. "Monotony" could rhyme with "botany", and Irving Berlin once built a whole song by pairing it with "have you got any", accent on the "got."

A celebrated jingle rhymed "monogamy" with "made a hog o' me", and that's good too. I trust you can hear the difference. I think people who believe that "monotony" rhymes with "monogamy" are largely to blame for the state of the world.

So, as it turns out, does MacDonald herself, though for reasons more moral than aesthetic.

Her musical -- it does seem to be her musical, more than the composer's -- is a paean to monogamy, even if of the serial kind. Straight, gay, or some of each, its characters end up wanting commitment.

The central romance is between Jinny, "research scientist by day, party girl by night", who has just broken up with the latest in a long line of unsatisfactory boy-friends, and Joel, a florist, whom she likes but whom she thinks is gay. Or rather whom she likes because she thinks he's gay. He isn't, and he's also in love with her, and much of the action concerns his desperate attempts to find the right moment to enlighten her (Keeping the charade going involves the author in some desperate measures herself.)

As a further caution against sexual stereotyping we have Tyrone, Joel's friend, who proves that it is possible to be a male chauvinist pig, even when your lovers are of the same gender, but who has his good side, too. Then there is Alberta, a funeral director whose business ("the last Tim Horton's on the highway of life") dovetails with Joel's for obvious reasons, and who is a lesbian who wants a child, or at least wants her partner to have a child.

There is a passing transvestite called Auntie Fey; and there is Jinny's mother, a recovering alcoholic taking it one day at a time while maintaining the standard abrasive relationship with her daughter; and Joel's long-distant father, a kilted military historian with skeletons in his sporran; so, altogether, all human life is there.

This is the first full-blown Canadian musical in at least a decade that is neither a campy spoof nor a rock-brained epic, and some of it is elating. Lapses apart, MacDonald writes high-gear lyrics, fluent and funny and occasionally penetrating.

There's a number called The Wardrobe Crisis in which Jinny, preparing for a date, enumerates the hang-ups in both her closet (literally) and her mind; it goes by at just the right speed and is a new addition to the treasurable list of list-songs. Allen Cole's music is more problematic; there are a couple of adventurous ballads, but in a more cheerful mood it simply tinkles.

But then Alisa Palmer's direction, though technically slick, never seems quite sure of its style; at various times the show recalls She Loves Me, Company and even Little Shop of Horrors. Mostly, though, it seems to derive from the innocent British shows of the '50s.

Passers-by in the street join in the hero's happiness and twirl umbrellas; it's as if Salad Days had decided to come out. But it's impossible to tell whether the resemblance is intentional or has just happened. A lyric that asks "why can't a straight man be more like a gay?" harks back very obviously to My Fair Lady but to no particular effect. It doesn't seem to be part of a whole structure of pastiche; it's just a nudge in the ribs, and less effectively knowing than it might think.

All the characters are very nice people, but they have their problems, and the playwright in MacDonald orchestrates and resolves these very skilfully in a dinner-party scene that takes up most of the second act. The songwriter in her seems more at a loss, as if she sensed she was no longer needed.

A forced show-stopper called Menopausal Mama disrupts both the text and the texture. On the other hand, a bitter father-son duet, Do You Remember?, finds both lyricist and composer at their best. At moments like these, and in the snappy duet for Joel and Jinny that succeeds it, there seems to be real hope for the human race. (The finale lets us down, but best not to dwell on it.)

Dan Chameroy, escaping from his usual musclebound roles, has considerable charm as Joel, and Tamara Bernier has considerable bite as Jinny. The others -- Marc Richard, Sandra Caldwell, Judy Marshak, David Dunbar -- are all fine.

It was part of the World Stage Festival (which ended at the weekend) but continues beyond it. The same goes for Dali, the revival of a 15-year-old show -- written, directed and designed by Jim Millan -- that starts with its central figure proclaiming "I am surrealism-m-m" while apparently suspended in a bubble. This is a great image, and the show never manages to top it, but it does keep them coming. It presents the artist in the terms -- calculatedly arbitrary and sometimes entertaining -- of his own art: good for atmosphere, initially terrible for biographical information, though more rewarding in its second half, which makes concessions to clarity. Richard Clarkin, in the title role, balances brilliantly and ineffably on the line between cunning charlatan and inspired natural; whichever he is, you're with him all the way. The rest of the show is split pretty evenly between delight and tedium.

Each of these shows makes a perfectly acceptable evening at its own theatre. In the context of an international festival they looked somewhat shrivelled -- as, perhaps inevitably, did most of the Canadian offerings; they were new productions, selected on their creators' past records, but up against shows from elsewhere that had been run in, sometimes for years.

It's unusual, in fact, for such an event to include home-grown offerings, but it's a risk worth taking, and one show this year more than justified it. Robert Lepage's The Far Side of the Moon not only survived comparisons; it was the pinnacle of the festival.

Not everything from abroad was golden either, but The Suit, The Oresteia, and Gaudeamus made Toronto feel like a privileged place to be. It was, in fact, an extraordinary event; and it cannot now be allowed, even without the sponsorship of duMaurier, to go up in smoke.
