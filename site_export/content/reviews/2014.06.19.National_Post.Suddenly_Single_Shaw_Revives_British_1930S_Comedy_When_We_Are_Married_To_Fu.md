THEAT REREVIEW

When We Are Married Royal Theatre, Niagara-on-the Lake The Philadelphia Story Festival Theatre, Niagara-on-the Lake

'There's none sane here but thee and me. And I'm not too sure about thee." The Shaw Festival's second round of openings pairs two classic comedies from the very late 1930s: one British, one American. Well, maybe not classic but certainly famous: plays that typify particular theatrical brands and have been fairly constantly revived. If we're using the more rigorous definition of plays that endure because they capture, humorously, some facets of human behaviour that refuse to go away, then only the British one might qualify. And, to misappropriate the old north-of-England saying quoted above, I'm not too sure about thee.

The phrase seems apposite when discussing J. B. Priestley's When We Are Married (1938), which is as north-country as they come. Priestley looks back, across 30 years and with a mixture of tenderness and horror, on the Yorkshire society in which he grew up. We are in the town of Clecklewyke, where three couples are about to celebrate their respective and simultaneous silver wedding anniversaries. The three men have risen to great importance in the community, with seats on t' council and also in t' chapel, chapel being where the real power in this bit of land resides. Chapel stands for stable conservative values, like disapproving of anything that's "la-di-da" and anyone who's southern. The chapel's new young organist offends in both respects, and is called on the carpet (a nice one, in an alderman's house) for, among other things, delivering his Messiah later than those of the other local congregations. (It's conceded that it were a first-rate Messiah when it arrived, but still.) He, however, isn't worried by his employers' threats, because he has the goods on them. He has discovered that the parson who had married them all on the same fateful day had not been properly qualified. These respectable couples have been living in a total of 75 years of sin.

I don't know how that revelation went down in 1938 but it doesn't seem especially shocking or hilarious now. That doesn't matter. The play focuses on how the couples react to the discovery, and on how it affects the dynamics of their relationships; and into these Priestley digs amusingly and perceptively. Add in two or three rich peripheral characters, and you have a play that can appear a timelessly shrewd and compassionate human comedy in an exceptional production (I've seen one) and a fair bit of fun in others (I've seen several). Joseph Ziegler's production doesn't make it into the first division, but it stands at the very top of the second.

Socially, politically and connubially, the three husbands are, collectively considered, bullies. Individually, though, they vary, which is the great strength of both the play and the production. The nastiest, whether dealing with uppity organists or with his own wife, is Councillor Parker, played with commanding creepiness by Patrick McManus. He's a self-satisfied soul, unable to conceive that the world may see him any differently than he sees himself; it's therefore especially satisfying when his mouse of a spouse (Catherine McGregor) emerges from the marital shadows to disillusion him, and to prove suddenly not being married can be liberating. The Parkers' relationship is reflected in reverse by that of their friends and neighbours the Soppitts. As played by Patrick Galligan, giving the performance of his life, the untitled Herbert Soppitt resembles an amiably timid vole, creeping along a few steps behind his more vociferous confreres, and with an exotic capacity for seeing other people's points of view. In plot terms he's a worm, of the turning variety; he becomes mentally and physically intoxicated to the point of contradicting his fearsome battleaxe of a partner (Kate Hennig) and even of slapping her face. (No need to be shocked. She hit him first; and internal evidence suggests it wasn't the first time.) Not surprisingly, the play bittersweetly hints at an unfulfilled prior attraction between the milder halves of these two pairs. The symmetry does, however, leave the third couple out on the play's periphery, even though it all takes place in their house. Alderman Helliwell (Thom Marriott) is a loudmouth whose wife (Claire Jullien) has long seen through him, and now has a chance to release her seething feelings; both players are on key, but neither tells us anything that we haven't learned more colourfully from the others.

The production's tact lapses when it comes to some of the surrounding performances. Mary Haney is unexpectedly and unforgivably loud and obvious as a refractory cook. (She gets exit applause, always a bad sign.) Lower down in the domestic ranks, though, there's a small peach of a performance from Jennifer Dzialoszynski as a maid with not just a mind but an entire sensibility of her own.

The American entry is Philip Barry's The Philadelphia Story (1939) which strikes me as an apology for a comedy of manners rather than the thing itself. It was written for Katharine Hepburn and what classic status it has derives mainly from the film version in which she co-starred with Cary Grant and James Stewart; it's also the source of the movie musical High Society, which itself gave birth to several less successful stage versions. It concerns rich girl Tracy Lord, about to contract her second marriage even though she and her first husband are still very much in love. At least we assume they are, because it's that kind of play. It's also the kind in which a father who manages to be both a habitual roue and a stuffed shirt addresses his daughter as "daughter." Both her daddy and her ex keep describing Tracy as an ice-goddess and the play is about how she, with an extra push from a visiting reporter dispatched to dig the dirt on the Pennsylvania plutocrats (i.e. to get the Philadelphia story), learns to step off her icy pedestal. In a better play she, and we, would be able to discover the truth without being lectured about it; it's that moralistic strain that sinks the piece, along with its indecision about whether to deplore or glorify the American class system and the fact that it has the veneer of wit but not much of the substance.

The faults are magnified, rather literally, in Dennis Garnhum's extremely slow production, which is partly done in by the sheer size of the Festival Theatre stage; palatially designed by William Schmuck (his sets get curtain-rise applause, which isn't necessarily a bad sign, but is a warning one since the audience is responding to prettiness rather than relevance) it keeps the actors miles away from one another and destroys their rather fragile relationships. There are good performances, but they all exist in vacuums. Moya O'Connell as Tracy lacks nothing in physical ranginess and little in vocal flair, while Gray Powell is right in there as her twice-beloved, amusedly biding his time; but there isn't much sizzle between them, partly because the script denies them scenes together which, if you aren't Grant and Hepburn bringing your legends with you, makes for a tough double row to hoe.
