First they vamp till ready, then they pontificate. At 8:40 p.m., with the first actual vote results trickling in, the Fox Network decided to chance its arm. "Fox News projects Hillary the winner" it said in determined you-heard-it-here-first tones. This was as exciting as Tuesday evening got. The folks at CNN were a little more cautious; it took them another twenty minutes to project Hillary the winner. A projection sounds more scientific than a prediction, and CNN in particular had batteries of charts and statistics with which to back up its forecasts. They had to have something to while away the hours.

I had forgotten just how tedious election coverage, and primary coverage in particular, can be. It really isn't anyone's fault. We have an essentially static situation that, by tacit consent, has to be covered on a minute-by-minute basis. It started with all the commentators fixating on the exit polls while simultaneously assuring us that these didn't really count for anything. Nevertheless they persisted, rattling off the candidates' constituencies: young, black and Protestant for Obama, old, female and Catholic for Clinton. All were agreed that this was Hillary's race to lose. When it was clear that she wouldn't lose it, they spent the rest of the night telling us that they'd told us so. Their unanimity, as a playwright once said about the rare occasions when people on stage agree about anything, was wonderful. They also reiterated that this still left Obama ahead in the competition for the actual nomination, especially with his campaign having more money in the bank. Everyone took for granted that more money entitles you to an electoral advantage: A dismal phenomenon -- and I'm an Obama fan-- that might have merited some discussion but didn't get any.

A peculiarity of the coverage was that the format and the personnel on each network remained constant whatever program was ostensibly playing: Larry King Live was Larry King absent, and if Bill O'Reilly was present on his own show, he was uncharacteristically bashful about it. CNN had, as mentioned, the classier graphics. Wolf Blitzer stood by, with every appearance of comprehension, while a man with a pointer explained with the aid of three adjacent pie-charts how big a slice Hillary could hope to consume in any number of demographic contingencies. My head spun. Later they ran a map of the state, divided into regions, into light and

dark blue (I guess it's too late to change this confusing convention) according to probable electoral preference. Barack, we were informed, absolutely needed to win the bottom right-hand corner. We had barely absorbed this before being shown the same map as it might have appeared in previous contests. The things they can do with a flick of the switch, or whatever they use nowadays.

Periodically, CNN would leave Wolf at the door and travel to another part of the studio where Campbell Brown, looking peremptorily in charge, would interrogate her panel of experts, all chained to their lap-tops. There was one designated Clintonite, one for Obama, one real live superdelegate (with pronounced Obama leanings) and David Gergen, who did very well as resident wise man. Over on Fox, which one might have expected to be frothing with Republican disdain, the equivalent team was surprisingly restrained. If they were delighted at the spectacle of the opposition tearing itself in half so their own man could pick up the pieces, they weren't showing it. There was some rare tension in the studio when they seemed unable to locate Karl Rove (current job description: Fox News political analyst) but when he did materialize, with no horns visible, he too sounded reasonable and respectful. Maybe it's a plot. Fox commentators got very insistent about the prospect of Howard Dean's knocking his candidates' heads together and forcing them into a dream team--one that might easily become a nightmare and not just for the Republicans.

Otherwise, the most drama came in watching the leap of Hillary's advantage from 8% ("doesn't matter") to 10% ("this means business"). The greatest suspense came from wondering just how long the captions at the bottom of the screen could keep telling us we were "Moments Away from Clinton Victory Speech." At last Hillary mounted the podium (husband to the side of it, looking as if it wasn't his proper place) and ... how to put this ... conceded victory. She was courteous about Obama, and when his turn came, he was the same about her -- in the teeth, I must say, of some of his rowdier supporters. Barack can tell a very good joke, but otherwise rarely smiles; Hillary never says anything funny but smiles all the time. His oration was stirring stuff, and though he couldn't have been making those rolling periods up as he went along, he sounded as though he might be. I know that great speech-making isn't all it takes to make a president, but it isn't as if the electoral process -- in America or anywhere else -- gives a candidate a chance to demonstrate anything else. Or that the media, obsessed with "image" and how things will "play," would want it to. There was one hopeful sign: Later on, a Fox anchor badgered a couple of correspondents about whether "bitter" (that word has become journalistic shorthand, which is depressing in itself ) had done Obama any harm. Neither thought it had. If Obama can do nothing but raise the standard of political discourse, that will be something. It might even filter down to television.
