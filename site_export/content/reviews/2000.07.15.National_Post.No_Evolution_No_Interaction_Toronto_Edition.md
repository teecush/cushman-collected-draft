Stan Rogers -- A Matter of Heart runs to Aug. 5 at the Jane Mallet Theatre at the St. Lawrence Centre, 27 Front St. E. Tickets are $29 to $49 and are available by calling the box office at (416) 386- 7723.

Stan Rogers songs tell stories. That is the reason a theatrical show has now been built around them. There is a difference, though, between telling a story and acting it, and this distinction is the rock on which Stan Rogers -- A Matter of Heart runs aground and founders. (There are a lot of songs here about shipwrecks, and the imagery is catching.)

The show is a moderately elaborate folk-pop concert. Half a dozen musicians play on an assortment of rostra, while four actors sing either on or in front of some shallow flights of steps. Behind everybody is a cyclorama that goes from red to blue and back again, and is pretty in either shade. The performers' choices seem similarly limited: They can sing their songs standing up, or they can sing them sitting down. The vast majority of the numbers are solos.

This is hardly surprising since Rogers, in his brief lifetime, wrote them to sing himself. I'm not a great fan of the kind of show in which one performer spends a whole evening pretending to be another performer, but that might be the best way to present this material in the theatre. It might forge a direct connection between the actor and the audience, as happened long ago in Hank Williams -- The Show He Never Gave, a piece that turned me for one night into a country-music fan (a not inconsiderable feat). This production never establishes Rogers as an individual voice. Its brief narrative passages tell us that he cared passionately about his own roots in the Maritimes, and about what recession had done to the people there. We are told that he gravitated toward characters who had been buffeted by fate but refused to give in to it, and that he chose his subjects -- contemporary or historic, public or private -- accordingly. We are also told, in the evenings one joke, that he wrote about all kinds of ships, including relationships.

As it happens, relationships, in both the emotional and the dramatic sense, are what is largely missing from this show. We can appreciate, listening to the songs here, that Rogers was a strong and honest writer, and that his melodies are more resourceful than those of most neo-folkies, though after a while they do start to sound the same. (This is accentuated by the shows habit of grouping the songs by subject; the first half of the first half seems to be one sea shanty after another.) And the songs are, indeed, about people. The trouble is that in each song a character reveals himself to us, finished and complete, usually in the form of a retrospective monologue; everything has happened before the song starts. We don't see anybody evolve, and we don't see any characters interact. Any of these songs might make an agreeable point of rest in a conventional musical, with a story; but you cant ask them to be the story. As soon as one song is done, we have to start all over again with another, and we lose the will to follow. To make matters worse, the sound is muddy.

Im not sure whether it's due to faults in diction, miking or overall balance, but the words can be very hard to hear, especially when the tempo quickens. This means that the vaunted stories never really get told. It also means that there aren't many laughs. Im sure that theres plenty of gnarled humour lurking in the lyrics and that, heard at close quarters in a pub or club, they'd be a riot. But they aren't fall-down funny in the theatre with a band drowning them out, and Jim Bettss direction seems desperate to compensate. Charlotte Moore, who is both the most centred of the actors and the most vibrant of the singers, has two potentially good numbers shot from under her by fussy staging.

Of her three male cohorts, Don Mackay comes on initially as the statutory juvenile, but manages to transcend the blandness that implies. Frank MacKay and Terry Hatty are eccentric stage presences, one louche and louring, the other beaming and canonical; neither has harnessed his personality very efficiently to the materials. In the angrier songs, they seem to be acting the idea of bitterness, rather than the thing itself; just as too many of the songs seem to be about drama, rather than dramatic.

The most effective number concerns a small-town hockey player of indeterminate age; its lyric contrasts young ambitions and old despair, love of the game and desire to use it as a passport from a dead-end existence, and here the contrasts truly work as conflicts. They also create a world; you can see what Rogers was on about (and hear it too -- most of the words are clear). Some other songs -- about Irish-Canadians scorn for their sectarian past or about the opposing desires for adventure and for security -- are stronger on conception than execution but are interesting enough to hold the attention. But the show goes on too long, and every one of its last five numbers sounds like it ought to be the closer. A recurring song, heard in fragments, is Fogarty's Cove, a location we are told that existed only in Rogers' mind but is none the less real for that. The test of the show, I guess, is whether it makes us believe in the reality. I couldn't, but, to judge from the first-night reception, others did.
