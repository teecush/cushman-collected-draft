NOTRE DAME DE PARIS

Pantages Theatre, Toronto

God help the Canadian theatre if we lose Quebec. It's not that the rest of us get to see very much Quebecois theatre as things stand.

Robert Lepage is a national culture-hero but he only brings his shows to Toronto as an afterthought when he has finished trucking them around Europe. As for Notre Dame de Paris, the most exciting Canadian musical since -- well ever, probably: It played a short Toronto season last year in the original French and is now back for another one, but if we in English Canada want to hear and judge it in our own language, then we have to get into line behind English Las Vegas and even English England.

Maybe we'd get better treatment if we became foreigners, too. We would, though, lose any right to think of the shows as our own, and the national mix would be far poorer for it.

Notre Dame de Paris is based, of course, on the novel by Victor Hugo who is, as they say, no stranger to musical theatre. One of the few things harder than having a first hit musical is having a second, and I couldn't prophesy whether this show will equal the success of Les Miserables; my hunch is that it will do well but not quite that well.

It is, though, an incalculably superior show. It has, to begin with, a far more engaging musical identity. Instead of the endless rootless bombast of Les Mis, Richard Cocciante gives us tunes: tunes that even bear some relation to the story's time and place.

There is a rock beat, and it gets more assertive as the evening goes on, but it is clear from the first number -- a solo scene- setter called Le Temps des Cathedrales -- that we are really listening to updated chanson, clear and refreshing.

It's conceivable that I will like them less when I hear them with English lyrics; like most anglophones I regard sung French as a euphonious noise on its own account but, come the translation, I shall have to worry about meaning, and even about dramatic sophistication.

There doesn't seem to be too much of the latter; most of the songs are solos or duets about how happy or confused or erotically driven the characters are, broken up by the occasional chorus of complaint or defiance from the local homeless. (Les Sans-Papier -- those without visas -- they are called, in an engagingly transparent attempt to bring Hugo's 19th-century treatment of a 14th-century subject right into our own arriere-cour.)

As usual in a rock-opera, the lyricist, in this case Luc Plamondon, also gets credited with the book; equally as usual, there isn't one. There isn't even the pretense of one; the show is blessedly free of the droning explanatory mini-songs that generally separate the big numbers and that their composers like to think of as recitative.

Narrative coherence depends heavily on the audience's folk- memory: our shared awareness that there is a bell-ringing hunchback called Quasimodo who hangs out in the big church, and a gypsy called Esmerelda who camps out in front of it and with whom nearly all the men in the cast are in lust. It's enough: though it does result in a pile-up of sweat-soaked ballads in the second act, making the show sound more like other shows at its end than at its beginning. I couldn't swear either that, in a dark radio-station, I would be able to distinguish Esmerelda's song called Ave Maria from all the other songs I've heard called Ave Maria.

I may have a few doubts about the score but I have none about Gilles Maheu's production. The time of the cathedrals turns out to be a great time to look at. Our Lady of Paris is represented by a huge back wall, divided into panels and studded with foot-holds. It is, in fact, a climbing-wall, plentifully adorned with gargoyles who hang down in perilous positions and turn out to be people.

The second act begins with a tune that sounded to me suspiciously like the old French hit Les Trois Cloches (known in English as The Jimmy Brown Song); maybe I imagined that, but it is a fact that immediately afterwards three huge bells descend from the ceiling, each with an actor suspended upside-down from its base and swinging, as the phrase goes, like the clappers.

The cast numbers, according to the program, 15 dancers and five acrobats, and all 20 of them are fearsomely intrepid. Martino Muller's choreography, thrillingly performed, is abstract and expressive, by Bob Fosse out of Martha Graham; the chorus are used to act out the characters' inner drives, reviving and transforming the old tradition of the dream-ballet.

Sometimes the sets get into the act; Frollo, the priest tormented by his desire, spends one number apparently in an attempt to prevent one ecclesiastical column from crashing into another, but really trying to prevent his vocation from crushing his libido, or maybe vice versa.

The male leads -- Mario Pelchat (a Quasimodo who, compared to Charles Laughton's super-masochistic hunchback, is a positive pin- up), Robert Marien (Frollo), Sylvain Cossette (Gringoire, the driving troubadour-narrator) and Charles Biddle (Clopin, master of the rebels) -- are all strong on lungs, personality and presence. Esmerelda (France d'Amour, I am not making that up) can't match them for voice, but she certainly has a sensual slink.

Indeed, in all departments, this show is a forcible reminder of just how sexless the Anglo-American musical has become, and it's a bracing antidote. Consciously or not, it's also French Canada's answer to Rent, with equivalent street-smarts. Somehow it splits the periods: The actors are visibly miked, rock-concert searchlights rake the stage, and the riot-gendarmes quell the mob with recognizably modern mobile barriers, but contact with the source is never quite broken.

The creators have kept the original title (no Hollywood hunchback to lure us in) and the original ending, with Esmerelda hanged, and her Prince Charming, the captain of the guard, turning out a real bastard. What they've done that's less admirable, and all too modern, is use pre-taped music; there were musicians picketing the theatre on the first night, protesting that they were protesting on behalf of the audience's rights rather than their own jobs.

This was disingenuous of them, but their cause was just; you can tell the difference, and it's bad. To the barricades: After all, they're there on the stage, just waiting.
