Something is wrong with Fiddler on the Roof from the moment that Harvey Fierstein opens his mouth, which is very unfortunate since it's virtually the first thing that happens in the show. Fierstein, who has taken over the role of Tevye from an ailing Chaim Topol in what was meant to be Topol's farewell tour, has already played it on Broadway and I was looking forward to seeing him; he seemed, frankly, the more interesting prospect. He has at least the right imposing height for the role of the patriarchal milkman who chats to God, while his voice maybe the deepest and grittiest known to man. But gravel, it seems, does not necessarily equal gravitas. Tevye is a religious Jew in a Russian shtetl, and the father of five daughters. He has to cope with the marriages of the three eldest to progressively less desirable (because more progressive) suitors and, eventually, with the expulsion of himself and all his co-religionists from the village in which their families have lived for centuries. That's a lot to endure, but Tevye is a great and humorous endurer. Fierstein plays him as a lightweight fusspot to whom nothing matters.

I don't think he's doing that on purpose; he's a good actor who knows what's required and is trying to provide it. But, at every point, his delivery and demeanour betray him. He isn't even a teddy bear; he's a big baby. His oldest daughter marries a poor tailor, his second a revolutionary, his third a Gentile, all of them of course without traditional benefit of matchmaker. The "Tradition!" of which the opening number sings so defiantly is largely abandoned as the show proceeds, leaving only the irreducible minimum everybody needs. All three girls are terrified of telling their father, in a series of encounters that grows steadily less amusing; here none of them seems to have anything to be afraid of, and we have nothing to respond to.

Fierstein doesn't have much of a singing voice, but that isn't the problem; neither did Zero Mostel, the terrifying clown who created the role. Far more serious is that he doesn't have an authentically comic voice. He establishes no rapport with his deity, his neighbours or his audience; he doesn't have much confidence to take us into, and he increasingly falls back on a deprecatory squeak to indicate annoyance. He actually does better with the emotional scenes, partly because in this respect the show itself is absolutely impregnable. The most moving scene is the one he shares with Hodel (Jamie Davis), as she waits for the train that will take her to join her imprisoned husband in Siberia; I have never been surer, when she says, "Papa, God knows when we shall meet again," that they never will and that both of them know it. Her marriageable sisters (Rena Strober and Deborah Grausman) are charming as well, and the Matchmaker trio, with a broom as their silent partner, is a delight.

This musical's book (by Joseph Stein) is a marvel in the way it reels out its overlapping plot lines, then gathers them up in a quietly shattering climax. Sheldon Harnick's lyrics have the simplicity of great craft, and Jerry Bock's music (notably the Sabbath Prayer and Sunrise, Sunset) seems richer every time you hear it. Sammy Dallas Bayes's production provides a nondescript but unobjectionable surround for the enduring greatness of Jerome Robbins' choreography, which is even better because it's more centred than his work on West Side Story -- though paradoxically, if we had to lose his dances, Fiddler would stand the better chance of surviving without them. In Fiddler, it isn't just a matter of steps; it's what goes on through, behind and around them. It's the processional joining of hands in the opening number. It's the three Chagallian musicians popping up behind the bed in Tevye's dream. It's the Cossack intrusion in To Life that starts as challenge and becomes celebration. It's the bright costumes of the wedding guests, and those same guests watching the solemn, immortal Bottle Dance. It's the way the wedding itself, a distillation of tradition, is followed by its defiance as men and women start dancing together and the incredible detailed way in which that number builds. It's the brief heartbreak dance as Tevye tries to banish the memory of the one daughter he can't forgive. It's the downbeat ending, and the curtain-call that follows it.

The part of Golde, Tevye's formidable wife, is a surprisingly unrewarding one, but Susan Cella gets more out of it than most by pushing less. Yente the matchmaker is a role that seems to bring out the worst in whoever plays it, but Mary Stout's performance hits new lows of ethnic pandering. David Brummel is endearing as Lazar Wolf, the butcher deprived of a bride; the two young Jewish wooers are competent but unexciting. The show (not just this production) soft-pedals the Gentile atrocities, so maybe it's karma that the Gentiles themselves, or the two with any prominence, are atrociously acted. None of this production's performances will challenge any-one's memories of the 10-year-old Stratford production, and Fierstein's Tevye doesn't belong in the same universe as Brent Carver's. But the show conquers; it's the most moving musical ever written, and I long ago gave up believing that it's in any way sentimental. Les Mis is sentimental. Spring Awakening is sentimental. Fiddler is tough.
