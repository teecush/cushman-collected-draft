THEATRE REVIEW

Who Killed Spalding Gray?

Berkeley Street Theatre, Toronto

I have to declare an interest.

About halfway through Who Killed Spalding Gray?, Daniel MacIvor's tribute to the late American monologist, the actor pauses to read extracts from a couple of reviews, naming the reviewers names.

One of them is the revered Pauline Kael, who was disgusted by Gray. His juxtaposition in the film version of his Swimming to Cambodia between his personal problems with the horrors inflicted by the Khmer Rouge was distasteful to her. I havent seen the movie or read the whole review, but on the evidence presented here it sounds to me like Kael was right.

The other review I have read, because I wrote it. It concerned an earlier oneman MacIvor play with the punning title Here Lies Henry. Henry was a man who might or might not have been lying in everything he told the audience, and my response to this was, Who cares? I also said that no actor in Canada was better than MacIvor at doing what he does and that his skill was spellbinding, but I can understand, and not just because I've been there myself, that it's the perceived slight that would have lingered. It causes MacIvor to say here that, though hes not comparing himself to Gray, he does know how it feels to be hurt.

I cant think of anything to say about his new show that is likely to hurt him, but of course you never know. My description of it as a tribute wasn't quite accurate, but I can't think of a term that would be. Its a piece that moves around and bounces off the man in its title; a man who in 2004 provided the most literal answer to the question in MacIvors title by jumping (or falling) off the side of the Staten Island Ferry after spending most of the day riding back and forth on it.

Three narrative streams flow through the play, feeding into one another. One is an account of Gray's last days, emphasizing the fact that the night before his fatal ferrytrip, Gray had been to see a Tim Burton movie, Big Fish, accompanied by his son. Sons figure prominently in much of what MacIvor tells us in this show. The last lines of Big Fish are: "A man tells a story over and over so many times he becomes the story. In that way, he becomes immortal." Maybe Gray, a professional storyteller, was putting his own immortality to the test.

Talking about Gray, MacIvor goes someway towards becoming Gray. At any rate he adopts the Gray pose, sitting behind a desk with a glass of water and a microphone. When he talks about himself, in the play's second stream, hes more himself as we know him from his other solo shows: roaming around the stage, amplification in hand. The personal story he tells is about being informed by an ex-lover that there is an entity attached to him, that this could be fatal, and that hed better take himself to a "psychic surgeon" to have it removed. So he did. MacIvor is very funny detailing the grim surroundings in which he found himself, as well as the offhand inquisitorial manner of his surgical practitioner. It seems that the entidectomy (I'm guessing that's the word) was successful. Unfortunately it exactly coincided with the death of Spalding Gray, leaving MacIvor feeling, as per his title, uneasily, if irrationally, guilty.

I dont know whether this story is true. Rationalist that I am, I rather hope it isnt, but rather suspect that it is. The point is that here, as not in Here Lies Henry, I cared to know, perhaps because MacIvor is an unquestionably real person while Henry was a fiction. An acknowledged fiction is the third element in Killing Spalding Gray. It's the story of a man named Howard who wanted to commit suicide and found the go-to guy to help him do it. But their plan misfired, for reasons too delicious to reveal, and Howard found himself sitting by the ocean, more disconsolate than ever. A mysterious woman greets him there (everyone in a MacIvor show is mysterious), speaking in the voice of Helena Bonham Carter. As well as enabling MacIvor to do a pleasing impersonation, this connects with the narrative of Spalding Gray's last days, since Carter starred in Big Fish and was Tim Burtons partner at the time. Its a theatrical practical joke that contributes to the play's total effect, which is to be agreeably unsettling, linking things without tying them up.

The agreeableness is a function of MacIvor's own performing personality which - here at least - is devastatingly unassuming. He gets applause at the top of the show, simply by walking on. He then proceeds to interrogate a member of the audience. I dont know what the point of this is but it does enable MacIvor to ingratiate himself still more by confessing that he doesnt feel very comfortable doing it.

In the main body of the show, MacIvor rises, seemingly effortlessly, to every one of the challenges he has set for himself in his script's three intersecting modes. His unobtrusive director is, as usual, Daniel Brooks who, years ago, was co-creator-and-performer of The Noam Chomsky Lectures - the only other production in which I have heard myself being namechecked from the stage.

That's probably a coincidence, but then Killing Spalding Gray seems to be suggesting that theres no such thing as coincidence.

Who Killed Spalding Gray Runs At The Berkeley Street Theatre In Toronto Until December 11.
