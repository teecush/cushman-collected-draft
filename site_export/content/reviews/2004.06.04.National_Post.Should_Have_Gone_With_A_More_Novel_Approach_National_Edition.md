THE COUNT OF MONTE CRISTO

Avon Theatre, Stratford, Ont.

- - -

There are certain novels that defy dramatization by virtue of their thematic range and psychological complexity. Alexandre Dumas' The Count of Monte Cristo is not one of them. It is a complicated story with a very simple thrust. Those complications, however, fill many hundreds of pages, which constitute another kind of headache for an adaptor, especially one shooting for a playing time of two and a half hours.

An even bigger problem is that the complications are a large part of the point. The implications of this may not have been taken sufficiently on board by the new version at Stratford, which is a resourceful staging of a dog of a script.

The Count of Monte Cristo, as is well known, is the ultimate revenge fantasy. Edmond Dantes, a young Marseilles seaman, is thrown into prison on a trumped-up charge of plotting for the restoration of the Emperor Napoleon. He has been framed by three men, of whom two are motivated by jealousy, sexual or professional, while the third, a local magistrate, acts from mixed motives of ambition and self-protection.

During his 20-year incarceration in the dungeons of the Chateau dIf, Dantes meets a fellow prisoner, the Abbe Faria, who tells him of a fabulous treasure buried on the isle of Monte Cristo. Dantes escapes, digs it up and becomes unimaginably rich. He takes the title of Count of Monte Cristo, and sets about rewarding his friends and, more importantly, punishing his enemies. His plans take him years to mature and months of intricate plotting to execute. He virtually plays God and finds, eventually and inevitably, that he has overreached himself.

Marshall Bordens adaptation remains fairly faithful to the first half of the narrative (the imprisonment part, which, mathematically speaking, is more like the first 10th) and takes wild liberties with the rest. A couple of his inventions are ingenious, but they all have the effect of diminishing the story, and not just in terms of bulk.

His Monte Cristo (the man, not the island) has no stature, no grandeur, and -- most crucially -- nothing to repent. In the novel, Dantes drives one of his foes to suicide and another to madness; the third, who happens to be the slimiest, he deprives of some millions of francs but eventually lets off. In this play, sword in hand, he takes each of them on in single combat, which is very decent of him, not to say heroic.

It's true that the man who wrote Monte Cristo also wrote The Three Musketeers (in fact, he wrote them simultaneously, a believe- it-or-not achievement in itself) but they're very different works. Dantes is no DArtagnan, and neither he nor his novel is a swashbuckler. When the action moves to the money-mad salons of Second Empire Paris, it comes close to being social satire on the scale of Balzac.

At the centre of it -- by sheer force of will, personality and bankbook -- sits Monte Cristo, spinning his spiders web schemes, and in their intricacy lies much of the book's appeal. There is probably no way of realizing them on stage other than by dividing the play over two long evenings. (I think that would be worth trying; Dumas, I learn from the Stratford program, tried it himself with his playwright's hat on.)

But back in the real world: Those who havent read the novel may feel differently, but Borden seems to me to have reduced it to a tale not worth telling. Some of his choices, even given his built- in time limit, are thoughtlessly destructive. He gives Dantes an ally and protector, thereby robbing him of his monomaniac solitude. He throws in a sentimental illegitimate-son motif, and supplies an unqualified happy ending in place of the ambiguous one in the original. He goes in for lines like "Fernand de Morcerf, I hold all your tomorrows in my hands." In fact he scoops up every cliche, verbal or theatrical, that offers itself to him.

A program note tells us that, in Dumas, "the good guys are good; the bad guys are monstrous." This isnt true; even when he wrote melodrama, he peopled it with characters of some vigour and even subtlety. In this version, though, the least suggestion of a mixed motive is ruthlessly ironed out, notably those belonging to the magistrate Villefort and the drunken innkeeper Caderousse, the latter of whom seems here to have stepped, along with his wife, straight out of Les Miserables, and I don't mean the novel.

The protagonist himself is done in by having his role divided between a young and an older actor -- inexplicably, since his contemporaries in the story are allowed to do their own ageing. This is probably the single most damaging decision of the evening: It reduces the performers, David Snelgrove and Brad Rudy, to sounding a single note apiece (ingenuous and implacable, respectively); it robs the audience of any handle on the central character; and it drives a final, decisive nail into the coffin of the hero's singularity. It also stifles the one potentially touching moment in the piece, when the Count re-encounters his long-lost fiancee and she instinctively but silently recognizes him.

Andrey Tarasiuks production has its own share of received ideas; you couldnt, apparently, set foot in a tavern in Marseilles in 1815 without finding the customers whooping it up in a production number.

Dana Green as Dantes' intended and Thom Marriott as his benevolent employer suggest at least the outlines of characters; other performances are simplistic, but the director has not allowed his actors to make fools of themselves as they did in last year's Hunchback of Notre Dame. And what might be called the hydraulic aspects of the staging are impressive. Guido Tondinos set starts as the deck of a ship; floors, walls and sails then rise, fall and slide to create all the other locations. Its ingenious and graceful.

The designer's idea of making the ocean a unifying image falls down, though, in the second, land-locked, act; the Comte de Morcerf's receiving room is done up in a shade of aqua-marine that could give the hardiest guest the collywobbles.
