The Next Stage Theatre Festival is an ambiguous animal. It began, or so I've always assumed, as a way of giving a second airing to shows that had proved successful at that older established festival, the Toronto Fringe. Now it seems more and more to consist of independent productions that have been in development, and are now ready to go to the Next Stage of confronting a live audience. The next Next Stage could be a run somewhere else: presumably not at the Fringe itself, though that would certainly be an interesting full circle.

Of the two plays I saw on the first day of this year's festival, one was surprisingly mainstream. For a Good Time, Call Kathy Blanchard could be the Canadian equivalent of an Alan Ayckbourn play, though the author, Michael Ross Albert, doesn't appear to have Ayckbourn's farcical inventiveness or his expertise in handling group dynamics; there are four characters in the play, but only rarely are they all on stage together. Also unlike Ayckbourn, the play is softcentred, or at least soft-ended. But like Ayckbourn it presents a comically bilious view of family life, with a tragedy going on in the background.

Well, perhaps not a tragedy but a drastic situation. Mother, we gather, is in hospital, suffering from dementia and from possibly fatal thirddegree burns. Lawrence, the eighth and youngest of her grown children, is chronically underemployed, and has fanciful ambitions of being a singersongwriter. The house is being run, dictatorially, by his sharptongued cousin Mary.

The two others who wander in are Sky, who is supposed to be renovating the place and has just been thrown out of his own house by Lawrence's sister to whom he is married, and Amanda, with whom Lawrence is in love and to whom he unexpectedly proposes.

All the characters have secrets to keep, all of them revealed to humorously discomforting effect. The biggest surprise, concerning his plans for the immediate future, is sprang by Lawrence himself, and it comes from so far out of left field as to blow a serious hole in the play's credibility. It's rivalled in this respect by the title, which turns out to refer to a sign that Lawrence once saw hung up somewhere. It has no other relevance, and it signals a dangerous taste for arbitrary whimsy.

There's some snappy writing, though, and a good instinct for extending comic tension to the limits of an audience's endurance. Jim Warren's production is well paced and well acted. Jennifer Dzialoszynski, who has done fine work at the Shaw Festival, makes Mary excessively high-speed and high-pitched, but one can see what she's getting at. As Lawrence, Daniel Pagett commands the stage by being ineffectual, which is quite a feat; Geoffrey Pounsett performs the same trick in reverse, finding the weakness in Sky who prides himself on practicality; and as Amanda, Caroline Toal is appealing which is all her role asks of her. There is an ongoing metaphor that involves all the quartet trying to watch the Stanley Cup final on a dysfunctional TV; the metaphor, like the TV, fails to work.

Mine, technically a more radical piece, presents two women, identified in the program as A and B, and in the dialogue as Abigail and Bea. The chronology is scrambled, but it seems they met when A was a TA, and B was a psychology major taking A's poetry course out of necessity rather than enthusiasm. A asks B out (something that would be frowned upon were teacher and student of different genders) and a relationship develops: one in which A seems to have the upper hand but in which B may call the important shots. A has the more assertive personality (Type A in fact) but B is the one with professional prospects.

One thing this play shares with For a Good Time... is a concern with young people's bleak employment situation; A may be teaching literature, but her real interest is astronomy. The best sustained scene has B signing the lease for a swish new apartment, something of which A is rapturously in favour of until she still considers what it signifies for their inter-personal dynamics. These, it's made clear, exist on the same shifting scale as those of any other menage; it's a constant battle of wills from which sex, graphically and gracefully presented, provides only a temporary diversion and of which it may well be an illustration.

Jenna Harris wrote the play, and she also plays B, very well, as 70% gaucherie and 30% assurance; a speech about how it feels to be a perambulating wallflower at a social gathering strikes especially home as both text and performance. As the more dominating A, Michelle Polak has paradoxically the less rewarding or certainly the less varied role, but she, too, does well. The physical production, by Clinton Walker, is very striking, on a set whose elements are symbolically constructed of books.

The play cuts back and forth between the couple's experiences at home, in school, at bars and clubs, and at a salsa class; the changes keep us on our toes but are not all that helpful.

To show what can be done with a relationship story, told chronologically but seamlessly, one scene melting into the next, last season's triumphant production of Lungs has returned. The acting (Lesley Faulkner, Brendan Gall), the spartan direction (Weyni Mengesha) and the writing (Duncan Macmillan) are as dazzling and ultimately as moving as before.

The Next Stage Theatre Festival runs at the Factory Theatre through Jan. 18; Lungs runs at the Tarragon Extra Space through Jan. 25.
