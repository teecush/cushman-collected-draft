This af ternoon in Toronto Robert Lepage will be presenting the Glenn Gould Protege Prize to the Quebecois musical performance group known as Lorchestre dhommesorchestres: LODHO for short. The ceremony takes place at the Theatre Centre, which has just moved a few doors west from its previous home at Queen and Dovercourt, to gleaming new premises in what used to be the Carnegie Library; the lobby areas are still unfinished, but the main performance area looks and feels good and is also, I am told, thoroughly flexible. In its second week of existence the new Centre is already on to its second production: the prizewinning LODHO in a show called Cabaret brise-jour. On this evidence LODHO deserves its award. Its members are very good at what they do.

That doesn't mean I have to like what they do, and as a matter of fact I hated it. Cabaret brise-jour is an exercise in deconstruction - how intentional I'm not sure - conducted at the expense of the music of Kurt Weill. Weills German, French and American periods are all represented, and the program includes a laudatory biographical note about him. It isnt quite clear why, because while all the songs included are indeed Weill's, most of them might just as well be anybody's. Their musical lines are obscured, and most of their words are rendered incomprehensible. Weill seems to have been chosen because of the whiff of transgression that still attaches to his work; his Weimar or Brechtian period has - many times over - been identified with, and exploited for, a vaguely defined idea of "decadence.

LODHO begin by exploring this concept fairly conventionally; the eight performers are seen lounging about in various states of deshabille, swigging from bottles and swaying gently to accordion and guitar accompaniment. Later the images get bolder; one of the performers goes through much of the evening with a stocking over her face, and one song shares the stage with an elaborate demonstration of what seems to be a heroin injection. Theres also a great deal of dressing and undressing. In the most effective of all the numbers, the singer perseveres with her singing while doffing and donning an uncountable number of garments including a football jersey and boxing gloves, egged on by the rest of the cast who keep throwing clothes up in the air, eventually covering the stage with them. Its a fun piece of performance art, culminating in an arresting image; though what it's meant to be an image of is uncertain.

All the performers sing - with varying degrees of accomplishment, but that's hardly the point - and most of them play. Most of the instruments are conventional, with a bias toward those of the more piercing variety, but some are homemade, ranging from the humble musical saw to contraptions on a Heath Robinson level of ingenuity. The visuals are strong, but the effect most of the time is not so much to complement the songs as to cancel them out.

Occasionally a new treatment justifies itself; Mack the Knife (the only German song done in an English translation) works well as a slow, sour rap, with the protagonists name omitted, replaced by a sardonic our friend." Theres even a case of a lyric - Ogden Nash's for The Trouble with Women - getting laughs on its own merits, though it seems somehow typical that the actor (who recites rather than sings it) should screw up a crucial rhyme in the funniest stanza. The show seems to harbour a special contempt for the songs Weill wrote with the American verse playwright Maxwell Anderson; some perfectly viable satires of theirs on war and bureaucracy are screamed and snarled into oblivion, and I had to look at the program to discover that the cacophonous first-half closer had actually been a Weill-Anderson song called Big Mole. The final blow to their catalogue is saved for near the end: The torch song Will You Remember Me - hardly so familiar as to need refreshing - becomes an agonized and interminable punk rhapsody, yelled out by a guy in a smock; I could have sworn at one point that he was singing Will You Dismember Me, which could have been appropriate.

Acting Up Stage, devoted to offbeat musicals, celebrates its 10th anniversary by reviving one of its earliest successes, William Finn's Elegies: A Song Cycle: another composer showcase, but a more transparent one than

LODHOs Weill. Actually its a self-showcase, since Finn assembled it himself. He's an uneven songwriter, lyrically and melodically, though the words here are generally more lucid than those for his betterknown Falsettos. The songs, as the title indicates, are all memorials to actual dead friends of the author's, not all of them victims of AIDS. The best, now as it was a decade ago, is The Ballad of Jack Eric Williams and Other 3-Named Composers, the surviving triple threats being a generation of now middle-aged, mainly off-Broadway champs: Michael John LaChiusa, Jason Robert Brown and Ricky Ian Gordon, to whom the song is notionally addressed. Williams, unlike them, was never successful; indeed until I heard this song, I never realized he was a composer - I knew him only as the skilful and extremely bulky actor who created the Beadle in Sweeney Todd. Finn makes me wishId heard Williams songs; he died while still, as he complained and the song repeats, "out of fashion." Thom Allison performs the number with caressing compassion and dazzling wit; he is indeed superb throughout, in full command of nuance and attack, his held notes high and ringing, his vibrato immaculate. He's also relaxed about it all; his colleagues (Barbara Barsky, Steven Gallagher, Joel Gomez, Eliza-Jane Scott) are good but inclined to pile-drive. The evening suffers too from a built-in monotony of theme, accentuated by the facts that most of Finn's subjects mean less to us than they do to him, and that the great majority of the songs are solos. At its best, though, the show is fine.
