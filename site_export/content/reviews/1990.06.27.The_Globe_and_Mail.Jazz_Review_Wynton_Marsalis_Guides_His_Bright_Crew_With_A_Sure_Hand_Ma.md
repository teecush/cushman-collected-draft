JAZZ REVIEW Wynton Marsalis guides his bright crew with a sure hand Magnificent Seven on a musical ride

Wednesday, June 27, 1990

ROBERT CUSHMAN

BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail THE group that played Torontos Roy Thomson Hall on Monday night should really be billed as the Wynton Marsalis Septet. The trumpeting prodigy, still a safe distance from 30, stands alone on the posters and programs, but not on the stage. He is fronting a band of musical equals even if he is its unquestioned leader.

The leadership is theatrical as well as musical. Conservative lookers though they are, these musicians are unusually satisfying to watch as each in turn weaves in and out of the solo spotlight. Sometimes the four horns will parade discreetly to the back of the platform while the marvelous rhythm section gets on with it. Spare though it is, it looks choreographed - a sort of post-modern homage to the showmanship of the big bands - and one detects the elegant guidance of Marsalis. His own announcements, though sometimes modestly witty (or wittily modest), lean too far toward minimalism. I mean, they're quite hard to hear.

I am fairly sure, though I had to strain to catch it, that the opening number was called Uptown Ruler. It was announced as a five-song suite, four-fifths written by Marsalis with the balance by tenorist Todd Williams, detailing the trials and triumphs of a young man in the big city. It did recognizably move from the exploratory to the exultant, with some plangent lamentation in the middle; but as usual with program music, jazz or other, the plot-line rapidly got lost. One responded to magisterial moods: to the way a questioning Marsalis solo emerged from a richly growling, almost Ellingtonian, ensemble.

A later and longer suite, Sugar Cane and Sweetie Pie, was introduced as a tale of boy chasing girl. It lasted more than half an hour, and the shape of the pursuit was difficult to discern, though it gave everybody ample blowing space. Hypnotic for most of its length, it finally outstayed its welcome, only to recover with a solo from Williams of liquid beauty and a brief extraordinary cadenza from the bassist, Reginald Veal.

Among the more brief numbers, Embraceable You was a jewel. Marsalis plays a lot of standards like this on his latest CD, which I think of as Wynton Plays Pretty For The People. He sticks to the melodies and his pianist father supplies the spice. But this was different: an object lesson in when to adhere to the Gershwin line (since some of it is unimprovable) and when to spin out a tribute to it. It was impossible to listen without feeling a beatific smile engulfing your face.

The Cat in the Hat is Back, another Williams tune, was as nimbly joyful as its name: a bright group workout to conclude the first half. The second officially ended with Marsalis's own much-bruited Majesty of the Blues, very heavy on mutes, which seemed to me lacking in blues and majesty alike. Both were abundant, though, in the encore, Jelly Roll Mortons Jungle Blues. Marsaliss style and stance for most of the evening had harked back to Miles Davis in his glory days with Gil Evans: the haunted, dignified tone, the economy, the general sense of a confident navigator on a deceptively glassy sea. His crew were pretty much in the same boat, except for the trombonist (his name escaped me) who kept waking discreet echoes of New Orleans. For the Morton number, everybody joined him there. It is, after all, Marsalis's hometown and he is a supremely accomplished eclectic, the heir to just about everybody. The result was not exactly traditional jazz, but it was a powerful, moving distillation of a great ancestral spirit. It rightly brought the house down.

If septet is too hard a word, they could always call themselves Wynton's Magnificent Seven.
