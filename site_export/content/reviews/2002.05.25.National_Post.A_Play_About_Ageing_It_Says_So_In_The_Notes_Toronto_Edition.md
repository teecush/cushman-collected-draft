THE OLD LADIES

Royal George Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

- - -

NIAGARA-ON-THE-LAKE - The double author credits on The Old Ladies show us the whirligig of time still doing its status-reversing stuff.

The play, first staged in London in 1935, is a dramatization by Rodney Ackland of a 1924 novel by Hugh Walpole. Walpole, one of the most successful writers of his day, is now virtually forgotten, though its hard not to feel that an author disdained by fastidious litterateurs from Virginia Woolf to F. R. Leavis must have had something going for him. Ackland, never much more than a cult figure in his lifetime, has enjoyed a considerable posthumous revival, at Britains National Theatre and elsewhere. Some of his rediscovered plays -- Absolute Hell or The Dark River -- look like prime candidates for the attention of the Shaw Festival. Meantime, we have The Old Ladies.

It's a refined thriller, set in Polchester, a cathedral town that Walpole dreamt up and used in several books. Here, in separate but contiguous rooms in a thin-walled boarding house, live three women, each aged around 70; they are the only people we meet. The rest of the world communicates only by letter, and rarely at that.

There is May Beringer, the newest arrival, timorous and birdlike, with only enough money to keep her going for six months, clinging to the memory of her dead dog and -- more literally -- to a piece of ornamental amber, a present from a long-ago partner in what we take to have been an unfulfilled lesbian relationship.

There is Lucy Amorest, who seems, by comparison with her two companions, to be solid as a rock: a widow, anxiously awaiting news of the ne'er-do-well son from whom she has not heard for a year, and wondering whether a deceased male cousin has left her any money.

And there is Agatha Payne, who tells fortunes with playing cards and is apparently of gypsy origin, bodeful and resentful and -- at least to Miss Beringer -- deeply frightening. She loves bright things; she is deeply fascinated by Lucys possible inheritance and will, it seems, stop at nothing to get her hands on the newest lodgers flashing amber.

The action, on David Boechler's dollhouse set, moves between their respective quarters and also strays on to the staircase and landing. Lucy lives on the ground floor, while the other two share the upstairs; it's probably nobodys fault but this leads to scenes in their rooms seeming very cramped and -- even in the small Royal George Theatre -- remote.

The screws turn slowly but by the end -- with one woman a terrorized victim on the verge of a heart attack, another a prowling harpy, and the third a willing protectress with chinks in her armour -- they get, excitingly, to the wrenching point. And there are some satisfying twists to prolong the agony. The house takes on a double identity: prison and haven. Half of you wants the characters to get away, the other half is rooting for them to stay where they are since the outside world, from all we hear of it, has very little to recommend it.

James MacDonalds production does not quite trust the play; at least I imagine that that's why he has stuffed it with screeching music, film-noir style. (Some of it is actually drawn from the work of Bernard Herrmann, Hollywood's master nerve-scraper.) The two media, though, have different rules. In movies, its accepted that the music will do most of the acting. In a play -- certainly in a naturalistic play, which this is -- it seems silly.

Ackland knew about this; he was, apart from anything else, a master of atmospherics, with a crafty ear for domestic detail. In his last play, A Dead Secret, which I heartily recommend to the Festival as a most superior thriller set in Shaws lifetime but written after it (it's loosely based on the famous Seddon poisoning case), the dynamics of an overstretched, overcrowded household in suburban London are brought to shuddering life, partly through the use of music in real time: kids play singing games, a neighbour practises Just as Song at Twilight, of all piercingly annoying things, on the cornet. In the play and production at hand, you merely wonder where all that funny noise is supposed to be coming from.

MacDonald may be compensating for the crippling fact that his actresses are all too young for their roles. In accordance with Shaw Festival tradition, an erudite program note makes claims for the play that seem far in excess of anything that actually happens on stage. This time it has to do with the plight of elderly women, genteel and poverty-stricken, who have outlived the values they were brought up on and whom a new world is heartlessly abandoning. They are, in addition, deeply suspicious of the encroaching other, represented by the gypsy Agatha.

As sociology it's all true, and you can extrapolate it from the play when you think about it afterwards, but it has more to do with a synopsis of the action than with the action as it unfolds. Only in the third and last act is Agatha given some speeches about how she and her contemporaries would be better off dead. If we are to read these implications in for ourselves, then we need actresses who actually look and sound as if theyre 70. Otherwise the characters and the play are insufficiently pitiable, insufficiently cruel, insufficiently real.

Two of them here do make it, eventually. (I withdraw that "crippling and substitute damaging".) Wendy Thatchers Miss Beringer -- she never does get on first-name terms with the others - - finally has us sharing her fears, and Donna Belleville's Lucy is not only strong but subtle; a look at her is sufficient to tell whether she has just received good or bad news. But Maria Vacratsis's Agatha remains thoroughly external, exuding the idea of menace rather than its reality. She sounds, throatily, like a man and -- especially at her first appearance -- looks like one too; in a thick wig and a red wrap she resembles a Restoration fop or perhaps a modern judge, probably of the hanging variety. A good actress, she plainly knows what is required, but cannot find the desperation in herself; and the production has not centred her. It's a pretty good play, but it takes unduly long to declare itself.
