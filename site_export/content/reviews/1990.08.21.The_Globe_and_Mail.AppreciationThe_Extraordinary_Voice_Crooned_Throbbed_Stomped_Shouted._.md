APPRECIATION"The extraordinary voice crooned, throbbed, stomped, shouted. It could soothe and it could lament Pearl Bailey never lost her rhythm or her humor

Tuesday, August 21, 1990

ROBERT CUSHMAN

BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail AN agency report on Pearl Bailey, who died Friday at age 72, announced that Pearlie Mae was best-known for her rendition of Hello Dolly! This must have been news to many of her fans. Pearl - she liked to speak of herself like that in the third person - did indeed play Dolly during the original Broadway run; she was something like eighth in line for the role and represented a desperate attempt by producer David Merrick to revive interest in his long-running meal-ticket by turning it into a black musical. Desperate and yet highly successful: Pearl, uniquely among take-over artists, was permitted to record a new cast album on the score and her singing of the title song, on stage or on record, probably raised the regulation number of goose-bumps. But identified with it? Oh, honey.

Anyway, Pearl Bailey didn't have hits: not Hit Parade hits. Turntable hits - songs played year after year on radio - she did have. There was Tired ("darlin', Pearl is exhausted") and Takes Two to Tango: mildly salacious double-entendre numbers. If we're talking Broadway, there was St. Louis Woman and House of Flowers, both legendary flop shows with golden scores by Harold Arlen, Pearl's favorite composer. She played a good-time girl in the first, and a madam in the second: both good-hearted, of course, and both more than a little skeptical about their work. That was the Bailey style: lazy, sensual, funny.

She was born in the same year (1918) and the same town (Newport News, Virginia) as Ella Fitzgerald, with whom she also shared a superb voice and an ineradicable sense of swing. But where Ella was pure singer, Pearl was half vaudevillian. She came out of the Harlem tradition of bawdy, bastardized blues exemplified in the twenties and thirties by the great actress-singer Ethel Waters. Ethel, before she turned respectable, could double any entendre in the book.

Pearl and Lena Horne were the two star performers who extended this line into the post-war golden age of jazz-tinged standard singing. Lena was the unattainable exotic temptress, preserving her image even while mocking it. Pearl was earthy and available. Lena brought out the sex in the ostensibly chaste mainstream catalogue of Broadway and Hollywood. Pearl cut albums with titles like For Adults Only and Come On Let's Play with Pearlie Mae, drawing on contemporary revue and nightclub material and on songs that, as she acridly reminded us, were older than she was: Row, Row, Row or - obviously a family standby - Bill Bailey, Won't You Please Come Home. Nobody ever sang them so well, and her explorations of the more sophisticated Porter-Gershwin-Arlen repertoire are among the best on record. Alone among her generation she remained unshakably loyal to the songs and styles that had nourished her. Even in the philistine 1970s she persisted in recording standard songs with big-band backings.

Typically she would sing a straight but buoyant first chorus and then embark upon a second in which the written words were interspersed or elaborated with wryly relevant asides; the end of the song would often wander off into free-associating mumbles. But her embroideries never destroyed the musical line; her sense of time was absolute. She was also one of the very few who could tamper with a lyric without destroying it. Hers was a rare example of a cabaret style transferring successfully onto records. She could even break up the archival solemnity of an Original Cast Recording; on one song in House of Flowers she instructs the back-up trio that has been raucously protesting its devotion to her to "stop yelling in my ear, I've got a terrible headache" before serenely returning to the canonical text, not a beat out of place.

In her later years, as UN goodwill ambassador and compulsive autobiographer, the world learned, not always to its fascination, that she had got religion. But musically she had always had it. Like most black singers she was raised on gospel. Her recording of the Arlen-Mercer mock- spiritual Ac-cen-tchu-ate the Positive is majestic; she delivers the verse as an a cappella sermon worthy of Mahalia Jackson and then moves pulsatingly through the chorus. She was often described as a great jazz singer sidetracked into comedy, as though the two modes were incompatible; in fact, it was rhythm that made her humor so effective, and she never lost it.

The voice was extraordinary; it crooned, throbbed, stomped, shouted. It could soothe and it could lament. There is at least one song, the tragic I Had Myself a True Love, on which it can be heard doing all these things. She brightened up several films, as actress as well as singer. And she could sing opera; at least she was one of the few undubbed performers in the movie versions of Carmen Jones and Porgy and Bess. Her solo in Carmen might have become her anthem if she had chosen to exploit it. It was Beat Out Dat Rhythm on a Drum, Bizet's gypsy celebration effortlessly transposed into swing, incorporating a guttural quotation from Stormy Weather, a dry interjection ("maybe I do") halfway through the line "I don't need nothin' more to start me off" and a final mood of controlled frenzy. One would like to think that the eponymous drum solo was played by Louis Bellson, one of the best in the trade as well as being her fifth and lasting husband. He was one of her two mentors; the other was Don Redman, one of the architects of black thirties swing and her musical director on her choicest LPs. Through him and then through her a tradition lived. The great ones are dying, one by one. Pearl was one of the best and most deservedly loved.
