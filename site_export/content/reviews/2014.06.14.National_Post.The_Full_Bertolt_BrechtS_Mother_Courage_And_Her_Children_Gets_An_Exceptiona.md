Bertolt Brecht famously believed theatre audiences should never be allowed to forget they are in the theatre. He once expounded this theory to the Austrian playwright Odon von Horvath, who replied but Brecht, what makes you think that they ever do forget it?" Brecht made no reply; according to Horvath, he didn't like being asked questions he couldnt answer.

That conversation never actually took place. It was invented by the British dramatist Christopher Hampton in his engaging counterfactual Tales from Hollywood, which imagines Horvath joining Brecht as a European refugee in wartime Tinseltown rather than, as was really the case, being fatally struck by a falling tree branch just as he was preparing to make the trip. It still seems to me the definitive comment on the Brechtian alienation effect: an elaborate mechanism for guarding against something that never happens. Brechts American sojourn is said to have had a significant effect on what is probably his most famous play, Mother Courage, and on the originating performance of it by his wife, Helene Weigel. According to a story that I dearly hope is true, the celebrated silent scream that Weigel emitted when forced to identify the dead body of her second son was inspired by Brecht's having seen Ethel Merman in Annie Get Your Gun go slack-jawed and open-mouthed at her first sighting of the man she loved. Weimar Berlin meets Irving Berlin; truly, there's no business like show business.

Seana McKenna, playing Mother Courage at Stratford, doesnt do the silent scream; she doesnt even do a vocal one. Hers is a tough, lucid performance in a tough, lucid production, directed by Martha Henry and using an exceptionally lively new translation by yet another British playwright, David Edgar. Both production and translation go the full Brecht when it comes to the captions, spoken and displayed, that precede each of the play's scenes. These are carried off (and carried on) with great brio, and are very useful besides. The play is set during the Thirty Years War, which devastated Europe in the 17th century, and on whose details most of us need all the information we can get. The interventions fill us in on dates, leaders, and who's up and down as between Protestants and Catholics. They also forecast whats going to happen in each ensuing scene: for example, which of her three grown children Mother Courage, who runs a canteen and general store out of her iconic covered wagon, is about to lose. The idea is that, with suspense eliminated, we will be able to judge coolly the extent to which her misfortunes can be blamed on her own rapacity and, beyond that on the military and mercantile machines in which, despite her delusions of autonomy, she is a helpless cog. It doesnt quite work out like that since a) audiences, being neither as naive nor as sentimental as Brechtian theory would assume, can and habitually do weigh these things up for themselves, without any nudging; b) the introductions supply the outlines of a scene but not the details, and anyway foreknowledge doesn't necessarily eliminate suspense; Romeo and Juliet is no less involving for our having been told at the outset that the lovers are not only star-crossed but death-marked. Shakespeare anticipated the alienation effect; so did the Greeks; so did musical theatre in all its forms. Nothing, after all, would seem better calculated to puncture naturalistic illusion than having characters burst into song.

Which, in fact, in Brecht they constantly do, without leaving us either more or less credulous; it's amazing what audiences will take in their stride. The songs, as it happens, are especially well-handled in this production, both in translation and performance. McKenna, who has most of them, is no great vocalist but she is excellent at communicating both the meaning and the flavour of the lyrics; her beggars duet with Geraint Wyn Davies on the despairing "Song of Solomon" (which also crops up in different form in The Threepenny Opera) is the evenings most powerful scene. Wyn Davies as the fleshly, venal Cook and Ben Carlson as his rival for Courage's affections, the refugee Chaplain clinging to the tatters of his vocation, give richly textured performances, fine in their scenes with McKenna, even better when, resentfully and suspiciously, they square off against one another. The play indeed becomes about Mother Courage and her men, rather than, as per the title, Mother Courage and Her Children. Antoine Yared as the fatally conscientious Swiss Cheese is the best of the offspring; elsewhere in the cast there are notable contributions from Deidre Gillard-Rowlings as a camp follower who strikes it rich and from Randy Hughson, doubling as a briskly cheerful master of ceremonies and a contrastingly nasty blackmailer and spy. McKenna herself lacks only the ultimate vein of commercial callousness; I could believe in her fatal haggling over her son's ransom, not in her refusing to donate tattered shirts to bind up the wounded. The play here is trying too blatantly to make a point, the same point that it makes in every other scene. (So much for making up our own minds.) Mother Courage suffers but she doesnt learn; this lack of development may explain why the role, at least since Weigel, can occasion very good performances but not great ones.

Stratfords Alice Through the Looking-Glass is a fiasco. It isn't uninventive; Jillian Keiley's production has the heroine guided on her trip through the mirror by a clone of herself: a great effect. Once shes on the other side, the stage sprouts Alice dress-alikes of both genders, photo negatives of the heroine who push the action along while riding around on bicycles: an increasingly tedious effect, and one that destroys the crucial point that, in this new world, Alice is unique. Keileys approach might work for a version that took Lewis Carroll's storyline as its starting point without using his actual words; the ultimate example of this would of course be the recent Alice in Wonderland ballet that by all accounts worked wonderfully. James Reaney's looking-glass adaptation, however, is doggedly literal, departing from the original only in a pointless repositioning of the Jabberwocky poem. So everything depends on how well the actors can bring the text to life; and there are only two-and-a-half performances, not counting Trish Lindstroms admirably sensible Alice, that measure up. The half is Sarah Orensteins up-anddown White Queen. The fully formed are Ryan Wilkie's unexpectedly charming White Knight (unexpected because one would have anticipated an older actor) and, crushingly superior to everybody else, Brian Tree's Humpty Dumpty. But even his scene is spoiled by having him treated as a kind of puppet, his long trailing arms manipulated by the faux-Alices. The show is full of set pieces like that, distracting and detracting from what the story is saying and - the ultimate crime - not funny. If we were in Wonderland, destroying Carroll's wit would be an off-with-their-heads offence. In the slightly more benign Looking-Glass world, where everything's a chess-game, we can merely say that the play checkmates itself.

Mother Courage and Her Children is in repertory until Sept. 21; Alice Through the Looking-Glass until Oct. 12, at Stratford. Visit stratfordfestival.ca for details and ticket information.
