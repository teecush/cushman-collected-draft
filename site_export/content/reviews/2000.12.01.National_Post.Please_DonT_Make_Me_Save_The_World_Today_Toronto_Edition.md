SCIENCE FICTION

Factory Studio Theatre, Toronto

There is the one about the two sinister strangers who snatch a mangy, destitute man from his mangy, destitute refuge at the behest of some mysterious outside authority; an example is Harold Pinter's The Birthday Party. There is also the one about alien forces who, confusing fact and fiction, seek the aid of some earthly practitioners of interplanetary fantasy; an example is Galaxy Quest. David Widdicombe's play Science Fiction borrows both these familiar motifs but still ends up as its own animal. Its origins may be mongrel, but, in this gloriously acted production, it plays like a thoroughbred.

The abducted professional fantasist is Spider, a once-successful sci-fi novelist who has fallen on evil times. He is crouching unshaven and otherwise dishevelled in a fetid hotel room, contemplating a refrigerator stocked only with dog food. (The dog is dead.) There's nothing very novel about Spider's plight -- if I had a dollar for every drunken novelist I've seen reduced to eating dog food, I could buy the next one a drink -- but both he and we are rescued from it, and in short order.

A young woman named Jill arrives. She is an omnivorous fan of Spider's work who has hitchhiked across the country to pay tribute and who encounters him being, as he frequently is, sick in a diner. Almost automatically, she starts looking after him, and even offers - - at no great risk, considering his condition -- to share his bed. They have a certain amount of inter-generational conflict; discovering in his room a cherished volume of Jules Verne, the patron saint of Spider's trade, she exclaims, "I never read anything old," as if afraid she might catch something. Jill's limitations, though, go with her strengths; shy but sure, she will be Spider's salvation.

Mostly she has to save him from Quint and Teddy, the two guys who come to take him away. Quint, the elder, is a former mercenary who, after being invalided out of service, was taken in by Teddy's family; Teddy, now orphaned, is his more-or-less faithful henchman, and very useful. Though physically small, he is able to hoist the sleeping, blanketed Spider over his shoulder, though he demurs at actually carrying him out of the room. Quint eventually lets on that his benevolent employers are aliens who want Spider to save the world; they recruit someone to carry out this amiable chore once every few thousand years. If Spider will co-operate, fine; if not, Quint -- also a devotee of his fiction but appalled by the state he has let himself get into -- will be more than happy to kill him, and perhaps take on the saving job himself, though he does seem to have it confused with world dictatorship. (He is one of Nature's fascists; cruel and servile at once.)

Killing is what Quint is good at, what he was trained if not born for, and the play takes shape as a duel between two rival ways of looking at the world. Spider's novels, we gather, were always fundamentally benevolent; the good guys were able to save the Earth or the galaxy or whatever, and it was worth saving. Quint wants to destroy, or at the very least to intimidate, and though statistically outnumbered (most people would rather be nice, and even Teddy proves a wavering ally), he is fearsomely powerful. He can get off on aiming guns at people, or by denying them their breakfast. The resemblance to Pinter's Goldberg goes quite deep. Quint too has come through a breakdown to find power in wielding power, and like Goldberg he threatens to run off with a play of which he is not technically the protagonist.

David Fox plays him magnificently, with remorseless weight and dazzling style. Quite the best-dressed person in the play (he has, admittedly, absolutely no competition), he wears his tie as tellingly as he wields his weapons; and he speaks with chilling casualness of the numbers he has killed. This is the most entertaining new play since The Drawer Boy, and Fox's appearance in both must have something to do with that.

As Spider, Hardee T. Lineham, who engagingly resembles Kelsey Grammer with a three-day growth, seems initially to be trying too hard; he delivers an initial soliloquy (not the author's best writing) in a resonant mumble that suggests he has something, possibly a microphone, stuck in his throat. He perks up as soon as he has people to act with, giving us someone to root for but not to gush over.

Kristina Nicoll as Jill is at once dowdily eccentric and magnetically attractive, and Dylan Roberts' Teddy is everybody's perfect foil. Ted Dykstra, who as director has done a masterly job of verbal and visual orchestration, speaks in the program of "this dream cast"; this is the kind of thing people say in their bios, when they're through thanking God or their mothers or whoever gave them the job, but this time it seems justified. This kind of acting and inter-acting provides real sensuous pleasure.

Steve Lucas, whose designs for The Vic I slammed yesterday, supplies four faultlessly evocative sets in a tiny space. There are times when the playwright's storytelling threatens to go off the rails, but like the heroes its hero writes about, it always finds a way out. Its long last scene keeps twisting beyond our expectations, and our central question -- do those space entities really exist? -- is played with brilliantly right up to the end.
