Theodore Bikel, actor and musician, has an easy, comfortable way of belonging on a stage that I haven't seen equalled since the death of William Hutt. At the age of 85, he has of course had time to develop it. Early in his career, he appeared in London with Peter Ustinov, and some of Ustinov's confiding jocularity may well have stayed with him. In his tribute to the Russian-Yiddish humourist and storyteller who re-named himself Sholom Aleichem, Bikel envelops the audience in a casual, non-pressuring bear hug.

Supported by a pianist and an accordionist, Bikel appears before us in three principal guises: as himself, fan and preservationist of Yiddish literature; as Aleichem, shrugging chronicler of the shtetl, who ended his days in New York; and as Aleichem's most celebrated creation, Tevye the dairyman. Tevye is now of course best known as the hero of Fiddler on the Roof, a role Bikel has played thousands of times, as a projection in the preamble bears witness. At the opposite end of the evening, he has Aleichem lamenting the unlikelihood of getting a play about Tevye put on in the declining (he says) New York Yiddish theatre of the 1910s; I think we can take it that these are the author's authentic words. We can also be pretty sure that when he goes to envision the possibility of a singing and dancing Tevye in the distant future, his impersonator is putting cute words in his mouth.

Still, Bikel manages to maintain his dignity while doing it, and when he gets around to portraying Tevye himself, he quickly banishes any suspicion that he may be about to break into If I Were a Rich Man. He gives us the Tevye we're used to, bending God's ear with respectful grumbles, but he paints a darker picture than we get from the musical. We find out what actually happened to the tailor Motel Kamzoil and what became of the marriage plans of Tevye's two youngest daughters, and none of it is pretty. We also hear, via the author's own bitter thoughts from a refuge in Switzerland, how much more there was to a pogrom than some carefully choreographed furniture-smashing.

Running just longer than an hour and a half, this is a scattershot entertainment. There are some songs, in excellent English translations by Bikel himself. There are random glimpses of shtetl boyhood, involving a rabbi who would as soon beat a student as teach him and a best friend with a marked resemblance to Huck Finn; indeed, we're told that Sholom Aleichem and Mark Twain regarded one another as kindred spirits. The show only partly lives up to its subtitle. There is laughter, certainly, and there's the occcasional sigh, but the tears have to be taken as read. - Sholom Aleichem continues to Oct. 18.
