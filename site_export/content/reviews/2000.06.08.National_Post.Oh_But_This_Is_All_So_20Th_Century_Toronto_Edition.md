RENT

At the Pantages Theatre, Toronto

To adapt one of the more powerful songs from Rent: "When you're living in America, at the end of the millennium, youre slightly out of date.

Much of this hugely successful shows music still resounds, and this third time around, I found it easy to walk into the theatre, intoning the pounding theme tune, the one that goes: How we gonna pay, how we gonna pay, how we gonna pay, last year's rent?" -- which is a bit whiny, but which later mutates into the more determined "we ain't gonna pay ..., and so on.

Most of us can sympathize with those sentiments. Weve been there. Its a stretch, though, to elevate them into a moral credo. And you may find yourself wondering just why the two young men who sing it should find their landlord's demands so unreasonable, and why the landlord himself, each time he puts in appearance, should be dismissed as Yuppie scum," self-evidently unworthy of a hearing. He may be all they say he is, but his treatment isn't dramatic. And the whole story of evictions and protests and lock-outs, which supplies the title, is confusingly raised and dropped during the show, and finally fades out long before the end.

As everybody knows, Rent is La Boheme, transferred to New Yorks East Village. Mark, an aspiring filmmaker and the shows narrator- figure, shares a currently freezing loft apartment (it's Christmas when we start) with Roger, a long-unproductive rock musician who, in common with half the principals, has AIDS. Mark has lost his girlfriend, Maureen, a performance artist, to her lesbian manager, Joanne, who has a day job as a lawyer.

Roger gets involved with Mimi, a junkie who works at an S&M club and who is fitfully involved with Benny, the landlord. Then there is Collins, a computer genius, who hooks up with Angel, a drag queen. Between them they go through an assortment of creative or emotional triumphs and disasters. At the end, Mimi seemingly dies of AIDS, like her operatic namesake of TB, but then makes a surprise comeback, having apparently had a vision of Angel, who is dead.

Well, you could probably make the story of La Boheme itself sound silly with a snappy synopsis. (Personally, I think it is silly.) Rent has at least outlived one canard -- that it owes its success to the death, just before its New York opening, of its then-unknown author-composer, Jonathan Larson. I doubt if many of its current audiences know about Larson, and I myself came to the show too late to be moved by the thought of his death while I was actually watching it.

Writing about it is another thing. It is distressing that the word that keeps coming to the surface is "promising. Larson succeeded better than anyone else in making contemporary pop theatrical. His lyrics are mostly rock-opera generic -- too much declaration, not enough detail -- with the bright exception of Tango Maureen, in which Mark and Joanne pool the exasperation they feel over their mutual flame.

The music, though, is varied and vivid, from Santa Fe, which suggests what Kurt Weill might have made of a country-and-western song, to a couple of lung-bursting bodice rippers for Mimi. There are also La Vie Boheme, a dinner-table celebration of everything the characters (and presumably the author) approve of, and the famous Seasons of Love, a sweet tune with significant verbal handicaps.

Good musicals are often about individual people loving one another. They are not about Love -- or, as this song has it lo-o- ove" -- in the abstract. That's pop: the mindless sickliness that has lain beneath the tough veneer since the 1960s. A U.S. theatre magazine, discussing Mimis resurrection, exclaimed "Ah, the American theater ending!" and blamed it all on Rodgers and Hammerstein. That is a lie. In a traditional musical -- and very notably in R&H -- when people die, they stay dead.

Rent has a peppy, atmospheric first act, and an increasingly clammy second. Its dramatic dishonesty really surfaces at Angels funeral. Mimi steps forward to say Angel was one of her best friends, though we've never seen them exchange a word. Other characters chime in, claiming for the deceased all kinds of noble qualities that were never dramatized. And from then on the name of Angel becomes an emotional beacon for the survivors. It's as if being a drag queen and dying of AIDS are automatic qualifications for sanctity.

When it opened in New York, and when it first came to Toronto, Rent had three things going for it: its music, its gritty-casual look, and its energy. The music remains, and so do the lineaments of Michael Greifs staging, but the performers of this touring version are sleepwalking.

The performances -- apart from Horace V. Rogers as Collins -- are zeroes. A charmless Mark -- who should be our window on the action - - and a raucously unenticing Mimi are especially damaging. If we dont warm to the performers, we're going to start seeing the characters as mawkish and self-pitying.

Why are we supposed to agonize with Mark when he gets a lucrative TV offer? Why should we care about Roger and Mimi when all they ever do, thanks to the musical idiom, is shout at one another? The sung- through rock-musical had always had problems with story and character, which tend to fall through the cracks between the screams, and this cast are especially incomprehensible.

Infrequently but regularly, a musical comes along that both depicts and speaks to disaffected youth -- or at least to those who would like to feel disaffacted for two hours or so. West Side Story is 45 years old and still cool (ask the Gap). Hair fell out, long ago. Rent will probably rank between the two. Its now-ness, so frantically proclaimed in the lyrics, was real but already feels nostalgic. It was last millennium.
