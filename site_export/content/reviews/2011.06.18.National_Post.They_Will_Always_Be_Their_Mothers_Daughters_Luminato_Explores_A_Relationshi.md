The stage floor at the start of Tout Comme Elle is covered with ladies' shoes: 50 pairs of them. We know there are that many, because there are 50 actresses listed in the program, and the action proper commences with each and every one of them coming onstage and slipping into a pair. It's a drawn-out, dream-like sequence; and since the performers include a phenomenal number of Toronto's finest, it's possible to while away the time quite happily by trying to pick your own favourites out of the crowd.

You can go on doing this for most of the show's 90-minute length since, for all the scintillating individuals involved, Tout Comme Elle is a choral piece. It's Luminato's presentation of Necessary Angel's production of a piece that originated in Quebec in 2006. Brigitte Haentjens directed it, which must to a large extent mean that she choreographed it; Louise Dupre wrote the text; and Erin Moure wrote the English translation. The subject is mothers and daughters.

Everybody onstage is by definition a daughter, and there is one sequence in which each in turn identifies herself and her real-life mother by name. For much of the time, though, they speak and move and sing in unison, speaking alternately for both generations. Even when one detaches herself for a single sentence or paragraph, she seems to be speaking for the collective. Initially we get the daughters' perspective; there are evocations of gazing across the tea table at the woman who bore you, realizing that you hardly know her and she is beginning to lose all memory of you. There are guilty reflections on how seldom you go to visit and how glad you are to get away. In one logistically uncharacteristic sequence, the older and younger performers square off, one on one, and chant reproachful imprecations at one another. Rather more thoughtfully, when the daughters become mothers themselves, they find their tolerance of their daughters increasing incrementally. The show is a multiple reiteration of Oscar Wilde's famous dictum that all women become like their mothers and that's their tragedy, though it suggests -as Wilde of course implicitly did -that it's their comedy as well.

There are individual observations that strike forcefully home, but they never develop. We all know that the mother-daughter relationship can be fraught. Having it repeated 50-fold gives it a certain theatrical impressiveness, and the show's pictorial accomplishments give it even more; the cast, colourfully costumed by a designer billed as YSO, move around the stage in great sweeping arcs. But truisms can only come to life on the stage when stories and characters are fleshed out, and the format denies that any chance of happening. It even becomes a form of emotional blackmail: as in, this must be important because so many women are saying it at the same time. Denied emotional and intellectual sustenance, the mind starts brooding on some of the great metaphysical questions: Is Fiona Reid only going to get one speech? Can I really sit through a whole show and fail to recognize Kristen Thomson? The sheer quantity of talent involved actually gets frustrating: all these terrific actresses and they don't get a chance to act. Or even interact. There are a few times when somebody gets to stand out briefly from the throng. Diana Leblanc and Barbara Gordon make sharp impressions. (The joint presence of Reid, Gordon, Fiona Highet and Kyra Harper prompts the frivolous thought that Tout Comme Elle might be Calendar Girls-plus.) Another question that comes to mind is just why it's impossible to imagine a similar show about fathers and sons. It's nearly as hard to envisage one about fathers and daughters or mothers and sons.

About the closest the show gets to admitting the existence of the other gender is a massed rendition of I Saw Mommy Kissing Santa Claus, executed with great poker-faced charm. (Kudos, by the way, to Lily Ling, musical director.) The cast contains one actual mother-daughter pairing, in Anne Anglin and Severn Thompson. Anglin, who I think is the senior person present, gets to deliver a wonderfully deflationary last line that may well be worth the price of admission all on its own -though I'm not sure that you negate pretentiousness simply by disowning it. Trivia note that may not be all that trivial: What may be the earliest play we have -The Suppliants by Aeschylus -also features a chorus of 50 women; indeed they are, collectively, its central character, though not only was that play written by a man, but in ancient Athens all those women would have been played by men. So Tout Comme Elle may be going back not just to the roots of Western drama but to its fathers. It's Just Like Him.
