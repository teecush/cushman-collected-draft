Next Monday sees the 32nd annual presentation of the Dora Awards. In past years, I've had malicious fun casting an eye over the nominees, wondering what could possibly explain who's been included and who's been left out, and hazarding guesses as to who would come out on top. This year, though, I'm writing from a glass house, as I'm one of the four daily critics who inaugurated our own Toronto theatre awards. We announced our results the same day as the Dora people announced their nominations, so there was no chance of either process influencing the other. But now, of course, if I or any of my colleagues castigate the Dora judges for their fallibility, they or anybody else are entitled to come right back at us. Myself, I think we made very good choices but then I would, wouldn't I?

As it happens, most of the recipients of the critics' awards are also up for Doras, so maybe there's more common ground between the critics and what is commonly known as "the industry" than we've often been led to believe. Where there isn't an overlap, it could often be due to a technicality. For example, the Toronto Theatre Critics Awards and the Doras have different criteria for what qualifies as a new play. We decided it could be any Canadian play new to the city, and we gave our prize to Stephen Massicotte's The Clockmaker, which had already been seen in Calgary. The Doras insist that the new play must be a world premiere, and they don't seem to worry about nationality; one of their five nominees for outstanding new play (they don't like the word "best") is Peggy Pickit Sees the Face of God by Roland Schimmelpfennig who is, believe it or not, German. It was a distinguished piece and I'd rate its chances very high, though it might be hampered by the fact that, as part of Luminato's Africa Trilogy, it was only one third of an evening. Also, it's up against Brothel #9, a show that probably stands this year's best cross-category chance of sweeping the board; its author, Anusree Roy, had two plays on this season as both author and actress.

The critics' choice for best production (or, as I like to think of it, best allaround thing) was The Middle Place, which didn't make the equivalent list at the Doras, but is up for new play; though it might be hampered there by the fact that its author, Andrew Kushnir, didn't so much write as edit and transcribe it. The transcriptions were riveting, but I'm inclined to think that we made the better category decision. The Doras' best production choices are The Railway Children (a surprise populist entry), Brothel #9 (what did I tell you?), The Africa Trilogy (ditto, though the two parts that weren't Peggy Pickit weren't a patch on the one that was), Ruined and Blasted. From that last pair of apocalyptic titles -indeed from the whole batch -my own pick would be Blasted, a ferociously powerful evening at Buddies in Bad Times. The one I think will win, for feel-good reasons, is Ruined.

The critics gave best actor prizes to performances in those last two plays, and both are strong Dora contenders, as well. In fact, David Ferry, whose scary ground-zero performance in Blasted was of a man both blasted and ruined, already has a well-deserved Dora this year: The special Barbara Hamilton for being (I paraphrase) a force for good in Toronto theatre. If he gets the best actor Dora as well, his mantelpiece will be groaning. His main competition will be -should be -Joseph Ziegler's heartbreaking Willy Loman in Soulpepper's Death of a Salesman. Maybe it's time to proclaim how blessed are Toronto in general and Soulpepper in particular to have both Ziegler and Diego Matamoros working consistently at the height of their or anyone else's powers. Matamoros is nominated, too, for his performance in Oleanna; he was brilliant, but the production wasn't enough liked for him to stand much of a chance. Yanna McIntosh in Ruined was the critics' best actress; of her Dora competitors I would root, fiercely, for Michelle Moneith's uncanny performance in Blasted, though this may be the most hotly contested category of all, what with Jane Spidell in Peggy Pickit and two performances -the author's and Pamela Sinha's -in Brothel #9. In which connection: It wasn't planned that way, but I'm personally pleased that no one production carried off more than one critics' award. Spread it around, I say.

It's a perennial complaint that the Doras, apparently for lack of sponsorship, always lump supporting (or "featured") performances in one category, be they male, female, musical or dramatic. This year's mixed bag includes Oliver Dennis, put forward for Jitters, in which he was indeed expertly jittery, rather than for The Fantasticks in which he was fantastic. All right, I'm being smug; we critics, flush with categories, gave him best supporting actor in a musical. We gave best actress in a musical to Louise Pitre in A Year with Frog and Toad, and here I hope the Doras do follow suit because hers was a selftransforming performance, and not just by virtue of being amphibian. We only, though, gave one design award, where the Doras seem to have dozens. Our choice, Studies in Motion, must be Dora-ineligible as it came from outer space, or at least from Vancouver. I'm glad at least that Charlotte Dean gets a costume nod for The Misanthrope, to my mind the year's most shut-out production; I'd happily have nodded at Richard Rose (director), Stuart Hughes (actor) and Michelle Monteith (supporting actress). I suppose they'll give best musical to Billy Elliot, though if you like 'em big Priscilla Queen of the Desert was a better show. There are four COC productions nominated in the opera division; I'd hate to have to choose between them, and I mean that in the nicest way.

? The 2011 Dora Awards are presented at the Bluma Appel Theatre on Monday.
