Some TV shows riff off real-life events. Some riff off other TV shows. Guns, though gripping on its own account, does both. This two-part, four-hour drama from CBC begins with the death of a young girl, felled by a stray bullet in a gang shootout at the intersection of Yonge and Gould Streets in downtown Toronto. It's where 15-year-old Jane Creba was killed in similar circumstances in 2005. "The authenticity of the location," says a press release, "was therefore of tantamount importance." Is that the same as what used to be called "paramount importance"? It sounds paramount to illiteracy.

More importantly, the show should be able to register its disgust at random violence on city streets without modelling itself so closely on a real and recent case. This isn't a documentary. The creators (writer-producer Jennifer Holness and writer-director Sudz Sutherland) have upped the horrific ante, making the victim a six-year old. Her death naturally leads to public outcry, and to a determination at City Hall to get guns off the streets, or at least to be seen to be doing so.

The police, of course, would be only too glad to see it happen. Their main stumbling-block is Paul Duguid (a surprisingly boyish Colm Feore), a well-connected arms dealer who supplements his substantial legitimate income by selling illegally to foreign governments. (His main client for the story's purposes is Honduras, which seems almost too topical, though the mini-series was filmed in 2007.) He has so far proved untouchable but he has a son, Bobby, who could be described with perfect appropriateness as a loose cannon. He smuggles guns in from the United States, for street gang use. In need of a mule, he enlists his girlfriend Frances, who's played by Elisha Cuthbert, renewing her Canadian cred. Her presence (and Feore's too, come to think of it) naturally turns thoughts to 24, and to one of its primary lessons, which is, when in need of help in a tight spot, never turn to Elisha Cuthbert. Her Frances is as accident-prone -- and also, to be fair, as plucky -- as her Kim Bauer. She even has a scene in jail, this time wearing an orange jumpsuit.

Paul's agent-provocateur tactics are also close to those of one of the villains in the last round of 24, but the American show that this one most closely resembles -- whether through accident or imitation -- is The Wire. Here, too, we have a panoramic view of a city, taking in the streets, the police, the politicians, the money (lots of party scenes in Rosedale), the schools, the strip clubs and even a newspaper office. Though, of course, this show's two nights hardly afford the scope of The Wire's five years, and there's a lot that feels skimpy and rushed.

Bobby (Gregory Smith) is remarkably similar to the crazy son of the dock union boss in The Wire's second season, if with far more disposable income. And there are two matched pairs of pivotal characters who are very Wiry. We have two cops: Rick (Shawn Doyle) who's white and his partner Ford (Lyriq Bent) who's black, working in a semi-fictitious Weapons Enforcement Unit. We have two young guys from the projects, running guns rather than dealing drugs: Conrad (Cle Bennett) who wants to get on, and Derek (K. C. Collins) who wants to get out. All four men have fraught home-lives, three with their wives or girlfriends, Derek with his kid sister. (He tells her they're going away West. "West to Mississauga?" she queries. "West to Vancouver," he replies). The similarities to The Wire extend to a snitch being murdered in prison with the crime passed off as a suicide, and even to the unexpected playing of a Billie Holiday record -- well, it's Nina Simone, but she's singing Holiday's Don't Explain.

Guns has nothing of The Wire's tragic sense. It has much the same worldview -- much is rigged, some bad guys get away, but a modicum of justice gets done--and it even has the courage to kill off sympathetic characters, but it doesn't go the distance. Its veneer is tough but its centre is soft. It also crams too much in; the bereaved parents mount a "take back our streets" campaign that adds nothing to the story, and is awkwardly written besides.

In fact, most of the script above street-level sounds contrived: It's painful to watch old stagers like Feore and Jim Mezon exchanging would-be smart dialogue that plays like jumped-up exposition. The domestic stuff, too, is mostly cardboard, and the authors like to involve us with their characters by having them talk to themselves out loud, a device I thought TV had outgrown. However, Doyle, Bent, Collins, Bennett and Smithall giveexcellent performances, and their scenes sound real. Interrogation sequences are especially good -- the cops seem as likely to crack under pressure as the crooks -- and the basic thriller stuff is exciting, as well as morally grounded. It's good to hear a top policeman pointing out that the gun registry helps. Maybe there will be a spinoff. Weapons Enforcement Unit might have the same legs as its big brothers Counter Terrorist and Major Crimes. There have been worse ideas.

- Guns airs Sunday and Monday at 8 p.m. on CBC.
