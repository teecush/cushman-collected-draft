THEATRE REVIEW

Rebecca

Royal George Theatre,

Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont.

I approach Rebecca in a state of comparative innocence. I have never read the novel or seen the film. I did catch the first half of the recent TV adaptation, but missed the second. I assume, though, that everyone else knows more than I do, and merely needs to be reminded that this is the one about the young second wife who is brought by her husband to his ancestral Cornish seat, only to find that the memory of her predecessor has taken threatening possession of both house and spouse. The previous wife's name is Rebecca, and her end was watery.

The book came out in 1938. Two years later, its author, Daphne du Maurier, turned it into a play; and it is this play -- with, I gather, a few re-interpolations from the novel -- that the Shaw Festival has revived. As a kind of ghost story, as a study of the hazards of second marriage and as an updated Gothic romance, it variously recalls Jane Eyre, The Turn of the Screw and Noel Coward's Blithe Spirit.

It also bears a surprising resemblance to Ibsen's Rosmersholm, a drama that also involves a tradition-encrusted house, its high- minded master and a drowned wife. In that play, it's the wife's successor who's called Rebecca, and we gather that she pushed the old girl in. It is gradually made clear to her that this was not, on the whole, a nice thing to do. You might think that was fairly obvious, but Ibsen seems to have considered it a moral conundrum worth spending four acts over.

Rosmersholm is an undeniably serious play. Rebecca is not serious; it gets into some similarly deep ethical waters, but it allows its characters an arbitrary escape route. Christopher Newton, its current director, describes it in his program note as a boulevard piece.

The best bits of his production come when the story takes on a momentum of its own, and investigations and revelations start flying thick if not fast. This accounts for most of the evening's second half, and it is riveting. But it's a long and frequently awkward time getting there. Newton may in part be inhibited by an excess of good taste. Each of the play's half-dozen scenes comes equipped with a thumpingly melodramatic curtain-line, but none of them is exploited; the endings just hang there.

Nor is the acting much help. There are three central characters. One is Mrs. de Winter, the incoming bride, at whom Severn Thomson has a fair shot, suffering with spirit and rebelling with grace. But we don't feel or fear for her as we should; she seems well able to take care of herself. Maxim, her husband, is reticent, tormented and -- this is England -- damn decent: qualities that Peter Krantz translates into stiff and querulous. It must, admittedly, be hard to animate such a stick figure 50 years on and thousands of miles off.

Then there is Mrs. Danvers, the famous one, the housekeeper, making life hell for the new wife on account of her passionate feelings for the old. My chief memory of the TV version I half saw is of Diana Rigg, looking like the wrath of God and sounding as if she were introducing a particularly blood-bolstered segment of Mystery! on PBS.

This, I feel certain, is the way to go. Sharry Flett seems to be trying for something subtler, and is consequently at her best when pretending to be agreeable. But her final, demonic actions, apart from being half-heartedly staged, are just unbelievable, and her recurrent appearances on the ancestral staircase are so many missed opportunities. (It's amazing that nobody has turned Rebecca into a musical. It has Andrew Lloyd Webber written all over it. It even has a fancy-dress ball. I shall regret having written that.)

The supporting cast are there mainly to dispense or withhold information. Brigitte Robinson, as the hero's sister, gets some teeth into her share of the exposition, while the veteran Tony van Bridge, as the local chief constable, gets some delightful flesh and blood into his.

William Schmuck's set, seductively lit by Elizabeth Asseltine, is an appropriate essay in classic baronial; the stairs are timbered, the windows are mullioned, the portraits on the wall look to be by Lely or Kneller, all that's missing is a suit of armour. It occurred to me that, if hard pressed, the festival could combine this setting with the one for A Foggy Day, the other country-house piece, that plays on alternate nights. Both these shows derive in part from 1940s movies starring Joan Fontaine as a damsel in distress. There are worse ways to build a season.
