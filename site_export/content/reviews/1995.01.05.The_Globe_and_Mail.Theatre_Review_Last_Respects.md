THEATRE REVIEW LAST RESPECTS

Thursday, January 05, 1995

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Toronto ONT -- BY DEBORAH KIMMETT Directed by Sandra Balcovske Starring Martti Arkko, Clare Coulter and Maria Lamont At Tarragon Theatre Rating: Sometimes we wonder what Last Respects would be like without Clare Coulter as the quietly witty Sarah. Other times, all too many of them, we find out Once upon an evening dreary BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Toronto CLARE Coulter, for whom thank God, spends most of Last Respects slumped in a chair. This is because she is dead. Obviously actors of her eminence are not engaged just to play corpses, and I am giving nothing away by revealing that the play frequently flashes back to a time when she was alive.

She plays Sarah, an old English lady now living - or, given the play's structure, now and then living - on Queen's Quay. Coulter is, if I may be parochial, better at British accents than any other Canadian actor I know. The one she adopts here is pinched, parched, quiet, relaxed and devastating: The voice of the London intelligentsia in reduced circumstances. "I have never," she says early on, "found much spiritual awareness in laundry." Well, who has? But she manages to make the admission sound like a masterpiece of wit.

She does the same for most of her lines, and without sounding as if she is bucking for some kind of one-liner award. (That would be an Epigrammy.) She never breaks faith with her character, a compassionate realist who has seen it all. Even just sitting, she looks understanding in a grumpy sort of way. A Buddhist, and a veteran of two marriages, Sarah has a fair idea of what to expect from life, both within and without. Hence her disclaimer about the laundry.

Sometimes we wonder what the evening would be like without her. Other times, all too many of them, we find out. When Clare Coulter is offstage. . . . I'll start that again, she's never offstage. When she is present but deceased, we are thrown back on two of her neighbours, young people who have been touched by her during their own messy and frustrated lives. One of them is Angie, a would-be model with an over-eating problem. Confronted by a man who's come to take her picture she simpers, "You're going to light me, that is so, like, hot." At this point, when she is comparatively carefree, she is probably meant to sound silly. But she isn't any more enlivening when she shacks up with the cameraman and starts in suffering on a steady basis.

One thing that afflicts her, and us, is the strange semi-literary dialogue in which Deborah Kimmett's play, like far too many other plays, is written. When it's not being oracular it tries for what might be called incremental comedy. Angie doesn't just protest that she's thin; she insists that "complete strangers come up to me and say, 'My God, you have small wrists.' " This is formula writing, but an expert comic might make it work. The central thread of the play is Angie's monologue, so she could use some sparkle. (So what if it were out of character? We would forgive.) Maria Lamont plays her doggedly, and without much range. If you take the humour of complaint, but hold the humour, you end up with whining. That is what happens here, and I honestly don't know whether writing or performance is most to blame, any more than I can tell how much credit the author deserves for the laughs Clare Coulter notches up.

Martti Arkko plays the man, who at one point renders the theme from Love Story on an accordion, a combination of atrocities to which it would take a Lady Bracknell to do justice. He drinks too much, and is Finnish; one of Sarah's former partners was Norwegian, a combination that occasions a line about Scandinavian gloom being caused by the sepulchral climate up there: offered, with no visible irony, as a totally new insight.

Audiences tend to suspect three-character plays about other people's domestic problems of being intolerably dreary, and Last Respects does little to disarm the prejudice. On the other hand, if you hang in there, you do get to hear Coulter's Sarah reflecting, with lemony stoicism, on her late husband's continuing inability to locate her clitoris, and that could make it worthwhile.
