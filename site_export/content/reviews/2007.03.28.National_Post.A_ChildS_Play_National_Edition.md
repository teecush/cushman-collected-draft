THEATRE REVIEW

THE RED HORSE IS LEAVING

Theatre Centre, Toronto

- - -

In The Red Horse Is Leaving, Erika Batdorf plays her own mother. That is Thaya Whitten, a Nova Scotian woman of multiple talents; primarily she was a painter, but she was also a jazz musician and some kind of performance artist. She was also mentally ill, with a doubleedged attitude to the drugs prescribed for her condition. She depended on them, but she also feared addiction and worried how they might affect her creativity.

Batdorf, herself an accomplished performer, begins this solo show with a long and sometimes very funny silent sequence showing the artist in her studio. Distractedly (the piece might almost be said to be a continuing pun on the different meanings of the word "distraction"), she juggles a cigarette and a cup of coffee, neither of them probably the most advisable of substances. At one point she seems to be attempting to paint with the cigarette. She makes a shaky but determined approach to the canvas on her easel, then realizes that the brush she has chosen is not the one she wanted and shakes her head in gentle frustration. When she sits down, her knee visibly trembles.

When she starts talking she talks directly to us, breaking the fourth wall in a manner that seemed to me too self-conscious for its own good. To draw attention to the fact that you're shattering a convention is a strange kind of double-bluff. It fits perhaps with Whitten's own nervous self-awareness as a performer. Drawing on her subject's journals, Batdorf presents her as simultaneously in a gallery and in a theatre. She is conducting a participatory lecture on painting. What, she asks, is the purpose of art or, more specifically, what is the artist's motive? Profit, communication, self-expression? She opts, I think, for the last, telling aspiring artists that "the only thing you have to offer the world is you." She alludes to her own reputation as "a controversialist" and to the way she shocked the folks back home: "Women in the Lutheran Church didn't do such things."

Her subject for the day, both as painter and lecturer, is fear. She suggests that the colour of fear might be red, and offloads a splodge of it onto her canvas. She then calls for further suggestions from the house, and various spectators step forward to add their own chosen shades to the composition. There is nothing embarrassing about this audience participation; Batdorf is an underplayer, and the attitude she presents -- firm in her ideas, diffident in their presentation -- makes everyone feel at ease. On the first night assorted volunteers contributed black, green and yellow to the picture; Andy McKim, Theatre Passe Muraille's newly appointed artistic director here making his debut as a directed artist, opted for aubergine, which Whitten- Batdorf translated as "black-purple."

I can understand being afraid of aubergines. In the end, though, I find it hard to conceive of fear as a colour, which may explain why I found this whole section of the show oddly whimsical. At the end of it, though, a phone rings, and it was in Batdorf 's reaction to that I truly saw fear, stabbing and unadorned.

One section of this show, sympathetically directed by Todd Hammond, bleeds imperceptibly into the next, and it seems that suddenly we are back in the studio, watching and hearing a verbalized elaboration of the opening silent sequence. We see Whitten battling with her psychological and chemical demons, trying to keep the world of patrons, dealers and caregivers both at hand and at bay. One sly reference to her daughter Erika summons up a whole familiar pattern of the grown child caring for the ailing parent, all the more piercing and constant when the disorder is mental rather than physical. It's also, of course, in the circumstances of the show itself, a fine inside joke. Batdorf, however, whether as writer-compiler or as actress, never presumes on the special relationship. This is a loving portrait, and both she and the audience are nourished that fact, but it's an objective and unsentimental one. It's also tantalizingly incomplete, but that probably comes with the territory.
