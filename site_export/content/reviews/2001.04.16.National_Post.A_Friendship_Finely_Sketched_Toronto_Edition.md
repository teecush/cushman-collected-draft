THE DRAWER BOY

Winter Garden Theatre, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - In the two years since it made its bow at Theatre Passe Muraille, The Drawer Boy, Michael Healey's play about myth-making, has made its own myth. Indeed, it was well on its way even on that first night. The sureness and steadiness of its narrative, its fresh approach to a familiar setting, the perfect teamwork of its three actors, and our complete unreadiness for what we were about to see (what, for a start, could the title mean?) combined to create a legend. In this revival, with the set glossed up for a bigger space and a commercial production but with the same director and cast, the magic still works.

The play is set in the early 1970s, on an Ontario farm run by two middle-aged bachelors, Morgan and Angus. Its heart is the story that Morgan tells Angus, every night, and that we hear toward the end of the first act. There was the farmer boy, Morgan himself, and the drawer boy, Angus, an aspiring architect -- that's the kind of drawing he did -- who went off together to serve in the Second World War. While stationed in London they met two young women -- "the tall girl" and "the taller girl" -- and two cases of requited love broke out. Tragedy struck when Angus, during the blitz, was hit on the head by a flying door and lost most of his memory. Still the two couples went back to Canada, had a double wedding, and happily worked the farm, until both the girls were killed in a driving accident. That's Morgan's story and some of it is true.

How much is true, and why Morgan invented the rest of it, is revealed by the arrival of Miles, a young actor researching a documentary play about farming; the reference, plainly, is to an actual and famous 1970s production called The Farm Show. Miles finds Angus to be an amiable simpleton who can do complicated sums in his head but forgets the names of people and things as soon as he has heard them. Morgan puts Miles to work in return for his keep; he has a sardonic sense of humour and has the city boy rising at 3 a.m. -- "to rotate the crops" -- and mucking out the cowshed with a fork. (Even Angus thinks this is ridiculous. "I'll get you a spoon," he volunteers.) Angus obsessively prepares sandwiches, which Morgan, with corresponding regularity, eats.

When Miles asks how it feels to be continually growing things in order to kill them, Morgan gives him a reply for the ages: "Miles" he says, tongue unfathomnably in cheek, "it's an emotional roller coaster." Miles's attempts to act this out, or to portray a neurotic cow terrified of slaughter if she doesn't give enough milk, apparently meet with little favour back at collective HQ. But he strikes gold when he overhears Morgan telling Angus his nightly tale. Without informing them, he has it put in the play and invites them to a run-through. Morgan is furious. Angus begins to remember things.

What eventually comes out is the story of a friendship -- a marriage, really -- that has withstood large amounts of suffering, cruelty, resentment and deceit, and will now have to carry the added burden of the truth. We are left in little doubt that it will continue, and that this is a good thing. The realization is moving and -- certainly in this performance -- remarkably unsentimental.

There is an irony here that maybe even the playwright hasn't noticed. Apparently, there was nothing in the actual Farm Show that resembled the story of The Drawer Boy. Nor should there have been since, when you think about it, the effectiveness of Morgan's story depends little on the characters having been farmers; it would have been touching whomever it had happened to. The Drawer Boy does tell us some interesting things about life on the land, and they are more compelling for being filtered through the author's own comedic sensibility. But what holds us is the very particular situation that he, as far as I know, has invented himself, and that radiates fascinatingly outward.

In part, of course, it's a tribute to the theatre itself. I have my doubts as to whether the effect on Angus of seeing his story acted out could really be as thoroughgoingly therapeutic as the play suggests, but I'll go along with it as a metaphor. Anyway, Healey caps it very craftily when he has Miles tell Angus the story of Hamlet, a character whom he once played and who had his own radical notions of drama as therapy. (It's also very funny, since Miles tells it in the first person, and the audience thinks for a few moments that he's alluding to his own student career rather than the Danish prince's.) Here it's Morgan who, though hardly a villain and certainly not a smiling one, turns out to be the guilty creature sitting at a play. No wonder he's angry.

Actually, one can sympathize with him. Most arguments about cultural appropriation are ridiculous; nobody owns history. But the direct personal experience of a living person is another thing, especially if you're going to parade it on stage in front of his neighbours. Miles, you feel, might at least have asked.

If he had, though, there would have been no play, either for him or for us. And what he does works out well, if at a cost.

It's at preserving this balance that Miles Potter's production excels. (He was in the original Farm Show but his work 30 years on seems a lot more tactful than that of his onstage namesake.)

David Fox's Angus retains its extraordinary power and especially its swift, decisive timing; his mind seems to be moving, with absolute assurance, backward and forward over the same minute patch of ground. The malice in Jerry Franken's Morgan is more to the fore than I remember it, but the complementary sense of stern concern remains unaffected. Tom Barnett's cavortings as Miles sometimes approach self-indulgence but never actually get there; in his other persona as concerned observer he's fine. It's not his fault that the moment at which he tumbles to the truth is the only one in the play that rings false, a contrivance for the author to get more quickly to the end. John Ferguson's spruce new set, complete with sky cloth, is unsuitably pretty but does no serious damage.

There have been a handful of fine new plays in the last couple of years -- mostly, like The Drawer Boy, from beginning playwrights -- but this remains the best.
