Irving Berlin's White Christmas

Sony Centre for the

Performing Arts, Toronto

The best critical summary that I know of the film White Christmas was made by one of its stars, the late, great and delightfully honest Rosemary Clooney.

Introducing its title song at one of her concerts, she said that it was a picture that could be enjoyed at the appropriate time of year but had best be avoided at all others. It's certainly no masterpiece of narrative logic. I was once able to share with Clooney my bewilderment over a plot development in which her character, up till then half of a struggling singing-sister act, suddenly gets a booking as the headlining chanteuse in a swank New York showroom. Rosemary, who knew all about the realities of show business, said that she'd never understood it either.

The new stage version of the film, which started out in San Francisco in 2004 and has been trundling around the continent every winter since, is billed as Irving Berlin's White Christmas, which is fair enough, as Berlin's music and lyrics are the most it has going for it. The show's book is credited to David Ives and Paul Blake, who have brought off the remarkable feat of fashioning a narrative even less coherent than its source.

For the benefit of those who keep their TV sets switched off each December: Two Second World War buddies, Captain Bob Wallace and Private Phil Davis, team up as a song, dance and comedy act. Ten years into peacetime they have the entertainment world at their shuffling feet.

They meet the aforementioned sisters, Betty and Judy Haynes, and romance ensues, with no rivalries but some misunderstandings, at least between Bob and Betty. They incidentally are named after the real-life siblings of the performers who first played them, Bing Crosby and Clooney. The other members of the original quartet were Danny Kaye and Vera-Ellen, and the movie traded heavily on the star status of its cast. It also tapped into the wartime nostalgia of a uniquely peaceful decade.

The two guys find their former much-loved general reduced to running an unsuccessful ski lodge in Vermont. They rescue him from bankruptcy by putting on a show (the lodge has a barn), calling on their performing colleagues to make up the cast and on their old military colleagues to make up the audience. The general cannot choke back his tears. Neither, helpless slobs that we are, can we.

The sentiment of the stage White Christmas is actually the least objectionable thing about it. What brings it down is the grinding anonymity of its performance. The script doesn't exactly help.

In the movie, Phil saved Bob's life and has been calling him on it ever since; this gives their relationship a hint of edge. That's completely vanished here, along with the subtler details of Phil's scheming that brings Bob to Vermont in the first place. (He thought he was going to Florida.) The structure is that of a putting-on-a- show show: We get to see some of the numbers on the way through, but virtually nothing at the finale. It's a let down.

Wallace and Davis are supposed to be top-of-the-line performers, able to make the all-powerful Ed Sullivan do their bidding. With Crosby and Kaye, this is credible. Without them, it isn't; the characters have no character, and the actors here don't supply any.

In a role designed to feed into both Bing's affability and his reserve Graham Rowat (Canadian born but American based, so splitting the difference in a bi-national cast), is somnolent. His partner, Tony Yazbeck, is a deft if slightly heavy hoofer with no particular comic flair. Their ladyloves, Kate Baldwin and Shannon O'Bryan, are livelier but hardly earth-shattering.

The only charismatic on board is Kate Hennig as an ex-trouper with an Ethel Merman voice and manner who's wound up behind a reception desk and can't wait to get back out in front of it. A late replacement, she's jubilantly and justifiably confident, despite having some appalling shtick thrown at her before, after and even during her big number.

But the authors are shameless; they throw in a (literally) screaming queen of a stage manager and a couple of squawking brain- dead chorines, the problem being not that these stereotypes are insulting, but that they're boring. And that's not to mention the ultimate horror: a supposedly adorable little girl who goes into a routine, even as the audience prays that she won't. Meanwhile, some of the best grown-up talent in town is consigned to cameos (Paula Wolfson) or to the chorus (Jennifer Stewart).

The director is Walter Bobbie, known for class work like the never-ending revival of Chicago. That makes his presence here mystifying, but his production is at least slick; the sets are both lavish and useful, and though Randy Skinner's choreography isn't going to get any marks for innovation it's certainly crisp, especially in tapping.

And there are the songs. Berlin's film score was made up of good new songs and better old ones, and the stage version widens the net, trawling up irresistible classics like Let Yourself Go and I've Got My Love To Keep Me Warm, plus a couple of treasurable rarities, Love and the Weather and Falling Out of Love Can Be Fun.

These last are done as plot numbers and they don't fit. Still, it's good to hear them. Otherwise: Well, it's that time of year -- you could rent the movie.
