THE SWANNE: QUEEN VICTORIA (THE SEDUCTION OF NEMESIS)

Studio Theatre, Stratford, Ont.

- - -

I am happy to report that, with its concluding instalment finally in place, The Swanne floats. It also rocks.

Peter Hinton's trilogy, unrolled over three consecutive Statford seasons, is as formally ambitious a work as the Canadian theatre can ever have created. Most of it purports to be a play written by the 18-year-old Queen Victoria immediately before her accession to the British throne: an allegory of the state of England with special emphasis on its underside. But as well as this play-within-the- play, we have the outer spectacle of Victoria -- "Drina," from her first name, Alexandrina, until her accession requires something more triumphal -- actually composing it. Here in Part Three, we have more of Drina herself than ever before. Her relatives get wind of the play and naturally they hate it, especially her mother, the Duchess of Kent. They tell her to destroy it. The question is how much of the world she has imagined, will the young monarch take into her future life.

Karl Marx said he wasn't a Marxist, and it's very possible that Victoria herself was no Victorian. All the same, this is the queen who refused to sign a law against lesbians, not out of tolerance but because she refused to believe that such women existed. Hinton's Drina, if we are to take his fantasia literally, must have gone into extensive denial in order to turn into history's Victoria; which may very well be the point. Drina's play deals, sympathetically and enthusiastically, with most varieties of sex, but the relationships it favours are the homosexual ones, male or female. Part Two ended, more or less, with a raid on a gay brothel. Part Three begins, more or less, with the public hanging of four of its clients, who make gallant ends. Meanwhile, the brothel-madam sits in the stocks, pelted with everything a generous law allows.

In an adjoining part of the action, two orphan youths, once lovers, roam London in search of one another and also of the truths about their respective parents. One of them, William, is black, the illegitimate son of Drina's late aunt Charlotte, his existence concealed by a vast conspiracy. This is known as confusing the royal issue; and it's the main reason Drina has written her play. Her mother's lover is pretty scathing about it:

The flaw in the premise is obvious.

An illegitimate child, no matter what colour, cannot become monarch of England.

Therefore, all the scandal Drina portrays is completely unconvincing.

Some of us raised that objection when we saw Part One; and in acknowledging it now, Hinton both confronts and disarms us. As he does again, a bit later:

Criticks are cruel and take revenge on the art

they wish they had the courage to create. But whatever nonsense people write about you,

there is a truth in it somewhere.

I call that decent. Those lines are spoken by the Scarecrow, a tragic (sorry, tragick) actress now old, bald, blind and committed to Bedlam who has functioned throughout the trilogy as the spokesperson for what Drina plainly sees as the subversive power of the imagination. Her play also finds plenty of room for more explicit protest, whether open and bloodily dispersed (the Peterloo massacre) or covert and betrayed (the Cato Street conspiracy). Victoria was a closet radical. Who knew?

If you don't know your early 19th-century English history, you may find these events confusing. I learned mine in school and I found them confusing, what with the allusiveness of the writing and the scrambling of the chronology. The published text, just out, puts dates on the scenes; the staged version can't be that helpful. The situation is worsened, of course, by the gap between productions; we're asked to remember characters whom we haven't seen for a year and who, in many cases, are now played by different actors. (They are also, in many cases, less well played, but we'll get to that.) We are also expected, unavailingly, to remember what the swan symbol itself is all about -- I never really grasped it in the first place - - while the subtitle The Seduction of Nemesis is plain confusing. Is Nemesis, fate, the seducer or the seduced, and either way what does it mean? On a smaller matter, what does the Duchess mean by saying that Drina's "notions of love are picaresque?"

Is that really the word she's after?

But the course, as we have now learned three times over, is supremely worth staying. The narrative pieces interlock and so, though more here has to be taken on trust, do the themes. Publication confirms what was suspected two years ago -- that the play is in verse. It's informal verse, hence the uncertainty, but it's tight enough to keep the dialogue, written as it is in an exuberant mixture of new formality and old slang, from spilling right off the edge of the stage. It's also muscular and very often witty. Two years ago I accused Hinton of indulging in quaintness for its own sake; I withdraw the charge. And as before, one revels in the energy with which he has filled the stage, both as writer and director.

Some of it goes into the names he has invented, which are Dickensian and then some -- fearsome names like Dud Grudge, for an exploiter of orphans. Sometimes, admittedly, we get more name than character. The program listings for the crowd scenes have rather more flavour than do the scenes themselves. For the most part, though, the staging is masterful.

Diane D'Aquila, whose scarefying Scarecrow has been the cycle's figurehead through all three plays, reaches greatness. Poignant, dignified and unerringly funny, she hits the mark with every line she utters. This accuracy, which goes hand in hand with economy, shows up the majority of her colleagues who handle the language with what, considering that the author is also the director, must be remarkable sloppiness. Nearly everyone acts in italics, and on the wrong words at that. There are exceptions: Margot Dionne is delightfully dotty as one of William's possible mothers (he has several), Michelle Giroux is in fine fastidious form as a repentant whore, and I enjoyed Michelle Fisk's broad turn as an unrepentant one, though I didn't believe in her for a moment.

Tanja Jacobs brings off a brilliant double as a bawd in the inner play, and in the outer one as Victoria's mama who seems aware of the coupling and resents it, though with so much multiple casting going on I don't see how the audience is supposed to notice. I approved of Thom Marriott's vigour and Donald Carrier's ambiguous gentleness. Brad Rudy lacks the definition that his predecessors brought to the tormented-villain role of St. John Voranguish, "a whoremaster"; Lara Jean Chorostecki moves in and out of correct focus as Drina/ Victoria; and as her hero William, Seun Olagunju is likeable but rushed. Though I was happy to see him finally reunited with his Jeremy, I was as glad the author keeps their rejoicings brief.

Their meeting occurs in the middle of a major outbreak of Canadian content, with all the more sympathetic characters in Drina's drama taking ship to found a New London. One of them has been over here before in less propitious circumstances ("no man's been ta 'ell and live to tell of it ... unless ya been to 'Alifax") but now their hopes are higher. They join in song; there may be irony here, what with their creator about to forsake them and embrace her royal destiny, but one's instinct is to share the joy, not so much for the characters or their situation as for the scale and achievement of The Swanne itself.

We are told that it took Victoria three years to write her play; it has taken Hinton 11. He has been a director for years, but The Swanne is his first play I've heard of making up for lost time, but this is amazing.;
