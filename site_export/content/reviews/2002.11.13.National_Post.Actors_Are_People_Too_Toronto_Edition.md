IN THE WINGS

Theatre Passe Muraille, Toronto

Moment to moment, In the Wings can be very enjoyable, especially in its first and lighter half. It is a play about the theatre, that is largely set in a theatre. In fact, as far as one can tell, it's set in the very same theatre at which its currently playing. The actors seem to be having a good time spoofing themselves and their colleagues, and the first-night audience was more than happy to respond. As somebody once said about that ultimate brother-and- sister play, Tis Pity She's a Whore, the incest was sustained throughout.

It goes deeper, though, than mere in-jokery. In the Wings is based on a novel of the same name by the late Carole Corbeil, and it seems to have been at least partially inspired by a production of Hamlet that happened at Passe Muraille in the brave young days of 1983. The prince was played by Layne Coleman, who is now the director of Passe Muraille and of this production; he was also married to Corbeil. Nicky Guadagni, an actress and a close friend of Corbeil's, has adapted the book for the stage. In the Wings revolves around a projected production of Hamlet, and several of its characters, like Hamlet himself, are carrying the baggage of past relationships.

Most Hamlet-like of all, appropriately, is Allan, the intense and self-conscious young actor who gets cast in the lead. A serious manic-depressive, he is haunted by the memory of his father, up to whom he finds it extremely difficult to live. His Gertrude is Alice, who is trying to come to terms with the recent death of her mother. Old enough to play, if not actually to be, Allans mother, she is now having an affair with him. Jimmy, the director, has cast his own girlfriend as Ophelia. Then there is Robert, who is not part of the production. He is, in fact, a critic whose wife has recently left him and gone off with their son to Vancouver. Robert, rather ineffectually, is chasing an actress-singer called Louise, who happens to be an old flame of Allans, one whom he intermittently re- stokes.

I keep hearing that in the book (which I haven't read) Robert is an evil creature, corrupted by the power he wields. This is a view of critics with which I would not presume to argue (and besides, Corbeil herself was a reviewer before turning to fiction, though I never heard she was a cruel one), but it certainly isn't what appears on the stage. The casting, if nothing else, puts paid to it. If you want malevolence, you dont engage Michael Healey, a performer with an outsize but unemphatic presence who could not be unendearing if he tried. The man he plays has an unfortunate habit of offering personal compliments as if they were good reviews; he is also the only guy in sight who isnt getting laid. And he has trouble at the office. All this is to sympathize with rather than to hate. He is said (again I'm only repeating what I've heard) to earn redemption at the end, and we do see him, a lapsed Catholic, returning to confession and coming clean about his marriage. But since weve never met his wife, the scene, charmingly as Healey plays it, fails to carry much weight. In fact, good though it always is to see him, it is not at all clear what the character is doing in the play.

This of course is symptomatic of a fault that commonly bedevils adaptations of novels. There isnt enough time; there isn't enough space. Guadagni has fashioned some individually effective scenes, but characters are constantly presuming on information that we don't have, and then trying to slip it to us undigested. This is an especial problem with Allan, who has to keep repairing to his rich, cold stepmothers so we can hurriedly be filled in on his relationship to his father. (There are flashbacks. They dont help either.) Alice's love and resentment of her mother are handed to us with more ease, but they remain things we hear about rather than things we experience.

Still, for a play that comes to pieces as you watch, this has a lot to offer. It incorporates some wicked satire of the theatre, and believable portraits of actors as people; and it is, for the most part, very well acted.

Jonathan Watton, whom I remember being very promising in the first season of the Atlantic Theatre festival, fulfills that promise as Allan. He is febrile and magnetic, with an unstable conscience -- someone who attracts trust rather than love. He is at his best when scouring his stepmom's medicine cabinet, intertwining his own racing thoughts with the words of Hamlets "rogue and peasant slave" soliloquy. The intended parallels with the Shakespeare play work more strongly here than at any other point, and the words sound fresher than they often do in their official context.

Brooke Johnson stonewalls her way through Alices wounded- daughter moments, but as mother-lover she is very fine: strong and needy at once. She and Allan first meet when filming a sex scene for a TV play. It's directed by numbers and makes an amusing commentary on the erotic encounters that we witness, quite frequently, in the play itself.

David Fox enjoys himself, and pleases us, in a variety of roles, most notably the senior actor who is Claudius to Alice's Gertrude. (Fox actually was the Claudius in the real Passe Muraille Hamlet; he is Johnsons real-life husband. Go figure, but try not to hurt yourself.) John Jarvis, on a roll this year, is very funny as an alarmingly pretentious director, who seems to think hes staging the Marat/Sade. At any rate, his final tableau shows the drowned Ophelia protruding from a bath. Deborah Hay, though burdened with a couple of inexplicable songs, presents Louise as the kind of tough waif who flowers eternally on the theatrical fringe.

Steve Lucas' unit set is confusing when the action strays outside the theatre, but perfect when it stays home, as of course it would be. Coleman's production makes spare but pleasant use of the auditorium as well as the stage, and the sight of Healey arriving in the first row on the first night, and self-consciously waving to acquaintances (do we really do that?) is wondrous.
