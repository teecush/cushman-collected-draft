The fancy name Watching Glory Die has been affixed to a decidedly plain play. The word Glory" in Judith Thompson's title may conceivably have some allegorical significance, but mostly its just a fictitious name bestowed on a real, now dead person. The play is a barely altered presentation of the case of Ashley Smith, the teenager who strangled herself to death in an Ontario correctional facility, under the eyes of guards forbidden to intervene until the very last moment.

The play consists of alternating monologues for three characters: Glory herself, her adoptive mother, and a correctional officer. The attentiongrabbing feature of Ken Gass production is that all three roles are played by Thompson herself, who trained as an actress but has not to my knowledge appeared on a stage in many years. She still has it; she's more than competent, and often compelling. She doesn't vary her characters very much; they all wear the same blue prison smock (she wouldnt have time to change anyway) and all speak in much the same tone and rhythm. But conceivably thats the point.

The most interesting of the three is Gail, the officer, because she's the only one who's conflicted. She has some sympathetic feelings for the women in her charge; but shes on guard against those same feelings. She doesnt want to be taken advantage of; she also has her orders, orders of which she doesn't always approve. In Glory's case, the conflicts come to a head. Glory has been in custody, at one facility or another and mostly in solitary, for six years; shes now within sight of release. During those last weeks Gail performs a rare act of kindness towards her; Glory takes advantage of it, which leads to her sentence being extended, which leads her to her suicide, strangling herself with a ligature. Gail knows and rails against the pointlessness of the new rules, born of exasperation, that prevent her and her colleagues from entering the cell until its too late for resuscitation attempts to be of any use. She also knows that she will take the blame. At our last sight of her, she's grimly contemplating a new career "as a crossings guard." It's a strangely haunting line.

Equally resonant is the announcement by Glorys mother thatshell never eat veal again: a decision prompted by her having seen the conditions in which the calves are kept, a sight that reminds her all too vividly of her daughter's plight. She also gives us useful information about the apparently harmless act that first caused Glory's incarceration, setting off a chain reaction: Glory in jail keeps acting up so Glory stays in jail. (Can you call her a recidivist if she never got out?) For the most part, though, Glorys mother can only tell us that she wants her daughter back, dreams of their reunion, and is angry at the system thats preventing it from happening. It's sympathetic but it's repetitive.

Glory herself brings out the expected intensity in Thompsons writing and the less expected intensity in her acting. But again we dont learn very much. Glory has fantasies about her mother (presumably her birth mother) as a crocodile; her resentment of what she's going through on a human level is easy to comprehend, self-defeating though it often seems. Dramatically, though, Glory remains as unreachable as her prototype was in real life. In the end the play's effectiveness depends on what it tell us about Ashley Smith, and it doesnt tell us much more than did the news reports at the time. There is an implicit claim, one with which I wouldnt quarrel, that watching Ashley die, at a decent artistic remove, is a worthwhile civic jolt in itself. Astrid Janson has designed a sternly effective set, lit with appropriate rigour by Andre du Toit. Glory inhabits the cell that's its centre. Her two fellow-victims - for that's what they are, but articulate ones, like her - address from outside it. Gass direction is mostly rigorous in keeping each one to her own corner of the stage, so thatwell know immediately who is who, though there's a confusing placement near the end. The show is produced by the Canadian Rep Theatre, founded by Gass after his ejection from the directorship of the Factory Theatre. Meanwhile, back at his former ranch, the new regime has mounted a new play by another Canadian playwriting icon, Daniel MacIvor. Bingo!, billed as a comedy, is an avowed attempt by its author to write something more popular, or maybe that should be populist, than the stark works for which he's best known. It concerns a Cape Breton high-school reunion, 30 years after its characters graduated (or, in one case, failed to) and its title is the victory shout in what seems, to an outsider, a spectacularly pointless drinking game. But then the whole play seems pointless, bearing as it does the startling news that people look back on their younger days with a mixture of fondness and regret, and that some move on, socially and/or geographically, while others dont. Also that they may resent the hell out of one another, and can be very aggressive about it. Nigel Shawn Williams production keeps the proceedings proceeding at a fair if relentless clip. There a couple of nice scenes, both involving John Beale and Sarah Dodd as the two most likable characters, or at least the two who don't shout. This is a low-key work from MacIvor, especially considering that he's given recent proof, in The Best Brothers and A Beautiful View, that he can be funny and compassionate without compromising any of his standards.

Watching Glory Die is at the Berkeley Street Theatre Upstairs; Bingo! at the Factory Theatre, both through June 1.
