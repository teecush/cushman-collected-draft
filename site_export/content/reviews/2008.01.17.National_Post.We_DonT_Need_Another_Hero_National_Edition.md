It seems likely that the Oscars, like the revolution, will not be televised. They will happen but, because of the writers' strike, just as a stage event, as happened in prehistoric times. I think this is defeatist. Why shouldnt the show go on?

My sympathies in the dispute are with the writers. The Oscars, though, are one occasion on which Id happily see the networks roll right over them. Every year I watch the final credits roll and think: All these people got paid -- a lot, presumably -- for writing this mindless, sycophantic rubbish?

This year why not let the actors (it's nearly always actors; outside of actual nominees no-one else is presumed to exist in Hollywood on Oscar night) make up their own presentation speeches? They could hardly do worse. Of course, we'd have to do without all the special tributes, and probably the production numbers that traditionally clutter up the evening, but think of how much earlier wed all get to bed. And how much more happily.

As for a host -- well, I suppose thered still have to be one, but if it's Jon Stewart, he's a stand-up guy and he can make it up as he goes along, or at least memorize his lines without first writing them down, just as we currently have to tell ourselves hes doing on The Daily Show. Im not sure whether the rules would mean sacrificing the interminable red-carpet interviews and sound-bite replies that ritually precede the main event, but let's ditch them anyway, just to be on the safe side. We could end up with a movie celebration that's actually about the movies. Theres a radical notion.

Meanwhile, the imminent disappearance of American drama series, cut off before they could get their stories straight, is supposed to herald a grand opportunity for shows made in Canada. The CBC, in particular, has been jubilant about its new slate of productions.

On the evidence so far, theyre being over-optimistic; their viewing figures may go up, but that in itself won't stop the audience from fondly missing the shows that it's missing. Bottom of the heap at the moment is MVP, which has been shamelessly plugged as Desperate Hockeywives. This is loose labelling, because so far the show has only come up with one wife who could be called desperate, and thats because in the storys opening moments she became a widow. More precisely, it's because she discovered that her husband, the supposed star of his team, had left no insurance and that his team, or at least its snaky manager, was not prepared to shell out to keep her in the style to which she had become accustomed.

By the end of the second episode, she's been reduced to working as a cashier at a gas station where the manager finds himself, one trusts to his embarrassment, facing her across the till. This scene might have seemed more convincing if the camera hadnt made such a big deal of showing us that this was a full-service station, meaning that the manager would have had no reason to enter the office at all.

Otherwise, the focus has been on desperate girlfriends: actual, potential or disposable. There are three principal ice-aces vying for, or trying to avoid, these ladies attentions: one new guy, one bad guy and one guy with no discernible characteristics except that he's the captain and seems intended to be the hero.

The main thing wrong with the show, apart from its plodding pace and slurpy background music, is that it has virtually nothing to do with its ostensible subject. Far be it from me to wish there were more hockey on television, but the characters here could be any spoiled, rich people.

There have been virtually no scenes in the locker room and none at all on the ice. John Ferguson, general manager of the Toronto Maple Leafs, was quoted in yesterday's National Post as saying nothing that goes on outside the game, outside these walls, distracts me or us from our purpose." Well, I suppose he would say that, but the implications sound more dramatic than anything on MVP, whose characters spend all their time partying. We dont have a sense of what the pressures are, where they stand in the league, or who their most dangerous opponents might be.

Its like the Oscars, come to think of it, where all that really matters is who's wearing the best frocks. Or, in this case, who isn't wearing them.

Its also very predictable. We know, from the moment they set eyes on one another, that there are going to be sparks flying between the widows bratty wannabe-a-ballerina daughter and the rookie who's taken over the departed's status, paycheque and house. We can guess that therell be trouble when the young mans small- town girlfriend finally manages to put her grandmother in a home and arrives to claim her share of the high life.

Meanwhile, the skipper pursues a virginal daycare worker while the script trips over itself trying to find reasons to keep them apart. (She's also being wooed by her old English Lit teacher, a far more eligible suitor, even if he does insist on quoting Chaucer in modern translation. And him a scholar, too.)

I was never a fan of the real Desperate Housewives but at least it knew it was ridiculous. MVP, completely devoid of wit, doesn't even have camp appeal. Maybe it isnt trying to be serious, but its certainly solemn. - MVP airs Friday at 9 p.m. on CBC.
