LOOT

Young Centre for the Performing Arts

---

Soulpepper's artistic director says in his program greeting that the two plays his company is currently alternating "couldn't be more different." He's right, in that Clifford Odets' Awake and Sing! and Joe Orton's Loot belong to different worlds, one a socially-conscious clarion call from the American 1930s, the other a disconcertingly cheerful black farce from the British 1960s. One preaches hope, the other offers a choice between despair and acceptance. Odets can sometimes seem confused or at least ambiguous; Orton's anarchic vision is uncompromising and undeviating. But the two plays do have something in common: both were trailblazers in their time that now look less than they've been cracked up to be. And both have been given productions -- one sensitive, the other smooth -- whose merits can't disguise the deficiencies and may even accentuate them.

Jim Warren's production of Loot is buoyed up by two splendid performances. One of them is by Oliver Dennis, who has a habit of playing uninviting roles and discovering things in them that previous actors may never have thought existed. He plays Mr. McLeavy, an ultra-respectable citizen who would regard "law-abiding" as the highest compliment in the English language. In other words, or at least in Orton's words, he's a dupe, a fool and a predestined victim. Mr. McLeavy is in the process of burying his wife, a process hampered by his son's being in league, and in love, with a young local undertaker who doubles as a bank robber. The two hide their latest swag in Mrs. McLeavy's coffin, having first removed the corpse, trussed it up and hidden it -- well, ina successionof locations, one of the rules of farce being that incriminating evidence is always about to be discovered and has to be kept on the move. Most of this, of course, is unknown to the bereaved husband until far too late. In the meantime he finds himself obliged to make a statement to the authorities on what has turned out to be a remarkably ill-fated journey to the church, and slips immediately into the appropriate official mode. The collision of the pseudo-literate with the absurd is Orton at his best. It's also remarkably difficult to perform. Dennis, earnestly informative and revelling in the opportunity to be of assistance, had me in hysterics from the start of his oration ( "We set off in high spirits") to its end. He also brilliantly defines the point at which respectability crosses over into vindictiveness, while his affection for his late wife and reluctance to incriminate his son would, in any other play, be touching. Orton, however, didn't do touching, and Dennis is fastidious about not doing it either. Which, in so likeable an actor, is integrity of heroic proportions.

Mr. McLeavy is a devout Catholic. A lot of the play's jokes are concerned with this, and they mostly fall flat. The assumption seems to be that the Catholic Church is the most sacred of cows and mocking it the ultimate taboo. That isn't the case now, and I doubt if it was then. McLeavy has to have a belief-system to make the play work, and he probably would be religious, but the horse doesn't have to be flogged to death. His concomitant trust in secular authority counts dramatically for far more, and here the play packs a punch that still lands. Michael Hanrahan, in the evening's other exhilarating performance, plays Police Inspector Truscott, who arrives in the McLeavy abode claiming to be from the water board and proceeds to abuse all the rules we might fondly have believed would govern either organization. The verbal association with waterboarding seems almost prophetic, and you may find yourself thanking heaven, or whatever, that Truscott came before Tasers. But he does pretty well without them. "Under any other political system," he says while interrogating young Hal McLeavy, "I'd have you on the floor in tears," to which the hapless Hal accurately replies "you've got me on the floor in tears." Orton had only one joke -- the saying of outrageous things with a straight face --but with Truscott, who bends all regulations without fear of self-contradiction and whose brutality and corruption are matched by his ineptitude as a sleuth, he hit gold. Hanrahan, comic energy under ferocious control, does the same.

Even while being brutalized, neither Hal nor his partner Dennis seems much afraid of being found out. That's a problem with Orton as a farceur; in his world, where everything's amoral, there's no threat. Matthew Edison's Hal (a pretty contrast with his shy role in Awake and Sing!) and Jonathan Watton's Dennis are good without really being convincing, certainly not as a couple. They do, though, deliver their lines with wit and despatch. In fact the only player who fails in this regard is Nicole Underhay, who plays a buxom nurse (also Catholic) anxious to add the widowed Mr. McLeavy to her already impressive list of mysteriously deceased husbands; she has the right seaside-postcard profile but she labours the epigrams, thereby slowing the proceedings considerably.

Neither the play nor its author are dismissible; Orton, unlike the self-satisfied rebels of London's Royal Court, was a real subversive, a working-class gay man who'd done time and ended up being bludgeoned to death by his lover. He had a genuine outsider's vision, one most completely and resourcefully expressed in his next and last play What the Butler Saw. Loot works perfectly on its own terms, but they're narrow terms. One personal complaint. When Truscott discovers Mrs. McLeavy's preserved corpse he asks, "whose mummy is this?" to which Hal replies, truthfully but without thinking, "mine." I always thought that was the funniest line in the play. It isn't here; the timing is off. But that doesn't happen often. - Loot is in repertory at the Young Centre through Aug. 1.
