THEATRE REVIEW

Dying City

Toronto Free Gallery

Parfumerie

Young Centre for the Performing Arts, Toronto

The best play in Toronto can be found in the back room of a small art gallery at Bloor and Lansdowne.

Christopher Shinn is an American playwright whose work is beginning to make inroads in Canada. Dying City had a prestigious Manhattan premiere at Lincoln Center though - not uniquely - it had to be staged in Britain before any producer in its homeland would take a chance on it. Its title is ambiguous or, to put it more invitingly, double-edged. It's used at one point to refer to Baghdad; it could equally well mean New York, which is where the action takes place and which here is still in the shadow, vividly summoned, of 9/11.

We're in the apartment of Kelly, a widowed therapist, who receives a surprise visit from her brother-in-law, a gay actor named Peter. It's especially surprising because a) despite her bereavement, she hasn't seen or heard from him in ages and b) the evening is still young and he's supposed to be on stage in a production of Long Day's Journey into Night, which normally takes quite a long time.

Peter has interesting reasons for taking early retirement from the world of Eugene O'Neill but they aren't the main points at issue. Kelly's husband and Peter's twin brother, Craig, was killed, in exceptionally absurd circumstances, in Iraq. Craig was an English major who put a promising academic career on hold in order to enlist. Just why he did it is an unanswered, perhaps unanswerable, question. There are all kinds of possibilities.

So there are in Peter's relationship with Craig, and in Craig's relationship with Kelly, and in Kelly's relationship with Peter. The play switches between two time frames. In the present tense, Peter vanishes into an offstage bathroom to be replaced a moment later by Craig, taking us back to the night of his final departure. The twins are of course played by the same actor; in this production he's Sergio Di Zio, who alternates two fully realized performances without ever letting one overflow into the other. The quick, and fairly basic, changes of costume matter less than the contrasts in voice, stance, manner and personality.

Craig is forceful, unyielding and at the end of his tether, certainly in his marriage; he's also terminally impatient with his brother, who seems to be juggling partners. Peter, by contrast, seems terribly open - as Di Zio plays him, the confidences seem to spill out of him uncontrollably - but he's holding plenty back, some of it to be revealed at the most hurtful moment possible. Clearly, he adored his late brother and also resented him; the mixed feelings were mutual and, in Peter's case, continuing. Peter also, as you might expect and in his own way, loves Kelly. She's in no state to love anyone.

Lesley Faulkner, who plays her, has probably the harder task; she spends as much time reacting as acting, and carries a load of pain that is only gradually revealed to us. At times it seems to be the actress rather than the character who's impassive; she doesn't come into her own until she's able to unleash some anger, at which points, both past and present, she's very powerful.

In 80 minutes' playing time, the play brings its relationships fully to life, including the one between two people who, for obvious reasons, can never meet. It also summons a whole world, both material and intellectual, beyond the room, the political playing itself out through the personal; it's especially interesting to be reminded how feelings and opinions changed about Iraq. It does all this in dialogue that's never forced and sometimes funny. It's a relief to encounter an American play that isn't soft-centred, emotionally or intellectually Peter Pasyk's direction, on a tiny stage, is first-rate, physically and tonally.

I should, though, qualify my opening sentence; Dying City is the best new play in Toronto. The best allaround, certainly the biggest treat, is Soulpepper's Parfumerie, which has returned with even more wit, point and unsentimental tenderness than it showed two years ago. If your heart has cold cockles, this is where to have them warmed.
