BHOPAL

The Theatre Centre, Toronto

- - -

The story and subject matter of Bhopal are historical, and calculated to make an audience feel angry. The calculation succeeds. The writing and presentation, by contrast, make the audience feel irritated, and that cannot have been the intention.

Rahul Varma's play concerns an industrial and environmental catastrophe that came to a head 20 years ago. The firm of Union Carbide was manufacturing pesticide at a factory in Bhopal, India, using a gas with highly toxic properties. In the surrounding areas, babies and animals were dying. In 1984 there was a gas leak, and an explosion. The resulting death toll is reckoned at 20,000 and rising. Union Carbide's American bosses shut the plant down and offered compensation on a scale that the Indian government, at least initially, thought derisory.

Varma's original intention was to write a documentary play about all this, but over the years of working on it, he gave his imagination freer rein, especially in the matter of inventing characters. So at least I gather from interviews included in the press kit that the management, like most managements, helpfully hands out to the critics. (Please don't stop; we'd be lost without it.) Mere audience members are denied this information, though it would not only be in their interests but in those of the play to have at least some of it included in the program. The spectators certainly deserve to be told that of the seven characters onstage, only one bears the name of an actual person. This is Anderson, CEO of Carbide International, and it can be no coincidence that he, despite being given a performance in which effortful brashness seems to be standing in for brutal confidence, is the most compelling, three-dimensional figure around.

It's to the playwright's credit that he allows even this man, whom he plainly despises, a case. He tries to do the same for the characters he has made up, but paradoxically they seem far more like stick figures, all on hand to represent something, with bits of human interest pasted on.

At least Anderson is history's pawn rather than his author's. The others display all the drawbacks of fact-based drama and none of the advantages. They include a lady doctor (Canadian, presumably for the sake of audience identification) who is on to what is happening and whom the local authorities are trying to silence -- at least until the factory blows up, at which point they decide they need her help. There is a Canadian diplomat, sent to extricate her, and weaseling around between the various factions. The doctor has little use for his services, and the play doesn't seem to need them much either.

The Indians include a senior economics minister whose political tune changes abruptly but inconclusively in the course of the play, and the factory manager, who was educated in the United States and who says that by taking the job, he's helping his people and his country. He probably means it, but once installed, he cuts corners and tries to cover up the results. He's also having an affair with his assistant, whom he gets pregnant.

This woman, named Madiha, emerges -- also abruptly -- as a key figure in the play. She is in violent denial over the prospect of her baby, like those of the slum women around her, being born with appalling defects. She is set against Izzat, who is just such a woman and just such a mother. The doctor wants to send Izzat and her child to the U.S. -- for treatment and as live propaganda. Izzat plays her and the company off against each other, getting what she can from each, though the play's structure is so confusing, jumping around in time and space, that it's hard to keep up with what she wants or at what stage she wants it.

The play eventually zeroes in on these two women, affording the actresses more extended opportunities than are available to anyone else in the cast. But what it offers with one hand, it takes away with another. As Izzat, Yashoda Ranganathan is saddled, among other things, with the convention that obliges any play set anywhere in Asia to begin and end with a woman reciting an inexplicable fable. Imali Perera's Madiha never recovers from the bald, bad writing through which she is required to convey her love for her boss and her hostility toward everybody else. There might be a play in her relationship with her opportunistic lover, but it cannot co-exist with everything else that's trying to go on. Her final gesture is merely that.

A charitable assumption would be that the play doesn't have time to treat all its elements in depth, and so has to resort to shorthand. Anyway, what happens is that the fiction keeps crowding out the facts, though the facts are far more compelling.

I am feeling friendlier than I used to toward "issue" plays. The issues usually have a built-in fascination -- this one certainly does -- and they generally come attached to interesting stories; that's how they got to be issues in the first place. Bhopal never actually forfeits our attention, and it has no trouble making our blood boil over corporate greed and managerial negligence. That, though, is the easy part. It fritters away our goodwill by devising stock confrontations for stick figures, going off at soap-opera tangents and leaving its political points hanging. The current Tiger of Malaya (another issue play with an Asian setting) suffers from some of the same diffuseness, but survives by being the story of a particular man and pushing that story through to its conclusion.

Most of the acting ranges from the overheated to the half-baked, and though Guillermo Verdecchia's production manages a few neat instant transitions in the earlier scenes, it does little to clarify what's going on. On the first night, some of the lighting cues were crazily out of sync; either that or the director has an exaggerated belief in the powers of darkness.
