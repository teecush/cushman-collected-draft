robertcushman@lycos.com; Homechild runs through Jan. 28 at the Bluma Appel, 416-368-3110. Bombay Black runs through Jan. 22 at Theatre Centre, 416-538-0988.

CanStage has a habit of mounting its bigger plays -- bigger, that is, in intellectual and emotional scope, such as The Goat or The Shape of Things -- in its smaller spaces and its smaller plays in its biggest. The latest example of this vice versatility is Joan Macleod's Homechild, a conventional domestic drama set in an Ontario farmhouse surrounded, on the wide stage of the Bluma Appel, by a lot of rocks.

Perched at the back on some of these as the play begins is a very young girl making a speech. What she is talking about is hard to distinguish since the actress (Lara Jean Chorostecki) is required both to shed years and to talk in a Scots accent while addressing the audience from what seems a very long way away, and the combination defeats her.

This gets the evening off to a confusing start from which neither the play nor Martha Henrys production ever really recovers. Its hard to concentrate on Scene Two when you're still trying to come to grips with Scene One, and so on down the line.

The overbearing ruggedness of Astrid Janson's set seems partly prompted by the plays depiction of an isolated way of life that is under present threat, but it may also owe something to a supposed larger, historical theme. I say supposed" because, as so often happens, that theme is more evident in the publicity and the program notes than on the stage. A hundred years ago, give or take, Canadas population was swollen by an influx of British children, dispatched by orphan charities, who were badly treated when they got here. The play is concerned to expose this ancient scandal, but it doesn't get around to it until the evening is almost over.

We're told, finally, about a brother and sister who came over from Scotland and were heartlessly separated. The brother, now 80, is Alistair, who owns the farm and who never talks about his sister but dreams of her constantly; hence, we eventually work out the mysterious prologue. Their story, though, doesnt seem all that terrible in proportion to the amount of time weve been kept waiting to hear about it. Indeed, and rather self-defeatingly, it pales in comparison with what we're told about their lives back home with their mother.

Alistair has a stroke, and his secret comes out, prompting his daughter, Lorna, who left for the city and feels self-conscious about it, to track her aunt down and effect a reunion, even though Alistair protests, bitterly, that it's the last thing he wants. The scenes involving father and daughter are the best, and the best acted, in the play. Eric Petersons Alistair starts out as merely another in his line of cranky old men (this is his third play in succession portraying some kind of invalid and the second in which hes been hooked up to a saline drip) but goes on to have a haunted, touching scene in which he gets to interact with his phantom sibling. He follows it up with a volatile mix of shame and anger that eventually and thrillingly explodes. Brenda Robins, always at her best when not required to play brittle, does an admirably straightforward, caring job on Lorna.

Barbara Gordon, a late arrival, is just as unsentimentally sympathetic, though it's hard to believe in the past she's said to bring with her. Others in a blue-chip cast -- Patricia Hamilton, Randy Hughson, Tom Rooney -- seem to be there merely to kill time as friends and family, though the capacity of Hughson and (especially) Rooney to make passably amusing lines sound like sparkling epigrams is much appreciated. Nor could I see what was added by Joyce Campions role as another old orphan girl, apart from the mystery of why she has kept her (London) accent while her Scots contemporaries have lost theirs. Anecdotally, there is too much going on. Dramatically, there isnt enough.

- - -

Much the same could be said of Anosh Irani's Bombay Black, though the anecdotage here is first-rate. The title refers to a cheap drug, "a mixture of hashish and boot-polish, available but not recommended at the dance parlour that's the plays setting. Its also a fair description of the desperate circumstances of the play's three characters. Apsara is an exotic dancer, Indian-style; that is, she really can dance, with her clothes on, and the customers can't touch. Padma is her mother and, for want of a better word, manager. (The mother-daughter relationship has all the coziness of a cross between Gypsy and The Beauty Queen of Leenane.)

Kamal is a bookseller who comes to see Apsara dance -- oddly, as hes blind. But it turns out the three have a shared village past -- one that explains Kamals blindness -- and the play unfolds partly as a love story, partly as a study in the oldest of all dramatic subjects, the ethics of revenge.

It isn't diffuse like Homechild but it does flag, partly because some scenes go on way too long (especially a couple of chaste erotic fantasies), partly because the script consists almost entirely of duologues.

On the rare occasions when the entire cast is allowed onstage, the author does nothing with them and can hardly wait to get them off again. Nightswimming, the group that developed the play, go on in their program note about having a bold visual style" well, Camellia Koo's set is striking with its carpet and swinging bed, and there is, obviously, dancing. Apsara is good at her job and looks especially terrific at the start, in silhouette. But basically the play is all talk.

It is, however, very good talk: pungent and lyrical and sometimes witty. Line by line, Irani never hits a false note. The actors, however, under Brian Quirts direction, hit several. They are liable to pump feelings into lines that dont need them, or to stonewall in the face of situations that do. Not that they don't have their moments. Anita Majumdar doesn't have the chops, or maybe just the experience, for the emotional assault-course that the play wishes on Apsara, but she can be touching and she can be tough. Sanjay Talwar (recently very funny in The Trials of Judas Iscariot) is sweet but doesnt have the variety to sustain Kamals essentially passive role. Deena Aziz's silky Padma is enliveningly cynical at the start, but when the play calls for passionate extremes, she settles for melodrama. Still, clap hands; there could be a dramatist here.
