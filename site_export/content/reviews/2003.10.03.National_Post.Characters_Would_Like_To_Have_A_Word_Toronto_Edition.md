PLAY MURDER

Buddies in Bad Times Theatre, Toronto

- - -

In four years on this beat, I don't recall seeing a good new play at Buddies in Bad Times. Standards of performance, too, have been, to put it gently, variable. Still, the company is now entering its 25th season, so maybe I've just struck a bad patch. Buddies itself seems to be getting nostalgic for past glories; it is celebrating its anniversary with a revival of a 10-year-old play by Sky Gilbert, founder and former artistic director. This too is bad, but it's bad in a confident way.

Bits of it are even good. Gilbert is a talented nuts-and-bolts playwright, with a first-rate ear; he can set his characters at one another's throats with a few well-chosen lines, and can even show them being convincingly and touchingly affectionate. After every such scene, though, his play dissolves in giggles; maybe he feels he owes it to his image.

Play Murder keeps jumping styles and time-frames, and generally sending itself up. It's about Libby Holman, a real-life torch singer of the 1920s and '30s who was also a minor Broadway star. In the latter capacity she introduced at least two standards, Moanin' Low and Something to Remember You By. In 1931, she married Zachary Smith Reynolds, heir to a Deep South tobacco fortune. The following year, aged 20, Smith, as everybody calls him in the play, was found dead, shot. Libby was widely suspected of his murder, but a grand jury investigation (unmentioned in the play) found insufficient evidence to try her. The official verdict was suicide. Libby went back to performing, but her future career was, understandably, jinxed.

This may not have been solely the result of her tragic past. Her records suggest she was a terrible singer, uncertain of pitch and doomy of manner. (That is not a prerequisite in a torch singer; Helen Morgan was dainty in her suffering.) In her later years, Holman grew pretensions as a folk and blues singer, and took herself very seriously indeed. She seems to have been totally lacking in humour, which in a popular singer is a pretty crippling limitation.

It's also one that Ellen-Ray Hennessy, who plays her here, is blessedly unequipped to present. She is all wit and a lot of sexiness, the latter a quality she can laugh at without losing. (The real Libby certainly looks sultry in photographs, and may well have been more fun off stage than on, but I still can't imagine her laughing at herself.)

Hennessy also has the ability to change mood on a dime, to be savage one moment and pitiable the next. Her Libby is an elongated jazz baby, running on nicotine and alcohol, frustrated by her husband's Southern home, by his disapproval of her career and, it seems, by his impotence. She delivers the play's excellent last speech, in which she sums up her life mordantly but without self- pity, with absolute command.

Libby is in a lesbian relationship that survives, though possibly only as a friendship, into her marriage, something that particularly riles Smith. The play, though, never holds course as an examination of Southern homophobia, or of its racism, a subject even more briefly broached. Somebody suggests that Libby's problem is that she expects life to be like the movies, though there's been no previous mention of this expectation, or indeed of the movies.

As for the lady and her music, Hennessy, who's meant to be drunk at the time, slurs her way incomprehensibly through one of Holman's trademark songs. Before the acts, we hear Bessie Smith, who was pre- Libby, and during the action itself we get bits of songs that are mostly of Holman's period, and one of which, What Is This Thing Called Love, she actually recorded. But it isn't her version we hear. The recordings are unmistakably of the 1950s; Sarah, Blossom, Chet and Mel are among the voices that float by. (So do those of Jane Russell and Marilyn Monroe.)

My first suspicion, which I discarded, was that the author- director couldn't tell the difference and thought, like many, that old songs were just old songs. My second was that he shared my opinion of Libby's singing, and much preferred these later models. My third, desperate but probably accurate, is that the anachronism was intentional and meant to be "commentative." It goes with having the actors step constantly out of character to comment unfavourably on the dialogue they've been given or the dance number they are suddenly required to perform.

Jason Cadieux, who as Smith shares the acting honours with Hennessy, gets a speech -- quite magnanimous, considering -- in which he first laments his position as a straight man surrounded by homosexuals, and then switches to complaining about being a straight actor in a gay play. Gilbert seems to think tricks like this are what make a gay play.

Occasionally -- when Libby steps out of time to remember later conversations with Tennessee Williams or Tallulah Bankhead -- the playfulness justifies itself, but for the most part Gilbert is so busy satirizing his story he never gets around to telling it. To put it another way: There's no point in deconstructing something that was never constructed in the first place.

Much of Act Two is taken up by a jokey reconstruction of the fatal events, volubly resented by the characters as characters and by the actors as actors. The audience, though, is in no position to join in the fun; it's not as if most people will enter the theatre as experts on the case.

I found myself thinking of Blood Relations, the Lizzie Borden psychodrama revived at this year's Shaw Festival, which also takes too much knowledge for granted. For all I know, Gilbert was consciously spoofing Sharon Pollock's play when he wrote this one. But the final impression is that he's sneering -- at his characters, his actors and his audience -- which is unfortunate, because he isn't a sneery writer.

Steve Lucas has designed a multi-chambered set, the grandest I've ever seen on this stage, though sadly underused for most of the evening. Ann Holloway plays Libby's drama coach, a would-be grande dame who recalls being booed when she appeared in The Wild Duck as a senile grandmother (this is not surprising as there is no such character in The Wild Duck). She is atrocious, but she may conceivably be doing it on purpose. Jane Johanson, having been instructed by the text to do terrible choreography, has gone right ahead and done it. She also plays Libby's love interest.

I read recently that even Daniel MacIvor has grown tired of writing plays about plays; it's the best theatrical news I've heard in ages.
