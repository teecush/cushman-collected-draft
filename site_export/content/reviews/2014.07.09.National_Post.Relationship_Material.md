THEAT REREVIEW

Company

Berkeley Street Theatre Downstairs

In a way that's unique among musicals, the action of Company takes place in a split second. Robert, New York bachelor just turned 35, imagines a birthday party thrown for him by the five married couples who count themselves his best friends. Its a strange party, since the people giving it dont seem to know one another and the guest of honour never actually shows up.

At least he doesn't always. At different points of the show, he envisions the party three different ways. At the first he's a happy participant; at the second a disillusioned participant; and at the third a nonparticipant. In conjuring up these dubious celebrations Robert (also addressed as Bobby, Bob, Robbie even - God forgive them - Robbo), starts taking stock of his life, which seems mainly to consist of dropping in on his friends marriages and contemplating them with various degrees of incredulous amusement. We see him with them, and also with three representative girlfriends who lament his emotional, as opposed to physical, unavailability. Finally, something explodes in his head, and he decides to stop living vicariously and to embark on Being Alive: that being the title of the climactic song in Stephen Sondheims vertiginously brilliant score. Being alive doesn't necessarily mean being married, though the show up to this point has been so focused on marriage that it's hard to take it any other way. But it does seem to mean being involved with someone ("alone is alone, not alive") and taking responsibility.

In its day, 1970, Company was a controversial show, though the discomfort it aroused seems to have had more to do with its tone than with its structure. Sondheims lyrics, unerringly focused on social foibles and human weaknesses, had probably the greatest concentration of wit of any musical up to that time, and the music was thought to be disconcertingly harsh and angular when it wasnt being disconcertingly beautiful.

In Theatre 20's new production, The Company song is a traditional showstopper with a twist: Bobby's friends express their devotion; he, leading the dance, suddenly realizes he has no place in it. Marc Kimmelmans choreography, here and throughout, is both slick and characterful.

The Berkeley Street space has always been acoustically unfriendly but other musicals (Light in the Piazza, Caroline or Change) have overcome it. Ken MacDonalds Company set leaves the stage open to its brick walls, swallowing up too many of the sung words and even some of the spoken ones.

The solo numbers, though, come off intact, especially Dan Chameroy's in the lead, and this is where the production importantly delivers. George Furth's book derives from his own series of one-act plays, each involving a different couple and an observing friend. The musical rolls the onlookers into one, leaving the built-in problem of a displaced protagonist. Its been addressed over the years by some rewriting, notably by reinstating the cut song Marry Me a Little, which, in its new halfway position, shows us Bob fighting angrily against what he doesnt want to believe. Chameroy, whose emotional range these days just keeps expanding, keeps the baffled anger going into Being Alive, where it finally explodes and transforms.

Anger, too, informs Louise Pitre's magnificent delivery of the showpiece "The Ladies Who Lunch." It's harder than it used to be to equate a collection of well-heeled Manhattanites with the Human Condition, but we do get a wide and talented spread here; Brent Carver and Nora McLellan as the karate couple with their competing addictions (his alcoholism rather halfheartedly presented, compared to her gluttony), Steven Sutcliffe and Nia Vardalos excellent as the novice pot-smokers. Outside the marital circles, Marisa McIntyre is delightful as April, the flight-attendant whom Robbo takes to bed. Their morning-after duet is one of Company's paradoxes; in a non-narrative show, it tells a complete story of its own. It also has maybe the funniest, and most logical, opening line (Where you going? "Barcelona") of any song in any musical. And then, later on, one of the funniest lines of any kind Sondheim ever wrote. The line is "June". You have to be there.

Company runs through July 13 at the Berkeley Street Theatre Downstairs.
