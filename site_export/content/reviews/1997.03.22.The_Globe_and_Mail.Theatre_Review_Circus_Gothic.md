CIRCUS GOTHIC

THEATRE REVIEW

CIRCUS GOTHIC

Saturday, March 22, 1997

Kudelka's many faces spin heads

Special to The Globe and Mail


  


JAN Kudelka is a lavishly accomplished actress who ran away to join a circus. She interrupted a promising career in conventional theatre to enroll in a classical clowning class, on the intelligent theory that learning to be a clown would make her a better actress.

Some weeks into the class, the teacher informed his students that they would be hitting the road and the sawdust. They were joining an American big-top troupe on a trek through the Maritimes. This show is her solo re-creation of the experience: A tale, theoretically at least, of three worlds colliding -- the circus professionals, the earnest novices and the audience.

Kudelka, it seems, can do anything. She can stand on a stage and be a still centre of energy. She can tell jokes, with minimum effort and maximum point. She can sing, melodiously and expressively. She can do tricks; at one point, out of nowhere, her face suddenly sprouts a red nose.

She can suggest any number of characters and switch instantaneously from one to another. Her own speaking voice is warm and dark, and physically she is somewhere between plump and compact. When she's playing herself, her tousled enthusiasm with a tart topping sometimes recalls Julia Louis-Dreyfus, Seinfeld 's Elaine.

There are, of course, different kinds of clowning. Kudelka's teacher, the late Richard Kosinko, used masks to release the inner clown, and hence the inner self. The circus types, not surprisingly, found this very high-flown and irrelevant and shocked their new apprentices by demanding slapstick.

It's an interesting battle (one in which my money is firmly on the Philistines), but it's never really pursued. For most of the show, the circus is on parade: the most unforgettable characters the author ever met, all briefly remembered. We learn of the tensions between performers and roustabouts, who, when young love tries to cross the lines, might just as well be Montagues and Capulets. We encounter stony-faced New Brunswickers trying to make the circus leave town, or their drunken partners taunting the acts from the sidelines. We spend a lot of time with Elly the elephant, one of Kudelka's most sympathetic assumptions, swaying gently from side to side as she bemoans the lack of water in her life: "Rain is no good -- you don't get soaked, you just get moist."

This is the second monodrama I have encountered in two days. Kudelka avoids some of the autobiographical pitfalls that beset George Seremba in Come Sweet Rain . For one, she isn't much of a presence in her own story, and when she wants to talk about herself she goes into standup comedy, a separate discipline in which she seems thoroughly accomplished.

Her story, though, is not as moving and compelling as Seremba's. In fact, I'm not sure it exists. She's a more resourceful performer, but she falls victim to her own facility. As she switches nimbly from one role to another, variety imposes its own monotony. We never feel we've met any of these people; they're snapshots in an album or butterflies daintily impaled behind glass. I wanted to get some actors in, cast them, and see if we might have a play.

She doesn't have a point of view, only the idea of one: something about "the rhythm of the circus is disaster." Mayhem does strike in the end, and it's quite powerfully conveyed, at least on a technical level. (Like Seremba, Kudelka gets invaluable support from her musician, Cameron Shook, who plays xylophone, guitar, musical glasses, drum and a variety of pipes.) But underneath it's just the dreaded spirit of Pagliacci descending again. It seems a waste: Jan Kudelka is too clever to be messing around with brokenhearted clowns.


  


Circus Gothic is running at Artword Theatre in Toronto until March 30.
