The tale of the Trojan war is the best tale ever told. It's being told again in Tantalus, a nine-play theatrical project that had its premiere last Saturday in Denver, Colorado, as a single 12-hour performance. Less than a great production, it is still a great and necessary event; and not just because of its length. Greek myth and Greek drama capture, as nothing else ever has, the ineluctable tendency of things to go wrong; nowhere else are the cruelty, the glory and the sheer capriciousness of human existence so starkly and vividly presented.

Tantalus is about war, and about human efforts, generally unsuccessful, to avert or contain both public and private violence. It is also about story-telling, and about acting, and it keeps reaching back into the pre-history of the events it depicts. In similar vein, one might outline the pre-history of this production. Forty years ago in England, director Peter Hall founded the Royal Shakespeare Company; he called on his friend John Barton, a Cambridge academic, to help him run it. Twenty years later Barton, still at the RSC, directed The Greeks, which told the Trojan story - - the war, its beginnings, and its murderous aftermath -- through abridged and altered versions of various Greek tragedies. About the same time Hall, now at the National Theatre, mounted a far more respectful production of The Oresteia by Aeschylus, one of the plays on which Barton had drawn for his opus. Barton spent much of the next 20 years writing Tantalus, covering the same ground he had traversed in The Greeks, but this time in his own words. Hall undertook to direct it (in collaboration with his son Edward) and the RSC to produce it, but they couldn't raise the finances. At which point Donald Seawell, founder of the Denver Theater Center and an old RSC patron, stepped in, to ask Hall how much he needed. Six million dollars? Seven? "Eight," said Hall, "to be on the safe side." So the money was provided; and the show became a co- production of Denver and the RSC, with actors from both countries. It has been in rehearsal for six months.

During this time the old colleagues' relationship itself went tragically Greek. Barton's original text (now published) ran to 12 plays, and he wasn't happy with Hall's demands for cuts. He walked off the show halfway through rehearsals and will be seeing it for the first time this weekend. This is doubly ironic, because Barton himself has been in the past a ferocious cutter-and-paster of other people's work; and because the show on the Denver stage has far more in common with his own previous work than with Hall's -- it is playful and trans-historical like Barton's The Greeks, rather than austere and ceremonial like Hall's The Oresteia. Hall has retained his attachment to masks, but this time they are not formal tragic masks but light coverings moulded to the actors' faces.

Tantalus himself -- whom the gods famously chained up with food and drink just out of his reach -- does not appear, though he is the ancestor of many who do. His story is told to us in fragments that are appropriately tantalizing, This is typical of the plays' strategy. The best-known Trojan-war texts are Homer's Iliad (which covers less than one-tenth of the 10-year siege) and his Odyssey; the action of the former is covered here in four lines and the latter in one. After them would come The Oresteia, whose events are likewise left offstage, and casually summarised at odd moments. Barton fills the space instead with less familiar narrative, drawn from the obscurer plays of Euripides and from cycles of epic poetry that survive only in fragments. A narrator-figure calls the result the "Saga of the Lost Bits." And if the bits sometimes contradict each other, that only reflects human inconsistency, especially with the unpredictable Greek gods in control. (They, unlike the Judeo- Christian God, signify how things are, rather than how we would like them to be.) Helen, we learn, may not even have gone to Troy; an insubstantial lookalike may have taken her place while she herself was spirited away to Egypt for the duration. The Greek general Agamemnon becomes an agonized liberal, trying to do the right and merciful thing and regularly failing. The pragmatic, unprincipled Odysseus probably does less harm; he is decent when he can afford to be, which isn't often. The great Achilles is an irredeemable boyish thug, his son a possibly redeemable one. The women, regularly raped, sold either into marriage or into slavery, survive as best they can, and get quite good at it.

The play begins, as did The Greeks, with the words "Who is to blame?" Each character might answer, "everyone else." The play itself perceives a deep, ineradicable flaw in human nature which means, paradoxically, that they may all be innocent.

Barton writes in short verse lines which are usually pungent, sometimes beautiful, and always compelling; they have the priceless ring of inevitability. His script is something between a tour-de- force and a masterpiece. Its weakness is that it often resolves into mere debate (not the same thing as the Greek convention of leaving violence offstage). The most powerful of the nine segments is one that cleaves closely to Euripides' great refugee drama, The Trojan Women. Some of Hall's cuts leave loose ends, but he was right to doubt the viability of Barton's prologue and epilogue; he has improved on the former, which now presents a group of modern girls (cultural tourists) being drawn into the action by a huckster- storyteller and becoming its chorus.

The production's greatest weakness is its lack of visual style. There are fine elements in the design (by a Greek, Dionysis Fotopoulos): watery entrances for the sea-nymph Thetis, flames that leap from the ground, the Trojan Horse represented by giant trundling wheels. But the costumes mix ancient and modern without much wit, and there are some devices that are momentarily effective but make no overall sense. Why do Agamemnon and Cassandra remove their masks for a love scene? Why is the Trojan king Priam on stilts? Greg Hicks, an English actor I have admired in the past, weakens both his Agamemnon and his Priam by adopting a monotonous singsong, but he has veteran compatriots who do very well: David Ryall playing an assortment of buffoonish kings (one of them making an intrepid final exit underwater), Alan Dobie as a chillingly blunt Odysseus, Ann Mitchell a doggedly crazed Hecuba. But the best are the American women: Annalee Jeffries as Clytemnestra and Andromache, Alyssa Bresnahan as Cassandra and Thetis, And we feel, in our minds if not in our eyes, the force of the play's central image: a rock that hovers over Tanatalus and may crush all humanity if it gets above itself, or maybe if it sinks below itself. It's no wonder dramatists and directors keep going back to the Greeks. They saw, and foresaw, everything.
