GOODBYE, PICCADILLY

Red Barn Theatre,

Jacksons Point

- - -

The Red Barn Theatre, north of Toronto on the shores of Lake Simcoe, has been operating since 1949, which makes it Canadas longest-running professional house. It has atmosphere as well as history. It's a link to the largely vanished days of summer stock, though these days it casts its shows individually, rather than maintaining a company for a season. In recent years it has neither received nor invited much critical attention. Its current artistic director, Jordan Merkur, seems to want to change this; and he has lured us in with a fairly new play (meaning a play not yet seen in Toronto) by an experienced television writer, Douglas Bowie.

Goodbye, Piccadilly is an entertaining and touching domestic drama with a misleading title. Half of its characters are Canadian and half British, and those from over there are paying an extended visit over here, but though the play is indeed about a culture clash, it isn't one thats primarily expressed in national terms. There is -- or, until just before the action starts, there was -- Brick, a long-serving mayor and general public benefactor, who has just been awarded the Order of Canada. He has a wife, Bess, and a daughter, Bobbie, who is 47, thrice-married and childless. Bess is on the phone, filling in her friends and the audience on her current circumstances, when she gets another call, informing her that her husband has died of a heart attack, in London. This is doubly shocking to her since she thought he was still in Canada, on a week- long canoe trip; hes been taking one at this time every year.

More surprises are in store. There appear from England Kitty, who claims she had an affair with Brick when he was serving there during the war, and Cecil, their son, a forever-young middle-aged man. A video, recorded by Brick himself, confirms their story. He has of course been seeing his English family once a year, for a week at a time. (It's amazing that he got away with it, but then most of it happened in the days before cellphones.)

Bess, an unyielding woman of the old stock, still refuses to believe that the pair are anything other than gold diggers. And -- though she's the only one who wants to fight it -- battle is joined.

Brick and Bess run a country inn, and Brick has bequeathed his English family a half-interest in it. Collaboration being impossible, the place is split in two very separate halves; the Bess- Bobbie sector continues to offer plain fare and hearty outdoor activities, while Kitty and especially Cecil go in for duck stew and evenings with Oscar Wilde. As the inn turns out to be such a focal point, its a serious weakness in the play that its existence hardly registers during the entire first act. It makes much of the second half look arbitrary. The counterpointed vignettes of rival hotel management are funny to begin with but they quickly become tiresome. Cecils Wilde routine is barely believable, and Bobbie's attempt both to puncture and to rescue it with a Fawlty Towers imitation is flat-out incredible.

But there are other things the play does very well. If I was gobsmacked by the baldness of the exposition, I have to admit it gets the job done, and very efficiently. The growing rapprochement between Bobbie and Cecil -- the aging younger generation -- is cunningly charted, and the second act brings further revelations from Cecil and his mother that are movingly written and delivered, and that deepen the issues with which the play is concerned. It's pushed too hard in the closing scenes, whose arteries get clogged with sugar and reconciliation, but the point is made that though blood may well be thicker than water, there are other human bonds more potent than either.

The play is that recurring phenomenon in the Canadian theatre, a good commercial piece with no commercial theatre to go to. The leading non-commercials in Toronto, with the likely exception of CanStage, would think it beneath them, though it might just -- by virtue of a Second World War flashback at its start -- scrape under the wire at the Shaw Festival.

Merkurs production features a unit set that cannot accommodate all the demands made upon it. Some exterior scenes involving rock climbing are especially awkward (and not especially necessary). The acting is uneven, though the springing of the plays main surprise - - the high point of the evening -- is beautifully handled by all concerned.

Brick, though dead, appears on stage, both in his testamentary video and as a commentator and observer. Reg Dreger's performance, however, falls short of the stature that all his survivors keep claiming for him. (Imagine this role played by someone like David Fox. It could transform the play. It could also, of course, capsize it.) Diane Hollingsworth's Kitty keeps being shunted to the side of the action and she looks excessively dowdy, especially considering Bess dismissive description of her as "a showgirl. (She did, we are told, have a brief shot at singing for the troops). But she sounds absolutely right, very caring and considerate, modestly dignified, generous in anger, worn out but not worn down.

Cecil initially seems to be composed of gay cliches; apart from the Wilde and the cooking, hes a devotee of musicals, though he draws the line at what he calls the dreaded Andrew Lloyd." As that phrase shows, Bowie has a very good ear, verbal and musical, though he seems to me inconsistent in making Cecil a fan of Les Mis. Anyway, partly by working through the stereotypes and partly by working around them, he brings the character -- faultlessly embodied by the actor, Stuart Dowling -- to quirky, solid life.

We get a far stronger sense of what life was like for Kitty and Cecil, during their one week a year with Brick and their 51 weeks without him, than of what it was like for Bess and Bobbie with the proportions reversed. It seems to me, too, that the effect could have been much the same had Brick found his secret life in Toronto or Montreal. Bess would have had just as much to be hurt by and to condescend to.

The balance, as I may have hinted, is tilted against her. We can tell, if we think about it, that she has been seriously hurt, but what we actually see and hear is a snobbish, unyielding woman behaving foully to some perfectly nice people. Brick, you come to feel, must really have been a brick to have stuck with her. Nonnie Griffin's performance, though perfectly competent, does little to modify the impression. Arlene Mazerolle looks far too young for Bobbie, whose chances of future childbearing are said to be remote, though I gather that in fact she is exactly the same age. This may mean that we should reconsider our preconceptions about the relationship of age to appearance, but I doubt if this particular play is the one in which to try out such radical notions.
