It was Theodore Roosevelt who said "I don't go so far as to say that the only good Indians are dead Indians, but I believe nine out of 10 are": a statement that's not only hateful but violently ungrammatical. (Nine out of 10 are good? Nine out of 10 are dead? He's unlikely to have meant either of those things.) Roosevelt's statement has been adopted, and adapted, by the Turtle Gals Performance Ensemble for the title of their new show, The Only Good Indian..., subtitled There's No Indians Like Show Indians...

Teddy himself is a character in the show; when he isn't outright vilifying native Americans, he and his wife are taking them to the White House to provide exotic entertainment. He also has an ongoing and not especially friendly relationship with Buffalo Bill Cody who keeps asking him for money. Cody, of course, was one of the founding fathers of the business there's no business like, and his Wild West shows were trailblazers in the matter of exhibiting and exploiting Indian performers. The experiences of four actual Indian writers, musicians and actresses from the 19th and early 20th centuries are here juxtaposed with those of a contemporary quartet being screen- tested for roles in movies. The man they're auditioning for is invariably the spitting image of Buffalo Bill.

The message seems to be that nothing changes. That, though, is a hard point to get across without falling into monotony yourself, and this piece keeps saying the same things. The four Turtle Gals onstage are Falen Johnson, Cheri Maracle, Jani Lauzon and Michelle St. John; they also wrote the show, along with Monique Mojica. The director is Yvette Nolan. The script, we are told, is largely drawn from the writings and utterances of the historical women themselves. There are also songs, not all of them of native origin; I'm still trying to work out what Music! Music! Music! is doing there. On the other hand, the inclusion of two songs from Annie Get Your Gun (one of them parodied) is a clear case of ironic justice.

The foursome do some lovely a cappella harmonizing. As actresses, they play women far better than they play men; their Bill and Teddy are not just caricatures but clumsy ones. They also do far better as the women of the present than of the past, who don't come with enough context to be compelling.

The movie-test scenes are far more immediate; the players are drawing, presumably, on their own experiences that seem, in their seesawing mix of companionship and competitiveness, to be the same as those of most other performers, only more so. Their main complaint is that they're being used, still, as sex objects; I can't say that I've noticed that in recent films to be more the case with native women than with non-native ones (except that the natives get less work anyway) but I defer to their first-hand knowledge. The world, showbiz and other, does seem to be defined here in terms of Indian women versus white men, which does leave a lot out: Indian men, for example. The show is better-humoured than I've probably made it sound, with the performers' own talents making the strongest part of their case.

From Turtle Gals to Theaturtle, the sole visible proprietor is Alon Nashman. This gifted actor is director, designer and narrator of The Snow Queen, a dramatical-musicalchoreographical presentation of the story by Hans Christian Andersen. This, when I was child, always struck me as grimmer than Grimm; its habitat, physical and spiritual, is Arctic. Shards of glass from a black-magic mirror provoke moral blindness in any eye they may fall into; one such victim being the boy Kay who falls under the spell of the evil Queen of the title. Kay's devoted sister, Gerda, travels the snowscape in search of him. The story must have given C. S. Lewis several hints for The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe, but his story is livelier; Andersen's, despite a kind of happy ending, is bleak. Nashman tells it well, though he's best when it gives him specific roles to play, notably that of a helpful crow. The Tokai String Quartet play a nerve-sharpening musical score composed by Patrick Cardy. The dance element is supplied by Kate Alton, whose scattered appearances as the Snow Queen don't add very much.

Turtle Gals, Theaturtle, Sweeney Turtle. Well, perhaps not; but Sweeney Todd, now with a new and improved leading man, has points of contact with The Snow Queen. The musicians in the latter show occasionally lean in to become part of the action; in John Doyle's production of Sweeney the actors, as is well known, are the musicians, and there is often a consonance between who they are and what and how they play. Moreover, the new Sweeney, Alexander Gemignani (he was Beadle Bamford when this production was in New York), is a musician by birthright, son of Stephen Sondheim's regular conductor. He's confined here, Sweeney not being much of a communicator, to a few blasts on the trumpet; vocally, he lacks the bravura to bring off the character's fall into madness, but he is excellent in the earlier scenes, the very picture of dour, impassive vindictiveness. I'd never registered before just how sparely and skilfully Hugh Wheeler's book (from a play by Christopher Bond) dovetails with Sondheim's score. Lauren Molina's dotty and faithful Johanna and Benjamin Magnuson's ingenuous Anthony are as good now as they were in New York. The show as a whole, with Gemignani's Sweeney and Judy Kaye's superlative Mrs. Lovett, is better than it was in New York.
