THEATRE REVIEW

Bring It On: The Musical Ed Mirvish Theatre, Toronto

One thing I learned from Bring It On: The Musical is that in U.S. high schools, cheerleading has become an end in itself. Maybe I should have known that already; all the same, it was a shock to find that the batontwirling and formation-forming has no connection to the sport that it's meant to support. I suppose that sport is football, but for all anyone actually says it could be tiddlywinks. Whatever it is, it could hardly be more fiercely adversarial than the cheerleading itself, whose teams compete against one another at regional and national levels. Ask not for whom the leaders cheer, they cheer for themselves. And they want you to join in.

Competitive cheerleading is not the deepest of dramatic subjects, and Bring It On - based, loosely, on a series of movies - is not the deepest of shows. It is, to be frank, shallow, but it turns out to be an enjoyable kind of shallow. The writers have pedigree. The book is by Jeff Whitty (Avenue Q), the music is split between Tom Kitt (Next to Normal) and Lin-Manuel Miranda (In the Heights), while the lyrics are part Miranda and part Amanda: Amanda Green, that is, who has no famous shows in her resume but is the daughter of Adolph Green who, with his collaborator Betty Comden, personified all that is best in the more urbane traditions of Broadway and Hollywood.

The show concerns two schools in the same area. Truman High is suburban in feel and uniformly pale in complexion. Jackson High is innercity and predominantly black. Truman seems to be defined by its cheerleaders' squad. Jackson doesn't know from squads; it has a crew. Hence, conceivably, the divided score, with Miranda on previous evidence handling the hip hop and Green and Kitt the rest. However it worked out, the music is lively if not classic and the lyrics are smart. I especially liked the peace offering of Danielle (crew) to Campbell (squad), which goes something like: "At first I thought you were a white, stuck-up bitch; now I just think you're white."

Campbell, who has dreamed all her life of captaining a winning squad, has achieved that eminence at Truman: the captaining part anyway. Just as she's about to approach the winning stage she's transferred, courtesy of a sudden rezoning decision, to Jackson, along with a misfit contemporary named Bridget, who had never got closer to the squad than being team mascot in a fat suit.

Campbell, impressed by the crew's moves, urges them to apply their talents by forming a squad and, after some misadventures, succeeds, as do they. Campbell is partly influenced by the suspicion that her transfer from Truman had been engineered by Eva, who has become captain despite having been only third in line when Campbell herself departed.

We are left in doubt for some time as to whether Campbell's theories are justified or whether she's just a spoiled kid with paranoia. The second possibility would be more interesting and plausible but - well, I mustn't give it away. I will say that the device by which the truth about Eva is revealed is beyond incredible, but it does give rise to an entertainingly hysterical song in which she tells how she managed to lower one rival's grades while infecting the other with mono - oh dear, I think I have given it away.

The show communicates several messages. One is that it's all right to be manipulative, as long as you're the heroine. Another is that if you're entering a high-level cheerleading contest, it helps to have spectacular gymnastic and choreographic routines that look as if they've been staged by a first-rate Broadway professional, which both schools of course have.

Andy Blankenbuehler, the choreographer responsible, is also the director, and he has done a sharp job on a mobile set mostly composed of lighting gantries. There are lively and likeable performances from Taylor Louderman (Campbell), Adrienne Warren (Danielle), Elle McLemore (Eva) and Ryann Redmond (Bridget).

Campbell also gets a romantic subplot that may be the most perfunctory on record. Her gradual acceptance of, and by, her new surroundings is, however, very well handled, and are eventually quite stirring; I'm not a great one for inspirational musicals but in this show the racial and social tensions and harmonies feel organic rather than - as, in say, Hairspray - sanctimoniously imposed.

The show also has an attractive vein of self-mockery that stops way short of being selfdestructive. It's also the first show to open at what used to be the Canon since it was renamed the Ed Mirvish: an honour well deserved, though I wish they'd gone the whole route and called it Honest Ed's Theatre.
