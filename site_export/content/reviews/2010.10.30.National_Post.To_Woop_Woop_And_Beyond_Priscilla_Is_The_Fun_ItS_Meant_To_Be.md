As you may have heard, Priscilla Queen of the Desert doesn't have a title character, it has a title bus. In this vehicle three Australian drag queens, Tick, Bernadette and Felicia, make their way from Sydney, where they live, to Alice Springs, where they have a booking. It's also where Tick, who's having a Cage aux Folles type of storyline, hopes to be reunited with his son, though in this case, the son is not a young adult but an adoring six-year-old.

It's a long way from Sydney to Alice -- 3,000 kilometres according to the program -- and there's not a lot that a stage show, as opposed to the movie that Priscilla originally was, can do to show us the variations in the territory between. The authors, Stephan Elliott and Allan Scott (adapting their own screenplay), settle for showing us signposts bearing such outlandish, or outbackish, names as Broken Hill, Woop Woop (invented) and Coober Pedy (real) and having the characters make jokes about them. Three times, our odd triple get off the bus to patronize a local bar and three times they hit trouble. The level of hostility they encounter, and the strength of their reaction to it, increases each time. At one point the bus gets defaced by hillbilly gay bashers, and has to be spray-painted new. A musical has to pass the time somehow. Still, all this stopping and bar-hopping gets to feel repetitive.

That the show stays triumphantly on the road is due to performances that are excellent, costumes that are splendiferous and musical numbers that are, if nothing else, lively. The production, already a big success in Australia and Britain, is trying out in Toronto prior to Broadway, and its cast is mainly American, with a couple of token Canadians and one hold-over from down under. (The script is full of lines like that but not nearly as mild.) The Aussie is Tony Sheldon, and he's here because he is, in all probability, irreplaceable. He plays Bernadette, a post-op transsexual whose glory days as headliner in a troupe called Les Girls are now far in the past, and who, at the start, is about to bury her--pronouns are tricky in this context -- late, or latest, stud. She's brave and sharp-tongued and vulnerable, a king of queens, and in the course of the journey she finds true love. Sheldon plays her with superb comic and pathetic timing, has one great spoken moment of surprise basso profundo and goes through his numbers with physical and vocal panache, shot through with delicacy. This is his show.

It comes damn near being Will Swenson's as well. He plays Tick, the so-to-speak straight man of the group; he too is both a flamboyant musical performer and a strong, sensitive and sensible actor. Against all odds (and the production, towards the end, stacks them pretty high), he establishes his love for his son as moving and unsentimental. Nick Adams, the final member of the star trio, suffers from the fact that Felicia, real name Adam and with the pop version of a Madonna complex, is in his unrelenting bitchiness nearly as irritating as the other two keep saying he is, but he's technically fine and he gets his touching moments, too. Near-equal billing goes, deservedly, to C. David Johnson as a gruff but tender mechanic whom they encounter in the desert, just when they and the show need him the most, and who is bowled over at meeting a former Les Girl. On the first night Johnson, one of Canada's own, endeared himself even further by the unflappable way in which he greeted a malfunctioning piece of scenery that effectively truncated his one and only song while bringing the whole event to a temporary halt. Way to get the audience on your side, show: seriously. The only other homeboy on the team is the redoubtable Thom Allison, so underused that I failed even to spot him.

This is a jukebox musical, using pre-existing songs. There's no good reason for this but, considering what most new scores are like these days, especially for shows like this, it may not be such a bad decision. It even includes one real song, A Fine Romance, lip-sunk by Sheldon, reprised by Johnson, together with a few almosts, like I Say a Little Prayer and the shamelessly irresistible I Will Survive, here rather cheekily used to send the audience on its way. The rest is mostly disco and sub-disco. There's a great moment when Tick sings "someone left the cake out in the rain" which someone has in fact just done, though it's spoiled when he proceeds to do MacArthur Park in what seems like its entirety. Admittedly, he does it accompanied by a chorus dressed as cupcakes, which is something to see. So are all of Tim Chappel's and Lizzy Gardiner's costumes, which range from giant Motown mock-ups via animated paintbrushes to a cross-section of Australian wildlife. Simon Phillips' production may not always be tasteful but it's certainly spectacular. It's also good-hearted. The program lists what may be a record number of producers, from Bette Midler to David Mirvish, with 18 people or entities in between. They obviously took their duties seriously. Or, as C. David observed during that opening-night snafu, "they spent a lot of money on the bus."
