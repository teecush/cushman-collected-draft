The National Theatre of Great Britain is going through what's generally agreed to be a golden age. It's lasted for "nine, nearly 10 years now," which is how long, by his own count, artistic director Nicholas Hytner has been in office.

His success has been twofold; he has directed a long line of outstanding productions himself, while presiding as an impresario over a comparable number of successes staged by others. Some shows in both categories have gone international. His production of Alan Bennett's The History Boys, which I caught on Broadway just before it swept the 2006 Tonys, was the most enjoyable evening - actually it was an afternoon - I have spent in a theatre in years.

More recently, his production of One Man, Two Guvnors, an update of the Italian comedy The Servant of Two Masters, has also conquered New York; its star, Jamie Corden, just won the Tony for best actor. Also from the National Theatre stable is War Horse, which at the moment seems to be everywhere, including Toronto; it was directed by Marianne Elliott, a comparative newcomer whose track record at the National is second only to that of Hytner himself.

I recently spent a week in London, during which I saw three of Hytner's own National Theatre productions and wrangled an interview with the man. Rumours have been circulating of his impending resignation; he would only say, smiling, that they were "exaggerated." He did allow, though, that he would be staying long enough "to see the renovations out."

The priority here, he says, is making the National Theatre "building closer to the river, rather than just a concrete fortress, facing Waterloo Bridge." The opening up of the South Bank of the Thames, from the National and the Royal Festival concert hall eastwards to the Globe Theatre, is the best thing to have happened to the city in years. It's a cultural wonderland that, Hytner says, "happened organically."

Making the National seem more fully part of it involves the closing for one year of the Cottesloe, the smallest of the building's three stages. When it reopens, Hytner wants it to be "more flexible - somewhere where you can put things on for just a couple of weeks." His own past policy for the Cottesloe has been "to put a moratorium on the great classic plays - before that, they'd been doing a lot of Shakespeare and Chekhov there. Actors were being seduced by doing great plays in small spaces."

This is hardly surprising, but Hytner thinks the place for the major plays is on the National's major stages - the platform Olivier and the proscenium Lyttelton. I remarked that in Stratford, Ont., the riskier Shakespeare works have been confined to the Tom Patterson theatre, our equivalent of the Cottesloe, eliciting from Hytner the transatlantic injunction, "Don't! The more fearful you are, the more conservative your audiences become."

The miracle of NT Live, beamed into your local multiplex, has demonstrated Hytner's own prowess at mounting big plays in big spaces; these have included Racine's Phedre, with Helen Mirren in the lead, and Hamlet, with the young Rory Kinnear, one of the few Hamlets who have actually moved me.

NT Live also brought us One Man, Two Guvnors with its original cast, the one that's appearing now in New York. In London, where the show's transferred to the West End, I saw the take-over cast. They aren't as unforced as their predecessors, but the show is still uproariously funny. Richard Bean, one of Britain's leading comedy writers, has transferred Carlo Goldoni's original to the seaside town of Brighton on the cusp of the 1950s, a time and place apparently rife with small-time gangsters and boasting - a dazzling new idea for the time - a pub serving food. This is the scene of the legendary set piece in which the hungry two-timing servant serves dinner simultaneously to both his employers, here a dangerous love-struck crook and an upper-class wonder.

In both story and spirit, the show is remarkably faithful to Goldoni; it's heartwarming that, nearly 400 years later, he should be a commercial success on two continents and on his own terms.

The idea was Hytner's: "I wanted to program something purely entertaining. I also wanted to bring back Jamie Corden." Corden had been one of the original History Boys, but one of the less-noticed. Corden had come back to Britain and written his own TV series, which Hytner describes as "very warm and human, not contemptuous." Those qualities infuse his performance in this show, and they're shared by his London successor, Owain Arthur.

Back at the South Bank base, I saw two more Hytner productions, both new plays with Russian settings.

The less substantial of the two was Travelling Light, by Nicholas Wright. The work is concerned with the birth of film, hence the too-clever title, and is mostly set in a shtetl; it's a surprise to enter a theatre expecting to encounter old Hollywood and to find yourself looking at what might be the set for Fiddler on the Roof.

The main action is framed by Hollywood scenes; an expatriate director is shooting a movie about how he got his start back in the old country, and there's magic in the way in which one period and location melt into another. I found it beguiling, if hardly major.

Collaborators, in the Olivier, also has movie connections; its author, John Hughes, is a screenwriter who's never written for the stage before. The collaborators of the title are Stalin and the playwright Mikhail Bulgakov, whom Stalin commissioned to write a celebratory play about him. This did in fact happen, though the play was never produced.

I wasn't crazy about some of the supporting acting, but the production is beautifully fluid and the central relationship is wittily written, and gorgeously played by two recurring ornaments of the Hytner regime, and of the British theatre in general.
