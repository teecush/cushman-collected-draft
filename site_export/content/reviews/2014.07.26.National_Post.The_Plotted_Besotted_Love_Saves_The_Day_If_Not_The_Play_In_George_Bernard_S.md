A lady and a gentleman are making love to one another in the drawing room." That is the opening stage direction of The Philanderer, Bernard Shaws second play, written in 1893. Given the date and the author, not to mention the location, its unlikely that "making love here means having sex"; but of course you never know. Lisa Peterson, an American director making her Canadian debut at the Shaw Festival, doesn't physically show us the lady and gentleman at it, but before the lights go up she lets us hear enough heavy breathing and rapturous moaning to make the libidinous point.

Even without this, we would probably not think that the emotional relationships in the play were platonic. Or maybe we would. The text contains no explicit sexual references, and though this may be partly due to the theatrical constraints of the time (Shaw's next play, Mrs. Warrens Profession, deals with prostitution without ever using the word) it probably points to something in the writer himself. His characters, certainly in this play, talk a lot about love, but give the impression that they would rather talk about it than do it. Still, they are supposed to be physical beings; and if Petersons mostly bold and imaginative production is untrue to the letter of the text, it's faithful to what one hopes is its underlying spirit. Or if not faithful, then certainly more interesting.

The play itself is much concerned with the virtues and limitations of fidelity. Its title character, the gent exerting himself in the drawing room, is Leonard Charteris, who is something of a Shavian self-portrait, though a satirical one: probably not the late sexual starter that Shaw himself was (though GBS does seem to have gone through an energetic phase of making up for lost time) but, like his author, a self-consciously liberated man who won't shut up about it. To the plays credit, it can endorse Charteris views, especially on marriage (constricting) and divorce (should be easier), while exposing his self-centredness; which is why Shaw was a dramatist, not a pamphleteer. His partner at curtain-rise is Grace Tranfield, a young widow, who shares her lover's views but not his egotism. Their idyll is interrupted by Julia Craven, an old flame of Charteris' who has no intention of being extinguished.

Julia wont let her ex-lover go, refuses indeed to believe that he really wants to be ex. Shes an emotional stalker and, as her first entrance demonstrates, a physical one as well. She throws fits, threatens suicide, drives people out of the room or into her arms; the latter tends to be Charteris' reaction, and whether it's primarily due to passion, compassion, or simple self-defence is unknowable, probably even to him. In the plays last festival outing, Julia was played as if she knew she was being outrageous and took a humorous delight in it. That was a mistake; Julia is the rare Shaw character who doesnt

have a sense of humour, even a sense of irony. Moya O'Connell, this time around, plays her with uncompromising masochistic passion, never once letting up. It's a dark, fearless performance, maybe her best. Gord Rand gives a louche compelling account of Charteris, only marred by the moments when the production, getting too high on its own concept, has him delivering key lines directly to the audience, as if to impress us with how relevant they are. (Honestly, wed notice anyway.) As the third and most level-headed member of the transient triangle, Marla McLean is charmingly cool.

Charteris, Grace and Julia are all members of something called the Ibsen Club, a haven for advanced thinkers or, as the clubs own promo material has it, for "unmanly men and unwomanly women." (Julia got in by having her nominators perjure themselves.) Shaw himself admitted, some 30 years later, that this aspect of the play was dated. What he didn't acknowledge was that it never had much point to begin with. The play's whole long middle section is set in the club, but it has hardly anything to say about the great Norwegian, except to treat him, lazily, as a standin for progressive thought in general. In fact, Ibsen was a congenital contrarian who, if I may plagiarize a line from my review of the Shaws 2007 production, would never have joined any club that would have him as a mentor. The characters here are swimming in some pretty shallow waters. Julias younger sister, a defiantly unwomanly woman sportingly and sportily played by Harveen Sandhu, is a member of the club; so, claiming that they're there to protect their daughters, are Julia's and Graces fathers, given enjoyably fruity performances by Michael Ball and Ric Reid. Both dads are proudly old-fashioned: Graces is a theatre critic (enough said); Julia's is a military man, resigning himself to an early death as a result of a liver complaint discovered by a young Dr. Paramore, who has named it after himself. Actually nearly everybody in the play might be said to suffer from some kind of paramour's disease, not least the doctor himself, whos besotted with Julia and is trying to nerve himself to propose to her, greatly encouraged by Charteris who sees this as his way out.

And this is where things, both in writing and production, get fascinating. In the plays official last act, Paramore does propose and is accepted, though Julia plainly doesn't love him. This act, though, was a replacement for the one Shaw originally wrote, and which no one at the time would accept. His original version took a time-jump, hurling us four bitterly disillusioned years into Paramore's and Julias marriage. This production restores that scene in place of the published one, and the change is electric. For one thing it enables Jeff Meadows Paramore to emerge as a feeling, rounded human being, rather than the amiable goof he has played in previous scenes. (His disease turned out to be a total bust, much to the disgust of Colonel Craven who had turned vegetarian when he thought he had it.) Indeed, everybody develops depths, most of them sombre, even the philandering Charteris himself, though he kicks against it. The entire scene feels more modern, not only than the play that's preceded it, but than practically anything else its author wrote. It certainly feels more sexual, even if the sex has gone sour. Here, too, Peterson's direction shows its hand. Sue LePage has designed two handsome, detailed interiors for the earlier scenes; this one she sets in what seem to be the ruins of a once-classical landscape. The characters, trying to escape an intolerable situation, are cast adrift from whatever certainties they may have had. Only the ending fails to convince. It seems intended to echo, or pre-echo, the union of Jack Tanner and Ann Whitefield at the end of Man and Superman. That ending, though is a believably happy one; Jack and Ann do seem made for one another, united in wit. You couldnt say the same about the corresponding couple in this play. The production, though, seems to be trying to.

The Philanderer is in repertory through October 12 at the Festival Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake
