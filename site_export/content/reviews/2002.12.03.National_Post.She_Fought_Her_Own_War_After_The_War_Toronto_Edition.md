THE DANISH PLAY

Tarragon Extra Space, Toronto

I was away when The Danish Play opened -- to laudatory reviews. I caught up with it a week into its run, and it seems to me extraordinarily overrated. God knows, its intentions are honourable. It's common knowledge that during the Second World War Denmark had the finest record of any occupied country in standing up to the Holocaust. A remarkable rescue operation that seems, morally at least, to have involved most of the population resulted in the ferrying to safety of nearly all the country's Jews. Beyond that, most of us know little of the Danish Resistance; Sonja Mills' play is in part an attempt to fill us in.

It's also the author's tribute to a member of her own family. Mills, who came to Canada from Denmark as a child, discovered that her great-aunt, a poet named Agnete Ottosen, had worked in the resistance. She was captured while on a courier mission, remained steadfast under severe torture and was sent to Ravensbruck -- which she survived, despite being subjected to Nazi gynecological "experiments." She even managed, after the war, to bear a son, though she ran afoul of her own country's bureaucracy by refusing to reveal the name of the father. Her behaviour became increasingly erratic, and she lost custody of the child, who grew up for the most part in an institution. In the end she succumbed to the tragedy she had managed, against all odds, to escape during the war.

There are two plays going on here, one on each side of the intermission. Both involve Agnete and four of her wartime comrades and post-war friends: Mads, a lawyer and part-time publisher; Michael, a gay playwright; Helga, Michael's sister, who runs a bakery; and Bente, who starts off as Helga's assistant. Their presence, though, is not enough to unify the evening; Michael, in particular, gets an excessively generous amount of attention. Presumably, both he and Agnete represent two kinds of marginalized figure, but this is never explored.

Individually, both sections are well-constructed -- the author is adept at dropping hints and springing surprises -- but the actual writing is undistinguished, chatty with some hopeful stabs at starkness. I except the interrogation and concentration-camp scenes, which are stark -- economically so -- among the best of their kind I have seen. The rest of the resistance story can hardly help being compelling, but the framing device of the survivors' annual reunion smells of soap opera.

The second act, less suspenseful but more interesting, is nonetheless waylaid by the fact we never know what proportion of Agnete's subsequent troubles can be blamed on her time in the camp. On the face of it, this would be enough to scar anybody, but the author seems to go out of her way to blunt this point with the character of Bente, who goes through many of the same experiences, including the medical ones, but who survives and is even happy. All right, they're very different women; we know Agnete is a difficult customer from the moment we see her applying to Mads for both a job in his office and a place in his underground organization. She might even be courting martyrdom, but again this isn't followed through. And it seems facile to treat Agnete's struggles with Danish red tape as on a par with her suffering under the Nazis, which is what she herself seems inclined to do, with only token contradiction from the script: A woman's right to choose single motherhood is not as momentous as the same woman's courage in defying the Gestapo.

One wonders, for example, if the Danish doctor who examined Agnete after her liberation was really as hidebound and unfeeling as the play makes her seem; while some of the objections to Agnete's parenting skills that are later raised by judges and social workers seem very reasonable. It sounds unfeeling, but I suspect the main reason Agnete is being treated as a tragic heroine rather than a sadly interesting case study is that the author is related to her.

The final catastrophe takes place offstage, depriving both us and the central character of the chance to add things up. Like the hero of an earlier Danish play (she even goes to see a production of Hamlet at Elsinore), Agnete comes to feel that "Denmark's a prison." Her friends, too, are given some desultory chat about the country's identity, including its posy-Viking colonial record. But if the author's aim is to question the idea of the nation-state (I got that from the publicity material, not the play), then she's telling the wrong story; Denmark's wartime history is glorious precisely because the Danes behaved differently from the citizens of other countries.

Kelly Thornton's production has one brilliant device: Agnete's interrogation and imprisonment are played out in the sharpest relief , on a small square of stage behind a scrim. The effect is both tactful and harrowing. (It's rather less tactful to use the same space for Agnete's later tussles with domestic officialdom, though it's certainly an efficient use of space.)

The rest of the direction is below the standard one would expect from the director of the superb This Hotel, as is much of the acting; the more workaday scenes are sodden, with nobody able to deliver as much as a believable conversational remark.

Christine Brubaker as Bente is a glowing exception; a scene in which she is confronted by a German soldier on the very eve of the Jewish evacuation is the tensest, fullest moment of the evening. Eric Goulem and Erika Hennebury also do well in a variety of purposely anonymous roles.

As Agnete, Kate Hennig is as split down the middle as the play itself. As the clear-eyed fighter of the play's first part, she is stridently wooden; as the character gets more impossible (or, if you prefer, more implacable), so the actress gets more plausible. Once pregnant, she's impregnable. The performance, of a woman locked up in her own logic, becomes very strong, even when revealing weakness; it has, in fact, a touch of the uncanny.
