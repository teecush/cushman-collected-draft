THEATRE REVIEW

Hedda Gabler Court House Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

Even people who have never seen Hedda Gabler generally enter the theatre with a fair idea of how it ends. Ibsens play has one of the famous final curtains, not to mention one of the famous curtain lines. This alone lends almost any production a quality that we call inevitability if the show goes right and predictability if it doesnt. The Shaw Festival's revival is very inevitable.

There's a driving logic to it - to Martha Henrys direction, to Richard Eyres adaptation and to the performances of the entire cast - that makes the play come up fresh and exciting. If the protagonist's journey shows anything, it's that the road to hell is paved with bad intentions. In this production, the path is charted with mesmerizing exactness.

Mesmerizing rather than moving, though. But then Im not at all sure this play, and especially its heroine, can ever be moving; and Im especially bemused by attempts to turn Hedda herself into some kind of feminist icon. Hedda is the adoring daughter of a deceased general who bequeathed her his pistols and an overwhelming sense of frustrated entitlement. Having been the belle of, probably, several seasons of Norwegian balls, she has come to the end of the last one with her dance card unmarked and her future uncertain. So she has settled for marriage to a fusty and, it seems, mostly sexless academic (special subject: the arts and crafts of medieval Brabant) whose research grant has bought them both a long honeymoon and a big house.

She hated the honeymoon, hates the house and is devoting herself to making the lives of her husband, his aunts and the help moderately miserable. She also has past male admirers to toy with or to fend off, both of which she is good at.

Still, claims the Shaw's program note, compared to the other characters, Hedda is luminous - she shines with rage and despair - her refusal to accept a monotonous, meaningless life at the mercy of self-centred mediocrities gives her unforgettable dramatic power."

Well, the power is certainly there, and Hedda is unquestionably trapped by a society that considers her proper destiny to be marriage and motherhood, the latter a prospect whose looming imminence she loathes. But I can't see that shes any less self-centred (or, truthfully, any less "mediocre, whatever that means) than the other characters. And in fact the play explicitly - in this production, very explicitly - contrasts her with another woman, an old schoolmate who has made the hard choices that Hedda has avoided; this Mrs. Elvsted has had to work as a governess, and has left her husband for the man she admires and adores: a dissolute genius who, under her guidance, has dried out and written a fine book, and who happens to be both one of Heddas enforcedly platonic exes and, in his reformed state, a threat to her husband's professional future.

Hedda is furious that this mousy girl, whom she used to bully at school and whose name she can't remember properly, has done what she herself has always dreamed of doing: inspire a man - especially this man - and shape his destiny. So she sets out to destroy what they have: to kill their child," or more prosaically, burn their yetunpublished second book. Hedda Gabler is a play about jealousy, which is what Henrys production brilliantly brings out.

Moya OConnell is a Hedda of vampiric beauty, and also of vampiric power, spiced with a vicious wit. She lacks the vulnerability that should ideally complete the picture, but she does rise, finely and ineluctably, to the two extreme actions: the literary infanticide (in a stove that, as the lights go down, glows like a Halloween mask) and the final gunshots that, despite foreknowledge, truly make the audience jump. Claire Jullien as the despised and envied Thea is a splendid match for her; the character may not know her own strength, but the actress does.

As Lovborg, the man over whom they fight their undeclared war, Gray Powell is remarkable. A rakish lock of hair over his forehead matches a rakish mode of speech; Eyre's text has him saying that he wrote his first, popular book to have "an ingratiating smile on its face." His proud determination to stay on the wagon, and his equally proud fall off of it, make up a scene of extraordinary tension, as does the barely veiled eroticism of his first conversation with Hedda as they sit on a sofa, ostensibly inspecting her holiday snaps. Their whole prior relationship flashes before their eyes and ours.

Patrick McManus gives Hedda's hubby a pedants petulance but also a pedants nobility; his heartfelt admiration for his rival's achievement rings absolutely true. Indeed, the whole production, as distinct from the program, seems intent on giving the opposition its due. Mary Haney's Aunt Juliana, after being snubbed by Hedda, sees right through her and hardly misses an opportunity to twist a benevolent knife. Jennifer Phipps practically makes a subplot out of the maid Berthes relationship to her new mistress, sometimes delighting in being in the way, at others cowering and scampering to get out of it.

The nearest thing to a weak link is the one I had expected to be the strongest: Jim Mezon is so ideally equipped to play the fleshy, menacing Judge Brack that he could afford to let up on the oily, lecherous menace from time to time. He contributes vitally, though, to the orchestration of the climax, and to the final modulation, when he unknowingly echoes one of Heddas own favourite dismissive phrases.

Ibsen once said that Hedda was a self-portrait; like her, he had spent his life preaching a doctrine of dissent that he was too timid to practise. The ending can be taken as an ironic kind of wish fulfillment. Hedda finds that her husband and her old friend can do without her, and that the judge can very much do with her; the alternative may be public disgrace. So you could say she's a frustrated playwright who, having failed to arrange her characters' destinies, didnt want to stick around for the reviews. She takes, if not quite the noble, then the only way out. It doesnt always convince. This time it does.
