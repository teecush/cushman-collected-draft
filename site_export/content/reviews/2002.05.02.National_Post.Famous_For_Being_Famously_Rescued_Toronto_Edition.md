Until June 2. Box office: 416-531-1827.

WELL,

Tarragon Theatre

- - -

Jonathan Wilson delivers in his new play Well, (the comma is part of the title) on the promise displayed in his previous piece, Kilt. You will note that, when it comes to finding names for plays, Wilson is the merry master of the monosyllabic moniker.

He certainly gets mileage out of this one, exploiting the various meanings of the word "well" almost to the limits of the dictionary. There is the kind of well you draw water from, or alternatively fall down, which is what Willie, the central character of this play, did when he was three.

There is the kind of well that you either do or don't feel; after being dramatically rescued, and becoming a local celebrity on the strength of that, the traumatized Willie was packed off to a mental hospital from which, 20 years on, he has now unofficially discharged himself. (He says he feels well, but he obviously has his doubts; others, including the authorities, share them.)

And there is the kind of hedging, qualifying "well" that people use in conversation, generally to play for time, and that denotes uncertainty. Carol, a television news reporter, is especially prone to this. As a child she fed on the story of Wee Willie in the Well, and she has recently done a "where is he now?" piece to commemorate the anniversary. Willie, on the run, catches the item on a motel TV. So he seeks her out, to answer her question, and to ask for her assistance in locating the scene of his former fame.

Willie, like many mental patients, is unnervingly straightforward; he has no defences. Carol seems to have nothing but defences, and they often rear up and hit her. (When we first meet her she is doing multiple retakes, caused by the word "deficit" always coming out of her mouth as "decifit." She never does get it right.) The play lines the two of them up in parallel, to show each eventually providing some of what the other needs.

They are both extremely well played. Aaron Willis is guilelessly and insistently watchable as Willie, and Melody A. Johnson, whose specialty is being vulnerably abrasive, is a sharp fountain of nervous energy as Carol.

Carol has been having occasional sex with her cameraman Quentin (played on an endearing note of rough-house bewilderment by Paul Braunstein), who would like it to be less occasional and maybe even official. When Willie falls -- inevitably and unthreateningly -- in love with Carol, Quentin's reaction is first violent, then understanding.

This three-cornered relationship is quirkily but solidly developed, helped by Wilson's flair for comic surprise, in individual lines and in whole scenes; Willie's opening speech to an unseen medical panel is a tour de force, as is Carol's first venture into Quentin's living quarters, a level of commitment from which she draws back appalled.

When it sticks to the present tense, the play does fine. When it reaches back into the past it runs, like so many other plays, into trouble. Willie's head is filled by the voice of a girl, whom we see and whom we take to be dead, and who is in fact inclined to goad and prompt him as if she were the ghost of Hamlet's father. Holly Lewis plays her, conscientiously though to little effect.

We also meet Willie's rural uncle, who is considerably more corporeal. Disillusioned by just about everything, he is now in the curio dealing business, and delivers rich, curmudgeonly tirades with which Richard McMillan is obviously having a lot of fun. (Too much perhaps, but as I also had fun listening to him, it would be hypocritical of me to complain.)

Eventually there are revelations about what really happened; and though they are not incredible, they are not burningly credible either ("Ding dong bell, Willie's in the well. Who pushed him in? That's where the doubts begin. Who got him out? That's what it's about"). Really, the author can tell us anything he chooses, and we don't have the evidence to call him out.

Whimsy creeps in, though not to the point of sentimentality. (Tears don't well up.) The last scene gets the play back on its feet, amounting to as much of a happy ending as we deserve.

Chris Abraham directs to the play's strengths, which are those of wit and unillusioned good nature. The evening confirms Wilson's place -- alongside Michael Healey, Adam Pettle, Florence Gibson, Kristen Thomson and the revered Morris Panych -- in the ranks of playwrights worth paying attention to. He has a voice and he uses it, well, well.
