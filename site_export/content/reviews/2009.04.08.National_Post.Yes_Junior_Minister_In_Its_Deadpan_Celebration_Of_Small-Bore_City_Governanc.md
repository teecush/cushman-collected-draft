From the office that brought you The Office comes Parks and Recreation. Since NBC in the U. S. and Citytv here have been promoting the new show as a companion piece to the old one, and since it has two of the same executive producers (Greg Daniels and Michael Schur, if you care about executive producers) there seems little point in going over the similarities. They exist, that's all.

The Office, of course, is set in a wildly dysfunctional private organization; Parks & Rec, as its obviously destined to be known, is set in a mildly dysfunctional public one. Both shows are carried along by soliloquies to the camera, and both feature a star comedian as an ambitious manager. A key difference is the ambitions the central characters. Steve Carrell in The Office is all ego and insecurity, Amy Poehler in Parks & Rec is ego plus overflowing self-confidence plus what seems to be a genuine desire to do good for her fellow creatures. The U. S. incarnation of The Office is of course kinder and gentler than the U. K. original, but Parks & Rec is nicer still. As satire on government its no Yes Minister; its setting -- the folksy municipality of Pawnee, Ind. -- is rather far removed from the seat of any actual power. All the same, I suspect that if this show were British, and aired on PBS, people would be raving about it. It has the casual air of, say, To the Manor Born: off the wall but not so far off that it collapses in a manic heap on the floor. I've seen the pilot, and I like it.

It had me from the pre-credits sequence which has Poehler as P&R superintendent Leslie Knope (the K is silent, so much so that you have to go to the show's website to find it exists) intently interrogating a six-year-old on the quality of her sandbox experience: Was it great fun, some

fun, or not much fun? From here she progresses to extricating a drunk from the childrens slide where, as an adult, he has of course no right to be. Leslie, blond, bright-eyed and 34, is a hands-on lady. We follow her back to departmental HQ, where nobody is remotely as enthusiastic as she is, and then to a public meeting which, she tells us, is where the rubber of government meets the road of real human beings. The real human beings who attend are few in number but full of complaints -- about graffiti, loud music and profanity generally -- and they voice them, well, vociferously. Leslie finds this gratifying: "What I hear when Im being yelled at is people caring loudly." From the throng arises one especially powerful voice; it belongs to Ann, a nurse who says that she doesn't like politics (general applause) but wants to draw attention to a large pit -- really more of a chasm-- that some feckless developers have left outside her house and into which her musician boyfriend has recently, and injuriously, fallen. Rashida Jones plays Ann, and she emerges as second lead when Leslie announces that she intends not just to fill the hole in but to turn it into, naturally, a park. I think this is called empire-building. Leslie thinks of it as her own personal Hoover Dam project.

It seems that this scheme, with the two ladies as allies, is going to be the season's ongoing plotline. Having griped so often about shows that dont have plots but simply dish up repetitive situations every week, I feel mean when I doubt the development potential of this one. This, though, puts me in the same camp as Leslies colleagues, none of whom thinks she has a prayer but all of whom, so far at least, are going along for the sake of a quiet life. Or a quieter one than they'd have otherwise. They're a rich bunch, and its on their potential as well as Leslies that I pin my hopes. These are especially high for Rob (Nick Offerman), the boss of the outfit, a government official who's opposed government on philosophical grounds, and would like to turn it all over to Chuck E. Cheese (his dream employer). Then there's Tom (Aziz Ansari), who hits unsubtly on every woman in sight and identifies himself as a redneck from South Carolina, even though Leslie seems to think hes Libyan. More laid-back is Mark (Paul Schneider), a disillusioned urban planner who can just about remember having slept with Leslie. Junior member is April (Aubrey Plaza, the first female Aubrey Ive ever encountered), a taciturn intern with an interesting glower. So far she's the only character not to talk to the camera, or to anybody else much, probably because she's too bored. The others unburden themselves at every opportunity.

Writing, performance and direction share a beguiling, and occasionally cutting, offhandedness. This isnt an original style, as already acknowledged, but its still unusual enough to be refreshing. Like most good comedy, this show takes the self-centredness of all its characters for granted. Leslie's aim, we are told, is to be first female president of the U. S. A. and, with commendable impartiality, she lists her role models as Hillary, Nancy and Sarah. Which reminds me: If we're going to compare comedies featuring SNL alumnae, then on the evidence so far I'll take this over the frenzy of 30 Rock any day. It makes a nice rec. - Parks and Recreation premieres tomorrow on Citytv and NBC at 8:30 p. m.
