The final three openings at this year's Shaw Festival are all, to some degree, plays about plays. We have The Divine, in which an aspiring dramatist sets about writing a play for a famous actress, a play that takes shape before our eyes. We have The Intelligent Homosexual's Guide, in which one man talks at length about having just seen a production of Major Barbara and another compares himself to a character in The Cherry Orchard, and which also prompts less explicit comparisons to Ghosts (parent asks child for help in committing suicide; in Ibsen it's the other way around) and Death of a Salesman (Brooklyn paterfamilias plans to kill himself because his American dream has foundered). These shows are major events. Light Up the Sky, which completes the metatheatrical trio is, to put it kindly, a minor one.

Mainly it's the play's fault. Light Up the Sky is a 1948 comedy by Moss Hart, one of the key figures of Broadway's golden age (roughly, the first half of the 20th century) and by all accounts one of the most likable. Those accounts include his own; his autobiography, Act One, is a classic. He wrote the book of a major musical (Lady in the Dark) and directed another (My Fair Lady); and as a playwright he helped create a distinct genre of American comedy: wisecracking and whimsical. The operative word, though, is "helped"; his most successful plays were collaborations with George S. Kaufman, a similar multi-talent but older and more acerbic. Their partnership started with Once in a Lifetime, which is up there with Singin' in the Rain as a satire on the birth of the talkies and on Hollywood craziness in general. (Mogul to idiot director: "You know what you've done? You've shot the wrong movie.") They stuck for the most part to indulgent comic commentary on their own cultural world; and Hart still stuck to it when he abandoned collaboration and struck out on his own. Light Up the Sky dramatizes that primal theatrical nightmare: trouble on the road. It all takes place in Boston, in the hotel suite of a star actress. She's about to open in a new play that's en route for New York: a play by an unknown author in which everybody passionately believes, except the star's mother.

In Act One, actress, author, producer, director and assorted hangers-on toast one another and the success they are about to have. In Act Two, a few hours later, they all come reeling back, convinced that their first night has been the most horrendous flop, and turn on one another; the novice playwright naturally bears the brunt of the displeasure, and decides to call it quits and head back home. In Act Three - well, you can probably guess what happens in Act Three. All these things unfold very slowly; the first act, in particular, takes an inordinate time to tell us very little. Blair Williams' production hardly manages to speed it up, though maybe the only way to do this would be through wholesale cutting.

The most egregious time-wasters are the actress's mother and the producer's wife: a pair of kvetching harpies, who spend much of the first two acts airing and sharing their grievances, sometimes over a game of gin rummy, sometimes not; it's a real downer to find them still in possession of the stage at the start of the third. Kelli Fox and Laurie Paton do what they can in the roles, though Fox's performance sometimes feels like karmic payback for her superlative work in The Intelligent Homosexual. If they have too much material, the supposed principals have too little. Hart with Kaufman excelled in the creation of sacred theatrical monsters, but these are just shadows; they're based on famous personalities of the time, but it's too late now for actors or audience to fill in the blanks. The director is prone to fits of crying, and that's about all that Steven Sutcliffe, in good times or bad, gets to do. As the pampered star, Claire Jullien works very hard at being gushing and temperamental. The only one to make anything of this material is Thom Marriott as the producer, a self-confessed vulgarian taking a flyer on Art; he's powerful and funny in desperate, bullying mode, and even better when sincerely apologizing.

A major problem is that we're told nothing about the play that has moved him to risk his shirt, other than that it takes place in a ruined landscape, and that its leading lady - we never hear from or about any other actors - is silent for most of the first act. It's a stereotyped idea of an "experimental" play circa 1950. (Of course the cutting-edge plays of today may not seem so cutting or edgy 70 years from now.) We have to take on trust the idea of the workingguy playwright as a budding genius. As Charlie Gallant plays him, roughedged and impressionable but with an incipient chip on his shoulder, he's certainly the realest person around. He has a couple of well-played allies: Graeme Somerville is a worldly wise author offering counsel and comfort from experience; Fiona Byrne is a writer, ghosting the actress' memoirs, whom the play forgets about after she's served its expository purpose but whom the production cleverly places as a silent vessel of artistic conscience. But the most delightful performance is Shawn Wright's last-act cameo of a shy stage-struck Shriner, whose fellow conventioneers have been adding to the hotel hell. He's there to bring news of good reviews (though the team would surely have read them anyway) and to apologize for his colleagues' rowdy behaviour at the premiere, which we can presumably blame for the general perception of disaster. The Shriners are altogether good value in this production; we get a brief enchanting glimpse of them carrying drunkenly on before the curtain properly rises on the last act. Projections also give us a delightful look at the exterior of the Ritz-Carlton before we venture inside. And the play does give a fair if sketchy impression of the turn-on-adime Broadway mentality. Or theatrical mentality. Or human nature.
