THE LAST EMPRESS

Hummingbird Centre

- - -

Warm on the heels of the Chinese musical Terracotta Warriors, which was about the first Emperor of China, comes the Korean musical The Last Empress, whose title saves me the trouble of balancing this sentence.

Terracotta Warriors originated a long way away from the country it depicted -- in Vancouver, to be precise -- and it aimed, fruitlessly, at cracking Broadway. The Last Empress, by contrast, is a native Korean project -- it started in Seoul -- and has already been to Broadway, via London. It is performed here, as it was not in New York, in the original Korean, with English surtitles.

Language apart, though, it is as Westernized an enterprise as the other. In some respects it seems rather more so. I am no expert on Asian choreography, but if the routines we see here represent authentic Korean dance, then Korean dance and the routines at Radio City Music Hall must have sprung from a common source.

The Last Empress is inoffensive but -- despite all the arm- waving and leaping about -- inert. It is, though, very instructive about a slice of modern history of which most of us will know nothing; and there are worse things to do with your time in the theatre than spend it learning something.

The show certainly beats Terracotta Warriors in the matter of its title figure, on whom it keeps its eye firmly fixed throughout and who appears to have been more interesting in the first place. She is Ja-Young Min, who in 1864 was recruited from common stock to marry the boy king of Korea, a country then known as Chosun.

As the two of them grew up, they found themselves facing the defining dilemma of traditional cultures in the 19th century (and indeed since): whether to stick with the old ways and isolate themselves or embrace modernity, meaning in practice doing business with the strongest foreign power around. The king being one of those good weak monarchs of whom Shakespeare's (and historys) Henry VI is the paradigm, the decisions were taken by his far more powerful wife. She favoured progress, with caution, and encouraged a deal with Japan. When the Japanese proved too overbearing partners, she switched allegiances to China. This eventually resulted in her murder at the hands of a band of samurai commandos. The killers were afterward put on perfunctory trial in Hiroshima and, naturally, acquitted.

The trial serves as the shows prologue and epilogue, and is itself preceded by footage of the atom bomb falling, 50 years later, on Hiroshima itself. The association seems, to put it mildly, meretricious, but it's an accurate forecast of the show itself; like the Occidental pop operas that have obviously inspired it, it will grab at anything.

International realpolitik makes itself felt half-way through the first act when naval envoys from Germany, France and the United States arrive and, perched in crow's nests, demand trading rights:

We dont foresee that you will be the least bit argumentative

So please ignore the man owar that acts as a preventative.

Those lines aren't from The Last Empress; they're from the Please Hello number in Stephen Sondheims Pacific Overtures, which shows the Western powers, earlier in the century, exerting similar pressures on Japan. I cant be sure, of course, but I doubt if the Korean lyrics include anything so elegantly cutting; the surtitles certainly don't. The show does function, though, as an unwitting sequel to Pacific Overtures. It shows the Japanese doing unto others what had previously been done to them. The Europeans aren't exactly squeezed out. They return in Act Two, in a number that looks more than ever like a reprise of Please Hello, mounted on stilts and with national emblems adorning their tall hats. But its the Asian nations themselves who have to fight it out.

They do it, though, to Euro-American music. Its ironic that Pacific Overtures, a Broadway show about cultural infiltration, should have embodied its theme with music that got purposefully less Eastern as it went along, while this one -- an indigenous Asian musical on Asian themes -- should sell out to the foreign devils from the moment it gets started.

I mustn't be hypocritical about this; if the show had used authentic music throughout, it would probably have bored me. But it is dispiriting that Hui Gap Kim's score should sound at its best like second-drawer Rodgers and Hammerstein (or, more specifically and predictably, like outtakes from The King and I) and at its worst like the filler between the big numbers in the flossier Andrew Lloyd Webber shows. (The kings father, a semi-villain, is pursued by chords that seem to be urging him toward the title song from The Phantom of the Opera.)

None of it is terrible, but -- as in all the Europop shows -- there is an air of painting-by-musical-numbers about the way the show is laid out. The queens difficulties in conceiving serve as the cue for a sorcerer's fertility dance. A faithful solider-lover, straight out of old operetta, appears at intervals just long enough to have enabled us to forget his existence. There is, of course, an anthem of hope at the end of the evening.

There is also some humour; at least, I think that is the purpose of the sequences in which counsellors in squabbling squadrons bend their knees and jump backwards while arguing over the future of their country. They seem, if the translation is to be trusted, to be singing lyrics made up of stirring abstractions in polysyllables. Maybe In Ja Yang's original lyrics are more truly lyrical but, from my experience of the equivalent Anglophone shows, I wouldnt bet on it.

The show, under Ho Jin Yuns direction, looks impressive. The designer, Dong Woo Park, keeps his stage high, deep and sparsely decorated. The backdrops and icons with which he does adorn it make a corrspondingly greater impression, and are mostly in sombre reds and glittering golds.

The only performer to make much of an impression across the language barrier is Tae Won Yi in the title role. She sings powerfully, especially as death approaches, though I have to say that is a long time in coming.

Her acting is hampered by the fact that the character is granted little complexity. People who agree with her politics love her, and those who don't hate her, but that gets us nowhere. The important contradictions should be within her, and they don't exist.

Evita, with which this show has been freely compared and which probably did much to inspire it, is by comparison a masterpiece of psychological insight and political acuteness. The Last Empress, though, tells a better story.
