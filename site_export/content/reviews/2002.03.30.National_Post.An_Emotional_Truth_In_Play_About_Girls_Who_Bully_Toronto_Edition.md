THE SHAPE OF A GIRL

Tarragon Extra Space

- - -

Joan Macleod's absorbing new play is about schoolgirl bullying. It's a monologue whose speaker, a mid-teen called Braidie, is neither full-fledged bully nor predestined victim; she inclines towards the first and plainly fears the possibility of becoming the second. Toward the close she looks at her best friend, Adrienne, who has been the prime mover in the near-fatal tormenting of their classmate Sofie. She sees, alternately, "a girl in the shape of a monster ... a monster in the shape of a girl."

This double image has appeared earlier, at intervals through the play, as Braidie absorbs the news reports of what is clearly the murder of Reena Virk. It is the realization that all Reena's schoolmates, not just those directly responsible, participated in the conspiracy of silence surrounding her death that pushes Braidie off the fence. The Shape of a Girl is a production of the Green Thumb young people's company in Vancouver, and a play about bullying from British Columbia -- site of the Virk killing and of the suicide of Dawn-Marie Wesley -- could hardly be more painfully topical. But this play would awaken fears and echoes at any time and in any place.

Macleod apparently began writing it before the Virk case, though it's hard to guess what the play could have been like before it found that focus. It might have been just another somewhat lyrical monologue about growing up female and misunderstood; Braidie, a Victoria girl, has literary inclinations and addresses her reminiscences to her absent brother Trevor. (Is she speaking aloud to an imaginary hearer? Is she writing letters? Or a journal? The riddling curse of the solo play reasserts itself. The more we hear about Trevor, the less substantial he seems.)

Braidie also has a fractious relationship with her mother, whom she refers to as "the voice of mom" and whose sole aim, as seen by her daughter, is to frustrate her every wish and generally ruin her life. This is darkly hilarious and rendered with ventriloquial accuracy. (Braidie speaks of her mother's "famous whisper-shriek" and describes life with her as "a crash-course in indignity.") But it still feels like padding. It has only a tenuous relationship with what really grabs us.

It's possible to wish that Macleod had not tied her play so closely to an actual event; that she had found another way of letting Braidie work out her salvation. But given the path that she has chosen (and maybe it was inevitable -- maybe we would have made the connection anyway and it's best to acknowledge it), she has followed it superbly. She traces the development of discrimination, beginning with Adrienne's invention, in Grade 4, of a game called Penalty Day, in which one girl is scapegoat for the duration.

As school progresses Sofie -- referred to as "It" -- is cast in this role full-time, to the point where, as Braidie observes with a mixture of wonder and shock, "even Sofie hates Sofie." When adolescence hits, Adrienne begins to take her boyfriend problems out on Sofie. This much perhaps is elementary. Subtler and more powerful is the observation of how -- on the rare occasions on which Sofie defends herself or when fate intervenes -- Adrienne herself acts the screeching victim, as if it were intolerable that she should be thwarted or that people should slip out of the roles that she had assigned them.

When Braidie finally finds it in herself to acknowledge and condemn the persecution -- including her own part in it -- the adjective she finds for it, after an enormous hesitation, is "despicable." Both the word and the pause that precedes it feel absolutely right: perfect as description, true to the speaker. Macleod's feeling for teenage girlspeak -- the rhythm, the vocabulary and the outraged conviction -- seems perfect; when she uses a term like "rancid" you feel that, if that isn't a current vogue-word, then it should be.

It is hard, though, to disentangle the writer's achievement from that of 22-year-old Jenny Young, whose performance, oval-headed and button-eyed, is a consummate wedding of mature technique to fresh impersonation. It is also, under Patrick McDonald's direction, superbly shaped; at its crucial point we feel that we are meeting Braidie in her prime.

The play's note of hope is perhaps not much justified by recent events, or by recent revelations about past events. But it has an emotional truth; the play earns it and so, I think, do we -- as audience if not as citizens. It feels right.
