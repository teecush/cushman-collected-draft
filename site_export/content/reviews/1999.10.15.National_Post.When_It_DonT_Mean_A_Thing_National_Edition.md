SWINGSTEP

Ford Centre for the Performing Arts, Toronto

The title of this musical is somewhat confusing. Swingstep looks like a single word for dancing, any dancing, done to the music of a swing band. In fact its more what Humpty Dumpty called a portmanteau word, yoking together two separate concepts. There is swing, as in the newly popular hot music of the 1940s with its attendant jitterbugging, and there is step-dancing, the old Scottish form of tap that still thrives, or so were told, among its originators' descendants in Cape Breton.

Once you've taken this on board you expect a show about a culture- clash. Not a bit of it, the two styles get along famously; as somebody remarks, when were still only at the start of Act Two, you can hardly tell them apart. This is not a show thats big on conflict. Still, the actual dancing -- swing or step or both together -- is excellent. It's the best part of the evening. It's the only good part of the evening.

Our hero, Sonny Stone, is a New York bandleader and swing revivalist whose grandmother, herself a famous wartime singer, originally hailed from Nova Scotia. After her death, Sonny goes on a pilgrimage to her hometown that he finds to be populated almost entirely by restless but wholesome teenagers kicking up a storm on the cobblestones. He decides to put them in his show, thereby falling foul of the one non-stepping youth in the neighbourhood; a hard-working no-nonsense sort of lad, he suspects Sonny of having designs on his girlfriend, who is the local star attraction. (She can dance and play the violin simultaneously: a fiddler on the hoof.)

Thus far the action -- city slicker brings showbiz to yokels, with jealousy sub-plot -- has a marked similarity to that of Crazy for You, which was a retread to begin with. It also recalls classic Broadway hootenannies like Brigadoon and Finians Rainbow. Brigadoon was mixing swing and Celtic 50 years ago, with a jealous suitor thrown in, and Finians had a dumb girl who communicated through choreography; this show has a dumb boy who does the same.

The inhabitants also include twin brothers who talk in unison, a mixed-up girl from the wrong side of the tracks (I quote, believe it or not) and an endearing old man who gives his granddaughter tender advice in song about sticking to your true love, like the Salvation Army geezer in Guys and Dolls.

Coming up to date a bit, the show also resembles Rent in that it devotes a sizable chunk of its second half to mourning the death of a character who was never dramatically alive in the first place. I'm not accusing anyone of conscious plagiarism, merely suggesting that this is a musical that dwells solely in the land of other musicals. It does not create its own imaginative world, nor --despite gestures to Canadian content with occasional murmurs about a mining disaster that has traumatized the town -- does it have any connection with the real one.

What it does have are flashbacks: lots and lots of flashbacks. It seems that old Al, the grandfather, was once in love with Sonny's granny and -- through possessiveness and pig-headedness -- let her get away. He is determined not to let the new generation repeat his past mistakes, which are acted out in some detail. This means that a story in which we cant take any interest is periodically interrupted by another that we cant remember. This plot-behind-the- plot builds to a surprise ending that I wouldn't dream of giving away, if only because its memory brings on incapacitating fits of the giggles; it also, to be fair, incorporates the only passage of half-way decent writing in the evening, when Al and his Mary meet for the last time in a New York night-club.

Their relationship carries whatever emotional weight there is, but it has nothing to do with the supposedly central dance theme, so the production seems pointless. I knew it would come back," says Al, an all-purpose guardian of tradition, about the swing music of his youth, and that's as close as this musical gets to acknowledging the really interesting question, which is why a recycled form of music should have hit so big as to be worth building a show around.

My opinion of the book (originally by Ted Swindley, "adapted by Ted Dykstra) must by now be fairly clear. The direction (Allen MacInnis) is limp and fatally unfocused; it takes ages to work out who, on a crowded stage, is meant to be singing the opening song.

The set (Jean Claude Olivier) is an unseemly arrangement of steps and balconies, with a blue sky occasionally thrown in for spectacle; the costumes (Mary Fulford-Winsor), which might have provided some style, are nondescript.

As for the score: Charles T. Cozens, music and lyrics, has provided a couple of accurate-sounding pastiche novelties and has fitted them out with persuasive orchestrations. He has also (with lyrical help from Stephen Mayoff) written the usual turgid, anonymous contemporary" numbers for the today scenes. Not only are these bad in themselves, but they invariably tell us things that we already know; you can see them coming a mile off. (You can hear them, too; I havent heard such heavy-handed orchestral intros in years.)

The new numbers are padded out with standards: vocal (Smile, Ill Never Smile Again, You Made Me Love You -- none of them my favourites, but certainly with more profile than any of the competition) and instrumental.

Among the latter is Benny Goodman's Sing, Sing, Sing: an extraordinary choice since it has already occasioned one of Bob Fosse's most spectacular dance-creations, and there are other big- band flag-wavers. Too many shows seem to be chasing too few songs; Fosse had to compete with Cabaret and Chicago, now Swingstep goes toe-to-toe with Fosse.

Given this daunting comparison, Sergio Trujillo has done pretty well; on the evidence of this show and the Stratford West Side Story he strikes me as more a first-rate dance captain than an original choreographer, but he gets his company hopping here with vitality, precision and a trademark display of vivaciously upstretched hands.

Few individuals emerge from the mix, but Paul McQuillan as Sonny is both a zoot-suited slitherer and a sympathetic leading man; Tamara Bernier makes grandma Mary the warmest of ghosts; and Douglas Chamberlain, a trouper, is Al to the half-life.

The shows "original concept" is credited to its producer, David A. Ballinger, once a member of an English pop group called the Barron-Knights, who had a couple of hits in the early 60s with some rock-bottom comedy numbers in which they imitated other groups; I think I can discern some artistic continuity here. The show does in fact have a strangely British air; it recalls the kind of hopeful musical muddles that used to stagger across London stages immediately before the ascent of Andrew Lloyd Webber. (There's a scary thought; what rough beast, its hour come at last, is at this moment negotiating in Bethlehem for a favourable corporate rate, in hopes of settling in for a 10-year run?)

It's the kind of thing I thought wed got beyond; certainly its appearance on one of Torontos prime stages -- one with its own distinguished, if brief, musical history -- is ridiculous.
