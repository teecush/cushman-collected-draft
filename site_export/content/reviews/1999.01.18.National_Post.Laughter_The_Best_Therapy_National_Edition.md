Wit's End

Winter Garden Theatre, Toronto

Sandra Shamas' shows are avowedly autobiographical, and this one's title is double-edged. Wit's End is a neat reference to her own state of desperation when her marriage ended just as she had decided to put her career on hold for a year. It's also, if she is to be believed, the name she gave to the remote country house that she took as part of the settlement, and where she still lives.

One of the responsibilities of art (a word I hate using but sometimes there's no alternative) is that you shut up about your own problems. You use them, of course, but disguised, and mixed in with other things, and your audience knows it. At least they used to. When an old-style comedian said "take my wife, please" nobody actually took him up on it. When one of his successors, usually female, says the same thing or its equivalent, there's a rustle of sympathy in the house, and we're out of the theatre and into therapy.

Of course, if the performance is funny, all is forgiven; successful comedy creates its own world. You know that there's some saving artifice because, however hilarious real life may be, it has to be worked on and re-ordered in order to get laughs on a stage. Most of the time, Shamas is very funny, and even when she isn't, she avoids self-pity; her references to her ex-husband, for example, are remarkably magnanimous.

But there are moments when she seems to be parading her suffering and expecting brownie-points for it. Even more uncomfortable is when she implicitly asks the audience's approval for having called her mother after a lapse of 20 years. Even if she isn't asking for it, she gets it. She plays to a very cosy house, ("my darling audience" she alarmingly calls them in the program): a pre-sold congregation that eliminates the sense of danger which has traditionally given stand-up comedy its distinction.

I musn't exaggerate. The sheer technical challenge of occupying a stage on her own for two-and-a-half hours means that hers is still a high-wire act. She offers a series of cartoon outlines on a slim but cohesive narrative thread. She has an impeccable sense of rhythm. Most of the time you take it for granted, as you should; in retrospect you realize how perfectly she has pitched every line, weighted every word.

The first half of the show is devoted to the break-up and its immediate aftermath, the second to her recuperation in the sticks. "I always feel," she says, "like an anthropologist around men," and it's true that in both settings she's a keen observer of the habits of the tribe. What she gets most acutely is her reaction to their reaction to her: a bracing double focus. She outlines the qualities of a neighbour. "He's sexist . . . and racist . . . and homophobic . . . and a white supremacist. And I like him very much. Because," (pause) "He has a snow plow."

That is comic poetry, or nearly, though for me the barriers really came down and the tears of laughter helplessly flowed, when she recounted a phone conversation with a female official who mispronounced her name and seemed very aggrieved when the mistake was pointed out to her. The note of possessive indignation is probably not recapturable in print, but, trust me, it was sublime.

She probably throws in too much detail in the second half, and you wonder how and when it can possibly end. But it does with satisfying suddenness; just as, at the opposite end of the evening, she has slid imperceptibly from introductory apologia into the meat of the occasion. I've come to her late but then Wit's End is probably a new beginning. Put me down, with reservations noted, as a convert.
