CREDITORS
Artword Theatre

August Strindberg, as almost any of his writings will attest, was not an easy man to please. But he would, I think, have approved of the new Artword Theatre on Portland Street, which has opened with a production of his short, sharp three-person play Creditors. The Artword is that rare phenomenon in Toronto, a comfortable small theatre. It also has a concentrated, intimate, head-on relationship between stage and auditorium -- exactly what Strindberg demanded for his chamber-plays, of which the prize specimens are this one and the better-known Miss Julie. Whether he would have been happy with the shallow production offered by the Actors Repertory Company is another matter. (The ARC is another new troupe devoted to matching up experienced actors with classic, and preferably unfamiliar, texts.) The form of Creditors is a sequence of three duets with a brief cataclysmic finale. Its content is a concentrated study of a destructive marriage, though with Strindberg thats pretty much a tautology.

Actually it's about two destructive marriages, superimposed. Adolph, an artist, is married to Tekla, a novelist; they are tracked down at a Swedish holiday resort by Gustav, Teklas discarded former husband and, in his own self- immolating words, a teacher of dead languages."

Out for revenge, and also for some kind of personal vindication, he sets about undermining their relationship. He does it mainly by messing, Iago-like, with Adolphs mind -- though this is an Othello in which the author thinks Iago has a case.

One of Gustav's tactics is to get Adolph thoroughly confused about whether he should be a painter or a sculptor, though it seems that whatever the medium the finished product comes out looking like his wife. Anyway, the play begins with Adolph sculpting: actually at it, before our eyes, according to Strindberg's stage direction. John Neville, the director, may have thought this would look like a cliche; in any event, he gives us Adolph sitting at a table with Gustav, amiably chatting. And so their long scene together goes; by its end, the ex-husband has persuaded his supplanter that Tekla has sapped his virility, stolen his ideas, cuckolded him with various young men, and generally sucked his blood.

Through all this, Alan Jordans Adolph just sits there, taking it on the chin and smiling. Strindberg wrote the play while in the throes of his own divorce from actress Siri von Essen and, as the most obsessively autobiographical of playwrights, he must have split himself between the two husbands. He may well have seen Adolph as a martyr but not, surely, as a wimp.

He also insisted that the play was "naturalistic, which this production takes to be a synonym for quiet." Considering whats going on, all this restraint seems remarkably unnatural. Later on Jordan throws a spiffy epileptic fit but the pressures that produced it have simply not been presented to us. We watch not a character in action but a participant in a debate, underlining his points with an assortment of earnest hand gestures.

This, though, is merely an extreme symptom of the production's general malaise. Brenda Bazinet's Tekla is a one-and-a-half-note performance. The single note is the teasing, patronizing one that she adopts for her entire scene with Adolf. The half- note, more interesting, is the confident erotic complicity that she briefly establishes when confronted by her ex.

(The sexual frankness in the play is, for a 19th-century piece, startling.)

Chuck Shamatas Gustav is indeed the most successful performance, a slug-like agent provocateur whose function in the play permits of a certain sinister blandness.

He does, though, make his authors insistence that the play was "humane, good-humoured, with all three characters sympathetic" seem more like wishful thinking than ever.

In theory, Strindberg is literature's supreme misogynist; in practice, once his characters reach the stage and take on flesh, both sexes seem equally diabolical, equally racked.

When he turns on the compassion, it comes out self-pitying and sentimental and I find the plays hard to take precisely because of his presenting his own hangups as an objective view of the entire human race.

Still, he not only based the character of Tekla on his own Siri, he actually offered her the role in the opening production and she accepted it, gleefully.

This suggests that though he may have been a bit extreme in his view of men and women, he was dead-on about playwrights and actresses.
