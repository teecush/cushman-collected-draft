So, there's this medieval thug called Robin Hood and he robs the rich. Period. He's obviously operating on the sensible maxim espoused by thieves of a later generation, that that's where the money is. He does not give any of the proceeds to the poor. That, according to the erudite program note to Mirvish Productions' The Heart of Robin Hood, a sentimental 16th-century addition to the original legend. In the show itself it's the brainwave of Marion, daughter of the Duke of York, who has heard of Robin's activities in the forest and has jumped to the conclusion that he must be acting benevolently. Where she can have got this idea is unclear; the romances couldn't have been written yet.

The Heart of Robin Hood is in fact a mess, with a few creative flashes. It was written by David Farr and directed by Gisli Orn Gar[eth]arsson: the same team that brought us last year's brilliant Metamorphosis. They aren't exactly repeating themselves; their new show is as relentlessly upbeat as its predecessor was devastatingly down.

There is the same emphasis on spectacular acrobatics, but here they aren't all executed by one transmogrified central character. This time everybody does it: goodies, baddies and in-betweens. The all-green set is dominated by a huge, practically vertical back wall. The preferred method of entrance is for the characters to appear from nowhere and slide down it. The first time this happens, it's astonishing. The subsequent hundred times it's still impressive, but decreasingly so. People also, with less intrepidity, climb up and down, which looks easier but may be harder. There are other visual trompes l'oeil scattered through the evening; I especially liked the duck that turns out to have unsuspected properties. As for plot: Marion ventures into the forest, and is duly disillusioned when Robin's robbers rob her. She returns, though, this time in male disguise, and sets up her own band public-spirited outlaws. She has a personal motive for going into the woods; she wants to avoid marriage to Prince John. He's taxing the peasantry so that he can raise an army to depose his brother King Richard the Lionheart, who is off on a crusade; or, as somebody puts it, fighting a meaningless war in the Middle East.

Marion, of course, falls for Robin at first sight, and he for her, though he's reluctant to admit it. He also finds her male alter ego easy to talk to, though as professional rivals they get off on several wrong feet. After they and others have outwitted John, the future king slinks off, threatening like Malvolio to be revenged on the whole pack of them. He doesn't put it in quite those words, but closely enough, as if to remind us that this show originated in Britain with the Royal Shakespeare Company, which I hope performed it with more finesse, though I wouldn't bet on it.

The script is full of Shakespeare allusions. Marion, like Kate in The Taming of the Shrew, has a petulant younger sister who can't be married until she herself is disposed of; like Viola in Twelfth Night she tells her lover, in male disguise, about her feelings as a woman; and like Rosalind in As You Like It, she flees to the forest accompanied by a jester and plays teasing verbal games with the object of her affections. Farr's script has a some nice moments in dealing with this last situation, but doesn't have the consistent wit to carry it off. The good jokes are few and very far-between.

Instead of jokes, there's jokiness. A country-rock band, Parsonsfield, provides songs whose relevance might be easier to judge if the words were distinguishable. As it is, the musicians seem to exist so that the real characters can draw attention to their non-dramatic presence. Haven't we had enough by now of shows that mock their own identity without creating one? The style is somewhere between Ross Petty and Monty Python, without the comic energy of either. Energy is not the same thing as mere physicality; the fights here are all done balletically, draining them of any sense of danger or excitement. Perhaps in compensation, there are scenes of nastiness. A lord has his tongue torn out. A tax-evader's hanged body is displayed, and John pursues a vendetta against the malefactor's two small children, one of whom is threatened with onstage decapitation. It seems the show wants to be taken seriously as well.

But it can't be, if only because, at the heart of The Heart of Robin Hood, there's a void, Gabriel Ebert's Robin having to oscillate between brute and patsy with nothing in between. It's Izzie Steele's Marion who makes all of the running, playing with guts and grace, and a self-deprecating humour in switching between genders; indeed this Maid outdoes even Rosalind, being not only smarter than her man but tougher. The production's final image is lovely. But it doesn't atone.

The Heart of Robin Hood runs through March 1 at the Royal Alexandra Theatre.
