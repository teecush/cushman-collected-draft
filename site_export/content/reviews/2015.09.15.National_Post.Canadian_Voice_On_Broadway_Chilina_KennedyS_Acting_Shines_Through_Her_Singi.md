For some half-dozen years, a favourite question among Canadian theatre audiences was "how long can we hang on to Chilina Kennedy?" Or, more precisely," how long before Broadway snaps her up?" Kennedy, a small vocal dynamo and one of the prized products of Sheridan College's musical theatre program, spent three years at the Shaw Festival playing ingenue roles in musicals: most delectably Eileen in Wonderful Town. She then spent three more years at the Stratford Festival, playing leading roles in musicals; these included the best of all possible Marias in West Side Story and an Evita who actually played the character, rather than the idea of a generalized rock-opera.

And now, the Broadway snapping-up having happened, she's in New York, being real as another real person. She's playing Carole King in a show based on the lady's life and songs and entitled Beautiful. At the moment in the show when her character is urged to make the transition from pure composer to singer-songwriter, she asks dubiously "Who wants to hear a normal person sing?" That's the best line in a jukebox musical whose songs are great to hear but whose script makes Jersey Boys seem like a literary masterpiece. The second-best line is the response she gets from a musician-friend: "Uh ... other ordinary people?" Kennedy plays, charmingly and believably, an ordinary person with an extraordinary talent.

She also, both in and out of character, conveys a modest delight in her own success. Here she is: the centrepiece of a Broadway musical with her name firmly at the head of the cast. "There are" she says in a phone conversation "no words." Still, she finds some.

She didn't create the role. It was originated last season by the American actress Jessie Mueller, who won a Tony for it. Kennedy had auditioned for it the first time around. She was already somewhat known in New York having played Mary Magdalene in the Stratford Festival production of Jesus Christ Superstar that went to Broadway. At that point her career became bi-national. She appeared in Hartford, Conn., in the comedymusical A Gentleman's Guide to Love and Murder. But she also came back to Canada to appear in the new musical Evangeline, and - less expectedly - to play Cecily in a Halifax production of The Importance of Being Earnest, opposite another Sheridan grad, Michael Therriault. Both actors represent a healthy contingent of Canadian performers who can switch, apparently effortlessly, between plays and musicals. Both were also in the Birmingham Conservatory, Stratford's post-grad academy for classical acting; Kennedy had a non-musical Stratford success in John Steinbeck's The Grapes of Wrath.

When the chance came to reaudition for Beautiful, she was prepared. "Fortunately, I'd kept the music I'd been working on the first time; usually I throw it away." She was only just in time. "I was 33 weeks pregnant - they don't let you fly after 36. I did the audition; they put me on a tape for Carole. I flew back to Canada, and went into labour from the stress." Soon afterwards she got a phone-call from her agent saying "I'd like two tickets for your opening on Broadway." It took her a moment to realize what he meant.

The bulk of the show deals with King's early career, as a composer to the lyrics of her husband Gerry Goffin (played by Scott J. Campbell, yet another Sheridan graduate and another Canadian, though one whose career has been entirely American). Her solo flights are kept till the end, though these songs, from the legendary 1971 Tapestry album, are the ones that get the biggest response, from an audience that plainly grew up with them. It's also the part of the legend that weighs most heavily on Kennedy, even though she was born several years after the record came out. "So many people attach themselves to Tapestry - what it meant to them in their lives - so they have expectations of me and the character." King herself is "so private, she didn't want to come see the show in the beginning" though when she did, she approved. She's described in the show - by herself - as "always wanting to please", and the storyline is largely about her learning how to please herself as well to outgrow Goffin as both a professional and marital partner.

Beautiful is running at the Stephen Sondheim Theatre, New York.
