CRITICS' PICKS

SPORTS

Its rare enough that teams in organized sport at any level can win 66 games in a row against comparable competition, and rarer still to get an opportunity to witness such a team. But tomorrow night at 8 p.m. at the Tait McKenzie Centre at York University, basketball fans can see the Carleton Ravens, 17-0 this season, winners of the last two Canadian championships and holders of the above-mentioned streak, play the Lions in an Ontario university mens East Division contest. York is perhaps one of the few teams that can claim a realistic chance of winning. The Lions are in second behind the Ravens with a 14-4 record and lost by only three points, 65-62, on the opening weekend of the regular season in Ottawa. Opening the bill, the 9-9 York women play the 3-14 Carleton women at 6 p.m. Ticket information, 416-736-5183.

THEATRE

Two of the city's best shows have had their runs extended. The Factory's Trout Stanley is worth seeing for Claudia Deys quirky, witty, image-drenched text, for Eda Holmes taut and imaginative direction and for the boldly expansive performances of Michelle Giroux, Melody Johnson and Gord Rand. It now goes an extra week, until Feb. 13 (416-504-9971). 'da Kink in My Hair will now run at the Princess of Wales until March 6. It's a rare opportunity to see a work that both comes from and speaks for a neighbourhood Toronto community (the Caribbean one) on a big central stage. There are some rough edges in the works transition from smallish play to biggish musical, but the best sections are truthful and vibrant; the very best are searing and joyful (416-872-1212).

There is, alas, no extension for A Whistle in the Dark (Berkeley Street Upstairs), though we must hope that it can be revived some time. Meanwhile, this week is your last chance to see the best ensemble performance in Toronto, not to mention a play that may be a masterpiece (416-368-3110).

DANCE

The Toronto dance community, becalmed throughout January, is surging ahead with a vengeance this week. Brazilian-born Newton Moraes unveils his techno-tribal Lamour en silence at Harbourfront Centre (see story above), while Toronto Dance Theatre convenes its annual choreographic invitational, Four at the Winch, at company headquarters (80 Winchester St., 416-967-1365). True dance connoisseurs, however, will not want to miss the incomparable Peggy Baker in a program of solos featuring live accompaniment by pianist Andrew Burashko and a pair of estimable dancing guests. The middle- aged but lithe Baker, eager to pass on her work, invites former National Ballet star Rex Harrington and modern dance doyenne Sasha Ivanochko to perform her powerful solo, Brute. Sounds like a compelling combination. Betty Oliphant Theatre (404 Jarvis), Saturday at 8 p.m. and Sunday at 4 p.m. Tickets from $22, 416-504- 7529 or at www.artsboxoffice.ca. Michael Crabb

THE TSO

An American in Paris is the theme as the Toronto Symphony Orchestra presents Jeffrey Kahane, conductor-piano, and Emmanuel Pahud, flute. Program includes Milhaud, Gershwin, Ibert. Tonight 8 p.m., Saturday 8 p.m., $27-$105. Roy Thomson Hall, 60 Simcoe St. 416- 593-4828. www.tso.ca.

Current and former members of the COC Ensemble Studio perform a recital featuring Canadian contemporary music at St. James' Cathedral, 65 Church St. Featuring Susan Ball, Peter Barrett, Michele Bogdanowicz, Robert Gleadow, Peter McGillivray, Steven Philcox, Kinza Tyrrell, Elizabeth Upchurch. 6 p.m., PWYC.
