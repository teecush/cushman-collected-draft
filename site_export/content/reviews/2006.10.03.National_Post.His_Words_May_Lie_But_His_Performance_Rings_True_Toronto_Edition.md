Theatre Review

HERE LIES HENRY

Buddies in Bad Times Theatre

Daniel MacIvor's Here Lies Henry -- accent on the ambiguous verb - - is the initial offering in the farewell season of his company, da da kamera. It's also a revival of a production first seen in 1995. MacIvor shares with Daniel Brooks the credit for "creating" it, with Brooks taking responsibility for "direction and dramaturgy" and MacIvor for "text and performance." The last of those four components is the most important. I didn't see the show the first time around, and my early exposure to MacIvor's work came through a series of shows that I didn't much like. But after Cul-de-Sac and now this one, I realize that my initial antipathy to MacIvor's plays was largely due to the fact that MacIvor himself wasn't in them.

I doubt if there is an actor in Canada who is better than he at doing what he does. What he does is assemble a battery of physical and vocal mannerisms and, at lightning speed and with immaculate control, fuse them into a coherent persona. The character he plays is invariably on some kind of edge. Henry, whom he plays here, is gay, lonely and angry. Or that's what he says; but he could equally well be straight, gregarious, happy (though I think that's a long shot) and answering to the name of Cedric.

For Henry is by his own admission, or his own proclamation, an inveterate liar. Though of course this, as in the famous Cretan paradox, could be a lie.

Which raises the question, one that arises in nearly every MacIvor play that I've seen (the recent A Beautiful View may be an exception): Who cares? It seems futile to wonder whether or not a fictitious character is telling the truth. It's especially so when he's the only character in the play, meaning there can be no checks or balances built into the structure. I know this may well be the point -- the unreliable narrator and all that -- but it's never struck me as a very interesting one.

There are certain points where MacIvor makes it work. Henry appears to us as a man with a load of grievances, willing to take the sins of the world on his shoulders after committing them through sheer spite; he's the guy who gave away Anne Frank's hiding place, he's the man who taught Mohammed Atta how to fly a plane "because New York thought it was so fucking great." This, though you may not believe me, is a very funny moment, not least because it carries a heavy moral charge.

One realizes that at this juncture Henry is certainly fantasizing, but one also accepts that he's the kind of man who, in certain frames of mind, would have wanted to do such things, and for just such reasons. At other times -- or throughout, really -- he has a lot of charm. This is partly because he's a frankly confessed loser, self-deprecating even at his most aggressive, and partly because MacIvor's skill is spellbinding in itself.

He's especially fine at raising running gags to a higher power; the tics that make Henry tick include a cough every time he mentions his father (a heavy smoker who has passed on his habit) and a very exotic kind of hiccup when he mentions his mother. These and much else are seamlessly woven into a fabric that also includes updated topical asides and occasional unembarrassing button-holing of the audience.

The naivest question you can ask of an actor is how he remembers his lines, but on this occasion one really would like to know, because it seems impossible. Brooks' direction puts MacIvor into a tight spare box bounded by Andy Moro's cruelly precise lighting and, from Richard Feren, a sound-score that is as much as any play will ever need. Some of the best craftsmen in Canadian theatre support one of its supreme talents -- who is still, as far as I can tell, in search of something substantial to say.

- Here Lies Henry runs until Oct. 15. 416-975-8555
