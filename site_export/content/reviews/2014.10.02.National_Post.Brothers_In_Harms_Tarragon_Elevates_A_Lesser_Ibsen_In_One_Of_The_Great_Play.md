Theat rereview

An Enemy of the People Tarragon Theatre, Toronto

I bsen scholars have generally ranked An Enemy of the People fairly low in their man's canon, maybe because of its spirited straightforwardness. That same quality, though, always makes it thrilling in the theatre: in the case of the new production at the Tarragon, immensely so.

Calling it new may be a stretch. It's a re-staging, by Tarragon's artistic director Richard Rose, of a production by the German director Thomas Ostermeier, one that he saw and admired at the Schaubuhne theatre in Berlin and that has since toured the world, though not as far as Canada. Rose's version reproduces the German design and staging, and he has had Maria Milisavljevic translate what was already Florian Borchmeyer's updated adaptation of the original. (Yes, we have an English translation of a German adaptation of a Norwegian play.) But of course Rose had to cast and direct the Canadian actors himself, and the results are superb.

It's surprising that Ibsen's play didn't get a rash of Canadian productions around the time of Walkerton. It could be even more topical now in a world where politicians at various levels discourage scientists and tells doctors to stick to their stethoscopes. The doctor here is Thomas Stockmann, consultant for the medicinal baths of a town that owes its new prosperity to the tourists who come to take its waters. He discovers that those waters are polluted by industrial waste, that they will cause disease rather than cure it, and that the whole system will have to be closed down and rebuilt, a process that will take a couple of years. He has the initial support of the local paper, which likes to think of itself as progressive, and of what the paper's proprietor calls, in the traditional translations, "the solid compact majority." He loses it when it's pointed out how much his proposals will cost. His principal opponent is his own brother Peter, identified in most versions as the Mayor, but in this one as just a member of the council. But he's also chairman of the baths; and when the doctor calls a public meeting to present his case, Peter stage-manages the proceedings so that they end with his brother being physically driven from the hall, denounced as "an enemy of the people."

All of that is still there in the new version. The scene, too, remains Norway; at least, no other place is mentioned and no characters' names have been changed. Some have been removed; sea-captain Horster, the only man in town to stand by Thomas and his family from first to last, has been eliminated. So has the doctor's similarly steadfast daughter, whose vestigial relationship with the newspaper's editor has now been transferred to her mother, by way of a few lines of dialogue that add nothing and go

nowhere. The Stockmanns' two school-age sons have also gone, replaced by a baby. The net result of all this is to make the doctor and what remains of his family more vulnerable. In the original, after briefly thinking of emigration, Thomas resolves to stay in town, home-school his boys, and preach his gospel of independence to however many of the uncorrupted will listen to him. This production ends with him and his wife, stripped of jobs and prospects and (a new touch) threatened with jail time, reeling against the wall.

That wall is none too solid. The set consists of chalkboards, decorated with drawings and slogans, telling us where we are, what time has elapsed, and what's on the characters' minds. When Thomas tells his brother just what bacterial demons are being unleashed, the words "e coli" take their place among the writings on the wall. The doctor's house is also the rehearsal space for an indie band consisting of him and his wife, Hovstad the editor, and a wannabe firebrand of a journalist named Billing who goes on singing and playing even when everybody else has had the decency to stop. ("Never have a couple in your band" says Billing, when the lady of the group goes off to attend to her infant: a line whose equivalent you would be hard-pressed to find in Ibsen.) Actors move the furniture around to transport the scene to the newspaper office.

In this production, the doctor holds his meeting in a theatre, represented of course by the theatre we're actually in. The houselights are turned on, and audience members are encouraged to participate. The night I went, most of us were too shy to contribute, and the vital questions were asked by people I took to be plants, who would be necessary anyway to keep the show on track and to give the actors their cues. Which is why I think this kind of theatre, though it can undoubtedly be exciting, is fundamentally dishonest; the performers have rehearsed, the spectators haven't, so it isn't an equal contest.

Forbidden, by bullying masquerading as democracy, to talk about the baths, Thomas instead takes aim at a bigger target: public ignorance, venality and stupidity. In this adaptation he does so by spouting an anarchist manifesto that on my visit escaped totally unheckled, possibly because its language is impenetrable. But he does in the end come back to Ibsen's original text, which is that the majority is always wrong. The majority then proves him right by physically and verbally assaulting him. Not that any of us actually in the Tarragon would have laid a finger on him, proving again the limits of audience participation.

Thomas, you may feel, has lost the plot; or at least enlarged it beyond recognition. But he was goaded into it. Ibsen, still smarting from the hostility directed at his previous play Ghosts, said that Thomas was like himself, only nicer; and he's usually played as an overgrown teddybear.

Joe Cobden, younger and slimmer than the norm, makes him more an overgrown student, still hanging with the other kids (hence the band); he's led a sheltered scientific life and, while his conclusions are unimpeachable, he's ill-equipped to confront or comprehend those who oppose them. Frustration leads him to hysteria, which seems to be a family trait. The revelation of this production has little to do with water-supplies or even cover-ups; it's that this is one of the great plays about siblingrivalry. Rick Roberts' Peter is a consummate portrayal of a politician, one with a skin as thin as his brother's but cunning enough not to expose it in public. (And he can think on his feet. At the performance I saw, he ad libbed a splendidly selfdeprecating response to an audience member's allegation that he was white and male.)

As the turncoats of the press Matthew Edison, Tom Barnett and Brandon McGibbon (painfully funny as the weathervane Billing) make a trenchant trio, while Richard McMillan oozes resentful malevolence as the doctor's factory-owning fatherin-law, the prime polluter on every level and the ultimate challenge to Thomas's integrity. All these performances are richly individualized while fitting perfectly into the frame. That it's a borrowed frame makes the achievement all the more remarkable. Anyone can have a concept; what counts is the execution, and this production executes in spades.
