MERRILY WE ROLL ALONG

Royal George Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

THE RETURN OF THE PRODIGAL

Court House Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

- - -

The received wisdom on Merrily We Roll Along is that it has terrific music and lyrics, sabotaged by a story that insists, suicidally, on telling itself to us backwards.

The first part of the assessment stands without question. Stephen Sondheim's score includes a sweeping, propulsive title tune; two marvellous ballads, the wistful Good Thing Going and the searing Not a Day Goes By (the latter surely the most moving love song he has written); the electric Franklin Shepard Inc., a nervous breakdown in song; the propulsively engaging Old Friends, nervous and confident at once; and much else.

George Furth wrote the show's book, which is a radical adaptation -- and updating -- of a 1930s play by those Shaw Festival favourites Kaufman and Hart. The old friends arrayed for our instruction are Frank Shepard, composer; Charley Kringas, lyricist; and Mary Flynn, novelist and, later, critic. The three first meet on a New York rooftop in 1957, year of Sputnik. That's the last time we see them. The first time we see them -- two of them, anyway -- is in 1976 in Hollywood where Frank is now some kind of big shot. To adapt the words of the absent and embittered Charley, we "really don't know what he does," but he is visibly on the verge of his second divorce and is presumed to have sold out. The rest of the show looks at how, and why.

Furth's dialogue is smart and sometimes telling; line by line I prefer this script to the one he wrote for the far more successful Sondheim show, Company. The problems are in the narrative and the characterization. Merrily may basically be about three people, but it throws in lots of others as well, and they pull the focus. Dramatizing a three-way friendship makes severe demands on a show's time and energy; whether backwards, forwards or sideways, the participants have to be established both as individuals and as a group.

An abiding frustration of Merrily, through all the many revisions it has undergone since its premiere, is its failure to provide its principals with songs that really expose them; which is ordinarily what Sondheim does best. Charley has one cracker of a number (Franklin Shepard Inc.) but it comes early in the show, and nothing else in his role measures up. Mary is permanently and unrequitedly in love with Frank; people mention this from time to time, but little is actually done with it. Frank, when seen in Hollywood, looks less creepy than ineffectual. His major mistake seems to be his actress-wife Gussie who appears snobbish, selfish and a bitch- on-wheels. Nothing we see of her later (and we see a lot) either deepens or complicates that impression.

People who claim the show would work fine if it just pulled itself together and moved in a straightforward fashion are, I think, crazy. Ending the evening with Frank in his misery would just be depressing. Taking him and his pals back to their youth allows for some easy irony of the "little do they know" variety but it also supplies a more authentic poignance. Memories of the future do begin to affect our view of the past, and there are moments that radiate in all directions; when Frank and Charley audition Good Things Going, which becomes their first hit and whose first line is "it started out like a song" it's not just a love song but an image of their collaboration and of its eventual fate ("a good thing going, going, gone.")

And, just on its own terms, the picture of young people trying to make it in New York in the 1950s is joyously effective, with a pile- up of lovely numbers. Bobby and Jackie and Jack (about the Kennedys) is a dead-on pastiche of the kind of nightclub revue number Sondheim himself never got to write at the time; the extended tour-de-force, Opening Doors, is a whirlwind of wit and promise. The concluding rooftop trio, Our Time, lifts the heart and tears at it, simultaneously; it's as stirring and moving a closing anthem as any musical has ever had. I don't know whether it was the authors' idea or this production's to bring the whole company on at the end to reinforce the threesome, but it's a bad one. It reduces what should be their moment to just another finale in a musical.

Jackie Maxwell's production is generally better in small scenes than big ones. The three chums are fine: Tyley Ross a Frank who survives by never growing up, Jay Turvey a clowning Charley who's at once boyish and avuncular, and Jenny L. Wright an alcoholically acerbic Mary. (How nice of the show to make its integrity-figure a critic.) In the second line, Charlotte Moore hardly manages to soften the fearful Gussie -- what we see of her Broadway-Vegas rendition of Good Thing Going suggests the boys had sold out long before Hollywood beckoned -- but Glynis Ranney is very appealing as her marital predecessor, except when she elects to slay the audience, rather than the other characters, with Not a Day Goes By. Gary Krawford is excellent as Joe, the producer Frank cuckolds, and who may be the best-developed character in the piece; connect his dots and you get a person.

The show's perennial casting-problem (how old?) has been met here by using mainly young or youngish actors; two Shaw veterans, Nora McLellan and Peter Millard, are fine in their featured roles as Frank's in-laws but look rather incongruous in the group scenes. These are not happily written anyway; we are given two separate sets of hangers-on who exist only to be shot down. Valerie Moore's choreography is largely limited to basic stepping, except when it takes off into an ill-advised imitation of Bob Fosse's Rich Man's Frug. Judith Bowden's sets have an oddly anachronistic Art Deco flavour.

The show maintains its reputation; it doesn't, as they say, "work," but is more rewarding than most that do. And you will not be able to get those songs out of your head.

- - -

Saturday night at the Shaw saw the return of The Return of the Prodigal, last season's revelation in better shape than ever, even after an electrical storm that caused its last quarter to be played in a half-light. St. John Hankin's acrid Edwardian comedy about the survival of the unfittest goes into areas few plays of its period, or any other, ever reach. Christopher Newton's production is among other things one of the richest portraits of a family, in all its chaos and contradictions, that I have ever seen on a stage; Bernard Behrens, Ben Carlson, Kelli Fox, Patricia Hamilton and Blair Williams are a perfectly interlocking clan, though Carlson has spoiled with some lily-gilding what was once a moment of perfect comic insouciance. Brigitte Robinson is now in steely command as the local dragon, and Roger Rowland is a delightfully useless physician, making spirited conversation with the birds when nobody else will talk to him.
