The opening line of Fernando Krapp Wrote Me This Letter is "Fernando Krapp wrote me this letter." It's a splendidly declarative beginning, not just for this play but for the new regime at Canadian Stage that it inaugurates. If the choice of script is a bracing departure from the previous management's staple menu of Broadway and off-Broadway naturalism, then so too is the manner of its presentation. The width and depth of the Bluma Appel stage have sometimes seemed an embarrassment; this debut production by the theatre's director Matthew Jocelyn glories in them. Staging and acting are extravagant, and yet ferociously disciplined.

It's a cosmopolitan style for a cosmopolitan piece. The play is a retelling by the German playwright Tankred Dorst of a story he found in a Spanish novella by Miguel de Unamuno. (The book dates from 1920, the play from 1992.) Fernando Krapp is an inordinately rich man; he can buy anything he wants, and what he wants is Julia, the most beautiful girl in what seems still to be a Spanish vicinity.

It's she who delivers the titular line, and she's furious about it. How dare he? Still, she accepts his proposal, and with it the proposition that beauty and wealth belong together. Call it a mix of practicality and arousal. Fernando and Julia settle down to a life replete with every comfort; as if to illustrate, a bearskin, with head still attached, descends from above and obligingly arranges itself into a rug.

It's one of two key furnishings. The other is a couch, on which Julia receives admiring visits from an impoverished local Count. The Count, who seems to suffer from a peculiarly refined strain of what D.H. Lawrence called sex in the head, pours out his adoration but seems disinclined to do anything about it. It isn't clear that Julia would want him to, but she is put out by

her husband's refusal to be perturbed. She wants him to be like Othello. I recall a school edition of the Shakespeare play, whose title page bore a quotation from Pushkin: "Othello wasn't jealous, he was trusting." I'd question that, but it's certainly true that Fernando isn't jealous, he's supremely confident -- of himself and his power. The idea that a pipsqueak like the Count -- a poor pipsqueak -- could be any kind of threat to him, is self-evidently ridiculous. He finds it equally ludicrous that Julia could resent his own goings-on with the female servants; he's Fernando Krapp, he can do as he wants, and that isn't what their marriage is supposed to be about. But if his libido can't be threatened, his sense of ownership can. When he believes, or professes to believe, that Julia has indeed succumbed, he summons the Count, terrifies and humiliates him. Later on, ostensibly oozing friendship and forgiveness, he terrorizes him all over again. The Count, obediently taking tea on the selfsame couch, literally turns cartwheels in his efforts to distance himself.

In a program note, Jocelyn invokes the Theatre of the Absurd, which I don't think is very helpful. (It seldom is.) Dorst's play is more socially, and even geographically, specific than anything in Ionesco or Beckett, though I suppose

you could think of Fernando's prolonged assault on Julia as Krapp's Last Rape. It's an assault that sends her for a time into a mental hospital. (Fernando has a couple of tame psychiatrists.) Her confusion about the facts is shared, on a less desperate plane, by the audience. The play's long title has a subtitle: An Attempt at the Truth. It's a quest that's both hopeless and essential. It seems, from what we've been shown, logistically unlikely that Julia and the Count should have had an affair. But you never know. Even Fernando doesn't know; and, unexpectedly and even movingly, the play explores the depths of his unknowing-ness. There's one thing that even the most powerful man in the world can't conquer. Tamburlaine the Great found that out. So does his modern equivalent.

The unpitying void of the stage is occupied, not filled, by Astrid Janson's ingenious revolving set, which has memories of old Castile secreted around its doors and window frames. Jocelyn, a professed Europhile, has translated the play himself, with a cunning, unsettling blend of the colloquial and the ornate; as a director, he has secured from his actors performances of remarkable physical and emotional boldness. In Ngozi Paul's Julia, the boldness sometimes tips over into coarseness, but she looks the part and is very fine in the play's last stretches, as bewildered victim and enforcedly elegant chatelaine. Ashley Wright captures Fernando in all his ineffable impregnability; portly and probably sweaty in a white suit, he's a man who doesn't have to care about appearances; he simply is and simply has.

Ryan Hollyman is piteously confused, and hilariously acrobatic, as the Count. Walter Borden completes the cast as Julia's father who, after vociferously encouraging her to sell herself, is consigned to benign contented silence, either pushing his grandchild's pram or, in a particularly delightful intrusion on a scene of high drama, twirling a yo-yo. The production's last image is of roses falling onto a garden wall. Like the plague of frogs in Magnolia, of which it's a gentler equivalent, it makes no factual sense, and it seems absolutely right.
