CINDERELLA

Pantages Theatre, Toronto

Fairy tales are natural sources for musicals. Your characters can burst unselfconsciously into song without offending anybody's sense of realism, and you can even have a happy ending. Its a wonder it isnt done more often.

Richard Rodgers and Oscar Hammerstein II did it very well in their television version of Cinderella, which was first seen in a fabled live production in 1957, and has had two further small- screen outings since. It has also, inevitably and often, been reworked for the stage, for which it is in many ways a natural. Its main drawback is simply that it's too short; we get to the ball in double-quick time, certainly by theatrical standards, and of course once we're there, the story has no choice but to roll rapidly to its close.

Still, better too fast than too slow, and the touring production that has reached Toronto this week is a brisk piece of storytelling, light on the magic but ready with it when needed. It is, frankly, a much better show than I had expected.

Two pop stars, of widely spaced vintages, get major billing, so maybe they should be dealt with first. Deborah Gibson makes a spunky, practical Cinderella (never referred to as Cinders) except for her singing, which unfortunately sounds as if she has a hole in her teeth. She has to do a lot of vocalizing; Eartha Kitt, playing Fairy Godmother, has to do very little, and what she does comes out the same way it has for decades: as a mocking, sensuous purr.

This may not be quite what you would expect of her character, but then, this is no ordinary conception of the role. This is a therapeutically minded spirit whose main trick is not to turn mice into footmen and a pumpkin into a coach (though she does both to the usual throat-lumping effect) but to turn her ward into the mistress of her own destiny. She is a Do-It-Yourself Kitt.

The lady could, in fact, be more effectively imperious than she is. She can still turn a witty line, but her chief function here is to stand around looking extremely well preserved. She is indeed best when stillest, especially when making an unheralded, admonitory appearance at the ball. There is a strong hint in this adaptation that Cinderellas fairy godmother is actually her dead mortal mother returned, you might say, to Eartha.

Once upon a time, in a kingdom far away -- it was called England - - somebody waved a wand and turned the R&H Cinderella from a musical into a pantomime. This version, however, has little truck with that. Nobody plays to the audience, nobody steps out of character. The one apparent panto touch is the casting of a man, Everett Quinton, as the Stepmother, but his performance turns out to be the most serious and uncompromising of the evening. (The few bits of obvious clowning seem added-on and unwanted.)

This Stepmother is constantly on the verge of becoming a really interesting character: She is believably and chillingly cruel, worried about her own future as well as that of her offspring, obviously consumed with jealousy of her late husbands first wife, and taking it out on their daughter. This is the first time I have ever had any sense of what life in that household might have been like; and one's sympathy for the heroine, who apparently constitutes the entire domestic staff, increases tenfold.

NaTasha Yvette Williams and Alexandra Kolb are less successful at humanizing The Ugly Sisters (who aren't called that, but nobodys fooled). The writing of their roles has been coarsened down the years, to judge from what Ive read of the original production.

I have to rely on my reading, because that first TV version pre- dated the arrival of videotape. The current program credits Hammerstein with book and lyrics, but also lists Tom Briggs as having adapted the show for the stage "from the teleplay by Robert L. Freedman," presumably the one for the 1997 version starring Brandy and Whitney Houston. The Briggs text is a considerable improvement on that atrocity, but it probably falls short of the Hammerstein standard of urbanity.

Prince Christopher (not Charming, though Paolo Montalban seems pleasant enough) is shown fending off the matchmaking plans of his grumbling mom (Leslie Becker) and bumbling dad (Ken Prymus). These royal encounters are mildly entertaining, but they must once have been wittier than they are here.

The Sweetest Sounds, a later Rodgers song with his own lyric, has been imported to suggest the unacknowledged mutual yearnings of hero and heroine; sung before we have heard a note of the original score, it doesn't wreck anything, but it does spoil it, especially as it's staged in the most standardized of village squares, full of the most standardized village dancers. Here we really do seem to be in for a panto.

Gabriel Barres direction, James Youmans designs, and Ken Roberson's choreography all improve markedly later on, especially at the ball where the dancing -- until Cinderella arrives to wake the party up -- goes, literally and wittily, like clockwork.

There is, though, one excrescence in the staging: a strange double dose of Disney. As in the old cartoon, Cinderella at home is surrounded by friendly livestock, in this case one cat, one bird and four bewhiskered rodents, all co-existing in idyllic harmony. They are represented by puppets, worked Japanese style by visible actors. This is abruptly introduced and quite out of style with the rest of the production; it looks like The Mouse King.

What truly carries the show is what carried it first: the songs. The score is the best of Rodgers' late (i.e., anything after The King and I) period. Cinderellas In My Own Little Corner is the perfect set-up number, wistful but strong. (So who needs The Sweetest Sounds?) The Prince Is Giving a Ball, with its endless lists of improbable royal names, is very funny, or would be if the ensemble here seemed in on the joke.

The grand waltz Ten Minutes Ago is not one of Rodgers subtlest explorations of three-quarter time, but its sweep is exactly what is needed, not merely to accompany the ball but to carry us to the romantic heart of it. From there, duly enchanted, we move with perfect ease to the quieter consummation of the show's one authentic ballad, Do I Love You Because You're Beautiful. And there is more; not enough to make a great theatre score, but that was never the original intention. What we have is a makeshift, but of the nicest kind.
