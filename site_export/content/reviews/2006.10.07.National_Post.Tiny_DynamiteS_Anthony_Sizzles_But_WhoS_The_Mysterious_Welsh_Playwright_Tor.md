robert.cushman@hotmail.com; Tiny Dynamite runs through Oct. 8 at Tarragon Extra Space, 416-531-1827.

Toronto theatre programs are usually flush with statements of intent, and with biographical information about the actors, directors, designers and technicians responsible for the play in hand. They can go strangely quiet, though, when telling us about the author unless he or she is actually on the premises.

"He or she" is right in relation to Tiny Dynamite, which is the inaugural production of an outfit called Theatre Smash. Having searched both program and press kit, cover to cover, I still have no idea about the life and career of the playwright. I don't even know whether the person, whose name is Abi Morgan, is male or female. It may be superficial to care about such things but I can't help wondering.

I have gathered that Morgan is Welsh, which with that name is no particular surprise, and the play does remind me of the kind of lyrical-psychological drama that was much in vogue in Britain's fringe theatres some 20 years ago and, very likely, still is. ("Theatre Smash develops and produces contemporary theatre work with a global twist," says the press release. I think that means they do plays from other countries.) It seems to have been discreetly Canadianized for this production, but that's largely a matter of accents. Like many plays of its type, it's stronger on mood than on substance.

It concerns two young men, lifelong friends, called Lucien and Anthony. Lucien is a risk analyst, by nature and by profession. Anthony is contrastingly rash; as a boy he was struck in the chest by lightning and he's been attracting electricity, literally and metaphorically, ever since. The experience doesn't seem to have impaired his brain, though Lucien sometimes treats him as if it has. It has robbed him of socialization skills or, perhaps more accurately, of the desire to have them. In the two men's shared past is the memory of a girl, now dead, who was important to both of them.

Taking their annual holiday together -- in the country, by the water -- they encounter Madeleine, a free-spirited part-time waitress whose presence brings everything back. (If this were Proust, she'd be a pastry.) Much compulsive retelling of their old story ensues -- the author is unlikely to have seen The Drawer Boy, but to a Toronto audience, the resemblance is striking -- leading to a kind of exorcism.

We have to take an awful lot on trust, principally the effect on the men of their new acquaintance. Just why does she stir up these memories? Would any girl have done? If that's the case, the situation isn't very interesting. If it isn't, we're hindered by the fact that Madeleine as written is just your standard theatrical waif; Perrie Olthuis, the first-time professional actress who plays the part, can't do much to give her definition. Also we have nothing to measure her against; however much they talk about her, the ghost in the boys' past remains just that, a ghost. The play presses for an emotional response it doesn't justify.

Then, too, Lucien, whose adventure the play turns out to be, is so reticent that the piece gets to seem pointless; other people have to tell us what he feels. Steven McCarthy plays him gaunt, gauche and -- we assume -- sensitive, but all the attention goes to Anthony who, as played by Dylan Trowbridge, is not merely magnetic but charismatic. This is a performance with a bubbling interior, a stew of exuberance and resentment, and also with a preternaturally sharp outline. The electricity the character courts and embraces seems literally to surround him, even when the lighting and sound effects aren't going full blast.

The play's technical and atmospheric demands are altogether extremely well-handled in Ashlie Corcoran's production. All three actors are required to take headers into the wings, where a lake is supposed to lurk, and their unnervingly persuasive dives are matched by equally blood-curdling splashes.

Now what else has Abi Morgan written?
