THE AWAKENING

Tarragon Theatre, Toronto

Julian Garner is a British playwright who has lived for 15 years in Scandinavia and apparently writes in both English and Norwegian. His play The Awakening comes to Toronto bearing the somewhat rarefied accolade of Best Norwegian Play of 1989; about 100 years ago, insular Anglophones were probably making similarly frivolous remarks about Ibsen.

The play is set in the 1930s on an island off the Norwegian coast and concerns the spiritual progress of a simple-minded child- murderer. Its Canadian production is encrusted by a thick film of worthiness, involving the singing of mournful and incomprehensible folksongs by an otherwise totally extraneous character.

The package could hardly be more remote or forbidding.The play turns out to be enthralling.

We meet Johannes, the protagonist, in solitary, obsessively reciting passages from the Bible and being beaten up by a guard. He is rescued by Captain Agnes, a formidable prison visitor from the Salvation Army ("I am not," she stresses, "your run-of-the-mill do- gooder,") who has turned him on to religion and now gets him a place working on a farm, part of a national scheme for the rehabilitation of loonies.

That last brisk term is the one favoured by the farmer, a young lady named Unn, who has been running the place single-handed for years. In fact, ever since Agnes, whom she had looked on as a mother, left the island to follow her vocation, or advance her career, or both.

Unn has felt bitter ever since, and initially she takes her bitterness out on Johannes. She thaws somewhat when he turns out to have a natural gift for gathering eggs and further still when, after a little coaching, he proves adept at splitting wood. (This is done on stage. So is the gutting of a fish. Actors these days need varied skills.) She completely melts when he reports in alarm that a fishing net is full of holes. She bursts out laughing; probably her first laugh in a very long time.

Johannes meanwhile has arrived in his Bible study at the ripe erotica of the Song of Solomon (its second appearance on a Toronto stage this year) and it turns him on no end. From there, it's a straight road to romance, and the start of the second act finds Unn happily carrying Johannes' child.

There are many rough edges in Sue Miner's production, but it gets this central relationship absolutely right. Joris Jarsky's Johannes is perfect: childlike, obsessive, devout, capable of deep hurt, deep affection, and deep anger. Anne Page's flaxen, angular Unn is also fine, at least in her scenes with him.

She is a lot less convincing, a lot less fresh, when she comes up against Randi Helmers' Capt. Agnes; the actress seems unable to find any kind of key to this character and so tries, as actors will, to batter it open. (The director is no help here; whenever Agnes appears, the staging goes awkward.) This is unfortunate since Agnes' revulsion at the idyll she has engendered should lead us to another level.

We have been led -- by perfectly honest dramatic methods -- into identifying with Johannes, and rooting for his happiness and that of his lover. They share the awakening of the title. But Johannes' initial crime, which he never denies or excuses, is that he raped and murdered an eight-year old girl. So there are complexities here and to expose them (they can't be resolved) the author resorts to desperate expedients. The vicious prison guard reappears and turns out to be the dead girl's avenging father.

(Peter Smith gives a strong performance in this role, but it is strength unharnessed to anything as practical as a characterization.)

First he confronts Agnes, raging at her religion as mere useless "words," then the couple, and at this point the story, or my comprehension of it, goes very cloudy.

Regardless, we end with Johannes back in jail, the pregnant Unn denied access to him, Agnes banished from the prison service, and the bereaved father looming in an unfocused way, as he has tended to do throughout: four broken lives, though maybe not irreparably so.

It's a bleak conclusion, although -- the play being a good one that has held us with its story and involved us in the thoughts and emotions of its characters -- not a depressing one.

I don't know what the author is finally saying. Maybe that we must all live with the consequences of our own actions and of other people's, which seems reasonable.

I mentioned Ibsen, and Garner, his adopted compatriot, seems to share his forebear's fascination with, and skepticism of, Christian ethics, which gives his play a bracing, unfashionable flavour.

It's performed against interlocking slabs of what I take to be (isn't it good?) Norwegian wood, which the actors move about, though it's never clear to what extent this is part of the plot.

That folk-singer mostly stays at the side, but occasionally wanders through the action as if she were part of it. Why don't they ask her to muck out the hen-coop?
