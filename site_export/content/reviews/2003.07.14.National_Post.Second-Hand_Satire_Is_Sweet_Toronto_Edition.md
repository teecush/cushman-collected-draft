NO MAN'S LAND

Premiere Dance Theatre, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - In future years people are going to tell their children how William Hutt, in Soulpepper's production of Harold Pinter's No Man's Land, trod daintily around a carpet, taking elaborate care never to actually touch it, in order to get at a glass of whisky. Hutt plays Spooner, an unsuccessful poet (or so he says), who finds himself in the house of Hirst, a successful poet (or so he says).

Hirst has apparently met Spooner in the literary reaches of North London ("do you often hang about Hampstead Heath?") and asked him back for a drink. Or for many drinks: There are few things in this play of which one can be sure, but if there is one near-certainty it is that Hirst is an alcoholic.

No Man's Land was first staged in 1975 by Britain's National Theatre, directed by Peter Hall and with performances by John Gielgud and Ralph Richardson that became legendary. I wouldn't claim that Albert Schultz's production, in which Hutt squares off against the Hirst of Peter Donat, improves on the original, but it's comparable, and in a couple of ways I prefer it. It makes less grandiose claims for itself; it has a homelier, more approachable setting (by John Thompson) and is played as the highest comedy. Pinter uses the English language better than any other living dramatist, and the wit of this production's first scene, both spoken and implied, has the audience purring with suppressed delight.

Hirst has success, or its trappings; Spooner, envious and possibly admiring, wants in. Spooner, whose speech sometimes suggests a pastiche of T.S. Eliot, has, we gather, sunk fairly low in the world, and earns a crust by clearing the tables in the pub where Hirst picked him up. Now he sits in the corner, stooped at attention, looking like an especially unkempt professor, in white crumpled hair and a brown corduroy suit. Anxious not to offend his host, or to seem to presume (hence his careful circumlocution of the carpet), he can still not resist tilting at him whenever possible, and Hutt's presentation of these discreet jabs is a miracle of sustained timing. He presents a civilized mind, angry at its own corruption; as with Gielgud, our awareness of the actor's own cultural status adds a teasing element to the effect, but does not create it.

As Hirst, Donat has to work harder at creating the massive cocooned eccentric than did Richardson, who could just be. Donat is a hawk-like figure with claws for hands; claws that twitch convulsively in expectation of the next drink, and a voice that's somewhere between a bark and a croak. He says comparatively little, but his impassivity in itself keeps Spooner on the hop. Barely moving from, or even in, his chair, he still commands the room until, after falling to the floor, he leaves it, slowly and on all fours. ("I have known this before" says Spooner; the first utterance, mournful and oracular, of what is to prove a recurring refrain.)

Suddenly, Spooner is surprised, by Foster and Briggs, two younger Londoners who seem to be Hirst's minders, and are not at all pleased to find their turf invaded and their authority potentially challenged. More vicious cat-and-mouse games ensue. The play declares itself an up-market reprise of Pinter's masterpiece, The Caretaker, presenting the same triangular power-struggle between the resident, the intruder, and the guardian -- or, in this case, guardians.

It takes a swerve when, next morning, Hirst reappears transformed, dapper in blazer and cravat. Pinter is a cricket fanatic, and Donat looks here as if he might be on his way to Lord's for a Test Match. For that matter, all four characters have the names of famous cricketers, and there is a sequence in which Hirst and Spooner debate, in cricketing terms, the talents of a former mutual lady friend; though the punchline -- "in other words, did she google?" -- has now, of course, taken on different connotations.

It may seem strange that the two men, apparently new acquaintances the previous night, should now be discussing in some detail a shared past. As a matter of fact, it is strange, and two rival explanations -- neither, of course, confirmed nor denied -- suggest themselves. One is that Hirst, on the previous night, was just too drunk to recognize Spooner, and that was Spooner was biding his time. The other -- which I think more likely, and certainly more interesting -- is that Hirst is fantasizing again (he addresses one of his servants by a different name) and that Spooner, with brilliant improvisational skills, is continuing to play up to him, working off a real resentment in an imaginary framework.

The reason I find it more interesting is that the gilded world the two of them now conjure up, of country houses and university and smart adultery and tea on the lawns -- is fine brittle satire, but is inescapably second-hand. It has nothing like the reality of the play's present, which reasserts itself in Spooner's last desperate speech, listing his qualifications for the role of Hirst's companion. Hirst might, Spooner suggests, address a literary society of which he is secretary and which meets upstairs at the pub, the room, the milieu, and their implications for both men leap before our eyes. This speech is one of the high-points of contemporary drama and, as spoken by Gielgud, it was a perfect fastidious marriage of text and performance. Hutt's delivery was, on the first night, a trifle ragged, but he superbly conveyed the need behind the cadences.

Schultz's direction orchestrates the battles with an acute sense of who sits where and who moves where. Especially sweet is the moment in which Foster and Briggs, sniffing defeat, make as one for the drinks table. The two wide boys are admirably played by Stuart Hughes, who has a daintiness of threat that suggests Mick Jagger, and Michael Hanrahan, though his accent sometimes prompted the suspicion that he would be happier if his character were Irish.

Each of them is granted a set-speech, one about Balinese girls who are thoroughly available at home but untouchable when they sign up at London language schools, and the other about the impossibility of ever finding your way out of a West End thoroughfare called Bolsover Street. Such is the power of Pinter that for years after the premiere, Londoners who had seen the play and who found themselves driving toward Bolsover Street, would freeze in terror.

I have never been happy with the ending, in which Hirst's announcement that he is changing the subject "for the last time" is taken to entail universal stasis. Even in so verbal a piece, this seems to place excessive weight on a mere turn of phrase. (A play on words in a play on words.) But it has a chilly effectiveness, especially here with Donat at once terrified and terrifying and the octogenarian Hutt, who has been using his quiet and silky side, suddenly sounding out like King Lear. He is a phenomenon. The new Toronto season starts here, at what may very well prove its highest point.
