THEATRE REVIEW

THE PASTOR PHELPS PROJECT

Cameron House, Toronto

---

This year's Summerworks festival was supposed to start with a protest. The Pastor Phelps Project, subtitled "a fundamentalist cabaret," was due to be picketed by members of the Reverend Fred Phelps' Westboro Baptist Church from Topeka, Kan. This congregation, notorious for their vociferous insistence that "God hates fags," had already signalled their attitude to the show pretty clearly by denouncing it as illiterate garbage: a piquant criticism for fundamentalists to make, even on the unlikely supposition that they'd actually read the script, since a substantial proportion of it consists of verbatim quotes from the Bible. Even the name of the group presenting it, Ecce Homo, is as much biblical as it is homosexual.

Anyway, the Phelpsians were prevented from entering Canada, let alone Toronto, so instead of protests, all we were left with on opening night were protests against the protests: local groups bearing signs reading "keep your hate out of Canada" (they've had to) and, more wittily, "you're not in Kansas any more" (though presumably they are).

The demo may have turned out to be a damp squib but the show itself, written and directed by Alistair Newton, surprisingly didn't. I say "surprisingly" since the Rev. Phelps' mission is so transparently hateful (in both senses) that satirizing it is like mounting an attack on a barn door; we're talking, after all, about people who were barred from this country because of their plans to picket the funeral of Tim McLean, the Greyhound bus victim, on the grounds that his beheading was a divine punishment for Canada's tolerance of sexual deviance. (Though, since they level this charge at virtually every state in the Western world including their own, it's hard to see why they singled out this one. For the publicity, I guess.) However, The Pastor Phelps Project is rousingly performed, with a lot of well-drilled hymn-singing that doesn't always come across as mockery. The show acknowledges complexity, even if it doesn't pursue it.

Fred Phelps himself appears as a frothing gesticulating figure in a fairground peepshow: Put a penny in the slot and activate him. We learn some surprising things about him, including the fact that he established his legal practice by defending African-Americans in civil-rights cases. Later, in a reversal of the old saw about strange bedfellows, we find him coming in for harsh criticism from the likes of Bill O'Reilly and Jerry Falwell. Admittedly they -- O'Reilly, anyway -- are attacking less for his sexual views than for his disrespect for U. S. soldiers killed in Iraq and Afghanistan. They, too, are signs of God's anger with sodomite America; it seems that, in the Phelpsian gospel, nearly everything is. At moments like these, the show seems to find it hard to decide which side it detests most, which is a kind of balance. It also brings in Ann Coulter, reeling off some of her craziest and most-recycled quotes, none of which have anything to do with Phelps. (Well, she does call Al Gore a fag.) She seems to be there purely so the show can parade another hate-figure (again in both senses). If they want to take her on, better they should give her her own show. Or, better yet, that they shouldn't.

Phelps, as portrayed by Carey William Wass, does have a kind of demented dignity; he sticks to his guns, to put it mildly. It's possible to see him as an originally good man gone bad, or at least mad. We also meet other members of his philoprogenitive family, notably his daughter and principal mouthpiece Shirley Phelps-Roper (excellent played by Evalyn Parry) whom we see interviewed by Tyra Banks for that well-known liberal bastion Fox News. Tyra is shocked by Shirley, and by the sheltered upbringing Shirley's wished on her own daughters, and so I guess we should be, but she too comes out of it rather well; at least nobody could call her a hypocrite.

The show skates over some of the Phelpses' other phobias: of the Jews for instance, who they claim are perpetrating the real Holocaust (and who apparently ran Nazi Germany. Not even pre-Nazi, but Nazi). And of Ireland. And Sweden. Much of what we do hear is cut-and-paste: a well staged version of the relevant pages from Wikipedia. But at the end the production transcends itself. A young boy, presumably with gay inclinations, has been watching and listening to Phelps and has been scared by him. Now, though, he prevents himself from being outright traumatized by conjuring up his own imaginings of the story of David and Jonathan; it's just as Biblical as that one homophobic verse in Leviticus, and it carries a lot more weight. - The Pastor Phelps Project runs until Aug. 17.
