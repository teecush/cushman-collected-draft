THEATRE REVIEW

The Life and Times of Mackenzie King Cameron House, Toronto

The Rez Sisters Factory Theatre, Toronto

The latest episode in Video Cabaret's staged comic strip of Canadian history is not quite as good as its predecessor, The Great War, but it still makes a smashing evening, with the usual complement of dazzling quick changes of both costume and character, magnificently exaggerated wigs and costumes, pinpoint lighting and spectacular action on a tiny stage.

The Life and Times of Mackenzie King is as much about the times as the life. Its a high-speed tour of the roaring 20s and the hungry '30s. The title character is portrayed by Paul Braunstein, on a beguiling note of cankered winsomeness, as a mother's boy whose mother happens to be dead, but who compensates by contacting her spirit and consorting with a highly supportive married lady while her husband dances attendance and delivers messages. Hes in office for much of the first act, out of it for most of the second, but were always glad to see him, and though Michael Hollingsworth's script and direction do their conscientious best to keep him in play, he does have to do a lot of hopeful hopping on the sidelines.

His conservative opponents, though, provide plenty of malicious entertainment, with Rick Alan Campbell's Arthur Meighen shooting himself in the foot as he wonders why vacating the throne doesnt make you the power behind it, and Jacob Jamess R.B. Bennett gleefully and gluttonously grinding the faces of the poor beneath the famous iron heel. Although I was surprised to see his eyes lighting up at the thought of concentration camps on the German model, a full three years before the Nazis came to power.

It may be a less serious anachronism, but I also felt that when the gangster Al (Greg Campbell) professes himself so happy that he can't even remember the lyrics of Top Hat, White Tie and Tails, the real reason might be that they hadn't been written yet. Hes part of the shows other half, which deals with the people who aren't in the history books, and part of his function is to remind us that prohibition and racketeering were games that Canadians could join in, too.

The prohibition stuff, though, takes up too much of the first act, thereby having an unfortunate knock-on effect on the second, which gets to feel longer than it actually is.

One of the triumphs of The Great War was that it managed to be humanly affecting (a new quality for VideoCab) while still maintaining its satiric edge. It brings off something of the same with Joe Slomkowski, its all-purpose oppressed worker and indomitable anti-Fascist, but this time it can't avoid a generic sentimentality. Joe (well played by Mac Fyfe), gets his head broke, volunteers for one of Bennetts work camps before leading a revolt there, and finally takes himself off to the Spanish Civil War. Like his spiritual cousin in The Grapes of Wrath, hes everywhere.

Meantime the title character, back in power, is getting starry-eyed about Hitler, proving Hollinsgworth's indisputable point that you can't trust anyone. At the end, and in a startlingly effective break with the prevailing staging conventions, King gets a vision of the carnage to come; it terrifies him while whetting our appetite for the next instalment, which of course will be the Second World War. Tomson Highways 20-year-old play The Rez Sisters owes an acknowledged debt to Michel Tremblays Les Belles Soeurs. It also recalls another sister play, the one about the three who longed to go to Moscow. Highway's characters, living on a native reserve, yearn to go to Toronto to participate in the biggest bingo in the world," and they actually get there, prompting the suspicion that Chekhov's siblings didnt know when they were well off.

The play stands up pretty well, especially in its sharp evocation of the conditions the women are relying on their bingo winnings to alleviate; one of them dreams of paving the streets and thereby setting off an economic revival. The seven female characters arent all literal sisters; in fact their precise relationships, in Ken Gass's production, remain blurry throughout.

The casting is ethnically diverse: a perfectly fine idea given that this isn't one of those plays (from Othello to A Raisin in the Sun) that depend on identifiable racial differences. It does mean, though, that the sense of community has to be established through acting alone and this production is too unfocused, visually and verbally, for that to happen.

We get a series of disconnected vignettes, some clearer than others, though only one actress, Jean Yoon, manages to build a character with a clear relationship to everyone else; the others - notably Kyra Harper yearning for a fully functioning toilet - flourish only in monologues.

One scene, on a very oddly shaped bus, does manage to create a mood. Against that, set the treatment of Nanabush (Billy Merasty), an allpurpose spirit, who hovers around in a manner that's not so much mysterious as inexplicable.

- The Life and Times of Mackenzie King runs until Nov. 27; The Rez Sisters until Dec. 11. For tickets, visit videocab.com and factorytheatre.ca.
