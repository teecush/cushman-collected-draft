GODSPELL

Bathurst Street Theatre, Toronto

This isn't Sunday-school camp, says Jesus to his disciples, as they arrange themselves in giggling formation for a military drill number. Hes wrong for once.

There was always a strong whiff of Sunday school about Godspell, the musical that repackaged the New Testament in the peace-and-love accessories of the 1960s. (The show dates from 1971 which, by theatrical standards, made it pretty hot off the mark.) The camp ingredient is supplied by the present revival which, coming to Toronto from the Georgian Theatre Festival, has summer theatre written all over it. It overlays the original with the pop-culture references of its own time, and is cheerfully indiscriminate about how and when it uses them. A show that was always irritating has become unbearable.

It is not in its own terms badly done. Michael McGinns staging has a lot of energy and a respectable amount of precision. Godspell's original productions are famous for breeding stars: Victor Garber, Andrea Martin, Martin Short and Gilda Radner in Toronto, David Essex, Jeremy Irons and Julie Covington in London. I don't know that any of the present bunch will make it to that level, but they should all have futures.

There are nine in the cast, though only two have identifiable roles. Adam Brazier gives us Jesus as hip holy fool, a gangling combination of clown and juvenile lead with a mask of innocence, a deep reserve of funny voices and a well-camouflaged broken heart; watching him is like seeing the Bible story acted out by Jim Carrey, however that may grab you.

Duff MacDonald is equally resourceful as the sardonic raven- locked character-man of the group, who starts out as John the Baptist and at some unspecified moment morphs into Judas Iscariot. Everybody sings well.

The material has its merits, too. The script is by John-Michael Tebelak, out of the St. Matthew Gospel; it begins and ends with dramatized narrative, but devotes much of the time between to acting out parables.

Tebelak doesnt seem to have worked much since but presumably he hasnt needed to. The score is by Stephen Schwartz, who has subsequently had an up-and-down Broadway career but is a considerable talent. His lightly rocking tunes (Day by Day and the others) sound, if anything, more infectious now than they did then, and his lyrics are refreshingly neat. They can even be powerfully dramatic. When this Jesus is on the cross he sings, "Oh God, I'm dying" to which the chorus respond Oh, God, you're dying, a simple change of possessive that shifts a remarkable amount of weight. The only thing wrong with the songs is that there arent enough of them, or rather that theres too much of everything else.

The actors in this production wear face-mikes: Those ugly and aggressively contemporary contraptions that make them look from the middle of the auditorium as if they've all grown moustaches, and from the back as if they're smoking cheroots. But basically they are got up as performers in this show as they have always been: a bunch of red-nosed troubadours, light of brain and pure of heart.

They begin by riffling through the pronouncements of great thinkers from Socrates to Sartre; these sound bites go by too fast to be understood but the implication seems to be that all these well- meaning intellectuals were wasting their time, and that all we need is love. That, at any rate, is what the show proceeds to give us in large sugary helpings.

This is a very soft show based on a very tough source. The Biblical parables are terse moral paradoxes whose implications, if people in any number were actually to act on them, would be cataclysmic. In Godspell their meaning, though not actually falsified, is smothered under a blanket of ingratiation: goofy young showfolk acting out all the parts and making nice.

There may have been some naive and sentimental logic to this in the shows counter-cultural youth; people then were able to identify their own sentimental and very commercial sappiness with the texts astonishing blend of fierce anti-materialism and all-embracing compassion. (It makes the term "religious right" seem a bewildering oxymoron.) Now, of course, all this idealism is long gone, so there might be room for a tough production that was consciously against the grain.

This one, though, wears the original concept like a quaint but endearing set of old clothes that needs tarting up a bit. They sense that it's basically a revue, so they bring it narcissistically up to date by stuffing in references to currently recognizable movies, songs, catch-phrases and stars. This may be all right in principle but the practice is outrageously self-indulgent; they throw everything in, from Bill Clinton to Celine Dion. Revue of all forms needs discipline; this is more like an out-of-control English pantomime.

I don't remember the original show being this long -- just when I thought it had to be coming to an end, Jesus prophesied an intermission. This was kind of him, but it was less than the mercy I craved.
