THEATRE REVIEW

In the Heights Toronto Centre for the Arts

A renowned U.S. director once made a distinction between "energy," which may be the most desirable of all theatrical qualities, and mere "energeticness," which isn't. The touring production of In the Heights that's arrived in Toronto is full of energeticness but the pace, tension, focus and, above all, the level of experience that might transform it into energy have largely gone missing.

In the Heights comes to us with a non-Equity cast, a worrying sign of the times, and it shows.

The audience is short-changed, and so is the material. In the Heights won the 2008 Tony for best Broadway musical and, unlike some other recipients, it deserved to. It also introduced a newish musical sound to Broadway and, again unlike some other shows, used it well.

The heights of the title are the Washington ones, up in Manhattan where the street numbers are in the high three figures. It's a largely Hispanic neighbourhood; to get there, as Usnavi, the show's protagonist and narrator tells us in his opening rap, "you must take the A train even farther than Harlem."

There's no way I'm going to resist a new musical that starts by invoking Duke Ellington, especially as it continues its scene-setting by informing us that, "Oh my God, it's gotten too darn hot like my man Cole Porter said."

Lin-Manuel Miranda, the debutant composer-lyricist who wrote the score and who also created the central role, is a resourceful talent who couples an ear for the life around him with a cockeyed respect for tradition; indeed, in terms of plot, the show may be too traditional.

In the Heights has been touted as a hip-hop musical but that idiom is pretty much confined to Usnavi's own commentative numbers where it belongs; even there it's more elastic and sophisticated, lyrically and especially musically, than your average rap: better-natured, too. Insistently urban though it may be, hip hop shares something with country music; they're both forms that I don't much enjoy outside the theatre but that can work very well within it: much better than rock because they supply something more than a wash of sound; they're specific, story-driven and character-driven. As is this score: soaked in salsa but not drowning in it.

Usnavi owns what in local parlance is a bodega but we'd probably call a convenience store. It stands centrestage; to its left there's a hairdressing salon, to its right a taxi dispatcher. Above and behind them yawns the George Washington bridge. For a current Toronto audience the set-up, minus the bridge, may seem somewhat familiar: the three businesses have parcelled out between them the various problems - rising rents, generational tensions, romantic attraction across cultural and racial divides, buy-out offers from outside the area - that in Kim's Convenience are all crammed into Kim's convenience.

The hairdressers (shades of another Toronto hit) are pulling up their roots and moving to the Bronx. Usnavi has a thing for Vanessa, one of their employees, but is too shy to do much about it. Nina, one of Vanessa's contemporaries, has made everybody proud by getting into Stanford, but has dropped out. Her reason: "I was working so hard to pay for the books, I didn't have time to read them."

Talking of books, that's maybe the most resonant line in the one that Quiara Alegria Hudes has written for this show. Nina's parents, who run the taxi company, are devastated; her dad's pride, in her and in himself, is especially hurt. Nina further upsets them by starting a relationship with Benny, their ambitious young despatcher, whom they distrust because he's black. Everybody dreams of winning the lottery. To top everything off it's the eve of July 4, which, patriotic considerations apart, accounts for the darn heat. Which in turn is accentuated when the electricity breaks down. As it metaphorically has in this production.

We're back in the line of American musicals set in enclosed communities: Porgy and Bess, Street Scene, Fiddler on the Roof. The resemblances to Fiddler are especially close, what with the narrating hero, the expository prologue, the threat of everybody having to pull up sticks, if for slightly less grievous reasons than those that caused the exodus from Anatevka.

In the Heights, though, isn't as good as Fiddler at spinning out its overlapping narratives. It's a shock, at the end of the first act and the start of the second, to find that we're supposed to have been centrally invested in the love of Benny and Nina; they've seemed like two supporting characters among several.

This, as already noted, is accentuated by the casting, or under-casting. These untried actors aren't necessarily untalented but they can't give one another the mutual support that might enable them all to blossom. To make matters worse, the original direction has been "recreated" by someone else, so it's like seeing and hearing the show through two layers of cotton wool: three if you count the sound system, which consistently obscures the lyrics.

This seems especially unfair to the tongue-twisting Usnavi, who on opening night wasn't even the listed touring performer but his understudy, Perry Young. In fact, though, he did very well: sweet and appealing if without the edge and the dynamism that Miranda himself, to judge from the CD and a promotional video, must have brought to the role.

Presilah Nunez has some sexy impact as Vanessa. Benjamin Perez obviously understands the anguish of Nina's father very well but doesn't have the weight, or maybe the age, to make us feel it. Even the dancing, of which there's a lot, suffers; Andy Blankenbuehler's choreography looks as if it ought to be exciting but the conviction isn't there. As Cole Porter said, we're down in the depths when we ought to be in the heights.
