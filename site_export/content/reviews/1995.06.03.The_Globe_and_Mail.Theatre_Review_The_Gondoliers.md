THEATRE REVIEW THE GONDOLIERS

Saturday, June 03, 1995

Robert Cushman

Written by W. S. Gilbert and Arthur Sullivan Directed by Brian Macdonald Starring Curis Sullivan, Craig Ashton, Pamela Gerrand and Eric Donkin At the Avon Theatre, in Stratford, Ont., until Oct. 28 Rating: A spoonfed audience, an irresistible show Reviewed by Robert Cushman THE Stratford Festival should commission David Mayerovitch to write a satirical contemporary musical. The updated lyrics he has supplied for some of the songs in The Gondoliers are technically immaculate and sometimes very funny. They are also inexcusable. Mayerovitch's claim that he has merely supplied new equivalents for Gilbert's own faded topicalities is, to put it kindly, disingenuous.

In the original, Giuseppe, one of two republican-minded Venetian gondoliers unexpectedly promoted to the imaginary throne of Barataria, sings about being a neurotically conscientious king at the beck and call of his subjects. Here he does a number about our very own deficit. It's not as if the revisions were part of some thoroughgoing new production concept. Most of the time we're trundling through the old familiar script; then we're in contemporary Canada for the duration of one number; then it's back to Venice or Barataria as if nothing had happened. Yes, Gilbert set his show in an anachronistic never-never land, but he stuck by his own rules; crazy logic is one of the things he's famous for. And he didn't believe in pandering to his public. This Stratford version is spoonfeeding an audience that already gets indulged far more than is good for it.

The author's humour takes a lot of tumbles. The male members of the company display an interesting range of techniques for defusing the dialogue. The young ones - cast, presumably, for their singing voices - sail blandly through it, quite in the manner of the old D'Oyly Carte. At the more accomplished end, Eric Donkin as the Duke finishes off each of his lines with a self-deprecating cough, letting you know that he doesn't find this stuff any funnier than you do. Then there is Lee MacDougall's virtuoso turn as the Grand Inquisitor, giggling, hysterical, in fact Pythonesque: the kind of Spanish Inquisition nobody ever expects.

This is an extremist production; the bad things are painful, the good ones brilliant. (MacDougall's performance is both.) First among the virtues is Susan Benson's design, beginning with the vista of white steps against sheer black space that knocks our eyes out when we enter the theatre. It includes the dream in gold that is the Baratarian throne room. Then there is the surprising vein of melancholy that runs through the show; its emblems are carnival zanies and fast-fading roses, and it crests in Allison Grant's beautifully relaxed singing of "when a merry maiden marries." She and Aggie Cekuta Elliot, as the brides of the gondoliers, are the production's performing jewels, warm and witty. But the other ladies are good too: Glynis Ranney's uppity princess; Douglas Chamberlain's Duchess, who, like the best pantomime dames, is rugged as well as regal; and Karen K. Edissi, spitting fire as the beldame Inez. Her appearance - on a portable rack - is one of the two great coups of Brian Macdonald's staging. The other is the second-act Spanish dance: an ensemble number that starts as vigorous but conventional, mainly notable for the swirl of the costumes. Then one dancer, then another, then all, pick up dummies as partners and proceed to swing them in a manner that might appall the most intrepid of adagio artistes. It builds and builds, to become the wildest, funniest, most intoxicating production number I have seen in years. From then on, there can be no looking back; and the show is irresistible.
