BEAUX GESTES & BEAUTIFUL DEEDS

Artword Theatre, Toronto

- - -

This will probably come out ruder than I would like, but the most dispiriting thing about Marie-Lynn Hammond's Beaux Gestes & Beautiful Deeds, other than its title, is the authors appearance in it. The show is a barely dramatized memoir, and it reawakens my prejudices, which have been known to extend all the way up to Long Days Journey into Night, against plays in which the characters' main claim on our attention is that they are related to the author. Having the author there in her own person compounds the offence.

Hammond, a Montrealer of mixed Anglo-French descent, stands centre-stage flanked by two actresses playing her grandmothers, one from each half of her inheritance. On her right is Elsie, a society lady with a lively past, two dead husbands, and a drinking problem. On her left is Corinne, also twice widowed, poor all her life, and the mother of 10 children. During the Second World War, Elsie's son met one of Corinnes daughters; they fell in love and married, the result, obviously, being Marie-Lynn herself. When they meet, very near the end of the play, Elsie and Corinne prove to have just one thing in common: They both disapprove of the match, Corinne, with her Catholic convictions, on religious grounds and Elsie, with her social insecurities, on snobbish ones.

Their encounter hardly qualifies as a revelatory confrontation. All the same, it raises the pressure of a play that has hitherto consisted of two parallel monologues, intercut with songs. These are delivered by the narrating author; one of her early offerings I at first took, unfairly, to be her own special lyric to My Favourite Things. (The melody did turn out to be slightly different.) I confess I didnt know until afterward that Hammond is a prominent Canadian singer-songwriter, and that she fronted a successful folk group called the Stringband.

The songs she offers here, the English ones anyway, have nondescript lyrics and whiny tunes; the French ones have a bit more muscle. They are all commentative numbers, designed to tell us about her grandmothers with an especial emphasis on what she herself thought of them: "Elsie" we are informed, more than once, was a wild one who, in the First World War, "slept with young men home on leave." The effect is of musical finger wagging. One of Elsie's flames was a daredevil pilot, who subsequently became both her second life partner and her flying instructor. Elsie's ambitions as an aviatrix, certainly bold for the time, are celebrated in the productions logo, somewhat misleadingly since it isnt that important in the play's scheme of things, nor, given that she's supposed to be sharing the limelight equally with Corinne, should it be.

There are hints that Marie-Lynn herself has inherited Elsies wildness, but not much can be made of it. The characters exist in different time frames and different conventions and they can never interact. Hammonds presence, in fact, does no favours to anybody, including herself. She seems intrusive, because the play's focus is really on the two grannies. Their theatrical dignity is undercut by the implication that their real importance lies in her being their descendant. The non-appearance of the generation in between doesn't help much either.

It wouldnt be quite fair to dismiss the play as a piece of nothing. The old ladies are excellently played, to a degree that would hardly be possible if the actresses werent able to draw at least the outlines of their existence from the text. Maria Ricossa's Elsie, the lacquered neurotic shell of a good-time girl, is somewhat by the book (or at least by a David Lean movie) but is still meticulously done. As the bedraggled Corinne, Deborah Lalonde- Grover gets a weary witty shrug into her voice as well as her shoulders; she's a woman whom life has beaten down but who isnt beaten. The performance has a lovely unsentimental spirit. It completely inhabits the womans skin, and also her soul.

The play, a great success 20 years ago, has been restaged by its original director, John Van Burek. His Pleiades Theatre has previously mounted a string of Marivaux productions that have been the best things seen at the Artword, and I rather wish he were continuing to make his way through the old master's complete works. They are far more pertinent than this much newer piece. It may once have been thought a piercing study of opposing cultures, but all it really does is sketch a couple of positions. It seems parochial, and there's too much of that going around as it is.
