Deja vu. Max and Jayne are having extra-marital sex in a Scarborough motel. Max is a cop, Jayne a lawyer, both of them disillusioned with their work and with one another. Outside in a car sits Max's partner Donny, who has gone past disillusion on the road to derangement and is now, as often, drunk. It might be a scene from a George F. Walker play.

As a matter of fact, it is. The publicity for The Line has been coy about this, but the first two episodes of this 15-part serial reproduce the action and dialogue from Adult Entertainment, one of the plays in Walker's Suburban Motel cycle of stage plays, an opus in which Max and Donny are recurring characters. Unlike the play, the TV show travels outside the motel room. We meet characters and witness events that in the theatre we only hear about. So when, at the start of Episode 2, Max staggers into the room with a knife stuck in him, we don't have to be told how it got there. We've seen.

That doesn't necessarily mean we believe. Even Walker's most enjoyable plays are finally unsatisfying, their characters insubstantial figures in a lightweight phantasmagoria. It's a personal tone all right, but it feels willed rather than imagined. His previous TV series, This is Wonderland (co-written, like this one, with Dani Romain), made this work; it was a series of pungent, darkly hilarious vignettes, with the minimum of connecting story. It also got up its characters for the occasion. Max and Donny, creatures of the stage, shrivel when exposed to the light of day, especially as the action they're involved in is, inescapably, a police procedural. And, even given that most of us derive our knowledge of cops and criminals from other TV shows, this one isn't very convincing. Max is a rogue cop; Donny, who has bought a part-interest in the motel and spends much of his time attending to it, is just a rogue. They don't seem to have anyone to report to.

The case they're concerned with, reprised and expanded from the play, centres on Carlos, a subordinate drug dealer just released from prison and trying, for economic reasons, to serve two masters: Andre, an established local kingpin, and Eddie, who works out of a Vietnamese restaurant even though, in Andre's sour estimation, he's really Hawaiian. Carlos's divided loyalties inevitably get him in trouble, and his complicated family life inevitably comes under threat. The cops have similar domestic problems: Donny is tempestuously estranged from his wife, and Max looks to be becoming so from his, an intelligent woman who suspects him of having married her for her exotic appeal. (She's black.)Max and Jayne keep bouncing in and out of one another's lives, or at least of the motel bed, and she enlists him to keep tabs on the unsavoury boyfriend of one of her clients. This doesn't turn out well, either. This show has more dysfunctional relationships -- husband-wife, parent-child, lover-mistress, gangster-hooker -- than anyone can keep track of. And that's before you get to the actual crimes.

The show does break new ground in the treatment of money laundering by showing us bills, of uncertain denomination, actually going round and round in a washing machine. By and large the crooks here are more vivid than the cops; though, as a close relative of mine remarked, "they wouldn't last a day in Baltimore." I've been trying to avoid the comparison to The Wire, but the similarities are inescapable, especially in a scene where Andre instructs his apprentice pushers on what to charge and to whom, like Stringer Bell lecturing his henchmen on the finer points of market share, only tamer. Both The Line and The Wire present concerned pictures of urban, or suburban, wastelands. But where The Wire quietly rages, The Line shrugs. It has the feel of a comedy show, though The Wire is in fact funnier as well as more serious. (Or because it's more serious.)

The acting here is good, though without the grounded electricity of Wonderland. Ron White, who's played Max onstage, plays him again, elaborating on his original troubled calm; Daniel Kash plays Donny, whose feet are so far off the ground it's irritating. Cle Bennett is fairly sympathetic as Carlos, while Wes Williams fairly menacing as Andre. Sharon Lawrence, of NYPD Blue, has been imported to play Jayne, as has Ed Asner, the iconic Lou Grant, to play her dad: a performance on which I can't comment as, in the four episodes I've seen, he hasn't appeared. Of course, he has plenty of time left; 15 hours is a long time in TV terms, or most other terms, and I do wonder how they're going to fill it. I can't claim, though, that my wondering has much to do with suspense. I may well keep watching. I'm interested; Walker, after all, is a real writer. But nothing so far has made me care. - The Line airs tonight at 10 p. m. on The Movie Network.
