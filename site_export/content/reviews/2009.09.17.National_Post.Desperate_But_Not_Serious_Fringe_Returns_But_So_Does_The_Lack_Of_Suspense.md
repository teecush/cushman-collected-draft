The first season of Fringe ended with our heroine Olivia of the FBI (Anna Torv) gaining entry to an alternate universe, one apparently reached via the elevator of a Manhattan hotel. Here, she finally got to meet William Bell, the man she's been chasing ever since Episode 1. Bell is the head of Massive Dymamic, one of those companies familiar in TV series that own and control all the resources in the world except one or two left over for general consumption. He turned out to be Leonard Nimoy, a development that seems to have surprised some commentators: inexplicably, since Nimoys name was right up there in the opening credits, and who else would he have been playing? He doesnt wear his Spock ears in this role, but he still has squatter's rights to anything on TV that's extraterrestrial. Id rank his cameo appearance on ringe as one of the shows periodic lurches into low camp.

This may count as a negative spoiler, but I have to tell you: Nimoy does not reappear in the opening episode of Fringe's second season. What happens is that Olivia is dragged back, and we along with her, through one of those rips in the space-time continuum that are omnipresent on this show and that I don't pretend to understand. Last time, while driving to her appointment with destiny, Olivia narrowly avoided a collision with another motorist. That, we may innocently have thought at the time, is how it goes in New York. Now it seems she didnt avoid it at all. She is propelled, in a severe state of disrepair, right out of what seemed to be her empty car, and rushed to hospital, where she spends the rest of the episode in bed, hooked up to stuff, while her colleagues fear for her life: naively, as she still has star billing. Not that death is the worst thing, or even the final thing, that can happen to a person on this show. Anyway, bedrest seems to do wonders for Olivias temper. She's nicer to people than she's been in months -- though she does yell at a friend to go get that bitch when faced with an unsympathetic nurse.

Otherwise, things on Fringe are much as they were, which is, as someone once said of Austro-Hungarian politics, desperate without being serious. Wherever we go, we get our bearings from free-standing place names, spelled out in graven letters that seem to belong on Mount Rushmore. (I like them. Theyre very helpful.) At one point, Joshua Jackson as young genius Peter Bishop, aptly described on the first-season DVD box as "sardonic and roguish," fills Olivia in about their latest nemesis a shape-changing soldier from another universe, and breaks off to ask, roguishly if not sardonically, "Do you think it says something that I can say that without either of us thinking Im crazy?" Well, it says something about the show. Peter, like Olivia, works for a semi-official occult-crimes unit, based in Boston and investigating what it calls "the pattern." As Peter complains to his boss, every week they discover some bizarre manifestation involving the deaths of from one to a hundred people, every time they cut it off at the pass, and every next time it happens all over again. It's called being in a procedural. When, Peter asks, are they really going to do something? Be careful what you wish for, son. His chief, though, has his own problems, dividing his time between barking at his subordinates and defending the operation against the higher-ups who want to close it down. He's played by Lance Reddick who seems to be parodying, one hopes intentionally, his own performance on The Wire. But until someone casts him as something other than a beleaguered upstanding cop, I guess well never know.

Fringes pilot was shot in Toronto, with the Boston freeway played by the Gardiner, and U of T realizing what may have been a lifetime dream by standing in for Harvard. Since being picked up, the show has been able to stay closer to home: maybe in Boston itself, for all I know. Harvard gets into the act because it's where Peter's dad Dr. Walter Bishop, whos even more of a genius than his son, used to have his lab before they locked him in a mental institution. Hes out now, and back in the lab, because he's a nice man, with a sweet tooth and endearing memory lapses, and they need him. Every evil manifestation that gets loosed upon the world can be traced to his old experiments, so he's the only man who can put them right. Hes delightfully played by John Noble, and he and Jackson have a great exasperation-affection double act going. Also inhabiting the lab are a cop called Astrid, who never seems to be allowed out, and a cow called Jean. Jean was requisitioned because of her species genetic similarity to humans. Nobody's ever used her for anything, at least not in any episode I've seen, but shes good for one cute cutaway shot per show. Blair Brown plays the acting head of Massive Dynamic who seems villainous but may not be; she admits to a bionic arm but looks and sounds bionic all over.

Fringe often plays like Ghostbusters or Men in Black without the laughs. Really good shows have comedy in their texture; this one just turns it on and off, and at that its seldom more than whimsy. More surprisingly, it lacks suspense; it holds you, but not on the edge of your seat. That's the penalty of opting for disposable plotlines rather than an ongoing story. But that may be about to change. This season premiere ends with a reversal involving a much-loved regular character. That has to have long-range implications: In one world or another. - The season premiere of Fringe airs tonight at 9 p. m. on A channel and Fox.
