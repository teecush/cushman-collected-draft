The new season of House starts with the protagonist in a new environment. This is all to the good as the formula of the show as it stood --House does snark, House does miracle cure -- was getting very tired. So of course was he. So here he is, having finally admitted he has problems beyond his gammy leg, checked into Mayfield Psychiatric Hospital and, needless to say hating it.

House's trump card remains Hugh Lauries performance in the title role, one that he now inhabits down to the last pore. (And of course this is one actor who doesnt have to fear either typecasting or total eclipse, once his iconic role has run its course. It's so far removed from everything he'd done previously that all he has to do is drop his totally convincing American accent and return to his roots.) According to expert medical opinion, Dr. House never had problems other than narcissism and anti-social behaviour;" well, that and OD-ing on his Vicodin. So he wants to get better and get back to his old job. Only he doesnt want to play along. "Ill miss you, he tells the staff and fellow inmates soon after arriving. In fact, I want to start missing you as soon as possible."

This ambition is at odds, of course, with his aversion to treatment of any kind. He takes badly to one-on-one sessions with a sympathetic senior shrink (massively played by Andre Braugher) and even worse, as you'd expect, to group sessions with his fellow patients: a feelgood environment from which every sarcastic, superior cell in his body recoils. Knowing the system, he tries to beat it from within. He avoids taking his medication and becomes a champion of patients' rights. For much of its two-hour length, this season premiere plays like an upbeat version of One Flew Over the Cuckoos Nest. And as long as it stays that way, its fine.

Thus, House recruits his roommate, a manic depressive rapper with an exaggerated belief in improvisation, as an ally. He successfully challenges table tennis rules that prohibit paddles and a net; even the man in charge admits he's right about that one. Perhaps less public-spiritedly, he encourages a patient with a superhero delusion in the belief that he can fly. It gets hard, even for House himself, to decide how much he's doing for himself and how much for other people; he is after all a doctor, with professional instincts.

Somewhere along the way, he gets his heart broken, or discovers for the first time that he has a heart to break. But much as we may be touched by his progress, it coincides with a wholesale surrender to mush. House has always had its own delusions, chiefly that having a misanthropic hero makes it more than another hospital soap. It belongs to the sizable group of network shows whose ambitions towards seriousness are constantly being shown up by whats happening on cable. This episode of House is up against In Treatment which, in its second season anyway, makes Houses stab at the symbiotic patient-psychiatrist relationship look soupy and superficial. There's no real pain here, just gestures, heavily underlined. In its second half, this episode reverses the Cuckoo's Nest pattern; there are no Nurse Ratcheds to rebel against, and the ideal is to take your meds and like it. In realistic terms, this is probably an advance; 1970s sentimentality about mental illness is something we can do without.

But this is TV, so any stab at maturity comes wrapped in compromise. It isnt enough that House helps to bring about simultaneous breakthroughs in the conditions of two of his fellow patients; there has to be inspirational music playing on the soundtrack. Not to get too specific, but at the end of the show, House is wearing a sweater with a smiley-face on it. Well, presumably, hes a better person for it, and perhaps when he gets back to his old stomping-grounds his show will be a different show. There's at least the potential for it. We shouldn't despair, but neither should we hold our breath. - The season premiere of House airs tonight at 8 p.m. on Global.
