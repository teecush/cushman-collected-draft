Decades ago and an ocean away, John Cleese made himself over from a writer and performer on Monty Python's Flying Circus, the best sketch-comedy show ever, to writer, performer and principal creator of Fawlty Towers, the best situation comedy ever. Other people have attempted the same transition -- it's an obvious one, after all -- but its Cleese of whom I, perhaps incongruously, find myself thinking when I consider Tina Fey and her progress from Saturday Night Live to 30 Rock.

30 Rock is a show that can be very funny in bursts, but is also strangely unsatisfying. The reason, I suspect, is that Fey, unlike Cleese, has brought her previous comedy ethic with her. Like Fawlty Towers, and like many other shows, 30 Rock is a workplace comedy. Unlike Fawlty Towers (and unlike, say, The Office, especially in its British incarnation) it doesnt create a workplace that's credible, and hardly bothers to try. Actually it's almost insulting to call Fawlty Towers a sitcom; its best episodes were classic farce, Feydeau on a miniature scale. This means they had to be logically structured, not just physically but psychologically; Basil Fawltys seaside hotel may have been chaotic, but its chaos sprang from the thoroughly believable personality-deficiencies of its inhabitants.

Back when it started, 30 Rock held some of the same promise. It was intriguing to see Feys Liz Lemon lured by Alec Baldwin's megalomaniac studio head into working as head writer for crazy movie star Tracy Jordan. But as the series has progressed, it's drifted increasingly far from that premise. The show-within-the-show still exists, but not in any tangible way. Its amazing how little work is seen to get done. The mugging Tracy Morgan plays his semi-namesake; theres presumably more to Morgan in real life than there is to Jordan here, but he never lets us know it, and he isn't half as cute as the show thinks he is. Neither is Kenneth, the Southern pageboy (Jack McBrayer) whom everybody loves and fraternizes with but who, at the start of the Season Three, is still just a page. It's a wonderfully egalitarian set-up. It just isnt very plausible. Or very funny: I wouldnt say that comedy depends on hierarchy, but it can be a very useful tool.

The main reason to watch the show is Baldwin. There's always a kick, of course, in seeing a respected actor in such frivolous surroundings; the kick is doubled when he turns out to be so perfectly suited to them. In a way, Baldwin's performance is an enlargement of the devastatingly sadistic motivational speech he delivered in the film version of Glengarry Glen Ross, but cut loose from its moral bearings. He performs here with a monstrous insouciance that perfectly conveys the character while simultaneously suggesting that, personally, hes having the time of his life. It was great fun watching him in last weeks season premiere, forced to start again from the bottom of the company -- which, as always, is the mailroom -- and emerging from it in record time. Like the hero of How to Succeed in Business Without Really Trying, he obviously had a real talent for mail-roomery.

It was also fun to see Fey herself trying desperately to prove her maternal credentials to the lady from the adoption agency (a fearsome Megan Mullally). It was a plot-line that actually built, and was pleasantly reminiscent of her attempts last year to impress the committee of the co-op she wanted to buy into. She's likeable, but either she lacks the comic energy to maintain herself at the centre of things (she consistently loses out to Baldwin) or her character does. Her devastating Sarah Palin on Saturday Night Live (there must have been many people who wondered, when John McCain appeared on that show last week, just which lady he was standing next to) suggests that she is a finer mimic than she is an actress. Even on 30 Rock, she's notably funnier when she puts her glasses on. It seems that as a performer as well as a writer shes still operating by the scattershot (and often scatterbrain) methods of the show that launched her. 30 Rock aspires to be free-wheeling, and sometimes it succeeds. When it suddenly throws in a musical number, featuring an otherwise-wasted Jane Krakowski, the sheer incongruity can be delightful. Its also fond of little flash-forward vignettes like the ones on Family Guy. But if you're trying to be a cartoon, live actors can be an encumbrance; watching Baldwin's misadventures in Washington at the end of last season, I found myself thinking how much more savagely and economically bureaucracy used to be skewered on The Simpsons. Though I confess that with that show, the operative phrase is "used to be."

Jennifer Aniston is in next weeks episode, and she -- or to be charitable, her character -- is no less irritating than she was on Friends. 30 Rock is unhealthily fond of guest-stars; it seems like an admission that the regulars dont carry enough weight. (And it can be frustrating. It's always nice, of course, to see Edie Falco, but shouldn't she have better things to do with her time?) Its interesting that Seinfeld, which remains the nonpareil of network American sitcoms, hardly ever used them. It didnt need to, because it had four thoroughly grounded, idiosyncratic central characters. As a personality Fey often comes off as a cut-price Julia Louis-Dreyfus, but her function should probably be more that of Jerry Seinfeld: a comic by profession but, in practice, everybody else's incredulous straight guy. But that would necessitate there being an everybody else worth responding to, and a framework to support them. Yes, I did say the Seinfeld characters were grounded; their jobs, or the absence of them, carried conviction. It was also determinedly and refreshingly misanthropic, something 30 Rock, a true child of its age, never dares to be. It may have roots in TV "satire" shows and even, judging by its pace and setting, in classic screwball comedy, but there's never any threat. Everybody -- even Baldwin --is too busy making nice.
