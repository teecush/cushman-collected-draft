Children's books Treasured stories

Saturday, November 05, 1988

ROBERT CUSHMAN

BY ROBERT CUSHMAN They don't all live happily ever after. Most childrens stories, of course, pretend that they do, but there are some bleak exceptions. Darkest of all is Peter Pan In Kensington Gardens, J. M. Barries trial run for his full-length play and novel about the boy who refused to grow up. Peter, having at one week old abandoned his mother for childhood everlasting in a London park, passes up the chance to return to the nest. It will not come again: "There is no second chance, not for most of us. When we reach the window it is Lock-out Time. The iron bars are up for life."

Which sobering words round off The Victorian Fairy Tale Book(Random House, 386 pages, $27.95), an exhaustive anthology of what the blurb reasonably calls the Golden Era of the fairy tale. Barrie was providing a mirror-image of his own misery; he felt as trapped in adulthood as Peter was in youth. Hence the peculiar tone - alternately gloating and coaxing, whimsical and ironic, queasy and compelling - in which he addresses his readers; he is the nice old gentleman bearing poisoned sweets.

But, this book has us asking, weren't they all? Robert Browning's The Pied Piper Of Hamelin with its ingeniously execrable rhymes (So, Willy, let you and me be wipers Of scores out with all men, especially pipers") offers a mass drowning of rats, told from the rodent point of view, as prelude to the abduction of a townful of children. This is a horror story for parents; since one crippled child is left behind to lament his loneliness, its a nightmare for children as well. And the kids never return; there is no second chance, not for Hamelin.

John Ruskins The King Of The Golden River, the first story in the collection, finishes off two skinflint brothers by turning them into rocks. Now there's a forever punishment. Their virtuous younger sibling is a chronic sharer of his last crusts with the elderly and indigent, and it's never suggested that he has read enough stories himself to know that in fairyland such behavior is bound to pay off. He is just naturally good, and he ends up making a fortune.

But it is the villains geological transformation that claims both the tales final sentence and the readers' shuddering imagination. As Oscar Wilde might have observed: "The good end insipidly and the bad unforgettably; that is what Legend means. Wilde's own The Selfish Giant, by the way, is the sickliest thing in the book, an exercise in Christian masochism.

But the book, with its scholarly introduction and exquisite but limited original line-drawings, seems primarily intended for nostalgic adults. My own most delightful discoveries were Ford Madox Fords The Brown Owl, very funny; George Macdonalds The Golden Key, a wonderful quest-story on the borderland of faery and science-fiction whose hero and heroine age decades in a sentence; and The Magic Fish-Bone, an unfamiliar bit of Dickens. In this one, a shabby-genteel London family is playfully given royal labels (there are about 20 princes and princesses" in the overstretched household) so as to qualify for magical relief from direst poverty. Only magic can do it. Outside the bounds of fairy-tale, as Dickens is mercilessly aware, the iron bars are up for life.

The Canadian Children's Treasury (Key Porter, 294 pages, $22.95) is weakest when closest to fairy tale. Eva Martin's Goldenhair, for example, is a painfully uninventive pendent to the European tradition (a gardener turns out to be a knight, and marries a princess. So what else is new?). Indian or Eskimo legends, authentic or fabricated, about the discovery of fire or of story-telling, are the kind of thing that gets Canadiana a bad name, solemnly banal.

Realism and contemporary fantasy are the strong points. The first category yields a masterpiece, Morley Callaghans The Shining Red Apple, a study of adult bitterness operating at a childs expense. It is a story written about rather than for children, but many juvenile classics started out that way. So this anthology takes in The Cremation Of Sam McGee (too macabre for kids? Only a grown-up would think so) and a chunk of Stephen Leacock which, like all the Leacock I have ever read, left me feeling that the really funny stuff must be going on somewhere else.

The production is child-oriented with large print, attractive if unexceptional color illustrations and a chattily informal introduction by Janet Lunn. Lunn claims that Canadian writing is distinguished by humor, darkness, common-sense and - carefully covering all bases - versatility. With criteria that loose you could prove anything. But an excerpt from Lunn's The Root Cellar whets the appetite for the whole book. Indeed, all the bits of longer stories are keenly - even cruelly - calculated to leave the reader screaming for more. They range from the arrival of Anne Of Green Gables, letting you see exactly why Matthew must have wanted to strangle Anne and why he didn't, to visions by Suzanne Martel and Monica Hughes of the world after apocalypse. The assumptions here are more nakedly brutal than anything in Dickens or Barrie: a contrast not in continents but in centuries.

Stephane Poulin's Could You Stop Josephine (Tundra, unpaginated, $12.95) is the third instalment in the continuing chase-story of a boy and his cat. This time they go to a farm, which gives Josephine unlimited scope for running and hiding. It also gives Poulin, as his own illustrator, the cue for dazzling aerial views of grass and wheat fields, as sharp and magical as the paintings of the Douanier Rousseau. With these come beguilingly unsentimemntalized close-ups of the shock-headed pug- faced Daniel. Words, with this writer, come a definite second. But there is great pace to the story, and a nice shape. For 3-year-olds and up.

The test of an alphabet is what it does with X and Z. Jan Thornhill's The Wildlife ABC (Greey de Pencier, unpaginated, $16.95) cheats with X- ray (of a dinosaur) and Zoo. H, more inventively, is for Housefly, "inspecting your fridge with a skewed, creepy-crawly illustration of him at it. My 2-year-olds (I have triplets) enjoyed the pictures, which are bold and, where water is concerned, enticing, but had no interest in the text. This may be just as well; exposure to rhyme and scansion on the order of K is for Killer Whale In the deep blue sea" L is for Loon Who swims excellently" might stunt their growth.
