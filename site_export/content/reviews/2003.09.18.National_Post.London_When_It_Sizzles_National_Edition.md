When you pack this much theatre into one city, you're practically guaranteed some brilliant performances

LONDON - Theatre in London is split in two, like everywhere else only more so. One half is a game reserve for the ubiquitous mammoth musicals. Toronto has its long-running productions of The Lion King and Mamma Mia! London's West End has them both, along with others like The Phantom of the Opera and Les Miserables that have run their course with us, and still others (Chicago, Fame, Stomp, Grease) that keep coming back for short seasons here, but over there have settled in for solid runs.

This precise situation is never going to be duplicated in Toronto or any other North American city outside New York. We don't have the stages, the audience, the tradition or the tourists. (Though we will get The Producers before they do.) So I wouldn't say in looking at the West End at the end of this hot summer, I have seen Toronto's future. I would say I have seen its present -- everything the traffic will allow -- multiplied.

As in Toronto, these globally anonymous shows are balanced by others that have some connection to their audience. Again, there are more of them. But here the difference isn't simply one of numbers. London has something no North American city, including New York, has or is ever likely to have: a system of subsidized repertory theatres mounting plays on their own premises, and transferring some of them into commercial houses. This makes for a rich menu.

It's unusual these days for a West End management to originate a major production that isn't a musical. It can happen, though, and it has done so now with a piece by, of all forbidding foreigners, Pirandello. Absolutely ... Perhaps (known in English textbooks as Right You Are -- If You Think So) was a revelation; all the things one has been told about Pirandello and never believed -- that he was a great comic playwright, that his plays are seething with Sicilian passion -- turned out to be true.

It helped, obviously, to have an Italian director (and designer) in Franco Zeffirelli. The play, offering us factually incompatible but desperately held accounts of a single domestic situation, keeps pulling the ground from underneath us, and so does the production.

It isn't quite true to say this show exists independently of the subsidized sector. The repertory tradition has helped create the audience for it, and Zeffirelli himself directed regularly for the National Theatre in the glory days of its first director, Laurence Olivier. Some of his present cast worked with him there, notably Olivier's widow, Joan Plowright, now the star attraction in what is nonetheless an ensemble piece.

The National itself has just sworn in its fifth director, Nicholas Hytner, an intellectual and a showman. The showmanship has been evident in his opening season, notably in his modern-dress production of Henry V. This is superbly mounted on a wide open stage, with actors jumping in and out of Jeeps, and much commentative use of video -- the king's rallying speeches, after being delivered live, are promptly repackaged as unusually eloquent sound bites.

The best thing I saw at the National was a revival of David Mamet's Edmond, about a man's descent into the urban inferno: a contemporary Woyzeck except that Mamet's protagonist -- who walks out on his wife, goes pussy-shopping, gets a gun and ends as a convicted murderer at the sexual mercy of his cell partner -- is articulate. He talks, but he has no centre. Kenneth Branagh, sliding between reason and rage, plays him brilliantly.

The Royal Shakespeare Company, similarly, has done great West End business with Brand, Ibsen's gruelling verse-play, by casting film star Ralph Fiennes as the pastor who makes untenable demands on his family, his flock and himself. Artistically, the results are less successful; Fiennes, following the rule book for actors playing fanatics, acts on one note, in his case a nagging one devoid of the whirlwind intensity that might shift the character's weight. Still, mountain-peak Ibsen is always more bracing than horsehair-sofa Ibsen, and there is one very fine performance from Claire Price, radiant as the hero's long-suffering wife.

Shakespeare's Globe, combining the charms of novelty and antiquarianism, remains a prime tourist attraction, almost the symbol of London's renaissance. Its actual standards are very uneven. I saw a real rarity -- Dido, Queen of Carthage, Christopher Marlowe's first play -- crassly staged but redeemed by two leading actors who could not only speak the verse but act it.

A brand new house, the Hampstead Theatre (replacing a makeshift old one), introduced London to our own Adam Pettle with a production of his Sunday Father that seemed mysteriously unaware that the play is a comedy. On this evidence, we do their plays better than they do ours. But, of course, we've had more practice.

The vogue in new British plays is historical-biographical. Most complex, in the spider's web manner, is After Mrs. Rochester, which presents novelist Jean Rhys through her life, her work and her obsession -- indeed identification -- with the madwoman of Jane Eyre. It takes three actresses to play her, all of them good. Polly Teale adapts and directs for the Shared Experience company, known for accomplished, if sometimes precious, literary reworkings.

The National offered Nick Dear's Power, with the young Louis XIV taking his first steps toward absolute monarchy by playing one finance minister, the feckless Fouquet, off against another, the calculating Colbert. (In romance terms, this is Man in the Iron Mask territory.) Tolerably smart, this is distinguished by the Queen Mother of Barbara Jefford, a classical veteran who can dispose of an opponent by snapping "I want him dead" as if her tongue were the axe.

Terry Johnson's riveting Hitchcock Blonde (ex-Royal Court Theatre) is a tale of two exploitations. There is a movie academic's seduction of his research assistant while investigating a lost fragment of early Hitchcock, and the maestro's own clammier and more complex relationship with the girl who stood in for Janet Leigh in the Psycho shower scene. Much depends on Hitch himself, played here to lip-smacking, syllable-rolling perfection by William Hootkins (an American, surprisingly). More great use of video -- actually, the best ever -- in William Dudley's designs.

This play just carries off the palm from Nicholas Wright's Vincent in Brixton, which concerns the historically dubious love affair of the young van Gogh and his middle-aged, depressive London landlady. I saw it in a non-air-conditioned theatre on the night before the hottest day in the city's history, and since the play's attractions include the cooking of realistic meals in a fully functioning Victorian kitchen, it's a tribute to the writing, the acting and the audience that all of us survived. (For once it seemed right that they should applaud us.)

This is a quiet, beautiful play, lit up by the transcendent performance of Claire Higgins, one of England's finest unsung actresses. A whole life's progress -- defensiveness, awakening, heartbreak, rage and new resolution -- was mapped out in her face. English acting is not always what it's cracked up to be, but when it is, it can make your heart swell.
