I am far too cautious to proclaim a new theatrical Golden Age, but I confidently announce that this has been a Golden Fortnight. The past two weeks have brought onto Toronto stages The Blue Dragon, The Penelopiad, The Golden Dragon, Kim's Convenience, Other People and Caroline or Change: each in its way a triumph. And now, to round things off, we have Penny Plain, the latest from master puppeteer Ronnie Burkett and his Theatre of Marionettes.

At the risk of bursting my own bubble, I have to say that as a whole Penny Plain does not reach the exalted level of the shows listed above, or indeed of some of Burkett's earlier work, though it does in flashes. Burkett isn't just a virtuoso puller of strings; he's a fine actor and a considerable playwright. A first response to his new piece is that the puppetry is the least of it, though that may be because we have come to take his skills for granted. Once again, he has created an array of extraordinarily lifelike miniature figures, whom he can guide through the subtlest of movements and expressions. Once again, when they aren't performing scenes - usually in twos - they hang from their wires at the back of the set, a haunting image in itself. This time, though, they don't go in for, say, the incredible musical interludes of Burkett's previous show, Billy Twinkle. Such trickeration would likely be out of place in the present narrative circumstances, which are grim.

Penny Plain herself is one of Burkett's frail but indomitable old ladies: a blind octogenarian who keeps a boarding house. Outside, it's all-purpose Armageddon; every economic and environmental disaster has materialized at the same time. Banks are sinking, and so are countries. A virulent new strain of flu rages uncheckably across the world, killing millions. In Australia, kangaroos are eating people (and, one hopes, depositing the leftovers tidily in their pockets). In a development that ingeniously wraps three contemporary nightmares in one package, the U.S. is part-paying its national debt by shipping fertile young women to China, which needs them having depleted its own female population through selective abortion. This information and more is brought to us in a series of newsflashes, recorded by guest voices; one of them, especially welcome since we no longer get to hear on it on the real radio, is that of Andy Barrie.

Indoors, Penny sits neatly on one of two straight-backed chairs. Sitting on the other, in a matching upright position, is Geoffrey, her guide dog and companion. Geoffrey talks with an attitude that suggests a crossbreed of Family Guy's Brian and Wallace's Gromit. Penny has always treated Geoffrey as a human being, but now he wants to leave and live among his own kind. This is obviously rash. It's a dog-eat-dog world out there; and that's the least of it.

Penny advertises for a replacement canine companion and, after auditioning a couple of extremely unsuitable applicants, settles on one who insists that she's a dog though everybody else, including the audience, can see that she's a girl. "Everybody" here doesn't of course include Penny, who every time somebody asks her, usually metaphorically, to see something replies that she can't: an all-purpose line that bears repetition surprisingly well. The newcomer's name is Tuppence. As in Penny Plain, Tuppence Coloured, though if there's anything more to this than an unspoken private joke, then I'm the one who can't see it.

We also meet Penny's lodgers, of whom the most cheerful is a bank manager who greets the end of things by indulging what must be a long suppressed taste for cross-dressing. There's Queenie, a mean and incontinent old woman with a walker, Burkett's manipulation of the latter device being an especially insouciant example of the taken-for-granted skills referenced above. There's her even meaner daughter Jubilee, an editor (she says) who stabs people with her red pencil when they annoy her by talking too loudly on their cellphones (we hear you, lady) or otherwise making noise; her murderousness is in line with the terminal state of the world, but the impression is that she's always been that way. There are the Titties, a highly sexed evangelical couple from the southern States who take refuge in Penny's basement while happily awaiting the Last Judgment and who are caricatures even by puppet standards.

Perhaps most important, and maybe a tip of the hat from one great puppeteer to another, there is the famous Gepetto, maker and father of Pinocchio, who has somehow fetched up in 21st-century Canada. To him there comes Evelyn, an unhappily childless woman who wants him to build another doll that will turn into a real boy. After much persuasion, he agrees but the resultant creation is shockingly far from what she had expected. It is, Gepetto says, a child fit for the world you have created, though by "you" he'd better not mean Evelyn personally. Anyway, one of our last sights is of Evelyn cradling her new infant. This could be an image of horror or of hope. It works either way.

The only thing that seems to be blooming in this blighted world is Penny's garden, and she calls Evelyn "Eve." So maybe we have a fable of regeneration; the last sections of the play are in fact quite beautiful. But they can't compensate for too many arid passages earlier on. The fact is that what's going on inside the house is dwarfed by what we're told is going on outside, and I frequently found myself wishing that the characters would stop chattering and let us hear more short and snappy bulletins from the front. Penny is an endearing creation, though with nowhere much to go dramatically. The others are, well, puppets. They've been given a fine setting, a stained-glass colonnade, designed by Burkett himself, who also did the costumes. There's a sweet-naturedness that shines even through his darkest imaginings. He is, to state the obvious, a major talent. But this isn't, though it seems intended to be, one of his major works.
