KITE

Tarragon Theatre Backspace

- - -

If Winnie, the heroine of Samuel Beckett's Happy Days, were to get up out of her mound of earth and encounter another person equally intent on making purblind proclaimations about the joyous rightness of things, the result might not be unlike the scenario depicted in Kite. And if the meeting turned into a squabble, as it almost certainly would, and if it took place beneath a tree, then we would certainly be reminded of Waiting for Godot. As we are here.

Rick Roberts, the author and director of Kite, is far too smart not to know he had that comparison coming; it is in fact built into his play. He offers variations on Beckettian themes, variations that are in some respects more naturalistic than anything you would find in the works of the master, and that in others are more fantastic.

The antagonists are Man One and Man Two, both very old and living in what, from the sound of them, are real-world high-rise apartment buildings. They meet on top of a hill overlooking what, from the sound of them, are real-world urban noise and chaos. Each claims to be having a "wonderful day" and disputes the right of the other to say the same. Man One is flying a kite, which naturally gets stuck in the branches of a tree. His objective, as an acting teacher would say, is to recover it. Man Two has an assignation with a much younger and apparently very beautiful woman whose name, he says (when he can remember it), is Sheila. He is wearing a nice Italian suit for the occasion. His objective is to persuade Man One to leave and stop cluttering up the scenery.

Man One is falling apart, literally. Quite soon after we meet him, he loses a tooth; and thats just a start. He is, though, in full possession of his marbles. He talks volubly and logically. Man Two is in better shape physically, but is mentally much likelier to drop off. In the wit combats with which they, like Vladimir and Estragon, pass the time, Man One invariably has the better arguments; but he cannot make then stick because Man Two refuses to acknowledge them.

The play is about the indignities of old age, as manifested in physical and, to some extent, mental decay. Thats all it's about, but the subject is big enough in all conscience: universal, indeed. It's taken here to extremes, at some sacrifice to even metaphorical credibility. The kite flyer and the datemaker are not merely old; theyre centenarians. (Incidentally, have you had enough of kites as images of aspiration? Me too.) I know hope springs eternal, and we all make fools of ourselves, but honestly.

All the same, the humiliations visited upon the couple do ring painfully true. Man Ones disintegration prompts some trompe l'oeil effects of the kind that Roberts as director is, as he showed in his earlier play fish/wife, a master. (I don't want to spoil it, but Man One turns into a headcase.) Man Twos intended does actually come within binocular length but to what end? (I dont want to spoil that either, but the young can be very cruel. And they can really rub your nose in it.)

I haven't been much of a fan of Roberts' previous plays. This one had me divided. Its initial stretches, especially some non-verbal ones, I found tedious. Then the play began to interest me, though without ever making me feel that it had carved out an identity independent of its spiritual source. Later still, it produced some passages of real resonance, and my engagement with it deepened. But as it worked itself out, it lost me again; all its points made, it still kept making them. Overall, though, I would call it Roberts best work to date.

It is superbly served by half its cast. Oliver Dennis, who has given an inordinate number of fine performances these last few years, here gives the finest yet. His Man One is a figure in whom dignity and grievance fuse to create real anger. His predicament makes him ridiculous, but he never plays ridiculous; the writing takes care of that. He is not, however, permitted a worthy match. Man Two is played by a woman, which immediately gives the proceedings an unwanted touch of the cutes, and also takes away much of the sting, seeing as this character is the one whose predicament is sexual. There are actresses who, even en travestie, could match Dennis for power, but Marjorie Campbell is not one of them. Piping and preening, she cannot help playing the thing as a joke -- with intelligence but without weight, physical or spiritual. Talking of spiritual weight: Dennis, I realize, must now be playing this piece at night while rehearsing Lucky, in Soulpeppers forthcoming Godot, by night. Could an actor's existence get more existential?
