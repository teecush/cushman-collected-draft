The Theatrefront company are known for going off to foreign parts and assembling plays about those parts when they get back. An investigative trip to Bosnia resulted in Return (The Sarajevo Project) and one to South Africa gave us Ubuntu (The Cape Town Project). For their latest show, they've been exploring closer to their own backyard, in space if not in time. The Mill, set in northern Ontario, is their Canadian project. So far, it's a winner.

I have to say "so far" because The Mill is a cycle of four plays, of which only the first two have yet been unveiled. Part I: Now We Are Brody takes place in the 1850s. Part II: The Huron Bride is set 20 years earlier, and I apologize in advance for any confusion. Both plays have the same timbered setting: the eponymous sawmill (brilliantly realized by Theatrefront's designer Gillian Gallow), whose accoutrements include a forbidding staircase; some even more perilous floorboards with ghastly secrets underneath; and, but of course, a supremely horrific circular saw on whose teeth several characters come to grief, one of them before our very eyes.

You'll have gathered that The Mill is a horror story. It may even have invented a new genre, Pioneer Gothic: one that Part I, written by Matthew MacFadzean, seems content to explore and exploit for its own sake. Charlotte MacGonigal, a young lady of neurasthenic tendencies but with a very strong will, comes to take possession of the mill, bequeathed to her by her father. She is not, on the whole, welcomed. The surrounding community, once called Jameston after James MacGonigal who built the mill, wants to forget the recent past and has renamed itself Brody, hence the unlikely title. The things they want to forget are summed up by the figure of Lyca, a white-clad girl who literally haunts the place. Outside are all manner of terrors: wolves, Indians, other settlers and unremittingly awful weather. Equally menacing is Charlotte's husband, a maniacally possessive preacher who's tracked her down and is disinclined to leave without her.

Some of this show's best scenes are silent; they depict Charlotte's early days in her new home, each day ticked off in a surtitle, subsisting mainly on tourtieres dropped off by a friendly Frenchman. (She can hardly have expected this. So how had she planned to survive?) The dialogue, when company finally comes a-calling, can be awkward; characters are at one another's throats before they've been properly introduced, either to one another or to us, and there are anachronisms that are jarring without being funny. But nobody could say that the proceedings aren't scary.

They don't, though, as much as hint at any overarching statement about Canadian history or Canadian identity. (For that matter, Theatrefront's previous two shows weren't as enlightening about Capetown and Sarajevo as they must have meant to be.) This is partially redressed by Part II, written by Hannah Moscovitch. It has a prologue in which a helpful modern archeologist (Richard Greenblatt, luxury casting in a cameo role) instructs his students on the advisability of consulting the indigenous population before digging. The play proper bears this lesson out. We meet James MacGonigal himself, fanatically proud of his mill, which he built on Native bones, pretty much literally. He has imported Hazel Sheehan, a distant cousin (all the way from Liverpool, in fact) to mind the store; after a series of encounters, fraught sexually and otherwise, he marries her. But she isn't his first bride; that was the Native woman of the title who haunts the play and the place as, again, does the eerie Lyca, who's alive at this point though not always so you'd notice. Coming after MacFadzean, Moscovitch has the task, conceivably the simpler one, of explaining his mysteries while planting a few of her own. The style and the setting are different from anything she's tackled before, and she copes beautifully. A striking thing about this writer is her sheer competence; she does all the things one should be able to take for granted but rarely can: draw characters, tell stories, write dialogue -- and not just monologue, which is the suspicion that has dogged her previous work. An ecological fable that might have been awfully heavy-handed is related with dexterity and even wit. A brief digression on raccoons should make any urban audience feel in touch with its rural roots.

The productions -- Part I directed by Daryl Cloran, Part II by Christian Barry -- are outright superb, with scarifying sound by Richard Feren and lighting by Andrea Lundy that, especially in the second play, explores new dimensions of the crepuscular. Both scripts make extravagant demands on special effects, all of them--in the tiny Tank House theatre -- flawlessly met; in Part I a couple of actors levitate. Michelle Monteith plays both Charlotte and Hazel, the two young wives put mercilessly through the Mill, and delivers brilliantly contrasted studies in terrorized persistence: one intruder who inherits her demons, one who meets them fresh. Each has a husband from hell, played with appropriate intensity if insufficient weight by Ryan Hollyman. Three characters appear in both plays: Holly Lewis is an indomitably fragile Lyca; Eric Goulem lays on the Gallic relish, rather excessively; and Maeve Beaty is a serially intrusive biddy. She, too, can overdo the eccentricity; but it's hard to resist her sharpness of line, especially when standing and moving at impossible angles, and her whiplash verbal timing. She and Monteith, with complementary styles and personalities, are extraordinarily present actresses, and having them onstage together is a treat on its own.
