TWO WORDS

Tarragon Extra Space, Toronto

Two Words tells two stories. Both touch contemporary nerves while having the aroma, if not the actual credentials, of folklore. Originally set down by Chilean writer Isabel Allende, they have been combined into a play by Denise Blinn and Gabrielle Kemeny and staged by the multicultural Cahoots company. Blinn is also the director.

The Colonel, a successful though illiterate politician, is discovered in bed with his mistress, on whom he is dependent for the telling of stories. He craves one, it seems, every night. His lover used to sell her words in the marketplace, and she sold the Colonel two (unheard, of course, by us) that enabled him to win the election.

The story she tells now -- the tale within the tale -- concerns a judge, upright but implacable, who tries to entrap Nicholas Vidal, a famous local bandit, by locking his mother, a former prostitute, in a cage and leaving her there, in the heat of the town square, without food or water. Nicholas, the judge's mirror image, declines to ride to her rescue. The two are playing a game somewhere between poker and Russian roulette, and they are perfectly matched opponents. The mother commits suicide. The judge, who has refused appeals for mercy from the entire town, is forced to flee with his family. Nicholas intercepts them. Their survival depends on the bandit being seduced by the judges wife, previously an icicle.

The Colonel, hearing the story, identifies with the judge. Both fables, too, depict authoritarian men who find themselves in the power of women. But the parallels are not very close, and when the two plots finally converge, the effect is more confusing than enlightening. In terms, though, of making us want to know what happens next and of administering intermittent shocks, the adaptation is efficient. The dialogue (the playwrights own invention; there is none in the Allende originals) is appropriately spare and doom-riddled.

The production, however, seems constantly to be promising a richness of imagination it never delivers. It begins splendidly with the actors in the inner story creeping out, hands first, from under the narrator's bed. But nothing that follows matches up, except perhaps for the sight of the woman in the cage, which is a given anyway. There is, too, a preponderance of blank performances. Exceptions are the adaptor Kemeny herself, fittingly cast as South America's tantalizing answer to Scheherezade; Michelle Polak, first sullen, then searing, as the martyred mother; Xuan Fraser, insolently self-possessed as her brigand son; and Simon Casanova, whose fanatic judge might have stepped out of a newsreel.

There are now two plays in Toronto in which utterances crucial to the characters are withheld from the audience. In The Shape of Things, I desperately wanted to know what it was the lovers whispered to one another. But as for the two magic words with which Allende's storyteller empowers the Colonel, I really could not have cared less what they were. Obviously, this is testimony to my greater involvement in one play than in the other. But it also suggests that magic is magic and realism realism, however the two may have been combined in a fashionable formula, and that they have different codes. I resented the silence of Neil LaBute, even while grudgingly admitting he had the right, maybe even the necessity, to keep it. The silence of Two Words, though, is the kind that cannot be broken.
