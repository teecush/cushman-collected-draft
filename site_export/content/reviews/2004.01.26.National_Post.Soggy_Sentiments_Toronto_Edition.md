WRITTEN ON WATER

Bluma Appel Theatre, Toronto

Somebody in Written on Water, groping for the right expression with which to describe a piece of writing, says "it's so ... precious." The same word had been on my mind all evening, and not in any complimentary sense. Michel Marc Bouchards play takes us back to dramatic territory that we havent visited in decades, and that very few of us have missed.

It takes place in a long, high-ceilinged room in what may once have been an impressive house but is now a ruin. The windows have been blown in. Outside, a young man wanders, wearing what looks like a pair of angel wings. Inside, a bevy of much older people discuss life, love and the pains of senior citizenship.

The room, it transpires, is called the writing room. We are in a small town, recently devastated by a flood, whose elderly inhabitants used to gather together to share the literary records of their existence. These have now been washed away, and need to be reconstructed from memory. So, at least, thinks Samuel, who presides over the writing and reading circle. Some of the others understandably feel they may have better things to do with what remains of their time.

The mid-20th century was full of plays set in elegant and/or dilapidated residences in which cultivated people, in the shadow of cataclysm, convened to exchange attitudes and aphorisms. Jean Giraudoux and Jean Anouilh wrote them in France, and their disciples, Christopher Fry and John Whiting, in England. The fashion never really spread to North America, though. If any part of this continent were to be hospitable to it, that part would, for obvious reasons, be Quebec.

But things have changed. The tradition of wit, which is what kept those older plays more or less afloat, seems to have died out. Maybe it survives in Bouchard's original French, but there's little sign of it in translation. If people on stage are going to talk at, rather than to, one another for a couple of hours, the least they can do is be funny. The audience at the Bluma Appel was obviously waiting, in some desperation, for them to start.

The town, we learn, was in a bad way even before the waters broke. All the young people had left for more populous parts, with the exception of Danny, the one lurking outside with the appendages. (I still dont understand those.) He spends the first part of the play longing to be let into the writing room, and the second actually inside it and happy to be there. I think he didnt know when he was well off.

He seems to want life to be even more literary than Samuel would like to make it. "If only," he enthuses, we could actually become metaphors. It sounds like a terrible idea to me, but in a way Danny has got his wish. He and the other characters seem more like figures of speech than figures of flesh and blood. It's as if the incredibly annoying Biographer of Bouchard's The Coronation Voyage had taken over the whole play. Theres a whole thread of narrative about a missing cat. It seems to exist solely so that the old phrase about letting the cat out of the bag can, for a second, threaten to turn into a physical reality. Its the liveliest moment of the evening.

I'm far from sure that Bouchard endorses Samuel's preference for words over deeds. Some incidents suggest he doesnt. Two women in the group start a long-delayed relationship. A man and his wife insist on facing up to the facts of old age. Young Danny gets to make love for the first time -- or so he, at some length, tells us. There is a fairly bracing attack on the euphemisms that, in todays world, obscure actual experience. But the play settles into a recurring rhythm: People make gestures of independence; the patriarchal Samuel calls them to heel.

I can see that some interesting questions are floating around here. Can things be said to exist if they aren't written down? Does the composition automatically distort the event? Does plain writing beat fancy? Can literature survive?

There may even be some kind of allegory of Canada or Quebec, though the state-of-the-nation defence seems to me increasingly the resort of desperate dramatists and even more desperate critics; it can be applied to virtually any play.

I cannot speak for Bouchard's earlier plays but, of the recent ones, The Coronation Voyage was overblown, but told a substantial story. It even had suspense. Down Dangerous Passes Road was an anecdote gasping for significance. It went on about its characters pasts without ever giving them a dramatic present.

Written on Water has the same fault but worse. We are told, for example, that Samuel treated his late wife badly but are given no reason to care. The play is lifeless, punishingly so, and Micheline Chevriers production, though it has no technical faults that I could discern, fails to animate it.

As Samuel, David Fox is, as usual, massively, dangerously genial - - a quality that can be electrifying in a supportive context but seems arbitrary outside it. The others -- Jerry Franken, Barbara Gordon, Carolyn Hetherington, Doris Chillcott and, as Danny-come-to- judgement, Jordan Pettle -- are visibly doing their best but seem, as a group, to be fussing rather than feeling.

Franken, acting crotchety, probably comes off best, though he and Fox (how often have they acted together?) strike surprisingly few sparks off one another.

Bouchard at least makes stimulating demands on his designers. Judith Bowden responds with an appropriately lofty acting-chamber, dominated by a long and draughty wall that makes its impressive way to an upstage point, where it meets a shorter one. The room's third wall is as invisible as its proverbial fourth. So a presumably oblong space becomes, from the audience's point of view, a triangular one; its an old device but it usually works, and it does here. In all other respects, I think youd be better off at Restitution.
