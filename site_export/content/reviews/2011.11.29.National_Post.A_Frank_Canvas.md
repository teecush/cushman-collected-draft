THEATRE REVIEW

Red

Bluma Appel Theatre, Toronto

Jim Mezon, in the past three seasons at the Shaw Festival, has shown himself a great actor, perhaps most notably as Big Daddy in Cat on a Hot Tin Roof. That performance was a brilliant display of nonstop, highly focused vocal and physical energy, and it should have made him ideal casting for the role of Mark Rothko in Red.

John Logan's play presents Rothko, New Yorks high priest of abstract expressionism, as a hectoring loudmouth who hardly stops glorifying himself, denigrating his competitors and preaching about art. Although both the author and the character are American, the play started in England, where it was an acting triumph for Alfred Molina, whose performance had whirlwind speed and volcanic strength.

I had expected Mezon to offer something on the same lines. In fact he tries, initially at least, something quieter and more contemplative. This is another mode hes very good at, and for a time it works. Our first sight of him is memorable, gazing out from the tip of a stage that's been angled to point at the audience. He's lost, it seems, in contemplation of the canvases on which he is presently at work. Hes disturbed by the entrance of the only other character, his new young assistant, who stretches out a hand that his employer pointedly ignores. That sets the tone for their subsequent relationship.

Its the 1950s, and what plot there is concerns Rothko's commission to paint a set of murals for the new and swank Four Seasons restaurant in the Seagram Building. As the job and play proceed, so Rothko's pronouncements get angrier and louder and Mezon is forced to shout. This he can certainly do, but it all sounds hollow and unmodulated, apart from one passage. This is Rothkos account of a visit to the Four Seasons, which left him horrified by the place, the patrons and even the menu, convinced that his work would be ignored by the people whose complacency its intended to puncture.

As writing, this is very suspect, but at least it's about an actual place and actual people and Mezon can make his passion specific, just as it was in his astonishing confrontation with his son in Cat.

The play is one of those middlebrow American prizewinners with monosyllabic titles that are thought to be profound because they take on, or at least mention, big subjects. They were a staple of the previous regime at Canadian Stage, but the new one was thought to have left them behind.

As an earnest of progress, the production has been entrusted to Kim Collier, an innovative director who must have seemed a logical fit for a play about an innovative painter. In fact, there's a severe limit to what she can do with whats essentially a talk piece. A curved wall slides apart to reveal David Boechlers big, bare and impressive studio set, on which objects from paint cans to cartons of Chinese takeout make powerful visual statements.

Less happy is having the lighting turn yellow when somebody mentions "golden ambition" and the introduction of a slide show of pop art where an intermission used to be.

The acting, meanwhile, could use more light and shade. David Coomber certainly holds his own as the sorcerer's apprentice but he's too unvariedly fresh-voiced as well as fresh-faced.

Meanwhile, the production has muffed what in New York was the plays highpoint: the two men furiously priming a canvas with the colour thats the play's title and the character's lodestar. Its meant to be orgasmic; Rothko even lights a cigarette afterwards. Here, its over too soon and there isn't enough passion leading up to it.

By coincidence or design this is Canadian Stage's second meditation this season on that same colour and its infinite varieties. I Send You This Cadmium Red, using artists' actual words, was more modest, more informative, and more moving.
