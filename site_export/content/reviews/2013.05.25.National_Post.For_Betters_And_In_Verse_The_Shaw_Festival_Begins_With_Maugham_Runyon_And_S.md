There's a quaint belief, expounded in newspaper columns and letters to editors, that our own age is significantly less moral than previous ones. Almost any worthwhile piece of past literature, fact or fiction, will give the lie to that, even if morality is defined, as it generally and quaintly is, as sexual morality. Our Betters, a sardonically titled comedy by W. Somerset Maugham now on view in a good production at the Shaw Festival, is a case in point, suggesting that in this respect the only thing we've lost is hypocrisy.

Maugham's play (written in 1915, premiered in New York 1917, not allowed a British production until 1923) admittedly deals with a special subset of a specialized group. Set in or near London among the rich and/or fashionable, most of its characters are American, and most of them are millionaires' daughters who have married into the European aristocracy. (This, we're told, was a frequent occurrence before the First World War, though already on the wane when the play was written. It's easy, though, to find later equivalents.) We have a variant on the Henry James theme of the Yankee innocent abroad, though here the corruption is Yankish as well. In fact we have two (comparative) innocents: a young man, Fleming Harvey, who's vigorously shocked by everything he beholds, and Bessie Saunders, his former fiancee, who's inclined to be dazzled by it. Bessie is staying with her sister Pearl, wed to an English lord whom she rarely sees (and whom we never see). Meanwhile, she disports herself with other men of various ages. Her two closest compatriot chums are the Duchesse de Surennes, divorced, and the Princess Della Cercola, separated and devoting herself, at least partially and certainly atypically, to good works. Some people's ethics and others' sang-froid are tested during a country weekend at which the hostess is discovered in a thoroughly compromising situation in the summer house with somebody else's presumed erotic property.

Morris Panych's production captures the play's acrid attitudes, though it fluctuates in tone. There's a tendency for characters to hug the edges of Ken MacDonald's two economically dazzling sets (for Mayfair, and someplace called Abbots Kenton) and it's hard to tell whether this is conscious commentary, placing them outside the action, or just a consequence of the small Royal George stage. It's also quite hard, in a loquaciously expository first scene, to keep up with who is who, and whose boy-toy or sugar-daddy is whose. The acting varies, but Claire Jullien holds the play together as a shamelessly glittering Pearl, hard as her own manicured nails and magnificently unwilling to take the blame for anything whatever. Writhing most painfully beneath the tables she turns are Laurie Paton's Duchesse, torn between righteous indignation and terrified loneliness, and Lorne Kennedy's besotted tycoon, apparently modelled on the man who founded Selfridges; these are both expert performances, but they seem to exist in their own little worlds.

Maugham famously wrote that sincerity in society was like an iron girder in a house of cards. His play, and especially this production, show that girders can be not only effective but attractive. Wade Bogert-O'Brien rescues the noble Fleming from any suggestion of priggishness, and Julia Course makes Bessie's awakening fairly sympathetic and almost entirely believable. Panych may give them too strong a hint of a happy ending, but his filling out of the script's bald final sequences is generally both sensitive and inventive, though an 11th-hour ballroom-dancing sequence also escapes its frame, and seems besides to have hired the wrong kind of gigolo. I enjoyed Ben Sanders' unexpectedly decent English lord, Neil Barclay's asexual professional gossip (once I'd got used to the patches on his suit) and Anthony Bekenn's disapproving butler (is there any other kind?).

The Shaw's other two opening shows are a Salvation Army pairing; indeed Major Barbara and Guys and Dolls turn out to have an amazing amount in common, right down to the hero who bangs a big bass drum for love of the mission doll (a major in one show, a mere sergeant in the other). They are also both concerned with the extent to which sin, or the wages thereof, may be employed in the service of virtue, though the play, as you might expect, agonizes about it more than the musical; the theme of where charity begins also figures in a minor way in Our Betters. Jackie Maxwell's production of Major Barbara is - as one of its minor characters, a Mr. Charles Lomax, might put it - a corker, clad in a cunning set (by Judith Bowden) that, even when we're in a West End drawing-room, keeps us aware of East End poverty lurking behind. The play seems more and more to be Shaw's greatest, and certainly his most perennially topical. The arguments about arms and government, poverty and exploitation, religion and humanity, are wittily broached in the first act, superbly dramatized in the second, and explosively discussed in the third; though the principal speakers' eloquence can never quite overcome our rebellious inclination to say "Yes, but..." (occasionally a character does, but nobody listens).

Nicole Underhay's professional preachings in the title role can be, as the same Mr. Lomax would say, a bit much; but her moment of disillusion is electric, and she goes on radiating thereafter. Benedict Campbell plays her father, the munitions millionaire to whom, as devil's advocate, Shaw gave most of the best tunes; he too might vary his cunning charm at the beginning and his thunderous eloquence at the end, but he does both very well. Graeme Somerville is quite unfaultable as the drum-beating professor of Greek, shy and incisive and with a justified hint of an Australian accent.

Equally on the ball, and this time perfectly within the frame, is Laurie Paton as a most original Lady Britomart, dictatorial lomat rather than dragon and - a great help to the play - very much her daughter Barbara's mother, right down to their hair colouring. Also doing invaluable service on the high-society front are Ben Sanders' Stephen, a born leader-writer who looks like a shrunken John Cleese, and Wade Bogert-O'Brien as the quotable Mr. Lomax. The richly written scenes in the Army shelter are here comparatively colourless, the actors (except for Catherine McGregor's Rummy Mitchens) out of touch with their characters' vocal and psychological rhythms; it seems that younger Shaw actors these days can do posh but not poor. The smallest roles, though, are very well done: Thom Allison as a patient factory foreman and Anthony Bekenn as another disapproving butler; no, there isn't any other kind.

Tadeusz Bradecki's production of Guys and Dolls starts gloomy, both in its opening numbers and in Peter Hartwell's set; Times Square may be sleazy, but it shouldn't be monochrome. The designs, the performance and Parker Esse's choreography all brighten up considerably for the Havana sequence; while in the ensuing return to New York in the small hours, the chiaroscuro comes into its own for the love-duet of SkyMasterson and Sergeant Sarah and their subsequent (temporary, as if you didn't know) estrangement. Elodie Gillett's mission doll is very good throughout, whether determined or intoxicated or determinedly intoxicated. Kyle Blair's Sky is too soft-boiled and fresh-faced for the gambler of the world, but he sings beautifully, and acts through his singing and also, when tenderness is called for, through his acting.

The real treasure of the show is Shawn Wright's Nathan Detroit, rueful and rubber-faced; he might have stepped out of one of Nicolas Bentley's illustrations for Damon Runyon's stories, and looks especially good as the helpless centre of the song hymning his permanent floating crap-game, the best-staged number in the show. He's also the only actor who can effortlessly get his tongue around the script's appropriation (and approximation) of Runyon's unique, and uniquely funny, prose. Jenny L. Wright is all right as his Adelaide, but could be better if she honoured the innocence that makes this showgirl both touching and hilarious, not to mention the lyrical architecture that should make her Lament the best comedy song ever written. There's some of this innocence in Thom Allison's Nicely-Nicely, nervous instead of fat, which works surprisingly well, making Sit Down You're Rockin' the Boat a charmer if not quite a show-stopper. His fellow mugs seem to have been cast for their dancing rather than their acting, and their crap-game ballet is indeed a highlight - or, being set in a sewer, a lowlight. As in Major Barbara, though, the low-lifes are less convincing than the high-minded; Peter Millard's Uncle Arvide and Patty Jamieson's General Cartwright do their Mission proud. But then, in Frank Loesser's score, everybody has the best tunes. Bradecki's direction has an especially neat and novel touch at its conclusion. Finishing this show has always been a problem; mainly, I suspect, because nobody wants it to end.
