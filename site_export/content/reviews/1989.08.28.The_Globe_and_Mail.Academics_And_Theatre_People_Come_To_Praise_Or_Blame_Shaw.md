Academics and theatre people come to praise (or blame) Shaw

Monday, August 28, 1989

ROBERT CUSHMAN

BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail "This," said one delegate to the Guelph University talk-in on Bernard Shaw , "is the nicest conference I have ever attended." He was an academic, and they know about junkets. Part of his point, though, was that university people were sharing the occasion with theatre professionals and theatre journalists, and all three groups were rubbing along famously.

Guelph split the sponsorship of the event with the Shaw Festival . Last Wednesday and Thursday were spent on campus; the caravan - actually a large bus - then moved on, to mingle with the tourists for two days at Niagara-on-the-Lake. (There may also be native Niagarans, but nobody ever actually sees them.) By this time, everybody was literally unbuttoned. Jackets were discarded, and with them went neckties, name tags and long pants. Scholars and directors stalked the streets, bare-kneed and anonymous. You may put it down to the heat. I think it was the encroaching camaraderie. Some people blamed Shaw.

Well, no, they praised him. They praised his friendliness, ebullience and unquenchable optimism. There were demurrers, most of them British. Irving Wardle of the London Times, delivering a paper on recent English stagings of Shaw, disclosed that there had been embarrassingly few of them and diffidently suggested that his unfashionable upbeatness was the cause. London's last Shavian sighting was a West End revival of You Never Can Tell and Toby Robertson, who directed it, was on hand to confess himself as being, in this context, an amiable fraud, or at any rate a skeptic. He had actually directed very little Shaw, and had got tired of hearing the professional Shavians tossing around such words as genius. Actually not words like genius: the word itself. Shaw was all very well but comparisons (Shaw's own especially) with Shakespeare and Mozart were impossibly inflated. If Shakespeare was Mozart then Shaw was Salieri. There were audible intakes of breath at this analogy; Robertson had Gone Too Far.

Professor Dan H. Laurence, editor of the Shavian corpus (official) and custodian of the Shavian repu tation (unofficial but floridly energetic), arose to do battle. If the British theatre despaired of doing Shaw, then that was the British theatre's fault; from what he himself had seen, it had forgotten how. Here in Canada Shaw had his own theatre and the tradition was safe: preserved by the old actors and diligently imbibed by the young. Belligerent though he was, he had a point or two. I would count myself a Shavian - largely because Shaw makes me laugh, and his prose rhythms are compulsive - but a couple of years ago I attended a Royal Shakespeare Company production of Misalliance that was like travelling the Road to Damascus in reverse. Suddenly I knew exactly how the doubters and the haters felt; the man just would not shut up. I dreaded the mention on stage of any new topic, because I knew that somebody would infallibly and interminably start sounding off on it. But the fact was that the actors had forgotten how to orate. They have the same problem with Shakespeare. It can only, given the instinctive distaste for rhetoric of the modern TV- bred performer, be conquered by practice, which is born of continuity. Hence the ironic advantage of Niagara and Stratford, Ontario, over comparable theatres in Britain, where long-term casting is a nightmare.

The Shaw Festival is, when you think about it, a pretty funny idea. Or a pretty Wilde idea: to dedicate one national theatre to a foreigner may be accounted generous, but to do it twice looks like desperation. But it has worked out, largely because the patron saint's annual contribution has been severely rationed. Christopher Newton, the festival director, said in his inaugural address to the conference that Shaw is harder to do than Shakespeare, which surprised me: I have always believed (I still do) that nothing is harder than Shakespeare. Shaw, being nearer to us and almost maddeningly explicit, should be a doddle. But a later Irish playwright, Hugh Leonard, said that Shaw must be a dream to speak and the devil to act; and this, I think, is part of what Newton was getting at. He spoke of constriction, of Shaw limiting the space around characters: "in Shaw you have to fight for the right to pause" and the plots - meaning how the characters progress beneath the dazzling surface of their dialogue - are difficult to enact. (He instanced the Don Juan in Hell segment of Man and Superman which, being pure debate, is extreme but crucial.) Newton has the knack of sounding self-critical, and fallible, and totally sure of himself. Even when he says he has got something wrong, you feel sure he will get it right next time.

Since the theatre's mandate is not just Shaw but plays written in his lifetime, Newton has the opportunity to preserve a century of drama: plays written for a tradition of acting that was, until recently, second nature to the English-speaking theatre and that Niagara may be able to prevent from slipping away, down the lake and over the falls: a tradition of eloquence and high comedy. It's a pity that Shaw's one successor in wit and confidence falls outside the time-frame. I mean Tom Stoppard, who has added to the glitter a modern vein of self-doubt. But that may be there in Shaw as well, an inner conflict obscured by the dazzle. Even if it isn't, we might do worse than pretend that it is, a hidden play within the apparent one. The gaiety will still remain to infect productions, audiences and future Shaw conferences.
