NEW FRANCE

Cameron House, Toronto

This is complicated, so pay attention.

TORONTO - In 1985, the theatre company called VideoCabaret began producing a 12-part sequence of what its writer and director, Michael Hollingsworth, describes as "comedies of manners," but which in fact amount to a comic-strip history of Canada. (The manners are very bad.) After adding new shows at a general rate of one a year, the project reached completion in 1999. Its overall title was The History of the Village of the Small Huts (a literal translation of the country's name) but the latest instalments, which bring the story up to the very recent past, have been labelled The Global Village; whether this is a whole new title or merely a sub-title is hard to ascertain.

Having in 15 years gone as far as it apparently intended to, Hollingsworth's troupe has launched a new five-year plan for doing the whole thing over again. They have kicked this off with a re- mounting of the show that began it all, an item called New France.

However, New France is itself sub-divided. It consists of four 55- minute plays; and you can tell they are separate plays, rather than just separate acts, because the cast takes a curtain-call at the end of each one. The four are being presented as two double-bills, in repertoire. The head spins. Just to make it rotate faster, you can take in New France in its four-hour entirety (plus generous intermissions) on certain days, matinee and evening. This is what I did, out of professional necessity, but I don't recommend it to the casual customer. I'm all for epics, but to keep you impressed over a whole theatrical day, a show needs to project a certain grandeur. VideoCabaret is into, at most, anti-grandeur.

I came in late on the original cycle -- three shows from the end, in fact -- but my enthusiasms and my reservations have remained fairly constant.

VideoCabaret's aim is to make their stage look as much like a television set as possible. They back this up with the usual specious rhetoric about appealing to "an audience raised on rock and TV" (so why not offer them something different?) but it has resulted in a distinctive visual style. Their stage is a small box, with low ceiling, raised floor and an unmediated black surround. Lighting pours down hard and bright, and the action does indeed look as if it's going on behind a screen. Each play is performed in a series of short scenes; five minutes, in this context, is regarded as a long time to go without a break. They haven't quite managed to achieve instant cutting from one episode to the next, but here the cabaret half of the equation takes over; at the end of each vignette, there is a black-out, then the lights go up to reveal -- with a speed that never ceases to amaze -- a whole new group of actors in place. It's especially impressive since there are only seven performers rushing in and out of costume, and one of them has the third play off.

All this quick change of course takes its toll, especially if you choose the one-day marathon option. It's the vehicle for an attitude toward characters and events that veers from off-hand to contemptuous.

New France covers the early period of French settlement in Canada, beginning in the 16th century and ending in the 18th. We see the French fighting and exploiting the natives, and both sides fighting among themselves, with the British joining in toward the end to take what they can from both.

The whites are consistently presented as cruel, scheming and corrupt, the Indians as just cruel and scheming. On a case-by-case basis, this could probably be justified, but over four hours it becomes achingly monotonous, if never actually boring. You begin to suspect the reason Hollingsworth keeps his scenes so brief is that if he went on any longer he might encounter somebody behaving decently, and he wouldn't know how to deal with it. In his chosen cartoon style he writes well, with a notable command of verbal rhythm, but it's notable that though his plays have the aura of comedy -- even torture scenes are played for giggles -- they are seldom actually funny. He and his actors are too busy sneering for the audience to feel much like laughing.

Each of New France's four constituent parts takes its title from a central character. The first, Donnacona & Cartier, shows the Iroquois chief of that name carried off by Jacques Cartier, pioneer French invader, to the court of Cartier's royal master, Francois I. Here he witnesses sexual and political intrigues that culminate in the incompetent aristocrat Roberval being put in charge of a new Canadian expedition, and being beaten soundly off by the Iroquois.

This is the best play of the four, largely because Donnacona himself is the only person in the show to be granted a modicum of dignity; he surveys the titled parasites around him with a sardonic bemusement that recalls the malcontent heroes of Jacobean tragi- comedies -- many of which were set, by their self-righteous English authors, in decadent French courts of precisely this era -- and he meets a grisly martyr's death. (So do many later characters, but their martyrdom is generally mocked.) Besides, the Parisian machinations, though only tangentially related to Canadian history, are compactly unfolded, and in retrospect one appreciates the change of scene.

The second play, Champlain, shows a new French adventurer establishing himself in Quebec, handing out land to his fellow colonists and allying himself with the susceptible Hurons against the intransigent Iroquois.

In Brebeuf, a fanatical and sexually suspect Jesuit who has loomed menacingly at the edges of the previous instalment dispatches members of the rival Franciscan order on doomed missions among the heathen, who finally catch up with him, too. In Frontenac, a foppish French governor presides over the annexation of the fur trade and also -- at wearying and complicated length -- over marital and extra- marital entanglements, precipitated by the arrival from the old country of fresh female stock. Unlike Donnacona, none of these three later titular characters really justifies his position; they are just three buffoons among many.

There are some unifying images: we begin with two Iroquois talking intently of beavers, and the subject recurs at least once per play. The author is also good at keeping seemingly irrelevant characters in motion, until their turn comes to join the parade. His actors have an absolute grip on the style, though their cod French accents gave me a headache, as if the Royal Canadian Air Farce were to set its sights on Quebec and keep them there for hours.

There is especially striking work by Billy Merasty as Donnacona; by Jonathan Fisher as the contrastingly sadistic Iroqouis chief Teganissorens, whose eye patch and gloating grin permeate three of the four sections; by Bruce Vavrina as Brebeuf, though he's more impressive on the sidelines than when he takes centre stage; and by Deborah Lambie, who executes a dashing gender switch as turncoat Etienne Brule, before she reverts to skirts as one of two calculating imported brides. The other, equally piquant, is Janet Burke, whose most notable previous role has been the puritanical Madame Champlain, a most unwilling immigrant and, on this show's showing, the first ever to ask if the winters are ALWAYS like this. Religion and its practitioners are presented with especially savage ridicule throughout, which is certainly the author's right, but he shows no inclination to explore why people might have believed, and the knee-jerk satire gets tedious.

Still, nobody could dispute the energy of the enterprise. The next batch is due in April; it's called The British, and I suspect that however appalling the French's behaviour may have been, we haven't seen anything yet.
