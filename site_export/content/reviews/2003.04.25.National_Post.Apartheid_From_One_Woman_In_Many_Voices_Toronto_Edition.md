THE SYRINGA TREE

du Maurier Theatre Centre, Toronto

Pamela Gien, author and performer of The Syringa Tree, is a South African living in America. So her one-woman play sails into World Stage under two flags. It's a quasi-autobiographical piece, entirely concerned with her youth in and around Johannesburg, but it was worked up in L.A. and premiered in Seattle. Its now in process of going round the world and being turned into a movie.

The festival has billed it as part of "Indaba -- celebration of South African culture" and it makes a neat pair with Thembi Mtshalis A Woman in Waiting, the solo piece that inaugurated the celebration three weeks ago. Each is a first-person account of growing up under apartheid by a woman who found adult success as an actress and writer.

There are, however, notable differences. Mtshali sticks, as far as we can tell, to the facts of her own life and uses her own name; Gien grafts her recollections onto an invented narrator whom she calls Lizzie. Mtshali's piece is a monologue done entirely in her own voice; Gien's is a play in which she plays all the characters, about 30. And Mtshali is black and Gien is white.

Its unusual in the theatre to hear the story of apartheid told through the experiences of a white liberal. We start in 1963, when Lizzie is six. Her father, a doctor, is a self-described Jewish atheist, her mother a lapsed Catholic. Lizzie, of course, is looked after by a black nanny, Salamina, who shortly after the play begins gives birth to a daughter of her own, named Moliseng. Lizzies father delivers her, but her existence has to be kept a secret; she has no papers. We follow the growing-up of both girls, their mutually devoted companionship and their inevitable enforced divergence.

Lizzie's parents are outstandingly decent people and, given the circumstances, model employers; they protect their servants, take risks for them, and say thank you when they knock off for the day. Compare this to Mtshali's memories of life as a domestic, and in particular to her story of the woman who, when she turned up late for work after being scooped from her home by a police raid, accused her of lolling in bed with her boyfriends. Mtshalis accounts of the white children she couldnt help loving are not that dissimilar to the relationship between Salamina and Lizzie, and if Salamina has the luckier deal, she doesn't have Mtshali's escape route into the theatre.

At first Lizzie is able to see the racist world beyond her home as a sad joke, personified by the Afrikaans pastor next door who has passed his bigotry on to his family. My father knows, says his daughter, Lizzies occasional playmate. "God tells him everything." Maybe somebody should write a play about them, the bad guys; it would probably be unsettling but it could be instructive.

Reality crashes in on Lizzie when Moliseng disappears from a township hospital and Lizzies mother, breaking unwritten rules and probably some written ones, drives there through the night in search of her. Lizzie, also disobeying orders, crouches in the back seat. A later, starker trauma comes when Lizzie's grandparents' house is invaded by "freedom fighters from Rhodesia" (the words are attributed to the South African police though its hard to believe theyd have chosen that term) and her grandfather is killed.

According to interviews, this murder and the birth of Moliseng are the only incidents in the play that Gien took directly from life. But they are enough to make the play feel autobiographical, and, as in every other example of the genre from Long Day's Journey into Night on down, this creates problems. You don't know what to believe, and you feel less credulous than you would toward a work of avowed fiction. After the killing, Moliseng leaves her employers house, apparently ashamed of her people. Is this an act of foolishness or nobility? It could be either, or both. But it is told, not dramatized. Its the author's creation, so she has a duty toward it. She can't just pass it off as a historical enigma.

Gien begins the play on a swing, beneath the tree of the title that symbolizes home. (The sense of place, despite a spare and evocative set and soundscape, by Kenneth Foy and Tony Suraci respectively, is willed rather than communicated.) She brings to her multiple roles a graceful, limber body that can contract and expand like Alice under the influence of cake, and an impressive armoury of voices: every role an individual, no duplicates. The piping tone she uses for young Lizzie -- the one we hear the most -- is ingenious but can be tiring; its a relief when she starts channelling the the grown-ups, especially when theyre Lizzie's parents, as humorous as thy are humane.

One begins to wish for our narrator to grow up, though when she does it's nearly the end of the play and all rather a scramble. We arrive at the Soweto riots, and a 14-year-old Moliseng becomes politicized. Lizzie emigrates, marries, has a child but refuses to take a trip home, even after the arrival of democracy and despite her continuing love for her old and still-exemplary dad, which I thought weird. Eventually she does return, under circumstances it would be unfair to reveal.

I can say theres a kind of reunion, and its moving. And I found myself slightly uncomfortable at being moved, because of something sentimental in the implied picture of blacks and whites. I was also uncomfortable at my own discomfort, because the situation is not one of which I have direct experience and I have no right to judge it -- not as theatre but as life.

That's the semi-autobiographical factor kicking in again. The Syringa Tree is on almost every level a more accomplished and sophisticated work than the more straightforward A Woman in Waiting, but though the black solo voice may not in itself be more trustworthy than the white multiple one, it is still, at this juncture and on this subject, easier to accommodate.
