Joanna McClelland Glass is for all practical purposes an American playwright, but her birth certificate is Canadian. Her play Palmer Park is set in middle-class Detroit, which is an important part of the Stratford Festival's catchment area. It has an interesting subject and it has been cast with good actors, a few of whom even give good performances. Those are the excuses, the only ones I can think of. The play is awful.

The title refers to a downtown area where Glass herself lived for the six years immediately following the 1967 Detroit riots. House prices went into steep decline, allowing the upwardly mobile, black and white, to buy homes previously beyond their means. This much is explained to us in the first scene by Fletcher Hazelton, a black pediatrician, and his wife Linda; and by their newly-arrived next-door neighbours Martin Townsend, a white physics professor, and his wife Kate. And they go right on explaining to us for the rest of the evening, joined after a short while by three other local couples, one black, two white.

The ratio is important. Palmer Park prides itself on its diversity, but not on its statistical equality. The area -- and, very importantly, its school -- maintains a proportion of 65% white, 35% black; and, in the play's most arresting point, it's the blacks who are the most determined to keep it that way. If the balance shifted in their favour, the place would be perceived as black, and there would go the neighbourhood. Somebody uses the phrase "tipping point" -- anachronistically, I'd say. And go, or tip, it eventually does.

Before that happens, all the Palmer Parkers get on (I was about to say like a house on fire, but that obviously wouldn't be appropriate). They have social evenings. They go out in bi-racial pairs and knock on doors to raise money for the school, meeting responses crabby, generous or both. (All minor roles are done as cameos by the 10 principals.) But reality comes calling, in the form of the Board of Education, which proposes to bus in black kids from the underfunded, overcrowded school in the next district. Our home team protests, and makes helpful suggestions about improving the other school's facilities, but there is no way. Eden has to be abandoned. To see the aftermath, take a drive today through present-day downtown Detroit and its endless strip-mall suburbs.

It's rare to see a play so unabashedly committed to the cause of NIMBY. Of course, we're all middle-class hypocrites who in real life would have been firmly in Palmer Park's corner; but the theatre is a place where hearts may freely bleed, and the plight of Detroit's blue-collar ghetto-dwellers is likely to hit us harder than that of its doctors, teachers and realtors. The only way around this is to make the characters mean something to us as people. But this is where the play falls down. The writing is appallingly lazy. The Townsends and Hazeltons have a couple of establishing scenes, showing their initial mutual embarrassment and underlying goodwill.

(We're also told that both families have dogs, whose mention will always be good for a heart-tugging moment later on.) But nothing goes below the surface, and these supposedly central characters soon fade into a fuzz of people feeding us facts and trying to make them sound like conversation; whether they're talking to us or to one another, it all comes out the same. At the end, we hear that one couple's marriage has cracked, but we hardly registered its existence in the first place. The play falls foul of the danger inherent in autobiographical drama: the author's belief that her experiences will be as fascinating to everyone else as they are to her. Maybe this one should have been called Glass Houses.

There are some hopeful and rather embarrassing attempts at flash: an interminable and vaudeville sequence in which the men bond over their love of baseball, and a party-turn performance of -- of all unexplored things -- The Darktown Strutters' Ball. (Good song, but still). We see nothing of the children whose futures are supposed to be all-important, unless you count a two-minute interlude in which Kelli Fox and Yanna McIntosh switch from playing Kate and Linda to impersonating their young daughters; this enables McIntosh to renew delightful memories of her performance in Danny, King of the Basement, and she does elsewhere get to show off the sense of humour that she's neglected in recent years. She's too dynamic, though, to be a credibly contented stay-at-home mom. As Fletch, Nigel Shawn Williams gives one of his sympathetic accounts of beleaguered decency. I felt sorriest and/or angriest for Jane Spidell, a fine wasted actress, who has to cope with lines like "let me, Mrs. Motormouth, cut to the chase." Ron O. J. Parson's production seems to be trying to make all this seem natural, but there's no way.

The only good moments come when the play settles for public address as public address, instead of trying to pass it off as dialogue: when Dan Chameroy's nervous prof makes his doomed presentation to the Board of Education, or when, in the most powerful performance of the evening, Kevin Hanchard's assembly-line worker makes his rejoinder. The setting consists of fairly basic projections (pop groups and other memorabilia), and the costumes change to reflect the passage of time; as often happens in such cases, much of the acting is done by the hairstyles. This seems fitting for a play whose idea of establishing period is to have someone say, "We've got a new album. It's that Broadway musical Hair." - Palmer Park plays in repertory at the Stratford Shakespeare Festival until Sept. 21.
