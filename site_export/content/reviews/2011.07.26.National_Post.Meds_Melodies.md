THEATRE REVIEW

NEXT TO NORMAL

Four Seasons Centre for the Performing Arts, Toronto

The musical Next to Normal goes irretrievably down the tubes at the moment, early in Act One, when one of its characters, already a driving force in the action, is revealed to have been dead all along.

The character is Gabe, who died when he was a baby. His mother Diana, the show's central character, is a depressive; maybe bipolar, though we're cautioned that this can be a glib generalization that doesn't fit all cases. When we first meet her she's slipping into one of her bad periods, to the agonized dismay of her husband Dan and the resentful anger of their teenage daughter Natalie. A psychiatrist prescribes a cocktail of pills, which she tries for a time and then flushes down the toilet, encouraged by Gabe, whom at this point the audience believes to be alive. Later on, when we know he's dead, he reacts similarly but even more violently when another psychiatrist prescribes ECT. He's afraid that it will make Diana forget him.

There's the problem. If Gabe simply represented a voice in Diana's head, then we might accept his presence onstage as merely a device, however clunky. But he's given feelings and motivations of his own. And it isn't only Diana on whom he imposes them; he gets to confront his father as well, in a near-to-closing number that's touching on its own account but facile in its implications. If there are issues here, then why didn't the show confront them earlier? Most of the time, Gabe's influence on the musical's score, and on the show as a whole, is negative; his contributions veer between hysteria and New Agey sentimentality.

Which is a shame, because Next to Normal, which comes to us on its post-Broadway tour, obviously earns respect for its ambitions and, to some extent, for its achievements. After all, there are not many big musicals about mental illness, and there may well be even fewer about the travails of middle-class families; musicals traditionally end with a marriage rather than throwing us into the centre of one. If the roots of Diana's condition seem pat, its progress isn't, and the show ends realistically balanced between hope and despair. At least, its storyline does; musically it can't resist ending with one of those patented uplift numbers, this one with the giveaway title of Light.

The show's score actually sounds better on the cast album than it does in the theatre. That especially goes for Brian Yorkey's lyrics, which reach what is nowadays an unusually high level of intelligence and craftsmanship; the best song, catchily entitled My Psychopharmacologist and I, has Diana singing, "He knows my deepest secrets/ I know his .name." Barbs like this, though, are often drowned or blunted by the sound of shrillness; it's not so much that the words don't sit well on the music as that the music doesn't provide them with much of a saddle.

Tom Kitt's score, as orchestrated by Michael Starobin, has some beautiful lyrical passages; it also has a lot of unfocused pounding. After the decades that American theatre composers from Irving Berlin to Stephen Sondheim devoted to evolving a musical language that could reflect actual speechrhythms, it's frustrating to find the post-rock generations throwing it all away.

Yorkey also wrote the show's book, of which there isn't enough. This isn't quite a through-sung score, but it's near enough to one for the occasional spoken lines to fall on the ears like manna. Alice Ripley's performance of Diana is remarkable for its bravura honesty; her singing isn't always pretty, and some of her vowel sounds belong to her alone, but it's always musical and it always illuminates or expands the dramatic line. She originated the role in New York, and she at least is far more impressive live than on the CD, on which she sounds like anybody. But powerful as her singing is, she tells us more, and more trenchantly, about Alice's condition, when she's allowed to talk: so much so that I began to resent it when the music started up again.

Asa Somers' Dan is initially bland, but purposefully so; as he gradually discovers that even being ultra-supportive isn't always enough, he becomes moving. Natalie is a schematic character: a music major who seeks escape from the pressures at home in the disciplines of the classics, and is wooed and won by a boy who's more into jazz. Under his influence, and even beyond it, she experiments; just as Diana is going off her meds, Natalie gets into them.

Mother and daughter naturally have screaming duets. The parallel between the older and younger generations (two quiet men, two noisy women) is very forced. Anyway, Emma Hunton as Natalie does, efficiently, all that's required of her; likewise Preston Sadleir as the boyfriend, Curt Hansen as Gabe, and Jeremy Kushnier as a couple of shrinks. Michael Greif's production takes place on an overpowering threelevel set, perhaps to demonstrate how far apart these family members are, perhaps because even an unconventional Broadway musical is obliged to look big. Pre-Broadway it was apparently a chamber musical, and - visually, aurally and dramatically if not commercially - it would probably still work better that way.
