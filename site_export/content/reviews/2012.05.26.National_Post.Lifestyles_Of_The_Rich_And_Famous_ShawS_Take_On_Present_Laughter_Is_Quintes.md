Steven Sutcliffe, playing the central role of Garry Essendine in the Shaw Festival's production of Noel Coward's Present Laughter, makes a wonderful first impression. He emerges from his bedroom, in the middle hours of a weekday morning, to complain about the terrible racket that's deprived him of his beauty sleep. Since Garry is pretty much a surrogate for Coward himself, he's wearing a splendiferous dressing gown; the first, it turns out, of several. What issues from Sutcliffe's mouth, summing up his character perfectly, is a passionate whine, liberally laced with sheer cheek.

The cheek is especially apparent because the noise in his house is entirely his own fault. Garry, who when cornered admits to 39 (which was both Coward's own age, and that of the 20th century, when he wrote the play) is London's premier matinee idol; the previous night he brought home the latest in an obviously long line of adoring young females (on gender issues, the play does depart from strict autobiographical accuracy) who has woken up to find herself alone and abandoned in the spare room. Come the morning after, her host has pretty much forgotten who she is and why she was there. For him, she's just another of the people, both strangers and intimates, who want something from his infinitely forbearing self. He actually believes this.

And in some ways he's right. He has an inner circle of assistants and associates on whom he certainly depends but who also depend on him. Without his fame and talent where, as he constantly and rhetorically asks them, would they be? Garry, as everybody says including himself, is always on, always acting. His performance though, is totally sincere; and it's that mix, that contrast, that makes the play tick. Sutcliffe conveys it, for the most part, dazzlingly well; there's also (as there hasn't been in some actors of the role) a sunniness about him that makes his status as everybody's confidant thoroughly believable. He only falls off the tightrope at what seem to be Garry's (and Coward's) most heartfelt moments, most notably his crucial lecture on the hard graft it takes to become a professional playwright. Here earnestness takes over: We get the message but lose the messenger, who should still be playing a role, even though he's playing it sincerely. Sutcliffe, unwisely, lets the mask drop. Though so, come to think of it, did Coward himself. Because Garry here talks like a writer, though there's no indication elsewhere that he, unlike Coward, is anything but an actor.

Present Laughter is my own favourite of Coward's major comedies, partly because it's the loosest; the plot takes some time to get into gear, but while it does we're diverted by a relaxed genre painting of the comings and goings in Garry's luxurious studio, and in his even more luxurious ego. When the evening does shift into situation farce, with everyone and everything converging on Garry at once, it feels initially like a letdown, but the author's expertise in these matters gradually asserts itself. The play heads, easily and logically, for the quintessential Coward showdown; the finale tableau, common to all his best plays, in which one group of characters furtively departs the scene, leaving another group to its own raucous devices.

David Schurmann, a favourite Festival actor making his directorial debut, deals admirably with both the relaxed and frenzied moods on a peach of a set by William Schmuck that features startling murals, the obligatory staircase and, through a stage-sized back window, a stunning daylight vista of the Thames, with Big Ben prominently featured; at night, the sky is illumined by a half-moon that seems to have been flown in from one of Garry's musicals. There is, again, no mention of Garry actually doing musicals but, also again, one feels that he, like Coward, should have done them. The production does, in fact, feature between-scene selections of song, not by Coward, but by his American visa-vis Cole Porter, including one that's Porter's parody of a Coward song. Neat on several levels.

Among the supporting actors Mary Haney surpasses even Sutcliffe's presence and timing as his acerbically devoted secretary. You know the type, but she humanizes it; asked whether she finds Garry more charming onstage or off, she replies, "I can never quite make up my mind." You wouldn't think so simple a line could be so lethal. A raven-haired Moya O'Connell does an even more spectacular, though slightly less consistent, rehabilitation job on the vamp who threatens that old personal and professional gang of Garry's; she brings real personality - and real sexuality - to what could be a mechanical role. The men of the group are usually cardboard, but they're enlivened here by Gray Powell and especially by Patrick McManus, whose adoption of an American accent does wonders for the role of Garry's producer (and may well be a nod to Coward's real-life producer-lover Jack Wilson). As Garry's not-quite-ex-wife, Claire Jullien is off the sophisticated beam, while Julia Course as his latest conquest never gets anywhere near it. Jonathan Tan, however, as the angry young playwright (from Uckfield) who comes to lecture Garry and stays, immovably, to worship, gives a scenestealing and eventually show-stealing performance that involves an intimate relationship with furniture; when he isn't leaping over sofas, he's curling up on them, exuding excitement. In the smallest roles, we have, delightfully, Corinne Koslo as a psychic Scandinavian housekeeper, wreathed in shawls and cigarette smoke; James Pendarves as a Cockney valet whose untroubled self-assurance is everything that Garry thinks he envies; and Jennifer Phipps who, as a titled granny, moves mountains with a line about a mountain.

Present Laughter is in repertory through Oct. 28 at the Festival Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake. For show times and ticket information, call 1-800-511-7429 or visit shawfest.com.
