Every 10 years or so, the novels of Jane Austen hit our large and small screens with a collective thud. As there are only six books in the Austen canon, the movie studios this time around have felt obliged to supplement them with such cinematic fancies as Becoming Jane (the early life of a classic author) and The Jane Austen Book Club (the posthumous life of a classic author).

Meanwhile British television has been working its way, yet again, through the approved texts. Three of these adaptations are playing on TVO over the next three Sundays.

Leading off is Northanger Abbey, Austen's first novel, though not published until after her death.

It starts with a mild shock. Theres an introductory voice-over, delivered in creamily ironic tones by Geraldine James, billed here as "the Voice of Jane Austen."

She introduces us to our heroine, Catherine Moreland, one of 10 children in a genteel English country family and known when growing up as a tomboy whose favourite pastimes were cricket and baseball. Baseball? Surely Andrew Davies, the adaptor, notorious for smuggling jokey anachronisms into ancient texts, is tossing a bone to the North American market. But no, its authentic voice-of-Jane. America's national pastime was an English ex-sport.

Catherine, we learn, progressed from ball games to reading. She immersed herself in Gothic novels like Ann Radcliffe's The Mysteries of Udolpho, the original bodice-rippers.

Northanger Abbey is at once a spoof of the genre, an example of it and a reproach to it. Catherine, at least in this TV version, cant take a simple trip in a stagecoach without visions of highwaymen and ravishments dancing in her head.

Her destination is the spa town of Bath, the mecca of Regency England high society. Everythings more free and easy in Bath, says one of the visiting bucks.

From there she progresses to Northanger Abbey itself, a Gothic pile after her own heart. Extending the invitation to stay is the owner, one General Tilney, who looks like the place's grinning ogre. There should be lightning playing around his head.

For Catherine the place has two major attractions. One is the General's son Henry, a highly eligible young clergyman. The other is the abbeys potential as a repository of ghostly secrets.

Davies is the most prolific of TVs literary adaptors and one of the most skillful. There are only two reasons for objecting to this.

One is that Davies is, or used to be, an excellent TV dramatist in his own right; in the 1980s he wrote A Very Peculiar Practice, a cunning combination of two popular genres -- campus and medical -- and one of the best miniseries ever, a comedy that got deeper and darker as it went on.

The other is that his adaptations get further and further away from the words the novelists actually wrote. He keeps the plots and characters but he loses the style. Without Jane Austen's running commentaries, all you're left with a series of courtship anecdotes in pretty frocks.

Davies tries to get around this by modernizing not only dialogue but action. In practical terms, that means he makes explicit what Austen kept veiled and decorous.

This Northanger Abbey may be the first Jane Austen ever to include a nude scene: a back view only, and part of a dream, but still. "Nothing to be afraid of," says the Rev. Harry. One of Catherines friends gets seduced by an unscrupulous officer, and we get an actual bedroom scene.

Yes, Austens novels really are about sex and money, but there's still a feeling of being nudged. As in: You thought Jane Austen was repressed and spinsterish? This'll learn ya.

Its no substitute for an accurate, witty account of the protagonists progress. Felicity Jones' Catherine is a pretty miss from 21st-century suburbia. She doesn't, as all Austen heroines should, learn from her mistakes; its hard to wince with her or to rejoice.

JJ Feilds agreeable Henry has the right stuff; so, in a quieter vein, does Catherine Walker as Eleanor, his guardian angel of a sister. She gets married at the end of the story, to a suitor we haven't met before, and with the blessing of her hitherto implacable father.

The Voice of Jane chimes in again, cheerfully acknowledging the improbability and pondering whether her tale may be said "to recommend parental tyranny or reward filial disobedience."

The artificiality is delicious, but the TV script balks at going all the way with it. It gives us an earlier meeting between Eleanor and her betrothed.

Northanger Abbey is its author's slightest work, and so the more difficult to translate to another medium.

Mansfield Park and Persuasion, two of the major novels, lie ahead. We'll see.
