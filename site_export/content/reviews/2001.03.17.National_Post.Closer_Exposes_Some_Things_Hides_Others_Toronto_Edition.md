CLOSER

Berkeley Street Theatre

---

At the first night of Closer, Patrick Marber's hit play from London, I caught sight of my old friend, Phyllis Stein, whom I had last encountered a year ago at this very same theatre refusing to be shocked by George Walker's Heaven.

Closer offers the same kind of come-on: warnings in the lobby about sexual content and explicit language, with smoking and strobe- lights thrown in for good measure, so I couldn't resist phoning Phyllis the next morning and asking her how she had got on this time.

"Well," she said with the hint of a girlish giggle, rather charming from a woman in her forties, "I wasn't shocked this time either. I must be a hopeless case. I know people in London got up in the stalls and protested -- you see I do read your paper -- but I think it's really the language they objected to. They couldn't see past that."

"You objected yourself," I reminded her, "to every other word in Heaven being 'f---.'"

"Heaven," she said judicially, "wasn't basically about sex. Closer is. If you're shocked by that, you're shocked. But the language is appropriate. And it's very specific."

"You can say that again," I said, knowing she was far too sophisticated to waste my time by doing so. "It's like a modern British La Ronde, isn't it, though with only four characters. Instead of a daisy chain, it's an eternal quadrilateral. They keep pairing and re-pairing strictly heterosexually and there seems no good reason why they shouldn't go on forever.

"The only thing is," I admitted, "I can't quite remember now who did what with whom and in what order."

"Oh!" she exclaimed "Men! The morning after, they never know a thing. All right, pay attention. Alice, she's a stripper, of no fixed abode, who picks up Dan -- remember, he's the obituary writer who wants to be a novelist. And they move in together, and Dan gets his book written, and when he's getting his picture taken for the jacket he makes a pass at Anna, the photographer, who rejects him. Then Dan accidentally sets Anna up with Larry, a dermatologist, right? And Anna and Larry actually get married. But Anna leaves Larry for Dan, though she says she still loves Larry. And Larry, who is devastated, takes up with Alice, but eventually Dan goes back to Alice again, but his jealousy destroys them and ... well, I don't have to remind you how it ends, do I?"

"No," I said hurriedly "please don't. But you're good. That was quite a synopsis. You should do this for a living."

"Well," she replied with surprising frostiness. "I don't. You do. So, tell me: Is it a good play?"

The things people ask you. "Yes," I said, very slowly, "I think it is. But it's limited."

"Explain."

That was easier. "You only see the characters at their crisis points. And since they are never together long enough for passion to subside, those crises are explicitly sexual. There must be more to them, to the people and the relationships and indeed Marber, who's more of a softie than people believe. But he hasn't put it on stage. What there is, is compelling. It's a kind of beneath-the-surface brutality you don't often see exposed."

"I'll say. You talked about who did what with whom. It's more a matter of to whom. And the ones doing are mainly the men."

"Oh dear, I thought that too, and I was hoping it was just male guilt. Alice is a real victim, isn't she? She doesn't act or talk like one, but she's -- what's the jargon -- objectified. There's her job, and Dan writes about her, and Anna takes pictures of her. The girl who played her in London was a real waif-type. Angela Vint, who does her here, is chunkier and saltier, which is sort of interesting but makes her seem less central."

"I knew you'd start mentioning the London production."

"Well, I did see it. What I mostly remember is Sally Dexter as Anna. She was very contained and completely sexual without ever trying to be sexy. The Toronto Anna, Gina Wilkinson, is aggressively bright, even when she's in pain; it's like every line is supposed to be an epigram. One problem in Dennis Garnhum's production is that nearly everyone is acting the accent rather than the character. Blair Williams as the doctor is an exception, but even he misses some transitions that ought to be there. Shaun Smyth as Dan I just didn't believe. He's a nerd in his first scene and a sharpie thereafter. Shows what the love of a good woman and a publishing contract will do."

"Perhaps they should have done it without accents."

"I thought of that; there's nothing here that couldn't happen in Toronto. But it's written in these English rhythms, and they impose themselves. When they do a Canadian play in London, not that they often do, or an American one, you expect them to do the accents. What happens here is that a very common problem gets exaggerated. Neutral lines get artificially coloured, usually with anger or self- pity. I know people think I have other obsessions, but that's the one I find myself going on about. That kind of acting isn't truthful and it punches holes in the play.

"But I liked the physical production. The play is set in rooms from an emergency ward to a strip club, which are done in modules; and for the last scene, in a graveyard, the whole stage opens up, right to the theatre's own brick walls. Peter Hartwell is the designer; it's brilliant.

"And now Phyllis, you have to tell me, what did you think of the famous cybersex scene?"

"Two men sending one another erotic messages, one of them pretending to be a woman? I thought it was a turn-on, like the scene in the club. And I loved the flushed eagerness on Blair Williams' face, and the irritation when he got a call from a patient: very funny. And you?"

"I found those scenes intriguing, but not arousing. The computer is actually a breakthrough: innovative and dramatically telling. But the sexiest scene for me was the first, the pick-up. All right, it's a male fantasy. But the detail is real; it's the most relaxed writing in the play. And how about the scene where Anna tells Larry, in great anatomical detail, what she and Dan did in bed, or on the couch?"

Phyllis paused. "I'm on my second marriage. And the first one ended badly; my ex was very jealous. I think he wanted to ask me those things, and if he had, I would have told him. As explicitly as Anna does. In those words. And with that grief and that anger. The play is about sex used as power -- verbally, hence the computer- scene, and physically. There's nothing new about it; we just have additional technology. A scene like that lays bare what all plays involving love or sex, which is nearly all plays, are really about but never say.

"Yes, it is shocking, in a good way. More than any nude scene. There should probably be an additional warning: No Nudity in This Play."

I was impressed.

"I think," I said, "I shall use that."
