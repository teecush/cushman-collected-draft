THEATRE REVIEW

Clybourne Park

Berkeley Street Theatre

The first act of Clybourne Park takes place in an American suburban house of the 1950s, and it plays initially like a typical domestic comedy of that era.

There's Russ, the householder, deep in his armchair and in the National Geographic, and there's Bev, his wife, bustling around and amiably bickering with him over the location and pronunciation of foreign countries and whether people from Moscow can seriously expect to be called Muscovites. It's all reliably entertaining in the writing and, in this Canadian premiere, in the performance. Then, suddenly, Russ snaps, displaying feelings and using language unwelcome in the polite society of the time, and certainly in its polite drama. And after that explosive moment nothing in Bruce Norris's play or in this exceptional production from Studio 180 is the same again.

This is America 1959, post-Korea, pre-civil rights. Russ and Bev are about to leave. They've sold their house to a black family, the first ever to move into this prosperous Chicago neighbourhood. If that sounds theatrically familiar, it's meant to. The play, a Pulitzer Prize winner from its off-Broadway run and only now about to open on Broadway (making the Toronto production something of a scoop), takes off from a much earlier American success, Lorraine Hansberry's A Raisin in the Sun. It gives us the other side of the story, one as acidic as Hansberry's was hopeful.

It seems the reason the Raisin family was able to buy was that Russ and Bev were desperate to sell. There's been a tragedy in their family, and in their house. One of its effects has been to sour Russ on the vaunted "community" of his neighbours, whose reaction to the imminent invasion ranges from bland discomfort to active sabotage. The latter is the province of Karl, the one carry-over character from Raisin where we met him there trying to bribe the Younger family into cancelling their bid. We now see him on his home front preaching, with ever-increasing virulence, the virtues of polite apartheid (have you ever, he asks, seen a coloured person skiing?) and even trying to enlist the moral support of Russ and Bev's black maid and her prince of a husband.

As Russ, his fuzzy amiability giving way suddenly and irrevocably to wounded bottomless rage, Michael Healey gives what may well be the performance of his life. Likewise, I have never seen Maria Ricossa be as good as she is here, wedding style to substance as she shows Bev's initial flightiness maturing into an honest perplexity over why people with different complexions shouldn't live on the same street. (It's only with the best intentions that she's patronized the help, like her counterparts in Caroline, or Change, and indeed The Help.)

It's a shock to realize that Mark McGrinder's Karl is the same character that Diego Matamoros played in the Soulpepper production of Raisin; that weaselly politico is hardly recognizable in the enflamed hothead whom we encounter here. The scarier he gets, the funnier he is; and it isn't a comfortable kind of funny. His wife, played delightfully by Kimwun Perehinec, is deaf, and the play isn't afraid to get at least one huge belly laugh from that either. But it isn't a cheap laugh; it comes at a climax that's at once farcical and threatening, and it pushes the multiple tensions even higher.

They escalate further in the second act, set in the same house an exact half century later. There, it seems, went the Clybourne neighbourhood. This time it's a white couple who are moving in, with plans to tear the place down and build something grander, and a black couple, comfortably professional, who are protesting the encroaching gentrification. The two sides meet to negotiate, flanked by a pair of lawyers (cool satiric portraits, wittily filled out by Ricossa and Jeff Lillico).

We have, on the surface, come a long way, baby. There's no explicit racial or cultural divide; a man can reveal that he's gay without the heavens falling, or being expected to, and people chat as equals. They exchange vacation tips, and quiz one another about the capital of Morocco, thus reprising and amplifying the geographic patter from earlier; the act is full of echoes. Underneath, of course, things aren't so sweet.

Subversive humour becomes not just the play's medium but its subject. The meeting becomes a battle, one mainly fought from seated positions. Or rather, it's a game of "my grievance can beat your grievance" played through an escalating series of calculatedly outrageous jokes. The jokes become the structural equivalent of Chekhov's revolver: you show 'em, you'd better tell 'em. As the couples square off against one another, the rifts in both marriages open up; and the play becomes a brilliant display of cross-cutting revelations. It keeps topping itself, bitingly and hilariously.

It's also, under Joel Greenberg's direction, a brilliant feat of ensemble work: fine in the first act, superb in the second. The performances, all individually excellent, mesh perfectly, bouncing off one another with exhilarating precision. McGrinder and Perehinec play the embattled newcomers, he as overbearing an underdog as, pre-intermission, he was an over dog, she all squawk and self-preservation. Audrey Dwyer and Sterling Jarvis, who as the first act domestics were all strength and forbearance, now get to explore all the self-protectiveness the middle class is heir to, she svelte and increasingly edgy, he relaxed and good humoured until his last amazing moment.

Studio 180's nine-year track record breaks down into quasi-documentaries and the rest; this is the best of the rest, by far. It did leave me wondering how the play will fare in its reincarnation on Broadway, which prefers its serious drama preachy and soggy (Wit, Proof, Doubt, Red) or gothic and rambling (August Osage County) rather than in the form of comedy as tight and tough as this. Maybe the Pulitzer will do the trick; this is one of the few award-winning plays that actually deserves its prize. It's also, underneath its ruthless equal-opportunity satire, a haunted house play that genuinely haunts .
