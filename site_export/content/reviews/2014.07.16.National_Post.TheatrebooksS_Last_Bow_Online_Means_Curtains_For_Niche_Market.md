Friday July 18. A sad day for Toronto book lovers, and for theatre lovers both within and without the city. It's the day of the closure of TheatreBooks, after 39 years of much-loved existence. Its the place where both residents and visitors went to find scripts of plays, and books, both learned and popular, about drama, opera, dance and film. Leonard McHardy and John Harvey, the two men who founded it back in 1975, have run it for the whole of its lifespan.

TheatreBooks loss is, of course, part of a general winnowing of specialist and independent bookshops: victims not, as they might once have feared, of the big-box stores who have never attempted to duplicate their particular inventories, but of the online world, where you can get everything and get it cheap. Never mind that browsing on a screen can never compare, for pleasure or surprise, with browsing actual shelves: we're all complicit. When the news of Theatre-Books' closure broke, one of Torontos best young actresses tweeted that she felt both sorry and guilty; she regretted that she hadnt been in more, but she had been unable to resist the lure of the mouse. Possible customers would ask McHardy if he could pricematch Amazon; he had to confess, emphatically, "No, we can't."

Michael Healey, actor and playwright, told McHardy, You have every right to be bitter and angry. McHardy says, "I don't want to be bitter" and indeed he doesnt sound as if he is.

He does, obviously, feel sad. He had originally wanted to work in the theatre himself, as a director; he had spent a year in England on a British Council grant, working in a regional theatre. Returning home, I couldnt find a place in the Toronto theatre; Canadian theatrical nationalism was at its height and "everyone was doing plays by David French or David Freeman."

Around then he met Harvey (his partner in life as well as in business) and we said, 'We' ll open a bookstore.

John built the shelves himself. I helped him. They were in virtually unexplored territory, but one for which there proved to be a need. "Being a niche market", says McHardy, protected us for quite a time. People just couldnt find books on theatre elsewhere. They certainly couldnt, when TheatreBooks started, find books on Canadian theatre, because hardly any existed. It's a market that grew along with the store itself. "Now there must be 40 to 50 textbooks on Canadian theatre."

Their first location was at Yonge and Charles, where there were about seven other independent booksellers around us. About the time they opened, two British theatrical knights, John Gielgud and Ralph Richardson, were appearing at the Royal Alex in Harold Pinter's No Mans Land. "Gielgud," says Mc-Hardy, saw the store, came upstairs, carrying a Persian cigarette case. I think he rather fancied John, my partner.

Gielgud had written books, which they had in stock, and they asked him if hed sign them. "We asked him if he'd sign one of his own early books. He said, 'Id do anything for you. " (McHardy does a very good Gielgud impersonation.) And thus began a tradition, TheatreBooks became the preferred venue for book-signings and any other promotional events connected with the performing arts.

Yonge and Charles was the first of four locations in the store's lifetime. Its last resting place has been downtown, at Spadina and Adelaide, but the longest-serving address, 20 years, was on St. Thomas, just south of Bloor.

Wherever it's been, TheatreBooks has had the reputation of being the place where you could expect to run into friends and colleagues. One thinks of it as the preserve of professionals and fans, but its also performed an important educational role. Teachers have brought students there; aspiring actors have gone there to locate plays theyve heard about but never read or to find material for auditions. It's always been a very friendly place.

Harvey, a former teacher himself, says that that is what he will miss: Young people coming up to me and saying 'I nailed that audition with the monologue you suggested  - a monologue that staff may have devoted a couple of hours to helping them find. "Weve had wonderful young people working in the store. Neither Leonard nor I have drama degrees, and we've learned so much from them."

Harvey adds: "People looking elsewhere for books they couldn't find would say, Well, theres always TheatreBooks.' Well, guess what; there isn't TheatreBooks any more."
