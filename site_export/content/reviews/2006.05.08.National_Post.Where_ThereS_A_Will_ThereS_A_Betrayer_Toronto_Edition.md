robertcushman@lycos.com

Tara Rosling gives an extraordinary performance as Catherine Sloper, the shy, plain heroine of The Heiress, betrayed by the two men who should love her the best. At least she sees herself as plain, and most of the other characters agree with her. The audience doesn't. The audience sees a woman of great physical and intellectual potential whose upbringing has deprived her of all confidence. At the end of the play she gains it, at the price of losing everything else.

The Heiress is an adaptation, by Ruth and Augustus Goetz, of the Henry James novel Washington Square. "Suggested by says the printed text; the Shaw Festival program denies James even that meagre credit, though it's full of other information about him. It reminds us of the often-noted fact that though the stage-struck novelist was never able to write a sucessful play of his own, his tales have consistently provided the basis for other peoples hits. Everybody gains, including those of us who find the books unreadably precious and who are grateful to the plays for providing us with easy access. The Heiress, first staged on Broadway in 1947 and a repertory staple ever since, may be the most successful of the lot. The Goetzes are presumably responsible for the narrative craft and James for the psychological complexity. In this production its gripping entertainment that never insults the intelligence.

Catherine is the only child of Dr. Austen Sloper, a prosperous New York physician of the mid-19th century whose wife died young. He has never stopped comparing Catherine to his wife and finding her wanting. His daughter's only talent seems to be embroidery and that's not enough. Help her to be clever," he asks his sister, sounding helpless; "I should so like her to be clever. He wants someone who will sparkle in his drawing-room.

Roslings Catherine might well be capable of cleverness, even of sparkle; she can say intelligent things and she can even laugh. But shes been told so often that she's dull that she's retreated into dowdiness. When she tries to break out, she tries too hard and gives up. At her first appearance she looks stunning, but self-conscious. And her voice, repressed and tremulous, is that of someone who has marshalled her thoughts and then lost all trust in them.

I wondered if this sound would be enough to see the actress through the play. It turns out that it is; the dynamics dont change much but the intensity does. Her voice carries her from repression to awakening to abandon to agony, and finally to a denial, like burning ice, of herself and everyone else. Catherine, for the first time in her life, is wooed, by a stylish but feckless young man named Morris Townsend. She falls headlong and unconditionally in love. The doctor has Morris pegged as a fortune-hunter, probably with good reason. Catherine survives the imputation that nobody could over her except for her money, though the scene is painful to watch; her world collapses when Morris seems to prove the doctor right.

Neither father nor lover earns a second chance. What keeps the play grown-up is that we are never sure that Catherine has made the right choice. Justice is on her side; the chance for happiness, however compromised, may not be. Accused of being cruel Catherine replies, in a line that sounds famous even if youve never heard it before, yes, I have been taught by masters!" A sense of tragic inevitablity haunts Rosling's performance here. Locking herself up in her father's Washington Square mansion, shes trapped in his estimation of her.

Mike Sharas Morris helps keep the options open; few actors can do sincerity as he can, and when he professes his love for Catherine there remains the tantalizing possibility that he isn't wholly faking it. There's little plumbing of ambiguous depths in Michael Balls doctor, but in its conventional way his unbending paterfamilias fills the space. As his widowed sister, and everybodys confidante and hanger-on, Donna Belleville grows steadily from dramatic convenience to person in her own right; she's also an audience surrogate, hoping against hope -- and against reason -- for a happy ending.
