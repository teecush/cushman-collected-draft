Chekhov's The Seagull famously flopped at its first production, and though there seem to have been purely local and technical reasons for this, the fundamental cause was that neither actors nor audience knew what to make of a play that could not be pigeonholed as comedy or tragedy, and that dispensed with an obvious plot line or protagonist. Instead it offered a shifting group of principal characters and shifting themes. It was ahead of its time in other ways, too. It is, as far as I can tell, the first play to focus on differing attitudes to the creation and appreciation of art. Put more prosaically, its the first play about fan-worship.

So here we have Irina Arkadina, famous Russian actress, spending a discontented summer at her brothers place in the country, accompanied by her lover Trigorin, famous novelist. We have her son Konstantin, aspiring author, who despises Trigorin and all his works, and is inclined to despise his mother and all hers, but is inhibited by the facts that she is his mother, and that he adores her. We have Nina, aspiring actress, for whom Konstantin has written an extremely abstract one-woman play and whom he adores; which is unfortunate for him, as she is entranced at the mere thought of meeting Trigorin and is swept off her feet, and eventually into his bed, when she does. Around this quartet, we have an array of friends and relations, most of them suffering from unrequited love or unfulfilled dreams, and nearly all of them with opinions to offer on the role of the artist (exalted) or the state of the theatre (in decline).

The new production at the Berkeley Street Upstairs is the work of the Chekhov Collective, who take their name and their principles not from the playwright but from his nephew Michael, a famous Russian actor and acting teacher. His techniques, according to the program, involved the actors meeting at intervals over a year "to build the ensemble and to explore the characters and the world of the play. I can't say that the results strike me as notably different from any other decent production of the play. But they are decent, and sometimes more than that.

Physically, and for reasons probably more economic than aesthetic, it's a modest staging, with a single set consisting of a slatted back wall with holes in it; some of these represent doors but not all, though it takes some time to work this out. In the meantime, you may find yourself wondering why the characters always seem to be taking the longest way round to reach the acting area. Peggy Coffeys production is at its most inventive in the first-act presentation of Konstantins play, beside a lake and before his mostly baffled acquaintances. He has Nina move among them, followed and covered by a large frame that sometimes threatens to engulf the onlookers as well; it makes him an early exponent of audience involvement, as well as of site-specific staging. (For which The Seagull itself might be an excellent candidate. We certainly have the lakes.) All this avant-garde stuff presumably adds to his mother's irritation and contributes to her appalling rudeness to her hyper-sensitive son.

Rena Polley, who plays her and who also wrote the adaptation, underplays this; indeed she hardly conveys Arkadina the famous actress at all. She's considerably better at playing the mother, trying to compensate for her neglect with a concern thats fulsome but genuine. Shes a bit of a Gertrude which Konstantin acknowledges by quoting Hamlet at her, thereby setting up Nina as Ophelia. The comparison holds since Nicole Wilson's Nina resembles most Ophelias by being a blur in her earlier scenes but maturing in madness, brought on by Trigorin's abandonment of her, the loss of their child and her frustrated acting career. All the same, the play seems to be saying, shell survive, personally and professionally. Konstantin doesnt; he may have the talent but he doesn't have the stuff, and Riley Gilchrist admirably charts his consequent descent into despair.

Patrick Garrow points up the contrast as Trigorin, careless about everything but his work, the dishevelled opposite of a matinee idol, but still a celebrity magnet for Nina; he's especially good too at showing the weakness that has him slithering between both women. Greg Ellwand, though almost too hale and tall for the role, is excellent as Irinas ailing brother, who laments a wasted life and physically declines before our eyes, but is close as anyone to being the plays moral centre; he's significantly absent from its final catastrophe, as is the hapless schoolmaster Medvedenko, who has at least commanded our attention with the play's famous opening line (Why do you always wear black?") and whom Andrei Preda plays with utter conviction. The world and the characters are there, all right; how completely depends on who at any given moment is embodying them.

The least satisfactory thing about The Seagull has always been the seagull: the bird that Konstantin shoots, that Trigorin forgets and that Nina identifies with? "Is this some kind of symbol? shes asked earlier, as if warding off the possibility that shell end up in an Ibsen play.

There's a flock of seagulls around right now; Peter Hinton has staged the play in Ottawa and next year, in a fine ornithological confusion, Crow's Theatre will be mounting it, also at Berkeley Street but downstairs. Currently, that space is occupied by Nightwood Theatres The Carousel. Denyse Karn designed the highly effective set for this, a hospital corridor stretching away from us in deep perspective and capable of becoming, at least in our imagination, a regional highway; shes also credited with projections, which are modest but help the imaginative process, and with costumes, though the plural is puzzling, since there's only one performer and she never changes her clothes. Allegra Fulton is the actress, and she's splendid. Megan Follows is the first-time director, and seems to have done a god job. The play, by Jennifer Tremblay, purports to present the same character as the authors earlier monologue The List, though its hard to see how they correspond. In this one the protagonist sits outside the room where her mother is dying, recalls her past travails and travels (mostly along that Route 138) and those of the mother and her mother, and about the men from whom they've had to take refuge. Despite Fulton's unfailing concentration and her prodigious technique, it's hard to know, or care, which generation she happens to be summoning. She also talks about her son, whom she takes for a ride on the titular merrygo-round in Paris, and whom she always addresses as my son". It's very lyrical, very sensitive and very dull.
