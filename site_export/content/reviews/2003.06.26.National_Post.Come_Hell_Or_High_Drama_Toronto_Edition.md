NO EXIT

Tom Patterson Theatre, Stratford, Ont.

Jean-Paul Sartre's Huis Clos has taken up permanent residence in the modern consciousness as a new vision of damnation, one in which the fires and tortures favoured in the Middle Ages are replaced by subtler and more economical methods. Three lost souls, each specially selected for the capacity to get on the nerves of the other two, are locked up for eternity in a Second Empire drawing- room. Ninety minutes of this is enough to bring them to the realization that "hell is other people."

The play, variously translated as No Exit (Stratford's choice), In Camera and Vicious Circle, is a college-course favourite and a staple of student theatre groups, but its professional productions, at least in English, are rare. On the evidence of this Stratford production, that strange combination of celebrity and obscurity is justified. The idea is indelible, the execution flat.

It may seem unreasonable to complain that the characters never come to life; they are, after all, dead. But you never believe that they could have lived. Besides, Sartre was an atheist, and his inferno is really a conceit, in which the things living people do to one another are carried to their ultimate conclusion. At least that's the idea. It isn't how the play works while you're actually watching it. To the extent that it works, it works as a vision of the afterlife.

There are two women and a man. The man, Cradeau, was shot as a deserter (the play was written at the end of the Second World War), and though he would like to claim status as a conscientious objector, his motives seem to have been a lot shadier. He was also, in the great tradition of French intellectuals, very mean to his wife. Interestingly, though he is quite willing to accept his status as one of the damned, he is very evasive when it comes to accepting detailed responsibility. He is the first onstage, and the play, not too surprisingly, is written largely from his point of view.

The women are denied even his limited measure of complexity. They're sketched in with almost contemptuous lightness. Estelle is vain and a man-chaser; she killed her baby because motherhood was too much trouble, and drove its father to suicide. Inez is a lesbian and a secretary, and those really are the only things we learn about her.

Sartre's eschatology is notably materialist. His characters have brought their bodies with them, and they are still wearing the clothes that they once stood up in (which are not, probably, the clothes they were buried in). Maybe the inmates are in for more physical torment than the authorities let on; the three unsupported circular pouffes that are the room's only furnishings are surely going to be hell on their backs.

We assume the characters do have physical frailties because they still have physical desires. Cradeau and Estelle would like to have sex together, but are frustrated by the jealous presence of Inez. The same would be true if lust were to strike in some other combination.

Over eternity, one could presumably get used to being watched; what these characters can't stand is being disapproved of. They are narcissists, who nevertheless depend on others for their validation. There are no mirrors in this room, so they can only find their reflections in one another's eyes. They can't escape those others, because the lights are never turned off and their eyelids appear to have been surgically removed. By an especially cruel twist, they are able to see the people who were once in their lives carrying on without them and saying unkind things into the bargain.

Put it like that, and it's no wonder Sartre's conclusion has passed into the language: Hell is other people, if you have to be locked up with them forever. It's so obviously true that it hardly matters who the other people are. Even one's nearest and dearest would probably pall. The play's choice of characters seems arbitrary; anyone would do. (Being confined with these three for a mere hour and a half is an infernal sort of experience for the audience, but I don't think that's meant to be the entire point.)

So the play seems frivolous, in a Gallically philosophic kind of way. I have a friend who thinks that what Sartre really meant was "Hell is other French people." He may have something there.

That said, there are probably subtler gradations of cruelty in the script than are realized here. Jim Warren's production is the staged equivalent of an intelligent reading. Everybody shouts when required, laughs when required (most noticeably when they realize the implications of the play's celebrated punchline), and nobody digs very deep. They seem, for hand-picked mutual torturers, to be letting each other off very lightly.

Claire Jullien as Estelle flounces about the stage, looking very blond and fetching in a blue dress but exhibiting the character's shallowness rather than exploring it. Chick Reid's Inez is flinty and hectoring, as if that were what defined a lesbian. Stephen Ouimette's Cradeau is somewhat subtler, imprinting himself on the mind as a man with a pervasive mental wince. His last illusions collapse when, doubled up in mirthless merriment, he announces, "I'm dead -- I've gone into public domain," one of the few memorable phrases in a disappointingly flavourless translation by U.S. poet Paul Bowles.

There is a fourth character, the bellboy who shows the principals to their room. As played by Andrew Massingham he is obseqious and opaque, which are the right qualities. I shall think of him as the spiritual (if that's the word) cousin of the minor devil who, in a recent New Yorker cartoon, inquired solicitously of a new arrival, "And now your choice for all eternity -- ranch, blue cheese or Italian?" Getting to choose: That could be a worse hell than Sartre's.
