THE WHIRLPOOL

Tarragon Theatre, Toronto

The set is a raised disc, green under the lights, with a line of plastic bags filled with water hanging down below. An additional supply of pendent bags hangs from an array of scaffolding behind; a construction that also supports some of the stage lamps and at times the actors. Sounds of plashing water fill the air.

It isn't especially redolent of Niagara, which is where the action is set, but it certainly seems marine. A sign in the lobby warns that there will be fog, but like so many weather forecasts this turns out to be a false alarm. Unless it refers to the writing and presentation which, if not quite foggy, are certainly misty.

Brian Quirts play is based on a novel by Jane Urquhart that I havent read. It is 1889 and David, a historian with strong nationalist feelings, has come to town to lecture; his theme is how what were apparently Canadian military defeats can be seen as victories.

With him, or much of the time at a discreet distance from him, is his wife, Fleda, who likes to spend her time reading poetry. She has a taste for the romantic verse of Robert Browning, but her husband is trying to wean her on to the more moralistic offerings of Coventry Patmore. He has presented her with a copy of Patmore's chef- d'oeuvre The Angel in the House, and he has promised to build her a house so that she can settle down to being the angel in it.

Soon she gets to meet a real, live and personably young poet whose name is Patrick and who comes into their orbit when he attends one of Davids talks. He has a dangerous fascination with whirlpools, especially the one in the Niagara River. Fledas thing is forests. Her husband's is Laura Secord; he tells Patrick of a dream in which his wife appeared to him in Laura's costume, bidding him remember her. (As Patrick acutely puts it, he remembers being told to remember.)

Meanwhile, Maud, a lady in the local undertaking business, is fixated on the names, histories and personal effects of those unfortunates who have come to watery ends nearby. This leaves her little time for her five-year-old son (played by a large grown-up actor), who has trouble speaking, or at least in arranging words in any meaningful order. Some encounters with Patrick greatly improve his fluency, though his mother seems less delighted by this than you might expect.

Things happen, but that doesnt in itself make a dramatic action. The characters spend their time mentally stalking one another (physically too, on occasion) and to convey this, Quirt, who directs his own adaptation, has enlisted the help of a choreographer, Julia Sasso. She gets the actors weaving around one another, dodging and avoiding, looking but apparently not seeing.

Sometimes they do indeed seem like swimmers caught in a whirlpool. At others they are more like wound-up automata, dodgem cars turned loose on a chessboard. This happens especially when Fleda, Patrick and David play a game of cards, the unacknowledged passions of their relationship simmering beneath. As they play their hands, the two men aggressively advance or smoulderingly retreat, suggesting nothing so much as the battle of the Red and White Knights in Through the Looking Glass.

At least this seems to be intentionally funny; much of the time this modified ballet just seems mildly, and not very communicatively, grotesque. The actors simply dont have the grace or the skills, though they certainly go through the exercises with unfaltering dedication. The only one, though, who can make them work for him is Andrew Massingham, who, playing the five-year-old, is in a strange place to start with.

I am willing to believe that the original material is neither as attenuated nor as pretentious as it appears here. But on stage the narrative doesn't cohere and the themes don't cross-illuminate.

There are some good evocative monologues, infused with a downbeat wit; Tracey Ferencz as Fleda, a woman half-protestingly trapped, does especially well by hers. Outside them, she seems involved in a husband-wife-poet triangle out of Shaws Candida, which the fragmentary dialogue scenes both underwrite and overvalue.

Jordan Pettle plays Patrick, Christine Brubaker does the funerary lady, and David is Martin Julien, who came into the show at short notice and has mastered a complicated script and movement-score with, on the first night, no apparent signs of strain.

His characters patriotic preoccupations seem to have no effect on anyone else, other than to provide them with a source of irritated amusement. (He goes around asking people if they dream Canadian dreams.) It's not his fault, or those of his authors, that commercial interests have turned the name of Laura Secord into an automatic source of giggles, but the fact remains. The Canadian content, though, does serve to infuse the audience with a certain amount of guilt when it feels bored or baffled.

Quirt's previous play was seen at the Passe Muraille, and he is resident dramaturge at the Factory; now here he is writing and directing at the Tarragon. Good for him that he gets around, but Toronto's leading small theatres do seem, to put it politely, close- knit. Maybe this is why much of the time they all seem to be presenting the same play.
