EASY VIRTUE

Festival Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

It's unusual these days for actors in a Noel Coward play to imitate the Masters voice; it would be thought just too, too painful. However, in the Shaw Festival revival of Easy Virtue, Goldie Semple (reprising and refining the role she originated last season) bucks the trend, making fascinating play with Cowards famous clipped drawl, taking it down an octave or so to accommodate her own range and her own intentions. If she sometimes seems to exaggerate, it's done with conscious wit; the character she plays is being aggressively defensive. She's Larita, a creature of Cowards own 1920s demi-monde, thrown into painful proximity with the most repressed and repressive stratum of English country life.

Larita is a divorcee in her thirties whose proudest boast is that she never had an affair with a man she didnt love. This doesn't sit well with the Whittakers, whose son John -- "a boy," Larita calls him -- she has just married. They mistrust her, and lecture her, and end up hating her. She gives as good as she gets (better, since she has her author on her side), but she is out of place and outnumbered, and ends up acknowledging her mistake and walking out, at the height of a lavishly terpsichorean house party.

The visual panache of Christopher Newton's direction (energetic Charlestons and a hugely handsome drawing room set) is now matched by verbal and emotional depth. The cut-and-thrust of Cowards dialogue is impeccably arranged; and there seems for once to be as much going on beneath the jokes as there is officially supposed to be. People give themselves away, and the moments are nailed down for us.

The scenes between Larita and her Johnny have especial resonance, sexual and social; she reads Proust while he plays tennis, and though they still love one another they are drifting inexorably apart. Ben Carlson has taken over as John, and his performance -- another of his intelligently spirited reclamations of potentially tedious young men -- clinches his position as the best jeune premier (traditional juvenile) in Canadian theatre. It also helps transform the production.

John is about the only character to make an ambiguous claim on our sympathies. Otherwise, there are those we and the author like and approve of, and those we dont.

In the first camp are Larita herself and her new father-in-law: An officer (retired), a gentleman, an oasis of tolerance in a morally thirsty land, an admirable fellow whom David Schurmann admirably plays. (One misses him in the last-act shindig, from which his absence is never explained: a surprisng lapse in dramatic craftsmanship, considering it's his house.)

In the other there are the female Whittakers, who show their colours early on by piously despatching a fallen servant-girl to a life of shame in the capital. They run a gamut of censorious narrow- mindedness: from implacable matriarchy (Patricia Hamilton, a caricature but a skilful one), through neurotic piety of the muscular-Christian variety (Kelli Fox, irresistibly hearty), to adolescent hysteria. It isn't that Coward doesnt understand them; he has Larita offer several lively and plausible analyses of their behaviour. He even tries to sympathize with them, or at least with their predicament. But he cant love them; so they remain cardboard, and the play, though richer than it seemed last year, stays one- sided.

That's not to say it isn't fun. Its always jolly to see hypocrisy exposed, especially if you feel yourself to be safely on the other side. The Whittakers particular prejudices may be outmoded, but the urge to condemn what you don't understand remains universal. You may even wonder whether Larita's own world is so very enlightened, or whether her virtue doesnt come as easily as that of her opponents. When she meets up with an old Riviera acquaintance, and they start swapping memories and dropping names, it all seems frightfully shallow, like the Marvellous Party of a later Coward song; except that here it isnt being lampooned. Maybe that's intended, though; whatever bed Larita is repairing too at the end, it won't necessarily be one of roses.

The play runs out of story in its last act, but the spectacle sees it through. And the high-octane precision of the central performance makes the experience invigoratingly Semple.
