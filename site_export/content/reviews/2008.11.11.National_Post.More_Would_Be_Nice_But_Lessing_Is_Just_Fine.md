THEATRE REVIEW

Emilia Galotti

Avon Theatre, Stratford, Ont.

This year's Stratford Festival -- uneven as they all are, but with an unusually high proportion of peaks -- concluded with something from abroad. This was a bold and welcome initiative. Its also, things being as they economically are, one thats unlikely to be repeated in a hurry. Pity.

The Deutsches Theater of Berlin brought us their much-travelled, much-heralded and much-reduced production of Emilia Galotti, a five-act drama by Gotthold Ephraim Lessing, here clocking in at a brisk 90 minutes, many of them devoid of dialogue. Lessing's play dates from 1772; it's a bourgeois tragedy about a prince who falls helplessly in lust with a daughter of the middle classes, the titular Emila, on the day shes about to marry someone else. An over-zealous chamberlain arranges to have the maiden abducted, her fiance winding up as collateral damage. Once he has her in his lair, the prince seems uncertain how to proceed; these arent the Middle Ages after all. Surprisingly, Emilia is allowed visits from her parents, who never had much time for aristocrats anyway. Their resentments are greatly encouraged by the prince's scorned fury of an ex-mistress, who gives Emilia's father a dagger for a present, and more or less tells him to get on with it. The play, at least in this version, ends up quite literally dancing on a knife-edge.

Michael Thalheimers production begins, also literally, with fireworks, presumably symbolizing the explosions taking place in the characters heads. It concludes with the heroine picking her way through a stage-full of elegantly swirling couples, none of whom we have seen before, and none of whom is permitted to take a final bow alongside the seven principals. (European actors sure love their curtain calls.) One wondered whether the dancing help had been flown over, or whether they were locally recruited, perhaps from imminently unemployed members of the Festival's company.

Between these bookend coups, the action unfolds in a long bare room with a single door at the back. It's anything but palatial, and theres nothing extravagant or historical about the costumes, either. The received wisdom on this production is that its banished the play's social conflicts in favour of the animal passions beneath. I only buy half of that. Emotion here is certainly front and centre, climbing up the walls and rolling all over the floor, but that doesn't banish the matter of class. Its built-in. Sven Lehmanns prince, poleaxed by his thwarted desire for a girl he's just seen in church, throws himself on the ground and thrashes about in agonies of frustration only available to someone who's been accustomed from birth to having anything he wants. Similarly, the chamberlain Marinelli (Ingo Hulsmann) ties himself into vocal and physical knots in which obsequiousness is bound up with imperturbability. He acts the perfect servant as if his life depended on it, which it very likely does, but the mask cant help slipping. Marinelli is one of those supporting characters who thinks the play should really be about him, and resentment is the natural result.

Its all excitingly theatrically, though it means that Lehmann has to go on graphically demonstrating that the prince is smitten long after we've gotten the message. A program note congratulating Thalheimer on his tried and trusted radicalism (isn't there a contradiction in there somewhere?) claims that, in his hands, the play is about "the failure of communication." Well, show me the play that isnt. Its true that what remains of the text in this production is raced through, with the actors jumping on lines that denote contradiction or misunderstanding, and that much is made of the royal lackey's misconstruing of his master's orders --or at least of his master, in time-honoured handwashing fashion, pretending that he has. But its also true that these actors arent concealing their feelings. They're letting them all hang out, in a manner rarely witnessed in polite company.

At least the men are. The women are more sphinx-like, and they're a fascinating pair. We never quite know what Regine Zimmermanns rail-thin Emilia is thinking or how much of a victim she is; she could be passive or calculating or just enduring. Probably shes all three. We can see what's driving Nina Hoss's cast-off countess (the rank is important). Shes a wonderfully poised and elegant figure, ravaged with jealousy, but exactly how much destruction she intends to wreak is left in doubt. The production is underscored with a single, almost continuous waltz; when it stops, the relief is brief and the silence even more oppressive.

It would be interesting to see this ruthless concentration applied to an English Jacobean play: The Changeling, say, or Tis Pity She's a Whore, to both of which this play bears some narrative resemblance. This is not the balanced, humane Lessing, Enlightenment personified, to whom Soulpepper introduced us with their production of his Nathan the Wise. (He must have been a good guy; he was the father of modern criticism.)One would like to encounter this play in its entirety some time, just to be sure. But if Thalheimer and his troupe have selected, I don't think they've distorted. This time around, Lessing is more.
