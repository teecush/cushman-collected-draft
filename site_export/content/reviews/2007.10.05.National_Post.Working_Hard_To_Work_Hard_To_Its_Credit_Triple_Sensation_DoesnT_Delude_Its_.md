'This is quite nostalgic for me" says Marvin Hamlisch.

Thirty-odd years ago, he wrote the music for A Chorus Line, a musical about the Broadway audition process that captured the thrills and despairs of a career in musical theatre. His place on the production team also made him part of the show's own mechanism of selection, life imitating art in the casting of a musical about casting a musical.

Now, he's one of the five "marquee judges" on CBC's Triple Sensation. This is a six-part series in which aspiring performers aged 16-26, from across Canada, compete for a $150,000 scholarship to any training institution in the world that will accept them. The title means that all the contestants have to prove themselves as actors, singers and dancers It could almost be the Chorus Line Reality Show.

The shows follow the winnowing process. This Sunday and next, we watch kids assembling in Toronto, Vancouver and Montreal for open call auditions. Forty from each region are chosen for assessment by the Marquee Panel. Apart from Hamlisch, they are Cynthia Dale, musical star at the Stratford Festival; Adrian Noble, former director of the Royal Shakespeare Company; Broadway choreographer Sergio Trujillo (Jersey Boys); and the show's executive producer and guiding spirit, Garth Drabinsky.

Drabinsky's great gift is to believe absolutely in the importance of whatever project he is involved in and to communicate that passion to other people. (I worked for him in the past, and enjoyed the experience.) I believe him when he speaks of this show as a producer's tribute to performing talent, and as a clarion-call to the importance of funding training in the performing arts.

He has also hit on something that goes beyond the immediate context.

The initial task of the Marquee Panel is to select a dozen finalists who are then treated, or subjected, to a series of rigorous Master Classes in each of the relevant disciplines. The 12 record their experiences in video diaries. What's captured is the strange mixture of collusion and conflict that takes over every class, every group audition, every rehearsal, and probably every business meeting.

The short list will go down to ten, then to a final six who will make it to the elimination round in front of the same five judges who, by this time, are themselves emotionally invested.

There is an element here of the American Idol blood-sports attraction. What's different is that the whole process has been completed before the show even goes to air. Another is that nobody gets publicly humiliated Another is the sheer range of abilities that are tested. And another is the judges themselves, who are rigorously but sympathetically involved, and do not line up in any good-cop, bad-cop formation.

"If this was just a talent contest," says Hamlisch, when I spoke to the group at the penultimate selection stage, "none of us would have showed up. I always hoped that there'd be a more civilized way to do this, but auditions are the only way."

An incorrigible performer and compulsive joker, Hamlisch at one point accompanies one of the auditionees when he leans that she's doing one of his favourite songs, Broadway Baby.

Trujillo points out the economic necessity of nurturing a breed, if not of triple sensations then of triple talents. The days of separate singing and dancing ensembles are long gone.

That leaves the acting. There's a strong emphasis here on delivering monologues, mainly from Canadian plays, and even more on Shakespeare, on the sound principle that if you can play Shakespeare you can play anything and that his rhythms are after all musical.

You don't have to be good at everything to be great at something; there have not, after all, been many Triple Sensations in the real world. The series will probably throw up some talents who have fallen at particular fences but could still be heard from.

What is impressive is a talent-show that doesn't present itself as the fast track to stardom, but as one in which the final goal is further training. The comment of one young man stays with me: "I don't necessarily want to be a star. But I want to make a living." - Triple Sensation airs Sunday at 8 p.m. on CBC.
