THEATRE REVIEW

ROCK 'N' ROLL

Bernard B. Jacobs Theatre, New York City

---

Everyone knows that it's unwise to assume that the characters in plays necessarily speak for their authors. Nonetheless, I'd always assumed that Tom Stoppard was waxing autobiographical in The Real Thing when he had his playwright hero say that he didn't like "the pop music which it's all right to like."

The example he gave of that category was Pink Floyd (as opposed, say, to Wayne Fontana and the Mindbenders). But it now turns out that Stoppard does like Pink Floyd. They're among the off-stage heroes of his newest play Rock 'n' Roll, along with former Czech president Vaclav Havel and a Czech rock-group called The Plastic People of the Universe. Havel of course was not a musician but he was, the play tells us, a fan.

Rock 'n' Roll, which is playing in New York after a triumphant run in London, is concerned with music and politics: to a large extent, with music as politics. Its action switches between England and Czechoslovakia in the years from 1968 to 1990.

The English scenes take place in a house and garden in Cambridge, the town in which Pink Floyd's lost hero Syd Barrett was born and to which he retreated after his early meltdown. He's glimpsed at curtain-rise leaning over the garden wall, playing on a pipe and singing, all summer-of-love like or, as the play would have it, Pan- like.

Currently in the house is Jan, a young Czech academic who's going home to greet the Prague spring, carrying with him a boxful of the rock albums he's come to love. When the Russian tanks remove Dubcek and restore repression, Jan remains seemingly uncommitted. His musical and personal idols are the Plastics, whom the regime detests not for what they say but for what they are: nonconformity personified. ("They're not heretics," Jan explains, "they're pagans.")

Jan, like other Stoppard protagonists, is sceptical of explicit commitment, which he interprets as an outlet for personal neurosis; he won't sign Havel's Charter 77. He still gets into trouble, and though the police don't bother to break him, they do break his record collection. Again, like other Stoppard protagonists (and probably Stoppard himself ) he changes his mind; takes a kind of stand. And, in a neat pincer movement, a more vociferous friend comes to admit that there is merit in Jan's idea of pure Plastic rebellion.

Rufus Sewell, from the London cast, plays Jan's mercurial qualities to the point, in the earlier scenes, of irritating whimsicality. But he's always attuned to what the character thinks and feels, and as both his manner and his hairstyle become increasingly and enforcedly sober, he grows, as he should, almost out of recognition. (His London successor was Dominic West, from The Wire; that I would like to have seen.)

The Czech scenes in fact are almost completely successful, especially in making us believe just how important the music was to these people, on every level. That theme is less convincing in the English scenes which can never make rock seem more than -- what in fact it probably was -- a pseudo-political indulgence.

Not that the music itself, which we hear in clips between the scenes, doesn't sound good; it's just that it doesn't connect with the characters. Head of the Cambridge household is Max, a fiftyish (when the play starts) professor who's a sorrowfully unreconstructed Stalinist. ("I speak," he says, "as one who's kicked in the guts by nine-tenths of anything you can tell me about Soviet Russia.") For him, the Czech Revolution is a frivolous sideshow, and Jan -- once his favourite student --a terrible letdown.

I can't imagine that Stoppard has much intellectual sympathy with Max, but he's given him some powerful speeches, especially about the way in which Russia becomes everyone's conscience-salving Aunt Sally. Max's wife, Eleanor, is a classics-teacher dying of cancer, and insistent on making her husband realize that his love for her is something that transcends materialism, dialectical or otherwise.

Two more fine British imports, Brian Cox and Sinead Cusack, play these roles very affectingly (Cusack doubles as Max's grown-up daughter who's also Jan's ultimate love-interest), but they can't make their part of the play coalesce with the other as anything more than theory. The point must be that emotions and beliefs have to inform one another, and that we are all capable of infinite self- deception about this, but the various romantic involvements don't really involve. Most Stoppard plays are about love and politics. In The Real Thing, the love elbowed the politics off the stage. Here it's the reverse.

There are also fewer laughs than in almost any other Stoppard play, though the text suggests that quite a few jokes have gone missing in the nads, or on the lips, of an American supporting cast obliged to speak with English accents even when they're playing Czechs.

Trevor Nunn's production can be surprisingly halting, with the actors -- including the leads -- prone to suggesting passionate conviction by addressing their companions as if they were slightly deaf. There are echoes here of many other Stoppard plays: Nothing wrong with that, but neither does it make the play -- what some British critics have proclaimed it -- his summarizing masterpiece.

Rather, it's an uneven play that no one else could have written and that at its best is very fine. I look forward to a Canadian production with, say, Joseph Ziegler, Lucy Peacock and Michael Therriault.

The piece ends with the characters, or some of them, joyfully attending a 1990 concert by the Rolling Stones, who've previously been hardly mentioned: Too bad it couldn't have been the Floyd or the Plastics, but history decreed otherwise. It's a good curtain, but it isn't a synthesis. It leaves too much out. - Rock 'n' Roll is at the Bernard B. Jacobs Theatre, New York City.
