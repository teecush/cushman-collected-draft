Its Martin Luther Kings last night on earth. He doesn't know it of course, but the audience does; and that simple contrast is what sustains The Mountaintop, this year's studio production at the Shaw Festival. The author, Katori Hall, is American, but her play was seen in London, where it was a major awardwinner, before getting a production in New York. It was her first play but shes written several more since. Its no particular surprise, though, that this is the one that took off. It has the subject matter and, up to a point, it has the flair. Both are given first-rate exposure at the Shaw.

The title of course refers to King's most famous speech (well, one of the two most famous): the one about getting to the mountaintop and seeing the Promised Land while doubting that he would live to reach it himself. History did drama the tragic favour of juxtaposing it with his murder the next day. Hall's play shows us Dr. King in his motel room in Memphis, a location about as far removed as possible from the top of any mountain, actual or symbolic. Its where the fighter and orator becomes a man subject to human doubts and human cravings. He starts the play by sending an unseen aide into town to get him some cigarettes. He also wants coffee, so he calls room service. With impressive swiftness, a maid appears with his order which, she says, is on the house. Her name is Camae, a reduction of the name of the authors mother, whose lasting regret was that she never got to hear the mountaintop speech. Camae, too, is a fan; at least that's how it seems in the beginning. As their conversation goes on, she becomes more critical. At one point, she stands on the bed and delivers her own fiery version of the kind of uncompromising firebrand speech she thinks he should be making: none of your non-violent protest stuff.

MLK and Camae are the only two characters, and it gradually dawns on us that there must be more to her than initially appears; staff don't usually get to hang out even in famous guests rooms for so long, at least not without management wondering what theyre up to. It even seems surprising that she should let him smoke some of her own cigarettes, the ones he sent for having unaccountably failed to materialize. Clearly this isn't your daddy's space-time continuum. What is actually going on it would be unfair to say, though I will admit that the crucial revelation isnt one that I much care for.

It does, though, involve some surprise effects that Philip Akins production handles beautifully. So does Judith Bowden's design; as in the Shaw's production of Juno and the Paycock, theres an all-important door on stage, though the things it reveals are less prosaic than the ones in Juno. I will reveal, because its irresistible, that both characters get to have entertaining conversations with God, who can be reached through the house phone and is female.

King has previously used the phone to call home to his wife, to whom he speaks very fondly, though the play acknowledges his reputation as a philanderer (another Shaw connection this year) and even has Camae call him on it; there's a brief hint of erotic contact between them. The real tension in the play, though, is between the man and his image. King's oratorical voice still rings resoundingly in all our ears, but one assumes that he didnt employ it in everyday, or everynight, conversation. He can still take a stand; he assures Camae that the sanitation workers strike he's come to Memphis to support will cave "over my dead body." (This qualifies as what a British critic once called, in another context, "a right crash of old irony".)

But he's also a man with fears, doubts and desires; a contrast thats brilliantly depicted in Kevin Hanchards superb performance. When called upon to capture the sonorous gospel-cadenced King, he delivers; the rest of the time he's nervously convivial, alternately at ease and on edge. Towards the end he's tragic, an agonized man obliged to confront his destiny. (The play makes explicit reference to Gethsemane.) Character and actor rise to the challenge simultaneously. His antagonist is more a series of attitudes than a person, but Alanna Hibbert inhabits all of them with gusto, gaining noticeably in wit and authority, the further she gets from her initial persona. Her last big speech is, I think, a dramatic fraud, but she gets it enthusiastically across, aided by a virtuoso sequence of projections, designed by Andrew Smith. The play is a co-production with Obsidian, wholl be bringing it to Toronto later this year.

It also caps a mostly excellent season at the Shaw: one with good work in all four houses, though the three best (The Charity That Began at Home, The Sea, A Lovely Sunday for Creve Coeur) have all been at the Court House. Next season, too, looks exciting, what with plays by such widely spaced American luminaries as Moss Hart and Tony Kushner; the Canadian premiere of the interesting-sounding Peter and the Spycatcher; a musical, Sweet Charity, thats been crying out for a Festival production; and Peter Hinton directing Pygmalion. I'm still wondering, though, when the Shaw will get round to doing Androcles and the Lion, one of its master's best and one for which few other theatres have the resources, physical and otherwise.

The Mountaintop runs through Sept. 7 at the Studio Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake.
