NEW YORK - Michael Crawford gets to his Broadway dressing-room at four every afternoon, 3 1/2 hours before curtain time. The 46-year- old actor is known for his devotion to his craft, but in this case, his daily vigil has little to do with easing himself spiritually or psychologically into the character. Playing the title-role in the wildly successful The Phantom Of The Opera, he needs all that time to have his make-up applied.

Compared with some other parts he has played - Barnum, for example, which turned him into a singing acrobat - the Phantom is not physically demanding. The character emerges occasionally from the recesses of the Paris Opera to terrify a recalcitrant producer, abduct a diva, sing an aria or two on his own; and his face is obscured by his elaborate disfigurement. Crawford has to do a lot of his acting with a single visible eye. The vocal demands are another thing; this, in keeping with its setting, is Andrew Lloyd Webber's lushest score. Surprisingly, though, Crawford does not regard the music as the role's principal challenge.

"The main challenge is the emotional involvement," he said, between blows from the powder puff. "It isn't necessary but it's the way I like to play it."

Phenomenal success

Some of this may be actor talk, but it does connect with the phenomenal success of the show, in London and New York. Despite its menacing title and reputation as a horror movie, Phantom makes its great appeal as a romantic musical. Some people have complained that it isn't especially scary, and Crawford agrees with them.

"It doesn't frighten me, either. I found Lon Chaney in the movie very frightening, but not very romantic. I couldn't copy his appearance; you can't pad your mouth out and sing." What goes for the character goes also for the special effects: notable the chandelier the Phantom sends crashing from the real theatre ceiling to intimidate those who dare to thwart him. As Crawford admits, it does not and cannot fall fast enough to be really terrifying: "there are safety regulations."

In the main, though, audiences at Phantom are not there to feel threatened, but to feel moved, especially by Crawford when his monster loses the girl he adores to the inevitable handsome young aristocrat. "By the end, the audience have forgiven him everything, and they all cry at the end when he just says, I love you. We've all felt like that, because we've all lost somebody at some time. We recognize that cry of anguish."

Crawford first heard about the show four years ago when he met Webber at the last London preview of the composer's previous musical Starlight Express. "He said to me, 'We must do The Phantom Of The Opera together.' I said, wonderful. Then I didn't see him again for a year and a half."

Quasi-operatic singing

As always with Webber, songs were released on record long before the show reached the stage, and Crawford noted that on disc the role of the Phantom was sung by rock singer Steve Harley. "I thought, well, he must have got my name wrong." However, Webber tends not to get things wrong and Crawford got the part.

An actor's life, especially in rehearsal, tends to reflect that of the character he's playing, and Crawford's existence became rather solitary. "I used to rehearse all by myself in the theatre boiler-room. I didn't meet anyone." He settled down to learn how to make an impact in a star role that actually occupies only 30 minutes playing-time, and how to do most of his acting with his cloak and, of course, do the most wih his voice.

He needed a more legitimate voice. "Every time Andrew heard I could do something, he wrote something else more difficult." It took Crawford months to learn, but he ended up singing in a quasi- operatic manner six evenings a week and two matinees.

His Phantom vocalizing has taken him back to the beginnings of his career. Shades of Benjamin Britten hover over the end of Webber's score, and it was Britten with his children's work Let's Make An Opera, who gave schoolboy Crawford his first experience of performing. "I got a taste for it. I liked being rewarded with people laughing when they were meant to - instead of being beaten, as I was during the day." School, acting and singing apart, was not a happy experience: the only place where Crawford has felt no inclination to work hard.

Big break

He made his escape, ironically, into schools broadcasting for BBC radio; starting at age 15, he did about 500 of these educational stints, gaining most of his own education in the process. He did another Britten opera (Noye's Fludde), professionally this time, and worked with the composer's English Opera Group. But he was also moving away from opera - into repertory theatres and TV soaps. His big acting break came in the London production of Neil Simon's Come Blow Your Horn, as an awkward young man trying to leave his Jewish- American home.

The role established a Crawford persona: shy, youthful, gangling. This was perpetuated in the mid-'60s in two Richard Lester movies: The Knack and A Funny Thing Happened On The Way To The Forum. In the latter, his first musical, he was officially the romantic lead, but in Forum the lovers are there to look ridiculous, which in ancient Roman costume, isn't too hard. The movie turns up on TV quite often, causing Crawford to writhe a lot. "If only I didn't have to look at my legs in that toga. It's so humiliating, those legs."

Barnum, which he played for four years in two separate London runs, was a landmark for Crawford. Playing the master showman and con man - "the prince of humbug" - was a decisive break from the line of gawky young men that had remained more or less unbroken since Come Blow Your Horn.

"Gormless" they call them in England and Crawford had become the paragon of gormlessness: a latter-day Harold Lloyd, his misadventures growing more outlandishly acrobatic with every role and culminating in 1974 in his first stage musical, Billy, based on Billy Liar.

Aggressively versatile

American musicals are famously dependent on dymamic leading ladies, from Ethel Merman on. Britain has always favored leading men, and in the last few years - the commercial glory years for British musicals - those men have been Tommy Steele, Jim Dale and Michael Crawford. They are, in a terrible but unavoidable showbiz phrase "all round entertainers," aggressively versatile. They are singers, hoofers, comedians, actors; their collective background runs from pop music to classical drama.

Of the three Steele is the most effervescent, the eternal Cockney kid, and Dale the most raffish; he created the role of Barnum on Broadway. His Barnum was far tougher than Crawford's and therefore more credible, but Crawford was more popular with audiences.

He is unquenchably motherable; and now, with sympathy pulsating through his vocal chords, he makes you weep for the Phantom Of The Opera. But nobody gets to the top through being a jellyfish. On stage, Crawford is appealing, off it he is courteous and thoughtful. But in neither environment is there any doubt of the iron - or the terrifying single-mindedness - beneath.
