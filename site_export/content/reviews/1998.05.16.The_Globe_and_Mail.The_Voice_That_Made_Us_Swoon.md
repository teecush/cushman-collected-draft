The voice that made us swoon

Saturday, May 16, 1998

Special to the Globe and Mail

FRANK Sinatra was the greatest popular singer of the 20th century. No other singer had a more attractive or more interesting voice; the flaws only added to the individuality. No singer was ever more of a musician; his interaction with his accompaniment was always extraordinary. No singer ever read lyrics more intelligently or with more feeling. And all these gifts were in the service of the song. Yes, he took liberties, but they were always purposeful. Yes, he had a huge ego, but he was able to channel it through whatever he was singing. Song and singer fused to produce something bigger than either.

He was, obviously, a great actor in song. He was also a pretty good actor in non-musical drama, though he was never much of a screen comedian. (In High Society ,the ever-insouciant Bing Crosby wiped the polished floor with him.) For years he was equally big in movies and on records, a feat otherwise managed by only Crosby, Doris Day and Barbra Streisand. Oddly, he never made a first-rate musical. His three best performances were in From Here to Eternity , The Man with the Golden Arm and The Manchurian Candidate ,in none of which did he sing a note. (Even Sammy Cahn, Sinatra's shamelessly resourceful regular lyricist, might have been hard pressed to come up with a title song for the last of them.)

Those three performances -- two underdogs and a sleuth -- were in-character roles. They had little to do with the self that Sinatra, at the same period, was creating in song. In the 1940s, first with The Tommy Dorsey Band and then as a phenomenally popular soloist on the Columbia label, he was a dedicated romantic with a voice of pure silk, bringing to pop music a new kind of hushed intensity. In the 1950s and '60s he fashioned a more complex version of the same persona. The sound became more sonorous, more lived-in, grainier. He sought out the bittersweet wit in sad songs and found a contemplative edge in happy ones.

It is interesting to go back to what may be his definitive up-tempo performance -- the daffily devotional You Make Me Feel So Young ,on Songs for Swingin' Lovers -- and discover that it is not actually all that fast. The first chorus is rather careful and subdued; the second breaks increasingly free. The song's sentiments are intentionally over the top. Sinatra, poker-faced, makes implicit acknowledgment of its absurdity without ever questioning its sincerity. We are charmed, touched and, finally, exhilarated. And it's only the first song on the album. It's a significant track in many ways. It's one of Sinatra's happiest collaborations with Nelson Riddle, who was to arranging what Sinatra was to singing. Both were musical architects. Together they built songs and whole albums, and built them to last. It heralds an LP that was to define a whole genre of singing. It reclaims a then 10-year-old movie song that had never been given its due.

Sinatra was, whether by design or intuitive taste, one of the major archivists of American popular song, perhaps the most important, since no other singer with a comparable repertoire reached so wide a public or was so searching an interpreter. He didn't sing all the major songs (try finding a Sinatra version of Smoke Gets in Your Eyes or Ev'ry Time We Say Goodbye )but he knew which ones were right for him, and discovered them, often in bulk, in the catalogue of every major composer. These Broadway-Hollywood-Tin Pan Alley guys may have spoken the same musical language, but they worked in widely varying dialects. The airy elegance of Cole Porter, the smart vulnerability of Rodgers and Hart, the metropolitan blues of Harold Arlen and Johnny Mercer. These are very different commodities, but Sinatra made them all into facets of himself without losing their individual essence. Not just the essence of each songwriter, but of each song.

He was a diction fanatic. That was an enormous help in imprinting each song so clearly on our consciousness. He had grown up in jazz, so knew how to play with melody and rhythm in the service of the lyric. (One of his ablest disciples, Julius la Rosa, observed that Sinatra sang like Fred Astaire danced.) He was historically lucky. Coming at the end of two eras, the big bands and classic Broadway, he was able to survey them both and preserve the best of each. He was part of them and yet apart from them, which is maybe the best summation of his art in general. He was magisterially present in each song, and yet detached. He played to his audience but rarely pandered to it. He sang not about himself but from himself.

He could be urbanely disenchanted while still the romantic (I Wish I Were In Love Again , I Guess I'll Have to Change My Plan ). He could have enormous fun with a song (Don'cha Go 'Way Mad , Pick Yourself Up ). And, of course, his ballads had a majestic, sculpted quality. It was here that his fastidiousness was most telling; he never simply sang another sad song. Last Night When We Were Young (on the magnificent Wee Small Hours album) was toweringly yearning, reaching a climax three-quarters of the way through on the word "clung." (I use the word "climax" advisedly; this Riddle arrangement, with its use of tension and release, is about the most sexual thing recorded.) One For My Baby (And One More for the Road) has taken on mythical status; it's uniquely lost . When he teamed with Antonio Carlos Jobim for a bossa novaalbum, he made the genre his own and, it seemed, more fully itself. Dindi ,my all-time favourite Sinatra track, is extraordinary, a hushed acknowledgment of unfamiliar beauty. After his faux retirement, he investigated and invigorated Send in the Clowns ,subtly shading every line. In 1982, he cut his last great ballad recital She Shot Me Down ,a set of very specific songs about wrecked relationships. One song, I Loved Her ,is a textbook example of how to colour a song dramatically by singing it, not talking it.

And he kept on doing it, almost to the end (though the two last albums of duets are best forgotten). When I started listening to him seriously, in the '60s, we were already looking back on the Capitol '50s and thinking "what a legacy." At the time it seemed unbeatable, but he at least equalled it. It will take a long time to get his measure.
