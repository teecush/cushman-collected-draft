The Next Stage Theatre Festival provides additional exposure for groups that have attracted attention at the Toronto and other fringes. Ive seen three of this year's shows.

The Red Queen Effect (Seventh Stage Theatre Productions) takes its title from the famous advice offered to Alice by the spiky imperious monarch in Through the Looking Glass: "Here it takes all the running you can do to stay in one place." The show's protagonist is called Alice, and she comes before us with the authentic straight-haired look of the John Tenniel drawings and with the determination with which Lewis Carroll endowed his heroine, though neither she nor anyone else has the wit that Carroll bestowed on everybody.

Alice works in a financial company; shes the new girl, indeed the only female in sight apart from the bosss unsympathetic second-in-command. A Red Queen figure, speaking in execrable rhyming couplets (why do people think these are so easy to write?) tells us that life in the finance business is like moving on a chessboard. Alice's immediate and very patronizing superior is compared to Humpty Dumpty, mainly because he suffers a great fall; there's very little resemblance earlier on. That about does it for the literary references. And in fact this Alice doesnt stay in the same place; she rises, through her own hard work and intelligence, arousing first the resentment and then the enforced acceptance of her male colleagues.

The show, devised by the ensemble, develops in Kelly Straughans production as a mixture of dialogue and dance. Its best scene, by far, comes when a visiting tycoon surprises Monica Dottor's Alice working late; looking to make a deal with her employers, he ends up offering her the chance to broker it. (He probably fancies her as well.) For this encounter, the script, which has previously been straining every nerve to be paradoxical, relaxes into actual conversation and allows the actors to do the same. Nicholas Campbell's intruder, in particular, lifts the proceedings to a whole new level of distinction. The only other actor to come near it is Dylan Scott Smith as the incumbent CEO, nervous but ruthless. Other leading performances are terrible. The remaining players (six of them) make up a male chorus line, executing jerky ballets of masculine conformity that are neat the first time and tiresome thereafter. Theres also a confusion of satiric aim. We spend the show rooting for Alice to crash through the glass ceiling (shes less like Alice than like Peggy in Mad Men); when she does it, the show turns around to tell us -- shades of Top Girls -- that she's become part of what she should be hating. They've left it a little late to change the subject.

Gas (jsquared. theatre) is another dance of splintering male camaraderie, though with choreography more verbal than physical, but again with a woman joining and beating the boys at their own game. Written and directed by Jason Maghanoy, it shows five U.S. soldiers guarding gasoline supplies in Iraq and torn apart -- both individually and as a group -- by their split reactions to a single violent incident. Writing and performance are at their best early on, exploring the squads bravado, their learned hardness, their hopes and fears and homesickness. There are two apparently separate plot lines that converge, too neatly to be believed. Increasingly, its the bravado that takes over; the actors, not just the characters, get high on style. The dialogue becomes literary-tough, and the performances take on the same distanced, fake-intense quality. It's effective, but diminishingly so, and only in a generalized way. And though they've obviously worked very hard at being authentic, theres still something second-hand about it, a sense of playing at soldiers. Its as if a Canadian theatre company, while implicitly condemning the American presence in Iraq, also envied it: not the experience itself, but the theatricality of it.

The best Next Stage show I saw was Quite Frankly (Screwed & Clued), though it seemed at first that it was going to be awful. It's a one-man piece, whose author/performer, Justin Sage-Passant, is a British-born performer who's been over here for several years but still has his native rhythms down pat. (His director, Jonno Katz, is Australian.)

The patness is initially a problem. Playing an inhibited, mother-ridden 33-year-old man in an English provincial town, Sage-Passant is adept at hitting off the other, rowdier people with whom his hero comes in contact, but he does them, one especially, at self-indulgent length. A digression on how to get served at a bar (the only place in England where people dont queue up, instead forming what customers and servers recognize alike as "a virtual queue") is funny, and absolutely accurate, but it belongs in a magazine piece rather than a play; it doesnt help the story. The central figure, holding himself at a clenched, skewed angle, comes gradually to life though, as a human, imprisoned in gentility, rather than a caricature. He's been humiliated since childhood and his mother, now incontinent and largely helpless, has been the prime agent and abettor of this. But she's all he has, and vice versa, and his acceptance of this finally gives him a kind of dignity. The performance is carefully shaped to this peak, and attains it.
