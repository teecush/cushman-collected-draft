THEATRE REVIEW" ANDROMACHE

Tuesday, May 14, 1991

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Toronto ONT -- WRITTEN BY JEAN RACINE Translated by Richard Wilbur Directed by Ian Prinsloo Presented by Orange Dog Theatre at the Glen Morris Studio Theatre, Shoestring production in a small space manages to project the essence of a masterpiece A powerful, witty Andromache Toronto BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Toronto THE director of this production of Andromache thinks that it is a play about war, best served by being togged out in the high fashions of the early 1920s while interlarded with projected stills showing the carnage of 1914-18. He is wrong on all these counts but still manages, powerfully and wittily, to project the essence of a masterpiece.

Andromache was the wife of Hector of Troy, whose death, as narrated by Homer, is the first tragedy in Western literature. She is one of the principals in Euripides unrivalled denunciation of post-war reprisals, The Trojan Women. A captive after the fall of Troy, she has to endure the killing of her infant son Astyanax. This judicial murder - offstage, of course - is probably the most searing event in world drama: a meeting- point of ancient tragedy and modern realpolitik.

Racine takes the traditional figures and rearranges them in a scheme of his own devising. His Andromache is a captive at the court of the Greek king Pyrrhus, the son of Achilles, who killed Andromaches husband. Her child still lives, but is again under threat; the other Greeks are demanding that Pyrrhus give him up. He refuses. This may be because he is a reformed character, with a genuine distaste for further atrocities. But he has an ulterior motive. He is in love with Andromache. He is also, of course, in a perfect position to blackmail her into marrying him.

The Greek emissary is the fury-driven Orestes, best-known for having killed his mother. His current problem is that he loves Hermione, daughter of Helen, while she is in love with Pyrrhus, and hence murderously resentful of Andromache. It all makes a perfect pattern: love and hate, politics and justice, cancelling one another out. It is resolved when all give way to their worst impulses. Jealous Orestes, at the prompting of jealous Hermione, assassinates Pyrrhus, thereby unwittingly creating a power vacuum. Andromache, who has spent the play feeling suicidal, ends it, irony of ironies, as proclaimed queen.

None of this would have happened if there had not been a Trojan war. But it is a leap from that to assuming, as Ian Prinsloo's production does, that the brute realities of warfare lie behind every chiselled, courtly line. The fate of one child does, and it is the genius of Racine to show us how the characters react to this. Orestes treats it as a diplomatic counter, Hermione couldn't care less, Pyrrhus remembers it when it suits him, and even Andromache wavers between the roles of protective mother and sacrificial victim.

Pyrrhus risks another war, but Racine, 17th-century snob that he was, was indifferent to the plight of the ordinary soldier. Pictures of gas- masked Tommies and bodies hanging on wires can make no headway against a text that has other things on its mind. The best that can be said is that the reflections make intriguing shapes on the stage-floor.

When the memory of war does erupt in the dialogue, it obliterates the visuals and everything else. This is when Andromache describes in detail Pyrrhuss record at the fall of Troy to explain why she can never forgive him. Jani Lauzon, who has moped her way uneasily through most of the role, rises magnificently to this, in a white-hot fusion of agony and anger. It must be the most powerful moment in current Toronto theatre.

It is the women who make the action in Racine, and Hermione rather than Andromache who does so here. Jealousy on the grand scale is hard to sustain, and Alyson Green, dressed up as a white-beaded flapper, substitutes pique, which is arresting to begin with but palls before the end. The trouble with deflating tragic characters is that once you have made your comic point, there they still are, orating. Orestes (Alex Poch-Goldin) comes over as a tremulous dupe and catspaw and though one feels there should be more to him, it would be hard to say what.

Richard Wilburs translation is, like the original, in rhyming couplets, to English ears an instinctively comic medium. The most complete performance here, and the only actor who firmly controls the verse, is Michael Healey as Pyrrhus, a neatly bearded monarch in knickerbockers who receives Orestes' embassy while stylishly dismembering a boiled egg. Only his grizzled confidant (Peter J. McConnell) has a comparable assurance. The other principals might be better off talking to themselves.

Racine in English is traditionally a no-go area; last year's Phedre at the Stratford Festival was, at best, worthy. Andromache is more accommodating: a psychological drama but devoid of modern sentimentality. This is a shoestring production in a small space with an unpaid cast. I expected little of it. I was bowled over.
