Previously on Being Erica ... Actually, that's a tough one, and I'd hate to be one of the people who has to prepare this show's instant updates. The thing is, when you look back at previous episodes, you find the action divided between the factually-previously and the fantasy-previously. Or between real reality and alternate reality. Or between the seriously-just-past and the might-have-been-more-distant-past. (Of course, even the factual parts are fiction, but enough is enough.)

It's not that confusing when you actually watch. Here is Erica Strange, 32, in Toronto and at an assortment of dead ends. She's a university graduate with literary ambitions, but neither her degree nor her talent have borne any fruit. In one day she loses both her next-best-thing-to-a-boyfriend and her job -- not that she liked either of them much anyway. Her family are constantly on at her about her career and marital prospects, or lack thereof. (Need I say she's Jewish?) To top it all, she suffers an allergic reaction from a free sample of flavoured latte and winds up in hospital. There she encounters Dr. Tom, a whimsical shrink whose professional credentials don't include a surname. He says he can help her. He asks her to write down a list of the events and decisions she most regrets. We don't get a very close look at this document, but it seems to contain enough material for 10 weeks of television. And so, almost before Erica knows where she is, she's somewhere else: back in high school in Episode one, and at university in Episode two, fortified in both cases by knowing now what she wishes she'd known then.

The show to date is half refreshing, half crackerbarrel. Much of the refreshment comes from Erin Karpluk, who plays Erica and who makes self-deprecation sexy. Michael Riley as Dr. Tom offers a pixie-ish variation on his accident-prone lawyer from This is Wonderland, the difference being that this guy doesn't make mistakes and is really pretty smug about it. (And speaking of Wonderland, Erica has a good claim to be today's Alice. That would make the doctor a cross between Humpty Dumpty and the Mock Turtle.) The today scenes are by and large delightful, well observed and entertainingly played. The problem is with the quasi-flashbacks. Erica, retuning to her old haunts and those with whom she haunted, does indeed make some different choices.

Some of them work out better than others, but at the end of them all she's still the same person. This moral, hinted at in the first week, is explicitly stated in the second, leaving you wondering where the show has left to go. We care enough about Erica to wonder what's going to happen to her now; what happened to her back then is beginning to get in the way.

Maybe one of Erica's mistakes is that she, a Torontonian, went to the University of Toronto; perhaps she needed to get away. Her alma mater appears, when revisited, to have been a terrifying place, ruled by elitist secret societies with appalling hazing rituals. But the locations are nice, and there's a sense of the city about the whole show that's pleasing, if occasionally puzzling; Erica applies for work with a publishing company that seems to live in the Opera House. Her writing ambitions came to the fore in Episode Two, which she narrates in voice-over as if reading stage directions; she even read the credits, getting one of them (I assume intentionally) wrong. There was none of this device in Episode One; and though I think I see its point, I hope it remains a one-off. If it persists, it could get seriously annoying.

---

Previously on Wild Roses ... Summarizing this, CBC's other new big one, is so efficiently done at the start of Episode Two that the viewer need feel no guilt about missing Episode One. Wild Roses is a simple tale of greed and grudge whose title remains mysterious. They might have called it Calgary, as it clearly yearns to be Canada's belated answer to Dallas, or perhaps Sex, Oil and Rock 'n' Roll. The sex to date is minimal. We do get to see some bull-on-cow action, though Mr. Big, as his owners fondly call him, fails to come through, and is finally sidelined by an overdose of bovine Viagra. This is one of the semi-comic interludes that dilute what ought to be flaring melodrama. The rock 'n' roll comes from Lucy, who gives up a European tour with her band so she can stay home and help her widowed mother and two sisters fight off the oil tycoon who's threatening to foreclose on their cherished ranch. He's doing this because a) the mother's spurned his advances; b) he's running low on black gold, and he's detected a fresh lode on the property; c) he's a bastard. This mixture of motives does not make him a complex character. Still less so is Lucy's daughter, pretty near the ultimate in spoiled bitches, who's cheating on her brand-new fiance, and shows her sluttish nature by always doing it standing up. This makes her easier to photograph, both for the show itself and for a blackmail plot. She also shows how stuck-up she is by listening to opera: not a convincing trait. But then the show's attitude to high culture is none too informed; its promos describe it as "Shakespearean," a claim largely unsupported by the dialogue. This in any case has to fight for its life against the onmipresent music, some of it fittingly country, most of it just bloated. The show's timing is historically unfortunate; we aren't really in the mood for the villainous David's gloating declaration that "the price of crude is going up." It's old-fashioned in more damaging ways: It's airless. To watch these dessicated lads and lasses riding the range is to weep for the network that gave us, only last year, The Englishman's Boy, and seems little inclined to follow it up. - Being Erica airs Mondays at 9 p. m. on CBC. Wild Roses airs Tuesdays at 9 p. m. on CBC.
