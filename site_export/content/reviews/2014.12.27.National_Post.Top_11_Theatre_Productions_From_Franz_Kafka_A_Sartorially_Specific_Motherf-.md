1. London Road That impossible beast, the verbatim musical, showing how the killing of five prostitutes affected the inhabitants of the British provincial town where it happened. Britain's own National Theatre production is unlikely to have been any finer than this one from Canadian-Stage. Jackie Maxwell directing 11 of our best actor-singers and musical director Reza Jacobs for steered them through an extraordinarily difficult score without a false note.

2. Metamorphosis Kafka's famous man-into-insect story theatricalized with unflinching imagination by an acrobatic and mainly Icelandic cast, in a script by David Farr (of London) directed by Gisli Orn Gardasson (of Reykjavik). Thanks to Mirvish Productions for importing it; the same team are about to offer us their take on Robin Hood. Quite some metamorphosis there.

3. Lungs A strikingly original British play, a continuous duologue that moves effortlessly through time and space, as the couple concerned wonder whether to bring a child into a polluted world. Mesmerizingly written, acted and directed, witty and moving. This was one of two outstanding shows at the Tarragon, the other being An Enemy of the People. 4. Of Human Bondage/Spoon River Anthology Two Soulpepper productions, both visually and aurally adventurous, that I'm going to pass off as one as they jointly represented an imaginative leap for their director Albert Schultz. Bondage, Vern Thiessen's adaptation of a Somerset Maugham novel, was also the year's nearest approach to a good new Canadian play. This was a hat-trick year for Schultz since he also directed the more conventional but comparably distinguished Idiot's Delight. This had a breakthrough lead performance by Dan Chameroy, who kept turning up this year in all kinds of contexts.

Soulpepper also did finely by another, more familiar American piece, Twelve Angry Men, directed by Alan Dilworth and a compendium of accurate acting with Joseph Ziegler in devastating form as the angriest and saddest man of all.

5. The Charity that Began At Home St. John Hankin, the acrid Edwardian, has been one of the Shaw Festival's major rediscoveries, and this take-noprisoners comedy of morals may be the best of the cycle.

6. King John Tim Carroll's Stratford staging moved with perfect control from formality to naturalism and back again. Tom McCamus and Graham Abbey gave the year's two best Shakespeare performances in the year's best Shakespeare production - unless you count the Canadian Opera Company's revelatory Falstaff: the only comic opera I have ever seen that was deeply and consistently funny. This multinational co-production was actually directed by a Canadian, Robert Carsen. Can't we keep him here and get him to do a play? A more modest adaptation: Cymbeline's Reign, from Shakespeare in the Ruff, which is now the premier Shakespeare company in Toronto.

7. A Lovely Sunday for Creve Coeur Lovely any day of the week. Another Shaw Festival discovery; a rare Tennessee Williams one-act, both laugh-making and heartbreaking. Blair Williams' lunchtime production was as perfect an hour as you could hope to find; Deborah Hay and Kate Hennig were sublime. Also at the Shaw and nearly as good: The Sea, Edward Bond's near-masterpiece, directed by Eda Holmes, whose last season's production

of Tom Stoppard's Arcadia came, joyously, to Toronto. 8. Tartuffe The first-best classical production and Soulpepper's peak, its finest in this area since the same director, Laszlo Marton, did The Wild Duck. Moliere's comedy was freshly thought through, stretched without distortion to its farcical and near-tragic limits. In the major roles, Diego Matamoros and Oliver Dennis were major. 9. The Beaux' Stratagem The last great Restoration comedy restored (not that we get to see the earlier ones much either). In Antoni Cimolino's crisp and beguiling Stratford production, the cherished memory is of Martha Henry's Lady Bountiful, brandishing

broadsword and cucumber with cheerfully hostile intent. 10. The Motherf-ker With the Hat/Moment/Blackbird/Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf? The last part of the year brought a remarkable string of independent presentations: invigorating productions of exciting plays, staged in intimate spaces and attracting top-ofthe-line actors (though, as one of them said to me, "you have to go into debt to do them"). The Motherf-ker with the Hat reacquainted us with America's best current playwright Stephen Adly Guirgis; Moment was a recent Irish piece, pitchperfect in its family dynamics; Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf ? was a revelation in close-up; and Blackbird, David Harrower's disturbing play about pedophilia was given its second Toronto production and the first to be truly harrowing. Like the vast majority of the best new(ish) plays seen in Toronto this year, starting with London Road, Blackbird was British. And another funny thing: most of them had one-word titles - Lungs, Cock, NSFW (if that's a word).

11. A Midsummer Night's Dream(s) The 11th entry in my Top 10. Neither of these two wildly different Stratford productions would have made the cut on its own, but taken together they offered interesting if tendentious views of the most familiar of plays. Chris Abraham's mainstage version, enchantingly set, celebrated gay marriage, and was particularly good with the fairies; Peter Sellers' studio abridgement seemed disinclined to celebrate anything, and was at its best with the quarrelling lovers. Neither made much of the rude mechanicals; Sellars, indeed, seemed hardly to know they were there. But if you were looking for funny: Sixteen Scandals was Second City's best revue in I don't know how long. Probably eons.
