THEATRE REVIEW

A LITTLE NIGHT MUSIC

Shaw Festival, Niagara-on-the-Lake

---

There's a wonderful tear-inducing moment at the end of A Little Night Music when the show's assortment of ill-sorted couples has been happily resorted, and the orchestra signals a reprise of the accidental hit song Send in the Clowns. Among the many pleasures in the Shaw Festival's pocket-sized revival is the discovery that the tune's re-entry can bring on as much sweet sorrow when sounded by a clarinet as by a full complement of strings. Musical director Paul Sportelli seems far more attuned to this work than to the brassiness of Wonderful Town. It's been orchestrated (by Jason Jestadt) for a chamber quintet, and though one misses the swell of a full orchestra propelling the first-act finale A Weekend in the Country, the taut tart beauty of Stephen Sondheim's score, the headiest written for a musical in the last 40 years, is undiminished.

The five instrumentalists balance the five liebeslieder singers who are built into the show as written. They function as commentators; the signal achievement of Morris Panych's production is to bring them closer than ever before to the characters and events on whom they're commenting, not only physically (hardly avoidable on this stage) but spiritually and emotionally as well. The chorus also take on personalities of their own, with Glynis Ranney at their head as a secular solicitous Mother Superior of the human condition. Conversely, the protagonists sometimes become observers, and very effectively. When Donna Belleville, wheelchair-bound as the ex-courtesan mother of the heroine, sings the autobiographical Liaisons, it's the most natural complement in the world to her actress-daughter's one-night stand with an old flame. When Juile Martell, as the kind of maid everybody ought to have, sings The Miller's Son, it's a realistic statement of her own sexual possibilities and a tolerant comment on everyone else's. The show is the most attentive and restrained production of anyone's work save his own that Panych has mounted since his CanStage version of Sondheim's Sweeney Todd. I guess that tells us who he respects.

There are drawbacks. The intimacy of the space and the sparseness of the backing permit unmiked singing, which is a blessing; but with the audience on three sides we lose words when the actors turn away, which is a pity (with lyrics of this quality, that's putting it mildly). And the constant peering of the performers through the birches that make up most of Ken MacDonald's set can get too cute.

More importantly, while the singing is mostly firm from the start, the acting takes time to cohere. The major exception here is Goldie Semple who, in this musicalization of Ingmar Bergman's Smiles of a Summer Night, plays Desiree Armfeldt, dramatic star of the Swedish stage, whose scheme to detach her former lover from his still-virginal child-bride -- a good idea from everybody's point of view -- steers the action. Semple seems taxed even by the narrow vocal demands of Desiree's songs; if her Send in the Clowns brought cheers on the first night, it could only be because she's the star and it's a standard. But her acting around the song, and in all her other scenes, is warm and sensitive enough to make amends; she's a mature, sensual woman, keenly alive to her own and everyone else's absurdity.

As Fredrik, the object of her affectionate designs, George Masswohl is as solid and reliable as a traditional family solicitor. Which, by profession, is what he is, but he's also supposed to be a passionate man in a mid-life crisis. Masswohl, when talking, conveys hardly any of this. When the music starts, though, he's another man, alert to every nuance. By the show's climax, which finds him fatalistically consenting to a game of Russian roulette, the two sides of the performance have come together.

Thom Allison, by contrast, remains bisected. He plays the Count, Fredrik's even-more-married rival, a role for which he has too much intelligence and too little machismo; his head-notes, though, are thrilling. As his acid-tongued and acid-minded spouse, Patty Jamieson is good in all departments, if you can accept that socially, if not intellectually, this Countess has married up.

The junior characters are delights. Panych puts Desiree's daughter, playing with dolls while eyeing her elders, at the centre of the action, and Michaela Bekenn justifies the position, especially with her ecstatic leaps as the county weekend looms into view. Justin Stadnyk as Frederik's frustrated seminarian of a son and Robin Evan Willis as the young stepmother he despairingly adores give the completest musicaldramatical-comical performances of all, though Willis could let up on the silliness later on. She does, though, confirm my long-held belief that her solo, Soon, is the most breathtaking song in the piece, rivalled only by the ensemble's miraculous Night Waltz; these are the two great true waltzes in a score composed in multiples of three-quarter time.

The worst of playwright Hugh Wheeler's book is its clunky attempts at epigram (mostly given to Belleville, who tactfully underplays them); the best is the way it organizes the story around the music. There are sections of the second half in which the plot rather elbows out the songs, but the two come together at the close. Shadowed by mortality, it's a perfect compromise of a happy ending. - A Little Night Music runs until Oct. 4. Call 1-800-511-SHAW or visitshawfest.com for information.
