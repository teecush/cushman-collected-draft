popcorn@nationalpost.com

Quentin Tarantino has said the sign of a good film is that it makes you want to go home, eat some pie and talk about it. With that in mind, our Popcorn Panel features film buffs feuding in this space each week.

- - -

THIS WEEKS PANEL:

- Chris Knight, the National Posts chief film critic

- Tara Ariano, the editor of the Web sites Fametracker.com and TelevisionWithoutPity.com

- Robert Cushman, the Post's theatre critic

THIS WEEK'S PIE: Peach.

THIS WEEKS SUBJECT: The Hitchhikers Guide to the Galaxy

CHRIS: After more than 20 years of trying, The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy arrived in movie theatres last weekend. The radio series on which all other versions of the Guide (books, TV miniseries, computer game, etc.) are based first aired in 1978, about a year after the opening of Star Wars, a science-fiction property that is almost exactly unlike The Hitchhiker's Guide. I only mention it because it, too, is about to finally wrap.

While laity of the churches of Douglas Adams and George Lucas are no doubt thrilled by these developments (and as a pantheist, I claim membership in both groups), 20-odd years seems like a bit of a wait for the Sixth Coming. How can any mere movie be worth the wait when the wait is measured in decades -- and when we already have several exciting and popular versions of the story? Its one thing to know where your towel is, but I cant help wondering if there comes a time when you should throw it in.

TARA: I haven't been waiting; I've never read any of the books. I can report, however, that at the showing I attended -- opening night at the gigantic Paramount Theatre here in Toronto -- there were many nerds attending in full geek regalia. A couple of them, decked out in their robes with their towels around their necks, stomped out about halfway through, and a dork quorum a few aisles behind us spent several minutes through the closing credits loudly registering their complaints.

Coming in without any baggage, however, may be the exact reason I liked the movie as much as I did: It was silly and British, very well cast (particularly Sam Rockwells George W. Bush-channelling Zaphod Beeblebrox, at whose every line I couldnt help giggling), intermittently pretty to look at and peppered with random acts of violence and cruelty. I understand that it wasn't completely faithful to the source material, but to complain about that is, I think, to ignore the fact that books and movies are different media, and that sometimes there has to be a love interest. Even nerds need to let girls, with their icky love talk, into the clubhouse.

I guess this is all by way of saying that I hear there were supposed to be three-breasted aliens at some point, but I didn't miss them.

ROBERT: I know the Hitchhikers Guide, not through the books, which Ive only skimmed, but through the original radio series, of which I was a devoted fan whenever I remembered to tune in. And it does seem to me that the story's radio origins are the most important thing about it.

Once you add visuals, you slow down the flow of jokes, which is what happens in the middle of this movie. It's no accident that the best performances are the disembodied ones: Stephen Fry, whose inhuman geniality is perfect casting for the voice of the Guide itself; Alan Rickman, hilariously lugubrious as Marvin the Paranoid Android; and Helen Mirren as the supercomputer.

Of the ones who appeared, Im afraid I thought Rockwell a real pain, just flailing around; weve seen this brainless macho hero a hundred times before and funnier. I was glad when he lost one of his heads; at least we were free of that gag. But Martin Freeman is very nice as Arthur Dent (I'm English -- I know how to queue).

But it all gets bogged down in the action parts, which tend to look the same these days whether they're in Star Wars or Galaxy Quest. The picture gets a terrific boost though, when Bill Nighy appears toward the end. Its worth the price of admission just to hear him say, "I do not know this cheese of which you speak." And the closing sequence, when the Vogons attack Arthurs new house, is great on every level. As is the theme song for the dolphins.

CHRIS: It's true, the jokes originated and still work best in the ether, where one can imagine hyperintelligent supercomputers, Vogon constructor fleets, two-headed men or (if you like) three-breasted women without worrying about how to film them.

By its very nature, the Guide (and Adams's humour) is about superlatives -- the worst poetry, the ultimate answer, the infinite improbability drive, etc. Try filming someone imbibing the best drink in existence, said to have the effect of being hit with a slice of lemon wrapped round a large gold brick. Cant be done.

On the other hand, one could view the movie as an expensive trailer for the Guides other incarnations. I was surprised to see a lot of youngish folks at the screening I went to, and if even 1% of them track down the books -- well, they'll be even more surprised.

As for the towel toters at Tara's screening, theyre probably also writing angry letters to Peter Jackson for failing to include "the cleansing of the Shire" in his Lord of the Rings trilogy. Some folks will never be satisfied, and I reserve the right to be one of them.

TARA: Thats true of so many books -- that the images in the movie can't possibly live up to what we imagined, or that our very favourite lines had to be jettisoned for the sake of forward motion. Believe me, I had plenty of bitter criticism to heap on the film version of Mansfield Park a few years back, so I feel for you, Chris.

But then I suppose the counter-argument is that a story should be strong enough, in its most essential elements, to exist in two separate media, and to mean different things to those who consume them in each incarnation. The Hitchhiker's movie isnt going to be the same product to me, with absolutely no knowledge of the series beyond the number 42, as it is to you, evidently steeped in it. The challenge for the filmmakers is to please us both, even if they didnt film exactly what may have been in your mind. But if they can't please us both, they'd probably be glad enough to know that movie fans dug it -- it couldnt have been just the bathrobers that put it in the No. 1 slot in its opening weekend.

ROBERT: I went with an 18-year-old who has read the entire trilogy -- all five books of it. His comment, which struck me as very reasonable at the time, was that though the film was enjoyable, it was bound to frustrate those who knew the original and confuse those who didnt.

I'm relieved to hear from Tara that he was wrong on the second count. Having now read more myself, I'm torn. Im impressed with some of the restructuring and bothered by some of the additions and omissions. Zaphod didnt have to be as annoying as Rockwell makes him; he's like an inferior version of Kevin Kline in A Fish Called Wanda. Part of me enjoyed the love plot; another part wished they'd refrained from putting it in. It was like MGM softening up the Marx Brothers after their anarchic days at Paramount.

But the basic flavour comes through, and though thats not all you could wish, its probably all you could reasonably hope for. It starts good and finishes good. Thanks again for the fish.
