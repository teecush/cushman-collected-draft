AN EVENING'S ODYSSEY

Tom Patterson Theatre Stratford, Ont.

THEATRE REVIEW

Homer, being a bard who literally sang for his supper, was never a man to keep his audiences hanging about. He taught all subsequent literature (which, in the West, means all the literature there is) a vital lesson by cutting immediately to the chase, and then keeping it going. In The Odyssey, he sets both the hero and his plight indelibly before us within the first two lines.

In Robert Fagles' translation, Odysseus is "the man of twists and turns. Driven time and again of course": a brilliant association of images that link the protagonist's unceasing and unscrupulous ingenuity to his incessant storm-tossed wanderings.

Fagles' version is poetry that arrests; it's also poetry that swings. It is, as befits a rendering of an oral epic, insistently speakable. An Evening's Odyssey is a three-handed entertainment in which Fagles himself speaks with quiet confidence about the poem and its themes, intones some resonant passages (notably the opening invocation to the Muse) in the original Greek, and sets the stage for more full-throated readings of some of the key scenes -- in his translation of course -- by two professional actors. At Stratford, they are Brian Bedford and Kathryn Walker, a U.S. actress with an extensive track record in this kind of literary theatre.

One notable feature of the show is how many of the famous bits of The Odyssey it leaves out. The monsters and temptresses who punctuate Odysseus' travels are much depleted; we get no Crice, no Sirens, no Scoylla and Charybdis (the rock and whirlpool so handily conjoined for your further shipwrecking pleasure). We do get the Cyclops, the giant cannibal whose one eye Odysseus -- passing under the name of Nobody -- so gleefully removes. The other Cyclops are momentarily perturbed by the screaming but when the sufferer says that Nobody is hurting him they shrug their gigantic shoulders and pass on. This passage is the Oldest Recorded Joke, and Bedford, having told the grisly story with restrained relish, makes the most of it.

The focus, though, is not on Odysseus the warrior but Odysseus the returning husband; and this turns out to be a sound decision. Of the two Homeric masterpieces, The Iliad has always struck me as intensely dramatic: a tragedy just waiting for the theatre to be invented. The Odyssey, in its picaresque aspect, resists drama, being one damned if exciting thing after another. But as a tale of reunion, moving toward a definite climax, it has immense resonance.

It is this element that Fagles stresses in his commentary, and the actors, while refraining from heavy characterization or excessive emoting, bring it home. Walker is the more lyrical and mellifluous of the two, but she also stresses the practicality of Penelope, the beleaguered wife, as resourceful at home as her husband is abroad. Bedford is playing, for the most part, in his lower register; he sounds at times downright grumpy. But after 10 years at war and 10 at sea, his hero is entitled to some weariness; and when he comes to defy (and destroy) his consort's battery of suitors, his voice rings and his anger blazes.

Taut or reposed, Bedford makes, as usual, every inflection tell. In some ways his Odysseus is a counterpart to his magnificent Sir Peter Teazle in The School for Scandal; the two worlds could hardly be more different, but both characters journey toward a hard-won domestic peace. The Greek one is archetypal, and one ends up believing that this is indeed -- as all the professors, including Professor Fagles, have told us -- the greatest presentation of a marriage in all literature.
