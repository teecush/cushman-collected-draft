Daniel MacIvor's Communion is, undeniably, a spellbinder. MacIvor's plays usually are these days, whether directed by Daniel Brooks or, as on this occasion, by the author himself. Here he delivers a clean-lined, meticulously staged and outstandingly well-acted production that squeezes all the tension and all the humour from a text that comes equipped with bags of the former and a reasonable supply of the latter. A show can often be judged by the quality of silence it provokes in the house; this one had its first-night audience taut and engrossed. Moment to moment, it compels attention. When it's over, however --or before that, as it begins to wind down -- you may wonder how much you are being left with.

Communion has three scenes and three characters; also three chairs, which are lined up handsomely in a row for our inspection as we take our own seats. When the play begins, this egalitarian arrangement gives way to a naturalistic arrangement of furniture that looks less formal but is actually more so. Where a person sits, and in relation to whom, becomes an index of power, just as it does in real life, especially in such confrontational situations as medical appointments and, especially, therapy sessions. Two such sessions bookend the action of MacIvor's play; sandwiched between them is an even less comfortable confrontation in a hotel room between a mother, Leda, and a daughter, Ann. It's the first time they've seen one another in eight months. Leda's husband has left her for another woman, and Ann has been living with them. She's also become a belligerent born-again Christian, having previously done time for attacking an abortion clinic. Leda and Ann have drastic news to impart to one another.

Her personal predicament, and her relationship or lack of it with her daughter, are what have driven Leda to see a psychiatrist, whose name is Carolyn and who has issues of her own, as who in her line doesn't? Even if you start without them, which is unlikely, you are likely to acquire them on the job; Carolyn certainly seems to, landed as she is with patients who keep questioning what possible help she can be to them, while lambasting her for her refusal to tell them what's wrong with them or what to do about it. The things, in fact, that drive any patient mad about any shrink, and presumably vice versa. (And can sometimes drive audiences mad about playwrights.) Besides this, it seems that Carolyn is having problems with her own (female) partner, though she doesn't say very much about them. This, of course, is her personal and professional right, but it makes her unsatisfying as a dramatic character. Leda, by contrast, is very forthcoming about her problems, though neither she nor the play does much more than re-state them. Ann is a problem every time she opens her mouth, though sublimely unaware of it. "Everything's not black and white," says Leda, provoked by her daughter's religious certainties. "Yes, it is," says Ann. Well, that's that then. But the question of how Ann reached this stage of undentable satisfaction is left unbroached. She even manages to disturb the quiet in the auditorium. "Women should raise their children and obey their husbands" she tells her mother, and the shock in the house was audible. I'll swear that if she'd proudly proclaimed that she was a serial killer, they'd have taken it without a murmur

The dialogue crackles, and the play bristles with small points of contact, personal and structural. A box of tissues, withheld in Scene 1, becomes a major statement when proffered in Scene 3. Kimberly Purtell, best known as a lighting wizard, is here the set designer as well, supplying a trio of starkly helpful interlocking environments. The three actresses get plenty of ammo, which they discharge with exhilarating accuracy. Only with Caroline Gillis's Leda does a gap open up between the performer and the performed, but then there's already a gap in the role itself. Leda with daughter is so much more measured and less distraught than Leda with doctor that it's hard to believe she's the same woman. (Interestingly, and maybe intentionally, Ann of Scene 2 is more like Leda of Scene 1 than Leda of Scene 2 is.) She's also the victim of some fancy writing; in a production that's a masterpiece of behavioural naturalism, with every movement of hands on laps making a statement, her long image-crammed speeches ring false. Athena Lamarre's Ann, by contrast, has few problems of credibility and none of consistency; hers is a one-note role but it's a very entertaining note. Ann has no self-censoring mechanism, and this kind of forthrightness, a pain in real life, feels irresponsibly liberating at a safe distance. As Carolyn, Sarah Dodd offers the best of both worlds, vocational restraint underpinned by private passion. She almost justifies her emerging position as the play's protagonist. She stakes this claim at the outset, with an opening soliloquy about doubt and loneliness and "the light behind the door." This motif also figures in a recurring dream of Leda's, but the light is more mystifying than illuminating. The play's closing gesture of hope seems similarly facile. It may be true, it may be false; we don't know enough to tell.TV's In Treatment was more detailed and informative about the therapist-patient relationship and its ramifications, as well as more moving and less pretentious; so, come to that, were the Dr. Melfi scenes in The Sopranos. It's false piety to praise the theatre for doing what television has already done better. - Communion runs through March 2 at the Tarragon Theatre.
