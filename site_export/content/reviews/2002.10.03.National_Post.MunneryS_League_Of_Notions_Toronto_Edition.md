THE LEAGUE AGAINST TEDIUM

Bathurst Street Theatre, Toronto

Simon Munnery is a famous unknown television comedian from Britain. The League Against Tedium, despite the plural implications of its title, is his one-man show. (Is there any other kind of show? This is the fourth solo bit to hit town this season, and the season is less than three weeks old. I begin to understand the concept of The Lonely Crowd.)

Munnerys League is successful in his, its, their declared intent; tedium is kept at bay. Tedium has, though, a less toxic but still dangerous brother called Monotony, and he proves harder to defeat.

Munnery seems to have done some conscientious research before coming to Toronto, and he throws in a decent smattering of local jokes. In essence, though, his is probably offering much the same show to us as he would to the folks at home. He specializes in dyspeptic one-liners, minimally connected, and liberally laced with sour whimsy.

We get a taste of his quality even before the start of the entertainment proper, as a series of texts unfold on a screen. Most of these are effortfully silly ("This show includes epileptics. No strobing, please) but a few are rather elegant (If you leave before the end of the show, you should be ashamed of yourself. If you stay to the end, you will be"). Perusing them, you wonder if the whole evening is going to be like that. It is, but the odds improve. It remains a collection of gags, but there are quite a few good ones.

Its only stand-up comedy, though, the stuff of a club. As a theatrical event, it's somewhere between ramshackle and non- existent. And it's best when it does least. Munnerys idea of spectacle is to come before us with a bucket on his head and to describe the reactions this get-up elicits from people on the street. He does this twice, once before the intermission and once after it. He makes some use of a video screen, on which appear images not only of himself but of his audience. It also reveals a series of famous visitors, from Socrates to Richard Wagner via the Ocean (I mean the Ocean is one of the visitors -- Munnery does seem to have thing about the sea), who make equally famous pronouncements so that Munnery can contradict them, which he does baldly and without argument. "Thus, he says, I win." No, he doesnt.

And, to be fair, I think he knows this. His is the comedy of a vanquished soul. His very title, when you think about it, breathes defeat. Having accepted this, we are free to applaud his gestures of defiance. Against cliches, for example, as when he questions the old saw that money doesn't grow on trees. ("Yes, it does. It's paper.) He asks why bees are attracted to honey, considering that they make the stuff (it must be vanity"). I was also very attracted to the peremptory instruction that I think sums up his philosophy: "Dont punish yourself. You deprive the world of its purpose.

Occasionally he does impressions. His Bob Dylan parody has the dubious distinction of being just as boring (i.e., obscure and inaudible) as the real thing. His Michael Caine act is far happier, and is carried on far longer. He says himself (and he may well be right) that the voice is addictive. Its also not too far removed from his own, which is New Cockney, a bilious sound that suggests the southeastern extremities of London encroaching on the centre and poised to take over. His black-rimmed glasses -- also the mark of Caine, of course -- go with the voice, even seem to be part of it. It's a barbed and jaundiced instrument, and it implies a view of the world.

On the totem pole of soliloquizing comics currently on view in Toronto, I'd place Munnery third; on top of Deb Filler but squashed underneath Sandra Shamas and Andy Jones. He shares some concerns with Jones -- they both go on about God -- but unlike Jones, he is not endearing. He is too cold to be cool, too noncommittal to be truly eccentric, a carper rather than a satirist.

But there is one thing he does better than Shamas. He deals with hecklers. He doesn't deal with them very subtly or inventively, but he does try. Shamas seems to regard voices from the floor as lese- majeste, and she gangs up on them. (Can one woman be a gang? Yes, if the rest of the audience is cheering her on.)

Aimless and fidgety as his performance often seems, he does end on a high. He recalls the true story of a BBC TV newsreader (he forgets which one, and so do I) who suffered a personal assault and remarked, when interviewed, that she had never known such terrible things could happen. But, says Munnery, pouncing on media narcissism, for years she had been reading the news. What did she think it was? A poem?" Well and clinchingly said. I had spent the most part of the evening teetering on the brink of liking him; now, at the very last, I fell.
