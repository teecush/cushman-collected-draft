Diego Matamoros, than whom Canada has no better actor, is the son of an El Salvador diplomat, and came to this country with his divorced mother at an early age. That much we know to be true. How much else we can believe of what he tells us in The Aleph is anybody's guess.

The Aleph, a one-man show that Matamoros created in collaboration with Daniel Brooks, is a devilish overlaying of what may or may not be his autobiography upon a story of the same name by Jorge Luis Borges. It starts with him telling us about an apocryphal Broadway production of Hamlet that had George Clooney as Claudius, Meryl Streep as Gertrude ( "with a Danish accent"), Sandra Bullock as Ophelia ( "horribly miscast") and Jim Carrey in a to-die-for cameo as Osric. All it lacked was a prince. After auditioning every young hopeful in town, they finally gave the part to a stooped, guttural 80-year-old actor who at his audition had miraculously transformed himself into Hamlet. Asked how he did it, he replied "that's actink."

Matamoros, who by this point has been doing some casually virtuoso actink himself, now drops that story and proceeds to tell us about a trip he took, as a shy post-adolescent, to Buenos Aires where his father was stationed. He talks about his father's womanizing, and about his own infatuation with his cousin's beautiful sports-car-driving fiancee who unexpectedly returned his feelings. Conquering his initial diffidence, he prolonged his stay and was rewarded. Returning a year later, he found that his beloved, soon after her marriage, had died of cancer. Still obsessed with her, he sought out her cousin Vittorio, a theatre director of monumental pretentiousness working on a production with the modest title of The Universe, and the subtitle of You in Verse, which seems to have consisted largely of an interminable monologue for himself. He allowed young Diego into rehearsals because, it turns out, he wanted to enlist his assistance in engaging another young Canadian actor named Colm Feore. Diego allowed that yes, he knew Colm, and had even acted with him at the Tom Patterson (this much is true; I've checked) but tactfully neglected to make the call. Anyway, it turned out that Vittorio had other plans for him. His theatre was about to be demolished and he wished

Matamoros to descend to its basement where was housed an Aleph: "one of the points in space that contains all space."

That's how it's described in Borges' tale, which was published in 1949 and whose hero and narrator is a man called Borges. His story duplicates Matamoros's in many respects. He, too, has a lost mourned love, and the two girls share a name: Beatriz. (The echoes of Dante are unlikely to be accidental.) The Borgesian Beatriz's cousin is not a director but an equally self-admiring poet, whose magnum opus is called The World. Both Borges and Matamoros are terrified when they realize that they've let a madman lock them up in a cellar. Both, to their amazement, behold the Aleph and see everything, simultaneously. Well, maybe not everything, but a lot of things, some of them connected with Beatriz. Both come to the conclusion that what they saw was not a true Aleph, though they are confident that such a thing exists. Borges' reasons, which are scholastic, are more convincing than Matamoros's, which are theatrical. On the other hand, because we hear them in real time rather than just read them, Matamoros's carry more emotional weight. His Aleph is a meditation on the sweet sorrow of romantic love and on the theatre, as well as a literary joke played on an author whose own stock-in-trade was literary jokes.

By coincidence or design, the Soulpepper repertoire contains another monologue with Argentinian connections, Guillermo Verdecchia's 20-year-old Fronteras Americanas. Verdecchia uses his own immigrant experience, from there to here, to plead in effect for a breaking-down of borders so that we can all get along as just Americans. I applaud the sentiment, though as an immigrant from Britain of Polish grandparentage, I felt somewhat left out; how about all being citizens of the world? I could certainly identify with Verdecchia's experience of feeling at home everywhere and nowhere. Some of his audience-baiting gambits, though, struck me as tendentious. He complains that American filmmakers depict Colombian (and, now, Mexican) drug lords as stereotypes while ignoring the fact that they can be quite philanthropic on the side. Well, yeah, you could say the same about the Koch brothers. He also lampoons the Distillery itself, as an example of the Saxon habit of selling things on what were once cultural sites; since the things that used to be made there were intended for sale, the moral difference escapes me. He alternates between two personae: his own, and a self-admitted Latin stereotype with three names. As the show progresses, you stop caring which is which. Some of it's funny: Verdecchia describes "white people dancing independently ... of the beat and the rhythm"; later he disarmingly admits that he himself "couldn't dance a tango to save my life." Much of the undercutting, though, just seems aimless, other than as a short cut to the next point-scoring. The end result is overbearing and underwhelming.
