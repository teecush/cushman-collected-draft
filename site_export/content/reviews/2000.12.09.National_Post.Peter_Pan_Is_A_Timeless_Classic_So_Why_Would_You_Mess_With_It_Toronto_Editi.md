PETER PAN

Elgin Theatre

This year's Christmas extravaganza at the Elgin is a strange mixture of play, musical and traditional English pantomime. Ross Petty's previous efforts in this line -- Robin Hood, Cinderella and the others -- have been pantos through and through, loose-jointed entertainments based on stories that rank as folk-myths, without readily identifiable literary sources. Peter Pan, though, exists as a play: Well-known, revivable, less than a century old, certainly a classic, probably a masterpiece. Even the much-loved musical-comedy version of the 1950s simply stuck songs into the existing text: Something that shouldn't have worked but did, delightfully.

J. M. Barrie went public domain some years ago, so legally you can do what you like to his creations. The trouble is that, with Peter Pan, nobody in their right minds would want to do much. This is a first-rate adventure story that fills an evening, and needs no padding. (Most panto sources beg for it.) There are all those characters: Peter himself, the boy who wouldn't grow up; his Lost Boys and his fairy sidekick, Tinker Bell; the precociously motherly Wendy and her two Darling brothers; the spectacularly paranoid Captain Hook and his comparatively scurvy crew; mermaids, Red Indians, and the crocodile who swallowed an alarm clock.

The current version keeps them all, except that the Lost Boys have been joined by a few Lost Girls ("political correctness," muttered my own daughter, disapprovingly) and the Indians have gone, for equally obvious reasons. Petty and his team can't really cut loose -- they are stuck with Barrie's story -- but are they wedded to Barrie's words? No, bicarbonate of soda, no! And their own, though serviceable, aren't as good. They have also lost, or given away, Barrie's dark attitude.

The show's greatest asset is the boss himself. Four years ago, reviewing Robin Hood for Another Newspaper, I suggested Peter Pan (the real one) as an ideal future vehicle for Petty and his wife, Karen Kain, whom I have every reason to believe can fly.

I've got half my wishes: Petty is, as expected, a baroque, farouche, peruked and swishing Hook, equally adept at threatening, singing, bantering with the audience and collapsing in a heap. He also, according to tradition, doubles as Mr. Darling, here transformed from a would-be tyrannical paterfamilias to a cheerful Edwardian alcoholic. (The play has gained a London street-scene that seems to derive equally from Oliver!, Mary Poppins and music-hall, and features a cockney policeman who not only drops his H's but picks up them up in the wrong places.)

Sheila McCarthy has less fortune. She makes a proper airborne entrance, arms and legs akimbo, but all the complexities of the developmentally challenged character are gone and she is left playing cheerful straight-boy, not only to Hook but to Sharmaine Ryan's insistently flesh-and-blood Tinkerbell.

Jayne Lewis sings sweetly as Mrs. Darling and with engaging shamelessness as a vampy mermaid -- Esther Williams gone Mae West. The pirates are pretty funny, and the lost kids lively. Glen Kotyk's production also incorporates some lively fights and dances, and (courtesy Judy and David Gershson) terrible would-be contemporary songs.

There is also, in panto tradition, as much audience participation as the unusually heavy traffic will bear. We lose, though, the most celebrated example of spectator-involvement in world drama: Peter's plea that we save the poisoned Tink by clapping our hands. Presumably the idea of a threatened fairy was thought too alarming. Also gone are Mr. Darling's masochistic self-confinement to the doghouse, Wendy's fears for her bereaved parents, the pirates' real bloodthirstiness, and Peter's haunting loneliness: Everything, in fact, that makes the play creepily interesting.

We gain Ernie Coombs, aka Mr. Dressup, padding amiably around the plot on a rather loose lead. He presides over the final singalong. Traditionally, this is a panto's penultimate item, just before everybody troops down the palace staircase, married. Here, though, there can be no palace; and all the characters are already married, or too young, or incurably celibate. It makes for a really damping anti-climax; and it suggests that a Peter Pantomime really is a contradiction in terms.
