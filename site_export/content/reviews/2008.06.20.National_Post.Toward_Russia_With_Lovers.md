The late Wendy Wasserstein described The Sisters Rosensweig, a 1992 New York success now being given a belated Toronto premiere by the Harold Green Jewish Theatre Company, as "neither a serious nor a comedic play." She then contradicted herself by saying "it is hopefully both."

It can't be both and neither. Let's not get into "hopefully," but just when did the pretentious "comedic" replace the perfectly serviceable "comic"? Maybe "comic" is considered too full-blooded a word. Chekhov's plays are both serious and comic. Wasserstein's, though moderately enjoyable in the Broadway middlebrow manner, is neither.

It's fair to invoke Chekhov because the play itself does. Not only is it about three sisters, but it starts with the birthday of one of them. We glimpse the celebration dinner table at the back of Phillip Silver's sumptuous set, just as we do in the first act of Three Sisters. Like their Russian counterparts, the Rosensweig ladies receive a succession of gentleman callers, some welcome and expected, others -- at least initially -- not.

The play takes place in London, though the sisters are American, originally from the Bronx. Sara, whose house we are in, has a high-profile job in the British office of an Asian bank. Pfeni uses Sara's place as a base. She's a journalist who used to cover international disaster zones but has now settled for glossy travel pieces; she's just got back from Bombay. That leaves Gorgeous, the only one to have stayed in the United States and the only one currently married. Back home in Massachusetts she's lucked into a career as a radio host, dispensing advice. She's managed to combine a gig as a tour guide with attendance at Sara's birthday.

Gorgeous is also the only sister to have stayed true to her Jewish roots. She's quite vociferous about it, as she is about most things that arise. Pfeni is indifferent, and Sara icily hostile. She's also a multiple divorcee, a situation that -- as she says in the play's most memorable line -- has given her a rich choice of surnames; the WASPish one she has settled on is Goode. She is desultorily dating a British businessman, whom her teenage daughter Tess describes as a Nazi. She also attracts the attentions of a furrier from New York, who seems to represent everything in her background from which she has been trying to escape. Pfeni, meanwhile, enjoys, when enjoyment is possible, an on-and-off liaison with a gay English theatre director.

This assortment of relationships gives the play a fractured, compartmentalized feeling. The same split focus occurs in Chekhov, but his plays are unified by some large event bearing down on all the characters: the sale of a cherry orchard, the army leaving town, a threatened or actual suicide.

The Sisters Rosensweig is set against something much bigger -- the disintegration of the Soviet empire -- but it's too big, and too far off, to impinge fully. It gives rise to twinges of social conscience in Pfeni and even in Sara. Young Tess is even planning a trip to Lithuania along with her activist boyfriend. But it doesn't amount to more than an intermittent plot device, and even the Jewish heritage theme doesn't count for as much as the author apparently wants it to. She seems to be blackmailing her characters with it, much as Gorgeous does. And it all seems mixed in, anyway, with an imposed guilt at having left America.

Why shouldn't Sara live in London if she wants to?

So the interest in the play comes down to the sisters' relationships with one another and -- more importantly, despite the title -- with their various men, and these don't carry enough of a charge. The not-quite-serious, not-quite-comic mode means in practice that both laughter and pain are muted. Wasserstein won't sell out with a happy ending but can't bring herself to write an unhappy one either.

Jim Warren's production is very assured, so well placed and well paced that you all but take it for granted. It doesn't manage, though, to place the sisterhood where it presumably belongs, at the centre of the play. There's a scene of reconciliation and reminiscence that works quite well as a set-piece, without ever persuading us these women could be related. (Their colourings, for a start, are wildly disparate, however many allowances you make for hair-dye.)

Individually, they're accomplished. Rosemary Dunsmore is icy, brittle and troubled as a Sara who's gone native with a vengeance (barely a trace of an American accent); Sarah Dodd is pained and confused, but with a winningly tolerant smile, as Pfeni; and Linda Kash shifts in to yenta overdrive as Gorgeous, though certainly garnering laughs on the trip.

Of the men, Richard Greenblatt's fur merchant makes wryness into a fountain of dramatic energy as well as humour, and as Pfeni's love interest, who turns out more loving than interested, Steve Cumyn has an effervescent time testing the waters of bisexuality. (Pfeni's character is apparently autobiographical, but that doesn't stop this relationship from seeming thoroughly manufactured.) Cumyn's performance is geographically impeccable, as is Michael Hanrahan's right-wing suitor with rumoured unsavoury tastes. Regrettably, he's confined to a single scene, but he does suggest unexplored depths, and he registers the evening's greatest moment of silent wit when he casts his eyes on a young man's ratty sweater and decides that it's taught him all he needs to know.

There's also a repeated device -- not quite a running gag -- whereby a character bursts into song, either sacred or profane, and is interrupted by someone's coming through the door. Music is important to these people, and the dramatist lets us know it. But she doesn't make us feel it. - The Sisters Rosensweig runs until Sunday.
