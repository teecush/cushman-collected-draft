'I waited, deepening, and the fish Listened for me." That's a line, or a line and a half, from a Ted Hughes poem, After Moonless Midnight. The central character of The River who is indeed a fisherman, amateur but passionate, has a copy of it lying around the cabin he calls home, and which is indeed by a river. At one point in Jez Butterworths play he beseeches a lady friend to read it aloud; an excerpt from it is also printed in the program. Heard or read, it mesmerizes. Or, to use the plays own metaphor, it hooks you, draws you in.

"They waited for me. The whole river Listened to me ... 'We've got him, it whispered." The Man (the play gives no names) is hooked himself, on his own devotion to fly-fishing and, by dangerous analogy, on the need to love and be loved. Over the years, it seems, he has brought a succession of women here on a succession of moonless midnights, taken them fishing, had sex with them, told them he loved them, but never obtained the same declaration in return. The sex, to judge from one of the best speeches in a play full of good speeches, was always fine but at the end of it the woman was turned off because the man turned away. Leaving everybody frustrated and the cycle primed to begin all over again. The play switches back and forth between two of these encounters, with partners identified as The Woman and The Other Woman. The Other Woman, at least in the Coal Mine Theatres Canadian premiere production, is younger. Butterworth is the British playwright who came to notice with the tough-guy play Mojo, and who achieved huge success with Jerusalem, a wild panoramic play that contains the best writing to grace an English-speaking stage in years. The River is a smaller play than Jerusalem, apparently written in conscious reaction to it, lyric rather than epic, melancholic rather than satiric. But there's continuity. Both plays have loner protagonists, though The River's is an introvert, where Jerusalems was decidedly the opposite. Both plays, as written, have English rural settings, richly evoked; though in The River the exact locale, like the characters, is anonymous. It goes quite beautifully into Canadian.

The play absolutely demands an intimate space, and the Coal Mine company, obliged to spend their new season shuttling up and down the Danforth, do it proud in their current lodging. Ted Dykstras direction is exquisitely tuned, and Steve Lucas' kitchen-table design perfect. David Ferry, who has given some extraordinary performances these last few years, excels himself here, with a study in watchful desolation that withstands the closest scrutiny. His smile is both childlike and Satanic. He also has impressive practical skills, gutting and cooking a fish under our very noses. He makes us share his terror when one of his ladies seems to have disappeared into the darkness, and both shocks and amuses us when he learns that she has merely gone fishing with another fisherman. The river proves a fertile spawning-ground for jealousy; baiting your hook the easy way counts as a particularly noxious form of cheating. The two Women are excellently contrasted: Jane Spidell amused and sensible, more inclined to read To the Lighthouse (another appropriate choice) than to witness a lunar eclipse, Dani Kind (the Other) flightier and more excitable; the two of them eventually alike in disillusion. The play's mechanics do become predictable, the characters and their situation intriguing rather than involving. But the writing holds, and a crafty piece of casting lends spice to an ending that might otherwise appear rather pat.

Gord Rands The Trouble with Mr. Adams is another play in which a man has a series of unsatisfying encounters with women, though in this case theyre presented sequentially rather than as a mosaic. Mr. Adams is a middle-aged gym teacher, and his trouble is that he may have made inappropriate advances to his star volleyball student when they were stranded in his car during a freak snowstorm and she was a day under 16. The first scene starts with a bang, Mr. Adams announcing to his wife that he's leaving her. He then has to backtrack when she tells him that she knows everything, and that the girl's parents are filing charges; shes had a terrible day in other ways too. Philippa Domvilles performance is emotionally and physically fearless. Scene two finds Mr. Adams consulting a lawyer (Allegra Fulton) who loathes him and shows it but, professional that she is, directs him toward a loophole through which he may crawl to an acquittal. After these two duets with aquiline actresses, the teacher finally gets a scene with his fresh-faced ex-pupil (Sydney Owchar). It's two years later and, having apparently been let off with a lot of cautions, he tracks her down in a motel room after her latest athletics meet. It's implausible that he would do this, and incredible that she would let him in; and their subsequent conversation does nothing to clear things up. He keeps protesting his essential innocence, she doesnt seem inclined to disagree, and her responses, then and now, remain cloudy. Shes always referred to by her full name of Mercedes McPfefferidge, suggesting a vein of dark or at least piquant comedy which surfaces in all the scenes but is never maintained. Mr. Adams' state at the end is pitiful but not pitiable; despite our having spent the whole play in his company, we simply don't know enough about him, and Chris Earles performance circles the character without getting inside it. Like The Man in The River he is, or believes himself to be, hopelessly in love but hes not so much mysterious as puzzling. robert.cushman@hotmail.com

The River runs through Nov. 22 at the Coal Mine Theatre, 982 Danforth Avenue; The Trouble with Mr. Adams runs through Nov. 29 at the Tarragon Extra Space
