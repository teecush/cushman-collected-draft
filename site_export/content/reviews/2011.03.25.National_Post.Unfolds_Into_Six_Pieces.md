PAPER SERIES

Young Centre for the Performing Arts, Toronto

In David Yee's Paper Series, presented by Cahoots Theatre Company, six actors perform six short plays, all in the space of an hour and a half. The segments vary in tone and tempo, but all are at least a bit funny and at least a bit sad. Some of them are a lot of both. Each actor gets to be the protagonist/narrator of one piece, with the others lending sharp, disciplined support as required. Acting, writing and Nina Lee Aquinos direction are all first-rate.

As for what links the playlets to one another and to the overall title: They are called, in order of appearance, Paper Burns, Paper Cuts, Paper Dolls, Paper Tears, Paper Folds and Paper Route. (Alas, no Paper Moons or Paper Tigers.) You have to consult the actors bios to discover these subheadings; most of them are incipient puns, as are the characters' names.

We start with Baht (Marjorie Chan), a schoolgirl chastised by her father for forging his signature on a sick note: not for doing it, but for doing it badly. Forgery is his own game, and he teaches his skills to his daughter, but not well enough; his bullet-ridden demise lands her in trouble with the Russian mob, who are unimpressed with the quality of her bank notes; she can burn paper but paper burns her. The action moves swiftly and internationally, getting us off to a breezy start. Entertaining though it is, however, it seems in retrospect the weakest part of the evening.

We shift into high gear as Hope (Rosa Laborde) tries, hopefully, to write a Dear John letter to a partner whose name happens, ridiculously, to be John. Trying to dump him, she begins listing all the things about him she can no longer stand, but discards the letter as too cruel; this paper cuts too deep. She tries a succession of softer messages, and with each she gives us a clearer and wittier view of the relationship itself. This is lovely writing and lovely acting.

Lovely, too, is Mutt (Rebecca Applebaum), who in her fifth or sixth foster home, comforts herself by making paper cutouts of her Scottish father and Asian mother, and imagines them loving her while gently bickering among themselves.

In a cast equally divided by gender, the three women all get to strut their solo stuff before the three men, which is unexpected but unimportant. First up among the guys is Wisdom (Lorenzo Garcia), a waiter in a Chinese restaurant, whose fed-upness with a loutish customer incites him to write the man and his date fortune cookie mottos that effectively ruin their plans for the evening. The idea catches on, and soon customers are flocking to the place, not to consume the cuisine but to sample Wisdom's wisdom. Brilliantly set up, the story rises to a climax of mayhem that feels forced as narrative, but is hilariously staged. The section, too, has a very good deflationary punchline, as indeed do most of the sections. (David Yee, Second City needs you.)

The paper that folds is an origami swan that Symbol (Byron Abalos) presents to his suddenly ex-girlfriend as a memento of their relationship. This fifth episode in the series suffers from being a photo-negative of episode two, but like its predecessor, its full of bittersweet insights into the pleasures and pitfalls of coupledom.

Finally, and most memorably, we meet Isaac (Kawa Auda), whose route is the one he follows as a Toronto taxi driver and whose paper, which he keeps in his cab, is the certificate he received on completing his medical studies in India. Hes one of a knot of immigrant taxi jockeys with no outlet for their real skills. Finally, they get a chance to put their training into practice. Triumph is shot through with bitterness, or maybe it's the other way about.

Laborde and Auda give the standout performances, but all half-dozen are fine, not just in their solos but in their supporting interjections. In an exceptionally light and fluid production, they bob invigoratingly up from the sidelines as thickvoiced Slavic gangsters, as the orphaned Mutt's pungently accented parents, as an infuriatingly yowling cat, as cocksure discomfited restaurant patrons. They also go into full-company kung-fu or Bollywood routines.

The paper, meanwhile, lives up to its title billing, filling an already-white stage (Camellia Koo, designer) like a snowstorm, as each new sheaf of it is discarded. Yee, already known as an excellent actor, received award nominations for his play Lady in the Red Dress. I missed it, but I'll take it on trust and say that this new collection establishes him among Canada's more-thanpromising playwrights. There it is, on paper.
