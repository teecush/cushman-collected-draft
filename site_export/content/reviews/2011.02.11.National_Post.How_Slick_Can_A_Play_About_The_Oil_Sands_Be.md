THEATRE REVIEW

Highway 63: The Fort MacShow

Theatre Passe Muraille Backspace, Toronto

---

"Be light" are the last words spoken, and the creators of Highway 63: The Fort Mac Show have taken them to heart. This very entertaining piece in a slice of old-style drama-doc theatre, the kind in which a group of writers and performers, or most often writer-performers, descend on a particular community, hang around, ask questions and serve up the answers and observations in a free-wheeling script that has been worked out and improvised in rehearsal. On this occasion, the territory under inspection is Fort McMurray, industry centre of Alberta's oil sands. Or tar sands. Or, as the show insists is the label with the least baggage attached, bitumen sands.

Of the three performers, two -- Georgina Beaty and Jonathan Seinen -- are Albertans themselves, while the third, Greg Gale, is from Newfoundland. They are young, funny, energetic and versatile;

at least each of them manages to switch quickly and with conviction between a variety of roles with no changes of costume, unless you count Seinen taking his glasses on and off. They provide amusing sketches involving schoolrooms and council meetings, but these are sideshows. There's a sustained narrative thread going through the evening, and it takes up most of the time.

It involves Steve (Seinen), a shy reclamation scientist, and Chad (Gale), a brash, self-confident Newfie oil worker. They share a flat, and one day when out grocery shopping they encounter Mary (Beaty), whom Steve, with encouragement from Chad, tries ineffectually to pick up. They meet her again at a bar, and this time Steve lucks out. He even gets her to come home with him, but at the last minute loses his nerve and heads for bed, leaving her alone with Chad, with expected results. The reason seems to be that Steve is gay, though closeted; an inset scene shows him placing an ad on Craigslist, in quest of a first-time male partner; I say "seems" because it's never mentioned again and it's quite possible, given the show's conventions, that Seinen at this point is playing someone else entirely, perhaps as a cameo designed to show off the varied social opportunities available in Fort Mac.

Mary works as a tour guide, but is about to move to Toronto to study dance. One thing the show keeps insisting on is that everybody who lives in Fort McMurray has either moved there from somewhere else, or is going to leave. Hence, I suppose, the main title; otherwise inexplicable since nothing here actually happens on the highway. Despite all this transience, the show keeps insisting that Fort Macis a nice and underrated place to live and, in its overall cheerfulness, it pretty much makes its point.

Tensions develop in the Chad-Mary story, due to their differing ideas about the possibility of a long-term relationship; and they finally explode in a presumably symbolic episode in which Mary's conservationist ideas, fostered by Steve, come into conflict with Chad's cheerful lack of them. At least I think that's what's going on; it's all rather murky, visually and otherwise. The general effect is of the creators' belated determination to wring some local relevance from a plotline that has been chuggingly happily along on its own terms, and that mostly could have happened anywhere. In its praiseworthy determination not to preach, the show almost avoids raising any issues at all, and certainly avoids dramatizing them. Basic information is dispensed in the first scene, and there's a shrugging attempt at synthesis in the last, but mostly the script, though full of nice one-liners and aphorisms, seems pleasant but slight. Every so often it steps outside itself and becomes, explicitly, about a bunch of actors researching a play about Fort McMurray.

The neat and speedy production is by Charlotte Corbeil-Coleman, with unspecified inspirational input from her father Layne Coleman, a veteran of Canada's theatrical documentary movement and the past artistic director of Theatre Passe Muraille, which co-produces the show with a new group, Architect Theatre.

The last substantial speech, delivered by Gale in a droll drawl, is a shaggy-dog story. It starts with a promise of ecological relevance but ends as a (very good) joke about marriage and, ultimately, about acting. Which in turn leads to the well-heed admonition, presented as Fort Mac's gift to the rest of us, which is how they go out and where I came in.
