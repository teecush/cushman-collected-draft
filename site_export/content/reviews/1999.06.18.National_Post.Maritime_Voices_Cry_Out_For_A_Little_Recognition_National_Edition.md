THE DOGPATCH

Factory Theatre Studio

There is an area of Halifax called Kline Heights that is also known, or used to be, as the Dogpatch. The nickname presumably derives from the mythical backwoods community depicted in the Al Capp comic strip. The cartoon made capital fun of drunkenness, illiteracy and all-American fecklessness; Li'l Abner, Moonbeam McSwine and company seem to have led carefree lives in their picturesque squalor. Their real-life urban Canadian cousins weren't, and aren't, as lucky.

Chaz Thorne is a young Nova Scotia actor and playwright whose mother once assembled an oral history of life on the Dogptach, derived in part from the memories of her own parents and grandparents. Thorne used his mother's researches as the launching pad for a play, written when he was 19. It's a promising piece of work, with dialogue that slides easily off the actors' tongues, and characters who are thoroughly believable.

There's a gulf, though, between being believable and being interesting, and this play doesn't always bridge it. Its first 20 minutes just lie there, the author setting up his characters while implicitly asserting: "These are my people -- look at them" -- a demand that always prompts the unspoken response, "Why should we?" The play only becomes gripping when it becomes violent, which it does with increasing frequency.

We are in the house of Reuben, a soldier back from the war, the 1939-45 one. He was a drunken, abusive husband and father before he went away and he is worse now, a spell in a POW camp having equipped him with a grievance. His battered wife, Liz, strives desperately to keep the family together. They have three sons of whom the eldest, Doug, hangs out with Wally, who has done time for stealing a truck. When Doug turns up for work bearing the marks of a beating from his father, he is fired, and all he wants is to get away. He and Wally team up to rob a cab, and the driver is killed. Wally will do anything to avoid returning to prison, and the action spirals into inevitable catastrophe.

There is a ring of truth about this, but not a ring of freshness. I suspect, too, that the author has given too much space to Doug (whom he plays himself) and not enough to his parents. Adolescent rebellion is a well-worn theme and, even in these extreme circumstances, it comes out onstage much as it usually does. Wife beating and family abuse are subjects we read a lot about in the papers but rarely see dramatized, and it would be interesting to see the dynamics fully explored. This play makes a start in the character of Reuben, who alternates between brutality and displays of maudlin affection. When he paws Liz and she resists, he asks her what's wrong with the neighbours seeing that she has a loving husband; later, she remarks that she played the loving wife because it was what she wanted. Reuben's self-pity is kept going to the very end, and it's both well-observed and well-illustrated. Liz, though, isn't allowed to get much beyond standard stoicism. There are times when the play resembles Sean O'Casey's Juno and the Paycock without the humour though also, and mercifully, without the rhetoric.

Brian Richmond has given the play a full-bodied unforced production, and the action moves fluently around the neighbourhood, cortesy of Kelly Wolf, who has designed a setting of netting.

The acting is uniformly good, right down to Griffin Benger and Joseph Weissgold who make Doug's two younger brothers a couple of piercingly ordinary kids, trying to find their way through an inferno. David Ferry has the most generous opportunities, doubling Reuben with the unlucky and very loquacious cabbie. Kyra Harper is Liz, whose face can become a screaming mask of pain; Adrian S. Churchill plays Wally who, like nearly everyone else in the play, can tell a good scary story; Frank McAnulty is his damaged dad, rather too firmly docketed as poignant comic relief; and Barbara Gordon is the local healer, physical and mental.

If the play lacks an individual voice, it does present a collective one, a pungent Maritime accent that Toronto doesn't often get to hear.
