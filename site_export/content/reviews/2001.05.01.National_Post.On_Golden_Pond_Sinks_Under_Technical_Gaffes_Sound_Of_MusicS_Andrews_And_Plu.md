Toward the end of CBS's live-drama production on Sunday night, we were treated to a full-length shot of a cameraman, though it may possibly -- since naturally they didn't linger on it -- have been a floor manager. On Golden Pond is a house-and-garden play, and earlier, in the middle of one of the indoor scenes, the camera had panned deliriously along a row of greenery belonging to the exterior. A boom microphone occasionally strayed into the top of the picture, but this may have been done on purpose, as a sort of live- production log. The awkwardness of many of the compositions was presumably accidental, suggesting that television has a lot to learn, or re-learn, about the art of catching drama on the hoof. Perhaps in acknowledgement of the abiding staginess of Ernest Thompson's play, we were treated to arbitrary views of the full set. The crowning moment of gracelessness came, fittingly, at the end: Julie Andrews and Christopher Plummer, the two stars, made their final exit by disappearing down the bottom right-hand corner of the screen.

Technical gaffes aside, On Golden Pond seems an odd choice for live production since it is, and probably always has been, so thoroughly dead. First staged in 1978, it's one of those Broadway confections (The Gin Game is another) that pretend to be honest in demonstrating that old people are not plaster-saints but pull back from showing them as anything worse than cutely cranky; that they are invariably cast with much-loved stars is the tooth-rotting icing on the cake.

The darling duo here are Norman Thayer, a retired English professor, and his wife, Ethel, of whose career, if she ever had one, we are not informed. She is pushing 70, he is turning 80. They have spent every summer of their 50-year marriage "on golden pond" - - their lakeshore residence in Main -- and here they are for another one. Norman's faculties are beginning to fail, and at one point he tells of getting lost on a familiar walk to the woods; strings welled up behind this speech to ensure that we didn't miss any of the poignancy. (Count your blessings: If this had been a TV film, it would have been drenched in tear-jerker music.) The play has no shame; it teases us by taking Norman to the brink, felling him with what looks like a fatal heart attack and then passing it off as a false alarm. The scene gives Ethel just enough time to display an improbably stiff upper lip; she is anguished, but unshakably well- bred.

Norman and Ethel have an only child, a daughter called Chelsea, now a 40-year-old divorcee. Long absent in California, she reappears with her new boyfriend, a dentist, and his son, a 14-year-old terror who represents everything in modern life that Norman detests and much he has probably never heard of. While the middle-aged couple go off to Europe, the old ones take care of the boy; and -- to nobody's surprise except the characters, he and Norman bond. The professor learns some new idioms; the kid starts to fish and even to read. Before, he dismissed the presence of books in the house as evidence that "they don't have cable." This is one of several indications that the script has been updated since '78. The juvenile language is more colourful than would have been tolerated, on the screen anyway, 25 years ago. Norman's ivy-league diction is now cut with coy hints at obscenity.

The thing is put together with undeniable slickness, and there are some good grouchy lines for Norman. Plummer, in great impish form, seemed to relish them -- and the enjoyment was contagious. There was also at least a hint of wickedness in it. The script tells us that the man could be a bastard, especially to his daughter. We are never shown any evidence of this, but Plummer at least made it seem a distant possibility. His reaction to the news that she has impulsively remarried in Brussels -- "get out of town!" -- could have been worth the price of admission, had there been one.

I imagine that Henry Fond in the 1981 film (which I avoided) was far less fun, being really frail at the time and unshakably noble. Katharine Hepburn, though, must have been an asset as his opposite number; when she called Norman "you poop," she must have given the word a real Pennsylvania tang.

Coming from Julie Andrews, still English after all these years, it merely sounded odd; and her acting hardly went beyond sweet reasonableness.

Still, this late reunion of the Sound of Music principles made a certain sense: With allowances for changes of time, place and accent, one can well imagine the von Trapps living out their declining years like this, lake and all.

Glenne Headly, woefully ill-at-ease, played the daughter, Sam Robards the prospective son-in-law, and Brett Cullen was saddled with the role that takes the lay to its lowest depths, that of Charley, the slow-witted mailman, who for decades has nourished a hopeless passion for Chelsea. "I love your laugh," Ethel told him, while plying him with coffee and biscuits; this was kind of her, since it sounded like a cross between a rusty hinge and a death- rattle, and it never stopped.

At the end we were shown a montage of highlights, and we got to hear someone -- the director -- congratulating everyone on their great work. The implication seemed to be that they had all come through some kind of historic ordeal. This really was unconscionable. Last year's Fail Safe, which must have been a far more complicated production, had none of the same glitches; and there was a time when all TV drama was live and nobody felt obliged to treat each play as a special event. That was also of course the time of black-and-white TV, so the plays seemed grittier all round. It was an accidental aesthetic but a real one.

On Golden Pond is a sentimental play about well-heeled, well- educated people with minimal problems -- the kind that Broadway, echoed by Hollywood and network TV, likes to call "civilized." They might just as well have shot it on a stage. It could hardly have seemed phonier. It might even have lent some charm.
