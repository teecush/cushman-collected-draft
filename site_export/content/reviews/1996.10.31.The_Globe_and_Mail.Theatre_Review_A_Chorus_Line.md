A CHORUS LINE

THEATRE REVIEW

A CHORUS LINE

Thursday, October 31, 1996

Directed by Baayork Lee

Written by James Kirkwood and Nicholas Dante


  


Lyrics by Edward Kleban


  


Music by Marvin Hamlisch


  


Rating: ***

Chorus Line gets another kick at the cancan

The Broadway musical still has an impact,

21 years after its first opening night.

Special to the Globe and Mail


  


THE only song in A Chorus Line that I dislike is its solitary big ballad, What I Did for Love ,sung by most of the company. The title ostensibly refers to the characters' shared vocation as Broadway dancers, but there are no specific references to any of the glories or humiliations of the trade. The lyrics have been kept remorselessly anonymous so that they can be sung out of context as a heavy romantic number, a defiant lament, which it was during its rather brief career as a pop standard.

The song would be annoying enough in any musical, but in one that makes a fetish of its own integrity, and for the most part justifies the claim, it is infuriating. The number is, in the strict sense of the word, egregious; it stands out in the wrong way. And yet, out of the lyrical fog and the musical soup, there emerges the show's most chilling moment.

A Chorus Line is,after all, the definitive musical on the subjects of insecurity and unemployment. We meet the characters at an audition, 17 of them trying out for a show that has chorus roles for eight. They are both colleagues and competitors. The song has brought them together in shared affirmation. When it is over each freezes: lonely, frightened, awaiting the director's godlike decision on their individual fates.

A Chorus Line still has tremendous impact. It returns 21 years after its off-Broadway opening and six years after its Broadway closing. In 1983, an extraordinary gala performance marked the show's arrival as longest-running Broadway musical ever, a position since usurped by Cats .


  


The new edition of the spoof revue Forbidden Broadway commemorates this event showing a furry and disgruntled group of Chorus Liners reduced to auditioning for the Andrew Lloyd Webber show ("step, kick, meow, purr. . .") and intoning "Oh, God I hate this show. . ."


  


Broadway chauvinism apart, their chagrin is understandable. A Chorus Line should have run forever, if only as a talisman. It is not the greatest of all musicals, but it may be the essential musical. It celebrates its own craft and enshrines its own people. The original production -- directed, choreographed, and masterminded by Michael Bennett -- was showmanship raised to its highest power, every moment perfectly keyed, each passing pleasure part of an overarching design.

Bennett is dead -- as are all the show's begetters, with the exception of composer Marvin Hamlisch -- and the revival is directed by Baayork Lee. She knows the piece intimately (she created the part of Connie Wong) and, as far as I can tell, she has reproduced Bennett's steps and his mise en scène with minute fidelity. Robin Wagner's sets and Theoni V. Aldredge's costumes look the same, starkly emphasizing the leotards and mirrors of the rehearsal room before exploding into the shimmering golden glitz of the finale. The number itself, One , is still exciting. Edward Kleban wrote most ingenious lyrics: A star celebration, in the Mame vein, that also works as a triumphant metaphor for the chorus itself, the treasured individuals merged into "one singular sensation". Hamlisch's music, with its wrenching key-changes abetted by a killer orchestration, pushes up the temperature to fever-point. But it's not as exhilarating as it was in the original musical. There's a telltale pause before it gets underway, so it seems less like a culmination and more like a run-of-the-mill encore. And that is symptomatic of the production as a whole. Occasionally it sags; the line is not drawn quite tight enough. And -- something I don't remember in any of the Bennett companies I saw -- there is distinct unevenness in the cast.

There are three stand-outs. Cindy Marchionda takes over the stage as the Puerto Rican who tells about the crass acting teacher who railed at her when she couldn't imitate an ice-cream; her song Nothing is the best in the score, and the best-sung. Luis Villabon is touching as the former drag-artist whose confessional speech still seems a bridgehead for the treatment of homosexuality in a mainstream musical. Jill Slyter, arresting in a red dress, has the required almost-star presence for Cassie, trying desperately to get back in the chorus after failing as a solo act. She dances out her frustration in The Music and the Mirror , a frustrating experience for the audience, too, since the routine never takes off -- I have never been sure whether this is intentional. The character may not be able to act but the actress can. It's her former partner, the director Zach (Mark Martino), who comes off woodenly. Their relationship is the show's trickiest area. We are told less about it than we need to know, so we'd rather not know anything. Zach meanwhile interrogates those auditioning so that we can discover what we need to know about them, but he doesn't have to do it as aggressively and unsympathetically as he does here.

They tell their stories in tearful or funny monologues (the line about suicide in Buffalo being redundant still gets the biggest laugh from a Toronto audience), alternated with sizzling group dances. And there are practice numbers that have almost documentary authority; you believe that an audition is really like this. Probably these are the best scenes in the show. But its greatest appeal, as the focus shifts from group to individual and back again, is the sense that the performers have wholly identified with the show and are offering it to the audience. That is true of all the best theatre, but here they are making us a gift of their own lives, and their art is such that we can accept it without embarrassment.

At the O'Keefe Centre until Nov. 3.
