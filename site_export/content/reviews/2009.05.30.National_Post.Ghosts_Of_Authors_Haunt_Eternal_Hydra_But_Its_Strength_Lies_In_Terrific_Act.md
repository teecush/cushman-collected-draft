The Crows Theatre production of Eternal Hydra is a visually impressive and finely acted staging of an interesting play. I have an unworthy suspicion, though, that the text and the presentation (but not the acting) may be working against one another.

Anton Piatigorskys play, which made a one-act debut in the Stratford studio a few years ago, now reappears as that almost extinct species, a two-acter, complete with intermission. It also belongs to the definitely thriving species, the literary fantasia. It begins by quoting the recently fashionable idea of the death of the author, and then proceeds to suggest that even writers and critics who might subscribe to it as a theory, tend to balk at it when it's their own rights that are being withheld. The author at the centre of this play, one Gordias Carbuncle (all names are fictitious, though no others are this unlikely) actually is dead, in the clinical sense, but won't lie down. He haunts an

English literary scholar named Vivian Ezra, whos adopted him as her pet cause and special subject, and has come into possession of the long-lost almost-complete manuscript of his magnum opus: 99 out of a projected 100 chapters of a novel entitled, with double appropriateness, Eternal Hydra. So: Who cut off its last head, and do we care? Or, as a possible publisher says of the book, "I know its great but is it any good?" Carbuncle had a James Joycean background, as well as Joycean ambitions: He is, or was an Irish Jew. That, somebody remarks to him in a flashback, is an unusual combination. Why do you think I left? he snaps back smartly. I have to confess, David Ferry's delivery of this line was my favourite moment of the evening.

Like all authors, dead or otherwise, Carbuncle wants recognition, and he gets very agitated when Vivien seems to be blowing his chances of posthumous publication. She demurs because the New York publisher wants simultaneously to issue a novel by a current black writer Pauline Newberry, in which Gordias is a character, meaning that thunder would be stolen and reputations possibly defamed. It turns out, though, that Gordias was quite the appropriator himself, when he got the chance. We see him in Paris, between the wars, exploiting, intellectually and sexually, the writer/researcher who's done much of his work for him. He makes a less successful pass at a stranded American writer from whom he nevertheless buys the story of her ex-slave grandmother in New Orleans, subsequently putting it in his book. And he fends off the advances of a gay admirer from whom he still succeeds in extracting a cheque.

My main memory of the Stratford production is that it left me wondering if that was all. Despite the extensions and expansions Piatigorsky has put in, I felt much the same now. The play has got more complicated but not necessarily more complex. It now ends with a complete departure: the acting out of the grandmothers story, depicting a suffrage meeting that turned into a race riot. Black people are killed, but a white politician is able to prosper by making their cause his platform. More appropriation, though presumably done with good intentions. And there the play ends, cutting us adrift from the characters we previously knew. Its frustrating, and not just on a narrative level. We're left wondering -- at least I was -- whether we've watched a literal enactment of the purloined story or whether Carbuncle might have touched it up a bit, maybe even transformed it. Does he really have any talent? He certainly has all the trappings, especially the drinking and the sponging. Really, of course, there is no "really." Piatigorsky himself invented everything, and he isnt dead, in any sense. He has a program credit to prove it. The play takes off from a critical theory that nobody has ever found interesting. And as for appropriation: All fiction writers appropriate. Otherwise, theyd have nothing to write about. Race doesn't make any difference. The only issues are copyright ones.

It's tempting to compare this play to those of Tom Stoppard or his lesser-known compatriot Terry Johnson. But they take off from actual figures and/or actual texts; theyre dizzying, but theres solid ground beneath them. They're less cerebral (meaning, among other things, that they're funnier, in texture and structure as well as in individual lines) and easier to follow, partly because you really want to follow them. In Chris Abrahams production of this one, the issues are murky, partly because the stage is. It has striking groupings and transitions, jolting lighting (John Thompson), scary sound (Richard Feren). But the atmospherics drown the text, blinding us with science. The acting, though, is terrific. Everybody doubles, in and out of time frames. Ferry adds the Louisiana pol to his Carbuncle, whos a double in himself: endearingly vain and insecure when dead, disturbingly so when alive, with a vein of self-hatred that may take this actor deeper, and stiller, than he's ever been before. Liisa Repo-Martell is authentic to the bone as a particularly British kind of scholarly sleuth, nervous but implacable, and Karen Robinson, forceful in her present-day persona, is both touchingly shy and heartbreakingly proud when transported to the past. Sam Malkin plays two generations of possible patrons, one of whom says of Gordias' masterwork "I know its great. But is it any good?"

I didnt know the Far East was obsessed with the Wild West, but I'll take Anand Rajaram's word for it. This shining ornament of Second City has devised a wordless entertainment, irresistibly titled Cowboys and Indians, in which Rajaram acts out a very rich fantasy life. A little mime goes a marathon for me, but it's wonderful to watch him dealing resentfully with an unwelcome alarm call, mounting a refractory and contemptuous horse (whom he also momentarily plays), cowering in jail, or climbing all over the audience, in angry search of whoever's done him wrong. He has an accompanist, Bob Wiseman, who conjures ruby-rich harmonies out of a honky-tonk; Rajaram turns on him, too, with a terrifying fury, suggesting meekness too long provoked Some people are appallingly talented.
