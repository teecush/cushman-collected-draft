ORPHEUS DESCENDING, AND THE MEASURE OF LOVE/ RUTH DRAPER ON TOUR

Stratford Festival

- - -

In the Deepest South setting of Orpheus Descending, Seana McKenna stands out as the only person in town with an Italian accent. It makes her the least operatic individual on stage, something that could only happen in a Tennessee Williams play. Her character, known as Lady Torrance, is the daughter of, to quote the local gossips, "a wop from the old country" who made himself very popular by opening a wine garden back in the days of Prohibition, but then, quoting again, "made a bad mistake. He sold liquor to niggers." Some prankish citizens burned down his establishment, and him with it. What Lady doesn't know, though everyone else does, is that the ringleader was the local dry-goods merchant whom she subsequently married and who now, 20 years later, lies dying upstairs.

It has been a loveless, childless marriage anyway, and it has left Lady with her own inner conflagration, a dry and bitter fire that here lights up the stage. McKenna's performance, magnificent even by her own recent standards, is a compact of anger, need and a storekeeper's practicality; it denies self-pity and substitutes, to the play's great benefit, a whiplash wit. Most of her key scenes are with Val Xavier, a young itinerant musician who, by vague mythological association, gives the play its title. In Miles Potter's production these duets are superbly orchestrated, and just as superbly performed, with lines of force between the couple sometimes spanning the whole depth of Peter Hartwell's spare, evocative set.

Jonathan Goad gives a marvellously practical, unselfconscious account of Val, a tougher role than Lady since he exists less in anything he actually does than in what he says about himself and what other people, including his fevered author, see in him. His temperature is "always a couple of degrees above normal, same as a dog's." He is coming, at the age of 30, off a successful career as a stud; his prized possessions are his snakeskin jacket and his guitar, autographed by Leadbelly, Bessie Smith and others. (Goad can sing and play, which helps keep the character rooted.) Lady distrusts him, is drawn to him, and in her own way uses him; the town tramp (a smashing performance by Dana Green, low-down and lyrical) whose rich family pay her to do it somewhere else, sees in him a vulnerable fellow-spirit and wants to get him away; a painter (a too-young Sarah McVie) with failing eyesight and mystical leanings, associates him with a vision of the Risen Christ, and falls on her knees before him. (I should perhaps have mentioned that the play is set at Easter. An earlier version was filled-to- bursting with Christian imagery, which still seems more central to it than Orpheus-with-his-lute.) The artist-lady is also the sheriff's wife and the sheriff is not pleased.

His jealousy, though, is not what precipitates the play's catastrophe, which is plenty catastrophic, involving lynching and blowtorches and flesh-tearing dogs. The various strands of the action themselves resemble a pack of dogs, running wild in different directions. The play has a lot of story but not much plot. It is possibly the ultimate Williams work, sweaty and lyrical, shrewd and naive, wild-eyed and wide-eyed, sometimes hypnotic, sometimes ludicrous and quite often both at once. There are frequent lines, and occasional whole scenes, that cut like knives. There is also a lot of intentional humour in the portrayal of the way a small town wags. And the underlying horrors, as we have recently been reminded, are real.

The cast is stuffed to extravagance with Stratford stars. Thom Marriott's sheriff is worthy of one of the best brooding Westerns; an intruder lurking shamefacedly at the back takes off his hat and stands revealed as Scott Wentworth, playing a single vital scene; Michelle Giroux is a chillingly efficient nurse, held in reserve to the last act. Fiona Reid and Brigit Wilson make an enjoyable pair of scandal-mongers, though if there's a flaw in Potter's direction it's in his handling of their opening exposition, which is too busy to be clear. Besides, it's unfairly true that Southern speech, however authentic one knows it to be, always sounds exaggerated when coming out of non-American actors, and one needs time to adjust. The ending, too, is a bit forced (and in excess, for once, of what the author ordered). But between those poles the performance is awesome. It doesn't prove the play a masterpiece, but it makes it unmissable.

The Measure of Love, Nicholas Billon's new play, is a duologue for two old friends from convent-school. One, who was a free- spirited girl, became an actress; the other, who had once, in her innocence, thought herself the result of an immaculate conception, became a nun. Might it not have been more interesting if they had exchanged destinies? Anyway, here they are; the actress has written a play to apprise the nun of some things in their past that she should know about, and they proceed to act some scenes from it, the nun, who has had no prior warning, proving to be a remarkably talented sight reader.

Some of this involves hearkening back to a school production of Romeo and Juliet in which they were the lovers, except that the sister-in-charge didn't approve of that kind of love and altered the play accordingly. We hear some of her reformed text, and it doesn't make sense, even in its own terms. Just what were the doomed couple, rewritten as just friends and cousins, doomed for?

Billon's own play, like his previous Elephant Song, has more contrivance than substance. One of its ostensible themes is memory, but nothing is made of the announced fact that the actress may be losing hers. However, what is revealed about the nun is touching, and Fiona Reid's face registers the news and its consequences. There is also a nice contrast in the ways she and her colleague, Diana Leblanc, address the audience to express their solicitude for one another: Leblanc urgent and conspiratorial, Reid bemused and benevolent. Jean-Louis Roux directs.

The play forms a double-bill with Ruth Draper on Tour, devised and directed by Raymond O'Neill. Ruth Draper, who died in 1956, was a world-famous American writer and presenter of comic and dramatic monologues. This show presents Lally Cadeau in a selection of these pieces, linked by extracts from letters that Draper wrote to a friend while shuttling between capitals. Sometimes the monologues are set in the cities she's travelling to, sometimes they aren't. The letters make her sound very pleased with herself, and the impression carries over into the vignettes themselves, which are intermittently amusing but seem snobbish and sheltered when dealing with Draper's own upper-middle-class, and sentimental when dealing with anyone else.

All of which is rather unfair to Cadeau, and very unfair to Draper. There are two CDs of her work on sale in the lobby, and these reveal that what her contemporaries said about Draper was true: She was a genius. Her ear was fantastic. She found and maintained a different accent for everyone, and that without ever leaving the vocal confines of the United States. Cadeau never captures the vein of crazy comedy with which Draper can catch you deliriously off guard. Draper was always hard-edged, while Cadeau's edges are soft. Then again, Draper's monologues are never soliloquies; her characters are always talking to somebody. Her admirers always praised the way she seemed to fill the stage with her unseen interlocutors; Cadeau can't do that and neither, probably, could anybody else. Most importantly, Draper wrote these pieces for herself; she did them perfectly, and there is no point in varying a single pause or a single inflection. The script supplies no compensating context, so the actress' task is hopeless. Buy the discs.
