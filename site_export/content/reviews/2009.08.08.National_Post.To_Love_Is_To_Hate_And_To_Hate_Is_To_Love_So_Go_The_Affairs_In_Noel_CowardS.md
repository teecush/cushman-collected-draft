Today, if you've got up early enough, you can see an all-day performance, spread across the Shaw Festival's three stages, of the 10 one-act plays that make up Noel Coward's Tonight at 8:30. Or, as they might have subtitled it on this occasion, This Morning at 9:30. Theatrical marathons tend to be their own reward, though this one won't have the customary insurance of a continuous narrative. The plays may have been written for the same performers, principally Coward himself and Gertrude Lawrence, but each concerns a different bunch of people.

Meanwhile, for those who like to take their pleasures less intensively, the Shaw has completed its staging of the cycle with Ways of the Heart, a triple bill whose portmanteau title has been assembled from its first play, The Astonished Heart, and its last, Ways and Means. The name of the middle one, Family Album, couldn't be fitted in. ( Family Ways, perhaps?) Blair Williams, who made a spectacular directorial debut with The President last season, now gives smart and confident productions of all three.

On The Astonished Heart he bestows a hard glitter that is positively Pinteresque. This suits, since Coward, rather to his own surprise, was a Pinter fan, and Pinter returned the compliment. "I'm on Pinter's wavelength, no doubt of it," Coward wrote in his diary after seeing the first production of The Caretaker -- a moving gesture of recognition across the theatrical generation gap. The two playwrights shared a cauterizing view of human relations and a taste for apparently simple dialogue that masks as much as it reveals. Like Pinter's Betrayal, The Astonished Heart is a play about adultery that plays tricks with time. Those in the Coward play are comparatively primitive -- they consist of framing the play so that the main action is a flashback -- but they provide an effective perspective.

Christian is a psychiatrist, Barbara his wife, and Lenora his mistress. Barbara knows what's going on but tolerates, even encourages it, intent on preserving a marriage that's become companionship. That doesn't stop her being worried, and even hurt. Christian and Lenora, meanwhile, have an infinite capacity for wounding one another; neither can be as uncaring as they would like. The needs of each for the other are never in sync; the balance of dependency is shifting and, eventually, fatal. Unusually for Coward, the play presents passion that is neither heightened into melodrama nor defused into farce. Its lovers are the sophisticated counterweight to the repressed brief encounters of Still Life, from elsewhere in the cycle. As was once said of his friend and disciple Terence Rattigan, Coward shows in one play that love unbridled is a destroyer and, in the other, that love bridled is a destroyer. Laurie Paton gets straight to the sheltered heart of the concerned and comfortable Barbara, as does Claire Jullien, a veritable Hitchcock blond, to the more demonstrative heart of Lenora, an elegant agonizer and the cause of greater agonizing. Christian's heart proves the most astonished of all, but David Jansen hovers around its periphery; 1930s suavity doesn't seem to be his thing, and he lacks the professional assurance of a fashionable physician who conducts his affairs as if they were consultations and is shattered when, psychologically speaking, someone sends him the bill. The play ranks with Fumed Oak as one of the surprise discoveries of the season.

Coward's verbal economy isn't always matched, in these plays anyway, by structural economy. The Astonished Heart has a last scene that's symmetrically necessary but redundant as narrative. It goes on after it's made its point, while the other two plays take forever to get to theirs. Family Album, the only one of the trio I've seen before, is here the most disconcerting. It shows us a Victorian family in mourning, discovering a music box among the mementoes and breaking into song accordingly. Gradually, it's revealed that the deceased was a tyrant they're glad to be rid of. I remembered it as a dainty if ironic period piece; Williams directs it for broad farce, at which his cast are unevenly adept. All, however, is validated when Michael Ball, in a heavenly release from avuncular roles, shuffles on as a butler who looks like Quasimodo blessed with a shock of golden hair. His demented loyalty, as someone says, lightens up a pale room. The paleness extends to the actors' faces, set off, in Paton's authoritative case, by eyes red-rimmed from conscientious weeping.

Sue LePage provides a simple but handsome set of sets, which are now spectacularly changed by the cast who simultaneously divert us by singing Any Little Fish (which seems to be the season's theme song), culminating in the descent of a bed, with Jansen and Jullien inside it, in blue pajamas. We're now in Ways and Means, in which they play a married couple of spongers who've outstayed their welcome on the Cote d'Azur. As in the first play, she's completely and, in this case, hilariously, on top of the style, while he has to struggle, though he's certainly struggling in the right direction. The play's first half restates the situation at a length made more tedious by clumsy supporting performances, with Jenny Young being a riveting exception as a guttural adventuress of uncertain extraction. Things buck up splendidly with the arrival of a burglar ex machina who knows his place (Patrick McManus, delightfully deferential) and helps the hero and heroine regain theirs. Morally, it's an unimproving tale; theatrically, it improves all the way. The complete project doesn't rank as a major revelation, but all the plays in it have shown themselves worth doing and most -- not all--have been well done.

- Ways of the Heart plays in repertoire through Oct. 11 at the Court House Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake. For ticket information, call 1-800-511-SHAW.
