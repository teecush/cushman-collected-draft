BROADWAY ANECDOTES BY PETER HAY Oxford, 395 pages, $24.95

Yarns and slow Burns

BROADWAY ANECDOTES BY PETER HAY Oxford, 395 pages, $24.95

Saturday, March 17, 1990

ROBERT CUSHMAN

ALL MY BEST FRIENDS BY GEORGE BURNS with David Fisher Putnam, 320 pages, $23.50 REVIEW BY ROBERT CUSHMAN A book of anecdotes, like a dictionary of quotations, is not something to be read. It is to be consulted, dipped into, rifled. A browsers delight, it is guaranteed to put a reviewer in a foul temper. He has to plow through it, thinking about all the real books in which anecdotes have been welcome diversions from the serious business of narrative and analysis. He knew he'd have to pay for them some day. Here is that day, and if he doesn't watch his language hell find himself dragging out the old image of an indigestible meal entirely composed of hors doeuvres.

There, I warned you.

Peter Hay's collection of Broadway Anecdotes is committed to myth- making. No matter how sharp the practices or inflated the egos revealed by his stories, everything contributes to the greater glory of the American showcase. As Hay points out, the alternatives to Broadway - the regional theatre, the road, off-Broadway, off-off-Broadway - define themselves by their distance from the Main Stem, and so pay inadvertent tribute to it. Broadway may not create much any more, but it remains the great theatrical market-place.

Like New York itself, Broadway glories in its own impossibility. New Yorkers deplore their city's violence, but they secretly pride themselves on their own ability to live with it and survive. Hays last tale is of a rehearsal fight between playwright Clifford Odets and director Harold Clurman. As these two luminaries swore, screamed and, in Clurmans case, rolled raving on the carpet, actor Sanford Meisner smiled and said, I wouldn't be in any other business.

Broadway's survival depends on making everybody believe that. And we do, easily. The slightest exposure to the disease and you catch it, even as a member of the audience. Hence Broadways devotion to its own legend. Hence the succession of backstage musicals. Some people even believe the Tony Awards.

And thus, apparently, it has ever been. This book takes us back to 1713, when New England Puritans, bridling at the rumor that a play was being planned, exclaimed, "Let not Christian Boston go beyond heathen Rome in the practice of shameful vanities." It seems that American censorship pre-dates American drama. It took some time for sinful vanities to get off the ground, but they finally succeeded, with encouragement at the highest level. We all know of Abraham Lincolns enthusiasm for the stage, and how it turned out; it seems that George Washington was an addict as well.

Hay reprints interminable and pointless accounts of the hucksterism of Phineas T. Barnum, who really does seem to have been the most flamboyantly boring showman in history. Moving on, we meet P.T.'s nephew, Herbert Barnum Seeley, who tried to make himself notorious by sponsoring scenes of debauchery, and not just on stage. He once threw a bachelor party with Little Egypt, the hoochy-koochy dancer, providing the cabaret.

There is a chapter on critics, reprinting famously rude reviews. They seem terribly mild; we could all do much nastier than that. A lot of them are just laborious. George Jean Nathan, revered for being not only smart but clever, could sink to this: I always said that I'd like John! Barrymores acting till the cows came home. Well, ladies and gentlemen, last night the cows came home. Faced with a play called Tonight Or Never, Nathan came up with: "Very well, then: I say, Never." Its not just on the Broadway stage that a reputation for wit can be lightly won. Really wonderful, though, is somebody's response - I always thought it was Dorothy Parker, but Hay leaves it unattributed - to I Am A Camera: No Leica. But he errs in describing the piece itself as a bad play; it would rank as a minor classic if Cabaret hadn't come along and disinherited it. Only Pygmalion has been able to beat the odds and lead a double life as play and musical.

But I am digressing. Anecdotage is contagious.

I am sure Hay misattributes the career-destroying review perpetrated on an actor called Guido Nadzo: "Guido Nadzo nadzo guido." He says it was Brooks Atkinson, long of The New York Times, who would have been too sober to think of it and too kind to use it. (Actually its not that great. How are you supposed to pronounce it?) All authorities agree in crediting it to George S. Kaufman, critic, playwright, director, oracle and general embodiment of the inter-war years when glamor was glamor, Broadway was the theatre, and most of this book was born. Everyone was witty and wore a tuxedo or a backless gown. Dont argue; I've seen the movies.

My favorite criticism here comes from a non-professional - critic, I mean - Noel Coward. I cannot imagine how I have managed to live until now ignorant of his response to The Seagull: I hate plays that have a stuffed bird sitting on the bookcase screaming 'Im the title, Im the title, I'm the title!' I am grateful too for Kaufmans question to a drapery salesman ("Do you have any good second-act curtains?"), and his terrified instruction to a chauffeur who was speeding toward George Washington Bridge (Dont cross that bridge till we come to it). On the same page, "a now forgotten playwright" called Edward Peple tells a universal story that begins, "It may sound incredulous." It doesn't. But I was.

Then I loved the Drama Bookshop's list of strange titles requested by customers. I Cant Bear You When The Waters Running loses narrowly to She Stops The Concorde. Canada, by Shaw, is another good one, and should definitely be revived by Christopher Newton. There is a treasurable quote from Louis Jouvet: The one thing constant in a changing world is the avant-garde.

I like anecdotes that have punch-lines. I'd be perfectly content if they had nothing but punch-lines.

Now, if you want a good anecdote, listen to George Burns on Al Jolson: "After his first few movies, Jolie wasn't a big draw, and gradually his career seemed to be over. Id see him sitting in a corner all by himself at the Hillcrest Country Club and Id go over to talk to him. Jolie was always very nice to me. In those days there was a law against bringing sturgeon into California, because it was only caught off the East Coast and it was so expensive to keep it refrigerated while shipping it west that it was usually spoiled by the time it got there. But Jolson had it shipped into California and kept his own supply in the Hillcrest kitchen. Then he'd sit alone in his corner eating it. I'd go and sit with him and compliment him, and every time I did hed offer me a piece of his sturgeon. Believe me, I liked sturgeon almost as much as I liked Jolson."

Then came The Jolson Story: Larry Parks impersonation of Jolson was so good that Jolson actually stole the picture without even being in it. Everybody wanted to sign him to do something. This was the greatest comeback in show business history. So one day I walked into Hillcrest and saw him sitting in the corner with his packet of sturgeon. I went over and said, 'That's the best picture I ever saw, and waited for my sturgeon. Nothing. So I tried again, That's the best soundtrack album I ever heard.' Finally, Jolie looked at me and said, Forget it, Natty. Im hot again. Get your own sturgeon.' The story is good enough in its own right, but if you imagine him speaking it, it's hilarious. In his 90s, Burns has entered into a state of comic grace. That wizened, apparently uninflected voice, with the snap- pause at the end of each line as the cigar re-enters the mouth, doesnt so much dare you to laugh as forbid you not to. It isnt a matter of bullying. It's a modest awareness of perfection. The timing isn't especially fast or slow. Its unhurried. The book capitalizes on this. Its set out as a routine. There are self-conscious references ("Thank you. You've been a wonderful reader"), but more important is the rhythm. How much of the credit for this should go to Burns's co-author, David Fisher, cannot be known, but I imagine its considerable. As Burns himself reminds us, once he and his colleagues got out of vaudeville and into radio, they depended on writers who played to their strengths.

This book is about those colleagues. Jolson, George Jessel, Eddie Cantor, Milton Berle, Jimmy Durante, Groucho and Harpo Marx and, above all, Jack Benny are the recurring figures in the tapestry. We hear of Jolsons egomania, Jessel's erotomania and Cantor's sheer mania, each motif recurring like a well-loved catch-phrase in a comedy series. There is little directly about Burns himself (stray bits of George-and-Gracie have to be cherished as they fly past) but then, This book is a little different than anything Ive ever done before. And believe me, at my age its difficult to find anything I haven't done before. And even harder to do it.

It's an important book. As Burns says, his group invented much of show business, and he tells us how they did it and where. "In retrospect, small-time vaudeville was fun - but as Groucho might say, everyplace else it was very tough." This book is a definitive portait of a theatrical form, one so voracious and with so many outlets that anybody could get work, but only the best could get anywhere else.

I think, Burns says, "I loved being in vaudeville more than any other part of my career." Later on, he says exactly the same about radio, movies and television (but he probably means it about vaudeville). The references are so widely spaced, though, that it takes you time to realize it. The running gag creeps up on you. That's his technique. Slow Burns.

In a book full of funny people being funny, the best line comes from a civilian. A coat-check girl refuses a handsome tip from Burns' closest friend. Please, Mr. Benny she says wistfully, "let me keep my illusions." Robert Cushman is Toronto writer.
