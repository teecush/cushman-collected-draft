THE HOBBIT

Young People's Theatre, Toronto

TORONTO - Hobbits seem to have changed their habits. They have become far less bourgeois in their pleasures. That, anyway, is the impression given by the dramatization of The Hobbit at Young People's Theatre. It's hard to imagine Herbie Barnes' Bilbo Baggins, the only hobbit we meet, smoking a pipe or quaffing beer, which seem to be the main recreations envisaged for them by their creator, the late (and culturally belated) J. R. R. Tolkien. This is a health- conscious, socially and environmentally responsible, YPT-stamped Hobbit.

None of this is meant as a criticism of Barnes' splendid performance, or of the equally fine production of which it is the centre. Kim Selody's adaptation originated at the Manitoba Theatre for Young People with the same leading actor.

It is directed now by Jim Warren on what struck me, perhaps through association of ideas, as a rabbit-warren of a set. Designed by Glenn Davidson, it's many-creviced and multi-levelled, fitting both the hobbit's underground home and the inhospitable mountains through which he and his dwarf companions travel in quest of the dragon's treasure. Its central feature is a cave-like opening that can expand and contract, and that gives on to a sky-cloth (superbly lit by Bonnie Beecher) that can also function as a screen.

Here, a giant spider's web takes shape to entrap the travellers, with menacing arachnid legs at the corner of the picture. Here, parts of the story that cannot be done live -- the deliverance of hobbit and dwarves by a troop of eagles, their further escape concealed in a long line of bobbing wine casks -- are depicted in shadow-play; and so smooth are the transitions that we relish rather than resent the break in convention.

The dragon is projected across the whole stage; what looks like the whorl of a tree-trunk turns out to be his eye. Sound-wise, Cathy Nosaty's Celtic-flavoured tunes do Tolkien's clumsy attempts at verse a considerable favour.

I'm not sure what I think about Tolkien. I distrust the cosy Englishness of his vision, in which an idealized village pub becomes the centre of the universe. I resent the ease with which his creations -- admittedly against his will -- were adopted by the soppy hippie ethos of the '60s. I find his excursions into lower- class dialect painfully patronizing, and his humour, at best, awkwardly jolly. (When a character is meant to be plebeian and funny and evil, then it's a groaning matter.) I dislike the implicit glorification of feudalism.

But there is something admirably ambitious in his conversion of linguistic study into a hugely detailed personal mythology (hobbits were his hobby), and while his black-and-white morality can be irritating, it can also be bracing. You know which side you want to win and this, coupled with his storytelling gifts, which withstand any number of digressions, makes him a compulsive read. I read The Lord of the Rings trilogy at a comparatively advanced age and while confined to a hospital bed (making me an easy mark) and I found its scope irresistible, but The Hobbit makes an attractive, and certainly less pretentious, prologue.

The story, and an engaging delight in theatrical inventiveness, are what carry things at YPT. Inevitably, it's episodic -- the book is small-scale picaresque to begin with -- and things, as tends to happen in dramatizations of novels, get very bunched-up toward the end.

But most of the episodes work, and Bilbo -- adopting a mantra of "eggs, bacon, toast" to remind himself of more comfortable days -- gives the show a sufficient through-line as he battles his way through from timidity to heroism through a resourcefulness he never knew he had. Though both the actor and the character are grown-up, his quality is boyish; he's the Jim Hawkins of Middle Earth.

The scene that strikes strongest -- the most famous in the book and the one to which the production sensibly devotes most time -- is his encounter with Gollum, the omnivorous amphibian creature from whom he steals the magic ring that grants invisibility; Greg Kramer communicates a fine relish in the character's warped mind and evil syntax. ("What has it got in its pocketses, preciousss?") He also doubles impressively as the voice of the dragon, whose intentions seem basically similar. Indeed every creature the companions meet -- goblin, troll, dragon, spider -- seems intent on eating them; this is an orally-fixated universe.

Alon Nashman, bearded like a choral figure from the famous Tyrone Guthrie production of Oedipus, is impressive as Thorin Oakenshield, whose calamitous surrender to greed is the aspect of the story that suffers most from compression. Talking of compression, five actors between them play his dozen attendant dwarves; and the manner in which they each rush behind the scenes to emerge immediately and be greeted by a different name is brazenly enjoyable.

Lorne Cardinal's sonorous Gandalf is a less wizened wizard than I had expected, and less schoolmasterly. He ends the play by observing, in a significant-sounding voice, that there is more than Bilbo's magic ring than has yet been revealed, so maybe the YPT is contemplating (as Tolkien himself must already have been) a triple- decker sequel. I wouldn't mind; this show is their best in years.
