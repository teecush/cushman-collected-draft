THEATRE REVIEW

STUDIO THEATRE, NIAGARA-ON-THE-LAKE

Iloved Carole Frechettes The Small Room at the Top of the Stairs when it was at the Tarragon last season. I therefore approached the Shaw Festivals revival of an earlier Frechette play, Helen's Necklace, hoping I would like it more than when it, too, was produced at Tarragon a few years ago. It wasn't to be; if theres any light, I still havent seen it. The Niagara production is in fact inferior to the Toronto one, but the main problem is still the play. The Small Room has a story. Helen's Necklace has only an idea.

Helen has lost her necklace. It has no great monetary but much sentimental value. She wanders all over town in a taxi looking for it -- town" being in a far-off, war-battered country whose inhabitants have suffered losses far more serious than her own. This is something she comes to learn over the course of the play, though it's surprising she should need the lesson. Her most wrenching encounter is with a woman looking for a lost child whom she insists is a neighbours. In the course of the conversation the woman is brought to acknowledge that the child is her own, and dead.

The play belongs to what seems to be a subgenre of French-Canadian playwriting: one in which Westerners have their consciousness raised and their consciences stirred by visiting unspecified places in the Middle East and being lectured about them. Wajdi Mouawads Scorched is a more elaborate essay on the same theme. There are obvious differences -- Mouawad has the greater territorial ambition, Frechette the finer fingertips -- but they both present their characters as symbols before, or rather without, establishing them as people.

Frechette's characters here seem unnervingly aware of their place in their playwright's pattern. Helens second most crucial encounter is with a man on a building site who tells her exactly what he thinks she and her necklace stand for. Of his own and his peoples situation he cries out, "We cannot go on living like this." Helen adopts this mantra as her own, repeating it during the rest of the play.

The trouble with this is that we have almost no idea of how she's been living previously. (A brief flashback to a broken relationship is no help, apart from the fact that it violates the conventions of the rest of the play.) Reencountering the play again reminded me of how much The Small Room gained from having the Bluebeard story as a point of departure. It seems to have freed Frechette's imagination, and spurred her inventiveness, in a way unmatched by her own predetermined fable. Shes in good company; Shakespeare worked best with borrowed plot lines. So did the Greeks.

MichelineChevriersproduction departs from the Tarragon staging, and also from all previous plays in the Shaw studio, by opting for a conventional one-end-of-the-room staging. This means that a play constructed entirely of duologues (all the people Helen meets are played by the same actor, regardless of gender) gets lost in a lot of unused space, on a set (by Judith Bowden) that tries vainly to be evocative.

Tara Rosling is appealing as Helen, but I preferred Susan Coyne's more businesslike approach at the Tarragon; she suggested the possibility of an offstage professional life, whereas Rosling seems more like sensitivity waiting to happen. Sanjay Talwar repeats his multiple performance from before, and is lost, angry and perplexed as required. The perplexity comes in his recurring role as the taxi driver, continually setting the heroine down and picking her up again, and generally earning what one hopes was a handsome tip.

At its Toronto premiere, the play's counterpointing of a minor private grief with huge public ones felt meretricious. Nine years later, measured against events in Syria and elsewhere, it seems closer to offensive.
