THE MALE INTELLECT: AN OXYMORON?

New Yorker Theatre

It seems a long time ago now, but The Male Intellect begins with something funny. Robert Dubac, author and sole performer, holds up a succession of self-help books on male-female relationships and recites their titles. He has arranged them so that each contradicts its predecessor -- no great feat, perhaps -- and he uses them to project a mounting sense of baffled incredulity.

His timing here is excellent. His timing throughout his ensuing 90-minute monologue is also excellent. His saving grace is that he is better than his material. His downfall is the material itself.

The show's full title is The Male Intellect -- An Oxymoron?, and your response to that may be the best index of whether you will find the piece itself hilarious, or intellectually challenging, or both.

Like too many items recently it is an unhappy compromise between comedy routine and full-fledged play. As a critic once said of Waiting for Godot, it doesn't have a plot but it does have a situation, and maybe even some suspense.

Dubac, in the tradition of comics from Jack Benny to Jerry Seinfeld, plays a character who may or not be himself but who has the same name. This particular Bobby has been deserted by his girl- friend for refusing to allow her cat in bed with them, but she has promised, or threatened, to call exactly two weeks later.

We are there at the moment of countdown, he can't leave until the phone rings and neither can we. I read somewhere that audiences in the halcyon days of movie-going, when they grew tired of waiting for the heroine to give in to the inevitable final clinch, would set up a ritual cry of "say yes, so we can all go home." In the same way, I wanted Bobby's girl to call early and maybe give him and us something else to think about.

As it is, his mind is naturally running on the gulf that seems to separate men and women. He is, in the literal sense, quite graphic about this, dividing the stage into two sections corresponding to the male and female halves of the human brain. (I don't think I want to hear about these any more.)

The male side, which he presumably knows most about, is very cluttered, crammed with sports gear and with filing-cabinets that turn out to be full of booze. This is in a show that ends by exhorting us to escape from gender stereotypes, and that preaches the sensible if uncontroversial doctrine that the sexes are complementary but different. The predictability of it all undermines even the good moments.

Bobby tells us that women dance because they enjoy it, men dance in order to get sex.

Well, that sounds accurate; I laughed. Then I thought about men I have known who do like dancing, and women who regard it as a tiresome social ritual. Maybe there's a difference between types -- who can certainly be the stuff of effective comedy, though they need a good story to activate them -- and stereotypes, who just hang around being dead. Dubac's format doesn't allow him to make a few good sexist jokes and pass on to other subjects; he has to hang in there.

Once again I appreciate the merits and the versatility of Seinfeld, both as a solo observer and as the organizer of 30-minute comedies of manners. Also, he wasn't concerned to make us feel fuzzily good about ourselves or other people -- which may be why, in this decadent age, people are turning against him.

Dubac comes from the same school as his fellow-American Rob Becker, whose extremely similar Defending the Caveman played here earlier this year; with a true comedian's long face and questioning eyes, Dubac has twice Becker's style but half his jokes. (Two instead of four.)

He is also a good mimic. He says that he has learned about women only from other men (why?) and impersonates a number of his old and young mentors and buddies to prove it. They come with an expertly- done variety of accents and grimaces, but all are essentially preaching the same insensitive doctrine, especially the one who talks about senstitivity.

You get the impression that Dubac is too intelligent for the company he keeps, even if the company is his own.

- The Male Intellect: An Oxymoron? Runs to Nov. 21 at the New Yorker Theatre, 651 Yonge St. Call 872-1111.
