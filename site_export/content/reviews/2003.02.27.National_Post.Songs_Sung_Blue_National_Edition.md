STORMY WEATHER: THE MUSIC OF HAROLD ARLEN

Opening Night, CBC-TV

Harold Arlen, one of the giants of 20th-century American music and possibly the most gifted popular composer of them all, spent the last 15 years of his life in a state of deep depression, rarely venturing outside his own apartment. That is the premise of Stormy Weather: The Music of Harold Arlen, a celebration of his work that airs tonight in CBC's Opening Night slot -- though "celebration" may not, in these circumstances, be quite the right word.

Arlen's late-life reclusiveness (he died in 1986) is a historical fact. It may have had something to do with the fall from supremacy of his kind of song, but its immediate cause was the death of his wife, Anya, following a long period of mental illness.

Stormy Weather has Arlen (played by Paul Soles, who looks and sounds exactly right) wandering around his apartment (which also looks exactly right, at least to begin with). He's attended by a nurse, who wants him to watch a TV special about his work (presumably a repeat, since he's in it himself, and it's in black and white; we see bits of it, and they look mighty interesting).

Most of his attention, though, is devoted to the composition of a letter to the dead Anya: part reminiscence, part contrition. Affected by this double dose of nostalgia, he starts wandering around, opening doors. Gazing at the pills in his bathroom cabinet, he mutters, "they're turning me into a drug addict," and suddenly there materializes one of the opium-themed numbers he wrote for the Cotton Club in the '30s, Kickin' the Gong Around, done in a very decent approximation of its original Cab Calloway style.

After that, the visions get wilder. Every time Arlen enters a room, one of his hits gets up and hits him back. I've Got the World on a String becomes a number about puppets. It's the singer (Hawksley Workman) who's strung up, which seems to indicate a rather careless attitude to the syntax of the lyric. It's Only a Paper Moon becomes tuneless, toneless and generally repulsive. And those are just the happy songs.

Now, it's quite conceivable that Arlen, in his devastated condition, would have looked back at his early successes and found their light-hearted sentiments bitterly ironic. But it's unlikely that that's all he would have felt, and anyway, it hardly does justice to the songs themselves.

Arlen -- the composer of Stormy Weather, Blues in the Night and I Gotta Right to Sing the Blues -- fought all his productive life against being labelled a blues composer. His tunes were a unique combination of earthy and elegant. He wrote ballads as beautiful as Rodgers', rhythm numbers as effervescent as Gershwin's, and exhibited a sense of form that was probably the most adventurous of any.

Some of his songs' distinction came of course from his lyricists. He wrote with the best: Johnny Mercer, E. Y. Harburg, Ira Gershwin, even Truman Capote. Their contribution, though, goes unmentioned in this program, perhaps because it would complicate the sentimental view of Arlen's songs as the mirror of his soul. The omission probably explains the belief of one of The Globe and Mail's TV writers that after Anya's death, Arlen "never wrote another word." The truth is, he had written very few while she was alive.

It's curious, too, that Arlen, in a show presumably set in the 1970s, should keep imagining his songs being performed in the styles and accents of 2003. No wonder he was depressed.

At one point he wanders into a barroom and has to endure Rufus Wainwright, earlier the assassin of Paper Moon, wreaking even worse havoc on the more delicate I Wonder What Became of Me. And if you really want irony, the show has something worthy of Ken Russell: Anya's incarceration in a mental hospital inspires staff and patients to a ghoulish rendition of Ac-Cent-Tchu-Ate the Postive. At moments like these, you wonder if the performers and producers genuinely despise the material or if, even more alarmingly, they think they're doing it a favour.

A few of the pop people do have some idea. Debbie Harry looks as if she might carry off Stormy Weather itself, if she wasn't done in by the dissonant orchestration. Sandra Bernhard delivers a Come Rain or Come Shine that makes up in power what it lacks in pitch.

And there even are a couple of people who have spent their lives singing this stuff. Ranee Lee, apart from bustling about as the nurse, gets to sing The Man That Got Away. A much older jazz singer, Little Jimmy Scott, does Over the Rainbow, on which, to be honest, he inflicts melodic distortions that are every bit as extreme as those perpetrated by the younger folks. The difference is that his have some musical logic to them, and that his bizarre, haunted sensibility bears some relation to the song's.

It's clever, too, to have this, of all legend-encrusted numbers, sung by a man; doubly so when the man is one who sounds like a woman. Scott also sings, poignantly, I Had a Love Once, probably Arlen's last song, and one for which he did write the words. Best of all, Soles lip-synchs to Arlen's own recording (he was a fine singer) of his own favourite among his songs, Last Night When We Were Young.

It's always a sign of desperation when a show resorts to Busby Berkeley aerial shots of girls in geometric patterns in order to suggest Hollywood in the '30s, and the example here is no exception. In general, though, the production is technically fine. It blends its disparate elements very smoothly. But you keep wishing Arlen would heed medical advice and just settle down and watch that primitive TV tribute, so that that we could watch it with him, uninterrupted. It may have airbrushed his biography, but you can bet it had a more authoritative handle on his work than the show surrounding it does.

As background to the closing credits, the producers treat us to the old Jack Teagarden record of I Gotta Right to Sing the Blues, and it comes as something between a joke and a blessing. It's late but here, finally, is what it's about.
