As befits a show about a photographer, Studies in Motion looks very good. Its subject is the life and work of Eadweard Muybridge, who in the late 19th century used multiple cameras, placed strategically along a route, to capture the movement of human and other bodies. You might say he invented the motion picture, and when the actual movies came along, he was their forebear.

Kim Collier, who this week was awarded the Siminovitch Prize for direction, begins her production with an extended, invigorating demonstration sequence. An ensemble of 11 actors run, walk, skip, jump and tumble across the stage in dizzying, seemingly endless succession. It's hypnotic because of the skill and endurance involved; also because it's distanced. This turns out be typical; we peer at much of the show through a scrim, while watching the actors disport themselves on a set laid out like a grid. Crystal Pite is the choreographer and Robert Gardiner the set, lighting and video designer. It's hard to be surprised any more by the things projections can do, but it's still possible to be impressed, and the work of this team is exceptionally impressive.

The production comes from the Electric Company Theatre of Vancouver, which originated it in 2006 and revived it in 2009. The present touring incarnation, which I saw at a preview in Edmonton, is headed to Canadian Stage in Toronto. As performance art it's fine, if inevitably monotonous; how many times do you want to watch the same performers propelling themselves across the same area? It would help of course if we knew and cared about the people concerned, but that's where Studies in Motion falls down. It isn't just a show, it's a play; and a badly written one.

Kevin Kerr's script is subtitled The Hauntings of Eadweard Muybridge, though apart from presenting some of its events in the wrong order, it's less imaginative than that might suggest. We first see the protagonist falling foul of Britain's Royal Society, whose members patronizingly inform him that they "keep abreast" of new scientific developments, and slap themselves on their chests to emphasize the point. (That's about as witty as the evening gets.) He finds a home for his research at the University of Pennsylvania and, though he runs up against the regulation amount of bureaucratic interference, he does manage to carry much of his

project through. In his dealings with his volunteer subjects he can amusingly anticipate some of the frustrations of later pioneers of spectacle, especially in the perennial problems associated with getting actors to act natural. Or in this case to walk natural. Otherwise all we learn about him is that he's a fanatical martinet with a beard, and that his first name, despite its alarming spelling, is pronounced just like that of any other Edward.

As for his private life, we see most of that in flashback, though it's a confusingly long time before the chronology becomes clear. Muybridge was married, and his devotion to his work, plus your standard genius' indifference to other people's feelings, led him to neglect his wife. So she took up with a smooth-talking cad introduced as "the theatre critic of the Post" (I give you my word), a position apparently so remunerative that its occupant could afford to pay an even lowlier hack to do the actual reviewing part for him. Carnage ensued; and when Muybridge describes the details (at his trial, and I'm not giving any more away than can be deduced from the program notes) he seems mostly concerned with the different types of physical movement that were involved. Either we're asked to believe that this gave him the inspiration for his life's work or, more likely, it's a painfully arch attempt to tie the play's strands together. Back in the present tense, Muybridge and his routine are periodically disturbed by messages about a boy at an orphanage. These aren't nearly as suspenseful as seems to have been intended -- in fact, we forget about them -- but they do lead to a final meeting that's much the best written scene in the play; it has some delicacy to it, and allows the protagonist to appear as something other than a tyrannical nonentity.

Otherwise it's scissors and paste, picturesquely presented. Muybridge's preference for photographing nude bodies, of both sexes, brings down the threat of censorship, which is elaborately built up and then abruptly and inexplicably dropped. (Whatever happened, happens in the intermission.) A Muybridge assistant, who obviously adores him, spends most of the play resisting the idea of posing naked herself, but finally agrees to do it. A friend who has no such inhibitions has an amusing scene in which she has to keep donning and doffing her robe due to a prolonged technical hitch. Andrew Wheeler's acting in the title role consists of deploying a deep voice to match his facial hair. The rest of the cast do all that is required of them, and are probably capable of more; when somebody, in a scene set in a theatre, says "let's pretend we're real actors," it sounds like a very good idea. For all its visual sheen, the production is strangely sullen. And the play belies its title; it's essentially static .
