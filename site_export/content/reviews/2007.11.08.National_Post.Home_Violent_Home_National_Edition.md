THEATRE REVIEW

LULLABY

Simone Interiors,

1690 Queen St. West

---

At the back of an interior design story in gentrified Parkdale, a new company called Dark Horse Theatre has staged a play about the gentrification of Parkdale. That, at least, is the hook. Looch, an aspiring young businessman, and his girlfriend Leandra buy a house, renovate it, and let out the basement. Their prospective tenant is a middle-aged guy named Kenny who claims to be an old inhabitant of the neighbourhood. He looks and sounds down and out but is able to offer $1,500, cash, in advance. You'd think he'd won the lottery.

As a matter of fact, he has won the lottery; at least so he says, and Thelon Oening's Lullaby never explicitly contradicts him. It does tell us that Kenny has done time, probably more than once. These two factors put together are enough to make him an atypical case on which to build a play about the old, local order being forced to give way to the new. (Well, maybe it isn't about that, but that's what it's been touted as being about.) In much the same way, our yuppie couple seem exception-ally feckless as home-owners; enough goes wrong with the place, quite apart from what Kenny gets up or down to, for us to wonder if they ever bothered to have it inspected. But then they do seem to be trusting a lot in Looch's rich dad to see them through; they need him, as both Looch and Leandra are having job problems. These, however, are kept shadowy.

The construction of Lullaby is about as rickety as that of the house it depicts. This matters, though, surprisingly little. Oening, a new writer, has a gift for pungent and free-flowing dialogue, and Kenny is a creation. He isn't a totally original creation; he's one of those cuckoo-in-the-nest home-breakers (as opposed to housebreakers) who abounds in modern drama and whose archetype is the tramp in Pinter's The Caretaker. David Ferry is a master at this kind of role; he played another wheedling, threatening, cash- flashing intruder in Daniel Karasik's In Full Light, at Summerworks. There are differences, though: Kenny is a yarn-spinning melancholic with anger simmering; Ferry is very powerful when it boils over. He plays deafening music, he keeps intruding on his landlords' living- quarters, drinks and does drugs. He also takes what seems to be a genuine -- if intrusive -- interest in

the lives of his hosts, especially Leandra.

She is going through professional and emotional withdrawal; and the play is very persuasive at charting her attraction-repulsion towards Kenny: She's afraid of him; she comes to depend on him (and his store of champagne and dope and painkillers); and she feels protective towards him. "You must stop trying to buy people," she tells him, and it's certain that his money isn't making him happy. Amy Rutherford's Leandra is her best performance to date. She sounds, for the first time, as good as she looks, and she achieves a remarkable vocal and emotional fullness in a confined space. Dusan Dukic's Looch, by contrast, is excessively low-keyed, but he does have the sketchiest, most schematic role; he grows as hostile towards Kenny as Leandra is indulgent, and ends up seeing him as a rival, though, of course, he can't bring himself to acknowledge it. This is the second play in two days to feature a fight between its two male characters, and this one is vicious.

Jennifer H. Capraru, the director, clearly lays out the play's emotional geography but runs into problems, probably not of her own making, with the physical. A site-specific production raises high expectations of scenic realism; otherwise, why not set the play on a conventional stage? Here we have a living-room that changes without warning into a bedroom, and a basement that seems to be on the same level as the rest of the house. When you stop believing in a play's spatial dynamic, you start having problems with its character dynamics as well. This aside, the design is excellent; as might be expected, since it's by Tamara Marie Kucheran, who's also responsible for the Passe Muraille revival of The Drawer Boy and, last year at Stratford, for the gorgeous Fanny Kemble. The play's title is a poser; it doesn't acquire any relevance until the play is almost over, and it seems arbitrary and sentimental when it does. But it isn't quite the end; and when Ferry's Kenny shuffles off into the night, leaving chaos behind him, he moves you not as a figure on a sociologist's graph, but as a complex, inexplicable human being. - Performances run through Nov. 18.
