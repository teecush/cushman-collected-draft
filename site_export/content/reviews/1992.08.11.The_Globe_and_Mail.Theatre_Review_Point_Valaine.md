THEATRE REVIEW POINT VALAINE

Tuesday, August 11, 1992

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont. -- WRITTEN BY NOEL COWARD Directed by Christopher Newton Starring Fiona Reid, David Schurmann and Simon Bradbury At the Court House Theatre Rating: Point Valaine's secret weapon is its location, and director Christopher Newton spendidly exploits it Chewing on Coward's Point BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont. MORTIMER Quinn, "a crabbed, embittered old writer" (his description) vacationing at an outpost-of-the-Empire hotel in Trinidad, consents to be interviewed by a local journalist. He deals with the situation by asking her all the questions. She has, to nobody's surprise, written a play. Comedy or drama, he inquires? She says, in effect, that it's drama but with a few laughs.

This is Noel Coward winking at himself. Point Valaine, the 1935 piece that accommodates this brief encounter, is drama with a few laughs; Coward could hardly have refused to supply them, nor would he have wanted to. "We get some pretty bad gnats in Horsham," says one resident, showing proper respect for the unfailing comic potential of minor English place-names. Mortimer Quinn himself is very Coward (although apparently modelled on Somerset Maugham): comic not so much in what he says as in the world-weary shadow he casts. His presence suggests what he himself, when finally pushed to it, explicitly states: that nothing is as tragic as we would like to think it, and that that is the bitterest thing of all.

"I must see about engaging a new headwaiter," says Linda Valaine, hotelier and protagonist, at the end of the play: a tour-de-force of stoicism since the previous incumbent, who was her lover, has just been eaten by sharks. A moody and muscular Russian he was, name of Stefan: her sexual outlet for seven years, driven furious when she finds romance with a clean young English flier. This boy has Mortimer to put his battered ideals in perspective. Stefan, a permanent exile among temporary expatriates, is less fortunate.

As University of Toronto drama professor Ronald Bryden remarks in a program note, the balance should have been more explicitly colonial and Stefan should have been black. In other respects, though, Coward is remarkably explicit. Linda is a capable and sympathetic woman shipwrecked by her own needs; make her more helplessly exotic and you are in Tennessee Williams country, more helplessly domestic and you get Terence Rattigan. A couple of gay young men, Mortimer's anti-chorus, are kept under only the thinnest of wraps.

However genuine the emotions may be, they are still expressed in the primly hysterical language - with little as the ultimate pejorative - that 1930s playwrights in general, and Coward in particular, thought proper for passion. Even the master's comedies can be undone by his own echoing speech-rhythms. But Point Valaine's secret weapon is its location, splendidly exploited in Christopher Newton's production. Down come the rains; and down, at every edge of the three-sided stage, come the tropical blinds. The veranda setting (designer, Yvonne Sauriol) is stereoscopically real, but distanced. Stefan's jealousy and Linda's despair are played out under gauze, and it filters out the mannerisms.

Fiona Reid's Linda may not need even this camouflage. She has recreated the role in depth: an angular Anglo with ease in her walk and sex in her nerve-endings. Stefan must have been a star role when created by Alfred Lunt, surprising casting for a vulnerable brute with only one big scene. Robert Persichini's Stefan is relegated in the Shaw Festival's rigid pecking order to supporting status, but imposes himself nonetheless; the worse he behaves, the more we sympathize. David Schurmann's Mortimer is amorphous but sufficient. Snob that I am, I thought some of the others half a class too low in the social scale, but Tony Van Bridge's brief appearance as a departing guest speaks splendidly for the old regime.

Point Valaine continues until Sept. 26.
