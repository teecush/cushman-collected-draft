The songs of Allan Sherman have come to town in a show called Hello Muddah, Hello Fadduh, and the title at least must be familiar. It's been in the North American bloodstream since 1963, when a comedy record of that name made No. 2 in the U.S. charts.

The song was the plaintive letter home of a boy whose parents had abandoned him to the mercies of summer camp. Since it was set to a very familiar tune -- Ponchielli's Dance of the Hours -- it was thought of as a parody-lyric, though it would be hard to say exactly what it was a parody of. The original, after all, has no words.

Lyric and music, seemingly incongruous, were a perfect match, rising and falling together: a deadpan list of complaints taking up the main melodic strain, then turning to an anguished plea ("take me home") that subsided into anguished blubbering ("please don't make me stay/I've been here one whole day.") Child or parent, everybody could relate.

The record's appeal crossed the Atlantic. I was commencing as an English undergraduate at exactly this time, and my contemporaries used to adapt the words to our own situation; we parodied the parody. (You're lucky; I can't remember any of our lyrics.)

The composer-performer was Allan Sherman. This was a name unknown to us, though as an inveterate haunter of record stores I soon became more familiar with it. He seemed to have three LPs to his name: all of them, I assumed, spin-offs from that initial hit. Apparently, though, I had this backwards. Hello Mudduh was a track from his third album, My Son the Nut.

Sherman had had a productive but checkered career as a deviser and producer of TV game and talk shows, but the early '60s found him out of work. He had been breaking his friends up with new words to popular songs, but when a record company finally took him up on the idea, it decided that life would be a lot cheaper if he were to attach himself to melodies that were in the public domain. So his first album was called My Son the Folk Singer. In between, a little prematurely, there had been My Son the Celebrity.

Investigation of the Sherman albums revealed further riches. Some of the songs I preferred to Hello Mudduh, partly because the executives occasionally relented and let him use better tunes. (Dance of the Hours really is an atrociously glutinous piece of classical lollipoppery.) I was especially fond of Sherman's adaptation of You Came a Long Way from St. Louis, which he turned into a primer on the French Revolution titled You Went the Wrong Way, Old King Louis. Actually this one has two great tunes, because its introductory verse appropriates the Marseillaise. Try singing along with this:

Louis the 16th was the King of France

In 1789.

He was worse than Louis the 15th.

He was worse than Louis the 14th.

He was worse than Louis the 13th.

He was the worst

Since Louis the First.

It may not be quite historically accurate (the assessment, I mean; the chronology is impeccable) but it's certainly funny. And notice how the rhyme snaps shut on the last line.

That song isn't in the show, but another of my exotic favourites is: a joyously literal interpretation of the Mexican Hat Dance ("but the Mexicans dance on your hat"), which manages to specify a different type of headgear in each stanza: like Irving Berlin, in You Can't Get a Man with a Gun, ticking off every known variety of firearm.

For the most part though, the show Hello Muddah, concentrates on domestic situations like the one depicted in its title song: "the kind of important moments in people's lives," according to Douglas Bernstein "that everybody can identify with."

Bernstein, who talked to me this week from his home in New York, is one of the two creators of the musical; the other is Rob Krausz, who also directs. It was done off-Broadway in 1992, and revived there last year. Krausz rediscovered the Sherman albums when his mother told him he had to clean out his old bedroom (a very Shermanesque situation) and shared his enthusiasm with Bernstein, whom he'd known since high school. The question then became: Did they try and find someone to impersonate Sherman. Or, in Bernstein's words, "do we have a big man with a crew cut and dark glasses, and fat, sitting in the middle of the stage, or do we tell a story?"

They went with the story: The saga of Barry Bockman and Sarah Jackman (whom Sherman once serenaded to the tune of Frere Jacques) who progress from birth through courtship, marriage, parenthood -- not all the way to death, but at least as far as Florida.

It's a classic Jewish-American trajectory, and certainly when Sherman first made his mark he was seen as an ethnic comedian: Alan King set to music. The titles of those albums, with their implicit obeisance to the classic Jewish mother, are evidence of that. They weren't being marketed to just a Jewish audience, though. This, as Bernstein says, was a time "when being Jewish was cool": when the hip comics were Mort Sahl and Lenny Bruce and (much the closest to Sherman, and not only for reasons of euphony) Shelley Berman.

Of course, Jews had always constituted a huge proportion of American comedians but until now those who became nationally famous had kept quiet about it.

The situation now is different again: Jerry Seinfeld's Jewishness is unmistakable -- far more than that of, say, Danny Kaye's -- but he has never, as far as I know, made any explicit comic use of it.

Sherman was, in his way, a pioneer: even a comer-out. He was one of those who found that Jewish suburban comedy could retain its local colouring, even emphasize it in a modest way, and still be understood. The family situations, after all, are universal. Before making its New York debut, Hello Muddah tried out in Phoenix and Kansas City, towns where, as Bernstein, notes, "there is not a large Jewish population." There were no problems.

Sherman's own moment was rather brief. He died in 1973, of problems connected with being overweight; he had also divorced, and his last years had apparently not been happy. Fashion has passed him by; his parodies assumed a situation in which Broadway and Tin Pan Alley were the musical lingua franca; and his whole gentle sensibility was pre-rock.

But if he himself was forgotten, Hello Muddah, the song, stayed around. Like all Sherman's best songs, it's a work of craftsmanship. Like some other comedy writers (Stan Freberg, Mel Brooks) he turned out to be a natural-born lyricist. Most musical parodies repeat a single joke; Sherman's tell stories.
