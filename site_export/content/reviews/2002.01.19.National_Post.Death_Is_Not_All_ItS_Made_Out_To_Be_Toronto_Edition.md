Until Feb. 3. (416) 872-1212

MORTALITY

The Theatre Centre

- - -

I am beginning to wonder whether death is such a great dramatic subject after all: death in the abstract, I mean.

Hamlet, as eloquent on the subject as any character is ever likely to be, ended up talking only about the fear of death, confessing that the thing itself is something that nobody ever comes back to talk about. He manages to say this despite having spent time with a loquacious ghost bursting with news of the afterlife; somehow the prince's skepticism rings truer than all his deceased father's certainties.

So you can write about the fear of death, about the approach of death, even about watching the experience of dying, but never about the experience itself.

This may account for some of the odder aspects of Mortality, a new production from Ross Manson's company Volcano, which had great success a couple of years ago with Building Jerusalem. That, however unconventionally structured, was a play; Mortality is a revue with a cast of two actors, male, and two dancers, female. Manson has commissioned prose vignettes on the subject of death from three prominent Canadian writers and has contributed linking material of his own.

He starts with one of the actors, Martin Julien, coming on stage and trying bumptiously to make himself the centre of attention. (This is not a personal slur. He is merely performing as instructed. At least I hope he is.) He is then followed on and edged off by one of the dancers, who complains about having to work with such uncongenial colleagues.

It is unclear what these displays of professional jealousy have to do with the topic at hand (survival tactics, perhaps) but they seem like nervous ingratiation.

So does a later sequence in which Prospero's "our revels now are ended" speech from The Tempest is windily burlesqued, or maybe just delivered very badly. It comes to us in a film-clip, and the program contains a credit -- "screenplay: W. Shakespeare" -- whose defensive flippancy is all too revealing. The mini-movie seems to be a spoof of an old Hollywood newsreel. Almost insultingly scrappy, it is not a good advertisement for the concept of mixed media.

Back on stage, the performers, when they get down to it, don't seem to rub along too badly. The actors also dance, the dancers also act: all very respectably. Kate Alton is a helpful and adaptable choreographer, and in general it is the non-verbal sections that come off best. Julia Aplin, goggly-eyed, delivers a piece by Paul Quarrington about a child's falling off a jungle gym and sensing her own mortality for the first time. Her performance is intelligent, characterized by a kind of hyper-active primness, but the material seems heavily cute.

"You're saying, 'Get to the point,'" she keeps telling us; and yes, we are. The style is jokey without actually having any jokes. It is a relief when she stops talking and gives herself over, gracefully, to dance.

George Leach, mellifluous if monotonal, utters a monologue by Tomson Highway inspired, we are told, by the death of the writer's brother from AIDS. Gripping as long as it remains specific, or clinical, it proceeds to float away into impersonal lyricism. It only regains its force when the actor moves into wordless chanting.

A text by Carol Shields is spoken by Peggy Baker with, uniquely, some interaction from the other players. The dramatization is only partial, and only partially successful, but it includes some resonant observations that suggest both the attraction of the theme and its impossibility: "The moment of death occurs while we're still alive," and "Life marches up to the wall of that final darkness." It's when you get to the wall that the subject becomes intractable.

The most poetic thing in the evening is the sound of an old record -- Louis Armstrong playing and singing La vie en rose -- played on a wind-up gramophone that winds down. There is also very accomplished live music -- characterized by a saxophone as piercing as a flute -- written by John Gzowski and played by a trio with the composer at its head.

Martin Julien finally redeems himself with his rendition of a pungent poem by Stephen Dobyns, about Death going on strike and so rendering life meaningless, at least according to the rich and powerful who need it to grease their wheels.

The political angle seems somewhat parochial; Swift, in Gulliver's Travels, presented the prospect of eternal life far more persuasively as a curse for anyone. Death, as this show finally acknowledges, is something we can't live with or without. But for it to hold our attention in the theatre, we need to know something about the person dying.
