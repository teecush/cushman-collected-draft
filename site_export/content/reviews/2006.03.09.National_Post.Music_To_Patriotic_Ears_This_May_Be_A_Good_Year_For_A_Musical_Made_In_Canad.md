Are musicals the last bastions of cultural nationalism? Even in this age of global co-production, each show zealously, or jealously, proclaims its point of origin. The pop operas of the last century often seemed as rootless in their stories as in their musical styles, but they were still identified, by their creators and everyone else, as the British (and occasionally French) invasion.

The imminent Lord of the Rings has its world premiere at a Toronto theatre, under Canadian management, and its cast boasts half a dozen members in good standing of Canada's roving super ensemble, but it still comes to us from another country, and I dont mean Middle-earth. Its origins, creative and entrepreneurial, are British, and it debuts here, as has been freely acknowledged, largely because there was no suitable theatre available in London. We know that if its a success, a theatre in London will magically open up, as of course will one in New York, still regarded as the ultimate test. If it can make it here, it still has to make it there.

As for the native Canadian musical, there hasn't been one that the outside world would recognize since Anne of Green Gables (and none before that). But that may be about to change. In May, The Drowsy Chaperone opens on Broadway. This is a show that has already progressed from the status of nice overstretched joke in its first Toronto outing to that of thorough delight in its second. We now wait to see whether its American production, which has already gone down very well in Los Angeles, can take it up a notch without losing the original's naive crafty charm.

Chaperone is in the category of affectionate spoof, a genre that in the past has produced hits of the magnitude of The Boy Friend (British) and Dames at Sea (American). One thing that argues for the integrity of the new production is the retention, among an otherwise all-American cast, of the original narrator, the very droll Bob Martin, who set the shows tone and without whom it is difficult to imagine. It may be an augury that Chaperone owes its New York booking (at the Marquis) to the early demise of Andrew Lloyd Webbers The Woman in White; we are coming to rely on parodies and revivals for injections of the traditional musical-comedy delight that has otherwise vanished in a sea of humourless self-importance.

Which reminds me: The show is directed by Casey Nicholaw, who already has a huge Broadway success with Spamalot, the musical of Monty Python and the Holy Grail. This is coming to the Canon Theatre in July and August as part of the Mirvish season. Wicked received the same kind of limited-run exposure here last year, and it obviously marks a retrenchment from the days when The Producers and Hairspray were set down for open-ended runs that unexpectedly closed.

I confess to some uncertainty over which country has bragging rights to Spamalot. The source material is obviously very British, and so, for all his current expatness, is Eric Idle, the musical's co-author and Python-in-residence. All the same, the power of the production (Mike Nichols being the man ultimately in charge) and its magnum-scale confidence are definitely American: one thing, at least, that Spamalot has in common with Camelot. The tone, though, to judge from the cast recording, is American with a difference, and that difference could only be English. Spamalot is a theatrical Tristram Shandy, a musical that is in part about doing a musical. So is The Producers. But The Producers is insiders' spoof (which is perhaps why it failed outside New York and as a movie); ultimately, it believes in the conventions, even takes them for granted. Spamalot is baffled by them; when, in a number titled The Song That Goes Like This, it lampoons the entire corpus of the Lloyd Webber ballad, it communicates -- very effectively -- a bafflement that such things should exist.

Meanwhile, we continue to get old musicals, mainly, though not entirely, American ones. The Stratford Festival, still working its way through the standard classics (My Fair Lady and Guys and Dolls have now come around twice each), this year reaches South Pacific, but twins it with the primal British show, Oliver!. The Shaw Festival, which last year joined the main-stage musical game with Gypsy, follows it with High Society. CanStage is about to give us Hair, and has already announced The Rocky Horror Picture Show for next season.

As for when and how the latest, surprisingly rich, Broadway crop will reach us (The Light in the Piazza, Dirty Rotten Scoundrels, The 25th Annual Putnam Spelling Bee), there is as yet no word. Meanwhile, we have some catching up to do. Recent Toronto productions of Jason Robert Browns The Last Five Years and Andrew Lippas John and Jen have reminded us how little we still know of the new wave of American composers, most of whose shows -- not all - - are too small for the commercial producers but don't fit the identity of most of our non-profits. Lippa's The Wild Party is worth investigating, and so is the exactly contemporaneous show with the same title and the same source by Michael John LaChiusa, both of them acrid explorations of the 1920s style and ethos. The LaChiusa show I most want to see, though, is Hello Again, his update of Schnitzlers La Ronde, which has some stunning musico-dramatic scenes.

Browns Parade, a grim show about a Southern lynching, has yet to be seen in Canada, even though it started as a Livent project. Anyone who saw Brent Carver as the victim, reprising his big number at the Tonys, must have been a) profoundly moved; and b) aghast when he lost the prize to Martin Short. (Also one of ours, but patriotism, as Edith Cavell said, is not enough.) And talking of Livent authors, Lynn Ahrens and Stephen Flaherty, who wrote Ragtime, have gone on doing fine work. Their A Man of No Importance, from a movie about a shy, gay Dublin bus conductor who lives for amateur theatre in general and Oscar Wilde in particular, is full of good things. So, even, is their ill-fated Seussical, though its delightful numbers never find a story to unite the songs and the Dr. Seuss characters and lingo.
