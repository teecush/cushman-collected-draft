From its panting title on down, Poochwater (Theatre Passe Muraille) is a mangy dog of a piece. It was first seen at the Toronto Fringe Festival, and it returns to town bearing Dora awards and four-star reviews. This is the kind of funny thing that happens on the way from the Fringe, where plays with mild appeal stand out from others with no appeal whatever.

There are a couple of appealing ideas rattling around in Mike McPhaden's play, but they do no more than rattle. It starts with a man coming into a ratty apartment intent on returning a wallet he has discovered in a park and whose contents declare it to be the property of a Mr. Martin Poochwater. Mr P. is apparently out, so his benefactor, a meticulous and ingenuous type, prepares to leave him a long letter of explanation. When he comes, however, to sign it, he finds he cannot remember his own name. The apartments owner returns and, discovering a stranger on the premises, makes a belligerent citizens arrest, using a neighbouring old lady's cane as enforcement.

The locale is a U.S. city, soon after the end of the Second World War. At one point there is a power failure. The two men, cast into darkness, find that as ex-GIs they can converse in Morse code, and the realization causes them to drop their antagonism and become, cautiously, buddies. This is a sweet sequence, certainly the best in the play. Otherwise, the choice of time and place seems gratuitous. The publicity material makes claims about the play showing a turning point in the history of America, but to transfer this to the text itself is really to take the will for the deed. A few hints are dropped about Americans' consuming religiosity, but none of them is specific to the period.

The 1940s setting is also supposed to dictate a bold performing style, deriving from the films of the period. Im glad they told me that, because otherwise I might have maligned the performances by describing them as oversimplified. The author himself plays the intruder, whom he makes both google-eyed and google-mouthed, and Jeff Miller is his rough-and-ready-but-good-hearted assailant. They arent bad but they are certainly insistent, not to mention loud.

There is one narrative revelation, which can be seen coming a long way off. To be fair, I don't think it's meant as a surprise. The first intimation of memory loss is powerful. This man, who can remember most other things, has forfeited his identity. Its a universal nightmare, and our stomachs lurch in sympathy with his. But the feeling dissipates as the writing becomes irrepressibly cute.

Our hero likes to see his life as a series of equations, which he scrawls on the apartment door. His host storms at the vandalism, and we agree with him, though maybe not for the same reasons. The real objection is that, though the writing-on-the-wall gag is all right the first time, it goes on, and on, and on. The show, which apparently first clocked in at 40 minutes, now runs for 70. Thats another funny thing that happens to plays on their way from the Fringe.

Poochwater's choice of time frame at least allows for the introductory playing of some nostalgic period records -- from the war itself rather than its aftermath. But then, the two characters are themselves inclined to nostalgia.

This leads me to a more exotic fringe, the one inhabited by those popular singers who partake of both cabaret and jazz. The best of them specialize in the intelligent reshaping of familiar songs, thinking them through, without obvious fireworks, and emerging with something fresh and touching.

A recent recorded example is Eric Comstock's singing of Imagination, with its whimsically conversational Johnny Burke lyric (and matching Jimmy Van Heusen melody). This occurs on No One Knows (Harbinger), a remarkably refreshing album that announces something rare: the presence in the genre of a young male singer whos worth taking seriously.

Comstock is a New York singer-pianist with a talent for landing on the right unexpected word and making it sound logical. He seems to have shed what was previously a nondescript preppiness. His voice is still light but it has body and it takes him to most of the places he wants to go. He can swing, crisply and modestly (like the young Jack Jones of the 1960s, before his style got bloated). His rhythm lets him rescue a potentially maudlin song like To the Ends of the Earth.

His repertoire is very impressive; it streches beyond the standard suspects to Paul Simon and Oscar Brown, Jr. He has even, most rarely in these latitudes, found an interesting new song, Easy on the Heart, with music by Charlie Haden and coherent words by Arthur Hamilton, the man who wrote Cry Me a River and who knows how to explore a conceit rather than exhaust it.

Even better is Comstocks title track, a magical discovery of a song by Billy Strayhorn, Duke Ellington's cohort, that, indeed, No One Knew.

There are misses: Ellington's Dont Get Around Much Anymore, whatever its lyric seems to say, does not work as a slow ballad. Making a march of If I Had My Druthers, a song in praise of laziness, is a joke that just seems perverse. (And given the speed he goes at, Comstock might at least have included more of Johnny Mercers lyrics.) Mostly, though, either doing lost-love songs or lost-in-love songs, Comstock is excellent. Superb musicians (Wycliffe Gordon and Frank Wess) offer rich support.

Carol Sloane's Whisper Sweet (High Note) is also named after a rare song, by the great stride pianist James P. Johnson. It includes the wonderful injunction be persuasive," which is what Sloane does, though she doesn't so much whisper as murmur sweet. But its a full- blooded murmur, and may be the most beautiful vocal sound anyone is making in jazz at the moment.

She can navigate the testing vocal line of My One and Only Love unaccompanied and not sound as if shes showing off. Where or When conveys its message of deja vu with insinuating urgency and runs to a very creative second chorus whose melodic variations still tell a story. The wistful This Time the Dream's on Me is taken at an unusually brisk tempo, with the magisterial Norman Simmons playing super-saloon piano. Sloane's seamless phrasing makes the implicit pain sound casual and offhand but nonetheless real.

For so thoughtful a singer, she can be cavalier with lyrics: What promises to be a definitive reading of The Night We Called It a Day is maimed when she reverses the meaning of the songs entire middle section, and her inaccuracies on Heres That Rainy Day (Burke and Van Heusen again) are hair-raising or, to purists, hair-tearing. Otherwise, this is a perfect disc -- the reigning singer in the field at the height of her powers.
