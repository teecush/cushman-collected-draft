So, there's Big Edie and theres Little Edie, mother and daughter. They live in Grey Gardens, a big house in the Hamptons. We first meet them in 1941 when Big Edie, full name Edith Bouvier Beale, is supposedly throwing a party to celebrate Little Edies engagement to Joe Kennedy Jr.: yes, one of those Kennedys. Also on hand are Major Bouvier, Big Edie's disapprovingly patrician father, and two little Bouviers, the future Lee Radziwill and Jackie Onassis. Jackie, of course, will pause on the way to marry her own Kennedy, which is more than Little Edie manages to do. A telegram arrives for her from her father Mr. Beale, announcing his imminent divorce from Big Edie. Little Edie commits the cardinal social error of reading it out loud to the assembled company, resulting in a scandalized Joe breaking off the engagement. Or rather, she sings it out loud, because this is Grey Gardens, the musical.

It was an esteemed show, if not quite a hit, on Broadway 10 years ago and has been brought to us now by Acting Up Stage, as the latest in its series of praiseworthy musical reclamations. It's an intelligent show, given an accomplished production, but it doesnt add up. Its avowed source is the celebrated documentary film by the Maysles brothers; there has also been an excellent made-for-TV movie with Jessica Lange and Drew Barrymore as the Edies. Their story has a built-in musical component: Big Edie was briefly a professional singer, and shes now a more than enthusiastic amateur, with a resident accompanist-cum-gigolo. For her daughter's engagement she has planned a program of nine songs though, when Little Edie complains of being upstaged, she graciously consents to reduce it to eight. She still contrives to sabotage the engagement, or so Little Edie believes. She has her own performing ambitions, and she storms off to New York to follow her dream. End of Act One.

Act Two takes place 32 years later. Big Edie, if not actually bedridden, never gets up. Little Edie, her career having failed to materialize, has returned to look after her though, by Big Edie's account, not nearly well enough. Grey Gardens is now a dump, overrun with garbage and cats; and the two Edies fight like felines themselves, each accusing the other of bad life-choices, especially regarding men and money. Doug Wright wrote the book, Scott Frankel the music, and Michael Korie the lyrics. The shows two halves are very different, obviously by design. Act One is full of bustle and upper-crust infighting; it plays like High Society with teeth. As in any conventional musical, everybody gets a number. In Act Two, as befits their shared solitude, only the Edies sing, except in one sequence thats Big Edie's fantasy. (It's a hallelujah thing, involving Norman Vincent Peale.) The problem with this is that Act Two makes Act One seem irrelevant; indeed even when its going on it feels curiously weightless. We dont care about these people. They're more intriguing in Act Two, but even this lacks forward motion. The mainspring of the action is the civic threat, spearheaded by an offstage Jackie O, to get Grey Gardens cleaned up, but there's never any dramatic urgency about it. (There was in the films, both of them.) Despite the title, and despite evocative sets (by Camellia Koo) and lighting (Bonnie Beecher), the house itself never seems much of a presence. At the end of the show Little Edie packs her bags again, but this time she cant leave. She and her mother are locked together. Its the Samuel Beckett musical.

The Big Edie of Act One and the Little Edie of Act Two are about the same 40-ish age, and they're both played by the same actress. This provides the opportunity for a star turn, but it makes it impossible to believe that the two Little Edies are the same person, which rather destroys the point of the story. Lisa Horner does the double here. She's efficiently brash in the first half and splendidly barbed and inward in the more interesting second. As a singer she deals beautifully with the two best songs in the score: the haunting Will You? (as Big) and the plaintive Another Winter in a Summer Town (as Little). Theres another intended show-stopper in The Revolutionary Costume for Today in which Little Edie, dressed as a sort of counter-culture nun, greets the 1970s; but she blares it, obscuring the words. Add Ann Hodges production to the list of shows, musicals especially, that have failed to conquer the Berkeley Street acoustics. Amplification means, perversely, that the orchestra drowns out the loud singers but not the quiet ones.

Nicola Lipman plays the older Big Edie with consummate crabbiness; Lisa Guloien does the younger little one with spirit. Jeff Lillico is a standout in two roles: the weak-willed Joe of the first act, victim of his family's expectations, and Jerry, a handy young fellow whom Big Edie virtually adopts because he really appreciates her sweetcorn. (As Little Edie tartly comments, he knows on which side it's buttered. Or, actually, margarined.) Of the rest, Matthew Brown makes a strong quiet impression as two generations of black retainer; he's especially good when registering pained disapproval of a Southern-fried rave-up called Hominy Grits. The song proves that the authors can do old-fashioned musical numbers; others show that they can write newfangled psychological ones too. What they haven't done is make one kind balance and illuminate the other.
