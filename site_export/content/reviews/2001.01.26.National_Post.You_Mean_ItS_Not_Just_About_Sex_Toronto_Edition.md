MAKING PORN

Bathurst Street Theatre, Toronto

Pirandello's ghost needn't bother to get up, but Making Porn certainly exemplifies the interaction of life and art. It's about exactly what its title says it's about -- the production of pornographic videos, gay male division -- and two of its actors are actual porn stars, in San Francisco, which is where the play is set. So, at least, say the publicity, the program, and even one of the other actors in a speech at the curtain call. He also lays great stress on his two colleagues being lovers in real life, but here art and life part company; in the play they barely (oh dear, it's starting) have a scene together.

Making porn and Making Porn have something else in common: They both have to face the problem of what to put into the script between the sex scenes. Ronnie Larsen, who wrote the play, seems to have taken the challenge to heart, and has evolved a complex structure that boasts three separate but interconnected plot-lines.

There are the efforts of Arthur -- writer, producer, director and all-round sleazebag -- to produce his movie, i.e. to recruit a cast and pay them as little as possible; his biggest current expense is hiring a highly rated performer named Ray to play a lead in his new epic Cops. (Cops is all about policemen. And a plumber. I forget which Ray is playing. He doesn't spend much time in uniform anyway.)

There is the romance that blossoms between Jamie, Arthur's much- exploited assistant and live-in partner, and Ricky, a young law student fulfilling his life-long ambition of appearing in a porno flick; he dreams of being a star, but for the moment he comes cheap.

Finally, there is Jack, an aspiring but monotonously unsuccessful actor, whom we see auditioning for Shakespeare and for musicals, very badly. (I assume the effect is intentional.) He is straight but can only find work from the likes of Arthur: a fact that he tries, unsuccessfully, to keep from his wife, a legal secretary named Linda.

Getting all this together, and keeping it together up to the actual onstage shooting of the movie, makes for a long first act. Much too long, in fact, but there are some surprisingly good elements: surprising to me, anyway, since I confidently expected the show to be terrible. The demi-monde the play depicts -- a mirror of the wider movie world with its elastic critical and commercial standards and its self-intoxicated award ceremonies -- seems to be etched from experience; and Arthur, who sees himself as the Orson Welles of skin flicks, is a very convincing figure. As played by Butch Cordora (the characters' names seem drably prosaic compared to those of the actors), he is mean, hard-headed in some ways and myopically obtuse in others, and touchingly intent on getting the actors to respect his script --and not to say "dude" when he has written "guy."

The preparation of the more action-oriented sections of the movie is played for laughs, which seems a practical decision, given the nature of the theatre: a medium, by the way, for which Arthur expresses thoroughgoing contempt. (He'd probably approve, though, of the present management's policy of charging five bucks extra for a seat in one of the front three rows. What the big spenders get for their money is another question.)

Arthur's deteriorating union with Jamie (Adam Beckworth) is quite trenchantly outlined, and Jamie's compensating affair with the debutant Ricky (John Hensley) is convincingly tender. Jack's marital life with Linda isn't, but then I don't suppose any of those responsible for the play claim to be experts on heterosexual relationships. What happens is that Linda is shocked when she Finds Out, and insists on accompanying Jack to the set. The resulting scene requires all the actors to look incredulous, though not nearly as incredulous as the audience is feeling. Linda stops being shocked and gets thrilled by the commercial potential she sees in her husband's new career. (Not that he had an old one.) None of this is impossible, but it's written by numbers, and Kym Kristalie can hardly be blamed for playing it the same way.

Now, about those two authentic porn luminaries. Apparently they weren't in the show when it first opened (five years ago in Chicago) but their presence now is an example of standard road-company procedure: Cast a Name, from wherever, and however specialized. One of them, Jason Branch, is cast, presumably to type, as Ray; he seems a moody performer whose line-readings sound as if they're being phoned in on a very faint connection. The other, Blake Harper, goes the opposite route and plays Jack, which means that we have a gay actor acting straight in the role of a straight actor acting gay. It all comes out in the wash.

Act Two is much shorter, and much worse, and is dominated by the onset of AIDS. (At this point we look at the program and notice for the first time that the play is set in the early 1980s.)

One of the characters dies, though he does stick around, benevolently materializing at the top of a ladder, all decorated with Christmas lights. Ray refuses to work without condoms though Arthur declares Death Sooner: not his own, of course. He is, however, talked round by Linda, with whom he has virtually gone into partnership and who has suddenly taken to wearing mini-skirts. She seems to have given up on Jack, who is last glimpsed playing a messenger in what you can bet is a non-Equity production of Macbeth.

So it seems all but the two least sympathetic characters have got out of the porn business. It makes for a surprisingly traditional moralistic ending. This may not have been quite the author's intention, but by this point it has become unavoidably clear that his method in writing this play was to throw in anything -- farce or sentiment or social consciousness -- as long as it filled the space and bore some relation to the title.

Making Porn is, of course, junk; but so is everything in the current Toronto theatre apart from Zadie's Shoes and Stones in His Pockets, and this play is by no means the worst. The set is dominated by a free-standing door that at one amusing moment on Wednesday night refused to open, but otherwise Caryn Horwitz's production manages the transitions between scenes and episodes a lot more smoothly than has recently been the case in some higher-toned establishments. I note that she is now engaged in making a film of Making Porn, and that really should have Pirandello rattling his chains.
