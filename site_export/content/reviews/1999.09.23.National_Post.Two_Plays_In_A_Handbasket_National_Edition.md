THE ROAD TO HELL

Tarragon Mainspace, Toronto

The imposing title of The Road to Hell turns out to conceal a pair of one-act, two-actor comedies, written and performed by Michael Healey and his offstage partner, Kate Lynch. Healey is the author of The Drawer Boy, which reopens tonight at Passe Muraille: Comparisons are best avoided, though I do wonder whether, without the success of that more substantial piece, Tarragon would have given this innocuous new offering pride of place at the head of its new season.

Cuteness is the main ingredient here: cuteness of writing, acting, and of basic idea. Each of the two playlets boasts a single comic notion; each runs it nearly, but not quite, into the ground. Both plays constantly skirt the edge of the unbearable but manage to pull back and disarm you with a joke that's funny or an observation that rings true.

Yodellers, the first half, concerns the relationship between a champion lady golfer and the male sports reporter who comes to interview her. He ends up spending the night in her hotel room, thus fulfilling the fantasy of every journalist ever sent on a star assignment. He does not, however, have sex with her; they spend the night talking about the game, and the conversation has a tonic effect on her performance the next day. (This is another hack's dream: that he who only writes about it -- change the genders as you wish -- will give instruction to she who actually does it, and she will listen and be grateful. I'm still waiting.)

Inevitably, he falls in love with her and she, after a fashion, with him, their passion impeded by him being married, which he brushes off, and her being gay, which she doesn't. But she takes him on the road with her (hiding him away so as not to draw the ire of the lesbian golfing mafia, whose power I had certainly never suspected) and they try to work out a compromise that will be platonic enough for her and physical enough for him.

Much of the humour stems, inevitably in the circumstances, from male erotic vulnerability; but by the end the scales are balanced with surprising evenness. The idea is stretched dangerously thin, but there is a shape. We also learn how easily golfing terms (the title is one of them) can become sexual double entendres. But then, what can't?

The second number, Kreskinned, is about a male antiquarian bookseller and a female dog-obedience trainer. Both of them wear duffle coats, a circumstance that has prompted a mutual friend to fix them up on a blind date.

They go to see a show by a hypnotist, who's billed as the Great Kreskin. (I have to confess that this last detail passed me by, so I found the title as mystifying as it is ungainly.) He pulls them up on stage and has her imitate a chicken and him a car; we see them at it, though (another confession) I thought at the time that he was doing a gorilla.

Anyway, it turns out that the hypnotist has neglected to un- hypnotize them, and at any given moment either partner can put the other into a trance. This, as their acquaintance progresses from restaurants to movies to bed to the verge of marriage, proves an invaluable tool for defusing or bypassing embarrassing moments.

A more blatant device than the one in the preceding play, it's also more theatrically resilient: a neat metaphor for the amount of blind-eye-turning needed to sustain any relationship. Certainly this couple can use it; he has trouble making commitments (the piece makes no claims to great psychological originality) and I'm not sure what her problem is but she seems very nervous. This play, too, has a neat ending, this time one that tilts the balance rather than maintaining it. But it still lies uncomfortably between revue- sketch and full-blown farce.

As performers, Healey and Lynch are adroit and agreeable, but limited. Both have excellent timing (as, by the way, does their director, Andy McKim); hers is quick-fire and his is slow-burn.

Lynch indeed pounces on every line as if it were a prize wisecrack, which tends to leave those that aren't, but would like to be, a trifle exposed. Healey shambles endearingly but sometimes to excess, especially as the slovenly lover of Kreskinned; I'm a slob myself, but I like to think that, for a first date, I would try to hunt out a sweater that didn't actually have a hole in it. In his earlier role, though, he looks and sounds ideal, somewhat pedantic but boyishly enthusiastic; if the Globe and Mail's actual golfing correspondent -- I assume they have one -- isn't like this, it's he who is miscast, not Healey.

To sum up: We all know what the road to hell is paved with; some of the good intentions on this one pay off, but not enough. Unless you are charitable, in which case you're on the other road anyway.
