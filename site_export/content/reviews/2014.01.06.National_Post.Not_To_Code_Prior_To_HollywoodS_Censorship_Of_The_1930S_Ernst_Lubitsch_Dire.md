Though I would count myself a musicals buff, there is one group of classic movies in the genre that I had never got around to watching. I refer to the musicals made in Hollywood in the early years of the talkies by the expatriate German director Ernst Lubitsch. For years I had read that these films were the Champagne of musicals, frothy and sophisticated and explicitly but tastefully sexy in ways that would have been beyond the ken of any American-born director. So finally, for a holiday treat, I curled up with Lubitsch Musicals (Criterion Collection; Eclipse Series 8), a DVD box set of the four movie musicals Lubitsch made for Paramount between 1929 and 1932, and then topped up my frosted glass with The Merry Widow (WB Archive Collection), a one-off in the same tradition that he directed at MGM in 1934.

Well, most of it is true. Compared to the static movie musicals that preceded them, and that looked like literal reproductions of stage shows even when they weren't, the Paramount pictures are wonderfully fluid; Lubitsch seems not so much to be breaking the rules as never to have heard of them. That includes the perennial Hollywood convention by which singing could only be deemed acceptable in a performance situation; here the characters, none of them singers by trade, break into song simply because it's a natural way of expressing themselves.

It's probably wise to allow more time between viewings than I was able to do, as the characters, tones and settings are very similar. They all take place in Paris, or in some other European location equally imaginary. They all star Maurice Chevalier, Jeanette MacDonald, or both.

To be precise: Chevalier and MacDonald are teamed together in The Love Parade (1929), the first of the Paramounts, and in One Hour with You (1932), the last. In between, MacDonald is seen dallying with Jack Buchanan in Monte Carlo (1930), and Chevalier with both Claudette Colbert and Miriam Hopkins in The Smiling Lieutenant (also 1930).

MacDonald is a dream, and a revelation to all who have thought of her solely in her later guise as the virgin goddess of operetta movies opposite Nelson Eddy. Her typical role is that of a besieged beauty, living on her wits to keep the wolves - often amatory, sometimes economic - from her door. She's a sparkling comedienne, wonderful at putting men in their place, but with an underlying vulnerability, so that she melts enchantingly once she has decided which man it would be sensible to melt for.

All these pictures begin with some kind of parade; in Monte Carlo it's a throng of wedding guests, one of whom bears a sign reading "happy the bride the sun shines on." Then it rains. Meanwhile the bride, having discovered that her wedding dress doesn't fit and with no time to put on anything else, is off to the railroad station in search of freedom. She then sings Beyond the Blue Horizon, the one classic song to come out of any of these films, and an acknowledged landmark for the way it matches propulsive words, propulsive music, propulsive sound effects (the whizzing loco wheels), and propulsive visuals (peasants in the fields waving encouragement at Jeanette as she hurtles by them), all in the service of a propulsive character and situation.

Chevalier provokes more complicated feelings. For those who know him as the complacently aging boulevardier of Gigi, it's a shock to see him as a young man, already inordinately pleased with himself and with his effect on women. At least in Gigi, he's just remembering it. Here, he's a coy boy, constantly rolling his eyes and flashing his accent, and it isn't only in The Smiling Lieutenant that you wish someone would make him turn the smile off. That's the bad half of it. The good is that, as in any good sex farce or romantic comedy (these films are both), his ego, to his own great surprise, takes a pasting, and Chevalier plays both the incredulity and the hurt very well. His redeeming charm when successful is that he can't believe all the girls are happening to him; when rejected, he can't believe that they aren't happening to him What really deepens him is wedlock; he spends a surprising proportion of these films being married.

Part of the mystique of the Lubitsch films is that they pre-date the studios' self-imposed morality code; they show people, not always married, in bed together and conversing in entendres that hardly bother to be double.

The Love Parade starts by proclaiming La Vie Parisienne, illustrated by cancan girls, a short song about "a little bottle of Champagne," and the unexpected arrival in Chevalier's rooms of a man whom the young lady present shriekingly greets as "mon mari"; which Chevalier helpfully translates for us. He's playing a Count who gets recalled to his mythical homeland of Sylvania for having had such a scandalously good time. The queen who sent for him to punish him instead falls in love with him, after he woos her with the title song, which tells her that she's all his previous girls rolled into one; a queen has to take what a queen can get and she takes him. He settles into the life, and the ceremonial role, of a Prince Consort. He has opinions, but nobody wants to hear them. He asserts his independence, thereby bringing out the actor in Chevalier and a satisfying substance in the film.

He again gets royally wed in The Smiling Lieutenant, where a virginal princess (Hopkins) has her father buy Chevalier for her despite his being attached to the far worldlier leader of an all-girl band (Colbert). Both ladies are charmers, if not exactly great singers, but it's married love that wins out, perhaps surprisingly. (Or perhaps not; there were limits to how far you could flout the code, even before it was written down.)

Reunited for One Hour with You, Chevalier and MacDonald are married from the start, and so devoted a couple that the chorus of Parisian Keystone Kops who start the picture off cannot believe that they're legal. This is the best and wittiest of the Paramount cycle, though the one with the fewest ambitions as a musical; the only song to get any kind of extended treatment is the title number, its lyric shared between half a dozen characters to reflect their various states of mind.

For once Chevalier plays a man happily faithful, but thrown off course by the uninhibited attentions of his wife's best friend. In the meantime his wife becomes jealous, but of the wrong woman, and herself gives half-hearted encouragement to a devoted but ineffectual wooer. Cross purposes abound, most notably in a classic encounter between Chevalier and his seductress's spouse, who has seen all this before; he's understanding but unforgiving.

It was Lorenz Hart who wrote new words to the famous Franz Lehar melodies in The Merry Widow, including such poker-faced delights as "I think he said Maxim's/He's going there, it seems/I heard that's where a man can/See ladies dance the cancan." Dance it they do; Lubitsch fills the screen with whirling skirts, not to mention, in one dazzling image, a human staircase of waltzing couples extending as far as the eye can see.

The great operetta score is there, and so are the barest bones of the operetta situation: MacDonald plays the richest woman in the Ruritanian statelet of Marshovia, weary of widowhood and ready to be merry, and Chevalier is Captain Danilo, the practised Casanova sent to Paris to marry her and thus preserve the Marshovian economy. She's at her most gracious and glittering, he at his most endearingly resilient. In some ways this is their most old-fashioned pairing, the one in which their mating is postponed until the final scene. It actually spins out its situation too long, but that's true of all these pictures.

Obviously the most melodious of the Lubitsch musicals, The Merry Widow is also the funniest. The Hays code had come in by now, but nobody seems to have told the director; at any rate he worked around it, notably in the scenes between the fussy, harried King of Marshovia (George Barbier) and his perky, restless Queen (Una Merkel), who's able to give Danilo her own seal of approval. Discovering them virtually in flagrante the King asks only that they keep it secret; assuring him that they will Chevalier adds, with his patented air of rakish innocence, "We weren't even planning to tell your Majesty."

Paramount had style, MGM had gloss; and when the Marx Brothers, about the same time, moved from one to the other, they lost a lot of their edge. Lubitsch somehow managed to keep his, perhaps because it was more "civilized"; the urbane societies he conjured up were the kind the Marxes lived to destroy. He was more tolerant of pomposity than they were, but he too liked to make fun of it, especially when it involved politicians (he went on to direct Garbo's Ninotchka, which is a kind of spy drama, and the riotous Second World War comedy To Be or Not to Be) and The Merry Widow is his satiric as well as his romantic summit.

It's also his musical summit; the one in which, more than ever before, the camera dances even between the numbers. This film lives up to its legend, the first three Paramounts almost do, and One Hour with You is even better than I'd been told. Watching them was a great holiday treat. But you don't have to wait for a holiday.
