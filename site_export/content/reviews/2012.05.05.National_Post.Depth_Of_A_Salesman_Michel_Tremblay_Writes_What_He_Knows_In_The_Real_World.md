I like The Real World? better than any other Michel Tremblay play I have seen. (There are quite a few I haven't.) It dates back to 1988, and it concerns a playwright who has written a play about his own family. That is something Tremblay himself had already done and would do again, so we are in effect watching the autobiography of an autobiographer. It gets quite dizzying.

The Tremblay surrogate is a young Montrealer named Claude. His father Alexis suggests Willy Loman mixed with Homer Simpson, with the more lovable parts of both surgically removed; he's a philandering travelling salesman who prides himself on his philistinism, his conviviality and his talent for telling off-colour jokes. (Oddly, we never get to hear any of them.) Alexis' wife Madeleine might be described as long-suffering, and it's Claude's view that she's suffered altogether too long; one of his reasons for writing his play was to give her the voice, and maybe the actions, he thinks she should have found for herself. The family is completed by Claude's sister Mariette, who's a go-go dancer and who still lives at home.

Claude has moved out; disregarding paternal pressure to go selling and travelling himself, he works at a grunt job by day and writes by night. Having finished his play, he's given it to his mother to read, and is appalled to find that she's appalled. At first it seems to him - or maybe just to us - that it's just good old-fashioned fear of what the neighbours might think. She objects both to what Claude has reported and what he's invented; she's especially furious that he's credited her with a taste for Mendelssohn rather than the middle-of-the-road pop to which she actually listens. But it goes deeper. There's nothing Claude can tell her about her husband that she doesn't already know. And she has no intention of behaving the way Claude thinks she should.

While Madeleine tells her son just what she thinks of his play, we get to see the scene in question for ourselves: a device that Tremblay continues to use, with increasing audacity and success. Everything is in double focus; weighing one perspective against another proves a witty, stimulating and occasionally scarifying exercise. The fictional Madeleine delivers a tirade on why she is about to leave. The real Madeleine makes an even more effective speech, explaining just why she intends to stay where she is. It's an excoriating outburst - perhaps the only thing in the evening that is actually moving - and, in this Tarragon revival, it's excoriatingly well delivered by Jane Spidell.

Her rage is streaked with embarrassment, and also enriched by it; she keeps confessing that of course she's never read a play before. You can see why Tremblay, years later, felt he had to atone by according her a theatrical apotheosis in For the Pleasure of Seeing Her Again, one of whose premises is that his mother had never seen any of his shows.

As the current play progresses, the focus shifts from the mother and towards the father, to whom Claude remains resolutely unforgiving. We're told just why or partly why, in a late revelation about which reviewers have been asked to remain silent. It is, in fact, fairly predictable, but not too easy to credit, though Tremblay, by piling on the emotional pressure, does wear us down. This revival, directed by Richard Rose, is uneven; though not, as you might expect, because of the play's two levels. There's good or bad on both. Spidell's "real" Madeleine is complemented, but not equalled, by Meg Tilly, whose performance as her alter ego is that the kind that critics like to call brave, meaning that, physically and vocally, it's way over the top, full of strange cadences and odd angles. Meanwhile, Tony Nappo's real Alex is little more than a shouting shell, while Cliff Saunders as his opposite number at least suggests a man with his own case to make. It's a shame that he isn't actually given one; the fictitious devil is, in fact, given better tunes than the actual one but they aren't allowed to sway us. Claude thinks that his papa is a resentful oaf, weeping crocodile tears, and Tremblay does nothing to contradict him; we're primed to expect something more complex but it never arrives.

Sophie Goulet and Cara Gee are decorative as the two Mariettes, which is about all that's asked of them; fact or fiction (and in this case there's very little difference), she's more a device than a character. There's only one Claude; as his mother acidly remarks, he's kept himself out of his own play. His defence is he thought that would be fairer; he might have added that the author is traditionally his own least compelling character. Still, on the ground floor that he does get to inhabit, Matthew Edison - who, just to make things even more meta, is a playwright himself - avoids that fate. He has us wondering, as he should, just how any playwright or novelist expects to face his family after using them as identifiable material: No wonder that O'Neill forbade any performance of Long Day's Journey into Night until after all concerned were dead, including himself. He looks down his nose at those around him with a fascinating air of pain and disdain, whose vocal equivalent is his response to his father's taunting reference to beatniks: "They went out a lo-o-ng time ago." Rose's production rises to a character's thrilling display of angry violence; then spoils it by having the set join in the self-destructiveness; finally redeems itself by having Edison's Claude take a silent look at his real-time dad, as if he too were a character in a play: conceivably, the play we've just seen.

Talking of levels: Upstairs in the same theatre, The Innocents, by the promising Daniel Karasik, is probably not autobiographical, but it is an attempt to put representative figures of his own twenty-something generation on stage. The trouble is that they're more representative in a general sense, and less in a specific, than the play cracks them up to be; circumstances, like economics and access to drugs, may change but every age has had equivalent problems. Also, despite some sparky dialogue, the symmetry between two young men of similar Candian-Jewish upbringing - one a hot-shot lawyer with no personal life, the other a drop-out with nothing but - seems forced, as does their exchanging of girlfriends and, eventually, identities. Of the four young performers, Amelia Sargisson comes closest to finding a personality behind her lines, with the author himself the runner-up as the lawyer who ends up shedding, very literally, the trappings of worldly success.

The Real World? runs through June 3 at the Tarragon Theatre; The Innocents runs through May 13 at the Tarragon Near Studio. For more information, call 416-536-5018 or visit tarragontheatre.com.
