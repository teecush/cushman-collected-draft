THE STEPMOTHER

Court House Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

---

The Shaw Festival's production of The Stepmother is a companion piece to its production of Getting Married. Eustace Gaydon is a widower, a gentleman-speculator sorely in need of a cash infusion. His hopes are pinned on the will of his late sister, and he is furious when she turns out to have left all her money to her companion, Lois. So Eustace does the obvious thing. He invites Lois into his house, first as governess to his two young daughters, then as his wife. It's an even better investment than he'd hoped. Lois turns out to have great talent as a dressmaker, and Eustace sets her up in her own business -- using her own money of course.

Ten years pass and Lois, without quite realizing it, is supporting both of them. She also attracts the attention, ardent but mostly honourable, of Peter, the conveniently wealthy barrister next door. The rest of her overstretched time is spent tending to the growing Gaydon girls, the elder of whom is now in love with a boy whose father, knowing rather too much of Eustace's character and prospects, is dead set against it being a match. If you believe the title, the relationship between Lois and the Gaydon girls is the central topic of the play.

Don't believe the title. The contrast between the traditional bad stepmother and this very good one may be the stuff of theories and program notes, but the play itself hardly admits it's an issue. We are told absolutely nothing about Eustace's first wife, nor do we get any glimpse of Lois in her governess period. By the time the main action starts the girls are unshakably devoted to her -- they unself-consciously refer to her as "mother" --and she to them.

What remains riveting about the play -- and about the production -- is the ambience of love and money. The law decrees that Lois's property is her husband's, practically that she herself is; precisely the situation that the reluctant brides of Getting Married were bridling at. But in Shaw that's all in the head; Lois has physically to live it, just as she has to deal with actual sexual temptation that the audience believes in. (Nobody believes for a moment in the supposed erotic triangles of Getting Married.)

All the Gaydons are living hand-to-mouth, a reality brought home to us early on by the character of Eustace's maiden aunt Charlotte who keeps house for him and has to beg him for cheques to live on; Jennifer Phipps plays her to perfection, in sickness and in health though not in that order.

The drama comes as Lois realizes how horribly she is trapped, and as she fights to escape-- from her own conditioning, as much as anything else. The strain tells; Claire Jullien admirably presents a woman who has to keep carving out new roles while remaining true to herself. We see, and feel, several sets of scales dropping from her eyes.

Blair Williams' Eustace overdoes the debonair blackguard; he's best when most desperate, trying to keep up at least the pretence of self-respect. There's an ambiguity, too, about Patrick Galligan's Peter; he loves Lois all right, and his armour genuinely shines. But he too manipulates her, even if it's for her own good. Eustace, come to think of it, would probably make the same claim for himself; and half-believe it.

Marla McLean, an ingenue plus, unforcedly projects spirit and precocious wisdom; she's her stepmother's stepdaughter. Jesse Martyn makes her a charmingly impulsive swain, and there are valuable glimpses of the everyday from Guy Bannerman, a solicitor as implacable as the law itself, and Jenny L. Wright, solicitous in a more amusing way as Lois's disapproving assistant. This, you feel, is how the world went round and -- in the more painful scenes between husband and wife-- still does. - The Stepmother runs until Oct 4.
