Joseph ziegler: Dual theatre player knows how a scene feels from the inside

"To tell you the truth," says Joseph Ziegler, I don't turn down very much work. Ziegler's work is acting and directing. The combination is a common phenomenon in Canadian theatre, almost a defining one: Robert Lepage, Martha Henry, Brian Bedford, Jim Mezon, Christopher Newton, Richard Monette, Albert Schultz, Diana Leblanc, David Ferry, the Daniels Brooks and MacIvor -- the list, traversing styles, backgrounds and generations, goes on and on. And thats not even counting the notable directors who were actors, and successful ones. Its a small world in which, as Ziegler says, "in order to make a living, you have to do everything." Generally, though, one side of the career equation takes precedence over the other. Ziegler, pictured below, uniquely, has kept the two in perfect balance.

His talents seem, if anything, to have been feeding on one another. Start with the acting, to which he brings the great complementary qualities of delicacy and gusto. In last year's A Whistle in the Dark, he gave a magnificent performance as a brawling Irish paterfamilias, a bully with a big mouth and a jellied spine. He followed it with a bristling account of the cynical Dr. Relling in The Wild Duck, for Soulpepper, of which company he is one of the 12 founding members. He'll be repeating the Whistle performance at the beginning of next year; before that, for the holidays, he recreates, for the third time, his delightful Scrooge in A Christmas Carol.

As a director, hes been a regular at the Shaw Festival for the past four seasons, distinguishing himself last year with a lucid and compelling Major Barbara (I love directing Shaw -- to try and get to what hes saying -- not soften it or make it polite) and this year with an intense production of The Heiress, with Tara Rosling in the title role ("we all say we strive for theatre that's 'dangerous; I think Tara really was dangerous"). He inaugurated Soulpeppers new home in the Distillery with Thornton Wilder's Our Town, which he first staged for the company in 1999; a fine production the first time around, it seemed just about perfect the second. And last week saw the opening of his Soulpepper production of King Lear, commonly supposed the biggest play of all, and the first show ever to use the Young Centre's Charles and Marilyn Baillie Theatre in its thrust-stage fomration.

I dont fool myself into thinking weve solved all the problems of a great play. Lear is huge. You can hardly make a dent in it. But we have a fantastic cast. It contains most of the actors who have appeared in different combinations at Soulpepper earlier this year, with the redoubtable William Webster in the title role. "Bill's been preparing for the part his entire life practically. It's impossible to do, but were doin it." (Ziegler shares with his leading actor a conversational habit of dropping g's when makin' a point.)

As youd expect, Ziegler has acted in the play in the past; he was Edgar to Douglas Campbells Lear, in John Hirsch's Stratford production of 1985. A lot has stuck with me from being in it and hearing it (and from seeing other productions over the years), but even late in rehearsals he's found himself making new discoveries and asking new questions. The realization, for example, that Lear regrets his irrational fury over his daughter Cordelias "most small fault" far earlier in the play than anyone remembers: Its his early insight, it obviously gets lost, but he's never afraid to look at himself.

This is Soulpepper's fourth Shakespeare production, and Ziegler has directed all but one of them. As an actor, he reckons that hes acted in some 25 or 30 Shakespeare productions. Hamlet, with Paul Gross at Stratford in 1999, was his first Shakespeare as a director, though he isnt certain that Stratford realized it at the time. He subsequently directed the play again, in a far more intimate production for Soulpepper, with the company's artistic director Albert Schultz in the lead. Both Gross and Schultz were, for our times, remarkably gentle Hamlets, a coincidence that I attributed to Ziegler's direction. He says now that "Albert was wonderfully vulnerable" in opening himself up to Hamlet, both in rehearsal and performance, but seemed surprised by my description of the performance as gentle. He does say "if I had any influence on Albert, it was in the get thee to a nunnery scene with Ophelia. He thought that's when Hamlet attacks Ophelia; I said 'Hes trying save her here. " And that's how it played.

It seems to have been pre-destined that Ziegler would spend his life working on classical and near-classical plays, and that he would do it in Canada, though he is in fact American. He grew up in Minneapolis, home of the Guthrie Theatre, founded by and named for the Stratford Festival's first director and run in Zieglers boyhood by its second: The plays and people I grew up watching were those that Michael Langham brought there. Logically, then, he came to Canada in 1976 to attend the National Theatre School, intending to return home immediately afterwards. However, by the end of his time there, "I knew I wanted to be here." Another Stratford veteran, the actor and teacher Douglas Rain, had instructed him, irrefutably, that if you can do Shakespeare, you can do anything. Ziegler himself went to Stratford, swiftly coming to notice there in juvenile roles, mainly with the festivals Young Company, which at that point, pleasingly enough, was run by the returning Langham. (Langham and Hirsch are the only Stratford chiefs never to have been actors. They were still, according to Ziegler, "real actors' directors. It's usually in TV that you work with directors who know nothing about acting.") His own break into directing came in 1991, when Robin Phillips, with whom he had worked at Stratford and elsewhere, took over the Citadel Theatre in Edmonton, and asked Ziegler and his wife, Nancy Palk, to come out for a season. Phillips instincts are unpredictable and, for the most part, impeccable. Most people whove been in the rehearsal hall with Robin will tell you that it's one revelation after another. He said, 'Should I think of you as a director? I said, I'd love to.'  His trial run, in the studio, was a two-hander about Jean-Paul Sartre and Simone de Beauvoir, with Bill Webster as Sartre and Phillips designing; the following year he did The Diary of Anne Frank "on the thrust" (an interesting challenge for a play that is, by definition, about confinement). He went on to direct for Theatre New Brunswick and did a couple of murder mysteries at the Shaw. And then came Soulpepper: "They were kind enough, or rash enough, to ask me to direct." And theyve continued to. Albert is very loyal to the founding members. Ill suggest something to him, like I'd like to do Noel Coward's Blithe Spirit, and hell say, why not Present Laughter instead. And Present Laugher is what they did. Ziegler presents himself, somewhat self-deprecatingly, as Soulpeppers voice of caution. "I thought we should only do one play in our first season; Albert insisted on two. He thought we should do five in our second;' I thought that was too many." Nobody, including Zigeler, doubts now that in both cases Schultz was proved right.

The two parts of Ziegler's career have of course affected and modified one another. I had a lot less patience as an actor than I do as a director. I am learning to think of theatre as a continuing process, rather than as just something on its way to a first night. When I act, I now think of the whole play a lot more; I used to be much more selfish as an actor -- in a way that actors ought to be. As a director -- I cant help but be affected by my work as an actor. Sometimes -- here he sounds a little bashful -- "I ask an actor if hell let me play it for a minute," the idea being not to have the actor imitate him but to give himself the sense of how a scene feels from the inside. Like many -- but by no means all -- actor-directors, he is, by his own admission, more a verbal, less a visual director though he points out that even for Phillips, an acknowledged pictorial master, directing was a matter of "text first, acting second, concept third." He himself spends "a lot of time fixing the physical form of a scene -- actors do much better, more effortless acting when they're in the right place." Major Barbara was visually very striking, and Our Town was beautifully choreographed. Those who work with Ziegler say that he's growing all the time. Those of us watching have been getting the same impression.
