THE DROWSY CHAPERONE

Winter Garden Theatre, Toronto

Despite appearances, The Drowsy Chaperone is not a satire on 1920s and 1930s musicals. It's a satire on people who love 1920s and 1930s musicals, and who also love satires of them. Its principal character, identified in the program as Man in Chair, is a gentleman of indeterminate years with a large record collection and a mind stuffed with theatrical trivia. He puts on an original cast recording, apparently issued as a 78 rpm album, of an imaginary 1929 show called The Drowsy Chaperone; and, to his delight and quite often to ours, the show takes physical shape before our eyes.

I pause here to remark that, for 78s, these seem to be very long- playing records, and that I know of no show from that period that was ever recorded by its stage performers, in full, dialogue included.

Man in Chair is played by Bob Martin, with an exquisite sense of controlled whimsy: That is, the character's whimsicality is out of control but the actor's isn't. The same Bob Martin wrote the show's book, in collaboration with Don McKellar, with Matt Watts given the intriguing, and as far as I know totally unprecedented, credit of associate writer. The same Bob was also one-half of the show's original inspiration; it was created to celebrate his wedding to an actress, Janet Van de Graaff, and the show-within-the-show actually concerns the threatened nuptials of a couple named, wait for it, Robert Martin and Janet Van de Graaff.

The in-joke worked well enough to be shown to the public at the 1999 Toronto Fringe festival, and from there it moved in expanded form for a run at Theatre Passe Muraille. At this point (with Bob in his present narrator's role but Janet playing Janet) the jest seemed to have more than run its course; blowing it up to still larger proportions for a commercial production, as has now been done, seemed like arrant folly. That at least is what I thought, and it shows how wrong I can be. The present version is not without problems but it provides plenty to admire and much to enjoy. How can you resist a show that starts with an accurate summary of every theatregoer's prayer just before the lights go up? "Oh God, let it be short -- nobody should have to stand two intermissions -- if it has to be a new Canadian play, let it not be a new Canadian play."

Well, The Drowsy Chaperone is short, has no intermissions, and far from being set in Canada, its main action seems to be going on in fairyland, or at the very least on Long Island. Prohibition is on, the Depression is not yet. The characters are showfolks, crooks, servants and the very rich. Janet the bride is an actress; Robert the groom is a goof. Their marriage is delayed by their own amiable stupidity and by a few plot complications. George, Robert's bald- headed best man, tries to keep the proceedings on track despite other characters' machinations. Janet is her producer's meal ticket and he does not want her to retire into matrimony; he owes money to a couple of gangsters who more than share his concern.

The producer's girlfriend is a dumb chorine who wants to be the star. Janet's mother is a dragoness, somehow lured into marriage with her butler. A continental gigolo makes a play for Janet but is deflected into seducing her sharp-tongued if somnolent companion, who is the chaperone of the title but a minor character nonetheless; she is an ineffectual guardian for Janet, who is a headstrong product of her time and place. "Here a girl can map her own/Life without a chaperone": Lorenz Hart, 1926.

They're all minor characters, and that is one of the aforementioned problems. There is nobody and nothing in this apocryphal vintage show we can latch on to. The man in the chair would have us believe that this doesn't matter, and that the enchantment of these old musicals lies in their silliness, in their unabashed stereotyping, in their shameless bad jokes, in their songs. Up to a point he's right, but he leaves some important things out, though he does allude to them in passing.

The real shows of this kind, or the best of them, starred people like Fred and Adele Astaire, and had scores by people like George and Ira Gershwin. Regularly, our narrator freezes the action to tell us about the actors we are supposedly watching; his capsule summaries of doomed careers are witty (and delivered with an enchanting blend of awe and regret) but of course these actors weren't real, and are being played by other actors anyway. This is not star quality. As for the score, which has been 50% overhauled for this production: Greg Morrison's tunes are very apt pastiche, and Lisa Lambert's lyrics are superb, in tone and in technique: I especially liked a feckless tap duet called Cold Feets and a wonderfully ruthless list song about disasters.

There is also an irresistible interpolation from another imaginary musical altogether. But none of the songs touch the heart or lift the show into a new emotional dimension, as the Gershwins or Cole Porter could. Also, there is a limit to how much enjoyment one can get from listening to bad jokes, even when they're bad on purpose and performed with panache. I suspect that the gangsters' malapropisms are meant to be funny in their own right; they aren't, but they sure try hard. Nor does the farcical action ever take off in its own right. In short, we don't care. I realize we're not supposed to; but the feeling still nags. It nagged me, anyway.

The show has been newly and transformingly directed by Daniel Brooks, who has previously demonstrated a witty way with weightier works and who is here equally fly with fluff. Christopher Richards, who designed gorgeous costumes last time round, has been happily retained. Of the cast, also half new, Dan Chameroy, vacantly beaming on roller-skates, sends himself happily up as what I suppose we have to call the hero, and Karen Hines as his opposite number does likewise. Frank Moore as the producer is dynamite, Paul O'Sullivan's best man is the best, and I withdraw my previous allegation that Don McKellar as the Latin lover looks like Hitler. Mary Lou Fallis, prominent comic soprano, puts in an overhyped appearance as a dea ex machina, and the lyricist plays the chaperone, whose bored song about the wonders of the world would be even funnier if she sang rather than talked it. I want to know: After this show and Honest Ed -- The Bargain Musical, could she write a show that would, please God, be equally comic but not a spoof? We need it.

The Winter Garden, stacked on top of the Elgin, is the nearest thing we have to a rooftop theatre, and the show might function best as did the late-night rooftop shows of the '20s: as a theatrical dessert, to be taken in after consuming something more substantial elsewhere.

To be honest, I lied somewhat about this show's lack of content; it has some, in the character of Man in Chair. (He does get out of it, quite frequently.) Before, he was the compere who stole the show; now they've given in and handed it to him. He tells us more about himself and about his devotion -- not uncritical but quite abandoned -- to escapist musical theatre. Other things about him we can observe for ourselves, and quite a character emerges; when he gets caught up in a production number, even more when the curtain calls go to his head, he becomes the audience as protagonist, and we realize that this is what the show was driving at all along. He is the star. He may even -- considering his sedentary posture and his tutelary relation to us, the other audience -- be the real drowsy chaperone. I never said the show wasn't clever.
