THEATRE REVIEW

The Best Brothers

Studio Theatre, Stratford

Daniel MacIvor's The Best Brothers may be the playwright's best play.

It's almost certainly his most conventional; in this case, and contrary to received wisdom, that's a good thing. It's far more believable and funny and engaging and purposeful than much of his earlier work involving more than one performer. (I confess: I've never seen Never Swim Alone). It doesn't have the mesmerizing power with which he as an actor invested his solo works, but after seeing several of those in fairly quick succession, I found the law of diminishing returns taking a rapid toll. They were scary, but even terror can get monotonous.

They could be charming, too, and MacIvor's charm, as a performer and especially as a writer, is in the ascendant here.

That doesn't make the play soggy or soppy. He's one of a cast of two, who do indeed play brothers. MacIvor is Hamilton Best, an architect. John Beale plays Kyle Best, a real-estate agent. You might say they're in the same business. Indeed, Kyle does say it, to Hamilton's chagrin. The opening scene finds them in separate professional locations, receiving the news of the sudden death of their mother, whose name is/was Bunny. Hamilton's response is to rush to the scene. Kyle is more about taking his time. This could mean that he's unfeeling. It could equally mean that he's practical. After all, whenever he gets there, it isn't going to make her any less dead.

The cause of death was a collision with a drag queen named Pina Colada, who fell from a float at a Gay Pride parade. Not, in Hamilton's uncontroversial opinion, a dignified way to go. He blames Kyle for Bunny's having been in the crowd in the first place. Kyle is gay, with a partner who's a sex worker, ("He isn't my sex worker," Kyle explains, "it's just what he does for a living.") Hamilton is straight and married, and proud of it, though there are cracks apparent in his life from the outset.

Underlying all the tension between them is, of course, an unspoken rivalry over which of them their mother loved the best (or the Best); add some songs and a Gold Rush setting, and they might be the squabbling siblings of the last Sondheim musical Road Show - except there it was the straight brother who was the feckless one. Tensions come hilariously to the fore in a funeral reception scene, in which the two boys jockey for position in the matter of eulogy delivery, and in which it finally becomes clear that there was and still is a third boy on the team, one who may have been the best brother, or perhaps the best son, of all. His name is Enzo.

Enzo is a dog. Bunny, an aggressively free spirit, found in Enzo, after a lifetime of experiences with less satisfactory two-legged males, the most reliable relationship of her life. At regular intervals in the play, Kyle and Hamilton take turns to don a pair of green gloves and become Bunny, who tells us how she felt about Enzo and, in amused tones, about them.

Meanwhile and - to my taste, more interestingly - the two men try to come to terms with their responsibilities towards the dog and to cope with the havoc, both mental and material, that he can and does wreak with their lives. Sorting that out helps clarify their feelings about their mother and each other.

It isn't the biggest subject in the world, and MacIvor certainly hasn't written the biggest play. But, under Dean Gabourie's adroit direction, it fills its space, physically and spiritually. Beale makes Kyle almost cartoonishly camp to begin with, but this might be a long-range strategy of deception; he gets, as the play progresses, stronger and more human by the moment.

MacIvor's Hamilton reveals, or at least emphasizes more than ever before, his strange duality as an actor: He can communicate any amount of emotion while appearing to repress it, but when called upon - by his own script, after all - to open out, he clams up, as he does here in a big speech about Lego. It seems, by the way, that the Best parents - one assumes that Dad had a voice back then, even if he doesn't get one in the play - displayed remarkable foresight in bestowing upon their sons names that would so perfectly suit their personalities in later life.

Also, by the way, the curtain call in this production comes with its own pay-off.

The Best Brothers runs until Sept. 16. For tickets and more information, visit stratfordfestival.ca.
