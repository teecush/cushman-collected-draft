A couple of years ago there was a best musical Tony award race between the critically dismissed Thoroughly Modern Millie and the critically praised Urinetown. Millie won, because it was thought that it would be the more saleable title on the road, the word "title" being taken very literally in this case.

The word around New York before last Sunday's Tonys was that history was about to repeat itself with Jersey Boys, a tribute to Frankie Valli and The Four Seasons loaded with name-recognition, beating out The Drowsy Chaperone, a more salubrious title than Urinetown but even more mysterious -- and Canadian at that. And so it proved in the end.

Jersey Boys is a mainstream show, even though it claims not to be. And Chaperone is a show that thrives on oddity, even while purporting to celebrate the mainstream. The only thing the two shows have in common is that they make their audiences very happy.

Jersey Boys, to judge from the performance I saw the night before the Tonys, is playing to a largely local crowd that just happens to come from the next locality. The house was crammed with New Jersey citizens, cheering every local reference and getting to their feet not just after numbers but during them.

That doesn't mean that Jersey Boys wont travel. It probably will. The Four Seasons music is still a force, and the show has plenty of energy, though that isn't exactly a rarity on Broadway. But it isn't very good. Its a jukebox musical that tries hard to be something more.

Its hard to write about the music business without falling into cliche. Most of the time, the show is stuck with trying to relate pop songs, generalized by definition, to specific lives and situations. Even the great songs fall short. There's a real rush when the boys launch into their first big hit, Sherry, but it only lasts a moment. And so it goes with their succeeding masterpieces, Big Girls Don't Cry and Walk Like a Man, which follow on the stage even more rapidly than they did in real life. The songs are apparently judged too sacred to be turned into production numbers, so the actors just face front and sing them, with some barely relevant Pop Art drawings projected onto the unexcitingly ugly set as some kind of gesture towards visual interest.

They sing them well; but they cant compete. The excitement about these numbers in the pre-Beatles 60s was not just that they had better tunes than most doo-wop but that they were funny. Nick's solemn low notes, chiming in at grotesquely unexpected moments, were just as important as Frankie's high ones, and the show cant duplicate the effect.

Thats despite excellent vocalizing by John Lloyd Young (winner of best actor in a musical), who makes Frankie's character nearly as angelic as his voice; ironic that the real Valli should in later life have earned a regular role on The Sopranos, a show that casts something of a shadow over this one. That's especially the case with the would-be wise guy Tommy, excellently played by Christian Hoff (best featured actor in a musical) who has a great rapport with the audience.

Its not as great, though, as the one established in The Drowsy Chaperone by Bob Martin, who should have won best actor and who has the house totally under his control, in the way that only a shy person transformed by the lights can manage. This show isnt just narrated by Martin's character, Man in Chair; it's about him, and the show-within-the-show -- the imaginary 1928 musical comedy with the same title -- that he conjures into flesh-and-blood existence by playing its music is entertaining less in itself than in what it tells us about him. Its fun because he thinks it is; its not just a case of his enthusiasm being infectious, he is infectious. And somehow the book (by Martin himself and Don McKellar) keeps finding new ways of spinning out what is, on the face of it, a very slender conceit.

I've gone through stages with this show. I didn't see it, obviously, in its first incarnation as a wedding present to Martin and his bride Janet Van De Graaff; I wasnt invited. Nor did I see its first public outing on the Toronto Fringe. I did see the first full-length version at Theatre Passe Muraille, and hardly liked anything about it except Martin himself. I then saw Daniel Brooks rewritten, recast and delightfully absurd production at the Winter Garden, and was enchanted.

There have been further extensive revisions for the Broadway production, especially to the score; and though I wouldn't swear to it, I suspect that some of them have been for the worse. I certainly remember getting more pleasure in Toronto out of Greg Morrison's music and especially out of Lisa Lamberts lyrics. Or maybe its just that the Broadway production values make you expect more. Casey Nicholaw's direction and choreography are as stylish and inventive as could be wished; likewise David Gallo's sets and Gregg Barnes costumes. The performers are uniformly excellent; the only Tony went to Beth Leavel, who, in the title role that definitely isnt the lead, meets all the demands of being imperiously sozzled. But the star turn is definitely Sutton Foster, who goes through a full vocal, terpsichorean and acrobatic repertoire while protesting, I don't want to show off.

That's a great idea for a number. In fact, theyre mostly great ideas for numbers. But the songs themselves never reach the plateau graced by The Boy Friend or Dames at Sea, of being as good as the things theyre spoofing. Sometimes they don't even seem to know the rules; writers the show specifically invokes, for instance, would never have fallen back on invented words when faced with the need for a quadruple rhyme. Even the book sometimes falls short; the silliest musical of 1928 might well have let the boy lose the girl in the way shown here, but it would have found a more ingenious way of getting them together again. The numbers are intoxicating without being satisfying. But this bubbly at least means well. Like love, as described in the show's best lyric, it "would never purpose to offend." And in the end it isnt about the songs, its about the Man in Chair, who at one terrified moment denies that he likes musicals because he doesn't want the embarrassment of having to define his tastes, but in the end rises -- literally -- above it, gets swept away -- again literally -- by it. In a way they were right to deny Martin best actor in a musical.

What he actually is, is best actor as a musical.
