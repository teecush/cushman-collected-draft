In repertory till Nov. 4.

THE SOUND OF MUSIC

Festival Theatre, Stratford

- - -

There is no excuse for the Stratford production of The Sound of Music. There is nothing wrong with Stratford doing musicals; they used to have a troublesome air of being brought in, but in the last couple of years West Side Story and, especially, the epic Fiddler on the Roof have looked like integral parts of the festival, classics among other classics and held to the same acting and production standards.

But nobody can do much acting in The Sound of Music, and the show itself has been feeble since its premiere 40-odd years ago. That it should since have mutated from family treat to camp cult doesn't make it any better. (The whole phenomenon of the singalong Sound of Music chills my blood; the news that somebody is planning the same treatment for Fiddler, a good show, freezes it.)

Let's start, as the show advises us to do, at the very beginning. In a musical, that means the music. This was the last of the Richard Rodgers-Oscar Hammerstein scores, and the least. Rodgers could hardly write a tune that wasn't catchy but there are only two here of any distinction: Sixteen Going on Seventeen, with its tongue-in- cheek lilt and, despite its lyric, My Favourite Things; John Coltrane didn't adopt those chord changes for nothing.

Everything else is jingles and dirges. There are also, to be fair, a couple of cynical duets for the show's two heavies, the Anschluss trimmers who want our hero, Captain von Trapp, to give in to history and get on side with the Nazis. These are surprising, since they were cut from the film (from which everybody knows the show) and are mordant to a degree that cultural amnesiacs do not associate with R&H. At least they could be; Kelly Robinson's production seems determined to destroy them with whimsical choreography, as if the show needed to be made more anodyne than it already is.

C. David Johnson, the likeable actor who plays the captain, has a rough time throughout, what with not being very imposing and not being able to sing, but he is never worse treated than during these two numbers, for one of which he should not officially be on stage at all.

As a romantic musical, the show is a non-event; its relationships don't exist, musically or dramatically. The juvenile couple get one nice number -- pointlessly extended with a lot of jumping on and off benches, presumably to prove we're in a musical -- and are then virtually dropped; the show keeps promising to explore the uncomfortable fact that the boy is a fascist but never gets 'round to it. (The film, oddly enough, did.)

The captain's ill-advised entanglement with a fashion-plate lady plutocrat, given an atrociously patronizing performance by Mary Ann McDonald, is similarly taken for granted; she's there and then she isn't. Meanwhile, the supposed central relationship between the captain and Maria, the governess from the convent down the mountain, simply isn't there. One of the captain's seven children tips Maria off that papa is in love with her, and she could hardly be more surprised than we are since there has been absolutely no chemistry between them: compare that to the similar situation in The King and I.

All the same, the revelation sends Maria back to the nunnery from which, as all the world well knows, the mother abbess sends her right back again with the adjuration to climb every mountain: advice whose vagueness is only exceeded by its volume.

This, for some reason, is taken to constitute a first-act climax. At least that's when they raise the lights and start selling the ice- cream. Cynthia Dale sings sweetly as Maria (the Dales, as well as the hills, are alive with the sound of music) and gets some feeling into her religious perplexities; in her domestic situation, though, she can be no more than pert.

Of the junior von Trapps whom she turns into a folk-pop group I cannot bear to write, except to allow that it isn't their fault.

That goodnight song must surely be Rodgers and Hammerstein's nadir, Hammerstein's especially. This is the show with cute kids, cute nuns, even cute Nazis; the appearance of a gauleiter in lederhosen gets a big giggle. Why the captain so staunchly opposes them, and whether Maria goes along with him through love or conviction, are questions left untouched. Don't say "But it's only a musical"; the authors wouldn't have thanked you.

The Festival stage's corners have been rounded off and the balcony has been covered with a shroud to suggest an Alp. The abbey scenes have some atmosphere: not so, the captain's estate. Given that Stratford should not be doing the show at all, the fact it has been done on the whole poorly may be taken as a perverse sign of integrity. I wouldn't push the point, though.
