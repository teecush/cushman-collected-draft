robertcushman@lycos.com; The Seven Days of Simon Labrosse runs through May 1 at Artword, 75 Portland St., 416-366-7723, ext. 290.

I have not been a great cheerleader for the plays of Carol Frechette, but The Seven Days of Simon Labrosse is her best that I have seen. It is more eventful than the others, and it's the first one to have made me laugh.

It's quirky, even if some of the quirks are excessively whimsical. Even its subtitle, A Darkling Comedy, is something of a deterrent that way; it isn't the darkness that scares, it's the cute diminutive.

Simon Labrosse is a young man who needs to get a life and, even more pressingly, a job. The landlord is pounding on the door of his one-room apartment, demanding weeks or months of back rent, while Simon's attempts to pretend he isn't home grow increasingly frantic. Otherwise, he spends his evenings recording taped messages to send to his girlfriend, Nathalie, who is in Africa "helping the helpless." His days he devotes to looking for work. He has a different scheme for each day. Sometimes he goes house to house; other times, he presses his services on strangers in the street.

On his first day he offers himself as "an emotional stuntman" -- someone who will feel your pain. Unfortunately, the customer whose door he darkens is an aggressively angry man who likes to feel his own pain, and reacts much as people in ancient times used to do to unwelcome vacuum-cleaner salesmen. On his second, a lady at a cafe is first intrigued but then appalled when he suggests she employ him as her imaginary movie director, going through the motions of filming her life as if she were a star and the world her audience. (Well, isn't this what everybody wants nowadays? Apparently not, when it comes down to it.) He approaches a couple and suggests himself as a finisher of their sentences, which are, like most people's, chronically incomplete. At first this seems like Simon's best idea yet -- it certainly makes for the play's best-sustained scene -- but all he achieves with it is to set his two prospective clients off in different, and antagonistic, directions. Simon has an infallible knack of exacerbating other people's insecurities, while doing nothing to alleviate his own.

His private life goes much the same. Nathalie doesn't reply to his tapes, and though he remains stubbornly optimistic, he does begin to wonder at her silence.

Now, when you consider the play's title, you may think Simon's apparent confidence in the speed and efficiency of Canada Post to be, shall we say, touchingly exaggerated. They are supposed to get a package all the way to Africa, and to bring another one back, in just a week? Especially as Simon seems unsure of just where on the continent Nathalie might be -- all he remembers is that the country is one of those that ends with an "a." As noted, the play's whimsy is sometimes close to unbearable.

It does, though, give us a window into desperation. We come to realize just how much Simon needs to hope, and how little hope he reasonably has. It is clear early on that his love is unrequited, and the girl of his dreams almost precisely that.

There is another layer. Simon's tale is presented to us as a play within a play. In an attempt to give himself an identity and his existence a meaning, he has hired two actors to play the supporting roles in the story of his recent life.

Unfortunately -- nothing in Simon's world ever happens fortunately -- the actors have issues of their own. One has apparently been engaged mainly because her name also is Nathalie. Given half a chance or less, she will prattle on about the state of everybody's internal organs, especially her own. She is studying this; it seems to bring her great joy. Leo, her opposite number, feels no joy at all. He suffers from a neurological disorder that makes him incapable of uttering a cheerful word. Whenever Simon's script presents him with one, he freezes, and the play with him.

This, too, seems at first insufferably arch, but as the play goes on it becomes funnier and, with that, sadder. Simon is incorrigibly nice, and it seems part of his reason for putting the play on is to raise money to get Leo cured. Fat, the world being what it is, chance.

The production at the Artword is presented by Pleiades Theatre, who up to now have specialized in producing classical French plays. Now they give us a modern one. (Frechette is French Canadian, but her sensibility, especially in this piece, is straight out of the Parisian drama of the 1930s: gentle satire with a punch.)

But it is also presented "in association with" the Montreal Young Company, whose founder, the late Bill Glassco, had intended to direct it, and to whom the show is a tribute. Michael Shamata is now the director, and an adroit one, the production simple and visually clean. Two of the three performers are Young Company alumni.

The exception is Mike Hughes, whose Simon, a battered but unquenchable innocent, has the air of a white-faced clown who's gone out without his makeup.

Manon St-Jules pitches the actress Nathalie a little hard, but gets the parts she plays -- a series of studies in withdrawal -- very well. The prize performance, though, is Paul Fauteux's as Leo, consumed with rage himself and spreading it over every role he plays.

In practical terms, this means he gets to deliver a series of furious arias, at least one of which -- as a courier who arrives with a much-awaited package and is not happy about it -- he executes with virtuoso panache. He can build to a climax, puncture it, then pick up the pieces and build again.

The translation is by John Murrell, the English master of the tirade, and it, too, is a virtuoso job, though it makes you wish yet again that he was spending his time on his own plays rather than other people's.
