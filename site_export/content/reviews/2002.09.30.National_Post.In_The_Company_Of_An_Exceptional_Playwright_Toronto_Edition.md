THE SHAPE OF THINGS

Berkeley Street Theatre, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - Neil LaBute's The Shape of Things is a genuinely shocking play: maybe the only one Ive ever seen.

The shock is sprung by the plays narrative, and it is concentrated in one late revelation that feels like a blow in the face from an icy fist. It also feels thoroughly logical. It has roots; it's about people going to extremes, and the fact that you believe in them makes the shock last. I would hate anyone who had spoiled it for me in advance, so I will try to tread carefully.

The play's four characters are graduate students (median age, 22) at a liberal arts college in the American Midwest. We are principally concerned with Evelyn, majoring in fine arts, and Adam, majoring in English. Adam has a part-time job as a guard at the campus museum, and this is where he first encounters Evelyn, who is standing in front of a sculpture, a fig-leaved male nude whose prudishness she is all set to rectify. She has, literally, crossed the line that is supposed to separate the art object from the spectator. Later, she proves herself willing to cross that line metaphorically as well, which is far more alarming.

Adam will certainly be alarmed, though for the moment he is just inhibited and intrigued: intrigued enough to ask Evelyn for a date. Before you know it, they are lovers and Evelyn is making him over. Under her guidance Adam loses weight, dresses smarter, swaps his spectacles for contacts and has a nose job. He is also, we gather, having the best time of his life in bed. The transformation is duly noted by his friends: not, as Evelyn points out, that he has many. But we do meet Phil, Adams robustly philistine former roommate, and Phils fiancee, Jenny. In Adam's shy days, Jenny, fairly retiring herself, sat next to him in class but could never get him to take an interest. Now, however, he does, proving that the great thing about sexual confidence is that it does wonders for your sexual confidence. How far their dalliance goes is unclear, but it goes somewhere.

At this point, the play looks like it might be the American clone of Patrick Marber's English play Closer. We seem to be in for an evening of serious partner-swapping; what else would four characters get up to in a sexually charged setting? It doesnt happen, though; LaBute (best known for such movies as In the Company of Men) has more on his mind. He also writes more believable dialogue than Marber, or than most other current playwrights. His language is nervous, humorous and real; he uses the hesitancies and repetitions of youthspeak without ever becoming, like, you know, tongue-tied himself.

The play has also been widely compared to that most celebrated of all extended transformation scenes, Shaws Pygmalion: not, I think, very helpfully. In the first place, Eliza comes to Higgins asking to be changed, and in the second and more important, there is no explicit sex in their relationship. In Shaw, no one really gets hurt. Adam and Evelyn are wandering through a far thornier garden.

Evelyn repeats some advice given by her supervisor: Strive to make art but change the world. In the old committed days, that meant change society, on a broad political level. Evelyn, though, belongs to a generation that has largely given up on explicit politics (I don't actually believe this, but I'll go along with it), so she takes it to be the prerogative of the artist to use people, on an individual basis. Maybe without knowing it, she has taken the "but" out of that slogan and substituted an and. She wants art to affect people, on a visceral level --as the play itself does. Adam, for many reasons, is dubious about this. He keeps coming out with literary references that Evelyn fails to recognize. He belongs, in fact, to an older culture. He believes in boundaries -- as the play also does.

Its emotional sympathies are with the man (or perhaps I think so because mine are) but its strength is with the woman. Adams sins struck me as venial; Evelyn is not only breathtakingly manipulative but self-righteous, as only proselytizing artists can be. Notice, though, how the very names trip you up. The battle is unwinnable by either side. The plays penultimate scene all but screams "follow that." Its last scene takes up the challenge, successfully. Jim Guedo's CanStage production does superbly by it.

Amy Redford has been imported to play Evelyn; the Sundance Kid's kid seems to have trouble with theatre-sized projection but otherwise offers an accurately ruthless, driven performance; the character never apologizes for her actions, and the actress never apologizes for the character. Allan Hawco is a magic trick in himself; as the swathed nerd of the first scene, he looks and behaves practically middle-aged, and then grows gradually younger, outside and in. The other two are there to register, like the audience, varying degrees of shock. Jacob Barker shows, gradually, that a jock can have feelings. Amy Price-Francis (Stephen Sondheim once wrote a song called Multitudes of Amys; this productions casting seems designed to realize his vision) is very adept at showing a kind but baffled mind running for shelter.

One scene places most of the characters among the audience, to brilliantly unembarrassing effect. The plays London production, directed by the author, apparently used a deafening soundtrack by the Smashing Pumpkins that reportedly drove Harold Pinter and Lady Antonia Fraser out of the audience. I suspect my sympathies would have been with Harold and Antonia, and I applaud the decision to substitute a subtler, more varied score, culminating in Elvis Costello singing My Funny Valentine, with its own comments ("Your looks are laughable, Unphotographable") on life and art. The play doesn't need to deafen us, even if its author thinks it does. It also, despite the sex scenes, gets by without nudity. We are all long inured to the worst that playwrights can try to do us, but this one gets through our defences, as Strindberg presumably did when he was new. It's the best American play since David Mamet's Oleanna, and maybe better. Recommended, without reservation.
