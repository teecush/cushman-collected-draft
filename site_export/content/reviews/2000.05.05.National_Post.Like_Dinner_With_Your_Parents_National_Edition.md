IF YOU EVER LEAVE ME ... I'M GOING WITH YOU

Winter Garden Theatre, Toronto

The American comedian Kaye Ballard, who is of Italian extraction, once remarked of her upbringing: "You know what Italian families are like -- they're so Jewish." If You Ever Leave Me ... I'm Going with You! is an extended sermon on this text, expounded by the acting- and-writing husband-and-wife team of Renee Taylor and Joe Bologna.

She is Jewish, he is Italian, they are both New Yorkers and they have been living and writing together for upwards of 35 years. The show, then, is a celebration of competitive and collaborative volatility. It seems to be their anniversary present to themselves, and the obvious dangers of narcissistic cuteness are accurately demonstrated in the title, which would be terrible even without the three dots and the exclamation point.

It is not, however, all like that. The evening proves to be entertaining and embarrassing in about equal proportions; which, considering the possibilities, is not bad.

The duo begin by telling us who they are and welcoming us into their lives. Bologna, who is dark and seemingly casual, manages this with laid-back aplomb. Taylor, who is blond and busty and speaks at this point in a whispering gurgle, seems less suited to it, and indeed appears likely to burst out of just about everything.

Then, though, they go into a scene from one of their early plays; and we realize that the lady has been holding out on us. Once relieved from the burden of playing mistress of ceremonies, once given a character and a situation to explore, she is revealed as a superbly accomplished comic actor, with quirkily impeccable timing and an unfathomably eccentric personality.

She is actually playing an actress in this bit: One who turns up to audition for a role in a commercial, a mainstream glamour part for which she is entirely unsuited, but who arouses the friendly and amused attention of the director. The scene, they tell us, is somewhat autobiographical, but it stands in its own right.

So Bologna was a director when they first met and Taylor an actor: It figures. Even when he is shouting and she is listening (a frequent occurrence) he seems to be detached and she seems to be involved. She is deep in her own concerns, and is rock-like. He is a counterpuncher, like Burns to Allen or Sid Caesar to Imogene Coca; he actually looks like Caesar, minus some of the height and all of the lunacy, and in fact played him (or a version of him, called King Kaiser) in My Favourite Year.

The evening progresses as a history of their relationship, reflected in excerpts from their scripts for stage, screen and television. Some of these are quite well-known (It Had To Be You, Made for Each Other, Lovers and Other Strangers), others aren't (Bedrooms, Bermuda Avenue Triangle). The titles sort of blend into one another, and so -- on the evidence provided here -- do the plays. They all seem to be wry domestic comedies, about squabbling but unshakeably bonded couples. They all, if the current staging is to be trusted, consist mainly of duologues conducted from a pair of adjacent stools; even a scene in a restaurant finds the diners seated at their invisible table side by side rather than across.

If the audition scene remains the best it's because, besides having a touch of genuine theatrical madness, it depicts a professional situation as well as an emotional one. There is a similarly welcome whiff of the outside world in a sequence where the couple, now middle-aged, lecture an unseen son who is about to disgrace the family by getting divorced.

But even here what we learn of the relationships is strictly from sitcom. The impression is that, as the performers' skills have sharpened over the years, so the writers' have dulled. They are set, spiritually if not materially, in Miami or the Catskills, and that, along with maybe Las Vegas, seems to be their target audience.

Usually Taylor and Bologna play married or engaged, but sometimes she's a widow or divorcee whom he is trying to seduce. A final encounter, in which they're a couple of numbly amorous octogenarians, definitely hits below the Borscht Belt. But even the jolts seem calculated for maximum ingratiation.

It would be easier to take the best and forgive the rest, if it weren't for the framework. Bologna and Taylor, it seems, have always lived out their relationship in public; this show is a culmination, not a departure. They got married on TV, on The Merv Griffin Show. (This was in the '60s; they were ahead of their time.) The reason they say, is that their respective families, who would otherwise have boycotted the ceremony, could certainly not resist appearing on television. Nor did they; the production has the footage to prove it.

The couple have re-plighted their troth at regular intervals since, always in a ceremony from a different religion, and we get to see the videos of those, too. It's as if we're asked to congratulate them on the durability of their marriage; there's a place for that, but I don't think it's the theatre.

When solo comedians, male or female, get up and tell us about their relationships or lack of them, the performer-audience contract comes with what Chico Marx might have called a sanitary clause; they aren't necessarily talking about themselves, they could be making it all up. But when both partners are onstage they bring an actual relationship with them; and they could be lying about it, or they could be telling the truth, but it leaves you feeling queasy either way. (Well, it does me.)

No director is named -- though somebody must have arranged the furniture -- but there is a choreographer: Danny Daniels, who did the great routines for the movie of Dennis Potter's Pennies from Heaven and has here devised a very funny tango that Taylor performs bent over in a terrified state of slump, her whole body suggesting that if she straightens up something is going to fall off.
