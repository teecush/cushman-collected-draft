VIGIL

Bluma Appel Theatre, Toronto

- - -

You have been summoned from another town to the apartment of your aunt, who has sent you a letter informing you that she is mortally ill. She is your only living relative. You enter the room and, naturally, you ask the woman lying in bed in front of you the same question that would rise to the lips of anybody in your situation. You ask her when she is going to die. You expect her, obviously, to hurry up about it.

That is the first scene of Vigil, a startling two-character play by Morris Panych first staged in 1995 and now revived under its author's direction. The man is called Kemp and the woman Grace, though we owe this information to the program rather than to the dialogue, in which neither name is ever mentioned. There are good reasons for this: Kemp, who works in a bank, is a buttoned-tight middle-aged man, not the type to get on first-name terms with anyone; while Grace utters, in the course of the play, hardly any words at all.

That first line of Kemps, as hilarious as it is abrupt, is followed by a blackout. The play proceeds through a series of such scenes -- some of them very short, a couple silent -- during which, offstage, the seasons pass. Kemp, who had envisioned acting the angel of mercy for two days tops, finds himself stuck with the role for the best part, if thats the right phrase, of a year. He cooks Grace innumerable butterscotch puddings and takes her (unseen by us, but he mentions it) to the bathroom.

She, meanwhile, eats, knits (a lot) and looks surprised, as well she might. Kemp's remarks about the desirability of her early demise never let up; in fact, they become more insistent. He even goes to the lengths of rigging up a suicide machine for her, a Heath Robinson contraption that, depending on which string she pulls or button she pushes, can either electrocute her or knock her on the head. (It also incorporates a noose, though Kemp doesn't dignify this with an explanation.) Grace seems intrigued.

This is, of course, a one-joke situation. But there are several mitigating circumstances. One is that many (though not all) of the jokes restatements are very funny. Another is that this is one of those rare, liberating plays that actually breaks a taboo. Kemp gives voice to the silent thoughts that hover around many death- beds. And he has to do it more than once, or we wouldnt believe that he meant it.

In some respects, Panych lets us off easily. It isn't, after all, Kemp's mother lying there. And it isnt as if he was that close to his aunt. He saw her, we discover, just once during his extremely miserable childhood, and never forgave her for not coming back.

His parents, meanwhile, never forgave him for not being a girl. He claims, surprisingly, that they tried to turn him into a homosexual and, even more surprisingly, that they failed. Kemp is asexual and proud of it, though for somebody whos never experienced physical contact he pronounces on it with suspicious authority.

(Maybe he should have been a priest; at one point he says, "not being a Catholic, I didn't know how misery and self-loathing can be a religion." He sounds a bit jealous.)

He is also an arch-Scrooge, making bilious pronouncements on Halloween and Christmas as he peers out through one barely opened window. He resembles, in this position, Clov in Samuel Beckett's Endgame, reporting back to his blind and chairbound master on the desperate state of the outside world. And yes, this is, among other things, a Beckettian double act, as what double act is not? Kemp also looks forward, if thats the right phrase, to the protagonist of Panychs own Earshot, who tried to shut out the world with earplugs. Panych writes great misanthropes.

The one joke, to sum up, is on Kemp himself, and he knows it; he's the one in love with death. It's just that he wants Grace to be the one to propose. She, in her mind at least, is rather spry.

Then, in the second act, theres a revelation that explodes nearly everything. The ramifications even allow for something approaching a hopeful ending, one thats brought off without either sanctimony or sentimentality. The writing throughout is heady, made of crisp, weighted lines that float.

Panych's staging is matchingly confident, as is Ken MacDonald's crafty, crooked setting, which suggests a Canadian Chagall. The actors are two of the best, though they are not equally well cast.

Martha Henry, her mouth a perpetual O, is wondrous as Grace, contemplating the intruder as if he were an alien species, which in part he is. Her expressions, witty and wistful enough at the time, seem doubly so with benefit of hindsight.

Brent Carvers Kemp is technically impossible to fault (though on the third night, when I saw the show, he had some edgy patches) but also hard to believe. This is the first time I have seen Carver play a role that is other than life-affirming; even his Tartuffe relished life for himself, at whatever expense to others. Hes at his best here when Kemp, against all the odds, develops a twinkle, packing his suitcase and talking hopefully of travels with his aunt. This is his journey's end, and a touching one.

The earlier stages have twinkled as well, but less convincingly; Kemp's negativity can be funny, but it emerges here as whimsical rather than desperate. It's not that Carver avoids the darkness; he is obviously making a serious effort to explore it. We know from the past that he can be as fine in tragedy as in comedy, but black farce -- the grubby ground between the two -- seems at the moment to be foreign to him.
