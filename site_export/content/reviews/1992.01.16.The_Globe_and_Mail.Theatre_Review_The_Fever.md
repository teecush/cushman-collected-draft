THEATRE REVIEW THE FEVER

Thursday, January 16, 1992

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Toronto ONT -- WRITTEN BY WALLACE SHAWN Directed by Andy McKim Starring Clare Coulter At Tarragon Extra Space Rating: The Fever's naivete is its strength BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Toronto WALLACE Shawn, who wrote The Fever, says that he "loves" Clare Coulter, who is performing it; says even that he had her in mind when he wrote it. I admire her myself, and I certainly applauded her tension and variety in this monologue. It lasted nearly two hours and seemed much less.

All the same, I kept seeing past her and what I saw was the author himself. Shawn is a droll button of a man, a solemn twinkler, familiar on the edges of Woody Allen movies and as author and co-star of the play and film My Dinner with Andre, in which he played himself. This, plus the fact that I know him slightly, is my excuse for getting personal. With most plays it would not matter, but The Fever, which Shawn himself has performed in London and New York, is as confessional as anything gets.

It is a guilt trip: the result of several more literal trips that Shawn has taken to various parts of Latin America. There he discovered repression, torture, murder. He had always known they existed, of course, and had deplored them. But this was the first time he had seen the scars, met survivors, talked to witnesses. As a U.S. citizen, he had to accept that his own government had kept the oppressors in power. But The Fever doesn't dwell much on this, and it impales citizens of all other Western democracies just as firmly on its hook. Insofar as the two can be separated, it is less concerned with politics than with economics. It charges, to split a few more hairs, not that things are evil but that they are unfair.

The speaker summons a moment at which he lay vomiting in a foreign hotel bathroom, consumed by the knowledge that at the same moment, in the same city, a man was being executed. From there, he spirals back to his own daily life, to the friends and the shows and the restaurants, to the parents who loved and schooled and pampered him. Themes dissolve and recur, there are whorls within whorls, but writing and performance are firmly controlled and we generally know where we are.

The script is devoid of place or gender so Coulter can play it without changing a word. But its details fit perfectly into everything I know or surmise about Shawn's life as a reasonably successful playwright and actor in New York. When the script spoke regretfully of its subject's liberal upbringing, I thought of Shawn senior, for years the gentle editor of The New Yorker, that bastion of stylish decency.

The play is about stylish decency not being enough: actually about its being irrelevant. It pushes toward the realization that "the life I lead is irredeemably corrupt . . . (nothing) justifies what the beggar has and what I have."

Right, for all of us. So what is he going to do about it? He raises a couple of options. He could give all he has to the poor, or he could become an active revolutionary. He has not, as far as I know, done either of those things, and I cannot say I want him to. It's not just that I have rational doubts about the efficacy of either course. I don't want to be shown up.

He has, of course, done one thing. He has written this play. But this is just compounding the problem. (He says: "Artists who create works of art that inspire sympathy and good values do not change the life of the poor.") However, I don't think there is anything shameful about that. Unless, and it's a plausible position, concerned drama is more shameful than escapist drama because it pretends to help, and doesn't. But if political theatre is valid, then this is a refreshingly honest example of it: one that accepts guilt rather than shrilly apportioning it.

The Fever lacks the nightmarish brilliance that its mode and its title seem to demand. It spells everything out. It talks flatly of "the poor" instead of using euphemisms like "the workers." It laboriously discovers things that, at some level, we have always known. But its naivete is its strength. The author wryly admits to discovering Marxism just when everybody else had discarded it. But if the Marxist answers are discredited, the questions still remain. And this time, I like the guy who's asking them.
