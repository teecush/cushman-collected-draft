THEATRE REVIEW

The Sankofa Trilogy

Taragon Extra Space, Toronto

Her performance in the original production of 'da Kink in My Hair made it clear that d'bi. young, as she was then billed, was an intense and magnetic actress, who could be remarkably moving. She seemed so at one with her role, which was essentially a monologue, that one suspected her of having written it herself. Apparently she didn't, but a little later, in her solo play blood.claat and renaming herself d'bi.young anitafrika, she showed that she was also a writer and a compelling one. She also, playing several characters with pinpoint accuracy, proved to be both versatile and, something she hadn't had cause to demonstrate before, extremely funny.

blood.klaat has turned out to be the first of three linked plays, centring on three generations of Jamaican women, and collectively known as The Sankofa Trilogy. In benu, which I haven't seen, she played the daughter of her original protagonist; and now in word! sound! powah!, she's the granddaughter. (All three plays are being done in repertory, with the newest one getting the lioness's share of performances.) The emotional impact of the third play isn't as great as that of the first, but the writing is wittier. And from being merely an impressive performer, young has become an astonishing one.

In word! sound! powah! Benu Sankofa, the central character, is a drama student and aspiring dub poet, which makes her seem a younger version of the author herself. At the play's beginning, she's being interrogated, which largely means being brutally smacked, by a police constable, for being involved with a group called Poets in Solidarity. We flash back from there. There's an election in process, with two parties running; they are the PNP and the JLP and it's presumably intentional that they are virtually indistinguishable. young, who of course plays everybody, has the most fun with the PNP, they being the incumbents and running on the stirring platform, "We will continue to promise to invest in employment."

The solidarity poets themselves are more sympathetically portrayed, especially Peaches, a single mother, and Stamma, who stammers, at least when talking; he finds his voice in song (or, more accurately, chant). Their leader, Bobus, likes to refer to himself as "the Robin Hood of poets," and he does it often enough and vaingloriously enough to make it hard for us to take him as seriously as he takes himself and as the play seems to want him to take us. It's hard, too, to place as much weight as the show would like on Benu's past and present relationship with her grandmother. As a text, the piece doesn't quite add up; and toward its end it gets confusing.

This, though, seems like quibbling in the face of young's lightning transitions from one character to the next, and of the precision with which she delineates every one of them. She can offer a savage cartoon of a bully, an affectionate swaggering sketch of a poseur (several of these), a full-blown painting of a heroine who can be fearful or triumphant. Indeed one of the play's great qualities is that Benu herself, who might easily have been a narrating cipher, comes so fully to life.

Many of the characters like to buttonhole the people they're addressing, but it's Benu, finding herself as writer and performer, who engages the audience most boldly and most warmly. This, not coincidentally, is one of young's own great strengths; she's the rare performer who, when she makes one-on-one contact, can make the recipient smile rather than cringe. This seems to apply not just to the people in the auditorium but to her three musicians, all excellent in their own right. When she talks she's moving, hilarious, beguiling. When song and dance come into the mix, she's electric.
