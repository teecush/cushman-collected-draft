THEATRE REVIEW ROAD MOVIE

Friday, April 19, 1996

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Written by Godfrey Hamilton Directed by Lorenzo Mele Starring Mark Pinkosh At the Studio Theatre, York Quay, until April 22 Rating: -1/2 A trip through bereavement Haunting portraits save play from the flaw at its centre. BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail ROAD Movie is a one-man show whose protagonist/narrator remarks on two occasions that there is a song about everything. I second that, and offer an example.

Theres a Wes Montgomery tune called West Coast Blues whose lyrics begin "I got a baby on the East Coast, I got a gig going on the West Coast . . ." and then proceeds to tell how the singer is tired of all this separation and intends to pack his bags and join his lover on the other side of the United States. Road Movie is a gay variation on this theme with the locations reversed.

Joel, our hero, has a gig going on the East Coast: an uptight and presumably corporate position in New York. Once upon a flashback he met Scott, who was leading a comparatively untrammelled life houseboat-sitting in Sausalito. It was only a one-night something-or-other but it promised more and now Joel is doing what Scott prophesied he would. Tired of bestowing and receiving insincere injunctions to have a nice day (they don't do that in California?) he sets off on the freeway to re-encounter the love of his life. Wherever he goes, though the tone of the play is comic, he meets distressed people. I was reminded of an old Peggy Lee LP called Blues Cross Country. (There is also an album title about everything.) What he really keeps encountering is bereavement. There is a woman in Texas whose daughter committed suicide with a drug overdose. There is another in Georgia whose son died of AIDS and who commemorates him by passing out the plastic: handing out condoms to other gay men. There is a girl in California who wears an earring for each of her dead friends; her ears are festooned. There is Scott's own obsession with a memorial to the American dead of Vietnam. Why, he wonders, was there not a similar shrine to the victims of AIDS? They too, he says, died for their country.

They didnt, at least no more than the victims of any other disease. A majority of people, homosexuals included, die of causes other than AIDS, and their passing inflicts just as much grief to the survivors. The plays insistence on its unsustainable analogy with Vietnam seriously weakens it, and might destroy it altogether if it were in a more conventional form. But a monologue, though it has its own inherent limitations, also has its own freedoms. "I want my friends back" says Joel repeatedly. As a general statement of loss, that's fine and touching. We can't respond to it very deeply because we havent met any of his friends; we only know Scott as the subject of one of Joels impersonations. As a fully realized character in a real play he could be far more moving. But of course if he were a badly realized character the whole thing would collapse. You pay your money.

Road Movie comes to us from Starving Artists, a California-based company of two. There are Godfrey Hamilton, author, and Mark Pinkosh, performer. Hamilton seems to be a competent craftsman with a good sense of structure and a weakness for easy effects. He tells his story economically and builds to the best kind of surprise ending: one that in restrospect seems inevitable. But he also permits himself spurious lyrical outbursts, one of them an opening sequence that gets reprised at the end, and one coincidence - Joel unknowingly stops off at Scott's birthplace - that is meant to be affecting but is actually sheer kitsch.

He also balances some stabbingly hilarious lines with standard bitch- comedy bits about Judy Garland and Liza Minelli. Enough already. Admittedly Pinkosh dispenses these very well. He does almost everything very well. He starts with a spiky, shaven presence: maybe too abrasive for the smooth Manhattanite Joel seems meant to be but a launching-pad for the rest of the show. He is a high-energy, high-precision performer who passes at lightning speed from one voice to another and back again. This is standard practice among monologists, as among ventriloquists, and it has little to do with the rhythms of real life, but it certainly moves the show along, while adding an air of desperation that is both relevant and useful. He can also relax it. His parade of sad and funny women goes beyond female impersonation and into acting. In each case, he starts with an accent and then works inwards. What emerges are tartly charitable portraits that haunt and linger. World Stage continues tonight with Line in the Sand at the Tarragon Theatre; Hysterica Passio at the Brigantine Room; Que-Cir-Que at Ann Tindal Park; The Waste Land at the Gooderham and Worts historic district; Riot at the Factory Theatre.
