HOSANNA

Tallulah's Cabaret, Buddies in Bad Times, Toronto

----

TORONTO - Hosanna, the ageing drag queen with a fixation on Elizabeth Taylor, and Cuirette, his leather-clad roommate and bedmate, have returned to the Toronto stage sporting very broad, or "verra braid," Scots accents. I was initially convinced the action of Michel Tremblay's 1973 Quebec play had moved with them, to Glasgow; and it was a surprise, when they started dropping place names, to realize the action is still supposed to be going on in Montreal.

In Scotland itself, which reasonably enough is where this translation by Martin Bowman and Bill Findlay was first presented, the convention would have been transparent; when everybody talks like that, the brogue can belong to any time and any place. The language does restore to the play some of the salt that it presumably has in the original French; for us, though, it also sets up some quite specific expectations, and I can't see that the play would be harmed in any way if those expectations were indulged.

The characters would make very believable Glaswegians. As things are, you find yourself wondering how these two guys, who the dialogue tells us went right through the Montreal school system, managed to retain unaltered the speech patterns of their parents, and by what fluky coincidence they have ended up together.

Tremblay's play, which stands up better than most of its period, shows each of the two reaching self-realization through self- humiliation. Hosanna finds himself the victim of a cruel joke when he goes to a ball dressed as Taylor's Cleopatra; as he tells us in a long monologue, "everybody was dressed like her -- only better." What was meant to be a personal peak turns into Armageddon.

Cuirette, meanwhile, who has been maintaining that he is not really gay and that he is in fact a great womanizer, is similarly jolted into giving up his fantasy. "You are a man," he tells Hosanna as the latter climbs into bed with him minus the Hollywood-Egyptian lingerie, also meaning, of course, "I love a man."

In a way it's a puritanical rebuke, ten years ahead of time, to La Cage aux Folles (musical version), where the transvestite's anthem, "I am what I am," really translated into, "I am what I'm not." Either way, this kind of affirmation always comes across as sentimental, though doing it without music certainly makes it more palatable, as well as more economic.

Tremblay, despite the play's tough veneer, is certainly not averse to the odd tear-jerking platitude. When Cuirette leaves the house after a quarrel (the two spend the entire first act metaphorically at one another's throats) he stops in the doorway to remark, "You want to know something really stupid ..." There is the predictable pregnant pause, and then the equally predictable punchline: "I love you." The audience could probably say it for him.

A two-person play that is half bitchy recrimination and half tearful confession needs virtuoso performance. What it gets here, from a company optimistically designated Shockalot Productions, is decent to disappointing; Both actors look right and sound variable.

Director Wendy Thatcher (who, as an actress, is a mainstay of the Shaw Festival) staged the play in London with an actual Scottish actor, Alistair Hudson, in the title role. He plays it for her again now, and is absolutely fine in the give-and-take of the first half; haughtily funny with poignancy peeping through. This does not ripen, however, into the magnetic desperation that would be needed to carry us through the long monologue. Here he is perfectly sensitive, perfectly intelligent, but no more.

Cuirette is played by a local actor, Tony Nappo, who manages the accent perfectly but doesn't seem to have enough energy left over for the rest of the role, so the concept begins to seem self- defeating. To put it another way, he's often inaudible, though to be fair, the acoustics of Buddies in Bad Times' cabaret space don't help any. (The sightlines are nothing to write home about, either.) David Wootton's set is a colourful assemblage of bric-a-brac.

Theatrical legend holds that Hosanna's divided identity in some way mirrors that of Quebec itself. This is to pile ludicrous weight on a small but effective domestic drama. It also suggests that 30 years ago, people were so hungry for political theatre that they would swallow almost anything in its name.
