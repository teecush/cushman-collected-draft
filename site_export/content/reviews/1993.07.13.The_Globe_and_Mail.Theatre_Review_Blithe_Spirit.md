THEATRE REVIEW BLITHE SPIRIT

Tuesday, July 13, 1993

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont. -- WRITTEN BY NOEL COWARD Directed by Susan Cox Starring Jennifer Phipps, Diane d'Aquila, Sarah Orenstein and David Schurmann At the Festival Theatre Rating: True to the spirit of Noel Coward The Shaw Festival production is one of the most familiar Cowards, with a plot best recounted in shorthand and adding up to seances and bickering, but it is a combination that fills out an evening of substantial length with no fatigue BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont. PROBABLY the salvation of the Canadian theatre will not lie in accomplished revivals of Noel Coward. Nevertheless, here is another one.

The play is one of the most familiar Cowards, with a plot best recounted in shorthand. Successful novelist consults medium for research purposes, and finds himself haunted by ghost of first wife. Second wife is chagrined, and scenes of three-sided jealousy ensue. That is all there is: seances and bickering. It is a measure of Coward's mature skill that the combination fills out an evening of substantial length with no fatigue.

Part of the secret is that the witching gets equal time with the bitching. This makes a change from the theatrical inbreeding of most Coward comedies. Madame Arcati, the medium, is probably the best role Coward ever wrote for anyone other than himself. Jennifer Phipps, who plays her here, is the first actress I have seen to, if you like, lay the ghost of Margaret Rutherford: a substantial ghost since the original performance is still available on film. The key to the character's magnitude is her professionalism: her pride in her work and her total commitment to it. Rutherford's aura was that of an enthusiastic sports- mistress: thoroughly at home in her own milieu and quite unfazed by the dry-martini sophistication of her hosts, but inevitably a little dotty when seen through their eyes. (And a standing rebuke to them because of it.) Phipps is a suaver sort, a clubwoman with good contacts in this world as well as the next, sublimely matter-of-fact. "My reply" she says when confronted with skepticism, "is fiddle-de-dee." Rutherford made that sound like a gleeful incantation. Phipps makes it an unanswerable debating point.

Elvira, the disembodied wife, is usually reckoned a pretty good part too, coming back from the beyond to wreak mischief and sow temptation; Sarah Orenstein plays her quite effectively without getting beyond the standard model of husky minx. Ruth, the embodied one, is usually deemed a pill: both to her spouse and to the actress saddled with playing her. But one of the greatest strengths of Susan Cox's production is that it makes Ruth the comic fulcrum of the play. Unlike her husband she cannot see Elvira, only be jealously aware of the havoc she's creating. Trying to be patient, she points her remonstrances, logically enough, at the spot where she imagines her rival to be sitting. This of course is a losing game; and Diane d'Aquila's gyrations as she strives to keep up with an invisible opponent have been honed to a fine farcical edge. When she finally explodes, goaded too far, it is a virtuoso job: a hairpin bend, driven at speed.

Charles, the disputed husband, is accorded no such release; and inbuilt frustration may explain why Coward himself never bothered to play him. He has to stand around being alternately pained and polished and you have to be Rex Harrison to make much of that. David Schurmann once played an interesting Macbeth (another character oppressed by ghosts and wives, though not simultaneously), but in lounge comedy, it seems, he merely lounges. He sounds fretful, bland, and finally smug.

Assisting at the seance (do they need a quorum?) are a local doctor and his wife. Mary Haney, who I imagine is the only actress ever to play Mrs. Bradman and Saint Joan in the same season, dispenses the special joy with which an actress who has proved herself in a major role can fill out a minor one. She is the quintessential physician's helpmeet, English county style, with a keen, friendly disposition and a snort for a laugh, while Richard Farrell as her elderly husband is a walking piece of history, an inch away from petrifaction. They add to one of the great strengths of play and production: their sense of place. You feel a social round, of drinks and dinners and trips to the cinema, going on at a comfortable distance from town. You also, though it's never mentioned, feel the engulfing presence of war. (The play was written in 1941.) We get amusing glimpses of the maid (Deborah Lambie) who's been told not to rush, alternately sprinting and moonwalking across the back of the stage. There is a world here to be disrupted; and Charles's house, which is its centre, makes a better battleground for looking so seductive when at peace. What is billed as the living room seems to double as an exceptionally desirable library. The audience plainly wanted to live there; and so did I. Leslie Frankish is the designer, and at the end his set does a terrific turn of its own.
