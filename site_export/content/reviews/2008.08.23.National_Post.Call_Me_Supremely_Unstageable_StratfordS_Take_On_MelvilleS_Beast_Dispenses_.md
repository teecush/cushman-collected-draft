Its surprising to find a dramatization of Moby Dick that doesn't begin with the summoning words Call me Ishmael. It is, of course, uncommon to find any stage version at all of the Herman Melville novel, given its bulk and metaphysical scope and its having as a title character what Kenneth Tynan, discussing Orson Welles's attempt half a century ago, "the supremely unstageable whale."

Morris Panychs new adaptation in the Stratford Studio not only does without the opening words, but without any dialogue at all. There is some voice-over narration, delivered indeed by Ishmael, the only survivor of Captain Ahabs doomed voyage, but it comes to us distorted by echoes and overlays, as if Panych, who also, of course directs, were determined to allow us as little verbal comfort as possible. There does seem to be progressively more of it as the evening goes on, in possible acknowledgment that the further the story goes, the more help we need.

Even those who haven't read the book can be counted on to know its bedrock premise: Ahab is in monomaniacal quest for the white leviathan that bit off his right leg. As crocodiles to Captain Hook, so whales to him. Hook, of course, was trying to avoid his fate, while Ahab positively courted his, but no matter: Each ends up in the belly of his respective beast. Melville clinches the matter, before the expedition gets under way, by having Father Mapple in the whalers' chapel preach a sermon on Jonah. (Did he, one wonders, deliver this same oration every Sunday? He could hardly have found another text as well-fitted to his congregation.)

Panychs production is, like his triumphant version of Gogols The Overcoat, a ballet for actors. As before, he has the expert collaboration of Wendy Gorling and Shaun Amyot, credited respectively with movement and choreography, though it's hard to see where one ends and the other begins. He also has rich spare settings (Ken Mac-Donald), wonderful weathered costumes (Dana Osborne), and superb lighting (Alan Brodie). Some things are brilliant; no one who sees it will forget the departure of the Pequod from Nantucket, the cast ranked on the rungs of a ladder, their shirt-tails billowing so they are at once the sailors and the sails. At the other end of the trip, each boat in the last whale-hunt is evoked by an actor running between two outstretched planks held by other actors. The final catastrophe, though, is a let-down; and though I didn't exactly tire of the continuous music by Debussy (to whose mysteries the show is nearly as much a homage as it is to Melvilles), I did begin to crave some variation. And I certainly didnt expect the Panych intermission; it breaks the spell.

But the main problem is that, where The Overcoat had a plot that could be recreated without words, Moby-Dick (he's a hyphenated whale in the original, though not in any adaptation) has a theme that's almost wholly dependent on them and a hero who only lives through them. You have to buy into the Biblical rhetoric of Melvilles Ahab, and to his habit of expressing himself in impromptu iambics, whether hubristically defiant (Id strike the sun if it insulted me) or incantatory ("what ho, Tashtego, let me hear thy hammer"); if you do, the effect is magical on page and could well be on stage. Without them, Ahab is just a seafaring man with one leg and a glint in his eye; David Ferry plays him effectively on that level, and there is an undeniable kick in seeing so verbal an actor robbed of his tongue. I could not, though, escape the suspicion that if he did open his mouth, what would come out would be the voice of Robert Newton as Long John Silver.

There isn't much conflict in Ahab; he defines the word "obsessive." But there are forces pulling at him; and these -- his good and bad angels --are convincingly externalized in the persons of Starbuck, the first mate (as opposed to the coffee shop), who's decently played by W. Joseph Matheson-- though he, too, could probably use some words -- and Fedallah, Ahabs personal harpooner to whom he virtually sells his soul, and who is wickedly well played by Shawn Wright, as a maritime Fu Manchu. (He doubles, ironically enough, as Father Mapple, preaching a commanding silent sermon.) But what emerges as the central relationship, and is virtually a love story, is that between Ishmael (Shaun Smyth, very sympathetic) and Queequeg, the heathen harpooner (Marcus Nance, both fearsome and endearing), who cradles the tiny idol of his tribal god, and bequeaths it as a sacred heartbreaking relic.

Great names these characters have, though most of them of course go unspoken; you have to a) know the book, b) know the actor and c) consult the program to work out that the droll figure doing imitations of Ahab behind his back is the third mate, Flask, played by Eddie Glen. There are three women in the cast, billed as Sirens, hence representing the lure of the sea and, finally, Moby Dick himself: a task for which they are far too dainty. So the supremely unstageable whale remains unstaged. Im not sure he has to be; the Welles production was before my theatre-going time, but I did once see a version at the Royal Exchange Theatre in which the stage was spread at the last moment with a huge tarpaulin which seemed to capture both monster and ocean. There's nothing like that here. But Panych's show has its own climax: an unexpected but perfectly logical epilogue that's a quiet knockout. Call me astounded
