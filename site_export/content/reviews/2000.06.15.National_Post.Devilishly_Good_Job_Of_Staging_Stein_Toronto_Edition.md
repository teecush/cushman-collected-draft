NOT FAUST

The Great Hall, 1087 Queen St. West, Toronto

Snappily subtitled "a play in purgatory," Not Faust is an adaptation by Jennifer Tarver, who also directs, of Doctor Faustus Lights the Lights, a short and characteristically teasing play written by Gertrude Stein, rather late in her life. It turns out not to be purgatorial at all. In fact it is, in its own way, heavenly.

It even manages, most of the time, to render Stein's syllable- chopping prose, or whatever it is, without giving the audience a headache.

Steins original play seems, as far as I can follow it, to be split in two. One half concerns a woman referred to as Marguerite Ida Helena Annabel, which mouthful incorporates the names of the two most important women in the life of Goethes Faust. She has scenes with a viper, who presumably is some kind of devil, and Stein's Faustus may wish to be damned in order to impress her. But her main importance in this adaptation lies in the ritual repetition of her name. The wittier, more dramatic, section of the evening shows Faustus as a man of the 19th century who has invented the electric lightbulb.

This seems to make him as much malefactor as benefactor, and, of course, a modern Prometheus as well as a Faust (but then, people have always gotten the two legends confused). As he himself puts it, after a little prodding, I am the only one who can turn night into day. Which, if you bring the theology of the Middle Ages into the science of the modern world, might well suggest a presumptuousness meriting damnation.

As a man enlightened as well as enlightening, this Faustus doesn't believe in hell. In this he resembles his Elizabethan namesake, Christopher Marlowes Doctor Faustus, who jovially proclaims, "I think, Hells a fable." To which Mephistopheles, his attendant devil, knowingly replies, Ay, think so, till experience change thy mind.

The Stein equivalent of this is to have Faustus ask, "What the hell did you want my soul for?" (the question could hardly be better phrased) and then to express serious doubts as to whether he has one. To which the devil replies -- soothingly, in this version -- Yes, dear Doctor Faustus, yes of course you have a soul. There seems to be professional pride involved.

Perhaps the greatest achievement of Tarver and her three actors is to take Stein's lines -- with their deliberately stunted vocabulary, their relentless repetition, their general air of prose pretending to pretend to be verse -- and to turn them into drama. Or, to be fairer, to release the drama that's lurking in them.

The story of Faust, especially when you put it into a modern setting, is a perpetual paradox, and the style here is a way of ambushing and exposing the absurdity without slighting the tragedy.

Marlowes Faustus feared and suffered damnation; Goethes escaped it; Stein's scoffs but ends up embracing it, intellectual to the end. (To qualify for the inferno, he commits murder, of a boy and a dog. But they, too, are figures of the devil. Go figure.)

James Kirchner plays him with momentous searching quietness, twisting and lucid at the same time. (At the beginning he wears a bowler hat, the perpetual mark of the modernist avant-garde going slumming in show business.)

The other two players split the role of the devil, Raul Tome being dangerously boyish and petulant and Michelle Polak intelligent and enticing, though not given the scope to be anything else. A scene in which these two very civilized fiends toast the ensnared doctor in what looks like champagne -- conceivably before toasting him by some other method altogether -- is especially vivid.

The playing space is a basement. in a long white room reached by a bleak flight of steps, illuminated -- to put you in the mood for a play about the risks and benefits of light -- by a couple of spare blue bulbs. The play itself is staged and lit with similar spareness, similar intensity and remarkable variety. Jan Komarek is the designer and also collaborated on the direction.

The play begins (of course) in darkness, our consciousness pierced only by what sounds like the working of a giant, rusty winch. For this, and for subsequent effects, Ben Grossman -- credited with "sound composition" -- deserves any awards that may be going in his department.

The pace is deliberate (Not Faust is not fast) and every word, noise, step and gesture, seems precisely calibrated, but nothing is tedious or mechanical.

This is my first encounter with Tarver's work. I knew she had a considerable underground reputation as a drama and opera director (this show was first seen in another form at last years Toronto fringe) and its good to report that the empress has excellent taste in clothes.

For rigour and imagination, this production -- at the moment the best show in Toronto -- bears comparison with last year's Tarragon production of Goethe's Faustian thing. The text of this one is hardly as rich, but in trimming and reordering it, Tarver shows it off to excellent advantage.

She gives us enough. And enough, in this context, is very nearly as good as a Faust.
