Robert Cushman is the theatre critic for the National Post

Theatre is about occasion, and the greatest night of the year was a last night at Stratford: William Hutt's Prospero in The Tempest, a valedictory performance in the greatest of valedictory plays: a play whose climax is a magician bidding farewell to his art.

Hutt is not apparently giving up acting but he is unlikely to play any more Shakespearean leads, and his half-century of achievement was magisterially summed up here.

In Richard Monette's lucid production, Hutt played with delightful ease and blazing authority. We cried and we cheered; it was like joining hands with history.

There are signs that the tradition might be maintained. It's too early to judge the effect of Stratford's new training schemes, but the standard of supporting playing there is creeping up (witness this year's Macbeth and Richard II) and, back in Toronto, the Soulpepper company's succession of shows unfolded like one long, exhilarating first night.

Their five productions -- from the urbanity of The Play's the Thing to the starkness of Endgame -- accounted for an almost embarrassing 50% of the year's best, culminating in the overflowing achievement of Platonov, a heartening and heart-warming display of casting (and acting) in depth. Quantity does count.

At the other extreme, Daniel Brooks' Tarragon Theatre production of Goethe's Faust Part One put an epic on the table with only five actors and hardly missed a trick.

This, like Platonov, was a pulsating show that took an unwieldy and supposedly unplayable text and made it live. I'd like to see Brooks move on to the even more problematic Part Two, or direct Ibsen's Peer Gynt, preferably for Soulpepper with Albert Schultz as Peer.

The most pleasant suprise of the year was Michael Healey's The Drawer Boy; an audience that had arrived at the Factory expecting nothing in particular found a funny, moving new play in a production to match. Healey's subsequent appearance as co-author of the humdrum comedy The Road to Hell -- this time we were expecting a lot -- was, let us hope, merely an aberration.

Fabrizio Filippo's Waiting for Lewis (from Theatre Direct) was children's theatre that didn't feel like children's theatre, and Jason Sherman's It's All True was flashily enjoyable if ultimately pointless. Both The Drawer Boy and It's All True were in part homages to older shows, making this seem even more like a year of revivals.

This, by the way -- unless you're one of those indomitably optimistic souls who expects a half dozen new masterpieces a year -- is a good thing.

Honest Ed: The Bargain Musical was a discount delight. The theatre of bigger musicals felt the draught left by the end of Livent (Swingstep made North York's Ford Centre feel like a ghost ship) but Ed & Son at least offered us a fortuitous Kander-and-Ebb retrospective, with Chicago coming through for the second and much- improved time and Cabaret over-heated but still hot.

Off my official beat, but certainly a theatrical experience, was Diana Leblanc's production of The Rape of Lucretia for the Canadian Opera Company. Canadian Stage (ironically perhaps, but who cares?) brought us the best foreign plays: How I Learned To Drive, The Beauty Queen of Leenane and Alan Ayckbourn's Communicating Doors, mysteriously described as an English bedroom farce by critics who can't have seen much English bedroom farce.

The year's most sinister development was the proliferation, in both commercial and subsidized theatres, of navel-gazing, one- person shows. Transcending this category, and most others, was Ronnie Burkett's Street of Blood: One extraordinary man with 40 puppets, all in the service of a genuine play whose reach only slightly exceeded its irresistible grasp.

And here, finally, is my off-the-cuff list of the year's best performances: Brian Bedford (taken for granted, as usual) in The School for Scandal; Richard McMillan in Glenn; Soulpepper's Diego Matamoros in everything; Peter Donaldson in Our Town; Kristen Johnson in A Streetcar Named Desire; the entire ensemble in Platonov; ditto in You Can't Take It with You (the Shaw Festival's best show); Sarah Orenstein in Heartbreak House and Getting Married; Brent Carver in Parade (admittedly at the end of last year and only in New York, but if you caught his number on the Tony Awards you'll know why he has to be here); John Jarvis in Love's Labour Lost; Alma Cuervo in Cabaret; David Fox and Jerry Franken in The Drawer Boy; William Hutt and Ronnie Burkett as aforesaid; Waneta Storms and Maggie Huculak in Faust; and Fiona Reid, crowning a wild year, as The Beauty Queen of Leenane.
