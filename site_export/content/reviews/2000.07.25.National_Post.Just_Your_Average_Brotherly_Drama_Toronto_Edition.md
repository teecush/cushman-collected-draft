ON AN AVERAGE DAY

Helen Gardner Phelan Playhouse

On an average day in an average American play, you might well expect to meet up with two long-separated, father-obsessed brothers, inspired by Arthur Miller while playing reverential homage to Eugene O'Neill. Their reunion could be staged in the kind of derelict shack that Sam Shepard might call home, and they would express their distrust of one another and of everybody else in paranoid exchanges of the kind patented by David Mamet.

All U.S. drama would be there, at least in its macho aspects; and though On an Average Day, which unites all these elements, finally manages to exorcise its ghosts and establish its own identity, it takes nearly as long about it as the brothers themselves. John Kolvenbach, who wrote and directed it, runs a company in New York called Rocking Chair Test, and he brought his play to this year's Toronto Fringe Festival, picking up a couple of Canadian actors on the way. The production is now enjoying an extended run on the University of Toronto campus.

The Kolvenbach siblings are called Robert and Jack. They were abandoned by their dad when Jack was 15 and Robert eight; they lived together, Jack taking care of his kid brother, until one day Jack followed his father's example and decamped in his own turn. (A lot of tunes are relayed over the sound system before the play starts, but Hit the Road, Jack is unaccountably not among them.)

Fifteen years later Jack returns, to find Robert in a state of slump, at a kitchen table on which stand several beer cans and a bottle of whisky. About the only other furnishing is a fridge, crammed apparently with additional cans, and emitting, as we are constantly assured, an overpowering fungoid stench. Robert's first words to the brother he hasn't seen in years are: "I didn't do it, Jack"; we discover that he is on trial for something closely resembling murder.

Jack is impassive and taciturn; Robert, understandably in the circumstances, is twitchy and unstoppably voluble. Gradually and twistingly, we learn the facts of his case; what he did and why he did it. We also learn his opinion of his lawyer, whose courtroom technique apparently makes great use of significant pauses. ("That's a pause for 'shall we clap?' " says Robert; "I hate that." It's at moments like these, charged with free-floating aggression, that the dialogue is at its most Mamet, though it doesn't have his wit.) We also learn that everybody is town is looking at Robert like they want him dead, which again is understandable.

Robert discovers that Jack has a gun, and immediately decides that his brother has come to kill him. The drama now jumps into higher, if not immediately plausible, gear, as Jack, threatened but seemingly unperturbed, starts telling his brother the truth about their past lives. In particular -- and this is where the play resembles the perennial Miller's tale -- he tries to demolish Robert's illusions about the father who once left them "on an average day" and who is assuredly not coming back. And why should Robert believe that he would? Because Jack used to tell him so, along with many other stories, as a way of getting him to sleep. Finally we discover Jack's own hang-ups; his fear that he may be re- enacting the sins of his father. In his quieter way, he is as desperate as his brother. And it is here that the play, skirting but avoiding sentimentality, at last becomes its own animal, resigned and reconciling.

Both the performances are decent, though the theatre's unfriendly acoustic tends to swallow up the high-register neurosis of Jonathan Higgins' Robert; John Robinson's Jack, laid-back even when agonizing, has a better time and provides one, too. Both play and production have the air of an exercise, skilfully carried out.
