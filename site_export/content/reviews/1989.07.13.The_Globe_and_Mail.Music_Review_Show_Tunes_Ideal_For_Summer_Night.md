MUSIC REVIEW Show tunes ideal for summer night

Thursday, July 13, 1989

ROBERT CUSHMAN

BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail AND NOW, said John McGlinn, raising his conductors baton over the Toronto Symphony at the Ontario Place Forum, "we'll give the folks over the hill a run for their money." McGlinn was presiding over a concert largely devoted to the Broadway songs of Rodgers and Hart, and rock music had been wafting competitively through the mountain greenery.

Admission may be free, but in other ways you pay heavily for the balmy atmosphere down by the lake. A huge circular amphitheatre is not the best environment for an evening of sophisticated song, especially with soloist and orchestra being whirled slowly and inexorably on the rotating platform to give every spectator a fair fragment of the action. McGlinn promised that his singer Kim Criswell would treat us to the pungent, full original lyrics of Bewitched and so she did, with perfect clarity; but our isolated chuckles could hardly jell into a homogenous response.

There is on the other hand a perpetual joyous youthfulness about the R& H catalogue that sits very well on a summer night. It infects even the sadder, more mature songs, such as the bittersweet masterpiece It Never Entered My Mind, which was taken at an unexpectedly brisk tempo, Criswell singing with a most effective toughness enhanced by some piquant interjections in the accompaniment.

What we were hearing, here and throughout, were the original Broadway orchestrations, long thought lost and now being reinstated by McGlinn with missionary zeal. Symphony orchestras give him a bigger string section than was ever penned in a Broadway pit; these gentlemen seemed to be having fun, and we got the essential keyboard duo who provided a fascinating contrast in visual styles, one intent and the other laid-back. McGlinn has already worked similar restorative magic on the music of Kern and Gershwin; he conducted the complete recorded Show Boat over which I rhapsodised some months ago, and this Rodgers and Hart programme is the warm-up for a forthcoming album of the songs with his Show Boat leading lady Frederica von Stade. Wasn't this, I inquired when I met him next morning, a little hard on the deputising Criswell?

Kim, he said, "is doing all right." She has recorded two Cole Porter shows for him: Kiss Me Kate (not due out for a year) on which she sings Bianca and, coming in October, Anything Goes on which she has the Ethel Merman role.

McGlinn is a plumply dapper thirtysomething New Yorker, still delightedly surprised at being able to make a good living fulfilling a personal dream. Criswell, who substitutes a trained mezzo belt for the instinctive Merman bray, has the air as well as the voice of a modern soubrette; from the perplexing angles at Ontario Place she looks a bit like Bette Midler. And she is alleged to hate Love Never Went to College, a song McGlinn says he loves. I'm with him; in its cheeky precocity it's the essence of Rodgers and Hart. But on The Lady Is a Tramp, the number with which McGlinn threw down his gauntlet to the acoustic disturbances, everything came together - words, music, voice and players - for four gloriously confident choruses. This repertoire, given interpreters who both know and care what they are doing, just refuses to lie down.
