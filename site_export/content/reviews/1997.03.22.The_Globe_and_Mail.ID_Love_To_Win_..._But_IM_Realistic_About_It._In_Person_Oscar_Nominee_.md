'I'd love to win, ... but I'm realistic about it.'

IN PERSON / Oscar nominee Brenda Blethyn isn't a a marquee type, but she does have stellar talent, says a critic who spotted her potential early on.

Saturday, March 22, 1997

Special to The Globe and Mail

FOR several weeks now my local movie theatre has been showing Secrets and Lies ,"nominated" as the marquee proudly proclaims, "for five Oscars." That, of course, is a recent announcement. Before it came along the sign said, "Winner -- Golden Globes -- Best Actress."

It didn't say who the victorious performer actually was. You can see the theatre's point. Brenda Blethyn took home a best actress award from Cannes before collaring a Golden Globe, and she has a chance at an Oscar. But she is not a marquee name. Perhaps, more significant, she isn't a marquee type. She is, as far as a 51-year-old never-out-of-work actress can be, the girl next door. Not American small-town -- which has always signalled an acceptably glamorous kind of ordinariness -- but British big-city.

British actors picking up showbiz prizes have an endearing knack of enjoying the hype without surrendering to it. Watching Blethyn pick up her Golden Globe was a treat. When she said that she'd been thrilled to get into the building, let alone win a prize, you believed her. She said it with the same kind of high-pitched breathlessness that Cynthia, her character in Secrets and Lies ,uses to greet extremes of good and bad news. You believe her , too.

As Blethyn said herself, in an interview from an L.A. hotel room, "You don't expect to see someone like Cynthia" -- a scatty, guilelessly earnest factory worker and single parent -- "at the Academy Awards." Secrets and Lies ,like all the films and plays directed by Mike Leigh, was created using an improvisational style, so the presence of Cynthia's creator at the luncheon for nominees seemed to have caused confusion.

"I was introduced to one woman who slapped her husband and said, 'I told you you should have voted for her,' " said Blethyn. The husband's excuse was that he'd thought the movie's performers were all playing themselves and, therefore, it didn't count as acting. Others have been even less sophisticated. One admirer, congratulating her on her newfound stardom, asked her, "But won't you miss all your friends at the factory?"

In fact, Blethyn said, "One rule of working with Mike is that you don't become the character. After every improvisation, you have to stand back and discuss the person very objectively."

And the people Leigh works with are certainly not inspired amateurs; he habitually casts the best and most experienced actors in the British theatre. Secrets and Lies was only Blethyn's third movie, but she has worked consistently on stage and television for 20 years.

The foundations of her career were laid at the National Theatre, which she joined in 1976 and where she stayed for nearly a decade. She didn't become famous, but she did get respect. In the star-oriented regime of director Peter Hall, she was the only actor to work her way up steadily from walk-ons to leads.

Initially she shone in contemporary comedy, especially the kind of sharp, class-conscious plays associated with Britain's most prolific playwright, Alan Ayckbourn; she was indelibly sad and perky as a put-upon young wife in his funniest play, Bedroom Farce .

Initially nervous of the classics, she became an expert in Restoration comedy. Comedy has been her lifeline; she has the intensity to convey the self-absorption that underlies half the great characters in comedy, and the vulnerability for the self-doubt that plagues the other half. (Cynthia has both.)

Maybe the defining point was a play called Steaming ,set in a Turkish bath, which she did just after leaving the National and in which she had to spend the entire evening being both naked and eccentric. Recently she has found a home at one of Britain's finest regional theatres, the Royal Exchange in Manchester. She is "most proud" of playing Nora in Ibsen's A Doll's House there.

She has also done a lot of TV sitcoms. The one she's proudest of is Outside Edge , which has gone into three series in Britain, winning awards for her and for the writer.

Bourgeois British comedy also led to her first brush with Hollywood, in Robert Redford's A River Runs Through It . "I was in a play in New York, Ayckbourn's Absent Friends . A friend of Redford's came to see it and said, 'You should cast the English girl.' "

She had worked with Leigh once before, on a BBC film called Grownups , which had left her with "a wonderful sense of achievement. You get to use your brain with Mike. When he called me for Secrets and Lies -- I hadn't spoken to him for a couple of years -- he just said, 'Well, are you going to be in my film or not?' When I signed on, I was told nothing. He said that he might like to do a film about adoption, but it might change.

"Mike is in charge of the circumstances in which we find ourselves; he has the map. But every line that I speak has come from me. The words come easy. Some things that Cynthia says are deeply tragic. And funny. Like when she says to her daughter, 'You want to get yourself a coil fitted -- that's how I got saddled with you.' " She's especially proud of the companion line, "I've got a Dutch cap floatin' about somewhere upstairs," spoken with earnest concern.

Having given his actors their heads, Leigh is a ruthless, rigorous editor. The meeting between Cynthia and Hortense, the child she had given away for adoption now grown to a woman, is an extraordinary 12-minute single take in the film; when first improvised it ran four to five hours.

"I thought the film would be well appreciated: I didn't expect it to reach these dizzy heights. It struck a chord. The number of people who've approached me saying they were adopted. . . . The records have been closed in the States, you can't trace who your birth-parents are. Some cinemas have been picketed by a group called Bastard America who want to get the law changed. And people like the optimism in it."

When not promoting Secrets and Lies , she's been making a domestic comedy called Music from Another Room in Pasadena, Calif., and after the Oscars she flies home to start on "a buddy-movie with Julie Walters," called Girls' Night. Since the nomination, she is being sent many more scripts.

"People say, of course you won't be doing any more theatre. Not 'arf I won't be. I'm not going to give up theatre. Have I changed? Certainly not. People's attitudes to me have changed. And they'll probably change again when I don't win.

"I'd love to win, I'd love to, but I'm realistic about it. My head may be in the clouds, but my feet are firmly on the ground." And she hasn't prepared an acceptance speech.

I have to get personal. When Brenda Blethyn had been at the National Theatre for about two years I reviewed her in a play called The Guardsman . She had already done a lot of good work, but to the world at large, her name seemed to mean as little on a theatre program as it would now on my local movie marquee. This time she played the maid, and was hysterical. "Miss Blethyn," I wrote, "deserves a paragraph" and proceeded to give her one. Shortly afterward we were introduced and she told me that her colleagues had taken to calling her Miss Paragraph. Anyway I did notice -- and she has confirmed -- that from that point onward, her profile in the company markedly improved. So for once a critic did good. I feel not only implicated but -- to use her favourite superlative -- proud. I shall be rooting for her.
