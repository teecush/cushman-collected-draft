It's been three years since Company Theatre made their astonishing debut with A Whistle in the Dark, a great Irish play for which they imported Irish director Jason Byrne. They have now brought him back to stage an Anglo-Scandinavian play, an occasion that only falls short of the previous one because the play itself does. It's still an extraordinary production with moments, sometimes whole sequences, of unsettling brilliance.

Festen is a stage version, by British playwright David Eldridge, of the 1997 Danish film of the same name, its script credited to Thomas Vinterberg (who directed the movie), Mogens Rukov and Bo hr. Hansen. It was released here as The Celebration, and if you smell irony there, you are right. Like A Whistle in the Dark (and indeed, on a smaller scale, Company Theatre's intermediate production Marion Bridge), Festen is a family drama, though hardly family entertainment. Helge is a rich man much into lodges; he is a Freemason and he also owns a hunting lodge. Helge's family -- less extended in the play than in the movie, for obvious reasons -- has assembled there for his 60th birthday. His two sons have followed him into the hospitality business, Christian, the elder, far more successfully than his brother Michael, who is embittered about it and about nearly everything else. He's a drunk and a wife-abuser. Christian isn't happy, either. They have a sister, Helene, a defensively free spirit who isn't ageing well, and they had another sister, Christian's twin, who recently committed suicide in her bath. All told, this must be the most dysfunctional Danish family to appear on a stage since the royal gang at Elsinore.

The text makes explicit allusion to Hamlet and to Hitchcock, both relevant sources. The latter yields a teasing mixture of propriety and suspense, the former a watery death, rumblings of incest and a hero given to guilting his relatives. Christian, rising at dinner to propose a toast to his father, opens a can of worms and flings it in the old man's face. The guests do their practiced best to pretend nothing is happening, but as the night proceeds and as morning takes over, the effort grows increasingly hard. No one can leave because the chef, Christian's best buddy, has thoughtfully removed their car keys. Community singing is the principal recourse; a raucous local equivalent of Happy Birthday is repeatedly struck up, and when Helene produces a black boyfriend, everyone joins in a merry racist chant.

The screen Festen is the best-known product of Denmark's Dogme group, whose puritan gospel enjoins handheld cameras, no extraneous music and strict location shooting. Byrne's production goes for a theatrical equivalent -- minimal furnishings, stage open to the bare brick walls, all stage lighting from visible lamps, house lights full on throughout -- that is actually the direct opposite. Lack of artifice is an artifice in itself, in either medium, but in the theatre you really see it. A single bed stands in for three bedrooms, simultaneously. Guests sit on chairs more spartan than you'd find at any posh party or any posh hotel. (The film prided itself on giving you the real thing.) I'm not complaining. This unlivable set (designer, John Thompson; lighting, Andrea Lundy) is thoroughly lived-in. Byrne is noted for not giving his actors set blocking; moves may vary from night to night. This works enormously well for a play whose characters are constantly required to react, or not, to the unbearable or the incredible. Each has to find his or her own way; and at the preview I saw, they all did. A small girl (Isabelle Lobo) eats her way with serene dedication through her elders' turmoil. Grandpa (Gary Reineke) favours the assembly with a fixed senile smile. The whole table gets up and plays a grimly funny game of nonmusical chairs. This isn't anarchy. The performances and the relationships are rooted.

They're less firmly rooted than in Whistle, because the text gives them less help. As in the film, you're left wondering what it's all for. Are we only here to see, yet again, the bourgeoisie exposed, the elders rotten, the youngers maimed? Probably yes. These characters don't just have skeletons in their closets; they're skeletons themselves. But they certainly rattle with style. Eric Peterson's Helge is more tetchy than tyrannical, if he carried more weight, the play might. Rosemary Dunsmore, though, is superb as his wife, alternately soothing and goading her offspring in a set speech dripping with poisoned politesse. (It's the high point of the play as it was of the film.) Allan Hawco and Philip Riccio, the company's founders, again play brothers, Hawco weak and vicious (last time he was strong and vicious), Riccio an ambiguous avenger. Caroline Cave is rather wasted as Michael's wife, who gives near as good as she gets but has to give most of it early. Tara Rosling transforms herself as Helene; as her nerves go, she seems more and more like a Nordic Janice Soprano. And so it goes, down a fine line that includes Earl Pastko's implacable maitre d' and Nicholas Campbell's understandably depressed tycoon. The entire cast is on red alert. This is about as close as we'll get to pure theatre, with all the limitations that implies and all the excitement. - Festen runs until Dec. 13 at the Berkeley Street Theatre.
