Profile of Christopher Newton.

Christopher Newton has now been director of the Shaw Festival at Niagara-on-the-Lake for 20 years, which is a long time to stay in any artistic job. Ive stuck with it," he says, steering clear of any high-sounding manifesto, "because I like actors. He expands on that somewhat: I like to see good things, and its wonderful when you can put together good actors, good directors, and good designers."

Good plays help, too. The Shaw Festival began in 1962 and it has a unique mandate -- though mandate may not be the right word, since no outside authority has ever instructed the festival to do anything. The festival has more of a self-limiting program, devised as a harmonious expansion of its original purpose, which was, of course, to produce the plays of George Bernard Shaw. Since 1965, these have been augmented with works by other playwrights who wrote during Shaw's lifetime. Shaw himself preached the virtues of longevity and lived up to his own prescription on a grand scale -- his lifespan stretched from 1856 to 1950 -- so that gives the festival practically a century of more-or-less modern drama to play with.

In conversation, and in his own productions, Newton is urbane, intelligent, genially earnest. He has lived in North America for 40 years but he still sounds very English, with an accent and delivery that's understated, gentlemanly, like a BBC announcer off duty. (I always imagine him wearing a blazer.) His opinions flow freely and firmly -- and they draw on a wide frame of reference: musical, literary, and historical, as well as theatrical -- but he never seems to be imposing them. One imagines that, in rehearsal or in a boardroom, he makes an excellent chairman; the kind of democrat who unobtrusively gets his own way. He has a broad, open face, and his silver hair doesnt make him look in the least old. In fact, he seems boyish.

Although he is well-suited to Shaws period, the peculiar qualities of the plays -- what you might roughly call the drawing- room tradition -- inhibit experiment to some extent. They don't offer the scope of Shakespeare, either physically or spiritually. "You can move a Shaw play a couple of degrees right or left, and that's all. Its difficult to change even the time its set in -- a staple device for making a Shakespearean play seem fresh, or at least different. On the other hand, the small changes that you can make in a Shaw play may count for a lot; by turning those few degrees, you may bring a whole new angle into the light.

Newton seems to have come to his long posting in Niagara through a series of happy accidents, the kind that, taken together, point toward the inevitable. He grew up in England in the 1940s and '50s vaguely interested in the theatre, playing with puppets, acting in school plays and so on. "I never thought I could make a career in it," he says. I was at Leeds University, and I wanted to go to the States. This was the late '50s and America was the land of dreams. I suffered from the British thing of wanting to get out of Britain -- that probably accounts for the Empire.

He took an MA in Indiana, "got bored with it, wrote round the universities, got an offer from Bucknell University -- tiny place in Pennsylvania -- to take over their theatre department, so I did it, keeping just a little ahead of the students in the textbook."

He came to Canada by accident. I read a review in Time magazine of A Midsummer Nights Dream, directed by Michael Langham, and thought that this was something I wanted to be part of. I went to Stratford, and auditioned, and Michael turned me down on the spot. He said, `We dont need any more Americans.' So I found myself in Toronto with $60 in my pocket. And the day before I turned up, the Canadian Players, who were about to tour Julius Caesar, had lost their Cassius.

So Newton made his professional debut ("I'm a lucky person") in one of the juicier Shakespearean roles, and came to see Canada by bus.

He remained an actor for several years. He did get to perform at the Stratford Festival (Richard Monette, now his Stratford counterpart, was a contemporary) and he made a precocious appearance at the Shaw in its third season. He moved into directing when a fateful, if ambiguously phrased, telegram arrived, inviting him to act with Theatre Calgary ("We cant get Tom Kneebone. Will you do it?"). He stayed on, becoming artistic director and turning what had previously been a semi-professional company, fully professional.

He was there for three years, and then ran Vancouver Playhouse for a further six. Success there led to an exploratory visit from a couple of the Shaws governors, and the eventual invitation to take over there. I've always suspected that the Canada Council told them to get me, he says. "I thought I'd stay here for three years, but then I dug my heels in."

Id heard that Newton had arrived at the Shaw Festival carrying one serious handicap: He didnt like Shaw. He confirmed that this had indeed been the case. I'd been in some questionable productions, and the ones I'd seen hadnt inspired me. I took the job because I liked Shaws contemporaries.

In his first season, however, he directed Misalliance, one of GBS's most notoriously loquacious plays, "and I realized I had been crazy. It was a revelation. The people were more interesting than I'd ever realized." The production seems to have been modestly experimental, and free of some of the traditional Shavian clutter. The set that Cameron [Porteous, the Shaws principal designer] had done was very airy. We got very bad reviews.

A few years later, he directed a second production of Misalliance, and this time the reviews were considerably better. At least one of them was; I know, because I wrote it. I was especially impressed by the productions command of imagery. The design this time was not at all airy, rather the reverse. The play is set in a conservatory, which the production rendered as a miniature jungle. "I've talked," Newton says, about there being a `surrealist' edge to Shaw, but really its just a theatrical quality.

Theatre," he continues, "is about putting a strong visual image together with honest talk. It sounds like common sense but there arent many directors who consistently bring off both halves of the equation. Newton has managed it both in and outside the Shavian canon. His productions of Noel Coward's Cavalcade and of the Victorian melodrama The Silver King were finely organized spectacles that also told lucid stories.

Coward's plays have been among the mainstays of the festival repertoire, one that often seems to concentrate on the fringes of its chosen century. Newton says hes interested in obscure corners in everything; as a tourist, Ill go to Romania rather than Italy." As an impresario, he has done Vitold Gombrowicz and Stanislaw Witkiewicz, Polish playwrights whose names are more often found in scholarly histories than on mainstream theatre programs. That's one side of the picture. The other is the frankly popular choices -- Agatha Christie thrillers, miniaturized American musicals -- that the festival generally mounts with as much care as it affords to its secular patron saint. What seems to go missing is the centre: Henrik Ibsen, Anton Chekhov, Johan August Strindberg, Bertolt Brecht, and Luigi Pirandello all wrote their greatest plays during Shaw's lifetime, but they havent been much in evidence at Niagara.

Much of this goes down, as it should be, to Newtons own taste: "You start with what you like and hope people will share your enthusiasm. I've never understood Irish theatre, so we haven't done much of it. Im not a great Ibsen fan. Stratford seemed at one time to have a stranglehold on [Oscar] Wilde and Chekhov, but now we have started doing them. We have done the more obscure Cowards and J. B. Priestleys, as well as the obvious ones. And one of our major achievements is our Barker series. This refers to Harley Granville- Barker, the Edwardian actor, producer, dramatist, and theatrical prophet, whose plays Newton and his colleagues have been systematically working their way through, with results ranging from strenuously interesting to absolutely riveting. They are plays of ideas, with thick textures and elusive surfaces, whose peculiar demanding quality Newton summarizes by saying that they absolutely cannot be done by amateurs." Two of them (The Marrying of Ann Leete and The Secret Life) have brought him on to the festival stages as an actor: an indulgence, if thats the word, that he permits himself once every two years.

When Newton took over at Niagara, the theatre, he says himself, was "on a down; and his achievement, over two turbulent decades, has been to establish and maintain an identity. Much of this stems from the existence of a regular company. It's quite practical. Actors here can earn a reasonable salary, and be proud of what they do. Film and TV aren't available to most Canadian actors so the stage is the centre of our work-face. We have an experienced ensemble; the younger actors get to watch people who can do it, and they take it in by osmosis. The best kind of company is where, out of 70 actors, you have 50 who were there the previous year, 10 returning after an absence, and 10 completely new." There are dangers ("you can become fond of people and give them parts theyre not capable of doing) but the advantages are huge and, in the fragmented entertainment business of North America, nearly unique. The secret of being a good artistic director is surrounding yourself with good people."

This year, Newton himself directs two plays: Rebecca, this seasons upmarket thriller; and one of those unfamiliar Cowards, Easy Virtue. He plans to stay on as director until 2002, and he will have a slightly altered mandate to hand on to his successor: The festival will now be allowed to include plays written since Shaw's death but set during his lifetime. (Tom Stoppard's Travesties and The Invention of Love immediately occurred to me, on grounds of style as well as substance. They occurred to Newton, too, but there are no immediate plans to do them.) Will this perhaps allow the Shaw Festival to be more cutting edge? I asked him this, wincing at the phrase."I'm in my 60s now, came the reply. Not very cutting edge. I tend toward the old codger myself."
