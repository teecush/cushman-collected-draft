Yann Martel's Beatrice and Virgil is a novel with, effectively, two characters. They are engaged in the writing of a play, also effectively with two characters. So it makes apparent sense to turn the novel itself into a play for two actors, each of them playing one character from each of the two levels. That's the approach that Lindsay Cochrane, a first-time playwright, has taken in her adaptation. Technically, it works; but there isn't much juice in it.

The two people in Martel's book are both called Henry. I'll call them Henries I and II. Henry I is a writer with one highly successful novel behind him. He has ambitious plans for a followup, one that would combine novel and essay in alternating segments. His publishers reject it, and our hero, licking his wounds and living on his royalties, moves to another country and gives himself over to clarinet lessons and amateur dramatics. Here he's contacted by Henry II who requests his help with a play he's writing. He encloses a scene: two characters named Beatrice and Virgil sitting under a tree discussing, in luscious detail, the shape and flavour of a pear. (Beatrice has never seen or tasted one; Virgil is an expert.) Intrigued, Henry I tracks Henry II to his lair, which turns out to be a taxidermy store. There, among all the animals that have been lovingly and expertly stuffed, are the originals of Beatrice and Virgil; Beatrice is a donkey and Virgil a howler monkey. Henry II reads Henry I random scenes from his manuscript, though he'll never let him see the entire work. We learn that Beatrice and Virgil are friends making noble efforts to comfort one another, hunted creatures who have suffered grievously. They are victims of man's inhumanity to beast, a scarcely veiled metaphor for man's inhumanity to man.

Cochrane's script hews closely to Martel's original, though it moves a few things around and has, like all dramatizations of novels, to leave stuff out. Some of the omissions are forgivable; one at least isn't. The novel tells us, but the play doesn't, that Henry I's abandoned book was an attempt to deal with the Holocaust. That's also, it becomes clear, the subject of Henry II's play. His motives in writing it could not, it transpires, be called noble. It is, in fact, an exercise, in selfexculpation. Henry II cares a lot more for animals than he does for people. And, however much he may bemoan the disappearance of species, he may well, artist in taxidermy that he is, prefer dead animals to live ones.

Henry I has decided, the hard way, that to write narrative fiction about the Holocaust is selfdefeating. Better, he thinks, to leave it to the autobiographers: to Anne Frank and Primo Levi. He's probably right; one doesn't want to see such an event become the object of some outsider's beautiful prose. Martel himself may or may not agree with his fictional alter ego. He seems to want to have it both ways. He dreams up a Holocaust fable, milks it for its horrific value (which is considerable), and then disowns it.

Henry II's abortive play is mostly a duologue, but it does have three other characters, one of them referred to as A Boy. In this it resembles a more widely seen drama, Samuel Beckett's Waiting for Godot. Beckett's Boy was a bullied innocent; Henry II's is anything but. We can hardly help thinking of Beckett anyway, since Beatrice and Virgil, like Sam's tramps, spend their days at the side of the road, under a tree, always saying they'll leave but never making a move. (Mind you, their Godot does in a sort appear, prompting the thought that Vladimir and Estragon didn't know how lucky they were.) Martel may be suggesting that Beckett's play is itself a Second World War memento, reflecting its author's experience in the French Resistance and implicitly contrasting it with Henry II's more inglorious wartime record. The literary self-awareness is something else that's disappeared from the adaptation. So has much of the stylistic poise that is the book's main attraction. Martel's novel is told from Henry I's point of view, but not in his voice. Cochrane's play makes him the narrator, which is probably the simplest way of doing it, but it presents the narration as a talk at a lectern, to some unidentified assembly. It might have been bolder, and more in keeping with the spirit if not the letter of the book, to have the actor directly address us, the theatre audience.

What remains? The agonized reminiscences of Beatrice and Virgil, necessarily shortened, retain some power; they also, when coming off the stage rather than the page, usefully evoke some more recent genocides. But these are isolated passages. The fable in general seems more precious than illuminating.

In Sarah Garton Stanley's production, designed by Amy Keith, the taxidermist's emporium is represented by a set of receding acting areas, each giving way to the one behind as we

get deeper into the story: a neat image, though it does result in the most important scene being played in the remotest space. We don't, alas, get to see any of the preserved inhabitants except the two in the title. They are represented, not especially evocatively, by various means: as puppets, as silhouettes, and - in more extended passages - by the human actors, playing without masks, makeup or any other kind of disguise. Damien Atkins (Henry I) doubles as Beatrice, and Pierre Brault (Henry II) as Virgil, though there seems no particular reason why it shouldn't be the other way around.

Both performers, in both their roles, are accomplished but neutral. Brault is the more individual of the Henries, though he can't prevent an unbecoming geniality from creeping in. He extends his visitor a welcoming smile; in the book he never smiles, wouldn't know how to, is doggedly humourless; it's what's most unnerving about him, to the other Henry and, if that's communicated, to us. Atkins, who has had quite the season, here hardly has a character to play; his domestic life (in the book he's married, with a child on the way) has been excised: not a major loss to the narrative, but still one that knocks a colour out of what's already a limited spectrum. In another way, the actor colours too much. The most disturbing part of the play, as of the novel, is its epilogue: a series of unanswerable questions about reactions to atrocity. They should, for maximum shock, be posed deadpan; the actor doesn't need to show us, as Atkins does here, how shocked he is. Even as it stands, though, this is the one part of the play, as of the novel, that even begins to do justice to its subject.
