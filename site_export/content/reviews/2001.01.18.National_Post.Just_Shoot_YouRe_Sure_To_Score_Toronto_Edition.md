DISCO GOALIE

Artword Theatre, Toronto

Jane Millers solo show Disco Goalie begins in darkness, pierced by loud thumping sounds that might be inspired by either half of the title. As the lights go up, we glimpse what looks initially like a huge spiderweb but turns out to be a hockey net. Before it stands our writer and performer, decked out in gloves, pads, helmet -- all the gear. The costume turns out to be a great sounding board; as well as talking to us, she sings a cappella, using her body as a source of percussive accompaniment, in the style of Bobby McFerrin. This is musically as well as technically impressive; the tunes, or fragments of tunes, that she sings may have originated as disco but - - initially at least -- she gives them some of the power and dignity of blues.

Which is all very fine, but does not in itself make a play. (Sporting pun not intended but gratefully received.) We need substance to get us through the evening, and Millers autobiographical monologue -- she uses her own name and has hinted in interviews that she is to some extent telling her own story -- wears increasingly thin. It starts promisingly; she tells, graphically and movingly but without self-pity, of being bullied at school, and of her parents' reactions when she told them about it. Her mother, very proper and straitlaced, instructed her not to cry because you're giving them a part of yourself when you do that"; her father ("my first feminist) advised her to hit back. She finds release in joining a hockey team and speedily discovers her niche as a goalie -- an aggressively defensive position of which her mother, who organizes the league, should thoroughly approve. Hockey becomes almost the most important thing in her life.

It improves her confidence, and hence her social life. But here a dichotomy opens up: On the ice, its her responsibility to keep the puck out; in her dealings with boys, and later with men, she lets as many in as possible. (The metaphor is her own, and endlessly reiterated.) Dancing, especially slow dancing, is the Dionysiac agent here. So the title supposedly sums up the conflict within her, or the two sides of her nature, or whatever; but really it doesnt, not in any dramatic or revealing way. There is just this girl who plays hockey and is sexually active -- and so what? Her description of her first boyfriend is wittily precise -- he was on the fringe of the popular group -- he wore glasses so he was attainable") but the account of her exploits progresses from mildly titillating to plain dull. She herself seems to come to this conclusion when, in her mid-thirties, she decides to give up on promiscuity and try for love. Her mother figures throughout in the story as a convenient, and endlessly shockable, opposing force, but she remains a very shadowy figure. Nobody else exists at all.

Even the music becomes an irritant. It gets to the point where every spoken line is interrupted by a few bars of some disco anthem. Lacking the usual instrumental trappings, we are thrown back, despite Miller's skill at body percussion, on the words, rapturously intoned as if they were gospel. (This time the pun is intended.) They don't stand up to it; and the effect would be monotonous even if we were dealing with a less constricted musical form. Miller is an attractive, energetic (God knows she's energetic) and technically versatile performer; and she may well have it in her to write speakable dialogue. What her range might truly be as either actor or writer is impossible to guess. The stage confessional may be cheap to produce, and it may be both great exercise and great therapy for the performer, but it's an achingly constricting form.
