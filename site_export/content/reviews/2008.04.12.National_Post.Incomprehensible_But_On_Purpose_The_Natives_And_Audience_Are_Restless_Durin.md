One of the characters in George F. Walker's Beyond Mozambique is Rocco, an Italian doctor with a Nazi past. These days, he lives in a well-appointed shack in some African country that presumably isn't Mozambique, and experiments on recently deceased human bodies in the hopes, he says, of finding a universal cure for cancer. His customary attire is jungle casual, but some mornings he puts on a tuxedo. This is to oblige his wife, Olga, who believes that she is her Chekhovian namesake from Three Sisters. She insists, though few would have guessed it to be an old Russian custom, on people dressing for breakfast.

The play is early Walker -- from 1974, when the actual Mozambique was still a year from independence while the Toronto theatre was revelling in having acquired it -- and its other characters have gone similarly Beyond. There is Tomas, a brain-damaged young Greek who supplies Rocco with fresh cadavers, not all of them by mere blameless grave-robbing. There is the Corporal, a trigger-happy ex-Mountie (the shows only gesture to Canadian content) who comprises the entire local police force and likes to have sex with 11-year-old girls. There is Father LiDuc -- half-Chinese, not that it shows -- the latest in a line of problematic priests sent out hebre by the Church in the reasonable hope that they wont come back; his particular hang-up is drugs, for which his tastes seem appropriately catholic. And there is Rita, a former porn star, whiling away the time until her boyfriend gets out of jail (for beating her up) and can produce her in an artistic sex film; she wants to be Susan Hayward in The Snows of Kilimanjaro or Rita Hay-worth in anything.

Well, doesn't that all sound like fun. Bits of it are. The young Walker arrived on the Toronto indie scene full of verbal flair and with a script stacked with monomaniacs. His gambit, like that of the British Joe Orton in the previous decade, is to treat the outrageous as normal, or at most as slightly vexatious. (You've murdered Old Joseph, Rocco admonishes Tomas one minute from the start. "I taught that old man to play dominos.") Orton loved to expose respectable hypocrisy while still showing it triumphant; Walker, here at least, is showing white society crumbling from within while besieged from without. Offstage, the natives are restless. At least, theyre going in for distant drumming which, over the course of the evening, becomes steadily less distant.

The difference between Orton and early Walker is that the former supplied firm farcical structures, leading to a climax. In Beyond Mozambique, the characters just flail around for the duration, exhibiting what an earlier era would have called their humours. Sometimes they get in one anothers way, but there's no real conflict or contact, and no build. There is indeed a climax. The alien sextet -- one of whom is by now technically dead, but that doesn't bother anyone -- are cornered by the indigenous insurgents, who send them a bloodstained message that reads "entertain us." The reviews from 30 years ago suggest that this was regarded as all very meta-theatrical; the rebellion was a mere symbol for the assumed displeasure of a bourgeois audience that had had too much. (Or maybe a radical audience that hadnt had enough. You never know.) Even now, its possible to feel more than a twinge of sympathy with the rebels' demand. It's what the relationship of audience to players should be: Weve shown up, you deliver. Nowadays, though, were less bothered about the nature of theatre, and we're likelier to take the ending straight. The Africans are saying "all right, let's see this culture of yours" and the Europeans respond with gobbets of Chekhov (Olgas obsession comes in handy here), backed by Tchaikovsky. Of course, Chekhov has always been Torontos (and many other places') favourite modern classic, so maybe Walker was being locally satiric after all. Or maybe he just wanted a flashy ending.

If he was arraigning Western decadence, I can't help thinking that lumping together porno-flicks with concentration camp experiments is being hyper-moralistic. Probably its all show; I cant believe that Walker was really intending to anticipate the ethical stance of Iranian President Ahmadinejad. Anyway this and much else is rendered moot in Ken Gass' revival, which unfolds in jerks and is vocally muddy to the point of incoherence. The performances arrange themselves on a steeply sliding scale. At the top of accomplishment is Sarah Orenstein, who sets up Olga as the type of the haughty, somewhat sexualized high comedienne, and clings to the convention like grim death. Joe Cobden comes in a good second as the doped-up padre, whom he plays with a kind of unshockable innocence that, come to think of it, typifies the play. (He may have the best single line when he describes the incurable insanity of both his parents as "a bizarre game of one-upmanship.") Somewhere in the middle are Dmitry Chepotevky's Tomas, who is meant to be incomprehensible, at least some of the time, and Richard Zeppieri, who makes the Corporals role seem unactable but has the excuse that it probably is. At the bottom are Tara Nicodemos Rita, with a delivery that strangles her words at birth, and--in the crucial role of Rocco -- Oliver Becker. This is an actor excellent at playing uncouth. But Rocco has to be thoroughly couth, at least on the surface. If he isn't, the play has nothing to mourn and nothing to demolish. That's on top of the fact that he too swallows his words so that vital information never gets dispensed. The play comes through as a curiosity. We also, the author included, have moved beyond.

- Beyond Mozambique runs through May 4 at the Factory Theatre; 416-504-9971.
