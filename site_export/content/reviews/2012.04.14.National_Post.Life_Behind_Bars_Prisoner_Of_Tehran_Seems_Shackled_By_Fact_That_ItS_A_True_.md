The program for Prisoner of Tehran carries the subtitle A True Story. That phrase sums up the play's claim to serious attention. The things it presents really happened. It also sums up whats wrong with it. Theres a bullying note about it: This is true so you'd better pay attention. It's special theatrical pleading.

The truthfulness of Marina Nemats book, on which the play is based, has in fact been challenged; I cant pronounce on that as I haven't read it, but her story as told here seems plausible enough, and I'm going to assume that its faithful to its source. Marina was a Christian girl in Iran, 16 years old when the Shah fell; at first a supporter of the revolution, she was bitterly if naively disappointed when the new system proved as cruel and repressive as the old. She was arrested, tortured, tried in absentia, condemned to death, reprieved just as the firing squad was taking aim, with her sentence commuted to life imprisonment. However, one of her interrogators fell in love with her and proposed marriage, threatening reprisals against her family if she refused. So she accepted, converting to Islam as part of the bargain. She was still confined to the notorious Evin prison (notorious under both regimes) but with privileges and with a chance of eventual liberty. Her husband raped her on their wedding night, but she liked his parents and came to have a grudging respect for him; at least, thats what the play seems to be saying. The last part, starting with the last-minute rescue, has a whiff of romance fiction about it but stranger things have happened.

The stage version has been adapted and directed by Maja Ardal, recently and justifiably acclaimed as author and performer of You Fancy Yourself, a theatrical memoir of her Scottish girlhood. Something of the same feeling, minus the wit, has crept into this play, and it doesn't feel appropriate. After her near execution, Marina was placed in a cell along with an unspecified but obviously substantial number of other women. She met some old friends there and made new ones. She even asked to be sent back there after her marriage. Female solidarity is one thing; all-girls-togetherness is another. Neither the dialogue nor the delivery make any allowance for cultural differences, or for the enormity of the stakes; we are told that terrible things are going on, but the atmosphere onstage is almost ludicrously cosy.

Earlier in her career Ardal was the artistic director of Toronto's Young Peoples Theatre, and the nannyish tone that I recall from her regime there (its disappeared under the current one) infects this production as well. Horrific things get mentioned, even get staged, but are never brought home. There is a senior prison official who, we are told, has it in for Marina; he has one scene, in which he utters B-movie villain threats, but his presence on-or offstage is never felt. The text is lightweight to the point of being giggly. Marina's present ordeals are juxtaposed with her memories of happier times, including trysts with a boy who's personally shy but politically passionate; the effect, as we jump from one time frame to the other, is to devalue both. The word "jump" is disconcertingly appropriate; especially in the earlier scenes, the actors never stop illustrating. When they arent doing strenuously helpful mime, theyre providing vocal sound effects. It's like sitting in on an evening's worth of dramaschool exercises.

A stark simplicity was probably the intention, but simplicity without strength becomes banality. It shows, inevitably, in the acting. Bahareh Yaraghi as Marina is impressive in her quieter moments, but is more often shrill than still. This kind of accusatory acting - it happens all over the place, not just here - may conceivably be the actors fault, but its more often the result of lax direction; bereft of context, the performer tries to pump in emotion. At least Yaraghi has the luxury of playing only one role. Two actors divide all the others between them. Maybe there's a way of making this look like a stylistic choice rather than an economic necessity, but the production hasn't found it; it robs the crowded cell scenes of any conviction. Razi Shawahdeh, playing the men, does a competent if unpolished job on most of them, but his various personae merge into one. The women, as played by Mirian Katrib, are uniformly bossy, even when theyre being oppressed. She does have sympathetic moments as the heroines understanding mother-inlaw (the most interesting character in the piece), and she also attracts sympathy, though for other reasons, when faced with the impossibly underwritten role of Marina's actual mother, which comes with a mad scene.

Nemat has said in interviews that her greatest trauma came after she and her parents had emigrated to Canada and she tried to make them understand what she had been through; but the play makes only the most cursory attempt to deal with that. It's writing by numbers. It's also do-gooding theatre. There's a lot of it about.

Prisoner of Tehran plays through April 28 at Theatre Passe Muraille. Visit passemuraille.on.ca or call 416-504-7529 for ticket information.
