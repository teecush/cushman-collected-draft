The stature of most musicals, as of most plays, is commonly determined by their ability to withstand stagings different from the ones they were born with. With West Side Story, no one has ever had the chance to put that to the test, since the show is driven by dance; Jerome Robbins' original choreography is literally built into the contract. Subsequent directors and choreographers have chafed over this, but I doubt if many audiences have minded. Those ensemble numbers remain thrilling and, with Sergio Trujillo in charge of their recreation, they work superbly at Stratford. Cool is a knockout, and the Dance at the Gym not far behind. In a lighter and less confrontational mode, I Feel Pretty is a delight. The dancing, then, is great. Otherwise, the production, with one very important exception, isn't a patch on the one that the festival delivered, also with Trujillo as choreographer, 10 years ago.

The exception is Chilina Kennedy's Maria, who takes a lightning journey from teasing and bewitching ingenue to devastated and devastating tragedienne; in the rage, despair and determination with which she greets her

brother's death at her lover's hands, she beats all other Marias I've seen, and most Juliets. In a show built for triple threats, she's the only member of the cast who acts, sings and dances with equal authority. Tony, like Romeo, usually comes in second (though he didn't here, last time around) and Paul Nolan's is, as one of his own songs puts it, no better than all right: engagingly, indeed aggressively, ardent in speech and movement but missing out vocally on the urgency and excitement of Something's Coming, which only happens to be the best song in the score. He gets his biggest applause for swinging himself up bodily to Maria's balcony: an impressive feat certainly but one that also lifts the audience, right out of the story. At that, though, it's of a piece with the self-admiring high note that all Tonys are all required to sustain at the end of Maria (the song, not the girl.). And that, of course, is Leonard Bernstein's fault.

How good is this score anyway? It's actually the least interesting of Bernstein's big Broadway quartet, less adventurous than On the Town or Candide, less fun than Wonderful Town. When it's violent, jagged and pulsating, it's very exciting, including the tough counter-themes that cut across the ballads. When those ballads are left to their own, strenuously soaring devices, you can only recall how much better Richard Rodgers was at writing passion, and how much better Stephen Sondheim, this show's lyricist, would be when he wrote his own music. But all right, as a totality the score works, In that respect it's like Arthur Laurents' book; Laurents thought that by giving his Jets and Sharks invented slang rather than the real 1950s thing, he'd avoid becoming dated, but the reverse has proved true. Still, however forced in its details, Laurents' script, which is certainly far more economical than Shakespeare's, grips and rushes you along; even the misbegotten movie has you gasping for breath, praying that stuff won't happen. Gary Griffin's Stratford production gets the adrenalin going, though I wondered more than usual why, when Maria demands Chino's gun, he meekly hands it over. With the grouping here, it just isn't a big enough moment. Tony and Maria's troth-plighting in the bridal store is, however, beautifully and playfully handled; such a shame that mock-hymn has to start solemnly up and spoil it.

But at least Kennedy and Nolan can sing it. Otherwise, the cast's vocal skills come in way behind their physical ones. The supports in this show don't require golden voices but they do need precision and a sense of how to phrase a line, especially a funny one. Gee Officer Krupke, which is full of them, is here just awful. The Tonight ensemble, usually a knockout, is ragged too. It isn't just the songs that suffer, it's the scenes and the characters. Anita (Jennifer Rias) is underpowered in everything except looks. Riff and Bernardo are colourless. Even the non-singing adults (with the possible exception of Mike Nadajewski's Gladhand) misfire. Take the cops: Lieutenant Schrank has been hardened by years on the beat but he isn't, as Dan Chameroy plays him, a B-movie sadist. And as for his subordinate (Bruce Dow): Gee, Officer Krupke, I never knew you were such a softie.

The production stakes a claim to originality by adding an extra character, a bemusing one. He's a boy (Kolton Stewart, a touching Macduff Junior in Macbeth) who wanders on at the beginning, the end and halfway through, as some kind of symbol of lost innocence and/or future hope. He gets to sing Somewhere, a song that reinforces its lyricist's contention that it should have stayed an instrumental. The show isn't already sentimental enough? With its dances, West Side Story still looks like a great musical. When, one copyright-expired day, it has to do without them, it may turn out to be not even good. - West Side Story plays in repertory until Oct. 31.
