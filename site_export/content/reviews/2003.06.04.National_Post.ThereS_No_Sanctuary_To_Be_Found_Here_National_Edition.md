THE HUNCHBACK OF NOTRE DAME

Avon Theatre, Stratford, Ont.

This adaptation of The Hunchback is a show in which things have gone so comprehensively wrong that it's difficult to know where to start laying blame. One might finger Victor Hugo himself for writing a novel so thickly-plotted nobody could hope to dramatize it in its entirety and still make sense. But he did not, after all, ask to be dramatized; though since his book has rarely been off the stage or screen since he wrote it, you might say he was offering an implicit invitation.

Those who have accepted it have usually taken liberties. In the words of the Stratford program, disassociating itself from such practices, previous versions have mucked around with "significant story points and even character destinies. I think the last phrase means that, in the past, happy endings have been imposed. There will, we are assured, be none of that kind of carrying-on here.

Jolly good. But unless you have eight hours of playing time at your disposal, such fidelity is likely to be self-defeating. Incident is not the same thing as action. In this show, plots, counterplots, playlets within the play, alchemical experiments, hysterical accusations of witchcraft, whippings at the pillory, stretchings on the rack, near-rapes, a hanging, and the occasional outbreak of true love succeed one another upon the stage at a pace I can best describe as a quick trundle. Along with them comes a motley horde (there really is no other phrase) ranging from Quasimodo, the deaf and deformed bell-ringer of the title, to that more elevated human gargoyle, Louis XI of France. And we are given no reason to care about anything that happens or anybody -- man, woman or beast - - that it happens to.

Rick Whelan has supplied a two-toned script, and neither tone is happy. Some of it is stiffly archaic and sounds like a bad old translation, the rest is gracelessly modern and sounds like a bad new translation. (I was worried sick" says a court lady to her returning swain.) The narrative method is equally inconsistent. We start with the actors telling us how the novel came to be written and filling us in on its earliest action: The depositing of the infant Quasimodo on the steps of Notre Dame and his adoption by the sinister archdeacon Dom Frollo; this prologue has some dignity and is, in retrospect, the highlight of the evening. The third-person voice is then dropped, only to return abruptly in the second half. One thing that plays and musicals have taught us about Paris is that its streets have always been crammed with picturesque, bloodthirsty rabble. So it is here, and a member of the crowd will sometimes step forward to fill us in on what the people are thinking. This habit grows until we reach the gypsies' assault upon the cathedral, and their repulse at the hands of Quasimodo. The cast, exhibiting no visible signs of shame, turns around and tells us about these exciting events; after all its put us through, the production throws its hands in the air and admits it doesnt know how to stage its own climax.

Dennis Garnhum, director and dramaturge (a double duty that may not have been a good idea) adds some incongruities of his own. The pet goat of the gypsy heroine Esmerelda is played by an actress, mounted on a trolley and holding up a pair of artificial forelegs: a piece of stylization that connects with nothing else in the production. Esmerelda herself has to be carried after being tortured, but a mere scene later she is running all over Notre Dame defending her virtue. Earlier she has enacted a marriage of convenience with the errant poet Pierre Gringoire, who according to the program notes loves first Esmerelda, then her goat, and finally and even more alarmingly the stones of Paris. Gringoire is played by Dan Chameroy in a vein of cringing comedy, with a voice that's a distant relative of Olivier's Richard III, suggesting were at a crookbacks convention. He does get a couple of intentional laughs, which puts him ahead of anyone else in the cast.

Nicholas Van Burek is affecting as Quasimodo, and probably benefits from the fact the hunchback's deafness makes him a man of few words. It is belatedly explained to us that he can lip-read; though this doesn't account for his apparent incomprehension when brought before a kangaroo-court earlier in the proceedings. Jennifer Gould is a spirited Esmerelda, though unable to convey the innocent strangeness that keeps her and society in a state of mutual incomprehension.

Stephen Russells Frollo has some initial presence, but is the victim of the most ineffectual series of exits and entrances ever visited upon a tormented villain. A couple of minor performances are irredeemably atrocious; the others are held hostage by a literally unspeakable text and a production that leaves them exposed and unsupported. Alexander Dodges sets, mainly in black-and-white photogravure, are reasonably atmospheric.

This production is the latest in a line of schoolroom classics marketed as "family entertainment." But it's far too dark and cruel for younger children, while those old enough for it would have a much better time at Pericles or The Taming of the Shrew. The show is the worst I have ever seen at Stratford, but something has to be and this will pass. What is more worrying, and should occasion some serious head-scratching at head office, is that it's a show aimed at no imaginable audience.
