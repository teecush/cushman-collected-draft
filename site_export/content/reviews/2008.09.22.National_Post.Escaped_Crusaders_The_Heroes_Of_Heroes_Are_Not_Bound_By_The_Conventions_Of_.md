Since Heroes concerns a new breed of superheroes -- inexplicably gifted creatures who agonize about their powers -- a bit of navel-gazing on the genre as a whole may be in order. I'd say that it has gone, within living memory, through three different stages.

In the 1930s, when people could certainly use some reassurance, there was the classic era of comic books and motion-picture serials. This persisted well into the '50s, when the comics were still being published and the serials were turning up on TV and at what my generation called Saturday morning pictures." We got involved in the stories, just as folks in the Depression used to do, in the sense that we were excited over what would happen next, but we never seriously expected Superman or Captain Marvel to materialize and solve our problems for us.

Scroll on, and we get to a more devoutly unserious time, the era of Camp, in which the old icons are resurrected on TV and in movies to be simultaneously mocked and celebrated -- or maybe mocked before theyre celebrated. How, these productions implicitly inquired, could people ever have believed such junk? (The answer of course is that they didnt.)

And now, in a new Dark Age that I suppose we have to trace back to 9/11, but that also has something to do with the internet's enormous utility as a facilitator of cults, we have a brave new race of superheroes with identity crises. If Clark Kent had ever grown tired of donning his cape, we would have known what to tell him: Make all your phone calls from the office. But the heroes of Heroes, spread out across the world and largely unknown to one another, have problems less easily solved, problems both psychological and physiological. And we're being asked to take them seriously, and even to care about them. The long-form TV serial, a gift to world culture but easily abused, practically demands that we do.

Heroes kicks off its third season tonight with a two-hour special. Ive only been trusted with the first hour, so theres a limit to the amount of spoiling I can do. But I can reveal that we start with what looks at first blush like a routine political assassination (routine in TV terms, that is) but turns out to be a benevolent warning shot fired across the bows of New York Congressman Nathan Petrelli who may be about to abuse his powers on a scale of which most politicians could only dream. In the course of the hour, Nathan finds God and gives a TV interview in church. This attracts the attention of a really corrupt politician, of the worst kind: I mean the kind who's never seen without a bimbo girlfriend. This palooka plainly thinks he can use Nathan. I don't think he knows what hes getting into.

Meanwhile, in another part of the city and of the plot, theres Suresh who, in the bet-hedging words of Wikipedia, "does not have any known super-powers and has to make do with plain old scientific genius. This could take him far; he manages to isolate the gene that distinguishes super-humanity. This is something really new: a biological explanation for superheroes. You see what the show is trying to do? It's trying to domesticate these guys (and gals). It's giving them backstories, and real adversaries. No more saving mythical cities called Gotham from mythical villains called the Joker. The bad people here are a Company, probably identical to the one that Michael Scofield, with his comparatively limited abilities, is simultaneously trying to bring down on the same network. Holy conspiracies, Batman.

With all this solemnity going on, its no wonder that Heroes cant afford a sense of humour. Well, it almost can't. Over in Japan, there's the very engaging Hiro, a millionaires son and heir, proving that lines like I dont need a fleet of corporate jets -- I can teleport" are much funnier when they come at you as sub-titles. I wonder if the same treatment would benefit such hardboiled epigrams from the other hemisphere as "It's all behind me now, like a long night after a bad taco." You have to wonder how the actors on shows as badly written as this keep their faces straight. The closest thing to a central character, cheerleader Claire Bennett, has the gift of immortality (that's a plot point, not an aesthetic judgment), which is a real suspense killer. And yet Heroes tries to operate as a thriller. Of course it's billed as fantasy, but it taps into a desperate need to believe in something that will save us from ourselves. That's something new in the genre, and a bit creepy. The 1930s were even harder times. But its consumers seem to have had harder heads. - The season premiere of Heroes airs tonight at 9 p. m. on Global.
