INES DE CASTRO

Tarragon Extra Space, Toronto

- - -

"You talk about people as if they were abstractions," says Ines de Castro, protesting an act of inhumanity aimed in her general direction, and sounding very much like an abstraction herself. She is the Spanish-born mistress of 14th-century Portuguese Prince Pedro whose father, King Alfonso, regards her with considerable suspicion, their two countries being at war at the time.

The king eventually permits his ministers to execute Ines and murder her two young children. He sends his son to the front, hoping he will relieve the general embarrassment by getting himself killed, but Pedro confounds expectation by returning victorious at the head of his Portuguese men of war.

The king drops obligingly dead, and Pedro, succeeding to the throne, takes a grisly revenge on his beloved's enemies. The chief of them is tortured to death at a banquet, while Ines's corpse is exhumed, enrobed and enthroned, so that the people may pay her that homage in death that she was never accorded while alive. Ines, who seems to be a decent sort, if overgiven to philosophical speculation, comments from beyond the grave that this is not what she would have wanted.

The story, which is historical and has attracted previous dramatists, has been turned by British playwright John Clifford into a perfectly dismal play that is now 11 years old. It consists of people talking, and the fact that they have been given literate- sounding dialogue to spout does not make it any better. They never enact their predicament; they talk around it.

It is born in on us that killing people is wrong, and that men (it always seems to be men) who commit atrocities can always excuse themselves by citing similar crimes committed by the other side. I trust you find this enlightening.

The actors playing the royal and noble principals also double as a chorus, one of whose functions is to rub our noses in contemporary parallels. So a Portuguese Jew, who has seen how his co- religionists are being treated in Spain, gives a long description whose details -- victims being forced to dig their own mass graves - - are drawn from the 20th-century Holocaust.

There is also a very familiar-sounding passage about domestic violence. And, of course, descriptions of raped women and slaughtered or traumatized children resonate easily. If you don't come out of this play about medieval Portugal thinking of modern Bosnia and Rwanda, it won't be the playwright's fault.

I suppose it's petty to ask a man whose gaze is fixed on the wider picture to avert it long enough to bring to life the characters he is ostensibly writing about, and flippant to remark that his generalizations tell us nothing that we don't already know and are, in fact, exploitative. Sometimes the chorus speaks, with embarrassing condescension, for the common people for whom life, as it usually does, goes on. A meat seller, who would get on well with Sweeney Todd's Mrs. Lovett, talks about the war's depressing effect on her business: "You wouldn't believe what they're putting into pies." A torturer lets us know that inflicting ingeniously agonizing deaths is a living like any other, and that he personally is a good family man. Maybe Brecht invented this kind of thing. It certainly seems to have been around a long time.

More immediately, Clifford recalls the elaborately facetious phantasmagorias of Peter Barnes, who wrote The Ruling Class and The Bewitched. I don't like his plays much, either, but at least he got there first.

Ines de Castro is presented by Whetstone Productions, who say that they are "dedicated to developing and producing provocative scripts through physical exploration of the text using rhythm, music and gesture, and to fostering creative talent within an ensemble setting." This is to distinguish them from all those other companies which deliberately go looking for unprovocative scripts, which are fiercely opposed to creative talent, and which wouldn't know an ensemble setting if it advanced on them brandishing switchblades and singing the Jet Song. Why do companies insist on making these claims? Why don't they just shut up and do the bloody plays?

I'm also not sure about the "physical exploration" bit. Diana Kolpak's production looked pretty static to me, though the emphasis on rhythm and music may explain why the play starts with a lot of wordless chanting, and why some later speeches are aurally supplemented in a rather literal manner: If somebody in a scene mentions moaning, those outside it will moan.

The text, meanwhile, might have more luck in making banalities sound like profundities if the actors could bring to it something more dynamic than earnest complaint (for the good), silky sarcasm (for the bad) and shoulder-shrugging jocularity (for the poor).

Elyssa Livergant plays Ines, Ron Kennell her prince, Cheryl McNamara his cast-off spouse, Robert Tsonos the evil counsellor whose end you don't want to hear about, and Patrick Conner the king, who looks and sounds slightly younger than his son. This may be generation-blind casting, and Heaven forbid that I should be so literal-minded as to condemn it, but there is a built-in balance to such dramatic relationships, and it might be better realized by casting actors who at least approximate to the correct weight.

Completing the cast, and possessed of more presence and variety than all the others put together, is Dinah Watts as Ines's nurse, a relationship that, what with Ines being a strong-minded princess in an unfriendly land with a weak lover and two vulnerable kids, seems to have stepped out of Euripides' Medea. She also appears as Death, who comes for Ines and, this being the kind of play it is, tells us all about it. She does this well, and if I couldn't help comparing her to John Cleese as the Grim Reaper, that was hardly her fault.
