I love radio drama. I've enjoyed working in it and -as a post-war child in England -I practically grew up listening to it. I doubt if thats possible for anyone now, even in the U.K. In Canada, where for years CBC ranked second only to the BBC in the production of audio plays, it certainly isnt. Chris Boyce, CBC Radio programming director, acknowledges that audiences fell in the 1990s, due mainly, he says, to inappropriate scheduling. Now they've figured out when people are likeliest to listen: 11:30 p.m. Thursday evenings with a repeat on Friday mornings. It's still only one slot, but Boyce says they are "constantly developing stuff" to fill it. What that stuff is, we may have to wait until 2011 to discover. For the present, Canadian radio drama consists of just two series: Afghanada and Backbencher.

Afghanada has been CBCs flagship drama production for four years now; at the moment, indeed, its the only one to be found on air. The last season--the fourth--is being re-run through the summer, with a fifth announced for the fall. It's the ongoing saga of Canadian soldiers -- three in particular -- serving in Kandahar. I've always found the title a little unfortunate; it suggests a satirical comedy, a vein that might be hard to sustain over so long a period, and anyway isnt being attempted. Afghanada certainly has its humorous moments, but its closer to The Hurt Locker than it is to M*A*S* H.

It was originally the joint project of four playwrights: Andrew Moodie, Greg Nelson, Adam Pettle and Jason Sherman. Nelson and Pettle are still regular writers on the show, usually in collaboration, but the stable has broadened to include many others. As with most long-running shows, though, on radio or TV, you'd be hard-pressed to detect anybody's individual style; Hannah Moscovitch wrote one of last seasons episodes, but I wouldnt have known unless I'd been told. The regular characters, since the show's inception, have been Patricia Kinsella, known mysteriously but endearingly as Coach, who over the years has risen from corporal to sergeant, and the two private soldiers under her command, Chucky Manson and Dean Donaldson. (Theres usually a third as well, but hes varied from season to season.) Kinsella is played, excellently, by Jenny Young, as troubled but determined. Chucky (Billy MacLellan) is the cut-up of the trio, a winsome Newfoundlander whose accent sometimes sounds too much to be true, but is probably authentic. Dean (Paul Fauteux) is the morose, sardonic one. One scene found Chucky avidly clucking over family videos sent from home, and Dean commenting "there's nothing worse than listening to people listening to things." In radio terms, that's almost meta-dramatic.

Afghanada is billed as a grunts eye view of the conflict and its kept true to that. I suspect that if you polled the show's creative staff on what they thought about Canada's presence in Afghanistan, they would

say that we should get out yesterday. In an early episode Chucky wonderered aloud about what good they could possibly be doing; a recent one found Kinsella arguing with a NATO superior, who wanted to burn a village for security reasons, that "were here to protect these people, not to destroy their homes." For the most part, though, the program is determinedly apolitical; its about getting through each day, keeping yourself and your buddies alive.

It's well done, though my sense (I haven't heard every episode of every season) is that its beginning to repeat itself. But then war is repetitive. The shows better when it's concentrating on character interaction rather than on firefights, which just come off confusing. Its main formal characteristic is that each episode is narrated by a principal character, not in retrospect but as the events are happening: stream-of-consciousness in fact, though tightly controlled. Originally this function was rotated among the three regulars, but guest characters have increasingly been allowed to join in as well: a journalist captured by bad guys (usually meaning Taliban but on this occasion warlords), a soldier who carries his son's action figure as a mascot and is invalided home after his legs have been blown off. (A season typically proceeds in three-episode arcs, and this was one of the more sentimental ones: not the subject, the treatment.) The narration constitutes a frank acknowledgment of radios main problem: the need to explain everything. I love radio drama -- I grew up on it -- but I have to admit that it has little room for subtext; Chekhov on radio sounds like aimless talk. The narration on Afghanada can sound literary, the dialogue self-consciously tough but not--this is the CBC after all--quite tough enough. If soldiers are going to say "freaking" all the time, better perhaps they shouldnt swear at all.

If Afghanada makes a serious attempt to overcome cliche, then Backbencher, CBC's other drama series, enthusiastically surrenders to it. This show, back next January for a second season, is about an ex-paramedic from Nova Scotia who becomes Canada's first MP with a nose ring. (Much is made of the nose ring.) Its announced in the opening credits as one womans journey through the snakepit of Canadian politics, and the writing that follows is on the same level of subtlety and sophistication. It's the kind of show in which the dialogue laboriously describes the characters immediately before they appear. Tactful acting and direction might mitigate this kind of thing, but the actors here seem to be wallowing in it; the production also goes in for portentous fades where Afghanada prefers the quick cut. Wendy Lill, the principal author, is an ex-MP herself, and for all I know her portrait of Ottawa is more factually authentic than Afghanada's of Afghanistan. It still sounds like soap opera.

Thats true, to some degree, for most drama series, whether on radio or TV. Whats become almost extinct in both media is the individual one-off play. Boyce says that CBC radio is commissioning some of these, so we'll see. He's right, surely, in saying that drama is the best of all forms for life beyond its scheduled transmission, whether as podcast, download, podcast or CD box set. "We're going" he says "through a bit of a revival." I hope.
