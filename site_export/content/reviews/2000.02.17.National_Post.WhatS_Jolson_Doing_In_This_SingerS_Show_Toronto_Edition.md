THE EDDIE CANTOR STORY

Solar Stage Theatre, Toronto

Eddie Cantor, comic and song-and-dance man, was a high-pressure performer and apparently a very nice man. He might be described as the light side of Al Jolson.

I'm not referring to their respective exploits in blackface, though for Jolson it seems to have been the life-blood of performance while for Cantor it was merely one routine among many. The jazz critic Gary Giddins once asked the veteran Avon Long, a great Sportin' Life in Porgy and Bess, if he hadn't been offended by Cantor's antics in burnt-cork. "We black people are not stupid," said Long. "We too can appreciate genius."

That's one of my favourite Cantorial tales. Another, also recounted by Giddins, concerns a 1950 Carnegie Hall concert at which Cantor made his audience very nervous by referring to "Senator McCarthy and other evil things that want to destroy mankind." And a third goes back to the early years of the 20th century, and the actors' strike that accompanied the formation of American Equity. George M. Cohan, the presiding showman of the day, proclaimed that rather than join a union he would quit performing and be an elevator- man. "Somebody" said Cantor, who was already a star, "better tell Mr. Cohan that if he wants to run an elevator he'll have to join a union."

None of these turn up in The Eddie Cantor Story that is a neat little lunchtime biography with an appealingly skilful performance by Glenn Roy in the title role. It tactfully avoids the blackface issue (thereby passing up the chance of making a back-handed contribution to Black History Month) but it does pay its respects to its subject's social and political conscience. It's 1940, and the sponsors of his radio show are threatening to cancel, offended by his on-air comments on the situation in Europe. He stands his ground: "The Nazis," he tells his producer "are fascists." It's rather an odd choice of phrase -- either it's redundant or it suggests that Hitler's bunch are nearly as bad as those guys in Italy -- but it's certainly honourable.

Otherwise, Aviva Ravel's script is a traditional rags-to-riches story, Lower East Side division.

Eddie loses his parents as an infant, and grows up in direst poverty. He survives on the streets through his urchin charm, his quick-wittedness, and his capacity for making people laugh. He realizes that he can make a living as an entertainer -- this despite the misgivings of his doting immigrant grandmother and the combined threats and blandishments of his prospective father-in-law. ("Look, here's some dollars. Go open a clothes store.") The last part of his career goes by in a triumphant blur; most of our time has been spent on the beginnings that are judged, probably rightly, to be far more interesting.

Basically, of course, the show is an excuse to perform some of Cantor's signature numbers. It abides by the usual convention that songs from any part of a performer's career may be used to illustrate any other part of it. Thus we hear Eddie, singing for pennies at the turn of the century, giving out with tunes that plainly belong to the 1920s or later; one of them even bestows icon status on Rudy Vallee, who was 10 years younger than Cantor.

I suppose only freaks like me care about this kind of thing, but it does seem a shame to tamper with the ambience. It's downright ridiculous, given the limited time available, to throw in a Jolson song while leaving out such genuine Cantor gems as Hungry Women ("I feed 'em and weep") and You'd Be Surprised, an Irving Berlin tune perfectly matched to Eddie's brand of lascivious coyness.

Scott White plays the piano and all the supporting roles, in a variety of accents and wigs. He even -- unwisely, since he physically towers over the star -- enacts the women in Eddie's life: his one wife, and his granny for whom he assumes a stoop, a shawl, and the tone of a gently muttering martyr.

Women who made it from the shtetls to the tenements must have been made of sterner stuff; and personally, I couldn't wait to see the old girl keel over.

There are hints that Cantor let his career imperil his marriage, but otherwise his behaviour seems exemplary. So does his performing; Roy does Makin' Whoopee and If You Knew Susie with impeccable vocal, facial and physical modulation. They don't make 'em like Cantor anymore, not since the invention of the microphone, and Roy is a gentler, tidier presence -- a quizzical flower-child rather than the eye-popping goatish momma's boy that was Eddie.

But there are enough points of contact to make sense, and I was able to abandon my usual prejudices against so-called plays in which one singer runs through another singer's repertoire.

Cantor was an excellent singer -- jazzier than Jolson, and one of the best pointers of lyrics, especially comic lyrics, ever -- but he was primarily a stage animal, and his songs can be acted. (And it's not as if the CD racks are bulging with Cantor reissues.) I'd suggest giving similar, but deeper, treatment, to the life, songs and monologues of Bert Williams, Cantor's idol and his colleague in the Ziegfeld Follies, whom W. C. Fields once described as "the funniest man I've ever seen and the saddest man I've ever known."

He was the first black performer to make it in white show business but, being light-skinned, he too had to black up: a scary concept and one well worth exploring.
