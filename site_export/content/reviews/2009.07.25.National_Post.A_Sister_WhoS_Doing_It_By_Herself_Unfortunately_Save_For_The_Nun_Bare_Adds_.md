There is, despite is title and its being a rock musical, no nudity in bare. The bareness depicted is all emotional, or at least purports to be, and is flourished in our faces as insistently as any naked bodies might be. The show was first staged in Los Angeles in 2000 and was done off-Broadway in 2004. Its Canadian premiere is the work of a new group called WaterEdge Productions, whose credentials are, at most, semi-professional. Most of the cast are present or recent students. They nearly all sing well, and a couple of them have strong stage presence. It isn't the performances that make the evening unbearable. It's the material.

Bare takes adolescent self-pity and self-importance to levels previously unsounded even by rock musicals; it's like the least tolerable parts of Rent and Spring Awakening rubbed together and going on forever. It isn't that the characters don't have reason to feel sorry for themselves; it's that their agonies are presented without wit, perspective or context. The boys and girls, hormones raging, are students at a Catholic boarding-school. The central love-interest is between Peter and Spencer. Peter is basically cool with being gay, except that he worries about what his mother will think. Spencer is a lot more conflicted, especially when he gets involved with Ivy, who is, according to who's singing about her, the cutest girl around or the school slut. Also suffering big-time are Spencer's sister Nadie, a self-described "Plain Jane Flat Ass" (or so says the list of musical numbers; I thought while she was singing that the words were "bad ass," which would make her more interesting), who's jealous of Ivy's looks; and Matt, who fancies Ivy, therefore hates Spencer, and takes it out on Peter. The show keeps shifting focus among this quintet, eventually producing a suicide, though not from the character of whom you might most have expected it. This leads the survivors to arraign the religious authorities for their lack of understanding, and indeed the show presents itself as a struggle between youthful rebellion and institutional repression. But it won't wash, because the church hierarchy is hardly present. The kids are left to their own devices, dramatically and, as far as we can tell, socially. They do drugs and have sex without feeling any noticeable pressure except peer-pressure; even gay-bashing is the sole province of the jealous Matt, who apologizes afterwards. An uncomprehending Father Confessor is wheeled on from time to time, but the staff member we see most of is a hearty black nun, who tells Peter that she understands gay males because she's been around so many priests. This is much the funniest line in the show, but the topics it suggests are not pursued. The show starts with a rather powerful chorale, paradoxically entitled Epiphany, but this, too, is a trail that's never followed

The nun, Sister Chantelle, is much the most engaging character around. She directs the school play, Romeo and Juliet, in which Spencer plays Romeo despite an initial reluctance to audition since he's more of a jock than a thesp (shades of High School Musical, one of this show's less culturally respectable antecedents). Ivy, of course, is Juliet; Peter, Mercutio; Matt, Tybalt; and Nadie, the Nurse, for which she seems ungrateful, considering what a great part it is. The entire cast sing the opening chorus, causing the Sister to say at rehearsal "that note's a C-natural; there ain't nothing natural about what you're singing."

She could have been describing bare's entire score. This is one of those almost through-sung jobs, and though some of Damon Intrabartolo's tunes are pretty, Jon Hartmere's lyrics are the usual agonizing mock-ups. Trying to be conversational, they end up clunky and literal-minded. None of the words sit easily on the music. We get an endless procession of solos in which the characters solemnly spell out their feelings, without ambiguity, without subtext, without movement, and therefore without drama. Though, of course, it's hard to be sure about subtext, when you can't hear much of the text. Even the R&J excerpts are sung, though, prologue apart, they don't seem to be doing it as a musical. The authors do throw in a few spoken lines of their own and, compared to the score, they sound like miracles of point and economy. Hartmere is also one of those lyricists who don't know what a rhyme is. I know that the above description would fit most rock musicals, but this happens to be the one I've just sat through.

Brian Gregory-Waters' production happens up, down and across a broad flight of steps, behind which hang stained-glass windows and a crucifix, to suggest the context that the show doesn't. Down in the hole between the structure and the backing sit the musicians; at least I assume the conductor, whom we do see, is waving that stick at someone. Having the band onstage is no novelty, but this may be the first ever onstage orchestra pit. Nichola Lawrence sings mightily as Sister Chantelle. Her role includes a dream-appearance as the Virgin Mary, here a Motown chanteuse with two back-up girls; she brings down the house, as black divas with powerful voices nearly always do, but she deserves it. She also gets to speak a sizeable proportion of the show's sparse dialogue; her bio says she's never acted before but she seems to be a natural.
