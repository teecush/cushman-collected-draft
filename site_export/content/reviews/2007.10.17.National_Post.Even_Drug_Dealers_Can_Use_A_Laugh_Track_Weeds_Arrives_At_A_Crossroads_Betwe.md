I was complaining last week about TV shows that refuse to stay in their allotted categories, or even decide what kind of thing they want to be in the first place. If any show has managed to confound preconceptions and get away with it, its Weeds.

It started, I suppose, as a sitcom: a sour-cute idea about a youngish widowed mother, a respected member of her California community and sexy as hell, supporting herself and her two sons by selling dope. Just about everybody in the town of Agrestic, it seemed, was her willing client. As a premise that's intriguing, but limited. What -- the bane of all sitcoms --could you do but restate it?

Weeds' solution has been to darken everything while remaining flippant about it. From the start it incorporated a broad vein of satire about shockable hypocrisy (the Christian right has always been good for a laugh) and municipal corruption, sometimes disguised as municipal incompetence. This has become steadily less good- natured.

Also, Nancy, the protagonist, has been plagued by roosting chickens. Back in the pilot episode, she was affronted at the idea of kids dealing or even using; the third and current season has found her own elder boy as a runner. The nice black family who were her suppliers have been supplemented by others -- black, Mexican and even Armenian-- who are not nice. The cliffhanger ending of the last season found Nancy in her kitchen with about a dozen guns trained on her. Its true that most of her assailants were too preoccupied with cracking wise at one anothers expense to pose a serious threat; collectively, they were the gangs that couldn't shoot straight. But there was always the possibility that, individually, one or two of them could. And people on the show have been killed, occasionally, though never the sympathetic ones. As the star, Nancy has tenure and she acts as though she knows it; she may get desperate but she's never despairing. She has, though, found herself in deeper -- or harder. Its no longer just pot that shes dealing with. Our heroine is involved with heroin.

Weeds is compulsively watchable (nobody writing about it has been able to avoid the word addictive) and it remains ambushingly funny. From recent episodes I treasure the memory of a filling- station attendant remarking of a cryptic customer, "I know it's a cliche, but I think he was being ironic," and the revelation that drug dealers like driving Priuses. (Because they're quiet.) Mary- Louise Parker brings a knife-edge comic presence to Nancy; also, the California climate permits, or maybe compels her, to go around all the time in skimpy black dresses. She has, too, a wonderful foil in her for-want-of-a-better word-friend Celia, who started as your average censorious neighbour and has matured into downright malevolent, though not without having a certain amount of right on her side. And since shes only a featured player, she really suffers.

All the same, you can still hear the grinding of gears, or perhaps the sound of bases being covered. One problem that has always dogged Weeds is that there was no premise behind the premise. We jumped in, as the ancients would say, in medias res. We never saw Nancy in her former life or learned how -- as opposed to why -- she got into her current one. In the first scene of the pilot, she was in a PTA meeting, virtuously opposing the presence of sugary drinks in school; in the second scene, she was on her merry way in her car, selling to what was already an established customer base. I know its a cliche, but I think the show was being ironic. And in its breezy way it worked. The trouble is, though, that Nancy is a premise before she's a person. The sitcom is just that: situation comedy. It resists development. Compare (and Weeds is good enough, and ambitious enough, to justify the comparison) The Sopranos, which wasn't primarily comic, and in which the menace was real, but which was funny --indeed funnier, because the comedy grew from the characters. Sitcoms used to be stationary, or at any rate repetitive. That tradition may have peaked in Seinfeld, where the characters aimlessness virtually forbade any possibility of a story. When a sitcom develops a continuing narrative, as the current trend is, it becomes a bastard form: not an impossible one, but the strain shows.

With its harried mom, loutish big brother and puzzled small one, Weeds is somewhat like Malcolm in the Middle, without a Malcolm in the middle. Though if young Shane isnt a genius on the Malcolm level, he's still plenty bright; he was able to supply the full text of the proverb "tempus fugit," which I, despite the classical education flaunted above, never could. Weeds falls between the comparative realism of Malcolm (a bolder show, despite its more conventional trappings) and the sheer camp of Desperate Housewives. I never thought I'd mourn the demise of the laugh track, but I do miss what the laugh track signified: a whole-hearted commitment to laughter. Weeds has left it a little late to become drama; its stuck at bizarre. Its also taught us that Little Boxes sung by Elvis Costello sounds exactly the same as Little Boxes sung by Randy Newman, and that it's a tiresome, patronizing song whoever does it.
