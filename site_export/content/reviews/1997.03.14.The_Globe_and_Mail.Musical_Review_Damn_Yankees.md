DAMN YANKEES

MUSICAL REVIEW

DAMN YANKEES

Friday, March 14, 1997

Written by George Abbott,

Douglass Wallop, Richard Adler


  


and Jerry Ross


  


Directed by Jack O'Brien


  


Starring Jerry Lewis


  


Rating: ***

Jerry Lewis in a devil of a role

Special to The Globe and Mail


  


A VETERAN comedian in full command of his means can take authority and relaxation to supernatural heights. In his last years George Burns, you will remember, took to playing God. Jerry Lewis has gone the other way.

In Damn Yankees he portrays Mr. Applegate, a.k.a. Satan, who purchases the soul of a middle-aged Washington baseball fan or, more precisely, takes it on consignment. Paunchy Joe Boyd is diabolically transformed into svelte Joe Hardy, the star hitter who will take the hitherto despised Washington Senators to victory over their hated foes, the New York Yankees. Hence the shows title, which has always seemed to me to come -- there is no other way to put it -- out of left field; the damnation is certainly pertinent, but the Yankees only function in the story as an offstage threat.

Like Christopher Marlowes Mephistopheles in Dr. Faustus , Applegate doesn't have to be conjured; he comes when he scents a likely customer. He arrives in a puff of smoke (the show is full of incendiary gags) but once there he just stands, Jerry Lewis immaculate in yachting blazer and glossy black hair, and waits sardonically. The script allows the devil no weak moments, and neither does the performer. It's an astonishingly economical and unsentimental performance, to rank with Lewiss egomaniacal talk-show host in King of Comedy .

He does make a few concessions, moments -- seconds, usually -- when he turns on his hysterical baby voice. He is also likely to seal a joke or a plot twist by turning to the audience with his trademark slack-jawed grin: a look that simultaneously signals complicity and denial. Having delivered what he must assume is expected of him (and the audience response suggested that his assumption is correct), he clicks back to business. He hasnt yet attained the Burns state of grace, which makes every line sound funny, and he doesn't exert himself too much in the matter of inventive line readings, but his timing is expectedly perfect. He has bite to match his bark. (All the actors in this show bark. So does the orchestra. It must be the amplification.)

In the second act he interrupts his one solo song to don a hat and jacket of appropriately infernal red, catch or artfully drop a series of canes hurled at him from the wings, and tell a string of jokes preserved in aspic from the palmy days of burlesque. They're rude and, at least as he does them, hilarious. Even as written, the second act of this musical tends to splinter into a series of specialty numbers, so this extra-special one cant hurt. Besides, if there is a devil he might very well be playing Vegas, and as most of the monologue involves sex, religion or both, it could even be defended as having character relevance. The great thing about this performance is that, though you have to admire Jerry Lewis in order to enjoy it, you dont have to find him endearing. That could even be a handicap.

The song itself (Those Were the Good Old Days ) is a barely serviceable comedy soft-shoe; this devil does not have the best tunes. Most of those go to his assistant, a vamp called Lola. Joe's contract has an escape clause, and Lola ("a little brains, a little talent") is enlisted to keep him off the straight and narrow. She does this mainly by gyrating. The role, which back in 1955 made Gwen Verdon a star, requires a performer who can project sex and satirize it at the same time. April Nixon is stuck at the first fence (all right, first base); whatever Lola wants, Lola gets, but this one's horizons seem unduly limited.

Still, she dances well, and in that respect shes in terribly good company. My memories of the movie, closely based on the 1950s stage original, are dim, so I cannot say how much Rob Marshalls choreography owes to Bob Fosse's; in the case of the spectacular mambo Who's Got the Pain I suspect its a lot. But that number, and a graphically funny losers ballet for the maladroit Senators -- with their famous morale-building Heart routine -- and the country cut-up Shoeless Joe from Hannibal Mo (dynamically led by Ellen Grosso as an intrepid sports reporter), are legitimate show-stoppers.

The ballads in the score by Richard Adler and Jerry Ross are far less effective (and the whole was a step back from their previous hit, The Pajama Game ). There has been much rewriting of the original book, and the tension between Lola and Joe has gone. Their duet Two Lost Souls has been reassigned to Lola and her master, who doesn't need it. This Lola never really tempts Joe, never goes soft on him, and the discomfiting moment in the film when the vengeful Applegate turned her back into an old crone has been cravenly eliminated. This makes young Joe (John-Michael Flate) into an innocent sap hardly worth playing. He spends a lot of time hanging round his old house and his wife, who seems surprisingly unperturbed by his disappearance; what emotion there is comes from Dennis Kelly's crinkly performance as his older self. Douglas W. Schmidts sets, including a kitchen in a bilious shade of salmon pink, do a neat and not unfriendly job on 1950s domesticity, but the script and the direction stick at automatic caricature; the suburbanites overact hysterically, except for those who underact hysterically.

These sins are serious but, in context, venial. There is a star performance, a smart staging, and a supercharged ensemble. (Did I mention Joseph R. Sicaris crackling coach? Well, I have now.) Star and production could probably exist independently of one another, but it's nice that they don't. This show is in far better shape than most of the roadshows that check in at the O'Keefe (sorry, at Hummingbird; old habits die hard). And now please can they bring in Carousel ?


  


At Hummingbird Centre for the Performing Arts through Sunday.
