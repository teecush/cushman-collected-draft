THE MADRAS HOUSE

Court House Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

A 90-year-old play that flashes prophetically, The Madras House is about the flesh-and-fantasy market and its impact on both sexes. The Shaw Festival has been steadily working through the canon of Harley Granville Barker, the all-round theatrical genius of the Edwardian period, and this is his masterpiece.

Its plot concerns the takeover by a U.S. company, in the first decade of this century, of a British drapery firm, specializing in ladies' fashions. There is also a sub-plot, centring on one of the drapers' female employees who has scandalized her paternalistic bosses by being unmarried and pregnant and refusing to act ashamed of it. Neither of these tales, though, is exactly mined for suspense; the takeover is a foregone conclusion and the fallen- woman narrative is confined to the second of the play's four acts. Barker was always more interested in ideas and patterns than in story.

He also had a highly developed musical sense and this play is his triumph in the art of theme-and-variations. You feel that if it were possible, he would have turned it into a fugue, staging the scenes simultaneously, cross-cutting and highlighting motifs. As it stands, it's a kind of intellectual collage, and Neil Munro's production is a sophisticated essay in verbal and visual cross-reference.

The Madras House is the flagship store; its founder, Constantine Madras, has grown rich by feeding -- and breeding -- the sartorial dreams of prosperous women and, even more crucially, the erotic dreams of prosperous men. An intelligent sensualist, Constantine has long tired of his trade and is retiring to the Middle East, there to adopt Islam and polygamy, the latter pretty much a rationalization of his existing lifestyle.

His son, Philip, wants out of the business for opposite reasons: A puritan, he is revolted by the contrast between the beauty and the luxury of his own social world and the misery and poverty everywhere else. He, accordingly, proposes to stand for election to the London County Council. (A revolutionary he ain't.) The only character to appear in every one of the play's four milieux, he can function in all of them and is at home in none, even though the last is his own house.

First, we see him in the suburban villa of the Huxtables. Mr. Huxtable runs the more down-market of the Madras House's two branches. He has a domineering and partially disabled wife and six grown, unmarried daughters. He also accommodates Philip's mother, Constantine' s long-abandoned wife.

The air reeks of male bafflement and female frustration: the atmosphere of a society that sees no role for respectable women but marriage, and leaves them miserable, both within and without it.

Act two takes us to the Huxtable shop, whose workers live on the premises in a cross between a boarding school and a model prison. Miss Chancellor, who presides implacably over their physical and moral well-being, has caught the dishonoured Miss Yates in the embrace of a timorous male employee whose wife (another dragon lady, constantly on the verge of hysteria) has now come to demand an official admission that her husband cannot be the errant father. Philip tries, conscientiously, to sort all this out.

In act three, we are at the heart of the Madras empire -- a West End hot-house where models parade the latest creations (some of them, in this production, more revealing than the period warrants) as a divertissement from the central business of the sale. The combination of financial and sexual stimulation sends the Yankee tycoon into prophetic transports. He has seen the future and it sells; he might be a retail Ziegfeld (was Barker aware of this, his polar opposite among showmen?) preparing to glorify the American woman.

Finally, we reach the domestic Madras house. Philip's parents have one last meeting: bitter and clinging on her side, urbanely dismissive on his. Philip tries to come to an accommodation with Jessica, his beautiful and cultured wife, the incarnation of everything that both attracts and disturbs him. They agree "to meet halfway:" on the marriage bed, anxiously watched -- as in a hallucination -- by everyone else in the play. This is the director's idea, and a good one. The stage, in Peter Hartwell's design, is backed by a wall of glass, sometimes a mirror, sometimes a transparent screen. The women are likely to be seen behind it, as if they, too, even the elegant Jessica, were mannequins, preparing to parade.

In a related visual pun, the Huxtable daughters crouch and scamper about, like the veiled inhabitants of Constantine's unseen seraglio. One of them ends the first scene cradling a dead pet frog: the nearest she gets to love.

Munro habitually builds the action round a central seated figure. Sometimes, as with the bigoted, swivelling Mrs. Huxtable (Donna Belleville), he overplays his hand. He does so even more when he turns the American (Douglas E. Hughes) into a laughingstock by having him orate into a megaphone. (This sequence begs for applause and, at the preview I saw, got it -- a besetting sin of the Shaw Festival and its audience.) On the other hand, he brilliantly brings off a scene in which Philip and his best friend, the play's exemplar of what the American calls "the mean sensual man," go walkabout, circling one another in wary incomprehension.

Both these roles are excellently played, with Blair Williams' Philip a lucid focus for the play's pinwheel galaxy, and Ben Carlson giving full value to the conventional intelligence of his pal: an ex- officer now reduced to eunuch in the fashionable harem.

Mr. Huxtable, beginning conscientiously if painfully to explore the world outside of trade, is a role that the genial Michael Ball could probably play in his sleep: To his credit, he keeps himself and us fully awake. Constantine can be an exotic star role (Paul Scofield played it in a London revival); Peter Millard's performance is decidedly plain, even shabby, but this has its advantages. There is no danger of him walking off with the play.

The women are generally less happy in their roles (which may, of course, fit the play's thesis): Jillian Cook, pointedly marooned in a corner, conveys something but not everything of Mrs. Madras' wasted life, while Philippa Domville gives us Jessica's glamour but not her brain. Shauna Black, though, is electric as Miss Yates, calmly confronting single parenthood, and Laurie Paton equally assured as Miss Chancellor, the proud, elderly singleton.

The play is periodically frozen by the director's taped reading of the author's elaborate stage directions. This is one aid we don't need: The text may be diffused, but the staging has centred it.
