FLOYD COLLINS

Courth House Theatre,

Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont.

- - -

Twice this season, the Shaw Festival's first-night audience has rightfully erupted. The first occasion was the full-length main- house production of Man and Superman, a triumph of expansion. The second is the production at the intimate Court House of the off- Broadway musical Floyd Collins, a triumph of compression.

Floyd Collins, with music and lyrics by Adam Guettel and book by Tina Landau, is a compressed epic to begin with. It's based on a historical event, and is set in 1925 in Kentucky, an area of the country that didn't have to wait for the Great Depression in order to be depressed. In Barren County (a name almost too good to be true) the 37-year-old Floyd goes exploring the neighbourhood network of subterranean caves, hoping to discover a new one that he can open up for pleasure and profit. He does find it, but a rock falls on his foot, trapping him. The rest of the show chronicles the attempts, increasingly elaborate and well-publicized, to rescue him.

It's literally true to say that a story unfolds on two levels. Down below there's Floyd in his loneliness and his desperation, neither of them as unvaried as you might expect. Up above, there is what the show's publicity claims to have been the first media circus. The first reporter on the scene is a rookie from Louisville, Ken., named Skeets Miller, who turns out to be the only man small enough to squeeze through the hole at the top of Floyd's cave and actually talk to him. Skeets' coverage eventually won him a Pulitzer -- that's outside the compass of the show, but we do learn that his initial story has been syndicated in thousands of papers. Floyd learns it too and is impressed, even though he doesn't know what "syndication" means. Skeets sums up in himself the divergent forces at work; he forms a genuine bond with Floyd but he also profits from his predicament. And like Floyd, he has unrealized dreams, he wanted to be an operatic baritone, a revelation at which Floyd heartily laughs.

Skeets' initial dispatch lures in a host of other reporters, print and film. A mining company arrives, offering to take over the entire rescue operation, at no charge. They're in it for the publicity, of course, and their boss is a bully. Nevertheless, he does sincerely want to help, or so it seems, and he persists after most others have given up. Floyd's own family is deeply riven. His fundamentalist father is tempted by all the money on offer; brother Homer, the proud owner of a new Model T, hops between agonizing over Floyd and posing for the newsreels; and sister Nellie, Floyd's favourite, is newly out of an institution and distrusted by everybody else. Add in the other locals and the vast assembly, mostly unseen but felt, of gawkers, sympathizers, refreshment concessionaires and hucksters. There's a brisk trade in souvenirs.

Guettel's score, his professional debut, is remarkable; far from conventional Broadway but very theatrical. He picks up the folk rhythms, reinforced by the Aaron Copland frontier sound, that his grandfather Richard Rodgers put to memorably jaunty use in Oklahoma!, and takes it much further and, as is appropriate, far deeper. (For that matter his mother, Mary Rodgers, wrote a score for Once Upon a Mattress that her dad found rather startling.) There is a long sustained solo for Floyd at the start, taking him from hope to disaster, that's a tour de force. It's an aria, really; and when late in the show we get to a threatening, doom-laden reprise of The Ballad of Floyd Collins (heard more innocently at the start), then we really do seem to have arrived at bluegrass opera of an intensity and sophistication to challenge Porgy and Bess or Sweeney Todd. It's this that seems to me the musical and dramatic climax, rather than - - as claimed by the show's expert musical director, Paul Sportelli - - the actual double finale, which is certainly moving but goes on, for my taste, a little long.

It isn't always the easiest show. A static musical is pretty much a contradiction in terms and Floyd, for obvious reasons, doesn't move about much. He is ingeniously released from his bondage for some yearning (and ultimately wrenching) fantasy sequences. Even while he's immured his author and composer give him interesting and even funny things to say. All the same one tends to feel relieved when the action shifts upstairs and the conflicts get more physical and more explicit.

Not everything is perfect here, either. The Collins family are late developers, theatrically speaking, and the character of Nellie, though beautifully sung by Glynis Ranney, is something of a cheat. The sympathy between her and Floyd is musicalized but it isn't dramatized. Still, overall this is a neat and gripping script, and sometimes a very funny one.

Talking of humour: When I heard the original cast CD and when I saw a London production (which didn't have the resources) of the number that I, in my unregenerate way, liked the best was Is That Remarkable. In it, a trio of reporters, overhearing things and invariably getting them wrong, send their scoops down the phone lines. It has a jazzy, urban bounce and it gives Guettel the chance to show that he can write snappy lyrics with the best of them. It is, in fact, the nearest thing in the score to a traditional show tune. Eda Holmes, who is both director and choreographer, has staged it very well with the three hacks, in a diagonal line, moving all over the stage in a series of predatory jumps and crouches -- I think it's a tribute to her production that it doesn't stand out. She and her team hold us just as firmly for the more demanding stuff. The staging, on a set by William Schmuck largely composed of mounds of newsprint, is beautifully fluid, moving us in and out and up and down with enchanting ease and unending ingenuity.

Best of all in Holmes' production are the performances she gets, from a cast who plainly know they are in on something special and are on fire to share it. Jay Turvey as Floyd, apart from singing like a lark, plays brilliant variations on the theme of a smile -- optimism got him where he is and it remains with him, even his bitterness has a tang of benevolence in it. Jeff Lillico's Skeets and Jeff Meadows' Homer are both excellent foils to him and pulsating characters in their own right. There is strong work, too, from Peter Millard, Kevin Dennis, Douglas E. Hughes and Ben Carlson. The unmiked singing is, as usual at Shaw, a rich treat; the offstage voices singing responses to Floyd's halloos, as he feels his way through the caverns, are more haunting than any electronic echoes could hope to be.

I was shocked when the reviews of Man and Superman failed to mention the overpowering audience response which, even if you didn't like the production, was an extraordinary happening in itself. (It wasn't the standard Pavlovian ovation, and you could tell the difference.) So, just in case there are any similar lapses this time: The audience at Floyd Collins, from at least halfway through the evening, was bursting with suppressed excitement, and when the time came to release it, they -- or rather we -- exploded. But then the whole show is an explosion. And, as Floyd himself has cause to confirm, an explosion in a confined space is really something.
