FIDDLER ON THE ROOF

Festival Theatre, Stratford

Brent Carver belongs in a select group of performers whose roots are firmly in the legitimate theatre but who have realized themselves most fully in musicals: Others on the list are Robert Preston, Angela Lansbury, Len Cariou, British actor Denis Lawson, and -- of course -- Rex Harrison. They all act in musicals as seriously as they would act in plays; and speech and song in their performances are of a piece. Few of them are great singers, but they all have the knack of projecting the essence of their characters through music. And it works both ways; the rhythm of their singing feeds back into the dialogue, giving their whole performances a lift.

Carver made this team with his superb work in Kiss of the Spider Woman, and consolidated his position in Parade. In both of these shows he was playing beleaguered men: victims, in fact. Tevye the dairyman in Fiddler on the Roof is also beleaguered -- he's a Jew living in a village in Czarist Russia on the eve of a pogrom -- but he is nobodys victim, and when Carver was announced for the role in the new revival at Stratford, there were widespread mutterings that he was wrong for it. As it turns out, he is wrong in some ways. There are other ways, though, in which he is quite wonderfully right, and they are more important.

To deal with the faults first: Tevye is traditionally associated with actors who are fat (Zero Mostel) or at least physically powerful (Topol), and who have deep, rolling voices to match. Susan H. Schulman, who directs this production, has said that since Tevye is a poor man with a wife and five daughters to support, he is likelier to be tall and stringy.

That sounds plausible; but the script never suggests that either Tevye or his family go hungry -- rather the reverse -- and he has a standing in the village that seems to transcend his social position. Hes the one Jew whom the town constable treats as a confidant. The timid tailor, Motel, who wants to marry Tevye's oldest daughter, professes to be terrified that his future father-in-law might shout at him.

Well, when Tevye actually does, Carver manages a very respectable bellow, but he never suggests a man of whom anyone would go in fear. Tevye himself is henpecked, but that isn't funny unless he seems imposing to everybody else.

In fact, a lot of the humour goes missing; Carver manages Tevyes pained or ironic asides beautifully, but he doesnt have the folk or vaudeville touch for the milkman's sustained crosstalk act with God; Tevye is an orthodox believer who nevertheless finds the deity's ways too inscrutable for comfort, but Carver isnt one of natures kvetches.

In the same way, he misses the roaring comic climax that's built in to his big song If I Were a Rich Man. Indeed, the songs, most unusually for this actor, generally go for little.

The recitatives, however, do not. You may not remember, from other performances, that Tevye has recitatives; but he has -- they're chants, really, of a moderately Hebraic character -- and they all concern his daughters. In the course of the show, he loses each of the three eldest to a progressively less eligible suitor.

The first is the poor tailor; he can cope with that. The second is a political radical, with secular leanings; he can just about accommodate that. The third is a gentile; and that both enrages Tevye and -- because he loves the girl so much -- breaks his heart. It breaks the audiences, too. Carver may be the best actor in the world at conveying vulnerability and emotional need.

Fiddler, though not nearly as sentimental a piece as its often thought to be, is a show at which crying comes easy; but it's never likely to be more moving than it is here. It's largely a show about the fortunes of a family, and in this respect, Carvers performance cuts right to its centre.

Because of his acting, and that of several others, this production seems a more integral part of the season than Stratford musicals generally do. As the story of a vanishing community -- it ends with their expulsion -- Fiddler has an epic scale that suits the Festival stage; the setting is a revolving mosaic floor backed by an evocative line of tumbledown houses.

Some regular company members of various vintages find comfortable homes here: Keith Dinicol as the rabbi; Jonathan Goad as the Russian Romeo; and, especially, Michael Therriault as Motel.

Of the imported specialists, the best are the three marriageable daughters. They give the evening a musical-comedy lift with their singing of Matchmaker (don't ask what a scintillating Broadway waltz is doing on the Steppes), and then separate out into individuals: Robin Hutton modestly determined; Tracy Michailidis increasingly strong, and matching her father for emotion when she leaves him to join her banished husband in Siberia; and Amy Walsh featherlight and enchanting.

Barbara Barsky overplays the nagging wife (no actress can make much of this role; it must be the writing), and Yente the matchmaker, a character almost guaranteed to bring out the worst in whoever plays her, is done by Theresa Tova strictly according to form. And a classical company should be able to find an actor who can bring more weight than Craig Ashton does here to the part of the implausibly humane Constable.

The ensemble feeling is fine, though, from the opening Tradition number (which Carver leads in fine style) to the final mass departure. Some clown claims in a program note that Fiddler isn't really a dance-musical, but this production suggests different. Everyone knows that one of the shows highlights is its wedding scene, with the gloriously solemn bottle dance, representing the old ways, and the first dancing of men with women, dramatizing the advance of the new. But the earlier To Life carries as much weight: as a celebration, it brings down the house; as a meeting between Jews and Cossacks, it shows both the possibilities and the limits of co-existence. Ive never known it to work so powerfully before.

Michael Lichtefeld's choreography preserves the outlines of Jerome Robbins' original staging, and supplies some new detail of its own. Joseph Steins book remains one of the best ever written for a musical, breathtaking in the way it keeps four plot lines running so they seem like one.

The production complements the Shaw Festivals She Loves Me, to give us a fortuitous festival of the shows of Jerry Bock and Sheldon Harnick. Song by song, this isn't my favourite of their scores -- Harnick's lyrics sometime seem to falter under the burden imposed by having to explain themselves to the gentiles as they go along -- but, as a whole and in context, its invincible. Some of Bocks tunes -- Anatevka and, especially, Sunrise Sunset -- have a similar power to You'll Never Walk Alone; they get to you, at a level far below your rational threshold.
