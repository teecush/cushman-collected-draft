George F. Walkers new musical -- you read that right -- includes a song whose refrain goes everybody wants what someone else has got." Walker isnt the best lyricist in the world, but he isn't the worst either, and that line makes a good point. It applies to the show itself, which is called King of Thieves and which is trying to catch some of the reflected glory from the 18th century's greatest hit The Beggars Opera and from its offshoots, some of which are by very gifted people. (Anyone on for a revival of Beggars Holiday, by Duke Ellington and John Latouche? Thought not.) Jennifer Tarver, Walker's Stratford director, writes in her program note that the story was "immortalized in the great Brecht-Weill composition The Threepenny Opera. Some people would say that John Gay had already done a pretty good job of immortalizing it back in 1728, but Walker's piece does indeed owe most to the Brecht. It takes off, really, from one of that shows most famous lines: What is the crime of robbing a bank compared to the crime of founding a bank?"

It also takes off from the fantasy-American setting beloved of Brecht and Weill in their other works: another case of wanting what someone else has got. Were in New York, mainly in a nightclub, in the 1920s; and if you don't believe we're on the eve of Depression, the show has a trio of bankers who can see it coming and are planning to use it to get even richer. Topical, huh? The opening and closing numbers suggest that this super-scam is what the show is about, but in between both the plot and the plotters get lost, obscured by a parade of double, triple and maybe even quadruple crosses on the parts of everyone else.

Walkers king of thieves is called, unsurprisingly, Mac; and his story more or less picks up where those of Gays Captain Macheath and Brecht's Mack the Knife left off. Mac is out of jail and married to his Polly; and his in-laws, the Peachums, still don't approve. Theyd like to do him in, but theyre up against FBI chief Brown who wants to use Mac as an anti-trust mole, to which end he puts him into a disguise (an Italian accent and a moustache) that even a banker could see through and eventually does. Brown, by the way, is not a nice man. Macsort of is, at least compared to those around him; looking up close at the rich, and then at the poor, he decides to turn Robin Hood. Or that's what he says; but, apart from taking a beggar off the streets and turning him into a house-breaker, he doesn't do much about it. Nor is it very clear what he could do. His starring role is paper thin, and Evan Buliung cant do much more with it than look cool and hope for better things.

The theme is much the same as that of Serious Money; both, coincidentally, opened in the same week, in studio productions at our major festivals, and with spartan but suggestive sets designed by Peter Hartwell. Im not the greatest fan of the Caryl Churchill play, but it does tell its story forcefully; while Walker's piece is one of those in which there's too much going on and not enough happening. A mostly excellent cast grabs what it can. Laura Condollns viperish Polly proves much tougher than her old man; she also does the best singing. Her parents are rollickingly played, Jay Brazeaus mountainous Peachum being a fence capacious enough for anyone to sit on, and Nora McLellan's Mrs. P an enthusiastic murderess with an incisive style and a stripping fetish. Nigel Bennett plays a chilling, authoritative cop, a type that isn't new to him; he also gets to play a scene in womans underwear, which probably is. (And he doesnt lose his authority.) The bankers, when allowed to take the stage, are a fine sneering crew, with Shane Carty as the regulation weakling, and Sandy Winsby and Scott A. Hurst as his conscienceless cronies. Sean Cullen plays a club-owner/narrator, because in this kind of show there's always a club-owner narrator. There's always a spiky jazz score, too, which John Roby duly supplies, though his pastiche falls short of John Kanders in Chicago, which itself falls short of the real thing. Tarvers production is swift and tidy, though she must take some of the blame for two act-endings which, considered either as play or as musical, are extraordinarily feeble. There's liveliness and vivacity in Walker's writing but its squandered on a useless conceit. We dont need another Beggar's Opera rip-off in a neverland setting. Maybe he should have been encouraged to place the same subject-matter in today's Canada.

Yesterdays Canada, Quebec division, gets loving attention in For the Pleasure of Seeing Her Again, Michel Tremblays recollection of conversations, from young boyhood to young manhood, with his mother. It's from her, plainly, that he got his dramatic imagination; she was a dominating fantasist who never lost, or ran out of, an argument. Lucy Peacock scores an unquestionable triumph in the role, even managing to get a French lilt into the English text. She does, though, let the woman's wilder flights run away with her; shes best, and most varied, when quieter: musing on whether the actors she sees on TV ever think about her, or, in an unutterably moving last scene, still tough but dying of cancer. Tom Rooney is subtly superb as the storyteller/antagonist, making flawless osmotic distinction between his ages without, apparently, moving a muscle. The play says less about the power of theatre than it thinks it does, so its beginning and end seem unwarrantably cute. But not, in Chris Abrahams production, offensively. It's the kind of minor play to which a major playwright earns the right.
