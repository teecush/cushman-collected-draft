THEATRE REVIEW

THE RUSSIAN PLAYS

HARBOURFRONT STUDIO THEATRE

Hannah Moscovitch's The Russian Play, the jewel of last year's Summerworks festival, is back in town, pluralized. The original three-actor piece has now been joined by a monologue entitled USSR that is almost as good. The resultant double bill runs until Sunday as part of the Hatch season of "emerging performance projects" at Harbourfront. It deserves, measuring shortness of run against quality of product, to be the hottest ticket in Toronto.

The original Russian Play starts off as a knowing satire on the traditional Western view of Slavic gloom. Mention is made of the tragic stories associated with Tolstoy and Chekhov, and we are then told a tale in which some of that spirit is transmuted into the later era of Stalin and the KGB. It concerns Sonya, who works in a small-town flower shop, conveniently placed near the local graveyard. At the age of 16, she has an affair with Petya, the gravedigger, who impregnates her and then goes back to Moscow, where he has a wife. "OK," says the narrator tartly (she says nearly everything tartly), "so heart of Sonya is broken, but heart is very strong organ." Besides, says Sonya herself, "what can gravedigger do for you -- dig your grave when you are dead." In fact, in the course of the play, Petya does precisely this for two of his nearest and dearest. Sonya moves to Smolensk, where she is kept by Kostya, a rich young kulak who treats her well until she starts pining for her sexy sexton. Kostya has secret police connections and calls on them to get her out of his way. The play's last section is an extraordinary combination of bleakly funny and heartbreakingly sad.

Moscovitch never puts a word wrong, and her mastery of tone is perfectly reflected in Natasha Mytnowych's direction. The production has now acquired a set (designed, like most sets at the moment, by Camellia Koo) consisting of a couple of screens craftily lit by Kimberly Purtell from behind, where characters glide on stage to miraculous effect. There is, as before, moody guitar accompaniment from Tom Howell, plus some other music composed by Claire Jenkins. The show also employs a choreographer, Monica Dottor, who has devised some haunting erotic pas de deux. Michelle Monteith is both the flinty narrator and the tender-hearted Sonya, two sides of the same kopeck, both ringing absolutely true. Everything she does is devastatingly strong, nothing is excessive and nothing unwanted spills over from one part of the play or the performance to another. There is no better acting currently happening in this town. As the two men by whom Sonya is loved and betrayed, Aaron Willis and Shawn Campbell are perfect on a smaller scale, one defiant in his perfidy, the other shamefaced.

USSR gives us an even later Russia, or perhaps a Russian diaspora. The protagonist-narrator is Elena, a Moscow doctor (unemployed, or at least unpaid) in the post-Soviet era who met a Canadian businessman, married him, came back with him to Toronto and bore an adored child. Loneliness and the language barrier undo her, and the ending is as stark as that of the companion play. The text is witty and so, in a satanically compassionate way, is the narrative development.

In both these plays, the law of unintended consequences is working flat-out and remorselessly. (As it also does, come to think of it, in Moscovitch's earlier play Essay, which proved that she could also handle a contemporary non-ethnic theme, without a hint of pastiche.) The publicity sells the play as being about "mail order brides," but that undersells it. It also implicitly claims that the play is about relations between Russia and Canada, and this maybe oversells it. It doesn't matter. This time the same director and lighting person also share the design credit; the production unfolds on a stage floor covered with table lamps, all but one of which have gone out by the time the action turns decisively dark. As Elena, Maev Beaty flawlessly conveys the sardonic vulnerability that, to this date at least, is the author's hallmark; I said there was no better acting in Toronto than Monteith's in the first play but I didn't say there was none as good.
