Sky Gilbert has never been short on cheek, so it's no particular surprise that he should have written a play baldly entitled I Have AIDS. (It is not, he insists, autobiographical.) Still, there is a mild shock when his protagonist, in the first scene, says to his live-in lover, in between commenting on the latest developments on Beverly Hills 90210 and asking what's for dinner, "I almost forgot; I have AIDS." His partner's response is equally urbane, unruffled and unlikely.

The partner's name is Vidor; he's an interior designer, who emerges from the kitchen wearing an apron bearing the slogan "not your average housewife," and it's down to him that the play's setting has the kind of elegance that we've come to associate with Gilbert as with no other Canadian playwright. Prodon, the one who's ill, is a stand-up comic -- as is Gavin Crawford, who plays him -- and he proceeds to take advantage of his profession by picking up a mic and talking to us directly. He tells us, if I'm following correctly, that what we've just seen is a parody of the way gay men who are HIV-positive are supposed to behave in plays, now that the diagnosis is no longer a death-sentence. The rest of the evening is a demonstration of how they do behave, or at least of how this one would.

Prodon takes an abbreviated five-step journey from denial to acceptance. Denial consists of aggressive promiscuity, which is what he was doing anyway. This is a style he throws in the face of a visiting female health-worker, who asks for a list of his contacts and is told that this is impossible since there've been more than 10,000 of them, mostly anonymous. The lady is shocked and, up to a point, mocked. But only up to a point; Prodon's author, if not Prodon himself, is prepared to grant her some dignity, thereby reinforcing my conviction that, beneath all the bravado, Gilbert is a decently, and rather conventionally, talented playwright.

The health lady is played by Ryan Kelly; it's the first of three performances he gives in this show and probably the best, though the least showy. The second is of Lady Booty, a drag-queen who accompanies Prodon to a club where some of the activities are graphically demonstrated and others are even more graphically described. In the aftermath of this, Prodon changes his ways, takes his meds and plays host to a third guest, Ron, a lonely man who claims that only through contracting AIDS has he found a place in the world. He gives a long speech that touches both Prodon and the audience (Kelly got exit applause on the first night), but that leaves Vidor unmoved. His function throughout is to act as the voice of reason, or at least to disagree with Prodon while continuing to love him; he identifies Ron as "a man who defines himself by having AIDS" and he doesn't want Prodon to go the same route.

Nobody in Canadian theatre writes more fluent, speakable dialogue than Gilbert, and once again, as his own director, he gets excellent performances. David Yee as Vidor makes an admirable anchor, while Crawford goes from acid desperation to acrid acceptance. At the end he reverts to stand-up mode, telling us that the purpose of his story is to remind us that AIDS hasn't gone away from the lives of Canadian gay men, even though people like Stephen Lewis would have us believe that nowadays it's all about Africa. I doubt the continued existence of the noble-suffering AIDS plays against which Gilbert claims he's setting himself; at least, I don't get to see them. More to the point, as I watched the author's talents struggling to find a story to contain them, I renewed my long-held disbelief in the viability of illness -- any illness -- as a dramatic subject. Either you write a medical soap about the Search for a Cure or you end up with a hard-luck story, in which characters suffer from something they can't control and for which they aren't responsible. It's sad, indeed it's horrible, but it precludes free will and denies drama.

For a dramatist who writes marvellous dialogue and tells good stories and deals with choice, I commend you to the American Stephen Adly Guirgis, author of The Last Days of Judas Iscariot. Den of Thieves, a slighter but still terrifically enjoyable play of his, is being premiered here by Surface-Underground theatre, an Equity co-op: very well too, with Jamie Robinson giving his most assured performance yet as an almost-reformed kleptomaniac with Robin Hood tendencies and Lisa Norton riotously forsaking her Shaw Festival roots as an all-purpose moll. They are half of a gang of four -- two confirmed crooks, two trying to go straight through yet another addicts' program -- who get involved in what promises to be an easy heist and find that they've invaded the home of a Cosa Nostra family who spend the play's second half wondering how, or whether, to kill them. The Mafia characters are from stock, though with twists; some of the plot-turns are predictable, but in a satisfying way. What takes us by surprise is the contest in self-sacrifice that the captives engage in when there's no other option; this is moving, both in writing and performance, and not remotely sentimental. Guirgis is on about redemption again, though not here in an explicitly Christian context. He's also wonderfully funny. He's the real deal -- America's best since Mamet -- and it's criminal that none of our established theatres have acknowledged him. Not that they'd likely improve on these fine independent productions, but they could at least provide greater exposure.
