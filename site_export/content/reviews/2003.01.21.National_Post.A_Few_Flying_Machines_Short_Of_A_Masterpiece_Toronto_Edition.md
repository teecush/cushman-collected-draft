VINCI

Bluma Appel Theatre, Toronto

Maureen Hunter's Vinci is an old-fashioned biographical- historical play, whose twist is that its central character never actually appears.

The Vinci in question is, of course, Leonardo, of that name and of that small Italian village. We know he was the illegitimate son of a lawyer and a servant, and that he seems to have been brought up by the people who more or less owned his hometown and who gave their name to it. On these foundations Hunter has erected the imagined story of young Pietro da Vinci who seduces Caterina, the family maid, despite being requested by his friend Bartolomeo, a novice of the priesthood, to leave her alone. Caterina becomes pregnant, and - - against all precedent for 15th-century Italian girls in her situation -- refuses to give the child, a boy, up for adoption. Instead, she goes into far less glamorous service with the chandler at the top of a nearby hill, and brings up her son, whose identity you can probably guess. He is, as you would also expect, a bright and precocious child and we hear her shouting encouragement to him as he plays, just off stage, with his pre-prototype flying machines. Hes always making things she tells a visitor proudly.

Meanwhile, Pietro, his oats mostly sown, makes an advantageous match with the richly dowered Albiera, who proves unable to bear him a child and is made to feel very guilty about it. Faced with the prospect of the da Vinci line dying out, Antonio, Pietros father, begins to think longingly of his grandson up the mountain, and puts all kinds of pressure on Caterina to surrender him. She resists, encouraged by Bartolomeo, who after a tour of duty in other parishes has returned to look after the souls in his native patch.

He is our guide to the action, and he provides a prologue and epilogue that show him, many years later, standing proudly in front of Leonardo's famous painting of the Last Supper. He has obviously kept in touch. Strangely enough, there is no mention in the body of the play of the young Leonardo evincing any kind of pictorial talent. The emphasis is all on his flair for making model planes. The painting is thrown in as cultural ballast. You might also call it cultural blackmail since, if we weren't aware of what the boy was later to achieve, we would not find the story of his first five years on Earth very interesting.

From what we are actually shown, the play might well be named for the place rather than the person: an everyday story of Renaissance countryfolk. Much of the drama centres on Caterinas proud fight to be a single mother. She seems, like her son, to be ahead of her time, but the characterization her author has devised for her is the combined product of sentimentality and wishful thinking. You dont have to subscribe to the philistine bromides of cultural materialism (which holds that all people and all art are products of the social forces acting at the time, that the idea of an unchanging "human nature" is a myth, and that no era has a chance of empathizing with any other) to suspect that people 550 years ago may have taken certain things for granted that we don't. The play is written in the kind of levelling schoolteacherly style that regards our ancestors as being just like us but in funny clothes.

Nursing sore memories of The Beard of Avon (the previous play at this address -- CanStage seems to making a policy out of trivializing genius), I can confidently assert that nobody wants an evening of mock-Elizabethan, which anyway would be anachronistic on its own account, quite apart from being in the wrong language. The trouble is not that the dialogue has no period flavour, but that it has no flavour at all other than that of cliche. At best, it suggests that the characters have been reading the wrong books. They can't make a plain statement without attaching a stale image to it. She hung suspended between life and death somebody informs us. "My sins, like pigeons, have come home to roost," laments somebody else - - we would get the point without the pigeons. You may notice, though, that both those quoted lines scan. One common characteristic of mediocre period plays is their tendency to fall unconsciously into blank verse.

Hunter can tell a story. Her play holds the attention more easily than do many that are, theoretically, better written. But she sets all manner of traps for her actors. Patricia Fagan keeps fastidiously clear of most of them. She manages to endow Caterina with a bit of passion and a great deal of dignity, though she cannot make us believe a final act of self-sacrifice that runs counter to everything for which she has hitherto been standing. Fiona Byrne is touching as the kindly, barren Albiera, thrown awkwardly into a tragic situation she did nothing to deserve. The two women share the best scene in the play, each of them bringing out unsuspected layers of feeling in the other. They are joined at its end by Craig Ericksons Pietro, who makes a believable playboy with an intermittently active conscience. Dylan Trowbridge is pleasing, though little more, as his younger brother, a character of little visible use.

As Antonio, a reluctantly heavy father who would rather cultivate his garden (literally), Robert Benson unwisely endorses every platitude his author foists on him. Gordon Rand worries amiably away at the role of Bartolomeo, without much success. Its never clear how central his character is meant to be. Are his struggles to realize his own vocation the point of the play, or are they merely a titillating side show? (I know Catholicism is a very theatrical religion, but aren't our playwrights succumbing too easily to the lure of the confessional? This play is the third in two months to make use of that all-too-convenient device.)

If the piece is about the priest's relationship to the artist, who presumably had his own vocational issues, then it's unfortunate most of it takes place in the years that have been left undramatized. The director is Dennis Garnhum, who seems recently to have been specializing in less-than-perfect plays. He generally puts a gloss on them without actually managing to elevate them. John Jenkins' set is a revolving contraption (also standard for history plays) that endeavours to suggest a da Vincian invention. And The Last Supper looks stunning under the lights.
