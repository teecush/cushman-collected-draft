THEATRE REVIEW GENTLEMEN PREFER BLONDES

Monday, May 31, 1993

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Niagara-on-the-Lake ONT -- WRITTEN BY JULE STYNE, LEO ROBIN, ANITA LOOS and JOSEPH FIELDS Directed by Susan Cox Starring Rosemary Doyle, Lee MacDougall, Paul McQuillan, Ian Simpson, Jan A. Smith and Karen Skidmore At the Shaw Festival, Royal George Theatre, Rating: A big show in its own day, Gentlemen Prefer Blondes is an inconsequential one in ours; and the Shaw Festival's miniature musical revivals are instantly disarming affairs that never pretend to offer us the greatest show on earth Playing fast and Loos BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Niagara-on-the-Lake SUSAN Cox has directed Gentlemen Prefer Blondes, quite niftily, as though it were a 1920s musical. In a way, it is. It is set in that decade and based on one of its classic texts: Anita Loos's study of the theory and practice of gold-digging, the beddable in full pursuit of the bankable. But Loos did not turn her novel into a show until 1949, by which time musicals were standing heavier in the scales. This one spends its first half on a transatlantic liner and its second in Paris, both locations being theatrical shorthand for frothy hedonism. Even so, it lacks the dizzy salacity of its forebears, and its music, written in the aftermath of swing, is beefier. A song called Homesick is so concerned to evoke the New York of the twenties, so crammed with nostalgic detail, that it could only have been written later.

There is much to be said for sweating some of the weight off. A big show in its own day, Blondes is an inconsequential one in ours; and the Shaw's miniature musical revivals are instantly disarming affairs that never pretend to offer us the greatest show on earth. We take what we can get, which often turns out to be considerable. Dialogue is invariably fast and frequently funny; unfamiliar songs can take us by delightful surprise, and so, when we hear them in context, can familiar ones.

Blondes is the story of Lorelei Lee and some others, the others being established with a song or a scene each, then left to languish for the rest of the evening, and abruptly picked up when needed. It gets increasingly hard to remember which swain is the prey of which floozy, which dragonish mother owns which emotionally stunted son.

Sensibly and economically, these scattered principals are here gathered up and made to do duty as a chorus. This often involves them in voicing sentiments that are clearly not their own, but nobody minds. Nor does it matter that the dancers, even the good ones, are constantly flashing nervous smiles at one another ("how are we doing?") or triumphant ones at the audience ("hey, we made it"). It's all fun.

But there are limits, and Rosemary Doyle's non-performance of Lorelei is way beyond them. It has to be dwelt on, because Lorelei is the show's reason for being. She is the quintessential predatress, fanatical in her determination to turn her temporary physical assets into permanent financial ones, genuinely appalled that anyone - especially her best friend Dorothy who starts with the same advantages - should fail to understand. She says outrageously cynical things with an innocent's conviction; Carol Channing on stage, Marilyn Monroe on film, patented a style of playing her for which nobody has ever found a better word than "wide-eyed." (And who knows in either case how much was luck and how much was judgment?) If there is another way of doing it, it has not been discovered here. Lorelei's unconscious epigrams slide tonelessly, witlessly by; you have to create your own entertainment by replaying them in your mind with appropriate inflections. In song, she is a little better; the house rose to her Diamonds Are A Girl's Best Friend (and how can you resist a lyric that rhymes "pear-shape" with "their shape" and "stiff knees" with "Tiff'nys"), but it is my opinion that they didn't know what they were applauding about.

Jan Alexandra Smith almost compensates as Dorothy, a role that starts out ace with a did-she-really-say-that song I Love What I'm Doing (When I'm Doing it For Love) -i.e. giving it away for free - but declines into romantic second banana thereafter. (But she too winds up with a millionaire. Discuss.) Paul McQuillan has presence and drive as Gus Esmond Jr., heir to a button empire, whose position as Lorelei's sugar-daddy is seriously threatened by the advent of the zipper. Choreography sometimes seems uncertain whether or not it is trying to be funny, but in these reduced circumstances Cassel Miles's hoofing almost justifies the script's trying to pass him off as the late, great Charles Honi Coles.

The real thing, though, is Jule Styne's score which ripples with energy and invention. It boasts Bye Bye Baby and Just A Little Girl From Little Rock (Dorothy helping Lorelei out so enthusiastically that it almost becomes Two Little Girls, as in the movie) and It's Delightful Down In Chile, a self-parodying tango. (Is there any other kind?) Leo Robin, the most overlooked of Golden Age lyricists, springs some surprises; a song called I'm A Tingle, I'm A Glow, turns out to be a hymn to laxatives. Christopher Donison's musical direction is fine, and I liked the reference to Look for the Silver Lining that creeps into the arrangement for Homesick. The sets, attractively featuring a blue sky and hatboxes, were designed by William Schmuck. Such names they have at Shaw. One of the actors is called Geoffrey Whynot, and whynot? Just think, if the original producers had brought in Howard Fast to help Anita Loos with her libretto, what headlines we might have had.
