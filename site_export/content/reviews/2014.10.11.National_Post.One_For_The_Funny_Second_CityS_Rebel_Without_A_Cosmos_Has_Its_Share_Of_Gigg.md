The new revue Rebel Without a Cosmos is good second-division Second City. That makes it a bit of a letdown after its predecessor Sixteen Scandals, which was very good first division. But let's concentrate on the happy things. These would definitely include Connor Thompson, a holdover from the previous show who, when not heavily disguised, could pass for a solid young leading man in a classical repertory company. A lot of the time, though, he is heavily disguised. The show's best single number finds him as a children's entertainer who, through a confusion of bookings, finds himself engaged at a Remembrance Day ceremony. Called upon to sing about the First World War he comes up with "The First World War was lots of fun /As World Wars go it was Number One," which I guess is incontrovertible, the second part anyway. He gets into deeper water when trying to make the number of casualties the subject of a counting game, while a similarly cheerful approach to the Second World War doesn't go down well at all. He also takes suggestions from the audience, demonstrating an impressive ability to improvise in rhyme. Later on in the show, we get a brief look at his opposite number, the war memorialist performing at a kids' party and scaring the daylights out of them.

Thompson also appears, beneath impenetrable hair, as Pete, who spends his days propping up the outside wall of a gas station in Owen Sound. (Owen Sound is a running gag in this show.) Pete is a kind of walking - well, sprawling - GPS, who can give you instant directions to any place at all, if you can decipher his high-speed mumblings, and can also hand out lifealtering advice, such as telling the young lady who's just hit town to leave it as soon as possible and to ditch the guy what brung her, an insufferable Film Studies major. Pete is a definite fount of wisdom.

Another for the books is Ashley Botting, accepting a Miss Average Canada award, while paying tribute to the 90% of other women in the room who must be equally deserving, as well as to Joe Fresh and Jackson Triggs who made her averageness possible. Botting also has a love song to and about Chris Hadfield, a number not only well-sung but well-written. Indeed musically this show is far above the Second City average.

Ethan Muskat and Kevin Whalen, both new Second Citizens, share a well-pointed sketch about Apple stores and their groundbreaking contributions to built-in obsolescence. (Old-fashioned customer: "I'll have you know I've been using the same razor for 20 years." New-fashioned clerk: "Gross.") The company is completed by Allison Price, tall and thin and generally helpful, though starved of good material. The director is Reid Janisse. The designer is Camellia Koo, whose set pays handsome tribute to the show's inter-galactic title, though not much else does. We're used to Second City naming its revues with inconsequential puns; usually they have some pretence to topicality but maybe this time, in taking a 60-year-old movie as their inspiration, they're going for timelessness.

Talking of titles: The Thing Between Us is the bond between Linda and Shannon who first meet as 10-year-olds, and whom we follow into womanhood. Shannon's father is dead, and her mother has gone away to a spa in Switzerland to recover. Linda's parents are Shannon's godparents, and they take the girl in, thereby providing their own daughter, an only child, with an instant sister. At first Shannon's behaviour suggests that she's been terribly spoiled; later it seems likelier that she was horribly neglected, emotionally at least. She lies, and manipulates, shearing Linda's long and beautiful hair and also taking her sex education in hand by leading her onto the roof to observe, through binoculars, her parents making love. (We never actually meet Linda's dad - he's usually on the road - but it seems that when he's home he makes up for lost time. At any rate Shannon is impressed. Linda, we later learn, is traumatized.)

Alison Lawrence's play sets the girls' relationship up economically and intriguingly but, as Shannon grows from problem child to full-blown sociopath, it can hardly help spinning its wheels. Shannon keeps getting into awful trouble and doing awful things, many of them to Linda; Linda keeps excusing and enabling her, and the main tension for the audience lies in waiting and hoping for the moment when she'll say "enough!" Rooting for her comes easy, as Emily Hurson, though tactfully restrained in Aaron Willis's production from any physical attempt at representing a 10-yearold, gets the young cast of mind and voice perfectly and then shows it growing. Mary Francis Moore, though, acts Shannon backwards; she sounds like an adult from the start, so there's no disturbing tension between how we'd expect a child to behave and how this one actually does. Randi Helmers does her best with Linda's mother whose continuing indulgence of Shannon may be even greater than Linda's own, and is certainly more inexplicable; well, an explanation is offered but it's late and unconvincing. She's also the occasion of Linda's final disillusion, in a scene that is certainly harrowing but is also crude and manipulative. At the end Linda learns that there was apparently more to Shannon than she had ever known. The news comes rather late for her to process, and altogether too late for us.

Rebel Without a Cosmos runs indefinitely at Second City; The Thing Between Us runs at Theatre Passe Muraille Backspace through Oct. 19.
