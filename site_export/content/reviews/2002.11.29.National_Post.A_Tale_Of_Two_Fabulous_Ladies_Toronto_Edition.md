JUST BETWEEN FRIENDS

Winter Garden Theatre, Toronto

ELAINE STRITCH AT LIBERTY

Old Vic Theatre, London

- - -

Bea Arthur and Elaine Stritch have a lot in common. Both are American actresses in their seventies with distinguished careers and distinctive voices, the word "distinctive" meaning, of course, that the noises they make, especially in song, are arresting rather than conventionally pretty. Both apprenticed at the same off-Broadway workshop, though apparently at different times. (At any rate, neither in her reminiscences mentions the other.) Both were offered leading roles in Golden Girls, a TV series that in celebrating longevity also managed to achieve it; Stritch turned the offer down, Arthur, as you may have heard, didn't. Both have stories to tell, and are telling them on stage in one-woman shows -- Arthur accompanied by a lone pianist, Stritch by a bouncing band.

Arthur got great notices when she tried her show out last year in the American hinterlands, but far cooler ones when she took it into New York. Her thunder had been stolen by Stritch who -- first off- Broadway and then on it -- scored the greatest personal triumph the city has seen in years. Having just seen them both -- Arthur in Toronto, Stritch last week in London -- I can see why.

Arthurs handicap in presenting a musical retrospective is that she hasnt actually done very many musicals. Those she has been in have mostly been high-profile, but in the most famous of them all -- Fiddler on the Roof, she played the matchmaker -- she barely sang. She was Lucy in the legendary off-Broadway production of The Threepenny Opera, and she gives us a song from the score, but not one she sang at the time. In fact, the only numbers that turn up from her performing past (since she sadly declines to sing a 1950s revue torcher called Garbage) are two from Mame, in which she played second banana to Angela Lansbury. One of these, Bosom Buddies, was originally a duet, so I guess it's logical that she only does half of it.

For the rest, it's all very arbitrary: songs from shows she wishes shed done or songs she presumably just likes. The latter category includes standards (Who Cares, Lets Face the Music and Dance, and a special favourite of mine, Fun to Be Fooled) that she dispatches very pleasantly and unfussily. Her voice is a flexible foghorn, and she seldom forces it beyond what it can achieve; also, and more importantly, she is a whale on colouring, but rarely over- colouring, a lyric.

Sometimes this isn't enough. She sings Some People, from Gypsy, and tells us how much she'd always wanted to play Mama Rose, though on this evidence she would have been very miscast; she doesnt carry the guns, and her Rose sounds more of a nag than a force of nature.

On the other hand, from Ballroom, a show composed by her accompanist, Billy Goldenberg, she takes Fifty Per Cent, a song usually guaranteed to bring out the screaming over-actress in any woman who sings it, despite the fact its supposed to be about settling for half. She banks it down, revealing it to be more specific and more affecting than I'd ever imagined.

She is very good at bitter. Pirate Jenny, the vengeful Threepenny Opera number that she offers in homage to Lotte Lenya, is hair- raising. It comes, though, with melodramatic lighting that is completely out of place in the casual impromptu entertainment she is pretending to offer us -- one that comes with the nudgingly ingratiating title of Just Between Friends.

Its conceit is that we are more or less in her living room and that she is talking directly to us, right off the cuff. For a time this works; she catches us off-guard by starting her performance with a string of recipes for roast lamb, and a couple of the defiantly off-colour jokes she tells are very funny. But her interplay with Goldenberg (an appealingly droopy and wizened figure, crouched at the ivories) looks and sounds appallingly unspontaneous; and her late avowal that she stepped on stage scared of being herself but that we had absolved her of her terrors is ludicrous. It's far too late for her to carry on as if this were her first night; we know shes being doing it for months.

And its not as if she reveals anything much about herself anyway. Most of her anecdotes are about other people, and she never casts herself in anything resembling a bad light. At times she can be unlovably self-approving: Musing on her TV triumphs, Maude and Golden Girls, she says, We picked a few bubbles, we broke a few taboos -- the kind of remark that might just be tolerable if offered to an interviewer on late-night TV, but not when planted right in the laps of a paying audience. If I were feeling cruel, I might describe her as a musical star who never was, revealing a self that never is. I'll settle instead for saying that her best numbers are worth experiencing, but that you have to shut out much of what lies between.

Elaine Stritch at Liberty is twice as long and very different. For a start, it's difficult to resist a 76-year-old woman who comes on stage in flapping white shirt and black tights, never puts on another thing, and gets away with it. Arthur fixes us with a patrician glare; Stritch shows us a tough mask that cracks, and she is tireless to the point of defiance about admitting her insecurities and indiscretions. She tells us about her long- preserved virginity and about her bad luck with men from Marlon Brando to Rock Hudson. She rhapsodizes (one of her few concessions to outright sentimentality) about her marriage, cut short by his early death, to English actor John Bay. She goes in moderately harrowing detail, laced with a little black humour, into her long history of alcoholism.

Confessional theatre is not my favourite thing -- I cling to the belief that actors are meant to refract their emotions through those of other characters, not to offer them to us neat -- but if its going to be done, it had better be done thoroughly and honestly. Despite all the name-dropping, Stritch skimps on her love-life -- we want her to tell us either more or less -- but her drink habit is as compelling to us as it was compulsive for her. Her script ("constructed by John Lahr, deconstructed by Elaine Stritch") is no masterpiece, but at least it exists. Its a serviceable frame, and it has something definite to surround.

To put it another way, Stritch has a musical history to go with the personal. Many of the London critics commented that her voice is not what it was; in fact it's almost exactly what it was: a rasp that sways. It's an attractive version of the Broadway belt -- soft- grained and hard-edged. By pausing, pouncing and driving a point home, she can squeeze the juice from a lyric and even from a melody; certainly from a rhythm. Her Broadway portfolio is not much more capacious than Arthurs, but at least it contains songs that are identifiably hers, and she sings them, if anything, better now than she did then.

From the 40s revue Angel in the Wings, her debut, she sings the tongue-twisting Civilisation (a.k.a. Bingo Bango Bango, I Don't Want to Leave the Congo) with unblushing insouciance; here, as in Broadway Baby (from Follies), her face looks blinkingly young. Clever lighting (originally by the great Jules Fisher) helps, of course, but in a show that never pretends to be anything but theatrical, that's acceptable. She does Zip, the mock-strip from Rodgers and Harts Pal Joey, and teases it out (that seems the right phrase) by telling the story of how she shuttled between theatres, and even between cities, when combining this one-song role with the job of understudying Ethel Merman in Call Me Madam; theres rather too much reconstruction-of-imaginary-conversations going on here, but it's fun.

There is nothing, alas, from Goldilocks, a flop show with some great songs, two of which -- Who's Been Sleeping in My Bed and I Never Know When to Say When -- would, by title alone, fit perfectly. But when she gets to Sail Away, written for her by Noel Coward, she makes more of Why Do the Wrong People Travel than anyone, including her, ever has before. And eventually theres Stephen Sondheims Company, with The Ladies Who Lunch and, even better, The Little Things You Do Together. Everything she sings has style, and intelligence, and authority; she even knows when to understate.

She does oversell Sondheim's I'm Still Here, a song that means something different, and something less, when removed from its context in Follies; it ceases to be a characterized chronicle and becomes a mere troupers anthem. Arthur, to her credit, explictly declines to sing it, though her pianist plays it as she takes her bows. In general, Stritch fares less well with unanchored songs that are supposed to serve as commentary, though theres one, the Gershwins' But Not for Me, on which she hits a splendidly appropriate note of vinegary regret. It's meant as commentary on her own situation; what sits in my memory, though, is not how the song illuminates her but how she illuminates the song.
