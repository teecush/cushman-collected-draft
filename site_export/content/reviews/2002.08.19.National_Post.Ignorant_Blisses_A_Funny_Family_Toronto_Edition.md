HAY FEVER

Festival Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

- - -

NIAGARA-ON-THE-LAKE - The Shaw Festival production of Noel Coward's Hay Fever has, most unusually for this play, a sombre beginning. Be reassured that it has a screamingly funny middle and end, but that opening is dark: literally.

Young Simon Bliss, son of the bohemian household in which the play unfolds, has drawn the blinds in order to contemplate a drawing of Lawrence of Arabia he has just completed. Sorel, his sister, reads a poem that Simon, justifiably, pronounces morbid. Actors playing this scene usually offer their lines half to the audience and half to the ghost of Coward, whom they are trying to appease by appearing as brittle and disembodied as possible. This couple, by contrast, are discontented young people having a conversation. They are setting up the rest of the play, to its great benefit, rather than filling in time before the arrival of the leading lady.

That lady, leading in all senses, is their mother Judith, the 1920s West End actress of sentimental melodramas (specimen title: Love's Whirlwind) gone into premature retirement and trying, with more enthusiasm than conviction, to conquer the role of country- house chatelaine.

Her husband, David, writes best-selling romantic novels of less than dubious quality (specimen title: The Sinful Woman). Judith never left the theatre in spirit and may soon return to it in reality: Meanwhile, the world is her stage, or at least the Thames Valley is. And it's catching. Sorel, the family member with the nearest approximation to a conscience, notes that "one always plays up to mother in this house; it's an unwritten law."

In Christopher Newton's production (his valedictory offering as the Shaw's artistic director) the differing degrees to which the clan members obey this law are very carefully marked out. The comedy's famous hook is that each individual Bliss has, without telling the others, invited a guest of the opposite sex down for the weekend. Partner-swapping ensues, wholesale but chaste, and worked out by the author with mathematical precision: The guests depart early, having been variously bruised, abused and -- most humiliating of all -- ignored. David is unselfconsciously, and Simon very self- consciously, exploitative. Sorel fights to be nice, or at least to be what the world calls normal, but she too gets sucked into the narcissistic whirlpool.

As Judith, Fiona Reid, like Seana McKenna in last year's Stratford production of Private Lives, displays the endemic tendency of Canadian Coward actresses to end up sounding exactly like Maggie Smith. Or rather, McKenna ended up that way, while with Reid it's an inescapable starting-point. This is initially disconcerting, but she goes on to carve out her own territory.

Smith's specialty is the comedy of alarm: Reid can do that, but what she gives us here is the comedy of wilful self-confidence; a woman whose dramas are her own invention, to be acted out strictly under her own direction. She enters from the garden wearing the kind of hat that might have provoked the French Revolution. Once launched into action she turns, repeatedly, on a dime. Having inveigled a helpless diplomat into kissing her, she cries out without missing a beat that "David must be told," and really does seem to believe that she is behaving, as she herself prescribes, with "absolute honesty."

She has at least three other moments for the anthologies. Speaking of a woman who "goes around using sex like a sort of shrimping-net" she irradiates what I have always thought a pretty vapid attempt at a comic line by topping it off with a brief illustrative mime. Remarking that "David's been a good husband to me, but he's wearing a bit thin now," she in effect ignores the comma, taking the line on one breath, treating the second half as a climax rather than an afterthought, and bringing down the house. Later, she discovers that same husband in the arms of another woman. According to Coward's stage-direction she observes them from the top of a staircase (a commanding staircase is de rigeur in a Coward play, and William Schmuck, the Niagara designer, duly provides one) and then comes down. Reid does nothing so commonplace. She is halfway down, by the normal method, when she sees them and executes the rest of the trip by coming down the banister in a sliding slump.

Michael Ball, in his best performance for several seasons, is sleekly self-absorbed as David, a mixture of lounge lizard and hermit crab. Mike Shara, who is cornering the Niagara market in willowy dilettantes, adds Simon to his collection, and Severn Thompson is Sorel, her fastidious intentions curdling into the most aggressive rudeness. (The director sees her development as the backbone of the play. It's apparent on the stage as well as in his program note, though maybe not to the decisive extent that seems to have been intended.) As in The Return of the Prodigal, one of the joys of Newton's production is the believability of this collection of monsters as a family. They belong together.

Hay Fever, famously, has no real plot, but it sure as hell has construction. For a play that its author claimed virtually to have improvised, it interlocks superbly. One thing leads, imperturbably but explosively, to another or, even more devastatingly, fails to. (The Blisses are better at furnishing cause than at recognizing effect: They seem to have invented collective amnesia.) A scene is provided for each possible male-female couple. Note the word "possible"; after the necessary expository scene at the top, no two Blisses are ever allowed to be alone together, because they would have nothing to say.

Among the victimized visitors here, Laurie Paton is sharp as the vampish Myra, the worm who comes closest to turning, and Kevin Bundy engagingly clueless as the sporty Sandy. He spectacularly initiates a running gag in which each of the guests in turn gets scorched by a breakfast hot-plate. This, breaking the oldest rule of comedy, is funny four times over: five, if you count the practised ease with which the Blisses themselves, hands wreathed in napkins, handle the situation.

This production defuses the complaint, once voiced by an English critic, that "the bores are more entertaining than the Blisses." This has its drawbacks; David Schurmann's diplomat and Lisa Norton's flapper make amazingly little of their embarrassed non-conversation on whether bullfights are crueller to horses or to bulls. Both warm up later. Note to old Shaw hands: In this production Michael Ball plays what would normally be the David Schurmann role and vice versa. Everyone is the better for it. The one extra character -- Clara, the theatrical dresser turned all-purpose domestic -- is played, to grim-faced perfection, by Mary Haney.
