Soulpepper, Albert Schultz reminded me, will be 10 years old next July. Its a decade since the classical theatre collective of which he is the artistic director (there's a contradiction in there somewhere but I don't think it matters) astounded us all with its production of Schillers Don Carlos, a 200-year-old play that nobody in Canada had ever seen. Since then, the company has become a vital part of the landscape. Its hard, depending on your angle of vision, to believe that it has been around such a short time or that it has survived so long.

And, of course, after eight inaugural seasons at Harbourfront, Soulpepper is now in its second year on its own premises in the Distillery District. When I met with Schultz this week in the pub down the cobbled street from the Young Centre, what was on my mind was not the upcoming anniversary but the current achievement. Soulpepper now has three productions running on its two major stages -- Top Girls in the smaller space, Our Town and The Time of Your Life rotating in the larger one -- and all are first-rate. I don't know of any other Toronto company that has ever achieved such a thing, or of one that could. What gives Soulpepper its edge and popularity is name-and-face recognition; broadly speaking, the people on stage are the organization. The core actors -- the dozen listed in the program as founder members, plus one or two others -- have, for the most part, stuck around. This kind of actor-based persistence in a main-stream company is very rare.

One factor in this is that Schultz himself, apart from blossoming as a director, also continues to act, more often these days than ever. He's currently in rehearsal in Chekhovs Three Sisters, directed by Laszlo Marton, the Hungarian who has already given Soulpepper some of its greatest productions including two other Chekhovs, Uncle Vanya and Platonov, and Ibsens The Wild Duck. The cast includes founding veterans like Diego Matamoros and others who have grown up within the company. Everybody knows one another, says Schultz. "That's the secret of this company, the driving force behind it. It's really exciting to be in a rehearsal room watching someone like Patricia Fagan, whom you spotted in theatre school and have seen grow every year, to see what shes doing, just a couple of weeks into rehearsal." A third layer of the cast contains the inaugural members of Soulpeppers Academy.

I've been with these people every day for 12 months. I've taught them, acted with them, directed them, made music with them -- theres not a single one of those 15 people I dont have a relationship with.

It helps to have your own building. "Joe Ziegler [actor, director and founding member] says that, when he walks into the front door of the Young Centre, he feels at home." Audiences, I think, feel the same, and I buy Schultz's statement that the company is richer and deeper because of its proximity to itself; all the operations are under one roof. That said, it's taken until now for Soulpepper to find its feet in its new surroundings. The companys two worst productions ever ( The Importance of Being Earnest and John Gabriel Borkman) have been here, and last years repertoire seemed lightweight, full of worthy but small-scale (and newish) plays. It can't be coincidence that this season, bigger and bolder, has also been better.

One thing Soulpepper has always had going for it is that in Toronto almost any classic is a rare classic; even Hamlet was the play's first major Toronto outing in decades. There are still areas that remain largely untouched: most of English-language drama before 1900 for example. Schultz wants "to explore the repertoire a bit more broadly"; he admits that the company has been nervous of Restoration comedy because none of us have any experience of it. Next year, though, they are mounting The Way of the World, directed by the National Arts Centres Peter Hinton, probably the most stylish director in Canada.

What else does Schultz dream about? "A bigger, 500-seat theatre across the street where we could put our successes." (That doesnt seem too likely.) I'd like to be able to tour internationally while still maintaining a 12-month repertoire at home. (Also difficult, but necessary. Soulpepper sometimes produces world-class work, and the world should see it.) "More money. We get the lowest urban subsidy of any company in the country. What I'm trying to avoid is either stuffing the repertoire with one-or-two-person shows or cutting down on rehearsal time. We do seven weeks average Thats what makes us what we are."

You cant, says Schultz, "put a bunch of actors together and expect them to be an ensemble." That's an exaggeration; ensemble has happened in ad hoc productions in Toronto, and in less concentrated circumstances at the Shaw and Stratford Festivals. And there have been Soulpepper productions full of good actors giving good performances but never amounting to more than the sum of those parts. But the higher mathematics of ensemble has occurred gratifyingly often: in Marton's Ibsen and Chekhovs, in Schultzs own productions of Ayckbourn and Pinter and Saroyan, in Zieglers Our Town, right back to that initial Don Carlos directed by Robin Phillips. Play, actors and audience unite in understanding -- the manifestation in performance of the unifying Schultz identifies in the best rehearsals: "an incredible amount of shorthand."
