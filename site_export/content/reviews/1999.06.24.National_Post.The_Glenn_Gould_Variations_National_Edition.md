GLENN

Tom Patterson Theatre

Stratford, Ontario

Four intrepid and talented actors make up the cast of Glenn, and they are all playing the title role.

David Young's play is a fragmented account of the life, ideas and personal torments of Glenn Gould, the Canadian culture hero who detested most of what is officially revered as culture. It chops its protagonist into quarters, slaps a label on each of the morsels and distributes them among the company.

There is the Prodigy: a monstrously gifted juvenile pianist, excitedly calling to his mother that he has just figured out the structure of the Goldberg Variations.

There is the Performer, who develops the Prodigy's insights and displays them from the concert platforms of the world.

These two personae tend to slide into one another, perhaps because what both actors chiefly project is youth. Paul Dunn, who plays the Prodigy, is a new young actor, and Duncan Ollerenshaw, who performs the Performer, has been a young actor for years.

Their older colleagues are more distinct. Richard McMillan is the Puritan, who has forsaken live performance for the recording studio and, increasingly, the piano for the podium. ("I have finally learned," he says in a resonant pun, "how to conduct myself.") This performance, lank-haired and loping, takes some time to come into its own, but once arrived it presents a moving and scarifying portrait of a man tortured by his own talent.

Finally, there is Rod Beattie as the Perfectionist who sums up all the others. Rasping and chunky, he is the Glenn Gould we know from his late public pronouncements, the misanthropic tease whose bite was considerably worse than his Bach and who once told us that he found comfort in the idea that behind every silver lining there was a cloud. He doesn't say that in the play, but he does delightfully remark, looking up from perusing a page of Kafka, that "this guy knows how to have fun."

I guess, though I don't know, that some of his lines are literal transcriptions of Gould's own words. You feel that if Gould hadn't existed Samuel Beckett would have invented him; and the play, given its hero's increasing obsession with recording as the one true medium of creation and of memory, comes to resemble Krapp's Last Tape rescored for quartet.

Our first sight in the play is of four capped, scarved and cloaked figures, looking like the chorus of some expressionist drama sent over from Poland, but Beattie is the only one who wears this uniform throughout. (Gould apparently informed a startled New York taxi-driver that this was how all Canadians dressed in June.)

The author, of course, is far too sophisticated to present these four personae sequentially; they overlap, as they would have done in real life.

Indeed, overlapping isn't the half of it; they interweave in a sort of dramatic fugue apparently modelled on the Goldberg Variations themselves. This way of having drama imitate music isn't altogether new (I once saw an Austrian play that patterned itself on the Trout Quintet, and it was deadly) but David Young mostly makes it work.

At its worst, the constant counterpoint keeps us on our toes; at its median, it makes familiar but still effective capital out of the juxtaposition of different stages of experience; at its best, it is dazzling. Throughout, it demands and receives superb ensemble performance interlaced with an abundance of striking solos.

Richard Rose, taking his second shot at a play he directed six years ago in Toronto, dots his platform stage with significant furnishings -- a chair, a keyboard, tape-decks and a mixing-desk -- as if mapping out the hero's biography simultaneously; his life and personality become a level playing-field and the actors stroll, sprint or hurdle across it. They also get to play other people; Beattie does especially well here, impersonating skeptical record producers, guttural sound engineers, and Herbert von Karajan.

Great play is made of Gould's dismissive and historically absurd denigration of live performance ("a bloodsport") and especially of live applause, but it's also hinted that this monumental hypochondriac may be just have been rationalizing his own neuroses.

Anyway, the actors faced their own audience's applause with every appearance of equanimity and even took a standing ovation without flinching.

The play manages to dispense with most of the musical information necessary to its own comprehension, but it takes too much factual knowledge for granted; however unorthodox its structure, it trades as shamelessly as any other biographical drama on its audience's built-in regard for a national icon.

It's still a brave and demanding choice for Stratford; and, on the whole, I liked it.
