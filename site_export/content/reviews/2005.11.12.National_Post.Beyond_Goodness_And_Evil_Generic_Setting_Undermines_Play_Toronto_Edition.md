robertcushman@lycos.com; Goodness runs through Nov. 27 at the Tarragon Extra Space, 30 Bridgman Ave., 416-531-1827.

High up among the Great Unanswerables, right next to "why do bad things happen to good people?" comes "why do good people do terrible things?" Why, in particular, do they turn on their neighbours and become participants in genocide? Goodness, Michael Redhill's new play, asks this question intelligently, absorbingly and -- as was almost inevitable -- frustratingly.

Part of the frustration is inherent in the method he uses, which is quasi-autobiographical. The story is told to us by a man, a writer, who says his name is Michael Redhill, though he is at pains to point out in his introduction that he is actually being played by an actor called Jordan Pettle. "You'll be in good hands with Jordan," says Redhill by way of Pettle, and so we are. This endearingly unassuming actor is here at his best, his habitual casualness, which can sometimes be excessively low-key, functioning to convey a quick mind, sensitive and inquisitive antennas and a capacity for feeling acutely embarrassed. His performance is in sharp and obviously intentional contrast to those of his colleagues, who -- as things in his dream or perhaps in his nightmare -- are acting in primary colours.

The character known as Redhill comes before us bearing two pieces of baggage: a failed marriage and a Jewish heritage, both of which, I believe, he shares with the real-life Redhill. The rest of his story is probably less factual. He tells of going to Poland after his divorce to find out about his many relatives who were killed in the Holocaust. The villagers he meets have little to tell him. They even wonder whether, amid the many holocausts of the 20th and 21st centuries, this one was really so special. Redhill starts wondering, too. Stopping off in London on his way back to Canada, he meets a mysterious man who refers him to a mysterious woman who tells him a mysterious story, which of course comes to life before our eyes.

It concerns Matthias Todd, an accused genocidaire put on trial by his victims' compatriots. Exactly which war, and which massacres are being referred to, we are not told; there are of course many candidates. The storyteller, whose name is Althea, had been Todd's jailer. The accused, when we flash back to his imprisonment, is an old man who may be unfit to plead. His prosecutor is ferociously convinced that this is a sham, and that Todd is in full command of both his mind and his memory. Todd's daughter, with equal passion, contends that he isn't.

Both sides are agreed that Todd's murderousness didn't spring from nowhere. His own family had been slaughtered by members of the ethnic group whom he himself went on to target, to whom he had already given both personal and ethnic offence. So we have a complex situation standing in for any number of other complex situations. To drive home the genericness of it all, the actors lace the action with singing, whose provenance varies from African to Eastern European and back again.

On top of all this, we have Redhill himself. The other characters suggest that his interest in them may have much to do with his own bitterness, not so much over his ancestry as over his ex-wife's infidelity with his best friend. He doesn't deny it. Philosophically, though, it seems irrelevant. Of course we all have agendas. Next question. Meanwhile, the story of Todd-in-prison takes on its own life but not its own meaning; his possible amnesia is another red herring. The tale's bloody end is revealed to us ahead of time, but this gesture toward Brechtian alienation, like most such gestures, does nothing to stop us getting involved. It doesn't even kill suspense. Effective though this is, the play ends up in a double bind. A situation cannot symbolize other situations unless it is first itself. But it can't become itself when it's continually nudging us about how representative it is. A play set in Iraq, say, might conceivably illuminate events in Rwanda, or vice versa. A play set everywhere is a play set nowhere. One ends up resenting the characters for being so voluble about their feelings and so secretive about the facts.

The Volcano company production, directed by Ross Manson and designed by Teresa Przybylski, brings Redhill's limbo to stern visual life. The Tarragon's Extra Space becomes a dungeon with one small door. Some of the cast seem to have been chosen for musical rather than dramatic credentials, and, as often happens with musical- theatre performers gone legit, their acting loses in subtlety what it gains in force. This is especially the case with Bernadeta Wrobel as the daughter, and it's also true of Lili Francks as the reminiscing Althea, who starts off in an immensely strong accusatory tone that she then hardly varies. Jack Nicholsen's prosecutor is contrastingly silky-smooth, but he, too, keeps hitting the same note. Tara Hughes as the younger Althea, exposed to an arch-enemy's possible humanity, has the chance to move between sympathy and implacability, and takes it. Ambiguity is built into Victor Ertmanis's role as Todd, and he's able to function both as contemptuous warlord (in Nazi terms, he's more Goering than Eichmann) and, more memorably, as bleary-brained survivor. He also has a set piece about a mass killing, designed both to shock and to make us, the good people in the audience, acknowledge our own capacity for evil. It succeeds on the first count, as well-written accounts of atrocities tend to do, much less in the second, which remains a debating point. A play that poses urgent questions never finds the right people to put them to. Maybe, as Redhill's Polish villagers told him, they don't exist. But it's strange that a piece that compels attention almost throughout leaves you with so little.
