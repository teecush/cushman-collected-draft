MILLERS COURT

Nightwood Theatre, Toronto

For the second time this year, the audience approaches a play by way of a meat elevator, this one taking us to the fifth floor of a building on St. Nicholas Street, at Yonge and Wellesley. Maybe the elevators in both cases were just plain old elevators, but while riding inside them I certainly felt like a piece of meat. The similarity goes further. The first play was about Paul Bernardo; the new one is about Jack the Ripper. The first play, I liked far more than did most of my colleagues; the new one I would challenge them, or anyone else, to like less. I suspect, though, that we will all agree: The amount of ineptitude on view here is awe-inspiring.

The piece centres on Mary Jane Kelly, the last of the Ripper's prostitute victims, who apparently met her end at the slum address in Victorian London from which the play takes its name. The show is the joint venture of two groups calling themselves Red Letter Theatre and Schmigeggy Productions. It was worked up in rehearsal by its five actors, one of whose number, Roxanne Deans, arranged their improvisations into a script; the results were directed by Adam Lazarus. They probably researched the historical record extensively, but the staged result suggests that they spent even more time poring over dusty manuals of what used to pass as experimental theatre. They proceed, earnestly, to throw their findings at us.

First, they give us painstaking naturalism. Mary Jane gets out of bed, while her landlord knocks at the door, demanding rent. Mary Jane performs her ablutions, which largely consist of taking swigs from a jug that we are meant to assume contains beer or maybe (and historically more likely) gin, but that she never persuades us contains anything stronger than mineral water.

Then it's off to the streets, which turn out to be full of expressionism; Mary Jane and two of her friends drape themselves round a handy post in grotesque attitudes likelier to frighten potential punters than attract them. This is presumably meant to illustrate their contempt for their clientele, but since they keep saying how much they need the money, it doesn't seem practical. We learn, in the course of the scene, that Mary Jane is pregnant.

The stage is then invaded by masks: The full company, in remarkably ugly visors, acting out some kind of street drama about the uncatchable, unidentifiable Ripper whom they seem to regard as a folk hero. Since they all speak in pinched accents suggestive of the English upper class, or caricatures thereof, I thought for awhile that they were slumming Victorian gentlefolk having a laugh at the expense of the working poor, but it turned out that they were meant to be actual 19th-century mummers impersonating Victorian gentlefolk doing the above. Either way, the performances they give with their masks on could hardly be more grotesque than the ones they have already given with them off, so the point, whatever it may be, is blunted.

Mary Jane's puritanical former boyfriend comes to call on her; they have a row, and she tells him to get out, which he does, without a murmur, in one of the least persuasive pieces of writing and staging I can remember seeing. She has a scene with a lady of charitable but patronizing intent, out to save her soul and body, whom she dismisses; and another with a working wife even poorer than herself, on whom she takes pity.

She drinks a lot and has a dialogue with a barman, who like her is Irish. The audience is beseeched in a program note to shout "To Ireland!" whenever these two give them the cue; soliciting audience participation in writing, in advance, may constitute some kind of record in naffness.

The motivation and identity of the Ripper are left in darkness, but a later choral scene (posh voices again) hints that it may all be the fault of society. There are two prominent male characters, each of whom we are led to believe may be Jack in disguise so the show may be credited with creating some suspense.

Mary Jane closes the proceedings with a detailed, posthumous account of her own disembowelling. Or maybe she's dreaming it in anticipation. We are left with the impression that, consciously or not, she was asking for it. That is probably a crudely reductive account of the complex web of destiny and victimization that the participants dreamed of creating, but I'm afraid it's the best I can do.

Jennifer Gillespie's performance of Mary Jane is slightly less awkward than what surrounds her, and that's the second-best I can do. Even the curtain call manages to be pretentious. The limpness of everybody's acting may be traceable to the lameness of everybody's dialogue, but, given that they all worked it out together, apportioning guilt is a frustratingly circular process. It seems safer and fairer to blame everyone.
