THEATRE REVIEW THE HALF OF IT

Tuesday, November 28, 1989

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Written by John Krizanc Directed by Richard Rose Starring Nicky Guadagni and Gordon Pinsent The Half Of It has full slate of problems BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail John Krizanc's The Half Of It is billed as "almost a comedy," and the archness of that should be fair warning. It also counts as unconscionable conceit. Krizanc is messing here with the destruction of the environment and the glamor of greed: topics reasonably likely to wipe the smile off any audience's face. To pull off that trick, however, you have to put the smile there in the first place. Krizanc can turn the occasional neat line, but in the production of his play by the Necessary Angel Theatre Company and the Canadian Stage Company that opened in Toronto last week, he leaves his spectators with little more warming than an embarrassed grin.

Krizanc wrote Tamara, a successful play that nobody has ever actually seen. All anybody remembers about it is the experience of tracking the actors from room to room of a large house and, speaking for myself, the high quality of the gateau they served in the interval. (The cookies at the Stage Downstairs at 26 Berkeley St. cannot compete.) Emerging from it with only a hazy idea of what was supposed to be going on, you decided charitably that the scenes you missed must have been the ones with the story and the characters. Richard Rose, who directed Tamara, must have learned the useful lesson that it's harder to hit a moving target. His Newhouse last year tried to disguise its portentous banality by keeping the audience trekking round the hall and playing everything simultaneously on stage and video. There was no life but there was the illusion of liveliness. Reunited with Krizanc for The Half Of It, Rose has rashly confined himself to a single conventional stage. He tries to disguise the fact with a few nervous gimmicks.

It starts well enough. A young science teacher named Jillson lectures her pampered private-school charges on the progressive degradation of our view of nature: from the primitive tribe who wouldn't lop a branch without asking the tree's permission to the modern acceptance of the principle of Dog Eat Dog. (Nobody, she points out, has ever seen a dog eating a dog, but we go on believing it anyway.) This is all good stuff, pointedly written, and it gains from being acted in front of the curtain, before we get a look at the set.

Soon we are into plot. Our heroine, Jillson (Nicky Guadagni), has been dismissed from her job, her advanced views having disturbed her pupils and their rich mamas. Jillson herself has a rich mama who fears, in the wake of her husband's death, that she may be down to her last million. Jillson's sister inhabits a similar rarefied atmosphere, being a successful real-estate agent. She sort of approves, though, of Jillson's boy friend, whom she describes as "an environmentalist with ambition - perfect combination." He is corrupted when he falls into the orbit of Freddy Boise (Gordon Pinsent), a stock-market entrepreneur with limitless assets and no scruples. Freddy thinks he may be Jillson's father, and his fixation on her unreachable idealism is his Achilles' heel. He also has an amputated arm, which sends him through the play looking successively like Dr Strangelove and Captain Hook.

Jillson and her family own a lot of timber, which they call Birnam Wood, though by the way they react to the idea of anyone chopping it down you would think it was a cherry orchard. Freddy, who by the end of the play has gone slightly off his trolley, is last heard of trying to fell it with his bare hands. (By the way, did you notice that his surname more or less means "wood" in French? There's richness.) All of which, plus the ecology theme, may account for the stage floor being covered with grass even though much of the action takes place indoors. Backing everything are three panels of blue-sky-with-fluffy-clouds, which look remarkably tacky, even before characters start making dramatic entrances by busting holes in them.

Dotted about the acting area are a desk, a bed and some garden chairs, because this is one of those plays that just can't stand still. It hops from one short scene to another, with actors who are naturalistically in one location frequently operating from another. This is all meant to be very fluid and sophisticated and subversive of the space-time continuum, but it just looks messy. The players hang onto handy bits of furniture for dear life. Right downstage and very pleased with itself is a real pool. People keep emerging from underwater.

Now, the pre-publicity has suggested that Jillson is not meant to have things all her own way and that the play presents the attractions of capitalism as well as its deformities. But that is not the way it plays: not with the boy friend asking rhetorically, "How did we get so ugly" (parody? who can tell?), and ol' Freddy bugging everything that moves. True, Jillson is rather a pain, but idealisic heroines always are. Nicky Guadagni does her opening number with a tremulous kind of authority that subsequently gives way to an unguarded and indiscriminate intensity. She pumps her arms up and down at emotional moments. She does have a lot to put up with; at various times she gets pelted with leaves, squirted with water and amorously debagged. To cap it all, she makes a naked, dripping entrance followed by a hasty retreat to the wings. I don't want to sound prurient, but this is cheating. If you are going to make a statement using the human body, you should try not to look ashamed of it. But Rose's production lacks all sense of rhythm. It keeps springing trick effects without knowing how to time them.

Gordon Pinsent as Freddy skims across the play's surface with tactful accomplishment. Sheila Moore and Lisa Bunting play the rich-bitch roles with appropriate creamy smoothness, and Janet Land deals energetically with a young aspirant to the same status. Greg Ellwand and John Bourgeois are stolidly competent as the men in our little girl's life.

She ends the play much as she began it, only making unfortunate references to a drowned-girl motif that the audience has spent much of the evening trying to ignore. Krizanc certainly piles it on, and without the effortful zaniness, the play might pass as an ambitious attempt at serious themes that has no idea when to stop. At least the environmental theme is serious; the rest, unless you actually believe that greed is an invention of the 1980s, is merely trendy. But, as presented by the Necessary Angel and Canadian Stage companies, that isn't The Half Of It.
