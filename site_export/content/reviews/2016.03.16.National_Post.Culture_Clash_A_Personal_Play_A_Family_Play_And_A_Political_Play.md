'I still have an excellent memory," says Edouard Beauchemin, exquisitely played by R.H. Thomson, in a tone that's half-bluster, half-regret, and all pride. It's the opening line of You Will Remember Me, so the irony and pathos of Francois Archambault's play hits you from the start. Edouard, a retired history professor, is in the early stages of Alzheimer's and knows it. He does indeed have impeccable recall of his academic subject: mostly war, according to his wife Madeleine, who is standing supportively beside him with what we take at this point to be tart affection. What he can't remember, as she rather less supportively announces, is what he had for breakfast that morning.

The two off them are appearing on the only Quebec TV show of which Edouard still approves. Before his illness, he had been, we gather, quite the media personality. He despairs of present-day culture in general and of the internet and social media in particular, perhaps because they are so relentlessly present, so cut off from the past, so disdainful or oblivious of tradition. He could be right, he could be wrong. It's notable that by the end of the play Facebook will have helped destroy his marriage while YouTube will have allowed him to share his last message with the world - which, judging by the number of hits it gets, is more than willing to receive it. Not that Edouard will know, or remember.

You Will Remember Me was staged, in French, in 2014. It reaches Toronto, translated by Bobby Theodore and directed by Joel Greenberg, in a co-production by the Tarragon and Studio 180. It is, in varying degrees, a personal play, a family play and a political play. Madeleine, desperate for a break, deposits Edouard with their daughter Isabelle, a TV news reporter who declines the responsibility as she's off to cover a flood disaster whose victims, clinging to their own memories, she describes as crybabies off-camera. Patrick, Isabelle's live-in boyfriend, volunteers to pitch in, and the two men seem to get on well, even if Edouard can never remember Patrick's name or what it is he does for a living - though whatever it is or was, he isn't doing it now and is staying home pleading burnout. Nor is he above taking off for a night with his poker buddies, consigning Edouard to the care of his own daughter, Berenice. How many levels of separation is that?

Berenice, being teenaged and techno-addicted, is everything Edouard distrusts, while she regards him as a fossil. She's especially impatient of his nostalgia for the passions of 1980 and the referendum: living history for him, pre-history for her. Actually, it's hard not to sympathize with her. Even set in a province where memory is a fetish, the play doesn't work hard enough to establish its political dimension. Though it makes a better job of it than Michel Tremblay's Hosanna, whose two characters were said to stand for opposite sides of the Quebecois identity, on no dramatic evidence whatever. However, a bond develops between Edouard and Berenice: She reminds him of someone, and of a long-buried family secret.

The trouble with this, dramatically, is that it is buried and then abruptly disinterred. We are given few details about it, especially no clues as to how it affected Edouard's wife and daughter; and the actresses are given no chance to show us. The play is all Edouard, and the other characters only come to life in relation to him. Fortunately, he's a rich creation: selfish, obviously, and a habitual seducer of his students (or is that just what he'd like us to believe?), but witty and inquiring and with a darkly humorous awareness of his own decline. He is never, it seems, self-pitying. Thomson's performance has a craggy edge and a quicksilver centre; he gives you a perfect sense of the man's mind working, all the harder against odds. Even without an accent, there are fast French rhythms - French public-intellectual rhythms - in his speech. With the set of his hair and chin and glasses, he even looks Quebecois. His wife's decision to leave him, though it may work for her, is tough on the audience, depriving it of the pleasure of Nancy Palk's company, which is considerable. As daughter Isabelle, Kimwun Perehinec is stuck on a single aggrieved note, but Mark McGrinder is shiftily likeable as her partner, notably when spinning Edouard a yarn about being an astronaut. (Why not? He won't remember.) Michela Cannon's Berenice gets finer as Berenice herself gets gentler, or rather as her firmness takes the shape of empathizing with Edouard rather then dismissing him. This makes for the play's last and most beautiful movement: an unsentimental sense of youth inheriting from age, of raging against the dying of the light while doing something, however incomplete, to preserve its memory. It's the first new play in a long time to occasion honest tears.

You Will Remember Me runs at the Tarragon theatre in Toronto until April 10.
