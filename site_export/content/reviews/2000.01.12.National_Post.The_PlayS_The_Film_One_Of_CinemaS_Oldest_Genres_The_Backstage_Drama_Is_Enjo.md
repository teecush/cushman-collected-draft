Every entertainment medium loves to tell stories about the others. The movie genre with the longest pedigree is the backstage drama, and the live theatre has been known to return the compliment, though not nearly as often.

Radio and television have been obsessively concerned with the older media, and with each other, ever since they came into being; while newspapers, of course, batten off the lot of them and are battened on in return. Whichever area we work in, we apparently feel that the real action is going on in the next yard, and we would dearly like to grab a piece of the glamour.

The theatre, being so much older than any of its rivals, was for centuries compelled to pay tribute to itself, as it still with some frequency does. But it also remains the favoured object of all its competitors; movie performers, movie makers, who would keel over at the idea of working in theatre conditions for theatre money, nevertheless act out public fantasy lives as just plain kids putting on shows. And here they join hands with their audiences; it is after all the possible dream, the one that most of us have come closest to realizing. The school play is still a more universal experience than the school movie.

The minute they had learned to make their own noises, films institutionalized this fantasy as the backstage musical. In Hollywood's heyday, this genre accounted for at least 90% of all the musical pictures made; there they were, singing and dancing on celluloid, while simultaneously affirming that the only place in which such things could rationally be done was on a stage.

This is, of course, the literal truth, and Hollywood has always been a slave to realism. But there is another reason.

The backstage musical comes with a built-in climax; we spend the story watching a show being prepared, vicariously experiencing all the attendant traumas, and finally we watch it explode in triumph, all difficulties overcome, with an audience on screen to bear witness to the experience while we in the stalls validate their validation.

The form still survives, however battered, and it's just taken a new turn. Most backstagers -- 42nd Street is the archetype -- have depicted the birth-pangs of one fictitious production, dreamed up for the occasion. Real shows, from the actual theatrical past, have only been dealt with in biographies of particular stars or composers -- an Al Jolson or a Cole Porter -- and they have arrived there in platoons, usually with total disregard for content or chronology. We now though have two films, each of which deals, unprecedentedly, with the creation of a single historic musical. Tim Robbins Cradle Will Rock shows how Marc Blitzsteins almost-eponymous labour opera evaded official muzzling to receive an electric impromptu premiere, with the composer playing on the stage and the actors delivering from the house. Mike Leigh's Topsy-Turvy -- which opens in Canada on Jan. 21 -- follows W. S. Gilbert and Arthur Sullivan from the opening of Princess Ida, a comparative failure, to that of The Mikado, their most unmitigated success.

Both films build to the traditional first-night blast, though it's indicative of the difference between them that Robbins ends with it while Leigh progresses to the inevitable let-down of the morning after. Hes made a film about the theatrical process itself, and that process is ongoing.

Robbins is concerned with The Cradle less for its own sake or those of its creators than as an emblematic skirmish in the sociopolitical battles of of New Deal America. The plots and sub- plots, as chronologically scrambled as any old-style biopic, get very confusing, but theres much that you wouldn't want to miss. There is, for example, the scene before the nascent House Un- American Activities Committee, one of whose members seems willing to arraign all playwrights -- not just that notorious Communist Christopher Marlowe -- as rancorous troublemakers; censors are rarely so frank or, from their own point of view, so logical. When the cradle finally rocks, it really rocks; the last sequence is such a sure-fire rouser that I wish Robbins had resisted the temptation to sweeten and sentimentalize what actually happened, since what actually happened was quite stirring enough.

He turns a brilliantly planned guerilla action into a spontaneous gesture by a bunch of warm-hearted thesps, reinforced -- most unhistorically -- by a couple of tootling musicians defying their union. But then a solo piano backing would be unthinkable backing for a Hollywood finale.

Topsy-Turvy, too, features warm-hearted thesps: A choral deputation who gallantly protest the last-minute cutting of the one solo number allotted to the Mikado himself and get it reinstated. Leigh typically doesn't show us what any other musical would: The audience rising to cheer what turned out to be one of Gilbert and Sullivans signature songs -- the one about letting the punishment fit the crime. He does, though, show us the two collaborators taking their first-night bows; they look as if they barely knew one another as, in essentials, they barely did.

This, Leigh being English and a theatre animal, is an actors picture, just like his contemporary movies: researched by the cast, with dialogue improvised by them and refined and rigorously edited by him. To me it sounded far more convincingly Victorian than the matey adaptations of Dickens or George Eliot that we've been getting recently.

Some things, on the other hand, never change; and where the business of putting on a show is concerned, actors are in a better position to know that than most. An American critic has complained that the rehearsal problems depicted in this film are no different to those that beset all musicals. Precisely.

I suppose it's ironic that the blistering Blitzstein should have occasioned a more obviously riotous celebration of theatre than the incurably frivolous G&S. You could argue though that The Cradle Will Rock, still powerful but a period piece, needs to be celebrated more than The Mikado, a still-living classic that can generate its own fun. Anyway, were not exactly starved for celebrations of theatre, even ones without tunes.

Shakespeare in Love implicated us in the premiere of Romeo and Juliet as enjoyably, and as shamelessly, as any songfest. And the mechanical media too can be celebrated, even by themselves.

There arent many musical films about musical films but one of them was Singin' in the Rain, the best movie musical of all. It's obviously harder to involve an audience in a process whose real culmination comes in the editing room rather than in public performance, but it can be done, with a little cheating.

Galaxy Quest -- the most purely enjoyable picture since Shakespeare in Love -- would be far less pleasurable without its coda, in which the intrepid actor-heroes take their places for their reinstated series, waving to the fans as if they were doing it live. From sci-fi they have come and to sci-fi they return; its very satisfying.

Its also satisfying, or at least titillating, that we know who they are: That Tim Allen is playing a TV star without a show and that the uprooted Briton doomed to wear the alien's hairpiece, apparently in perpetuity, is Alan Rickman, whose character signs autographs with unimaginable disdain and finally redeems himself by breathing new life into the mystical catchphrases that he's always hated. Thats acting: or at least acting of acting, and it somehow cancels out and becomes the real thing.

There are scenes in Topsy-Turvy that work in a related way, scenes set in performers dressing rooms as the first night approaches. We believe so strongly in the actors as actors that when the civilians -- Gilbert or Sullivan or Richard D'Oyly Carte -- look in we forget for the moment that they are actors, too. I wouldn't make profound claims for it, but I think it can be a lovely game, like relishing the studios deft iconic use of Astaire and Rogers, or of Garland and Kelly when they played performers, or applauding John Malkovichs sportsmanship in being himself. The presence of actors as actors, or of a play within a play, doesn't necessarily guarantee a searching study of appearance and reality; or if it does we should be consistently solemn and rank 42nd Street with Pirandello. (Somebody somewhere probably has.)

It's a lightweight theme anyway, and its best handled with the steely delicacy that we saw on stage last year in The Plays the Thing. Or better yet with the superlative grace of A Midsummer Night's Dream, the play in which the professional theatre, with no other target yet available, sets out to mock the amateurs -- us -- and ends up doing us homage. It was the first play about doing the play right here, the first show about putting on a show, and the mould has never been broken.
