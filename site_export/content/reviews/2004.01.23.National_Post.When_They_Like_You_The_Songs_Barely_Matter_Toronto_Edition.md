RAY JESSEL

Top of the Senator, Toronto

Ray Jessel -- he was Raymond Jessel then -- was the last man to write words to Richard Rodgers's music. He was brought in as a replacement lyricist on Rodgerss final show, I Remember Mama, in 1979. Before that he had collaborated with a Canadian composer, Marian Grudeff, on a Broadway musical about Sherlock Holmes. Before that, he and Grudeff were in Toronto, writing numbers for the well- remembered Spring Thaw revues. And before that -- well before that - - Jessel was growing up in Cardiff, Wales. He is, as he tells us in his cabaret show at the Senator, a Welsh Jew -- a rare ethnic species; unless we mate, were gonna die out.

Jessel, who is 73, took to performing at the piano a couple of years ago, doing his own material of course. These days he writes both words and music, usually with his wife, Cynthia Thompson, as co- lyricist. He has a thoroughly charming presence and, like most songwriters, very little voice, not that that matters; he knows perfectly how to pace and point a lyric.

He is a short, endearing man with wispy grey hair tied with a bow at the back that he musses when he wants to look like Albert Einstein. He does this in a number called I'm a Genius, whose rhymes are indeed ingenious, but which is not actually very funny, maybe because the premise on which it's based (Einstein as egomaniac) is not very persuasive.

Its almost the first thing he does, and I found myself with the same tepid response to its immediate successors: a spoof on songs about towns entitled Oxnard, a place he introduces as being two hours drive from Los Angeles (where he lives) or three if you're lucky; I'm All Right Now, a "serious" song which turned out to be yet another essay in the laughing-to-keep-from-crying genre whose classic is I Get Along Without You Very Well; and a Noel Coward parody, which gets the musical tone dead to rights but is way off on the lyrics. (They sound more like Paddy Roberts, a classically unfunny English songwriter of the 50s and 60s.)

All the same, Jessel is building huge likeability through these numbers, so when the good songs do start coming, they sound wonderful. One is That Old Kurt Weill Song, which gets Weill's Bilbao Song in a presumably affectionate hammerlock hold and pins it to the floor while also taking a savage detour in the direction of Surabaya Johnny (I'd like to cut your throat, Johnny/Cause you only sing one note, Johnny.) He can do French as well, as in Mon Amour, for which he dons a beret (which both suits and transforms him) and unwinds a magically disconnected succession of Gallic names and phrases. And then theres The Shakespearean Tragedy Rag (didn't T.S. Eliot once make mention of one of those?), which is not only hilarious but very sound on Shakespeare; and Life Goes On, a serious song that works and puts a fresh spin on the inconvenient human capacity for surviving heartbreak.

Mostly, though, Jessel's ballads are his weakest work, written in a torch-jazz style that seems to have been preserved in aspic from the 50s. Those songs, to put it bluntly, have all been written. Jessel, as he tells us in numbers like Whatever Happened to Melody, is a hopeless nostalgic or maybe, on the strength of I Want to Sing a Show Tune, a hopeful nostalgic. But I think hes got it wrong. The acknowledged fact that they're not writing them like that any more means that it's no longer possible to write them. This doesnt mean that writers who treasure the Broadway and Tin Pan Alley traditions have to surrender to rock or rap. It means they have to take the tradition and expand on it, as has been done by Stephen Sondheim and Cy Coleman and David Shire and the great Dave Frishberg (of whom Jessel sometimes reminds me) and as may be done, though its too soon to tell, by the latest generation of New York show-writers.

Jessel offers a couple of songs from a work -in -progress, a musical version of Moll Flanders entitled, as you might well guess, Moll; there's a pub rouser that might have come from Lionel Bart and a love song that might have come from anywhere -- Defoe's cynical matter-of-factness is not, I suspect, on this shows agenda. Jessel is joined on these and other numbers by our own Theresa Tova, a contrastingly overpowering (and much taller) performer: so powerful in fact that its sometimes hard to tell whether she's spoofing her songs or selling them straight.

I've left out a few treats: a song that knocks Cabarets Two Ladies into a cocked hat, and a satire of all songwriter retrospectives called And Then I Wrote, whose punchline is "and then I wrote And Then I Wrote: which is this song." Fairer than that he could not say. I may have qualms about some of Jessels material, but there's much I like; and the man himself is a modest marvel of showmanship.
