THEATRE REVIEW

DIRTY DANCING

Royal Alexandra Theatre

---

There's a cute variation at Dirty Dancing on the customary pre- show mixed message of welcome and admonition.

We're advised that the show takes place in 1963, "long before cellphones." As I remember, it was long before drum machines as well, but that doesn't stop the proceedings from commencing with a pounding beat that sounds decidedly anachronistic. No intrusive beeps from the auditorium could hope to compete.

Dirty Dancing is subtitled "The Classic Story on Stage." I confess, here and now, that I never saw the classic story on screen, but my impression is that Eleanor Bergstein, who wrote both, is trying to recreate the movie experience as closely as possible with live actors.

If I did know the movie, I would probably consider this a pointless exercise; I don't usually have much interest in reliving cherished memories second-hand, and un-cherished memories may be even worse.

Coming -- so to speak -- fresh, I was impressed by the dogged and inventive determination of director James Powell to make the show as fluid as possible. The stage rises and falls with military precision and metronomic regularity, projections (designed by Jon Driscoll) are brilliant in their clarity and family-album evocative.

In a world full of We Will Rock You and High School Musical: The Musical, or whatever they called it, Dirty Dancing looks positively artistic.

It doesn't always sound that way, but then I don't suppose many people are going to see it in order to savour the wit and wisdom of Bergstein. The level of acting -- let's not get pretentious here, the level of linereading-- is atrocious, but then so are many of the lines.

MonicaWest, thefemale lead, does have a freshness, or at least a naivete about her that suits the character she's playing, who goes in for being painfully sincere. She is, as everybody in the theatre but me already knew, Frances Houseman, a doctor's daughter who comes with her father, mother and jealous-but-loving older sister (who looks and behaves younger) for a last holiday in the Catskills.

It was, she tells us as the family car makes a circular progress around the stage, the last summer before the Beatles (man, you North Americans were late developers), the last time anyone ever called her by her nickname of Baby. For some reason, it never occurs to anyone to call her Frankie, which would suit her perfectly.

Baby is an idealist whose ambition is to join the Peace Corps. In the meantime, she has more immediate moral and social problems to confront and, it must be said, she comes through them all with flying colours: She never meets a spasm of doubt that can't be dispelled in three seconds. She falls for Johnny, a boy from the other side of the tracks; he teaches Latin dancing to guests bored with the foxtrot.

This -- the Latin dancing, I mean -- is the dirty dancing of the title, though it looks pretty antiseptic here. Still, it's enough to prejudice Baby's decent-but-blinkered dad against Johnny, especially when he's called in to salvage a botched abortion suffered by Johnny's regular mambo-partner, a situation for which he holds Johnny morally (i.e. biologically) responsible.

There's no reason why he should hold Johnny responsible, except that without this misunderstanding, the central relationship would have no complications whatsoever. In terms of what actually happens between them, the boy never comes close to losing the girl for a moment. Doctor Dad, I regret to say, is played in an especially appalling performance by actor Al Sapienza (Mikey Palmice of The Sopranos) who sounds as if he wouldn't want to be held accountable for a word that falls from his lips.

Meanwhile the show treats us to snapshots of resort life and entertainment (including a full half-minute of authentic-sounding borscht-belt comedy), and generally behaves like Wish You Were Here updated by a decade.

Given the speed of the proceedings, this works. So, against all the odds (which include a strenuous, but blank, leading man in Jake Simons) does the main story. We can even stand for the insistent morality which includes inflicting a last-minute comeuppance on the camp's resident loose woman.

The show only gets unforgivably cheesy when it gets on the subject of civil rights; A very '70s sounding rendition of We Shall Overcome is followed by a momentary projection of Martin Luther King, Jr. It demeans a great man to reduce him to the theatrical equivalent of a T-shirt logo, and it's insufferable of the show to congratulate itself on how far ahead it is of the times it depicts.

It's all supposed to tie in with the mental struggles, dropped in at widely-spaced intervals, of a trainee hotelier over whether to journey south to join the Freedom Riders. First he will, then he won't, then -- but I wouldn't want to spoil anything.

Big musicals in Toronto always include at least one recruit from the classical theatre, served up as a sacrificial offering; here it's Dylan Trowbridge, late of Shaw: having seen him at his best, I can testify that he isn't at it here.

The dancing, given the show's premise and title, is routine; the climax of choreography, story and show is supposed to come when Baby, as Johnny's replacement partner, finally manages the Lift; but it never really registers that she hasn't been Lifted already.

As a musical, though, the show does have its own integrity in that it isn't really a musical. There are many songs, either authentically of the period or written for the movie, like the famous and deeply undistinguished The Time of My Life, but they're all used as backgound, whether prerecorded or rendered live. There is some late slippage when a couple of minor figures take on the function of a commentating chorus, but none of the leads ever sing in character, or sing at all.

This story-with-dance idea has been done before, in an artier way, in Contact, but this is somehow more likeable. A convention is established and adhered to. I admire that. - Dirty Dancing is at the Royal Alexandra Theatre for an indefinite run. Call 416-872-1212 for more information.
