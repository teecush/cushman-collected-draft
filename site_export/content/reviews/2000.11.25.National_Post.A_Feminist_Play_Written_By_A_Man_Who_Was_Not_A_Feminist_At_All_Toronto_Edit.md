THE DOLL HOUSE

Canadian Stage Theatre, Upstairs

The Henrik Ibsen play usually known in English as A Dolls House has become, at least in its title, more definite and less possessive: Pretty much the course of action that Ibsen recommends to his characters. The Doll House, in John Murrells sparky and speakable new translation, has been moved forward from 1879, when Ibsen wrote it, to 1912. So, at least, says the program, though I doubt if many would notice the difference.

Radical updating would anyway be pointless, robbing us of the thrill that always comes from discovering our own concerns anticipated in an old text. Ibsen's play, which climaxes in the most famous marital walk-out in world drama, gives the lie to the strange idea that feminism was some invention of the 1960s, though Ibsen himself professed to have no interest in what were known as women's rights. He was interested in human freedom, its uses and abuses; and he found marriage the ideal site on which to explore this. He wrote a great polemical play without being himself a polemicist, which is probably the way to do it.

The doll in Ibsens house is Nora Helmer; the householder is her husband Torvald, a banker on the brink of a major promotion. Torvalds favoured conjugal style might be called the Endearment Smothering; he obsessively calls his wife "songbird" and squirrel and professes to find her delightfully unworldly and impractical.

That is all he knows. Some years ago Nora saved him from a fatal breakdown by raising the money for a long holiday in Italy. Being short herself of ready kroner, she borrowed them from a shabby, disgraced lawyer named Krogstad and forged a signature to close the deal. Krogstad now threatens to tell the hitherto oblivious Torvald. Caught between her creditor's blackmail and her spouse's craven recriminations when he finds out, Nora grows up. She was already a clever manipulator; now she wants honesty and she wants knowledge, most of all about herself.

The last of the plays three acts contains two great duets. By way of prelude to the Helmers passionate break-up, we get a low- key union, clear-eyed to the point of cynicism, between Krogstad and Nora's equally needy friend Mrs Linde. It seems to be a kind of love; but then, as Nora says not long after, who knows what that word means?

This double climax remains thrillingly effective, but Vikki Anderson's production takes its sweet time getting there. I saw it a week into the run and I got the impression that whatever glue had held the performances together was starting to evaporate. Each actor seems to be operating separately. There is much forcing of conflicts; people get up and stride around on the least provocation, which is domestically unconvincing and also unsightly.

The setting, too, is unimaginatively shabby, although the director is a practised designer. I was reminded of Bernard Shaw offering assistance to the impoverished producers of another Ibsen play: "If I have a ten-pound note, they shall have it; if not, I can at least lend them a couple of decent chairs." In this context, the enlistment of James Kudelka to choreograph Noras tarantella (staged here for desperation rather than sexuality) looks like overkill.

Ben Carlson bestows on Helmer the same passion and personality he has brought to unpromising young men in Shaw Festival productions. Indeed, one began to fear for the plays finale; what would Nora have to complain about? But it works splendidly; we expect better from this Torvald, just as Nora does, and so we share in the bitterness of her disillusion. Carlson, as a good actor should, respects his character and fights his corner.

Fiona Byrne's Nora starts almost exaggeratedly giddy; you can see the exact moment of her transformation -- when she realizes, silently, that Krogstad will betray her -- and from that moment she never stops growing. The two actors meet, as Nora says their characters should, as equals; and though their last scene lacks its fullest measure of shattering suspense, it comes close.

Jordan Pettle's Krogstad is a fine study of shady self- justifying desperation, but its style and tempo -- very jerky and off-hand -- have nothing to do with those of the other actors. Melee Hutton's Mrs. Linde, meanwhile, is modest almost to vanishing point; in her scenes with Nora she is not so much confidante as stooge.

Raymond O'Neill has some poignancy as the stricken family friend, Dr. Rank, though the erotic potential of his scenes with Nora is severely diminished. (He came late into the production, having a couple of years ago replaced Peter Donat, who had given a memorable account of the same role at the Atlantic Theatre Festival; can you make a career of being second Rank?)

Ellen-Ray Hennessy brings unusual fullness to the tiny role of the Helmer family nanny. In this version she has only one child to chaperone -- half the number Ibsen specified. The reduction is damaging. It is important that when Nora slams the door at the end, she leaves behind it not one child but two; or, if you count her soon-to-be ex, not two but three.
