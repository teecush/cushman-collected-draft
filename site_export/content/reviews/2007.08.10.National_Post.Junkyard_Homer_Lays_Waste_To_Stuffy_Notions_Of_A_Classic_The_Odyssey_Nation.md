Studio Theatre, Stratford, Ont.

In Woody Allen's Stardust Memories, an admirer of the artist- director contemplates his swish automobile and says, "I think it symbolizes the authors car." In Peter Hintons wondrous production of The Odyssey, a vandalized and dilapidated car occupies the centre of the stage, and it symbolizes practically everything. At the start it's both the sacked city of Troy and the famous horse that brought about its downfall. When Odysseus sets off on his 10-year voyage home to Ithaca, it becomes his ship, with a mast pointing up from somewhere behind its trunk. Later, its sail unfurls to represent the shroud that his wife, Penelope, weaves to keep her suitors at bay.

The car even plays a car; at least, when Menelaus dispatches Odysseus' son Telemachus with a friendly "take my chariot," he tosses him the keys. With actors crouching and lurching behind its rain-soaked windshield, it provides a spectacular image for storms at sea. (Odysseus and his men spend a lot of time being tossed on the waves; that is, when they -- the men anyway -- arent being eaten like sheep by one-eyed monsters or turned into pigs by bright- eyed witches.) And there are times when the car functions simply as an intriguingly versatile acting space. When Nigel Shawn Williams Odysseus finally makes it home, dressed as a beggar, he encounters the faithful swineherd Eumaeus (Walter Borden). The two of them take shelter beneath the hood, sharing crusts and embittered observations and generally sounding one another out. It's a quietly amazing scene, magnificently played by both.

Obviously, this version of Homer's epic, first staged in England 15 years ago, crosses periods as well as countries. Its the work of Derek Walcott, the West Indian poet, and its principal inflection is Caribbean. Its Homeric substitute (powerfully rendered by Jeremiah Sparks) is a singer, Blind Billy Blue.

The text remains remarkably true to the structure of the original -- sometimes, with its flashbacks within flashbacks and its glancing allusions to the obscurer corners of myth, to the point of confusion. But the details are punched-up. Its man-eating Cyclops (Roy Lewis) is a dictator and Philistine and proud of it, from the period of the Colonels. This allusion, grimly funny as the episode is as a whole, is one of the few mistakes. Timelessness is one thing. Faded topicality is the opposite.

Conversely, when Odysseus descends into Hades, its delightful to find it modelled on the London Underground, with the hero's late mother (Joyce Campion) impatiently awaiting her train, handbag in hand.

Walcott ties the story together with constant references to, and even appearances by, Odysseus' former companions. Agamemnon, struggling in the fatal net, is, in this production, an unforgettable image. Odysseus young pilot Epenor is the haunting double of Telemachus (both played excellently by In-Surp Choi.) As for the theodicy

in The Odyssey: the goddess Athene makes periodic appearances, usually in disguise, and toward the end theres a brief but startling attempt to turn the ragged, mocked Odysseus into a Christ figure.

The first half of the show, depicting the wanderings, is endlessly inventive, but it's the second, the homecoming, that's the more powerful. A centred story will always be more dramatic than a picaresque one, and this one builds superbly to the drawing of Odysseus bow and the slaughter of the suitors, their hundred names called out as the same few actors fall obligingly dead, time after time, in an endless variety of poses. It doesnt get dull. The subsequent hanging of the maidservants, the most controversial passage in the poem, is mooted but never executed. It's left, so to speak, hanging; we await the modernized sequel, The Penelopiad, due in Ottawa next month.

Williams' Odysseus is a seven-league step forward for him as an actor: witty, lucid, anguished, commanding every moment of the play. The most heart-stopping of them all he shares with Barbara Barnes- Hopkins as the old nurse Eurycleia when she is the first to recognize him.

Allegra Fulton survives an initial brittleness to become a riven, independent Penelope. She also plays Circe and a delicious Helen who, post-Troy, has become a high-suburban hostess in a yellow pant- suit and stilettos. Steve Ross makes a strikingly insolent principal suitor, Sophia Walker does an alluring teenage princess, and Ian Deakin is bluffly likable in a string of roles.

The production is not so much multicultural as cross-cultural. Black and white actors throw themselves with equal enthusiasm and equal success into the shows alternating idioms. The wonders in Hintons staging hardly stop: great balls that drift down from above and turn into what look like balloons (though solid enough not to burst when people sit on them); the revelation, in Carolyn M. Smith's set design, that Ithaca, furnished with transparent Philippe Starck chairs and tables, was aeons ahead of its time; Robert Thomson's solid walls of light dividing the stage. Walcotts script is poised between celebration and requiem, as, come to think of it, is Homers, and the production honours both of them. It's a great ride. - In repertoire until Sept. 28.
