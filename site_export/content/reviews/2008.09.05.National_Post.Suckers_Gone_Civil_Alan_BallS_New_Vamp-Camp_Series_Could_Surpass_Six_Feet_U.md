After creating a successful TV series about people who bury the dead, what rests but to create another series about the dead who refuse to stay buried? Alan Ball ( Six Feet Under) returns with True Blood, which is about vampires. Balls subjects, though, are not your ordinary blood-sucking revenants. Or rather, they are ordinary. Theyre domesticated. True Blood postulates a world in which vampires have come out of the coffin (I quote) and are demanding acceptance. Next thing you know, they'll be running for office.

Ball's victims have been liberated by the invention of a synthetic blood product (I think I heard someone say it was Japanese) that relieves them of the need to slake their thirst on humans. Needless to say, this is not enough to make all humans trust them. It certainly isnt enough in the small town of Bon Temps, La., which is where True Blood is set. Though the vampires have progressed enough to have their own pressure-group, the American Vampire Legion (we see their designated spokesperson, in a prologue sequence full of witty reversals, being interviewed by Bill Maher), they arouse extreme responses. There are the "vampire-drainers," who viciously prey on the former predators, and the fang-bangers, who want to have sex with them. It can be quite hard, psychologically speaking, to tell these two groups apart.

The Christian right, naturally, detests the vampires; its TV representative denounces them even while refusing to recognize their existence by actually talking to them. (Its for non-encounters like these that split screens were invented.) On the outskirts of Bon Temps someone has hung a sign reading "God Hates Fangs." So you can see where we're at. Vampires in this show are stand-ins for the victims of real-life intolerance, especially as encountered in pockets of rural America.

The obvious trouble with this approach is that gays, blacks and immigrants actually exist, and vampires don't. So the metaphor seems, shall we say, frivolous: certainly slight to build a whole TV series on. Here I

have to declare a lack of interest. Vampires as a subject bore me, whether theyre being used to induce thrills or giggles. (Yeah, I know about Buffy. So slay me.) Obsession with the occult seems to me the least attractive legacy of the 1960s. Still, if any show could convert me, this one would. At the end of the first episode I thought, well that was good, well written and well acted, but I dont know that I need to stay with it. At the end of the second, I was hooked. For now, at least.

True Blood, as you may have gathered, does not belong to the solemn end of the vampire spectrum. It's vamp-camp. But there is more to it. At the least, it represents the meeting of two kinds of Gothic: Transylvanian and Southern. Its central character (the heroine of a line of popular novels that I of course haven't read) is Sookie Stackhouse, whos a waitress at Merlottes bar (and undoubtedly grill) and is, by the look of things, over-qualified. She too has supernatural attributes, and they're far more interesting. She can read minds. It's caused her trouble since early childhood (she was diagnosed with ADD, which of course is the precise opposite of her actual condition) but shes learned to tune it out, at least some of the time. Its still played havoc with her love life; she can never get through a date because her knowledge of what her companion is thinking causes her either to laugh or to throw up. She's also a self-reliant person, who can swing a very nifty chain when confronted by villains.

She's played with abundant wit and gumption by Anna Paquin, once the little girl of The Piano, now proving how impressively nine-year-old child stars can grow up.

Scarcely less beguiling is Sookies best friend Tara (Rutina Wesley), first seen working at a hardware store where she confronts a troublesome customer with an aggressive lack of interest that would do credit to Canadian Tire. Tara would rather read a book, which she continues to do in her next job, behind the bar at Merlottes. Tara speaks well; she tells a male friend at a party your standards are so low, you always get lucky (not with her, he wouldn't be interested) and she affectionately sums up Sookie as "trouble waiting to happen." Tara has an obvious unspoken crush on Sookie's brother Jason, whose standards may also be low, or at least risky; he spends an impressive proportion of the show in and out of a succession of beds. ( True Blood has some of the raunchiest sex and most brutal violence yet seen on HBO. That is not a condemnation.)

Ball has talked in an interview of the shows "moral complexity." Ive yet to see it. But the characters are quirky, and the setting attractively earthy and, to viewers outside the locale, exotic. (Ball's a Southerner himself, so I suppose we have to take his word for a milieu in which half the cast sound like T-Bag from Prison Break, and a quarter seem about to behave like him.) And there is some variation among the vampires themselves; they all seem traditionally scary and repulsive, except for Bill (Stephen Moyer), Sookie's love-interest, a clean-cut respectful son of the old South who expired during the Civil War: a circumstance that fascinates Sookies grandmother (the great Lois Smith) who wants him to address her library group, the Descendants of the Glorious Dead. This elicits from Bill, who presumably should know, the response that theres nothing glorious about death, certainly not in war. Ball wrote and directed the opening episodes, and so far they're better than Six Feet Under. They're also less insufferably lofty than his screenplay for American Beauty, though I suppose if I lived in Louisiana I might think differently. The best thing about the show is that for longish stretches you can forget that it's about vampires at all. You may, however, find yourself thinking about the Republican Convention.

-The premiere episode of True Blood airs Sunday night at 9 p. m. on The Movie Network.
