THEATRE REVIEW

War Horse

Princess of Wales Theatre, Toronto

The transforming moment in War Horse - transforming in every sense - comes about 20 minutes in. Joey, the title horse, changes before our eyes from foal to full-grown stallion. It would be unfair to reveal exactly how, but up until this point Joey has been a life-size skeletal puppet, visibly manipulated by three performers whom it is sometimes confusingly easy to mistake for characters in the story; stable hands perhaps?

The puppeteers are still in evidence when Joey grows up, but they're now so disposed that we can tune them out. For the rest of the evening we believe in Joey as an actual horse, even while remaining happily aware that he's a product of theatrical wizardry. The magic is manifold. The technical peak coincides with an emotional peak. And that synergy, of spectacle and feeling, keeps going throughout the show.

It makes a great evening out of a less-than-great script. The play is Nick Stafford's adaptation of Michael Morpurgo's novel, brought to the stage by the National Theatre of Great Britain in a production (by Marianne Elliott and Tom Morris) that's been restaged here (by Alex Sims) with a Canadian cast.

Joey is a natural-born hunter bought at auction by Ted Narracott, a drunken Devonshire farmer, mainly to spite his more successful brother Arthur. Ted's 16-year-old son Albert takes care of Joey and, under paternal pressure, trains him to pull a plough though he's clearly meant for nobler things. The year is 1914; when the First World War breaks out Ted sells Joey to the British Army. A heartbroken Albert lies about his age and enlists, in the hope of being reunited with his beloved steed. His quest, which gets us all the way to 1918, takes up the second half of the play.

A not-unexpected climax is quite shamelessly delayed, in both the long and the short term. There's a string of coincidences that seem to be pointing to an early reunion; but each of them is bypassed in favour of a protracted finale that plays every trick of suspense and remorselessly assaults every tear duct while uplifting every heart. Remorselessly but forgivably: It feels sentimental but, like the show as a whole, it doesn't feel untruthful. It's underpinned by an anger about war that is ages old and always new. The First World War remains the perfect crucible because it was both the last of the glamour wars and the first of the modern ones. In War Horse's terms, it was the war where cavalry charges met machine guns.

In a program note Morpurgo lists several inspirations for his novel. One he doesn't mention, but that must have been read by every English schoolchild of his (and my) generation, is Black Beauty, the fictitious autobiography of a Victorian horse, one who in a more domestic version of Joey's trajectory goes from country horse to cab horse and ends up reunited with his first stableboy. The stage version of War Horse drops the book's narrative voice; a talking horse would hardly strike the right theatrical note. Instead it gives us the perspectives of two theoretically opposed humans who in fact have a lot in common but who never meet.

There's Albert, and there's Friedrich, an enemy officer who, in Patrick Galligan's warm but somewhat wavering performance, is so good a German that he speaks with an English accent. He puts the captured Joey to work ferrying the wounded, thereby drawing unknowingly on the wonder horse's early training. There's another German, who seems more of a proto-Nazi but who still ends up cursing the war and asking Friedrich (and by implication, the play and the audience) why he can't care as much for the sufferings of humans as of horses.

The most affecting scene in the play makes us care for humans because they care for horses; in an episode apparently modelled on the famous 1914 Christmas Eve truce, one soldier from each side, both waving white flags but unable to speak one another's language, combine to rescue Joey from his impalement on barbed wire and then toss a coin to decide which will keep him. It's well written and, by Dylan Roberts and Richard McMillan, delightfully acted.

This is not, though, a show that much extends its actors, except perhaps physically. The obvious exception is Alex Furber, a newcomer whose Albert carries all before him (or in Joey's case, underneath him). He gets good support from Tamara Bernier Evans, alternately sharp and sweet as his mother; Brad Rudy, dogged or maybe just hang-dogged as his dad; and Patrick Kwok-Choon as his buddy in the trenches. But mostly this is a production kind of production, and a triumphant one.

It isn't just the puppeteering, wonderful though that is; the Handspring Puppet Company, from South Africa, bring to life a subsidiary horse called Topthorn plus a goose and the odd vulture, but of course it's Joey who's the king of their world. "Back legs of the horse" will from now on be a more honourable actor's credit than ever before.

In terms of staging techniques you might say that this is where Equus meets The Lion King, though there was nothing in The Lion King as shattering as this show's first evocation of the actual shock of battle. It's a confluence of design (Rae Smith), music (Adrian Sutton), lighting (Paule Constable) and, perhaps above all, projections (59 Productions) that are preternaturally stark, clear and economical.

To cite some comparable British theatrical epics: Nicholas Nickleby this ain't; it doesn't have the humanity or the humour. Oh! What a Lovely War it also ain't; it doesn't have the bite or the wit. But it has heart, and just enough mind and dazzle that's never merely an end in itself. It also cured me of any desire to see the film. I know that movies can do horses and battles. I also know - check out the prologue to Henry V - that the stage can't, not easily; which makes it all the more exciting when it does. War Horse gives manipulation - again in every sense - a very good name.
