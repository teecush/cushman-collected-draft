Trudeau and Levesque, the latest instalment in VideoCabarets cartoon chronicle of Canada, enables Mac Fyfe to colour in the rich Prime Ministerial portrait that he began to paint in the previous episode Trudeau and the FLQ (which is being revived in repertory rotation). Serenely arrogant, courteously aloof, he commands every scene in which he appears. His titular opponent is less impressive. This can probably be blamed on history, rather than on Michael Hollinsgworth, Video-Cab's redoubtable authordirector. It certainly isn't the fault of Richard Alan Campbell, whose performance of Rene Levesque is admirably ferrety. But he has to share the separatist limelight with Cyrus Fairds ballooning Jacques Parizeau and with Craig Lauzons Claude Morin, whose double-agent doubledealings become rather tedious. Opposition split is opposition diluted, dramatically as well as politically.

Trudeau and Levesque is one of the four nominees for Outstanding New Play in this year's Dora Awards, which are to be presented on Monday, and I wouldn't be sorry to see it win. Its competitors are Maria Milisavljevics Abyss, which I thought overbaked; Infinity, Hannah Moscovitch at less than her best; and Hooked, Nicky Guadahgnis solo performance from a book by Carolyn Smart, on which I can't comment because I didn't see it. Notably absent from the list is Michel-Marc Bouchards Tom at the Farm, though it was certainly among the highest-praised plays of the year and has already won the Toronto Theatre Critics Best Play Award. Maybe its Quebecois origins prevented it from counting as "new" in Doras eyes. The voters or jurors or whatever they are can hardly be accused of snubbing it in other categories; it's on the list for Outstanding Production (theoretically the top prize of all), for Direction (Eda Holmes), for Male Performance, twice (two Jeffs, Irving and Lillico), for Scenic and Lighting Design (Camellia Koo, Rebecca Picherak), and for Female Performance (Rosemary Dunsmore).

These are all worthy choices, and the last of them is especially gratifying, since Dunsmore's role was something less than a show-stealer; if the Doras acknowledged a separate category for Supporting Performances (they should) she would most certainly

be on it. As it is shes up against, among others, Fiona Reid whose performance in Vanya and Sonia and Masha and Spike won her the critics award for Best Actress (we're retrograde enough to use words like best and 'actress") and who was here at her high-comic - meaning, among other things, indignantly vulnerable - peak. So: I hope she makes it a double. Some of us, after all, are still mad about her being passed over for her amazing performance in Homebody/Kabul years ago.

Nominated Male Actors Not Named Jeff are Joe Cobden (An Enemy of the People), Kevin Hanchard (The Mountaintop), and Kawa Ada (Accidental Death of an Anarchist). My money, as gambler rather than critic, would be on Ada though I actually preferred his performance in Tarragons Much Ado About Nothing. Accidental Death is another multiple-choice show; its on the Outstanding Production list, and Ravi Jain has been nominated for Direction. There are four nominees in that category, Richard Rose having declined his for An Enemy of the People on the honourable grounds that his Tarragon staging was based on a pre-existing German production. The remaining candidates line up in two camps: sober (Alan Dilworth's Twelve Angry Men, Holmes' Tom at the Farm) and jokey (Accidental Death and, inexplicably,

Gisli Orn Gardassons The Heart of Robin Hood; well, at least, they didnt nominate that British import for best play; oh wait, they couldn't.)

Holmes could equally well, or additionally, have been nominated for her direction of Arcadia, which shared with Mr Burns: a Post-Electric Play the critics' award for Best Production. Disgracefully, Arcadia has not received a nomination in any Dora category, though it merited inclusion in practically all of them. Are they prejudiced against imports from the Shaw Festival? Or just against literacy? The remaining Dora candidates for Production are An Enemy of the People, Hooked and Twelve Angry Men. The last of these is also a nominee for Outstanding Performance - Ensemble, which I think of as the Actors Caucus Race Prize: "Everybody has won and everybody must have prizes" - Alice in Wonderland. Despite the competing claim of Trudeau and Levesque, my own vote would go to Soulpeppers dozen angries. Talking of Soulpepper, their Tartuffe failed to make any of the Dora cuts, though it was the most distinguished production of the year, doing justice to a tough masterpiece. In all fairness, it failed to win any critics' award either, despite the best efforts of two of us.
