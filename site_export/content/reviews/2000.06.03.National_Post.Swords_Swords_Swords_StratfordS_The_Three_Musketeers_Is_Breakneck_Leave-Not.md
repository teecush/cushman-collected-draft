The Three Musketeers is in repertoire until Nov. 1 at the Festival Theatre in Stratford. Tickets $38.50 to $67.50. Call the box office at 1 (800) 567-1600.

DArtagnan, a brave and hot-headed young man from Gascony, comes to the Paris of Louis XIII, hoping for a place in the Kings Musketeers. He is befriended by three members of that crack regiment, known by the mysterious names of Athos, Porthos and Aramis. Together, they foil one of the major plots of the Machiavellian statesman Cardinal Richelieu, venturing to England to retrieve some diamonds that the French Queen has bestowed on her would-be lover, the Duke of Buckingham, an indiscretion by which the Cardinal had hoped to ruin her. They also fall afoul of Richelieu's beautiful and fiendishly unscrupulous agent, Milady de Winter, whom they eventually track to a lonely village and judicially execute.

That is the storyline -- it isn't really a plot -- of Alexandre Dumass The Three Musketeers, and you probably know it already, if not from the book, then from one of the innumerable film or TV adaptations. It is, of course, only the skeleton; the novel is an intoxicating essay in the swashbuckling-picaresque, with a huge supporting cast and any number of incidents. The amazing thing about Peter Rabys dramatization (first staged at Stratford in 1968 and now back in a new production) is how many of these it manages to get in; most versions use up most of their time on the diamond-studs episode, and then rush hurriedly to some kind of climax. Raby's script has to rush everything, though never, amazingly, at the expense of clarity. It's a whirlwind scenario, and Richard Monette has staged it accordingly, in a superbly drilled and mounted production that glories in its own superficiality. (I had better make it clear, that is not a put-down.) It seems to have a cast of thousands, though the program reveals 40 actors, rushing about in an infinity of hats and cloaks.

It plays like a musical, with duels instead of duets. There is a mass fight, by way of overture, and when the real action gets going, it involves more swordplay than any entertainment since Errol Flynn last hung up his scabbard. It hardly encourages subtle acting, but it rewards players who can make a hard, fast and preferably amusing statement. Graham Abbey comes out very well as DArtagnan, and does develop before our eyes from callow bumpkin through ardently predatory lover to military leader: less a matter of tracing a psychological arc than of taking each moment triumphantly as it comes. Two of the musketeers are shadowy, but Benedict Campbells Athos is excellent in its hard-bitten hauteur. Kate Trotter is a superbly uninhibited Milady, and Stephen Russell stalks the stage to great aquiline effect as Richelieu.

The Queen of France seems a touch parvenu, and the King is played (by Ian Deakin) as an outright ninny. Some of the Parisian bourgeoisie have French accents, but -- mysteriously -- not all; Robert King, as D'Artagnan's poltroon of a landlord, sounds like Jean Chretien, or, to be more exact, like Chretien as rendered on the Royal Canadian Air Farce. Anachronisms abound, some more blatant than others. Berthold Carrieres music signifies its Frenchness with snatches of the Marseillaise, though the action takes place more than 150 years before the Revolution. It also runs to what sounds like Son of Star Wars (or maybe of Indiana Jones -- I can never remember which theme is which). Milady turns, in her last extremity, into a leather-queen with whip; and a posse of nuns includes a refugee from The Sound of Music. At one point, Cyrano de Bergerac crosses the stage, nose in air; but thats a fair deal, since D'Artagnan is a cameo character in the actual Cyrano, another Stratford favourite that's like The Three Musketeers with a play thrown in.

Some of these japes I would categorize as high jinks, and others as low. The rationale for all of them seems to be that the book is being read by a modern young boy -- whom we see for a few seconds at the beginning of the show -- and that we are watching the story as it would unfold in his imagination. (You may, of course, believe that Monette would have thrown in all these gags, no matter what.)

I wonder, though, if that boy got anything out of Dumas book that we dont see. Because there is a lot missing, much of it witty. At one point, in Raby's version, the musketeers plan to hold a council of war, but are prevented by pressure of events. In the novel, they are always holding councils of war, and these are among the best things in the book. They're how we get to know the characters; theyre four bright, young men (well, Porthos isnt bright, but he's lovable) scoring off one another. We lose much of the flavour of the quartet as individuals -- some interpolated, coarse comedy for Porthos is the production's lowpoint -- and therefore the all-important sense of them as a group. One of the originals set pieces shows them planning their next moves, while simultaneously fending off an invading army.

It would be a great scene, but theres no time for it. The play is composed of short, humorous dialogues, the novel of long, humorous dialogues that cry out to be acted. They would, of course, make the play last about 10 hours, but that would be all right with me.

Dumas was a playwright before he was a novelist; and he remained, in all senses, a very theatrical writer. Only three of his stories seem to be available in English at the moment: this one, The Count of Monte Cristo, and The Man in the Iron Mask (popular for its title, though it's actually part of a much longer novel, and must be incomprehensible to most of its readers). There is an enormous amount of other stuff, worth reprinting and worth adapting. The Musketeer sequels, in particular, are very rich; D'Artagnan at 40 and 50, witty and wise, is a figure of Shakespearean dimension, with soliloquies that compare with Falstaffs. Meantime, this Musketeers is fun. Its the best production of the festival, so far, and that's fine; if it turns out to be the best of the whole year, there'll be trouble.
