THEATRE REVIEW MISALLIANCE

Friday, May 25, 1990

ROBERT CUSHMAN

NIAGARA-ON-THE-LAKE, Ont. -- Written by George Bernard Shaw Directed by Christopher Newton Starring Sharry Flett, Barry MacGregor, Jennifer Phipps and Helen Taylor At the Festival Theatre in Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont. Shaw Festival opens with a triumph of the comic spirit BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail NIAGARA-ON-THE-LAKE, Ont. "READ Whatsisname," says John Tarleton, absent-mindedly seeking a reference for the idea of the Superman. Tarleton, self-taught intellectual and self-made millionaire (very big in underwear), is a walking library catalogue. "Read Tennyson." "Read Darwin." It's a running gag. Whatsisname, of course, is George Bernard Shaw, whose lifelong habit of flattering himself by deprecating himself reached its apogee in 1909 when he wrote Misalliance, a production of which opened the Shaw Festival Wednesday night.

"Democracy," says another character in the same vein, "reads well but it doesn't act well, like some people's plays." Misalliance can act agonizingly badly, like an anti-Shavian's nightmare come true: a formless debate that never stops, so that you feel like screaming every time a new subject appears on the horizon, knowing that it and you are about to be worried to death.

For the same reasons it can, if taken with lightness and decision, be a delight: thought made manifest, transformed into shimmering, self-mocking and terribly serious farce. So it is here. Christopher Newton's production is a triumph of the comic spirit.

Its main weapon is a ferocious insistence on form. Shaw set the play in the greenhouse of Tarleton's capacious garden in the Home Counties. Newton and his designer, Leslie Frankish, have elaborated this into a spectacular jungle of a conservatory, full of overgrown undergrowth. One of the main strands of the play's action - it has plenty of action, despite its author's playful pretenses to the contrary - is the pursuit of Tarleton's daughter Hypatia by various admirers and her pursuit of them. As they chase one another through the trailing ferns the comedy takes on the aspect of A Midsummer Night's Dream.

And why not? "Read Shakespeare," advises Tarleton, "he has a word for every occasion." When these rich, bored characters express their chronic wish for something exciting to happen, strange sounds reverberate in the air.

Excitement does descend from on high,in the shape of a crash-landing airplane. This is the cue for cascades of smoke. Through it all looms the goggled figure of the aviator, obviously charged with some oracular pronouncement. What he actually says is, "I'm really very sorry," to which Tarleton has the only appropriate English reaction: "Not a bit. Come in and have some tea."

Accompanying the flier is a Polish lady acrobat, an extravagantly free spirit who arouses the libido of every male on the premises. Even this luscious apparition is eclipsed by the play's subsequent intruder, an underfed clerk. Long ago Tarleton seduced this young man's mother and he is intent on revenge. Gunner, the name bestowed on him by his intended victim in honor of the weapon he ineffectually brandishes, is a sure-fire comic creation. Every word he says in defiance of capitalist exploitation is justified, and every word is joyously undercut by his novelettish posturing.

Newton does not quite trust his author here, allowing Gunner a couple of speeches' worth of unnecessary (and inconsistent) dignity. The actor, Simon Bradbury, takes some time to hit his grotesque stride, and when he does he gets his effects as much through a mock Socialist salute, a political nervous tic, as through his lines. But his first-night ovation was understandable, as was that bestowed on his opposite Pole, Sharry Flett, for whom the accent does wonders and who marshals her anti-romantic rhetoric very tidily. The production sets her up for exit applause and she gets it. Neither performance is quite as good as the audience took it for, but both are more than all right.

The evening's foundations, though, are laid by the solid older characters: by Richard Farrell, plangently disillusioned as an ex-colonial governor; by Jennifer Phipps as Tarleton's imperturbable wife; and above all by Barry MacGregor, whose Tarleton is the bluff Yorkshire businessman turned inside-out, making every point both for and against himself, and never lingering over any of them. A self-indulgent Tarleton can ruin the piece.

The younger actors have more trouble, as is customary. Newton sees Tarleton's son, Johnny, as the villain of the piece, complacently preening on the edge of national and global catastrophe. So Mark Burgess plays the role as a nasty, thin-lipped Philistine, though it is surely written as a beefy good-hearted Philistine. Like father, like son. Helen Taylor is a forthright Hypatia, maybe too much so; too little the young lady for people to be scandalized when she fails to behave like one. Peter Krantz gives her chosen prey a damn decent profile and demeanor, which may be all that can be done with a role stronger on build-up than on substance.

Two minor quibbles. The show's joyous momentum is halted by one interval too many. The play's final burst of self-parody - Tarleton admits that there is nothing more to be said, and Hypatia exclaims, "Thank goodness" - is muted in the interest of a pastoral dying fall, effective on its own terms. But the total is exhilarating: a moody, sparkling reclamation that affirms what the Shaw Festival should be about. It does credit to actors, production team and Whatsisname.
