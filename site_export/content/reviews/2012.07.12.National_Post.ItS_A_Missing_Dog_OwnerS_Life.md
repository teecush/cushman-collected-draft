THEATRE REVIEW

Come Back, Little Sheba Royal George Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

The two best Canadian performances I have seen this year are being given by Corrine Koslo and Ric Reid, in the Shaw Festival's production of William Inges Come Back, Little Sheba. They have a fullness and an immediacy so true it hurts.

Koslo plays Lola Delaney, an early 1950s Midwestern housewife suffering from a severe case of emotional, and occasionally physical, voyeurism. To put it more kindly, she lives through other people because she has so little to live for herself. Reid is her husband, a reformed alcoholic whom she addresses alternately as Daddy and "Doc," though they have no children, and he dropped out of medical school and settled for being a chiropractor.

If it comes to that, we only hear Lolas name quite late in the action when at a time of crisis she puts in a longdistance call to her mother, who isn't much help. To the neighbours she's Mrs. Delaney, a messy woman with a messy house. Shes also Mrs. Delaney to her lodger, an art student named Marie who uses a campus stud known as Turk for a combination model and fill-in boyfriend, until such time as her legitimate fiance Bruce shall swoop in from their hometown of Cincinnati and claim her.

This two-timing is deeply upsetting for Doc, who - nursing a crush that he cant acknowledge to himself or anyone else - idealizes the girl and thinks Turk unworthy of her. His frustrations boil up inside him with predictable results. He keeps a quart of whisky in the kitchen, just to prove he can resist it, and it functions of course like Chekhov's revolver: you got it, you use it.

Lola, meanwhile, gets a vicarious thrill out of both of Marie's entanglements: She spies on her and Turk, and prepares a bang-up dinner for her and Bruce, apparently seeing no contradiction. She also invites the postman and the milkman into her house, not to seduce them a la Blanche DuBois but to absorb their lives and spin half-truths about her own. She also misses her dog, who wandered off years ago and never returned.

Hence, of course, the plays title, the most notorious and, as it turns out, least necessary thing about it. Once a day Lola steps outside her door and calls unavailingly for lost Little Sheba to trot back. I once heard of a competition for Lines That Would Stop a Play Dead in its Tracks. The winner, hands down, was "Good evening, my name is Godot." I now think that a runner-up might be, Hey, lady, is this your dog?

Little Sheba is clearly a stand-in for everything thats missing in Lola's life and marriage, but since the rest of the play spells out much of what that is, and strongly hints at the rest, it's superfluous, not to say pretentious: A prime example of the fake poeticizing that infested American drama in the 50s and is still there today. At least this play doesnt end by reprising the title line. Sorry if that's a spoiler.

I'm not sure, though, that the dog is any more meretricious a device than, say, the imaginary childin Whos Afraid of Virginia Woolf? And though Inge hardly has Edward Albees wit, his may be the more painful and penetrating dissection of a marriage, a ride on a bitter merry-go-round that, however good the riders' intentions, is fated to go on forever.

A big hit in its day, it preceded his more familiar Picnic and Bus Stop and may well be better than either of them. Certainly Jackie Maxwell, who's directed both those plays at the Shaw, does her best work with this one. It may seem an odd compliment to call Koslos performance "shapeless," but the great thing about her is that she doesnt fit Lola into any kind of mould.

Distress, anxiety, bewildered good-heartedness, forced cheer and efficiency flow out of her, all swirling around an aching void, an unappeasable sense of loss and, crowning everything, love. Reid, whose first action in the play is to get breakfast, starts out kindly, competent, friendly. Then a peremptory edge appears, as he alternately confronts and avoids things he would rather not think about. Finally, and violently, he explodes, pouring out grievances we've known about and others we'd hardly have guessed. Things are said that, if not technically unforgivable, will surely prove unforgettable.

Reid cant prevent his big drunk scene from appearing something of a set piece, but thats inherent in the play. Its sequel, as he pleads not to be taken to a hospital of which he's already told us horrible things, is terrifying, moving, disturbing, everything.

Christina Poddubiuk has designed a ramblingly evocative set, and there's a jazz score that sounds a bit hip for the house, but is very atmospheric. Music counts a lot in this play. Lolas and Docs happiest memories, recreated, are of dancing the Charleston. One of Doc's, and the play's, finest moments finds him transfixed by a stray classical piece on the radio. Lola, unthinkingly and quite unmaliciously, changes the station.
