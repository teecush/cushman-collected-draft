THEATRE REVIEW THE SILVER KING

Tuesday, June 01, 1993

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Niagara-on-the-Lake ONT -- WRITTEN BY HENRY ARTHUR JONES Directed by Christopher Newton Starring Stuart Hughes, Neil Barclay, Sharry Flett, Barry MacGregor, Jack Medley and Robert Benson At the Shaw Festival, Festival Theatre Rating: The Shaw Festival's The Silver King is a big, fat, teeming melodrama; and it does seem at times to hold the whole of late Victorian England in its hands. This, though, is more a matter of breadth of canvas than depth of theme Facing a Dickens of a dilemma BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Niagara-on-the-Lake THEY applaud easily at the Shaw Festival, and they erupted when the lights went up to reveal scaffolding, three tiers no less, with lots of actors standing on it. The lights then went up a little more, and we beheld machinery. A massive wheel was there, and it turned, and somebody was crushed beneath it. It recalled the Hal Prince production of Sweeney Todd, and it too seemed to be signifying the fate of man in a newly industrialized society, and to be impressive, and somewhat tangential to the piece in hand.

Only somewhat tangential. The Silver King is a big, fat, teeming melodrama; and it does seem at times to hold the whole of late Victorian England in its hands. This, though, is more a matter of breadth of canvas than depth of theme. Like many of its breed it plays a game of cops-and- robbers. The hero is Wilfred Denver, who has many good qualities plus a wife and children, but who gambles and drinks too much, and is framed for a murder he did not commit but would have quite liked to. In fact he thinks himself guilty, and only escapes the law by what he takes to be a miracle. A train in which he is believed to have been travelling crashes, causing many passengers to lose their lives and Wilfred to thank his "Merciful Father" for a second chance. God moves in mysterious ways but, as Peer Gynt once said, nobody could call him economical.

Wilfred takes himself to the silver fields of Nevada and returns a millionaire: hence the title. He rescues his loved ones from direst poverty, punishes the guilty and, by dint of some virtuoso skulking and disguising, establishes his own innocence. His opposite number is Captain Herbert Skinner, alias The Spider, a villainous toff who robs, kills, and evicts widows and orphans. Between them stands the regulation implacable detective, keeping tabs on both.

The only Victorian play with which I can make comparisons is Tom Taylor's The Ticket-of-Leave Man, an earlier piece about an innocent who slips through society's net: cruder in many ways, but at least its victim does go to jail and is truly at odds with his world. Henry Arthur Jones, who wrote The Silver King, went on to compose Ibsenite problem-plays, and he was obviously trying for some sophistication. He, unlike Taylor, gave his hero flaws. But they are superficial. Wilfred's line "Oh God, put back thy universe and give me yesterday" is supposed to strike deep chords, but they are undercut by our knowledge that it's all a mistake; he never done it. The Count of Monte Cristo, who knew all along that he was wronged, was a more impressive avenger, and Dickens's Magwitch (Great Expectations) who knew his own guilt, a far more impressive benefactor. Dickens of course is behind all these panoramic plays, and especially behind their portrayals of crime; The Silver King features a patriarchal fence called Eliah Coombe who is nicknamed Father Christmas but who sounds - in this production anyway - like Fagin. Dickens really hated the social order. For Jones, there are no corrupt or cruel judges, and the police are really quite nice. Once the upstart Spider's stolen jewels are returned to their rightful aristocratic owners, all's right with the world.

But it's an exciting resurrection, and an exhilarating show. If asked what makes it superior to the Agatha Christie down the road, I can only say helplessly "its size." I like plays with lots of scenes and characters, and so does Christopher Newton, judging from the relish with which he moves this one: from town pub to country inn, from railway terminus to dockside wharf, from criminal hang-out to stately home (not to mention chez Spider, which doubles as both.) Each locale taps a new vein of humanity, and this prodigality of minor figures is a virtue in itself.

Among the majors, Barry MacGregor is a handsome Spider, not subtle exactly but relaxed, making the most of the qualms - esthetic rather than moral - that link him to his refined wife (Jan Alexandra Smith, good) and distance him from his emphatically unrefined colleagues (Robert Benson, Simon Bradbury, excellent.) But you expect villains to be enjoyable. More remarkable is Jack Medley as the old retainer who stands by the Denvers in all weathers, advancing them his savings and taking any reference to wages as an impertinence. He's impossibly, even unhealthily, virtuous, but written and acted with such gusto as to be the success of the evening. The comedy, played to the limit, doesn't overwhelm the pathos; it preserves it. The enduring Mrs. Denver has less variety in her suffering, and Sharry Flett nags at her a bit, but she has strength when called for. Wilfred himself is required less to act than to radiate: to stand there and project conscience, or endurance, or triumphant serenity. Stuart Hughes lacks the full romantic glow but manages an effectively intense gleam. The production ends abruptly. It needs a celebratory finale to balance its implacable start.
