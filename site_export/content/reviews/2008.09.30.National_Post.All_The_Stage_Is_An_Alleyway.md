THEATRE REVIEW

The Crackwalker

Theatre Passe Muraille Backspace

Twenty-eight years on, Judith Thompson's first notable play stands up very well. The Crackwalker is a tragedy of the urban and marginalized, one that unlike later entries in the genre -- including some of Thompsons own -- never condescends to its characters or treats them as prepackaged ingredients in some political or metaphorical stew. The play is a social indictment, but it grants its people free will and watches compassionately as they abuse it.

There are four active characters and a fifth on the sidelines. This is the Crackwalker himself, an ambiguously named (crack cocaine? falling between the cracks?) homeless man who haunts the dreams of the others and sometimes intersects with their realities. He is what any of them might become. He falls just short, or breaks just free, of being a symbol. Michael Murphys production for the Staged and Confused company, which specializes in Canadian revivals, does a fine job of keeping the Crackwalker constantly on the fringes of the action and of everybody's consciousness, whether he's roaming a catwalk to the side of the main acting area or stumbling onto the stage itself. He gives the play an outer shape.

The inner shape is largely determined by the brief blossoming and steep decline of Theresa and Alan. Theresa, somewhat mentally challenged, is blessed or cursed with emotional amnesia. Her chief means of support is prostitution; she takes up unwelcomed residence on her best friends sofa. Marie Jones plays her with a powerful mixture of innocence and defiance, unshakably fixated on the next good and invariably passing thing.

One of those things is Alan, more intelligent but equally unable to cope: indeed, with fewer defences. He alternates between gentleness and rage, the latter fuelled by the memory of what the system, doctors especially, did to his father. He and Theresa marry, on what seems like a whim, and even more ill-advisedly, have a child. (Alan angrily rejects social services advice that Theresa should have her tubes tied.)

What happens to them and their baby is painful to watch, the more so if you've seen the play before and know what's coming. But it isnt sensational; theres a deep vein of pity caused by the fact that both parents are acting as lovingly as they know how. Rick Jon Egan doesn't quite capture the deep-seated paternal tenderness

that makes Alan both affecting and disturbing, but in all other respects he's admirable, a lanky vulnerable figure straight off the streets.

Its a peculiarity -- actually a weakness -- of the play that it sets up four principal characters and then largely neglects two of them. Alans friend and unwilling host (Hannah Miller) is contrastingly capable, except for being unable to break away from an abusive partner who is also Alan's partner in petty crime. (Craig Pike comes on in this role like a nice young actor uncertain of his surroundings and trying too hard.)

There is something pat and predictable about this second relationship; its participants are reduced to playing chorus to the central drama, rather than being co-equal with it. Nor, in this production at least, do the quartet's rare moments of peaceful co-existence function as the points of rest for which they were presumably intended. There are holes in the pattern. But the main threads are very strong. Very little has changed in our cities since the play was written, and the production doesn't have to strain very hard to remind us of that. But even if it had, the story of Alan and Theresa, and even the Crackwalker himself, would still strike home. - The Crackwalker runs until Oct. 11. For tickets and information, call 416-504-7529.
