THE PAJAMA GAME

Princess of Wales Theatre

Richard Adler and Jerry Ross, who composed The Pajama Game, are famous for bringing what in 1954 were contemporary pop rhythms into the theatre.

Theirs was a world hovering unknowingly on the verge of rock 'n' roll; their love songs were more relaxed and sinuous than the Rodgers-and-Hammerstein norm, the kind of things that the young Tony Bennett or Rosemary Clooney could have crooned or belted (and did).

The air was full of Latin-Americana, of eccentric comedy numbers (novelties in the trade), and of twangy, muscle-flexing cowboy songs. There Once Was a Man, the principals post-coital duet in Pajama Game, is an exhilarating elongation of the latter form; crack a whip under it and its Rawhide. The blue-collar ethos extends from the score to the book; this show and the team's follow-up, Damn Yankees, are among the few musicals with convincing scenes set in kitchens.

In its day the show was controversial; it concerns a labour dispute at a pyjama factory in the Middle West, and there were many who thought this gratuitously bleak for a musical. The heroine is a union militant, the hero a boss' man who sees the light, and the ending is a happy compromise. The politics seem pretty innocuous now; all the same, a recent history of the musical describes the piece as "strident and leftish" so the creators must have been doing something -- you should pardon the expression -- right. Anyway, it ran for years, and the score took over the English-speaking world. In 54 and 55 you couldn't turn on the radio without hearing Hey There, the show's best ballad; and I can testify that when the show came to London, a coffee bar immediately opened just down the road from my school calling itself Hernandos Hideaway, after the shows main mambo. That, for those days, was clout.

Simon Callow's new British production (on a detour to Toronto between dates in Birmingham and London) is in intention a tribute to, and crystallization of, the high spirits of those sedately brash mid-1950s; the show may be a cartoon but the production is a caricature. It's quite an interesting caricature. It boasts eye- popping designs by Frank Stella. The main factory set, an assembly line of sewing-machines, is a stark, witty symphony in black and white with everybody costumed to match. (One character does sport a tiny blue necktie; you can tell hes a cut-up.)

When the action goes outdoors for the workers picnic, the stage explodes in colour: glorious whirling cascades that match the Stella murals and ceiling of the theatre itself.

There's one drop-curtain that the Mirvishes might consider taking legal action to hang onto.

As with sight, so with sound: Rather than the blaring Broadway noise of the '50s, John Harles orchestrations suggest the cool, inventive jazz that made up the periods alternative soundtrack, as if Gil Evans had scored The Pajama Game rather than Porgy and Bess for a collaboration with Miles Davis. The treatment demands singers who can track the original tunes through a luxuriant jungle of accompaniment and the two leads here really deliver. Graham Bickley is the best straight-arrow leading-man to appear in years; he acts with authority, displays the occasional glint of humour, and sings like an especially resilient lark; his falsetto ending to Hey There is a wonder. As intended, Camilla Scott is more standard-issue musical comedy, but she has great vivacity and has improved immeasurably since Crazy for You.

But even some of the production's merits work against it. The designs upstage the performers, and the sophisticated arrangements may prevent the songs from landing as forcefully as they should.

Numbers that should, on the face of it, stop the show play to polite applause. Some of this is due to David Bintley's choreography; he stages the duets nicely but his ensemble numbers dont build. For the picnic number, Once a Year Day, he steps outside the shows spiritual boundaries and takes us into Grease territory with a lot of energetic but random jiving. Still, this is at least another aspect of the '50s.

When Hernando's becomes a leather-bar and the shows dancing soubrette (Alison Therese Limerick), a 70s soul-singer, the production is well outside even its own rules. It is admittedly tough to stage the Steam Heat number, which only exists for Bob Fosse's choreography, in a town that recently saw a re-creation of the original.

The Pajama Game by the way must be the only show to boast four superdirectors on its original creative team; apart from Fosse it had George Abbott as co-author and director, Jerome Robbins as choreographer's babysitter, and the infant Hal Prince as producer. Apparently it wasnt too many cooks.)

Apart from the two leads and a couple of old reliables (Anita Dobson as a secretary, Peter Edbrook as the heroines dad) nobody in this revival is believable. The boss (John Levitt) is a puffing, gyrating buffoon, his main antagonist (Jonathan D. Ellis) an exaggeratedly preening narcissist, so bang goes the conflict; even the labour hymn, Seven and a Half Cents, which is well staged, doesn't mean much. John Hegley, the main comic, is a hyperactive performer who apparently can make his face, voice and body do anything except be funny. However much you may love a show's style, you cannot do it justice by neglecting to play its substance. I suspect that Callow, a fine actor and uniquely astute theatrical commentator, would in other circumstances be the first to acknowledge this.

But no man ever sees his own work through the same eyes as he sees everyone elses; and none of us could survive if we did.

Runs until July 24. Box office: 416-872-1212.
