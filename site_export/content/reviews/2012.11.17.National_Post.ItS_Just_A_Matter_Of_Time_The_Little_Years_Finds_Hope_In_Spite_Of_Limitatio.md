Kate was a schoolgirl in the 1950s with a consuming interest in physics and mathematics.

She was especially obsessed with the nature of time. However her teachers thought that, as a woman, she did not have the right kind of brain to pursue a scientific career in what even they recognized to be a changing world. They persuaded her somewhat reluctant mother to enrol her in a vocational school: something secretarial perhaps. She dropped out, and spent the rest of her life, up until something resembling the present day, moving unhappily from one unfulfilling job to another. Always shy and abrasive, she became clinically depressed. After being institutionalized and theoretically cured, she settled for merely being bitter and withdrawn.

That is the narrative spine of John Mighton's The Little Years, a play more entertaining and even uplifting than I have probably made it sound. It was first produced by Theatre Passe Muraille in 1995; a revised version was staged at Stratford last year, and that production, with a few cast changes, has now been brought to the Tarragon. I liked it a lot at Stratford. I still like it; its sharp and economical and compassionate without being sentimental. But I now have more doubts about the relation of the story to its ideas, and I also feel that some of the life has gone out of Chris Abrahams production.

Mighton, himself a scientist as well as a playwright, writes in a program note that new and almost paradoxical ways of thinking about time and space" mean that "we may be forced to re-examine our unconscious beliefs about art. If time isn't linear, then art isn't eternal, because - Im struggling here - if time doesnt exist, then eternity has nothing to defy. I'm not sure that's such a revolutionary idea; George Orwell wrote long ago that like all writers, Shakespeare will be forgotten some day," and I doubt if Orwell was up on Einsteinian physics. Anyway Kate, who isnt by conventional terms an artist, has a brother, William, who is; hes a poet, acclaimed as a genius since childood. Kate probably adores him, as everybody else seems to, but growing up in his shadow hasn't helped. William dies in the course of the action, and we never meet him even when he's alive, though we do, in one of the plays wittiest touches, behold his funeral urn - by which time the value of his work is already being called into question. (But doesnt this often happen when a writer dies?) I can see the theoretical point of William's absence from the stage, but I found it more frustrating the second time around. We do meet his wife Grace, a social and environmental activist whose passion also wanes with the years; and through her we meet Roger, "the Barry Manilow of painting," a compliment when it was first bestowed but not so much later. He and Grace have an affair, which also proves fleeting though doubtless very nice at the time.

Mighton has honed his dialogue so that every mention of time, however casual, rings an ironic bell. It's here that the production has congealed; too many lines are delivered with a heavy weight of Significance. This affects Irene Pooles performance of Kate, but she can afford it; for one thing, her character specialises in saying things that make people uncomfortable, and for another her assumption of the role - vocal, physical, and psychological - is absolute. She ages with grave and impeccable conviction, and with no obvious external aids. Hunched in an anorak, she retreats ever further inside herself; she even looks monosyllabic. It takes the plays length for her to shed a tear; when she does it's infectious, or so I found it. She cannot, however, make the link between her preoccupations and the play's seem more than a convenient coincidence. She has to do double duty, proclaiming a theme and exemplifying it.

As Grace, Pamela Sinha also ages well; in her younger scenes she seems not so much naive as silly. Shes one of the two principal newcomers; the other is Ari Cohen whose Roger is magnificent, and far removed from anything else Ive seen him do; after his string of American tough guys he offers us a long-haired ponytailed artist, nervously craving validation, professional or sexual, but philosophical when it's withheld. His and Grace's scene of halting mutual seduction remains a highlight: ground-zero endorsement perhaps of the message that, if nothing lasts, we may as well grab the moment. Most revealingly, it has no noticeable consequences. Chick Reid reprises her role as Kates mother, at her best when gnarled and obstinate in a retirement home. Bethany Jillard doubles, beautifully, as the 14-year-old Kate and as her niece who, born into a less restrictive age, is Kate without the baggage. Her aunt, it turns out, was her inspiration. This is the plays epiphany, though to my mind an ambiguous one. It could be a defiance of time, as popularly conceived. It could also be a vindication of it.

The Little Years runs through Dec. 16 at the Tarragon Theatre.
