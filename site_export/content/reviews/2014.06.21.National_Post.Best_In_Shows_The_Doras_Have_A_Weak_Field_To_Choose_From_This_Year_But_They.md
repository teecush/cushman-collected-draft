The Dora Awards presentation gala, on Monday evening at Harbourfront, is being hosted by Naomi Snieckus and Matt Baram. The venue is a new one for the awards; the two funsters doing the hosting are holdovers from last years ceremony, at which they did a refreshingly entertaining job. Their return should at least guarantee that the Doras this year will be less tacky than their big American cousin, the Tonys whose host, Hugh Jackman, seemed under the embarrassing impression that the show was all about him.

The actual Dora nominations seem remarkably classy this year, which I suppose is a critics way of saying that nestled among the possible winners are most of those who were actual winners in this year's Toronto Theatre Critics' awards, plus many who were close contestants in our blood-spattered, furiously argued, marathon-length, closeddoor deliberations last month. (Actually our proceedings, as has consistently been the case in the four years weve been playing this game, were surprisingly short, refreshingly sweet, and conducted in a secluded corner of a public space.)

Its been a poor season for new Canadian plays. There hasn't been one that kept its eye on its object and that turned believable characters and speakable dialogue into action: nothing in fact to match Hannah Moscovitch's This Is War which lit up the previous season and has just been awarded the Trillium book prize. The critics choice this year was Robert Lepages Needles and Opium, a dazzler in writing, staging and performance; it had been seen before in Toronto but had been so extensively re-worked that we felt it qualified as new. The Doras have looked similarly outside the box in choosing as one

of their five Best Play nominees Tim Luscombe's PIG, which isn't Canadian. It was however a world premiere, since in its native Britain its presentation of gay sado-masochism had apparently been considered too hot to handle. The resurgent Buddies in Bad Times picked it up and gave it a superb production. The competition includes only one orthodox play, Sean Dixons A God in Need of Help; I was surprised that the lot didnt fall upon Joan Macleod's The Forest which everybody else seemed to like more than I did. The other contestants are The Gay Heritage Project (Buddies again), a collage of autobiographies; Winners and Losers, a semi-improvised cross-talk act, in which the talk got very cross; and Of Human Bondage, an adaptation of a novel. If I had a vote at this stage it would go to PIG, imperfect - indeed confusing - though it was, though if I were a betting man I'd put my money on Soulpeppers Of Human Bondage because of the number of other categories in which its been nominated. It's called momentum.

Those categories include both best production and best direction; in fact this year four out of five nominees in each of those categories figure in both: a comfort to those of us who have always wondered how you're supposed to tell the two apart. The other three doubles champions are the aforementioned PIG, directed by Brendan Healey; another British import Lungs which, if the Doras rules were different, would be an almost unbeatable contender for best play and was flawlessly directed at Tarragon by Weyni Mengesha; and - to my great satisfaction and delight - The Double, the impish Dostoevsky adaptation that was also a concerto for three performing virtuosi, one of them being the director Adam Paolozza; physical theatre has never been more delightful or more intelligent. All four double entries are shows of which Toronto can be proud. The ringer among the best production choices is Venus in Fur, which was (or is, since its coming back for a third run) a triumph of performance over material. It's certainly an event, which may be what best production is about. The remaining best direction nod has gone to Richard Rose for A God in Need of Help.

Albert Schultz has already won the critics' direction award for Of Human Bondage. Our production award was split; we divided it between Lungs and Soulpeppers other blockbuster Angels in America: a miniature and a mammoth, two shows that were, in the strict sense of the word, incomparable. Soulpeppers season has unquestionably been its best since the company took up residence in the Distillery; it included another, homelier epic The Norman Conquests and such a smaller-scale success as Entertaining Mr Sloane. Schultz's staging of Bondage has been justly celebrated, but Angels, which he also directed, is the more spectacularly acted; indeed it has the greatest concentration of superlative acting of any Soulpepper show since The Wild Duck. (Well, maybe The Time of Your Life.) It was also, most definitely, an Event.

A personal note: I left Angels in America, which came early in the season, convinced that Nancy Palk, who played three roles in it, already had a lock on best supporting actress; nothing that happened later shook that opinion, and she indeed received the critics' award in that category. There is no such slot, however, in the Doras which wastefully lump together performances principal and supporting. Anyway, Palk is in there but only for her performance in Angels Part II, which seems a pedantic distinction and anyway leaves out her amazing rabbi at the top of Part I. (For some inexplicable reason Angels seems to be counted as two plays but the three-part Conquests as one.) Its a daunting field altogether among the females: Carly Street (critics choice) in Venus, Lesley Faulkner (extraordinary) in Lungs, Fiona Reid (hilarious) in Sloane, and Holly Lewis (haunting) in Tribes. Major omission: Laura Condolln in Conquests. As for the males: I'll be very surprised if Damien Atkins, who has already won two awards for his performance in Angels, doesn't carry off a third. Its been his year; hes been in four shows (one of which he co-wrote) and was good to great in all of them. I'll admit that in contemporary plays any distinction between principal and supporting roles is increasingly hard to discover or justify. Hence, perhaps, the Doras' fallback category of best ensemble. This year that includes Bondage (but not, crazily, Angels), The Double, and - a really welcome surprise - Sixteen Scandals, the best Second City revue in, probably, decades.

There is a corresponding ensemble category in the musicals division, but here I cannot imagine the prize going to anything but Canadian Stages London Road; I will be equally horrified if the show itself doesnt get best production and if Jackie Maxwell doesn't win for her direction (and Reza Jacobs for his musical direction of an extraordinarily tricky score). Reid and Atkins are also nominated for their individual performances but, good as they were, I can't see that they were any better than their colleagues in what was, really, an incredible assemblage of Torontos finest. Besides, shouldnt we be spreading these things around a bit? As for your actual best new musical: the pickings are thin, as is demonstrated by the inclusion of The Barber of Seville (another Soulpepper show; nobody's perfect), though operas are included as well including the other Figaro story, Figaro's Wedding. Figaro here, Figaro there. For musicals per se,Id go with Ross Pettys The Little Mermaid if it were nominated, but it isn't. At least, there's a separate opera division in all other categories. For female performance theres a fair embarrassment of riches, but Sondra Radvanovskys Elizabeth I (Roberto Devereux) must surely be the richest of all.

I can't say much about the independent (i.e. small theatre) division, as I have family connections with two of the nominated shows. (I liked them both, by the way). But, among the nominees not involving relatives, I thought very highly of VideoCabaret's Trudeau and the FLQ and Mac Fyfes performance in it; of David Ferrys direction of After Miss Julie and Claire Armstrong's performance in that; and of Alex Mc-Cooeye's seven-foot alfresco Richard III: nice to see Shakespeare in the Ruff getting a nod. Reverting to the general division: the nominations for Of Human Bondage include scenic, costume, lighting and sound design: all thoroughly deserved. The critics simply gave the show an overall best design award. I do carry a subsidiary torch for Camellia Koo's set design for The Gravitational Pull of Bernice Trimble, a new Canadian play that was underrated in a season that could hardly afford to be sniffy. On which subject: the award for Show Most Consistently Shut Out of the Doras must go to Cock, a play not nominated in any category at all. It was another of the British plays without which this Canadian season would have been very sad indeed.
