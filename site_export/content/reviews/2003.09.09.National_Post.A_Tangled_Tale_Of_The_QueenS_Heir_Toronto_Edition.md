THE SWANNE: PRINCESS CHARLOTTE (THE ACTS OF VENUS)

Studio Theatre, Stratford

- - -

When the first instalment of Peter Hinton's trilogy The Swanne bowed in at Stratford last year, it proved to be an elaborate exercise in the what if or "just suppose" school of historical drama. We were told that Princess Charlotte, daughter of George IV of England (a.k.a. the Prince Regent, Beau Brummells fat friend, that one) had a liaison with her valet, who happened to be black, and that she gave birth to a son. Some complicated baby-switching ensued; and as the play enfolded, continually changing time-frames and social strata, we watched the boy hounded from pillar to post, finally landing in the dressing-room of an old, blind actress.

Theres no question of the boy, whose name is William, challenging the succession; his colour alone would declare him illegitimate. Nor do I believe that Hinton believes he actually existed. This is awkward; Shakespeare, for example, may have told all kinds of lies about Richard III, but he probably thought he was telling the truth, so his play carries weight as a view of history. Hinton's story is a fairy tale; so the picture of 19th-century England (and, by implication, of other times and places as well) that he attaches to it can never quite be taken seriously.

That's the downside. The good news is that for much of the time the sheer scale of Hintons ambition, which sometimes verges on the insane, generates its own energy, carrying cast and audience along with it. The newly-opened Part Two, subtitled Princess Charlotte (The Acts of Venus) strikes me as a great improvement on its predecessor, though that may partly be because it has that predecessor to build upon. The gain is in density of theme and atmosphere, rather than narrative. If your main pleasure in the theatre is the simple one of knowing what happens next, you will be frustrated. Hinton offers nothing as straightforward as a sequel. He goes back over the same ground, showing it off from different angles. We have, by the end, moved on a little chronologically, but not much.

As before, the play is presented to us as having been written by the future Queen Victoria, four years before her accession. It seems she has spent a lot of her girlhood (she must be 13) reading Elizabethan and Restoration plays. That at least would account for the strange, if colourful, lingo spouted by the staff and clientele of the brothel (largely, but not exclusively, homosexual) in which much of the play takes place. Victorias view of the state of England is far from what we think of as Victorian. (But then, who knows what goes on in the minds of impressionable young girls before they become virtuous wives and widows?) She also shows great disgust for the rich, especially her own relatives, and great sympathy for the poor; as well she might.

Her play has a chorus-figure, the goddess Venus, who comes to Earth in search of her son Cupid. In other words, she is Looking for Love in all the Wrong Places. (She's one of several mothers here in search of sons). What she finds among humankind is love destroyed and distorted, though it probably doesn't need her presence to point this out.

What else happens? The play keeps switching between periods. In 1817, Charlotte has her affair and her baby, while St. John Voranguish, the royal whoremaster, falls in love with a street girl named Jaquenetta, who also gives birth. She was formerly -- flashbacks within flashbacks -- the mistress of Fred Dobing, an actor in a touring company run by and starring a Mrs. Cox, who is devoted to the Greek classics and is in fact the younger self of the blind Scarecrow from Part One. Fred quits the stage in favour of political agitation; he gets mixed up in the Cato Street Conspiracy, a sort of pre-Victorian Gunpowder Plot, which here inspires some of the simplest and strongest writing and playing of the evening. Meanwhile -- so to speak -- in 1833, the now-adolescent William takes refuge in the brothel, and is pursued there by the authorities, who seize the excuse to tear the place apart. The scenes in the brothel and theatre go on a lot, maybe because Hinton enjoys writing them, but they do have relevance. "I do value words and what they mean" says Jaquenetta, in what I take to be the plays key line. Life would be better if we told the truth, respected style, loved honestly, paid decent wages, refrained from raiding gay bath houses, and maybe abolished the monarchy. I think thats the message.

There is little continuity between this year's casting and last year's; about the only survivors are Seun Olagunju as William, Julia Donovan who sports a royal lisp as Charlotte, and Diane DAquila who as the Scarecrow seems more than ever to be having both the role and the time of her life. Some of the actors seem to have trouble getting their tongues around the lines, which is surprising, since the author is also the director and, in terms of atmosphere and staging, an excellent one. The newcomers include Jane Sidell, superb as Jaquenetta; Scott Wentworth invaluable as Voranguish, in both tough and tender moods; John Dolan, very strong as Fred; and Sara Topham, hard-bitten as the only whore without a heart of gold. Ian White makes a quick, sharp impression as the conspirator Thistlewood, and Margot Dionnes oddly distanced style of acting suits Venus very nicely. As the bawd, Maria Vacratsis is uncharacteristically aloof.

There is surprisingly little humour, apart from the use of long words in sexual contexts, but the overall drive and colour compensate. Next year, presumably, we'll get Part Three. And at some point, surely, all three plays must be staged together, maybe in a single day. I think there's a very good chance that we would be blown away.
