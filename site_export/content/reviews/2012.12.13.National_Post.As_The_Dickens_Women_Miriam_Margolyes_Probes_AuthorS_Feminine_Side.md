Many people, by Miriam Margolyes own account, know her best for her role as Professor Sprout in the Harry Potter films. Others, though probably not quite as many, think of her for her TV performances in Blackadder. And there are some fans who warmly congratulate her on her appearance in a recent London stage production of Samuel Beckett's Endgame.

Everybody seems to know her for something. And during the past year, audiences all over the world have come to know her as an acting ambassador for Dickens' Women -- the title and subject of a one-woman show that shes been taking on a global tour that winds up this week at the Young Centre in Toronto, part of the theatre houses Word Festival, which celebrates the bicentenary of Charles Dickens.

Margolyes isn't exactly a star, but she has a star personality, on and off the stage. Say the name Miriam to anyone in the British acting world and they will know exactly who you mean. This has been going on for a long time, something for which I can personally vouch.

We attended the same English university; I arrived the year after she left, and she was still a potent first-name legend. "She calls everybody darling," said a student newspaper, but, being Miriam, she means it.

For years she insisted that we had been contemporaries, and that I used to give her bad reviews. I've finally persuaded her, during the course of a phone call this past week, that she was wrong about this, but it wasnt easy. Miriam -- I cant call her by her surname at this late date -- is one of those people who seem shy after you get to know them --shy but determined.

The Dickens show has been part of her life for some 20 years, though her enthusiasm for the novels goes back to her childhood. She and another actress, Sonia Fraser, came up with the idea for a two-person show about Dickens.

"We wanted to make a parallel between his life and his work," Miriam tells me. We commissioned a script from a playwright, but it wasn't good. So I wrote it myself: the first and last time I've ever written anything.

They took the idea to the then-director of the Edinburgh Festival, who gave them a booking. It was a two-person show then, with an actor named David Timson playing Dickens himself, and several of his male creations. But "after Edinburgh, Timson got married and decided he didnt want to tour."

So it became a solo show, which she performed with great success, both in England and abroad. Norman Lear, the TV producer, had booked me for a show for a year, she says. "He thought Dickens would be interesting--and new to a Los Angeles audience."

He was right. She plays 23 characters in the show, 20 of them women, including herself. Id have thought this might come naturally to someone who's always been a great character actress and a great character, but she describes it as the bravest thing I do. I talk directly to the audience. I slightly startle them; I come in and out of character without warning.

This week in Toronto marks the end of the Dickens show, though she qualifies that by saying that at least she won't tour it again.

"Ill be a complete wreck when I get back on the plane. But Ill be a very satisfied one."
