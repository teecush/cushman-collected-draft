Adam Baum and the Jew Movie runs until May 4. Box office: 416-368- 3110.

ADAM BAUM AND THE JEW MOVIE

Berkeley Street Theatre, Toronto

NOVECENTO

Du Maurier Theatre Centre, Toronto

The 1947 film Gentleman's Agreement was courageous by Hollywood standards and craven by all others. It was the first big-studio treatment of anti-Semitism; Gregory Peck played a journalist who posed as a Jew in order to experience discrimination at first hand.

Darryl F. Zanuck, the mogul who made the movie happen -- and who was, almost uniquely among the studio bosses, a gentile -- asked one of the technicians what he had learned from working on it. "Well, sir" was the reply, "I've learned never to be nasty to a Jew, because he could be a Christian in disguise."

Around the same time, Samuel Goldwyn, a movie mogul who was emphatically not a gentile, apparently met with the WASP leftist screenwriter Ring Lardner Jr. (later of the Hollywood Ten and much later of M*A*S*H). Their encounter has inspired Adam Baum and the Jew Movie, a newish -- as in "funny, it doesn't look newish" -- play by Daniel Goldfarb, a young Canadian writer who lives in New York. It is intelligent, and it throws up some painfully amusing paradoxes, but it doesn't dramatize them.

The problems begin with Goldfarb's Goldwyn, who is here called Samuel Baum; the disguise is even thinner than Gregory Peck's but the dramatist won't own up to it. He sprinkles the dialogue with familiar Goldwynisms like "include me out" (as well as others like "leave messages to Western Union" that have been attributed to just about everybody). Better he should have called the guy Goldwyn straight out, or have invented his own manner of speaking.

Anyway, this Sam has been planning his own picture about anti- Semitism, and is both alarmed and incensed to discover that Zanuck is about to beat him to the punch. There is room in this town, he says, for just one Jew movie a year. (That is actually, by the standards of the time, a generous estimate.)

He has commissioned a script from a Nordic Oscar-winner called Garfield Hampson Jr., and Gar, after years of writing fluff, is delighted at the opportunity to exercise his social conscience. He favours the title Soil of Utopia; Sam doesn't, and who can blame him? Gar has done loads of research, and has tried to come up with a realistic account of American Jewry; unprejudiced, unglamorized. This, apparently, is not what Sam envisaged. For this he hired a goyish writer?

It is unclear just what kind of Jews Sam wants to put on his screen -- unclear to Gar, unclear to us, unclear perhaps to Sam himself. Anyway, he invites Gar to attend his son Adam's bar mitzvah party, there to see some real Jews.

Gar is appalled by the vulgar ostentation he beholds. Sam, definitively turning the tables, asks what right Garfield, the child of old money, has to criticize the ways in which first-generation immigrant paupers may choose to flaunt their hard-won wealth.

Sam, it transpires, would really like to make a movie as much like Zanuck's as possible. Hampson Jr.'s opinion of Gentleman's Agreement is similar to Sam Goldwyn's of a verbal agreement: that it isn't worth the paper it's written on. Baum Sr. thinks the movie -- scripted, incidentally, by the Jewish Moss Hart -- is the only kind of Jew movie an American audience will sit still for.

It's a good point, one of a few, but the play never amounts to more than a debating-piece. This works all right in the first half, which is a duologue in Sam's office; the characterization may be thin but the issues are strong, and we are further held by Sam's constant impish wrong-footing of his idealistic opponent.

But when we move to the Baum house, things get sticky, structurally and emotionally. We meet Adam, and observe -- indeed, have it thrust in our face -- his father's love for him. The time, remember, is immediately after the Holocaust, and a Jewish parent is going to be even more protective than usual.

It is also just after Hiroshima, and the fact that "Adam Baum" sounds like "atom bomb" is obviously not accidental. What good it's supposed to be is another matter. Is Adam Baum one portent of the future, and the Jew movie another?

Sam, we realize, knows in his bones what anti-Semitism is and what it can do, and he is entitled to fight it on his own terms. Well, all right, but the play ends with young Adam and his very secular father antiphonally reciting the boy's scripture portion -- a shameless (and, to this hard case, bootless) assault on the heartstrings. Enough already.

The sentimentality is enhanced by the performance of Richard Greenblatt, who is too young, too trim and too nice to play Sam. Goldwyn, by recent biographical accounts, was a monster, and he must certainly have been ruthlessly tough. How else could he have clawed his way up from direst poverty, and how can we be moved by his soft centre if we haven't first had to penetrate his his hard shell?

Garfield also seems amazingly innocent for a man with four Tracy- Hepburn pictures under his belt, but Rick Roberts plays him excellently as he steps blithely into all the traps his author has laid for him.

Alisa Palmer's direction speeds the first half along, and runs thoroughly aground on the second. Dylan Rosenthal's Adam is not so much written or directed as sacrificed; he looks right but is saddled, among other things, with one unbelievably crass joke.

The play also goes in for melodramatic padding. Sam gets alarming phone calls. By the time we find out what they're about (it's not what we thought) it's too late to tie the information to the theme. And talking of red herrings (which might be a good alternative title, Gar being the red and Sam the herring), Gentleman's Agreement has its uses as a stalking-horse but it finally gets in the way. We keep being diverted from Sam's feelings about his Jewishness to his feelings about the competition.

- - -

Alessandro Baricco, the Italian author of the monologue Novecento, seems to have been unsure what he had when he had it. Was it a short story or a play?

I think it's the former, but Tom McCamus' performance of it (brought to WorldStage by the Theatre de Quat'Sous of Montreal) makes that seem like quibbling. He is hypnotic. He plays Tim Tooney, a jazz trumpeter who worked a Trans- Atlantic liner between the word wars, and there met and befriended a miraculous pianist named Novecento who literally never left the ship; he was born there, abandoned there, adopted there, and you can probably guess the end.

The philosophic import of this is unclear, though the writer clearly thinks it has one; and there are some groaningly whimsical attempts at musical poetry (ragtime is described as "the music that God dances to when nobody's looking"). But as anecdote -- or a series of anecdotes, including a cutting-contest with Jelly-Roll Morton -- it is riveting.

McCamus, alternately awed and impatient, delivers it with hunched, sandy-voiced perfection. He works in chiaroscuro, as does Francois Girard's production, on a set (by Francois Seguin) that gradually reveals itself as a giant engine room.

Nancy Tobin, the sound designer, mixes the sounds of sea, ship and piano, cunningly and hauntingly. The show was here for three performances. It must return.
