Auditions, says Martha Burns, probably speaking for most actors, are not my favourite thing. But when you don't have to learn lines for them, it's a plus." She was speaking of her tryout for Penelope Buitenhuis A Wake, an improvised film cast, naturally, from improvisatory auditions. The candidates were given an outline of the intended story, situation and of their own characters. The punning title refers to a gathering in memory of a Hungarian-born Canadian theatre director named Gabor Zazlov, recently felled by a heart attack. Burns part (yes, she got it) is that of the director's patron and principal fundraiser. This makes her one of the few actors in the picture not playing an actor.

Other roles cut closer to the professional bone. Graham Abbey, for example, plays "an actor with classical rep experience, whose mentor had died and who had gone off to do a cop series on TV. This parallels Abbey's own track record: a leading player at the Stratford Festival who then became a TV regular on The Border. At one point his character owns up to having appeared in a Hollywood film called Revenge of the Snow Yeti, though Abbeys at pains to make clear this title does not derive from his own experience.

Gabor, the genius-tyrant of A Wake, is playing what seems to be a posthumous practical joke, gathering together the people who used to work for him so that they can pick at one anothers scars. The process involves having them do an impromptu performance of Hamlet, the play he could never get on. His wife films these proceedings, and also installs a camera in the powder room, to which each of the participants can record his or her own reminiscences of the great man, For Abbey, this produced another art-imitating -life moment, since shortly before filming his own bathroom soliloquy he'd received news that one of his real-life mentors, the theatre director John Wood, had suffered a heart attack (from which, happily, he recovered).

Like the guests at the party, the actors in A Wake spend a lot of time waiting for their next confrontation, in Abbey's words holed up in the basement of a home in Guelph" -- actually a snowbound farmhouse whose owners, Burns says, couldnt have been more hospitable."

Unlike in a Mike Leigh film, where the actors perform a written script, derived from an extended period of improvisation in rehearsal, this team were allowed to make lines up on camera. Rehearsal time had been short and there was only about a week for the actual filming. Even here the amount of spontaneity had to be limited, at least in the big group scenes; otherwise, Abbey says, the cameramen would go nuts.

Burns says that it "gave you a different kind of energy -- it was a big task to lose your self-consciousness. There was the adrenalin of what can you do in that span of time. It was amazing to me that Penelope had complete stories when it was finished." Buitenhuis certainly approached the project with the right combination of credentials: from horror films such as Killer Bees to the improvised TV comedy of Train 48.

Gabor himself, whom we meet in a brief prologue, is played by Nicholas Campbell, who was in his own words "an afterthought -- I

came in after theyd shot most of the movie. I had a day to

come up with the accent. There were fights and

everything going on when I got there. But

I've known Penny for years; she always struck me as someone who would get things done on her own."
