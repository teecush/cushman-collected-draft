Till March 10. Box office (416) 368-3110

WIT

Bluma Appel Theatre

Margaret Edson's play Wit arrives from the United States brandishing rave reviews and wearing every conceivable garland, including the Pulitzer Prize.

These facts, especially the last, are not automatically encouraging. The Pulitzer judges have a history of rewarding whatever ennobling drama is fashionable in a given year; and Wit, which is about a woman dying of cancer, seems all too obvious a candidate. Some years ago, they gave the prize to a piece of calculated uplift called The Shadow Box, which managed to be both cute and sentimental on the subject of cancer but could not bring itself to utter the word.

Wit, to its credit, has no difficulty saying it; and it is not, except towards the end and thus forgivably, sentimental. It is, however, cute; it begins with the central character appearing before us in a blue hospital gown and red baseball cap and asking briskly, How are you feeling today?"

This, she explains to us, is the standard hospital greeting, directed at a patient even when she is in the act of throwing up. She uses it to usher us into her own drama, of which she serves as both protagonist and mistress of ceremonies; and the convention involves repeated jokey intimacies that the audience laps up.

The heroine, Vivian Bearing, is a professor of English diagnosed at 50 with inoperable ovarian cancer and consigned to eight months of agonizing chemotherapy, at the end of which she dies. The play is performed on a generic hospital set and much of it charts Vivian's clinical progress, or lack of it.

It is, of course, moving, and the construction, which involves flashbacks to the patients past life, is remarkably deft and economic.

Indeed, for a first (and, by the authors avowal, only) play, Wit is extraordinarily accomplished; I'm tempted to use the word "slick. It doesn't have a single boring moment, and its Toronto presentation is ideal. I cannot imagine a tighter production than Glynis Leyshons or a more complete and affecting central performance than Seana McKennas.

All the same, I didn't much like it; and many of my doubts centre around the title. Much of the dialogue, and especially of Vivian's commentary, is witty in the conventional sense; the resident who has clinical charge of her turns out to be an ex-student of hers to whom she gave an A-minus; I wish," she murmurs ruefully, "Id given him an A, and the remark legitimately brings down the house.

But wit" here has a more specialized meaning, too: its the complex but light-hearted juxtaposition of words and ideas, set in dramatic conflict with one another, that distinguishes English metaphysical poetry, especially that of John Donne, whose Holy Sonnets are Vivian's academic specialty.

Everybody in the play goes on about these poems as if they were the hardest things in the language (actually, compared with, say, Emily Dickinson's, they are a breeze), and everyone, including Vivian herself, makes comparisons between Donnes unflagging intellectual rigour, the uncompromising demands Vivian made on her students and herself and the attitude of her doctors, who see her less as a person than as a research opportunity.

The case is rigged. Donne wrote erotic poems as well as religious ones, and the two categories overlap. Vivian can hardly have taught him without having come up against this, but we are meant to believe that her choice of subject reflects her own coldly ascetic nature; she has no friends, no lovers, no spouses -- no visitors. And though the fact is acknowledged it is never explored.

She also, it seems, has no interest in religion, though you can hardly approach Donne in purely secular terms. The poem "death be not proud is given a lot of attention, but even its peroration death thou shalt die" is not that baffling a paradox if you accept that its author, a clergyman, believed passionately in Christian resurrection.

One of the flashbacks shows us Vivians own university teacher returning an essay and telling her not to be sloppy, "This isn't Shakespeare. When Vivian is nearing death this teacher reappears (in what I devoutly hope is a dream sequence) to see her off with a line, not from Donne, but from Hamlet: Flights of angels sing thee to thy rest." There is subtlety in this progression, but the feelgood factor outweighs it.

Nor could I quite believe in a doctor (excellently played by Jim Mezon) who enters a room and briskly announces, "You have cancer, or in a nurse, the play's emblem of non-intellectual compassion, who doesn't know what soporific" means. In short, I don't think the play, clever though it is, justifies its own, to use the appropriate metaphysical term, conceit.

Alex Poch-Goldin, Joy Coghill and Kirsten Williamson give good acting support, and Ms. McKenna achieves a triumphant blend of technique and understanding. The role is written on one-and-a-half notes, which she never tries to soften or expand; she concentrates and distills, and the effect is spellbinding.
