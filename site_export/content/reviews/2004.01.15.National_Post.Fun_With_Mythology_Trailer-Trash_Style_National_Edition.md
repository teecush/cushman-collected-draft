PHAE

Factory Theatre Studio

One of the less easily pronounceable titles of recent years, Phae turns out to be short for Phaedra. She was the mythical princess, wife of Theseus, who destroyed herself and others with her passion for her virginal stepson Hippolytus. The story was most famously dramatized by Euripides, who named his play after the boy, and by Racine, whose Phaedre is firmly centred on the woman. Now here comes a young Canadian actor and playwright, Julian Doucet, keeping the Racinian focus and the Euripidean scorn of romantic sub-plots; and setting the whole thing down in a contemporary Deep South trailer park. It works remarkably well.

Doucet plays Hippolytus, though his best acting moments come before the main action, as he talks about the Greek legends he has learned about in school. He gets to wondering what all them old gods might be doin' right now, on account of all the spare time they must have on account of not havin to listen to folks' prayers, what with nobody now believin' in them an all. The schoolteacher who set our heros mind working in this way is Miss Minerva, and Doucet is pretty smart all around with his classical references. Before marriage, his Phae was a Miss Judge; the original Phaedra was the daughter of Minos, who presided over the sentencing of the dead in the underworld.

The American South is a mythology in itself, and Doucet's cunning is that his white-trash characters are aware of this and of their own place in it. They read the tabloids; they watch daytime TV. And though having the hots for your stepson may rank fairly low on the Jerry Springer scale, it's enough to bother Phae. She may not be burdened by a queens responsibility toward her subjects, but she does care what the neighbours think. She seems noticeably less eaten up by guilt than her dramatic predecessors -- her fatal compulsion seems more like simple selfishness -- but her lust, fear and anger are real enough. The fear is both of her own passions, and of her husband, Big Daddy, king of the trailer park or at the very least of his own particular trailer.

The anger, as well as the lust, is directed at the unresponsive Hippolytus who gets on better with animals than with people and who likes to hang out in the woods dressed only in shorts. (He is one character who gets to keep his Greek name, though it turns out this is only because his late mother couldnt pronounce hippopotamus, which would have been her preferred pet name for him.) These people have size, even if the play looks at them cockeyed.

At the moment of unavoidable truth, Leanna Brodie's Phae lets out a feral cry worthy of her Racinian prototype. It's an inspired touch to have her afflicted by a (phantom) lump in her breast, which weighs her down as surely Racines Phaedre was crushed by her consciousness of tragic guilt. Instead of the nurse, Oenone Phae has for confidante and go-between, Una, proprietress of the local beauty salon. Caroline Azar in this role, like Brodie in hers, gets the best of both worlds; both of them are credible inhabitants both of their immediate society and of the long tradition that they have lately joined.

None of their colleagues brings this off, and the dreaded question "what for?" begins to haunt the evening. The play wants to have fun with the myth and the milieu, while simultaneously evoking the old tragic thrill; the subtitle "a trailer park trashedy," with its air of amused condescension, gives it away.

Daryl Clorans production is sure-footed with the drama and the blacker comedy, club-footed with the overt light relief. Doucet's young Tarzan is not very interesting in either the writing or the acting, and the chorus (Kimwun Perehinec and Tova Smith as a couple of gossips under the hair dryers) are downright tiresome. Nor does anybody, including Oliver Becker who plays him, seem sure just how fierce and fearsome Big Daddy is meant to be.

At only 90 minutes, the play in its later sequences begins to seem long. But the language is rich and the promise great; and both the concept of Phae and Brodie's execution of it a treat.

Them gods aint just stayin home bein' lazy; they's a usin all that free time to wreak havoc with human lives, just the same as they always did. And you know sumpn? With no more of them divine responsibilities to worry about, they's a-probably enjoyin' it more'n they ever did.
