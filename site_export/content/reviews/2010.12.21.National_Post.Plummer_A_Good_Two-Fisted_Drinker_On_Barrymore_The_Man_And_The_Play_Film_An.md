'It's an octogenarians' festival." So says Christopher Plummer, an extraordinarily dapper, elegant, spry and courteous 81, while taking tea in the Four Seasons' Studio Cafe. He's about to open -- or re-open -- in Barrymore, the sort of solo play (there are two actors, but the other one spends all his time just offstage) that he originally played at Stratford in 1996 and subsequently took to New York and Toronto. His "very astute" director, Gene Saks, who among other things has been encouraging him "to talk to the audience more" is 89; the author, William Luce, a youth of 79, has also been "very good, realistic; he's cut a lot of unnecessary things."

Saving, or maximizing, time has become a priority for Plummer. "When you get to my age, you're in a terrible hurry. You ask yourself, 'Why am I doing this, going backwards?'My only regret about the original was not having done it in London."

He's found some other reasons. "It's much more touching than when we did it before. The minute we started, I got excited about doing it." And there are also more practical reasons: "There's the promise of putting it on screen. And in the theatre, it's just a five-week run, five performances a week: no more."

And, of course, there are the reasons that first drew him to the play and the character, 14 years ago. John Barrymore, the pre-eminent American theatre actor of the first half of the 20th century, was a formative influence on Plummer himself. "I was 14 when I read Gene Fowler's biography. I swore to emulate him, this wonderful bon vivant. So I blame Jack Barrymore. I'm disappointed that I never saw him in his prime. None of his films do him justice. Except Twentieth Century [in which Barrymore plays an egomaniacal actor-manager, clawing his way back to the top]; that was really him."

There's also The Show of Shows, the 1929 omnibus picture in which Warner Bros., like all the other studios, cashed in on the birth of the talkies by displaying all the performers on its roster who could actually make a sound. Barrymore's contribution was a Richard III soliloquy (actually taken from the prequel, Henry VI Part 3), which he delivered standing triumphantly atop a heap of fresh corpses. (The play Barrymore revolves around the alcoholic actor's apocryphal attempt to revive his career by playing Richard.) Plummer pronounces it "some of it terrific, some not so good. There's a mystery surrounding him still. He had the ideal look for Hamlet, the ideal manliness for Hamlet. He was princely and handsome, not a pretty boy. He must have driven all the girls wild -- all the boys, too. And he had a wonderful humour." (Humour is one of the qualities that Michael Langham, the director who brought Plummer to Stratford in the 1950s, singles out among those that make him a natural classical star.) "And of course Barrymore didn't have much competition. There weren't many great classical actors in America then. And he didn't sound old-fashioned, not from the age of Henry Irving or Forbes-Robertson." At this point, he pauses to deliver a hilarious impersonation, based on an old recording, of Johnston Forbes-Robertson, the Edwardian heroic actor for whom Bernard Shaw wrote one of Plummer's own recent successes, Caesar and Cleopatra, and who, I have to confess, I had never imagined sounding quite so Scottish.

Plummer in the past has named Barrymore as the inspiration for his own thespian generation of "good two-fisted drinkers," though you have only to inspect his own recent resume to realize that he has been spared the Barrymore self-destructiveness. He's always claimed that he likes food too much to be a really dedicated drinker. Especially now: "You need fodder during rehearsals. I've been getting fatter and fatter in Toronto." (I can't say it shows.) He's also had to interrupt rehearsals by commuting to Stockholm ( "Canada and Sweden look exactly alike--no light at three o'clock." Our conversation, I should make clear, occurred before the recent bombing) where he's filming the second part of the Dragon Tattoo trilogy, English-speaking version. "Ours will look more beautiful--I play the patriarch of a huge and ghastly family.

He's the only nice person in the whole friggin' thing." His film career has of course moved into its highest gear, since his performance in The Insider, though apparently it didn't feel like a breakthrough while he was doing it and "trying to get through 40 takes; I had to tell Michael Mann, the director 'I can't do it differently, I won't be Mike Wallace anymore.' " Then this year there was his proto-Prospero in Terry Gilliam's The Imaginarium of Dr. Parnassus, which he loved doing though he thinks that Gilliam should get himself a writer, and his first Oscar nomination, for playing Tolstoy in The Last Station. He and his co-star Helen Mirren were each limbering up for their own attempts at Prospero (or in her case, Prospera) in stage or film versions of The Tempest; they used to tease one another about it. Now he'd like to try some gender-switching himself. He amazed me, because it's not a play anybody thinks about anymore, by saying he'd like to play the title role in Jean Giraudoux' The Madwoman of Chaillot. Sounds like a great idea; we're unlikely to see the play any other way and it could be very timely. (Arthur Miller once called it the most outspoken attack on exploitation he'd ever read.) He downplays rumours that he might be playing James Tyrone in a revival of Long Day's Journey into Night: "I want to have more fun than that at my age. Though if somebody like Meryl Streep would play opposite me ..." What he really wants to do now is "an outrageous comedy. I wish they were writing them now. They only play TV comedies." To spend an hour in his company is to wish to see that sense of fun, and that charm, fully unleashed.
