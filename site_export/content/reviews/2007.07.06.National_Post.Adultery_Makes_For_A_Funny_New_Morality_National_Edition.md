I love Ken MacDonald's set for the hotel in Hotel Peccadillo. It's the rich relation of his crazy design for the one-man play Earshot, with its violently exaggerated perspective. Here we get a corridor flanked by two lines of doors that get outlandishly smaller the farther away from us they are. At their apex is a tiny chair. At one moment, a full-sized actor brings it right downstage, where proportions are normal, and sits on it. It's hilarious.

The Hotel Peccadillo, then, is quite the kinky establishment. It, and the play that contains it, have previously been best known in England as Hotel Paradiso. The original is by Georges Feydeau, the master of 19th-century French farce (and his sometime silent partner Maurice Desvallieres, who lives on in parentheses). The new version is a free update by Morris Panych, who is also the director. He has stayed fairly close to the classic structure. In Act 1, various couples plan independently to commit adultery in a shady inn. Act 2: They all arrive simultaneously and are all elaborately thwarted. Act 3: They have to explain themselves to jealous spouses and, for the most part, get away with it. Feydeau wrote many

plays of this kind, and the proof of his genius is that his third acts, where the knots are tangled tighter before being cut, are invariably the funniest.

Panych has also found equivalents for all the original characters though he has changed their external features and, in some cases, their essences. His protagonist, Pinglet, is now a psychiatrist with a dragon lady for a wife. Pinglet has written books in praise of monogamous marriage, so it's somewhat embarrassing that he should find himself with an urge to seduce the wife of one of his patients. She's willing, indeed eager, since her husband neglects her; this is because he's a professional critic (of everything, apparently, from paintings to, yes, hotels) for whom nothing -- in art, life or probably plumbing -- can ever live up to his ideals. A new character is Feydeau himself, who takes over the role of the hotel manager and combines it with that of a commentator, lamenting how much more uptight everybody is now than in his day. He has some very funny lines. So, at one time or another, do most of the other characters. There is also some very funny staging.

But it's all to no purpose. Or rather, it's to too much purpose. Panych has come up with something that nobody needs: a moralizing farce. The fact that his morality -- for which he makes a very good case--is other people's immorality doesn't slow the play down. His target is today's new puritanism, as exemplified by Pinglet, whose couch is a site for preaching fidelity. It seems to me that Panych overrates the new repressiveness and underrates the old, of which, as it happens, Feydeau himself was an exemplar. (In his work, I mean. In life, if that's the right term, he died of syphilis.) Panych has got Feydeau wrong. Those belle epoque farces, however many frilly knickers they have on display, are the opposite of aphrodisiac. They show respectable bourgeois people making fools of themselves and very relieved to return to normality with their virtue, and more importantly their reputations, still unscathed. "This would happen when my wife's not here to help," says Feydeau's protagonist (though not Panych's) when overcome by tummy troubles in the act of coveting his neighbour's wife. At least, he says it in the version I'm familiar with. Maybe it's a translator's caprice. But it fits.

The point is, they all have reason to fear being found out. In Panych's version, nobody does, except Pinglet, and even he's a borderline case. The mainspring of farce has been removed, and what remains is mere rushing around, well-choreographed, but essentially aimless. The mechanism has been tampered with, too. Feydeau's cast includes an upright barrister with four young daughters and a stammer that comes and goes, a device that proves crucial to the denouement. Panych substitutes a dashing airline pilot with a retinue of nubile flight attendants. This looks like a wholesale lift from a later and lesser French farce, the 1960s hit Boeing- Boeing, currently enjoying a brilliant surprise revival in London, in accordance with the rule that yesterday's escapism is today's classic (and vice, quite often, vice versa). The pilot also has a speech impediment, but it means nothing, since in this set-up he has nothing important to conceal or reveal.

The most centred and economical of the Niagara performances is given by Benedict Campbell as the critic. The slyest and juiciest comes from Lorne Kennedy, whose Feydeau has the delivery of a scripture-citing devil and the coiled posture of a jackknife. The most rewardingly energetic, vocally and physically, is Patrick Galligan's Pinglet, who in addition to driving the plot along gets to deliver some verbal tirades that fizz with the authentic farcical mixture of doubt and desire. The object of his attentions is Charlotte Gowdy, whose character's subtleties have gone out the window along with her inhibitions, but who is a delightfully sullen sex object even so. And there is that set, exploiting the full depth and width of the Festival stage but still unable to dispel the impression of a lot of talented people rattling around in a void. - In repertoire until Oct. 7.
