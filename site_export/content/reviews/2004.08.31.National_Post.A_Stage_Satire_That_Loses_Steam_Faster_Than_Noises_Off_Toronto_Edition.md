HARLEQUINADE

Royal George Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

- - -

Here's a poser. The Deep Blue Sea, Terence Rattigans best full- length play, was first staged in 1952, two years after the death of Bernard Shaw, so it would be ineligible for production at the Shaw Festival, whose original rules limit the repertoire to plays dating from the patron-saints own lifetime. It wouldn't even qualify under the new amended guidelines which permit plays that are set in that period but written later; The Deep Blue Sea, like most of Rattigan's work, is set in Time: The Present. If the festival people wanted to produce it (and they should) they would have to rationalize the situation somewhat like this: Plays arent written or produced overnight, so Rattigan must at least have been thinking of this one in 1950, which would have been time present when he started writing. Such logic is not unheard of

No such sleight-of-mind would be necessary to justify a production of The Browning Version (1948), Rattigans best one-act play and a minor masterpiece, but the festival hasn't programmed that either, though I'm sure it will one day. What it has offered us to be going on with is Harlequinade, which was originally devised as an afterpiece to The Browning Version, and is a very light satire on the theatre. This is its first professional Canadian production, and, more surprisingly, the first time that any play by Rattigan, once the monarch of the West End boulevards, has ever reached Niagara.

It presents Arthur and Edna Gosport, a long-serving and popular acting couple, who are on the road as Romeo and Juliet, long after it has ceased to be appropriate. Edna is closer to 40 than to 14, much closer, and as for Arthur, best not go there. But they soldier on, bickering and bantering but devoted, to their profession and to one another. We meet them dress-rehearsing, at their latest touring date. Another opening, another show: If Harlequinade reminds us of Kiss Me Kate (which came along a year later) its partly because the married stars in both the play and the musical are based on that most celebrated of acting couples, the Lunts, who at this time were not just a legend but a living one.

Its great fun, though it needs to be seen in relaxed circumstances, either paired as originally with something more substantial or done on its own, as here, as a lunchtime diversion. It went over especially well at last week's premiere with a largely pro audience who laughed as knowingly at the actors playing it as at the actors they were playing. Peter Hutt is especially delicious as Arthur, looking in his balcony-scene costume like a superannuated buccaneer, earnestly essaying a "little leap" that he has never tried before and that throws his partner into deep confusion and then sportingly giving it up, devil-may-care in what passes for his private life, and devoted to his craft even in the grimmest of circumstances. Goldie Semple as Edna lacks his lightness of touch, being more the battle-axe and less the butterfly than the role seems to warrant.

When Harlequinade first appeared, it was hailed as the best backstage farrago since Sheridan's The Critic two centuries earlier, a position it continued to maintain until being decisively displaced by Michael Frayns Noises Off. Like Noises Off it runs out of energy but it does so far sooner and with less discrimination. It stops making jokes about the theatre and throws in a more mundane farcical subplot. This concerns a previous professional engagement of Arthurs at this very same Midlands town, during which he absent- mindedly got married and unknowingly fathered a daughter. That child is now grown up and presents herself, with her own husband and her own baby. This blast from the past naturally throws Edna into even greater perturbation than did Arthur's little leap; it qualifies, when you think about it, as quite a big leap. Arthur himself isn't much perturbed; he is in fact imperturbable. (Twenty years before, hed managed not to notice the General Strike. This is taken as a great joke on the self-centredness of actors, though they are hardly the only people who live in worlds of their own and are oblivious to politics.) This insouciance is an admirable quality in its way, but it does rather disqualify him as the hero of a farce.

The subplot isnt that bad in itself; and it's firmly nailed down here by Diana Donnelly as the very voluble lost girl and Graeme Somerville as her very taciturn spouse. But Neil Munro's production is at fault in presenting the play as if it were about Arthur and Edna. It isnt. Its about their stage manager, Jack, who does all the play's work while they get its glory. (This is art imitating life.) It's Jack who has to cope with all the turmoil while fending off a crisis of his own, in the shape of his fiancee who wants him to get out of the theatre, like now. He also has many of the sharpest lines, some about how the audience loves the fact that Arthur and Edna are actually married, some about the role of arts subsidy -- a new-fangled thing at the time -- in taking plays to audiences who dont want to see them. These last have been cut in the current production, which is surely rather craven of the Shaw. I dont agree with the sentiments either, but they're funny in context.

Kevin Bundy's performances as Jack is no more than amiable; and consequently neither is the play. Jack is a soul in agony; the hero that every farce needs. (Noises Off is an exception; it presents a group in agony. But the lack of an identifiable protagonist may explain its obstinate refusal to come to a final boil.)

I should not, though, get too heavy about Harlequinade. A decorative style of Shakespearean acting and production, fashionable in the 1930s and already fading fast in the 40s, gets entertainingly lampooned; Judith Bowdens decor is a monument to the Higher Tat, all the way down to an etched backdrop that looks more like Venice than Verona. But doubtless the Gosports had a Merchant in their repertoire as well.

There is a delightful performance from Jennifer Phipps as Arthur's mother, a Dame, now playing the Nurse, who always has a bottle and some advice handy, and is thoroughly indomitable; and another from Bernard Behrens as a small-part actor who detests the business and is highly domitable. David Schurmann puts in a flavourful appearance as a morose manger, Peter Krantz drifts periodically and silently across the stage, looking even more menacing than usual; and Mark Adrians makes a definite shy mark as a super, entrusted at short notice with the single line "Faith, we may put up our pipes and be gone." Rattigan himself, as First Musician, spoke this line in an undergraduate production at Oxford; and he claimed that his delivery of it always brought a perceptible frown to the brow of Peggy Ashcroft, who had been imported from the professional stage to play Juliet, and was supposed to be acting dead at the time. He wrote The Deep Blue Sea for her, regardless.
