There were times in The 25th Annual Putnam County Spelling Bee, which comes to us as a hit Broadway musical with an off-Broadway size and sensibility, when I felt like standing up and shouting at the people on stage "Shut up and spell. This is not really a reflection on William Finns music and lyrics; they are hit-and- miss, like all of his work, but the hits are palpable. It is a compliment to Rachel Sheinkin's book, though maybe a backhanded one. After all, the script and score of a musical are meant to work in perfect synergy, neither one making you miss the presence of the other. The best spoken jokes in this show are so good that it would take a Porter, a Loesser or a Sondheim to compete with them in song.

Competition happens to be what this show is about. Putnam County is a generic American small town, six of whose high-school students (all played by adult actors) have reached the local finals of the arcane national ritual so painstakingly identified in the title. Presiding over the proceedings is a former winner who has grown up to be a successful realtor, a career choice that Roberta Duchak, who plays her in this touring production, makes seem very credible. Helping her out is a school vice-principal who once had an unspecified cloud hanging over his head but claims to have got out from under it now. His job is to read out dictionary definitions of the operative words when the contestants request it, as they invariably do, and also to use those words in sentences, which he apparently makes up out of his own head. His inventions can take plodding literalism to hilariously inspired levels (Billy, put down that phylactery, we're Episcopalian"); they may not be very illuminating to prospective spellers but they certainly shine a light on the mind that came up with them. Douglas Kalbs pinched pedagogue shares the evenings comic dividends with the young finalists themselves, who are prodigies to their parents and both freaks and geeks to their peers. That's a double burden even before you factor in the pressures of the game itself. It's fun to see them wrestle with the words, especially in the initial stages when nobody gets anything wrong, even when, say, a girl with a lisp is obliged to cope with the word "cystitis. The process is varied by having four members of the audience play additional contestants, who are first flattered with easy words (would you believe, cow?) and then flattened with impossible ones. One splendid lady at the Toronto premiere temporarily derailed the show by getting her hard word right, which is certainly more than I could have done, forcing them to find another one with which to dismiss her. It was the highlight of the evening, but there is, of course, no guarantee that it will be repeated.

Back in the plot, its initially funny when the kids, too, begin to mess up and get eliminated. Chip Tolentino (Justin Keyes) successively succumbs to embarrassment, incredulity and rage when the sight of a rivals sister in the audience gives him an erection. (It doesn't help that the word he's being asked to spell is titup.") Gradually, we realize, glumly, that these young people have issues, which are illustrated in little inset scenes:Meet Logainne Schwartzandgrubenierre (Dana Steingold) whose name, a spelling bee in itself, derives from her having two live-together daddies who make irreconcilable demands on her; Olive Ostrovsky (Va- nessa Ray) with, or rather without, her self-absorbed absentee mother; and the multilingual Marcy Park (Katie Boren), trying to face down impossible parental expectations. All three performers are excellent, with Ray especially charming and Boren hitting sudden rebellion out of the ballpark, but the only character who makes adolescent angst entertaining is a touchy, though definitely not touchy-feely, fellow called William Barfee. He is overweight and spells out words with what he calls his Magic Foot: cue the choreography. Eric Roediger, who plays him with a marvellous air of injured self-absorption, is making, amazingly, his professional debut; though since in the program he thanks his wife, it must be a belated one.

As for the score, it starts with a winsomely jolly title tune, in a familiar vein, and reaches its creative and thematic peak with Olives My Friend the Dictionary. ("I love the indented border/ Every words in alphabetical order.") It has turbulent sequences that manage to be simultaneously apt and distracting, and strenuously off-kilter dramatic numbers that try hard to flesh out what the book has barely sketched in. It recalls the confessional mode of A Chorus Line, with the difference that those characters had a vocational reason for expressing themselves in song and dance, and had real desperation driving them. American musicals used to glorify winners; now they're likelier (cf. Wicked) to glorify losers, which might be a step forward if only they were prepared to dig deeper into the people with whom they profess to sympathize James Lapine, who usually does more complex pieces, directed this show that comes on oddball but ends up deep in the mainstream The laughs predominate; go for them and you'll be all right.
