The lion of this year's Shaw Festival, at least in terms of share, is not the Irish maestro but an English one. The festival is reviving, in its entirety, the cycle of 10 one-act plays that Noel Coward wrote as vehicles for himself and Gertrude Lawrence, and that were first staged in 1936 under the umbrella title Tonight at 8:30. Niagara's opening triple bill is called Brief Encounters, after the legendary movie version of the playlet originally known -- and still known here--as Still Life.

This is the piece, a classic in both media, that taught the world about English railway stations and unconsummated English adultery. (At least it's unconsummated in the film; in the play it's an open question as to how thoroughly interruptus the coitus -- offstage, of course -- actually was.) Alec, a married doctor, meets Laura, also married, while they're both changing trains, and wins her heart by removing a piece of grit from her eye. Their affair, dogged by guilt and respectability, proceeds along a passionate but unhappy track, until finally derailed. (Well, if ever a metaphor was going begging ...) All their meetings that we see take place in the station cafe, where the staff conduct comparatively carefree flirtations, in ironic contrast to the customers who frequent the place in which they serve.

Jackie Maxwell, the director, has lined this play up with two others and found links between them, beyond the obvious one that they're all concerned with social codes. In We Were Dancing, a tongue-in-cheek fantasy set on Coward's favourite imaginary island of Samolo, the actors who played Alec and Laura reappear as a couple who fall into adulterous love with similar abruptness (in their case, because they were dancing) but refuse to keep it to themselves. Friends, spouses and relatives, once over being scandalized, give the lovers their blessing, at which point they rather rapidly get over it: a brief encounter indeed, and a nice insight into what might have befallen Alec and Laura had they persisted. Finally, we have Hands Across the Sea, which isn't about infidelity but which maintains continuity by transporting us from an outpost of the empire to a place obsessed with such. Lady Maureen Gilpin (a. k. a. Piggie) and her naval husband play host in their Mayfair flat to a couple they thought they knew from Malaya. This is a farce, of mistaken identities and ceaseless pixilated phone chat, that has been known to be very funny indeed, though it isn't here.

The plays -- the first two, anyway -- look great. William Schmuck has designed a travellers' rest that manages to make institutional austerity spectacular, thanks in large part to its remorselessly revolving doors, and a dazzling tropic isle that suddenly reveals the most wittily shimmering seas you ever saw. The changes between one play and the next are pretty spectacular, too. It's what goes on between them that's the problem, especially in Still Life, where Deborah Hay and Patrick Galligan make it hard to follow what's going on between the lovers, emotionally or even factually. The play is supposed to break your heart over the lost chances and brave choices of people who, because they're British, are too decent not to be repressed. (This is a myth, but everybody believes it and it's too late now.)But there has to be some passion to be repressed, and it's barely evident in this production; Hay is too young for the bind she's supposed to be in and Galligan too uniformly nice. They both have and provide a much better time as absurd reflections of their former selves in We Were Dancing, at one point breaking spontaneously into the title, and only, song. At least that's what the text says they do. Here, as if in defeated acknowledgement that they're not Noel and Gertie, they're joined by the whole company, in what looks like a Dorothy Lamour number infected by Bollywood. It's deafeningly lip-synched--I know that sounds like a contradiction in terms, but believe me -- and it's sort of fun, but it undermines the insouciant magic of the original. To the production's credit, it does manage to restore the right mood, with the curtain rising again on everybody's having apparently forgotten what they've just been doing. That's right; there are one-and-a-half plays on either side of the intermission. Cute.

The evening's secondary couple are Corinne Koslo and Thom Marriott, she at her best as the cafe manageress in the first play, he as a bemused intruder in the last. Michael Ball makes a lovely appearance in the middle one, looking like a wounded walrus. Goldie Semple is jarringly over the top in the first instalment, blessedly so in the other two. Her sozzled indignation is almost a lifesaver for Hands Across the Sea, a piece that otherwise looks and sounds marooned on what is suddenly a big and empty stage. It even swallows up the basic story. Meanwhile, up in the skyscape we get a procession of increasingly larger airships, as if that could somehow compensate for the lack of entertainment down below.

Back in Toronto, I recommend Fear of Flight, though more for its presentation than its substance. Originating (quite some time ago) with the Artistic Fraud company of Newfoundland, this depicts a storm-tossed cross-country plane trip, with vignettes of the passengers' past lives supplied by playwrights from all points along the flight path. Only one of these (written by Daniel MacIvor, performed by Mia Mansfield as an innocent lady travelling at the behest of Jesus, who in this incarnation sounds like a con man) is especially enlivening, but it's the group effort that counts. While hanging firmly on to their individual identities, the 14 actors combine brilliantly in a kind of acted ballet to which they also provide non-stop a cappella accompaniment. Jillian Keiley's production justifies the mountainous regard in which she's held across Canada. Actually, she'd already done that, for me, with her magnificent staging of Creon, the penultimate play in Ned Dickens's City of Wine cycle. The ultimate one, too--Seven, directed by Sarah Garton Stanley -- was a fine piece of vocal and visual orchestration, with the highest overall standard of acting (by mature York students) of the entire enterprise. Both plays brought the chorus to the forefront of the action: brilliantly so in Creon, which brought the Antigone story to new life, with too much repetition but to ultimately stirring effect in Seven. Collecting these last two episodes confirmed some of my earlier preconceptions, overturned others.
