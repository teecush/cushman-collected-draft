Oprah Winfrey is on the phone, asking if she may speak to Kim, please. Happily, Kim is at home, able to take the call. She is overcome ("I can't believe this!") to hear that Oprah her very self is on the line. She is practically delirious when she learns that she has been chosen as one of the contestants on the new charity game show, Oprah's Big Give. Isn't it lucky that the TV cameras just happened to be there in Kim's living room?

There is a lot to hate about Oprah's Big Give but the hysterically faked spontaneity of that early exchange is as good a way as any of conveying the tone. Kim is among 10 people selected to compete in the matter of giving away some of Oprah's money. (Well, let's assume it's Oprah's money.)

We were never told on Sunday's opening show whether the 10 had undergone a rigorous screening process or whether their names had been picked out of a hat. But it seems likely that they were chosen on a mix of throb-appeal and sob-appeal; they included Cameron, 22, "dot-com millionaire;" Brandi, 23, pageant queen but still equipped to empathize with the less fortunate ("I've had girls sabotage my efforts to win"); Rachael, 32, singer-actress, and a reality show natural ("I experienced physical and sexual abuse"); and Carlana, 38, TV producer and paraplegic.

They were sent out into Los Angeles, in teams of two, to raise money for deserving cases. Their efforts were monitored, and at the end of the show they were assessed by three -- I think we're supposed to call them "celebrity judges": a footballer, Chris Rock's wife and Jamie Oliver.

Their job, as on any "reality" elimination show, is to thin the ranks, week by week. Also on hand, again as in any such contest, is a breathless host, Nate Berkus, to tell us at the show's half-hour mark that the big givers were now "halfway through the challenge of changing someone's life."

His commentary tended to blend into the announcements ("coming up -- the pressure is on!") designed to hold us through the commercial breaks. And, sparing in her personal appearances but spiritually present, is the fairy godmother herself, who has done some good in her time but has turned into a caricature of gush: "This is a show designed after my own heart -- the biggest giver becomes the biggest winner." Because -- something of which the competitors were apparently not apprised until the very end -- the judges' ultimate favourite gets a US$1-million prize, which may or may not be more than he or she has raised in the course of the series. So much for virtue being its own reward.

With all these levels of personality involved, it was hard to concentrate on the actual charitable objects. We and the cameras raced back and forth between the widow of a murdered man, now facing the loss of her house; the mother of a Down syndrome child who herself is running a disability centre; a destitute Iraq war vet; and an idealistic young doctor carrying US$200,000 of student debt.

They all merged into one, especially as the focus was less on their problems than on those of their instant teams of caregivers. They too were confusing, not to say confused: one pair got lost on the road, earning them much eventual scorn from the judges.

They all succeeded in raising something: from friends, neighbours and the occasional well-primed corporation or approachable movie-star. (Make Target your target! Jamie, generous like a Foxx!) And the recipients were tearfully the better for it.

That much, I guess, is a good thing. And, intentionally or not, the show does reflect some painful truths. Charity is a competitive sport; in real life, the various organizations have to compete for our compassion, and they have the advertising and the fundraisers to prove it.

It also prompts questions. What happens to all the deserving American cases who don't happen to have attracted the attention of Oprah's researchers? What kind of society makes compassion dependent on the whims of millionaire entertainers? Why is there no safety net? Why, to take the most obvious example from Big Give's big opening, have the country and its army abdicated responsibility for the soldiers of whom they profess to be so proud? Jamie Oliver asked the couple on that case why they hadn't thought of calling in the marines. He may have had more of a point than he knew.

Of course, the show itself never grapples with these questions. It's too concerned with being an American Idol for charity-volunteers, with Oliver shaping up as a nice man's Simon Cowell.

In fact, it's worse; the more unfortunate contestants on Idol are embarrassing nobody but themselves, but Big Give makes a feel-good spectacle out of other people's poverty and bereavement. The judges' favourite moment, they told us, was of children sending balloons up to heaven with a message for their dad. The inspirational music barely stopped, though it was amusing that one of the background songs told us to "be careful what you wish for." Someone may be fired for that piece of irony.

As an extra touch of mild sadism, the contestants were judged not as pairs but as individuals, so everyone would know who'd let down the team. Just one person was let go this week, her identity of course kept secret until all the others had opened their envelopes and found plane tickets inside. So, the loser was expected to walk home?

Being America, there has to be a loser; otherwise, we'd never know about the winners. They're a generous people all right; in so merciless a society, they'd better be.

- Oprah's Big Give airs Sundays at 9 p.m. ET on CTV.
