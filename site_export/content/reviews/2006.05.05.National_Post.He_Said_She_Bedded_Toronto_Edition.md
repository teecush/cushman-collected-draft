MATHILDE

Young Centre for the Performing Arts

- - -

A man and a woman face one another in bitter, bodeful silence. Then they start talking. It transpires that they are a married couple, middle-aged and well-off, and that she has just returned from serving a 15-month prison sentence. Her crime was corrupting a minor; she bedded a 15-year old boy. She is a writer, her husband is an oncologist. He seems to have spent the last few days gathering up the physical evidence of their former life, and neatly packing it. "I've become the box king," he says.

Critics have it easy. It was simple enough for me to do my own packaging job, to write "it transpires" and then lay out the relevant information. It must have been much harder for the author of Mathilde to dispense it in the form of a conversation between two people who know it all already but never once seem to be going over it again just for our benefit. My hat is off to the French playwright, Veronique Olmi, for writing an expository scene that flows naturally, easily and with suspense; she never appears to be willfully withholding her revelations but she times them all for maximum effect. And I'll keep the hat off in salutation to her Canadian translator, Morwyn Brebner, whose original plays have never satisfied me but whose actual writing skills have never been in doubt; she comes up here with dialogue that's charged with wit and freighted with both the affection and bitterness of shared experience. Mathilde, the wife, says how tired she became, in her pre-prison existence, of being "the perfect accessory for the well- to-do oncologist." The husband, Pierre, recalling how her literary friends passed the time that she was serving, recalls "the number of dinner parties your incarceration saved from tedium."

As exposition gives way to explanation, we discover that Pierre and Mathilde want one another back -- he's more explicit about it than she is -- but that they are both unwilling to let go of their respective grievances. Whether their reunion would be a good thing, or how long it might be expected to last, are questions the author necessarily leaves unanswered. (My own guess is that routine would conquer, with all the negative and positive fall-out that entails.) The tensions, apart from the extra fillip of the wife's criminal record, are familiar enough, and the characters are of the kind we often meet in upscale drama. But the author makes them fresh and presents them with a fierce concentration that seems, from this side of the Atlantic, peculiarly French.

They could indeed be archetypes, and Nightwood Theatre's very fine production (which gives the Young Centre's second space its professional baptism) presents them as such. Mathilde herself is like Jean Anouilh's idea of Antigone, a no-half-measures heroine whose revolt happens to be sexual rather than political. She says little about the actual affair (though Pierre throws in what he takes to be the telling detail -- that her teenage lover went along with the law and claimed he'd been exploited), but when she does talk about it she exults; she says that it was like discovering her body, maybe her sexuality, for the first time. And, as with Antigone, one is torn between admiring her and telling her to get a grip. Martha Burns lacks something in vocal steel, but the spirit is there; she strikes directly, sometimes frighteningly, at the spirit of non-compromise -- and at the desperation when it threatens to break down. And when she wears a white raincoat, she looks like Paris transplanted.

Tom McCamus' Pierre is her perfect rumpled complement, the pragmatist pointing out that she thinks she's got problems; he lives every day with the imminence of death, even if it's other peoples'. Affability may come easy to him, but it takes a battering. Each of them is an expert at searching out the other's tenderest points; and though the man is, on the face of it, the injured party, that only makes him better at it. Tenderness has a habit of flaring up as well; in fact, the depth and texture of a marriage are present here as they are in few other plays. (It's stretched a bit long, even at 80 minutes, but that's another thing, and in this case a minor one.) The play builds to a fitting near-climax, reflecting both their vocations. The novelist relates her feelings; the physician writes them down. The notes are read back; she doesn't believe them. Both performances occasionally fall into the forced aggression that is most actors' default mode; otherwise Kelly Thornton's direction, tightly focused on a stage sandwiched between two slices of audience, is flawless.
