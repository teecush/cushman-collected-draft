THE BOY FRIEND

Royal Alexandra Theatre, Toronto

I can never think of The Boy Friend, Sandy Wilson's "new musical comedy of the 1950s", as a parody. I suppose it qualifies as pastiche, but even that word suggests a degree of aloof calculation that hardly fits. I've said in the past that Wilson, who knocked this show off as a very young man around 1953, used the '20s style quite naturally, as if he really thought that was the only way to write musicals. His subsequent shows give the lie to that, but though some of them are interesting, none have been anything like as successful. On this show, he landed on a peculiar kind of home base, and created a peculiar kind of masterpiece.

It's a crystallization. Wilson squeezes the '20s form to its essentials, which he sees as charm, innocence and verbal and musical ingenuity. Sometimes he improves on the originals. The carefree love duet, I Could Be Happy with You, which defines the show, is far more delicate and elastic than I Want to Be Happy, the Vincent Youmans tune that must have inspired it, while Wilson's Room in Bloomsbury is at least as desirable a residence as Noel Coward's Room in Bloomsbury. Each of the songs has a teasing modulating verse to set it up, while the multiple rhymes and the air of precocious wisdom that they generate are pure '20s if not purer: "He is simply a necessity/If you want to get on/And we may as well confess it, he/ Is our sine qua non." Only Stephen Sondheim in Follies was to catch the tone so exactly, but he used it to make extra-curricular points. Wilson revels in it for its own sake.

One thing he neglected, though, was narrative. A real '20s or '30s musical comedy -- No No Nanette or Anything Goes -- has a profusion of storylines that are positively Dickensian compared to The Boy Friend which gives us boy getting, losing, and re-getting girl with the absolute minimum of complication. Love at first sight is a lovely thing; it saves so much time. There are no comic sub- plots, principally because there are no comics. One thing The Boy Friend lacks that its models had is bad jokes. It doesn't have many good ones either, but there are a few. Anyway, Polly Browne, at finishing school in the south of France, falls in love with Tony. Each thinks the other is poor, which shows how nice they are, but they're both really rich, which shows how happy they'll be if the niceness ever runs out. At one point Polly thinks Tony's a thief, on remarkably flimsy evidence, but she realizes he isn't when he turns up at the Carnival Ball wearing a Pierrot costume. Or something. The show's last act has nothing to do but postpone this reunion with a series of divertissements, most of which in this production do divert.

The Boy Friend is a hard show to get right, partly because we are now further from the '50s than the '50s were from the '20s. It's all old now, and likely to be packaged as generalized nostalgia, which was certainly not Wilson's attention. It can easily be either too quaint or too raucous. Julie Andrews, who was Broadway's first Polly and directed this revival, gets the tone mostly right, though some of the details make the teeth clench. I accept that the ensemble of Polly's friends and fellow flappers have to squeal from time to time, but they shouldn't be doing it in quotation marks. Polly herself (Jessica Grove) has to try to be sweet, which is a self- defeating exercise. Her Tony (Sean Palmer) is good on demeanour but short on personality. Wilson's devout belief in what he was doing can make us believe, too, and even care, but it doesn't happen here.

The show looks, however, great, especially when there's dancing going on. I don't think I've ever seen a bad Charleston on stage; I guess you have to do it well to do it at all. But the two routines here -- the first a pas de deux, the second a pas d'everybody -- are exceptionally knock-out. John DeLuca is the choreographer; Andrea Chamberlain (Maisie) sets them alight as she does everything else she touches, even the dialogue. Rick Faugno, her vis-a-vis, is no great actor but his feet hardly touch the ground. The still-great Tony Walton has designed a second-act set that brings the Riviera to refulgent carnival life, and the still-great Richard Pilbrow has lit it superbly; he ends one night-time number with a positive sunburst, but nobody said musicals were realistic. Paul Carlin is charmingly embarrassed as a reluctant older swain; Nancy Hess as the head mistress (that's one of the jokes) who ensnares him is insufficiently formidable but can certainly sing. A great production of The Boy Friend (the best I've seen was a London revival directed by the author) can be both intoxicating and enchanting. In this one, you settle for intoxication.
