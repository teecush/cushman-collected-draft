Critics cool to Old Vics Blondel

Friday, November 11, 1983

ROBERT CUSHMAN

London ENGLAND -- BY ROBERT CUSHMAN LONDON THERE HAS been unanimous praise from the British press for Ed Mirvish's renovation of the historic Old Vic Theatre. The theatre critic of The Times, after recalling how dowdy the place used to look, says that it is now a superb compromise between Victorian restoration and modern open- plan design." The openness is particularly noticeable in the lobbies, which used to be horribly cramped; though the most spacious design in the world could not have afforded much space to the crowds who turned up for the press night of Mr. Mirvish's first offering.

This is Blondel, a comedy musical with lyrics by Tim Rice (of Jesus Christ Superstar and Evita) and music by Stephen Oliver, previously known for operas and incidental music for classical plays. It is based on the legend of a twelfth-century minstrel, the loyal friend of the Crusader king, Richard the Lion Heart. When Richard was captured by an Austrian duke, Blondel is supposed to have sung and strummed his lute under the window of every prison in central Europe to discover which one held his beloved monarch. Finally he located him, thereby greatly speeding up the ransoming process. In Mr. Rices version of this historically dubious story, Blondel personally rescues Richard, with the help of a persistent girl-friend.

As well as being a hero, Blondel is something of a clown. He is also an aspiring medieval pop-star with his own three-woman back-up group the Blondettes. He is in search not only of his king but of that elusive number one hit madrigal; and finally, thanks to hard-earned royal patronage, he gets it.

The critics were far cooler toward the show than toward the theatre. Its unceasing jokiness especially depressed The Guardian ("not much more than revolving medieval wallpaper) and The Daily Mail (tawdry showbiz pastiche"). Others were kinder, The Financial Times summing it up as a "a slight, good-natured romp that nonetheless fails to hit the heights. That is accurate, but still a little ungenerous. The show is often very funny, and this quality carries it successfully into its second half where both the story and the anachronistic jokes begin to wear thin. Everybody has noted the shows resemblance to Joseph and the Amazing Technicolor Dreamcoat, Tim Rice's first success with Andrew Lloyd Webber. Both are deceptively guileless entertainments about innocent but victorious young men, decked up in jaunty tunes and lyrics. But this is by far the most appealing side of Rice's talent. He obviously loves jokes, and he is good at them; even though he sometimes has trouble fitting his relentlessly ingenious lyrics into the musical pattern.

The tone is established from the start when a quartet of close-harmony monks inform us that in 1189 nobody thought of the king as Richard I because, of course, they had never heard of Richard II. If you laugh at that - as I did, heartily - you will enjoy much of what follows: Blondels lament that he nearly made it to the charts with Send in the Jesters or the wicked Prince Johns enthusiastic demonstration of the limitless opportunities his name presents to an ambitious rhymester. (Where, he asks triumphantly, is a rhyme for Richard? Mr. Rice finds a few.) Less sucessful is some crude anti-Thatcher satire entrusted to Blondels beloved; indeed the love-interest is pretty much of a liability throughout, since it is difficult to care about the emotions of a couple of cartoons. They do, though, get to sing a charming duet The Least of My Troubles that represents the most of Stephen Oliver. Elsewhere his music has plenty of wit and taste, but lacks theatrical size: it has, in fact, the opposite qualities to Andrew Lloyd Webber's.

The show has been given very smart direction by Peter James on a glowing and ingenious set by Tim Goodchild that combines medieval imagery with modern technology. (Not unlike the new Old Vic itself if you substitute Victorian" for "medieval.") The cast is mainly composed of young veterans from London's most successful recent musicals - Cats and the National Theatre's Guys and Dolls. Paul Nicholas sings his heart out, middle-of-the-road style, in the title role, but most impact is made by two villains: David Burt as the vainglorious John and Chris Langham as an Edwardian-looking assassin whom he hires to kill the king though the despeardo, knowing his limitations, is far keener to murder the minstrel.

These two benefit from actually being allowed to talk for short periods. Otherwise this show, like most others by Rice and/or Webber, is entirely sung. And that, though nobody has pointed it out, is one of its main weaknesses. It is very hard to be funny to music for a whole evening. Trying to bring it off is a strain on the writers; trying to take it in is a strain on the audience. Which may explain why, despite much evidence of enjoyment on the way through, the reception at the final curtain fell short of the hysteria that is traditional on musical first nights, and was surely to be doubly expected on such an opening as this. Mr. Cushman is drama critic for The London Observer.
