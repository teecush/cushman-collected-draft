It's no great surprise that The List, by Jennifer (fated to be known as "no relation") Tremblay, should have won a Governor General's Award before even being staged. The play is a monologue, and monologues read better, or at least easier, than texts with a multiplicity of voices. It deals with, or at least invokes, serious-sounding subjects like loneliness and alienation. It is, in fact, competently written. It is also, in its Englishlanguage premiere -- a joint venture by CanadianStage and Nightwood Theatre -- competently presented. It still seems pointless. Or, funnily enough, listless.

I don't mean the play itself has no point. I mean there is no particular reward for the audience in sitting through it. A character billed as The Woman confronts us from her kitchen. She's a wife, and the mother of three, who has moved from the city to the country in the mistaken belief that this would let her spend more time with her husband. She didn't count on his having to commute. So she stays home and cooks and keeps house and tends to her children; she also makes, and compulsively recites, lists of all the tasks that this entails. She looks down on her bumpkin neighbours, and for that reason hardly ever leaves the house.

The one time she did, somebody got under her guard: another wife and mother, named Caroline, whose life and home were as messy as The Woman's are neurotically neat. They became unlikely friends. Caroline became ill, and asked The Woman to do something for her and put it on her list. The Woman, it seems, obliged with the list-making part but never got around to the doing. Caroline died in hospital. The Woman can't shake -- doesn't seem to want to shake -- the feeling that it was her fault.

So this might be a play about a broken trust (file under Guilt) or it might be about domestic self-exploitation (file under Control-Freakiness and Agoraphobia). Obviously it wants to be about both. But the causal link between The Woman's forgetfulness and Caroline's death is of the thinnest, so we merely wonder why the protagonist is beating up on herself and on us.

Thin, too, is the connection between this and the rest of the play. There is some grim humour to be found in The Woman's repeated recitation of her daily agenda, and Allegra Fulton, whose performance is technically flawless, expertly mines it. She does the same for the painful suspense, and even more painful certainty, surrounding the discovery and the aftermath of Caroline's death.

The play makes an efficient enough job of the nuts-and-bolts narrative. There are some puzzling things, though, about Kelly Thornton's production. The gleaming kitchen that Denyse Karn has designed is enviably, even superhumanly, scrapless and spotless. Nothing disturbs its purity but apples, which The Woman is mechanically slicing at curtain-rise. At a later point, she opens the fridge to disclose more apples, rows and rows of them. And that's all, apart from a profusion of identical jars, also in rows, that may very well contain apple sauce. If this display is to be taken literally, it suggests that The Woman's family is in imminent danger of succumbing to malnutrition: which would seem at odds with everything else in the play. Or maybe it's meant to be symbolic, an image for the monotony and repetition of her life. There seem here to be at least three possible interpretations of the old adage about a woman's work never being done.

Or The Woman's work. Calling her that reads, as such labels always do, like an attempt to blackmail the audience into feeling guilty about her situation, while absolving the author of the responsibility to get specific. It also feels like cheating: She can hardly be Everywoman if she has a friend who actually has a name; and if Caroline could get christened, then why couldn't she?

The program reveals that the author is working on a sequel -- something to which playwrights called Tremblay seem especially prone -- that will show her heroine in later life. It's to be called The Merry-go-round, and I can't say that my heart is racing. It's hard to get excited over the further adventures of somebody to whom you haven't been introduced in the first place.
