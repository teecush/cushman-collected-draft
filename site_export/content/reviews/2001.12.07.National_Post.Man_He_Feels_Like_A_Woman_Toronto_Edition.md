REAL LIVE GIRL

Buddies in Bad Times Theatre, Toronto

One for the record books: Real Live Girl is a musical whose title song is never sung. At least, I assume the show is named after the hit number from the 1960s musical Little Me: "Pardon me, miss, but I've never done this with a real live girl." It was written, obviously, to be sung by a man. Damien Atkins, author and performer of the current entertainment, is a man but he specializes here in songs written for women. He uses them to punctuate a first-person narrative about his struggle to express and acknowledge what he calls his inner girl.

Atkins, whose play Good Mother was produced at Stratford this year, is a clever writer and a more than clever actor-singer, but his new show suffers from an acute case of double focus.

The spoken and sung parts lead separate lives, though both have their attractions. Damien -- he uses his own name -- talks about the problems of growing up not only gay but as a girlie-boy. He offers a very funny montage of interviews with a succession of guidance counsellors ranging from smotheringly sympathetic to murderously hostile. (The latter counsellor, imported from England and hating it, tells him, somewhere around Grade Seven, "You've got to be prepared for the life of abject misery that lies ahead of you.") He tells us that at the age of 13 he could do a great Ethel Merman impersonation, but he doesn't let us hear it. In his later teens he preferred to hang out with girls, and he offers lightning vocal sketches of a few, though it's never clear whether he is presenting actual girls or just the ones in his head.

Quite early on he sings, with defiant petulance, I'm Gonna Wash That Man Right Out of My Hair. The show's publicity says he sings it as a reaction to a schoolyard bully (or at least as the reaction he wishes he'd had) but without that advance information I doubt I would have made the connection. The lyrics do not really fit the situation, and as the song progresses -- at some length, and incorporating sections that never made it into the South Pacific movie -- it pulls right away from its supposed context.

Another Rodgers and Hammerstein number, I Enjoy Being a Girl, has a kind of generic appropriateness. It has become, according to current political canons, a song that only men can sing, though, of course, they usually do it in drag. Atkins does not do the song in drag; he identifies with how women think and feel rather than with how they dress, and he stays in his own clothes throughout. At one point he goes into a kind of vocal drag, offering a series of quick- fire movie-star impressions, but he generally avoids the often cruel mockery that usually underlies the genre. He does not imitate specific performers, choosing instead to sing the songs for their own values. I would still rather hear them sung by real live girls but he does them very well.

Most of the selections are lesser-known show tunes. They include Craig Carnelia's moving Just a Housewife (from Working) and his more calculated but still effective Home Coming Queen (from Is There Life After High School); Just One Step, an overlong but interesting piece of vengeful desperation, from Jason Robert Brown's Songs for a New World; and I Had Myself a True Love, a superb Harold Arlen-Johnny Mercer number from St. Louis Woman. This last, a blues-opera lament, really needs more burnished tones Atkins' gently-pointed tenor allows, but he performs it skilfully, as he does everything he sings, his body language economically in sync with his voice.

But the songs, however much he protests to the contrary, tell us nothing about him except he has generally good taste. Each is a mask pulled tight over whatever may be the central Damien. (All right; they do tell us he's an actor.) I don't object to this, as I think there is far too much self-indulgent soul baring going on in the theatre; I merely point it out. And of course we cannot know if Atkins is really speaking for himself or if he has just invented a persona and given it his own name. When he talks about his macho father or when he speaks as his mother or his sister, we wonder if he is offering an accurate picture of his family, and what they think about it if he is.

David Oiye directed; John Hughes is the pianist. The show fits agreeably, if a little oddly, into its own self-created niche between drama and cabaret.
