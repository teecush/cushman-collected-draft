THEATRE REVIEW

Next Stage Theatre Festival Factory Theatre, Toronto

This years Next Stage Theatre Festival, spread across three spaces at the Factory Theatre, is a festival of love. Four shows, out of a lineup of 10, have that word or a derivative of it in their titles, and of course it crops up as a subject in several more. Last week I wrote about a couple of these love stories that were, in two senses, straight: heterosexual and non-musical. Today we have some that are gay, with songs.

Living with Henry, a musical by Christopher Wilson, is about living with HIV. Henry, despite a pointless trick opening that leaves the audience confused for the next 10 minutes, is not the sufferer; he's a personification of the virus - they have the same initials - and he leers around the stage, alternately taunting and encouraging the real protagonist, Michael. It's a clumsy device that underlines the shows main failing; it lurches from one scene to the next, grabbing at instant emotional effects without establishing any kind of centre.

Illness of any kind is a perilous subject for a play, let alone a musical, because it turns the central character into a victim; you can feel for him but not with him. Michael, played as resourcefully as possible by Ryan Kelly, keeps saying that he doesnt want to be defined by his condition, but the show automatically defines him that way, despite showing him with two successive live-in partners, the first dangerously feckless, the second angelically supportive.

Other prominent characters are Michael's best female friend and his mother, who greets his diagnosis with amazing composure (most moms freak when their offspring so much as sneeze) but otherwise acts and reacts totally on cue. So does everyone; the script never brings the characters to any kind of spontaneous life and the songs, with pedestrian lyrics and anemic soft-rock music, keep labouring the obvious. There are maybe two moments at which singing and staging make a point more powerfully than dialogue could: Michael's realization that hes infected and the conclusion of a bathhouse tango at which hes left alone. Otherwise there seems no reason for this piece to be a musical.

In Loving the Stranger or How to Recognize an Invert, Hume Baugh gives a remarkable performance as Peter Flinsch, an artist who grew up in Germany in the 1930s, joined the Hitler Youth and the army because he had no choice, and was court-martialled and imprisoned for kissing another man. As a soldier he was spared the worst the Nazis could do to gays, and after the war he emigrated to Canada.

His story, as told in interviews with this show's authordirector Alistair Newton, is the backbone of what aspires to be a political-historical cabaret; and Baugh's performance is notable for avoiding all the usual pitfalls of the actor as raisonneur: He neither under-acts nor overstresses. With Flinsch (who died in 2010) as quiet and thoughtful linchpin, noting the remarkable advances made in his long lifetime, the production juxtaposes Germany in the first half of the 20th century with North America now.

From the former, we have some sinister pseudo-scientific documents, one of which gives the show its subtitle, contrasted with some startlingly outspoken 1920s gay anthems composed by Mischa Spoliansky, who later wrote music for very mainstream British movies. From the latter we have Proposition 8 and a family values speech by Stephen Harper, after which we get a rendition of Deutschland Uber Alles. Thats a pretty silly connection, unless you believe that Canada is planning world domination.

The show, like many before it, latches enviously on to the mythical spirit of Berlin cabaret. Gosh, wasnt it decadent? Gee, wasn't it political? It's a legend that these days owes less to the actual German repertoire than to the Kander and Ebb musical Cabaret, especially in its recent and more hysterical revivals. True to form this show ends by hitching a ride with Cabarets title song, performed with a genderaltered lyric. A reverse stereotype is still a stereotype.

Theres more of the same in Love Is a Poverty You Can Sell, a cabaret homage to the music and musical influence of Kurt Weill, that's hosted by a giggly duo who might be Cabaret's emcee amoebically split. Apart from being strenuously unfunny, they take up time that could easily be occupied by a couple more songs. Thats an issue, since the half-hour show is a cut-down version of a provocative musical idea.

Weills inheritors turn out to include not only Kander and Ebb but Sondheim, whose Sweeney Todd emerges as a cousin in cutlery to Mack the Knife. The performers are mostly too callow and insecure for the material but there is a lovely duet version of September Song, from Weill's Broadway period, its message running serenely counter to the narrators' description of love as "the lack of a healthy solitude."

Thats a definition that would be hotly contested by the heroine of Modern Love, a glorious show written and performed by Jessica Moss. She doesnt find solitude healthy; she finds it debilitating. With 660 friends on Facebook and none anywhere else she despairs of finding a connection. Only occasionally overdoing the franticness, Moss or her alter ego navigates herself and us through the wilderness of online dating, or online anything, including a bait-and-switch cyber dialogue with one Charlie Brown, who seems to be a good man. She herself signs in as Funny Girl; and though both names would surely be already taken, she more than justifies her own, in technique and observation and thoroughgoing appeal.

It is of course impossible, despite her confessed pudginess, to believe that she can't get a date. An unassailable theatrical truth is that anyone who can hold an audience on her own for five minutes, let alone 60, is by definition attractive.

The Next Stage Festival runs until Jan. 15. For more information, visit fringetoronto.com.
