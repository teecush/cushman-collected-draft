Brian Bedford's intelligence and skill, the affability of his manner and the sharpness of his wit, should be familiar by now. But they're so exceptional, while seeming so deceptively easy, that they take you by surprise every time. It may be that we don't take them for granted because he doesn't; he's on his mettle every time, super-relaxed but never complacent. Recently, too, he's shown an enriching new quality, one that I don't remember being much evident in his earlier work: compassion. It was movingly evident in his King Lear a couple of years ago, and it shines out in his solo performance Ever Yours, Oscar, an assemblage of the letters of Oscar Wilde.

One in particular shows a side of its subject as well as its performer with which we haven't been too familiar: social concern. It's a letter to a newspaper, written after Wilde's release from Reading Gaol. It's a response to a report of the dismissal of a warder in the same prisonfor having slippeda sweet biscuit to a hungry child prisoner. It moves outwards from there to denounce the iniquity of locking up children of 14 and younger, let alone placing them in solitary confinement and feeding them a diet guaranteed to make them ill. It's very detailed, very measured and very contained. There's obviously anger underneath, but it's phrased as humane bewilderment. (Listen to Bedford as he painstakingly contrasts a child's uncomprehending attitude to being punished by "the system" with an adult's.) It's also, in contrast to some of what we thought we knew about Wilde, selfless. He doesn't complain about his own treatment at the hands of the Reading Gaolers; he goes out of his way to describe some of them as humane. They too are victims of the system. It's worth remembering, though neither the linking material nor Bedford's passionately reasonable delivery stresses the point, that Wilde himself was sick at the time he wrote the letter. He didn't have to take the time.

If that is the show's emotional high point, its comic counterpart is a letter sent by an Oscar on his uppers to the actor/manager George Alexander, asking for an advance on a new play that was to become The Importance of Being Earnest. He supplies a plot outline which, it's suggested in the introduction, he probably made up as he went along, and it's fascinating to hear how much was preserved from this improvization and how much altered or enlarged. There is, shockingly, no Ernest and hence no thematic earnestness in this version, but there is a Prism: also, the delightful last-minute suggestion that a clergyman should be brought in as her partner. Of course you have to know The Importance fairly well to get the underlying joke, but for this year's Stratford audience that shouldn't be a problem. See Bedford in a dress as Lady Bracknell on other nights, and in a suit as himself here.

Ever Yours, Oscar has been devised by Peter Wylde; Bedford directs himself. It doesn't pretend to be a play, and it's fragmentary even as a recital. Bedford reads or speaks at a music stand, placed about three quarters of the way upstage (anyone sitting behind in this three-handed space will have only his back to look at) with a large portrait of Wilde hanging on the back wall. In between

epistles, he delivers snippets of basic biography. The Sturm und Drang of Wilde's life are given bare-bones treatment; we hear very little of, or to, Bosie, though what we do hear makes him sound more appalling than ever. There's nothing from the anguished De Profundis, technically a letter but hardly an informal one. Basically, the show gives us sidelights, mainly delightful ones; ranging from the young Oscar admonishing his mother on what to send him at prep school to the enfant terrible Oscar on his lecture tour of the United States, instructing silver miners on the subject of modern art. (They hated the art, loved the artist -- i. e. him.) In short --and it is short -- this is not a momentous evening or, preferably, afternoon. But it offers insights, and one of our very finest actors being very fine. - Ever Yours, Oscar is in repertory through Aug. 29.
