GHOST TRAIN

Young Peoples Theatre, Toronto

"Every picture tells a story, doesn't it?" says the narrator at the end of Ghost Train, taking the curse off an ancient platitude by turning it sweetly into a question. The answer in this case has to be a delighted yes. The pictures in this show are stunning; not only does each tell a tale of its own but together they make a saga.

They do this with no more than token support from the text. The ostensible subject matter of Ghost Train is serendipitously the same as that of the new opera Iron Road: the building of the British Columbia section of the Canadian Pacific Railway by immigrant workers from China.

Betty Quan's script, based on a book by Paul Yee, tells this story from the point of view of Choon-Yi, a one-armed Chinese girl with an artistic gift, whose father leaves their village for Canada, promising to send for his daughter at a later date. When Choon-Yi is 16, the money for her passage arrives and she embarks on a long and nightmarish ocean crossing, packed tightly with other passengers in the hold of a steamship. When she arrives in Salt Water City, otherwise known as Vancouver, her father is nowhere to be found.

The rest of the play details her quest for him, which reaches its conclusion when, after several abortive attempts, she manages to paint a satisfactory picture of the "first car" -- the train to which her father and his compatriots have dedicated their labour and their lives. The completed picture turns out to have magical properties, but the mysticism is scrappy and unsatisfying. It's preferable, though, to the recurrent vignettes of life back in the village, scenes whose gossipy humour is extremely forced and -- given that it's directed at children -- patronizing.

The play's main flaw, though, is its failure to tell us much about the building of the railroad. The underlying assumption is that the Chinese workers were shamefully exploited. I can well believe it, but it isn't dramatized. Certainly, working conditions are shown to be tough, but what else would you expect on a major construction project in the middle of the 19th century? No evidence is presented that the Chinese labourers were worked harder or paid worse than anyone else; it's just taken as read. (And a sloppy comparison is made in the accompanying study guide between desperate Third World immigrants now, and these railwaymen, who at least had a definite job to go to.)

When we do get to see Choon-Yi's father at work, he and his companions seem rather cheerful, despite the dangers, and they show no signs of resentment. This is meant to be a key scene, or at any rate it's given pride of place at the end of the first act: a mystifying decision, since it suggests that the play is really not about Choon-Yi but about her father, though nothing else in the play supports that. The focus is otherwise all on her, and Karen Ancheta comes through with a determined and (at least on Tuesday morning) winningly throaty performance.

She gets sympathetic, if sometimes smilingly bland, support from Laurence Tan as her dad, from Denis Akiyama as the narrator, and from Richard Lee as a young restaurateur who befriends and woos our heroine and who has a second career as a carnival clown.

The only occidental character, seen in silhouette, is a ticket collector who gives Choon-Yi a hard time when she tries, appropriately enough, to search for her father by train. It's possible to mount a pungent drama about racism without letting us meet any of the racists, but it presents problems and they have not been solved or even, apparently, recognized.

Pierre Tetrault, artistic director of Young Peoples Theatre, is credited on this play with direction and dramaturgy, and his achievement in the first capacity far exceeds that in the second.

Donald Quan has composed a haunting musical score, though some occasional songs seem a needless concession to accepted conventions of children's theatre. (They also, in the China scenes, sound disconcertingly Western, even Wild Western; at one point, I expected the Magnificent Seven to gallop over the soundtrack.)

Glen Charles Landry is the set designer, but an additional phalanx of names is credited with visual creations, visual creations and visual art. That may seem like overkill but the results are inspired. At first, it seems that the production will be backed by slides: handsome but predictable. Then we notice that even if the pictures aren't moving, the camera is, pulling in and out as in a TV documentary.

In fact, there is a film running behind the play, but without upstaging it; rather enriching it with superb vistas of authentic places and faces. Three panels divide much of the acting area, each able to bear another set of pictures that can be faded in and out in time with the actors' movements. Shadowy scenes unfold within these compartments, especially useful for staging close-packed journeys by sea or rail. At one point, the train appears to be moving, because the film projected above it is moving, but in the opposite direction.

This is superb staging. It's enough to give "mixed media," that ghastly phrase for a usually ghastly concept, a good name.
