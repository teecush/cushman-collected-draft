Jessica Moss made a terrific debut as writer-performer of the hilarious Modern Love and somewhat cemented it in Was Spring, Tarragons puzzlingly titled minor MacIvor.

Megan Follows and Kelli Fox in The Penelopiad - the standouts and figureheads in an extraordinary female ensemble, with specially honourable mention for Pat Hamilton and for Pamela Sinha, who after her delectably self-obsessed Helen went on to do wrenching work as author-actress of Crash. Nightwood Theatre was the company of the year, following The Penelopiad with The Happy Woman and the hypnotically tense Between the Sheets. All three were directed by Kelly Thornton.

Everyone in The Golden Dragon, but especially David Fox, who as a flight attendant called Barbie was a scream, even in a play (German) and production (Ross Manson) built on age and gender reversal. The opening shot in a (mostly) exceptional year for Tarragon.

Paul Sun-Hyung Lee in Kim's Convenience - star representative of another stellar ensemble. Ins Choi's play, a candidate for best of the year, was certainly the warmest. A departure for Soulpepper, which generally does revivals; though since it had previously been done on the fringe, it maybe qualifies. And it was freshly directed by Weyni Mengesha.

Ronnie Burkett, actor-writer-puppeteer, in Penny Plain - this all-purpose Armageddon wasnt one of his major works, but hes still a multiple threat in everything he does. It was also the most notable thing to happen this year at Factory, unless you count the theatre board's extraordinarily clumsy dismissal of founder-director Ken Gass.

Arlene Duncan in Caroline or Change, which joined the forces and specialties of Obsidian (race) and Acting UpStage (genre). It was, hands down, the year's best musical - writing, staging (Robert McQueen), performance - additional special mention for young Michael Levinson and for the self-transforming Deborah Hay, later to score a minor Stratford triumph in Henry V and a major one in Much Ado About Nothing.

Theodore Bikel - not just for Visiting Mr. Green (actually he was Mr. Green), but for showing us how at 87.

Adam Paolozza in TheDouble, his own Dostoyesky adaptation. Pure theatre, including the musical contribution of Arif Miroldolbaghi, who, also for the first time, acted. From TheatreRUN, one of the small companies that made so much of this years running.

Nancy Palk in Long Days Journey into Night, heartrendingly completing a remarkable run of drama's legendary mothers. She was later delightful in You Can't Take It with You (another mother)andwonderfulinTheCrucible (a grandmother). Fine work too from Joseph Ziegler, Evan Buliung (as later in Bloodless) and Gregory Prest (as in, it seemed, just about everything - one of Soulpeppers finest finds).

Amy Nostbakken, Canadian-born and British-based, in The Big Smoke, a breakdown in a capella, and a revelation.

Eric Peterson - ideally scrappy and cranky in Seeds, impressive docudrama, ideally meek, cranky and garbage-canned in Endgame.

Oliver Dennis (eternally reliable) and Michael Hanrahan (who just gets better and better) in High Life and then again in Home, where they played a lovely duet.

Natasha Greenblatt - playing a boy in David Craigs The Neverending Story (YPT).

Nicole Underhay in The Small Room at the Top of the Stairs - superb play, production (Mengesha again) and performances - everyone, with Sarah Dodd (also in The Penelopiad) being outstandingly witty. Later at the Shaw, Underhay was defeated by the unplayable protagonist of The Millionairess, but victorious in His Girl Friday.

Maev Beaty Quality meets quantity: The Penelopiad (in a beard), The Happy Woman (knockout), Proud (even more so), Terminus (which I couldn't review, for family reasons, but in which she was spectacular).

Kawa Ada, a bright spot in My Granny the Goldfish; Clare Coulter, whose opening monologue in Was Spring held out unfulfilled promise.

Michael Healey in Clybourne Park - this, and his later performance in his own Proud, may be the most impressive acting he's ever done. Clybourne Park was an all-round fine production of a crackling, mercifully unsentimental, American play.

Petrina Bromley - fine in the soggy Newfoundland import Oil and Water.

Trish Lindstrom - redeeming feature of CanStages The Game of Love and Chance, and good later in Bloodless, in which I confused her with Jan Alexandra Smith: Sorry, they were both good. Peter Mooney and Janet Porter in Gruesome Playground Injuries - another fine, off-the-wall American play, another invaluable independent company (BirdLand),

Jane Spidell in The Real World? - uneven production of Michel Tremblays best play?

Brenda Robins and Maria Vacratsis - the other duet in Home. Albert Schultz, who didn't act this year, directed Soulpepper's best new productions, this and The Crucible, and also brought back Death of a Salesman. But is Soulpepper ever again going to give us a play written before 1900?

Ben Carlson in Much Ado - he and Hay (see above) dug deep into Bene-dick and Beatrice. Stratfords best all-round productions though were, by remarkably unanimous consent, Cymbeline, The Matchmaker and Elektra. Id add Wanderlust, though many wouldn't.

Kyle Blair in Stratford musicals; Martin Happer, jolly good chap at the Shaw.

Corinne Koslo and Ric Reid, heartbreaking couple in Come Back Little Sheba, the Shaw's best show, closely followed by Hedda Gabler. Honourable mention for Present Laughter, especially Steven Sutcliffe. For consistent distinction in the ranks: Gray Powell, Graeme Somerville, Patrick McMaus, Mary Haney, Kevin Bundy.

Stuart Hughes, magnificent in The Crucible. Ari Cohen in Speed-the-Plow, the revived Salesman and, at Tarragon, The Little Years.

Between the Sheets, Jordi Mands playwriting debut, apart from being maybe the years best new script, was also a riveting acting duet for Susan Coyne and the now consistently magnetic Christine Horne. And here's another duo: David Ferry and Michele Monteith in The Lesson.

Tear the Curtain, Kim Collier's production from Vancouver, gave mixed media a good name, and had acting, too (especially from Dawn Petten.) Seems like all the good stuff at Canadian Stage was a partnership with someone. Witness Company Theatres Speaking in Tongues, from the fascinating Australian dramatist Andrew Bovell. Cast were a fine fivesome, with Richard Clarkin maybe best.

Kaylee Harwood, two roles in Bloodless, so maybe counting as a multiple. As does, in spades, Melody Johnson as her own Miss Caledonia (and friends).

Christopher Sieber in La Cage aux Folles, best male performance in a musical, no contest. The show stood up surprisingly well, best in Mirvishs succession of fluffy imports. Their prize production of course was War Horse, but you didn't go to that for the acting. But don't forget that at the beginning of the year they brought us The Blue Dragon, prime Robert Lepage. You didnt go to that for the acting either, but it was good.
