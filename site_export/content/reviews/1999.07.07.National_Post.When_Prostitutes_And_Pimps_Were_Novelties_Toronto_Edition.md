TORONTO FRINGE FESTIVAL

It's a funny old world we live in, and it's been that way for quite some time. That seems to be the message of this year's Toronto Fringe Festival, though admittedly these conclusions are based on a sampling (three shows out of more than 90) that lacks something in scientific rigour.

Back in 1978, apparently, the whole town was talking about The Jones Boy, a play by Tom Walmsley that, in revival, looks as if it were written yesterday. That is not necessarily a compliment. It takes a stern effort of historical imagination to realize that in its day this was not just another despairing play about pimps and prostitutes caught in a spiral of violence and addiction; it was, perhaps, the first -- the first in Canada anyway.

Twenty years on, it might still pack a fair punch in a suitably claustrophobic setting. For now, though, it's been deposited on the stage of a movie theatre (the Bloor Cinema, 506 Bloor Street West), a space that amounts to a narrow strip of platform confronting a cavernous auditorium.

Of the two male leads, one (Adrian Churchill) hardly concerns himself with anything as mundane as projection; the other (Shaun Smyth) makes only a weaselly kind of impact. They play a pair of junkies -- one is a bully disguised as a weakling, the other vice versa -- living off their female partners. Both of them are foredoomed victims, and neither writing nor performance can escape the dreary determinism of it all. (It's certainly true to life, but that's no consolation.)

The girls have more individuality, not to mention jobs that get them out of the house, and, therefore, more interest. One (Deborah Hay) has the tough resilience that comes with a narrow focus; the other (Brooke Johnson) has a tremulous sensitivity suggesting the kind of hooked hooker that the young Judy Garland might have played, had MGM ever countenanced such a thing.

The estimable Tom McCamus makes what amounts to a guest-star appearance as a falling-down-drunk client. (At some performances, this role is played by the equally estimable David Fox). His sprawling resentment sets off a chain reaction that results in the death of one character and the terminal desolation of most of the others.

The violence is random, but in retrospect it seems inevitable; the author has shown it built into every facet of the characters' lives and relationships.

Structurally, the play is an achievement, but it remains a genre piece, even if it invented the genre. It's being touted as a Canadian classic, but in the Canadian theatre, classic status comes cheap. It's a title bestowed on any play that makes it into a second production.

By that criterion, Mysterium Tremendum (at the Poor Alex, 296 Brunswick Ave.) will probably be a classic by this time next year.

It's written by Anton Piatigorsky and directed by Chris Abraham, who took home sacks of Dora Awards for their previous collaboration, Easy Lenny.

The new one is a piece of apocalyptic whimsy that taps right into our current discontents and may even start a few of its own. A serial killer is at large in the city (Which city? Don't ask.) and he strikes sometimes with a gun, sometimes with a knife, which in this production is the size of a sword; this is not your naturalistic drama. His victims include a priest, a young man who eats excrement and his understandably disenchanted girlfriend, a couple of street people and the detectives who are on the case. They make frequent reappearances after their deaths, sometimes escorted by a creepy celestial pair known as the Bald Archangels, who eventually cart everyone off to heaven: everyone, that is, except the two homeless.

The killer, meanwhile, is a yuppie lawyer; nobody can say this play isn't loaded with social comment. It also takes a broader view: A photographer wanders through the action, reflecting on the atrocious scenes she has encountered all over the world and, indeed, all down the ages, as far back as the attack on Troy.

The more you consider the piece the more meretricious and derivative it seems; but it goes with an undeniable swing, propelled by snappy staging, a few genuinely funny lines, and a couple of excellent performances.

Michael Waller is very smooth as the murderer; Jim Jones is beautifully witty as a pained sensitive Jewish cop, the first one I recall since George Segal in No Way To Treat a Lady.

A yuppie and an artist come into further conflict in Hot August Night (at the Helen Gardiner Phelan Playhouse, 79A St. George St.) They are brother and sister, reunited at a morgue to identify their father's body. They express their mingled love and resentment of one another and of their parents, sometimes by means of flashbacks. These demonstrate that people with tedious presents are likely to have had equally tedious pasts.

The intended tone seems to be mordant banter concealing unspeakable pain but relieved by tenderness; the actors cruise through it with their autopilots unwaveringly set to "sensitive."

One of them is the play's author, Teresa Pavlinek, who has given herself an opening monologue that promises better than what follows. The other, Jonathan Wilson, is also an author, though not of this play; he wrote last season's promising Kilt. He is additionally credited with "additional wisdom," which seems to me to speak unintended volumes.
