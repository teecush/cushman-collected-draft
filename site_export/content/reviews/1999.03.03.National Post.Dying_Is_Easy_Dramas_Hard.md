For thirteen years now, Alberta Theatre Projects, one of the two resident theatre companies based in the Calgary Centre for the Performing Arts, has presented a winter festival of new Canadian plays under the banner Pan Canadian PlayRites. The shows are presented in repertory at ATP's Martha Cohen Theatre, a golden, welcoming auditorium surrounding a modestly sized but insistent thrust stage. This year's acting ensemble was solid, if not spectacular; the overall ambience could hardly have been warmer.

It could be savoured full-strength at the concluding Blitz Weekend, offering visitors, as well as locals who had been saving themselves, a chance to be bombarded by the entire program: four full-length plays in two crowded days. It's probably the best way to see them; the event takes over and stops you brooding too obsessively on individual merits. On this year's evidence, that's just as well.

The most interesting, and by far the most intelligent, was Richard Sanger's Two Words for Snow. This gave centre stage to two historical figures last seen in the theatre playing walk-on roles in Ragtime: Admiral Robert Peary, who may or may not have been the first man to reach the North Pole in 1909; and his black assistant Matthew Henson, who may or may not have reached it with or without him.

The play raises these multiple doubts but not does resolve them. Though there are many flashbacks to the expedition, the play is anchored in 1936. Peary is dead, but his son pays a visit to the American Museum of Natural History where an aged Henson is now employed as a kind of living relic; he is also a research resource for German-Jewish anthropologist Franz Boas in his studies of the Inuit culture and language.

Henson is not the first Arctic veteran to be put on show here; forty years earlier, Peary had brought back half a dozen Inuit and placed them in this same Eskimo Room, and they had died in the New York cold. A hierarchy of racial exploitation is suggested, criss- crossed by tentative moments of contact, intellectual or erotic. Peary and Henson have clashed over an Inuit woman, whom Henson accuses Peary of stealing from him. The most piercing moment in the play comes when old Henson hears that she bore him a son: a black man growing up in the tundra, though a little less black than himself. The same note is more mordantly sounded when Henson is told "the North Pole's a pretty, white place, Matt; Peary's not taking you."

Despite such resonant moments and an attractive sensibility (Sanger is a poet), the play is short on excitement; professing to be a fantasia on historical themes, it comes off more as a meditation. Peary junior, who has a lot of stage time, never becomes more than a mechanism. In Bob White's production, Nigel Shawn Williams see-sawed affectingly between the bewildered fire of Henson's youth and the quietly smouldering embers of his age, and Christopher Hunt shone out with a mischievous cameo of Dr. Boas.

There was more surface liveliness in Conni Massing's The Aberhart Summer, but far less going on beneath. Based on a novel (by Bruce Allen Powe), it probably tried to get too much in; the narrative was clear, but the dramatic point went missing.

This play, too, was set largely in the '30s, and it was the only one of the four with an Albertan setting; William Webster, the veteran actor who had given a sinister gloss to Peary, now did the same for William Aberhart, the prophet of Social Credit. Despite having the title role, he was marginal to the play, and though the rise of his party got a lot of attention it, too, turned out dramatically to be a large red herring. (Perhaps not red considering its virulent anti- Communism, but a herring nonetheless.)

The main story concerned the mysterious hanging of a teenage boy; unfortunately, he was dead before we had properly registered that he was alive, and all the flashbacks in the world couldn't revive him. Nor could all the tributes paid to him by his surviving friend, the play's narrator and an unrealized figure on a much larger scale. A character of whom we had seen too little eventually revealed the guilt of another character of whom we had seen even less; neither of them had any connection with Social Credit. We had to settle for a genre picture of a town wriggling in the grip of the Depression.

Stephen Heatley's production was impressively drilled, and the cast doubled and trebled with aplomb, but this kind of local epic has developed its own cliches: Speaking in unison as a shorthand for community spirit ranks high among them. When detached from the chorus, several young actors -- Christopher Hunt, Kevin Kruchkywich, Brian Marler -- made agreeably sharp impressions.

The latter two were similarly helpful in Frank Moher's Weather, which, theoretically, brought us up to date, being set in "Vancouver, recently." This also had a narrator-protagonist, a homosexual manic-depressive with a schizophrenic son. "You're in for a number of surprises this evening," he warned us, sounding rather smug about it, but the only really surprising thing was that he expected us to be surprised. The play had a pedagogical flavour, as of something to be taken 'round the schools to instruct older children about an issue.

"Everybody is mad in this play" we were told, and those not clinically affected had their own obsessions about how to treat those who were. The title seemed a metaphor for their collective state of mind, or perhaps for that of society as a whole. The son had designed a machine for controlling the weather, and though both he and the play soon dropped this idea, he did seem more in control of life in general than anyone else on stage.

Characterization was sketchy in the extreme: a matter of outlining each social and/or medical role, and letting the actors add a few tics. D. Michael Dobbin, artistic director of the company, also directed this play.

Dying Is Easy, another play about now, had some tastier writing, but was even more of a mess. "Dying is easy -- comedy's hard" are famous last words attributed here to "some old Shakespearean actor, British; I can't remember his name." I've always believed it to be the valediction of some old American vaudevillean (I can't remember his name), but since the character quoting it is an obnoxious loudmouth, the author may have meant the mistake as a subtle comment on her.

I wouldn't bet on it, though. She's a TV editor, making life hell for a pair of writers who are trying to develop a sitcom; if they fail with comedy, dying may well be their best alternative, or so they frequently feel.

Paul Quarrington frankly admits that the play is his revenge for having had a series idea of his own rejected by the CBC. At the risk of sounding unkind, if that idea was anything like the one his two heroes have come up with -- an adult comedy about rampant infidelity as seen through the eyes of the family dog -- my sympathies are with the Corp. A parallel plot about the pair being stalked by a wronged and vengeful female they call the Wild Woman is unfunnily unbelievable, or unbelievably unfunny.

One of the guys is an agoraphobic, so the play doesn't travel much, and in Bill Glassco's production it seemed unduly exposed, both by the open stage and by the acting of two thirds of the cast.

Peter Smith, the remaining third, had one splendid moment of anger and was a pretty good counter-puncher, but his colleagues (Nigel Shawn Williams, Mieko Ouchi) gave him nothing to feed off. They also displayed an unerring knack for emphasizing the wrong word in a sentence. That would be most enervating in any play, but in gag- comedy -- which this is, or aspires to be -- it's disastrous.

Nobody expects four masterpieces in one season; it may have happened in ancient Athens, but never since then. But when one considers that each part of this quartet had already been workshopped somewhere else, it was distressing that they should all have arrived on the Calgary stage sporting such gaping holes.
