A CHRISTMAS CAROLYoung Centre for the Performing Arts55 Mill St., Bldg. 49 - 416-866-8666On view for the third time, Soulpepper's production, adapted and directed by Michael Shamata, fully captures Dickens's ability, not so much to make us laugh till we cry as to keep laughter and tears in play simultaneously. Joseph Zieglers magnificent Scrooge is a fleshy, flinty businessman with, for better or worse, years of life still before him. His final joyous release is overwhelming. Oliver Dennis remains the prince of Cratchits. Through Dec. 23.RADIO CITY CHRISTMAS SPECTACULARHummingbird Centre1 Front St. E. - 416-872-2262The Rockettes have been performing their Parade of the Wooden Soldiers for decades, and its some kind of high-precision classic. Everything else in this lavish Spectacular is utterly generic; it's like nothing, and everything, you've ever seen. Theres a sliver of continuity, about Santa Claus and how hard worked he is; something called Santas Gonna Rock and Roll might be described, if one were feeling unseasonably uncharitable, as an abomination. The finale is an ultra- traditional involving three real camels (or are they dromedaries; as Ogden Nash wrote, "I'm never sure, are you?"). Through Dec. 31.HEAD-SMASHED-IN BUFFALO JUMPYoung Centre for the Performing Arts55 Mill St., Bldg. 49 - 416-866-8666This collaboratively written piece begins with four actors prowling the stage miming alienation, a feeling that the main action, which varies between the simple and the maddeningly obscure, proceeds to throw at us in handfuls. The characters, lost souls in Parkdale, are compelling at varying levels, like so many doomed mammals (like the titular concussed buffaloes in Alberta), but the show can't then think of much to do with them. Closes tonight.GLORIOUS!Bluma Appel Theatre27 Front St. E. - 416-366-7723Beware of plays (as opposed to musicals) with exclamation points in their titles. Peter Quilters Glorious! is a lamentable piece of writing with a delightful performance at its centre. This has a lopsided sort of appropriateness; Nicola Cavendish plays, with the perfect blend of dignity and absurdity, Florence Foster Jenkins, who through sheer self belief, forged a career as a concert singer despite a complete inability to carry a tune. Hers was an (unconscious) one-joke routine; the play has nowhere to go, and goes there. Ends tonight.SEUSSICAL -- A MUSICALLorraine Kimsa Theatrefor Young People165 Front St. E. - 416-862-2222An economy version of Lynn Ahrenss and Stephen Flaherty's Broadway musical based on the characters, stories and linguistic contortions of Dr. Seuss. It takes the audience's interest too much for granted (the Cat in the Hat seems to be there only because he couldnt be left out) but it can be charming, and it works once the basic narrative gets started. Lovely performances from George Masswohl and Corinne Koslo as Horton the elephant and Miss Gertrude McFuzz, respectively -- both, in their different ways, paragons of fidelity. Through Dec. 31.SONGS FOR A NEW WORLDYoung Centre forthe Performing Arts 55 Mill St., Bldg. 49 - 416-866-8666The most exciting Toronto musical in ages. Jason Robert Browns song cycle doesn't have the dramatic value of his later work and its inspirational quality can be monotonous, but rhythmically the songs are killers and four superb performers -- Thom Allison, Jason Knight, Sharron Matthews, Tracy Michailidis -- take them through the roof. Chris Abraham's staging is amazingly stylish and inventive. Ends tonight.THOM PAIN (BASED ON NOTHING)Tarragon Extra Space30 Bridgman Ave. - 416-531-1827Youve changed, she said, the night we met. Will Enos New York success is an exercise in wrong footing the audience; it plays with our expectations about theatre, life and narrative logic, is stimulating and exasperating in equal measure -- which may be the only predictable thing about it. Tom McCamus, the sole performer, is not as fine a stand-up comic as he is an actor but he does very well embarrassing us and himself from behind a pair of thick glasses. Jennifer Tarver, an expert in Beckett and post-Beckett territory, directs. Ends tomorrow.THE OXFORD ROOF CLIMBER'S REBELLIONTarragon Theatre30 Bridgman Ave. - 416-531-1827The roof climber is Lawrence of Arabia, a fellow of All Souls', after the First World War. Robert Graves, poet, is his contemporary, and Stephen Masicottes play deals with Lawrences anger and guilt, Graves's shell shock, their possible homosexuality, Graves's marriage and his wifes feminism, the reactionary Lord Curzon, a fathers bereavement, and the birth of the modern Middle East. That's a lot to get in and the result, though comprehensive, lacks weight and style. Good performances from Tom Rooney, Jonathan Crombie and especially Michelle Giroux. Ends tomorrow.ALADDIN THE MAGICAL FAMILY MUSICALElgin Theatre189 Yonge St. - 416-872-555Kids who know Aladdin only through the Disney movie may be surprised to learn that the hero had a comic, widowed, cross- dressing mama and she had two funny, incompetent assistants. That may not be authentic Arabian Nights but it's pure English panto, as interpreted in Ross Pettys seasonal transplant, one of his best. Petty himself is, of course, the villain, and in the prologue a down-and- out sheltering in a library; he acts this well. Jennifer Dale is the very sexy librarian. Let us pause to consider this universal fantasy. Now let us move on. Jamie McKnight is the charmingly gormless skateboarding hero, who rubs his lamp and gets a wrestler; thats Bret "The Hitman" Hart returning in the role of the genie. Robin Williams he isn't, but at least he's there in the flesh.
