STAY

Theatre Passe Muraille Backspace, Toronto

First produced in 1990, David Demchuk's Stay is the third notable Canadian play to be revived in Toronto this year, following Michel Tremblays Les Belles Soeurs and George Walkers Better Living. It is the least celebrated of the three, and the most compelling.

It is also the starkest. It consists of three monologues, divided among two characters. The "stay" of the title is a stay of execution. Joey, who begins and finishes the evening, is a convicted child-killer who has spent years on death row, waiting his turn. Now, however, his lawyer has mounted a successful appeal. Joey is happy for the lawyer's sake rather than his own. He wants to die: not necessarily to atone, just to die. To give up.

The speech that tells us all this is Joey's letter to Roberta, a close friend of his childhood who has never once visited him during his imprisonment. We find her in a graveyard, delivering a long, ruminative soliloquy, the filling in this theatrical sandwich. She, too, is living with (if not actually under) a sentence of death; her husband is in hospital with liver cancer. It also transpires that she and Joey carry the joint burden of an earlier killing.

Joey and Roberta will have one more meeting whose details -- the play being in part a thriller, and an effective one -- it would be unfair to reveal. We learn something about it from Roberta, and more, thanks to some chronological scrambling, from Joey, to whom we return for some final, devastatingly ironic words. Irony these days usually means an inflated kind of sarcasm, but this is the real thing: simple words that carry a dramatic weight beyond the knowledge of the speaker.

I expect to be bored by monologues, unless theyre funny. Those in Stay are not funny (though they have their wry moments), but they held me almost without a lapse. Joey, a child-killer who was also once a child killer, may take us further inside a murderers head than any dramatic character since Macbeth. (I'm not making absurd claims, just offering a historical observation. There really isn't much competition: Woyzeck, perhaps.)

What we discover there is emptiness: hardly regret, but acceptance, a willingness to let go of life, his own or anyone elses. He is interested in comic books, but not much else; he wishes his mother, his lawyer, and his former girlfriend well, but no more than that; he doesnt like being targeted by the other prisoners, but he hardly protests. It may not be true of all killers, but Demchuk convinces us that it's true of this one. Mark Harapiak plays him with an appropriate deathly calm, amiable but passionless.

Caroline Gillis, though technically quite accomplished, is less convincing as Roberta, but she has a harder job. Joey, in writing to her, can direct his speeches at someone; she is talking into a void. He has a present to act; she has only a past to remember. Since she is more intelligent, her consciousness ranges further, and is harder to anchor.

The playwright's attempt to centre her thoughts around a consuming interest in the stars struck me as a contrivance, and makes both writing and performance sound literary (though her observation that Cancer, the crab, is absent from the heavens tonight is a bold stroke, bitterness masquerading as acceptance). As the survivor, she should be the more frightening of the two, but this never registers.

The speeches are interspersed with some bits of video footage that looked, from where I sat, impenetrably blurry. Anne Driscolls direction is otherwise fine; she and the designer, Steve Lucas, manage, in a confined space, to nail the play with a couple of visual images. Both are doors. One is that of Joeys cell. The other is the screen door of Roberta's house, confronting us at the very end like the implacable entrance of a Greek tragedy.
