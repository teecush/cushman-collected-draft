I may never have properly grasped the meaning of the word "thorough" until I read George Gershwin: His Life and Work by Howard Pollack (University of California Press). This 900-page volume takes its subtitle both very literally and very logically. Most life-and-work books take the two components in tandem. This one segregates them. The life gets the books first part, which is long, and the work gets the second, which is longer.

Each part is, of course, further subdivided. The biographical section proceeds by topic, starting with Gershwin and his Family. But of course George, though childless, had a family -- the most artistically significant being his elder brother and prime lyricist, Ira -- throughout his too-brief life, so he dies at the end of the first chapter. He dies again at the climax of other chapters concerned with his friends, his personality, his relation to the music of his time (popular, concert, theatre and jazz) and his own compositional studies. These last, Pollack, fighting the myth of his subject as an "untutored genius" who preferred to remain untutored, reveals to have been far more wide ranging and persistent than has usually been suspected.

Gershwin was writing for Broadway (including one full score) before he was out of his teens. He hit his stride as a successful commercial songwriter in 1919 when, at the age of 21, he wrote Swanee, which in hit single terms -- sheet music then, of course, rather than record sales -- remained the biggest smash he ever had. Five years after that, he made his debut as a concert-hall composer with Rhapsody in Blue, another Greatest Hit. He had already composed his first opera, a one-act piece titled Blue Monday, which had been inserted in (and speedily removed from) a Broadway revue. When the Rhapsody was unveiled in 1924, Gershwin's best musical-comedy work still lay ahead, though only by a few months: Lady Be Good, starring Fred and Adele Astaire and bringing a new Jazz Age pulse and wit to the American stage, premiered at the end of that same year. He went on alternating concert with light- theatre work, though statistically the latter predominated by a wide margin.

So he confused people, though not, it appears, himself; he never seems to have regarded one kind of musical form as intrinsically superior to another, or to have thought of any assignment as being a diversion from his natural path. He knew his own talent, but he wasn't boastful, merely confident. He seems to have charmed most who knew him and everyone who worked with him. Pollack argues that musical and critical opinion about Gershwin has always split along popular and "classical" lines, but not in the way you might expect. Classicists, suspicious of Gershwins philharmonic ambitions and skeptical about his structural skills, have always claimed to prefer the show tunes. Populists have been tickled pink that one of their own should have made the crossover, and may even have felt shamefaced about holding him back. Exhibit A here is the 1945 biopic, significantly titled Rhapsody in Blue, which strongly suggests that George broke the heart of his old music teacher -- an imaginary character -- by his continual backsliding into writing pop songs.

Pollack debunks some small but persistent Gershwinian myths. Countless books and articles, attempting to convict Porgy and Bess of racism, have quoted Duke Ellingtons objections to Gershwin's lamp-black Negroisms. This has always seemed to me to sit oddly with Ellington's personal and professional admiration for Gershwin (the feelings were mutual), and, it now turns out, the Duke never said it. The phrase was written by a theatrical journalist with an ideological axe to grind who appended it, along with a few other comments of his own, to an interview with Ellington. Ellington was upset, and tried to get the record changed, but the words stuck.

John OHara, on the other hand, did say, "George died on July 11, 1937, but I dont have to believe it if I don't want to." It doesn't mean, though, that he had lost a friend; OHara, we learn, was one of the few who disliked Gershwin the man. That, though, may make his words all the more moving. For nearly all who heard it, Gershwins music epitomized his time and his place, so when he died, at the age of 38, both friends and strangers felt that America had lost its voice. The early death contributed, of course, to the legend; it cemented the golden-boy status, making Gershwin seem like the Gatsby of composers. And it still hurts: I remember as a child, decades after the event, listening to a radio show about him and my incredulity at discovering that the man who had composed all those wonderful songs had died so young O'Hara's words, quoted at the very end of the program, seemed to me exactly right.

Having dealt with the life by category, Pollack, a professor at the University of Houston and an all round music man, like his subject, proceeds through the work in strict chronology. A fiend for detail, he gives you all the facts about all the music; who produced, conducted or performed it, who replaced whom on tour, what luminaries were in the first-night audience, what the reviewers said. He makes a good analytic case for all the concert music. He makes me want to listen again to An American in Paris, which I have always thought of as movie music before the fact. Its easy to see why. Most of these shows were farces, and its hard to keep the plot of a farce in your head when you're not actually watching it. And they all seem to have the same plot, involving mistaken identities and the temporary separation of Boy and Girl by rich and snobbish parents. All the same, it's worth persevering. Its received wisdom that the songs are only loosely anchored to their texts, and can easily be moved from one spot to another, or even from one show to another. Pollack proves this wrong. Some of the songs, the ones we dont know, are quite specific plot songs, and even the famous ones have reasons for being where they are. In Lady Be Good, the title song -- which isn't a love song, more a conspiracy song -- serves a vital narrative function, and a witty one, while Fascinating Rhythm is an anthem to syncopation that has no place in the story but comes along just when the show needs to explode.

It's become the fashion to reassemble these shows and their scores into new products, as in the Broadway successes My One and Only and Crazy for You. (The former is being staged this year at the Shaw Festival.) In narrative terms, though, neither of these shows makes any more sense than did the originals; in fact, they make less. To judge from the book of Girl Crazy, the Gershwin show with the greatest concentration of standard songs, the main fault with these old libretti is not inconsequentiality but longwindedness; they run their jokes into the ground. They need streamlining rather than radical alteration.

And on a related note: Hearing these scores with their original orchestrations, or informed reconstructions when the originals have been lost, is like hearing them for the first time. Check out the Roxbury series of recordings (on the Nonesuch and New World labels) and be scintillated. Some Golden Age composers, Richard Rodgers or Harold Arlen, may have written finer autonomous melodies, but none has had Gershwins focused theatrical energy. Pollack even makes a persuasive case for a couple of semi- operettas, Rosalie and Song of the Flame, to which no one else has paid attention in decades.

Better known are the political comic operas: Strike Up the Band, Of Thee I Sing, Let Em Eat Cake. These are exercises in the whimsical- satirical, and are recurringly topical, though it's possible that they seem harder-hitting when described than when actually staged.

Of Thee I Sing seems to demand revival every time there's a presidential election (it starts with a parade of voters brandishing such placards as Vote for Prosperity and See What You Get), but the one Id most like to see now is Strike Up the Band, which demands it on a more permanent basis: i.e., every time theres a war. This show exists in two versions. In the first, which closed out of town in 1927, the United States, at a tycoon's behest, goes to war -- with Switzerland -- to make the world safe for cheese; in the second, a hit in 1930, it embarks on a similar conflict over chocolate. I guess that means they sweetened it. Pollack challenges the received opinion that they also softened and weakened it. He may be right; the revised version certainly has the stronger score, augmented as it is by I've Got a Crush on You and Soon and a rip-roaring showstopper titled Mademoiselle from New Rochelle. There must be a way of amalgamating the best of this shows textual and musical worlds.

Even a writer as conscientious and comprehensive as Pollack has to skimp on something. In his case its the individual songs. Some he examines in detail, throwing in exhaustive lists of artists who have recorded them. Many others, including my own personal favourite, They Can't Take That Away from Me, he merely acknowledges as existing. Nor is he always the most enlivening writer. (He has a habit of saying "for his part" or for her, their, or its part whenever he wishes to switch attention from one person, group or thing to another. For my part, I wish he wouldn't.)

He catches fire, though, when he comes to deal with Porgy and Bess, the summit of Gershwins achievement, the work to which everything else leads, though not necessarily directly: "The opera essentially sounds like nothing else, not even, as sometimes claimed, Gershwins own musical comedies."

For all that, it's an opera made, quite legitimately, out of show music, which I think accounts for Pollack's conviction that its sheer verve makes many other operas sound tepid in comparison. We take for granted that Pollack loves Gershwin-- why else would he have bothered? -- but this is the only place where his enthusiasm shines through his prose: "Gershwin's superb abilities as a musical dramatist, including terrific handling of elaborate ensembles shows its author to be deeply humane in his sensitive understanding of the weakness and resilience, the haplessness and dignity, of an oppressed but tight-knit group. Better than anything else he ever wrote, the work reveals Gershwin as not only a brilliant artist, but a great and complex one."

He did write more. He spent the two years left to him in Hollywood, where he wrote three movie scores (two of them for Astaire) that show him at his highest power as a songsmith. Literally all the songs are winners, and most of them are classics. Then he died of a brain tumour. Ira, who lived another half century, never got over it; that was his tragedy. American music never got over it, either. And that has been part -- a large part -- of its glory.
