PELAGIE

Bluma Appel Theatre, Toronto

Many, perhaps most, musicals are based on something else. This rule seems to hold good everywhere except in Canada, where the best shows, with the odd happy exception like Leslie Arden's Happy Prince, are originals. Adaptations here tend to fall into the category immortally labelled by Mary Rodgers as "why musicals," which means, according to her friend Stephen Sondheim, why bother?

It seems a harsh question to put to Pelagie, a show with epic ambitions over which many people have bothered hard for a period of seven years. Still, it has to be asked. I havent read Pelagie-la- Charrette, the novel by Antonine Maillet that serves as source; but on the evidence presented at CanStage I would guess that its undramatizable, musically or otherwise.

The main action covers a five-year period, from 1773 to 1777. The characters are Acadians, survivors of the Great Expulsion; 20 years before, they were violently uprooted from their homes in New Brunswick by the British and shipped to the coast of Georgia. Now, with their oppressors up to their eyes in the U.S. War of Independence, it seems possible for them to make the trip home, overland, by wagon. The moving spirit in this daunting endeavour is Pelagie herself, widowed mother of three, a tireless taskmistress and cheerleader, pulling her cart or having her family pull it like an idealistic Mother Courage. As they travel through Louisiana, Massachusetts and points progressively north, they lose some members of their party and acquire others.

So this is the story of a journey, and as such a theatrical minefield. The obvious technical problems are the least of it; in Michael Shamata's staging they are neatly, and at times impressively, solved. The real handicap is that the structure is, by definition, picaresque, meaning it's just one damn thing after another. There is very little room for cause or effect, and no overriding idea that might compensate for the lack. The characters - - partly through the nature of the narrative, partly through deficiencies in script and score -- are all cardboard cut-outs; their only function is to strive, and -- this being a post-pop- opera musical -- they have the anthems to prove it.

As Pelagie, Susan Gilmour sings powerfully, sometimes beautifully, but the woman never comes to life. She is least believable when in love, a condition that seems to have been wished on her for no other reason than its part of her job description as a musical heroine. Her sweetheart, sung with matching robustness by Rejean J. Cournoyer, is a dashing skipper of a privateer. Theirs is an on-and-off kind of courtship, because hes constantly away performing daredevil feats other people tell us about afterward. He does get to perform the onstage feat of saving Pelagie's cart from falling off a bridge (which, periodically descending from the flies, is the chief architectural feature of John Ferguson's set). I tried to believe this was a thrilling dramatic climax, rather than a transplanted circus act, but I couldnt. Despite conscientious efforts to maintain Pelagies status as a Strong Woman, the romantic relationship is a throwback to the maiden-in-peril and hero-to-the- rescue days of Rose Marie and The Desert Song, though without the melodic sweep that made the old operettas, in their own terms, credible.

Even more damaging than the show's failure to create compelling individuals is its failure to create a compelling community, since the latter is what it keeps telling us it's about. "I will bring back / Many people / To Acadia / THIS MUCH I VOW" sings Pelagie, which tells us nothing except that the shows creators have an all- too-conventional idea of how to bring down a first-act curtain. Again, the problem seems inherent in the material.

No one (including the authors in interviews) can help comparing this musical to Fiddler on the Roof; it even has a capering violinist. The older show, however, doesnt begin with an expulsion; it ends with one, and we have had time to feel for ourselves what its people were being expelled from. We've also had the chance to get to know them as individuals in a particular place, rather than as a crowd on the road. Both pieces have a wedding scene early in the second act. The one in Fiddler is moving because we know the bride and groom, and what they and their parents have been through. (That's why Sunrise Sunset has become the ultimate Jewish wedding song; it expresses universal feelings through individualized mouths.) Pelagie unites two young people who have hitherto expressed barely a flicker of interest in one another. The ceremony is followed by a song for the heroine, expressing her conflicted feelings as mother and woman; it sounds both generalized and arbitrary.

The evening is mostly score; the characters never meet a crisis they dont want to sing about, sometimes, as in the above example, they seem to have invented the crisis for the sake of singing the song. They always sing exactly what they mean; you sit there all evening hoping someone will discover subtext. There are no unforced emotional peaks, though there are a couple of comic Cliffs; Cliff Saunders and Cliff Le Jeune seize avariciously on every scarp of light relief thats thrown to them. Mostly, though, the performers, able and vigorous though they all are, have nothing to act.

Allen Cole's music avoids the Les Miserables level of turgidity, but even at its best it offers stray haunting phrases rather than whole rounded tunes. Vincent de Tourdonnet's lyrics merely plod, somewhat erratically. When he ends a line with a reference to the brine you wonder what could be wrong with the simple word "sea." (It turns out that it wouldnt rhyme with time; but then, of course, neither does "brine.") The stand-out number, suggesting the show might have found a style of its own, is one of the folkier items -- a very old mans song about "the wagon of death." This sounds like a leitmotif in preparation, and it's no surprise when the wagon itself appears. It is a shock to find it driven by a red- dressed woman whom I took at first to be a camp-follower but who turns out to be Death herself.

The show's central conflict is revealed, very belatedly, to be between her and Pelagie; or, if you like, a race between their two wagons. I wont reveal the outcome except to say the story seems to end merely because the show has been running rather a long time. The productions last-ditch attempt to prove this really has been a show about Acadia is, almost literally, to run up a flag and wait for us to salute it.

My own throat remained unlumped, still longing for some sign of irony, of genuine romance, of unpumped passion, above all of wit. I wonder if there's any chance of Nellie McKay writing a musical.
