THE MYSTERY OF EDWIN DROOD

Royal George Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont.

- - -

NIAGARA-ON-THE-LAKE, Ont. - One of the liveliest musical numbers in The Mystery of Edwin Drood comes about halfway into the second act and is called Don't Quit While Youre Ahead. Beginning as a solo, it is gradually taken up by the entire 18-strong company. Musically and choreographically, it builds and builds, its excitement greatly enhanced by the sudden appearance of a stage- wide flight of steps on which all the performers can strut their stuff in delirious unison.

Obeying the precept of the songs title, they give us an encore, which is suddenly interrupted by the deflating announcement that it was at this point that Mr. Charles Dickens, who wrote the novel on which the show is based, laid down his pen for ever. He quit, leaving the rest of us behind. Cruellest of the cruel, the novel was a murder story, and we will never know definitively who done it or even -- since Dickens had yet to arrive at the discovery of the body -- if it was actually done. The actors, needing to complete the show, are on their own. Except that they have us.

The Mystery of Edwin Drood, the first interactive musical, is a smart, rowdily enjoyable and deeply unsatisfying show. It always was all of those things (it was first produced in 1985 in Central Park and then on Broadway, where it won most of the available awards), and the Shaw Festival production accentuates both its good and bad points.

A proper synopsis would take up all my space: Dickens' story -- set in a somnolent English cathedral town and centring on the disappearance from it of the rich, young, arrogant but essentially blameless Edwin Drood -- is complicated enough, but Rupert Holmes, who wrote the book, music and lyrics, has erected his own elaborate structure on top of it. We are in a Victorian music hall whose troupe is presenting its own Drood drama. The man who calls himself Your Chairman, who on an ordinary bill would merely introduce the acts and orchestrate the singalongs, now functions as narrator, though he retains his traditional fruity demeanour -- very much so, in Neil Barclay's performance.

There are various tensions and temperaments and non-appearances through drunkenness floating around among these artistes, and though they are not explored in much depth, they add to the mixture. A few of this cast, notably Our Chairman, do the beery direct-address thing very well. Others are more shamefaced about it; they also seem to have the patronizing idea that music hall performers, required to act dramatic scenes, would act them very badly.

It seems charitable to assume that theyre doing this on purpose, but its still painful to see John Jasper, the jealous opium- smoking choirmaster, last of Dickens' great tormented villains, transformed (by Michael Querin) into a vacuous marionette; or the Reverend Mr. Crisparkle (Douglas E. Hughes) become a mildly posturing stick figure. Melodrama, no less than music hall, needs to be played for its own values; camping it up is an admission of failure.

The show draws on yet another Victorian genre, by presenting the vanishing Edwin as a pantomime principal boy, i.e. played by a girl; Blythe Wilson has the piping voice for this convention but not the thigh-slapping spirit.

We, the audience, come into it when the Dickensian original has reached the end of its rope. We are invited to cast our votes on the questions outstanding. The company will then act out, and sing out, the remainder of the story as we have determined it. This is for real, and they have prepared material to cover any combination of choices we might make. It must have added hours to the rehearsal schedule, and that's without counting the understudies

Problem One is the identity of a mysterious detective who turns up late in the original text and is plainly someone else in disguise. At Fridays premiere (and, Im sure, at most performances in most productions) the house settled unanimously on a minor functionary named Bazzard, who in the first act had endeared himself to us by his very insignificance. Or rather, we warmed to him because his insignificance had been made so very significant; the Victorian actor cast in his role had complained that he, too, was an overlooked supernumerary, desperate for the big break. He had even sung a song about it, something about the walk-on's dream of seizing the limelight on, as the lyric neatly has it, some starless night. Guy Bannerman, the Shaw Festival actor who plays HIM, fairly hurls himself into this, with all the pent-up gusto of a performer who, to be frank, has not had too many juicy roles at Niagara himself. Of course we wanted to hear him do another number, and by voting for him we got our wish.

Our second and more onerous task was that of selecting the murderer. We are presented with seven candidates who had motive and opportunity for doing Edwin in. Being a Dickens student, I voted for Jasper, who, apart from being sexually repressed and a dope fiend, was jealous of young Drood's engagement to the beauteous Rosa Bud.

I was jeered at for making such an obvious choice, though that of course is why I made it: Dickens wasnt Agatha Christie trying to be born. The anointed killer when I saw it was the opium-den proprietress known as Princess Puffer, and here too I think the house was influenced by its desire to hear the performer sing again.

Personally I never knew that Corinne Koslo could sing at all, let alone that she could sing so well, and she faces her audience head- on with terrific buxom verve. I might also have fancied seeing the fair Rosa convicted: I would like to hear the actress, Tracy Michailidis, sing her way out of that since she seemed to me rather wasted on just being good. I could also have lent an additional ear to Jenny L. Wright, who plays the Oriental lady Helena Landless with what she herself winningly describes as "a geographically inaccessible accent."

We also get to vote for a happy-ending romantic couple, and we take pleasure in choosing the least congruous pair. This is because, frankly, we dont care -- and that is the show's major problem. All this audience participation is good, but not great, fun; and it comes on top of a script and performance that have not allowed us to believe in a single character and have been very sketchy about the actual story.

Much of Rupert Holmes' score is too clever for its own good, which means not clever enough. Make no mistake: For a first (and so far, alas, only) musical, its a fine achievement. The music is not inexhaustibly melodic, but it ranges impressively from invigorating rampages -- though the first and best suffers here from being performed with the house-lights up -- to at least one sweet ballad, Perfect Strangers.

Dennis Garnhums production has inspiriting energy; and its looks -- with credit to Timothy French's choreography and Allan Stichbury's good sets and better lighting -- are great.
