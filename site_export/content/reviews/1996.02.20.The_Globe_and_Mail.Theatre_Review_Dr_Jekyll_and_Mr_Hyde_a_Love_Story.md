THEATRE REVIEW DR. JEKYLL AND MR. HYDE - A LOVE STORY

Tuesday, February 20, 1996

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Toronto ONT -- Written by James W. Nichol Directed by Neil Munro Starring Les Carlson and David Storch At St. Lawrence Centre until March 16 Rating: Jekyll plus Hyde is one too many One character with two personalities calls for one actor. With two on stage, the essence of a classic duality is lost. BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Toronto RIGHT from the start, a horrible thought assailed me. Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde are the most famous double act in fiction. You may talk of Don Quixote and Sancho Panza, Holmes and Watson, Jeeves and Wooster, even Rosencrantz and Guildenstern, but you have to be modestly literate to do so. None of them trip as persistently and proverbially off the common tongue as do the doctor and his double. They did not, of course, reach this exalted state by being buddies; one is an unholy emanation of the other's worst instincts. Robert Louis Stevenson's brilliant fantasy dramatized a psychological phenomenon of which the world was uneasily aware, but for which it lacked a name. He gave us a double-barrelled one.

Being one guy, Jekyll and Hyde has long been reckoned an actor's treat. In most of its adaptations, play or movie, the story has been more or less an excuse for a transformation scene. The Canadian Stage Company, however, has cast two separate performers. That was what bugged me as the lights went down. It could only mean that Jekyll and Hyde were going to have scenes together. When one part of your psyche meets another part of your psyche they will obviously have much to talk about, but the encounter may, like an overheated family drama, leave outsiders firmly outside.

Funnily enough, in Neil Munro's staging the couple's first encounter shapes up as if it is going to be a transformation scene. It happens some 20 minutes in. We have met Dr. Jekyll, upright Victorian scientist coming apart at the seams. We have seen him scandalize his respectable friends with his free thinking, and seek consolation in the arms of a brothel keeper who believes in sharing her employees' workload. (This is a proclivity at which Stevenson barely hinted.) We have watched him embrace his teen-aged daughter with more than fatherly affection. (The doctor of the book is a childless bachelor.) So, after a hard day of tentative sinning, he comes home, locks himself in his lab, mixes a potion, swallows it, and starts writhing on the floor. He begins to crawl round the sofa, like a man who has something to hide: presumably himself. But no, he remains in sight, and when his alter ego flashes into view, they begin the first of many discussions. These amount to Jekyll saying "I summoned you," Hyde saying "you summoned me," and both of them saying "it's your fault."

Hyde seems somewhat of a lost child, and he, like the young Miss Jekyll, addresses their common progenitor as "pappy." To me this sounds more like Mark Twain than R. L. Stevenson, and such lapses in tone are a recurrent feature of James W. Nichol's script. They are, however, the least of its problems.

Les Carlson's Jekyll is grey, worried and uncommunicative. In a bizarre way this testifies to the truth of the legend; it's no fun being Jekyll if you don't get a crack at being Hyde. What's more surprising is that it works in reverse; David Storch's Hyde is even duller, being more of a victim than a fiend. He isn't even ugly. Eventually Jekyll uses him to act out his incestuous impulses; since Hyde is Jekyll's creation, this may count as a double case of child abuse. This, the fashionable issue, is the charge that sticks; presumably it occasions the play's ironic subtitle. Or maybe it's approving, and the play is wildly subversive. Or maybe the love story is between Dr. J and Mr. H themselves, which is why we need two of them. Along the way they do get up to a few other tricks. They hit an old gentleman in the stomach. (He and his friends take it with amazing sang- froid.) They cut up a prostitute. They murder.

They do all these things in different locations, and the production huffs and puffs to keep up with them, as do Cameron Porteous's sets, whose one graceful feature is a gorgeously receding set of street lamps. In one scene a character gets needlessly into a railway carriage and chugs away while his bags remain mysteriously on the platform; Munro, whose haunting production of The Petrified Forest was the Shaw Festival's best show in years, seems similarly stranded. The acting unfailingly heads for the nearest cliche, and which akimbo arms and wagging chins are due to incapacity and which to desperation is impossible to say. I have seldom sat among an audience so palpably disaffected; as the evening's shocking climax petered out, they felt - I felt - baffled, bored and finally beached.

But not, strangely, angry. There is some kind of ambition here, and I have seen or can imagine worse. Going round the United States at the moment is a musical on this subject with a score by Leslie Bricusse, which is made up, to judge from an advance CD, of generic ballads that could come from anything. Compared with Jekyll and Hyde - The Musical, or with my nightmares of it, Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde - A Love Story is merely a bad dream.
