Until May 10. 416-870-8000.

1984

Studio Theatre, Toronto Centre for the Arts

The worst mistake made by Tribal Productions' stage version of 1984 may be its neglect of the novels roots in George Orwells England. Or it might be the decision to use Winston Smith's ordeal in the cellars of the Ministry of Love as the frame for his whole story, thereby letting us know his chief torturer's identity from the start rather than springing it as a cruel surprise. Or it could be the wholesale sentimentalization of the story and characters. Or mabe its some other misjudgment. There is no lack of gaffes from which to choose.

Fairs fair: Tribal is a shoestring operation and most of its actors are non-Equity. Toronto's more affluent theatres can come up with work just as naive and pretentious as this, but they usually lend it a gloss and authority that makes the evening easier to get through. Putting the most celebrated of dystopian novels on the stage is a challenge for any company, and Eli Lukawitz, director and principal adaptor, has managed to get quite a lot of the story up there. He has even had a couple of good ideas. Chief among them is the prominence he gives to the telescreen announcer who is the face of the Party in Oceania. Whether inciting its members to hysteria in the daily Hate sessions or feeding them the latest over-production figures, she is always the same woman; and, in Tara Beagan's performance, the most compelling figure on stage. Though, of course, she isnt on stage as the others are. That may help her, as may the fact that she doesnt have to pretend to be a character.

One difficulty in dramatizing the book now is that the calendar year 1984 has come and gone. Orwell was enormously prescient because he was enormously observant; to adapt one of the Party's own slogans, he sensed the future by knowing the present and the past. But his nightmare of universal terror did not come to pass exactly as and when he said it would. (Nor has it now, despite this production's earnest efforts to persuade us otherwise, with an opening montage of Bush, bombs, Osama and Saddam, the last admittedly looking very like Big Brother.)

Now this is no problem when reading the book. One simply adjusts to Orwells vision, which in many respects is a naturalistic one. On stage, a theatrical genius might be able to keep Orwells theme and narrative while abandoning the homely details of the vision and transmute the particular into the universal. Anybody less gifted would be well advised to follow the original, and let the audience do its own transmuting. This production is done on a grey abstract set of obelisks and cubes, and with its actors fitted out in futuristic one-pieces rather snazzier than the drab overalls specified by Orwell, so it's probably going for the universal. In the theatre the universal, though, has an unfortunate habit of turning into the anonymous, which is what happens here. I don't think I heard the word London spoken from one end of the play to the other. Maybe theyre trying to make it an allegory about Canada. Maybe they just cant do the accents.

Here's an example: Parsons, the eternal Boy Scout, the cheerfully sweaty and unflinchingly loyal workhorse on whom the Party depends, and who calls Winston and everyone else "old boy," is English-lower- middle-class English -- to the core: not so much in what he is as in what he says. Andrew Penner's performance is at least on the right lines but the juice has gone out of the role. Others fare far worse; indeed, the productions capacity for misreading the characters is almost inspired in its consistency. Mrs. Parsons, the harried mother who lives in terror that her indoctrinated kids might turn her in for thoughtcrime, is played as a metallic vessel of menace.

I mentioned sentimentality. In the book the imprisoned Winston meets a drunken woman, also called Smith who, after vomiting copiously in front of him, says why, I might be your mother. Thats all. Here he meets a younger, very sober woman who says she might be his sister, and the production seems to want us to believe her. It even tries to make a Moment out of it. Winston himself is played (by Samuel Owen, who also worked on the script with Lukawitz and Ben Bain) as a whining, downtrodden zombie. The idea seems to be that he is only awakened to rebellion by his affair with Julia. There are two objections to this: 1) Winston is already thinking heretical thoughts and doing heretical things -- like keeping a diary and talking to proles -- long before he meets Julia, and not even this adaptation can disguise the fact; 2) Winston, as acted here, shows no sign of awakening at any point whatever. Lauren Brotman's Julia is the nearest thing to a passable performance, but she too is sentimentalized, or perhaps idealized. When Winston tells her "you're only a rebel from the waist down," the sexy Julia of the book takes it as an excellent joke, not that this Winston seems capable of making one. Here, she stalks off the stage, miffed. If theyre trying to turn her into a feminist flagbearer, theyve got the wrong girl.

The third principal, Don Tripe's O'Brien, is a smiling cliche of a villain, unlikely to have taken anyone in. In fact, the flashbacks that break up his interrogation of Winston come as a relief, one doesnt want to hear a sustained debate between two characters who dont appear to have a brain between them. The production is noticeably short on sex and violence: a forbearance that might be virtuous in other contexts but is nonsensical in this one.

Lukawitz can be forgiven for failing to stage the Two Minutes Hate convincingly, and he may even be forgiven for messing up the ending. It takes place, after all, mainly in the protagonist's head. He cannot be forgiven for muffing the lovers' arrest, which only needs to be played as written. Plumbing the depths of the unpardonable is Winstons capitulation when hes faced with the horror of the rats and shouts out "do it to Julia." He isn't allowed here to do it of his own accord; O'Brien has to plant the idea in his mind. So he's spared the ultimate humiliation, and the audience is spared the point of the fable. The stupidity of this hardly bears contemplating. There's also a whispering chorus, letting us know from the outset that we are in for an abysmal evening.
