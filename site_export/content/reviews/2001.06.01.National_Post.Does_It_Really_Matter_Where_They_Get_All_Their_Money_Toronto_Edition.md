PRIVATE LIVES

Avon Theatre, Stratford, Ont.

- - -

There are few plays whose plots are as simple and symmetrical, or as satisfying, as that of Noel Coward's Private Lives.

Elyot and Amanda, a tempestuous English couple now five years divorced, meet up unexpectedly when each is on a second honeymoon. In fact, they have adjacent suites in the same French hotel, and their encounter takes place on the balcony while each waits for the new partner to dress and emerge for cocktails. (It's that kind of play.)

Of course Elyot and Amanda are still violently in love, and of course they run off together to Paris, where Amanda conveniently keeps a flat. (It's still that kind of play.) Here they decline rapidly from bliss to blows, as they always used to and as they knew full well they would again.

They are discovered, locked in combat, by their pursuant spouses, the stodgy Victor and the silly Sybil. These two, having been forced into one another's company, begin to enact what amounts to a parody version of the relationship that they were intending to disrupt; that is, they forgo the bliss, for which they have not the wit, and go straight to the blows. Elyot and Amanda creep off and leave them to it.

In Coward's other familiar plays -- Hay Fever, Blithe Spirit, Present Laughter -- we know how the characters are able to maintain their enviable lifestyles; they are successful actors or writers or both. Private Lives furnishes no clue as to how Elyot and Amanda can afford their drinks, their dinners, their hotels or the glamorous globe-trotting to which they are both addicted. Apparently, they were born to it and take it as their right. That gives the play a fascinating kind of purity, and it is fitting that, in the 1960s when Coward's critical stock was at its lowest, it should have been a modest London revival of it that sparked off the Coward comeback, or what he himself referred to as "Dad's renaissance."

To my surprise, I have not seen the play from that production to this, and I realize that most of my impressions of it have been derived from the celebrated recording that Coward and his 1930s co- star Gertrude Lawrence made of the balcony scene, all clipped badinage with passion quivering decorously beneath. Speech rhythms are, I guess, a matter of fashion, but I could never take this combination of stiff upper lips and flaring nostrils at all seriously, either as acting or writing. It's very brittle -- and I would like you to repeat those words, Coward-fashion, with the accent on the adjective.

Coward was an adjective man. Brian Bedford, in his immaculate Stratford direction and in his own magnificent performance as Elyot, goes for nouns and especially verbs, and the result is unstrained revelation. Moment to moment, the play is beliveable; cumulatively, it is truthful.

Elyot is described in the stage directions as "about 30, quite slim and pleasant looking." Bedford is very pleasant looking and sounding, but he has admitted in interviews to being more than about 30, however generously you interpret "about," nor is he any longer what I would describe as slim. This, however, helps. Physically and metaphorically he puts flesh, not fat, on the character's bones and on the play.

The first-act reunion, in which Elyot and Amanda listen to a distant band playing Someday I'll Find You and talk feverishly about the potency of cheap music and the flatness of Norfolk while drawing ever closer together, has become a canonical example of subtext; talking about one thing and thinking another. In fact, the hidden meaning is so obvious that the text can hardly be described as sub.

What's remarkable here about Bedford's acting are the tenderness and the energy he infuses into Elyot's explicit declarations of love; yes, they're there in the writing, but not in how we usually hear the writing. Just as fine is the passionate tact with which he advances Elyot's, and Coward's, creed of gaiety in the face of mortality. If it sounded forced it would be embarrassing.

Best of all is the unbroken line of thought that carries right through his performance. Even when he makes an automatic joke about his mother-in-law, it's not a detachable gag but an integral part of his evolving but abortive relationship with his new wife, and an implicit step toward the predestined reunion with his old one.

He gets, of course, every laugh there is in the script. His timing on the first night when he spoke of the Catholic Church's non- recognition of divorce ("it's nice to think they'd sort of back us up") may even, from the look of it, have surprised Seana McKenna, his Amanda.

The play's middle act finds them fully enmeshed in the quarrels they have sworn to avoid, and they chart the onset of each squall and the eruption of the final storm in cartographic detail. They play excellently together, she the edgier of the two, he oddly protective, even in violence.

She has not quite his ease or his experience, and she has a fortuitous technical problem; by the time she has forced herself into the upper register of her voice and adopted an English accent that I can best describe as bohemian-suburban, she ends up sounding remarkably, if inadvertently, like Maggie Smith. The personality she projects is also remarkably Smithian, combative intelligence barely concealing stark terror. It happens to be right for the role, but it cannot help awaking echoes even for those of us who never saw Smith's Amanda, with or without Bedford's Elyot.

Coward once confessed that as a playwright, constructing a vehicle for himself and friend, he had been monstrously unfair to the supporting couple. Here he was being half-hard on himself. The function of Victor and Sybil is to provide foils and props for Elyot and Amanda. It is credible that Amanda would have turned to Victor, as solidly played here by Wayne Best, for temporary support. It is far less believable that Elyot could have endured two hours with Sybil, let alone an entire courtship; Sarah Dodd plays her shrill and flighty, but it is difficult to see what other option she has.

The cast is completed, and the symmetry enjoyably broken, by a maid who speaks only French, and that with a heavy cold; Kim Horsman is a champion sneezer and wheezer. John Lee Beatty's two sets are the kind that both demand and deserve applause, and Jane Greenwood's costumes effortlessly dazzle.

The sum total is as near a masterpiece as makes no difference.
