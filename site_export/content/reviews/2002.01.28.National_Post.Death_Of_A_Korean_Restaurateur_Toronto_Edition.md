DREAMS OF BLONDE AND BLUE

Theatre Passe Muraille, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - For 15 encouraging minutes at the start, M.J. Kang's Dreams of Blonde and Blue seems to be off and more or less running. A young Asian woman appears before the curtain in the guise of a stereotypical Oriental sex kitten, all self-abasement and pidgin English. This, in a forward-looking Toronto theatre at the start of the 21st century, is somewhat startling; but there is a neat reversal. We discover that the young woman is an actress, auditioning in New York for yet another role as a prostitute and all too aware of what is expected of her.

Her name is Julia, and we follow her as she pays a rare call on her parents, who are immigrants from Korea. Father runs an unsuccessful restaurant, and when he talks to his daughter he speaks rather like the character for whom she was trying out: "You look like bimbo," he tells her. In his dreams -- which are staged for us - - definite articles are no problem. He imagines himself returning to Korea, running for prime minister, and getting in, on what seems to be a strident Americanization ticket, with Arnold Schwarzenegger brought along as celebrity guest.

In real life Father (that's how he's billed) has violently conflicted feelings about his adopted country. He has good reason to hate Americans, but he also wants to be one of them. He keeps having cosmetic operations, and he himself performs one on his restaurant. He switches from ethnic to indigenous cuisine and calls the new place Uncle Sam's Hamburger, a name that one feels would play better in any country of the world than the one he's actually living in. But then Father is a transparently terrible businessman.

His prime ministerial ambitions are never, after the initial fantasy sequence, mentioned again. This is a shame, since they are the most engaging part of the play, however unlikely. (Of course, the point of pipe dreams is that they are improbable. What strains belief is that Father should confide his to his wife and daughter.) After this, we are stuck with the story of Father's decline and fall, which is believable but dreary, not because he is a failure but because, dreams apart, he is a barely individualized failure. And at that, he's the liveliest character on the stage.

Everything is neatly symmetrical. Julia, who has betrayed another parental dream by dropping out of school, finds her personal life all too closely reflecting her professional one, especially in her relationship with a sadistic American boyfriend. Mother, a scientist by training, now focuses her energies on clothes, and especially on shoplifting.

Flashbacks to the old country introduce us to Father's own father and to his sister, even more spectral figures for whom we are nonetheless expected to feel. (The technical term for this is sentimentality.) A final retrospective shows us the childhood trauma, dating back to the Korean War, that shaped Father's attitude toward the two countries. It is, in fact, that trusty commercial- theatre device, the late revelation that explains everything.

One thing it explains is why the play is set in New York at all (as opposed to, say, Toronto). It certainly has very little New York electricity, even given that it takes place in Queens rather than in Manhattan. Steve Lucas, usually an excellent designer, this time contributes a dispiritingly anonymous set. And the director, Guillermo Verdecchia, is unable to extract much pace or excitement from either the text or the actors.

Most of the performances are glumly inadequate. Julia, played by the author herself, is drably overemphatic, and Mommy (Brenda Kamino) even more drably under. Paul Braunstein, playing various roles, is at least efficient; and Denis Akiyama as Father is considerably more desperately bright and incorrigibly sad. He, alone in the cast, has rhythm: that of a man hurtling optimistically toward disaster as -- to adapt Stephen Sondheim -- he careers from Korea to career. I'd vote for him.
