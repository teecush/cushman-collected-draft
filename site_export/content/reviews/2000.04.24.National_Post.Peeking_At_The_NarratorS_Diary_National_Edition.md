MOTEL HELENE (TARRAGON); SHAKESPEAREAN GALA (WINTER GARDEN)

Francois, the narrator of Serge Boucher's Motel Helene and one of its three characters, is a young Quebecois who reads a lot of books and has ambitions to write his own. In the meantime he works for his father, who owns a convenience store across from a small apartment. This is the home of Johanne, a seamstress, who has packed rather too much living into her 25-odd years. At 16 she gave birth to a son; two and a half years ago the boy vanished from outside the apartment, never to be seen again. Johanne still maintains a monogamous relationship with Mario, the child's father, who comes around regularly to have sex; you might call him her live-out husband.

Francois, who is virginally gay, becomes Johanne's confidante and, one supposes, her friend, though an oddly passive one. He seems, if such a thing is possible, compassionately dispassionate; he resembles the narrator-figure of a Tennessee Williams play or, perhaps more precisely, the Christopher Isherwood of I Am a Camera (a fine, neglected play, obliterated when it became the source of the musical Cabaret).

He, too, treats the lives around him as raw material for some future literary work; he, too, makes copious notes that are pressed into service as dramatic exposition -- though, since we live in a more theatrically complicated time, Francois' notes (actually they're diary entries) are less straightforward, more compromised in their effects, than Christopher's ever were. To put it another way, Francois isn't as nice a guy and this is not as nice a play. (That's a description, not a value judgment.)

As Chris with Sally Bowles, Francois is fascinated -- part amused, part appalled -- by Johanne's sexuality. Her body is what defines her for Mario and -- dangerously -- for herself. Whatever else may have been in their relationship has been destroyed by the loss of their son, an event that both have tried, and failed, to blot out; the memory keeps resurfacing in shouts and tears.

Johanne makes her own dresses, experiments with the colour of her hair and -- under protest -- the size of her breasts. (Mario now says they're too big, though one doubts he always thought so.)

The play takes place in suffocating heat, during Johanne's summer break, and it unfolds in a series of short, sharp scenes, separated by bursts of pounding rock. This gives it a clinical air, as if Francois' detachment had seeped into its bones.

The action is sexually explicit, and legitimately so; the sex is necessary information. The visual rawness also contrasts tellingly with Johanne's crucial and rapturous account of a night of passion, spent with a stranger at the notorious Motel Helene. The ending is stark, and also logical, but not altogether satisfying; there is a disjunction between what we are told and what we actually feel.

Nearly all the action takes place in Johanne's kitchen, which doubles as a living room and is indeed very lived in. The domestic detail in Jackie Maxwell's production is superb; everything is in place, down to a trio of books that Francois brings Johanne as holiday reading at the beginning of the play, and that lie there unopened for the duration.

The script, adapted (this may mean roughened up) by Judith Thompson from a literal translation by Morwyn Brebner, whizzes compellingly by, though it also goes in for literary flights that the actors have trouble integrating with the rest of their performances. Tony Nappo's physically convincing Mario has recurring attacks of deadness. Francois' narrative fluency (very 1950s American) defeats Brandon McGibbon, but his conversational reticence comes over very well.

Jane Spidell is similarly in and out of the central character of Johanne. When she's out, she's telling us about her emotions, without much nuance; when she's in, she's perfect. The core of the character is there, a battered pride, and the look is wonderfully right.

Climbing into the World Stage ring after the two French-Canadian heavyweights, Lepage and Tremblay, Boucher can hardly compete with the former but easily outpaces the latter, in the way that salt can generally conquer sugar.
