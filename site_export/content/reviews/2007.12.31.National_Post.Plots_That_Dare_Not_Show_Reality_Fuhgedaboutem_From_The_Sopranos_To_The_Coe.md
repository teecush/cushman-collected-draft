Letitia Prism, the Victorian governess immortalized in Oscar Wilde's The Importance of Being Earnest, once wrote a three-volume novel. Few ever got to read it because she absent-mindedly confused the manuscript with the baby she was minding at the time, depositing the former in a perambulator and the latter, famously, in a handbag. This mischance condemned her to the ranks of the great unread, but it didn't stop her from firmly enunciating an aesthetic credo. Asked how her novel ended she replied, "The good ended happily, the bad unhappily. That is what fiction means."

And that is what it did mean, for centuries. There were some variations allowed, depending on what kind of piece you were writing. In Shakespeare's comedies -- the pop hits of their day -- justice was dispensed strictly on the Prism principle. His tragedies were more, shall we say, realistic. The bad still ended unhappily, but so very noticeably did the good, or at least the more prominent among them. A few virtuous people were permitted to survive, to clear up the bodies and to suggest the persistence of a moral order in the universe.

This, though, was not enough for the puritan critics of that or subsequent ages. One in the next generation, contemplating the cruel ends of some of Shakespeare's loveliest ladies, asked "in that case, what profiteth it us to be virtuous?" It's a good question, to which no one has ever come up with a good answer. If literature and drama say anything about virtue, it is that it had better be its own reward.

Our own pop culture has a long tradition of denying this. The Hollywood Code laid it down that virtue should always be triumphant, or at the least that crime should never be seen to pay. Television paid implicit allegiance to the same Code. In recent years, though, the cage has been rattled and this year it has shattered. The TV event of 2007 was the finale of The Sopranos. The best-regarded movie was No Country for Old Men. And in both, the arch-criminal walks away.

It would be a stretch perhaps to say that Tony Soprano ends happily. His world has contracted, there are criminal charges in his future that he'll find difficult to beat, his friends and colleagues are either alienated or dead. Nevertheless, last thing we see, he's still alive and eating with his family, even though the series' tone, and all our expectations of the gangster genre, have led us to expect a violent ending in which he may very well perish. The deal was: "You die, and we'll forgive you your sins, and we'll forgive ourselves for liking you." Well, it worked for James Cagney; his was a black-and-white world in which the black was more exciting but was also condemned. The Sopranos is a crime story told almost entirely from the criminals' point of view, a theme on which the best subsequent HBO series have been playing variations. The Wire alternates cops with robbers, suggesting there's little to choose between them; Deadwood mixed them up almost indistinguishably. Showtime's Dexter is a sympathetic serial-killer serial. And this has filtered through to other networks -- the most likeable characters in Prison Break are the ones behind bars, not all of them unjustly. It's a grey, grey world.

No Country for Old Men presents a black one. Once Hollywood would have soft-pedalled Cormac McCarthy's novel or ignored it entirely. The Coen brothers take it literally, with bleak relish. The character we identify with is morally ambiguous; he's also brutally - - but casually -- killed off. Much the same thing happened in Hitchcock's Psycho, but this time it happens near the end of the picture, leaving us no time to readjust, and there are no good people to avenge him. There are decent folks, but they're ineffectual. The villain meanwhile is as close as the movies have come to presenting cold, unredeemable evil. And he gets away, wounded but alive. Maybe justice will catch up with him somewhere down the road, but there's a strong suggestion that he's invincible. And there's naught for our comfort; he isn't even charming.

Wilde's Lady Bracknell, who did get to read Miss Prism's novel, pronounced it a work "of more than usually revolting sentimentality." If nothing else, our new works are an escape from decades, maybe centuries, of having to view the world through Prism's prism. Lady B might well have found the ambiguities of The Sopranos disturbing, but she would have been right at home with the bleakness of No Country. It could have been her country. She was, after all, "a monster without being a myth. Which is rather unfair." Like life.
