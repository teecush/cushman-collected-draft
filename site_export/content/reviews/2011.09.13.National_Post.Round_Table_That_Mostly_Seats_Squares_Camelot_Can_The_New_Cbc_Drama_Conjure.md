Im not sure whether it's due to thoughtlessness, ignorance or plain old cashing-in, but Camelot, television's latest stab at domesticating the King Arthur legends, shares a title with the celebrated musical on the same subject. The two treatments do not, on the evidence of the first two TV episodes, have much else in common; though they do share a conception of Arthur himself as a starry-eyed idealist intent on bringing not only order and chivalry but a democratic spirit to the realm.

In the musical, human nature, as expressed in both love and politics, gets tragically in the way. Its too early to say whether the new Arthur (Jamie Campbell Bower), who looks and behaves like a male ingenue of 16, will be faced with the same challenges as he matures, or even if he will mature at all. I will just say cautiously that if youre an Arthurian fan who's also in search of literate writing, imaginative production and threedimensional acting, you'd be better advised to buy tickets for this years Stratford festival.

Not that this material hasnt always been up for grabs. Its hero is a supposedly ancient British monarch whose canonical treatment - Thomas Malory's Morte d'Arthur - didnt come along until the 15th century. It was born anachronistic. The TV Camelot, like most of its predecessors, is set in a hybrid of post-Roman Britain and medieval no-mans-land.

It starts by hewing tolerably close to Malory. King Uther Pendragon falls down dead. Merlin (a skin-headed Joseph Fiennes), who seems to be a mixture of court doctor and visionary counsellor with just possibly a bit of sorcery on the side, rushes off to reclaim Uther's son Arthur whom he stole when he was a baby, to be brought safely up by gruff old Sir Ector.

Arthur shyly accepts his destiny, attended by his devoted foster-brother Kay, whom he subsequently makes marshal of the kingdom. This is a departure, since in Malory Arthur starts out as a bit of a Cinderella, with Kay as his bullying Ugly Brother, a disposition that he maintains even after his promotion. Making Kay a faceless good guy seems rather less interesting.

On the other hand, the witch-like Morgan le Fay (Eva Green, much given to flaunting herself, vocally and physically), who in most versions malevolently haunts the story's outskirts, has been pulled firmly into its centre. Shes Arthurs half-sister by his father's first wife, and very jealous, though with occasional redemptive moments. Morgan seems to have sold her soul to a wolf, who presumably represents yet darker powers, and she goes into convulsions that make her look like a character from True Blood. She allies herself, in and out of bed, with King Lot, a warlord (note the timely terminology) with no redeeming features at all.

It seems for a time that there's going to be no Excalibur in this version, but eventually Arthur climbs a cliff to dislodge what Merlin calls the sword of the gods as opposed to just having it handed to him by what Monty Python and the Holy Grail, for which I often felt nostalgic, called "some bint in a river." That description would do very well for this shows Guinevere; at least we first see her emerging from a seaside dip. Arthur sees her too, and they are playfully attracted to one another. Turns out, though, that shes betrothed to one of his loyalist followers, which plainly points to breakers ahead.

The knight's name is not, as you might expect, Lancelot (there's no one of that name in sight) but Leontes, which suggests a displaced riff on the jealous king from Shakespeares The Winters Tale.

In principle, there's nothing wrong with any of these variations. In practice, though, they seem to have been drawn from a grab bag of perioddrama cliches, and written by a committee of the tin-eared. The first line is King Uther, there's a visitor; she wont give her name and it doesnt improve. When it isn't flat, it's pompous: "Leave now and salvage your life," Morgan tells Arthur: what's wrong with save?

Maybe a few more Canadian actors might have helped; Canadians these days get more experience in the classics than British actors and do them at least as well. Camelot, like The Tudors, is a Canadian coproduction (CBC, no less) but you wouldn't know it.

Actually, for all my carping, I prefer it to The Tudors, not to mention The Borgias; it gets itself fewer airs. It starts with the customary blanket warning - or promise - regarding "nudity, sexuality, violence and coarse language," but as it proceeds, it tells us which of these appetizing ingredients will appear in a given segment. So, depending on our preferences, you can stay glued or wait until after the next commercial break.
