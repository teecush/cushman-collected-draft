IN PERSON"In a generation of writers stridently clear about what they are against if about nothing else, Christopher Hampton stands out as one of nature's liberals. His latest play, which deals with his own childhood, paints a portrait of a vulnerable survivor Hampton gets personal

Tuesday, March 19, 1991

ROBERT CUSHMAN

London ENGLAND -- BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail London CHRISTOPHER Hampton's biggest success as a playwright has been the stage version of Les Liaisons Dangereuses, which, being based on somebody else's novel, is of necessity a rather impersonal work. His subsequent and latest play, however, makes full amends. White Chameleon, just opened at the National Theatre in London, deals quite explicitly with its author's childhood.

That childhood was spent in various parts of the globe. Hampton's father was an engineer working for the British company Cable and Wireless, which moved its employees around as if they were minor diplomats. The Hampton family's most fateful posting was to Alexandria, which is where the 10-year-old Christopher found himself at the time of the 1956 Suez crisis. He had a front-row seat for the death of the British Empire.

These two themes - the beginning of a life, the end of an era - are superimposed, one upon the other, in Hampton's play, and they make a neat fit. Only in scenes devoted to the onset of the protagonist's playwriting instincts does another drama seem to be flailing about inside. This is, of course, a constant danger in autobiographical drama - things happen because they happened - compounded when the subject is also the narrator. In White Chameleon an excellent actor, David Birkin, plays Christopher as a child while an even better actor, Tom Wilkinson, plays his reminiscing older self and doubles as the father reminisced about.

A caveat in the program warns us against taking the self-portrait too literally, but Hampton confirms that all the incidents in the play are true, although some have been enhanced and reshuffled.

Central are experiences of being bullied on two continents: reviled by Egyptian schoolmates as "filthy English" and, after repatriation, by British schoolmates as "wog-lover." The incidents balance, like most elements of the play, and they leave their victim essentially unscathed and unembittered. This, too, seems to be true to life; in person he appears the most equable of dramatists. In his stage persona, he muses that "these symmetrical assaults made a valuable contribution to my political education," and adds, "I've been wary of every known brand of confident certainty ever since." A playwright contemporary advised Hampton to cut this line, on the grounds that there is far too much of that kind of pronouncement going around these days. But he protested that he was not following a weak-kneed fashion of the 1990s. He had always felt that way.

In fact, in a generation of writers stridently clear about what they are against if about nothing else, Hampton stands out as one of nature's liberals. But talk of generations can be misleading. As he put it during an interview in London, "it all depends when you started."

He began very young. In White Chameleon, the elder Hampton, hearing of his son's literary ambitions, advises him to waste no time, citing in support writers obviously dredged up from his own boyhood: Arthur Conan Doyle, H. Rider Haggard, Robert Louis Stevenson. None of them, apparently, wasted any time. This is true to life: Despite the fact that "there was never a book in the house," Hampton's father was very encouraging. He died in 1965, a year before his son, still an undergraduate at Oxford, saw his first play performed at London's Royal Court Theatre. Subsequently, Hampton joined the Court as resident writer.

The theatre produced four more of his plays, including Total Eclipse, about Rimbaud and Verlaine, and The Philanthropist, a modern comedy of academic manners that shows the benefit of Hampton's own scholarship. He majored in modern languages, and The Philanthropist is a systematic inversion of Moliere's The Misanthrope, beginning with the title. Its hero defines himself with the memorably Hamptonian line, "I'm a man of no convictions; at least, I think I am."

The Court, the theatre that pioneered the right to fail, was a prickly environment for the perennially successful Hampton. He tells of the directorate watching the previews of The Philanthropist in mounting dismay, finally deciding that it had to be faced, the trouble was the play. It opened, and fulfilled their worst fears. It was a success, transferring to the West End for a marathon run that prefigured that of Les Liaisons (which ran for years in London, did very well in New York and is now playing in Toronto at The Elgin). Hampton said he left the Court with a definite feeling that he had let down the school. Again.

Hampton believes in the importance of "choosing the right moment to sit down and write a play." Maybe it is letting the moment choose you. He acknowledges that "the theatre is more subject to fashion than other literary forms," and quotes Stephen Frears, who directed Dangerous Liaisons, the film version of Choderlos de Laclos's novel, as thinking that the play "hit its moment. It spoke to the selfishness of the 1980s. It would have failed in the seventies."

Hampton has fallen foul of fashion in his time. "There was a woolly moment for me at the end of the seventies. It was a period when the most interesting work took up a forceful position about social issues." He liked some of these "drift-toward-fascism plays," and their authors were more or less his own age - Howard Barker, Howard Brenton, David Edgar, David Hare. But their sensibility - and it's a matter of sensibility rather than opinion - was more radical than his. He turned to writing film scripts, mostly unproduced, and "kept in touch with the theatre through translation."

He did versions of Ibsen, Moliere and, most significantly, Odon von Horvath, a prewar Austrian dramatist rediscovered by a new generation of German writers as an antidote to the ideological certainties of Brecht. Hampton eventually put him on stage in his own right in Tales from Hollywood. In this, Hampton's favourite among his own plays, Horvath is reprieved from his historical death and brought to California among the movieland population of European emigres. He serves a useful structural purpose in linking characters - Brecht and the brothers Thomas and Heinrich Mann - who were rarely on speaking terms. But so warmly does Hampton identify with his weary scepticism that Horvath takes over the play, making it Hampton's most obviously personal prior to White Chameleon. In general, the fugitive Austrian, who was killed when a tree fell on him in the Champs Elysees, emerges as a very English kind of loser.

So does the Christopher of White Chameleon, against all the odds of his real-life success (and he has a fine house with a spectacular view of the Thames to prove it). All right, not a loser. The Christopher of the play, who speaks of his own "optimistic temperament," is more a vulnerable survivor.

It's an attractive stand, and not unlike Tom Stoppard's. The difference is that Stoppard's plays, however serious, are always farces, while Hampton's are essentially drawing-room comedies. (No disparagement is intended in either case. Both are perfectly valid genres.) This gives Hampton's a slighter flavour; even Liaisons is less metallically stylish than it would probably like to be. But they are intricately, elegantly patterned; and they wear well.
