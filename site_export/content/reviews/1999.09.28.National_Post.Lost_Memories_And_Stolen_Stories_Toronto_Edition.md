THE DRAWER BOY

Theatre Passe Muraille

David Fox, playing Angus in Michael Healey's The Drawer Boy -- back in town with its original cast -- has a large, craggy face with a steep forehead and jug-handle ears. It's a lunar landscape, lit up by grins that come and go in constant succession. Innocent though it is, it's also a used face, suggesting that long ago its owner got into fights.

That's quite apart from what we learn actually happened to this Canadian architect-turned-farmer years ago in the London Blitz, when the fallout from an air raid took off part of his head, leaving a gap through which, as his comrade Morgan so vividly tells it, "his memory escaped." Angus cannot now hold a thought for more than a second at a time, making him, from an actor's point of view, both a gift and a trap; the role is the kind from which the performer (think of Dustin Hoffman in Rain Man) usually reaps more respect than the character.

Fox, however, never patronizes the man he's playing, never begs for sympathy; Angus lives in the moment and Fox fills out each moment with massive gentleness and with the kind of sharp, unhesitating candour that comes from having no defences. He maps out the limits of Angus' consciousness, and then -- as he responds to new and unexpected pressures -- shows them expanding to take in bewilderment and even anger.

Gentleness also underlies Jerry Franken's Morgan, though his surface is rasping, sardonic and watchful, worn down from years of running the farm and caring for the friend whom he both loves and resents, and for whom he feels, in two senses, responsible.

The story of these two men and their wartime girlfriends -- the tale that Morgan tells to Angus every night, of the drawer boy and the farmer and the tall girl and the taller girl -- has already become part of Canadian theatrical mythology. Healey has found words for it that both fit the moment and resonate beyond it. He is almost equally skilful at developing the relationship when events force it out of its accustomed groove, and the two have to find a new way of living together.

Their partnership, impressive as it is (and dramatically unique as far as I know), is only half the play. The other half comes from their involvement with Miles (Tom Barnett), the young actor who comes to research his role in a collective show about farming, and who appropriates their story as a means of padding out his own role. Seeing him rehearse it reopens the hole in Angus' brain and sets off the changes in him that give the play its second act.

The bringing together of these two worlds seems more contrived on a second viewing, but the benefits remain substantial. We get Miles' impersonation of Daisy the stressed-out cow, desperate to give milk in order to avoid being sent to the slaughter -- a performance, we learn, that leaves his friends back at the collective deeply unimpressed. (Hence his determination to secure his place in the troupe by rendering Morgan's nightly monologue; as he says, like Daisy, like the farmers themselves, he has to produce or die.)

We get Miles telling Angus, once the dam in Angus' mind has burst, the story of Hamlet with special reference to his own performance of the title role, and his hearer's fascinated reception of it; this scene is worked up with delightful slyness, though it strains credibility when Angus later goes around talking about his own "antic disposition."

We get Morgan's brisk and bitter lecture on the economics of farming with Angus, an idiot savant where figures are concerned, muttering instant statistics in the background. We get Morgan's merciless practical joking at the city-boy Miles' expense, and the way in which Miles neatly and subtly turns the tables at the end.

Performance apart, the play remains remarkable for the ease of its dialogue (a few literary awkwardnesses aside) and the craft of its construction. It also offers the attractions of a well- burnished theatrical mirror. The Farm Show, the 1970s project to which The Drawer Boy tips its hat, was the creation of the Theatre Passe Muraille, whose Toronto stage now houses the play. Fox was a member of that company; so was the present director, Miles (note the name) Potter.

The Farm Show was intended as an instrument of social change; The Drawer Boy, more modestly but no less ambitiously, depicts it as the accidental cue for an individual transformation. It does so by placing a tall story, which we believe when we hear it, inside a taller story, which by natural progression we also accept. (It's at least as likely as having your stepfather reveal himself as a murderer by sitting him down in front of an old play to which you've contributed additional dialogue.)

Theatre paying tribute to itself can be bloated and embarrassing. This example, with much else on its mind and in its heart, is intimate, funny and touching.
