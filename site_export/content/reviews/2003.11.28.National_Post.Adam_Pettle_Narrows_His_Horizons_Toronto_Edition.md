MOSLEY AND ME

Berkeley Street Theatre, Toronto

- - -

When I first heard that Adam Pettle's new play was to be called Mosley and Me, I fancied that it might concern somebodys encounter with the old leader of the British Union of Fascists. That would be a good challenging subject for a nice Jewish boy playwright, especially one whose plays are now being done over there. It turns out, though, that on this occasion Pettle is not broadening his horizons but narrowing them. The play, set in a bagel shop in Montreal, is the latest to have reached the boards with his name attached -- and, though it has its occasional charms, much the least interesting.

The plays first-person character -- he doesn't actually narrate it, but he does top-and-tail it -- is Nathan Mintz, whose family has been making and selling bagels for generations. He is an unwilling inheritor; he studied creative writing at university because, he says, "that's where the big money is," (hollow laughter from a substantial part of the house), and he still dreams of a career in screenwriting.

Johnny Mosley is his new assistant -- for baking, not writing, though he has an influence on the latter as well; he has, after all, inspired Nathans narrative. Johnny is an ex-con and a Gentile. (Though, as Nathan explains, bagels arent really Jewish in origin; they're Austrian, like Hitler. They're certainly stateless now.) The two men have one thing in common. They are both crazy for poker. Nathan has a regular game going in the back of the shop, at which Johnny soon becomes a valued player.

Nathan can be a something of a schlemiel. He thinks, for example, that Guys and Dolls is the greatest movie musical ever made. (The show, yes; but the movie?) But hes still, to make a connection that we can hardly help humming along with, good old reliable Nathan, though subject to fits of insecurity and temper. Johnny, it turns out, will go recklessly and maybe ruinously for any kind of gambling. Initially, he has a beneficent effect on the bagel business -- which has been, figuratively speaking, in the hole -- and he helps restore Nathans morale about his writing. But the differences between the two assert themselves, and there is a scam.

Johnny (Mosley) and Nathan (Me) are all of the shop and all of the play. The other poker players are described and spoken of, never seen; ergo, we never see a game (though play and production do add a chapter, or at least a footnote, to the history of theatrical realism by showing us Nathan and Johnny actually making bagels). I confess that I usually fall asleep when forced to watch a card game, either in real life or on stage or screen. The British play Dealer's Choice was an invigorating exception, but that put a whole complex dynamic before our eyes, the kind of thing that Mosley and Me only attempts at second hand. Just hearing a game described, either before or after it happens and even when there is cheating involved, turns out to be even more enervating than witnessing one.

The friendship the play describes is sketchily portrayed, so its threatened dissolution seems like no particular threat. It jerks the play partially to life but not completely.

Vikki Anderson's production (for her own company, DVxT) may be too grandiose for the small story within it. I know people said the same thing about Pettles last play, Sunday Father, but that play was big enough to look after itself, and it dealt far more wittily and suggestively with the Jew-Gentile contrast, which here is surprisingly lacking in flavour. The director is also the designer; a couple of dirty beige slabs summon the basic setting well enough, but when, quite late in the game, they move around, they seem to be echoing the plays own sudden rush into action without much helping it. The relevance of the huge 17th-century painting that hangs, half- obscured, behind one of the flats is something I am still trying to figure out.

The casting, though, could hardly be bettered. Mosley is one of Randy Hughson's less blustery performances, combining a hulking strangeness -- it isn't exactly menace -- with what seems to be an innocently inquiring warmth. When the screws are turned on him, or at least when he talks about it, his body and his personality seem to tighten, so that what previously seemed genial expansiveness gathers into a single forceful stream of desperation.

Alex Poch-Goldin, who gets better at each appearance, is his opposite: a man who tries to be guarded and defensive, but is actually wide open. The Montreal neighbourhood, unknown to me, in which the bakery is situated, is called Mile End. This is also one of the East End neighbourhoods that would have been one of the late Sir Oswald Mosleys stomping grounds in the 1930s (a London equivalent of Torontos Christie Pits), so maybe there's a connection after all.
