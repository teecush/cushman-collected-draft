There are some puzzles attached to Last Days of Graceland at Theatre Passe Muraille. One is, what is the play supposed to be about? Another is, what is it doing on a main Toronto stage? A third is, why is it being done so poorly?

The first query is perhaps disingenuous. Franca Miraglia, the author, makes it clear at the end of the evening, and at various points on the way, that the subjects on her mind are love and forgiveness, and that these are things of which she is in favour. So, probably the question should shift to that of why she has chosen to deliver this unobjectionable message in the way that she has, and especially to why she has set it where she does. The Graceland of the title is the one you thought it was, Elviss mansion; he isn't mentioned by name but his music is occasionally heard and his image appears once on the sleeve of an old LP that someone is brandishing. The place has been bought by a very rich unseen man, referred to (by an equally unseen, though obviously not unheard, public service announcer) as our imperial host, Kwan Wah Heen. Mr. Heen sounds like a piece of work: "surrounded by expensive wealth" according to the announcer (which, if I heard it right, must be the play's one intentional laugh-line). Before he closes the place for good and all, he has invited the public to come take a last look at it; 10 lucky visitors, chosen by lottery, will also get to cart off some memorabilia.

We focus on two of these pilgrims. One is Sandra, a young woman who has until recently been plying the specialized trade of "professional eulogist" -- that is, she writes and delivers nice speeches at the funerals of people she has never met. The other is Robert, a somewhat older science teacher (and, he rather ruefully tells us, after-school drama coach) whose still older male lover is dying.

It turns out that the meeting of Robert and Sandra is more than coincidence. Even before the mechanical details are revealed, we have gathered, with the assistance of copious flashbacks, that they both have problems with their nearest but not necessarily dearest. Sandra has long been estranged from her father, and the accumulated fallout has soured her on the eulogizing business. An admired author she was hired to glorify turned out to have more clay in his shoes than she could comfortably handle; confusingly, the play begins with her tribute to him, making us wonder whether we have wandered into the wrong stately home. Robert has issues around a girl in his past life and a boy in his present and maybe future one, plus the memory of a terminally ill younger brother on whom he turned his back; four separate people in his memories ask him to come to bed -- its his personal leitmotiv -- though the motivation behind the requests tends to vary. These back stories are uniformly banal, especially in the snippet form in which we receive them here. Patricia Vanstones production seems, to begin with, reasonably spry in its pacing, but it soon resolves into an awkward affair of bringing phantom characters on from the wings, spotlighting them behind screens, or -- the ultimate clunkiness --pushing those screens laboriously around to make room for the next unrevealing revelation.

So: Sandra is cured of her spikiness, Robert stops beating up on himself, and it seems they have one another to thank. The mechanics of bringing this about could hardly be more contrived; and what's Graceland got to do with it? The play has nothing to say about Elvis and hardly anything about his fans. I suppose there are meant to be symbolic overtones in the name itself, plus some intimations about hanging on to the past or letting go of it --it's unclear which course the play is advocating. Maybe it wants us to have a bit of both.

Apart from its physical liabilities, the direction is almost completely unsuccessful in getting credible work from its cast. Supporting actors, including a couple with good track records, either overplay remorselessly or retire defeated. Of the two leads, Catherine Bruhiers Sandra is just awful: wooden and strident. Jim Joness Robert is this Graceland's nearest approach to a saving grace; he can play anxiety without seizing up, and seems alone among the cast in not finding his lines literally unspeakable. I'll spare you the details of the uplifting final image; I'll merely say that the evening is a rocky road leading to a vat of syrup. This has not been a good season for new Canadian drama.
