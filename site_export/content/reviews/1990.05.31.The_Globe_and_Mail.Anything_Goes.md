ANYTHING GOES

Thursday, May 31, 1990

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Music by Cole Porter Directed by Jerry Zaks Starring Mitzi Gaynor At O'Keefe Centre, Toronto Time-tripping on a musical luxury liner BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail ANYTHING Goes, which opened at Toronto's OKeefe Centre on Tuesday, is the quintessential thirties musical. The Cole Porter score, studded with standards, has a lot to do with this, but the shows main attraction may be its setting. It mostly happens on a luxury liner making the transatlantic run. Here are the rich and frivolous, Porter's people, in their natural habitat and with very little chance of getting out of it. Something about the holiday mode makes the transition from speech to song seem very natural. Besides, Art Deco nautical can be a very attractive style.

This production is a travelling spin-off of the quick and confident version, directed by Jerry Zaks, that played successfully at New York's Lincoln Centre two seasons ago. Tony Waltons set is a symphony in whites, blues and creams against which his costumes, with flashes of scarlet on the passenger list cutting across the resplendent white ducks of the crew, fairly dazzle. This is the most painless revival of a pre-war musical that I have seen.

Anything Goes - written by Guy Bolton and P.G. Wodehouse, revised by Howard Lindsay and Russel Crouse, re-revised by Timothy Crouse and John Weidman - has the reputation of being a "silly" musical. Actually, it is a very good farce, far worthier of serious attention than many intended heavyweights. The set incorporates a line of ceaselessly swinging doors, and there is always a perfectly timed surprise waiting behind them.

The story centres on Billy Crocker, a dashing young Wall Street broker who defies his employer to stow away in pursuit of his beloved who is destined for marriage with a silly-ass Englishman (the Wodehouse-Bolton touch) who is turn pursued by Reno Sweeney, a nightclub evangelist (you heard) who also has a soft spot for Billy as does a bumbling gangster who is pursued by just about everybody. As is Billy.

Are we all clear, so far?

The high point comes when Billy is himself mistaken for a prominent mobster - Public Enemy Number One - and feted by his celebrity-crazed fellow travellers. As he says, if he had just been a stowaway, they would have put him in irons. Pertinent satire in the 1930s, this still strikes a chord in a world less loveably confused than the one whose topsy-turviness Porter mocked and celebrated in his title song.

Apart from that hymn to amorality, the score includes I Get a Kick Out of You, the greatest of hard-boiled love-songs, and Youre the Top, the nonpareil of list-songs, plus such minor classics as the yearning ballad, All Through the Night (which, typically Porter, depicts frustration rather than loss) and Reno's rouser Blow, Gabriel, Blow. To these five we may now add Easy to Love, which fits very well, perhaps because it was written for the show in the first place. It was cut because the 1934 Billy found it too taxing. Other interpolations pad the evening out excessively but at least the selectors have had the grace to draw them only from scores that Porter wrote, as he did this one, for Ethel Merman.

Here looms a problem. There are, apparently, no more Mermans. In olden days an actress oughta Know just how to sing Cole Porter But now, God knows, Anything goes. Old ingenues may do it sweeter Than divas who sang Evita Right through their nose - Anything goes. At Lincoln Centre, Patti LuPone's Reno played a sour Brechtian toughie rather than a Broadway broad, honking the lyrics - in this of all shows - into inaudible oblivion. Mitzi Gaynor, starring in very large lettering and a long solo curtain-call, has far better diction but the tone defeats her; she cannot sustain the brass for more than a couple of phrases at a time and lapses into metallic harshness. Merman spent her career doing what came naturally; her successors have to work at it. Gaynors natural mode is apple-cheeked healthiness - essentially, she is still playing Nellie Forbush in South Pacific - and when she tries for Renos straightforward sexuality, she becomes aggressively coy. She still has great legs, though whenever Tom Mahoney's crisp choreography really hots up, she makes a tactful exit through one of the doors and leaves the chorus boys to get on with it. At the number's climax, she returns in an outfit cut even more arrestingly away than before and carries on, not altogether inappropriately, like a manic Tammy Faye Bakker.

Billy is even more of a problem, a mixture of collegiate charming and Broadway brash. Scott Stevensen doesnt exactly torture Easy to Love, but he sounds as if its torturing him. In fact, nobody sings very well but there are plenty of good comic roles, and lines, to compensate. Robert Nichols as musical comedy's eternal crumpled crook (Public Enemy No. 13) heads a valuable echelon of revitalized types. Most important, the music and the comedy reinforce one another. The evening begins with Porter's own brittle rendition of the title song fading into the orchestras modern tribute to him, and the double focus is maintained throughout. This is us looking, with delighted but irreverent respect, at them. So though its not a great revival Still anything this alive'll Make quite a show - Everyone - go.
