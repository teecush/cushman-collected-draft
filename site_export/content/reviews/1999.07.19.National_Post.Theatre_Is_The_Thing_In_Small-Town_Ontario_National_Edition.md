Toronto is, or so it keeps telling us, the third most important theatrical city in North America, but -- by some quirk of fate or the tourist industry -- Canada's two most prestigious theatres lie well outside the city. In 1953 it was decided that a town called Stratford -- one standing on a River Avon, no less -- just naturally had to have its own Shakespeare festival. So, from an initial short season of two plays, the current Stratford Festival has grown into a seven-month festival, spread over three theatres, and possessing the de facto status of a national theatre. Like all national theatres, it attracts reverence and revulsion in equal -- and, in all fairness, equally unmerited -- proportions.

Meanwhile, in another part of Ontario, the second greatest British dramatist has been allotted his own playground. There are no ties of name or fame linking Bernard Shaw to Niagara-on-the-Lake but, 30 years ago, an enthusiast decided to stage a couple of his plays there, and that idea, too, has developed into a full-scale triple-staged festival.

Neither theatre is exclusively committed to the canon of its patron saint. Niagara confines itself, by self-chosen mandate, to plays written during Shaw's lifetime. (Next year they're expanding to include plays set in that time-span, but written later.) Since Shaw, the prophet of longevity, lived for nearly one hundred years, from the mid-19th century to that of the 20th, they have a wide -- and still pretty accessible -- field to choose from. It's far narrower, though, than Stratford's; the whole world repertoire is, theoretically, their oyster; and it must be agonizing wondering where to start. Not unnaturally, a season at Niagara will always appear more homogeneous than a season at Stratford.

There are other reasons for this. Both theatres have a reliable core of actors who return there year after year, but the permanent Shaw ensemble is larger and more faithful. (I should add that the kind of continuity you find at both companies would be the envy of the institutional theatres in Britain, and is unheardof in the United States.) There is a pervasive note of intelligent urbanity at Shaw, stemming both from the personality and the philosophy of its director Christopher Newton. For a director primarily concerned with very loquacious plays, Newton has a rich, painterly visual sense, a quality shared by his most regular colleagues, Jim Mezon and Neil Munro. All three of them -- like Stratford's director, Richard Monette -- began their careers as actors, and they still appear occasionally or, in Mezon's case, frequently.

There are two Shaw plays in this season's repertoire, both discussion pieces, both imaginatively presented. Heartbreak House, in some respects Shaw's masterpiece, is this year's flagship production on the main Festival stage, given a somewhat expressionist production by a visiting Polish director, Tadeusz Bradecki. The play's subject, according to its author, is "cultured, leisured Europe before the [First World] War," oblivious of the coming cataclysm and represented, somewhat whimsically, by an eccentric English family, presided over by a prophetic, absent- minded and extremely ancient mariner, composed of flirts and dreamers and confronted by cheats and money-grubbers who prove to be almost equally ineffectual. After three acts of finely orchestrated verbal music, the play concludes in the cacophony of an air raid, summoned up with majestic force. Douglas Rain makes a lightweight job of Captain Shotover, Shaw's home-brewed King Lear, but Sarah Orenstein is both commanding and bewitching as his number one daughter.

The same actress also dominates Getting Married, potentially Shaw's most tiresome play, an unabashed gabfest that is dated in many of its details. Some of it, though, remains pertinent; and Jim Mezon, directing at the small Court House Theatre, has cunningly staged the whole thing as an extended verbal dance. Individual performances vary markedly but the ensemble effect is excellent.

For some years now the Festival has been exploring the plays of Shaw's friend and contemporary Harley Granville Barker. This year they have arrived at his most substantial piece, The Madras House. Like Getting Married, this is less a story than a series of dialogues on relations between the sexes; like all Barker's plays it is more demanding than Shaw's, less funny, more complex and elusive, in many ways more modern. Neil Munro has directed it, barring a few gaffes, with great sensitivity to its musical theme-and-variations quality, and he gets especially good performances from an old Shaw regular, Michael Ball, and a newer one, Blair Williams.

The same director is responsible for the Festival's most successful all-round production this year, the 1930s Broadway hit You Can't Take It With You, by Moss Hart and George S. Kaufman, in which a joyous cast revolves around a wonderfully understated central performance by Bernard Beh- rens: a rare case at Niagara of a leading role being entrusted to an actor completely new to the company. Another play about a crazy escapist family (it actually owes a lot to Heartbreak House though it takes a far more benevolent view of the phenomenon it depicts) it is the kind of sturdy middlebrow piece in which a crack company, nurtured on sterner stuff, can reveal unsuspected riches. It leaves the repertory this week, so hurry.

The Shaw Festival extends the same unpatronizing care to the thrillers and musicals that nestle at the edges of its repertoire: this year Rebecca, and A Foggy Day, the latter being a cheerful if somewhat haphazard collection of Gershwin songs gathered around the plot of an old Fred Astaire movie.

This show's director, Kelly Robinson, is also responsible for this season's major musical, West Side Story, and the contrast is instructive. Musicals at Niagara are modest crowd-pleasers (A Foggy Day is back for its second year); at Stratford they are cash-cows, produced on a large scale and performed mainly by specialists -- which means a splintering within the company.

This year that price is probably worth paying; West Side Story is an exceptionally exciting production, probably the best musical Stratford has done. A new and smaller show, Dracula -- a sort of mini megamusical -- has closer casting ties to the rest of the season, but has little else to recommend it.

This is magic year at Stratford: Macbeth, one of this season's heroes, speaks of "supernatural soliciting" and a firm of supernatural solicitors seems to have advised on the choice of repertoire.

Dracula is one example; another is Ben Jonson's The Alchemist, a very great and very difficult Jacobean comedy that here unfolds in an undisciplined sprawl across the main Festival stage; it might have done better in the more concentrated environment of the Tom Patterson, Stratford's third and smallest theatre. Three of this year's four Shakespeares are fairy stories.

A Midsummer Night's Dream, being both dauntingly delicate and wearisomely familiar, is the hardest of all plays to make fresh, and Richard Monette's production hasn't managed it. The Tempest has Canada's pre-eminent Shakespearean, William Hutt, as Prospero, a valedictory performance in the greatest of valedictory roles. It's stirring as an event and as an interpretation, and something at least of Hutt's virtuosity conveys itself to the rest of Monette's production. Diana Leblanc's small-scale production of Macbeth has attracted a mysterious amount of hostility, and it does have its oddities; but it is immensely exciting with many scenes of stunning immediacy, among them Martha Henry's account of Lady Macbeth's sleep- walking.

This actress also directs the year's remaining Shakespeare, an extremely intelligent Richard II with a king (Geordie Johnson) who has his limitations but is very effective within them.

That leaves two shows: a dramatization of Pride and Prejudice, which is pretty but pointless, and -- at the opposite extreme -- Glenn, a taxing but rewarding collage in which four actors split the life and personality of Glenn Gould between them, and the audience tries to put it together again.

Stratford is patchy where Shaw is smooth; a distinction that says something about the two festivals' given policies, something about their methods of implementing them, and something, too, about the qualities of Shakespeare and Shaw themselves, qualities that seem to communicate themselves to the other plays that come into their respective orbits. It interests me that the two finest productions I have seen at Stratford in recent years have been Diana Leblanc's versions of Eugene O'Neill's Long Day's Journey into Night and Anton Chekhov's The Cherry Orchard, two shows possessed of a depth and turbulence that I would be surprised to encounter in Niagara though both plays fall within that theatre's mandate. Anyway, we need both; we need heroic emotion and we need heroic wit, and we need them both on the continuing basis that only organizations on this scale can provide.
