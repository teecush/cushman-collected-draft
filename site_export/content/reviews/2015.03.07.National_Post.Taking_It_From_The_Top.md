I have now seen Once twice. The Broadway-Irish musical was first in Toronto in its New York production, touring edition. It's now returned, but with a specially recruited all-Canadian cast. Honours between the two versions are more or less even. What was good about the show remains excellent. What was flawed stands out more sharply.

To begin at the beginning. Once may have the most charming opening of any musical since the beautiful mornin' of Oklahoma! We are in a Dublin pub, magically rendered in Bob Crowley's set-design, and full of music. Some lucky or intrepid audience members are invited on stage for a glass. After they have been returned to their seats, the cast members take over for more riotous renditions of songs from the catalogues of those great composers Trad, and Anon. Almost imperceptibly, we slide into the main action, when one of the characters is persuaded to give out with a song of his own. He's maybe shy, maybe just uncommunicative. But he does communicate to one chance hearer, a young woman with an accent that we discover to be Czech. She's more than impressed.

Guy tells Girl (those are the only names we ever have for them) that his day job is repairing vacuum cleaners. She says that's lucky as she has a vacuum cleaner that needs repairing. And so a relationship is born: undeclared love at first sight. Girl tells Guy that he should take his songwriting seriously, should move to New York, try to get a record deal. (She makes it all sound so easy.) She even offers to write lyrics for tunes of his that don't yet have them. Two things stand in the way of her becoming an active part of the dream. One is that she is married, with a sixyear-old daughter, and that her absent husband might reappear at any moment. The other is that he has an exgirlfriend whom he has yet to get over and who is in New York already.

Guy and Girl are inspired by, if not literally based on, the real-life songwriting partnership of Glen Hansard and Marketa Irglova; their songs are the score. Ian Lake, the new Guy, can't match his predecessor as a singer but surpasses him as an actor, conveying the raw pain of a recent breakup, and the bemused joy of finding someone to help heal it. Trish Lindstrom's Girl also comes with bitter baggage, and a practicality that enables her to rise above it. Her principal project is to secure the money, time and personnel that will enable Guy to make a demo. In plot terms, that's about all that happens, and the show has to be filled out with subplots involving others.

It turns out that just about everybody Guy and Girl knows can play an instrument of some kind: a state of affairs that may or may not be commonplace in Dublin, but that dovetails with the casting of the show; everybody involved is both actor and musician. When it comes to the climactic studio session, Girl plays piano herself, two of her expat relatives play drums and guitar, her volatile music-store employer also plays guitar and supplies the drum-kit (a source of manufactured tension in the second act), and the bank manager who's been cajoled into advancing the cash doubles on cello.

Laurie Murdoch gives an affectingly restrained performance as Guy's widowed Da; Brendan Wall has little to do as the studio manager, but he, too, is wonderful, rampaging through a ditty that once enjoyed pop-hit status in a version called The Thing.

I much preferred these old-time interpolations to the songs written by Hansard and Irglova, misty folk-pop creations that trade heavily on being autobiographical and thus, in some sense, "true" - a notion that's been responsible for a lot of bad songwriting. The only number here with stagefilling size is "When Your Mind's Made Up," the one we see and hear being done at the demo; Lake's does a passionate lead vocal, and the others support him with an intensity that belies the amateur status we are told they have; put it down to the transforming powers of music and theatre.

The direction (John Tiffany) and the choreography (Steven Hoggett) remain excellent; the script (Enda Walsh) is, as intended, a love letter to Ireland. The resolution of the love story, though, has become problematic. The first time around I thought it honestly ambiguous; this time, it feels merely inconclusive. We're left with a feeling of, "Is that all?" Having been eased beautifully into the show, we're ushered very abruptly out of it.

Once runs through May 31 at the Ed Mirvish Theatre.
