The ensemble work in Ash is as good as the ensemble work in Assassins. In this, the fourth and last play in the Theatrefront cycle known as The Mill, we shift from a haunted past to a post-apocalyptic, or maybe just apocalyptic, future. We are still in the mill, which seems to be the safest place to be. The world outside is covered in the ash of the title, the result of some unspecified disaster that might be nuclear or ecological or, very likely, both. Inside, five young adults huddle together, hoarding and dividing their painfully meagre rations; and their interaction, under Vikki Anderson's pitch-perfect direction, fills up much of the play.

The five are known, at least in their current habitat, as Beaver (Maev Beaty), Rabbit (Frank Cox O'Connell), Bear (Eric Goulem), Fox (Ryan Hollyman) and Bird (Michelle Monteith), and the collective rhythm of their performance is remarkable. They seem telepathically linked. Individually, they're all excellent, though two stand out: O'Connell, gangling and embarrassed as the one with the most detailed memories of a former life, and the miraculous Monteith as the de facto den mother, who cooks and parcels out the provisions and who, from our first sight of her, is obviously on track to be a real mother. Monteith gives extraordinarily centred performances of women at physical and mental extremes, and what she does here, especially in scenes concerning childbirth, is uncanny.

The actors in The Mill have remained constant, but each episode has had a different author and director. Ash is written by Damien Atkins, who tells an exciting story, while making his way through a landscape previously charted by William Golding, Cormac McCarthy and, closer to home and surprisingly, Judith Thompson; some tense and tender scenes towards the end recall Crackwalker. Before we get there, stocks grow low, jealousies grow high, anger flares and violence erupts. Atkins writes dialogue and action that his actors can feed on. Where he's less successful is in linking this final instalment to those that have gone before. The three episodes that preceded the current Great Leap Forward, traced the story of the eponymous lumber mill backwards from 1854 to 1636. We found that it was built on what was previously Wendat land, and that its subsequent history was one of rape, both physical and territorial, and of some especially grisly deaths. ( Saw, meet Mill.) Presiding over the whole saga was the spirit of Lyca, a girl half-Huron and half-European, whose own mother and grandmother had been violated and who was killed by the then-current mill-owner in 1834; since, at that point, she was already 200 years old, it's hardly surprising that, though dead, she refused to lie down. She was already out for revenge and, in the current play, she still is, egged on by her mother, played very effectively as a hologram (by Michelle Latimer, reprising a role she formerly did as flesh and blood). However, given the extreme external circumstances, Lyca's intervention here seems hardly necessary. One complicating factor is that we don't know whether the whole world has been devastated, or just Canada, or just the neighbourhood. The two fables, those inside and outside the mill, don't exactly cancel one another out, but they do tend to crowd.

Rather mysteriously, Lyca, who's embodied in the other three plays by the dangerously ethereal Holly Lewis, is played in this one by Natasha Greenblatt, who's almost equally haunting but not quite. The actress's father, Richard Greenblatt, appears as a character actually called Father; it's he who has taken in the five young'uns, given them their new furry and feathery names, and generally infantilized them: for their own good, he says, but also for his own ends. For much of the play, he's absent, foraging. When he returns, it's to tell his instant family to stay indoors, as the world outside is even bleaker than they'd imagined; as, in these plays, it generally has been. Indeed, the fine line between protection and exploitation has been one of The Mill's recurring themes. Anyway, he also lights the fuse that leads to the play's sequence of explosions. Hollyman, who has glowered and grumbled throughout, turns up the threat: an interesting change of pace for an actor I generally associate with gentleness. Goulem, in his best performance of the cycle, keeps springing surprises. Beaty surprises, too, though some of her changes of front seem to come out of nowhere.

There is less Grand Guignol in this play than in its predecessors, but Anderson's production maintains the same, literally awesome, technical standards: in Gillian Gallow's brooding set, Richard Feren's scary sonics, and especially in Andrea Lundy's lighting. One trivial note: The mill has a precious stream of pure water running beneath it, just like the one in Wingfield Lost and Found, which opened in Toronto a couple of nights earlier. A less trivial note: I'm not sure that the overall tale of exploitation and expiation works as a foundational Canadian myth, which seems to have been the intention. But then I've only been here 20 years. Anyway, all four plays are now running in repertory and open for inspection -- on Mill Street.
