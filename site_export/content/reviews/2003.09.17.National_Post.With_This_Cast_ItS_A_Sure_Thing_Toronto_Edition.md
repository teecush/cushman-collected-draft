THE PLAY'S THE THING

Premiere Dance Theatre, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - Four years ago, on a momentous evening, Diego Matamoros' opening speeches as Sandor Turai in The Play's the Thing offered proof beyond doubt that Soulpepper meant business. This was the opening production of its second Harbourfront season, back in 1999. It had taken us by storm with its inaugural two plays the previous year, and had since mounted a good one-off production of Our Town at the Royal Alex, but these had the feeling of special events. This by contrast was the first exposure, Soulpepper's and ours, to what would have to be the company's normal buckle-down circumstances.

From the moment the lights went up, we knew that things were going to be all right. In fact, we knew it even before they went up, as we gazed at the preliminary sight of three cigars glowing in the dark. It was an invitingly cheeky moment, to be followed by more elaborate outrageousness as the three smokers took us through the opening of Ferenc Molnar's comedy. This deals with the problem of exposition by having the three men -- all, we gathered, theatre professionals -- discuss the problem of exposition. How easy it would be, muses one of them, if one of the characters could simply step forward and tell the audience who they are and why they're here. Which is just what he proceeds to do.

The director, Laszlo Marton, was later to be responsible for Soulpepper's memorable productions of Chekhov and Moliere; here, on his home ground -- both he and the play are Hungarian -- he took the pulse of that opening sequence, and of the entire play, perfectly. Matamoros, in the role of a highly successful boulevard playwright - - suavely impudent, tongue exactly poised in cheek, as deadly serious as only assured playfulness can be -- set the tone, a quiet one, bursting with suppressed comic energy, and the rest of the cast took their time from him. Nothing was forced. The Canadian theatre, or a piece of it, had discovered style. It had grown up.

Not to keep you in suspense, Matamoros' performance in the current revival is as good as ever it was. As before, he is delightfully reinforced by William Webster as Mansky, his authorial colleague, jovial or nervous depending on how the wind blows; Jim Warren as an impromptu prompter; and Michael Hanrahan as a butler so resourceful and punctilious that he might have been invented by P. G. Wodehouse. As indeed he partly was; Wodehouse adapted the Balkan original, and if he didn't interpolate jokes of his own (I bet he did) then he put the originals into spruce and sparkling English.

He was also responsible for changing the play's title. It was originally Play at the Castle, and it is to a castle (which seems to double as a hotel) that Turai, Mansky and their composing collaborator have come to meet the leading lady of their new comic opera, script and score in hand. Molnar's is a highly -- indeed exorbitantly -- theatrical piece, and there's a play within the play. But Wodehouse's Shakespearean handle is more specific than that. The play, it turns out, is the thing in which Turai catches the consciences of the diva Ilona, and more especially of Almady, the vain, older married actor who is laying siege to her.

Ilona, as it happens, is engaged to Albert the composer, who is young and romantic and behaves as if life were something for which he'd written the music. He and his two friends overhear Almady apparently making love to Ilona in the room next door (the castle walls, it's explained, are very thin) and she apparently not rebuffing him. Albert is ready to destroy himself and his career and -- this is what really rattles them -- his librettists' careers as well. So Turai devotes what remains of the night to writing a play ("one is either a playwright or one's not") that both incorporates and excuses the incriminating words that had wafted from Ilona's boudoir. He then blackmails the miscreants into learning his lines and performing them on the ensuing evening.

Having got this far, pretty much on wings, the play and the production sag a bit. It's a one-idea piece, after all, however glittering that idea may be. There's an inconclusive subplot concerning the best ways of ministering to Albert's state of mind, and Molnar should not have attempted, just before intermission, to reprise the comedy of self-consciousness with which he commenced operations. What was delicious the first time around is tiresome the second.

But everything rights itself in the last act, when Turai's new play is rehearsed. He's fashioned it, we discover, not only to repair young Albert's illusions but to teach the philandering Almady a lesson. C. David Johnson repeats, and may even improve upon, his masterly performance as the ageing matinee idol, ritually humiliated by every line he is enforced to utter. There are two new arrivals: Allegra Fulton, a vast improvement on her predecessor, gives Ilona a wit and also a sense of caring that enrich the play's texture immeasurably; Jonathan Watton's Albert gangles charmingly. Some shows have the quality of a smile, and this one is a veritable Cheshire Cat: teasing, immovably assured, and lingering long after you might think you've seen the last of it.
