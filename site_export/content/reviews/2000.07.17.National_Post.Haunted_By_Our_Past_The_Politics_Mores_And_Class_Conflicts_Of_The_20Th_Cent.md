THE APPLE CART, COURT HOUSE THEATRE;

STILL LIFE, ROYAL GEORGE THEATRE;

LORD OF THE FLIES, FESTIVAL THEATRE

Niagara-on-the-Lake

NIAGARA-ON-THE-LAKE - David Schurmann may be the quintessential Shaw Festival actor. Most of his roles require him to be immaculately turned out, but there is something about his voice -- a creased, rumpled quality -- that inescapably suggests tweed jackets with leather patches at the elbows. He is intelligent, rueful, what used to be called civilized. I don't know his actual origins but he seems, in a pleasantly old-fashioned way, very English -- in the parts he gets, he needs to be.

Cast in The Apple Cart as Magnus, King of England, he reaches some kind of apogee. This is one of the greatest roles Shaw ever wrote -- one of the longest, anyway -- and the actors qualities carry him a considerable way with it, though less than the full distance.

The play, written in 1929, inaugurated its authors long, last period. He subtitled it, as he was to do many others over his remaining two decades, "a political extravaganza." This is a self- invented genre in which increasingly wayward ideas predominate over action (which tends to be either arbitrary or non-existent), social setting and character. The Apple Cart, being the first of its kind, still maintains links with convention; it has, if not a plot, a governing situation, and Magnus, who is hardly ever off stage, is a dominating presence, if not quite a unifying one.

He is a constitutional monarch, at loggerheads with a cabinet composed of one astute manipulator, one thwarted idealist and half a dozen clowns. Cleverer than any of them, he has started alluding to the royal veto; i.e., the fact that the one political power of his own is that of preventing them, in a last resort, from exercising theirs.

The politicians want him to shut up, or else. (What else may entail remains vague, but they seem to mean it). Eventually, Magnus finds a way of calling their bluff, though other events conspire to rob his victory of most of its savour.

Shaw set the play 40 years into its own future; Richard Greenblatt's production compounds the math, and pushes it 40 years into ours, though if he had merely set it down in 2000 it would still seem eerily topical. The world it presents is caught in a spiral of cynicism: rich countries stay that way on the labour of poor ones; politicians are controlled by business interests (national, though, rather than global); England survives as a dispenser of luxury goods; and democracy is falling into disuse because few people bother to vote.

Perhaps the least familiar part of the landscape, from a Canadian as well as an English viewpoint, is the presence of a hereditary monarch, both willing and able to challenge the general complacency; about the nearest approach we've had in the last 80 years is the putative Charles III, to whom Greenblatts slightly adapted text makes cheeky reference.

Shaw takes the traditionally appealing theatrical figure of the threatened king and gives it a comic twist; the fate of this monarch is not to lose his title but to keep it. In the meantime, he fights: with charm, with irony and with passion. Schurmanns performance has all the charm and its own brand of irony, but the latter is of the self-defeated kind, conceding points even as it makes them, so the passion leaks away. He has a great prose aria that prompts one of his hearers to remark "you spoke that piece beautifully, sir"; we should want to echo that sentiment (while chuckling at the author's self-deflation), but here we can only say that he spoke it very lucidly.

Kelly Wolf's set gives us a big shiny mirror to see ourselves in (this is always an effective device, but its been used a lot and, honestly, weve got the point) while her costumes combine a Regency cut with garish colours that pessimists might describe as futuristic. They certainly attract attention, but the actors get lost inside them.

Supporting acting is weaker than one expects at the Shaw, and only three of the cabinet are much good. There is Corinne Koslo, ringingly committed as the energy secretary (Shaw called her the powermistress-general) driven to distraction by a conglomerate known, all too persuasively, as Breakages Limited. There is Michael Ball as the aggressively proletarian Bill Boanerges, though he should really be a roaring bull rather than this actor's familiar cuddly bear. Pre-eminently, there is Peter Millard's prime minister, again not quite what the dramatist ordered -- he should probably be a more docile-seeming politician whose smartness takes us by surprise -- but commandingly incisive. Though, given that the play is basically a duel between him and Magnus, it hurts that they are - - tonally and temperamentally -- very similar kinds of actor.

The king has other problems. There is Orinthia, his mistress, with whom he shares one of Shaws most infuriatingly sexless love scenes, and who is jealous and demanding in every way, it seems, except physically. Its hard to make this kind of poseuse sacree attractive, and it's possible that Pamela Rabe, who plays her with ferocious langour, isn't trying; she actually comes off not only less comfortable than the queen (Wendy Thatcher) but less alluring.A final tableau -- undreamed-of by the playwright but very effective - - really turns the screws on His Royal Loneliness.

Then there is America, which puts domestic problems into perspective by applying to rejoin the British Empire, thereby effectively annexing it. In 1929, the year of the crash, this must have seemed whimsically amusing; now its just logical. The production turns the U.S. ambassador into an ambassadress (Lynne Cormack). This works amazingly well; it is also much, much scarier.

OH, FOR A 10-PENCE GLASS OF BRANDY

The Shaws second-favourite playwright is Noel Coward, who this year has as many plays in the bill as GBS himself, though one (Easy Virtue, just closed) was a repeat from last year and the other is a lunchtime show. This is Still Life, the one-act piece that became the emblematic British movie Brief Encounter.

It is the archetypal story of thwarted adultery; a confluence of timetables causes two married people to collide once a week at a railway station, and encourages them to arrange further collisions elsewhere. Furtiveness and guilt (hers especially) put an end to the affair, and he, a doctor, does the gentlemanly thing by accepting a position a very long way away.

The play derives from 1936 (the film came 10 years later), and Dennis Garnhum's unemphatically atmospheric production gets us duly and wistfully nostalgic for the manners and fashions of before-the- war. Also for the prices: The entire play unfolds, over the course of a year, in the railway refreshment room, and the cost of tea, cakes and brandy (ten pence a glass) are meticulously noted.

Behind the central couple, the station staff play out their own more robust romances; Coward, a suburban boy himself, was probably trying hard to do good by his roots, but the lower-class characters are patronizingly written and, for the most part, patronizingly played.

The middle-class figures, too, have an uphill struggle. The actors have to contend not only with the stiff-upper-lipness that befits their station (and I don't mean Milford Junction, wherever that is) but with Cowards own style which, sad or funny, is heavily reliant on bloodless adjectives.

Beneath this double overlay there is an unquestioned truth, but its hard to reach. Simon Bradbury, pleasingly released from his line of flat-capped northern roles, often manages to cut through; Coward himself, who originated the role, was probably far less convincing.

Jan Alexandra Smith (in the Gertrude Lawrence part) is less fortunate. I felt so cheap, she says, when one of their assignations is interrupted; yes, that is how she would have felt and what she would have said, but she can't help sounding like one of the parody heroines whom this text has so prolifically inspired. Her emotions seem buried inside her coat, until a silent, desperate movement brings them tearingly to the surface.

The play may be less touching than anticipated, but it's still thoroughly gripping. The masters technique is at its most magisterial, the structure as clipped as the dialogue, with the unseen details of the relationship seeping through the silences. When he brings on a new and fateful character near the end, its such an agonizingly clever stroke that you could kill him.

THE CALL OF THE CONCH

The pent-up legacy of civilized English loquacity (Shaw) and civilized English reticence (Coward) was devastatingly exposed in William Golding's novel Lord of the Flies. This is one of the few modern novels that everybody knows, largely because, being about schoolboys, it has for decades been an educational set-text.

A group of young evacuees, aged 12 or thereabouts, find themselves alone on a desert island, and act out, in horrifyingly short order, the decline of civilization. Arriving in dramatic form on the main festival stage, it is, as it should be, very tense and very frightening.

Its first act -- there are three in all -- is brilliant. The rhythms, both of Nigel Williams' adaptation and Neil Munros production, seem exactly right. The boys cluster together for their first assembly, an attempt that keeps breaking down, disrupted by compulsive horseplay that becomes progressively less innocent.

Piggy, the asthmatic and myopic state-school kid, is robbed of his specs so that his public school (which, translated into North American, means private school) confreres may use them as a burning glass to light a fire: Not a bad idea, except that they seem to have no intention of returning them. Ralph, the conscientious boy elected leader, has to insist on fair play, an insistence that gets steadily harder to enforce. The boys are played by young actors in their twenties, but we accept them completely.

As the castaways begin to lose it, so, unfortunately, does the play. It remains exciting, but it does not stay convincing.

One difficulty is the set: basically three wide carpeted steps, in a shade that the fabric catalogue may have listed as Basic Beach. Its anonymous but it isn't unlocalized, because landmarks keep being planted on it -- a pig's head, a sacrificial altar -- that cant be removed. So when three separate groups of characters, supposedly in three different places, all start wandering about it at the same time, they look silly.

Another problem is that, as the action unfolds, it stumbles into the waiting arms of some well-worn theatrical cliches. Youll remember the story: Jack, a bullying head choirboy, turns his angel- voiced cohorts into a gang of hunters whose appetite for blood doesn't stop at the local pig population. A small boy claims to have seen a Beast in the bushes, and it becomes the object of a cult. Simon, an epileptic choirboy, discovers the prosaic truth about the Beast -- it's already, with unfortunate literalness, been revealed to the audience -- and is set upon and killed when he tries to break the news. Jack says -- repeatedly -- that he wants his adherents to hunt, and eat, and dance: So a lot of tired ritualistic prancing takes place.

Also ritualistic are the politics. Williams was one of the more heavy-handed of the 1970s generation of British playwrights, and he makes class warfare -- a subsidiary theme in the novel -- the mainspring of the action. Jack is a lot nastier, a lot sooner, to the proletarian Piggy than the source gives warrant. (When, by the way, is all this meant to be happening? Presumably the war the boys are escaping is the Second World one; anything later would put the script outside even the festivals new extended mandate of doing plays set during Shaws lifetime. If it's now, as a couple of lines and many of the idioms suggest, then the choir would be as likely to imitate Piggy's accent as to insult it.)

As Jack matures into a full-blown fascist, so the writing of his role grows increasingly self-conscious; and so, accordingly -- despite Mike Sharas best efforts, which are very good efforts indeed -- does its acting.

The attendant savages are similarly effortful and largely indistinguishable, even before they put on their war-paint, though Matthew MacFadzean develops a belated sinister glimmer as Jacks lieutenant who is even nastier than he is.

But it's the good boys who deserve favour: Craig Erickson's Ralph and Dylan Trowbridges visionary Simon are both excellent, while Pete Treadwell as the doggedly decent Piggy is perfect. His end, most ineffectually staged, is unlikely to satisfy anyone who recalls Ralphs lament in the book for "the fall through the air of the one true friend called Piggy."

It's unfortunate, too, that boypower shortage keeps offstage the smaller kids, the "little'uns," who are mentioned but must be presumed to be on another part of the island, acting out some other fable entirely.

We can still feel in our bones the abiding power of this one. Golding was on to something: Something that confirms our worst fears about ourselves and our children, and that insistently invites dramatization. Forget the Peter Brook film; the best version I ever encountered was on BBC radio, the best radio play that I ever heard, better in fact than the book, and more terrifying than any visual representation is likely to be.

This attempt falls uncomfortably between styles, and the big stage hinders rather than helps. But it's plucky. Another '70s English playwright once said that there are only two kinds of plays: plays set in rooms and plays not set in rooms. Most plays at Niagara are definitely set in rooms; this, which could hardly be less so, makes a change.
