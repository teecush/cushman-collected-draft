JAZZ

REMEMBERING MABEL

Mercer: Volume 2

Joyce Breach

(Audiophile)

- - -

SEPTEMBER SONGS

Wesla Whitfield

(High Note)

- - -

Much of the finest singing of classic American song is being done by those performers, cult rather than popular, who cluster somewhere around the cusp of jazz and cabaret. These CDs are by two of the best.

Joyce Breach, bred in Pittsburgh, based in New York, offers the second in a planned trilogy of albums drawn from the repertoire of the late Mabel Mercer. Mercer, the doyenne of Manhattan nightlife, was an unassuming woman whose fans perceived her as regal, but whose authority was leavened with a refreshing raucous wit.

Breach's persona is in a lower key. Her singing is soothing, sensible, and quietly confident, with a hint of remembered sadness in the voice. Looking in the other direction, this can become grave apprehension, as in Cole Porter's After You, an apparently happy song that is also a heartbreaker. She's a fine contemplative singer, notably in Love-Wise, which comes to the conclusion "you're perfect for me." Her understatement does wonders for This is All I Ask, a testament of a song that can be unbearably overbearing.

Sometimes her keel is too even. Two worldly Cy Coleman tunes, When in Rome and The Riviera, suffer from a lack of laughter in the voice. Keith Ingham, a fleet and sensitive pianist and arranger, leads a trio with support from bassoonist Laurie Goldstein. Especially pleasing is his mournful obbligato on Blame It on My Youth, one of the few famous songs here, and one that Breach does well -- title notwithstanding, it's a song that needs maturity to do it justice.

Breach's opening tune is While We're Young. It's the closer on Wesla Whitfield's September Song, whose opener is its title track. This gives the album a shape; it's bookended by songs about seizing the moment, at different times of life.

This disc focuses on three composers, and should perhaps have a big "W" logo: Wesla Whitfield warbles Wilder, Weill and Warren. There may just be too many elements to make a satisfying whole, but song by song, it's lovely.

Whitfield, from San Francisco, had classical training but not a classical career. She does evanescence exceptionally well. She can spin out flowing lines and then cut them off -- especially on September Song -- with what sounds like brusque regret.

Her backing groups include the Kronos Quartet, whose chamber sound in Weill's Speak Low sits intriguingly over a pulsating trio led by the singer's husband, Mike Greensill. These strings can swing too, as in Jeepers Creepers, which crops up with delightful abruptness halfway through a medley. Whitfield is at her best on I Had the Craziest Dream, a bittersweet song on which, uniquely, she emphasizes the bitterness, without letting it quite take over.
