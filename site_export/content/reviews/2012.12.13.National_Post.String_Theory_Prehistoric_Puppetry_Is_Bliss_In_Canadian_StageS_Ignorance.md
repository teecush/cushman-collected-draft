The title of the latest show to reach Toronto from the Old Trout Puppet Workshop of Calgary is Ignorance. Its announced subject, though, is happiness. The message seems to be that the two are the same thing. Ignorance, after all, is proverbially associated with bliss. It makes sense; the moment you start asking whether you're having fun yet, it's plain that you aren't. I asked it a lot during the course of this show.

It presents us with three visible puppeteers -- Nicolas Di Gaetano, Trevor Leigh, Viktor Lukawski -- wearing moustaches, grey overalls, and single-horned Viking helmets. The set they inhabit suggests a cave, with the makings of a fire centre stage. The dolls they wield come in two flavours: prehistoric and contemporary. This is to contrast primitives who, it appears, were sometimes able to achieve happiness because they didn't worry about what it was, and moderns who worry all the time and thus can't have it at all.

A program note informs us that in 1934, an Andorran adventurer named Boris Skosyreff, hiding in a cave in the Pyrenees, discovered "the first palaeolithic puppet." I took this to be a joke, but if it is, then Wikipedia, which supplies a biography of Boris, is at least partially in on it. (It vouches for his existence, but doesn't mention puppets, palaeolithic or otherwise.) The note is credited to Sir Reginald Chute, GRMPH, who certainly is a joke, and one that pervades the show itself. His cod-scholarly style is reflected in the recorded narration, voiced by Judd Palmer in unctuous tones that might well be Sir Reginald's own, that is the dominating feature of the evening itself. Its pomposity is obviously intentional, and probably intended to be funny, but that doesn't make it any more pleasant to listen to.

Not that voice-over Sir Reginald doesn't make some good points. He tells us that the average person experiences only 14 minutes of pure happiness in a lifetime. That strikes me as an over-pessimistic estimate, but maybe not by much. He adds the even more telling statistic that most of these moments will have been experienced by the age of 12. Yes indeed: Childhood is the best shot we have at feeling unadulterated (there's a funny word), un-self-conscious pleasure. Of course by the same token, it's the best, or likeliest, time for feeling pure defenceless misery. There are no mental defences against either sensation. It's why cruelty to children is so horrible.

Anyway, the Old Trouts take us back to the childhood of the race. Here is prehistoric man discovering the delights of, among other things, prehistoric woman. He finds love; and isn't he lucky not to know the word, and hence not to have to wonder what it means. He also, though, discovers jealousy and loss. His cave is invaded by another, Alpha Male, who knocks him out and makes off with his mate. This presumably makes Alpha Male happy. Or, as the narrator tells us, "happiness is Mother Nature's reward for being a winner." That seems an unduly cynical definition. Happiness may well be when you stop worrying about winning or losing. Ask a child on a swing. Or rather don't ask; you'll spoil it.

The Stone Age, in this show's mythology, co-exists with the Garden of Eden. Eve gives Adam the apple, and with it the gift, or curse, of imagination: conceiving of something better and hence wanting it. So the human desire for happiness becomes a search for ever shinier toys, and the show plunges into the present to illustrate this. The puppets playing our predecessors are pudgy assemblages of sticks and stones, appropriately eager to break one another's bones.

Present-day humanity is represented by more compact creations who seem to have waddled out of old advertisements for lemon squash i.e. they look like squashed lemons. They try to manufacture happiness on mass-production lines, or to discover it in driving kiddie-cars. Filmed projections illustrate how deluded they are, showing us what might be outtakes from Modern Times or footage of cars proceeding in disconsolate lines along the highway, aimless and probably joyless. A similar lack of progression affects the show itself. It keeps cutting back from present to primeval, without telling us anything new. The satiric effect of the modern scenes quickly palls. The Stone Age at least offers the prospect that a woolly mammoth might drop by, or the near certainty that the males of our species will continue bashing one another up.

This they do with some ingenuity. The puppetry is very skillful. But it never makes you hold your breath or lifts your heart. There is some ingenuity but no joy. Ignorance, as they say in the courts, is no excuse.
