ELLA FIZGERALD BY STUART NICHOLSON Gollancz, 286 pages, $30

The life and songs of a scandal-free first lady

ELLA FIZGERALD BY STUART NICHOLSON Gollancz, 286 pages, $30

Saturday, February 26, 1994

ROBERT CUSHMAN

THE COMPLETE ELLA FITZGERALD SONG BOOKS Verve 314 519 832-2, 16 CDs Review by ROBERT CUSHMAN FEW singers get good biographies, but Stuart Nicholson's Ella Fitzgerald, timed to the singer's 75th birthday year (she was born on April 25, 1918), will more than do. Famously shy and reclusive, notoriously straight, Fitzgerald is a tough item for a chronicler. She has been successful since she was a teen-ager, her life has been devoid of scandal and everybody loves her, though few really know her.

Nicholson endearingly quotes a press agent's description of Fitzgerald as "sweet and charming as can be" until asked to do an interview, at which point "her face will fall and she'll stamp her foot and say, "Gosh, darn it, Virginia, I can't do it - I have to go SHOPPING]'" Her petulance here sounds little-girlish, as befits a singer who had her first (and biggest) hit at age 21, with A-Tisket A-Tasket.

She had already been dubbed the First Lady of Song, a nickname that has stuck ever since. Youthful prodigies rarely endure so well, so her life might seem to have been the plainest of sailing. In fact her early life was tough - tougher, on Nicholson's showing, than has previously been suspected. Childhood (Yonkers, New York, in the 1920s) wasn't bad; it was poor, and her parents split up, but she was good in class and apparently happy. When she was 15, though, her mother died, and soon after, Fitzgerald dropped out of school. In 1934, when she entered an amateur- night contest at the Harlem Apollo, she was living on the streets. What happened then is legend. She was going to dance, but nervousness made her switch to singing at the last moment. She faltered, recovered herself and won.

Professional work was slow to follow, largely because she wasn't presentable. A friend says: "She hadn't had a bath and smelt like it could have been a year . . . . She lived with people she talked to, and she ate with them, she slept wherever she could." Eventually, though, she was taken on by drummer and band leader Chick Webb, who groomed her and spotlit her (though he didn't, contrary to legend, adopt her), and, in an age when singers other than Bing Crosby were still second-class citizens, made her a star. After Webb's premature death, Fitzgerald fronted his band for a couple of years, then became one of the first band singers to go solo.

Musicians, it seems, were awed by her range, her tonal warmth and her innate swing, but they weren't too fond of her personally. They found her snippy. As the years pass the quotes change, and adoration takes over; insecurities that are forbidding in a newcomer become endearing in a celebrity. Fitzgerald - though hardly prey to the demons that destroyed Billie Holiday or Judy Garland - has obviously had her share. She has inevitably faced racial prejudice; as a girl she felt gawky and as a woman overweight. She has had two brief marriages, and a series of shadowy affairs. But her prime relationship has been with her audiences, and unlike some other love affairs between performer and public it has been creative rather than morbid.

Nicholson tells of a celebratory evening for Fitzgerald in 1955 at which she told the audience: "I guess what everyone wants is to be loved. And to know that you can love me for my singing is too much for me. . . . Maybe I can sing it and you'll understand." I'm especially fond of that quote since I originally encountered it on the back of the first Fitzgerald album (and one of the first records of any kind) that I bought: Ella Fitzgerald Sings the Cole Porter Songbook, Volume One. It was the first of a series recorded between 1956 and 1964: 19 LPs devoted to the songs of 10 major U.S. songwriters. They have been constantly reissued, and here they finally are in a single package: 16 CDs in a cute red box.

The songbooks were the brainchild of Norman Granz. Granz had presented Fitzgerald in his Jazz at the Philharmonic concerts since 1949, he became her manager in 1953, and in '56, after serpentine negotiations with Decca, who owned her disc, he began recording for his own new label, Verve. Their association endures to this day. Granz had discerned in Fitzgerald virtually two distinct vocalists. One was the rhythmic and harmonic virtuoso, out of swing and into bop, who could scat and improvise with endless bravura and invention. The other was a ballad singer whose range, ease and beauty of tone - a comfortable, unthreatening beauty rather than an awesomely operatic one like Sarah Vaughan's - could make any song a pleasant experience.

As well as being a jazz buff, Granz was a song freak. He assembled generous quantities of the best songs of the best composers, sat Fitzgerald down in front of them, and let the tape roll. The results, as John McDonough says in his new liner notes, "change the way we hear our music." A canon was established of the American popular song. For myself, and I imagine I am one of many, the songbooks were a major part of my education.

There have always been objections to the songbooks, from two schools of purists. Jazz fans with no interest in songs point out that the albums' heavy orchestral settings preclude improvisation. Theatre fanatics, mindful that most of these songs come from shows, cite Fitzgerald's limitations as an actress. Both object to the singer's lack of "personal involvement." Actually the absence of heavy drama is often a blessing. One of the rarest treats of the cycle is Irving Berlin's You Can Have Him, in which a woman feigns gladness to be rid of her man by listing the mundane domestic things she wanted to do for him. The few other divas who have tackled it (Nancy Wilson, Nina Simone) have slowed it to a self-pitying dirge; trying to bring out the irony, they have squelched it. Fitzgerald's voice is gentle, her pace brisk, her manner matter-of-fact, and her effect extremely touching. This may be luck or it may be judgment - hers or Granz's or arranger Paul Weston's; what matters is that it happens. And that in a song crammed with detail, every word tells, simply because you can hear and understand it.

Her perfect (though totally unaffected) diction lights up the list- songs that typify this era: most spectacularly, Porter's You're the Top. Even if she didn't get all the references, we do. Actually I think that Fitzgerald's deficiencies as a lyric interpreter have been grossly exaggerated, certainly in comedy songs. She does have a comic personality: a wryly innocent, self-mocking wickedness, a husk in the throat that's the vocal equivalent of a raising of the eyebrows or a hopeless throwing-up of hands, whether she's assassinating a succession of husbands (To Keep My Love Alive, Rodgers and Hart) or contemplating life with just one creep ( Boy! What Love Has Done to Me, Gershwin). And on the Kern songbook, she makes I'll Be Hard to Handle, which has an atrociously clumsy lyric by somebody's nephew, a lot funnier than anyone else is ever likely to.

If the songbook series has two hidden heroes, they are Fitzgerald's long-time accompanist Paul Smith, who plays sensitive and inspiring piano on every record though not on every track, and Alvin Stroller, whose drumming sparks every disc except the last one. The 32 Porter songs are a reasonable, if bowdlerized, cross-section of his output - but jaded was never Fitzgerald's style.

She is far happier with the bittersweet of Rodgers and Hart, and here Buddy Bregman's functional arrangements are less of a handicap; the songs are the thing, and their wit and tenderness are dazzling, eternally youthful material for an externally youthful singer. This was the songbook that opened most windows for me. Still, it's a relief to hear Fitzgerald in the next installment with the Duke Ellington band, in one of its best incarnations. A ringer in the series, since Ellington's were not show songs, it holds up very well. The sound is rich, Fitzgerald gets to stretch out (hear her joy on Drop Me Off in Harlem), Johnny Hodges has a honey-dripping solo on I Got it Bad, and there are some lovely small-group sides on the numbers the Duke's band didn't want to play.

The Berlin set has the series' most imaginative selection of songs, and from Paul Weston, working in the fifties cool style, the most distinctive arrangements so far. They are eclipsed, though, by the next batch. Granz brought over Nelson Riddle for a recklessly expensive five-LP set of George and Ira Gershwin. The 53 songs had perforce to be "ground out" in the studio, but as Paul Smith comments in Nicholson's book, it somehow never sounds that way. Nearly every song comes complete with verse, each given as much thought as the chorus that follows. Riddle's orchestrations are suave and endlessly inventive, and Fitzgerald's easy swing is an exact translation of George's own; while Ira's lyrics, love songs that keep an amused and beguiling distance from their subject, are perfect for her.

Some prefer the succeeding Harold Arlen volumes, with muscular arrangements by Billy May that give the musicians (notably Benny Carter) an unaccustomed main chance. The choice of songs, though, is almost unrelentingly mainstream. Arlen's earthy style is at the opposite extreme from Porter's refined one, but it reveals Fitzgerald's limitations in much the same way. She is best in the middle ground.

The two remaining songbooks are addenda, tributes to Jerome Kern and Johnny Mercer, given a scant one volume apiece; either money or enthusiasm was running out.

No straight or theatre singer could hold your attention for so long; no jazz or personality singer could cover so many bases, though some have dealt more memorably with individual songs. Fitzgerald's mixture of self- assertion and self-effacement, her strengths as little girl and first lady - or Granz's selection among her strengths - are exactly right for the project, and the project itself is a major service to humanity. It does not exhaust the repertoire but for three decades it has pointed the way to further discovery.

A word about the packaging. This is a Chinese box set. The outer case contains two smaller cases to house the multi-disc Gershwin and Ellington sets, plus a mini-sleeve for each of the less ambitious compilations. Each disc also has its own inner sleeve. The compacting of everything means that we get all the original liner notes, the Gershwin ones amounting to a doll's house book; they come in print too small to read without a magnifying glass but it's comforting to know they're there. The producers would, I thought, be caught out by the original Gershwin sleeves; Granz, at his most prodigal, had commissioned a Bernard Buffet lithograph on each of the five LPs, and these could hardly fit onto three CDs. Not to worry; they come anyway, in a separate envelope. Who could ask for anything more? Robert Cushman is a writer and broadcaster who hosts Songbook on CBC radio.
