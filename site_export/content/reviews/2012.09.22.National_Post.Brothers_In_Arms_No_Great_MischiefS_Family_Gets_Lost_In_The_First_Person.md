The best scene in No Great Mischief - really, the only good one - occurs in the second act and takes place in the 1960s, in a mine on Cape Breton. Most of the miners are members of the family, or clan, MacDonald, and a good proportion of those are named Alexander. One of them has just graduated from college, as a dentist, but hes helping out down the mine following the death there of another Alexander MacDonald, a cousin who used to bully him when they were at school. There now appears a third Alexander, a draft-dodging, guitarstrumming relative from the U.S., referred to as the California Cousin. They all get to talking about family history and traditions, and someone references the MacDonalds valiant service on the Plains of Abraham, where General Wolfe apparently remarked of them that, brave though they undoubtedly were, it was "no great mischief if they fall."

This is the only time that David S. Young's play makes direct reference to its title, which is also that of the Alistair MacLeod novel on which it's based. Coming where it does, its a rueful illumination, all the more powerful for its casualness, of both the anger and the stoicism of a family accustomed to being treated as cannon fodder, which translates fairly easily into coal fodder. The tradition goes back to the Scottish Highlands, where the MacDonalds had a long history of being used and butchered, a history that the program, though not the play, helpfully explains. The scene gives way to a longer and showier, but less satisfying, sequence in which the mines Scots contingent face off against their francophone counterparts in a contest of musical cultures. Everyone onstage, it seems, can play something, and the encounter develops from aggressive confrontation to uneasy, mutually, or musically, respectful truce. It's like a less amiable version of the tavern scene in Fiddler on the Roof; or, given the Cape propensity for dancing while playing, Fiddler on the Hoof. The peace, such as it is, doesn't last; there will, shortly, be blood, whose ultimate cause may be California Cousins dirty tactics when playing poker. He might be said to start the whole scene off with a rousing, and I guess relevant, rendition of The Times They Are a-Changin. But the music and the mayhem aren't well tied together, and the former seems to be prolonged mainly for its own roof-raising sake. (It does get the applause it begs for.)

The problem, as when the show was first done at the Tarragon in 2004, is that the play remains an undigested first-person novel. Dental Alexander, as we may call him, tells the story and hence does most of the talking, but he isn't the protagonist; hes somebody who sees things, and to whom things sometimes happen, and who tells us about them, but he hardly ever initiates them. The one, fatal, action he does take is done almost by accident, and even this he has to describe to us, while its being elaborately illustrated. (I think I could happily live without seeing another scene of slow-motion violence.) Otherwise, he's the bright boy whose family wants him to get on and get out, which he does: a perfectly believable situation but one that needs exploration and individualization if it's to avoid seeming stock, and that doesnt get it.

The more dynamic character is his elder brother Calum. The boys were orphaned when Alexander was a child; loss of parents, usually at sea, is another family tradition. Calum had to look after Alex; in later years, the roles were reversed. The plays framework is that Alex visits Calum who's now holed up in Toronto, practically dying of drink. This sets off the flood of reminiscences, personal and historical, that make up most of the evening. Eventually, we find out how Calum got to be the way he is, but it's the answer to a question that hasnt been asked. The action has been too diffuse for there to be any suspense. Richard Roses production includes some flashy lighting and sound effects, and amiable "character" cameos from various grandparents, but no unifying interest, other than a generalized sense of dynastic doom.

The two principal performers are, to an extent, the victims of their own virtues. R.H. Thomson is one of nature's interlocutors, but his corkscrewing carefulness adds to the evening's frustrations; he worries exquisitely rather than getting a move on. David Fox is, as always, powerful, but he drowns his words deeper than usual even before seriously hitting the bottle. Both actors, it must be said, have extraordinary visual moments, lit solely from within, of reverting to their characters far younger selves.

Ben Irvine lightens things up in a different way as California Cousin. He leaves far too early considering what an enlivening presence he is and how strong an influence he has on the action - another example of the gulf between what is effective in a novel and what in a play. Actors in the eight-strong cast not involved in a particular scene are lined up facing the back wall of Charlotte Deans set, looking for all the world as if they're standing at a row of invisible urinals. The play's last section devolves into a hymn to Cape Breton, as if this were a play about a place rather than, as its been insisting, a family. As a play about Canadian history, it left me nostalgic for the trenchant cartooning of VideoCabarets saga of the Village of the Small Huts.

No Great Mischief runs through Oct. 21 at the Tarragon Theatre. For more information, call 416-531-1827 or visit tarragontheatre.com.
