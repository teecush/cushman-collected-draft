Sarah Bernhardt, the great French actress whose fans called her the Divine One, undertook a Canadian tour in 1905, one of her dates being in Quebec City, whose archbishop tried to have her banned on the grounds that both she and the plays she presented were immoral. These circumstances have inspired Michel Marc Bouchard to write The Divine: A Play for Sarah Bernhardt, commissioned and premiered by the Shaw Festival. It's his best work, and the most exciting new Canadian play in years.

The title alone is worth a couple of looks. It refers most obviously to Bernhardt herself, and shes a flamboyant figure in the play; but her role is actually subsidiary to that of two young men, both trainee priests at what the program identifies as "the prestigious Grand Seminary of Quebec," and both therefore in at least nominal quest of the divine in their own lives and vocations. Then theres the subtitle: not a play about Sarah Bernhardt but a play for her. Michaud, one of the two seminarians, is a stage-struck Bernhardt fanatic, and he's writing just such a play. It may even be the play we're watching; the program also says that the seminarys dormitory "houses all the scenes of the play" so that its possible that those that take place outside it (and it ranges quite far) are all Michaud's projections. Jackie Maxwell's irreproachable production doesnt press the point: Michael Gianfrancescos dormitory set, with its high bare walls, is singularly impressive but the beds that define it vanish unobtrusively into the wings when the locale shifts, just as they might do in any old naturalistic piece.

The play begins with Michaud atop a high ladder, rapturously reporting from the dorm's only window on the legendary Sarah's arrival in Quebec. Our aspiring playwright is a rich boy with connections, which makes him atypical; in a society dominated by the Catholic Church, most of his fellows have turned to it as the only way out of poverty. Talbot, our other principal, is one of them. He arrives under a cloud, having attacked and disabled a senior cleric and (shades of Les Mis) made off with his silverware. For reasons easily guessed, the church has decided to hush this up.

Talbot is escorted to the seminary by his mother and his younger brother, who have toiled in a shoe factory, under sweatshop conditions, to make his education possible. They still work there, and in the plays second scene he and Michaud pay them a visit. Michaud is shocked by what he sees, and develops a social conscience on the spot; he even starts writing about it. In the third scene we get to meet Bernhardt, whos reading a play somebody has sent her in which, she indignantly notes, she doesn't appear until the third scene. (Shades of The Play's the Thing, and its immortally cheeky opening.) Michaud visits her too, to present her with the archiepiscopal injunction to kindly leave the stage, entrusting him with this mission being his superiors idea of a curative joke. It misfires, as the lady flatly refuses to obey the orders order. She also takes a flattering interest in Michaud's work-in-progress, even though she has doubts about social drama as a genre; a political radical but an aesthetic conservative, she describes it as preaching about the poor's problems to the rich converted. Meanwhile Talbot, who has tagged along, also has a life-changing encounter with an actress, though in this case shes younger and its physical.

Sex embraced or coerced, religion used or abused, exploitation industrial and institutional, theatre celebrated and satirized and satirizing itself: it's a heady mix, and I wondered halfway through if the ingredients could be kept in balance. It turns out that they can. This is a play with an all-conquering narrative drive and an abundance of twists; there are no unabsorbing moments. The engine is Michaud's determination to shape what he sees into a play, with Talbot as its working-class hero; but he has to keep re-writing as his protagonist continually refuses to fulfil his authors expectations, to keep to Michauds script. The great set-piece scene, in which themes and storylines collide, comes when Bernhardt visits the factory that has supplied her with some fancy footwear. (Shades of Kinky Boots: I never expected to see two shoe factory shows in one season. And the boots are even the same colour.) A conflicted priest (Martin Happer, in a fine anguished performance) arraigns the factory boss for inhumane treatment of his employees; the boss (Ric Reid, in a fine oily performance) responds by pointing out the beam in the church's own eyes, and in those of society in general. Both are right; both are hypocritical; both are self-serving. Meanwhile we contemplate the workers themselves: Talbot's brother (Kyle Orzech, cheekily and tragically rebellious) and his mother, a role in which Mary Haney rings marvellous changes on bossiness, sullenness, wit and agonized anger.

As for the leads: Fiona Reid duly coruscates as Bernhardt, sublime egoist, telling Canada (this is historical) just what she thinks of it. Particularly taking, in both writing and performance, are here bouts of talking to new acquaintances as if they were auditioning for her. The boys are superb: Wade Bogert-OBrien gives a wrenching account of Talbots traumatized past, a joyous one of his brief awakening, and is paradoxically at his most heroic when at his most defiantly ignoble; Ben Sanders as Michaud has the tricky task of not just living but maturing through everybody else; his is an observer role taken to the highest power, and he fills it brilliantly. Physically slight, vocally sharp and continually surprised by events, he acts by reacting. Almost his first task in the play is to check off the clothes and possessions of a new arrival and to tell them the rules. He's a playwright already.
