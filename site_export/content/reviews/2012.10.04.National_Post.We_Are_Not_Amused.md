THEATRE REVIEW

Queen for a Day

Richmond Hill Centre for the Performing Arts

Soap opera, despite its name, does not need songs. Or maybe because of its name. A form whose whole reason for being is to push easy emotional buttons doesnt need to be made any more blatant than it already is. Queen for a Day is a soap musical, and the tautology sinks it.

It takes its name and its story from a 1950s radio (and later TV) show in which four women each day told the American public about their dreams and their problems, and in which the audience voted on which would have her problem alleviated and her dream made true. The musical's heroine, Claribel Anderson (based apparently on an actual contestant), gets on the show almost by accident and is pronounced the winner. She seems to have won the voters' hearts partly by the artless impetuosity with which she answers the hosts questions before he asks them, but mostly by her generosity when it comes to making her pitch; she asks that the wish of another contestant, in greater need, be granted in preference to her own. Whose heart wouldnt melt?

The radio program comes over as a slick mix of exploitation and compassion: a perennially popular combination that might more recently have been dubbed soap Oprah. The 20 minutes or so during which it's recreated onstage are by far the liveliest part of the evening. The program credits a bewildering number of people with book, "additional dialogue" and creative concept, but Chris Earle and Shari Hollett seem to be the primary writers, and their revue talents are probably responsible for the pace and sharpness with which the sequence unfolds.

The shamelessly recurring commercial jingles also seem to bring out the best in Linda Barnett, the musical's composer, lyricist and originator. Actor Alan Thicke, of whom my previous awareness was even vaguer, is perfect as our host Jack Bailey, gliding through the proceedings like a human oil slick. He keeps the persona going through a second-act appearance as a deus ex machina, as of a man programmed to do good deeds in his sleep. He should not, however, be called upon to sing.

Nor should anybody else in this show, including those who technically do it very well. That includes Blythe Wilson, who plays Claribel and is required to spoil the fun of the game show scene with a strenuously dramatic number called A Woman Has a Right to Change Her Mind. As weve already seen her change it, this doesnt add very much. That's the trouble throughout; the score takes obvious situations and makes them more obvious. The songs have the kind of generic titles (A Few Years from Now, One Wonderful Day, Maybe It's Time) that practically invite you to write your own synopsis, and though the tunes are reasonable facsimiles of the period, theyre never any more than that.

Claribel is a waitress in a Los Angeles diner. We learn that shes an independent spirit when she steps offstage to pump gas on the forecourt, and is roundly rebuked by her boss, who says that's man's work. He then goes into a number called Men Who Run the Show, in which he celebrates a surprisingly wide spectrum of red-blooded role models, from Hugh Hefner, who is a liberal, to Senator Joseph McCarthy, who on the whole wasnt. Soon after this Claribels husband, who keeps losing jobs and resents her having one, has a song called My Own Man, which says the same things all over again.

Having got us on its side on the subject of sexism, the show proceeds to tackle race; the fellow contestant whose cause Claribel champions is black. That Hollywood liberalism we keep hearing about seems to have made early inroads, since the game show staff, heartwarmingly if not quite believably, all back Claribel up. The studio audience are less enlightened, as the black contestant acknowledges when, thanking Claribel, she says that "those people wouldn't have voted for me for all the tea in China." It may not be the most original image, but it's a resonant and economical one, the best line in the show. But it, too, is vitiated when the actress (Angela Teek) has to go through the obligatory faux-gospel number. The show keeps fighting battles that have already been won, making brave statements that nobody in the audience is going to disagree with. The combination of cliche and uplift has admittedly carried many recent musicals to success. But they have generally applied the formula with a bit more know-how.

They have not, for example, weighed themselves down, as this one does, with a framework set 60 years after the main events. The elderly Claribel is a pack-rat whose apartment is being forcibly cleaned up by a juvenile offender doing community service. Of course the two apparent opposites bond while sorting through Claribels memorabilia, and the younger one learns some invaluable life lessons. What these might be, however, is unclear, since we are told absolutely nothing of what Claribels life has been like from that day to this. All we can take away is the knowledge that the veteran Denise Fergusson can hold a spirited end up in both speech and song; when her teenage help lays an angry rap number on her, she raps right back.

Meanwhile, back in the 1950s, the plot runs out and padding runs in. As queen for the day (and night), Claribel is taken to a nightclub, where a painfullyirrelevantCaribbean-crossed-with-Latin number happens. A mild romantic fling (Cory O'Brien, likeable) transpires out of nowhere and as speedily returns there. A squawky, screen-struck young friend (Marisa McIntyre) gets bewilderingly blackmailed. A more dubious acquaintance (Lisa Horner) is forced into a would-be show-stopper of vengeful hysteria that bears no relation to what has previously passed for her character. Even caricatures need consistency.

Tim French's production has one fine pictorial moment, posing the four aspirant queens against a bare studio backcloth. The musical staging operates on the assumption that anybody, regardless of narrative or persona, can be pressed into singing or dancing service at any time. They cant, not in an old-fashioned, quasi-realistic, dialogue-heavy show like this one. It puts serious, or at least solemn, sentiments into the mouths of dummies, and asks for brownie points.

The Queen for a Day phenomenon itself can be viewed as either liberating or degrading. To its credit, the musical sees both possibilities, but it never contrasts or reconciles them; it just grabs at whichever will make the easiest effect at any given moment. At the end, its implied that Claribel's day as queen was, pun made explicit, the crowning one of her life. That seems sort of sad. The show tries to present it as inspirational.

Queen for a Day runs until Oct. 7. For tickets and more information, visit queenforadaythemusical.com.
