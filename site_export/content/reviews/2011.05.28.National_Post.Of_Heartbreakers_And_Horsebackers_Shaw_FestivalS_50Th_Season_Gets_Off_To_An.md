Bernard Shaw's Heartbreak House was written in, and set during, the First World War, though theres no mention of this in the play until the last 10 minutes, when the English country home in which its set is visited by an air raid. Two of the houseguests, a burglar and an industrialist, run for cover and get blown up. The others, residents and visitors alike, stand their ground and even turn up the lights to make things easier for the bombers. They survive this time, but the play ends with them fervently hoping for another raid tomorrow. There is, of course, a certain grandeur to their defiance; and this, coupled with the fact that they are by and large the most charming and amusing people in the play, usually means that we end up in their corner. Not so in the revival with which the Shaw Festival begins its 50th season. This time we see the ending as Shaw seems to have meant it: as an image of a useless, used-up ruling class embracing its own merited destruction. So, one up to Christopher Newton's production.

The house, which looks like a ship, belongs to the octogenarian Captain Shotover, for many years a mariner and now marinating -in rum and in a despairing philosophy. Learn your business as an Englishman" is his most famous pronouncement, the business being "navigation . neglect it and be damned. He's certain that all around him are damned, and he keeps a store of dynamite to blow up the human race when it goes too far." In his less oracular moments, hes an amiable, absentminded old codger, with two married daughters. In his preface, Shaw divides the British establishment into two categories: the Heartbreakers and the Horsebackers. Of the Shotover sisters Ariadne, whos married a colonial governor and who believes that the people who hunt are the only decent people, is definitely a Horsebacker; Hesione is a vocational Heartbreaker, who enchants every man she meets and whose husband, Hector Hushabye, enchants every woman. The distinction is clearer-cut in the preface than in the play since Ariadne, a.k.a. Lady Utterword, has her heartbreaking moments, too, being if anything a more determined, more cold-blooded flirt than her sister. The play, like many of Shaw's, is full of coruscating oneliners about marriage, though they seem loosely attached to the matters at hand. The second of the play's three acts ends with an exasperated Hector, enslaved to his own romantic image, cursing the world in general and women in particular. In this version, he begins the third act by repeating his imprecations, at which point Leslie Frankishs set obliges him. Its already earned applause by its combination of the domestic and nautical, setting most of the indoor action on a deck with billowing sails for curtains. It earns more by violently tilting, the deck becoming a patio, the perfect setting for the discursive nocturne that constitutes the play's last act. The house is full of electronic noises and this transformation occasions the most striking of them. Two up to Newton's production.

Add to this a batch of good performances and a couple of very effective scenes. But set against all these the lack of an overall rhythm, which raised in my mind all manner of doubts about a play I thought I loved. Its sometimes been described as a dream play; it can be a dream of a debate play. But if it splinters, youre left wondering how it was ever meant to fit together, and why an anatomy of society should devote so much time to its characters' love lives, especially when their emotions are more discussed than displayed. Much depends on Shotover himself. In his early scenes, Michael Ball is funny and endearing, as he wanders on and off delivering perfectly timed dismissals of people he obstinately refuses to recognize, one of them his own child. When challenged about his refusal to stick around whenever someone challenges him, the Captain blames it on his age: "I must go in and out. But going in and out isn't enough to get him through the last act, where hes stationary. His prophetic stature defines the plays; Shaw compared him to Lear, and it's at least true that of the play's three principal women, two are his biological daughters and the third a kind of surrogate. If Ball were to play Lear, I can imagine him bringing off a, well, heartbreaking reconciliation scene, but I find it harder to envisage him on either the throne or the heath.

Blair Williams Hector is splendid from start to finish, at once full-blooded and ironic. Others tend to ease into their roles. Patrick McManus, for example, as the gentle failed industrialist Mazzini Dunn; at first, with his curly hair and tilted nose, he looks like a cartoon of ineffectuality. He is ineffectual, but he also has his share of wisdom, and McManus makes this increasingly and ringingly apparent. Mazzini is also the only man who can resist the charms of Hesione Hushabye which, in Deborah Hays clear-eyed and warmthroated performance, are considerable. The scene in which she recoils from him recoiling from her is both the deepest and the most scintillating of the evening. Laurie Paton's Ariadne begins as a snobbish stereotype; when she finally gets to state her case, it's intolerable on one level and irresistible on another -a case of the she-devil having some of the best tunes. The same grace isnt afforded to Boss Mangan, the tycoon who has bankrupted Mazzini and many others while pretending to rescue them; he perishes while the Lady survives. And though he too is allowed a credo, its bumptious rather than assured; he even confesses that his riches are mainly fictitious. He's also out of his depth in this household, and of course, he falls hopelessly in love with Hesione; and his continuous confusions and humiliations can make him a perversely sympathetic figure. Benedict Campbell will have none of this; his Mangan displays no audible class differences from his hosts and is undeviatingly, sneeringly malignant. He also executes some magnificent twitches when awakened from a trance. The odd thing about Campbell's performance is that he commands the stage while hes on it but one forgets about him when he isnt: a fault perhaps of the production, but also of the play, which is so intent on exposing and undercutting the Boss that it removes him as a threat. Or maybe it just likes being paradoxical.

There's solid support from Patricia Hamilton as the brisk house help and Patrick Galligan as Randall the rotter," a character the play cuts down without bothering to build him up. William Vickers' heartbreak housebreaker would probably be more hilarious if less of a meal were made of his mock-repentance. Which leaves Robin Evans Willis in the pivotal role of Ellie: Mazzinis daughter, Mangans possible prey, Hesione's friend and the Captain's convert. She does very well, but without solving the roles tough if not insurmountable problem: Her progress, if any, is the plays. It's she whose heart is initially broken, by Hector, in a relationship talked about but hardly shown. All the subsequent talk of heartbreak remains just talk. Other things resonate: "What, asks Hector, of the bigger ship, this soul's prison we call England?" Now it could be Canada. Or the world. Two minor cavils: Newton surprisingly fails to make much of the play's lurch into rhymed verse at the end of Act 1 which, if boldly handled, can jerk us into another world. He also flouts the stage directions by not allowing Randall to play Keep the Home Fires Burning on his flute at the end. This may accord with his wish for a downbeat ending. But it's a loss.
