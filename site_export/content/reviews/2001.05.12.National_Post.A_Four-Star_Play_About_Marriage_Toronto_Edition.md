THIS HOTEL

Theatre Passe Muraille

- - -

The stage is initially hidden from us by one of those red billowy things that, according to legend, used to be found in theatres all the time; I think it's called a curtain. It's very provocative. When it rises, it reveals a living room with a sofa, just like in the old days.

A man and a woman are there, and the woman talks about having the sofa re-covered. There is sex in the air. The couple are not married, at least not to one another. This is revealed when a door opens -- one of several on the set -- and in walks the lady's husband, carrying flowers. He is played by Randy Hughson and he looks, in these discomfiting circumstances, as amiably woebegone as only Mr. Hughson can.

He does what husbands, faced with disintegrating marriages, traditionally do. He leaves the house and checks into a hotel. And in This Hotel -- as the play's author, Alex Poch-Goldin, calls it -- he meets his life, broken into taunting fragments and rotating around him.

Not to hold out on you, This Hotel is a brilliant evening: masterfully written, acted, directed and designed. One of its hallmarks is a vivacious versatility. Steve Lucas's set is composed of simple units that keep revolving, with unnerving ease, to take us from bedroom to bar to dining room and back again.

Of the actors, only Mr. Hughson as the wandering cuckold Lester is confined to a single role. Alon Nashman, indeed, is billed as only one character, called The Bellhop, but he turns out to constitute the entire male staff: receptionist ("We treat you", he says with alarming cordiality, "just as ourselves") porter, valet, waiter, barman, and -- as the hotel likes to cater for every taste - - rent-boy.

He is, as he would have to be, super-knowledgeable and super- efficient, although something about his service -- maybe it's his intermittent oily accent -- does not inspire total confidence; we seem to be in an Expressionist hotel of the damned, presided over by Manuel from Fawlty Towers.

Brenda Bazinet's cut-and-dried style finds admirable expression (and also some expansion) in the hero's wife, who cheats on him to maintain his flagging interest and finds herself aroused and frustrated simultaneously; she also plays a frigidly flirtatious Southern lady who seems to have wandered into the hotel under the impression that it was Blanche DuBois' Tarantula Arms. (The bellhop has a speech that seems to confirm this, likening the place to a spider's web, with Lester, of course, the fly.)

Veronika Hurnik is a psychologically and physically damaged guest who plays an elaborate attract-and-repulse game with Lester, and also a French chambermaid who seems to be bearing the secret of life.

Richard Zeppieri plays all the remaining men, ranging from the wife's temporary stud (a nice study in bewildered brutality) to a bisexual husband looking for rough trade and then retreating from it in respectable alarm. He and Lester share an admirable scene in the bar, each shunning his own image in the other; it's counterpointed with Lester's memories, full of touchingly clumsy lyricism, of his first meeting, also in a bar, with his wife Arlene, whose name inspires him to some unaccustomed flights.

The advance word on the play (from a previous outing in the Fringe Festival) was that all the characters are aspects of the protagonist. I don't buy this, even if the author does. I think they represent parallels and possibilities; and while Lester may be imagining his wife's encounters with her boyfriend, his imaginings - - like his recollections of the marriage itself -- are meant to be substantially correct.

It doesn't matter; what counts is that we have a gripping, funny phantasmagoria with a dramatic logic that leads Lester to a breaking point that Mr. Hughson makes very moving.

There are weaknesses. I don't see the point of the Southern belle (and it's difficult to satirize a Tennessee Williams heroine, given that Williams himself, in his lesser plays, did the job so unintentionally well).

The hotel image, not to mention the spider-fly stuff, is not new, but Mr. Poch-Goldin (in a great advance on his previous play, Jim and Shorty) makes it his own; the curtain, framing the conventions of a door-laden bedroom farce, is a helpfully ironic touch. It's probably just as well that the maid delivers her message of hope in French, since we might not stand for it in English. Its effect is to send Lester back to his wife. This is the right thing for him, but their last confrontation does not suggest untroubled seas ahead.

It's a stark, questioning image, held to perfection by the director, Kelly Thornton, whose work throughout has faultless precision and panache. Playwright and director are also husband and wife; that, at least, says something hopeful about marriage.
