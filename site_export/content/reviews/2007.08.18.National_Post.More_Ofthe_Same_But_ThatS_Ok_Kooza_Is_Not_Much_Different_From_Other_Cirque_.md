The new Cirque du Soleil production is called Kooza, which apparently is a Sanskrit word meaning both "box" and "treasure." According to the program, the show "tells the story of the Innocent, a naive but charming clown striving to find his own place in the world." We're also told that it's an attempt to take the Cirque back to its roots and to pay tribute to the spirit of the clown who "cajoles us into tenderness and paints our smiles into sweetness."

In fact, it's nothing like as bad as that sounds. Indeed it is, like most Cirque shows, rather wonderful. But I can't see that it's much different from its predecessors. Cirque shows, to the extent that I've ever been able to follow them on a narrative or -- so help me -- an intellectual level, have always seemed to be about naive clown-figures going on journeys and finding themselves in kingdoms populated chiefly by acrobats, jugglers and, yes, other clowns. The protagonist wanders among and around them until it's time to go, at which point he does. I'll concede that this quest has an especially magical beginning. Our young pilgrim opens what I have to call the Semi-Eponymous Box and out of there springs -- as if on a spring -- a Jack. He functions forthwith as the hero's partner, guide, philosopher (I guess) and friend.

Another concession: The show does send in more clowns than usual. One of them is a clown in a crown: an august master-of-ceremonies figure who can still be as giggly and irresponsible as any of his confreres. A departure in the lineup is a large shaggy dog, or rather -- in keeping with the no-animals-allowed policy by which this circus has set itself apart from all others -- a human being in an outsize doggy costume. There are also cops, from the Keystone precinct. There are more chases in this show than in any Cirque I recall.

As always, though, the performers who make us catch our breath are the daredevils. The Cirque always hires the best, and at least two of this year's troupes within the troupe are phenomenal. About half-way through the evening, there's a high-wire act. Of course, there's always one of these, and part of the suspense that they generate lies in our curiosity as to how they can possibly top the memories of previous years. This crew ups the ante by pretending to lower it. There are two wires, one above the other. The one beneath, though terrifying enough to you and me, is for beginners, who can be satisfied with little more than walking from one end of the line to the other and back without falling hundreds of feet to the floor. Wimps, really.

It's on the high high wire, literally in parallel, that the advanced stuff happens. Up here is where a couple of guys insouciantly pedal bicycles along the wire, singly at first, and then in a kind of tandem, courtesy of a plank that hooks on to both riders' shoulders. And then, from somewhere even higher in the Big Top, a chair descends and settles snugly on the plank. It goes without saying that a third man will then sit on the chair and perform a few casual stunts of his own. Somewhere around this point, we notice that some kind souls at ground level are holding out a security blanket. This may be the greatest shock, since Cirque du Soleil has always disdained anything remotely resembling a safety net. It turns out, though, that the contraption isn't there to cushion any falling human bodies (not, of course, that there are any) but to receive the chair when its occupant tosses it down with what we would once have referred to as gay abandon. Well, chairs cost.

But you ain't seen nothin' yet: not until a turn that the program calls the Wheel of Death but that I think of as the Human Hamsters. Two men spin through the air on a pair of gigantic rings, rotating high, low and wide on a single axis. They perform comparatively modest gymnastic feats while cradled within their respective discs; tiring of this, they climb on to the outer surfaces and perform acrobatics that are absolutely incredible.

The geometric beauty of such feats eclipses even their daring. The performers, poised perpetually on the edge of self-destruction, seem to be defying time as well as space; there's a kind of serenity in watching them, a hypnotic sense of mortality being held at bay. Elegance rules, too, in the exploits of a fellow who does his stuff on top of a self-assembled tower of chairs. Chairs seem to be very important in this show.

It isn't all lovely. There's a more literal kind of magic act, a conjuror who belongs more to music-hall than to circus and who, unlike the athletes and acrobats, is hardly the best of his kind. He also breaks with Cirque convention by talking to us, at length, in English. He does have some respectful fun with audience participation, though in this respect the clowns have beaten him to the punch. On the night of my visit, they commandeered a charming young lady from the house, made her disappear in the first act and didn't bring her back until the second, after we'd forgotten about her, I'm sure they treated her well wherever they'd taken her, but it did mean she'd had to miss the high-wire.

There's also a garish tinge to the show, probably bearing witness to the Cirque's long residence in Las Vegas. There are dancers dressed up as human skeletons, and a nightclub chanteuse who sings behind some of the acts without adding much to them. What any of this has to do with Kooza's proclaimed Age of Innocence, I cannot figure. But it must be hard to stay pure and ethereal in the midst of all that earthly merchandise.

- Kooza plays through Oct. 7 at the Grand Chapiteau. Call 1-800- 361-4595 for details.
