In Jennifer Tarver's beautifully proportioned Stratford production of Waiting for Godot, Tom rooneys Vladimir emerges, if not as the central character - impossible given the plays egalitarian structure - then as the defining one. Tarver's program note suggests Vladimir may even be a stand-in for Samuel Beckett himself: the stoic philosopher asking questions of the universe, and maybe foolhardy enough to expect an answer. He may even be that most doomed of Beckettian figures: an optimist.

For a tramp, he's remarkably dapper. But then the play never actually says Vladimir and his companion Estragon are tramps; its just a conclusion that a couple of generations of audiences, abetted by a tradition of bowler-hatted productions, have jumped to concerning the likely status of two men who sleep in ditches, subsist on rotting carrots and turnips, and spend their evenings in a barren stretch of country awaiting the arrival of a chronically non-appearing benefactor.

rooneys Vladimir seems to accept this situation but not altogether to belong to it. He can't help interrogating it, trying to put the best possible face on it. He appears educated; he might even be a lapsed academic. He certainly never tires of trying to make his pal see sense, or at least of making him admit some continuity in their existence, if only in the matters of recognizing his own pair of boots or recalling where he spent the previous night and how many people beat him up during it. There are vestiges of a humane tradition in Vladimir; faced with the plutocratic Pozzo and his slave Lucky, whose neck has been rubbed raw by the rope around it, he cries out - impulsively and even passionately in rooney's performance - its a scandal." Its only a passing moment of protest, quickly withdrawn under pressure, but it's something. Something more is his meditation as he watches his friend asleep (there are few things to be sure of in this play but friendship is one of them, even if it's defined as shared endurance) and reflects that someone, somewhere, is watching him; that he, too, is a character in a play, a tragicomedy both comic and cosmic. Theres no suggestion, by the way, that the person watching is a merciful God, let alone a merciful Godot. I confess there were times when I had difficulty accepting this Vladimir, because he departs so far from the norm: Even the late William Hutt wasnt this cultured. But that's my fault, not his.

If rooney's Vladimir is a fine performance, Stephen Ouimettes Estragon is a great one: as down at heart as at heels, and perversely glorying in his destitution. He understands very little of what Vladimir says, and what he does understand disgusts him. He certainly doesnt share his buddy's humane instincts: His reaction to Lucky's plight is to kick him when hes down, literally. Hes only afraid Lucky might kick back; and Ouimette keeps the balance between fear and vindictiveness beautifully tight. Morally considered, Estragon is a deplorable person, but we can't disown him, if only because Vladimir, and therefore Beckett, doesn't; hes us, give or take a vegetable or two. And hes the realist of the pair; when Vladimir boasts that at least they've kept their appointment and asks "how many can say as much?, Estragon replies millions." It's the cruel puncturing of a rhetorical question, and possibly my favourite moment in the play, both funny and final. Meanwhile, they suffer with their respective physical infirmities; Ouimettes Estragon continuously but resignedly with his feet, rooneys Vladimir spasmodically but agonizingly with his bladder.

However dark its ultimate meaning, Waiting for Godot is, line by line and moment by moment, an exceptionally lively play, kept brightly lucid by the cross crosstalk of its vagrant double-act. Tarver's production doesn't let a connection go by,

and makes most of them casually fresh. This goes for the physical content as well as the verbal; the juggling of three hats between two men rises to superb heights of puzzlement. The same goes for the rubbery facility with which they fall down and get up. The Tom Paterson stage, for the third time this season, comes up trumps; in Teresa Przybylskis set (at the furthest stylistic remove from her sumptuous Lady Windermeres Fan at the Shaw) it's sternly dominated by the essential unexpectedly flowering tree, mercilessly surveyed by a swivelling silvery moon, and bisected by a strip of road that, Tolkien fashion, appears to go ever onward. It is of course the highway for the two appearances of Pozzo and Lucky. Pozzo is Brian dennehy, who flashes a pearly-white grin worthy of Mack the Knife, and finds a perfectly matching vocal tone, as of one unbending to his inferiors from unimaginable heights, with which languidly to interrogate his new acquaintances about their ongoing appointment. Called upon, however, to articulate the encompassing despair of his second-act blindness, he merely shouts, without the genuine power to back it up. real despair is the province of randy Hughson's Lucky, taut and tortured at the end of his rope, and erupting on command into his one long speech. Most actors deliver this as a virtuoso display of sheer nonsense; here its something deeper and more painful: an anguished attempt to find hope and meaning through a mash-up of philosophical jargon, collapsing in the admission that despite such temporary distractions as "tennis of all kinds," there is none to be found. The speech has shape and meaning, even in denying it, like the play itself.

Though I have to question: If Pozzo and Lucky are returning in Act 2 from the fair to which they were en route in Act 1, why dont they re-enter from the direction in which they left? I've heard of the Absurd, but this is ridiculous.
