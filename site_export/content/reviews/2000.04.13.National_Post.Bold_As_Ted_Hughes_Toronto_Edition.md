THE ORESTEIA, PART ONE: THE HOME GUARD

Du Maurier Theatre, Toronto

The last du Maurier World Stage season -- but, please the gods, not the last World Stage season ever -- begins with a brave raid on the foundation-stone of Western drama. Britain's National Theatre has brought over its new production of Aeschylus' Oresteia: the only full-blown Greek tragic trilogy that we possess, given in a magnificent translation by the late Ted Hughes and spread over two evenings.

This turns out not to mean one-and-a-half plays a night. Part one is entirely given over to the artwork formerly known as Agamemnon, first and best-known instalment of the original serial.

It's here re-titled The Home Guard, and in Katie Mitchell's production it turns out to be a play about a quartet of veterans from some unspecified war of the 20th century.

They are still wearing their frayed and crumpled demob suits, with poppies in their lapels, and they are in wheelchairs. They seem powerless, but their rulers pay them a lot of attention, so they presumably represent public opinion.

They are, of course, our old acquaintances the Greek chorus, perpetual challenge and perpetual bane to anyone who would stage one of these tragedies and anyone who tries to respond to them.

The dramatists themselves seem to have wearied of the convention, and the choral role in the extant plays grows progressively less.

But in The Oresteia the chorus is still in full flood: filling us in on the story so far, commenting, philosophizing, responding to or interrogating the principals, and listening outside the royal palace to the horrific deeds being committed within.

The climax of this play is the murder of Agamemnon, king of Argos returned from the Trojan war, by his wife, Clytemnestra, in revenge for their daughter Iphigenia, ritually sacrificed by her father 10 years before so that the Greek fleet might set sail for Troy.

As the king and his Trojan concubine Cassandra are slain in the royal bath, members of the chorus debate what they should do, in the end doing nothing.

A.E. Housman once wrote a hilarious parody of Greek tragedy, and especially of the old translations of it, in which the chorus, on hearing a series of blood-curdling screams, observes, "I would not be reputed rash, but yet/ I doubt if all be well within the house."

There is none of that prim vagueness for Ted Hughes. His translation is bold, iron-hard, and colloquial.

It seems to be written north-country; certainly much of it is here spoken north-country, especially the choral parts. Between them, Hughes, Mitchell and the actors have taken the curse off the chorus.

A modern audience, confronted by the traditional line of imposingly masked figures talking in unison, naturally assumes that it's talking for the author, even when it's talking in platitudes (perhaps especially when it's talking in platitudes). Mitchell's approach, veering about as far toward naturalism as Greek tragedy possibly can, establishes them as just another character in the play: a collective character with its own biases and limitations.

If we want to know why they don't intervene at the end, their chairbound state provides the perfect illustration and the perfect excuse. (Since the four actors also play the four named male characters in the piece, there's a sly pleasure in seeing each of them wheel himself offstage -- when we're not supposed to be looking -- to assume his new identity.)

I've never listened to a Greek chorus with more attention or more enjoyment. But this clarity, welcome though it is, comes at a price. In their zeal to communicate, the actors practically button-hole us with each line, frequently prefacing it with a chatty "now" or "look."

Trying so hard to avoid sounding rhetorical, they end up with a new and equally emphatic kind of anti-rhetoric. The principals don't have this option; or at least, and thankfully, they don't exercise it.

But they have other problems. They, too, are modernized. Agamemnon, returning home telling of rape and plunder is, by inescapable inference, a Serbian warlord; Clytemnestra, constantly posing and orating at a microphone, seems to be Eva Peron. Psychologically, these parallels are limiting; mythologically, they're demeaning.

Helen Schlesinger's Clytemnestra has power, and a sense of festering grievance under a politician's politeness, but she runs out of resources.

The old approach to Greek tragedy believed in heroes; the new doesn't. But it seems -- and I readily confess that here I'm at the mercy of what translators tell me -- that the plays believed in them, not necessarily with admiration but with awe.

This play contains the textbook example of hubris: Agamemnon, daring comparison with the gods, is persuaded to tread a ceremonial red carpet into his home. With Michael Gould playing Agamemnon as a cynically weary opportunist, it has no resonance, though the production tries hard to pile it on. The carpet here is composed of his dead child's dresses; incredibly in this realistic context, he doesn't notice. Or if he does, he doesn't say anything.

The production is surprisingly short on visual ideas. There is the carpet and there are the wheelchairs.

There's the sight of Agamemnon and Cassandra dead in the blood- filled bath, as if Charlotte Corday had climbed into the tub with Marat.

There's also a lot of pointless fiddling with tape recorders and video screens. And there's also the figure of the slaughtered Iphigenia, wandering accusingly through the action with a gag on her mouth.

You get the point early on and resent the repetition, but it pays off when her father is killed, and takes her ghostly place. The chain of blood, with two plays still to go, stretches on.

It stretches back, too, in the endless perspective accorded by Greek mythology. At the play's end we meet Aegisthus, Agamemnon's cousin and Clytemnestra's lover and accomplice, who reveals a whole new set of motives, stretching back into the previous generation of the proverbially cursed House of Atreus.

This sudden shift is confusing to us, but the Greeks would have taken it in their stride, as another leaf from a shared book of legend that could be opened or closed at will: a story in which, roughly speaking, everybody is guilty. Similarly the play provides us with a surprisingly complete retrospective of the horrors at Troy, vividly conveyed here in the herald's (Robert Bowman) matter- of-fact account of the city's destruction, or by Lilo Baur's Cassandra in her last prophetic trance.

The play has its own kind of timelessness, conflating past, present and future into a vision, pitiless but humane, of human violence and human suffering. That's what's in it for us now, and it's a lot.
