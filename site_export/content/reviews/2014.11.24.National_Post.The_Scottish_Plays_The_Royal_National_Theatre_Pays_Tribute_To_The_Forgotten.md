The last six kings of Scotland, before its union with England, were all called James. They didnt have an uninterrupted run; between the reigns of James the fifth and sixth came that of Mary Queen of Scots, a sovereign who has never lacked for theatrical attention. Her male predecessors, by contrast, have remained obscure, both on and off the stage. The James Plays are an ambitious attempt by a Scottish playwright and TV writer, Rona Munro, to even the odds.

She has written three linked plays, each devoted to one of the first three Jameses. They have been staged by the National Theatre of Scotland, a peripatetic company now eight years old, whose production of Black Watch was a sensation when it visited Toronto a few years ago. After opening at the Edinburgh Festival, the James trilogy has come to London as a co-production with the National Theatre of Great Britain (which presumably, and for the time being, includes Scotland, but let's not get into that), mounted in the South Bank complex's largest house the Olivier, a platform stage transformed for the nonce to a theatre-in-the-round. It deserves the honour.

The three kings it treats have more in common than a name. All were married off to foreign heiresses, and all met violent ends. Its the 15th century; the Jameses, from the Stewart family, are murderously opposed by greedy and obstreporous rival clans. James I passed his youth as a prisoner of Englands Henry V, victor of Agincourt, who helps us get our historical bearings by making a personal end-of-life appearance, foul-mouthed and foulblooded. He wants to teach James how to be a king; and he posthumously succeeds, better than he could have hoped. Returned to Scotland, James imposes something approximating a rule of law, and proves coolly efficient at disposing not just of enemies but of potentially embarrassing friends. His is the most straightforward tale of the three, and the most straightforwardly effective.

James II spent his early years as a frightened pawn of the feuding landowners who disposed of his father. His play starts with a long nightmare sequence showing him hiding in a series of boxes of increasing size. It's a brilliant image, superbly realized in Laurie Sansom's production; the boxes go whizzing about the stage, while their dreaming former occupant sees his younger boxed-inselves impersonated by puppets.

Unfortunately, the play never progresses once hes woken up. Like its hero, it pursues much the same political course as its predecessor; and the one new ingredient - James relationship with a feckless boyhood friend - never justifies the amount of space it occupies. James III, however, changes the pattern; instead of a king learning to impose his will on peers and parliament we have one who would rather just let them get on with it. His principal demand of them is that they fund a choir to follow him around and provide appropriate incidental music.

He is that familiar figure in historical drama: the aesthete king up against a philistine establishment. He's also, because of his wit and impudence, good dramatic value. The real power, however, both in the kingdom and the play, resides with his wife, a Danish princess played by a real Danish actress (Sofie Grabol, from TV's The Killing). She takes cool and constructive control of the familys, and the countrys, finances; she also imports, from Venice, a new brand of mirror, one that enables its users to see themselves as they actually are, to their almost universal embarrassment.

This is another fine stage image, from an author whose visual sense is matched by her verbal; Munro writes forceful, colloquial dialogue that bridges the centuries; it's modern without being embarrassing. She respects her characters, and the audience. I can't imagine any Canadian company would stage these plays, but World Stage or Luminato could and should import them.

From the Scots boys to The Scottsboro Boys: This is the John Kander-Fred Ebb musical that had a discouragingly short run on Broadway in 2010 but has triumphantly resurfaced in the West End. Its about the case of nine young black American men who were, literally, railroaded; travelling by train through Alabama in 1931, in search of work, they were accused of rape by two white women who were themselves trying to avoid arrest for prostitution. The nine were tried, convicted, and sentenced to death. Appeals and re-trials followed, but the verdicts stood, despite one of the accusers having recanted her testimony. One of the "boys" died in prison, 20 years after his arrest; the rest were freed or paroled, four of them briefly becoming a vaudeville act.

This last circumstance gives the musical its concept; its presented as an old-time minstrel show with the crucial difference that, instead of white performers in blackface, we have blacks playing whites and blacks, finally donning an extra layer of makeup to drive the point brutally home. A racist trope is turned against itself. This is the finest use of showbiz metaphor in any Kander and Ebb musical: better than Chicago, where the style is in paranoid excess of the subject; better than Cabaret, where the horrific subject cannot help dwarfing the style. Here, hand and glove are an exact fit.

Kander's music is dangerously, seductively tuneful; Ebb's lyrics are among his most polished and pointed. David Thompson's book places the story perfectly inside its theatrical frame. Susan Stroman has recreated her New York staging, bringing over four key members of the original cast; her direction and choreography are her best yet, stylish and inventive without a drop of superfluous splash. Her routines, terrifically executed really can make you, if not laugh and cry, then smile and wince at the same time.

The British recruits include, delightfully, a veteran classical actor, Julian Glover, in the one white role: a paternal Mr. Interlocutor who doesn't get it. It sometimes seems that every musical since the year 2000 has been trumpeted as the best of the new millennium. This one actually is.
