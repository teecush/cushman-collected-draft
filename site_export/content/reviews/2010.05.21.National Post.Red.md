The hottest tip for this season's best new play Tony Award is John Logans Red (Golden Theatre, New York), a two-hander in which the principal hand, and voice, is the painter Mark Rothko. Plays about art and/or artists get brownie points on Broadway, especially if theyve been imported from England. Red originated at the Donmar Warehouse in London, though its author is American. Logan's most prominent credits are the screenscripts for Gladiator, The Aviator and also for Sweeney Todd, which prompts me, especially in this Sondheim celebration year, to think of his painter play as Sunday in the Park with Mark.

Red is a discussion about art, or rather a series of tirades about it, directed by Rothko at the aspiring young painter whom he's taken on as an assistant, meaning a glorified gofer. On no account must he expect to do any actual painting; the most he can hope is to help apply the primer. And we do indeed get to see the two men furiously lathering a previously blank canvas with the heros favourite eponymous colour, thereby earning the rapturous applause that audiences always bestow on actors for performing strenuous real-life feats that they never expected to see on a stage. It also, I suppose, makes a point about art being work.

Theres one legitimately great moment. Rothko tells of someone passing his house and saying, "Hey, all those Rothkos: I wonder who owns them." It's the 1950s and he's getting hot, after years in the cold. Some of his preachments are interesting and all of them are vigorous. But theyre generic. He could be any self-centred practitioner letting off steam. He lauds his own work, expresses qualified admiration for some of his peers (hes guardedly OK with Jackson Pollock), and pours scorn on his juniors, Andy Warhol and the pop art generation, whom he accuses of giving in to commercialism rather than standing tall against it. (All artists, he says, in a predictable but still funny line, should be poor, except me.) The dramatic fulcrum is Rothko's real-life commission from New York's Four Seasons restaurant, a deal on which he reneges when he visits the place and realizes that, however uncompromising, his art doesnt stand a chance of disturbing the diners.

Late in the play, Ken the assistant, in a welcome bit of worm-turning, points out that his boss has never once asked him anything about himself -- he might be single, married, straight or gay. But the same accusation could be levelled against the play itself. I learned from Simon Schamas program note that Rothko's trip to the Four Seasons was made in the company of his second wife; the play never lets on that he had a second wife, or indeed a first one, or any children. It grants him no humanity, only opinions. The show has earned additional Tony nominations, deserved, for its director (Michael Grandage),

its designer (Christopher Oram's studio set is a stripped down battleground) and both its actors; Eddie Redmayne is a keen Ken and Alfred Molina a Rothko of high energy and even higher definition.

A Tony-nominated performance as laid-back as Molinas is aggressive is given by Christopher Walken as a man who years ago was forcibly deprived of one hand by a locomotive careening down the railroad track to which he had been playfully bound by a small gang in Spokane, Wash. (Well, thats his story. There are some holes in it.) The play is actually called A Behanding in Spokane (Gerald Schoenfeld Theatre) and it's the work of Martin McDonagh, who has transposed to an American setting the same tone of grisly flipness that characterizes his comparably titled Irish plays ( A Skull in Connemara, The Lieutenant of Inishmore). What he has left behind is the socio-political colouring and the narrative skill. Walken's character is on a lifelong country-wide search to recover his severed appendage, a quest that inevitably makes him a target for all kinds of con-artists. He doesnt take this kindly, and at one point he has two of his would-be exploiters handcuffed in a motel room that he has primed to blow up; inexplicably, they dont call for help, even though they have access to a phone and at one point even take a call -- from, oh the cuteness, their captor's aged mother.

Walken, who started his career as a song-and-dance man, glides through his role, both physically and vocally; he's always graceful, often terrifying (like an accident-prone version of Javier Bardem in No Country for Old Men), sometimes hilarious, and once -- in a spasm of self-doubt thats the plays finest moment -- both baffled and piteous.

Sam Rockwell impishly plays a bellboy-ex-machina, and Anthony Mackie ( The Hurt Locker) is one of the two tricked tricksters, both of whom are black. This has prompted accusations of racism from a New Yorker reviewer who professes to have no problem with MacDonagh's uniformly unflattering portrayals of the Irish. What's really troubling is that its MacDonagh himself, one of the few real playwrights around, who seems to have lost a hand, or at least to be writing with one tied behind his back.

The best dramatic evening I had in New York was at a two-year-old revival of a 70-year-old play. I never expected to see a better production of Thornton Wilders Our Town than Soulpepper's in Toronto, but David Cromer's acclaimed off-Broadway staging is as good, and different.

At the Barrow Street Theatre, which has the feel and dimensions of a community hall, Wilders prescribed simplicity of setting is taken to extremes: just two small tables and some chairs with the audience on three sides. The narrating Stage Manger (Michael Shannon) passes among us in a performance of virtuoso unassumingness, and a vanished society, narrow in its horizons but rich in feeling, comes movingly to life. The final graveyard scene is especially fine, crowned when the just-buried Emily is granted her one day back on Earth; a white curtain parts, disclosing the sights, sounds and smells of a real kitchen. Besides being a great coup de theatre in its own right, this ironically honours the plays own conventions. The play rejects naturalistic scenery, in the pursuit of truth; the production restores it--in the one scene that's pure fantasy.
