MOTHER TONGUE

Factory Studio Theatre, Toronto

- - -

This time around, the Factory Studio has been turned into a traverse theatre. That means the audience sits on either side of a platform that snakes down the middle of the hall. Unusually, the platform leads up to a small, conventional picture-frame stage. The platform recalls the runways of the Oriental theatre, and its used here for myths, dreams and monologues. The end-stage reeks of what we used to call kitchen-sink drama, and is in fact used here for scenes set in a kitchen. East versus West, real life versus fancy: These are what Mother Tongue is about, and Jim Warren, the director, and Glenn Davidson, his designer, have found an appropriate method of illustrating the contrasts.

I wish I could say the contents matched up to the packaging, but Betty Quan's play comes over as an ordinary domestic drama with a few exotic trappings -- and the trappings are actually less interesting than the domesticity.

It is a play about communication or the lack of it, and the theme has been assiduously worked out. We are in Vancouver, in the home of a widowed Chinese immigrant, who speaks only Cantonese. (Clever title, by the way.) She has two teenage children, both of them born in Canada. Mimi, her daughter, has won a scholarship to study architecture in Ontario.

Mimi's younger brother, Steve, is 16, and has been deaf since suffering an accident at the age of 11. Mimi speaks and thinks in English, though she tells us she sometimes dreams in Chinese. Steve communicates through sign language. As Mimi says, in the most poignant and pungent line in the play, he doesnt like to speak, since, not being able to hear himself, he is afraid that he might sound stupid.

Mother can understand only a few of her daughters English words and none of her son's signs, so she is doubly isolated. At some level, though, she wants it that way. She resents her daughter's independence, and especially dreads her imminent departure. ("A woman should only leave home to get married" she says, grasping helplessly at ancestral straws.)

And Mimi, though she may rage at her mother's intransigence, also fears she may be missing out on something.

She thinks of her dead Chinese father, and connects him with legends about an emperor and his daughter.

The father materializes from time to time. He is sympathetically played by Paul Sun-Hyung Lee, the picture of a hard-working shirt- sleeved immigrant entrepreneur, but these sequences never take off. They are lumpy, and somewhat soporific. Better are moments in which the mother talks directly to us -- in English -- about her hopes and fears when she first came to Canada, especially her fears. Jane Luk is excellent in the role, the unforced immediacy of her recollections contrasting with the fearful mask she wears in company. Steve (Sean Baek) also has his soliloquies but these poetic releases from silence do not universalize his plight; they obscure it. Marjorie Chan as Mimi does the bulk of the talking, both to her family and to us, but she never gets beyond a one-dimensional portrait of chippy adolescence.

She does, though, share in the most effective scene in the play: the one in which she and her mother square off over what the former sees as liberation and the latter as desertion. We can probably all respond to that conflict on some level. The trouble is that while watching, we feel sorry for ourselves or for people we know, rather than for the characters.

Sure, drama is meant to reach the general through the particular, but if the particular doesn't first come to life in its own terms, then a vital stage has been skipped. The characters are, at best, blanks who have struck a nerve. The misty superstructure, the elaborate play with different forms of non-communication, seem like an elaborate way of playing the multicultural card and the disability card simultaneously.

The father-daughter dream encounters seem a recurring theme for this writer: They recall a similar motif in her YPT adaptation of Ghost Train. (Though Mother Tongue, I believe, was written earlier.)

Ghost Train deals with the building of the Canadian Pacific railroad, a subject also treated, of course, in the opera Iron Road, recently seen at the Elgin. I drag this in -- by the heels, I admit - - because this may be my only chance to pay tribute to that stirring, gripping and moving Tapestry production. I hope it comes back.
