FAME

Pantages Theatre, Toronto

Up until Tuesday night at the Pantages, Fame had passed me by, and I rather wish that it had continued to do so. Fame, we are often assured by those who have it, can be a terrible thing. Fame--The Musical certainly is.

Though I missed the movie, and never caught so much as a single episode of the TV series, some basic information about the franchise had got through to me. I knew it was about New York kids attending a high school for the performing arts. David De Silva, who originated the film that set the cycle in motion, earns our goodwill by writing in the program about the importance of the arts in education. A worthy and sympathetic notion, though, is not enough; and though the stage production keeps proclaiming its own idealism, the actual product is thoroughly debased, an imitation of an imitation.

I gather that the musical takes themes, characters and situations that featured in the earlier versions, stirs them around a bit and changes the names. It certainly seems to be adhering to a formula. Its score is mostly new but it retains the title song from the movie, probably for identification purposes. Certainly the number itself, as rendered here, is no great shakes. It's an unshaded hymn to showbiz ambition, notably only for the odd fact that the character who belts it out eventually comes to a bad end. This could be mordant irony, but it's likelier to mean whoever planned the show was thinking one moment at a time.

The story unfolds in the early 1980s, in a gaunt rambling building that is about to give way to swishier premises at Lincoln Center, all this information being more succinctly conveyed in the program than it is from the stage. We follow an assortment of students who are variously talented as actors, dancers and musicians, and who train in all those disciplines while also having to maintain good academic grades.

We focus on three pairs of kids, one from each stream. All three couples have a similar dynamic, each consisting of one conformist and one rebel. The son of a virtuoso violinist, so straight that he even wears a tie on his first day in school though not thereafter, falls for an overly ambitious singer who just cant wait. A precocious ballerina whose obsession with dancing has so far kept her from having a social life is initiated by a gifted but angry hip- hopper whose graduating chances are threatened by his being dyslexic. A boy whose acting ambitions go beyond his previous success in commercials (I want to be a real actor, have you ever read Stanislavsky?") maintains a strange on-off relationship with a girl who at one point suspects him of being gay, when apparently all he is is dedicated.

The show tries hard to be rousing, and occasionally has a stab at being tough. One character dies of a drug overdose, a gesture at tragedy the production has done nothing to earn.

Even in its earlier incarnations, Fame always sounded to me like son of A Chorus Line: a series of confessional vignettes of aspiring performers, caught this time at an earlier period of their aspirations. As a full-blown musical, its like Hello Twelve, Hello Thirteen, Hello Love -- the big puberty number from that show -- indefinitely extended. It has, of course, taken on other influences as well. Bits of it are like a low-rent Rent, while the heroine, or anyway the noisiest girl, makes a final triumphal appearance atop a car that seems to have been driven over from Grease.

The show's residual documentary aspect makes its first half tolerable; there are scenes in class that are simplistic but interesting. In the second, the relationships take over, and with them the songs. These (music by Steve Margoshes, lyrics by Jacques Levy) are a deadening procession that never heard of subtlety or dramatic progression; most of them tell us, loudly and at length, what we already know.

An early number called I Want to Make Magic gives fair warning, as Nick the actor tells us, recklessly mixing his dramaturgical standards, that "I want to do The Lion in Winter/Brecht and Harold Pinter." He goes on to cite acting role-models including Jason Robards, who's a tough one to rhyme; his girlfriend, though, compensates later with a song bearing the all-time catchy title Think of Meryl Streep. The English and dancing teachers come to melodramatic musical blows over a students future; this is embarrassing, not least because its a break in convention. The show has acclimatized us to think of singing as something that kids do, and it's much too late for the grown-ups to join in.

Other students supply comic relief. A horny Hispanic tells the acting class about his erections, in a song called I Can'T Keep It Down. A black girl does some soul shouting about her eating- disorder; she can keep it down, and is overweight in consequence. The fat lady has sung, but the show is far from over.

Hot on her heels comes a big number in which Tyrone, the street- dancing champ, lets out his frustrations; its meant to be his showcase but its climax is a series of somersaults executed by somebody else. (The choreography, by Lars Bethke, who also directs, is the worst: as shapeless and cliche-ridden as anything gets.)

The English teacher does some more emoting about regarding her pupils as her children, since she never had any of her own; the actress is black, and she seems to be destroying herself up there, so she gets the regulation cheers from the white audience that thinks its hearing gospel, but less of them than usual, maybe because this is the third time in the evening the show has tried it on.

Finally -- or anyway at the lowest point -- the fame-crazed young diva who unwisely escaped to California, returns for an anguished workout called In L.A. that keeps restarting when you think it's finished and most of which she sings bent over sideways at an angle of 45 degrees, as if she didn't have enough pain in her life already.

I have to praise something, so let me say that the set, by Norbert U. Kolb, is authentically barracky. The cast are all too old to be credible high-schoolers, but that is not their fault, and maybe not anybody else's either. None of them deserves an individual mention, for good or ill. They all work hard, and achieve little. That, in a show about youthful hopes, is realism more bitter than anybody needs.
