WIT'S END II

Winter Garden Theatre, Toronto

Sandra Shamas is back, with another very funny update on her life and I-don't-think-they're-so-hard times. Wit's End, her previous show, had her in the wake of a divorce, ensconced in a farmhouse deep in the wilderness, or at least some miles away from the nearest town. Wit's End II finds her in possession. She even gets to pay off the mortgage.

Some of her material harkens back to her pre-rural days which, as an urban animal, I find a relief. Shamas has never reduced me to helpless laughter (unlike, say, the English comedienne Victoria Wood) but she does induce helpful laughter, which is not so much the opposite as the next best thing. She can take a cliche and twist it into a wisecrack, as when she tells us that she was mad at her body for so long that she'd forgotten what they were arguing about. Then she twists it still further, into something personal and imaginative, musing that she didn't have really have any reasonable quarrel with her body; it was holding up her head just fine.

On the subject of body image, she tells us of how she decided to celebrate winning an arts awards that entailed selecting a protege and commissioning a new work from her. She decided to go for a nude painting of herself by an artist who works from photographs. The photo shoot was an exposing experience that left her wondering "will she call me tomorrow?" When the painting was completed, she liked it, until it dawned on her (in a beautifully-rendered moment of mental double-take) that it was going to go on public exhibition right in downtown Toronto. Shamas is also very amusing on the differing reactions that the picture, now hanging on her wall, produces from her male and female visitors.

She talks about ageing (she's 45) and how she's read that among the first things to go are your nouns. This, apparently, has proved true for her, but the condition seems to be treatable; she could hardly tell us about nouns if it wasn't. Male-female relationships continue to take up much of her time on stage, but they are not explicitly sexual ones. If you live on a farm without being a farmer, then you need to import one; and apparently the thing to do in such a circumstance is drive to the nearest store and ask them if they have one in stock, or at least if they can have one delivered.

Later on, she finds a man to help her with the chainsawing. She enters into states of deep if taciturn communication with these men, and I think she goes on about them at rather too great a length. I don't know if what she's giving us is straight autobiography but, if it is, then maybe it could stand to be gingered up with a few falsehoods.

Her life takes another strange turn when she cancels her mortgage. To begin with, the finance company seems none too keen to let her out of its clutches. But once they've given in and the deed is done, or torn up (this isn't something that most of us know about), she feels at a loss.

Logic, even her own brand of it, seems to fly out the window. Owning her own home, she no longer needs to work. So she must give up being a performer. But she likes being a performer. What can she do to fill the void? She must get a job. We seem to have come round in a circle without our navigator having quite realized it. Or, to put it another way, the lady has left her non sequiturs dangling all over the property, and sooner or later -- maybe in Wit's End III -- she is going to trip over them.

Nor am I quite sure why her new landed state makes her decide that she must be a lesbian, or why she regards lesbian life as one long pajama party. A couple of real lesbians put her right on these points ("you're not a lesbian; you're just screwed up") and everyone heaves a sigh of relief. I don't want to reveal the sentiment with which Shamas leaves us -- it's a throwaway rather than a punchline and apart from it being unfair it wouldn't mean much in print -- but in its place it works just fine.

She fluctuates, like most people, between questioning and acceptance, and her lithe body language reflects this; she looks, sideways on, like a question-mark continually in the process of straightening out into a statement. A shrewd and likeable performer, she is clearly far from being at her wit's end. Still, I hope she will, at some point, return to the city.
