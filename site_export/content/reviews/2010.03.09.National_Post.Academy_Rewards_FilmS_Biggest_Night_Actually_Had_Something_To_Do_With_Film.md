Canadas own and native sons bookended Oscar night, and both were required to play the good loser. Christopher Plummer, in the evenings first prize-giving, lost to Christoph Waltz as best supporting actor. Plummer himself had predicted he wouldn't win, and personally I think he was up for the wrong performance: for his dying-by-inches Tolstoy in The Last Station rather than for his delightful display in the far more entertaining -- and indeed imaginative -- Imaginarium of Doctor Parnassus. Anyway, he looked very gracious and amused whenever the camera caught him, as it did frequently throughout the evening.

James Cameron, when the camera caught him, looked grumpy, as if he had heard enough jokes about Avatar to last him several lifetimes. But he rallied at the end, embracing and applauding his ex-wife Kathryn Bigelow when she bested him not only for best director, which was sort of expected, but for best picture.

People have been saying for ages that Bigelow's The Hurt Locker was the only movie with a chance against Avatar. This isnt fair; Up in the Air, which scored no wins, was a better film than either; so was A Serious Man, shut out even from the nominations. But The Hurt Locker is a strong war picture that only falls apart toward the end when its characters start saying what it all means. Clearly it deserved to beat Avatar.

I say this with complete confidence despite not having seen Avatar. Or perhaps because of not having seen Avatar. Oscar night is one of the last bastions of permissible prejudice. I now know for sure that I dont want to see Precious; any movie whose participants break down in tears when accepting their prizes goes straight to the bottom of my to-do list. I do want to see Jeff Bridges in Crazy Heart, but then I always did. I like Bridges anyway, and on Sunday night he was Plummer's only competition in the leonine stakes. The only thing that might have put me off him was his actual acceptance speech which, on a generally tight night, was folksy and rambling. Stop saying "man," man.

Still, I liked the idea of having each of the anointed 10 saluted in advance by a former co-star. None of them were embarrassing, and Tim Robbins' roasting Morgan Freeman was even funny. Funniest turn of all, though, was Ben Stiller announcing the make-up awards with his face and, for all I know, the rest of him painted Avatar blue. And not failing to point out that Avatar wasnt up for a makeup award. Maybe hes bucking for a future host gig; they could do worse. Having James Taylor singing In My Life during the obits was a bad idea; having anyone singing anything at that point would be, but this choice took the cake. A mini-documentary telling us what sound editors actually do was helpful. Indeed the whole show seemed, more than usual, to have something to do with film. Hence, perhaps, the retro feel of the staging and music, though some of the golden oldies on the soundtrack seemed chosen at random: As Time Goes By will fit any occasion, but Moon River? The dance sequence was spectacular, if hard to connect to the films it was meant to evoke. Randy Newman, always the bridesmaid, again proved himself (twice, in fact) the only class songwriter left in Hollywood.

Sandra Bullock's dress was accurately described by one of the red carpet experts as looking like an Oscar; which means that, if she hadn't won, she could have gone happily home with herself. Increasing the best picture slate to 10 had surprisingly little impact, except at the end when a rushed Tom Hanks, perhaps fearful of turning into a pumpkin, had no time to list them all before midnight and the big prize. (Hands up, anyone who could have named them.)

Sacha Baron Cohens name had been mentioned as a possible presenter, and that really might have broken the mould: not because hes irreverent, though I'd love to see him do it as Ali G, but because he isn't American. That, I think, would be a first. American hosts have to pretend to be mocking the system, but we know theyre faking it; British or indeed Canadian performers wouldnt have that problem. That said, team Steve 'n' Alec did a fine job. I was grudging about Baldwin in yesterdays paper, but at that point I hadnt seen the whole show. He was given the last word, took it, and ran.
