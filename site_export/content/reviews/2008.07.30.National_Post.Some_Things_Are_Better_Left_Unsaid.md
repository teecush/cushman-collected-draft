THEATRE REVIEW

MRS. WARREN'S PROFESSION

Festival Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

---

Bernard Shaw's first three plays, the ones he called Plays Unpleasant, are among his best. This shouldn't be the case; they deal with specific social abuses of the 1890s that are supposed to have passed into history. As it happens they haven't; and Mrs. Warren's Profession, the last of the trio, has the most pertinent subject of all: prostitution, prompted by poverty, on an international scale. Even if it were dated, though, it would still retain theatrical force; Shaw the pamphleteer can be a more engaging writer, even a more human one, than Shaw the self-satisfied sage.

Mrs. Warren's Profession is what used to be called a "strong" play, one in which social issues are expressed through family conflicts hot enough for melodrama. The central relationship here is what may be the strongest and most volatile of all: mother and daughter. When Mrs. Kitty Warren was a girl, she saw one of her half-sisters dying of lead-poisoning from working in a factory and the other respectably married off to a man who took to drink. She herself, possessed of looks and charm, worked as a barmaid for starvation wages until the penny dropped -- many pennies in fact -- and she followed her full sister Liz into a life of profitable shame. They progressed to opening their own chain of brothels, in Belgium, Austria and Hungary. Mrs. Warren has been able to raise her daughter Vivie handsomely on the proceeds, and to send her to Cambridge, where she became a mathematical prodigy. Mother and daughter have had very little contact over the years, but now -- at a holiday cottage in the English Home Counties -- they confront one another and Vivie, for the first time, learns the truth.

She learns it in two separate stages, which are the occasion of two great duets. These are the heart and soul of the play and of Jackie Maxwell's engrossing production, her best at the Shaw Festival so far. Vivie is an advanced young woman who is preparing for a career in accounting and prides herself on being realistic and unsentimental. It's Mrs. Warren's resentment of this attitude that brings on the revelations. Vivie's initial shock gives way to sympathy and approval as she agrees that, given her economic circumstances, her mother had no option. The first act curtain comes down on the two women in an affectionate embrace

It turns out, however, that even if Mrs. Warren is no longer on the game, she is still in it, and that it's still yielding munificent returns -- on which, of course, Vivie has been living. This shock doesn't wear off, Vivie flees to the London chambers of her friend and business partner Honoria Fraser (a vital offstage female character to balance the equally absent Aunt Liz, now living in cathedral-town respectability). Her mama follows her there, for a second confrontation which leaves them as irreconcilable as they were formerly reconciled.

"I am my mother's daughter," admits Vivie, and the two women overlap to a fascinating extent. Both are practical, active women with a horror of idleness and of hypocrisy, though they may differ on what constitutes the latter. Mary Haney's Mrs. Warren shifts accent, from suburban to outright cockney, at moments of anger or indignation, and the change reflects her willingness to fall back on emotional blackmail, packaged as maternal sentiment. "I wish you wouldn't rant, mother," is Vivie's reaction, but her own rationality is its own form of excess. The play ends with her implicitly forswearing not only daughterly affection, but emotional engagement of any kind. Shaw directs that she does this "with joyous content" but Moya O'Connell's Vivie suggests that something valuable is being given up. Haney has moments of playing the argument rather than the woman and O'Connell sometimes comes closer to tears than either Vivie or Shaw might think decent, but these are two very fine performances, electrifying in the way they advance, bond and then retreat. O'Connell has the passion of great candour, and Haney a residual warmth and jollity that show how Kitty Warren got where she is.

There are also four decidedly subsidiary men. The most fully drawn is Frank, a clergy-man's son and reprobate-in-training who courts Vivie and is part of what she gives up; Andrew Bunker gets both his charm and his honesty (about himself, at any rate) very well. His despised father, one of Mrs. Warren's many old flames, is Ric Reid who takes expert, if probably exaggerated, advantage of the opportunity to play a hangover. The man who drove him to it is Sir George Crofts, man-about-town and Warren enterprises' prime investor, whom Benedict Campbell plays with surprising but textually justified daintiness, with some of the airs of the traditional wicked squire. The quartet is rounded off by David Jansen's gentle Praed, an art-fancier whose function in Mrs. Warren's past (or even his knowledge of it) is left oddly ambiguous. His function in the present is to get everything wrong, by being idealistic about it. Here he stands in for the audience, led to expect a traditional country-house drama with family values (I owe this to David Edgar's brilliant introduction to the Penguin edition) and then seeing them overturned. Sue LePage's sets support this; they start with applause-begging vistas of the Surrey hills, reinforce, and then powerfully contract into a counting-house, which is what this finely unpleasant play is about. - Mrs. Warren's Profession runs until Nov. 1.
