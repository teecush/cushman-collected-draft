HIS MAJESTY

Court House Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont.

Drama is an obdurate medium, maybe a reactionary one. It likes kings, and has rarely succeeded in making heroes of republicans. It has, to its credit, no time for dictators or demagogues. Also, with rare exceptions like Henry V, it prefers its royals to be unlucky; in the interests of tension or pathos they have to be predestined losers (Richard II or Oedipus) or successful villains who finally get theirs (Richard III or Macbeth).

As monarchs themselves get thinner on the ground, so do non- escapist plays about them. Maybe the last to be written by an Englishman was Harley Granville Barkers His Majesty. It was also Barkers own last play (composed between 1923 and 1928) and, unless they decide to give stage-room to a couple of leftover oddments, the concluding item in the complete cycle of his work that the Shaw Festival embarked on in 1988, and that is one of their finest achievements.

Barker is never likely to knock his old mate Shaw off the perch of his own festival since he was neither as funny nor as prolific, but he may well have been more sensitively intelligent, and he certainly comes off as less complacent. His Majesty makes a great capstone. Never staged in its author's lifetime, and only once between then and now, it tumbles off the shelf far more approachably than did its predecessor The Secret Life. In fact, it's as fine and dense a political play as the 20th century has to offer.

It may also be the only good play ever written about an imaginary king, though its protagonist does have historical models. His Majesty, whose given name is Henry, is the deposed monarch of Carpathia, a country whose name sounds rather too cosily Ruritanian, but which seems to be standing in for Austria-Hungary. Kicked out after the First World War, he now resides in Zurich, raising chickens and keeping a gloomy eye on events at home.

The government there is presided over by a pedant-bureaucrat called Madrassy, who survived any number of upheavals by running the Ministry of Education for a succession of regimes that hardly knew they had one. Now it seems his luck may have run out; he faces challenges both from more radical elements within his own party and from a royalist insurrection without.

The king agrees to be flown into Carpathia (by a freelance English pilot), accompanied only by his wife and an unobtrusively invaluable aide. Henrys self-imposed mission is to prevent as much bloodshed as possible. He says he will stay if hes wanted, leave if he isn't. What's more, he means it; he truly believes in the dignity of constitutional monarchy. His queen, more of a traditionalist, wants him to stay, whatever, and she intrigues behind his back to bring this about. There is a dizzying series of alliances and counter-alliances, and people do get killed.

In Neil Munros production the first act, set in a Swiss hotel, is fidgety, marred by some nagging, weightless performances. Things pick up, and the play itself seems happier, when we move to Carpathia: first to a devastated chateau, home of a countess who is both the queens oldest friend and the mother of the rebel leader, then to a becalmed railway carriage which serves both as the king's headquarters and as his prison. It is also the site of a conference involving all interested parties.

This begins the play's second half, and from the moment the lights rise on half a dozen men around the table -- some of the more mysterious and significant with their backs to us -- we are away, in a massively textured world of realpolitik, slippery as ice, thick as mud. And we learn all over again why kings are so theatrically useful. The pressures of a nation are easier to summon when they bear down on one man who may relish the responsibility or loathe it but who is -- in every sense -- constitutionally unable to deny it. Even to abdicate is to make a royal gesture.

Barkers king is a mixture of urbanity and heroics. David Schurmann, who plays him here, is perfectly at home with the first quality and rises firmly and unfussedly to the second. His performance is, in its own terms, faultless, but the terms are familiar. This, for the Shaw Festival, is easy casting, but the play might benefit from an actor who could make the characters choices seem harder, and who could take us beyond regret and into agony.

A couple of years ago Schurmann played the equivalent role in Shaw's The Apple Cart, a play confessedly indebted to this one, but with the crucial difference that Shaw's king is not just intelligent and humane, like Barkers, but politically clever; he is a winner, which makes his play much slighter. (Theres far less at stake in it anyway.) The two performances, though, are identical.

Similarly, Michael Ball, who was a mixture of ally and antagonist in The Apple Cart, is in a similar position here as Madrassy. He too does all right by his own lights, and by his own sounds, which are those of vocal cords encased in whiskers, but one longs for an interpretation with less of the bumbler and more of the knife-edged pedant.

There is plenty of dead-on work at various levels: from Ben Carlson, who puts a rocket under the play as a right-wing Che Guevara; from Lorne Kennedy's unobtrusively resourceful Colonel; from Richard Farrell as another colonel, now demoted to caretaker and an expert at chess and munitions; from Dylan Trowbridge as a delightfully faceless (and virtually silent) officer; and from Roger Rowland as a farmer who, one scholar has observed, bursts on near the end like the asp-bearing clown in Antony and Cleopatra, both heralding and delaying tragedy.

There is also George Dawson, as a fascist-in-waiting who has been compared to Hitler but here comes on more like Goebbels, invincibly slimy. It is more than unfortunate that Munro has cut an exchange in which he recalls a former life blacking the boots of the king and his friends and reveals that he had been sent to prison for "optimism ... belief in the brotherhood of man, adding, I'm quite cured." Little time has been saved, and a whole dimension of the play -- perhaps its most chilling -- has been lost

The finest performances come from the women. Mary Haney as the queen schemes, bullies and -- in memorable silent agony at the end - - suffers with equal conviction. Sharry Flett is better than I have ever seen her, as the countess who goes in the play from one loss to another, and communicates grace through all of them. Their first scene together -- re-establishing an old relationship in drastically new conditions -- goes very deep. Also leave us not forget Severn Thompson as the countess daughter, an incipient partisan who already has the beret.

Most rewardingly, the play gets us into a world in which we can be moved by the feelings of respect or horror inspired by a monarchs fallen sword, even if the monarch has had to borrow the sword for the occasion. We can also see its absurdity. This is not one of the Shaw's perfect productions, like The Return of the Prodigal or Detective Story or Candida, but it winds up being as exciting.
