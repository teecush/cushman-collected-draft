WINGFIELD: LOST & FOUND

Panasonic Theatre, Toronto

---

A t the beginning of Wingfield: Lost & Found, Walt Wingfield's herd of cows wanders off. In this, the seventh play of the Wingfield series, Walt, stockbroker-turned-farmer, is still writing rueful, humorous letters to the editor of a local paper, detailing his adventures and misadventures in Persephone township. The cow-catching, or at least cow-hunting, episode is something of a teaser, in that it has nothing to do, in plot terms, with the rest of the piece. In thematic terms, it's very relevant; see the title. It's also very funny. The unpractised efforts of Walt and assorted friends, neighbours and relations to maintain contact via texting are especially hilarious.

This Wingfield play, like all the others, is written by Dan Needles, directed by Douglas Beattie and acted, all on his own, by Rod Beattie, whose timing, sympathy and vocal versatility remain immaculate. This one is about a drought. Walt's well dries up, which is bad enough, and it seems to be connected to all the other wells around, which is obviously worse. He doesn't know where on his property to start drilling for water, and the cheerfully malevolent firm of professional diggers who charge him $8,000 per failed excavation are no help. Somebody suggests calling in the water-diviners or, as they're known locally, water-witches. Walt, who has been around Persephone long enough to know when to keep his skepticism in check, interviews three of them, this despite the warning of Maggie, his enchantingly soft-voiced and sensible wife, that three witches have been known to signal tragedy.

In fact the real true witch has been around all the time,

and everybody knows it. This is Delbert, the estranged father of Walt's dairy-farming neighbour Don, who seems to have lost his touch: both for finding water and playing the piano. How he regains these things, and how all kinds of metaphorical fences are mended, is the matter of the play. The losses include Walt's anniversary present to Maggie. The resolution of this comes right at the end of the play, and is thoroughly predictable -- the kind of pleasing predictability that makes the theatre, if not the world, go round. On its own it would be sentimental; as part of a pattern it feels both right and rewarding.

One piece of the pattern, involving the linguistic and bureaucratic problems of emailing money to Italy, feels like padding; and the overarching threat to he community from a water-bottling company doesn't register as strongly as seems intended. But the writing Beattie keeps the central tension going, while throwing in wry details, such as Walt's mathematical demonstration that his carbon footprint in the country is orders of magnitude heavier than ever it was in the country. The acting Beattie switches from one role to another, and then another, and then back, with his usual unassuming knife-edge virtuosity. Old Delbert, slow and deliberate, is a weighty new creation; Walt's brother-in-law Freddie remains the most entertaining of the familiar ones. (He's an oracular stammerer: a brilliant device.) We discover, once again, that we could listen to Walt and company till the cows come home.
