THEATRE REVIEW

THE MADONNA PAINTER

Factory Theatre, Toronto

Michel Marc Bouchard's The Madonna Painter is the latest carrier of a virulent strain of drama originating in Quebec. If that image is over the top, so is the play. It also seems apt since the play is set during a flu epidemic, though the topicality of that is of course fortuitous; in its French version, the play has been around since 2002. Previous prize-winning incubators of the genre are Woujdi Mouawad's Scorched and Bouchard's own The Coronation Voyage, plays in which the author takes a metaphor and beats us over the head with it. They all give us drama aspiring to the condition of fable, which sounds all right except that what we actually get is attitude and gesture, devoid of human content.

All these plays have their moments, and The Madonna Painter begins with three of them. We are in an isolated Quebec village in the last days of the First World War. Peace is on the way and so is the flu -- the dreaded Spanish variety. To ward it off, the new young parish priest envisions a fresco for his church, a Madonna triptych; his reasoning may seem peculiar but we can at least believe in his bright-eyed idealism, as reflected in an opening monologue.

After this we meet some of the local young women, all of whom, as is customary in Catholic communities or at least in plays about Catholic communities, are named Mary. Mary Louise is a laundry maid who can tell, by examining sheets, everything about the people who've been sleeping in them and who delivers a vivid speech about it. Mary Frances and Mary Anne are taking a walk when they see a naked man emerging from a stream. At least Mary Frances sees him; Mary Anne, who's never seen a naked man, closes her eyes while Mary Frances describes a terrible shape-changing monster. It's an amusing scene -- the only bit of fluent interaction in the play -- but Mary Anne, incredibly, goes through the rest of the play disabused of her mistake. Because her author needs her to. She projects her obsession onto the priest, whom she guiltily fancies, with horrific results. Most of the characters, in fact, are obsessives, which is a good way of writing farce. Bouchard does write the odd intentionally funny line (there's a real roof-raiser about a pregnant Madonna) but his mind is generally on higher things.

With financial input from the town doctor, a sardonic fellow who looks on people as incipient corpses, the priest engages a painter, an Italian. All the Maries in town line up to be his model; for some unexplained reason they recite for him, as if they were auditioning for a play. He chooses Mary of the Secrets, so named because she's been poaching on the preserves of both doctor and priest by ministering to the dying and hearing their unauthorized confessions. The painter seduces her (an event that does not go undetected by Mary the Sheet-Sniffer) and, just like an artist, tires of her soon after, with consequences for her life and his work.

There's a coupling scene in which one partner climaxes while feverishly reciting previous life experiences. It's true of course that people having sex aren't always concentrating on the person they're having it with, but I didn't believe that I was being told anything truthful about this character in this situation. I merely heard a playwright trying very hard for an effect. I didn't believe there was or had been a war on, however far away, or that these people had been touched by it. I didn't believe in the flu, even when one of the inhabitants caught and died of it. It's all words, words, words.

The style's side effects include recurrent outbreaks of whiny or rhetorical acting. In Eda Holmes' production, only two actors are immune. One is Juan Chioran, who as the painter is required to speak Italian and to sing, both of which he does with great melodiousness, but who is equally cool and commanding when talking in English. The other, in a tougher part and with less experience to draw on, is Marc Bendavid whose troubled, passionate naivete as the priest is the evening's driving force, He doesn't bring off the end when, having suffered a dire transformation, he has to persuade us that something has actually happened, but I doubt if anyone could. The normally luminous Jenny Young is bafflingly uneven as Mary of the Secrets, sometimes in command, sometimes just shouting. As the physician, Brian Dooley hardly gets beyond gruff rudeness, though I think he's meant to be a cynical teller of unwelcome truths. Nicola Correia-Damude begins arrestingly as Mary of the Sheets, but then finds nowhere to go; ditto Miranda Edwards' Mary Frances who gets very sulky. As the deluded Mary Anne, a key role judging from her position in the curtain call, Shannon Taylor has some piercing moments but mostly picks her way through her words as if they were a minefield. If I haven't engaged with the play's deeper themes (faith, art, etc.) that's because I can't detect where they are. And to be honest, I don't care. - The Madonna Painter runs until Dec. 13.
