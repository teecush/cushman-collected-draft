SHE STOOPS TO CONQUER

duMaurier Theatre Centre, Toronto

- - -

A wise British director once said that there is nothing much you can do with Oliver Goldsmith's She Stoops to Conquer, a play so inherently sturdy, charming and amusing as to resist anything you might call interpretation. I am not sure whether Albert Schultz, who has directed it for Soulpepper, is of the same mind. My guess is that he is trying to do something with the play. My problem is that I can't make out what it is. And this may well be because he himself never quite decided.

She Stoops is mistaken-identity comedy raised to a higher power. The plot premise is that Young Marlow, a London gent, mistakes a private country house for an inn. The psychological premise is that this misunderstanding suits him just fine. Marlow is terrified of women of his own class but a wolf with females of the lower orders. He has been sent to woo an heiress but, taking her for a barmaid, sets about seducing her instead. She, knowing the truth and attracted by him anyway, takes respectable pleasure in leading him on and then confronting him. Goldsmith, a shy and dissolute young man, based Marlow on himself, and the character still rings true, 200-and-some years on.

Did I say "rings"? Obviously this play is full of people falling into error and then, abruptly, realizing the truth. In this production, every time either of these things happens -- indeed every time there's a twist of plot or a change of mood -- the moment is signalled by some kind of bell tone. (Actually it's the twanging of a harpsichord.) You would think, after the dire experience of A Flea in Her Ear a few years ago, that Soulpepper would have learned the folly of stopping plays in their tracks by underlining the comic points. But, no, here they go again.

To be fair, the effect this time is less destructive. The play does survive. But all this nudging is patronizing to the audience and no great compliment to the actors, who should be playing the transitions themselves, rather than standing around with invisible thought balloons coming out of their heads. The production has the odd effect of turning the play into the opposite of what its first audiences liked about it. In 1773 it was welcomed as an oasis of realism in a desert of sentimental artifice. Here it goes right back into artificiality.

Some of this seems to be intentional. Patricia Fagan plays Kate Hardcastle, the heroine, as a girl who likes striking and then mocking attitudes. When she's alone with her cousin Constance (Molly Atkinson), the two of them enjoy exchanging confidences as if they were the heroines of what another character calls "a whining modern novel." That's neat. But this Kate never lets up. Flitting between her indulgent father (pleasantly played by Oliver Dennis) and her bemused lover, she is less a sensible young lady who knows what she wants and how to get it than a dyed-in-the-wool designing minx. Some of what she does is clever, but the smiling arabesques that she describes all over the text are too much. This is an actress who should be relaxing into her own charm, not sqandering it. Marlow takes it for granted that every serving girl is a tart; Kate doesn't need to act the part quite so enthusiastically. Stuart Hughes' Marlow is an unexpectedly searching portrait of a gentleman-lout, but hard though the couple play together, there is little spark between them, and no particular delight in the happy ending.

Delight, in fact, is what the show lacks -- delight and warmth. Most of the jokes land, but they do so in isolation, without the joy that should make a pyramid of them, each level more welcome than the last. That takes personality, and the right blend of personality. Paul Manz makes the scapegrace Tony Lumpkin a persuasively dropsical figure, rather lightly touched in. I can't hold it against him that he isn't especially funny; funny Lumpkins are even rarer than funny Falstaffs.

As his doting mother Mrs. Hardcastle, Fiona Reid, not surprisingly, is funny. When she complains of her husband's "Gothic vivacity," she transports us into another world of wit, one in which an actress can suck all the juice from an adjective and then come back in triumph for the noun. She can also drop an aside in the audience's lap more purposefully than anyone else on stage. The problem isn't that she's acting in a different style from everybody else but that the production makes no allowances for the fact -- it just has her swoop on, do her stuff, and swoop off again. It also robs her of the one chance to make her character human when, lost in her own back garden, she fancies she's met a highwayman and offers her own life in exchange for her son's. The cute staging hereabouts - - actors waving fronds, pretending to be trees -- stops us caring about that or anything else.

There is, despite Astrid Janson's high and handsome set, little sense of domesticity, though I did enjoy the set-piece scene in which Mr. Hardcastle's lumbering servants (led by Michael Simpson's immovable Diggory) convulse at the memory of one of his ancient stories. Jordan Pettle gets an unusual amount out of the second- lover role of Hastings, whom he plays as a caustic commentator, full of sound judgment except when his own interests are at stake. He is not, admittedly, the smoothest dispenser of 18th-century prose. But this only points out a gap in Soulpepper's record as a classical company. Very few of their successes have been in plays written before, say, 1830. She Stoops, indeed, is one of the few dead- centre English classics they have even attempted, and maybe we were naive to expect that they would romp through it. The play's subtitle is The Mistakes of a Night; this production only makes one real mistake, but it's a big one, and the night is full of it.
