A few weeks ago, Lynn Slotkin, who reviews theatre for CBC Radio, noticed that she had received no invitation to the premiere performances of The Shape of Things and Proof, the two opening productions of the new season at Canadian Stage.

Surprised by this, she e-mailed CanStage's press office to ask if there had been any mistake. She was informed, also by e-mail, the neglect was intentional: "We have removed you from the complimentary list at CanStage." She was of course at liberty to buy her own tickets and broadcast whatever opinions she pleased.

Producers have been known to fume at critics who have given them bad reviews, but they seldom take this kind of action. Indeed, Slotkin hadnt thought her reviews of CanStage productions had been especially hostile. She believed they had, like those of most critics covering the work of a company with so large an output, been mixed.

Martin Bragg, who holds the top CanStage post of artistic producer, denies anyway that its a matter of good or bad reviews. Like most people who have worked in the theatre for any length of time, he's used to both and inured to both. For him, it seems to be Slotkin's tone rather than her opinions that grate. He said, when I spoke to him over the weekend, this is about an individual -- Im not going to call her a critic -- who has been extremely destructive, personal and vindictive.

Grant Ramsay, CanStages director of public relations and promotions, takes a similar line. Slotkin's reviews, he says, have been "consistently mean-spirited, negative and personal."

I don't get it. I dont usually manage to hear Slotkin live on air, but I have been listening to tapes of four of her CanStage reviews from last season. All were of plays for which her enthusiasm was, at best, muted, which seemed a fair test. (Despite their protestations, I doubt CanStage objected greatly to the tone of the rave reviews -- three, by Slotkins own account -- she had given them over the same period.)

As a broadcaster, Slotkin can come on a bit strong for my taste - - like an overbearing mother handing down reviews rather than recipes -- but I could detect nothing in what she said that was malicious, and I always found her well informed and able to argue her case.

In at least one instance, the Judith Thomson play Habitat, her review was kinder than most -- certainly kinder than mine. Conversely, she liked Steve Martin's Picasso at the Lapin Agile, and especially its production, a lot less than I did; but there were others who disliked it more. Among the shows she liked are two -- Wit and The Lonesome West -- for which I shared her enthusiasm, and a third -- Goodnight Desdemona, Good Morning Juliet -- for which I find it inexplicable. Others would agree or disagree, in varying proportions.

There, in other words, you go: Diversity rules, and is surely to be welcomed. Bragg, Ramsay and their colleagues are as entitled to their opinion of Slotkin's reviews as she is to her opinions of their productions. Their course of action is another matter.

Before reviewing for CBC, Slotkin, a compulsive theatregoer in London and New York as well as Toronto, posted her opinions in her own on-line publication, The Slotkin Newsletter, which she still maintains. In this capacity, she never received or requested media tickets. And she says that even now, when she writes in the newsletter about a show she has already reviewed on CBC, she goes to see it again and pays for her seat. She had no quarrel with Ramsays claim that a theatre can hardly be expected to hand out precious free tickets to anybody with access to a Web site.

It sounds reasonable, too, for Bragg to say free tickets are given as a courtesy, and he, like any host, has a right to choose his own guests. But in fact its disingenuous. We are not dealing here with a straight hospitality situation: Your typical dinner invitation doesn't include the right to insult the cooking, at least not in public.

This is a business transaction. Theatres don't let the critics in free because they love us; they do it because they want good reviews, and in order to get them are willing to take the risk of receiving bad ones. Good reviews may send readers or listeners to the box office; they can also be torn into strips and used in ads. Indeed, press quotes have become the predominant mode of advertising plays. Critics, it occurs to me, must be the worst-paid copywriters in the world.

Its a Faustian bargain, with the difference that both sides can get burned. It would be morally healthier, both for the media and for the theatre, if we -- or, let us pray, our employers -- paid for our seats, and if producers refrained from using our reviews as fodder for commercials. (Producers complain, accurately, that a review is just one persons opinion; they then proceed to give that opinion the status of holy writ by exhibiting it on their own marquees.)

But the system isn't going to change: It works too well, not least logistically. It makes life easier for critics to be told where we have to be and when, and it's helpful for the theatres to know when were going to be around and to be able to give us seats from which we can at least see the stage.

We, too, should accept being quoted or even, if were unwary enough to give the opportunity, misquoted. Few things strike me as more absurdly pompous than the shrieks of reviewers claiming their deathless phrases have been taken out of context. We spend our lives misrepresenting plays and the people who create them. Who are we to complain when they misrepresent us?

I don't think this state of affairs is actually corrupt. Reviewers may be subject to all kinds of subtle pressures (most of them generated from within our own heads), but the lure of the complimentary ticket is not one of them. A freebie does not guarantee a rave, as any producer will ruefully testify. And shows survive both raves and pans, which are seldom unanimous anyway.

But if the system is going to work, then it has to operate on an equal-opportunity basis. If we're gong to be given privileges, we all have to be given the same ones. For CanStage, a publicly funded organization, to claim Slotkin can continue to review their shows if she pays her own way is, again, disingenuous. It places her in a false position in relation to the rest of us critics, and I dont blame her for refusing to accede to it. It also intimidates the other critics, who dont know if they will be next. (It won't, in practice, have this effect, but we're concerned with principles here.)

Robert Crew, who alternates with Slotkin as CBCs reviewer, has refused to cover CanStage shows until the ban is lifted, and he is doing the right thing. I would feel happier if CBC itself had taken a more explicit stand as well. The sanest arrangement is for theatres to send media tickets to the organization concerned, and for the organization to decide which reviewer to give them to. It should not be the producers prerogative to choose their own judges.

Bragg admits that in this instance CanStage may be shooting itself in the foot, since its constituency, certainly for its Bluma Appel main stage, largely overlaps with that of CBC radio: It's not serving us from a business point of view; we'll deprive ourselves of a prime marketing tool if we were to get a good review.

All the same, when I put it to him that a sort of unofficial pecking order applies here and that he would be unlikely to deny tickets to the Globe, the Star or the Post, he didnt deny it. Nor did he claim Slotkin had singled out CanStage for special abuse: "I think its just her style for everybody."

Probably there is a point at which a critic's continued rudeness or obtuseness justifies a theatre in taking preventive or retributive action. I don't think Slotkin has crossed that line, or even approached it; but I do believe critics should be held accountable. There is no point in calling them on matters of opinion, since these cant be proved, but they can and should be challenged if they make obvious factual errors, or if they seem insensitive to the ways in which shows actually get on to a stage. (This is a trickier area but -- in a collaborative form such as the theatre, where praise and blame have to be apportioned -- a crucial one.) But the best way to do it is to write a letter, or to demand time on the air. This means citing specifics. Barring a critic, or making him or her feel unwelcome, is a blunt instrument by comparison.

Its been tried before. In London, in the 1960s, Hilary Spurling, an especially bright critic who wrote for the Spectator, was removed from the free list at the Royal Court for saying, repeatedly, that that theatre's famous tradition of social realism was actually rather old-fashioned. The Court's directors, a famously sensitive bunch, were livid. Why, they knew they were revolutionary. People had been telling them so for years. Eventually, the Arts Council of Great Britain stepped in and Spurling was reinstated. The Royal Courtiers were left looking petulant. I would hate CanStage, a generally sane organization with a decent track-record, to go the same way.
