The word "dybbuk derives from the Hebrew for to cling." So says the Soulpepper program. The company's production of the Yiddish classic takes that definition very literally. The Dybbuk, written and re-written by S. Ansky between 1912 and 1919 and set in a Russian shtetl, tells of the passionate but undeclared love of Leah, a rich mans daughter, and Channon, a poor student. Leahs father arranges a profitable marriage for her. Channon, learning of this, falls dead in despair. But he doesn't disappear. His spirit takes possession of his beloved's body and has to be exorcised, though not, as it turns out, appeased.

In Albert Schultzs production of an adaptation by Anton Piatigorsky, the possession happens in two stages. It first declares itself when two voices are heard issuing simultaneously from the potential bride, her own and that of her late admirer. This, as far as it goes, is scary and effective. But it doesnt go very far, as in the next scene Channon emerges in person from under a table, takes hold of Leah and, yes, clings to her until he is forcibly expelled. This is not especially scary, and is much less effective. The inconsistency is never explained. And besides, neither manifestation is what Ansky wrote. His text calls for Leah to utter all the invisible dybbuk's words "in a strange, masculine voice. It could be a great tour-deforce for the actress, and so indeed it was when Marilyn Lightstone played Leah in the legendary John Hirsch production, an inspired staging that she helped to greatness.

Hailey Gillis, the Soulpepper Leah, doesn't get that opportunity, though she does whats left to her extremely well; theres a freshness to her and a sense of encroaching terror, even before the dead come to call. Colin Palangio is good too, though less polished, as

the live Channon, and I suppose his reincarnation could be defended as giving us a chance to see more of him. But he's meant to be a spiritual presence, not a physical one. The heart of the play has been torn out.

Which is a shame, because much of the surrounding flesh and bone is impressive. Piatigorsky gets the author credit, with Ansky relegated to based on." There's a new prologue to orient us culturally and historically; it depicts the kind of Sabbath that Tevye of Fiddler on the Roof might have hosted had he been a rich man. Later on, the adaptation improves on the original by showing us some scenes that otherwise would be only described. (They involve a visit to a graveyard - a favourite Soulpepper locale - and theyre crucial.) Otherwise, the new version sticks closely to the old. Lorenzo Savoinis set designs are spartan to the point of shabbiness; the synagogue in which the story starts has nothing to delineate it except the Ark of the Covenant. The

general darkness is magnificently pierced by Bonnie Beecher's lighting, which comes in all shapes, sizes and intensities. The acting ensemble here is big and dedicated; we get the sense of a community.

Ideologically, it's split. On the one side we have Channon, whos been immersing himself in the Kabbalah, generally regarded as forbidden knowledge and perhaps furnishing the passport for his demonic return from the beyond. (The play is subtitled Between Two Worlds.) On the other side, there are the synagogue habitues, endlessly discussing Talmudic parables and the hairsplitting differences between them, and doing it here with gossipy conviction, despite containing in their ranks some of the least Jewish actors imaginable. Cutting through their debates is a mysterious outsider identified as the Messenger and endowed by Diego Matamoros with an authority that raises the dramatic temperature the first time we hear it, and re-charges it every time he reopens his mouth. Somebody in the play says that you should always be hospitable to strangers because you never know when one of them may turn out to be the prophet Elijah. Maybe thats who this Messenger is; he certainly seems to know everything, though he never explicitly claims to. Ansky apparently added this character in his own second version, and Piatigorsky has enlarged his role still further, even employing him as a medium in a trial scene in which the principal witness is a ghost. This, along with some of the other supernatural effects in the production, works magisterially well.

William Webster, an old hand at playing virtuous rabbis, gives the chief exorcist a fine and kindly blend of professional fervour and personal gentleness; he has doubts about his own powers but not about the principles that underly them. Jordan Pettle pleasantly complements him as a learned man - older in the original, definitely younger here - called in to give specialized support. Back in the village there are stabbing if all-too-brief appearances by Diana Leblanc and Laura Condolln as disadvantaged women who help define the society by their existence on its periphery.

Richard Lam has a charming moment as a bridegroom scared of what he might be getting into (and, as it turns out, lucky not to get into). But the key performance is that of Alex Poch-Goldin as Leah's father, who stands convicted of destroying two young people's happiness and incidentally of betraying the memory of an old dead friend. He was mostly unaware of what he was doing, but he accepts his guilt nonetheless, a process that the actor makes both moving and significant. The most striking thing about this play full of conflict is that it has no unsympathetic characters, no evil people: only evil spirits. And even they have some justice on their side. They were once human, too.
