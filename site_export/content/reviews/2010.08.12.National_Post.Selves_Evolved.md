Last week marked the beginning of SummerWorks, Toronto's indie theatre and arts festival, which is presenting performances on various stages throughout the city.

The oldest play on view this year, by two and a half millennia, is also, of those I've seen, the most exciting. Euripides' Iphigenia at Aulis (at the Factory Mainspace theatre) was his last play, its chosen myth being that of the princess sacrificed by her father Agamemnon, the Greek general, so that the winds might change and the Greek fleet sail for Troy. Euripides' method, as usual, is to ask how real people would have behaved, given the extreme circumstances dictated by the legend. Their reactions here are humanly inconsistent. Agamemnon vacillates between paternal horror and professional pride; his prestige, as his wife Clytemnestra bitterly points out, is at stake. His brother Menelaus, husband of Helen, for whose sake the whole expedition is being undertaken, is unexpectedly ambiguous as well. Iphigenia is lured to the Greek harbour at Aulis on a promise of marriage to the hero Achilles; he knows nothing about it, and is furious when he finds out. He vows to defend Iphigenia, but it seems that he too is motivated as much by amour-propre as by compassion.

The heroine herself seems to resolve the situation by opting for martyrdom in the national interest. This is genuinely moving, but over the whole play hangs the image -- as potent in Euripides' own time as in ours--of young lives being wasted in a dubious struggle initiated by their elders. Alan Dilworth follows his fine production of the faux-Greek tragedy If We Were Birds with an equally powerful staging of the real thing. The text (Nicolas Billon's adaptation of a translation by Roger Beck) occasionally goes for easy laughs, and it certainly shouldn't say "flaunt" when it means "flout," but it's mostly primed for irony: the real, dark thing, not the giggly modern substitute. The tensions between the characters are brilliantly realized; and though individual performances vary in quality, everybody on the white-clad team gets the tone right.

The bar is set by David Fox as Agamemnon's old servant and personified conscience: superbly human in speech, magnificent when he just sits in silence at the side of the stage, which he does for much of the play. Stephen Bogaert is a persuasively riven Agamemnon, and Sarah Orenstein grows steadily in vengeful stature as Clytemnestra, whose grievances include having had her child by a previous marriage ripped from her arms and killed by her current spouse. That useful bit of the myth is ignored in other surviving tragedies, but a more general piece of knowledge haunts the play without needing to be mentioned: the fact that when Agamemnon returns to his wife after 10 years, she will kill him. Greek mythology was a forcefield that the playwrights could darken or illuminate at will, greatly to their benefit. The chorus is stationed among us in the auditorium, thus fulfilling their proper function, as a funnel between us and the action, even though reduced to three and deprived of most of their lines. The text of this play is much in dispute, the doubtful passages include a surprise ending that this version retains but over which it casts a mordant pall. The closing sections of this production are drainingly powerful, the real Athenian McCoy.

A lighter-hearted venture into a more recent past is Or (also at the Factory Mainspace theatre), whose central figure is Aphra Behn, England's first professional female playwright and one of the most entertaining if neglected authors of Restoration comedy. To support her writing habit, she also worked as a spy; this American play, by Liz Duffy Adams, finds her first in a debtors' prison, from which she's sprung by King Charles II himself, who appreciates both of her talents and her body as well. This scene, in Kelly Straughan's, is dark and cramped in more than its setting, but things liven up considerably when we move to Aphra's apartment where she juggles careers, storylines, theatrical metaphors and lovers of both sexes. Sophie Goulet gives her a warm presence that's both amused and amusing, and Damien Atkins executes an awesome series of sartorial and psychological quick-changes as the womanizing monarch, a cringing secret agent and, most delightful of all, Lady Davenant, the polite but demanding manageress of London's principal playhouse, who doesn't like plays with double titles, even if Shakespeare did it. Hence this play's title, though it's also meant to signify ambiguity in general. The titles of at least two of Behn's own plays make their way artfully into the dialogue, the kind of thing for which I am a sucker.

Another triple, if not more, threat: Johnnie Walker, who plays three roles in his own play Redheaded Stepchild (at the Factory Studio theatre). This solo piece, astutely directed by Morgan Norwich, is about a loner of a boy who has both of the title's characteristics, and so gets bullied. The play has a more elaborate structure than it needs, and it owes something to I, Claudia, both in subject and in its hero's pleadingly indignant tone, but it's funny and a bit harrowing; Walker's writing is good and his performance just about flawless.

Melody A. Johnson, author-performer of Miss Caledonia (Factory Studio) does better in the latter role than the former. Drawn from life, though not her own life, this piece about a farm girl finding realization through entering beauty pageants, is a pedestrian treatment of a commonplace theme. "Commonplace" isn't usually what Johnson, a great eccentric comedienne, is about, so it seems a waste, but she's irresistible at the end, singing "I've been everywhere, man" at tongue-twisting speed while twirling a baton at similar velocity. The end is calculatedly heartwarming; the calculation works.
