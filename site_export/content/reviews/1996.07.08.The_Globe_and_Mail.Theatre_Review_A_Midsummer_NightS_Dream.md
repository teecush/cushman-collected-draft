A MIDSUMMER NIGHT'S DREAM

THEATRE REVIEW

A MIDSUMMER NIGHT'S DREAM

Monday, July 8, 1996

Toronto --

This lively summer Dream suffers from double trouble

Having the same actors play the fairy

royalty as well as the earthly rulers makes for staging

problems in this Shakespearean revival.

Special to The Globe and Mail

ABOUT halfway through A Midsummer Nights Dream in High Park, darkness descends, as its naturally bound to do, and enchantment comes with it. There is a new stillness in the air. Scampering actors, energetically trying to bash their words across while maintaining some kind of line on their characters, suddenly take on weight and definition. Silly, squabbling lovers turn into funny, touching emblems of the folly of youth down the centuries. Types turn into archetypes.

This, of course, is a blessing that descends on all open-air shows. Tom Wood's production, revived and improved from last year, is lively enough to deserve a blessing. It does not come bearing revelations; it is, in fact, rather cheerfully old-fashioned. You almost expect to hear the Mendelssohn music. The costumes worn by the more aristocratic humans actually suggest the old Athens in which the play is notionally set. The fairies stake their claim to supernaturalism by rushing around the parkland in their underwear, pausing occasionally to break into full-throated (and fully amplified) song.

Puck (Barna Moricz) demonstrates that he is a very bad boy by dressing up as shrubbery and jumping out at people. A relentless gymnast, he also has a habit of popping in and out of trap doors, with which this forest comes fully equipped. Less traditionally, he rounds off his first scene by attempting to have sexual relations (consensual or not, I was too far from the stage to tell) with one of Titania's gang. However, his master Oberon puts a quick stop to this and calls him back to the plot.

The king and queen of the fairies are doubled with the earthly rulers Theseus and Hippolyta. This is an idea sanctified, though not actually invented, by Peter Brooks legendary production of 1970, since when it too has become almost traditional. Of course it comes in handy for economy, but it does create problems. The two couples never have to appear simultaneously, but there is a point at which one is supposed to enter on the others departing heels. Brook's actors stayed on stage and simply donned new cloaks: a moment that was magical in itself, and suggested a shared identity for mortals and spirits. Wood's performers rush behind the bushes for a quick change; to cover it, the awakening speech of Bottom the weaver, newly deprived of his asss head, is moved up from its official position at the end of the scene. What should be a marvellously unexpected comic epiphany becomes a passing interlude. The cost to the play is not worth paying.

Nor do we actually get much for it. There is no suggestion here that Theseus/Oberon and Hippolyta/Titania are doubles in any but the logistical sense. (Im not at all sure that they should be, but if you cast them that way you inevitably raise expectations.) There are no premarital tensions at court that need to be smoothed out or sublimated in fairyland. The actors have trouble fleshing out one role apiece, let alone two.

John Ralston, in both his incarnations, is thoroughly competent and thoroughly bland. Alison Sealy-Smith has more temperament though she sounds happier lavishing insults on Oberon than endearments on Bottom. The production at this point, as at others, is trying hard for eroticism (Bottom hopefully pricks up his ass's ears when Titania speaks of having him "to bed and to arise "), but it seems pretty forced, as it very often does in productions of this play.

Most of the actors are half-way toward making the text work for them. They have found the meaning but not the flow. The young lovers' scenes, in particular, work in fits and starts. Clive Cholerton (Lysander) is the most in control; Philippa Domvilles properly self-pitying Helena would be twice as effective with half the gesticulation. Sarah Evanss Hermia is at her best when awakening in the wood, abandoned by her fiancé and clearly terrified.

This is one of the strengths of Wood's production. It does communicate a sense of alarm. Bottom and company arrive for their midnight rehearsal, feeling their way and carrying lanterns: obvious enough, but I've never seen it before. And when they come to perform their interlude of Pyramus and Thisbe, they are real enough characters for their disasters to be doubly hilarious. Snug the joiner (Jeffrey Smith), timid and bashful, terrifies himself into tears when playing the Lion: a remote ancestor, perhaps, of the one Bert Lahr played in The Wizard of Oz . Flute (Steve Ross) moves from indignation at playing a woman into stage-stealing triumph at having a death scene to emote.

And as Bottom, the ever-reliable Brian Tree is the top. He's a thoughtful egomaniac, quietly sure of his own prowess and on easy terms with everyone he meets, regardless of social or spiritual status. He has a crisp, solid Cockney delivery that reminded me of Cheerful Charlie Chester, a British comedian who was a formative influence on my youth and I suspect on his as well. As Pyramus he enters on a hobby-horse and nearly kills himself trying to keep up with the galloping sound-effects provided by his colleagues.

Ideas like these may have originated with actor or director or both, but I mean it in the nicest way when I say that the performer transcends the production and makes each moment his own. Or that you can't see the Wood for the Tree.
