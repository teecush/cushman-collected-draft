Marion J. Caffey is a director, choreographer and occasional author who has achieved success with a series of achingly superficial shows about popular music. He reaches some kind of nadir with 3 Mo' Divas, an evening of gold-plated copper-bottomed garbage.

It's a companion piece to his earlier 3 Mo' Tenors, which I haven't seen but which was apparently a response to the phenomenon of the original operatic Three Tenors. Caffey found those classical gentle-men's forays into the pop repertoire painfully forced, as well he might. So he rustled up three male singers with a pop grounding and an operatic range, and set them loose on all kinds of songs. 3 Mo' Divas is the female follow-up.

The rangy vocal demands necessitate two alternating casts, billed in strict no-losers-here fashion as Cast 1 and Cast A. (This brand of egalitarianism was anticipated years ago by Stephen Sondheim and Arthur Laurents, who in their musical Anyone Can Whistle divvied up a whole town into a Group A and a Group 1. They meant it, though, as satire.) I saw Cast A. I doubt if it matters much which you see, since the format is designed to stamp all the individuality out of both of them and their material, with every

song choreographed to within an inch of its death. Anyway, I saw the A team, which consists of Ramona Dunlap, Andrea Jones-Sojola and DeVonna Lawrence, all of whom have powerful and, when they're occasionally allowed to relax, pleasing voices.

They lift them first in a series of operatic arias (Puccini, Handel, Rossini, Gounod). They present these with a mildly competitive edge, flashing suspicious glances at one another out of the corners of their eyes; these displays of one-upwomanship are designed, I imagine, to reinforce the popular idea of the jealous classical diva, and to stop the audience from getting restive from all that culture. From this section, labelled Classical, they proceed to others billed as Broadway/Movies, Jazz/Blues, Soul and R & B, Soul Medley (hey, that's a lotta soul) and Spiritual/ Gospel. These ladies certainly

have chops; every opportunity to yell is enthusiastically taken and, I have to admit, enthusiastically received: White audiences seem to feel that if they scream back at black female singers who are screaming at them, they'll have soul, too.

The lowest point, provoking the loudest cheers, must be Dunlap's singing of God Bless the Child. This starts out as straight Billie Holiday impersonation, which seems pointless but is at least unobjectionable. It then lurches into wild-eyed melodrama that has nothing to do with the Holiday style or, more importantly, with the understated bitterness of the song itself. It crops up, surprisingly, not in the jazz section but under Broadway, on the grounds that it was once included in a compilation show called Bubbling Brown Sugar. Let the Good Times Roll, pure R & B, has made the same crossover, due to its originator Louis Jordan having once been the subject of the revue Five Guys Named Moe. You'd think that there were enough great Broadway and Hollywood songs in existence for this show not to have to go raiding other larders. The authentic ones they have chosen are hardly first rank: Little Shop of Horrors, the anthem from Rent about "seasons of lo-o-o-ove" and something from The Wiz. Conversely, an actual Broadway song, My Funny Valentine (let no one say that this show shies away from obvious choices) is filed under Jazz/Blues, a sequence incidentally that contains no authentic blues at all. But if jazz means originality, then Lawrence here brings off something unprecedented -- in my experience anyway -- by taking a breath between "valen" and "tine."

But then it doesn't matter what style they think they're singing: All the songs come out the same -- loud, superficial and vapid. A tribute to "Ella Fitzgerald, the first lady of scat," turns out to be a rote reproduction of Fitzgerald's famous How High the Moon routine, but stopping before it gets to the hard parts: i.e. the ones that would require actual improvisation. The whole is delivered with a glassy smugness that could hardly be further from the real Ella's selfless desire to please.

Yes, they prove that operatic voices can sing pop, or vice versa. They don't prove that there's any point in doing it when everything is homogenized: when opera gets miked and pop loses all connection with words and with meaning. Anyway, wouldn't it make better dramatic sense to start with the pop stuff and work up to the classical as a surprise? Not that this show is much concerned with drama, except insofar as it confuses it with hysteria; it's a concert whose connection with theatre is confined to real estate. The spirituals do make a reasonable finale; they can legitimately draw on both traditions, European and American, and they at least start out quiet. Earlier on, the threesome's harmonizing on Moody's Mood for Love is quite stylish. The avowed aim of 3 Mo' Divas is to provide a musical something for everyone -- which, as usual, results in nothing much for anyone. Director and entrepreneur Caffey seems to have never met a cliche he couldn't embrace. No mo'. Please.

- 3 Mo' Divas plays through June 16 at the Winter Garden Theatre. Call 416-672-5555 for more information.
