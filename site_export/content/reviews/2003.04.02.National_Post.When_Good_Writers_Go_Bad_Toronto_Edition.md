Until April 13. 416-504-7529

THE BLUES

Theatre Passe Muraille, Toronto

Hrant Alianak, the veteran dramatist and director, is currently bringing off a grand slam at Theatre Passe Muraille. Upstairs in the Backspace (confusing, I know, but that's how it is), there is a remount of The Walls of Africa, his excellent, and excellently acted, play from last season. On the main stage downstairs, we have The Blues.

The Blues is also a revival, though from a more remote time. It was first staged in 1976, and its reappearance now is very encouraging; it shows how much Alianak has improved as a playwright. It also holds out some hope the Canadian theatre may have grown up a bit; like most of the plays that have been resurrected from the supposed golden age, it suggests that in the '70s it was such an exciting novelty for Canadians to be writing plays and getting them on that each new arrival was greeted as an instant classic. Something of that attitude still persists, but not as strongly; and the plays, or some of them, have got better.

The Blues, set in a New York bar in the early '50s, is a nostalgia-trip even as written. For a brief time we can have fun just drinking it in; Steve Lucas has designed another impeccably evocative set, and there's a Billie Holiday record playing. In fact we get to hear a lot of Holiday; at least two more of her songs are piped in during the proceedings, and one of the play's four characters, a tart called Ripples who's been jilted by her abusive protector, identifies strongly. As Aldo the barman remarks, Ripples' conversation is peppered with titles from Billie's repertoire. (She's lost her old flame -- or he's changed -- so she's got a right to sing the blues, and now she covers the waterfront, with no detour ahead, at least until the real thing comes along.)

Not all the play's literary borrowings are as frankly acknowledged as its Holiday snaps. A Salvation Army lass, name of Virginia (yes, there are jokes) wanders into Aldo's bar and tries to convert him to temperance, and he seduces her after spiking her milk: a sequence of events unlikely, except by its sheer barefacedness, to surprise anyone who has ever seen Guys and Dolls. Having fallen heavily for Virginia, Aldo says that what he sees in other women drunk, he sees in her sober, and you wonder if he knows he is reproducing, verbatim, the opening of Kenneth Tynan's classic essay on Greta Garbo. The remaining character is Tyrone, who writes hardboiled fiction; the play itself, since we hear him doing the occasional narrative voice-over, may be one of his stories, but the Alice-in-Wonderland point is not pursued. Tyrone has the enviable ability to type out his stuff in the bar, very fast, and to read it aloud even as he does so.

One of the sentences he lays on us -- "this is a story about passion, bloodshed, desire and death: all the things in fact that make life worth living" -- shows him to be a plagiarist of prophetic gifts, since it's the opening statement of the musical Irma la Douce, whose English version didn't come along until 1958. Still, Tyrone is the best reason for seeing the play, or at least Eric Peterson's playing of him is. Peterson, nailing down his recent return to grace in Boy Gets Girl, starts his performance by tossing his fedora at the hat stand. it lands perfectly, to his amazement and -- to judge from the look that creeps into his eyes -- terror. Later on, he has two long, bravura speeches, magnificently delivered. The first is a catalogue of imagined carnal delights, towards the end of which the actor, still without pausing for breath, mounts the bar and struts along it, Cagney-fashion. The second, less well-written but just as irresistibly spoken, this time with punctuation, is about seeing Citizen Kane and not getting it the first time but having it haunt your memory and having to go back for a second and more rewarding look. I can't see what it's doing there except passing the time, but like its predecessor it brings down the house as if it were an especially spectacular musical number, which in a way it is.

All the characters get to do at least one aria, with the neat exception of David Bolt's stoical Aldo; he does at one point embark on his life-story, but the others shush him before he has completed a sentence. He's forgotten that the bartender's role is to listen to everyone else's troubles while keeping quiet about his own. The defrocked Virginia greets her fall by fantasizing, again with very few pauses, about a future career submitting to every sexual degradation she can conjure up at such short notice; Janet Burke, emphatically wide-eyed, gives the speech amusingly, though it's hard not to apply to it the best line in the play that Aldo, in a different context, delivers to Tyrone: "Tyrone, you're showing me a side of yourself that I don't particularly want to see." I have already mentioned Ripples' name-that-tune recollections: Linda Griffiths puts them over as a mixture of self-pity and bad temper, but that may well be how they're written.

None of these characters interact, or even come to life. It's as if pop-art archetypes were guaranteed to be touching or hilarious with no extra effort, and that all an author had to do was assemble the cliches. The spectators, meanwhile, can congratulate themselves on spotting the references; this, as you will have gathered, is something for which I'm a sucker , but I was bored nonetheless. It's amazing that a writer as demonstrably talented as Alianak should ever have come up with such a piece of aimless camp, and it passes belief that critics across the country should ever have acclaimed it. (It was praised for its satire. Of what?) Crazy I call them.
