David Hare's Stuff Happens is a historical play about the Iraq War in which none of the characters is Iraqi. Well, almost none: There are a few words from Saddam Hussein mouthpieces early on, and there's an epilogue, a powerful one, in which an exile laments the crucifying of his country both under Saddam and under the Americans. Our only fault, he says, was not taking control of our own destiny. How that would have been achieved, he doesn't say. After all, he himself had to leave.

This is the last of a series of chorus-like speeches that dot the play. An early one, delivered by someone identified as Angry Journalist, says that a dictator was removed, and that arguing about the characters or motives of those who removed him is moralizing self-indulgence. Palestinian Academic chimes in halfway through to claim that the invasion was a sideshow, designed to deflect attention from the Middle East. These declarations stand starkly in the play, though the main action hardly engages with them. In a way, it embodies the worst suspicions of Iraqi Exile, Angry Journalist and Palestinian Academic. It's a racy account of the build-up to war, and of the manoeuvrings within and between the American and British governments. The last thing anybody has on their minds, or at least on their tongues, is the fates and wishes of the people on the ground.

This queasy vacuum is both the play's strength and its weakness. It may be Hare's liveliest play, one in which the characters don't just discuss politics but do them, and in which the story and characters come ready-made. The words, for the most part, don't; there are some direct quotes from documented speeches, but most of the dialogue is what the author himself has described as guesswork. It's very persuasive guesswork, as we eavesdrop on what may have gone on behind closed doors with the Bush and Blair cabinets and at the United Nations. Some of it undoubtedly improves on the original. (Colin Powell knows Iraq once had weapons because "we still have the receipts.") And even the recognized sound bites gain from being heard in a context. We get the whole of the Donald Rumsfeld response to looting from which the play takes its title. That doesn't make his words any more endearing, but it does make them less cavalier.

The play's thrust is that Bush and his closest advisors ducked and wove and stonewalled all qualms and disagreements, in order to pursue a course on which they had been set since 9/11--earlier, in Dick Cheney's case. This is unlikely to be news to anyone, but it's vigorously presented with a genuine sense of cut-and-thrust. There's a climactic White House meeting in which the gloves come off, and Cheney roams the room, expressing his contempt for conscientious objectors in general and Tony Blair in particular. It's capped by a shorter, more rueful scene in London where Blair's advisors tell him that power doesn't need to do deals; it just is.

The gathering sense of power, unanswerable and unanswering, is palpable in Joel Greenberg's production for his Studio 180 company. It goes at a driving but lucid pace, is shrewdly cast and well--in many cases, superbly -- acted. Fifteen actors, initially confronting us from three rows of mobile office chairs, play upwards of 70 roles. Half a dozen get a part apiece. If there's a centre, it's Nigel Shawn Williams' warm, troubled Secretary of State. Indeed, the play is largely The Passion of Powell. He's presented, with some simplification, as the unceasing voice of conscience and prudence, increasingly isolated by his colleagues (in one of the best scenes, he sits miserable and silent while they all join in Amazing Grace) and then outfoxed by his hoped-for allies at the UN. The play notably sidesteps the question of just why he finally fell into line. Nor, surprisingly, does it give much weight to his or anyone else's purely tactical misgivings concerning the invasion and its aftermath. (Which, if heeded, might conceivably have granted Iraqi Exile both sets of wishes.)

He's up against a trio of bullies and a couple of sphinxes. David Fox gives a wonderful account of Rumsfeld as a pedantic serpent, lost in contemplation of his own rhetoric; Hardee T. Lineham's Cheney is all withering vitriol, with the voice and stance of an assault-weapon; and Neil Foster weighs in as Wolfowitz. Yanna McIntosh's Condoleezza Rice, to whom I had greatly looked forward, is the nearest thing to a disappointment; this Condi plays her cards so close to the chest as to leave the actress almost nothing to do. Barry Flatman jumps into the front rank of Dubya doubles while chilling most of the obvious laughs. He's genial and he's impassive; he leaves open the question of how cunning he might be underneath it all.

Andrew Gillies cannot escape the patches of wetness that cling to most Blair impersonators (except Michael Sheen in The Queen) he still emerges with unexpected dignity, especially in a play from the British left. Indeed, it goes easy on him; the WMD dossier is eagerly mentioned, and then completely forgotten. (David Kelly is a no-show.) Among the multi-taskers, there's ear-catching work from Sarah Orenstein, Paul Essiembre, Sam Kalileh and Guy Bannerman.

The play essentially ends with the president's drop-in victory speech: the only scene actually set in Iraq. Ahead and unmentioned lie Abu Ghraib, the insurgency and the prospect of civil war. It's after we've all gone home that the worst stuff happens.
