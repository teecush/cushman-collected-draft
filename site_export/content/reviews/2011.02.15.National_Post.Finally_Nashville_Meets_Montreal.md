SAINT CARMEN OF THE MAIN

Bluma Appel Theatre, Toronto

---

I would love to see Peter Hinton direct a real Greek tragedy. For the moment, we have his production of Michel Tremblay's Saint Carmen of the Main, a 35-year-old play acknowledged by everyone, including its author, to be constructed on the Athenian model. Hinton's revival is a joint venture of Canadian Stage and the National Arts Centre.

The curtain rises -- and for once in the institutional theatre there actually is a curtain and it does actually rise, slowly as if to savour the moment -- to reveal 11 pairs of red shoes, some of them platform-soled to approximate the old Greek tragic buskins. It rises higher, to show the shoes surmounted by equally garish red dresses. Wearing them are a line of prostitutes from the Main, the poorest corner of Montreal, among them an indeterminate number of transvestites. They are the chorus, and their speaking, both individually and in unison, is impressive. They have two leaders: Karen Robinson, strident but compelling, and Robert Persichini, marvellous as a tender-hearted hulk. Leaders and followers together bring off the evening's most stirring sequence, a heartfelt ode on how Carmen's singing has brought meaning to their lives.

I've got ahead of myself. The Carmen who's canonized in the play's title is what was known in those old hyphenated days as a country-and-western singer. She grew up on the Main and, having achieved success in the local clubs, took off to Nashville to study at the source. While there, she had a revelation: She no longer wanted to sing the old favourites, she wanted to perform her own material, which would honour and celebrate her old exploited friends of the streets. They start the play, though as yet they know nothing of her transformation, by heralding her return; apparently she's made the sun shine like it never has before. This makes them resemble less a Greek chorus than a Broadway one, the kind that in shows such as Hello, Dolly! and Mame insist the star is wonderful because she's the star, without providing any other evidence. And that's pretty much how the play proceeds. As in a Greek tragedy, though generally not in a Broadway musical, the exciting stuff tends to happen offstage, and that includes the show that Carmen gives to unveil the new her. We get the reaction, affecting as noted, but the action we have to take on trust. Laara Sadiq, standing out from the scarlet crowd in sacrificial white, is believable when Carmen describes the awe she felt on arriving in the country-music capital, but in her prevailing moods of triumph and defiance, she aims at forceful but winds up with forced.

The defiance is aimed largely at Maurice (Jean Leclerc), her manager/pimp, himself in thrall to a more powerful manager/ pimpknownasToothpick. Carmen's evolution into yet another preachy and pretentious singer-songwriter is greeted by the bosses as a challenge to the established order that will give downtrodden hookers ideas above their station. The scene in which Carmen and Maurice thrash these things out is interminable; neither actor has the guns. Joey Tremblay, though, has considerable slinky power as Toothpick, and he does well by the play's climactic messenger speech. The two rats contribute to the production's variegated colour scheme by wearing identical black suits and white shirts, a fashion choice they share with Harelip, Carmen's fiercely loyal lesbian dresser and Exhibit A in her campaign to dignify the masses. Diane D'Aquila, physically unrecognizable and vocally unforgettable, makes of Harelip an underdog with bite; when she's on, the play flies.

Eo Sharp's red set adds to the hieratic effect. It's possible Hinton has squeezed some everyday life out of the play by stylizing it so emphatically, but given the material he seems to have made the right choice, even if not all his executants are up to the task. The Greek echoes are the play's most interesting feature; when Carmen,

against all advice, goes off to take a shower, there is an echo of Agamemnon in The Oresteia, crossing the red carpet to his doom.

There is, though, one final gaffe. Carmen has a female nemesis maybe deadlier than the males; this is Gloria, a retired star singer who specializes in Latin American, and who was Carmen's patron until the young woman started acting uncommercial. Jackie Richardson makes a glorious Gloria, prowling the stage in lazy circles, walking rings around the competition. We don't hear Carmen sing, but we do hear Gloria, making her comeback in a big number at the end of the play, which, given the play's own pro-Carmen logic, ought to be terrible. So the production wants us to believe that Richardson is singing badly, which is not going to happen. Alternatively, it just wants a slam-bang finish, and in the circumstances, who can blame it? The received opinion is that Tremblay is the great Canadian playwright, but as one revival succeeds another, my doubts increase.
