PSYCHEDELICATESSEN

The Second City

- - -

Second City titles never mean much. Psychedelicatessen is as arbitrary a name as any other, and more cumbersome than most. Do not let it discourage you -- the troupe's 51st show is its best in years, and by miles.

The early buzz has centred on the teams obligation to measure up satirically to the aftermath of Sept. 11. That may have something to do with it, but probably more important is the engagement as director of Chris Earle, a very pointed actor and playwright who is here the cause of pointedness in others. His half-dozen writer- performers are all in top gear; remarkably, there is not a dud sketch in the evening.

A few of the numbers relate directly to current events. Canadian troops arrive in Afghanistan and receive operational instructions from their American allies (Mind our stuff). A bevy of concerned Torontonians, or Torontonians who feel they should be concerned but arent sure how to go about it, gather on the terrace outside a party to puff smoke and pay homage to international crises ("Terror - - that's the topic, isn't it?").

Other moments evince a concern with air travel and especially with immigration. A malignant extraterrestrial applies for entry (occupation -- overlord) and damned if he doesnt get in.

A recurring character played by Paul Bates, who looks reassuringly bland and sounds tooth-baringly alarming, the alien turns like so many new arrivals to cab-driving, reverting to venomous type when a fortunately uncomprehending fare asks to be taken somewhere on College Street. ("You will pay for this!" I expected to.)

He is also on hand for the shows conclusion. I wouldn't want to give too much away so I will merely say that this finale also re- introduces us to other former acquaintances with accents, and that, whatever your previous feelings about Adrienne Clarkson, they will change.

Revue in recent years has become an end in itself, the comedy taking place in a whimsical never-never land inhabited only by standup comics. This one returns us to a recognizable Toronto, a recognizable Canada, a recognizable world. Even the obligatory embarrassment-in-a-restaurant sketch takes on a new dimension as a satire on the conventions of improvised revue itself, with Jennifer Goodhue, a pocket-sized fury, as a performer's nightmare of an audience participant. (It does go on a bit, though).

"Music With Borders," a salute to the Canadian troubadour market, has Paul Constable and Nathan David Shore, the groups two sardonic male heavies, providing nostalgically lachrymose impersonations of Gordon Lightfoot and Leonard Cohen, while Carolyn Taylor offers a Jann Arden in state of permanent vocal hiccup, and Aurora Browne a politely purring Diana Krall (Sony took the part/That once was my heart/America, you own all of me. Hang on, though; doesnt she record for Universal?)

Scenes of domestic discord, another Second City staple, seem this time to be about real people. Two Hispanic ladies passionately discuss the rival merits of baby formula and mother's milk; the breast advocate is rewarded by a strikingly healthy son able to sweep his provider up in a tango while taking nourishment.

As well as being highly inventive, these items occasionally cut surprisingly deep. An Italian-Canadian husband grudgingly agrees to turn down the sound on the TV when his wife requests some quality time. Another couple tries to rekindle the passion that had once briefly flared when the man returned unscathed from a subway accident.

My favourite sketch -- I think -- has the unquenchable Goodhue as a small child poured into a snow suit so that her estranged father may take her to see the Lord of the Rings.

Everything here rings true: the father's car horn honking outside; the child flailing, stifled, while her mother takes a long call from a sympathetic girlfriend; the mothers response when asked for a ring like the one that made Frodo invisible. ("Your father gave me a ring and he became invisible.")

I cant recall how it ends, and I have to admit that nearly every scene has trouble in finding a punchline. But then, who doesn't?
