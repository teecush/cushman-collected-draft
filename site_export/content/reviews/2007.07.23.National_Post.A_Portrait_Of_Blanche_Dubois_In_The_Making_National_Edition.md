SUMMER AND SMOKE

Royal George Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont.

---

Tennessee Williams' Summer and Smoke contains as much pointed or lyrical or funny writing as any of his plays, but it lacks a driving force. It's the neglected elder sister to A Streetcar Named Desire; they were written more or less simultaneously, around 1945, but Streetcar got to Broadway sooner and became first the hit and then the classic. It's the stronger play, but there is a chronological irony in its having been the first to arrive at the ball. Streetcar is, in some sense, the sequel to Summer and Smoke: not a literal or immediate sequel -- Streetcar takes place just after the Second World War and Summer just before the first one--but a spiritual and psychological one.

More specifically, Alma Winemiller, the heroine of Summer and Smoke, suggests what Blanche DuBois might have been like before she went off the rails (or the trolley tracks). The play concludes with Alma taking the first step toward becoming Blanche by picking up a newly arrived travelling salesman, in emulation of the town tramp -- the town in this case being Glorious Hill, Miss.

Up until then, Alma has been the picture of cultured propriety. The daughter of a minister, she runs the town literary society and is a singing teacher by profession. (Blanche was a teacher, too.) Her name, as she makes sure everybody knows, is the Spanish word for "soul," and love for her means finding a soul mate.

Ever since childhood, though, she has been fascinated by John, the boy next door, a doctor and the son of a doctor, whose philosophy is strictly materialist and whose notions of affection are notoriously physical. Halfway through the play he makes a pass at her, which she rejects. The rest of the evening shows both of them changing, passing one another -- I hate to say like ships in the night, but it's unavoidable -- and ending up, or so we are meant to believe, in one another's former places.

Alma is in some ways a more interesting character than Blanche. She's more obviously intelligent, sharper-tongued and less of a poseuse, and she presents us with a process rather than a result. At the Shaw Festival, she's magnetically played by Nicole Underhay, who manages to be convincingly vibrant and convincingly repressed, not just by turns but at the same time. Which is the essence of Alma: She has something to repress, something fierce. The things she doesn't have, that Blanche does, are a compelling narrative and a worthy antagonist.

It isn't that Summer and Smoke lacks structure. It's loaded with it, indeed loaded down with it. There are two matching acts: a summery one in which passions begin to flare and a wintry one in which they are cruelly doused. And, of course, there are two matching principals with opposing principles. John, though, both in the writing and here in the performance, is a shadow, though a very loquacious one. Unlike Stanley Kowalski, he comes out of no recognizable social or family background; he's just the idea of a debauchee, with the details of his debauches left conveniently vague. He claims by the end of the play to have come around to Alma's side, but the evidence -- he's getting married and taking his doctoring seriously -- remains strictly theoretical. Jeff Meadows, looking goofy and acting stolidly, can do nothing to bring him to life, and for all that Underhay can bring -- she's especially fine at conveying agonized disappointment, nobly borne but with a sardonic edge -- their scenes together, which take up much of the play, begin to drag horribly.

The director, Neil Munro, hasn't helped by cutting the play's prologue, which shows us the hero and heroine as children and sets up their relationship for the rest of the play. (Or are we using different texts?) This means that in the later scenes, ghostly childish voices that we don't recognize keep referring us back to things that we haven't been told about.

The production overall has a moody, broody quality that holds promise for the first 10 minutes but then declines into sluggishness. It also looks gloomier than Williams -- who knew about atmosphere if he knew about anything -- seems to have intended, with Peter Hartwell's harsh-looking set mostly suffused, courtesy of Alan Brodie's lighting, in dark leaf patterns. It's all right for the town square, but most of the action takes place in John's and Alma's adjacent houses, which here have a perplexing habit of encroaching on one another's territory. They are also full of lace curtains that the actors unaccountably keep hiding behind. This staging plays hell with a crucial plot development that's confusing enough in the first place: the shooting of a character we know little about by another of whom we know less.

There are some excellent supporting performances. Sharry Flett is outstanding as Alma's mother, who has driven her further into herself by declining into a premature senility in which mischievous irresponsibility is hard to distinguish from outright malevolence. Peter Hutt is fine as her clerical spouse, ostentatiously long- suffering. Guy Bannerman is good as his medical neighbour and counterpart, and Brigitte Robinson and Melanie Janzen are delightful as contrasting pillars of Alma's poetry circle; that old-time gentility, censorious or timed, is very economically summoned. Jonathan Gould brings up the play's rear with surprising sweetness as the visiting fireman who may be setting Alma on the road to ruin. Unless it's the other way around. - In repertoire through Oct. 27. For info, call 1-800-511-SHAW.
