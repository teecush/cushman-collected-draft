The acronym NSFW stands for "not safe for work." The play of which the acronym is the title has a further subtitle: Money, Sex and Photoshop. It's the work of a notable young British playwright, Lucy Kirkwood, and it's being given its North American premiere by Studio 180. It's been widely described as a play about the objectification of women, but that is selling it short. It's about the objectification and humiliation of both sexes, by both sexes.

It's set in the London offices of two magazines, both of them, come to think of it, photo shops. First we're in the Doghouse, that being the name of a girlie mag trying to keep a foothold in an increasingly precarious print market.

The current issue features a picture of a big-breasted young woman who turns out to have been only 14; the photograph was submitted by her ex-boyfriend, and selected for prominent display from among hundreds of other offerings. The girl's father has come across the picture, and is naturally incensed. Aidan, the editor, has to set about damage control. Or, given the possible legal consequences, damages control.

He invites the father, a Mr. Bradshaw, down from his Manchester home, puts him up in a five-star hotel, offers him coffee and sympathy, and produces a cheque for a substantial amount, all signed and ready to go. Mr. Bradshaw declines both the coffee and the money; he wants blood. He remains immovable, even though Aidan, having taken expert and expensive advice, assures him that he doesn't have a legal leg to stand on.

Our sympathies, of course, are all with the aggrieved and disadvantaged dad up against the metropolitan smoothie, and they remain that way. But he, too, turns out to be compromised (how, among other things, did he come across his daughter's picture in the first place?); and though we hope that he will stand his ground, we realize increasingly that it's no sure thing.

This, in Joel Greenberg's production, is as tense and multi-faceted a scene as you could hope to encounter. Patrick Galligan slides effortlessly along Aidan's trail of slime, implicitly threatening even, or especially, at his most ingratiating; he exploits his opponent's weaknesses from behind the blandest of concerned smiles. Ian D. Clark is heartbreakingly flesh and blood as Bradshaw, voice, stance and raincoat all correct. There is a third player: Charlotte, Aidan's assistant who, the job situation being what it is, has compartmentalized her life. In the office, she's an efficient cog in her boss's machine, and possibly his girlfriend as well; outside it, she belongs to a women's group. Her function in this scene is to watch and wait, stiff-lipped; Jessica Greenberg makes that lip remarkably expressive, silently communicating disillusion with one party, disappointment in the other, and disgust at just about everything.

We've already met Charlotte, keeping her end up in off-colour banter with Aidan's two male subordinates. One, Rupert, is a trustfund kid, whose advantages enable him to tell Aidan to stuff it when asked to embarrass himself for the sake of a story. (Class resentments between the two men are alluded to, but I suspect it would take a British production to make them clear.) The other, Sam, just one step above an intern, declines a similar assignment out of loyalty to an adored fiancee. Both these men reappear in the play's second location, which moves us from David Mamet's territory into Caryl Churchill's. The Top Girl here is Miranda, editor of Electra, an upscale publication whose aim of empowering female readers amounts in practice to exposing their imperfections, in the hope they'll spend money to correct them.

Rupert now has a job at Electra and Sam is applying for one. Rupert's involves putting himself as a man through painful procedures routinely undergone by women: most ironic, considering his previous protests and also, in James Graham's enforcedly silent performance, most hilarious. Sam, endowed by Aaron Stern with a touching brand of eager shyness, is put to a test; Miranda asks him to identify flaws in the girlfriend to whom he remains passionately loyal.

Like Mr. Bradshaw before him, he squirms and protests and wriggles. Miranda is an even greater master of office doublespeak than Aidan; she's continually wrongfooting others, under the smiling guise of wrongfooting herself. Susan Coyne, in a splendidly considered and beautifully timed performance, makes clear that, underneath the assurance, there's a genuine layer of self-hatred. What unites all the play's characters is the desperate instinct for selfpreservation, whatever the cost. The final stage image, implicitly asking, "Is this what feminism has come to?" is sadly unsubtle. Up to that point, though, its wit is devastating. I didn't laugh all that often, but when I did I laughed loud.

The Sextet of Morris Panych's new play is a chamber group, marooned at a motel because of a blizzard. Harry is silently (except to us) and unrequitedly in love with Dirk, who fancies Sylvia, who is actually attracted to Harry; Otto, a test-tube baby, may have similarly impregnated Mavis, whose cross-dressing husband Gerard, despite certain physical difficulties, would like to believe that he himself is the father. It takes time to sort all this out, what with the dialogue moving very fast, and I'm still not sure I got it all right.

Panych, as usual, directs his own play and, also as usual, his staging is excellent, on a neatly terrifying set designed by Ken MacDonald, six unnervingly identical beds lined up in a row. But the play itself feels mechanical, with only two of the characters coming to life.

One is Damien Atkins' Harry, who has the advantage of functioning as a kind of a narrator with a direct line to the audience and who builds on it with a study of embarrassed frustration that may add up to 50 shades of green; the other is Laura Condolln's Sylvia, who is simply real. Matthew Edison (Dirk), Jordan Pettle (Otto) and Bruce Dow (Gerard) all contribute high expertise, though Rebecca Northan, a clown deprived of her red nose, is pallid as Mavis.

In addition to putting the sex into sextet (I'm not trying to deny paternity, but it's Panych's joke) these musicians, or most of them, care about their music, especially Gerard, who wants them to play Schoenberg, while the others would much prefer Schubert. Gerard gets his way, because he's the boss. And, judging from what we hear at the end of the play, his way, in this case at least, may be the right way. The show goes on; but I don't think it makes a metaphor.

NSFW runs through Nov. 30 at the Theatre Centre; Sextet runs through Dec. 14 at the Tarragon Theatre.
