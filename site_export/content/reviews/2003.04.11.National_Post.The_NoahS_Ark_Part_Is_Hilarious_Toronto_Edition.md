YIIMIMANGALISO: THE MYSTERIES

Elgin Theatre, Toronto

- - -

OUTLYING ISLANDS

du Maurier Theatre Centre, Toronto

- - -

In the beginning is God, dressed in a sarong of many colours. He announces his identity, plus the creation of the world, in the short, clipped verse lines characteristic of the English medieval mystery plays, from one of which his lines are in fact taken. A chorus of angels hails him in liturgical Latin. Then the beat to which they're singing gets syncopated, and they break out into an explosion of song and dance as wild and joyous as you are likely to encounter on any stage. Within its opening five minutes, and in three separate modes, Yiimimangaliso: The Mysteries has declared itself, and the hand it shows is a dazzler.

The production is the brainchild of Mark Dornford-May and Charles Hazlewood, respectively the director and conductor of the Broomhill Opera, which operates out of Wilton's Music Hall in Londons East End. They were invited to stage an opera in Durban, South Africa, and, while there, conceived the idea of a show that would utilize the talents and traditions of the countrys township choirs. When they held auditions, they were overwhelmed with applicants eager not so much to get on to the stage as to get a job. They emerged with a show that has gone round the world.

It's a multiple, post-apartheid example of the story that was told to us earlier in this World Stage season by A Woman in Waiting, and was unforgettably exemplified, long before that, by the international progress of John Kani and Winston Ntshona in Athol Fugard's plays. South Africa may not send much theatre to the world, but when it does it catches fire.

This show is a superb matching of talent to material. There are four sets of mystery plays extant, including the Chester cycle, which this production has taken as its source. They were performed, and presumably written, by the tradesmens guilds of the time as a user-friendly method of retelling Bible stories. In other words, they are amateur shows. There have been some fine professional revivals of them, but all have suffered from some degree of self- consciousness. The performers here were technically amateurs when they started out, and they recast the plays according to their own talents, personalities and traditions, which is what the originals must have done. They also share their predecessors bent for creative anachronism. It's a safe bet that local stars were born on the city streets of the 14th and 15th centuries, and their performances might well have taken on the international circuit, had such a thing existed.

Only a minority of the lines come to us here in Middle English. The rest are in a variety of African tongues, including Zulu, Xhosa and Afrikaans; a line spoken in one language is quite likely to be answered in another, and all are regularly reinforced and transfigured by music. Actors double as musicians, playing mainly on a variety of pots and pans.

The story proceeds from the creation to the revolt of Lucifer and his supporters, who are cast into a pit surmounted by gleefully realistic flames. We go through Adam and Eve, Cain and Abel, and -- a favourite set piece in all the mystery cycles -- Noah's ark, which may be the first English sitcom: With no biblical warrant, all the versions capitalize on Mrs. Noahs respectable reluctance to set foot on board and leave the neighbourhood. Noah, as played here by Sibusiso Ziqubu, has the dimensions and demeanour of Porky Pig. His reception of his sailing orders is, fittingly, a high-water mark of the shows linguistic freedom; meaning is transmitted mainly by incredulous cries and obedient looks. (Noah also has some trouble getting the chickens on board.) The flood's end is greeted by a swelling, and moving, chorus of You Are My Sunshine. The sons and daughters-in-law, bid to go forth and multiply, make enthusiastic exits.

The Old Testament, as far as the mystery writers were concerned, ended with Abraham and Isaac. (In other words, they leave out everything that might count as Jewish history.) We fast-forward, come to the start of Act Two, to the Annunciation, and follow the Jesus story through to the Resurrection. The Nativity is heralded by the tossing on to the huge, bare stage of a bale of hay and the crisp announcement: "Bethlehem.

A similar shorthand is employed for most of the scenes. I was slightly taken aback by the ease with which I, a nice Jewish boy, picked up the references, but then I had a post-war English education, with religious (i.e., Christian) instruction still embedded in the curriculum. This may have messed with my mind, but it taught me something about the icons and sources of Western civilization. Politically and socially, I'm in favour of the secularization of education; culturally, its a problem. The abandonment of the King James Bible, even in churches, has already made it far harder to speak or understand Shakespeare, and some of todays kids might well be confused by parts of The Mysteries.

Vumile Nomanyama plays both God the Father and God the Son -- an obvious double that I've never seen executed before. An authoritative, sharp-tongued actor, he makes a peremptory deity, proud of what he's wrought and aggressively aggrieved when it lets him down. His transformation into Jesus is announced by having him join his mother in a dance. Off comes the sarong, and hes human and vulnerable in a pair of short pants. He, in turn, teaches his disciples to dance; I wouldnt swear that Judas (Steven Hicks) has two left feet, but you can see his persecution complex growing.

The company have a Beggar's Opera in their repertoire, in which Nomanyama plays Captain Macheath; that I'd like to see. In their production of Carmen (which they are alternating with The Mysteries) the title role is played by Pauline Malefane, a presumably versatile actress who in this show is a vibrant Virgin. Andries Mbali is a sly, red-suited Devil, tireless at his job and equipped with horns for his role in the New Testament, though not in the Old.

It isnt all fun. The Massacre of the Innocents is harrowingly done. The mothers are singing lullabies when their babies are torn from them. The soldiers are black-clad secret police, and their leader (Zorro Sidloyi, a striking bass) joins chillingly in the singing as he closes in for the kill. The elements of the show come as effectively together here as they do in the sequences of celebration, of which the last would have any audience on its feet.

- - -

From the Traverse Theatre, Edinburgh, comes a show far more subdued, and far more baffling, but also with its share of atavistic elements. Outlying Islands, a new play by David Greig, is set just before the Second World War. Two clean young Englishmen, just out of Cambridge, have been sent to one of the remotest of the isles that dot the Scottish coast to study the bird life. They take up residence in what was, as little as a hundred years earlier, a pagan chapel. The place is now uninhabited, though leased by an elderly sheep farmer, Kirk by name and also by puritanical nature, who is now their remarkably surly guide. His closely watched niece makes up the company.

It transpires that the ministry that financed the trip has interests beyond birdwatching. They actually want to use the isle and its non-human population to test the efficacy of -- hold your breath -- anthrax. This news provides an undeniable frisson for us and for the characters. The two boys are shocked to their ecological boots. Mr. Kirk has already delivered himself of some observations on the corruptions of modern life (Women are uncovering their heads! Cinemas have arisen!") that make him sound like one of the railing prophets who got left out of The Mysteries. He is more amenable, though, to some of the other ways of Mammon, and starts calculating what he stands to make from the government in the way of compensation. Battle is joined. And the old man, who shouldnt have been drinking, drops dead of a heart attack.

This leaves Ellen, the niece, free to come out of her shell. Both the young men lust after her -- though John, the virginal junior of the two, is ashamed of his feelings, and Robert, who got himself a sexual reputation at college, isn't. Ellen, who doesn't subscribe to her uncles view of the movies and who has a particular passion for Laurel and Hardy, describes her two potential suitors, not imperceptively, as "Laurel and Laurel." There is a charming sequence in which the three sing The Trail of the Lonesome Pine, a song that makes a memorable appearance in Stan and Ollies Way Out West. Ellen, who has seen the film 37 times, can sing all of it; Robert, who has seen it once, can sing some of it; and bashful James, who has never seen it, can just about join in on the title phrase the last time round.

All this love-interest part of the play is well developed, at least until it succumbs to the misty portentousness that envelops everything else. Themes -- war and peace, paganism and Christianity, freedom and inhibition, the birds and the bees -- are picked up and dropped again, and it's hard to see how they're all meant to cohere. Lyrical dissertations on the migratory and nesting habits of the petrel must be meant as metaphors of the human action, both on stage and off in the wings of history, but I couldnt make any specific connection.

Robert, whose mental and imaginative processes are especially obscure, is given a monotonously pushy performance by Laurence Mitchell. But Sam Heughan makes a nice, fresh John, and Lesley Hart an emergingly edible Ellen. Robert Carrs Kirk has a strange pinch- vowelled accent, but this is a Scots company, so I imagine it's authentic. Philip Howard directs, rather slackly, on a split-level set that might be more evocative if it were less dingily lit. Even a cave should have its moments of illumination.
