The new Second City revue, entitled Sixteen Scandals and one of the troupe's sharpest and funniest ever, begins by disposing, briskly and musically, of the weather. The customary half-dozen performers (three male, three female) line up to complain about how endlessly terrible it's been. We hear them. The run is scheduled to last until July, and by then of course this lament may have lost some of its bite. (Then again, perhaps not.) The show promptly moves on to Torontos second favourite object of complaint. A downtowner is visiting an old friend who has relocated to Etobicoke and who entreats him to do the same. Well, he doesnt so much entreat as threaten. Join us, he says direly, "or we'll elect him again." This is the evening's one Rob Ford joke, and it doesnt even mention Rob Ford, at least not by name.

The same force and subtlety are evident throughout the evening; the performers, as always, take joint credit for the script, but I suspect some writing as well as editorial input from the director, Chris Earle. Even the title turns out to be more than the usual random pun. It inspires a first-act closer in which five-sixths of the cast, in various and (for Second City) unaccustomed states of undress merrily carol, Mr. Rogers-style, about the scandals in your neighbourhood which turn out to be merely heightened gossip. The remaining performer, Connor Thompson, whose solid

presence conceals impressive reserves of angst, tries to remind them of the really scandalous things in the world. They pronounce him boring. Even when he takes off his own clothes, his nakedness camouflaged only by a placard arraigning child poverty, it stops them in their tracks for no more than a moment.

Kevin Vidal, who is black and who would be a good actor in any context, has a beautifully crafted monologue addressed to an unseen son who has come out - as black. The double entendre (reminiscent of Flanders and Swanns The Reluctant Cannibal if anyone does remember that one) progresses from denial through anger to the qualified acceptance of "maybe next year I'll march with you in Caribana." Denial is one Vidal's specialties; earlier hes given us an office worker near hysterical in his insistence that Toronto has to be the capital of Canada, just like New York is the capital of the U.S. (Well, he has a point. I mean, whoever heard of Canberra?) As a soloist, he yields only to Ashley Botting, in her motivational lecture on attaining happiness by becoming someone else. As she unanswerably asks, If you were happy being you, wouldnt you be happy by now?

Musically, this is the strongest Second City show I remember, running to a duet for a hit-man (the straight-arrow Thompson) and his neglected wife, sung through Andrew Lloyd Webber style, lugubrious underdone words set to lugubrious overcooked tunes. (Musical director, Matthew Reid). There are actually fewer marital or domestic skits than usual, and those there are tend to the refreshingly outlandish; a husband finds comfort in dropping through his very own Black Hole (it's in his very own closet) because, he says "I need my own space - and time." And then there are the cross-dressed sketches: a matched pair. The three ladies - Botting fitting in between Allison Price, who's tall, and Sarah Hillier, whos droll and compact enough to play a goblin, which she elsewhere does - appear as a trio of would-be jocks, united in their frustrated conviction that "bitches be crazy." Then - later enough in the proceedings for it to be a surprise as well as a treat - Thompson, Vidal and Craig Brown team up as a trio of mature, respectable lady shoplifters. The men-as-women have the wittier lines, but the womenas-men definitely win out on the moves, which in their case are balletically grotesque, or perhaps wincingly winsome. The show gets in to some refreshingly dark and astringent places. Camellia Koos set includes a large circular aperture that suggests a washing machine (and at one charming point, does more than suggest), and the customary Second City steep ladder, here confined to a corner of the set and used, as far as I could observe, only once.

There isn't always safety in numbers. If Sixteen Scandals is a triumph, Six Essential Questions is a disaster. It's a first play by Priscilla Uppal, based on a novel of hers that

Im told is autobiographical and, on the evidence here, would have to be. As staged, it presents Renata, a Canadian doctor, who comes to Brazil in search of the mother (billed as Mother) who abandoned her and her brother and their quadriplegic father years before. Renata has every reason to believe that Mother was a very bad mother, and the play takes a long and crushingly undramatic time to prove her absolutely right. There must have been more intended than this, but if Mother was supposed to have a case of her own it never emerges; shes a jolly sociopath, whose nonstop conversation mainly consists of a numbered list of the things she likes about herself. (It goes into the hundreds.) Her conviction that she's the life and soul of her own South American party is wearyingly reinforced by Elizabeth Saunders' performance and by Leah Cherniaks would-be carnival of a production. There is some respite in Maggie Huculaks graciously loopy account of Mother's own mother, and in Mina James' Renata, who has little chance to be anything but earnest. Veering between these poles of exhibitionism and restraint is Richard Zeppieri as Mothers brother, known as Doctor Garbage, and emerging from a symbolic pile of it: symbolic of what, I wouldnt know. The six essential questions are those that Renata asks, when she finally gets a word in, concerning her mother's selfishness; it's a relief to hear them, but a final letdown to realize that there is no answer to them. I have to ask a seventh, even more essential question why was this play that isn't a play put on?
