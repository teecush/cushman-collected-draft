JIM AND SHORTY

Factory Theatre

Alex Poch-Goldin, the author of Jim and Shorty, is an actor who writes admirably for other actors. I don't mean that he writes them great lines -- the dialogue of this play is actually its weakest component -- but he imagines them clearly, and brings them to strong physical life.

Jim and Shorty are a couple of homeless men who spend three scenes interacting, sometimes helped out by a third man called Tom. They occupy a largely blank stage space that stretches to the back wall of the theatre, and is dominated by a bench and a garbage can. It could be a dilapidated parkette behind an invisible bus-shelter. Layne Coleman, the director, and Sherri Hay, the designer, have done a fine job here, staging the play in the theatrical equivalent of deep focus.

Most productions concentrate on stringing their actors out across the width of a stage, and the perspective here is exhilarating. When the dramatist contrives an effect -- when one character enters unexpectedly on a motorized cart, or another is discovered huddled, shocked and freezing -- it stands out in the sharpest relief.

When we first meet them, Jim is in tatters and Shorty wears a suit, and the contrast defines them: how we see them, and especially how they see one another. Shorty is indignant that the welfare officials -- government employees, for Gods sake -- come to work these days in T-shirts, and he lectures Jim, who looks back on his schooldays with bewildered distaste, on the importance of an education.

History teaches him to be aware of the difference between yesterday and today. In a life like theirs, where one day threatens to melt indistinguishably into another, maintaining that awareness is crucial to preserving a sense of self. Math helps him count any money he may have. The cost of things -- of doughnuts, beer and bus fares -- is on their minds, especially on Jims, who keeps comparing them to what they used to be. (He has perhaps learned Shorty's lessons better than Shorty thinks.)

If neither man is strictly an alcoholic, drink is still their most pressing concern. They also discuss the merits of the different shelters available, and the best ways of squeezing cash out of the system. They gossip vaguely about mutual acquaintances.

They even mention politics. There is an election brewing and Tom, the third character -- who is Indian, or native, or First Nations, depending on the awareness of the speaker -- starts the play by loudly recommending a candidate who is going to change everything. He doesn't give a name, but it certainly isnt Mike Harris.

Jim and Shorty consider voting, but they dont seem to get around to it. Inevitably, we start thinking -- as in so many previous plays --of Beckett and early Pinter, who taught us in the starkest terms that passing the time is both the business of drama and its subject, and in whose stretch of theatrical history we are still living.

The thought would hold even if the author had never heard or read a word of them (which, for a working actor in his thirties, is admittedly unlikely.) There is, though, a built-in twist. We are conditioned to think such characters as lacking a coherent biography; but each has one. If they are living an absurdist drama, they are living it for real.

It turns out, too, to be a conventionally well-made absurdist drama. Its second half is a black (well, grey) comedy of role- reversal. Jim, thanks to the vagaries of bureaucracy, now has the upper hand, and he uses it: not viciously but meanly. He truly has learned to play the system, at least for now. This is undeniably effective, but also undeniably glib.

I suspect the author was in a bind. The play was first performed two years ago, without its current last scene. I didn't see it then, but I imagine I would have found it unsatisfying, a pointless vignette needing development. Now it has been developed and it still doesn't satisfy.

Increasingly, one wants the text to resonate more. It carries the suggestion of wit without its substance. This is as good as it gets: Contemplating a burned-out butchers store, Jim thinks of "all that meat -- cooked."

The writing is never actually tedious and it certainly gives the cast enough to bite on. They could hardly be bettered, with Earl Pastko especially distinctive as Shorty, reaching out but essentially in a world of his own. Ron Gabriels Jim is a more conventional sly hobo (Estragon to Shorty's Vladimir) and Lorne Cardinal is a loomingly sympathetic presence as Tom.

There is intermittent kindness in all these characters, and the cruelty is all in the circumstances. The play opened on a snowy night, with some at least of the banned squeegee kids still at work. It couldn't have been more suitable.

Alex and Shorty runs to Feb. 26 at the Factory Studio Theatre, 125 Bathurst St. $15 to $18. Call 416-504-9971.
