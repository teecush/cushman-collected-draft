THEATRE REVIEW FIELD

Friday, March 06, 1992

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Toronto ONT -- CREATED AND PRESENTED BY POW POW UNBOUND Directed by Darren O'Donnell At Buddies in Bad Times Theatre Rating: Dexterity of speech is not what you expect from this kind of spectacle, but the words here, such as they are, are delivered with stinging expertise A fighting-fit mix of vocal and physical skills BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Toronto FIELD is an extremely efficient production that begins by having its seven actors chant, each in turn, "welcome to the show - the show Field" after which they take their bows. Having thus provided us with an unassailable beginning and end, they proceed to the middle which is a lot less easy to describe.

The troupe - that seems the right word to do justice to their fighting- fit combination of vocal and physical skills - springs about in red track suits, all but one menacing woman who is in black and resides mainly in a large box of the same colour which is usually centre-stage but can be moved. From here she spits out the story of Kathy, who moved from one part of Canada to another, and was exploited by men every step of the way.

Not too much is made of this; it seems, the show implies, to be just what you'd expect. And Kathy's story is only intermittently part of the proceedings which are otherwise rather abstract. Or to put it another way, I didn't understand them. Which isn't to say that they don't evoke a kinetic response. Early on, an elaborate syncopated counterpoint is created of screams, grunts, and the hypnotic movement of an actor on a swing. It is prolonged to a head-splitting point, but anger is defused by admiration. There is inventiveness here, and there is also precision. Dexterity of speech is not what you expect from this kind of spectacle, but the words here, such as they are, are delivered with stinging expertise. The performance level is the highest I have encountered on the Toronto stage in some time.

You would be safe in assuming that it is all about sex. A recipe for nipple cupcakes (in which the ingredient "homo milk" is afforded gleeful reptilian emphasis) is nipped in the bud, so to speak, when one performer claims to have lost her nipples, which is even worse than it sounds because she cannot experience orgasm without them. Another actor then mimes, hilariously, to a 1950s-sounding novelty record about how to look for something you have mislaid. I don't think they ever turn up, but I may have missed something.

There is also a grisly sequence - meant, presumably, to conjure up memories of childhood intimidation - in which masturbation is satirically discouraged because it can call up monsters. (The word used to be "monsterbation.") There are horrific nursery rhymes, some passing theatrical satire in which a tyrannical director is used as a metaphor for cruelty in general, and a lot of screaming putdowns, some of them funny. Some actors have roses protruding from their crotches; toward the close they remove them and strew them elegiacally on top of the now-deserted black box. The mood is enhanced when somebody runs back and forth across the stage spinning wires across it as he goes. It's magic.

The show projects a mood of embattled disdain. It is not my preferred way to spend an evening, but they do it awfully well. Field continues at Buddies in Bad Times Theatre, 142 George St., 863-9455, through March 16.
