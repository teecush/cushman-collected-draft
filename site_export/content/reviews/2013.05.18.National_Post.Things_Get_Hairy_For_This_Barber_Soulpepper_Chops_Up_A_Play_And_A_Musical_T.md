A celebrated British actress once counselled a celebrated British actor against accepting the role of Figaro in a revival of the Beaumarchais play: "It never works, darling. That's why they had to turn it into a musical." The play in question was The Marriage of Figaro (1784), source of course of the Mozart opera (1786). The Marriage, though, was the second of Beaumarchais' Figaro comedies, the first being The Barber of Seville (1775), source of the equally famous, though paradoxically later, opera by Rossini (1810). It isn't true that the original plays never work; in France, they're staples of the classic repertoire. But their track record in English is certainly sparse, with productions of the Barber being even thinner on the ground than those of the Marriage. Soulpepper's Barber is an amalgam of play and opera, in which Beaumarchais' text is periodically interrupted by Rossini's music. The script, though not its setting, has been updated and otherwise mucked about with, and the tunes are served up with new words, in mostly altered contexts, and in much reduced orchestrations. In fact, they have turned it into a musical. And it still doesn't work. The first-night audience greeted it with hysterical enthusiasm, and it isn't devoid of good points, but overall it's a self-indulgent mess.

Michael O'Brien's adaptation (first done, if I may throw another date at you, by Theatre Columbus in 1996) isn't altogether at fault. Its dialogue is often racy, if equally often repetitive, and the lyrics, at least the ones that are audible, are neat. Its main impulse seems to be to democratize the play, both by adding in as many topical references and extended farcical gambits as possible, and by bringing to the fore political impulses that are at most latent in the original.

Actually what O'Brien has done is to read back the sequel into the prequel. Pierre-Augustin Caron de Beaumarchais was a Jacques-of-all-trades, whose non-literary activities included ship-owning, music-making, gun-running (to the rebellious American colonies), and some spells in jail. He was also a pungent polemicist, who prompted Louis XVI to exclaim of The Marriage of Figaro that it was seditious and could never be produced. (It was; to the applause of Louis' courtiers, who laughed all the way to the guillotine.) What aroused the king's shocked displeasure was a speech of Figaro the valet in which he questioned the aristocratic right of his master Count Almaviva to, for example, seduce Figaro's fiancee. That's in the future though. In The Barber of Seville Figaro's function, as endlessly resourceful servant, is to facilitate the union of his employer the Count with the beautiful Rosine who, in accordance with comedic custom immemorial, is being held virtually prisoner by her elderly guardian Don Bartolo who wants her for himself. O'Brien transforms the Count, a mere rake in the original Barber, into a cynical, not to say stupid, exploiter of women and servants, whose final relationship with Figaro would, as presented here, make the later play impossible. Meanwhile Don Bartolo gets gradually upgraded from possessive pantaloon to pathetic victim. The show ends with he and Figaro vowing revenge on their ill-users while bits of the set swing up in the shape of a barricade, as if we were about to embark on a production of Les Miserables. (Which isn't about the French Revolution, but try telling that to the fans.)

I'm not suggesting any of this should be illegal. O'Brien has aright to spin and re-order the material as he likes (it's billed as his play, not Beaumarchais') and the shout-outs to the gathering French storm are the most effective parts of the evening. That it's actually set in Spain was just the original author's safety-precaution. What does go horribly wrong is the general galumphing about that characterizes Leah Cherniak's production and that dilutes and obscures a fairly simple intrigue. The alternation of play and opera does justice to neither; it just prolongs the evening to intolerable lengths. People sing and dance on no provocation and with minimal relevance; the production number that closes the first half makes it impossible to discern what in the story is supposed to be going on. The idea seems to be that Soulpepper actors letting their hair down is a sufficient treat in itself. Which makes it ironic that the standout performance, the one that saves the show from being a complete shambles, is given by a company newcomer, Dan Chameroy. His Figaro is masterfully mercenary, tough and resilient and rather touching when wounded. He is also the only performer who can do justice to the music; his introductory number, the Largo al Factotum constructively rewritten, raises hopes for the evening that are never subsequently restored. The others seem undecided as to whether they're singing cod opera or the real thing. This is particularly the case with Courtney Ch'ng Lancaster's big aria as Rosina; her acting, though, of a heroine ruthlessly reconceived as an emerging New Woman, is splendid. She also plays a mean flute in the overture: another item that, under John Millard's musical direction, promises more than is subsequently delivered. Gregory Prest as her lover is less fortunate; having nicely established the Count as narcissist, he has to undertake a couple of overdone impersonations, mercilessly extended. Yes, they're in the original play, but not quite like that; in the second case, a bosomy drag act, not at all like that.

Oliver Dennis, an actor with modest musical pretensions (he didn't sing a note in The Fantasticks, but got a deserved award anyway), does charmingly with the two interpolated ditties Don Bartholo is given here, one of them borrowed from The Phantom of the Opera, perhaps as a comment on the domestic tyrant's taste. He's also beautifully at ease with this Don's alternating championship of reaction and revolution. His foil Don Basilio (William Webster) was written as a man who'll do anything for money. The production, never able to resist a gag, destroys that by making him a coward as well; there really are times when complexity isn't called for. Raquel Duffy provides some welcome moments of restraint as a loyal housekeeper.

One of the biggest successes in London and New York at the moment is One Man, Two Guv'nors, an adaptation of Goldoni's The Servant of Two Masters that takes great anachronistic liberties with its original while still remaining joyfully true to its spirit. This Barber of Seville has no spirit to be true to and, hard though it tries to seem otherwise, isn't joyful at all.
