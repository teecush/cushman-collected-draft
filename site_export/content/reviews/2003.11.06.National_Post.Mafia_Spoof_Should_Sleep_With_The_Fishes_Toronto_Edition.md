GODFATHER KNOWS BEST

Mysteriously Yours, Toronto

- - -

In the course of enthusing over Mysteriously Yours' previous production, Murder in Casablanca, I speculated that a logical source for the troupes next spoof might be The Sopranos. Thats the last time I try to influence the course of modern drama. The producers have taken me up on the idea, or maybe they had already had it, and I must confess that it doesn't work.

Godfather Knows Best is based on an earlier Mafia epic, but the principle is the same. As in all shows at this dinner-theatre enterprise, actors move among us somewhere around the dessert stage and fill us in on the details of their pasts. Those of us who have been here know that very shortly someone will get whacked, and we will spend the rest of the evening guessing the identity of the killer. We also know that everyone will have had an opportunity and an abundance of motive.

And so it proves here. Don Provolone is about to relinquish the savoury cares of office and has summoned to his farewell party representatives of the other major families: the Asiagos, the Mozzarellas, the Parmesans, the Romanos and the odoriferous Blue Cheeses. (All these subsidiary characters are played by us. Most of them are undemanding, requiring us only to eat, drink, and pay attention.) Soon enough, one of the Don's sons bites the dust. A detective, played as always by the redoubtable Jean Daigle, conducts the investigation.

Now you dont usually find cops playing prominent roles in gangster family sagas, so in order to recruit one, this show has gone outside its core material to a totally unconnected TV show. Daigle appears before us as Inspector Columbo. But this mixing of myths is a weakness; it suggests the basic source isnt rich enough. At first blush, this seems absurd; The Godfather films are full of memorable characters. But when you think about it, none of them, apart from Brando's Don, have become mythic (i.e. they are not conducive to impersonations). Compare Casablanca, in which not only Bogart and Bergman, but Howard, Rains, Greenstreet, and Lorre, have taken on existences in our shared imagination. I still think The Sopranos would have yielded more opportunities; but I suspect the whole Mafia world, at least as depicted in our favourite fictions, is so inbred that when you bring an outsider in to open it up it simply shrivels.

Anyway, it doesn't throw up many good jokes, which are what Mysteriously Yours relies on to keep the engine running. The plot turns on a conspiracy to involve the Provolones in the processed- cheese business, a business that true sons of the old country hold to be degenerate. The authors should be congratulated on their heroic restraint in never once referring to Don Provolone as the Big Cheese; and they have managed to work in a nice line about the kind of cheese you would expect to find in Muskoka. But there is not much to take your mind off the mystery; whose thinness may be attested by the fact that, for the first time, I was able to guess correctly who did it. There is still some fun. The action is whipped along by Daigle, who does his usual inquisitorial act -- bullying with a touch of bemusement. Of the rest, Brian Smith stands out as consigliere, Tom Dugan, "an Italian stuck in an Irishmans body," whom he plays with a richness of accent and cringe that we never got from Robert Duvall. Other characters include the Dons banished son, Michael, and his peculiar son, Frodo (a slave to slices); their very blowsy sister Connie; and the importunate Kay; they are all recognizable, but though the actors work hard, neither they nor the writers are able to strike many sparks. As always, though, the cast show extraordinary skill in remembering the names of those spectators whom they have dragooned into the action. The food, this time with an Italian flavour, remains above dinner-theatre standard; and the service is better than you'll find almost anywhere.
