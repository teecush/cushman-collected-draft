THEATRE REVIEW

The Addams Family

Toronto Centre for the Arts

There seems to be a consensus that the touring production of The Addams Family works better than the one that opened on Broadway last year (and will shortly be closing). As the closest I've come to the original is hearing the cast CD, I can't make comparisons, beyond noting there have certainly been changes in the score, and probably in the book.

The show that's touched down in Toronto is "supervised" by the illustrious Jerry Zaks, who was merely credited as "creative consultant" in New York, and it's a slick piece of goods, scenically and choreographically. The performances are fine, too. But it still isn't a good musical, and I doubt that it ever could be.

The insurmountable flaw is that the authors have taken something static and tried to make it move. The cartoonist Charles Addams invented his ghoulish namesakes in a long series of drawings in The New Yorker: Not even a cartoon strip, just a succession of creepily funny one-offs depicting a clan that had turned conventional moral values upside down, prizing death over life and laughing where others would shudder.

It's hard to write a story about them without falsifying them, especially if you don't want to rock any boats. (Yes, I know they were made into a successful TV series and two movies. So?) The musical's solution is to pit the Addam-ses against a straitlaced family from Ohio, which, despite there being 49 alternatives, is the state that musicals - see Wonderful Town and Bye Bye Birdie - always seem to pick on as emblematic of small town, small world America.

In this case the Ohioans, if that's the word, are Mr. and Mrs. Beineke, whose son Lucas has fallen in requited love with the Addams daughter Wednesday. They met in Central Park, which is where the Addamses, due to zoning laws established in the 15th century (by the Queen of Spain) have their spooky abode.

The lovers are nervous as to what their families may make of one another, but the Beinekes get invited to dinner anyway, dinner being one of those theatrical Last Sup-per affairs in which everyone sits on the same side of the table. In accordance with a Broadway trope that stretches, at the very least, from You Can't Take It with You to La Cage aux Folles, the eccentric family has vital life lessons to teach the visitors, being not only better-adjusted than they are but nicer, too.

You get that? The Addam-ses are nice. Papa Gomez (the show makes a great point of his Hispanic lineage) is over-flowing with what looks suspiciously like the milk of human kindness, wanting only the best for his daughter; he's like Tevye with castanets. He offends his wife Morticia, a thoroughly desirable wraith with what her husband aptly describes as "a neck line that stretches all the way down to Venezuela," by keeping from her the news that the young folks want to get married, maybe because it's so obvious that it hardly seems worth talking about.

Other pieces of narrative desperation include a hurricane that arrives at the end of Act One to keep everyone indoors but seems to have thoroughly dissipated by the start of Act Two, and a young heroine whose attitude to her betrothed seems to change from moment to moment according to the writers' whims. There's the standard strangled-voiced Gothic retainer; Mr. Beineke takes it as evidence of the Addams' oddity that they have a zombie for a butler; it's surely more uncommon that they have a butler at all. There is also a loony grandma, who's rather entertaining; an enthusiastically masochistic little boy; and an especially annoying uncle who's in love with the moon. You wouldn't expect the Addamses to stand for this last bit of sentimental whimsy, but they seem to lap it up, which is more than I could.

The book (by Marshall Brickman and Rick Ellice) has some funny lines, but not enough to stop the plot from yawning. Mandated tango rhythms apart, it's hard to decide whether An-drew Lippa's music is meant to be pastiche or just old-fashioned; much of it sounds like a lesser Jule Styne score from the '60s, which, admittedly, there are worse things. His lyrics are neat, in a rather mechanical way; putting macabre sentiments to jaunty tunes is by now hardly a new idea, but Ma and Pa Addams do have a duet (Live Before We Die) that can stand on its own melodic feet.

Douglas Sills makes a delightful Gomez, better I would guess than Nathan Lane's original since Sills, feasting on the Latin, has had to invent his own sweetly extravagant persona whereas Lane sounds as if he's just doing his usual. As Morticia, Sara Gettelfinger looks stunning but feels wasted; I imagine that Bebe Neuwirth on Broadway was even more so, both ways.

Two other musical vets are landed with the elder Beinekes, who are the wrong kinds of caricature. Crista Moore, as Mrs. Frustrated, at least gets a showpiece opportunity to let it all hang out. Martin Vidnovic, as Mr. In-sensitive, gets nothing. This may be part of what I deduce from the CD synopsis to have been a general softening up. It also suggests that there was once a sound structural reason for the chorus of Addams ancestors to have emerged from their tombs. Now, they're just bony background, though well disported in Sergio Trujillo's choreography and in the set and costume designs of Julian Crouch and Phelim McDermott.

These latter two were the show's original directors; and I wonder if their vision may not have been darker, perhaps closer to Charles Ad-dams, than the giggly merry-making that's being served up in his name here.
