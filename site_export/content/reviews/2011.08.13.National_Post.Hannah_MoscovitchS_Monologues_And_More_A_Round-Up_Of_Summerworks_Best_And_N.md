Hannah Moscovitch's Little One (Summerworks, Theatre Passe Muraille) is about growing up with a sociopath. There's Claire, who as a sexually damaged four-year-old was rescued by an Ottawa couple with exemplary liberal principles, and there's Aaron, her adoptive elder brother, to whom their parents entrust much of the responsibility of looking after her, understanding her and forgiving her more outrageous actions. These include hurting Aaron physically and drowning his goldfish. When he's older, he acquires a more challenging pet, a kitten, who is the Little One of the title, and whose presence really seems, Claire being Claire, to be asking for trouble. Claire also develops a fixation on the people next door, a techie and his Vietnamese mail-order bride. She harbours dark suspicions about them and, disturbed though she is, she may not be wrong.

Claire begins the play by talking about this couple as though she were telling a story, the creepier for being understated. Then Aaron appears and starts telling his own story, which is also the story of the play. I sometimes worry about Moscovitch's reliance on monologue. But she writes uncommonly good ones, and Aaron's narrative is a riveting fusion of hurt, compassion, dread and humour. (His description of suburban Ottawa as the sound of a million people being quiet together is a classic.) The increasingly impressive Joe Cobden doesn't miss a beat or a transition of it. In his good intentions and his exasperation, he's very easy to relate to; and it's characteristic that his most moving moment should be about somebody else's feelings: his parents' tearful admission of guilt that they've neglected him in their efforts to do right by Claire.

Claire, by contrast and as Aaron comes to know only too well, is almost impossible to relate to. Michelle Monteith does unreachable better than anyone, and her performance here is expectedly and impeccably scary, the more so because even at her most capricious, she seems logical. Her finest moments, though, come at the very end when she is, for the only time, nakedly vulnerable; she also does childlike better than anyone. The play isn't exclusive monologue; the two actors have scenes together, mostly brief and psychologically or physically violent. Their last, delicate encounter is an exception, though as haunting a conclusion as it is, it leaves a few loose ends, especially concerning the neighbours.

One thing that connects the two households is music; Claire's and Aaron's mother teaches piano, the Vietnamese lady intermittently studies it. A 13-year-old pianist, Kaylie Lau, sits at the side of the stage punctuating the proceedings with Bach, and seeming in her own person to unite the girl we see and the one we only hear about. Natasha Mytnowych's production is as well orchestrated visually as verbally; Aaron talks to us from the light, Claire keeps irrupting from the dark, and from progressively scarier places.

My other Summerworks choices this year have been less happy. Hannah's Turn (Lower Ossington Theatre) at least offers a fine performance by Severn Thompson, who plays Hannah Arendt at two stages of her life, presented in alternating segments. She's an earnest young German-Jewish philosophy student, intellectually and physically seduced by Martin Heidegger before he became a Nazi; and she's her later self, famous and in America, questioning and haranguing a student of her own whose father was the only member of his family to survive the Holocaust. She's especially impressive in her grey-haired incarnation, as a careful thinker and, in what is after all her second language, talker. But the play itself (by Mark Migotti and Richard Sanger) is trite, taking the known facts and their most obvious ethical consequences and doing nothing with them, except perhaps to instruct us in some interesting niceties of concentration-camp slang. The love-passages of Hannah and Heidegger give us the student-teacher-seduction cliches without illuminating what he, in particular, subsequently became. Then, when they meet again postwar, she lectures him on the terrors of Nazism and his own insensitivity, neither of which are revelations. It doesn't help that Richard Clarkin plays the philosopher as an absentminded professor who hardly seems to believe his own ideas.

The Safe Word (Theatre Centre) is about love, or at least the current dating scene. Two new roommates dance around one another, she abrasively, he helplessly. An older, Russian, gay chess master looks on. The guy has a disturbing encounter with an emotionally older woman. The play stops. There is the occasional sharp line, as there tends to be when sex is the subject. The author is Nicolas Billon and the director Lee Wilson, both of whom have done better in the past. The overall nervelessness may partly be blamed on the weather. Maybe our theatre schools should institute a new class: How To Act Over Air-Conditioning.

The Physical Ramifications of Attempted Global Domination (Passe Muraille) brings together, for the first time on any stage, Napoleon Bonaparte, Adolf Hitler, Oliver Cromwell, Pol Pot, Idi Amin, Pancho Villa, Mao Tse Tung and Pope Pius XII, who seems a little out of place in this company, whatever you think of his war record, but is maybe standing in for popes through the ages. They're all played by women. They compete in a game show. Each boasts of his own atrocities while blaming the others for theirs. At the very end we hear about their respective illnesses. The performances are eight shades of awful.
