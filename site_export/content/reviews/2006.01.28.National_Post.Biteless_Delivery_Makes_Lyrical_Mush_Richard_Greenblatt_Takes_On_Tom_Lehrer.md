robertcushman@lycos.com; Letters from Lehrer runs through Feb. 25 at the Berkeley Downstairs, 416-368-3110.

When Richard Greenblatt decided to do a one-man show about Tom Lehrer, he naturally wrote to his subject, full of hope and admiration, requesting the old musical satirist's permission and, if possible, his blessing. He got the first, grudgingly, but not the second. "If all this isnt enough to discourage you, Lehrer wrote, outlining his reservations, let me know, and maybe I can come up with some more."

This correspondence continued over about three years, with neither man changing his tone or his stance. Its incorporated in the finished show and is the most obvious reason for the show's title, Letters from Lehrer. There seems, however, to be a secondary cause. Greenblatt sees Lehrer's songs, including those written in the early 1950s around the time when he himself was busy being born, as a coded message directed at him personally. He doesnt believe this literally, of course, but he cant help thinking of them that way. He first heard them in the '60s when, as the child of a left- wing couple who had quit the Communist party in 1956, he was himself becoming politically aware. The songs spoke to him, and Greenblatt's show is about What Tom Lehrer Means to Him: a blend of recital, commentary and spiritual autobiography.

The mixture doesnt take. Its nice to hear the songs again, at least the bits that you can hear. It doesn't matter that Greenblatt doesn't have a voice. Neither did Lehrer, but he had killer diction and phrasing that nailed every joke. Greenblatt has an even, biteless delivery that makes mush of the lyrics (a muddy amplification system doesnt help) and he seems to be on vocally distant terms with the tunes. He plays better piano than Lehrer, but even that seems counter-productive; his performance of the songs is keyboard-directed rather than audience-directed.

Nor am I sure that the songs can bear the dramatic weight Greenblatt wants to place on them. Lehrer stopped writing and performing 40 years ago, leaving Greenblalt feeling, by his own account, personally abandoned. The songs we have are the products of two separate bursts of creativity: in the 50s, when Lehrer wrote and performed them as a sideline while pursuing a scholarly career in mathematics; and in the early '60s when he resurfaced as in- house minstrel on the TV satire show That Was the Week That Was.

I remember, if I, too, can get autobiographical, the first batch making a great impression at my school when they were new. They were, in those days, unbroadcastable, but we lapped them up via the records and the published songbooks. What attracted us about them was the bloodthirstiness, and the occasional sex. We thrilled to the comparatively mild Hunting Song (the protagonist bags "three game wardens, seven hunters and a cow) and to the more advanced cynicism of My Old Home Town, I Hold Your Hand in Mine, The Wienerschnitzel Waltz and The Old Dope Peddler (he gives the kids free samples/ because he knows full well/that today's young innocent faces/will be tomorrows clientele"), not to mention Lobachevsky, the ultimate paean to cheating on exams.

Greenblatt doesnt sing any of these, and I don't think their absence is solely due to the familiar limits of anthology shows. The songs don't fit his thesis. The ones that he sings from the 50s have a more obvious political edge: The Wild West is Where I Want to Be (about nuclear testing), I Wanna Go Back to Dixie ("I aint seen one good lynchin' in years) and We Will All Go Together When We Go, in which he's the apostle of apocalypse. These certainly place Lehrer at the sharp end of what Greenblatt calls the 50s liberal consensus" but its a stretch to call them protest songs.

Most of them are lethal parodies of various kinds of pop song, something Greenblatt's uninflected delivery tends to obscure. When, in the '60s, Lehrer took on The Folk Song Army, he was irked not just by the folkies self-importance ("it takes a certain amount of courage to get up in a coffeehouse or a college auditorium and come out in favour of the things that everybody else in the audience is against, like peace and justice and brotherhood") but by the fact they wrote terrible songs. Greenblatt does later on make a valid point about the morale-building virtues of preaching to the converted. He might have added that, anyway, the Right does it just as much as the Left.

It was Lehrers superb craftsmanship, rather than the uniqueness of anything he was saying, that set him apart in the '60s since, by this point, satire was not only hip but pop. Nor has the kind of thing he was doing ever gone away; Jon Stewart is doing it now, and to bigger audiences. This makes it difficult to accept Greenblatt's view of Lehrer as the light that failed. Its true for him, obviously, but thats not enough to hang a confessional show on.

The best part of the performance is not Greenblatt on Greenblatt or Greenblatt sings Lehrer but Greenblatt speaks Lehrer. Lehrer's style in prose was sabre-toothed self-deprecation. He used it in his liner notes, and he's evidently still using it in the occasional interview and in those letters. Whenever Greenblatt quotes him, the evening lights up. I especially approved Lehrers dictum that the only worthwhile response to a joke is laughter; applause means merely that the audience agrees with you. (There was a bit of that going on, the night I saw the show.) I must also say that theres one, entirely apolitical, number that Greenblatt puts over excellently, the breathless rattling-off of the periodic table as a Gilbert and Sullivan patter song; I'm in awe of any non-scientist who can even remember it.
