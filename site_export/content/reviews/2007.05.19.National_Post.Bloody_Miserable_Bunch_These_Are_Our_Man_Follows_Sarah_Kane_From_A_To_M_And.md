Sarah Kane hanged herself at the age of 28, having written six plays and suffered a short lifetime of depression. Those facts hang heavy in the mind of anyone entering a theatre to witness one of her famously bleak plays, which provoked both shock and awe in England in the 1990s and are gradually making their way over here. Their dark vision, the implicit argument goes, is drawn from the author's own experience, so it has to be authentic.

It's a kind of blackmail, but I'll give in to it. I'll also agree that the actual language of her plays is remarkable, particularly in the case of Crave, a kind of quartet for voices that's staged here by Nightwood Theatre. But these qualities in themselves don't necessarily make a masterpiece. Crave is hard work. It repays the effort, more in stabbing moments than as a whole, but the difficulty isn't a recommendation in itself.

The four people in Crave are identified simply as A, B, C and M. In Jennifer Tarver's production they stand in a series of adjacent alcoves, created by the bends of the folding screen that constitutes Teresa Przybylski's set, lit with cruel virtuosity by Kimberly Purtell. They deliver alternating monologues, many of them composed of a single line. There are hints of plot lines, in which the monologues sound like splinters of dialogue; one speaker seems to be answering what another has said, though frequently the call and the response will be separated by a remark from somebody else altogether.

The speakers are, to an extent, characters. A briskly introduces himself by saying "I'm not a rapist I'm a pedophile." He also, disconcertingly, has the play's tenderest lines about love and longing, many of them assembled into the play's one extended and extremely eloquent speech, composed as a single run-on sentence and delivered as such, or at least as far as human breath permits, by Hardee Lineham. His performance is altogether a memorable blend of seediness and torment, a sharp physical and mental outline, sadly set off by a bulging belly. He talks at one point of "this woman with the desolate eyes for whom I would die." This could be C, who seems as a child to have been abused by her father or grandfather or both, and will carry the scars to what will undoubtedly be an early death. It's impossible not to see her as figuring Kane herself, but it's also a tribute to the writing that she's no self-indulgent mouthpiece. As played by Michelle Monteith, who can do amazing things with the word "bereaved," she swings between soulful and spiteful.

B -- the sketchiest figure, though Carlos Gonzalez-Vio plays him well enough--is a self-consciously tough young man, locked in an approach-and-avoidance scenario with M, a self-possessed sophisticated lady whose biological clock is beginning to sound alarms. (B: "I need to be seduced by an older woman." M: "I'm not an older woman." B: "Older than me, not older per se.") M is played with unfaultable style by Maria Ricossa. Her alphabetical distance from the other characters suggests that she may exist on a different plane. Perhaps the play is an elaborate therapy session and she's the shrink. Or perhaps the author was just being a tease. But when C cries out, over and over (and over and over) "what have they done to me?" it's M who sharply tells her to "grow up and stop blaming mother." C sees M as her neglectful mother anyway (maybe that's what's the initial's about) but M vigorously denies it. It's some ingenious cat's cradle that we have here.

The text is studded with black aphorisms. "The outside world," says A, "is vastly overrated." "If I die here," proclaims C, a little less resonantly, "I was murdered by daytime television." The latter, though, may be a comment less on the world at large than on the kind of institution in which Kane herself ended her life. It's surely the author herself whom we hear through the confession "I am an emotional plagiarist, stealing other people's pain." That's almost a writer's job description, disturbed or otherwise.

As for what the characters are craving: It has to be that old thing love, sought as always in impossible places. The climax must be when the four entreat "LEAVE. COME BACK. STAY" in unison and in capital letters.

"Don't forget," C admonishes us, "poetry is language used for its own sake." I don't know if that's a recommendation or a warning, but it's tempting to refer her back to her author, with the observation that dramatic poetry is language used for drama's sake. It doesn't even have to be verbal, and in fact Tarver, a director who excels at making the abstract concrete, does the visual thing superbly here.

I am three-quarters convinced. If the production is revived, I shall go again.
