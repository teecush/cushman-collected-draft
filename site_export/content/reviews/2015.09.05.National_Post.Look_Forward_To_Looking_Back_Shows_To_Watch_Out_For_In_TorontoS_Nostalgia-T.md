Summer bows out, the festivals wind down, and the Toronto theatres gear up for a new season. From what's been announced, it looks like being a nostalgic one. It's full of plays we've seen before, sometimes in productions we've seen before. That isn't necessarily a bad thing. enjoyable shows are there to be enjoyed, by as many people as want to see them.

Factory theatre The most throughgoing retrospective is at the Factory Theatre, which is offering a whole season of Canadian classics: a description, rather promiscuously employed, that seems to mean plays by Canadian writers that have already been done by someone else. The season's umbrella title is Naked, and it has a subtitle as well, of machine-gun briskness: "Text. Audience. Imaginations. The Possibilities Are endless." What this seems to mean is that these will be barebones productions, with no horrible decorations to get in the way. my own imagination runs to the possibility that this might be an economic necessity as much as an artistic choice, but that's probably unkind. "Naked" says Peter Hinton, who directs one of the plays, "is the invitation to engage the imagination fully."

Hinton is certainly a director who knows about engaging the imagination, as well as having plenty of his own, though lately he's been exercising it on a far larger scenic scale at Shaw Festival. The play he's staging at the Factory is bombay black, by Anosh Irani, previously seen at Theatre Centre in 2006. Set in an Indian dance hall, it concerns an exotic dancer, which doesn't mean there what it does here; her managing mother, who really is her manager; and a bookseller from their village who comes to see the dancer dance, despite being blind. I remember it as having first-rate dialogue but woolly construction. I look forward to seeing what Hinton, one of our very best, will make of it. (Factory mainspace: Nov. 17-dec. 6).

tarragon The Tarragon has an impressive lineup of new Canadian work, including plays by Gord rand (better known as an actor), Kit Sandler, and - someone we haven't heard from for a long time - Fabrizio Filippo. It starts however, with a couple of re-treads. One is a show previously seen at Harbourfront: blind date, in which clown-improviser rebecca Northan interacts each night with a different member of the audience, who presumably doesn't get paid even though he ends up doing a lot of the work.

The other is a re-tread of a re-tread. The Tarragon's most exciting production last season was An enemy of the People, a version of the Ibsen classic about a Norwegian doctor who alienates his community by pointing out that its water supply is contaminated. Since the town in question is a spa, this poses serious moral and economic questions. richard rose, Tarragon's artistic director, saw a modern-dress production that greatly impressed him at the berlin Schaubuhne theatre, and re-created it, in english, on his own stage. The play is now billed as "created by Henrik Ibsen," in tactful acknowledgement of the departures from the original. The crucial public meeting scene, for example, is revamped to include audience participation. The new staging will see a further departure: the protagonist, dr. Stockmann, is to be played as a woman, by the admirable Laura Condolln. For the trivia-minded: also new to the cast is the redoubtable david Fox who once played dr. Stockmann at Stratford (as a man). (Tarragon mainspace: Oct. 7-Nov. 1).

Soulpepper Soulpepper has a full slate of past hits, starting with The Play's the Thing, a stylish delight from their second season and one of their formative productions, and proliferating over the holiday season for a "family festival" so extensive that it will spill over from the company's own distillery stages onto that of the bluma Appel theatre in the St. Lawrence Centre. meanwhile, back at the ranch, a rarity: Peter Weiss's The Persecution and Assassination of Jean-Paul marat as Performed by the Inmates of the Asylum under the direction of the marquis de Sade, more conveniently known as the marat/Sade. This play, one of the most celebrated of the 1960s, has been little seen since then, at least in english, probably because Peter brook's London staging (subsequently seen in New york and then filmed) has been deemed an impossible act to follow. Albert Schultz, who follows it, has two Soulpepper regulars ideally cast in the title roles: Stuart Hughes as the carbuncled revolutionary marat, diego matamoros as the disillusioned philosopher Sade. And it has a new score - the play is virtually a musical - by the company's own mike ross. (young Centre, opening Sept. 14).

coal Mine Still, my choice for the season's most promising lineup is the one at the Coal mine, a basement space under a restaurant on the danforth, where some of our best actors have been doing some of their best work for what I take to be a pittance. They'll be doing four plays, and I feel compelled to list them all. First is The river, written by the british playwright Jez butterworth whose Jerusalem is one of the most justly acclaimed plays of recent years; it's directed by Ted dykstra, the Coal mine's "artistic curator." (Nov. 1-21). Then there's the promise of something Toronto rarely sees: high-level wintertime Shakespeare. Graham Abbey, Stratford luminary, makes his directorial debut with, fittingly, The Winter's Tale with a cast of other Stratford luminaries. (Jan. 28-Feb. 20). Then there's Killer Joe, by Tracy Letts, author of the American hit August: Osage County, which was one of the most unjustly acclaimed plays of recent years, but one shouldn't be prejudiced. (April 3-24). And finally - an actual new Canadian play: Instructions (To Any Future Socialist Government Proposing to Abolish Christmas) by michael mackenzie. I've no idea what it will be like but I love the title. (may 29-June 19).
