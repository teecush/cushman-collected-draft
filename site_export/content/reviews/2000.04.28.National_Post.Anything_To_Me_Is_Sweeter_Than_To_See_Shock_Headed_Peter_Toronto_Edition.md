SHOCK HEADED PETER

Elgin Theatre, Toronto

The set for this British contribution to World Stage looks like a Pollocks' Toy Theatre -- one of the miniature playhouses, with plush curtains, three sets of wings, a balcony and two flanking doors, that middle-class English children used to be given for Christmas (perhaps still are given, in some enclaves of tradition) as a reward for being good. Its dimensions make one expect a pupet show; and, what with Ronnie Burkett and The Lion King, we have been having a basketful of puppets.

There are, it turns out, some dolls involved in the proceedings, but most of the work is done by grown-up, more or less human performers. They have to adapt themselves to their confined circumstances, rather like the actors in the between-floors scenes in Being John Malkovich. The show is not that witty, but it carries on as if it thinks it is.

The production is based on Struwwelpeter, a fringe-classic volume of poems and drawings first published in 1844 and the work of a Victorian doctor named Heinrich Hoffmann. Searching for a book to give his own children, he found only thunderously moralizing versified sermons, and so set out to write his own parody of the genre -- a collection of cautionary horror stories involving boys and girls who were not good and who came to deservedly ghastly ends. Struwwelpeter himself, a.k.a. Shock Headed Peter, refused to cut either his hair or his nails, and so turned into a monster of tangles and talons. He certainly gave me bad dreams when I first encountered him, though they were probably inspired by the illustrations rather than the text.

Hoffmann's verses ("Any thing to me is sweeter/Than to see Shock- headed Peter") are not especially smooth or inventive; actually they're rather poor stuff to have come from the century that gave us Lewis Carroll (whose lampoons of moral examples for kids are far more suave and lethal) and Edward Lear. But he paved the way for other, more accomplished, sick comics: for Harry Graham, with his Ruthless Rhymes for Heartless Homes, and especially for Hilaire Belloc, whose Cautionary Tales include the hilarious fable of Jim, who ran away from his nanny while visiting London Zoo and was eaten by a lion .The reaction of Jim's elders is very Hoffmanesque: "When nurse informed his parents they/Were more concerned than I can say./ His mother as she dried her eyes/Said, 'Well, it gives me no surprise ...' "

The current show, directed by Phelim McDermott and Julian Crouch, builds up the matter of Peter into a study of his concerned parents. Having waited long for a child, they are delighted when the stork delivers one (I do not speak in metaphor) and appalled when their son proves so spectacularly unconcerned about his personal hygiene. They push him under the floorboards, from which he eventually bursts forth, though not before his mother and father have taken on some of his most alarming phsyical characteristics.

In the intervals of this anecdote's slow unfolding, we hear other tales of Hoffman, concerning such delinquents as Harriet who played with matches, Conrad who sucked his thumb and Fidgety Philip who could not be discouraged from handling the cutlery at table.

They are told through music, provided by the Tiger Lilies -- a piercing lineup of accordion, percussion and double bass. They play a kind of art pop, which is sometimes appropriately haunting (notably in the song of Fidgety Phil) and sometimes just screechy.

Every story goes the same way: Child misbehaves, child perishes horribly, parents take it in their stride -- so essentially we are hearing one joke again and again and again.

A certain suspense is provided by Martyn Jacques, the white- faced leader of the band, who sings in an impressively sustained falsetto that makes the words extremely hard to grasp. He also wrote the lyrics, which are "adapted" from Hoffmann and not, as far as I could tell, adapted for the better. The show is described as a junk opera. They got it half right.

I hated it, but every so often it got under my guard. The staging is very assured, with many monstrous props festively deployed. Julian Bleach, one of the show's five credited creators, also presides over it as an actor-manager in an advanced state of rundown. He enters through one door, informs us in sepulchral tones that he is the "the greatest actor in the history of the world," gives up, leaves the stage and re-enters through the other door with no better success. He goes on to play many parts, some of them human, with a battery of snarls and grimaces that are certainly energetic but also seem endearingly half-hearted. When he's really disarming, though, is when he abandons all attempts at the grand manner, drops into the vernacular and treats us to the comedy of anticlimax.

Finally, even this proves insufficient and he explodes -- at us. "All right, Toronto" he yells, "I'll have you know I was trained in London." (I wonder what he said in London.) "This is absolutely meaningless to you, isn't it?" he grumbles, conveying equal disgust with himself and us.

It isn't quite meaningless; it seems to be a satire on 19th- century theatre and parenthood. But as both are caricatured from the outset (it's always open season on the Victorians), it doesn't mean anything. Even worse is the show's attitude to its own source; it tries to parody material that was parody to begin with, and that could only be funny if it were delivered fast and economically, with a straight face. It's comedy at the expense of comedy -- a theatrical definition of futility. The first-night audience seemed genuinely to love it, so by all means go and make up your own minds. And always keep ahold of nurse for fear of finding something worse.
