Beyond a doubt, John Patrick Shanley's most famous play is Doubt. But this American favourite has written many others, and in recent seasons Torontos independent theatre companies have fashioned a cottage industry of bringing us Shanley plays. That ante has now been upped. Three indie groups - Flip Jaw Productions, the Storefront Theatre and Side-Mart Theatrical Grocery - have joined forces to bring us, not just the Canadian but the world premieres, of no less than six Shanley plays. Or rather playlets, bundled together on a single evening, under the umbrella title A Woman Is a Secret.

Whether they are cumulatively worth doing is another matter. The first episode shows us a young man and his girlfriend at a restaurant, the two of them interacting with an aggressively French waitress who keeps bringing to the table things that havent been ordered. His date, meanwhile, is disturbed by his checking out the waitress's physical attributes, a charge he denies. It ends with the two women more or less ganging up on the man, and with the suggestion that the whole thing may be a dream, or perhaps a time warp. It's hard, despite some amusingly annoyed lines, to care.

As a trio, that first scene turns out to be, perhaps cleverly, misleading. Those that follow are all duets. Certain other features, though, stay constant. All the scenes feature a table, in one position or another. All are sexual, or pre-sexual, jousts. All involve the participants distrust or disbelief. Most hint at some degree of fantasy.

One is labelled Tennessee, which may indicate not just locale but a tongue-in-cheek homage to the playwright of that name. The two people here could certainly join the Williams line of acid-tongued no-hopers. The male is a perpetual adolescent who would rather be anywhere else; the female is a small-town psychic whose services he craves and who already knows more about him than is comfortable. The two of them are well drawled by Jade Hassoune and Katie Swift.

Tiny Tragedy is about a couple of insects, a spider and a moth. Or maybe thats just a metaphor, and what we have is an encounter between a sadistic time-biding man and a self-destructive woman: more shades of Tennessee. Trent Pardy and Holly Flood play them with appropriately languorous intensity.

In Poland (that's another title, though the scene isn't set there), an eastern European charmer attracts and teases an American multimillionaire who wants her to help him solve a jigsaw puzzle. She reads a lot into that: practically psychoanalyses him on the strength of it. The performances of Karen Knox and Jonathan Higgins are, again, flavourful.

All these mini-dramas involve the characters making pseudo-profound statements about life and love. Shanley, like many semi-serious American playwrights, can more or less get away with this, if his actors can; and these players - a mix of veterans, neophytes, and in-betweens - are nearly all up to the job.

Under Andrew Shavers direction, they find the right tone and rhythm for every scene. The only ones who dont are Anna Hardwick and Anand Rajaram, who play a newly and unhappily married woman and her reappearing old flame. Instead of gliding stylishly along the text, they plumb it for depths that aren't there; most of the scenes are a bit too long, this one is interminable. Redemption is at hand, though, from the two heaviest hitters in the lineup, cunningly kept in reserve until the end.

All the plays seem to say is that relationships are plagued by misunderstandings and that, contrary to the overall title, these are not the province of one gender. If a woman had written them and called them A Man Is a Secret, you wouldn't know the difference.

In Soulpeppers Subway Stations of the Cross, Ins Choi gives us a Toronto homeless person as intermittent preacher. He is hypnotic. His words arent always immediately clear; he seems, when he first shuffles into view, to be talking about eclairs. Actually he is, chocolate ones, but the word is there for rhyming rather culinary effect. Rhymes, rapid and clenched and inexhaustible, are his medium, and they are far more exact and exhilarating than those of most hiphoppers; Choi actually understand accents and syllables.

And they do sometimes connect, as advertised, with those of biblical prophets, Old Testament rather than New. He's very good when he's talking about Jerusalem. Periodically, theres a train rumble, and the lights go down, suggesting that our preacher has had to move on to another station. These various, and scattered, elements, though, hardly unify the piece or justify its title. Its impressive but fragmentary.

A Woman Is a Secret runs through April 5 at the Theatre Centre; Subway Stations of the Cross through April 4 at the Young Centre.
