THEATRE REVIEW

Buddies in Bad Times, Toronto

Bruce Dow, an actor whose spiritual stature grows by cubits, comes before the cur-tain - yes, there really is a curtain - and tells us that he believes in light. He goes on to name some other things he likes; cellphones are on the list, and DVDs very closely followed by porn and brutality. Finally, and lingeringly, he tells us that I believe in death.

Thus the opening of PIG, a British play by Tim Luscombe being given its world premiere at Buddies in Bad Times, apparently because it was deemed too hot to handle on its home turf. Dow appears as four separate but similar characters, as you may judge from their names which are Harry, Barry, Larry and Garry. I'm not sure which he is when hes speaking the prologue, but all four are various degrees of voyeur, with a particular taste for sado-masochism. There are two other actors, Paul Dunn and Blair Williams, each of

whom also plays a quartet of characters united by sexual role. We watch them, still be-fore the curtain, engaging in an online pick-up; Williams is the top and Dunn the bottom.

The curtains part and there are the same two actors, now apparently in a live-in relationship. We find out later that theyre married, though "later" in this play is a loaded word, as the writing juggles time frames, and also levels of reality. Stevie, the Williams half of the menage, is a writer struggling to repeat his initial success; Joe, his partner, has literary aspirations himself that are becoming, from Stevie's jealous point of view, embarrassingly close to being fulfilled. Stevie is writ-ing, or has written, a play mak-ing use of their relationship. It's unclear just how many layers of illusion are involved here, but Stevie has an alter ego called Knife, who takes his sadism seriously and professionally, and another whom the pro-gram lists as "Knife." Joe is similarly billed as Pig, both in and out of inverted commas, not to mention getting into the plays title in capital letters. I mean Luscombes play's title, but I could probably just as easily mean Stevie's.

The structural secret is re-vealed with exemplary deft-ness; the play turns out to have a false bottom. (Pun unintended but apposite.) Unfortunate-ly, it also turns out to have any number of false endings. The need to wind up the action in all its manifestations extends the second act unmercifully. This is a shame because what has gone before is often sharp, occasionally poignant and sometimes very funny, in a dark way.

Dark may be an understatement. Gay sex and S&M are presented as synonymous, which seems simplistic from either direction, and the cruelty here, though more discussed than demonstrated, is extreme. It takes in gang-rape and goes beyond to forcible - or, per-haps more disturbingly, willing - infection with AIDS. This is where the rhyming-with-Harry characters come in, or some of them; they want to see and inflict the worst, and are willing to pay good money for it. Dow plays them brilliantly and chillingly, though his best moments are as a comparatively benign figure: a jealous ex-lover of Joes who offers a hilariously barbed toast to the happy couple, and delivers an equally witty lament for the days when gay meant furtive. This is tradition-

al camp at a high level, from an author whose bio doesnt quite jibe with the bad-boy image of his play. (As a director he seems to have specialized in Coward, Rattigan and Ayckbourn, and as a playwright in adaptations of Jane Austen.) Dunn is equally fine as the variously Piggy permutations of Joe, which encompass shy, frightened and professionally aggrieved when requested to inflict pain rather than suffer it. He also at one point con-fesses to a coke addiction; it's rather charming to discover that he's referring to cola.

Williams, an actor whose natural mood is civilized, doesnt always convey the ex-tremes that lie beneath Stevie the Knifes veneer; when those extremes break through, how-ever, he's excellent.

All three actors help keep confusion somewhat at bay by switching flawlessly between accents and, this being Eng-land, classes. Brendan Healy's high-gloss direction is, again, superb; he injects style even into the cast's statutory re-arranging of the furniture.

PIG runs until Oct. 6 at Buddies in Bad Times Theatre. For tickets and information, visit buddiesinbadtimes.com.
