The Shaw Festival's Cabaret proves, again, that there will never be a production of this musical as good as the first one. History - including theatrical history - is against it.

That original 1966 staging, directed by Harold Prince, was a seduction. It lured you, titillated you, amused you with its evocation of the louche nightclubs of Weimar Republic Berlin. You knew, intellectually, that Nazism was on the horizon but for much of the evening it did no more than hover; it didnt frighten. Then two things happened. At the engagement party of Herr Schultz, the Jewish greengrocer, and Fraulein Schneider, his Christian landlady, a stone was thrown through the window: a minor act of violence but one that, coming apparently out of nowhere, sent shivers. Sometime later, we got to the gorilla number, the one in which the nightclubs master of ceremonies waltzes serenely with his simian lady-love and ends by sweetly crooning "if you could see her through my eyes - she wouldn't look Jewish at all.

The show wasn't actually accusing 1930 clubland Berlin of being anti-Semitic; it was depicting some apparently apolitical entertainers having a deliciously dangerous topical giggle. Reflecting the tenor of the times, they became complicit in it; and we, with the smiles wiped off our faces, realized what we had been getting into. That was shocking: one of the few really shocking theatrical moments Ive known. At the time, in fact, it was thought too shocking; early in the New York run, the songs last line was replaced with something meaninglessly anodyne; and the same thing happened in London (where I was lucky enough to see it on the second night, before the sting had been withdrawn). Nowadays, of course, we've got way beyond that; and besides, thanks largely to the film version, everyone knows the punchline anyway. So, given that the intention of the show is to shock, later productions have had to find ways of upping the ante.

The favoured method is to immerse us in the supposed decadence of the cabaret itself. This approach, with heavy emphasis on a leeringly androgynous MC and backup, has been institutionalized by the 1990s revival directed by Sam Mendes and Rob Marshall: a version so successful that it's just been revived itself in New York and, presumably for contractual reasons, is credited in the Shaw program as the original production. (The Prince production, a triumph of stagecraft, seems to have been written out of history.) The Mendes-Marshall treatment is so in its audiences faces from its opening number onward that it has nowhere to go. Peter Hintons Shaw production has loftier ambitions and some interesting ideas, but it's even more muddled and, despite some terrific performances, lacks even its predecessor's showbiz smarts.

It has a new beginning. Before we have heard so much as a wilkommen," we see our observer-hero Clifford Bradshaw sitting at his typewriter thinking up the title-speech of the John Van Druten play I Am a Camera, on which the musical is based, and which is itself based on the Berlin stories of Christopher Isherwood for whom Clifford is an Americanized substitute. Maybe, to adapt Fred Ebbs gorilla lyric, we are to see the show through his eyes. We would certainly like to, given the exceptional subtlety and authority of Gray Powells performance (his singing isn't great, but he doesn't have to do much of it); and indeed the notion is embedded in the sourcematerial and at least implicit in Joe Masteroff s Broadway script. But the production doesnt go through with this. Instead it complicates matters by having the MC mimic Clifford's writing position, and at the end having one of them ventriloquize the other. It's a notion that hovers on the verge of being intriguing without ever getting there. Are the two meant to be doubles? Isnt one narrator enough? To make it worse, Juan Chiorans costume as the MC makes him look both pointy-headed and pointy-bodied, and his performance, in a traditionally show-stealing role, never manages to break free of it.

It isn't the only visual constriction. The Prince production rigorously distinguished between the commentative cabaret numbers and the reallife story, only merging the two at the end when devil-maycare Sally Bowles returned to the Kit Kat Klub to, in effect, comment on herself. Later versions have made the club a framework for everything, unsubtly asserting that life is indeed a cabaret (old chum). Michael Gianfrancesco's set suggests that its more of a treadmill; the space is almost entirely taken up by an assemblage of huge revolving staircases. They can accommodate a lot of people, and occasionally they make a point; while the Money song is performed downstage, we see Clifford earning some deutschmarks of his own in his brief unwitting career as a Nazi courier; while Deborah Hay rapturously sings Sallys Maybe This Time we can also watch Herr Schultz and Fraulein Schneider enjoying their new brief happiness. The set, though, is hardly a choreographer's dream, and Denise Clarke (of the Calgary performance group One Yellow Rabbit) is hardly this show's dream choreographer. Mostly she confines herself to pointless echoing; when the Schultz-Schneider duo sing their Pineapple song, everyone on the stairs eats a pineapple; when the same couple sing about being Married, the corps divide up into their own nuptial pairings, all of them, it seems, same-sex.

Meanwhile, back in the 1930s, Benedict Campbell is a first-rate Herr Schultz, chipper and troubled. As his intended, Corinne Koslos acting is similarly rooted, though her singing (and she has the best songs, in pastiche Brecht-Weill style) may be too consciously ominous, summoning the period rather than the particular woman. Hays Sally is the best I have seen since Judi Dench, both in the tremulous sophistication of her book scenes, and her handling of the Kander and Ebb score. Her title number is a spine-tingling combination of false bravado (the character's) and true bravura (her own).

There's fine supporting work, too, from Jenny L. Wright as an incipient Nazi and Jay Turvey as a chillingly real one. Less happy is the productions attempt to bestow party membership on Max (Lorne Kennedy), the clubowner, and to outfit his entire staff with swastika armbands. In reality, the club would have been one of the first places the Nazis shut down. The stone through the window still chills as much as ever, but the proliferating sinister images of this productions final moments are self-defeating. We know already.

Correction: In Thursday's review of The Charity That Began at Home, I attributed the costumes to Judith Bowden. They were in fact designed by William Schmuck.
