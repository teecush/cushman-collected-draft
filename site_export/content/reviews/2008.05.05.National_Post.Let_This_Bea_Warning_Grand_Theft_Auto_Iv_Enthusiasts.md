Edward Roy's latest cautionary tale for teenagers centres on a violent death. It begins with a vivid enactment of a video game whose name is the same as that of the play, Dead Ahead. Three whirling male contestants vie for gangland supremacy in Mega City. Game over, the trio of 17-year olds who've been battling it out are left to cope with their own lives, which are more humdrum but still potentially explosive. All three are the problem kids of problem families.

Andrew, the smartest, has controlling parents who've placed him in a private school away from his friends. Tiga, who's very needy, has a father who's dating a woman -- Tiga calls her "the skank" -- much younger than his mom. Cory, who has bullying tendencies and in whose basement the game is played, has lost his mother; ever since her death, his dad has turned drunk and -- especially towards Cory -- violent. He's also threatening to relocate his son and himself to Vancouver. By the end of the play, all three boys have witnessed or participated in a real-life killing. Will they cover it up or will they go to the police? Their friendship fractures under the strains of guilt and mutual suspicion. These are intensified by the presence of Megan, Andrew's girlfriend and part-time conscience. The other two resent her, especially Tiga, whom she riles up at their first meeting by pretending to believe he's gay.

The author is his own director, and his work in both capacities is tense, sensible and convincing. It avoids the platitudes of both the old and newer schools of youth theatre; there is little moralizing and less self-conscious toughness. There are some instructive peeks into the younger generation's store of general knowledge; they've heard of Hitler (he's mythology) but not of Stalin (he's only history). As to whether the four young people -- the only characters we see -- should or shouldn't snitch, the question is left open, though there's little doubt of the direction in which the play itself is tilting. (It may not match up with the audience's instinctive sympathies.)

The problem, when you consider it, is not unlike the one famously raised by E.M. Forster as to the comparative merits of betraying one's country or one's friend, though the question here is rather more immediate; it involves self-preservation as against self-sacrifice. And if you're looking for further hallowed comparisons, the sickening difference between mayhem on a video screen and in real life was anticipated nearly a century ago in the Irish classic The Playboy of the Western World, whose heroine came to recognize the "great gap between a gallous story and a dirty deed." Megan comes to the same conclusion, and much faster, though she may not express it as colourfully.

That said, the video game metaphor is the play's weak point, whether you take it as just a metaphor or as a possible case of cause and effect. There's no evidence that these kids, however they may horse around, really see themselves as candidates for gangland supremacy or that they're either the victims or the exponents of some culture of violence. They stumble into violence in real life, they're appalled by it when it happens and even, judging by their conversation, before it happens. The rest is teenage braggadocio and peer-pressure, of a kind that long predates the computer, in a pervasive broken-home environment that is distinctively contemporary but is also a different problem, or set of problems, altogether. All Dead Ahead the game does for Dead Ahead the play is get it off to an eye-catching start.

The acting is excellent, if occasionally forced, the actors being young but not that young. If there's a standout, it's Ben Lewis, who as Andrew has the most nuanced role, but Eli Ham (Cory) and Ashton Doudelet (Tiga) are equally involved and involving, while Sabryn Rock's Megan has the rhythms and attitudes of 21stcentury youth down to perfection. I saw the play with a house of seventh and eighth graders, and they never talked, coughed or snickered. - Dead Ahead runs until May 16. Call 416-862-2222 for more information.
