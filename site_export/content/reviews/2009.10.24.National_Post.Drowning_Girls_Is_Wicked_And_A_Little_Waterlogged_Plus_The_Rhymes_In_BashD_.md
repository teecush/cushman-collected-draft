Two studio-sized shows, all scoring higher on presentation than on content.

The Drowning Girls started in 1991 at the Edmonton Fringe, and the play has been in heavy rotation ever since, picking up several awards on its travels. It's the story of an English serial-murder case of the 19th century. Three women in succession were found dead in their respective baths shortly after getting married. Each, after a prolonged period of singleness, had wed the same man, one George Joseph Smith who, using a different name on each occasion, had drowned them, pocketing the modest sums of money they had brought with them and the larger sums for which he had had them insured. He got away with passing their deaths off as accidental until the mother of one victim read a newspaper account of the death of another. Smith was tried, hanged and attained nationwide notoriety as "the Brides in the Bath Murderer."

The brides and their baths supply Charlie Tomlinson's production with its central image, strikingly realized. Bretta Gerecke, responsible for the resourceful kitchen-and-dining-room set of True Love Lies, now gives us an epic bathroom: three full-length tubs, resplendent on a black floor, each with a functioning shower and filled with what one hopes is warm water. The design proves to be a constant source of arresting effects. The three murderees arise in succession and commiserate with one another. They then proceed to act out their stories, which of course have much in common.

Tomlinson wrote the script in collaboration with Beth Graham and Daniela Vlaskalic who also make up the cast along with Natascha Girgis. The writing's at its best in its earlier, more abstract phases. When the narrative takes over, it becomes repetitive. The first story takes forever to tell, while one realizes, with an appropriately sinking feeling, that there are still two more to go. We are given a thumbnail biography of Smith himself; it seems he was abused before becoming an abuser, but that's hardly revelatory. Vlaskalic has some poignant, trusting moments as his first prey; Girgis is very sharp and economical as his last -- she seems too smart and mature to succumb but her very loneliness betrays her. Girgis is also the best at supplying instant cameos, including a delightful one of a local gossip. But it's hard to see the play's purpose beyond dispensing historical information. It's apparent that it's trying to go beyond mere victimology; at the end, the three rise from their onstage watery graves to pronounce Smith guilty. This seems superfluous, as a judge and jury have already reached the same conclusion and made it part of Victorian mythology. Last time I checked, the Brides in the Bath Murderer, in waxwork form, was still on show in the Chamber of Horrors at Madame Tussaud's.

Then there are the heroes of Bash'd, billed as a gay rap opera. In this twohander (another import from Edmonton, this time via off-Broadway), Chris Craddock and Nathan Cuckow play TBag and Feminem, hip-hoppers with angels' wings. They tell of Jack and Dillon, a Toronto couple whose happiness was destroyed when Jack was all but killed by gay-bashers and Dillon tried to avenge him. Jack's the urban sophisticate raised by a gay couple, Dillon's the small-town boy whose dad is a redneck (but eventually sees the light). It is, avowedly, a Romeo and Juliet story; the parallels are especially acute when the lovers first meet at a club, and when Dillon falls into tragedy when trying to defend Jack. My companion, who knows far more about rap than I do, thought the beats monotonous, but most of it worked for me. Rhyme, whether in rap or Racine, is a treacherous medium for drama, but it can have clinching force when properly handled; the rhymes in this show only get sticky when the show gets preachy. It tells us, too often, that it's reclaiming a traditionally homophobic form, when it's best at just getting on and doing it. As performers, Craddock and Cuckow are impressively energetic, hurling themselves around vocally and physically without missing, you guessed it, a beat.
