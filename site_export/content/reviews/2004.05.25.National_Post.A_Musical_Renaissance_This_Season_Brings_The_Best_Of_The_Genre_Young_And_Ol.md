This theatre season in Toronto has not been notable for very much. But it has managed to do one unexpected thing. It has born witness to a renaissance of the musical, a form that, for as long as any of us can remember, has had a glorious past but has only intermittently had much of a present. It has a present at present; signs are that it may even have a future.

By "the musical" I mean the American musical, whose fortunes may not, strictly speaking, be Canada's problem. But they send us the shows anyway so it's in our interest that they be good. The season has brought four new, or newish, American shows, and between them they cover the waterfront of available styles. They also connect with some of the best of the older shows, which, as it happens, are on abundant display at this years classic festivals. This week sees the opening of Urinetown, which, if reports are to believed, revives the tradition of the satiric or microcosmic musical in which a town, usually rotten, stands in for a whole country, or maybe for Western civilization in its entirety. As John Kander, composer of Chicago, has delightedly observed, "corruption never goes out of fashion."

Neither does simple gaiety; or even when its unfashionable, it's popular. The Producers puts us back in touch with the tradition of burlesque, using that term in both its senses: of broad lampoon and of even broader sex-farce. It's what Cole Porters musicals were like, in between the sophisticated songs (and sometimes even during them; Porter, outside the ballads that made it on to the Ella Fitzgerald songbooks, had an unbridled taste for double-entendre). Its a gals-and-gags show, spiced up by the act that some of the gags happen to be about Nazis. It's also a reminder, such as we haven't had in years, of the power of positive choreography.

So, in its way, is Hairspray, which is the first decent contribution in years (actually the first ever, apart from Little Shop of Horrors) to the rock n roll movie-exploitation genre. That may not seem like much of an achievement until you consider the cut- price road-company horrors that have been loosed upon the world in recent years: Saturday Night Fever, Footloose, even Grease, which was a decent stage musical before being revived as a cheapo copy of its own film version (i.e. an adaptation of an adaptation). Instead of their imitation high spirits, Hairspray offers the real thing: real humour, real professionalism, real energy. It nearly has a real story.

The Producers and Hairspray are Broadway products to the bone. Their size is part of their appeal. Urinetown, though rapidly transplanted, started off-Broadway, which is probably its spiritual home) and it's playing here at the Bluma Appel, rather than the Royal Alex or the Princess of Wales.

The Last 5 Years, which completes this season's quartet, will always be a small-house show, and not just because it only has two characters. Unlike its companions, it is not overtly nostalgic, but it too is part of a tradition, that of the intimate psychologically- driven musical. Even in its use of contemporary rhythms, it is doing for its own time what Gershwin and Sondheim, among many others, did for theirs. The same claim has of course been made for a long line of would-be theatrical rockers, but they didnt have theatrical flair or theatrical training. Jason Robert Brown has both.

Brown wrote other shows before The Last 5 Years, but he certainly counts as new and, at 32, as young. That is one of the cheering things about this batch of shows; theyre all the work of new writers, new at least to the musical theatre. All right, nobody expects Mel Brooks to follow The Producers with a whole stream of new musicals (age apart, how many scores can you write as pastiche?) but the others look good for shows to come. Brown, in particular, is routinely grouped with Adam Guettel, Michael John LaChiusa, Andrew Lippa and others, as adventurous composers whose new musicals are getting on. They are not, for the most part, getting on Broadway (and when they have, they haven't lasted) but that worries me less than it probably worries them and their agents. The American musical, for reasons of finance and demographics, can never again be simply the Broadway musical.

What counts is that it should maintain the best of Broadway craftsmanship. This -- after all the years of Anglo musicals, Anglo- French musicals, Anglo-Scandinavian musicals (Mamma Mia!), even American musicals trying to be Anglo musicals (Jekyll and Hyde) -- seemed to be falling into disrepair. You notice it in the music; you notice it even more in the words. (A couple of seasons ago, in a real gesture of despair, the Tony went to Contact, a ballet posing as a musical.) Certainly the old hands noticed. The above quote from John Kander is taken from Colored Lights (Faber & Faber, $34.50), an instructive and entertaining book of conversations between Kander, his inseparable lyricist-collaborator Fred Ebb, and an interviewer, Greg Lawrence.

Ebb: "I don't think many people writing today even know hasically what constitutes a rhyme."

Kander: Many contemporary lyrics dont rhyme not because they are intentionally not rhyming but because the people writing the lyrics dont know the difference.

This isn't just pedantry. A sharp rhyme makes a sharp point, and helps to create sharp stories about sharply-observed people. Dull rhymes mean dullness of effect, besides being a form of aural torture. Crystallizing meaning is something that songs, and hence musicals, are uniquely equipped to do. And it seems -- miraculously - - that knowledge lives on. It may have skipped a generation, but it's back. The lyrics in Hairspray may be fallible in this regard but frankly, if Hairspray were as bad as things got, wed be living in a golden age.

After seeing Hairspray Ebb, less charitably, "wanted to write and tell [its authors] to listen to Frank Loesser, which would be good advice for anybody." It would, though my own guess is that Marc Shaiman and Scott Wittman, the writers in question, have already taken it. We all have the chance to take it, as spring gives way to summer and the Stratford Festival revives Guys and Dolls, the greatest of Loessers five Broadway musicals. Of all the old masters he may have the most to say to us now, because he came to the theatre by a roundabout route. Before and during the Second World War he wrote lyrics -- and later, music -- for musical movies. He brought a blue-collar sensibility into popular song. In every respect -- except wit, accomplishment and the fact they were both composer lyricists, he was the opposite of Cole Porter, whose Anything Goes is also on the Stratford bill this year. The Complete Lyrics of Frank Loesser (Knopf, $75.00) -- the latest in a handsome and invaluable series that has previously covered Porter, Lorenz Hart, Ira Gershwin and Irving Berlin -- allows an overview of his career. It's no news that in Adelaide's Lament, the confessions of a love-lorn, bronchially challenged showgirl, Guys and Dolls contains the greatest of comedy lyrics:

You can spray her wherever you figure the streptococci lurk

You can giver her a shot for whatever shes got but it just wont work

[Kander and Ebb rightly point out that, for whatever reason, streptococci is funny in a way that "streptococcus" could never be]

And again:

You can feed her all day with the vitamin A

And the Bromo Fizz

But the medicine never gets anywhere near

Where the trouble is

If she's getting a kind of a name for herself

And the name ain't his,

A person can develop a cold.

Has there ever been a better build?

But this is only the culmination of a long line of Loessers working-girl songs, most of them written for Betty Hutton in the movies. For a turn-of-the-century New York seamstress:

Oh, the sewing machine, the sewing machine,

My pride and joy

If I didnt have my sewing machine

I'd marry James McCoy.

But I bobbin the bobbin, and pedal the pedal,

And that's the end of Jim,

Cause by night I get so weary

I dont even look good to him.

Or, on the oddities of a jive-talking boyfriend (this was 1942):

He says, Hep, hep, with helium,

Now, babe, we're cookin'!"

And other expressions, to wit:

He says, "Were in the groove

And the groove is good-lookin.

He sounds like his uppers don't fit.

Loesser would protest I'm in the romance business," but even when writing a love song he could hardly help writing funny: From Love Me As I Am:

And dont try to make any changes;

It wouldnt be worth your tears.

Don't try to make any changes -

My mother's been trying for years!

In an age suspicious of romantic ballads (rightly, considering the overheated specimens that have plagued the theatre in recent years) Loessers demotic off-handedness sounds mighty congenial.

Richard Rodgers and Lorenz Harts Pal Joey (1940) comes, chronologically, between Anything Goes (1934) and Guys and Dolls (1950), and its composer and lyricist also fit temperamentally between the urbane Porter and the street-wise Loesser. Pal Joey, opening this week at the Shaw festival, is famous as the first of the hard-boiled musicals: a distant ancestor of Kander and Ebb's Chicago, and set in the same city. It shares the musical bill at the Shaw with Adam Guettel's Floyd Collins, one of the most adventurous of the new wave. Guettel also happens to be Rodgers' grandson. In more ways than one, the line carries on.
