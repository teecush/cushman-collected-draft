THEATRE REVIEW

DON JUAN

Stratford Festival

This year's Stratford Festival has been rife with classic French comedies. The Studio Theatre offered a production of Pierre Corneilles The Liar in which the actors changed roles at every performance and goosed the script and one another throughout the evening. It was fun, but the laughs had almost nothing to do with the text. They could have been doing any play.

As impressively formal as The Liar was engagingly informal, Stratfords production of Moliere's Don Juan began its run in English and closed it in French. The casting, with a couple of presumably mono-lingual exceptions, was the same in both versions. I caught the last English performance and the first French one, and I preferred the latter. I have no quarrel with David Edney's translation, but the playing seemed zingier in the original, and Molieres own text stood up better to the monumental style adopted by the director, Lorraine Pintal of Theatre du Nouveau Monde in Montreal.

Don Juan is a curious play and, even by Molieres standards, a loose-knit one. It is, despite its hero's international reputation, remarkably unsexy. It's one of the authors jokes that in the course of the play the legendary lover never actually scores with a woman. We meet him in the aftermath of his seduction of Dona Elvira, who twice appears to announce her retreat to a convent, first reproachfully, then forgivingly. In the course of abducting her Juan slew her father, whose statue famously appears at the end of the evening to drag him down to hell. Theres also some talk of his pursuing a couple of beauties by sea, but he's shipwrecked and instead has to work his charms on a pair of peasant girls, quite successfully until they start comparing notes. Much of the play is an extended debate between Juan and his servant Sganarelle, who speaks for conventional society and whose main beef against his master is not that he exploits women but that he refuses to be ashamed of it, any more than he is of killing his elders or bilking his creditors. What it all comes down to is that Juan doesn't believe in his societys morality, for which the shorthand is God. Theres a famous scene in which he offers gold to a starving hermit on condition that he blaspheme -- though, like other famous scenes, it never seems to have a theatrical impact to match its critical prestige. (It doesn't help that the production overplays its hand by making the poor anchorite seems more symbol than man). The closest Juan comes to repentance is to pretend a reformation when his father threatens to disinherit him. But he admits to Sganarelle that he's merely experimenting with another vice, though a peculiarly fashionable one; he may be a hypocrite, but he isnt hypocritical about it. Hes also physically brave, and his code stands up well against that of Dona Elvira's avenging brothers, whose values don't differ much from those of the Taliban. In the end, hes like the heroes of some Greek tragedies; he falls victim to a system that his author doesnt necessarily endorse but knows will always win. On a less exalted level, Moliere's play recalls the much-misunderstood last episode of Seinfeld, whose four principals ended up condemned by people far more selfish and sanctimonious than they were.

Moliere may have admired his Juan but he didn't much like him. That at least is the impression left by Colm Feores performance, which is fast and fluent (in both tongues), steely and unyielding but bloodless, even faceless. This last quality, though, may come with the productions territory. In Daniele Levesque's bleached settings, which are both gorgeous and austere, Juan lives in a house whose most prominent furnishings are a quintet of tailors' dummies, crowned in the last sacrificial act by candelabra. He changes identities as naturally and frequently as he changes his clothes; he cant really be a hypocrite because he has no centre to which he can be false. Benoit Brieres Sganarelle, by contrast, is foursquare and full-blooded: a combination of fear, greed and pious horror, who is endearing because he is recognizable. He never gets to change his clothes. He's also a master of clowning routines; indeed he starts the show with one, done almost entirely in Italian. Moliere, a Frenchman adapting a Spanish story, naturally set it in Sicily, though the geography throughout is very vague.

The costumes, by Francois St. Aubin, gracefully elide periods. Sevententh-century fig is reserved for Jean-Louis Roux as Juan's papa, who pays social calls in full Louis Quatorze regalia, incorporating a hint of armour; his performance, unhappily stiff in English, makes iconic sense in French. Similarly but differently, Gareth Potters Pierrot, slowed down in translation by a laborious Newfoundland accent, is charming in the original. The Statue of the Commander is transfixing in both versions; hes a hologram, with a crumbling visage and with the voice of Christopher Plummer. His impact is blunted, though, when Juan's death and damnation are represented by a dance with a female demon whom he, gallant and unrepentant to the last, is unable to resist; it makes intellectual sense but theatrically it's an anti-climax. Brieres final plaintive cry for his "wages," however, redeems it. In all its a grand, imaginative production: the kind of thing that may be commonplace in Quebec but can knock our chaussettes off in Ontario.
