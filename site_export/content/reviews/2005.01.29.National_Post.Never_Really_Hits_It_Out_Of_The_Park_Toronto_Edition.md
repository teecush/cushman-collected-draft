Clever baseball play's structure just seems out of whack

Never really hits it out of the parkYou would expect a play called Take Me Out to have something to do with baseball, and in the case of the new show at the Bluma Appel, your expectations would be correct. But there is, of course, more than one kind of outing, and the action here is set in motion when the star player of a U.S. team publicly declares himself to be gay.

Hold those thoughts and add another. To take somebody out can also mean to dispose of him, and this story does involve slaughter on the diamond. (And you thought hockey was a rough game.) That makes the title a triple pun. Im impressed.

Im also confused as to what the piece's real subject might be. Not that there's anything confusing about the narrative: Richard Greenbergs play, a Tony winner in 2003, has great drive, much humour and hardly a dull moment, and the CanStage production (which introduces Toronto to one of the most prolific and successful of current American dramatists) honours all those virtues.

It is also, like so many garlanded Broadway (and off-Broadway) plays, a bit glib and brittle, with characters who can be unnaturally articulate. Just to make things more complicated, it seems aware of these tendencies and concerned to avoid them. It raises some painful issues and it seems wary of making them pretexts for platitudes, but in doing this it ends up ignoring them. Pain -- as opposed to lyrical regret -- has no place in its last scene. The most obvious of its titles meanings turns out, if only by default, to be the important one. At its core, the play is a celebration of the game. Life's a mess; let's play ball.

Darren Lemming, the player who comes out and who, despite his surname, seems anything but self-destructive, is black as well as gay -- half-black, anyway. He is also supremely confident (people say he behaves as if he were God, and he doesnt contradict them), having gone through life hitherto without encountering any kind of discrimination; this, as his adoring financial advisor tells him, makes him an extraordinarily fortunate person, a statistical freak.

At first his luck seems likely to hold. His teammates take his declaration in stride, though their behaviour toward him in the showers begins subtly to alter. (The play is punctuated with shower scenes. I hope theres a warm-water clause, as well as a nudity clause, in the actors' contracts.)

The antagonism gets less subtle when a spectacular new pitcher arrives, an uncouth orphan named Shane Mungitt, from one of three redneck states -- he isn't sure which. He gives an interview to the press, full of abusive references to the kind of guy hes now expected to share a locker room with, and is suspended. After making a public apology, hes allowed back. But things only get worse.

It's a possible virtue in the play that Darren, far from being a noble martyr, is not very likeable. It's a definite flaw that he isnt very interesting, either, and that his crucial relationship with an old buddy, a player of equal talent who shines as a bigger star by virtue of playing on a lesser team, never comes to life.

This opacity is reflected in the performance of Thom Allison, whose surface assurance shows little sign that things might be churning underneath. Nothing much, in writing or acting, seems to be going on inside Darren; hes all smooth declaration.

Shane, by contrast, the racist homophobe (words much too big for him), is full of sullen life and loaded with motivation; he had a horrendous childhood, and baseball has been his lifeline. Mike Shara plays him with electrifying conviction as a mix of rugged resentment and blank incomprehension. Making us understand the bad guy is a laudable dramatic aim, but here the author's very success paints him into a corner. On the face of it, Darren and Shane are the prime antagonists, but the play more or less abandons any conflict between them. They have, after all, nothing to say to one another.

To be fair, this point is very well and wittily made in a late scene in which the two are brought together by a third player, name of Kippy, who talks as if he had wandered on to the field out of a library, which indeed proves to be the case. (He claims to have been a late developer, baseball-wise, and here he is playing on a major team. I've no idea how credible this really is, but I had trouble believing it.)

Kippy tries to build bridges; to make Shane, for instance, understand the subtle differences between calling someone a coloured person and "a person of colour." He doesnt get very far. Kippy is for much of the time the plays narrator, its puzzled mouthpiece, though he, too, fades before the end. While he's around he is, in Matthew Macfazdean's earnest and beaming performance, excellent value. Its significant, though, that a character so peripheral to the plot should loom so large in the play.

The same goes for Darrens money man, a repressed soul who is turned, by having a ballplayer as a client, into a rabid fan. David Storch is brilliant, twisting and turning through ecstasies of vocal and physical discovery. In discovering baseball he becomes part of America; and if the play is indeed a celebration of the game, then he, the outsider, is its prime celebrant. He has half of the final scene and all of its final moment. For practical purposes, his is the star performance. He does not, however, get a star curtain call, though two other actors do. I cite this as external evidence that the play's structure is out of whack.

As a further bonus we get Randy Hughson exercising his talent for rapid-fire good-humoured exasperation as the team manager. Morris Panych directs with what, borrowing from a song, I will fall back on calling his usual flair. And Ken MacDonald, placing the world within a locker room, designs with his.

The production is of comparable, though not identical, quality to that of A Whistle in the Dark; it falls short mainly because the play does. Where the Irish play is fierce and undeviating, the American is, at the crunch, wavering and sentimental. Both, though, are very funny. Among other things.
