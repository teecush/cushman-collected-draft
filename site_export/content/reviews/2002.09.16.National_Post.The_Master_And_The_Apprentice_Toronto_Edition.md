ABSOLUTELY CHEKHOV

du Maurier Theatre Centre

- - -

We take our pleasures where we find them. Soulpepper's Absolutely Chekhov is a program of five short plays, either by or about the Russian master. Two of them work well. The best moments of the evening, though, come between plays, when the actors double as scene- shifters. One of them is Oliver Dennis, whose unassuming demeanour conceals enchanting skills as acrobat and clown. While two burlier companions heartlessly grab all the lighter work, he is left to grapple all alone with the frame of a bed. He falls underneath it. He is almost crushed between its two ends. Finally, having apparently triumphed over the monster, he takes an unwary step back, and exits by somersault through an open window.

The plays themselves, made over by a bevy of current Canadian playwrights, are what Chekhov called his vaudevilles: the sketches with which he practised up for his major dramas.Theyre mostly farcical; an exception, placed at the end of the bill, is the one called Swan Song. It begins with the Falstaffian sound of an old actor snoring behind a curtain. He awakens to find himself in a deserted theatre, alone except for a sympathetic prompter (who apparently lives on the premises; at least, he seems to be pattering around them in a nightgown). The actor, once a star tragedian and now in the twilight of his career, relives and regrets old triumphs and current defeats.

William Webster, raging against the dying of the light, deals richly with the remembered glories and furiously with the present self-disgust. David Frenchs text allows him to close appropriately with Macbeth's out, out, brief candle -- the "poor player" speech - - after giving us tastes of Lear, Othello and even Hamlet. This -- the role furthest from Webster's own age and range -- is the best of all, with the prompter (Oliver Dennis, at rest) weighing in sympathetically with the prompt lines. The same device was used, at length, in the recent Christopher Plummer vehicle Barrymore. It is, of course, sentimental; and the director, Joseph Ziegler, could probably take it leaner and faster than he does, but it is very affecting.

Ziegler as actor commands the evenings other peak, The Dangers of Tobacco. This monologue finds him, in high Dickensian form, as an oppressed husband dragooned into delivering an anti-smoking lecture. The direction, by Albert Schultz, is very cunning. The stage is in the same sandwich formation -- spectators flanking the performers -- as for the concurrent production of The Maids. This is, on the face of it, the worst set-up for a play that consists of a man standing at a lectern. The production, though, turns this to comic effect; and Michael Healey, the adaptor, throws in a lot of good jokes. The speaker never says a word on his ostensible subject, except to remark that he himself rather likes a good puff now and then. What he does talk about, the words spilling aimlessly but compulsively out of him, is his miserable domestic situation, his monstrous wife, his six or seven -- he seems unsure -- unmarried daughters. (He practically invites his auditors to come and get them.) This is early stream-of-consciousness comedy, and very influential -- or at least prophetic. A friend of mine once characterized Alan Ayckbourns plays -- one of which Schultz directed with great success this season -- as speeded-up Chekhov; it's an accurate description but in fact Chekhov himself, in these apprentice pieces, was writing speeded-up Chekhov.

That doesn't mean you want a whole night of them. Previous Soulpepper productions have rewardingly brought out the farce in the full-length Chekhovs, but you cant expect too many brownie points for bringing out the farce in farce. Back in the days when audiences wanted more bang for their ruble, or just had longer attention spans, these playlets were much in demand as curtain raisers for plays too short to fill an evening. (Soulpeppers Miss Julie or The Maids would have been prime candidates for partnership.) Grouped together, they cancel one another out. It makes little sense to play The Dangers of Tobacco back-to-back with The Tragic Role, another piece (previously unknown to me) about a henpecked husband, though one apparently higher up the financial scale. It's set in cottage country, which I guess gives it Canadian flavour. Diego Matamoros, as a man lumbered with errands and parcels, calls for sympathy on his contrastingly unencumbered brother, who is Ziegler in role- reversal mode. The closing twist is visible someways off; it's comforting to discover that Chekhov had the same trouble with punchlines as everybody else. Adam Pettle is the adaptor; Daniel Brooks directs, modestly confining himself to a single corner of the stage.

The Bear, probably the best-known of the bunch, is a variation on the centuries-old Widow of Ephesus theme: a woman vowed to mourning is lured rapidly out of it by the advent of an unlikely suitor. In this case, hes come to collect a debt incurred by the late lamented. The production, again by Schultz, begins promisingly with Martha Burns, in mob cap and voluminous black, the very picture of determined Victorian bereavement. Thereafter, it loses all finesse. Jason Shermans script tips its hand by having a long-suffering domestic (Webster, otherwise lovely and moving jerkily from the hip as if stiffened by servitude) step out of context to exclaim "what I'd give for a hammer and sickle." All together now: Farce depends for its effectiveness on the performers' integrity and the audiences belief; it has less tolerance than any other form for cheap laughs. It also helps, in a whirlwind courtship farce, for us to want the characters to come together. They shouldnt beg for sympathy but their energies have to be focused.

Sherman is also the co-author, with Susan Coyne, of the new play The Old Business. This is harmless but pointless. It shows us Chekhov, reeling in retreat from the first-night failure of The Seagull, and having also to defend himself against those theoretically closest to him. Both his publisher- producer (Victor Ertmanis, shouting much as he did as Orson Welles a few seasons ago) and his ex-mistress (Nicole Underhay) accuse him of appropriating their stories for his play. The man's objections come oddly late -- he was around for the whole process, after all. Those of the woman, who sees herself as the model for his Nina, seem more substantial. His defence -- that Nina "endures" -- is suggestive, but would also apply to any other Chekhov character; they all endure, perforce, except the ones who get shot. Moments in this sliver of Chekhov's life echo or prefigure moments in his plays, both the long and the short ones, but the bits are arbitrarily assembled; we already know that imaginative writers hurt their friends and act wounded when told about it.

Fans of Matamoros and of Chekhov will be pleased to see how persuasively the one impersonates the other; he too endures (and he has a lot to put up with). Leah Cherniak directs the play, which it seems was originally intended to close the proceedings but now opens them, wisely leaving Swan Song to be the swan song. Or almost: There is a very taking curtain call, which brings the actors on singly, in the order of their first appearance. The accumulation is oddly touching: another of those unexpected fleeting pleasures.
