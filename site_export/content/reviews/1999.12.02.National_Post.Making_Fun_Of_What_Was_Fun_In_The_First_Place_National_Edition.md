THE DROWSY CHAPERONE

Theatre Passe Muraille, Toronto

Just over a year ago a Toronto actor, Bob Martin, married a Toronto actress, Janet Van De Graaff. A group of their friends surprised them with a pre-nup cabaret, a spoof 1920s musical comedy whose plot revolved around the wedding of two characters named Robert Martin and Janet Van De Graaff. They called it The Drowsy Chaperone. It went down well, and was expanded and re-mounted: first at the 1999 fringe festival; now for a regular run at Passe Muraille.

The real-life happy couple, safely spliced, have advanced from members of the audience to members of the cast. Janet plays Janet, a role in which she seems oddly miscast. Bob is a narrator-figure, billed as Man in Chair, and he has much the best of the deal.

He plays a helplessly enthused musicals buff. He sits at the side of the stage, spinning original cast 78s of his favourite show, The Drowsy Chaperone, and drawing our attention to its salient points as it comes to life before our eyes. "So many rhymes for Graaff!" he exclaims, after an opening number built around the central characters' names.

The song serves to introduce all the other characters as well: a device whose self-consciousness brands it immediately as parody rather than the real thing.

Among the throng are a pair of self-described "nameless, generic gangsters" whose presence immediately sends one's mind -- well, they sent my mind -- hurtling forward to the 1940s and Cole Porter's Kiss Me Kate. Sure enough, the two mugs get to perform an old-time Bowery waltz modelled in music, lyric and choreography on that show's Brush Up Your Shakespeare. When it's over our host steps forward to acknowledge the resemblance, thereby infuriating any critics who might have been itching to point it out for themselves. (Guilty.)

At other times he fills us in on the unglamorous future lives of the show's original performers. Ruefully, he acknowledges the gulf between the world of musicals, where bursting into song gets you applause, and the real world, where it gets you committed.

Martin, who somewhat resembles Danny Kaye and has the same mad glint in his eye, is a touchingly fragile figure who communicates, both through what he says and what he is, some very apt comments on showbiz nostalgia.

His role, though, has the unfortunate side-effect of killing the rest of the show stone dead. It's a tenuous concept to begin with and it never gets a chance to stand on its own feet.

The nonpareil of this kind of pastiche entertainment is Sandy Wilson's The Boy Friend, which offered songs that could stand comparison with those they burlesqued and took its story and characters absolutely seriously; spoof though it was, it actually involved you. It was some high-wire act. This show exists solely for its jokes, only a few of which make the grade. (There's one involving a telephone directory that's a gem.)

There is another way to use musical satire, which is to comment not only on other musicals but on the world that engendered them; Kander and Ebb did it in Cabaret and Chicago and Stephen Sondheim in Follies.

Come to think of it, the authors of this show -- Lisa Lambert, lyricist; Greg Morrison, composer; Don McKellar, librettist -- did it earlier this year, quite delightfully, in Honest Ed -- The Bargain Musical. Their biography of Ed Mirvish was a fanciful lampoon, but it gave them something to write about. Here all they can do is make fun of a form that was tongue-in-cheek to begin with.

Technically, their work is unobjectionable. The lyrics are, as the man in the chair observes, pretty good with the rhymes. I especially relished the couplet "down with your dour puss/hey little sourpuss" whose raised-eyebrow wordplay is redolent of Ira Gershwin. Indeed, the Gershwins seem to be the show's main point of reference, and it got me pining for a revival of Lady Be Good.

A dance-tune called Cold Feets has a Gershwin-out-of-Harlem touch about it, as if Duke Ellington had taken to writing show-tunes; our narrator duly mentions this, though a reference to "Harold Arlen drinking bath-tub gin while appropriating black rhythms" is an affront both to Arlen's musical genius and to his fastidious personal tastes: Also to history, since Arlen didn't get started till the '30s and the tone of this show is definitely, to adapt a Cole Porter phrase, pre-Depresh.

The Porter phrase the authors really seem to have in mind, though, is Anything Goes; they don't scruple to bring in Carmen Miranda in her tutti-frutti hat, to provide a finale that's anachronistic by at least 10 years. If all you have to sell are style and period, you better get them right.

The supporting characters are the usual-suspect bunch of cartoons -- harried producer, dumb showgirl, gigolo (one of those Continental types who used to come between Fred and Ginger and who here looks like Hitler), maid and butler -- and the cast, who are almost uniformly unappealing, do little to put fresh wit into them.

The plot, such as it is and sluggishly directed, just lies there. The costumes, designed by Christopher Richards and executed on a shoestring, are really stylish.
