EASY VIRTUE

Festival Theatre,

Niagara-on-the-Lake

The title Easy Virtue is double-edged. The play, one of this year's mainstage productions at the Shaw Festival, is Noel Coward's 1920s tribute to that old staple of English turn-of-the-century drama, the woman with a past. (Prize exhibits: Arthur Pinero's The Second Mrs Tanqueray and Oscar Wilde's Lady Windermere's Fan.)

On the surface, the title refers to the married lady whose position in high society is destroyed when it is revealed that before her marriage she had sex with someone other than the man who is now her husband. She is more to be pitied than censured -- except, of course, by those born censurers who make up the bulk of her present acquaintance.

In its second sense, the title refers to the puritans and stone- throwers who have come by their sense of moral superiority without in any way earning it.

There are still plenty of them around, but the playing field has changed, and it was already much altered in Coward's own day. His heroine, Larita -- who has lived most of her life in France but has no trace of an accent -- has in her past a marriage, a couple of divorces (one of them her own), and an undisclosed number of lovers.

She has now gotten married for a second time, to a much younger and much less intelligent Englishmen, Johnny Whittaker, who brings her home to meet his very rich and very hidebound family. They live in the kind of gargantuan splendour customary among people whose domestic affairs have to occupy large stages. Even before they meet Larita they disapprove of her; once they have met her, they loathe her.

The difference between Larita and her fin-de-siecle predecessors is that she believes in herself.

Poor Paula Tanqueray in the Pinero play thought deep down that society had a right to ostracize her, and she committed suicide over it. Larita thinks it a lot more appropriate to ostracize society, having first told it what she thinks of it. She does this at considerable and rather enjoyable length in the second and strongest of the play's three acts, after one of her skeletons has fallen out of its closet and her in-laws have attempted to beat her over the head with it.

We cheer her on. We agree with every word she says. That's the problem; Larita is banging on a door that is now completely open and must have been at least ajar when the play was new. Even if these complacent, dance-giving, tennis-playing philistines were as bad as Coward says they are, they must have had something to say for themselves.

But Coward was never good at characters of whom he disapproved, and he never lets this bunch get beyond generalized expressions of shock and disapproval; it may well be true that the prejudiced can do little more than keep repeating their prejudices, but maybe a dramatist, for the sake of conflict, should pretend that they can.

Goldie Semple makes a warm and glamorous impression as Larita, but the lack of effective opposition continually betrays her into over-emphasis -- that and the fact that Coward, much as he loved laying down the law, was never very good at it; Larita's rhetoric keeps buckling under the weight that the actress wants to put on it.

Larita is extremely articulate. The Whittaker family are defined for us by what she says about them, rather than by anything they actually do. We learn that Mrs. Whittaker (Patricia Hamilton being as imperious as the traffic will allow) is blinkered and self- pitying. Her older daughter Marion (sportily played by Kelli Fox in a voice that wears tweeds even if the character doesn't) is a pious backslapper, revolted to find that her new relation reads Proust. Larita does a first-rate demolition job on her, pointing out how both her religion and her heartiness derive from repression -- such a shame this isn't dramatized. Younger daughter Hilda (Fiona Byrne) is a jealous sneak who, when she tries to make up with Larita, is sternly instructed not to be a little toad.

The play ends with a house party at which our heroine, who has been sent to her room in disgrace, makes a spectacular surprise entrance, dripping with jewels. The female Whittakers then stand about sulking, for all the world like Cinderella's stepmother and the Ugly Sisters at the ball. The head of the family, Colonel Whittaker (latest in David Schurmann's line of basically decent chaps), also bears a marked resemblance to Cinders' dad, standing up for her when he can, and absenting himself from the merrymaking in his own home.

John (Kevin Bundy) proves, however, to be no Prince Charming, or rather charming is all he does; stretched between loyalties, he breaks -- but this, too, is underwritten, discussed (with a sensible former sweetheart, attractively played by Glynis Ranney) rather than shown.

Christopher Newton's production, handsomely designed by William Schmuck, has both clarity and sweep. In the aural and visual orchestration of the last act, he is at his showmanly best, bringing the curtain down on a crowd of insanely Charlestoning feet. The message presumably is that Larita goes off and life goes on, not knowing what it's missed.

Frustrating as it is, the play is never boring, and as Coward here is against all the same things as the rest of us, we can at least share in his relish at demolishing them.
