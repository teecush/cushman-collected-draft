THEATRE REVIEW

SHIRLEY VALENTINE

Bluma Appel Theatre, Toronto

---

Nicola Cavendish, playing the one and only role in Shirley Valentine, brings down the house with her description of marriage: "It's like the Middle East, isn't it? There's no solution." It's a neatly turned wisecrack, delivered with a winning mix of diffidence and confidence, as if the speaker were dubious about posing the question, but quite sure of the answer. I laughed as loudly as anyone else. But it was also the moment at which I stopped believing.

Like Hedda Gabler, with whom she doesn't have too much else in common, Shirley comes before us under her maiden name as a sign that she's locked in a stultifying marriage that has stifled the real her. She's 46 years old and living in Liverpool; she and her husband, after a long marriage with a grown-up son and daughter to show for it, have long since stopped communicating with one another. So Shirley talks to the wall (literally), letting off observations like the one quoted above. And though I've great respect for the wit and wisdom of Liverpudlian housewives, I doubt if they talk quite so much like late-night talk-show hosts. It's being done, far too obviously, for our benefit.

Willy Russell, another Liverpudlian, wrote the play just over 20 years ago. It first arrived in Canada in 1989, in a Vancouver productionfeaturingCavendish, who has been doing it on and off ever since, always under the direction of Roy Surette. Its current appearance at the Bluma marks her farewell to the role. (It's also a farewell for Martin Bragg, CanStage's outgoing artistic producer.)We see Shirley in two locations and at two stages of reawakening. In Act One, she's in her kitchen, preparing a dinner of "chips and egg" for her old man, who will undoubtedly be displeased since this is the day of the week when he eats mince meat. (Shirley's timing is impressive here, too. The eggs are done at precisely the moment her husband's shadow appears outside the door.)

Her friend Jane wants Shirley to accompany her on a two-week trip to Greece. She wants to go, but is sure she can't. But a final burst of spousal insensitivity tips the scales, and Act Two finds her on the beach at Corfu, counting an impressive array of blessings. She's briefly found love, or at least a man who knows his way around her body. More lastingly, she's found herself. Shirley Valentine has been reborn and she doesn't intend -- spiritually or geographically -- to go back.

Act One has her reflecting on her past and present lives. It is not without whimsy: Shirley's repeated "hey, wall" drove me up it. It's also an evasion; the play would have more credibility if she talked, honestly and consistently, to herself or to us. (She starts Act Two by addressing her confidences to a rock, but she doesn't keep it up.) Some of what she says is very funny indeed. What slew me the most were her reminiscences of her son's appearance as Joseph in the school nativity plays, greeting the news of no room at the inn with an indignant "but we booked!" (No mystery where that talent came from.) Some is authentically, enragingly sad, as when Shirley recalls a school principal who stigmatized her as stupid and ignorant, and refused to be convinced otherwise. And much seems like padding. Shirley tells us of a recent re-encounter with a once-posh former schoolmate who's now an expensive hooker. As she's Shirley's contemporary, she must really have husbanded her resources. (Or resourced her husbands. Or other people's.) It feels implausibly coincidental. And though I suppose you can connect it to the theme of realizing your destiny, it also feels irrelevant.

Because, however much she tells us about herself, we never know Shirley as a real person. The play's method is to tickle us, and then say "but seriously, folks." Cavendish, gorgeous though she is (and wonderful at an array of accents), is complicit in this. She just about rubs our noses in Shirley's sensuality and in her naivete. She gets so cozy with the audience and gets so many easy laughs -- they may be technically hard, but they're intellectually and emotionally easy -- that she loses any overall sense of loneliness and vulnerability. So in the second half of the play, she has to practically start over, and with material that's less grounded; she does her damnedest to move us, but she succeeds as an actress rather than a character. By accident or cunning, Russell has kept Shirley vague; he gives us a vessel into whom we can all pour our fears of having wasted our lives and our knowledge that the worst thing of all is regret, and then he throws us a redemption. And there are no ambiguities, no sense that freedom may come at a price. Shirley pays lip service to the idea that her husband may be as much a victim as she is, but we'd have to meet him to have an opinion about that. Compare that with the uncomfortable realities of the woman currently played by Nicola Lipman in Another Home Invasion. (That makes two one-woman plays in Toronto, performed by virtuoso actresses with the same first name. And they say Canadian theatre doesn't have trends.)

Of the two, Shirley Valentine is certainly more of a fun trip. Cavendish is irresistible. So is the whole package. But that isn't always a good thing. - Shirley Valentine is at the Bluma Appel Theatre until April 18. Call 416-368-3110 or visit canstage.com for tickets and information.
