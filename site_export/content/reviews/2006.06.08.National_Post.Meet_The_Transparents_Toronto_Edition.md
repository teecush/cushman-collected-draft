THE GLASS MENAGERIE

Avon Theatre, Stratford

- - -

Did you take in the Century of Progress? the Gentleman Caller asks the crippled, and cripplingly shy, Laura Wingfield. Laura didn't; nor is she ever likely to. The whole concept represented in the name of that exhibition, the idea that so excites her brash, well-meaning visitor, has passed her by. As, to a lesser extent, it has forsaken her mother, Amanda, the former Southern belle, reduced in late 1930s St. Louis to selling subscriptions for the Ladies Home Companion. As for son and brother Tom, the aspiring writer who is the plays narrator and its only other character, he's torn: Aware of the present ("Guernica!"), straining towards the future, lured by the past. Though of course, since, as he tells us, the play is memory, it's all the past, including the future.

Miles Potters production of Tennessee Wiliams The Glass Menagerie faithfully incorporates all the atmospherics that the author specified, but it's still the hardest-edged, least wispy memory-play imaginable. It's hypnotically gripping, every moment charged; the first night held the auditorium in a pin-drop silence broken only by a laughter that was surprisingly strong and frequent. Most of it was raised by Seana McKenna, whose Amanda is bracingly efficient and startlingly devoid of nostalgia. When she harks back to the gallant days of her youth she does, indeed, note the decline in manners and in her own level of comfort (her current apartment, as designed by Peter Hartwell, is uncompromisingly shabby). But she takes it as the way of the world; she certainly doesnt wallow. This is much at odds with all the fragile, flighty Amandas one has seen or read about, but there isnt a moment at which you can catch McKenna playing against the text. So maybe the legend is wrong; maybe we've been confusing Amanda with that other early Williams heroine, Blanche DuBois, who trumpeted her Dixie upbringing as the repository of higher values. Then again, maybe Blanche herself wasn't quite as breakable as weve liked to think. Williams himself said that she would never run short of kindly male strangers to depend on.

Something is lost, though -- not so much in the character of Amanda as in the knock-on effect on the fugitive Tom. Instead of fleeing from a smothering mother and all she represents, hes merely escaping a strong, nagging woman to whom he can give as good as he gets. Steven Sutcliffe plays him excellently, with delicacy and shamefaced wit and the occasional biting outburst of frustrated anger. There is excess baggage on this character, and I think it's the author's fault. Tom admits that memory -- a metaphor, I suppose, for the dramatists art -- makes him an unreliable narrator, but we have nothing against which to measure his unreliability. The play is rooted in autobiography, but an audience can hardly be expected to come to the theatre primed with the facts of the authors life. Certainly the original audience couldn't have done so; in 1945, when the play was premiered, Williams was new and unknown; he wasn't Eugene ONeill, rounding off a career as Americas top playwright by dishing the dirt on his family. It has to be Tom Wingfield's memory we're concerned with, not that of Tennessee Williams. And since this Tom doesnt have much reality outside of his role as chorus, theres something frustratingly circular and closed-in about the process. To put it another way, Tom acts as if the play's about him, but the dramatic facts don't bear him out.

He talks as if its about Laura. The last, legendary epiphanic line is directed at her, or at the memory of her: "Blow out your candles, Laura." Oddly, though, we dont see very much of her in the earlier parts of the play; we spend more time hearing about her family's worries about her, her with her collection of glass animals and her obsessive playing of the Victrola. But we do finally get to her scene with the Gentleman Caller, one of the most pitch-perfect duets in modern drama, a wonderfully tactful presentation of tactlessness. Matthew MacFadzean, stocky and crew-cut, is the breathing image of the former sports hero, current apostle of progress and self-confessed "stumble John." He is genuinely admiring of Laura, wanting to help, stirred into kissing her, then admitting he's engaged, and breaking her glass unicorn and also her heart. Only maybe he doesnt; maybe he only breaks ours. As Sara Topham plays her, Laura is hurt, but not beyond healing; her protestations that it really doesnt matter ring unexpectedly true. It's surprising, because earlier she's veered towards the historical reality of the authors sister, who spent most of her adult life in a mental institution. But she now emerges as her mothers daughter: a possible survivor. And McKenna, dropping the last trace of Amanda's affectations in angry, selfless concern, helps make it moving. Our knowledge of what really happened does, I must admit, add to the poignancy, just as Tom's protestations that he isnt a drunk take on an added sour dimension by our awareness of how much Tennessee himself became capable of putting away. This is having it both ways, but as the possibility is there we may as well take advantage of it. Its a relief, overall, to see Williams rescued from the sentimental swamps, and a treat to see the rescue so immaculately performed.
