THEATRE REVIEW NIGHT LIGHT

Tuesday, May 21, 1996

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Toronto ONT -- Written by John Lazarus Directed by Robert Metcalfe Presented by Green Thumb Theatre for Young People in Toronto THE CASHORE MARIONETTES Presented by Joseph Cashore A CHILD'S PORTRAIT OF SHAKESPEARE AND MACBETH Written and Directed by Lois Burdett Performed by Grade 2 students from Stratford's Hamlet Elementary School Young drama goes beyond child's play Performances geared to the younger set prove enchanting for adults, too. THE MILK FESTIVAL At Harbourfront on Saturday and Sunday BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Toronto AMONG the formative influences of my youth was a book called Down with Skool. It was supposedly the work of an English schoolboy of about 12, named Nigel Molesworth, a.k.a. The Curse of St. Custard's.

Among Nigel's observations were the following on the hazards of encountering gangs of older boys. (Spelling regularized.) "There is always a big one called Ern. Ern is a bully. People always say stand up to bullies, they will run away, BUT DON'T YOU BELIEVE IT."

This refreshing wisdom is partially echoed in Night Light, brought to the Milk Festival at Toronto's Harbourfront by the Green Thumb Theatre of Vancouver. Victor, a nice kid, good at lessons, is regularly set upon by Farley, who forces Victor to do his math homework for him.

Victor reads a book on how to deal with childhood fears but finds its pat recommendations no help at all; after a moment of surprise, Farley simply laughs at him.

Digging deeper, Victor identifies Farley's weak spot: his terror of having his poor grades detected by his "genius" father. Even this one-to- one correlation between a bully's bullying and his insecurities seems suspiciously neat; problems in real life are not so easily solved. But it works in the play.

This is due, in part, to a shrewd stroke of construction by the playwright John Lazarus; we are introduced to Farley and his worries before we see him terrorizing Victor. For a crucial moment, the victimizer seems like the victim.

The writing in general is economic and unsentimental, as is the performance. Matthew Smith as Victor is especially fine, an adult actor clearly conveying a growing child's blend of innocence and experience.

Less successful is a parallel plot involving Victor's younger sister and her equivalent trauma over their father's hospitalization. The younger you play, the harder it gets; and the stylization, which involves a live floppy monster creeping out of a closet, doesn't belong to the same world as the rest of the play.

The nine-going-on-10-year-olds who accompanied me to these junkets thought it a little beneath them. That at least is what they said afterward. During the performance, they were thoroughly held.

They were similarly enraptured by the Cashore Marionettes, although younger kids were not. It probably was irresponsible of the organizers to announce this show as suitable for ages five and up. Joseph Cashore, a superlative puppeteer from Pennsylvania, does not tell stories; he composes visual tone-poems. On the end of his strings, a venerable European violinist fervently attacks a Vaughan Williams piece, bowing perfectly synchronized. An amateur trapeze artist, terrified but determined, manages to get through a professional routine, to his own amazement and the audience's delight. A homeless scavenger seems to find some hope at the black-clad leg of his manipulator; gazing upward, though, he encounters only the devastating shake of his master's head: God saying no. A perkier puppet is luckier; he manages to climb all the way up Mount Cashore to perch cheekily on its shoulder.

Technically, these performances are a marvel. Having the operator in full view only adds to the wonder of it: so many strings controlling so much detail. Each doll is perfectly costumed and characterized, their faces so detailed you could swear that you see them change expression.

The spirit of the performance is benign but stern. Cumulatively, it is rather frightening. Out in the world, puppetry is a metaphor for mental cruelty, and that is what emerges here. The more domestic items (a mother trying to quiet her baby, a girl avoiding her homework) are the least interesting. You remember instead the images of humanity in a bleak landscape, at the mercy of a capricious controlling fate. Cashore's art may be accessible to children, but it isn't kids' stuff.

Cashore is American, and the Green Thumb troupe stopped off in Toronto as a diversion from a U.S. tour. The Grade 2 kids of the Hamlet Public School in Stratford, Ont., are due to visit Utah later this year but their appearance at Harbourfront counts as a wholly Canadian achievement. It has, though, world-wide implications.

Teacher Lois Burdett insists, rightly, that her creations, which are being marketed under the rubric "Shakespeare Can Be Fun]," are not primarily designed to introduce her pupils to Shakespeare.

Rather, she uses Shakespeare - the stories of the plays and a little bit of the language - to introduce the children to themselves: to their own imaginations and their own inventiveness.

That they get, in the process, astonishingly close to the heart of the plays reflects great credit on all parties, Shakespeare included.

Of course, once you place these classroom projects in front of an audience, you add new, and conceivably distorting, dimensions.

What was a self-sufficient exercise becomes a show, inviting judgment. Children, if my companions are any guide, react with admiration and envy: Why can't they have so much fun in school? The answer seems to be that Burdett is an exceptional teacher, therefore unduplicatable. Still, this kind of stimulation ought to be generally available, even if it can't take this precise form.

I had already seen A Child's Portrait of Shakespeare performed at Stratford for a conference of theatre professionals, who reacted with predictable self-flagellating enthusiasm. I was as charmed by the kids as they were, and as awed by their clarity and assurance, but I had some doubts that were reinforced on a second viewing.

We are offered a dramatized biography of Shakespeare, researched by the children and cast into rhyming couplets by Burdett herself. It is acted out with several kids sharing the central role. It is charming and cute, but the presence of a grown-up audience ups the cuteness quotient automatically and dangerously.

Even as a child's portrait, it is dubiously sentimental: Shakespeare is unlikely to have slipped back from London to Stratford to spend weekends with Anne Hathaway; it would have been logistically impossible, and all the evidence suggests that he couldn't wait to get away from her. The burning of the Globe Theatre didn't break his heart and end his career; he was already in retirement. About the plays themselves we are told nothing. But without them, Shakespeare's life, what we know of it, is not very interesting.

The proof is in the school's version of Macbeth, which is riveting. The basic staging is the same; the performers sit in two rows, detaching themselves when their cue for action comes. Within this convention, the staging is very inventive; most professional productions gloss over the procession of Banquo's royal heirs, for example, but here we had a full implacable lineup with crowns. There is some very selective quotation ("bubble, bubble, toil and trouble" is in; "tomorrow and tomorrow and tomorrow" is out) but the basic medium, once again, is Burdett's couplets.

These are far less jingly and predictable than in the Portrait, probably because they are rooted in response to an actual strong story rather than to biographical wishful thinking.

There are also some outstanding individual performances. If Macbeth's self-torture is a little underdone, his wife's mixture of scorn and ambition is very compelling, and there is a strong avenging (and female) Macduff. (There are far more girls in the cast than boys. Are Grade 2 boys less interested?) As education and as entertainment, this is triumphant. Or as Nigel Molesworth might for once have put it, Up with Skool.
