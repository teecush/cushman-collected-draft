I promised myself not to compare The West Wing with The Sopranos, but the temptation -- after the stand-off at this year's Emmys -- is irresistible. The West Wing took nine awards to The Sopranos' one: a political putsch or a gangland massacre.

They were up against one another as TV drama series. Really, though, they are different animals. Remember the Sopranos episode in which Chris, Tony Sopranos nephew, tried his hand at writing a movie screenplay, and lamented that his life, unlike those in the handbooks hed consulted, didn't have "an arc? He was right; none of the characters on The Sopranos has an arc, except one. The show is centrifugal: Its subsidiary actions are only important for how they illuminate the life, and possible downfall, of Tony himself. The Sopranos, in fact, is not a series at all: It's a cohesive drama that, for reasons of length, unfolds over a period of weeks. Whether it can maintain this achievement over successive seasons is, of course, another question.

On The West Wing, everybody has an arc, which is the same as nobody having one. Each is there to play out his or her personal soap opera, and they could obviously go on forever, or at least until the next election. The president gets special billing -- hes played, according to the opening credits, by and Martin Sheen" -- and you might claim that hes the protagonist; the other regulars depend on him for their jobs, and what they do might theoretically affect his position. But he doesn't dominate dramatically, and he doesn't have enough at stake. He isnt subject to a code of kill-or- be-killed, and he isnt even in danger of a nervous breakdown.

He's also, despite occasional crotchets, very nice. He bears no resemblance to any recent U.S. leader, but he does fit into what seems to be an imperishable American fantasy, that of having the country governed by Henry Fonda.

Departing from Hollywood tradition, The West Wing is brave enough to tell us which party its president belongs to. (He's a Democrat). Otherwise, its antiseptic as only American television can be; it looks like a hospital series in which the doctors and adminsitrators have mutated into politicians and civil servants. Its opening sequence is positively reverential: a flag waves, music swells, the White House facade is faded in, and the principal actors smile for their identifying close-ups. Apart from that last detail it might be a commemorative documentary left over from the Bicentennial.

An early episode in the first series had a cute-as-hell subplot in which Charlie (Dule Hill), a bemused young black man who thought he was applying for a job as a messenger, gets appointed to the post of presidential aide. He finds himself, at segments end, in the very room where the president is addressing the nation on a crisis in the Middle East. "I've never felt anything like this before whispers the new recruit to the superior standing next to him: It doesn't go away" is the reply. The music swells again, as does everybodys bosom, with respect for the democratic process. Charlie wins the presidents respect by finding his lost glasses, and the president wins his by promising stricter gun controls. (Charlie's mother was a cop, killed on duty.)

The show is also in favour of imposing measured sanctions on rogue states rather than zapping them into oblivion (the president, who can be something of a hothead, has to be persuaded on this, but he sees the light), and though it feels the emotional pull toward imposing penalties for hate crimes, it thinks that the law should punish deeds rather than thoughts. It disapproves of intolerant fundamentalists, and of self-righteous investigators who persecute loyal presidential assistants for their alcoholic pasts.

It believes, endearingly, that there are measurable differences between Democrats and Republicans, and that the former are the good guys, though some of the others can be decent at heart. It is, in fact, embarrassingly liberal: embarrassing to me, at least, because I share most of its assumptions and still wish that it wouldn't keep trotting them out so virtuously.

One of the series five Emmys -- the writing one -- went to a Christmas episode, titled In Excelsis Deo. Here, as might be expected, the cuteness quotient went to danger levels. The president played uncle to visiting parties of quarrelling schoolchildren, and did it to the manner born; maybe hed been an actor. He also went present-shopping at an antiquarian bookstore, and refused to turn the trip into a photo-op. (The show, of course, turned it into one for him.)

Josh (Bradley Whitford), his deputy chief of staff, protested that he'd rather be dropped from the Washington Monument than go along. "This is Christmas, Josh, said the Chief, with a twinkle. There'll be time for both."

On this show, even the twinkles are solemn. Another storyline involved Toby (Richard Schiff), the communciations director, described in the publiicty material as "sleepless -- to be distinguished from C.J. (Allison Janney), the press secretary, who is merely tireless."

Toby discovers that a dead street person is actually a Korean War vet, and organizes a state funeral for him, in the process encountering the sweetest, least threatening bunch of homeless you ever saw. This prompts the presidents secretary to recall her own twin sons ("I dressed them different but they still did everything together) who were killed as medical officers in Vietnam. They were so young," she says, "its hard not to think that right then they needed their mother." The music got especially insistent at this point.

It's probably a reflection on the Emmys rather than on the series that this tear-jerker of a show was singled out for special notice. The succeeding instalment was far jollier, with Roger Rees (known to me from Nichlas Nickleby and to everybody else from Cheers) turning up as a dissipated English aristocrat to sort out a border-conflict between India and Pakistan; the Oriental restaurateur from a famous episode of Seinfeld promoted to the post of Chinese ambassador; and Whitford and Rob Lowe invigoratingly turning the screws on a puritanical sneak who seems to be the series' stand-in for Ken Starr. Indeed, as pretentious soaps go, The West Wing is decent entertainment. (Nearly everybody in it seems to be single, allowing increased scope for romantic entanglements.)

But theres no way it can be taken seriously; everybody in it is too nice and the language is far too clean. With what we know of the real White House, and what we can imagine of the pressures, can anyone credit this absence of profanity? As a theatre critic, I know only too well how boring four-letter words can become; but The Sopranos has shown us how creatively they can be used.(So indeed has The Larry Sanders Show, which in its own mode was a far more credible political drama; while as character creations The West Wing people have nothing like the depth of the brothers in Frasier.) I dont believe that the way ahead for TV drama is just to make more gangster series (though it's a safe bet that the networks now do) but The Sopranos has acustomed us to a thickness of texture that makes the competition seem thinly transparent. The mutterings and the overheard dialogue of Tony and his various families may sometimes be trying to the ear, but once you've heard them the polite WASP lucidity of the presidents men and women is, inescapably, yesterdays news.
