We are now two weeks into Rubicon, three if you count a preliminary instalment that aired a week before the official premiere, and so may be regarded as an optional extra (Episode Minus One). Rubicon seems to be a spy show, though both its story and its characters are so heavily draped in mystery that it's difficult to tell what anyone involved is up to. It does take place, more or less undoubtedly, in the New York HQ of something called the American Policy Institute, where some unglamorous, and probably underpaid, figuresareresearching the movements and motives of people who have been detected doing questionable things in foreign parts. Conversations among the employees are glacial, meaning that they're both slow and icy. It's like 24 slowed down to the methodical pace and down-at-heel ambience of John Le Carre, with an atmospheric dash of The Prisoner and the possible philosophical blessing of Franz Kafka. The air of stealth and subterfuge, with heavy musical underlining, is plainly intended to be hypnotic. I think it succeeds, but I don't deny that it can also be frustrating.

Our hero is Will (James Badge Dale, looking tense and inquisitive), who starts as a mid-level functionary at Inscrutable Investigations Inc, but is promoted when David, his superior, mentor and friend is killed in a train crash. You need to have watched the prologue episode to have clapped eyes on David when alive; on the other hand, he may return, since before boarding the train he deposited his car in Parking Space 13, and Will knows how unlikely it is that David, a highly superstitious man, would have done such a thing. He thinks that David was trying to tell him something. David has also made Will a present of his motorcycle which Will, short of space, keeps in his kitchen. When he disassembles the bike, he discovers clues and a gun, the latter of which should come in especially useful if the show, which sems to have taken a vow of onscreen non-violence, ever allows him to use it.

Will, in his new position, presides over a disconsolate team of three. There's Miles, youngish and mildly bearded, who worries a lot about riots in Nigeria, and can't get anyone else to take an interest in them. There's Tanya, whom somebody suspects of being an alcoholic and, who as the only woman on the team, has the responsibility of bringing the doughnuts. (Yes, I know.) There's Grant, who's very particular about his doughnuts, and who gets teased over whether his namesake, Ulysses S., was one of the five worst presidents in U.S. history or only one of the 10. Grant is both prickly and pompous, and wears a shirt and tie. All the others dress casual, including Will, who is upbraided for it when his immediate boss, who is unpleasant, takes him to meet his ultimate boss, who

is comparatively polite and so probably untrustworthy. The Big Boss asks Will and his team to look into the activities of a German called George ( "not even Georg," laments Grant), and they spend much time looking at footage of him going through airport security.

They can't find much out about him, except that his financial records suggest that he's impeccable to the point of sanctity. At other times, they seem to be looking into Hezbollah's acquisition of former Soviet missiles, and the travel plans of David Cameron; they're nothing if not up-to-the-minute except that they seem to live in a pre-computerized age: when they talk about "files" they mean the heavy, bulging manila kind. Will, I should have mentioned, lost a wife and child on 9/11, so his cheerlessness is more than excusable.

Meanwhile, in another part of the plot entirely, there's Miranda Richardson, playing the wife of a recently deceased rich man who has left her a townhouse of whose existence she was previously unaware. She's suspicious, especially when she goes to look at it and find it contains a copy of a novel by Graham Greene. "He hated Graham Greene" she protests, apparently pondering whether literary infidelity might be grounds for posthumous divorce. She also finds a four leaf clover, which isn't as lucky as might sound. Because the only link so far between her and the rest of the show is that a 4LC (dandy acronym, don't you think?) seems to be the motif of a secret cabal of posh gents who may be responsible for David's death, or for his not being dead. Dead or alive, David has a jumpy son who wants Will to return his dad's motorbike. Will also has a secretary, Maggie, who wants to be his love interest but is being forced to spy on him and his team as well.

And if Will thinks he's being followed, he's right. He's already detected one stalker, who turns out to be nothing but an FBI man doing clearance in his clearance. He doesn't know about two other guys, who seem to want him dead, or to be working for someone who does. He also finds secret codes in newspaper crossword puzzles, and takes them to a wise old cryptographer; he's played, I'm happy to note, by Isiah Whitlock Jr, who was Clay Davis on The Wire.

I suppose it will all come together. The show has an infuriating air of knowing what it's doing, even while refusing to tell us. Just one thing: If there turns out to be another of those world conspiracies, like all the other TV shows have, then I want to know what its goals are, how it's financed, and just who's in on it: no more chickening out. As for the title: It sounds pleasantly ominous, but I've yet to notice any reference to the river or to anyone crossing it, literally or otherwise. It may of course be a pun, given the tricksiness of everything. And I am beginning to think of Will, a good fellow in an impenetrable maze, as Cubik's Rube.

- Rubicon airs on Sundays at 9 p.m. on AMC.
