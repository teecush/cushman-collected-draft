I recently caught up with the Stratford production of The School for Scandal and found it an intriguing mixture of good and bad. The characters in the play, as in most respectable English comedies of the 18th century, also divide into good and bad, morally speaking. What was interesting at Stratford was that, contrary to the general belief that vice is more interesting than virtue, nearly all the best performances came from those playing the nicest people.

The play itself is split between being a waspish satire on the theory and practice of character assassination, and a warmly charitable comedy about marriage and family. In this version, warmth wins. The stand-out performance, his every line both piercingly funny and poignantly true, is Brian Bedford's Sir Peter Teazle, the old man yoked in love and law to a young wife. The redoubtable James Blendick and the very promising Donald Carrier give him firm support as the most charitable members of the Surface family. In fact the only one of the goodies who fails to register is Brian Tree as the helpful moneylender Moseley.

Scholars of the works of Richard Brinsley Sheridan may rub their eyes at this point, and protest that there is no such character. That indeed is the problem. Sheridan's moneylender is called Moses and he is, of course, Jewish. This fact doesn't actually set off a flood of ethnic jokes, but it does underlie everything he says and how he says it. Deprived of this source of energy, a usually excellent actor has nothing to act.

As a Jew, I have never felt uncomfortable with Moses in previous productions; and I do recall that the broadest and funniest I have seen was in a British production directed by another Jew, Jonathan Miller. (This is the same Dr. Miller who years ago, in Beyond the Fringe, made the best of all anti-anti-Semitic jokes: "I'm not a Jew, actually. Just Jewish. Not the whole hog.") The Stratford decision, by the way, must have been made quite late; the original name figured in all the advance publicity. I wonder if Richard Monette, director of both the play and the festival, was responding to actual pressure, or if he was maybe attempting some kind of pre- emptive conciliation pending the next Stratford production of The Merchant of Venice. Forcible baptism is a discredited procedure, and I do find it piquant that in christening Moses, Stratford has lighted on a name only one vowel removed from that of the most celebrated of English Fascists.

A more celebrated literary Jew is about to undergo a similar conversion. A new adaptation of Dickens' Oliver Twist is being shown on BBC Television, after which it will undoubtedly make the customary trek to our own screens. The adaptor is Alan Bleasdale, author of the memorable GBH, and his leading actor from that series, Robert Lindsay, is playing Fagin, apparently as "a Czech magician who is only incidentally a Jew." I owe this information to Steven Berkoff, the ferocious British -- and Jewish -- actor, director and playwright who greeted the news with an aghast article in The Independent headlined "Don't deprive us of our best villains."

It's a new sensation for me -- I suspect it would surprise him too -- but I agree with Berkoff. He has, admittedly, his own axe to grind here; not only is he himself a powerful player of villains, he has even done a one-man show about them, including, I am reliably informed, a Shylock of quite mesmerizing political incorrectness. He also, with acknowledgements to someone's unerring sense of poetic justice -- played the only convincing Hitler (in the miniseries War and Remembrance) that I have ever seen.

He bases his Independent argument on two grounds: that Fagin, like Shylock, is a realistic illustration of the criminal straits into which Jews were driven by a Christian society; and that Fagin, like Shylock, is the creation of a writer of genius and should not be tampered with.

Whatever you make of The Merchant of Venice, there's no way any actor can play Shylock as a gentile. Fagin is theoretically a different case. He is, after all, a character in a novel, bound to suffer some alteration when transferred to stage or screen. And Fagin's Jewishness, unlike Shylock's, has no direct bearing on the plot. Dickens habitually calls him "the Jew" in the narrative, but the matter is hardly ever mentioned in the dialogue. Indeed, a non- denominational Fagin has already been tried, right here. A few seasons ago that bastion of right-mindedness, the Toronto Young People's Theatre, presented him as an indeterminate figure who sounded -- if you'll pardon the racism -- somewhat Irish.

It didn't work. And as usual in these cases you felt that nothing beneficial was being done for society but that those responsible were making themselves and a few adherents feel good. Like Stratford's Moses, the YPT Fagin faded into the woodwork -- and far more damagingly since, as written, he's the most vital character in the book. And that vitality seems inextricably tied to the charming, wheedling, sinister language that Dickens gave him, and that he obviously thought was Jewish.

At the end of his story Fagin, like Shylock, is brought to trial and condemned. Replying to the Berkoff article, John Sutherland of London University has protested that this is intolerable, that Dickens executes Fagin out of sheer malice: "what villainy is he hanged for? The novel never tells us." Professor Sutherland is an accredited Dickens scholar but he's wrong. The novel tells us, quite explicitly, that Fagin is hanged as an accessory to murder; it was he who goaded on Bill Sikes to the horrific killing of Nancy (the episode with which Dickens used to make ladies faint at his public readings).

The professor also points out that in his last completed novel, Our Mutual Friend, Dickens tried to make amends for Fagin by presenting a Jewish paragon, Mr. Riah. What he neglects to add is that Riah comes to life just once, and that's when his author finally loses patience with him; off to render charity to a man who has atrociously wronged him, he is told by his Christian friend Jenny Wren that "if your people don't know better [than that] it's a shame they ever got out of Egypt." Fagin, you feel, would have agreed with her. And Fagin, the solitary old man in the dock implictly demanding of his accusers "what right have you to murder me?", makes a far stronger case against his society and its prejudices than any plaster-saint could, simply by virtue of being alive.

Maybe, if you can't bear the idea of Fagin as Jewish villain, you should get rid of his villainy rather than his Jewishness. That is what the late Lionel Bart did when he wrote Oliver! which is about to come among us again, at Toronto's Princess of Wales Theatre. Turning a horrific novel into a family musical, Bart was unable to save Nancy or to whitewash Bill Sikes; the plot wouldn't let him. But he was able to turn Fagin into a lovable old rogue, with the accent and the timing of a great music-hall comedian, and I fancy that he didn't even realize he was making much of a change. That is how he saw Fagin and how, despite everything, generations of readers have seen him. That's why the musical works so well; it's a response to something that's actually in the book. Fagin is energy. So, in a much smaller way, is Sheridan's Moses, who doesn't even have to be bad to be good. The deracinated Mr. Moseley, who stands in for him at Stratford, doesn't manage to be anything. Possibly Berkoff got his war-cry slightly wrong. He should have been trumpeting not just Leave Us Our Villains but Let My People Stay.
