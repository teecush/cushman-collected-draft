Robert Lepage, Canada's favourite

theatrical son, received the Europe Theatre Prize here last Sunday. You don't actually have to be European to get the prize, which is given "in recognition of a life devoted to art theatre." You do have to have made an impact on Europe and to play according to European rules, which basically mean "director as auteur." Lepage obviously qualifies on both grounds, big time.

In his acceptance speech -- which was charming, marked by the surface diffidence and deep rooted confidence that characterize his stage work, and like his stage work was equally fluent in French and English -- Lepage said "it may seem strange that the European Prize should go to a North American, but Europe is not just a continent, it's maybe a modern idea of the world, of rapprochement." He added: "Maybe the jury noticed this in my work. In which case, I'm profoundly grateful."

At a symposium two days earlier Don Rubin of York University's theatre department had noted that, within Canada, Lepage was regarded as a Quebecois artist, while to the rest of the world he was simply Canadian. (Other countries really don't care about the differences between our provinces.)

But this distinction seemed to break down in Thessaloniki, where the word "Canadian" wasn't heard very much. If Lepage himself seemed to prefer "North American," the emphasis elsewhere was very much on his identity as a Quebecer. On a later panel I was asked how Lepage's work was received in English Canada,

to which I replied "not often enough." We seem to see most of his shows only after they've toured the rest of the world. I was able to add, though, that when they were received in Toronto, it was generally with immense enthusiasm. There have been missteps such as Geometry of Miracles (the one about Gurdjieff and Frank Lloyd Wright) and Elsinore (the virtuoso but pointless oneman show that gave new meaning to the phrase " Hamlet without the prince"), but there have also been triumphs that have been received as such. These have ranged from one- man plays that really were plays ( Needles and Opium, The Far Side of the Moon) to the ensemble masterpiece The Seven Streams of the River Ota. I might have added that his opera doublebill of Duke Bluebeard's Castle and Erwartung showed that he can work magnificently within the disciplines of someone else's prescribed texts. And I did say that his own works, however collaboratively they may have been created, have ended up as texts, the best of which have proved sturdy enough to withstand production by other people.

For their public presentation Lepage and his Ex Machina company offered an hour's worth of "Works in Progress." From The Andersen Project, commissioned by the Danish government to mark the bicentennial of Denmark's most celebrated fabulist, came The Shadow, a scary piece about a dark fairy tale (a sort of Silhouette of Dorian Gray) that overwhelms everyday life. Lepage himself claims to identify with Hans Andersen, but this scene on its own could do little more than tantalize. Two scenes from a piece called Lipsynch gave little indication of what the whole might be like, or even why it has that title. The first, a monologue about child prostitution, was disturbing in a surprisingly conventional way; the other, a more complex episode about a brain surgeon whose work follows him remorselessly home, allowed one of his male actors to give an unnervingly convincing performance of an old lady. Finally from The Far Side of the Moon, a work now surely very far in progress, we had Lepage himself reprising his rolling-on-the-floor dance of a man imitating his own filmed image: still haunting, though suggestive out of context of the Marx Brothers' mirror routine in Duck Soup without the laughs.

A Latvian director, Alvis Hermanis, received a subsidiary Europe Prize for "New Theatrical Realities." His acceptance speech was worthy of note.

"Artists," he said, "now wear suits like businessmen [and] drink mineral water rather than whisky." But they still present themselves as rebels, prizing anger over compassion: "Our brains are too large, our hearts are sometimes too small. But we have to try anyway."

Lepage, I think, has always done this. The dazzling high-tech of his shows never effaces -- indeed it highlights -- their humanity. He shows us man adrift in space but not dwarfed by it. European or North American, Quebecois or Canadian, he deserves to be honoured.
