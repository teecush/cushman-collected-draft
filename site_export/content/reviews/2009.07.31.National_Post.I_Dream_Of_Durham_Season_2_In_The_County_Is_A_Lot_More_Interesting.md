No one can say that Durham County didnt give fair warning. Its first season began with a peeping Tom, in a peaceful woodland setting, witnessing the rape and murder of two schoolgirls. The voyeur went on to perform a couple of copycat killings of his own. We watched him; the show was in no way a whodunnit. By the halfway mark his guilt was also clear to the drama's protagonist, Mike Sweeney, a cop who, were he or the show given to lightheartedness, might reasonably have described the killer, Ray Prager, as his friendly neighbourhood psychopath, since they lived next door to one another. The showdown came when Ray staked out Mike's teenage daughter Sadie as his next victim. Mike arrived in time to rescue her, but it was Sadie who fired the gunshot that put Ray out of action without taking his life.

This was a disturbing production about disturbed people. It was stylish, brooding, left many questions open and made no emotional or narrative concessions. Among Canadian crime-dramas, it was the most intelligent since Intelligence. The publicity for the shows second season, now halfway through its six-episode run, has likened it to the best American cable series. Ive no wish to be unpatriotic but that's going too far. Durham County is intense, but nobody could call it rich. Probably the HBO series it most resembles -- in being domestic rather than panoramic -- is Six Feet Under: my least favourite of the bunch, but it did have a sense of humour. Durham County, mining a narrow seam, comes over as gratuitously creepy.

Or so I thought about Season 1. Season 2's story grabs me more and so do its characters, both new and continuing. Its something very rare in television: a genuine sequel. Most returning series merely rearrange the same old pieces on the same old board. But Durham County ended its first round with plenty of unfinished business, which the second is duly moving forward. Season 1 depended far too much on the relationship between Mike and Ray, boyhood friends as well as present neighbours, and on Rays reiterated claim that he

and Mike were really brothers under the skin: a proposition not nearly as fascinating as either Ray or the show seemed to think it was.

In Season 2, Ray is awaiting trial; he still exercises a malign influence on Mike and especially on Sadie, but he does it in short bursts and from a distance, which is far more effective. There's a new actor, Romano Orzari, playing Ray this season; the show's obsessive use of dreams, usually nightmares, and flashback means that he and his predecessor, Justin Louis, sometimes turn up in the same episode. Meanwhile Hugh Dillons shaven-headed, bat-eared Mike continues to be loaded with issues; he may not be a killer but he can be frighteningly violent. Dillon does a good three-way stretch between thug, father and sleuth, and a two-way as unfaithful but caring -- or maybe just guilty -- spouse.

He has, this time, a worthier antagonist, whos also his lover: Dr. Pen Verity (Michelle Forbes, TV's ubiquitous Lady Macbeth-in-waiting), a forensic psychiatrist whose young daughter drowned and who holds on to her son with what her estranged husband (the excellent Geordie Johnson) holds to be dangerous possessiveness. Pen is a consummate liar, and also a consummate fantasist, with a death wish for herself and others. This makes her a twin to Ray, and even more dangerous.

In some ways the central character is Sadie, herself a resourceful amateur detective with professional aspirations (Mike thinks she's better than he is), still traumatized by the assault, tortured when she starts doubting her dad and, just to round things off, wrestling with the assailants son being her boyfriend. Laurence Leboeuf is first-rate in the role of Sadie, as is Helene Joy, in the comparatively placid role of her mother.

With the exception of one goodcop(most sympathetically played by Patrick Labbe), Laurie Finstad Knizhniks script seems to regard the entire male sex as actual or incipient gangbangers; the female villain at least has a ghastly past to excuse her. It's a shock, of the wrong kind, when the plot suddenly turns out to be governed by events that happened 20 years ago; it plays like desperation. Conversely, key elements from Season 1 seem to have been forgotten: Nobody appears concerned to track down the original double murderer, and no one mentions that Ray's first victim was Mike's almost-lover. Some ambiguities are rewarding; others seem merely tight-lipped. But I'm sucked in.

- Durham County airs Mondays at 9 p. m. on The Movie Network.
