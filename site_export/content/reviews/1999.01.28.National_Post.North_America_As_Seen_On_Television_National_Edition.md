SPIRITS

Bathurst Street Theatre, Toronto

There are eight people on stage during Spirits, four of them actors, and four musicians. On the night of my visit, there were exactly the same number in the auditorium. Several of the performers were also involved in the writing and staging, and one's heart naturally went out to them in all capacities. Which is ironic, because our hearts don't seem to be what this piece is angling for. I reclaimed my own fairly early in the bleak, 90-minute proceedings.

The show is a through-composed mini-opera. A girl goes out on the town. She is beset in a bar by two guys -- one smooth, one awkward - - who ply her with booze, very much with her own connivance. She drinks too much, chokes on her own vomit, and dies. Her father finds out and kills her killers. End of story.

Spirits, announces Peter Reitzel -- author, director, designer, actor, and program-note writer -- "is a show about North America." He goes on to say that he isn't concerned with the real North America, but with the image of it that might be conceived by some outsider -- in Eastern Europe, say -- who had been steadily ingesting schlock Western television. It turns out, by a sort of sleight of mind, that this is the real North America after all. We produce and consume this stuff, and by our dreams we shall be known.

Everybody who has written about this show -- except, to be fair, its author -- has invoked the names of Bertolt Brecht and Kurt Weill, and I'd like to join the party while questioning its logic. The Brecht-Weill operas of the early '30s were typically set in a fantasy Chicago, or a fantasy London, that its authors had constructed from bits of old plays, current movies, and dime novels. Stuck in Berlin, they naturally presented a Berliner's view of Anglo- American culture.

It's rather different to sit in Toronto and imagine how somebody thousands of kilometres away might respond to your society, rather than responding to it yourself. I don't think there should be a law against it, but I do suggest that it adds more levels of artifice than anyone is likely to need.

Spirits is not big on dramatic coherence -- that much it has in common with Brecht and Weill, though not with schlock TV -- and it never tells us, for example, how the avenging father discovered the identities of the guilty men, or how he tracked them down.

It does find time, though, for the actor (who happens to be the author) to turn to the audience and say, in effect, "Wasn't that exciting? I bet you didn't recognize me under my disguise." In other words, we're being chastised for lapping up suspense and mayhem instead of concentrating on the ruined, empty lives and the burning moral issues that are implicit in what we have just seen -- for behaving, in fact, like any dumb TV audience.

Well, we weren't, and you know something? TV audiences don't either. Even the most crass crime series makes its audience aware of the society that encloses these events; even when it isn't brought sanctimoniously to our attention, as it often is, it's still built into the structure of the story.

Alienation -- the deliberate breaking down of dramatic illusion - - can be a titillating theatrical effect in its own right, but it's kicking at an open door; it presupposes that there is such a thing as realistic drama, which there isn't, and that audiences completely surrender to it, which they don't. (The moment you say how good or bad the acting is, you've shattered the illusion -- and it's the first thing people always say.)

It also licences bad performance. If the cast of Spirits don't sing well, if two of them can't even be heard above the band -- well, that just keeps us on our toes. The exception here is Dennis Frey, whose characterization of the lumpier lover is excellent. He's also the vocal arranger and, with Christopher Cumming, co-composer.

The tunes are agonizingly limited, though Sam Allison's sharp, shrill orchestrations give them some edge. The lyrics are marginally better: "tonight I'll score / with some young whore" is an effective couplet in its own way, but that's the best it gets. The constriction may be a comment on the media's contempt for people, or it may just be down to the author's: I don't see how an audience could tell the difference.

Visually, the show is very assured; the design capitalizes on the dilapidation of the theatre itself; a set done in stylish sackcloth falls away at the end to reveal a piercing black vision of the beyond.

The play proper is preceded by a long vaudeville section -- a comedian who gets the hook, a conjurer who gets some laughs -- all intentionally tacky, so that we will know the show itself is just a show. The author disavows any intention to preach, but he does nag.
