OUTRAGEOUS

Canadian Stage, Berkeley Street Theatre

Brad Fraser's musical Outrageous begins with an elaborate company number called Carnival Country. It covers a lot of ground, scenically and otherwise, and it goes on long enough for my feelings about it to have progressed through three separate stages.

Its a busy item, scenically and musically, designed to start the show with a bang while setting up story, atmosphere and characters. First off, I was seduced by its high-powered theatricality. After a while, my enthusiasm was dampened by the realization that Joey Millers music, though kicky, was basic imitation Broadway. And after a longer while I decided that this didn't matter, that it was good at what it did, and that it perfectly supported what was going on among the actors.

The show is loosely based (via a film whose cult I haven't joined) on the early career of Craig Russell, the late Canadian female impersonator, and this number introduces him and the drag- queen world in which hes going to move. It also introduces us to the other principal character, a schizophrenic young woman we see gaining her discharge from the mental ward. The speed and clarity with which the focus shifts from one narrative to the other, and the energy and lucidity with which both are infused, is exhilarating.

You feel that if they can keep this up, the show may turn into the great Canadian musical. They cant, and it doesn't, but it's still the most exciting attempt Ive seen.

The energy keeps going, pretty much non-stop, and so -- in strict plot-synopsis terms -- does the action. The things that go missing are character and conflict. Russell has here been renamed Robin: a hairdresser with, at the outset, unsuspected talents. Liza, the schizophrenic, arrives unexpectedly at his apartment and moves in with him. Before you know it they are friends, the closest, for life.

Supposedly, this is the shows central relationship, but it proves unexpectedly hard to dramatize. Fraser, as author and director, promises in a program note that his will be no ordinary musical. And -- after the usual patronizing (and irrelevant) guff about chandeliers and helicopters -- he proceeds to swear off easy endings and "simple answers."

I don't know who he thinks he's kidding -- himself, probably -- but he concludes his first act with a syrupy song in which Robin and Liza pledge eternal devotion and his second with another, more mistily philosophical, in which they do the same thing. In other words, he offers us standard musical-comedy sentiments, and it makes no difference that the people concerned arent actually lovers. The show presents plenty of graphically simulated sex (of all persuasions) and lots of language, but in essence -- compared to a recent serious musical such as Kiss of the Spider Woman or an older one such as Carousel -- its kids' stuff.

The demons that reportedly possessed the real Russell have here been exorcised. This Robin is a nice young man, understandably shy at first and a little unsure of his talent, but once it's released, theres no stopping him. (He does full impersonations, outpacing his early colleagues, who stick with lip-synchs.) He has in the course of the show to make two crucial decisions, and theyre both no- brainers. The first is whether to go to New York, where he's been offered a club booking. The second is whether to return to Toronto, given that work in the Big Apple has temporarily dried up, and that Liza needs him. She has a taste, indiscriminately indulged, for casual flings with taxi drivers, and has had a miscarriage.

Robin has also been crossed in love: His manager, a bisexual cowboy stud, has become involved with the girl-friend of a lesbian club owner. But the situation carries no weight, because the depth of the two men's relationship has never been established. (Maybe they should have a duet.)

Liza is constantly besieged by people -- friends, family, social workers -- who want her re-committed, theres more obvious drama in her life than there is in Robins, but there's less obvious music. Robin -- whose life is glitz, feeding on more glitz -- is always good for a number, and so are his friends. A number called Have a Nice Day points the contrast, rather too acutely.

A succession of drag queens invade Robin and Liza's apartment, and what they sing is neat, pointed and funny. Liza has some very straight visitors, and the attempt to musicalize their shocked-but- polite reactions is embarrassingly clunky. Im not here to give advice, but it strikes me the number would work much better if the showbiz people sang and the civilians simply talked. This isnt opera, and they don't have music in them.

Miller proves better at composing set songs, which are competent if not striking, than at putting conversation to music. Maybe the latter, despite appearances, is the harder job. He and Fraser are jointly credited with the lyrics (Miller's are additional) and they are indeed uneven. Sometimes theyre doggedly prosaic; others, they positively dance along, with only the occasional bum rhyme to remind us that civilization is dead. Everything comes to a head in the title song, which is complicated.

Robin is playing the lead in a musical about Joan of Arc seen as a drag queen. Its obviously a terrible show, but our show's spoof of it -- with hooded priests denouncing Joan as "outrageous" -- is wonderful.

But we also keep switching back to Liza, who is experiencing similar pressures in Toronto and for real, and these bits are terrible. And yet the good parts of the song couldn't exist without the bad, so we have to accept them and -- thanks in part to Sergio Trujillos whirlwind choreography -- we do.

The staging throughout is first-rate: fast and witty and scary. Fraser has always been able to write funny, and he has directed his cast with such style and point that every joke lands.

Thom Allison is a magnetic Robin, strong and supple and sympathetic. His impersonations, most of them cannily saved up till late in the show, are vocally variable (he does Carol Channing very well, but shes probably easy). In manner and style and appearance, though, they're impeccable, with his Judy Garland especially, even painfully, acute.

Lorretta Bailey's Liza may be even better: very economical, with an unnerving, and clinically accurate, directness of approach. Both performers are finely served by their designer: David Boechlers costumes, obviously all-important, do great things for Robin and for the atmosphere in general. His set, a symphony in string, provides a menacing environment for Lizas lurking mania. Hands reach out at her from within its webbed recesses, simulating the entrapping voices in her head. (There's supposed to be a parallel between these voices and those of the divas waiting to be released from inside Robin. Joan of Arc ties the idea up but, frankly, I wouldn't have known if I hadnt been told.)

The performances dont stop with the stars. There's a special joy in a show in which everyone gets a chance and takes it, while remaining loyally within the framework. It's called ensemble, and this is the best example Toronto has seen since Platonov.

Timothy Murphy and Ed Saheely are riots as Robin's early partners- in-drag and permanent friends; Sharron Matthews and Karen Leblanc electrically tough as the New Yorkers; Tim Sell a tower of ambiguous strength as the manager; and Tim Howar unshakably insistent as a fellow patient of Liza's whose presence threatens to send her back over the edge.

Tamara Bernier and Susan Henley do more than might be expected with the ordinary people, and everybody doubles and trebles with furious devotion.

The show is too long and too sentimental -- outrageously so, in fact -- but it grabs from the start, and never really lets go.
