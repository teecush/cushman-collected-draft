MY MOTHER'S COURAGE

THEATRE REVIEW

MY MOTHER'S COURAGE

Monday, March 17, 1997

Written by George Tabori

Directed by Jennifer H. Capraru


  


Starring Elizabeth Shepherd,


  


Richard Partington, Bruce Alexander Pitkin,


  


Margaret Lamarre and Tom Barnett


  


Rating: ***½

Jennifer Capraru's direction of George Tabori's play,

about the playwright's mother who must face the enemy,

is spare and superb.

Special to The Globe and Mail


  



  


ELIZABETH Shepherd's performance in the title role of My Mother's Courage is magnificent, possibly the finest piece of acting I have seen in Canada. Before exploring it I had better sketch in the background.

In his more than 80 years of life, the playwright George Tabori has lived and worked in half a dozen countries. The first was Hungary from which he escaped when Nazism loomed. His father was arrested and gassed; his mother too was taken but survived. My Mother's Courage is Tabori's dramatization of her miraculous escape. She had extraordinary luck, but the miracle was within her.

A modestly elegant, self-effacing Jewish lady, Elsa Tabori is picked up one morning in a Budapest street (by, in this telling, a couple of superannuated Keystone Kops), taken to a railway terminal crammed with human fear, misery and bewilderment, and herded into a cattle car. At the border they are to be transferred from a Hungarian train to a German one. At the checkpoint, Elsa finds herself propelled (almost literally) into the presence of the supervising German officer. Through her terror, she senses a discord between him and his Magyar subordinate and insinuates herself within it. She tells a story that the German chooses to believe. He sends her back home. The evening finds her in the apartment of her sister and brother-in-law, which is where she had been going that morning when she was arrested. She has been through the entire Holocaust experience, or a good part of it, in a single day, and come out the other side.

In his voluminous writings on the camps, George Steiner, the cultural critic, has repeatedly emphasized one of their salient horrors: that they existed in the same time frame as what we call normal life and that a victim could slip in a moment from one world to the other. This play makes it real. Mother Tabori acts out her account on stage (presumably as she told it to son George), and he, also on stage, introduces, narrates and comments on it. Does he ever. The play is partly about their relationship as mother and son, and even more as author and subject. It's also, I suppose, about the impossibility of dramatizing the events it depicts. This last point the play comes uncomfortably close to proving. The Mother complains that George, clever as he is, distorts things by embellishing them; and one often wants to throw something at him, to make him shut up.

At Theatre Passe Muraille the problem is compounded. On the first night, Richard Partington, who plays the son and apparently came into the production late, was not on top of his lines; there are so many of them, and they are so literary. Even without his obvious insecurity, the bland geniality of his performance might have seemed overdone: too much for the space, too much for the play. For the familiar lesser horrors of the beginning, such as the yellow star and the neighbourly insults, the script employs a tiresome cartoon style, to which the narrator liberally contributes. As the grip tightens, though, we are increasingly able to tune him out. We take in the facts and discard the mannerisms.

Jennifer H. Capraru's direction is otherwise spare and superb, visually and emotionally. Bruce Alexander Pitkin and Margaret Lamarre between them play a variety of victims and persecutors with wit and precision. Tom Barnett gives a troubled and troubling sympathy to the merciful Nazi who will balk at eating meat, speak sorrowfully of anti-Semitic incidents in his home town, and spare one woman (and order her a hot meal) while dispatching thousands of others in squalor to be slaughtered.

In the play's great speech, the Mother reflects on her mingled love and hate for this man. She had caught his attention, established herself in his mind as an individual, by fixing him with her still-beautiful blue eyes, but now she says, "beware of looking the enemy in the eye, my darling, or you might stop hating him and thus betray the dead." (She too can only hate people in the mass.)

One of the glories of Elizabeth Shepherd's performance is that she can play this passage for anger, with no hint of weakness and no drive for sympathy, and still move you with it: to tears in my case. This is authentic passion, experience distilled. Technically, the performance is immaculate; within its first two minutes she is required to sigh fatalistically in half a dozen keys, and she keeps each modulation distinct, appropriate, and, given the circumstances, hilarious. Physically she is perfect: a comfortable, golden-haired woman with more bloom, outside and in, than she might suspect. (Her journey includes a furtive and bizarre sexual encounter.) She affects no heavy accent, but her voice still resonates with what we take to be Middle European wisdom.

The play's title is, of course, a joke on Bertolt Brecht; and Tabori has been a leading Brechtian in his time. He is still using some of the techniques; and the play proves, what I have long suspected, that "alienation" is not a means of denying emotion but of questioning, channelling, and finally strengthening it. Some of the tricks are irritating, but the story transcends them. What Tabori is finally saying about his mother is that he never knew she had it in her; and she, whatever their differences, would agree.

Here, we have an added dimension; I have always respected Shepherd's acting, though I would never before have called her great. But, as with the character, the situation has called something forth. Our first sight is of her carrying a candle, presumably for remembrance, with grave, silent authority. The image returns at the end, and it's a consummation.


  


My Mother's Courage is on at Theatre Passe Muraille's Backspace, through March 30.
