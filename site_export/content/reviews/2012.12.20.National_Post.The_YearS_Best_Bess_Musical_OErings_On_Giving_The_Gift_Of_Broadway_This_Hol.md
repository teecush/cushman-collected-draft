There are frightfully few days before the holiday season takes full effect, but still just enough shopping hours to get the Broadway lover in your circle a few of 2012's best offerings.

For starters, this year saw a Broadway production billed as The Gershwins' Porgy and Bess - a curious title since it omitted the name of DuBose Heyward, on whose novel Porgy the opera was based and who also wrote most of the libretto, with Ira Gershwin contributing only a few of the lyrics. Perhaps, more importantly, the title misrepresented the nature of the show itself.

The Gershwins' (and Heyward's) Porgy and Bess was an opera - an opera built of show music. This production, with new dialogue supplied in place of the original recitative and new orchestrations to replace the ones George Gershwin wrote himself, turned it into a musical.

I can't judge the success of the staging, since I didn't see it. I expected that on the cast album (from the in-valuable U.S. label PS Classics) the alterations would be unnoticeable, and I'd just hear a rendition of the score with Audra McDonald being wonderful as Bess. She does indeed sound superb, but the changes around her are impossible to ignore or to approve, literally from the start.

Gershwin wrote an instrumental prelude; here we get an overture compiled from the score's greatest hits, followed by a Summertime that's been changed from a solo lullaby to a duet with chorus, thereby losing most of its poignancy. The new script by the dramatist Suzan-Lori Parks seems, as far as I could tell from the copious extracts supplied, to have devoted itself to solving problems that don't exist: giving the drug peddler Sportin' Life a backstory, for example.

Out of context, the Porgy songs can sound great when given a jazz or pop treatment; those, after all, are the traditions Gershwin came from. But when sung as part of the score, the choppy staccato treatment that Norm Lewis affords to I Got Plenty o' Nuttin' actually sounds less hip than the buoyant, flowing original. He doesn't have the vocal heft for Porgy, and the adaptation makes it worse by trying to dignify him. The opera's Porgy was a cripple in a goat cart; this one merely walks with a cane. The women mostly come off better than the men, and the ensemble work is excellent.

Porgy and Bess was Gershwin's last work for the theatre. One of his first - indeed his first book musical on Broadway - was Sweet Little Devil (1924), written with his principal pre-Ira lyricist, the preternaturally hard-boiled B.G. (Buddy) DeSylva. A success in its time, it's never been revived. PS Classics has, like Porgy and Bess, now given it its premiere recording, the latest in this label's string of rescues of lost, loopy musicals (Gershwin and other) for which they have perfected the right high style: insouciance with a sting.

This one begins with a threatened strike by Ziegfeld chorus girls, without whose gorgeous pins, as they accurately sing, "The Follies won't have a leg to stand on." From here it abruptly switches to a typical '20s tale of ingenues and hucksters, except that the ingenues aren't that ingenuous and the handsome hero is as much mocked as celebrated.

The most arresting number, Matrimonial Handicap, has a couple of couples placing odds on their chances of a happy married future, expressing something of the mood of Company in the imagery of Guys and Dolls. None of the songs is a neglected classic, but together they add up to something very engaging. It's performed by a cast of musical revival reliables including Rebecca Luker, Philip Chaffin and Danny Burstein, who was a terrific Buddy in last season's Follies and here plays a dodgy operator with amorous delusions.

Songs from the Jazz Age - which doesn't necessarily mean jazz songs - are the meat of Diana Krall's latest CD, Glad Rag Doll, whose selections from the 1920s and early '30s include one treat I was delighted to discover You Know - I Know, Ev'rything's Made for Love. Krall can be breathily sad, as on the title track, or twangily cheery, as on the opener We Just Couldn't Say Goodbye, in which her tongue seems only just contained within her cheek. The guitar-heavy production (by T-Bone Burnett) lends the album a bluegrass feel, and also a certain sameness; it's probably best listened to a few tracks at a time.

The supporting musicians include one Howard Coward, a.k.a. Mr. Elvis Costello Krall, who is featured on mandola (there's versatility for you) and voice, supplying a fairground showman's intro to his wife's enjoyably doomy closing vocal, When the Curtain Comes Down. Composers' names, important to some of us especially on this kind of collection, are given in inexcusably small print.

Brian Stokes Mitchell, the original Coalhouse Walker of Ragtime and now one of Broadway's leading men, pays tribute to some of his predecessors and their medium on Simply Broadway. And it is simple, just him and a pianist, the terrific Todd Firth. It can be disconcerting when Mitchell tries to sound English (How to Handle a Woman) or Jewish (If I Were a Rich Man), at least in the spoken bits; when he sings he gets right inside the characters, accents and all.

Indeed he acts all the songs, but without overacting them; except for atrocities such as Stars (from Les Miserables) or The Impossible Dream, where it's probably unavoidable. He finds a snarky Manhattan tone for Sorry-Grateful, while his It Ain't Necessarily So is hugely better than the one on the Porgy album, both as characterization and as sheer vocal. He ends proceedings with Leonard Bernstein's Some Other Time, the best possible way to end anything.
