THEATRE REVIEW

HOUSE

BUDDIES IN BAD TIMES THEATRE

- - -

Daniel MacIvor's House begins with just a chair on stage. Well, it is billed as a stand up, sit down, one man comedy nightmare. When the author-performer, the one man in question, walks on the stage, the first thing he does is pick up the chair and throw it offstage. He then walks off himself and returns with another chair, a straight-backed thing indistinguishable from its predecessor. This doesnt meet his standards either; he flings it away, walks off again and returns with a third, again identical. And this time - - obeying the comedians sacred Rule of Three--he does sit down on it and starts talking. He is standing up again pretty soon; there is, it turns out, more standup than sit-down in this routine. The sedentary position would hardly do justice to the character's rage.

So what else is new? This revived play, marking positively the last appearance of MacIvor's production company da da kamera, is another of MacIvors studies in anger and loneliness. Its the most concentrated of them, the least complicated apart from a few sequences where the hero's misery train gets derailed for some spookily-lit monologues within the monologue, and probably the most entertaining. MacIvor plays Victor, who works for a septic-tank company. This isn't what he wanted to do with his life. He wanted to be an engineer. But like the man in the Peter Cook sketch who wanted to be a judge but "didnt

have the Latin" and so went down the mines instead, Victor has been mis-routed by circumstance. In this case the circumstance is a hysterically controlling mother, determined that her son shall go into septic tanks like his father before him. His father isnt still in septic tanks; he got out to go work for a circus -- actually it sounds more like a fairground--as the Saddest Man in the World. One way and another, Victor seems to have inherited a lot from him.

Vic also tells us about his sister who has a houseful of dogs, with

MacIvor controls the play with one of which she's in love. He also speaks of his wife, who is also his third cousin and who despises him for his job and, probably, for other reasons. She also seems to have a secret career, secret from Victor anyway, as a dominatrix. And Vic, with good reason, belongs to a therapy group presided over by a cheerful soul to whom he refers to as Joseph- call-me- Joe-and-I-won't. (This recurring phrase is both funny in itself and a sign of the heros obsessiveness.) Vics best friend in the group is a kleptomaniac. And he isn't happy at work because, not only is it not what he wants to do, but everybody there ignores him, especially his boss.

MacIvor controls the play, under Daniel Brooks's direction, with crack timing and a gimlet eye. The play is one of his earliest and he seems to have updated it a little. When welcoming the audience -- hes very upfront about the fact that were all in a theatre -- he congratulates us on not having stayed home to watch "a TV show about an angry doctor." That reference to the other House must be newly added, but it ties in very well with one of the main script's chief devices: a violent spasm, coupled with a shout, that overcomes the speaker every time the word "house" crops up in his text. Finally, we get to his issues with his own house; these turn out to be crucial and to bring together elements from both his marriage and his job.

This cumulative section, though, is the weakest part of the play. There's too much that strains credulity, even given Vics lavish capacity for self-deception. The play is stronger on anecdote than on narrative. The details are sometimes scary and sometimes the best kind of funny, the kind that takes you totally by surprise, while also being totally logical. There is also an epiphany, whose intended seriousness I wouldnt want to guess at and to which your response will depend on your taste in epiphanies. Anyway the production, crowning da da kamera's final retrospective, is a testimony to MacIvor's high talent, and a stimulus to wondering where he will take it from here.
