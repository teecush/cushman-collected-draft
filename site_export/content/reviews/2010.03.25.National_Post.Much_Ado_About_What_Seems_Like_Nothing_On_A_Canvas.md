Just closed at the Jane Mallet Theatre in the St. Lawrence Centre: Talk, a two-character play in which a friendship is compromised by the mention of the word "Palestine." Just opened at the Bluma Appel Theatre in the St. Lawrence Centre: Art, a three-character play in which a friendship is compromised by the mention of the word "art." For a few days, two shows on essentially the same theme coexisted under the same roof, the similarities enhanced by the fact that both are conversation pieces in which -- in these productions at least -- spotlights isolate the speakers when they switch from dialogue to monologue.

Yasmina Reza's Art is a widely produced play, a hit in Paris, London and New York and probably qualifying by now as a modern classic, while Talk is a newish piece by a little-known Canadian playwright. I don't want to use the former to bash the latter but I will say that Art wins by miles on sheer flexibility. Having three characters instead of two immediately makes it static rather than dynamic. It also has a subject -- or rather a metaphor -- that allows those characters to duck, weave and reveal themselves.

The start of Art comes when Serge, a dermatologist, spends thousands of francs on a painting: an all-white canvas that may, depending on who's looking at it, have a couple of white lines on it. This purchase arouses not just the mockery but the anger of his friend Marc, an engineer of conservative aesthetic views who distrusts just about anything that could be called "modern art" and becomes positively apoplectic at the mention of the word "deconstruction." (Bear in mind that the play is now at least 15 years old. Nothing dates faster than modernism. Except of course postmodernism.) Marc, who regarded himself as Serge's mentor, now feels personally betrayed.

Serge is married, Marc divorced. The play's third party is Yvan, who once worked in textiles, is now in stationery, and is about to get married himself. He isn't very happy about any of these things, and his situation is made worse by the fact that his two friends, on whom he is emotionally dependent, are now at loggerheads. He doesn't know which to side with and, trying to please both, inevitably ends up pleasing neither. But this worm can still turn, though he does himself a mild physical injury in the protest. He obviously doesn't care much about the painting either way, but even for Marc and Serge it becomes less text than pretext. The three men fall to badmouthing one another's spouses -- past, present or prospective -- but even that's a diversion; the rifts that are now revealed were always incipiently there. The men manage to patch up their differences or, more exactly, to paper them over, but it's clear that this only works through a process of willed self-deception.

Art isn't about art, but it is art, of a kind: Like Serge's painting, it's a plain surface on which we can project our own interpretations. It isn't deep, but it's smooth, as its characters group and regroup, each reflexively fighting his own corner.

Morris Panych's production isn't the most emotional I've seen, but it is the funniest, triumphantly so in the case of Evan Buliung's unconscionably uncomfortable and perpetually pleading Yvan. His long-winding wound-up complaint about the impossibility of wording a wedding invitation to please every member of both families always brings down the house; here it stops the show as well, the first speech to get mid-scene applause since Ben Carlson's great tirade in Man and Superman. He's as good, if less spectacular, when retailing gnomic advice from his shrink about the perils of letting yourself be defined by other people: counsel of which the entire trio stand in need, and on which none of them can act.

The only flaw in Buliung's performance, a venial one, is that he seems too young to have been the lifelong buddy of the other two. Peter Donaldson lays on Marc's vulnerability at the expense of his presumed vulnerability, but his presence is undoubtedly felt. Colin Mochrie, the quietest of the three, is a formidably placid and then surgingly indignant Serge. Panych's use of between-scenes video, showing the guys shuttlingbetweenoneanother's residences, hardly seems necessary; but once they've arrived he places them to maximum comic and dramatic effect, on and around the Le Corbusier furniture that they unavoidably have in common. Ken Macdonald's set design still allows them some measure of individuality through the light fixtures in the ceiling and, of course, the pictures on the walls. And the videos do remind us that we're in Paris: the only place in the world where people dispute with the same passion, the same flippancy, and virtually in the same breath, on the meaning of life and where they're going for dinner.
