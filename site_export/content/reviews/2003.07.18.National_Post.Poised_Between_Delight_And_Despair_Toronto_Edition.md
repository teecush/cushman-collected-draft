HAPPY DAYS

Premiere Dance Theatre, Toronto

- - -

"That is what I find so wonderful," says Winnie, the heroine of Samuel Beckett's Happy Days, "a part remains of one's classics to help one through the day." I have that line handy because it is reproduced at the head of the press release for the new Soulpepper season, of which Happy Days is the second triumphant production. As a slogan for an ambitious theatre company, it strikes me as disingenuous. The classics, as experienced by Winnie, are a few literary fragments disconnected from their sources, part of the mental and physical detritus of her life. If they help her through the day, that isn't necessarily a compliment to them.

Or maybe it is. The distinction of Beckett's vision, certainly in this play, is that it's pitiless without being heartless. Winnie, a presumably middle-class woman of dainty tastes, sits buried in a mound of earth -- up to her waist in the first act and to her neck in the second. Plainly she is being encroached upon, by time and by the routines of her existence. A program note says this is "considered by many to be Beckett's most cheerful play," but if that's true it suggests that those "many" have a talent for optimism equal to Winnie's own. The play may well contain Beckett's most cheerful character, but that's not the same.

It's another presentation of the things that we do in order, as one of the tramps in Godot put it, "to give ourselves the impression we exist." The things in Winnie's life may be even more disturbing than those in the lives of Vladimir and Estragon, since her lifestyle is closer than theirs to what most of us are likely to experience as quotidian reality. Winnie's day starts with the ringing of an alarm clock -- at a volume that scares the living daylights out of the audience, but Winnie takes it in stride. (Or would, if she were in any position to have a stride.) Once awakened, her day revolves around a few simple props: a parasol, which at one exciting moment catches fire, a capacious handbag, a gun -- this last apparently confiscated from her husband.

The pleasures she takes in these possessions have, she realizes, to be carefully parcelled out; if she got through all of them too soon, who knows what emptiness would engulf her? The bag and its contents, in particular, are treats to be carefully rationed out. And she has to judge, very carefully, the time to sing her song. Apparently she knows only one, The Merry Widow Waltz; the second time it's appeared in a play in two weeks.

The play unfolds in two dimensions: the metaphysical one, presenting a cross-section of Winnie's existence, one day very like another; and the temporal one, tracing the progress of a relationship. I mentioned Winnie's husband. His name is Willie and he apparently lives behind the mound. We hear little from him and see less. But the back of his bald head occasionally pops up, while his wife admonishes him on how to protect himself from the sun and how best to re-enter his burrow. (Backwards, on all fours.) Occasionally he reads ads from the newspaper, chosen for their sardonic content ("smart boy wanted") or replies, even more sardonically, to Winnie's incessant questioning: Asked to define a hog he replies, with feeling, "castrated male swine."

The end of the play finds Willie putting in a more extended personal appearance, appearing in front of the mound in evening dress, then crawling up it, encouraged by an expectant if somewhat disparaging Winnie. ("Not quite the crawler you were, Willie. Not quite the crawler I gave my heart to.") Whether he is trying to get at her, as she excitedly anticipates, or at the gun, is a moot point. Whether, if the latter is the case, he intends to use it on himself or on her is even mooter.

Michael Simpson plays Willie perfectly, but the play of course belongs to its Winnie, Martha Burns. I approached her performance with some trepidation since, though I have always respected Burns' acting for its intelligence and imagination, I have often felt these qualities betrayed by a lack of vocal colour, a tendency to droop and hence to make every line come out the same. Unexpectedly, this matters far less in the context of what is virtually a monologue. Here, she can make her own rules and set her own rhythms. Her timing is consummate; she is the wittiest Winnie I have seen as well, incidentally, as the comeliest. She is also, behind her debonair smile, the most frightened, and therefore the bravest; the most conscious of the things she is fighting not to recognize. Her recurring mantra -- "This is going to be another happy day" -- is, like her whole performance, beautifully poised between delight and despair. And -- though the author must of course take ultimate credit for this -- the sight of her face sticking out of the earth as the lights go out on Act Two is irresistibly funny.

Some of the lightness of touch can presumably be attributed to Vikki Anderson, who is both the director and designer. The mound, as she has envisioned it, is an extension of Winnie herself; it spreads out around her like a hoop-skirt. There is also a false proscenium, which points up Winnie's own occasional awareness of being a character in what was once an avant-garde play. She talks about a couple who came to look at her and asked what she was supposed to mean, stuck there like that -- the kind of joke Beckett had already played in Endgame. Anderson may also have speeded up the play by cutting bits of it. The end comes very abruptly, and I missed the line in which Winnie exhorts the laboriously climbing Willie to "put a little jizz in it."

Some streamlining, though, is forgivable. Line by line the play, like just about all of Beckett, is perfectly judged, but it pushes its one image excessively hard. You forgive it, because the metaphor is so strong, but you get the point early and you don't have the (comparatively) huge casts of the earlier plays to compensate. Nor do you have the ferocious economy of the later playlets, to which this one looks forward. "What a curse, mobility," says Winnie, apparently speaking for her author, who was to supply his actors with progressively less room for manoeuvring.

To end where I begun, a Beckettian thing to do: The word "classic" is applicable here, not in the general sense of something rather old, but as denoting a particular style, the one that's the opposite of "romantic" and whose gift is to find joy in rigour. Soulpepper, with No Man's Land and Happy Days, has now richly delivered on that style twice in two weeks. Next, the sternest test, is Phedre, as classic as plays come.
