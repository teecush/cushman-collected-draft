Plays about rival poets are clustering on the Toronto scene. We have Moliere, which gives us an undeclared conflict between the eponymous comic genius and Racine, the tragedian who wants him dead. And we have Sylvia Plath Must Not Die, where the contenders are the suicidal Sylvia and Anne Sexton, her friend and fellow scribbler, who wants her alive. Or at least who wishes that Sylvia would refrain from suicide until she herself has committed it. She doesn't want her thunder stolen.

In this production, brought to us by the esteemed One Yellow Rabbit troupe from Calgary, Plath and Sexton share the stage with their respective husbands, Ted Hughes -- also of course a poet -- and Kayo Sexton -- emphatically not. "Is this going to be depressing?" Kayo asks Ted at the start of proceedings, disarmingly voicing the fears of everyone in the audience.

It turns out not to be depressing, at least not much, but it isn't very enlightening, either. Basically, it's a poetry recital, with the two heroines -- seated in their off-duty moments on twin Muskoka chairs -- taking turns at performing their own verses, interspersed with someprosejoustings with the twomen. Verses apart, the sources seem to be Plath's journals interspersed, I would guess, with brief linking sequences of the company's own inventions. Within these limitations, the production is up to One Yellow Rabbit's established standards of visual panache. Much of the credit for this must go to Denise Clarke, doubling as choreographer (her actual credit is "staged by," but same difference, I think) and Sextonimpersonator. The series of pas de deux she has contrived, done mainly to a Miles Davis record, gives graceful expression to the tensions and occasional rapprochements. The most seductive image she contributes, though, is her own as an elongated and inebriated Anne, sashaying and swivelling across the stage in an elegantly revealing black-and-white number, poetry in erratic but irresistible motion.

She also makes a good fist of making the spoken poetry flesh, though not quite good enough. She has a lot of style, but doesn't convey much substance. Making non-dramatic verse into viable theatrical language is always the Devil's own job, certainly over the course of a whole evening. There Reigns Love, Simon Callow's biographical arrangement of Shakespeare's sonnets seen this year at Stratford, was a case of the actor biting off more than the audience could chew. The poems of Sexton and Plath are more accessible, if only because they're more recent, but their cumulative effect is deadening. Either the poems are too dense or I am.

Clarke's attractions apart, the Sexton poems may work better because they seem directed at someone, and from a frank and engaging point of view, that of a free-wheeling promiscuous alcoholic. Plath's are far more tightly wound and inward-driven--more self-pitying, to put it unkindly -- and Onalea Gilbertson, fetchingly if primly adorned with an orange Alice headband, doesn't begin to bring them to life. She's muted until she gets to the showpiece poem Daddy, a dodgy enough piece to begin with, what with its comparison of the poet's father-and-husband problems to the Holocaust, but one that has been known to work theatrically as a kind of incantation. Here it's shouted, coming across as stridently sulky.

Sympathy, to the extent it's aroused at all, swings over to Hughes, a much-maligned figure in any case and played here by Michael Green with persuasive North of England pawkiness --meaning he comes across as earthy and puzzled. Andy Curtis' Kayo is not just puzzled but bewildered, a salesman by trade and agreeably all-American. Their scenes together presumably fall into the what-if category: conjectural but believable. The foursome seems to have been an ill-assorted couple of couples, Kayo and Sylvia monogamous, Ted and Anne anything but. It's doubtful, though, that they would have been any happier had they changed partners. - Sylvia Plath Must Not Die is at the Young Centre for the Performing Arts until Dec. 13.
