Our first sight at My Mothers Feet is the welcome one of Tom Rooney, talking and, from time to time, blinking at us. He does both with a shy charm whose potency is increased when he adds to the mix a conspiratorial, self-deprecating smile.

Rooney, who last year went triumphantly from Hamlet to Hairspray, is here playing a man named Dan. On the floor by his side stand, strangely, are a pair of prosthetic feet. They are intended, he tells us, for his mother, who has lost the feet she was born with. He proceeds to riff on this subject, firing off a series of apparently heartless puns concerning what Fats Waller would have called the old ladys "pedal extremities. It appears she was attached to them for more than the usual reasons, or at least Dan's imagination makes it seem so. She was a very active and controlling person, and as soon as he was old enough, he used his own feet to get as far away from her as possible. She is now, he says, de-feet- ed." The outrageousness of this culminating zinger induces mixed feelings -- I think I let out an admiring groan. Which is to say: The joke is terrible but it makes its point.

This opening sequence establishes what the rest of the play confirms: that Gina Wilkinson -- a first-time playwright already known as an actress and, to a lesser extent, as a director -- has a striking if erratic way with words, one that compels attention without always rewarding it. The play's ideas are thinly stretched over its action. For a start, there is the matter of the feet themselves, which hardly earn their place in the title. Not until the last scene do we learn how Dans mom came to be deprived of her original pair, and by that point weve forgotten about them. They have played no part in the intervening imagery or the intervening story.

That last phrase may be misleading. This isn't a sequential sort of play; there is no "now to it, except in Dan's narrating mind. What we see is what he chooses to tell us, and we follow him back and forth across the years. As a boy, indeed, well into adolescence, he was not so much mothered as smothered. He was encouraged to believe in his own superiority to those whom his mama calls peasants" or "hoi polloi, meaning, apparently, just about everybody else in the world, especially teachers. Even on the brink of manhood he is compelled, before going out on a date, to stand still, protesting, while she hems his trousers. (This is a very well- observed scene.) Just how intense the mother-son relationship becomes I should not reveal, but youll probably have guessed anyway.

Dan finds himself a bride, which is rather surprising, though we may be meant to feel that he has rushed into marriage -- or been rushed -- rather too precipitately, as if he has something to prove. The newlyweds move as far from Dans mother's home as they can manage without emigrating, and they have a son, whom Dan adores. When the marriage breaks up, the wife, for some unexplained but dramatically convenient reason, makes no attempt to gain custody. Jane Spidell, with no physical alteration, plays both mother and wife, and is admirably chilling as one and admirably warming as the other. I suppose the two are meant to be aspects of the same woman, but I can't see where, beyond the obvious, that gets us.

Completing the cast is that most unassumingly versatile of actors, Jerry Franken, doubling as Dans understandably ineffectual father and as a more assertive small-town cop who, we note, addresses him as son." Dan goes to him because he becomes convinced his mother has tracked him down and is stalking him (even, he says aggrievedly, at Dominion). He takes his son off on a rite-of- passage camping trip into some symbolic-sounding woods where they meet with a realistic-sounding bear. But then Dans wife's name is Ursula, meaning "female bear, a genus against which his mother, who thinks in unforgiving images, has prophetically warned him -- though we only hear the prophecy after we've seen the marriage.

Probably the whole play is more metaphorical than Ive made out, but then I have a literal mind. Its beautifully directed by Micheline Chevrier, who makes simplicity and concentration into a style. Deeter Schurig's suggestively vernal set helps. So, very much, does John Munro's lighting. What comes over, though, is an extended case study with no apparent reason for being except as a flexing of writers muscle. Wilkinson does have muscle to flex. She writes well about sex, which is rare in plays, and gives Ursula a wonderful speech on the subject. She can get into a mans voice, too, which is just as well, as Rooney's Dan does most of the talking. He does it magically, with great lucidity and lightness of touch, especially in the matter of whimsically distinguishing man from boy. He seems to be able to play any age, and he hereby joins my short list (available on request) of actors who should play Peer Gynt: In fact, he's at the top of it.

All the same, if the play is meant to be an exorcism for its central figure, it doesnt come off, in part because he is so central. Plays with narrator-figures outside their action can, sometimes, work: e.g. Henry V and Our Town. Plays in which the narrator is also part of the story are far dodgier, though they have proliferated ever since The Glass Menagerie and are now a positive plague. One character gets privileged over the others, and the evening becomes a series of shortcuts.

For a contrast, admittedly an exalted one, look at Macbeth. It leads us inside its protagonists head, but doesn't demand that we take up residence there. Peter Farbridge, playing the role for the Modern Times Theatre Company, makes a highly intelligent tour guide. However, he looks and sounds neither warlike nor haunted, and even in an avowedly non-realistic production, these are handicaps.

Farbridge, with his director, Soheil Parsa, is one of the founding members of Modern Times, a company that has been in existence for 15 years, and has been doing this play intermittently since 1995. Parsa's staging, with a cast of nine, is both imaginative and ingenious, and since its billed as an adaptation, one can hardly complain of the liberties it takes.

In fact, most of these are very effective -- the text is cut, but its also embellished, with silent actions depicting scenes that usually happen offstage.

Much of the show is about scarves, long ones. Sometimes they enfold the actors, enabling them to play the witches (known here as the wayward sisters"), who are elastic in number, sometimes stretching to include the entire company. Other times, the scarves are unfurled, so that the players can attach themselves by them to posts, or to other actors. It's also about robes. Duncan is completely enclosed, face and all, within a gold one, through which there issues, muffled but recognizable, the voice of Alon Nashman. Maybe this is to suggest the unreachability of kingship, maybe it's so that the same actor can more easily double as Macduff, a role he plays with contrasting immediacy. In this version, we actually see him discover the kings body -- which is, come to think of it, his own. Malcolm, as heir apparent, sports a matching golden cravat, but Macbeth himself stays strictly dun-coloured. He may have murdered his way to the throne, but he still isnt a king, not a real one. Well, I presume that's the idea.

The production does have a habit of undercutting some of its own best moments. There is, for example, a first-rate staging of what I would call the cauldron scene except that the cauldron has been eliminated, along with all the savoury ingredients that are customarily tossed into it. (Fillet of a fenny snake is off, dear.) It culminates in a brilliantly resourceful staging, much the best I've seen, of the show of Banquos royal descendants -- which also, incidentally, caps an unusual and very rewarding emphasis on the muted ambitions of Banquo himself (Brendan Murray). You can see why Macbeth is spooked by it. We dont need him to go into a manic war dance, as if to show what a very bad boy he is now becoming. Similarly, a spare suggestion of the massacre at Macduff's castle is only cheapened when the assassins go on another choreographed rampage.

This is a multicultural staging, aesthetically speaking, with a multi-ethnic cast, urbanly speaking.

Those of us who long to see the sexy Iranian lady from 24 play Lady Macbeth get a mini-preview from Stavroula Logothettis, who is Greek, though it's unfortunate she doesnt bring much to the role beyond the idea that the lady uses her sexuality to enmesh her man.

And you get that from every production. The director himself plays the Porter who has lost most of his jokes but gained a reeling encounter with the drug-addled grooms who get framed for Duncans murder, two gentlemen who in more conventional circumstances we seldom get to meet. It's a more than fair exchange. The production doesn't plot much of a course for its two leading characters, but it leads us excitingly into the world that surrounds them.
