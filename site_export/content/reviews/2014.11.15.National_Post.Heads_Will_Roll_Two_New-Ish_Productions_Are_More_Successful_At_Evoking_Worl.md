Stephen Adly Guirgis is the New York playwright responsible for The Last Days of Judas Iscariot, whose Toronto production was one of the great theatrical events of the past 10 years. It established Guirgis, in my eyes anyway, as the most exciting dramatist now working in the U.S., though since then Bruce Norris (Clybourne Park) has provided some substantial competition. Guirgiss other plays that I've seen or read are on a smaller scale than Judas, physically and philosophically, but they have the same wit, the same unexpectedness, and the same concern with how we live, love and believe.

The Motherf**ker with the Hat, which may come to be known as the asterisk play, is Guirgis's latest, brought to us now by a new company called Bob Kills Theatre, operating out of The Coal Mine, whose fittingly subterranean location is beneath the Magic Oven restaurant at Pape and Danforth. The space is tiny but the performance and production team is top-flight.

The play takes us, giddily and profanely, into the working-class, and underclass, world of upper Manhattan. Our protagonist is Jackie, a recovering alcoholic with some good intentions, a short temper and an itchy trigger-finger. Recently out of prison, he comes home to his girlfriend Veronica, whom hes loved since high school, announcing that,

maybe for the first time in his life, he has a respectable job. How this works out for him we never have the chance to discover, since other concerns supervene. Jackie detects an unfamiliar male scent in Veronicas bed and an unfamiliar male hat on her table. Veronica, who has her own issues with vodka and cocaine, indignantly denies any wrongdoing. Thoughts of a cozy evening at their favourite pie-shop and/or in bed are banished. Jackie storms out.

He goes to see Ralph, his AA sponsor, who in his own recovery has become an avid consumer of, and evangelist for, health-food drinks. In addition to dietary advice and behavioural instruction, Ralph offers Jackie a couch on which to crash. This leads to complications with Ralph's wife Victoria: not, as you might expect, because she doesn't like Jackie but because she doesnt like Ralph. Meanwhile, there are other complications;

Jackie has shot up the apartment of the man in his building whom he believes to be the malefactor in the hat. He now needs to hide the weapon. So, with Ralph in tow, he turns to his Puerto Rican cousin Julio who agrees to take care of the gun, though not before reading Jackie a lecture on his selfishness and irresponsibility. It does indeed turn out that Jackie has been unfaithful to Veronica, with a previous, female, sponsor. Although it also turns out that Jackies behaviour may count as ethical, compared to the lines that Ralph has crossed. It all depends, as Jackie might say, on your code.

This is the only one of Guirgis's plays to have been produced on Broadway, which may account for the conventional feel-good vibe that surfaces in its last scene. The play begins with Jackie and Veronica, and it ends with them, but if their relationship is meant to be its heart, the emotional case isn't made. The womens roles arent as richly written as those of the men. Nor (by Melissa D'Agostino and Nicole Stamp) are they as distinctively played.

Sergio Di Zio's Jackie is a subtle performance that starts as a standard angry tough but goes on to find new levels of hurt, bewilderment and conscience. His final words to Ralph - that the fact he hasnt killed him doesnt mean that he might not do it in the future - are Guirgis at his best: a simple statement that radiates in all directions. Ted Dykstra's Ralph, with the strangest and greasiest ponytail ever sported, is drop-dead hilarious in the prose arias, both solicitous and self-serving, that his author has bestowed on him; he's also very persuasive in his discomfiting arguments that sex, with anybody at all, is no big deal, and that after the age of 25, there are no friends, only associates."

Speeches even more joyously spectacular go to Juan Chioran as Julio, the self-described "f-got cousin" (in boyhood anyway) with a threatening sideline in martial arts; he is blissfully absurd and, in one spellbinding speech of reminiscence, even more blissfully serious. Layne Coleman directs a very tight production, on a set by Steve Lucas that convinces as three separate locations. Simon Fon has arranged fights that look, at these close quarters, as dangerous as any Ive seen. The play is more successful at evoking a world than at making a point, but its still a dazzler.

It does, I guess, say one of the same things as Tom Stoppard's Arcadia, which is that theories, however cosmic, get messed up by people fancying people they shouldn't. Stoppards two-timing modern classic illustrates this more persuasively in its 19thcentury scenes than its 20th-century ones (the two periods alternate, and finally and famously co-exist) but the minor complaint is outweighed by the major compliment.

Eda Holmes studio production from last year's Shaw Festival has expanded into the Royal Alex with no loss of definition. In fact, it's gained; the actors now inhabit their roles and bandy their words with enchanting assurance. Gray Powell nails every line and thought as the tutor who might be called Lord Byron once removed; Kate Besworth is a poignant delight as his fated genius pupil; Diana Donnelly is splendidly dismissive in a cardigan as the modern lady academic; and Patrick McManus as her professional and psychological opposite is wonderfully uninhibited in a suit as her professional and psychological opponent, whose real name is Nightingale and whose assumed and far more suitable one, as he careers flamboyantly toward a fall, is Peacock. Like most Stoppard characters, indeed like most people, hes trying desperately to make himself feel important.

You get more out of this play every time you see it, not so much in wisdom as in the joy of picking up connections, some of them structural, some (like an allusion to the sub-title of The Importance of Being Earnest) thrown in for the fun of it. The production has gained, too, in gravitas, both at its end and in a passing image like that of Kyle Orzech, the one newcomer, as the mute boy Gus covering his ears against the intolerable noise (a keyword) of his squabbling elders. Festival casting means that we get major actors like Ric Reid, Sanjay Talwar and, as a butler, an immovable Michael Ball glorifying minor roles. There are Stoppard plays of which I am personally fonder but all right, I give in, this is his masterpiece.

The Motherf **ker with the Hat runs through Nov. 30 at The Coal Mine; Arcadia runs through Dec. 14 at the Royal Alexandra Theatre.
