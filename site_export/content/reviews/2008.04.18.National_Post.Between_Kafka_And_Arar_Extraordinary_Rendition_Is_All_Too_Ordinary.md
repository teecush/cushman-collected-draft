A ccording to TVO, Extraordinary Rendition is very loosely inspired by the case of Canadian Maher Arar. The basing, if thats a word, seems to have been very loose indeed. In fact there's no internal evidence that the makers of this British film had the Arar case anywhere on their minds.

The story told here resembles the Canadian one in that its central character is kidnapped by secret service agents, flown to a Middle Eastern country to be interrogated on suspicion of having links to terrorist organizations, tortured into making a confession, and eventually released.

But Zaafir, the protagonist, is a British citizen seized not in an airport but on a London street, and his government seems totally unaware of his plight. There isn't even the token intervention offered by Canadian officials during Arars imprisonment, and there seems no question when Zaafir returns home of his being awarded any compensation. Canadian viewers lured in by the publicity may be either bemused or relieved to find that, in this telling, Canada is completely off the hook. It looks for a time as if the United States will be, too. Zaafir seems to have been abducted by homegrown operatives with a semi-official mandate, sounding as nasty as only British thugs in British TV films can. Were well into the story before we find that they're in cahoots with the CIA.

Zaafir (Omar Berdouni) is a college teacher, Muslim by religion and of Pakistani origin, with a newly pregnant, and Catholic, wife (Ania Sowinski). We see him before, during, and after his ordeal, though part of the point is that in experiences like this there really isn't any "after." The physical scars may heal, but the psychological ones remain indefinitely.

To drive this home, the three time frames are scrambled together. When Zaafir is in his cell or in the torture chamber, he hallucinates about his wife; when hes reunited with her, he hallucinates about when they were apart. Which is very credible, but no great revelation.

In fact Jim Threapletons film doesn't reveal much at all; his script is too little and his direction too much. The music, pounding or scraping, is constant, the dialogue muted, the editing jumpy and the chronology, as noted, scrambled. The intention must be to make us feel Zaafir's ordeal for ourselves, but what were mostly aware of us is the films technique. The precise locale of the rendition is left indeterminate. So is the background, and certainly the accent, of the chief inquisitor, though the fact that he's played by Andy Serkis may send extra chills down a viewer's spine. If you were going to be interrogated, you wouldnt want it to be done by Gollum in hot pursuit of his precious.

Or, in this film, in cool pursuit. Serkis plays good cop, lamenting that the people around him are animals. This statement, of course, means that he himself is the silkiest animal in the pack, continually complaining about how all this giving orders for beatings and waterboardings is preventing him from spending quality time with his family.

A subtler part of his method is to tell Zaafir as little as possible not only about where hes being held but why. Unfortunately, this leaves the audience as much in the dark as the victim. This isn't Kafka; we're dealing with a specific kind of abuse thats happening now. We dont need arty atmospherics to persuade us that torture and rendition are unspeakable practices. We could do with some insight into how it's allowed to happen and what it's supposed to achieve. In Zaafirs case this remains thoroughly obscure. Once hes confessed, he's released. The film starts with him being repatriated, i.e. unloaded at dead of night in a deserted British airport, bruised and confused, to the horror of the security staff. Everything else is flashback, or flashback within flashback.

Much the most interesting part comes, chronologically, after the release. To Zaafir, every conversation now seems like an interrogation. Even his wife loses patience. She sympathizes but she cannot comprehend. Neither can the official helpers. A wearily pedantic functionary (well played by Hugh Ross, though I couldn't tell whether he was meant to be a lawyer, psychiatrist, or civil servant) asks, "Were you maltreated?" Zaafir, understandably, storms out of the office.

The film starts with a caption: "Men without conscience are capable of any cruelty the human mind can imagine." It ends with another, more dryly informing us that there are currently 39 abduction complaints against the CIA. Those two statements provide more food for thought, and even for nightmares, than anything that comes between. - Extraordinary Rendition airs Sunday at 9 p.m. on TVO.
