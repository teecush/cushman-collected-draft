Judith Thompson's White Biting Dog begins with a depressed and divorced 31-year-old lawyer, who goes by the name of Cape Race, preparing to kill himself by jumping off the Bloor Street Viaduct. (The play dates from 1984, when this was a more practicable endeavour than it is now.) He's talked down, kind of, by a small dog whom the audience can neither see nor hear, and who informs him that he can save himself by saving his father. Father, whose name is Glidden, has been brought to the point of dissolution by his own depression. It isn't exactly a case of like father, like son, as Glidden's sadness has something winsome and quirky about it, while Cape is just desperately glum. Still reeling from his canine encounter, Cape encounters Pony, a former paramedic who now practises her healing arts in a shopping mall. Pony is also a medium whose control is a dog seemingly not unlike the one that appeared to Cape. Cape and Pony team up, to rescue Glidden by reuniting him with his absconded wife, Loamia. As luck would have it, she turns up at the family home that very night, accompanied by Pascal, her latest young stud. They need refuge because their own place has been torched by a meth addict.

If all that sounds weird, extreme and more than a little ridiculous: well, it is. It does have energy, and even a certain charm, at least in its earlier stages; and these qualities are very well captured in Nancy Palk's Soulpepper production, her debut as a director and, in terms of attack and actors' togetherness, an impressive one. It scores less well on quotidian believability, but that might not have been intended or even appropriate. All the same, Christina Poddubiuk's set does seem to have mortgaged any appearance of domesticity for the sake of providing an upper level for the first brief scene on the bridge. In the Thompson plays that I think I like best (Crackwalker or I Am Yours), the excesses spring from some defined social background. Here, they seem to be there for their own sake. I'd describe the play's tone as savage whimsy, with emphasis on the noun.

Cape's schemes for preserving his dad are murky, and very much made up on the fly, but we want them to succeed, if only because Glidden is played by Joseph Ziegler at his most commandingly endearing. Whether crawling about his house covered in earth or, at a hopeful moment, erect and tuxedoed, he's sympathetic without ever being squishy. Most moving is his realization that though he loves his wife, she doesn't love him, at least not carnally, which for her is what counts. Fiona Reid, who goes through most of the play in a slip, makes this blazingly clear in a performance glittering with wit and need, like a Tennessee Williams heroine gone north. Gregory Prest's Pascal is an appealing third side to the triangle, with a Mohawk hairdo and, initially, a hoarse mutter of a voice because, we are told, he despises the English language. In the second act, though, he talks like anyone else, which suggests the play has a fondness for short-term effects.

This is confirmed by a series of more or less erotic encounters - between Loamia and Pascal, Pascal and Cape, even Cape and Loamia - which are arresting in themselves but have no particular connection with how the characters behave before or after. The Loamia-Glidden relationship does hold and develop, but everything around it is just attitude. And that goes especially for Cape and Pony. Pony hardly stops talking, and Michaela Washburn can't muster the resources to prevent her saintly earth-motherliness from becoming tiresome. She claims that in making love to Cape she let "evil" enter into her, a crucial claim (or so I assume) that isn't credible. Mike Ross can do many things, including beating a terrific drum for Pony's seances, but embodying evil doesn't seem to be one of them. I admired his intensity and his watchfulness, the latter a quality much emphasized in the staging, without ever understanding just what dog was biting him. The name Cape Race, we are given to understand, is self-bestowed; his mother called him Sonny. Well, sometimes funny names work and sometimes they don't. I still have fond memories of Claudia Dey's Trout Stanley whose hero, questioned on that point, said simply "it's a fish name." If Dey hadn't given up writing plays she might rank with Michael Healey, John Mighton and Hannah Moscovitch among those who are producing work better than anything from the supposed Golden Age of Canadian drama.

From a play with a dog to its name but nowhere else, to a circus without animals. In other words, Cirque du Soleil is back in town with Totem, directed by Robert Lepage and announced as "the Odyssey of the Human Species" but not in chronological order. In other words, anything goes. But there is more continuity and sense of purpose than usual, and of course, some of the visuals are extraordinary. The basic setting is a turtle-shaped island, suffused with water without an actual drop being spilled; it's all done with projections. (Get this man to direct The Tempest.) Among the acts that show what humanity is capable of, I was especially dumbstruck by a quintet of lady unicyclists who balance on their heads what look like dim-sum baskets and who toss them from one to another with dazzling abandon, sometimes doing it back-handed. They top it off with teapots. There's also a twoman high-wire act that demonstrates the true meaning of trust. And there are animals. At least, there are performers in gorilla suits who ambush what looks like a team of chartered surveyors, one of whom turns really nasty when they try to stomp on his cellphone. They then strip off and perform acrobatics so intrepid that even the anthropoids applaud. The clowns are as tedious as ever, and the stunts in the second half are a bit disappointing, declining from the flatly impossible to the merely incredible; though there's still a bewitching male-female double act, on a trapeze: a graceful altercation for two real-looking people. My companion thought they represented Adam and Eve, and she could well be right. A figure clad in silver foil descends on a wire at the start to get the species started, and returns at intervals to pick up the odd chair. Furniture removal is the bane of many directors' lives, and this show may have found the perfect graceful solution.
