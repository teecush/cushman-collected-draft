Hannah Moscovitchs The Childrens Republic stakes out its ground very clearly. Literally, too: When the audience enters the theatre it sees the words Warsaw 1939" written on the stage floor. When it returns after the intermission, the words have changed to "Warsaw 1942. Since the principal setting is a Jewish orphanage, we know what we're in for. The orphans themselves begin the second act by chalking on a wall the new restrictions that confine them to the ghetto. They then partially deface them, in a silent sequence infused equally with desperation and defiance.

The play, a joint commission of Tarragon and the Harold Green Jewish Theatre Company, is in part a celebration of and memorial to Dr. Janusz Korczak, who ran the orphanage and was a pioneer in the advocacy of children's rights, physical and moral. In one of the earliest scenes, he holds a microphone against the chest of one of his charges and announces to unseen auditors this is how a childs heart sounds in the presence of adults" -- a wonderful line that I hastened to write down, only to discover that it had been printed in the program, twice. Hardly less resonant is Korczaks rationale for transforming himself from physician to pedagogue: "You can't cure poverty with Aspirin.

These may be direct quotes from source -- Moscovitch admits to lifting a few -- but that would just prove she knows how to borrow as well as how to write and construct. She retains the rare ability to sketch large historical subjects on small domestic canvases, without cheapening the first or inflating the second. This is the best new Canadian play in a longish time. But it's also second-tier Moscovitch in which the authors scheme, like her heros if less tragically, gets derailed by history.

The first half of the play is mostly about children's autonomy. Korczak sets up a children's court" in which the orphans settle their own disputes by putting one another on trial and then rendering judgment. But once the Germans have invaded, these things become moot. Life in the ghetto becomes about survival; Korczaks children are in a prison within a prison, forbidden the streets for fear that they would be traumatized, or worse, by what theyd find there. One boy, a troublemaker from the start and deeply troubled, breaks out, in all senses. He's perhaps more autonomous than Korczak would like; they're equally self-willed but the relationship is reiterated rather than explored. The play is tough but sparing in its depiction of the approaching Holocaust; of course, the audience can fill in most of the blanks. The odd result is that the second act is more exciting than the first but less interesting. The author also permits herself a note of uplift at the end that feels both uncharacteristic and unearned.

The play is moving and compelling, and Alisa Palmer has given it a tense and sensitive production, though she cant prevent the first half, made up of short scenes, from seeming choppy; the second benefits from having fewer transitions. Peter Hutt, valued veteran of Shaw and Stratford, has a rare chance at a leading role and takes it beautifully, showing Korczak as a man keeping despair at bay while radiating intellectual passion and practical concern. As his assistant, Kelli Fox has to alternate between nagging and supporting him; she does both forcefully but there are times when she seems to be stating the obvious rather too obviously. Mark Correia as the rebel heads a striking selection of orphans whose other members are Elliott Larson (touchingly tremulous), Katie Frances Cohen and Emma Burke-Kleinman. Theres also a delicate performance by Amy Rutherford as a genteel schoolteacher who, both in what we see and hear of her, embodies the terror of the times.
