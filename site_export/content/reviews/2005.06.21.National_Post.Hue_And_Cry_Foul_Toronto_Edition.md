BLUE MAN GROUP

Panasonic Theatre, Toronto

- - -

On my way Sunday to the Panasonic Theatre, formerly the New Yorker, and don't you hate these name changes, I encountered a nice lady who asked me if I was going to the boycott. This took me slightly aback, since a boycott, by definition, is not something you go to; its aim is to keep you away. But I knew what she was getting at. She meant was I going to the demo across the road from the theatre, organized by the local entertainment unions to discourage us from patronizing the Blue Man Group, who have opened operations in Toronto without benefit of union-approved contracts.

My sympathies here are all with organized labour, and I felt a twinge of guilt when I heard one of the speakers opposite asking us not only to refrain from seeing the show ourselves but to encourage other people not to see it. If anyone's in a position to do that, I am. Still, a job's a job, and I have to do mine by saying that if you're looking for highly skilled undemanding entertainment, the Blue Man Group will provide it. If, on the other hand, you are in search of a life-changing experience, you can pass this one up. The show is by turns charming, boring and annoying.

As everyone must know by now, the three original Blue Men (Matt Goldman, Phil Stanton, Chris Wink) started out in New York in the late 1980s as street performers, in the business of administering harmless surprises to equally harmless passersby. Since what they did qualified as performance art, they moved into the art scene (for which they claim they were too irreverent) and then out again, into an off-Broadway theatre. From there they expanded worldwide, becoming something between a conglomerate and a cloning device.

Three men -- it's impossible to tell which of the names in the program are actually theirs -- appear before us, as the show's creators used to do in the days when they had the time. They are wearing tight-fitting cobalt-coloured masks, which make them look as though they have painted themselves that way. They don't say a word. That's another prejudice they have to live down -- not only are they blue-faced black legs, they're mimes. Still, they don't put on insufferable airs about it. It's just the way they are.

Their main talents seem to be percussion-pounding and juggling. They pound vigorously and rhythmically on drums and other less conventional instruments --not, obviously, until they're blue in the face but until they have a right to look that way. (There are full- time musicians backing them up, very well.) At the same time, they toss marshmallows back and forth, catching them in their mouths. A lot of candy-munching gets done in this show, and the chomping too is rhythmic. Breakfast cereals are also consumed. Whatever Equity's qualms, none of these actors looks like going hungry. They also do a certain amount of regurgitating, but keep it surprisingly within the bounds of good taste. They have perfected a form involving gum balls that might be described as oral action-painting.

They project a certain amount of personality, really a remarkable amount considering what is plastered all over their faces. Their range of expression is narrow but sharply defined. Two of them can look aghast at the things the third has done, while the third can look innocent, or maybe just oblivious. (It's what's known in the trade as "who? me?" humour.) They can also be appealingly inhuman. When, as we knew they would, they lure a spectator onstage, they don't -- of course, they can't -- humiliate her verbally, as more conventional comics might do. Just sitting down with her at a table for what feels like minutes on end, they prove that silence can be far more intimidating -- and, yes, far funnier -- than any words. Eventually they relent and instruct her by example in the fine art of eating Twinkies in public.

It wouldn't be true to say that the show's humour is strictly non- verbal. Some of the best parts involve words; they just happen to be written rather than spoken. Even before the proceedings proceed, we are disarmed -- at any rate, I was -- by a stream of neon messages, making sly fun of our expectations as a group and of some of us as individuals. One audience member, who may or may not have existed, was advised on how to cure a headache by thinking of it as a cow. (I think, in retrospect, that this was the highlight of the evening. But my tastes may not be typical.) The show proper plays gentle mind games, concerning our inability to follow three sets of messages at the same time, and our determination to try regardless. There is also a short diagrammatic film, with recorded commentary, on the subject of modern plumbing (the one thing that binds our society together).

There is also, in this very high-tech show, a constant effort to debunk technology, or at least to remind us of its existence. You may remember an episode of The Simpsons in which Sideshow Bob went on television to denounce television, winding up by remarking that he was perfectly aware of the irony, and viewers needn't bother to write in and tell him. He was of course on a TV show within a TV show. The Blue Man Group never get as sophisticated as that (the closest they get to satire is a feeble routine about the dance movements of rock stars), but they seem to be saying much the same thing.

That must be what has got them the name of post-modernists. That and their disregard of narrative conventions. But in the latter respect, they're no more advanced than any old-fashioned revue or variety bill, while their penchant for blurring the line between stage and auditorium would hardly have surprised the Marx Brothers or even the more primitive anarchists of my London youth, the Crazy Gang. It was probably also anticipated by old shows that I've read about but have never seen, like Hellzapoppin' or the Parisian revues of Robert Dhery. The recent Toronto show Revolutions in Therapy was kicking at the same can, though more cerebrally. Wherever there's theatrical convention, or any kind of convention, there will be a corresponding urge to tear it down, and it can be fun. I bet it was great fun when the original Blue Men were mentally goosing people on the streets of downtown Manhattan. But when that's the only idea, and when the techniques employed keep repeating themselves, then a show, even if it only lasts 90 minutes as this one does, can get monotonous.

It can also get desperate, as in this production's occasional resort to video footage of things supposedly going on off-stage. Why does mixed media, which certainly sounds exciting, always end up seeming such a cheat? Anyway, the whole thing builds, for want of a better word, to a prolonged finale involving music that's relentless, strobe lights that are more so, and the swathing of the audience in endless lengths of tissue paper. Some people whose taste I respect, and whom indeed I live with, thought it the best time they'd had in ages. To me, it felt like forced celebration, which is why I left feeling angry. There were Blue Men waiting in the lobby, who when asked if they will pose for photos with audience members, reply very politely that they will. That's right; they talk. But maybe they aren't the True Blue Men.
