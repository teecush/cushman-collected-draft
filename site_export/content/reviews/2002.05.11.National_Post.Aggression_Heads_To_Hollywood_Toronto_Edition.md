SPEED-THE-PLOW

Jane Mallett Theatre

- - -

David Mamet's Speed-the-Plow is another of his plays about resentful male aggression. Abandoning the Chicago small-timers of American Buffalo and Glengarry Glen Ross, he now takes us to Hollywood, his second home, to meet men who have hit the big time, but are still small-timers at heart.

The play takes his familiar dialogue gambits and tragi-comic themes, but reworks them as satirical comedy. This may be inevitable. It seems impossible to write a Hollywood play that doesn't come out satirical.

The players here are Bobby Gould, who has been appointed head of production after years in the studio trenches, and his old buddy Charlie, whose career has always trailed at least a step behind and who is still toiling.

Now, with the walls of Bobby's office not even decorated yet, Charlie comes to him with a property: a prison movie with a major star. Charlie has only 24 hours in which to exercise his option.

Bobby, whose contract gives him the power to green-light one cheap (i.e. under $10-million) movie a year, is thrilled; he will take the story to the head of the studio, whose consent will surely be a mere formality. The project reeks of money, and Bobby and Charlie, prospective co-producers, are already counting it. (Bobby must, by most people's criteria, be rich already, but of course his standards are more exalted than ours.)

Trouble comes in the shape -- that seems the right term -- of Karen, the temp in Bobby's outer office whom he, in high-flying mood, has bet Charlie he can seduce. So he hands her a book -- a no- hope book that he has been entrusted with to give "a courtesy read" - - and asks her to come to his house that night and tell him what she thinks of it.

It's a novel about hope, despair, radiation and the end of the world; Karen finds it inspiring. She tells Bobby that this must be his movie, and he wins his bet. But he also lets himself be convinced, by her appeal to his nobler nature and to the motion- picture industry's power for doing good.

Next morning, as they are about to go into the crucial meeting and make their pitch, Bobby tells Charlie that he has changed his mind. Charlie is very upset.

This is a small, tight, witty play: The dialogue between the two men is prime Mamet (though it never rises to the comic poetry of his very best work) and the observation genially remorseless. But Hollywood is, after all, an obvious target, which makes it all the more important that it be hit sharply. It cannot afford to lose focus.

In this production by Jeff Seymour, an American actor-director who also plays Bobby, it sprawls. It literally sprawls. One side of the unwieldy Jane Mallett stage is taken up by Bobby's office; the other by his living room. Both are in view throughout. It seems a reasonably elegant solution (no designer is named) but the play is written in conventional long scenes, so at any given point half the acting-area is going to waste. Nothing is centred.

Seymour's own performance is competent enough. He seems to be aiming at giving Bobby a boyish vulnerability, or maybe that's his quality anyway, but he never confronts the question of how genuine - - or at least temporarily heartfelt -- Bobby's new-found idealism might be.

Bill Elliot's Charlie is stronger and meaner, and the resentment that seethes beneath his fulsome protestation of friendship makes itself felt. But the interplay between them is a shadow of what it might be, and when it erupts into violence -- as, in a Mamet play, it always does -- it merely seems surprising. It ought to seem surprising and inevitable.

Nor does it help that the production, well, pulls it punches; most of the incident occurs out of sight, behind a sofa. It has to be real, and seen to be real, to be funny; and it is funny, or supposed to be. The humour is that neither of the participants seems to regard it, really, as anything exceptional.

The part of Karen was originally played on Broadway by Madonna: a performance that I saw, but cannot remember a thing about. (It wasn't even memorably bad.) Here, maybe in a case of casting by association, the role goes to an actress best known for being the daughter of a pop singer: Joely Collins, daughter of Phil. But she turns out to be very good, with a warmth that I've certainly never seen brought to the role before. She cannot persuade us that the book she champions might be good (the passages Mamet lets us hear put paid to that) but there does seem a chance that she might genuinely believe in it without being an idiot.

She also rises, though not strongly enough, to the ambiguities that rise to the surface in the last scene, when the fight becomes a three-cornered one. Nobody here is pure, and the trap is laid, the point made, with a chess master's economy and finesse.

Seeing the play for the third time, I still wonder why Bobby doesn't solve his problem by pitching both pictures: one for the studio, one for himself. Given, though, that the choice has to be made, one ends up rooting for the corrupted Charlie rather than for the lady idealist; his need is more naked, and if a bad film has to be made, better it be one that some audience might actually enjoy.

This is a transitional work between the Chicago masterpieces where the guys tear one another apart without outside assistance, and Oleanna, the gender-war play in which the woman wins when the man self-destructs. Here everybody loses; some more remuneratively than others. This production draws most of the play's sting, but a little lingers.
