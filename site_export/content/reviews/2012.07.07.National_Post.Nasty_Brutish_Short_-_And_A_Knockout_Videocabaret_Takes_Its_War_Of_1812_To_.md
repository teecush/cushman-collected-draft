'What kind of war is this?" asks someone, to which someone else replies, "It's like all wars, ugly and stupid." I think the someone is Laura Secord, but there's so much doubling going on that I could well have got it wrong. The someone else is a grievously wounded soldier; his response sums up VideoCabaret's view of wars, and indeed of history in general, and it can be pretty hard to argue with. The conflict dramatized here is The War of 1812, which is also the title of the relevant instalment of VideoCab's ongoing cartoon epic, The History of the Village of the Small Huts. Usually this unfolds in the backroom of the Cameron House in Toronto (where it's now on its second go-round) but this show, for obvious celebratory reasons, is being presented at Stratford in a space - the Studio Annex - new to public performance, that's been set up to replicate the troupe's customary black box as closely as possible.

The surroundings may be different but the quality is the same. That is to say the writing (by Michael Hollingsworth) is pungent, the staging (also by Hollingsworth) is brilliant, the set and lighting (Andy Moro) are superbly precise and economic, the costumes (Astrid Janson) are even more superbly garish, the actors are without exception quick-change virtuosi as they appear and reappear in a dizzying succession of frocks, wigs, accents and personalities. For those who have never seen a VideoCabaret show: The video part derives from the action taking place on a tiny oblong stage suggestive of a TV screen. Each brief scene is succeeded by a blackout (this isn't quite as efficient a process in Stratford as it usually is at the home base) during which the next group of actors takes position, faster and more accurately than you would think humanly possible. The dimensions of the stage mean that none of the performers is ever visible below the knee, thus lending a whole new meaning to the phrase "footloose and fancy free."

The brevity of the scenes also accounts for the cabaret part. It's cabaret as the Berlin of the 1930s conceived it: nasty, brutish and short. That's not an aspersion on the creators (Hollingsworth and his associate director Deanne Taylor are lovely people) but it is the view of life that they present. Everybody who figures in their dramatic universe is a buffoon, a poltroon or a dupe; some manage to score in more than one of those categories. As I've noted before, keeping the scenes so short is a vital part of the world view; no character sticks around long enough to exhibit any redeeming features, or indeed any complexity of any kind. This is certainly bracing, and it can also, though less frequently than you might expect, be extremely funny. It does, though, end up feeling monotonous.

There are eight actors in The War of 1812 and between them they play, if the program and I have counted correctly, 55 characters. The first one we meet is U.S. President James Madison who, with Britain busy fighting Napoleon on the other side of the world, decides to invade its northern dominion because - well, just because. His wife is concerned about the effect on her social calendar, and their murderous frivolity sets the tone for the evening. The powerful people are despicable and the powerless, with one important exception, deserve them.

My favourite of all the plays in the cycle has been The Great War, which, without ever sentimentalizing, did discover a vein of compassion for the soldiers caught in the never-ending crossfire. The one character in the current episode to be treated with comparable sympathy and respect is Tecumseh, the First Nations chief lured into allying himself with the Canadians. Anand Rajaram gives him wit, dignity and an ironic awareness of everybody else's incompetence. Dying early, Tecumseh was spared the final humiliation of seeing his treaties dishonoured and his lands lost, and one can't help wishing that the script had cheated history and left him alive to bear poignant witness to his own and his people's disillusion. That task falls instead to his brother the Prophet, who does it nobly, though as played by Michaela Washburn it's hard not to think of him as Tecumseh's sister. Before joining VideoCabaret, Rajaram was one of the jewels of Second City, specializing in Asian roles; in this show he is, to put it doubly incorrectly, one kind of Indian playing another kind of Indian, but he also appears as a halfdozen other characters and makes an economically devastating impression in each of them. Mac Fyfe, who may be the signature VideoCabbie, is especially effective here as blood-andguts Captain Fitzgibbon, who gains a kind of respect by at least being efficiently ruthless and doing it in a crisp Celtic accent. Richard Clarkin is contrastingly, and entertainingly, foppish as General Brock, and everyone else is absolutely fine.

Nobody could accuse VideoCabaret of making cheap patriotic fodder out of a bicentennial. Both the play and the program note end with the pointed reminder that, after all the bloodshed, the war ended with the borders exactly as they were when it began. Though, given that the intention of at least one side was forcibly to redraw or remove them, that may not be quite such an insignificant achievement. Anyway, anyone (and I include myself) who's avidly following the Canadian story as told by this company will find this chapter of it well up to snuff. Anyone encountering it for the first time will be knocked out.

The War of 1812 runs through Aug. 12 at the Studio Annex, Stratford. Visit stratfordfestival.ca or call 18005671600 for more information.
