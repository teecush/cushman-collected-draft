Noel Coward is revealed as his own disciple

LIVES / A new biography of Noel Coward comes the closest to understanding the genius of one of Britain's most famous playwrights and tax exiles. NOEL COWARD: A Biography

Saturday, June 1, 1996

By Philip Hoare

Sinclair-Stevenson, 605 pages, $50

WAS anyone ever as completely a creature of the theatre as Noel Coward? Moliere perhaps; but Moliere, as far as I know, never had much of a career as a songwriter or cabaret performer. Coward was actor, playwright, director, lyricist, composer, sort-of-singer -- and he also turned out novels, filmscripts, short stories, light verse and amateur but respectable paintings. How did he decide which of his talents to utilize on any given day? And how does a biographer, conscientiously following his tracks, keep from dropping in them?

Philip Hoare's account is the most comprehensive so far, and probably the best. Some of the story is familiar. Coward found his metier early, beginning as a child actor, spurred on by an adoring and ambitious mother. Before he was out of his teens, he had also discovered that he would write. A postcard, prophesying great things for him as long as he avoided the influence of Bernard Shaw, was sent to him -- by Shaw. (Now in Canada Coward's plays are a mainstay of Shaw's festival. It isn't only what goes around that comes around.)

The 1920s, which coincided handily with Coward's own twenties, established him as a cult figure: icon or iconoclast, depending on where you stood or how old you were. He wrote songs, and then whole scores, for hit revues. He established himself as a master of high comedy with Hay Fever, in which some innocent civilians (i.e. non-theatricals) go through a weekend of hell in the household of a famous actress. The American star Laurette Taylor was supposedly the inspiration for Coward's grande dame but she afterwards protested that the play did her and her family an injustice; they, unlike Coward's feckless individualists, were rude to people on purpose.

The solemn twin to this celebration of stylish bad manners was The Vortex, whose heroine was a married woman with a young lover and whose hero, played by the authyor at his most clenched and high-strung, was her drug-taking son. In those days, and for all of Coward's career, Britain had a theatrical censor, the Lord Chamberlain, and he was shocked. "This picture of a frivolous and degenerate set of people gives a wholly false impression of Society life". In other words, addiction and adultery were all very well, but not in evening dress; it might give the lower orders subversive ideas. The Chamberlain's deputy was more tolerant; he reported that the characters were "frivolous people who . . . attempt wit with rather poor results" (the Chamberlain's men, paid to protect the morals of the nation, seem to have been remarkably free with gratuitous literary criticism) but he also judged that "there are certainly people like Florence Lancaster (the mother) whom it would do good to see how they look to an observer." Since The Vortex borrows rather freely from the mother-son relationship in Hamlet, all this mirror-up-to-nature stuff was rather apposite; and the reporter had a fair sense of what Coward was up to. He may have enjoyed rubbing a smart audience's noses in some selected facts of life, and his young admirers and epigones may have got an even greater thrill out of it, but at heart he was a finger-wagging moralist. The same double message *isn't this deliciously decadent and see what it gets you) is musically delivered in his two Jazz Age admonitions, Dance Little Lady and Poor Little Rich Girl, basically the same song written twice; they can easily be sung in counterpoint.

In two slightly later spectacular productions he made his peace with the prudes. The operetta Bitter Sweet showed him as a lush sentimentalist; the pageant-drama unquestioning patriot. In fact his patriotism, though genuine and essential to him, was complex and critical. (He wrote some bitter plays at this time, but he kept them under wraps.) By the time the war broke out, Coward was empowered to speak for England; he was successively employed in the secret serv ice, in entertaining at the front, and in making propaganda films. None of this work brought him official recognition, and he became increasingly estranged from his homeland. Since success first struck, he had always preferred America anyway. His dismissive views on the emerging Welfare State were expressed in There Are Bad Times Just Around the Corner, a song so trenchant in its cheerful misanthropy that it crosses all party lines; when the Tories are in power, it sounds like a protest from the Left. He became a tax-exile, with residences in Switzerland and Jamaica. Though he always maintained a large nostalgic constituency, he became an emblem of the unwanted and the out-of-date. I remember my father complaining about people who were great patriots while the war was on, and great expatriates when it was over.

In 1960 Coward attacked what was then the New Wave in the British theatre. Contemplating the Osborne-Wesker-Pinter generation he seemed appalled, more than anything else, by the squalor of their settings, just as they were offended by the unremitting gentility of his. (Ironic that they should find common ground with the Lord Chamberlain of 40 years before.) Coward had come on the scene when plays, earnest or frivolous, were automatically set in drawing-rooms, and it had hardly occurred to him to rock that particular boat. He had no social or political agenda. His rebellion, such as it was, had been the product of youth, ambition, and an instinct for the limelight.

Also of his homosexuality, on which Hoare is far more forthcoming than any previous biographer. Coward once publicly listed, among his components of the good life, "sexual intercourse in moderation" he did not of course specify the kind of intercourse, and the idea of moderation seems to have been a blind as well, designed to bolster his image as a paragon of cool. (He may have been trying to fool himself as well as others.) He had a long list of lovers, some of whom were also professional associates; these included John C. Wilson, Coward's long-time New York representative until Wilson's drunkenness and financial shadiness put an end to both aspects of the partnership, and Graham Payn, a South African-born actor and singer whom Coward tried vainly to turn into a star, casting him in nearly all his post-war shows. This relationship at least endured, as a live-in friendship; Payn now administers the Coward estate with, according to Hoare, surprising efficiency. There were also briefer flings with more notable actors: with Michael Redgrave and with the brilliant English character player Alan Webb. And he got his heart broken by a succession of shadowy young men; in this respect, "moderation" was hardly in it. Coward, the first to condemn emotional self-indulgence in his colleagues -- he thought it "unprofessional", the most damning word in his vocabulary -- was actually as vulnerable to it as anyone else.

It was, for obvious reasons of self-preservation, a subject he hardly ever wrote about, though of course it's possible to read it into some of his ostensibly heterosexual plays, notably the menage a trois piece Design for Living. There was an early long-suppressed piece called Semi-Monde; and in his last play A Song at Twilight -- produced in 1966, seven years before his death -- he came close to coming out. It was the only time I ever saw him on stage and -- insofar as one can judge a living legend -- I thought him excellent, his clipped hauteur a perfect mask for inner pain. He played a novelist confronted by the cover-up that had been his life.

The ostensible model was Somerset Maugham, but it was obviously Coward's own story as well. His sexuality, illegal for most of his life, had forced him into the role of an outsider: double ironic in one who so desperately wanted in. To most of the world he WAS the Establishment but the Establishment itself knew better, denying him a knighthood until 1970. He also, according to Hoare, had a complex about his origins. He liked to present himself as a boy from nowhere who made spectacularly good; in fact he came from a well-connected family fallen on hard times, and was driven by the desire to regain former status. This puts him in a respectable line of actor-playwrights; Shakespeare seems to have been consumed by the desire to erase the memory of his father's bankruptcy and prove himself a gentleman. His professional sympathies, though, were wider. Coward's portrayals of the working-class range from the icily snobbish to the embarrassingly patronizing. His nadir, I think, is an interminable Cockney number in his last musical The Girl Who Came to Supper, called London Is a Little Bit of All Right: the work of a man who had long lost touch with popular feeling and indeed with London.

Neither Shakespeare nor Moliere wrote much about the theatre. Coward seemed unable to get away from it. Even his non-professional heroes and heroines seem to be actors under the skin; the poor civilians are aliens, to be gazed atm, joked about, and touched with tongs. The same sensibility pervades his songs, whether punctiliously witty or ripely (sometimes overripely) emotional but, being much shorter, they are often easier to take. Unlike the plays, even the best of which languished until the Coward renaissance of the 60s and after, they never went out of fashion. Hoare, who does a masterly job of keeping track of the plays, gets rather overwhelmed by the songs; too many of them slip past him, though I was pleased to find him acknowledging Nine (from Argentina) as "sublime." (It rhymes "besought her too", "taught her too", and "Cole Porter too" it also tells a very funny story.) On the other hand he mis-identifies the song in Private Lives as I'll See You Again when it's actually Someday I'll Find You. Strange how potent cheap music is; it can even fog the minds of biographers.

In one respect Hoare's book is seriously disillusioning. I knew that Coward's was up-and-down, that his critical and political views were increasingly cranky, and that he could be excessively pleased with himself, but I had always understood that his behaviour to frÑiends and strangers was kind and honourable. It now turns out that he could be a petty tyrant to his tight and devoted entourage, sometimes downright cruel; and that his quip "I can take any amount of criticism as long as it's unqualified praise" was all too true. He preferred sycophancy to honest dissent, and his later work suffered because of it. On the other hand he faced life and death with consistent gallantry, his energy and efficiency were prodigious, and the style he worked so hard to perfect was authentic. He rarely wrote a graceless sentence and if his view of the world was narrow it was genuine and, within its limits, penetrating. "A camp classic, a nostalgic turn, or a lasting genius?" asks Hoare. The answers are yes, and yes, and yes.
