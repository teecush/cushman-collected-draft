Robert Cushman is the theatre critic for the National Post.

LILLIAN GISH: HER LEGEND, HER LIFE

By Charles Affron Scribner,

445 pp., $53.50

---

In 1944, Lillian Gish got fourth billing in an unmemorable movie called Miss Susie Slagle's. It was 20 years after her greatest triumphs and 40 before her death, but her name, says Charles Affron, "still meant something to moviegoers. Even if many of them would have been hard-pressed to say precisely why." His book, though it never manages to bring the actress to life as a woman, does explain why.

Just in chronological terms, her career was extraordinary. It began in 1902 when she was eight, with an appearance in Detroit as a threatened child in a touring melodrama that had begun its life in Toronto as The Little Red Schoolhouse but had been fitted out for the more raucous United States with the bodefully alluring title, In Convict's Stripes. She made her last appearance in 1987 in the film The Whales of August, co-starring with Bette Davis, who was younger but in far worse shape, and supported by Vincent Price and Mary Steenburgen. There could hardly be a more comprehensive rebuke to the accepted belief that child performers burn out early.

It is true, however, that she reached her peak young and her subsequent career was partly a refinement, partly an exploitation of what she had achieved then. She was the star and muse of Biograph Pictures, D. W. Griffith's silent movie repertory company: the eternal threatened maiden of the flickers raised to a higher power. Her acting life began when her alcoholic father walked out on her, her mother and her younger sister Dorothy. All three turned to the stage, and Lillian and Dorothy did well at it. They progressed from touring to New York, and from the stage to movies, which at that time were still based in the east; the exodus to Hollywood was only just beginning. They made their joint film debut in 1912, in An Unseen Enemy, and by the end of that year, Lillian had appeared in 13 pictures, of which Griffith had directed all but two.

She was the valiant child of a drunken daddy, so she took easily to melodrama, first on stage, then on film. She could identify with the stories and she internalized their firm, simple moral values. So did Griffith, and he added a messianic belief in the power of film that rubbed off on her -- to the point that, when talkies came along, she disparaged them as an adulteration of the pure art of the movies and especially of the pure art of acting. She may even have been right; certainly most of her contemporaries agreed with her.

Gish appeared in Griffith's most brilliant and most troubling movies. In the 1915 film The Birth of a Nation, with its stupefying glorification of the Ku Klux Klan, she was the flower of Southern womanhood, rescued from rape by a mulatto. In the epic Intolerance (1916), she is the spirit of time, eternally rocking a cradle: She is scarcely seen, but she is still the actress Griffith chose to hold the film's four plot-lines together. In Broken Blossoms (1919), she is allowed to die, whipped to death by a drunken father infuriated at his daughter for having taken refuge with a Chinese shopkeeper (billed as "the Yellow Man.") Dragged back, she hides in a closet, a terrified child, while her prizefighter parent batters on the door. Affron makes a persuasive case for this scene as one of the peaks of silent acting and an ironclad one for the picture itself as "the perfect introduction to the art of Lillian Gish."

The Sisters Gish were more of a double-act in life than on film: Dorothy, the sprightly disordered one, Lillian, the loving guardian, the self-conscious embodiment of American principle. Griffith seems to have driven her out of his company for her own good; he knew she had to be a star, but he had no place for any beside himself. She was never as big a name or as big an earner as Mary Pickford or Greta Garbo but she certainly stood for something. A lifelong Republican, she became a vocal supporter of the pre-Second World War America First Committee, taking stances ranging from the naive to the regrettable. But at least her isolationism was based on a genuine hatred of what she had seen in the previous war.

After the silents, she was, as Affron admits, famous for being famous. That's not to say her talking career was negligible. Returning to the stage, she triumphed in the very tricky passive- enchantress role of Yelena in the first major American premiere of Chekhov's Uncle Vanya. This book reminds us just how fecund and vital Broadway was in the '20s, '30s and '40s, and Gish was an important part of it. She still got roles in films too, including one near-masterpiece: Charles Laughton's terrifying The Night of the Hunter (1955), another film about hunted children -- a Griffith movie with its eyes open -- though by now Gish has graduated from victim to protectress.

She had always been adored by intellectuals who generally despised the movies; one of them, the vitriolic (and often insufferable) theatre critic George Jean Nathan, probably became her lover. One has to say "probably" because there seems no evidence that Gish, who never married, ever had a physical relationship with anyone. Nathan, though given a considerable build-up, fades out of the book, as he did from Gish's life; Affron never even tells us when he died.

Griffith, who does get an obituary notice, had also long been out of her life, though never, it seems, out of her thoughts. She never ceased to pay tribute to him as the greatest of all filmmakers, but on her reactions to his painful decline, (and on the details of that decline) the book is reticent.

Nor does it fulfill its announced aim of depicting an entire epoch in American culture. We register that Gish worked with many interesting people and had many famous friends; the names pile up, but few of them to much effect. The book is better on the legend than on the life, and best of all on the iconic early movies. Gish's own autobiography was subtitled The Movies, Mr. Griffith and Me, and though Affron manages to make us rethink the order of the billing, his own mixture remains essentially the same.
