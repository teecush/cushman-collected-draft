Friedrich Schiller, Germanys national playwright, died just over 200 years ago. For most of that time, hes been a reproachful emblem of how the English-speaking theatre neglects the European classics. Things, however, are looking up, Schiller-wise. Nine years ago, his Don Carlos was Soulpepper's inaugural production, and a triumph. The same company's Mary Stuart, in a tense and thrilling production by Joseph Ziegler, is a worthy companion-piece.

Mary Stuart is the greatest what-if play ever written. Its central scene is a confrontation between Mary Queen of Scots and her cousin, captor and nemesis, Elizabeth I. This never happened, indoors or -- as the play has it -- out. The historical dethroned Mary did indeed cross the border into England seeking asylum, and the historical Elizabeth did lock her up in Fotheringay Castle and finally have her beheaded there on charges of treason. But the real Elizabeth refused Marys entreaties for a face-to-face encounter, since she knew that if she agreed, she would be obliged -- for reasons personal, ethical and political -- to pardon her.

Schillers Elizabeth knows it, too, and so do her canniest counsellors. But in the play, she disregards their advice and yields to flatterers who persuade her that an encounter will be a perfect chance to humiliate her rival. For a time it looks as if both ladies will get their druthers. Mary, who's been through three husbands and a lover at the least, gets to act the tactful penitent; her virginal relative gets to gloat. And then they both lose it. Elizabeth twists the knife once too often; Mary snaps and says unforgivable things. And history, having received a powerful new kick in the farthingale, returns to its appointed path.

The threat Mary represents to Elizabeth, and to England, is part genealogical, part spiritual and part erotic. If Elizabeth is literally a bastard, then Mary has a claim. The Catholic church thinks so; it also needs Mary as a devout champion of the true faith against the Protestant usurper. But she's not only a religious symbol, shes a sex symbol. These attractions combine, to intoxicating effect, on a young gentleman named Mortimer who has been converted simultaneously to the worship of Rome and of Mary, and has vowed to deliver her. (The collateral damage would include her keeper, his own uncle.)Mortimer is Schillers own invention, but he had plenty of doomed historical equivalents.

Peter Oswald's translation (recently staged in London, and I prefer the Canadian production) is sharply angled for modernity. The politicians who, equally devoid of remorse and of personal malice, manoeuvre for Mary's death, ring any number of contemporary bells. So, even louder, do the inflamed zealots like Mortimer who set off on assassination missions after taking the sacrament and being granted absolution in advance; they could be suicide bombers.

Zieglers direction only falters at what should be the climax of the climax. The queens quarrel-duet has built up a good head of steam, but it fizzles at the end; instead of a last locking of eyes and minds, we have Mary delivering her last defiance to Elizabeth's departing back. Otherwise, the production (which I saw at a preview) has tremendous energy, pace and point. The play is, among other things, a good romantic yarn; it starts with a bang and ends with a shock.

Schiller may have blackened Elizabeth, and he undoubtedly sentimentalized Mary, but he packed both roles with subtleties, showing how each finds reflected in the other what she most fears, and most desires, in herself. Yanna McIntosh's Mary is beautifully in the moment, bursting with suppressed pride, packed with grace. Nancy Palks Elizabeth is a regal flirt, toying with everyones emotions, including her own and -- in a familiar image that is here made fresh -- left alone with her power at the end.

There is a very strong supporting cast. David Storch may be somewhat mature for Mortimer, but he digs deep -- deeper, possibly, than his author could have anticipated--into the psyche of a twisted idealist, a philosophic firebrand. Stuart Hughes balances him with a flamboyant account of the Earl of Leicester, a self-hating survivor who flatters both queens (unhistorically in one case) and deceives both. William Webster's Lord Burghley offers a third way: an understated statesman, the most civil of civil servants, the most conscientious and the most lethal. There is good work as well from C. David Johnson (unrecognizable as a terrified secretary), Jim Warren and, as the one retainer Mary's allowed to retain, Hazel Desbarats.

ChristinaPoddubiukhas designed a handsome and sombre set, though one that denies the one exterior scene its proper sense of release. Her costumes, too, are rich, though their scheme is a bit hard to follow. The ladies wear period frocks; the men have suits but also ruffs -- except for Leicester who goes ruffless (maybe he has the wrong kind of neck) and the French ambassadors, who are thoroughly modern millefeuilles. But then, when it comes to style, Paris has always been ahead of everybody else's time.
