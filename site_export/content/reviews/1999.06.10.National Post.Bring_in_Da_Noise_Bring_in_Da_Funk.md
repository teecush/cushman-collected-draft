BRING IN 'DA NOISE,

Bring in 'Da Funk

Hummingbird Centre

There are a couple of young guys in Bring in 'Da Noise, Bring in 'Da Funk by the names of David Peter Chapman and Dennis J. Dove, and they play drums. That's a serious understatement.

In the first part of the show, they appear before us, decked out in an assortment of pots and pans on which they proceed to execute a dazzling, intricate series of rhythmic tattoos. It's an interactive duet: Each beats on the other's armour as well as his own. They juggle, too, sending their sticks whizzing through the air and landing them always on the right beat.

In the second act, they execute an almost equally exhilarating routine using half a dozen upturned garbage pails as their instruments. I have decided, after carefully inspecting my fantasies, that I would rather be a drummer than a tap dancer.

Maybe, though, I don't have to choose. The point of Noise/Funk seems to be that drumming is a form of tapping and tapping a form of drumming. The whole show is a tribute to, and demonstration of, percussive rhythm -- 'da Beat. It was conceived by Savion Glover, the young American prodigy tapper, and it was presented in New York largely as a showcase for his talents. However, the touring production now in Toronto shows that it works only slightly less -- which is to say awesomely -- well without him.

Glover choreographed the show, George C. Wolfe of New York's Public Theater directed it, and the show is little -- though not much -- more than a non-stop battery of visceral excitement. There is a bit of a story in there, though it keeps (this seems to be the right expression) wrong-footing itself. It appears to start out as a history of tap; we're told that the black slaves on the plantations were forbidden to play drums, and therefore took to communicating messages with their feet. It could be that the two kitchenmen (billed in the program as the Panhandlers) are another part of this; denied proper instruments, you use what you've got.

As things progress, tap dancing turns from message to medium -- a means of representing and interpreting black history onstage. The show has started with the slave ships. After emancipation, it turns to the first years of supposed freedom. A number called The Lynching Blues begins with a cheerful dance done to the sounds of a washboard and ends with the miming of a noose around the performer's neck. A narrator and some projected captions help to keep us in touch, as both the show and the characters move north, lured by the promise of plenty. The beat, visual and aural, is brilliantly used to depict life on the assembly line. We are in Chicago, scene of especially virulent race riots, and then in Harlem, for a sardonic look at the birth of black show business and of new opportunities for exploitation.

After the intermission, the show gets increasingly fragmentary. What we have been seeing is an underground tradition of tap -- hard, fast and frequently angry. The more familiar mainstream kind -- economical, graceful and ingratiating -- is exhibited only to be mocked, in a sequence about the most celebrated of black tappers, Bill Bojangles Robinson (played by Dominique Kelley), and his movie career as a sidekick for Shirley Temple, which was lucrative -- but not as lucrative as hers. (There was more to Robinson than this, and if he was giving the white folks what they wants then so, in an altered climate, are Wolfe and Glover. Once again, you use what you've got.) A sequence about a character called The Kid (Christopher A. Scott) trying to make it as a dancer in Hollywood and falling in with a sultry chanteuse plays like a blackface version of the Broadway Ballet from Singin' in the Rain.

There is an extraordinary sequence in which Jimmy Tate, playing the Glover role, recreates the styles of four seminal tappers of the past, each more intricate than the last. There is a scene called Taxi in which four performers dance out the different kinds of rage engendered by trying to hail a cab on the streets of four different cities -- mockingly restrained in Paris or Rome, flat out furious in New York. (It's uncertain whether this is being presented as purely a black experience or as part of the universal urban agony.) Increasingly, as the show progresses, tap meets rap, and they seem made for each other.

There is a chipper if not always helpful narrator (Thomas Silcott) and a singer (Debra Byrd) who is sonorous but inflexible. Monotony, indeed, is a liability of the show, except presumably for dance buffs; but it's a hypnotic, dazzling kind of monotony. It's pretty much guaranteed to bring you to your feet, in homage to theirs.
