POND LIFE

Factory Studio Theatre

- - -

There are these two heterosexual couples, one married, one not, the former having invited the latter over for dinner. The married man is called Dick, and the living-together guy is called Richard. Dick's wife is named Sandy, short for Alexandra. Richards partner is named Daisy, which looks at first like a break in the pattern, but it turns out that she is thinking of adopting the stage name of Alexandra Rampage; she is, God help us, a singer-songwriter.

It seems at first that we are in for an absurdist satire about interchangeable suburbanites, on the order of Ionescos The Bald Soprano. However, the tone soon shifts to something more resembling Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf only nastier, if you can imagine, with the twist that here it's the hosts rather than the guests who are the aggressors. Their opting for full-length names rather than diminutives is the outward sign of that. Not that the modest hosts are any bargains either.

Pond Life takes its title from an inaugural discussion between Dick, whos a doctor, and Daisy on the mating habits of frogs. These, as the play progresses, begin to seem preferable to the habits of humans, mating or otherwise. Its an elegant piece, though a bit over-extended for what it is, written and directed by Gord Rand, an excellent actor most recently seen in Soulpepper's Translations.

Richard and Daisy are late, which enables Dick, once he's through with his biological lecture, to while away the time putting his hand up his wifes skirt. Just as shes beginning to warm up to the idea they hear noises and -- despite the fact that they're expecting visitors -- react as if they had burglars. With a skilful tackle, the normally meek Dick brings the rugged Richard to his knees. This is about the last aggressive thing Dick does in the play since, once the apologies have been made and food has been proffered and refused and drinks accepted, the black-clad Richard reveals himself, proudly, as a life-style bully. He's cool with it because its cool on him. He says so himself and, interestingly, nobody gives him any arguments.

The ostensible reason for the get-together is that the two women are friends at work. It turns out that the bonds between -- and across -- the couples are more complex than that. There have been amorous complications in the past, and they threaten to persist in the present. (Nobody here gives much thought to the future.) Also, Dick and Richard are business partners, nefariously trading in human embryos. Dicks job gives him the access. Toward the end the worm does turn, but that just makes him seem even wormier. Sandy's bubbling innocence begins to seem a lot too true to be good. Daisy is high on vanity and colonic irrigation.

My usual reaction to plays that inform us how rotten we all are (we in the audience being, by definition, as middle-class -- and therefore as guilty -- as the people we are gaping at) is to ask to be told something I don't know. There is nothing revelatory about arraigning our society as a collection of unhappy breeders, festering in slime. But it can be done well or badly, and Rand does it well. Its uncommon for a play to sustain an insouciantly bleak comic tone as persistently and successfully as this one does. It only falters, as plays so often do, when the characters start talking about other characters whom we havent met and in whom we don't believe. At these moments the script does raise its voice, break out in sobs, lose its own cool. But there aren't many of them.

Rands direction, on an economic and fastidiously virginal set designed by Chad McMillan, is even more aassured than his script. The actors, mostly non-Equity, are perfectly in style, with Ryan Blakelys nervous goodwill as Dick and Jeanie Calleja's nervous good humour as Sandy bending under pressure but, alarmingly, never breaking. Kerry McPherson's Daisy delivers her character intact, though she sometimes rushes out to grab her egotism rather than letting it come to her. As Richard, who uses boorishness as both an armour and a weapon, Ryan McVittie is insuperably insufferable. Which is, of course, a compliment. Note that in a mixed-doubles cast, both male actors are named Ryan. Is this a sign of the times? How long has it been going on? Was it confusing at rehearsals? Note too that it's a name that cannot easily be shortened. That, on the play's showing, is scary.
