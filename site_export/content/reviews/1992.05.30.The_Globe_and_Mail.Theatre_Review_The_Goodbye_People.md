THEATRE REVIEW THE GOODBYE PEOPLE

Saturday, May 30, 1992

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Toronto ONT -- WRITTEN BY HERB GARDNER Directed by Douglas Campbell Starring Al Kozlik At St. Lawrence Centre Rating: A dogged portrayal of how you gotta have a dream' BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Toronto THE Goodbye People is one of those pieces, regularly turned out by U.S. playwrights, about how you gotta have a dream. They go back, I suppose, to William Saroyan (The Time of Your Life) who thought delusions were necessary and desirable, and Eugene O'Neill (The Iceman Cometh) who thought they were necessary and destructive, though not quite as destructive as the truth.

Some of the appeal of the Saroyan and ONeill plays lies in their archetypal bar-room settings, and in the generous size of the clientele that inhabits them. Its always entertaining to watch the ball being passed among 20 actors. The orchestration counts for as much as the theme. Herb Gardner's works are more sparsely populated, which may be due to deteriorating economics but probably reflects his own taste as well. He writes chamber-pieces for appealing derelicts, Samuel Beckett sunny-side- up. And he sets them out-of-doors.

His most recent and successful slice of down-and-out whimsy was I'm Not Rappapport, two old men in a park, which I enjoyed though Ive forgotten most of it. It had the occasional barbed line and, when I saw it, it was performed with flair. The Goodbye People, which is about 20 years older (the St. Lawrence programme is coy about dates) is being done with doggedness. It takes place on Coney Island out of season, here represented by a bit of pasteboard design that will have no nonsense about finding beauty in ugliness or style in sleaze. It may be truthful, but its oppressive.

It is not as if the play were offering searing insights into the human condition. Gardner's attitude toward the dreams we're alive for is cautiously approving. His prime mover is Max, an old Russian immigrant who once owned a hot-dog stand - Maxs Hawaiian Ecstasies - on this stretch of beach. It went quickly bust, but he has always wanted to try again. All he needs is a partner"investor with $10,000. He finds one: a young man who has been trying for years to quit his job with a Christmas-toy company and realize his ambitions as an artist. Hes been coming to the beach every morning to see the sun come up, and missing it; the opening moments find him phoning the New York Times and giving it hell for printing inaccurate sunrise information. He can only express his feelings over the phone.

However, meeting Max's daughter brings him out of all that. Though, needless to say, she has her own hangups.

The project, despite repeated warnings from members of the uptight outside world, goes ahead. The excitement kills Max, courtesy of an onstage heart attack. So reality wins out. But wait: the younger people, the man especially, have caught the bug, and as the curtain falls it looks as if they will persevere with both the business and their relationship.

There is some sense in this. At least, there is some emotional satisfaction. I was quite happy to see the end of Max, and the thought of Hawaiian frankfurters makes me ill. But the others have much of their life before them and might as well try spending it together. That's my rationalization.

Some of it is due to the acting. The role of Max has Shameless Virtuoso Performance written all over it - written so large, in fact, as to leave little space for characterization - and Al Kozlik is, at best, okay. He functions all right in dialogue, but in the long solo riffs he stagnates. The author has not made it easier by decreeing that the more excited he gets the thicker his accent becomes; the actor is not magnetic enough for us to make the effort to understand him, or to love him without understanding him. The character becomes unbearable, with his endearing refusals to recognize his daughter because shes had a nose-job or his son because hes a lawyer. He even dies with cuteness on his lips. Other characters make a case against him - Gardner is a conscientious playwright - but the play is doomed if we agree with them. I agreed even before they'd begun.

I believed, though, in Peter Binsley as the artist. Not at first, when I thought him too insistently strident, but I came to value the detail with which he built up his picture of a salvageable neurotic, and the rare ease of his silences when he watched the others. I did not believe in Brenda Robins as the daughter (who, as is customary in this kind of play, sees through her dad but not far enough) but I did enjoy her, with her swagger and her crackle.

Besides, what do I know? Al Kozlik got exit applause after his first big tirade, and so did Sandy Webster for a grimly unconvincing performance as his former partner, who arrives late to do a number about the advantages of accepting old age. They were being clapped for playing old men who make long speeches. So, clearly, the play has succeeded. The audience has bought into its values. The Goodbye People plays until June 20.
