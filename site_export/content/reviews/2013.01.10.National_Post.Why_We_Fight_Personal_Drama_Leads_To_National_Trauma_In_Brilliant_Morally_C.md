THEATRE REVIEW

This Is War Tarragon Extra Space, Toronto

The central event of Hannah Moscovitchs This Is War is an atrocity committed in Afghanistan, one in which Canadian forces are indirectly involved. It happens offstage, and its details only gradually become apparent. They're revealed to us by four Canadian soldiers who are testifying before an invisible military tribunal. At least, that's what we initially think theyre doing. It turns out that theyre answering questions from a broadcasting organization back home. It still looks - and, to them, feels - like an interrogation.

This ambiguity is, I think, needlessly confusing. Butthat's my only reservation about a play that grips unceasingly as a story, is brilliantly constructed, morally complex and takes us inside a world. Moscovitch was one of the writers on CBC Radio's Afghanada, and her play obviously owes a debt to that show, though the tone is much starker. The four characters - consummately acted in Richard Roses production - are all members of the same platoon.

One of them is a woman, a master corporal, and two of the men are in lust with her. The younger, a teenage private, seems besotted with her; for the other, the platoon commander, older and married, shes a diversion on an especially drunk and desperate night, following a game of poker. Her own degree of desperation and tedium at a given moment are likely to govern how lucky either of them gets with her. Jealousy results.

In the context of the war itself, these personal emotions may seem transient and trivial. Moscovitch, with superb cunning, lays a trail that leads inexorably from these apparently petty conflicts to the final catastrophe. It determines who goes on a mission and who gets left behind; how they behave under fire and how much responsibility the Canadians hand over to their less disciplined Afghan allies.

The corporal, the captain, the private take turns at telling the story to their interviewers and hence to the audience, and their separate accounts are acted out before us. Some scenes get repeated. In terms of actual events, the different viewpoints hardly vary. What changes is our appreciation of the emotional effect on each of the participants; a scene may go on a little longer because of whom it hurt or whom it made angry.

Underlying everything is a sense of guilt: the knowledge of having made mistakes in a situation where anyone, especially a child, could be either friend or foe, and the determination not to have it happen again. Some of the worst events in the play are the fruits of the best intentions. The characters are conscientious professionals, cynical but vulnerable, in a hopeless situation. Moscovitch gives them tense, searing, sometimes witty dialogue that, at least to this civilian, rings absolutely true. This is a text without an ounce of fat on it.

Rose's direction is similarly lean, in a set by Camellia Koo that places us with the actors in their camouflaged tent, and with lighting by Rebecca Picherack that it wields like a weapon. (One of the most effective scenes takes place in total darkness.) Lisa Berry's corporal is wonderfully tough and troubled. Ari Cohens captain, world-weary and battle-weary, has tremendous force, the more unsettling because the actor has the ability to turn his rages on and off in an instant. Ian Lakes private covers the most emotional territory, aging as much psychologically as physically; gauche and open-faced in the main action, elaborately bitter when giving his testimony.

One of the play's subtleties is the way the characters effectively close ranks when bearing witness; whatever their individual fates or differences, they are all soldiers facing down an alien authority. They may not have been coerced into talking, but they all behave as if they were. Another is the way the play takes us beneath the uniforms by reminding us in which parts of Canada each of them grew up.

Our fourth neighbour, played subtly and sympathetically by Sergio Di Zio, is an army medic who seems for much of the time to be outside the main action; he's everybodys friend and confidant, always appearing when needed, which suggests that hes more than fulfilling his job description. Reticent about himself, he isn't in the opening line-up of interview subjects. Eventually, though, he does get to speak for himself. In fact he has the last word, and it's a stinging one. It performs, in fact, everything the play's title promises. It caps what is, on every level, a magnificent evening.

This Is War runs at the Tarragon Extra Space until Feb. 3 before heading to the Prairie Exchange in Winnipeg Feb. 20 to March 10.
