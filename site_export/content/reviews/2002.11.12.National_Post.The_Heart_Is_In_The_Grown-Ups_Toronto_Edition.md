ALPHONSE

duMaurier Theatre Centre, Toronto

If by some impossible twist of fate, a man crossed paths with the child he used to be, and they both recognized each other, they would both crumple to the ground, the man in despair, the child in terror.

That infinitely suggestive sentence occurs at the outset of Alphonse, a one-actor piece by Montreal writer Wajdi Mouawad. It has been brought to Toronto by the resolutely unpatronizing young people's company Theatre Direct, making a bold and rare incursion into a public theatre.

The earth-shaking confrontation it suggests never actually happens. What the play does is chip away at the idea, producing some fragments that are merely whimsical, others that are haunting. Alphonse is a schoolboy who one day fails to come home. His family - - father, mother, brother and sister -- seem more resigned than worried, but they do notify the police. The inspector is a humane but world-weary fellow who regards the assignment as a welcome change from the sordid criminal cases that usually come his way. He goes to Alphonse's school, interviews his contemporaries. Some of them are indifferent to the missing boy, regard him as a dreamer with no particular aptitude for work or games (other than marbles), but he seems to have had no real enemies and at least one close friend. Or at least a former friend. It appears that, of late, Alphonse has been keeping his distance.

Instead, he has developed a relationship with an imaginary friend, a boy called Pierre-Paul Rene, whom he dreamed one troubled night and who now fills his waking thoughts as well. And what Alphonse is doing, while his parents and siblings sit home for a week implausibly refraining from tearing out either their own hair or one another's, is walking down a country road inventing adventures for Pierre-Paul Rene. His friend can handle adventures, since he is described as "a gentle boy with a one-note voice who is never surprised by anything."

Alphonse himself, about to enter adolescence, is at the stage of being surprised by everything, not always agreeably. Even his first possible girlfriend scares him as much as she fascinates him, or at least his feeling for her does. Alphonse would gladly trade places with Pierre-Paul Rene, and in his mind this is what he does.

He sends Pierre-Paul Rene off on a mission. He is to journey to Pastryburg and confront the evil Flupan, who has stolen all the recipes for cake and turned the cooks into popcorn. The mythology here seems second-hand; besides, it's difficult not to think of Pierre-Paul Rene as a male Dorothy, from The Wizard of Oz, being sent on a mission by Peter Pan, or perhaps, given the schoolroom scenes, by Peter Pan crossed with Adrian Mole. Along the way he meets an old man who asks him the kind of questions ("Why does a tree grow tall? Why does a man grow old? Why does a river run into the sea? Why does the Earth keep turning?") that used to be asked in preachy pop songs and probably still are.

That's the worst of the play, but there is also a best. It comes out in a dark, troubling remark remembered by Alphonse's school friend: "A last dream, in Alphonse's opinion, is a beautiful story to tell." It sets the inspector's mind whirling, and ours, too. There is a magical rhythm in the writing that comes through admirably in Shelley Tepperman's translation, and it is set off well by the incursions of the adult world.

This is the strength, too, of the production. The direction, by Lynda Hill and Alon Nashman, is somewhat prosaic, save for the descent of a magical shower of popcorn, but Nashman's performance, narrating and playing all the characters, is ideal. There is a buoyant, bright-eyed quality about him that summons up both Alphonse and his alter ego. There are also a grace, speed and facility that enable him to change gears without crashing, and a toughness (not hardness) that serves beautifully for the grown-ups, who come over not only as believable people but as believable francophone people. Much of the heart of the play is in them: in the inspector who longs to keep the found Alphonse in the police station for an extra hour, just so the boy will look at him, and in the taxi driver who takes him home and worries about his health.

If the efforts of the adults to enter the mind of a child are more persuasively rendered than the thoughts of the children themselves -- well, the author is an adult, and maybe he is proving his own point. There is an anguish that comes over most of us when we recall pieces of our childhood, or at moments when we look at our own children. The play doesn't quite capture it, but it drops hints.
