STEEL PIER

MUSICAL REVIEW

STEEL PIER

Monday, May 26, 1997

Written by David Thompson,

John Kander and Fred Ebb


  


Directed by Scott Ellis


  


Starring Gregory Harrison, Karen Ziemba and Daniel McDonald


  


Rating: **

Dance marathon takes a few missteps

THE ritual of 1930s dance marathons will be familiar to anyone who knows They Shoot Horses, Don't They ? -- the novel or the brutally exciting film. The Broadway musical Steel Pier is not directly adapted from either of them, but it has, unavoidably, many of the same features. Here are the children of the Depression, dancing till they drop in a seedy, glittering ballroom for the sake of free food and lodging, and a big cash prize for the couple who endure the longest. Here are the grinning radio sponsors, the vocal trio chirping out encouraging jingles, and the gradations of despair and ambition among the contestants.

Here also are a very similar trio of leading characters: the world-weary female veteran cranking herself up one more time, the idealistic young man who partners and loves her, the glibly corrupt master of ceremonies. This time the heroine and the emcee are secretly married, and working a scam at the expense of the other players. The hero, an aerial stunt man, also has a secret that is not fully revealed until the very end, at which point it torpedoes whatever credibility the show may still possess.

This, as it happens, is not much. Though the musical is professionally mounted and everyone in it moves very smartly, it has almost no energy. The show takes its name and its setting from a dance hall in Atlantic City, N.J., and Tony Walton's set lowers effectively through the spurious glamour; the evening's predominant visual impression is of black occasionally pierced with gold. Scott Ellis's direction is efficient but stolid. A dance marathon is a wonderful subject for a musical and one rather expects dance to drive the show, the way the late Michael Bennett once did with far less promising subject matter in a show called Ballroom . Susan Stroman's choreography is a persuasive synthesis of late-twenties and early-thirties steps, with emphasis on the sexier ones, and there is an extended first-act routine that really shakes the place up. There is also an unhappy attempt to do a dream ballet on old-fashioned airplane wings, in the manner of Flying Down to Rio , and there are moments in the staging (when the leading lady detaches herself from the ensemble for a personal showdown) that strike thunderous echoes of equivalent moments in A Chorus Line . Good or bad, original or derivative, the dances are kept strictly subservient to the book.

Unfortunately the book (by David Thompson) has no life. The characters go through standard permutations of yearning and betrayal, and the principal performers do little to vitalize them. The men -- Daniel McDonald as the nice boy, Gregory Harrison as the crook -- are two shades of bland. Karen Ziemba is livelier but charmless, and though she works hard at her big breakdown number, it just seems like hard work. The song itself, Running in Place ,suggests desperation on the creators' part as well as the character's. In all, this sounds like the weakest score that veterans John Kander and Fred Ebb have written since The Rink , another show that tried for bleak and ended up with dank. In fairness, I like that score considerably more now than I did then, and the hero and heroine in Steel Pier have one song, a playful poolside duet called Wet , that I expect to grow on me.

The two best songs, though, go to a supporting character. Debra Monk plays an aging good-hearted gal, and brings warmth, depth and bite to everything she does. First, to win pennies and applause from the dance-hall crowd during a break, she sings a song called Everybody's Girl that is so blatant it hardly qualifies as double-entendre. She makes it funny; she also conveys the woman's pride in getting the number across and her humiliation at having to do it at all. In the second half, she has a song in character, asking a younger man to take her away with him. This time the power comes not from denying the words of the song, but from conveying that she means them more urgently than she can bring herself to acknowledge.

Others stand out in a smaller way; Alison Bevan as a distressed gentlewoman among the marathoners and Ronn Carroll as a contest factotum, jollying up the dancers, counting them out when they fall, and strictly monitoring their styles for excess eroticism ("you can bunny-hug but you can't grizzly-bear"). We seem to have met them before, too. A musical of They Shoot Horses has been mooted for years; maybe the rights were unavailable, but its ghost hangs heavy over this show, sucking its blood. They shoot remakes, don't they?

Steel Pier is at the Richard Rodgers Theatre, New York, for an indefinite run .
