THEATRE REVIEW

BURIED CHILD

National Arts Centre, Ottawa

---

Sam Shepard's Buried Child, strikingly revived by the English theatre company of the National Arts Centre under Peter Hinton's direction, is a play full of half-articulated relationships. It seems fitting that its first scene, maybe its best, should show a man in his seventies, lying on a couch in front of a silent television while sipping from a whisky-bottle, being harangued by his wife whom we can't see as she is offstage and presumed to be upstairs. Their conversation -- censorious and controlling on her part, sullenly defiant on his -- sets up a dynamic between them and reveals selected details of their family life, past and present, while leaving others in shadow.

The septuagenarian paterfamilias is called Dodge; his wife is Halie. They live in an Illinois farmhouse circa 1978. (That at least is when the play was first produced.) Halie, a vociferously religious woman, is about to pay a call on her priest, with whom she may be having an affair. She and Dodge had three sons; one, who might have been a football champ, is dead, in what are hinted to be sinister circumstances. The other two are still very much around. They are Tilden, who appears brain-damaged and who keeps coming in from the backyard

bearing corn and carrots that nobody can remember planting, and Bradley, who lost a leg in a chainsaw accident (well, how else?) and is fearsomely angry. Dodge lives in fear of Bradley, who has a habit of creeping up on his sleeping dad and cutting his hair off, which seems sort of Biblical. Hanging over, or perhaps under, them all, is the memory of the buried child of the title, whom we finally learn to have been the fruit of incest. This preoccupation may explain why when a real, now grown-up child appears -- Vince, the son of Tilden -- almost nobody recognizes him. With him he has brought his girlfriend Shelly, who at first recoils in horror from the scene-- she expected Norman Rockwell -- but soon rolls up her sleeves and does her best to sort it out. Among other admirable things, she defangs Bradley by depriving him of his artificial leg, leaving him twice emasculated.

It's tempting to describe this neo-Gothic set up as Cold Comfort Farm without the laughs. In fact, though, there is plenty of humour, all of it intentional. Shepard's writing glistens, in both inflationary and deflationary modes, and most of his characters stand tall -- even, or especially, Dodge, who takes nearly everything lying down. The relationships, taken one at a time, give actors and audience plenty to bite on. I can't see, though, that it all adds up to much beyond itself. There are too many holes in the storytelling (as opposed to the actual story) for that. And calling it post-modern doesn't help; it rarely helps anything.

There seems to be a presumption, extended to Shepard's plays since they started appearing in the '60s, that if you put a dysfunctional group of American people on a stage, you automatically have a statement about the whole country. There's an online study guide to Buried Child in which the phrase "the American dream" crops up in practically every sentence. But untilled soil, a deceased sports hero and the play's debut in the post-Vietnam era aren't enough to raise it above its station as fairly powerful personal fantasy. And maybe not all that personal: Its home-invasion plotline wakes echoes of Pinter's The Homecoming, while the abandoned child motif recalls the collected works of Edward Albee. The big revelation, when it comes, doesn't reverberate.

The NAC's program synopsis suggests that the ending, in which Vince takes over both the house and his granddad's signature red cap, may also be a hopeful new beginning. It looked to me more like the cycle about to repeat itself. (His girl has left.) American drama, though, is almost incapable of resisting a redemptive finale, so the program may be right. It's one thing that makes the rare plays that do face the darkness, Death of a Salesman or Glengarry Glen Ross, so impressive. And they are statements about America.

All that said, Hinton's production is hypnotic, monumental in scale and gesture, delicate and detailed in feeling. Eo Sharp's set, which I can best describe as all-over slatted, fills the vast and largely unfurnished stage so that the characters, under Robert Thomson's jagged or mellow lighting, seem marooned but not dwarfed. Troy Slocum's sound design is sharp and scary; when a glass breaks here, the next town must be quaking in its shoes. Company teamwork is impeccable, though individual performances vary. David Fox's Dodge, alternately craven and commanding, is superb; Randy Hughson shambles, perhaps a touch too endearingly, as Tilden; and Adrienne Gould, red-haired and determined as I've never seen her, climbs by degrees as Shelly should, though with some audible effort when she reaches the top. Clare Coulter's Halie is so piercingly right when merely heard that her actual appearance is an anti-climax, maybe because Halie is a self-righteous pillar of the community while Coulter always seems a born-again eccentric; the bright yellow dress that she wears when returning from cavorting with the clergy doesn't look right on her, though it blends perfectly with the yellow roses that Shelly is carrying at the time: an instance of the care with which this production calibrates its visuals. Alex Ivanovici's Bradley works hard for menace; Christie Watson's Vince is personable but without a perceptible arc, though he's the character who needs one. The play is widely regarded as its author's masterpiece; I find it imposing, but frustrating. My favourite Shepard play remains the compact and poetic Fool for Love; until I saw it I thought Shepard's followers so many sheep. - Buried Child runs until Jan. 24 at the National Arts Centre in Ottawa; Feb. 1 to 22 at the Leonor and Alvin Segal Theatre in Montreal.
