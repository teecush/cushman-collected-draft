Robert Lepage is a guy who takes his time. It's not just that he goes in for marathon productions. By some standards, they arent even all that marathon. The Royal Shakespeare Companys recurring history-play cycles have kept audiences in their seats from 10 in the morning to 10 at night; so has Tom Stoppard's The Coast of Utopia. Compared to that, the nine hours of Lepage's Lipsynch, the theatrical centrepiece of this years Luminato festival, might count as chicken-feed. Within them, though, he takes extraordinary hypnotic chances.

Theres one right at the start. The show begins with a woman standing, in front of curtains and on a foreshortened stage, singing, beautifully. She is, we subsequently discover, an opera and concert performer, as is the actress playing her. The frame disappears and we're on a plane, the most convincing I have ever seen on a stage, partly because it's night and for some time nothing seems to be happening. Then something does; a woman collapses and, despite the crews best efforts, dies. Its all very discreet. Meanwhile, a baby cries.

Lepage can stage some things so well, you wonder why anyone else bothers. Another example from much later in the day, again creating high drama out of apparent banality: A woman, working as a caregiver in a London flat, sings along with -- yes, lip-synchs -- a Burt Bacharach song that's coming off the radio: something that anyone might do, but here rendered so naturally, so believably, you feel you know the woman even though she's yet to say a word.

So, whats the connection between the woman in the flat and the baby on the plane? Follow carefully: Sarah, the caregiver, is an ex-prostitute who was abused by her brother, now a BBC newsreader, when they were both in foster care. He grew up with unsavoury sexual tastes, bordering on pedophilia, and one of his victims was a 17-year-old Nicaraguan girl, forced into white slavery in Hamburg. She was the woman we saw die in the opening scene; rescued by a Canadian investigator, she was on a flight to Montreal. The baby was hers, fathered by the BBC guy.

Ive picked two moments more or less at random, but almost any two would do. Somehow or other, everything ties in. The tying is done, I must say, with pieces of string that threaten to snap under the sheer weight of coincidence. The show is avowedly less concerned with unity of plot than unity of theme: "voice, speech and language, which Lepage tells us in a program note are three very distinct and totally different things." Well, maybe. Anyway, every scene involves at least one of them. But then what scene in any play wouldn't? The idea is far too generalized and all-embracing to mean very much

It does get some ingenious applications. The opera singer Ada, who's German, was also on the flight and she ended up adopting the child. (She must have had enviable skills at dealing with bureaucracy.) They moved to London; theres a scene on the Tube thats as technically simple and transfixingly believable as the one on the plane; cars are the only mode of transport that give Lepage trouble, bringing him out in fits of the cutes. The boy, Jeremy, develops his own vocal talents, turning to rock; tiring of music, he goes into movies (another virtuoso sequence), making a picture about his birth-mother, about whom he's been fed inadequate information. Meanwhile, Ada hooks up with Thomas, a German brain surgeon who leaves her for Marie, a Canadian jazz singer whose life (and voice) he has saved. Marie has a sister, Michelle, who helps others find their voices through reading and writing poetry. This nine-hour play has nine principal characters, each given their own named sequence, with its own visual identity. Michelle's, summoning a snowbound town behind glass walls, is one of the finest; as is the performance of Lise Castonguay, who gets off one furious and funny tirade.

Other episodes are less successful; Sarahs story turns into a routine procedural, and so, even more, does its sequel, involving a Scottish detective called Jackson (somehow, the Scottishness seems very TV) with, for themes sake, an estranged French wife. Even weaker are the scenes for Sebastian, a Spanish sound engineer, which are pointless farce. Equally, some things are stunning. The invalid in Sarah's care is a speech-therapist, whom we've met in an earlier scene in which the actor (Im not sure who) does an uncanny job of lip-synching to an authentic filmed interview. Well, either that or were hearing real speech acted, in all its imperfections, as we've never heard it before. There's a very moving moment when Marie finds within herself the voice of her dead father: an epiphany that follows a lengthy period of boredom. When Jeremys mother, Lupe, finally gets to tell her own story, its shocking, but it doesn't tell us anything we don't know from documentaries. The themes of sexual abuse and of relations between siblings seem at least as central as anything else, but they arent often personalized. And if its all about memory, well what isn't?

The multinational, impeccably accented cast includes Rick Miller, very lively as Jeremy; and as Ada. Rebecca Blankenship, whose singing is as vibrant as her acting is subdued, though the latter somehow adds to the reality of it all: for a play about speech, this one has a high quotient of inaudibility, but maybe that's ironic. The actors are also the writers, along with Lepage and a dramaturge. The phenomenal sets and lighting are by Jean Hazel and Etienne Boucher. Look at the stage at almost any moment and you'll see a masterwork, but not a masterpiece.
