The Vanya of Christopher Durang's Vanya and Sonia and Masha and Spike is nobody's uncle. He is, though, a brother. Masha is his birth sister and Sonia was adopted. Masha is a famous actress with five marriages behind her - as well as, probably, as the best years of her career. Still, her success has enabled her to send money back to Pennsylvania where Vanya and Sonia have been tending to the house, and also to their now-deceased parents. These parents had been pillars of community theatre, and were devoted to the plays of Anton Chekhov. This accounts for them having given their children Chekhovian names. Christening, it seems, is destiny. Vanya and Sonia, marooned in the country, have grown into disconsolate middle age, mourning their lost potential, like their namesakes in Uncle Vanya. The parallels strengthen when Masha (who doesn't have much in common with the Mashas of Three Sisters and The Seagull) arrives, partly to attend a party but mainly to announce that she intends to sell the house out from under them.

This may be the place to mention that its grounds include 10 cherry trees; whether that's enough to constitute an orchard is a matter of fierce debate. With Masha on her trip to the country is Spike (his parents obviously weren't Chekhovians), a 25-year-old hulk attractive to both sexes until they get to know him.

That completes our roundup of characters who make it into the title. There are two others, who don't. One is Nina, the young lady next door, who like her counterpart in The Seagull wants to be an actress, and is overwhelmed at meeting a reallife movie star; she also seems to have designs on the star's boy-toy. The other is Cassandra, a cleaning-lady who, as her name suggests, has wandered in from another dramatic canon altogether. Like her ancient Trojan counterpart, she goes around uttering prophecies that everyone disregards, largely because they're incomprehensible. She also does things with voodoo dolls. She doesn't fit. But then, as we've been told, the local amateurs couldn't bring off Greek tragedy either. Maybe the curse persists.

Durang's play, a Tonywinner in 2010, arrives in Toronto in a first-rate production, directed by Dean Paul Gibson and presented by David Mirvish by way of the Royal Manitoba Theare Centre, and it contains some funny stuff, along with some that's irritating.

Some of the shadowboxing with Chekhov's originals is delightful. Vanya tells us that he could never decide whether Konstantin's play in The Seagull was meant to be ahead of its time or just a terrible play. (I hear you, brother). So he's written one of his own, expanding Konstantin's post-apocalyptic scenario into a scarifying fable on climate change; there's an impromptu reading of it with Nina, as in Chekhov, playing the leading role, in this case a Molecule. The performance sets off some unexpected, and

unconvincing, confrontations and role reversals.

Around here, Broadway wish-fulfilment sets in. It looks as if Durang's characters may actually change their lives. That's what Chekhov thought their prototypes ought to do, but he knew that they wouldn't and was honest enough not to pretend otherwise. Durang's play, like 90% of American drama, takes the easy way out.

Paying homage to Chekhov, Durang sentimentalizes him. He still provides treats for actors, two of them in particular. Steven Sutcliffe's Vanya, coming down to breakfast in a smock, actually looks Chekhovian; he proceeds to give a superb account of a man who has always gone along to get along, and knows it. In the second act something bursts; he's handed what I can only describe as a shy tirade celebrating the shared cultural experiences of his youth, as opposed to the atomized multi-tasking of Spike's generation. Some of his examples sound suspect, but he acknowledges this, tempering his defiance with apologetics but still plowing exhilaratingly ahead. It's a virtuoso display of changing tempi within an overall charging rhythm.

Having Fiona Reid in the role of Sonia is beyond a blessing, since her own comic style, and especially her timing, have always been of the Smith persuasion - though I think she may have bettered the instruction; she's easier to believe. Anyway, a scene in which she takes a phone call, alternating between Sonia's real voice and her assumed one, is a jewel. Nobody does hilarious indecision like Reid, and we're all pulling for her to make the right choice. (We're sentimentalists, too.)

The role of Masha is loaded with famous-actress cliches, but Jennifer Dale sails through them with a conquering mix of bravura and bravado. Ellen Denny's Nina is a delightful bluestocking ingenue, and Luke Humphrey brings a fitting shamelessness to Spike, who likes to parade in his underpants. Making Cassandra fit with the rest of the play may be an impossible task, but Audrey Dwyer might do better at it if she lowered the volume.

Blood Wedding (1933) by Federico Garcia Lorca is a play I've always half-wanted to see: only half because I feared that its Spanish poetry, verbal and visual, might wilt in an Englishlanguage production, or be swallowed in what directors like to call "ritual," and that its story might not hold tight. I was more than half wrong.

The wedding is that of a bashful but determined groom and a bride who has had amorous dealings with a family on the other side of a blood-feud. The ceremony goes ahead but the bride is then abducted on horseback by her former lover Leonardo, the only character allowed a name; the hoofs of Leonardo's horse are a recurring motif in the text (translated by Langston Hughes) and in Soheil Parsa's direction, which offers the diagram of a firstrate production: not quite the thing itself because some of the performances are lacking.

Derek Kwan's Groom and Carlos Gonzalez-Vio's Leonardo, however, are fine; and Beatriz Pizano as the groom's mother is a bodeful presence. The production is a joint venture by Parsa's Modern Times company and Pizano's Aluna Theatre. Trevor Schwellnus' design consists of windows, that darken or crack to thrilling effect; he also provides a lovely frame for the apparition of the Moon who appears, along with Death, to preside over the final pre-destined mayhem.

Vanya and Sonia and Masha and Spike runs until April 5 at the Panasonic Theatre; Blood Wedding runs until March 29 at Buddies in Bad Times Theatre.
