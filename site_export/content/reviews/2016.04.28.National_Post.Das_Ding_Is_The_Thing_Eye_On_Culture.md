Theatre Smash, a company with a track-record of producing contemporary German plays, is billing their latest under its attractively euphonious original title of Das Ding. The actual performance, though, is in English, and the translated title of The Thing appears (in parentheses) as a helpful appendage.

So: what is the Thing of Das Ding? It's a bolt of cotton fibre, and the play belongs in that line of works - more often novels or stories than plays - that are united by an inanimate object passing from one character, or group of characters, to another.

Our first sighting of this object is as something big, flowing, bulbous, and stagefilling: solid and capacious enough for a man wearing a crown to be sitting on top of it. He turns out to be King Manoel I (died 1521) of Portugal, wearily granting an audience to Ferdinand Magellan (also died 1521), famous for discovering the Straits that bear his name, and at this moment in dire straits of his own.

He has been wounded in the royal service, and would like a new job, or a pension, or the king's support for a world-circling, tradeseeking voyage around the southernmost tip of the Americas. While he makes his case, his projected route is illustrated by clever little arrows projected onto the great big cotton ball: a delightful effect. Not that it does Magellan any good. All his requests are jovially rejected by a monarch who has had quite enough of explorers seeking handouts, even if they are subsequently vindicated by history. Nothing else in Philipp Lohle's play is as engaging as this prologue. We are thrust forward into the present, where circumnavigation has become commonplace and mainly electronic. It's a world in which those who plant cotton are still forgotten, and the fortunes of those who trade in it or make stuff from it can be equally precarious.

At first, we meet a pair of cotton-growing farmers, Siwa who is African, and Guy who's French-Canadian. Canada has replaced Germany in this production as the play's home-base, with Birgit Schreyer Duarte's translation snappy enough for the transference to be credible. Then, we're introduced to two Chinese men: Wang and Li, who manufacture and export cotton T-shirts. They also hire out janitors, though the cotton connection here is none too clear: if it's a sideline for them it's a sideshow for the play.

Back in Canada, there's Patrick, a young man - a boy, really - who has taken a photograph of his sister's bedroom that has gone viral, for reasons that are never very clear; maybe it's just meant to illustrate the randomness of social media. Patrick wears one of Wang and Li's T-shirts. He has another sister, Katherine, who's married to a man named Thomas and who, to his displeasure, has taken to exhibiting herself sexually on the Internet. One of her online admirers is Li, who crosses oceans in hopes of finding her and making her his own. Katherine and her husband wear Wang-Li Tshirts too.

All these roles are shared between five actors, who also serve as a chorus. The Thing cleverly splits apart to reveal them, reacting - always with interest, occasionally with alarm - to the various uses to which they are put. It's all very cute, or meant to be.

Drew Facey's set design, or rather Thing design, is as progressively ingenious as it's initially impressive, and the cast, under Ashlie Corcoran's direction, hurtle around the stage with great collective enthusiasm. As individuals, though, they're less effective. Naomi Wright comes off the best, as a series of international interviewers quizzing Patrick about his famous photo, hearing what they want to hear in his answers while hardly bothering to listen to them; a neat piece of cultural comment, both in writing and performance. She also gets the evening off to a good start as the suppliant Magellan, with Qasim Khan as a blithely uncaring king.

None of the other characters come to comparable life; the text gives the actors little beyond raw ideas and the actors return the compliment or lack of it. It's revue-sketch presentation but without revue-sketch wit. Commentary and captions help us keep track of where we are and whom we're dealing with; though in a play about competing cultures, ethnically-blind casting seems an idea more virtuous than helpful.
