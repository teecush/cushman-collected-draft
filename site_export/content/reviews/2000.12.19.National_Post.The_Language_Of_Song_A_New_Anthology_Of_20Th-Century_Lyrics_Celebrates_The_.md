Irving Berlin's White Christmas really is a great song, as fine an example as could be found of Berlins genius for matching his words to his music. It isnt a generalized greeting card; it's a first-person-singular song about someone who is missing out on everything except memories.

It only shifts into the second person in its last two lines, May your days be merry and bright/And may all your Christmases be white." Note how that coda, with superbly satisfying artfulness, reverses the title. Note, too, that the lines I've quoted are themselves in quotes; they arent a pat on the head for the audience, theyre what the singer is inscribing in all those Christmas cards he's writing. In fact, they're part of the story.

I confess I never got that last subtlety -- nor did I fully appreciate the quiet ingenuity of Berlins rhyme scheme -- until I read the lyric in Reading Lyrics, a generous and indispensable anthology just published by Random House ($61) and edited by Robert Gottlieb and Robert Kimball.

Gottlieb was, for too brief a time, editor of The New Yorker; he was also a publisher whose services to humanity include the midwifing of Joseph Hellers Catch-22. Kimball is a musical-theatre scholar who has edited sumptuous variorum editions of the lyrics of Cole Porter, Lorenz Hart and Ira Gershwin.

Reading Lyrics gathers the texts of more than a thousand songs from the American golden age, here defined as stretching from 1900 to 1975. It gets us, chronologically and temperamentally, from the supremely extroverted writer-performer George M. Cohan (flourished around 1905) to the supremely introverted writer-performer Dave Frishberg (flourishes around now.)

It is, among other things, the long-needed complement to Alec Wilder's pioneering American Popular Song, which as it happens was published just about the time Gottlieb and Kimball have chosen for their cut-off date. (Wilder, himself a composer-lyricist, is represented in this collection.)

Wilder's was a capacious critical study, devoted to composers; others have since tried to give lyricists the same consideration, but they have lacked the breadth and the sensibility -- sometimes, it seemed, even the knowledge -- to pull it off. Maybe its best, rather than giving us words about words, to give us the words themselves, and let the selection carry its own value-judgments.

The structure of the two books, though, is remarkably similar. Each deals with the major writers, here represented by a minimum of three lyrics and a maximum of 39 (thats Berlin's figure, earned both by quality and longevity). The others in the 30-plus category are Hart, Porter, Gershwin, Oscar Hammerstein and Johnny Mercer. Then, in both books, comes a coda or final chapter, mopping up important songs, one apiece, by writers who in the editors' judgment (some may disagree) dont make the full honour roll.

The two books also share an agenda, declared in Wilders work and implicit in this one. Though much of the material they deal with necessarily comes from shows and movies, they are concerned with the songs as individual entities that can be taken out of context. This probably explains why, while the authors are presented in order of birth, their songs are arranged alphabetically; we are asked to read the lyrics for themselves, rather than as parts of a score.

I don't think there's a song in this book that couldnt be done by one singer, or sometimes two, in a cabaret or on a CD. This doesnt matter too much with the earlier writers, in whose day theatre songs were pop songs, and who wrote in full awareness of that fact. It skews the record when we get to the 1960s -- the Stephen Sondheim generation -- and find ourselves rather arbitrarily confined to a menu of supper-club classics. So the choice from Sondheim's score for Follies includes two rather similar ballads, but not the magnificently evocative Waiting for the Girls Upstairs (an octet) or the venomously vengeful Could I Leave You (too scary, or too tied to its character, or both).

Even with the older songs, there can be problems. The received texts often depart from the originally performed ones, and the editors here seem to have relied -- except with the major writers whom Kimball has already edited -- on the published sheet-music, which often sanitized the songs or just shortened them. So we get a sinless, ginless reduction of Paul Francis Webster's I Got It Bad and That Aint Good, and a Thanks for the Memory in which Leo Robins wonderfully colloquial catalogue of bitter-sweet divorced reminiscence is only half the length it could be. (This song is the classiest of tearjerkers: I wouldn't trust anyone who wasn't reduced to a puddle by it, just as the technicians on the movie-set are said to have been when Bob Hope and Shirley Ross first recorded it.)

There are also some horrendous typos and mislineations, some probably carried over from the sources. Gentlemen, look what youve done to the last lines of Mercers Satin Doll and Jack Yellen's Hard Hearted Hannah. And Jerry Herman may have his limitations, but he would not (in I Won't Send Roses) have rhymed "find things with kind of things."

My first reaction to this anthology was that celebrated by so many of the songs: I fell in love. (How can you not, with a book that even recognizes the existence of forgotten men like Eddie DeLange and Ralph Freed?) Then, as in so many love affairs, I began to notice what was missing. The one really unforgivable omission is They Didnt Believe Me. Remarkable for its music but also for its uniquely-angled lyric (by one Herbert Reynolds, a.k.a. M. E. Rourke - - definitely a one-shot writer), its the first great and still- singable American theatre ballad, on a par historically with Alexander's Ragtime Band and St. Louis Blues, both of which do make the cut.

It's only slightly less startling to come to the end of the Noel Coward section and realize it hasnt included If Love Were All (Cowards "talent to amuse song), or to see that the game loser's By Myself isn't ranked among Howard Dietzs finest hours, or to find the beautiful, contemplative Too Late Now absent from the Alan Jay Lerner portfolio; Id trade it for On the Street Where You Live any night.

Most important totally-excluded lyricist: Oscar Brown Jr., jazz singer-songwriter of the '60s. Runners-up: Jon Hendricks, master of vocalese (and his colleague Annie Ross, for her virtuoso lyric to Twisted); Tom Jones, of The Fantasticks; the Marxist Marc Blitzstein; and Ann Ronell, who went from Willow Weep for Me to Who's Afraid of the Big Bad Wolf -- actually the Disney songs are an important sub-section the book neglects completely.

Its because the book gives you so much that you cant help wanting more. We discover that the American song was laughing at itself almost from birth: The flag-waving Cohan writes a sentimental song (Down by the Erie Canal) that he demolishes in an introductory verse:

The orchestras murder it near and far

It's usually sung by the female star,

The publisher gives her a motor car

And the chorus goes something like this.

(Frishberg, 55 years on, was up to the same thing in Another Song About Paris.)

P. G. Wodehouse, an Englishman on Broadway and as enchantingly deft in song as in prose, is as off-hand as Cohan but far more madcap, as in his song about Napoleon. (He was five feet high/But one tough guy/And I take after Nap.")

Then with Berlin, the immigrant alive to new American sounds, the Jew who wrote White Christmas, lyrics began to swing, and the best of them never stopped. This really is the secret; good popular songs have always been conversational, but the 20th-century American song, whether low-down or highfalutin', has been as alive to musical rhythms as to verbal. In fact, its made the two one. A showpiece example is Larry Harts Thou Swell: formal, slangy, jazzy, funny and tender all at once.

It all came together in the 1930s, when the Fred Astaire-Ginger Rogers movies, in a conscious response to the Depression, reached a zenith of fairy-tale sophistication, and inspired the best songwriters to some of their best work. This was echoed, at ground- level, by less-inspired glamour songs like Sam Coslow's Cocktails for Two ("in some secluded rendezvous ...), and balanced by more realistic New York subway-riding material from the remarkable Harold Rome (Life is a bitter cup of tea/Now I'm just salesgirl seventy- three/I used to be on the daisy-chain/Now Im a chain-store daisy.")

The editors do excellently by Porter and Berlin, juggling comic with romantic, familiar with obscure, and pretty well by Hart, though they largely stick to his most famous songs, with just a couple of classy rarities thrown in. The same treatment is given to Mercer, but he slips through their fingers; verbally resourceful and musically sensitive to an amazing degree, his tone of voice is yet extraordinarily hard to pin down (I know of no recorded collection that has managed it), perhaps because he had little to say, even about romantic love, though he said it angelically.

Some of the lesser names are revelations. Roy Turk, known for Ill Get By, made me chuckle with his Ain't That the Way It Goes, a song for two voices. ("I suppose you dance your best with Mary?/I always step on Rosie's toes/And yet Rosie says you dance divinely/ Aint that the way it goes?)

The team of Livingston and Evans, notorious for Mona Lisa, made me laugh out loud with a lubriciously detailed item called Femininity. Both these songs belong to the 10% in the book that Ive never actually heard, and under those conditions comic lyrics are easier to appreciate.

Love songs are much harder to judge. On the page they can look oddly shaped or obviously phrased; until you hear them with the music, you can't tell whether they're graceful or awkward.

There are one or two songs here that I suspect the editors dont much like themselves. They drily remark of Websters grandiose Love Is a Many-Spendored Thing that it can stand for an entire genre of overblown film anthems." (It comes here with a verse about meeting an oracular little girl in Hong Kong -- a sort of nature girl -- that was new to me and that makes it even worse.)

How do you read Reading Lyrics? Most of the time, if you're lucky, you sing along; the book is an implicit celebration of composers as well as lyricists, and of their Falstaffian partnership above all (not only witty in themselves but the cause of wit in one another). When you get to Martin and Blane's Have Yourself a Merry Little Christmas you will, if youre like me, substitute the bittersweet movie lyric for the saccharine commercial one (where "until then well have to muddle through somehow becomes hang a shining star upon the highest bough").

And did you know that Haven Gillespie's Santa Claus Is Comin' to Town is "perhaps the most recorded song in American music history" (I didnt, but it figures) and it has a verse (ditto) and a second chorus in which Santa arrives bearing little tin horns and little toy drums, rooty-toot-toots and rummy-tum-tums (I did, and Im glad)?

And, maybe it's much too early in the game -- ah, but I thought I'd ask you just the same: What Are You Doing New Years Eve? Actually, that one of Frank Loessers isn't in there, but some of his funniest are. And so is that incredible patter number Ya Got Trouble by his (older) disciple Meredith Willson, four whole columns of it, and for that I'd forgive almost anything.
