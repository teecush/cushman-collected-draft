THEATRE REVIEW

Sister Act

Ed Mirvish Theatre, Toronto

So where do you hide if youve just witnessed a gangland killing, and the shooter wants to rub you out as well, to prevent you from squealing? Well, if you're a male musician (or two) in Prohibition-era Chicago, you dress up in drag and join an all-girls' band. Take it from Some Like It Hot. If youre an aspiring female soul singer in 1970s Philadelphia, as in Sister Act, which takes a lot from Some Like It Hot, you get into a nuns habit and join a convent.

The lady's name is Deloris, and though she doesn't much enjoy her new environment (its no vacation and she has no vocation) she makes the best of things by giving the nuns choir, previously tuneless and moribund, a hip new makeover. Under Deloris's guidance, they so rock the joint that both the pews and the collection box are soon full to overflowing. This is a godsend (so to speak) for the convent and the church to which it's attached, since theyve been under threat of closure unless they can bring in more business, just like the Save-a-Soul mission in Guys and Dolls.

As a narrative, Sister Act, based on the 1992 movie of the same name, is so eclectically derivative that it seems a triumph of restraint for the cop who arranges Deloriss refuge not to send her on her way with a song called Get Thee to a Nunnery.

If the religious house in Guys and Dolls suffered from a shortage of sinners, this one eventually finds itself with a glut of them. (So does the one in Guys and Dolls, but they're nicer.) Deloris's nemesis, who has the near-iconic name of Curtis Jackson and who also happens to be her married boyfriend, but isnt one to let sentiment stand in his way, tracks her down, to find her encircled and protected by all her new friends, from abbess to novice. They seem to have learned their pro-activity from the Nazi-defying sisters of The Sound of Music, and the show does have the decency to acknowledge this with an explicit shout-out.

Curtis pulls a gun on Deloris, thereby suggesting that either he or his authors have taken leave of their senses or, to put it another way, that theyve vacated their premises. Get this: The villain's whole motivation is to prevent the heroine from bearing witness to a murder, and to achieve this he proposes to commit another murder in front of a whole convent-ful of witnesses. He also, more understandably, fails to appreciate a rather delicious irony. He's been moving heaven and earth (fairly literally) to stop his ex-lover from testifying, meanwhile shes been testifyin in a whole different way. And doing it very well.

Just to point this up, the show starts with Deloris and a couple of wannabe supporting Supremes auditioning a song called Take Me to Heaven, which later, with a gospel swing and a more overwhelming vocal arrangement, becomes the singing sisters' first big hit. It sounds very good in both its fleshly and its spiritual guises; in fact, and for once, the best thing about this new(ish) musical is the music.

Alan Menken (Little Shop of Horrors, Beauty and the Beast) has supplied a pulsating set of tunes that make pop theatrical in a way unmatched by any composer since Cy Coleman; there are also a couple of unlaboured ballads with some rich turns of musical phrase. There are neat turns, too, in Glenn Slater's lyrics, and sometimes some whole neat ideas, such as Curtiss vengeful When I Find My Baby, which turns a familiar song-type inside out.

As often happens, the songs with the least wit are the ones that try hardest to be funny, like Its Good to Be a Nun, based on the notion that the mildest off-colour remark will sound hilariously naughty if it's done by a nun. The book (by Cheri and Bill Steinkellner) holds tightly and shamelessly to this idea, which might be called the Nunsense formula. There was once a revue of that name whose one joke was that all the performers wore wimples. Sister Act varies the recipe by adding uplift. Deloris doesn't get religion, but she does get friends; faced with a three-way choice between career, love (theres a vestigial romantic plot) and her sisters, she indulges in some soulful soul-searching, though there seems no good reason why she shouldnt have all three.

"Additional book material" is credited to Douglas Carter Beane; I can't prove that he's responsible for the great one-liners that occasionally turn up, but Im going to believe it anyway.

Jerry Zaks directs a slick production, incorporating one of those silent-movie chases in which people keep smiling while going in and out of doors and bumping into the other people they most wish to avoid. TaRea Campbell does everything possible with the role of Deloris except put an individual stamp on it. Hollis Resnik, though, does have a distinctive take on the Mother Superior, especially in her handling of the wryest lyric lines, throwaways that land. Much of the action pivots on her distrust and dislike of her new resident, though it's never clear whether this is down to piety or just jealousy; as in Billy Elliot, the conflict is painted in because otherwise there wouldn't be a show and painted out because otherwise there couldnt be a happy ending.

The other nuns are all called Sister Mary Something-or-Other; it might be funnier if just one of them wasnt. They're all the kind of crabby that's supposed to be endearing, except for a shy young postulant (Lael Van Keuren) who continually suggests conflicts that never get explored. (A loud song called The Life I Never Led doesnt count.) Among the men, Kingsley Legges Curtis is suitably menacing in his suit; Charles Barksdale droller than such a character has any right to be as his dim nephew; and E. Clayton Cornelious endearing as Sweaty Eddie, police officer and torch-carrier.

There's some blatant heartstring-tugging towards the end, and any suggestions of spiritual depth are best ignored, but the music and some genuine good-heartedness carry it.

Sister Act runs at the Ed Mirvish Theatre in Toronto until Nov. 4. For more information, visit mirvish.com.
