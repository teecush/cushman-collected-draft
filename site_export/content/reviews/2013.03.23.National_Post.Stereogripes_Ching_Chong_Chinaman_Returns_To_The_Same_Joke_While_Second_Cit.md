The California family who make up most of the cast of Ching Chong Chinaman are all Wong. That's the family name; they're of Asian origin, obviously, but they've been in America long enough to have become as insular as the waspiest Wasps. This is the play's basic and remorselessly repeated joke. It might be summarized as Oc-cidents Will Happen. Lauren Yee's play, a success in New York, is being given its Canadian premiere by the fu-Gen company.

Father Wong is a businessman, devoted to golf. Mother is the kind of ditzy homebody who used to be a sitcom staple; that the form's social awareness has advanced somewhat is demonstrated by her remarking, during the family photo-session that constitutes the play's opening tableau, that she's heard the Ku Klux Klan may not be very nice. They've named their daughter Desdemona, though she demonstrates little of the racial adventurousness of Shakespeare's heroine; she's principally concerned with getting into Princeton, and is quite unscrupulous about playing the ethnic card to do it. Their slightly younger son is called Upton Sinclair, presumably after the most socially conscious of American novelists, but here too the name has failed to take; Upton is obsessed with video games, of the warcraft variety. Meanwhile, to help him with tiresome necessities like homework, he's imported a young man from China, providing a new spin on the enduring American dependence on imported and/or sweated Asian labour.

The visitor's name is J, short for Jingquiang, which the family can best pronounce Ching Chong; they're faintly aware that an old racist joke is lurking there, but they doubt their guest will mind as he doesn't speak English. He, in turn, is silently amazed by their inability to order Chinese takeout coherently (which would, I suppose, make them less than typically American, certainly in California) or to handle chopsticks when it's delivered. This bit of cultural reversal comes early enough in the evening to be funny. The tone might hold if Nina Lee Aquino's production were less frantic and most of her actors lighter of touch. Richard Lee's J is an exception, registering an amiable disbelief with which it's very easy to empathize. He also gets to vary the formula a bit by conversing with his mother, in the few moments she can snatch from a call centre back home. (Jane Luk plays a succession of outsider cameos, and this is the best of them.) With the Wongs, J's expanding duties include being caddy for Daddy and impromptu dance-partner for Mom; he has his own talent-show ambitions, and they dovetail with his hostess' private escape fantasies, which are rather delicately realized by Brenda Aquino.

The 90-minute proceedings grow increasingly desperate, taking in plot twists about adoption, and parallel trips to Mexico and Korea. These, the Mexican one anyway, allow for the theoretical up-ending of a few more racial cliches. But a stereotype reversed is still a stereotype. This needn't disqualify it as a source of comedy, and especially of farce, but it needs more wit and invention, and less self-congratulation on its own supposed outrageousness, than are on offer here. That none of the characters is sympathetic doesn't matter; that none of them is interesting, does. The play pursues its one idea with a tenacity that might be called tireless but is in fact tiresome.

The idea itself might make good material for Second City, who of course would do it more economically. Their new show, The Meme-ing of Life, has its own fun with racial expectations. The current troupe's stand-out performer, Nigel Downer, is a virtuoso at holding long, exquisitely calculated pauses; he also happens to be black, and part of the suspense he exerts is due to the uncertainty in the minds, both of the other characters in a particular sketch and of the audience, as to whether his skin colour will prove to be a) relevant and b) threatening. Since one of Downer's roles here is that of a customs guard at the U.S.-Canadian border, you can see how that might apply. (It's also one of the best-developed sketches, even boasting a good punchline.) Downer's other persona is that of a bemused innocent, which makes a very good contrast. He's one of two welcome veterans in the six-strong cast, the other being Jason DeRosse, generally the little man (or boy) who's having a busy day. Items about family in this show are unusually, even uncomfortably, penetrating. So, more expectedly, are numbers about dating. There's also good stuff about politically incorrect female cheerleaders shocking their sensitive male coach, and about the perils of resigning from a gym. There are, too, some duds, but none that are really painful; while the physical production (director, Kerry Griffin) is above-average stylish. The title idea doesn't work, though.

Neither, I'm now inclined to think, does that of Iceland, Nicolas Billon's three-hander promoted from last year's Summerworks. That country's financial meltdown doesn't make too persuasive a metaphor for the domestic greed and insecurity that are the play's subject. The story itself, though, remains gripping. It's an anecdote of greed, sex, poverty and revenge, told by the participants in alternating monologues. Also as before, the acting is excellent. Lauren Vandenbrook, the one newcomer, is less hypnotic than her predecessor, but she's still both tense and moving as the temporary-resident university student who, by finanical necessity, moonlights as an escort and finds herself in a murder story; she also sports an Estonian accent that could probably only be faulted by an Estonian. Kawa Ada is in droll command as the condo-flipping Mammon-worshipper responsible for bringing Iceland into the conversation. Subtlest is Claire Calnan as the lady evicted from his latest acquisition, a religiously sheltered lady who wanders out armed with pepper-spray, and with mouthwash for cleansing herself if she should be provoked into uttering an obscenity. Both come in handy.
