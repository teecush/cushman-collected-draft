TIGER OF MALAYA

Factory Theatre, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - A good story goes a long way, and Hiro Kanagawa, an experienced actor but a new playwright, has one in Tiger of Malaya. It's drawn from recent and still resonant history, and that helps keep it interesting.

At the end of the Second World War, the Japanese General Yamashita, who had earned the tigerish soubriquet by his skill at taking Singapore from the British, was put on trial for the atrocities committed in the Philippines by troops under his command. The play is concerned less with the actual trial than with its preparation and its aftermath. It centres on Yamashita himself and on the two American officers assigned, with less than his full co- operation, to defend him.

There is no question here of the accused pleading that he was merely obeying orders. He was the one giving the orders, and the question is of whether he gave the right ones, and whether his men would have taken any notice if he had. Yamashita, who regards the proceedings as victors justice, has already given himself up for lost. His main concern is whether he will be honourably shot or dishonourably hanged. However, when roused to defend himself, he claims that the situation in the last year of the war was so chaotic he had no hope of controlling it.

His American counsel believe him. They also discover that he had been a moderate, opposed to the war, tolerated by the Japanese high command only because they couldnt do without him. They think he is being railroaded by a vengeful General MacArthur (who never appears, though his recorded voice is heard), and they could well be right. However, though Yamashita may not be guilty as charged, he may still have things on his conscience that escaped the official indictment. He is a haunted man from the beginning of the play. Toward the end, he tells his lawyers -- by now his friends -- the terrible truth. One of them is disgusted, the other reacts with a kind of barbed tolerance.

There is a lot going on here, and though it mostly works as narrative, its theme hangs in tatters. If Kanagawa is attacking the integrity of war-crimes trials in general, at least when carried out by the winners, he hasn't made his case. Nuremberg (to which the play makes occasional sidelong allusions) may have been imperfect but it was also indispensable, and the play simply isn't big enough to cast any light on the worlds abundant supply of later hideousness; the most it can offer is a few automatic twinges.

Structurally, too, it is split. Yamashita, as played by Denis Akiyama, is a compelling character, quiet and grey and charming and powerful. Continually moving toward and then away from us, he is consistent even in his inconsistency. His defenders, who have as much stage time as he does, also have issues, both regarding him and between themselves, and their roles are less successful.

They seem to have derived, by osmosis rather than direct imitation, from other characters in similar circumstances. Captain Lederman, the conscientious young Jewish intellectual, might have stepped out of The Caine Mutiny. His superior, Colonel Hilroy, is a kissing cousin of Rod Steigers redneck sheriff in In the Heat of the Night. He's a salty Southern bigot whose sympathies turn out to be surprisingly wide -- he has, in fact, a history as a civil rights champion. The American colonel and the Japanese general have something in common -- neither of them is what the world would take them to be. But the point is only made at the end, and faintly at that. Before that, the play seems to be tugging in different directions.

Neither the writing nor the acting is well served by Ken Gass' direction, which is forceful but flat; it forges ahead, disdainful of nuance and heedless of pitfalls. Each performer is left to find his own rhythm, and they rarely cohere. As Lederman, Jordan Pettle, though likeable as always, is tentative, often reduced to smiling through. Ken James has a whale of a time as Hilroy, who is indeed a whale of a role, though perhaps a beached one. His elegant bluster, when he takes his clients case to a higher court, is the evenings high point, at least as entertainment. It is, of course, a solo turn.

Two women complete the cast, and neither has a happy time. Ginger Ruriko Busch plays Daisy, a Californian of Japanese parentage who is interpreter for the defence. Her mixed race seems to be her qualification, not only for her place on the legal team but for her place in the play. It produces additional tensions, mostly described rather than presented, that the play doesn't need, or at any rate can't use. Both the character and the performance are ineffectual.

And then theres Rosario (Aura Carcueva), a figure whose taunts and entreaties fill the generals nightmares. Dramatically she is irritating, as phantoms often are. She gets in the way of what is, after all, an exciting tale, the more so because it's only late in the second act that we have any idea what she's talking about. She turns out to be the inhabitant of a village where the general discovered the worst in himself. He doesnt actually say, "The horror! The horror!" but he seems to be thinking it.

But presenting this information in a kind of stationary flashback, split between narrative and re-enactment, deadens its impact; even Akiyamas performance becomes incoherent. (The war criminal traumatized into facing his responsibility is another familiar trope, from The Andersonville Trial to Judgement at Nuremberg.) And the production makes no allowance for Rosario's existing on a different plane from the other characters. She simply happens, like everybody else. Her image, which begins and ends the play, is presumably meant to haunt us as it did the general, but nobody seems to have told the lighting designer.

Still, despite everything, this is a play with a subject and with a certain amount of weight. It's both an impressive debut and the best new play at Factory in some time, probably the best since Belle. (It's a co-production with the National Arts Centre, and the show goes to Ottawa after its run in Toronto.) And, in a world in which something like Miss Saigon not only makes millions but is taken seriously as a compassionate look at East-West relations and the aftermath of war, it's a humane corrective.
