I was quite shocked the first time I saw Johnny Carson. Shocked and frustrated. I was visiting the U. S. from Britain, where I had become accustomed to TV interview shows in which the host not only asked the guest questions but seemed interested in hearing the answers. Some of these conversations even lasted for longer than five minutes. Carson couldn't let an exchange get underway without derailing it with a gag. This, we were never allowed to forget, was his show, and nobody else was going to be allowed to usurp the centre of attention. The fact that underneath it all he was probably very intelligent only made it worse.

In the long years since then, of course, things on the late-night talk-show front have only gotten worse. At least Carson was dapper, in the way he spoke as well as the way he looked. Conan O'Brien, the newish incumbent on NBC's Tonight Show, seems to want to make a style out of being flustered; his claim to maturity is that he doesn't look a day under 23. The trouble is, the nervousness seems genuine: understandably since his predecessor, Jay Leno, is about to come back with a heavily publicized new show of his own that, we're promised, will focus on comedy rather than interviews. This seems sensible of Leno; in the days when he used to be Carson's regular stand-in, he was funnier than Carson, perhaps because he had stuff to prove. That disappeared when he himself assumed the Tonight Show mantle; the format took over and he became just another host. It's mandatory to get a comedian to front these shows, so the presumption is that he has to try to be funny, but you can never get a really great comedian, because they have better things to do.

Meanwhile the conventions have petrified. O'Brien has revived the clasped-hand gesture of benison that was Carson's trademark, but it's unclear whether he means it as homage, satire or propitiatory offering. He also has a sidekick on the Ed McMahon model -- Andy Richter -- though the guy has almost nothing to do. And he has the obligatory cackling bandleader. The role of the musical help has diminished on these shows, since the visiting rock acts tend to provide their own accompaniment. Of course it's nice to see live musicians holding down a steady job in these hard and increasingly mechanized times, but their talents seem to be wasted on blowing fanfares and laughing, in a scattered and sporadic sort of way at the host's jokes, as if the studio audience weren't trusted to be sycophantic enough. The idea of the bandleader as comedian probably dates back to radio days, when Phil Harris did it on the Jack Benny show; it's meant, I suppose, to emphasize the informality of what are, in fact, stiflingly formal proceedings.

You get the monologue, in which the host hearkens back to his stand-up roots. It's difficult to get good material five nights a week, so the style is to make the unfunniness the point. O'Brien falls back on this, and David Letterman on the CBS Late Show is of course the past master of it. He certainly wears the best-tailored suits. I've always found the style-- so "ironic" that you lose track of what it's being ironic about -- extremely irritating; and now that everybody else has adopted it as a fallback, the best to be said for Letterman is that he got there first. There are usually a few political gags -- more of them since The Daily Show started providing competition on cable -- but they're just name-dropping. They fall victim to the overall attitude that nothing really matters and nothing is important. The lesson of The Daily Show is that you have to mean it.

On to the interviews, which have given up any pretense of being more than plugs for movies. If the performer is only an actor, then the chat will be short. If he or she (usually he) is a comedian, then host and guest will have some common ground and the audience gets to enjoy them buttering one another up. It's all-purpose fawning, the show business boys' club, which often seems what the whole world has been reduced to. There is, of course, a lot of convergence on the circuit. Last week you could probably have seen most of Funny People simply by checking out the clips on the chat show. Judd Apatow, guesting on Jimmy Fallon, was quite entertaining about the protocols of going from show to show: You start your day on Howard Stern, doing penis jokes; you end it on The Daily Show, doing political penis jokes.

Fallon, coming on after O'Brien, is supposed to provide an antidote but in fact offers the mixture exactly as before, though without O'Brien's residual charm. The other Jimmy, Kimmel on ABC, is no more likeable but sharper. One night last week he offered a parody on Paris Hilton's search for a New BFF; I didn't even know such a show existed (though I'm not surprised) but I'm not sure he nailed it. Sitting on a throne or lounging on a chaise, he had showbiz narcissism down cold.

The best commentary on that subject, though, comes from the one guy for whom it's worth staying up late (or, in his case, late late) Craig Ferguson. His Scottishness is a tonic in itself; so are his self-deprecating whoops, which he exercised last Tuesday when recounting the experience of recording his own autobiography as an Audiobook ( "I thought, this is a very long book") and anticipating listening to the result while re-reading the book and looking in the mirror. "I've just given myself a bad review for my book--good, I deserve it, smug bastard that I am." Coming on after Letterman, he does seem an antidote, operating on a set that seems to be identical except that he's the only person on it and the view through the windows behind him is not appetizing. He talked about someone who'd sued the network for $40-million: "If CBS had that kind of money, they wouldn't have an emigre host on a talk show. If they had 40 -- dollars, not millions -- they could fix the windows." He's shamelessly literate, sounds as if he means what he says, and his mea culpa about his past alcoholism is a classic, painful and hilarious. (Watch it on YouTube.) He postpones the interview until the last possible moment, and when it does arrive -- on this episode it was with Tony Shalhoub -- it's substantial if not searching. The rumour is that he's being groomed to be the next Letterman. Personally, I don't think he could do it.
