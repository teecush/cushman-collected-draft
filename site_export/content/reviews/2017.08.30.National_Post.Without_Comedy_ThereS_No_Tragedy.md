THEATRE REVIEW Tartuffe Festival Theatre, Stratford

Moliere's title characters are usually obsessives. They are also usually his protagonists. Tartuffe, subtitled The Hypocrite, is an exception. Tartuffe himself is not delusional; hes a con-man, a villain, who knows exactly what he is about. The obsessive in the play is Orgon, the man who falls for Tartuffes professions of piety, takes him into his home, and persists in worshipping him, even as his entire family tell him how crazy he's being. Title notwithstanding, Orgon is the central character, the role that Moliere himself, no shrinking violet as an actor-manager, chose to play in Paris back in 1664.

He's certainly the main attraction in 2017 at Stratford. Graham Abbey plays Orgon brilliantly, as a middle-aged man intent on bettering himself, spiritually and physically. Racing up and down the steps of his beautifully appointed 21stcentury home (Julie Foxs set design is clean-lined and sumptuous), pausing for pushups or to blend himself a smoothie, hes a bit of a smoothie himself. Like all the best comic performances, Abbey's catches you constantly off-guard; his verbal and psychological evasions always come that fraction faster than you expect, in stark contrast to too many of the other performances in Chris Abraham's production which go furiously for the obvious.

Even at the end, when the scales have fallen from Orgons eyes, Abbey has the audience doing mental double takes. He gets every possible laugh, every possible shade of meaning, out of Ranjit Bolts rhymingcouplet translation, which has four beats to the line rather than English verse's customary five. This means the rhymes come around even more rapidly than in, say, Richard Wilbur's unbeatable version. Bolts lines, though, are generally snappy, even if I note with alarm that his latest adaptation is of Ben Jonsons Volpone, which I'd always thought was written in English to begin with.

Others in command of what they're doing and saying are Maev Beaty as Orgons wife Elmire, especially in her earlier, cooler scenes; Michael Blake as her pontificating brother; and Rosemary Dunsmore as the unstoppable mother-in-law, dominating a family squabble by shouting, in aggrieved tones, "may I just this once be heard? Then theres Tartuffe himself. Tom Rooney should by rights be magnificent in the role, and he certainly looks wonderful in the posters. In performance, though, he comes on looking and sounding like Rasputin. Maybe the inspiration was a line in the translation where he refers to himself as a slave," here self-consciously altered to "Slav. It makes no sense. Why, even in this updated setting, would Orgon, a church-going French Catholic, succumb to a professed holy man who's plainly Russian Orthodox? Physically if not verbally, Rooney's Tartuffe is up to his usual standards of impeccability, but theres no centre to it and no threat. The accent, in a not uncommon phenomenon, short-circuits the performance.

The production suffers, for me at least, from comparison to Laszlo Martons superb Soulpepper staging which I described at the time as taking both the play's farce and its incipient tragedy to the limits. Abraham's production also goes for the farce but with less finesse, notably in the scene for the two young lovers trying to outdo one another in self-sacrifice. And what are we make of the scene in which Tartuffe rebukes the maid Dorine (Anusree Roy) for the depth of her decolletage? This production has her bumping and grinding all over him, in plain contradiction of her (and the plays) point, which is how little it apparently takes to set him off. Abraham is great at directing high comedy (see The Matchmaker and the central scenes of his Taming of the Shrew) but when he goes low, he gets indiscriminate, just throwing everything in.

The plays key scene shows Orgon disillusioned by being made to hide under a table while Tartuffe tries to seduce his wife. In this staging he crawls inside an ottoman, making him less visible and the scene both less funny and less serious. Why, in every production now (including, I confess, the Soulpepper one) does Tartuffe have to strip off? Back in the 1970s John Hirsch directed this play in this theatre with Brian Bedford, fully clothed, as Tartuffe and Domini Blythe as Elmire. Their seduction scene remains one of the tensest, most grown-up things I have ever seen on the Festival stage.

Out with the comedy goes the potential tragedy. For all my delight in Abbey's Orgon, I never felt as alarmed for him as I did for Oliver Dennis at Soulpepper when his world, almost literally, collapsed around him. For that matter I never felt that he genuinely loved his daughter as Dennis'Orgon did, making his attempt to marry her off to Tartuffe all the more horrible.

As for the ending, Orgon is saved when a messenger arrives, announcing that King Louis has seen through Tartuffe and will be hauling him off to prison. Here, the messenger's speech is sprinkled with words like bigly," as if he's representing President Trump. But since the king, however artificially, is doing the right thing, that would put Trump on the side of the good guys, which is unlikely to have been the intention. The effect is facile, yet another attempt to appear "relevant" by dropping - or, in this case, hinting at - a hated name. Actually a play, classic or otherwise, can only be relevant if the story is well-told and the characters convincing. If that can be achieved, it hardly matters where or when you set and dress it.

National Post Tartuffe is in repertory through Oct. 13.
