Profile of Kristen Thomson.

Kristen Thomson talked her way into her first role on the Toronto stage in 1994, and she has been a welcome presence there ever since.

The play was David Mamet's Oleanna, about the conflict between a young female student with failing grades and the male university professor whom she accuses of sexual harassment. In legal terms she wins the contest but many people have thought that the play was slanted against her. Thomson was one of them, and she went to the audition thinking, "this play bugs me so much I don't want to see anybody do it who isn't as irritated by it as I am."

She reflects now that "there are certain plays where it helps to be mouthy about them." Anyway, she got the job, played the part beautifully, held her own against the far more experienced and unrelated R. H. Thomson, and definitely arrived.

Now 36, she "didn't start acting until I was 22." She grew up in London, in "a very athletic family." Not being an athlete herself, she was quite withdrawn and, though she enrolled in a few acting classes in high school, she didn't much like them.

She went on to the University of Toronto, where she had friends who encouraged her to join them in the acting program at University College.

"That was very good for me. It's not a hobby course; it has to be a major. There's a strong academic component. You don't need it to be an actor, but it gives you a useful perspective."

(Two of her classmates were Waneta Storms and Ruth Madoc-Jones; she was reunited with both of them this year in the all-female production of A Midsummer Night's Dream at Theatre Passe Muraille.)

The course must have taken. Thomson went on to the National Theatre School and got her first job straight out of there when Miles Potter, now best known as director of The Drawer Boy, cast her in Dancing at Lughnasa in Winnipeg and Ottawa. She went on "getting decent work out of town," which led to Bob Baker, then director of Canadian Stage, asking her to audition for Oleanna.

That was the beginning of her breakthrough; it was consolidated by her appearances in two of George Walker's Suburban Motel plays, with the author himself directing: a decidedly un-academic experience for an actress of her background. "It was like being on a holy mission for everyone involved. George really pushes you to go out on a limb. He takes your mind out of the driver's seat."

It's one thing to make desperation compelling. It may be a rarer achievement to make the same impact as a character of rock-like sanity. She brought this off as Stella, the contented sister of the manic Blanche DuBois, in the Soulpepper revival of A Streetcar Named Desire. Diana Leblanc, who had earlier directed her in Skylight at Manitoba Theatre Centre, "wanted someone earthy for Stella" and was rewarded by a perfect performance, sensible and sensual.

It began a continuing association with Soulpepper which has since taken in a comic servant in The School for Wives, a contrasting pair of maids in an Ionesco double bill, and -- most arrestingly -- Yelena, the flame to the various male moths in Uncle Vanya.

When Laszlo Marton, Soulpepper's Hungarian Chekhov specialist, cast her in this she was taken aback. "Yelena is supposed to be beautiful. I'm not a dog, but I'm not beautiful." Not to be ungallant about it, the men in the play have little to compare her with, and Thomson brilliantly presented a woman not above trading on her looks, but frightened of the response to them; her response to a declaration was to reach desperately for a drink.

She is, as it happens, one of the most attractive performers on the Toronto stage, in terms of both looks and warmth; and the goodwill she has earned could be felt flooding across the Tarragon Theatre's studio auditorium at the beginning of this year, even though she was appearing there behind a set of masks.

The play -- undoubtedly Toronto's hit of the year -- was I Claudia, a series of monologues she had written; there are four characters, the principal one a pre-teenager recoiling from her parents' divorce. A child of divorce herself, she "wanted to write about that emotional territory." She did it, however, without wallowing.

The masks helped (she discovered them on a return visit to her old theatre school), provoking a wonderfully heightened blend of plaintiveness and farce. She won twin Dora awards as actress and playwright, though she has doubts about the latter category.

"Fundamentally, I don't believe that just because I wrote something, I'm a writer," she said.

I Claudia was largely improvised and then set down, and Thomson still can't see herself sitting at a computer and hacking out a script. The work on I Claudia goes on; it comes back to the Tarragon in January, slightly revised, and then moves on to the Belfry Theatre in Victoria. Nobody expects it to end there.

I calculated that in 2001, Thomson played nine different roles spread over four separate productions. Two of them were in the Dream (her Shakespearean debut): Bottom the weaver, with whom she had some problems that she traces to the character's multiple identities; and Hermia, whom she played as "a bit of a princess" delightfully and very boisterously.

"I tend," she says, "to have an abundance of energy that can get overpowering. But I've got not bad at accepting myself. I haven't come out limping or wincing from anything I've done."
