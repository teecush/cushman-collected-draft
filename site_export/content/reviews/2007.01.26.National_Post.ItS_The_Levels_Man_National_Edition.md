Mabou Mines Dollhouse

Premiere Dance Theatre,

Harbourfront Centre

- - -

No need to unroll a red carpet to welcome Mabou Mines to town; they've brought their own. The stage floor is covered in red when we take our seats, though everything around and behind it is dour and bleak. Then billowing red curtains start descending, enfolding the backs and sides of the stage and even parts of the auditorium. (Hey everyone, we're in a theatre.) A lady enters and takes her place at a piano, and her face is red too, at least the area around her eyes; it turns out much later that this is to make her look Chinese, which I don't think she is. An even plusher curtain falls at the front of the stage, making it look as if the show is over before it's even begun. But it rises again, revealing a large packing case, which when opened discloses a fully operational doll's house, which is quickly put together to serve as the environment for what follows.

Which, under the title Mabou Mines Dollhouse, is a re-imagining by New York's senior experimental company of the Ibsen play generally known in these parts as A Doll's House and regarded as the modern theatre's first and finest feminist manifesto. The beginning is rather impressive. The show's ending is very impressive. The miniature setting falls away for Nora Helmer, pampered and patronized wife of the banker Torvald Helmer, not just to declare her new-found independence, but to declaim and even sing it. The scene has been transported to an old-style opera house, the heroine waxing passionate on a balcony, surrounded by puppet couples in private boxes; the consciousnesses of the lady puppets, or some of them, are visibly elevated while the gentleman puppets look, as far as I could tell, perturbed or simply uncomprehending. Nora, announcing her departure, strips off everything she wears, including her flaxen Nordic wig. From below Torvald, who is in bed, looks up aghast.

Nora is somewhat under six feet tall, Torvald somewhat under four. The other female and male characters are cast to the same scale. (The tallest of all, presumably on the principle that the last should be first, is the maid, who is also pregnant.) This, along with the nursery setting, which incorporates tiny furniture and a rocking horse, is the production's central and most obtrusive device, and it makes most of the actual play -- everything in fact until the finale -- very heavy going. The set is a relentless actualization of what the play offers as a metaphor. The casting takes that concept and duly dolls it up. It has, of course, its own built-in ironies. Here are these women, submitting to these men who are actually much smaller than they are. Here are these men, condemning their women to live on a scale to which they themselves are or should be accustomed. Once you're past the initial surprise, the evening settles down to become an interminable statement of the obvious.

For all his revolutionary credentials Lee Breuer, the director, isn't out to undermine Ibsen; he's out to underline him. This goes not only for the play's message, which is unaltered, but for its method. The plot involves blackmail, guilty secrets and convenient confidences; most productions try to play down the melodrama, but this one, in a joyless sort of way, revels in it. The actors strike exaggerated attitudes at every twist of plot or change of mood. The music played is by Grieg, since the work's national origin is another thing the production is at pains to emphasize. The language is English, but the pronunciation is, or so I assume, authentic Norwegian. Nora's name comes out as something like "Nula," and she is always Fru Helmer, never Missus. And since her husband calls her a doll and a little bird, she twitters and flutters throughout. Indeed, the entire cast is in a constant state of pirouette.

The text, already substantial, is lengthened by some nightmare sequences in which a corps of bogeys act out the heroine's worst fears of having corrupted her children by her earlier feckless behaviour. The production also offers the revelation that 19th- century people, even people in classic 19th-century plays, had sex. The minor characters of Krogstad and Mrs. Linde go down on one another -- sequentially, not simultaneously -- right in the middle of their friends' dollhouse. More importantly we're left in no doubt that riotous times in bed are what kept Nora and Torvald together, especially Torvald. But even this isn't new. A fine production in one of the Atlantic Theatre Festival's first seasons, directed by Michael Langham, set the whole last duet in the bedroom, achieving devastating results without getting childish about it. That's one of the most irksome things about productions like Breuer's; they behave like they're first with the latest when they're actually old hat.

Bad acting, whether done accidentally or on purpose, is never invigorating to watch. It's especially enervating when the actors themselves are discernibly good, as most of them are here. Maude Mitchell as Nora does everything that's asked of her and then some, and Mark Povinelli strikes some very impressive sparks off Torvald, especially when strutting all over his domestic domain or when recalling his fury at having the obsequious Krogstad working in his office. I've never heard the latter bit better done. It's one of the few occasions when the production is genuinely funny, as opposed to just trying to be. Another is the same actor's cry of "No more melodrama!" immediately before launching into another bout of it. Not funny is having the Chinese pianist exit in a huff when someone makes a derogatory remark about her native land. She has to be pacified with cries of "It's in the script!" Ron Rosenbaum's fascinating new book The Shakespeare Wars has a wonderfully apposite sentence about "antiquated academic avant-gardistes who can't get over the stunning revelation that a play isn't real ('It's, like, artificial, man')."

The actors all talk through throat mics, in accordance with the director's longstanding belief that unamplified sound is unacceptable to modern audiences who want to hear every subtle modulation; not that many of those are in evidence here. Audiences who know the play will feel patronized; those who don't will probably feel baffled. But then there's that last scene, to which there's even more than I have outlined. There's a suggestion that Torvald is dreaming it. Then he wakes up and finds that it's real. He runs offstage and out through the auditorium, calling out his wife's name. It's her triumph, his tragedy. And it beats any amount of traditional door-slamming.
