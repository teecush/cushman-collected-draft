GUYS AND DOLLS

Festival Theatre, Stratford, Ont.

- - -

The name of the classic American musical comedy Guys and Dolls arouses very joyful feelings in the hearts of members of the audience, and even of dramatic critics, whenever it is displayed upon a playbill. This is a show that dates back to 1950, and ever since that year it is well known for providing members of the audience, and even dramatic critics, with evenings of great merriment, and also of tunefulness. And I have to say, right off the bat, that this Stratford Festival production is definitely one of those evenings, and that there is a smile on the lips of everyone who leaves it, and probably a song in most of their hearts as well.

But in the breasts of dramatic critics, though probably not of members of the audience, the prospect of witnessing a new production of Guys and Dolls is apt to provoke trepidation. For when reviewing such a production, it is a point of honour among certain of these critics to pay tribute to the style and spirit of the late Damon Runyon, on whose short stories of the gamblers and showgirls and other characters who congregate around the Times Square district of Manhattan this musical is based; and this tribute is not too easily paid, for Runyon is an author with many distinctive qualities, one of which is that he is such a writer as hates and despises the use of the past tense. He is also such a writer as has no objection whatsoever to the deployment of long sentences, and indeed takes a positive pleasure in deploying them.

Another very clever scribe who is now deceased and who is named Abe Burrows writes what is called the book, or script, of Guys and Dolls, after many other scribes try and fail. And an even cleverer guy named Frank Loesser, also deceased, is given first shot at writing the music and lyrics, and no other shot is ever necessary because when it comes to fitting words to music and music to words, this Loesser is maybe the smartest guy to operate on Broadway in the 20th century or maybe on any street at any time.

One of the original producers is Cy Feuer, and he is still with us though naturally a most senior citizen, and in fact he recently writes a book titled I Got the Show Right Here, which is a title he borrows from the opening line of Guys and Dolls: Namely and to wit, "I got the horse right here, from a song called Fugue for Tinhorns, which is a trio with overlapping words and notes and a very classy way indeed of commencing a musical comedy. On the matter of Guys and Dolls, Feuer says the secret of Loesser's and Burrows' success is that, not being dramatic critics, they are faithful to the spirit of Runyon but not to the style because they know that when spoken, either on stage or screen, said style is likely to fall flatter than any pancake. So the big love duet of the show is called Ive Never Been in Love Before, whereas in strict Runyonese it would of course be called I Am Never in Love Before, which is by no means as mellifluous or as romantic.

The parties who sing this duet are Sky Masterson, the highest roller among all the hotshots in New York, and Sergeant Sarah Brown of the Save-a-Soul Mission, whom he inveigles into travelling with him to Havana but whom he brings home safe and sound. This ill- assorted couple make up what is called the serious love interest. There is also a comic love interest, which is furnished by Nathan Detroit, sole proprietor of New Yorks most respected floating crap game, and Miss Adelaide, who sings and dances at the Hot Box nightclub and is Nathan's fiancee of 14 years standing, though whether standing is her customary position during this relationship is a question I am too much of a gentleman to pursue.

These two affairs of the heart are most cunningly intertwined in the plot, though according to Feuer's volume, one or the other of them will always dominate depending on where the show is being played. For example, in Philadelphia, where the show first tries out and which is a very respectable town, it is a show about Sky and Sarah. In New York, which is by no means as respectable, it becomes a show about Nathan and Adelaide.

I have to say that Stratford must be a very respectable town indeed because here Sky and Sarah walk off with the show. Indeed, Scott Wentworth is indubitably the best Sky Masterson I ever see. He plays the part at Stratford before, 14 years ago, and it may well be that he is 14 years too old for it now, and indeed his voice frays a little and he is not helped by his costume, which is surprisingly drab. But his command of the stage and of the idiom are absolute, and he delivers his lines with such wit and economy that they sound like the funniest remarks in the show, which is not a possibility I ever contemplate before now.

Cynthia Dale plays Sergeant Sarah, and I have to say that I never enjoy her singing as much before as I do here, especially when she sings Ill Know and If I Were a Bell. Geordie Johnson plays Nathan Detroit and is both amusing and endearing, though more than somewhat hyperactive, even for Nathan, who is a great worrier.

Sheila McCarthy as Miss Adelaide is likewise very funny in spots, though the Brooklyn honk she adopts is by no means original for this kind of role. The plush delivery case from which she emerges to sing Take Back Your Mink looks like a very Hot Box indeed, though the overt eroticism of routine itself is surely misplaced. Likewise, the fast tempo at which this Adelaide is forced to deliver her Lament is most unfair to her, depriving her of laughs even on such a line as if shes getting a kind of a name for herself, and the name ain't his," which is only the single funniest line ever written in a musical. The proof of this comes in the second act, where she gets to sing what is known as a reprise at a much more reasonable pace and slays the audience three times in the space of a single chorus.

Kelly Robinson's production is generally cold to begin with, perhaps overawed by the size of the stage and by the triptych of unwelcoming arches that dominate Debra Hansons set and that pay inadvertent tribute to the Festival Theatre itself by looking 1950s Shakespearean. But it warms up when we get to Havana, as is appropriate; while in both the crap game ballet and the Save-a-Soul revivalist meeting, Michael Lichtefels, the choreographer, and his jumping jack-knifing performers come up aces.

The songs Luck Be a Lady and Sit Down Youre Rockin' the Boat have me walking on air while glued to my seat. The latter number is led by Bruce Dow as Nicely-Nicely Johnson, whose girth is commodious and whose voice is practically a counter-tenor. His pal Benny Southstreet (Shawn Wright) is also somewhat refined, and even shy. For real roughneck flavour, such as distinguishes the original production, which is cast practically from off the streets, we must rely here on Harry the Horse (Nigel Hamer), who is truly threatening. His friend Big Jule (Grant Linneberg) looks and sounds solid enough, though personally I think that bringing him on in a Cadillac with two beautifuls in attendance is a ploy that is somewhat desperate.

The forces of good, however, need no artificial support, being impeccably represented by Patricia Collins as General Matilda B. Cartwright and by Douglas Chamberlain as Brother Arvide Abernathy, who here sounds Scottish rather than, as is customary, Irish.

By the end, cast and audience come to an understanding common at performances of this piece, which is that none of us can believe our good fortune at being involve. If you ask me who, apart from Wentworth, is the star of the show, I can only say that the show itself is the star. With every production it looks more like Broadway's masterpiece, though of course to some of us it always is.
