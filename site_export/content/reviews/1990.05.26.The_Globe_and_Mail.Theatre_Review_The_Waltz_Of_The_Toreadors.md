THEATRE REVIEW THE WALTZ OF THE TOREADORS

Saturday, May 26, 1990

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Niagara-on-the-Lake ONT -- Written by Jean Anouilh Directed by David Giles Starring William Hutt Missteps break this Waltz's rhythm BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail NIAGARA-ON-THE-LAKE INTHE LAST year of his life, Bernard Shaw wrote to his old friend and impresario Barry Jackson who was about to present a play by Jean Anouilh: "I never heard of the unspellable and unpronounceable M. Anhouilh. Is he available in English? Are you translating?"

Since then, Anouilh has come into fashion and gone violently out again. His tragedies seem soft-centred and many of his comedies merely whimsical. Probably the most durable plays are his black satires on marriage, age and disillusion: Ardele (the play Jackson was gearing up for) and its sequel, The Waltz of the Toreadors, which opened at the Shaw Festival Thursday night.

As a festival choice, The Waltz barely scrapes under the wire. Shaw died in 1950 and this play was written, according to the program, in 1950- 1 and first produced in 1952. Along with this information comes a note by the director David Giles, correctly pointing out that a play once thought savage now seems "almost tender" and identifying one of the major keys in its constantly shifting score as being "sheer farce." His production does quite well by the tenderness, muffs most of the farce, and misses out almost entirely on the horror that underlies them both.

Everything hinges on the play's central character. As in the previous night's production, Misalliance, we are in the run-up to the First World War, this time of course in France. General St. Pe, veteran of several military campaigns and rather more amatory ones, sits dictating his memoirs and seducing chambermaids. Lechery is a habit with him, mechanically indulged to help him forget his jealous ex-opera-singer wife, lying apparently bedridden in the room next door, and to escape the tormenting fantasies of the man he could have been and the young man he once was. Seventeen years ago, a dashing major at a military ball, he met the love of his life, danced the eponymous waltz with her, but never bedded her.

Now she returns, still faithful and with evidence that his wife isn't. Divorce and happiness are within his reach. But, of course, they elude him. Too much time has passed. A pair of ungainly daughters bear witness to that. So, in another way, does the presence of his 20-year-old secretary, a personable male virgin. Even his besotted beloved begins to feel and resent the pressure of the years. And besides he cannot leave his wife, however she may bore and torment him. He lacks the courage to be cruel. That is the diagnosis of his friend, a doctor as kind and cynical as doctors always are, at any rate in plays, at any rate in this kind of play.

The General is a buffoon who suffers, and we cannot get at the suffering except through the buffoonery. William Hutt is too old for the role and would not have been right even when younger. He is a gallant actor - in both senses: brave and courteous - and he won all hearts at the curtain call. His technique, despite some fumbling with words on a heavily jinxed first night, remains impeccable: give him an ironic aside and he will time and dispatch it with perfect finesse. But most of the time, his distinction works against him. Vulgarity and bluster are not in him; his balloon arrives on stage already punctured. There was once a movie version with Peter Sellers who probably played it as Major Denis Bloodnok. (You don't remembr Major Bloodnok? Suffer.) That, however overdone, would have been nearer the mark.

The core of the play is the General's confrontation with his spouse, who stands up in bed to denounce him: a grotesque effect that Anouilh planned with exquisite care and is severely impaired if, as here, she has to hold onto a bed-post to do it. Marion Gilsenan lends this harridan far too much glamor but is otherwise efficient. The scene, though, is chaotically directed; it has no rhythm and therefore no impact. You get more domestic horror in the average episode of Fawlty Towers.

There is nothing much to say about other performances. Allan Gray's surprisingly Caledonian doctor finds his form in some scenes of worldly philosophizing, that also find Hutt at his mellow best. Diana Leblanc as the aging female ingenue and Blair Williams as the young male one are mechanically moonstruck: on the right lines but not very far along them. A maid and a priest are grotesquely overplayed, perhaps to compensate for the lack of comic energy elsewhere.

Kenneth Mellor's set has spacious suggestions of the General's past campaigns in North Africa. The audience applauded it before it became fully visible, which is typical. As with Misalliance, two intervals wreck the flow. There is, of course, less flow here to wreck, but that is not the play's fault. Anouilh uses a superb variety of styles to get at an anguish we can all recognize. This could be one of the funniest plays of the twentieth century. Its time will come again.
