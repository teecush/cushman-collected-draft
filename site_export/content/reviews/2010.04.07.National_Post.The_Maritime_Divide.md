A BOY CALLED NEWFOUNDLAND

Tarragon Theatre Extra Space, Toronto

The play is called A Boy Called Newfoundland and this is the set-up: Bill Willow and his wife Marianne live with their son and two daughters in what the program, reinforcing the tone established by the title, identifies as "a Nintendo-blue house at the top of a Maritime hill." From these premises they edit and distribute a newspaper called the Romantic Times. Whether this is a weekly, a monthly or a twice-a-yearly is left unclear but, even factoring out the Internet and the recession, it sounds like an uncommercial proposition. Bill Willow seems to have come to this conclusion himself, as he has vanished from the scene, leaving a hole in his family's lives, and also in the front page of their upcoming edition.

The three children are all in their teens. Arley-Rose is blond, and dating a divinity student named Hadley; they eventually marry. Brigid is brunette and a frenetic cut-up, initially devoted to making her younger brother's life a misery but eventually revealed to be in love with him. And playing Cinderella to his two sisters is the boy called Newfoundland himself, so-named because his parents were vacationing there when he was conceived. He is a shy but amiable 14-year-old, constantly inventing tunes on a portable keyboard. Marianne Willow wants to bring her son out of himself, but isn't sure how. A possibly encouraging sign is that he is conducting a telephonic relationship with a Quebec girl whom he once met at a French camp. They converse by exchanging lyrics from pop songs of the non-rock persuasion. This explains why, though the time is "the present," the air is filled with sounds from the '50s and early '60s. Arley-Rose and Brigid set off in their mother's car to find their father whom they locate ice-fishing in the far north. Later on, Flounder makes the same pilgrimage, but Bill Willow remains reluctant to return to a household that likes to keep its Christmas lights on for most of the year.

Graeme Gillis, the author, is not aiming at literal plausibility. Emotional plausibility is another matter and he does get that in isolated exchanges, plus a measure of charm and a streak of humour. What he doesn't do is build and develop. We're told, repeatedly, about Arley-Rose's dalliance with Hadley, but we don't even meet the guy until late in the first act, and then it's for a scene with Newfoundland; the two young fellas start playing video games together. This apparently does wonders for both of them, but it's never explained how. When Hadley proposes (to Arley, that is) it's the best scene in the play, no contest, but it could as well be an isolated sketch. Most damagingly, the spine of the play is supposed to be the sentimental education of its title character but it's just one thread among many. We're asked by the end to believe that he's grown but we haven't seen or felt it happen. We don't know if he's re-asserting the values of the Romantic Times or, really, what those values might be.

The last thing this play needs is an extra layer of whimsy, but in Ashlie Corcoran's production for Theatre Smash, that's what it gets. The action is sliced and diced across a set (by Robin Fisher) consisting of a wide set of multi-coloured steps; a protruding bit at the top looks like the fin of an uncommonly gaudy shark. Holes open up on different levels, to show the girls recklessly driving or Bill fishing, but the general effect is to keep the characters even further from getting to grips with one another or with recognizable reality.

Martin Happer's performance as Hadley is the most solid and also the funniest. Patrick Kwok-Choon, a hitherto uneven actor (promising in The Tempest, dreadful in Rock 'n' Roll), redeems the promise in a sweet performance as Newfoundland. As the sisters, Lara Jean Chorostecki and Natasha Greenblatt start out over the top but pull back very satisfactorily as the evening progresses. Layne Coleman and Martha Burns are the elder Willows, he pleasantly stoic, she alternately flighty and concerned, and more starved of motivation than anyone else on the stage. The play points toward a resolving scene between them and then withholds it. The production can't make a virtue of this; indeed, both acts conclude with the audeience going "Huh?" Vivien Endicott Douglas plays the hero's long-distance love, whose occasional appearances in a remote alcove sum up all that's gone wrong in both script and direction.
