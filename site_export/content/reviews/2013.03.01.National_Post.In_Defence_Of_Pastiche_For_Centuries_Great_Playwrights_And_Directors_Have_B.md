The headline on last Saturday's letters page in the National Post was: "Why pay $100 to watch the pastiche of a masterpiece?" Leaving aside the matter of the price-tag - which I agree is steep - the answer has to be "Because there's no alternative."

The letter beneath the headline was a response to Terence Corcoran's article of the previous day, expressing barbed bemusement at the two latest productions of the Canadian Opera Company (COC), and suggesting that the COC was trying to attract Toronto audiences with "increasing doses of sex, purple pajamas and self-parody." The implied message of statements like that is that there is an obviously right way of staging these works and that the COC - perversely or opportunistically, or both - is avoiding it. I don't think that's true.

There are some arts, like painting and sculpture, that can stay as they are, through the ages, without mediation. Literature can do the same, as long as it remains in its original language; as soon as it's translated, it is, unavoidably, transformed according to the personal choices of the translator. And there are the performing arts - music, drama, dance, opera, the last one combining at least two of the previous three - that don't exist until they're transformed. Or, to use a less contentious term, "interpreted."

A common response to that is that the interpretation ought to conform to the author's intentions. And the definitive response to the response was uttered by the French director Louis Jouvet who, when accused of having betrayedMoliere's intentions,inquired of his accuser, "tu lui as telephone?" You can't put a call through to Shakespeare or Mozart either. We don't know what their intentions were.

Well, that's not quite true. We don't know their interpretive intentions; but we do know, something, of their technical intentions. At least, we know the conventions within which they were obliged to work, whether or not they personally approved of them. We know that Shakespeare wrote most (not all) of his plays to be performed in daylight, in the open air, with the audience standing in a courtyard and probably surrounded by pickpockets and prostitutes, and (this does apply to all the plays) with the women's roles played by boys. Yet somehow, I don't believe that those staging conditions are what the people who clamour for "straight" productions of Shakespeare have in mind. What they're yearning for are productions like the ones they themselves grew up on. It's an understandable feeling, and I'm not immune to it myself. But one shouldn't confuse nostalgia for the 1950s with fidelity to the 1590s.

The fallback position I've adopted is this. We can't know a dead author's intentions. (Or, quite often, a living one's. There's a famous story of Harold Pinter directing one of his own plays, and saying to the actors at one point, "Of course, we can't be sure what the author meant here.") Conceivably, in fact more than likely, those intentions changed during the writing of the piece anyway. What we do have is a text - the piece as it exists before it goes into rehearsal - and that text, if it's worth doing at all, has, not so much a meaning as an identity. But that identity can only be discovered in rehearsal and realized in performance: freshly every time, by each cast and each audience.

And sometimes it isn't; if what the performers are doing strikes the audience as being out of sync with the material they're delivering - and especially if the performers seem embarrassed by the discrepancy - then that production has failed. But of course whether that has happened in a particular case is a matter of taste and opinion. There aren't any absolutes.

There certainly aren't any in the matter of period sets and costumes. We know, from surviving illustrations, that Shakespeare's plays were presented in what was then modern dress; in his Roman plays, the actors wore Elizabethan clothes, maybe topped off by a cloak or toga for the leading man. Again, I don't think this is what the self-proclaimed purists are looking for. (Nor should they; done now, it would represent the worst of both worlds.) We know from the scripts that the author wasn't fussy about period detail: Witness the ticking clocks in Julius Caesar.

Shakespeare's learned colleague Ben Jonson took him to task for lapses like that, and strove to avoid them in his own Roman tragedies, which were meticulously researched, with annotations to prove it. One of them, Sejanus, is a superb portrait of what we would call a police state, complete with show-trials and informers. It could be very thought-provoking if done now in modern dress, and probably equally so if done in Roman dress, with the audience picking out the parallels for themselves.

Again, there aren't any rules. It's still true that Jonson's painstaking approach has never been, and will never be, as popular as Shakespeare's free-wheeling one.

Richard Wagner staged his own operas in his own opera house, and gave them, as far as is possible with stories featuring gods and giants and dragons and Rhinemaidens, elaborately naturalistic productions. I confess to an atavistic desire to see the Ring cycle done that way, just to find out what it would be like. But I doubt that I would get as much out of it as I did out of the COC's eclectic staging, in which the villains and schemers of Gotterdammerung came far more vividly to life as sleek-suited politicians sitting around a giant boardroom-table than they could possibly have done if they'd been stomping about in horned helmets.

The most exciting Shakespeare production I've seen in recent years was The Tempest, brought by the Royal Shakespeare Company to the University of Michigan at Ann Arbor, with Prospero's island transported to the Arctic; Miranda clad in her father's cast-offs, including a galumphing pair of boots; the shipwrecked courtiers in dinner jackets, as if they'd been interrupted while playing bridge on the Titanic; and Ariel erupting from the splintering body of a giant seal, spread upon a banqueting table. None of these effects can have been precisely envisaged by the author, but they worked brilliantly at telling his story.

The best Shakespeare production I've ever seen was the same company's Macbeth, directed by Trevor Nunn with Ian McKellen and Judi Dench in the leads, staged in a converted shed in Stratford-on-Avon, the actors in unobtrusive but unglamorous contemporary clothes, sitting on a circle of orange boxes observing the scenes in which they weren't directly involved, the hero delivering his "tomorrow and tomorrow and tomorrow" speech beneath a single, naked frantically-swaying light-bulb and taking the audience deep inside his head, where the play's real action is.

Coming nearer home, the most revelatory production that Toronto's Soul-pepper company has given us since taking up residence in the Distillery District is the Restoration comedy The Way of the World, re-set in the 1950s, and rescued from a world of periwigs and middle-aged stars reciting epigrams, to emerge as a play about two attractive and intelligent young people trying to stake out a rational way to love: a play, in fact, about sex and a city. (It was also, unusually for this play, very funny.)

Getting down to current cases, the ones discussed by Mr. Corcoran: Like him, I'm only an amateur opera critic, though as a theatre critic I've found that - with rare exceptions such as the recent Il Trovatore, with its pantomime scenery and the inordinate waits to change it - the COC offers the most consistently innovative and imaginative stagings, not to mention some of the best acting, in Toronto.

As regards the new Tristan und Isolde and La Clemenzadi Tito, I agree with some of Terry's strictures. The continuous videos in Tristan, touted as being a poetic complement to the action rather than an illustration of it, struck me as actually being rather literal; Act One is set on a ship, so we got lots of footage of waves. I, too, was relieved to find that the nude male undergoing some kind of ritual purification on screen was not supposed to be Ben Heppner who, down on stage, was presenting a rather tubby Tristan. But the videos did seem to me an interesting approach to a peculiar staging problem. Tristan is an opera in which, for most of the time, nothing happens; no purpose would be served by designing, say, a realistic shipboard setting, because there'd be nothing for the characters to do on it. By confining the action to a couple of platforms the director, Peter Sellars, ensured that the moments at which somebody moved from one to the other carried enormous weight; meanwhile the pictures on screen gave us something to look at. I, too, thought that the most tremendous performance came from Franz-Josef Selig as King Marke, but I don't think that this was despite the homoerotic slant that the production gave to his feelings for Tristan; I think it's likely that the overwhelming force of his huge sustained sung monologues was partly due to his being given something to act as well as sing. It's what's known as subtext.

Christopher Alden, who directed La Clemenza, has been much vilified for his COC productions, though his Die Fledermaus was very entertaining if over-politicised, while the smoking-room setting of his Rigoletto evoked superbly the self-satisfied male society mocking and tormenting the hunchback jester. His new production has itself been mocked for presenting the Roman Emperor Titus in purple pajamas, though history suggests that part of the point of being emperor was that you could get away with wearing whatever you liked (at least these were imperially purple pajamas) and that many holders of the office were indeed, as Terry says of this one, "demented neurotics."

Mozart's Titus is revered for, and prides himself on, his quality of mercy. And he may have been quite sincere and untroubled about it. Equally, he may have been desperate to think well of himself; it's a perfectly plausible interpretation, and one carried out here with great comic aplomb. (Mozart, who spent much of his career writing comic operas, is unlikely to have put his sense of humour on hold here.) Nor, given the production's overall scenic playfulness, did I object when Titus contacted the senate via a red telephone. Of course he wouldn't historically have done that, but then he wouldn't historically have been singing in Italian rather than talking in Latin. In context, the phone made good sense.

So did having the would-be tyrannicide Sesto paint anti-imperial slogans on the wall, like an earnest modern revolutionary. He's one of two characters in the opera originally written to be sung by male castrati. Honouring the composer's intentions in this regard really would be the unkindest cut of all, so nowadays the roles are sung by women. This being the case, gender-bending comes with the territory. People who complain about an over-emphasis on sex in these and other productions of both plays and operas seem to think that our ancestors had cleaner minds than we do. There is no evidence for this.

Sex is actually what theatre is about. What it isn't about is recovering conventions that are irrecoverable and honouring intentions that are unknowable. We have to do the best we can with what we have and who we are. "Pastiche" isn't the first word that comes to mind for that endeavour. But it will do.
