Peter Hinton's Shaw Festival production of Lady Windermere's Fan is, both visually and tonally, a masterpiece. That's not a term that many people would have used for the play itself, one of the three "society comedies" with which Oscar Wilde limbered up for the full-blown farce of The Importance of Being Earnest, and which are generally regarded as maudlin melodramas periodically redeemed by quotable quips. Hinton's redemptive solution is to drill into the real and even complex feelings that underlie the histrionics; to make the jokes into commentary on the jokers themselves as much as on those they are joking about; and to place the whole within a shifting frame whose contents, as well as being beautiful in themselves, supply an ongoing assessment of the characters and the time and space they inhabit.

Lady Windermere is a high-minded young wife and mother whose happy sheltered life in equally high fin-desiecle London society is disrupted when she's informed that her husband has been paying calls on, and giving money to, a Mrs. Erlynne, widely regarded as a woman with a past. He has his reasons, but won't say what they are. He further humiliates his wife, atleast in her own eyes, by insisting that she receive Mrs. Erlynne at her upcoming ball; Lady W threatens that if Mrs. E is so brazen as to show up, she will strike her in the face with her fan. (does she? Find out.) A despairing Lady Windermere seems about to yield to the entreaties of the moderately dashing Lord Darlington to run away with him, and even arrives in his apartments. She's followed there by Mrs. Erlynne, who begs her not to throw her life away, and takes drastic measures to stop her doing it. By the end of the play, Lady Windermere has learned not to be judgmental, Lord Windermere has learned to be more trusting, and Mrs. Erlynne has learned, not too surprisingly, that she has a heart. The world in general, of course, has learned nothing at all.

That world is here revealed to us gradually, and in sections. Before the play properly starts, a line of fine ladies parades slowly across the stage, each carrying her own emblematic fan. The opening scenes are shown to us as through a camera lens, in expanding and retracting sections; in one interpolated vignette, we see the curdled cream of society in a theatre box, peering with delicious shock at the shamelessly fallen woman who's dared to attend. The previously compartmentalized stage opens to its full width for the ball itself, and remains so for all succeeding scenes, each of them gorgeously and precisely realized in Teresea Przybylski's set and William Schmuck's costumes. The various interiors are patterned after painters of the period, with glimpses of the indispensable drawings of Aubrey Beardsley. There are also fireworks, real ones.

For all this, it's the acting that drives the evening. Marla McLean's Lady Windermere may have a mysterious accent, and Martin Happer as her husband may rush some of his emotional fences, but they both trace detailed, and in her case moving, maps of the breaking down of prejudice; the image of the penitent McLean by her baby's cradle is unforced and unforgettable. Tara Rosling's Mrs. Erlynne looks too young to make the play's most startling revelation credible, and she's more convincing as passionate saviour than as femme fatale, but the former element may well be the more important. The supporting roles are superbly done. Gray Powell finds some moody variations in the seducer Darlington, Lady Windermere's greatest fan. The duchess of Berwick, a role traditionally played as a dragon, is magnificently re-imagined by Corinne Koslo, who presents her instead as a viper, a censorious gossip with the blandest but deadliest of bites. She regrettably fades out of the play after triumphantly marrying her daughter off to a lanky but wealthy Australian, but there's compensation in her replacement by a chorus of world-weary young men, headed by Kyle Blair and Patrick McManus, who foregather in the third and best act to make a sustained self-satirizing ensemble out of what might otherwise be an anthology of free-floating quotes. (The play contains an amazing number of Wilde's most famous lines.) Hovering amiably on the edge of this group is Jim Mezon as an older a risto who ends up a touchingly amusing amalgam of victor and victim.

Wilde reigns on the Shaw Festival's main stage, while GBS himself is confined to its smaller houses. At that, there are only one-and-a-half of his plays on view this year, the half being Peace in Our Time, John Murrell's adaptation of Geneva, which is somehow both one of Shaw's least-seen works and one of his most notorious. First staged in 1938, and constantly revised to keep up with events, Geneva is both a satire on, and a wistful celebration of, the League of Nations. Its finale is the appearance before the International Court at The Hague, and under only slightly disguised names, of Hitler, Mussolini and Franco. The play could hardly be accused of sympathizing with them, but it does go imaginatively easy on them. (It also fails to put Stalin up there with them, where he belonged.) Shaw was perennially incapable of recognizing, or at least of dramatizing, real monstrosity; nearly all his characters speak with the same suave eloquence, and these are no exception. Shaw's preface, written after the war and never explicitly mentioning the play, shows that he knew about the Nazi camps, but his shock seems to have been intellectual rather than visceral. In the play an anonymous Jew (nearly all the characters are anonymous) has Hitler brought up at The Hague on charges of persecution; the scenario now, a sit was then, blandly disproportionate to what was actually going on.

For all that, the play as written is witty and provocative. even in Murrell's version, all the play's best lines (apart from a very funny one about sheep) are Shaw's own. Murrell says that one of his aims was to "bring some of the offensively jingoistic language ... up to date"; though since Shaw's play is in large part a satire on jingoism, or on the murderous idea of any nation's exceptionalism, I'm not sure what that means. In a funny way, though, Shaw's play is patriotic; this displaced Irishman gave the most silkily persuasive speeches to an English diplomat. Murrell's adaptation does the same, with Patrick Galligan polishing off both authors' contributions very nicely.

Murrell's first act, and Blair Williams' direction of it, are rather a mess. Michael Ball puts in appearance as a Bishop, shocked into a fatal heart attack by meeting a Communist; I was sorry to see him go, as at this point he was almost the only person on stage who wasn't over-acting. Some other British characters have been turned into stereotypes of other nations; one of them is now Canadian, occasioning a fusillade of audience-baiting jokes that would be unforgivable if the actor in question, Andrew Bunker, weren't so good in the role. A South London typist, who's as near as anyone to being the major character, has become middle American; Diana Donnelly plays her with grating exaggeration though she improves later on when the character turns, more or less, into Sarah Palin. Claire Jullien (South American) and Moya O'Connell (Russian) contribute other national caricatures that are most charitably described as determined. O'Connell's commissar was male in the original, but another female role has been (unwisely) cut so the gender score remains as before.

The show's second half, though, meaning the court scene, is a rip-roaring success, largely owing to the arrival of the three anti-Marx brothers: Neil Barclay, a mountain of Aduce, Ric Reid, a furious Fuhrer, and Lorne Kennedy, a strutting bantam of a Generalissimo. All three exemplify the combined wisdom of Shaw and Laurence Olivier that actually there's no such thing as over-acting if you can go high enough to fill the space - though with Barclay it's more a case of going broad enough; he's spaghetti bolognaise made flesh, and topped off with a soothingly disdainful voice. All three, indeed, show a contempt for one another, only exceeded by their contempt for everybody else. Murrell has preserved their best Shavian material, and creatively supplemented it. The democratic opposition is strongly voiced by Jeff Meadows as the despairing League secretary, and by Sanjay Talwar as a more optimistic judge, though if he as he claims is a Dutchman, then I'm one. The ending Murrell has given him is, by today's lights, less facile than Shaw's; and here at least our blushes are spared.

Reverting to "mere" melodrama: it can, if honestly done, be both suspenseful and moving, as is proved by Benedict Campbell in A Wife for a Life, a very early Eugene O'Neill play let, in which he plays a prospector torn between revenge and forgiveness. Much of the writing is jaw-droppingly blatant, but that was to remain a problem with O'Neill, right the way through to Long Day's Journey into Night. ("you've told me your memories. Wanna hear mine?"). This revival is part of a lunchtime double-bill called Trifles, named after a roughly contemporary piece by Susan Glaspell: a play whose greater fame is probably justified but which is here the less powerful. One play takes place in "a lonely kitchen", the other in "a lonely cabin"; Meg roe's production, designed by Camellia Koo, emphasizes the similarities.

Lady Windermere's Fan is in repertory at the Festival Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake, through Oct. 19; Peace in Our Time and Trifles at the Court House Theatre through Oct. 12
