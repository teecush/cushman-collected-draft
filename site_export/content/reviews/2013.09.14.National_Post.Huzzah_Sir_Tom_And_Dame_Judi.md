Karl Marx famously said that history repeats itself, the first time as tragedy, the second time as farce. In fact he said it so famously that, if he were able to collect royalties for every time it was quoted, he would be a very rich anticapitalist indeed. As well, of course, as a very longlived one. Tom Stoppard's Arcadia,, which has just closed at the Shaw Festival but had better be revived, teaches a different lesson. Juxtaposing events in the same stately home 200 years apart, it says that history happens twice, the first time as farce, the second time as farce.

Or maybe Karl was right, because there is a tragic element hidden away in the older of the two storylines: Thomasina Coverly, the 16yearold mathematical prodigy whom the audience has come to cherish, is destined to die in a fire immediately after the play's last scene, her first love unconsummated, her genius unknown. The only reason we know about it is that it's one of the facts discovered by the presentday section of the cast, in the course of their (generally misguided) efforts to investigate the house's past. They only mention it in passing and it's quite easy to miss, as is their reference to the fate of Thomasina's beloved tutor Septimus who despaired and became a hermit. So, come to think of it, is the key to the play's title, taken from an iconic memento mori: "Et in Arcadia, ego," says Death; "Even in Arcadia, I am here."

Stoppard does like to make his emotional points in a sidelong, almost deflationary way; it's something that links him, surprisingly perhaps, to Chekhov. Think of A. E. Housman in The Invention of Love lamenting of a passion that dared not speak its name: "I would have died for you, but I never had the luck." Or of Rosencrantz exclaiming to Guildenstern after discovering that they're escorting Hamlet to his death: "But we're his friends" Or of Henry Carr in Travesties failing to stop the Russian Revolution in its tracks because he's too busy with an amateur production of The Importance of Being Earnest. Or of the fact that the most wrenching moment in any of the plays is a man stepping on a tortoise in Jumpers: tragedy and farce happening simultaneously.

The tragic undertones of Arcadia are especially hard to bring to the surface, there being so much else going on, and Eda Holmes' Shaw production didn't manage it; it was one of

the evening's few weaknesses. But perhaps, as Septimus tells Thomasina preconflagration, it doesn't matter: everything that's lost will be rediscovered. It would be nice to think that might be true of the production itself: that it should have a future. Even in Niagara, where it sold out before it opened, it was seen by very few people; given a short run in the Studio while Faith Healer, a play with far less obvious appeal (i.e. fewer jokes), was holding the larger stage of the Royal George. Though, to be fair, Holmes was able to use the Studio space in ways that wouldn't have been possible in more formal surroundings; the audience entered and exited through the playingspace, as if we too were part of the history of the house and of its allimportant garden.

Still, even if that effect had to be sacrificed, this production should be given a longer and wider life. There are precedents. Last year's Studio production of Topdog/Underdog was successfully transferred to Toronto (its being a coproduction with Obsidian may have made the process easier); the Tarragon has played host to the Stratford studio production of The Little Years and is about to do the same for The Best Brothers. The logistics of transferring a major mainstage production might well be insuperable, but it would be wonderful to have Arcadia and another recent Shaw studio triumph, When the Rain Stops Falling, playing in repertory at, perhaps, Berkeley Street or the Young Centre. And after they've played the centre of the universe, they could be shown off to the rest of Canada.

Let us now praise a famous actress; maybe I could be biased here the best one in the world. I'm always bemused when I hear TV comedians referring in mocksepulchral tones to "Dame Judi Dench" as if she were Dame Edith Evans intoning "A handbag." Actually, when Dench herself played Lady Bracknell, which she did with uncompromising rigour but also with a novel hint of flirtatiousness, she downplayed that line, as nearly all postEvans Bracknells must, in sheer selfdefence, have felt obliged to do. It didn't matter; she got every other possible laugh.

Anyway, she may be a Dame but it's hard to think of an actress, or a woman, less grande.

I'm lucky; I've been able to follow her career from its very beginning, which was in 1958 when, straight out of drama school, she played Ophelia at the Old Vic to John Neville's Hamlet. The critical consensus, which had my adolescent endorsement, was that she was ineffectual in the early scenes but very good, very real, when she went mad. And real, it turned out, was what she was about. She confirmed it later that season when she played a lively and funny Maria, the waitingwoman in Twelfth Night. This was an in genue with the soul of a character actress.

Ingenue was what she did for the next few seasons at the Vic, culminating in her Juliet for a generation, in a groundbreakingproduction by Franco Zeffirelli. (If you've only seen the Zeffirelli movie, without Judi, you haven't lived.) Jump forward 20 years to her playing the tough, loving Juno in Trevor Nunn's perfect production of Juno and the Paycock, brisk and warm and unsentimental, which is a good summation off her, onstage and off. Everyone's sweetheart had turned into everyone's mother. She was also the only Englishborn performer among a cast of Irish expatriates, and she fit right in. Now, after three further decades, she's again impeccably Irish in the movie Philomena (shown at TIFF), as a workingclass Catholic mother searching for the illegitimate son whom the nuns had torn from her and given up for adoption 50 years before. It's an overcute film with roadmovie ambitions, hobbled by the determination of writer and costar Steve Coogan to make it as much about him as about her. She has to play as much for mischief as pain, but you believe every moment of both. An added pleasure for me, and I imagine for her, is the casting, as the most monstrous of the sisters, of Barbara Jefford who, though only a few years older, was leading lady at the Old Vic when she was a beginner. Within a few years they were playing together, marvellously, as Gwendolen and Cecily in The Importance.

Philomena is one of two doubleacts I've seen her do this year. The other, live in London and opposite Ben Whishaw, was in Peter and Alice, about the embittered later lives of the children who were the models for Alice in Wonderland and Peter Pan; a pointless piece except for giving her the opportunity to play aged, hardbitten asperity while radiating undiminished, unforced star quality, onstage and off. An actor who worked with her at the English Stratford once said that even just sitting in the green room she knocked him out, and I know what he meant.

Repression exploded has always been one of the things she's done best. It can take many forms. In Harold Pinter's A Kind of Alaska, she played a woman awakened from years of sleeping sickness. Watching her take her first steps across the room, tentative but determined, as if her muscles were remembering what to do, was like watching human motion reduced to its essence. It was one of the purest pieces of acting I've ever seen.

And if you're wondering what any of this has to do with Arcadia: Well, if Tom Stoppard had got around to writing it earlier say in 1962 Judi would have been the perfect Thomasina. That was the year she played another young woman, from roughly the same period, also experiencing a doomed first love but surviving it better, in John Whiting's A Penny for a Song.

It was one of the plays in which the young Judi Dench showed how enchantingly good she was at falling in love, and what fun it could be to watch her and to share the feeling. It hasn't gone away.
