Passions can flare over many things. Toward the end of the first act of Pentecost, the stage erupts in a dispute over a religious painting. It's a fresco, on the wall of a church in an unnamed Central European country, that might be either an imitation Giotto or something that Giotto imitated. If the latter is true, then perspective, the Renaissance and the modern era of painting all got started long before, and far away from, their official time and place of origin. Three art historians, all with a personal as well as professional stake in the outcome, are arguing furiously. Just when it seems the tension can't go any higher, it does. The church door is flung open and a Jeep rolls on stage, peopled by displaced representatives of every country and culture east of what used to be the Iron Curtain. They have guns. They take the experts hostage and, with them, and perhaps more valuably, the painting.

Pentecost is many things, and one of them is that rare commodity - - a viscerally exciting play in which you can't wait to discover what happens next. Its author, David Edgar, is one of Britain's most accomplished public playwrights, writing large plays on large themes; since the 1970s he has been anatomizing his own country, present and past. (He wrote the legendary adaptation of Nicholas Nickleby whose revival we will shortly be seeing in Toronto.) More recently he has gone global. Pentecost, which was first produced in 1994, seems if anything to have gained in topicality. It may originally have been a response to events in Yugoslavia. It could just as easily have been sparked by Iraq and Afghanistan.

Of the three original disputants, only one, Gabriella, is a local; she's a museum curator for whom the early dating of the painting is a matter of both professional and national pride. Oliver, a visiting English critic, is easily won over to her side, and he gets the backing to have the picture removed and cleaned. The third party is Leo, a U.S. academic, specifically hired to prove them wrong. That's before his life and theirs get torn apart. His paymasters are the local Catholic and Orthodox hierarchies, opponents in most things, including which of them is the rightful inheritor of the building, but united in their determination to keep the fresco where it is. This church, like the country it stands in, has had many identities: It was a mosque under the Ottomans, a prison under the Nazis and a shrine of atheism under the Communists. Today, the country, as Gabriella says, is making a mess of the free market.

The insurgents have got in, from a camp on the border, with the intention of getting out. Survivors of atrocities from across the continent, they demand asylum in the west. Threats are made, offers are offered, suspense ratchets, and

--as expected--captives and captors bond. They all speak different languages (including a made-up one for what the play calls "our country") but, mercifully for the audience, most of them have English as a default tongue. Edgar is very scrupulous as to when and how this gets used.

Language here is subject as well as medium: from the inscription on the Statue of Liberty, both a standing promise and a standing rebuke, to the local peculiarities of a tongue that has the same word for "to" and "from." ("This would explain the taxis" says Oliver.) Pentecost is the festival of speaking in tongues. And with Stoppardian neatness, if not quite Stoppardian wit, at least two of the play's climaxes turn on linguistic misunderstandings and resolutions. It connects with an ongoing debate about art: as value in itself, as religious or national symbol, as bargaining chip. Themes and story are thrillingly advanced when the refugees erupt in a multi-cultural songfest that's both convivial and competitive and that has a bitter explosive finish. There's even a love plot. It feels perfunctory (Edgar is at his weakest when conversation is divorced from ideas), but it also feels necessary. And it is beautifully set up: "This is Illyria, lady" says Oliver to Gabriella, at once Balkanizing the story and universalizing it.

And, of course, Stratfordizing it. Indeed, the play began its life, like that other current studio triumph The Odyssey, in the equivalent space at the English Stratford; they premiered there within two years of one another. (Here, it's been within two days.) The refugees' Jeep dominates this production, just as a wrecked car does The Odyssey. There must be more garage space backstage at the Stratford Studio than anyone suspected. A Bulgarian director, Mladen Kiselov, has been appropriately recruited, and he gets unflinching commitment from his cast. Lucy Peacock caps a terrific season with a Gabriella who's both flinty and flirtatious, wringing amazingly various shades from such English-as-a-second-language expressions as "okeydoke" and "tallyho." John Koensgen is a wry and gentle, if excessively diffident, Oliver; Jonathan Goad may be too fresh- favoured for the abrasive Leo, but both his tirades and his one- liners are magnetic. Adrienne Gould gives a firebrand account of the refugees' leader, a Palestinian who's a multiple exile on her own account.

The staging goes against the published script in denying us a proper look at the painting, which is a loss since its subject -- Mary mourning the dead Jesus -- is as important as its provenance. Someone in the play describes Marxism as a good idea that hasn't been tried; the same of course has been said of Christianity. The play wants, I think, to harvest the most humane elements of both while lamenting the improbability. It calls to mind George Orwell's description of Dickens: "generously angry." It's a genuinely inspiring evening. - Pentecost is in repertoire until Sept. 21. Call 1-800-567-1600 for ticket information.
