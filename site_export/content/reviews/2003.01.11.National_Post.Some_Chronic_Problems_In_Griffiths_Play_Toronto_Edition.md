CHRONIC

Factory Theatre

- - -

Petra, the protagonist of Linda Griffiths' new play, suffers from what is probably Chronic Fatigue Syndrome, a condition that is difficult to treat and whose causes are unknown. The play takes the view that its viral, and even brings the virus onstage as a character. Virus is, of course, a word thats rife in our society - - virulently so, indeed -- and the author has no trouble spreading it around. The mysteriousness of the disease gives Chronic some of its initial interest. Its handiness as a metaphor, applicable to just about any situation, proves its undoing.

Petra works, necessarily part-time, in a small, ambitious software firm. Unless I was missing something -- and the dialogue gets pretty dense around these techies -- the phrase "computer virus" is never used in the sense with which most of us are familiar; rather, we are invited to believe that the information economy itself is working like a virus in our society; this company, at any rate, seems a lot more informative than economic.

Petra's co-workers are also her friends; one of them, Chris, is her boyfriend. When we first meet them, they have been together for six months, something of a record, it seems, for both of them. At some point in the second act, it transpires that they've lasted five years, though there is very little sense of time having passed.

Petras condition seems to be part of her attraction for Chris, who is very involved with her treatment and finds it a challenge. So, after an initial period of nervousness, does her younger colleague, Amber. A lesbian relationship seems about to blossom, in a scene where Amber sponges Petra down, though its diverted when Amber learns their boss Oscar, also young and geekishly driven, has fancied her for ages.

It's Petra who passes on this information, which dramatically comes right out of the blue. Nothing we have learned about either participant makes it sound either plausible or the reverse.

Meanwhile, Chris has an affair with Diane, Petra's doctor, a try- anything practitioner operating on the borderline between orthodox and alternative medicine; her specialty is hypnosis and she has a perpetually worried look. The person shes really attracted to is, of course, Petra. Chris, on first meeting Diane, contributes to the universal-virus theory of society by belligerently asserting that the Pentagon deliberately manufactured AIDS. This could, though, be meant as comic relief.

The play, in its earlier stages, is not as bad as all that makes it sound. It somewhat resembles Impromptu on Nuns Island, the new play from Michel Tremblay, in that its a debate with a storyline tagged on. Tremblay's story is the easier to follow, but at least with Griffiths you're spared the feeling that the author came to the party with all her ideas worked out years in advance. She does seem to be exploring, and theres a texture to her depiction of her heroine thats worthy of respect.

But the development is stagnant; at times Petra seems better, at times she seems worse, but the impenetrability of her condition makes it hard to know how or why to care. She conducts recurrent debates, which seem longer than they probably are, with the embodied Virus. He, like most of the cast, wants to possess her and obviously has a better chance than any of them; his invasion and her resistance are persistently restated, though admittedly it's hard to imagine what else they could have to say with one another.

The Virus is a peculiarly unhappy invention (dramatically, I mean, rather than medically, though that, of course, as well) who keeps coming on like Mephistopheles but going out like a pantomime Demon King without the charm. Eric Peterson, who has appeared these last few seasons in a succession of unusually irritating roles, cavorts mercilessly in this one, mincing any meaning that his lines might have.

Otherwise, Simon Heath directs a pleasantly cool production to which Caroline Gillis as Petra supplies a firm, unsentimental centre. I'm not sure what qualifications the character has to be considered a moral touchstone but the play implicitly treats her as one (even while denying that illness in itself makes anybody special) and the actress has the gravitas for it. Her authority may seem the greater because none of the other characters comes to life; though the actors seem to be doing their best, and the lines of communication between them are admirably unfussed.

The plays sense of humour is less than intermittent, but J. D. Nicholsen displays the occasional sharp glint of it as Chris; Graeme Somerville and Holly Lewis, as senior and junior nerd, are decent, if amorphous. As doctor Diane, Brooke Johnson doesnt get much beyond wearing a white coat and projecting shame-faced concern.

Andrea Lundy's lighting rings ingenious colour-changes on David Boechler's set, which is a vast quilted bed. All the world, it seems, is a sickroom, and that is the dispiritingly familiar conclusion to which the play conveys us. It's like having the sanity- is-madness rhetoric of R. D. Laing redirected from the mind to the body.

Petra, who has spent the play as a patient, now ministers to all the others, who have found blissed-out release from a stressed-out world. Some of them are even mimicking the most striking symptoms -- one that she herself describes as a kind of oral dyslexia, and which in her case takes the form of an inability to pronounce the word "home." Maybe she gets there in the end; I couldn't tell.

The play, though it fights against its fate, ends up as a rarefied disaster-of-the-week piece. Any deeper meaning dissolves on the tongue.
