THEATRE REVIEW STILL ALIVE

Monday, December 05, 1994

ROBERT CUSHMAN

WRITTEN AND PERFORMED BY ANDY JONES Directed by Jackie Maxwell At the Factory Theatre until Dec. 31 Rating: One-man show is built around an insight found on a crowded bus Mid-life crisis clocked for laughs BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail A FEW seconds into Andy Joness solo show, his glasses fall off. He never replaces them, which may be a statement in itself. We are easily thrown off course, and once this has happened, struggle as we may, we may never get back on again.

Sort of tubby and with a hairline situated somewhere in the middle distance, the despectacled Jones looks a little less like Jason Alexander in Seinfeld, but not much. He certainly has the same demeanour, that of a man who earned his right to a mid-life crisis long before reaching middle age but is really going to collect on it now. Perkily despairing, he tells us "I'm 46 years old and I know nothing."

Jones, once of Codco, makes a fair number of Newfie jokes, mixing local pride with mild hostility. His religious education seems to rile him more. As one of his three rules of life he offers the Catholic Church should completely close down. He goes into some detail about his reasons for this, but not much.

What he is after now is insight. He had one once, and he wants to recapture it, and if possible share it with us. He was on a crowded bus and it came to him: "We're all in the same boat." You might not want to give him a responsible job in transport, but you can sympathize. In fact, I reckon that, given reported world levels of existential angst, he could count on 100 per cent audience agreement. However, Jones is anxious about his own anxiety, and he hands out a questionnaire, with a space for patrons to describe their sexual fantasies. (Its strictly confidential. Well, he does read some of them out at subsequent performances, but you dont give your name.) Meanwhile, an erotic toy is passed around the house. We behave, naturally, like embarrassed good sports.

Jones also refers to philosophers (Martin Heidegger and his brother Jerry) and to his own goal of the pure or Ontological Truth. Trying to pass this off as Irish ("the O'Truth") struck me as one of his more desperate expedients, but then he also says that "the critic is an impediment to the truth" (and that the better you know said critic personally, the more likely you are to realize this) and he is probably right. When really at wits' end, he does a routine that he calls dancing his life, expressive of frustration.

The show has a shape but Jones, like most stand-up comics, spends the evening interrupting himself. He is an expert at this, but not quite a virtuoso. I wanted most of his riffs to be more sustained, more imaginative, funnier than they were. His most inspired idea is to have a stage manager come on stage to clock our laughs. Watching her keeping meticulous score, we cant stop ourselves laughing and pushing the count still higher. Jones also has great extrovert-victim charm and he gets a standing ovation. I dont join in but I don't begrudge it.
