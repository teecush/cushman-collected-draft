DREAM MACHINE

Theatre Passe Muraille, Toronto

The long-standing, or long-jumping, Calgary troupe that calls itself One Yellow Rabbit has arrived at the Six Stages festival with a largely hypnotic and wholly inventive tribute to the Beatnik prophets of the '50s. Dream Machine takes its name from a device invented by Brion Gysin, one of the lesser-known Beats, by which I mean that I'd never heard of him before. The show itself focuses on Allen Ginsberg and William Burroughs, of whom I had heard. No mention of Kerouac, but he's already had a show in Toronto, though it was nothing like as good as this one.

Gysin's dream machine, which if this production is to be believed looked like a silver decanter with holes in its side, was intended, says the program, "to transport the user into a waking dream state" and to produce "a drugless high." That could have saved a lot of people of lot of money, not to mention a sizeable amount of jail time. Its inventors hoped -- rather touchingly, I think -- that it would become "a common household appliance" but apparently "it never caught on." Anyway, the show aims to present a theatrical equivalent of that high, and -- though the spell wears off toward the end -- it succeeds.

It doesn't dramatize the lives of the Beats and, most of the time, it doesn't quote them. It starts, though, with one of Ginsberg's own poems: America, a magnetic compound of wit and paranoia delivered, with hair-trigger precision and four-wheel drive, by Michael Green.

Having made this initial splash, this actor takes a back seat to the other four in the cast, leading me to suspect that he is the only one among them who is not a singer. For the rest of the show is a musical, and virtually through-sung. In another part of the program Blake Brooker, writer and director, asks -- a bit rhetorically, at this stage in the game -- "could we create a musical without characters or plot?" Well, he has, or they have, and not all that surprisingly, since it's been done before, often. It used to be called revue, and it's a form with a respectable non- commercial history, as well as a lucrative commercial one. I'll bet there were Beat revues in the '50s, as there were certainly surrealist revues in the '20s and '30s.

Brooker's lyrics are a fine modern gloss on the Beats' own attitudes, very angular and stylish, and illuminating that broad strip of human life bounded by imperfect orgies and writer's block. "We are the Beats, clever and effete" chant the five performers in unison, bursting with their own dignity and alive to their own absurdity. There is, after all, a limit to how far you can go in passing self-gratification off as a form of benevolence, and the Beats do seem to have been intermittently aware of this. (The show stops singing for an exchange of letters in which Burroughs says that heterosexual intercourse is all very well but it's still like eating tortillas when you'd prefer steak, and Ginsberg replies that, actually, he's become a vegetarian. I hope that's authentic but if it isn't, my compliments to the author.)

Some of the self-mockery may be put down to this production's hindsight, but I don't think it all can. The music, mainly by David Rhymer, is very good: pungent and jazzy, as befits the time, and surprisingly melodic as well.

It's especially well sung by a rangy (both physically and vocally) and seductively confident enchantress named Onalea Gilbertson. Andy Curtis has the fair disdain of the Bohemian poet, of any ear, down pat. Brad Payne does some surprising backflips, while the remaining performer, Denise Clarke, is also the choreographer. Her inventions -- some languorous, some positively militaristic -- are very commanding, and faultlessly executed by all.

There are three musicians, half out of sight: Peter Moller, whose drums give the show a lot of its pulse, also doubles on percussive typewriter, and trebles on unearthly yells. The visuals (set by Chris Cran, lights by Cimmeron Meyer, costumes uncredited) are superb. I thought Little Mercy's First Murder was a stylishly mounted show (and not dissimilar in adopting an earlier decade's louche rebellion for its own purposes) but this one has it, well, beat.

It goes limp in its last stages, partly because its heroes come to seem seedier than the show itself has bargained for, partly because the writing becomes flaccid: The mystical self-pity outweighs the wit. Some of the later tunes sound like Kurt Weill, always a danger sign since he's so easily pastichable. Others are what you might call contemporary or what I might call bad. Most of the time, though, the show is superbly accomplished. And it makes you feel good, like a dream machine should.
