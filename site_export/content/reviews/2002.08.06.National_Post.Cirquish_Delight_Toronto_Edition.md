VAREKAI

Ontario Place, Toronto

People are saying that Cirque du Soleil's new show is uneven. They're right. Varekai, the Cirque's 14th spectacle, fluctuates between the merely amazing, the frankly unbelievable and the outright sublime.

Sublimity arrives early, in the production's take on the myth of Icarus (Anton Chelnokov), who here falls to Earth in the middle of a forest inhabited by a motley medley of beaked, winged and hooded creatures. What better protagonist for the Circus of the Sun than Icarus, who flew too high and had his wings fatally singed? This one, luckier than his classical prototype, survives, but is captured and trussed up. He is then hoisted aloft, and performs a breathtaking acrobatic dance, winding himself in and out of the net that constrains him. The moment at which he first seems to be throwing it from himself is ravishing. As visual poetry it beats anything in The Lion King.

It is matched, much later on, in a number billed as Aerial Straps. A black-clad figure, hanging on wires from his wrists, breaks to one side and is revealed to be two; he has a double, poised exactly behind him. The image of a person multiplying before our eyes is extraordinary. Hardly less remarkable are the synchronized pyrotechnics that the pair, all leathers and feathers, proceed to execute. (They are from England, of all places, and bear the impeccably public-school names of Andrew and Kevin Atherton.)

The Flight of Icarus is lyrical, Aerial Straps sensuous; the closing routine, Russian Swings, is a rumbustious fireworks display. A whole troupe of Russians (plus one Ukrainian and a Moldavian) perform on a matching pair of swings, one at each side of the stage. So far, so unsurprising, until the ever-widening arcs described by these liberated Slavs begin to reach 180 degrees. They then start to use the swings as trampolines, bouncing from one to another or landing in the folds of the huge circus tent. Again, you would expect nothing less from such accomplished swingers, but the heights to which they jump, and the complexity of the manoeuvres they execute while airborne (I still don't believe the scissor-kicks) and the sheer numbers involved become intoxicating. Don't try this in the playground.

When Cirque du Soleil, as one of the foundations of its identity, forswore animal-acts, it made an aesthetic gesture as well as a humanitarian one. Plenty of daredevilry remains, but watching the Cirque's routines you find yourself thinking not, as at any other circus, how dangerous they are but how beautiful. Some other items are almost on a par with those already described, notably a Georgian dance that shows what traditional Cossack steps would be like if executed by a small group of whirling dervishes. Even the more mundane acts are prodigiously charming, the trio of rope-twirling Chinese children billed as "Water Meteors" being charming prodigies as well. A juggler (the Mexican Octavio Alegria) does wonderful tricks with hats and sprays billiard-balls out of his mouth.

There are also clowns: a necessary evil in most circuses but a bonus here, a necessary change of pace and texture. A buxom libidinous soubrette (Mooky Cornish) makes audience participation a pleasure. Her male counterpart (Claudio Carneiro) is both her tormentor and her victim; he reaches his particular hell in an attempt to sing Ne Me Quitte Pas while chasing a spotlight that insists on doing just that. This too is an old gag, but it's made funny by having the torture carried to relentless lengths: at last, the Samuel Beckett musical, a man reduced to a robot. This couple also figure as a Papageno-Papagena Magic Flute subplot, against the more heroic love-interest of the principal figures.

I suppose I should have mentioned this earlier, but the show itself is late in getting round to it. Icarus encounters a woman (Olga Pikhienko) who does a graceful contortionist act on canes. He rescues her and they get married. It's hard to take much personal interest in them -- after all, we hardly know them -- and the two governing figures of the forest who seem to control their fate are similarly cryptic.

Also somewhat Mozartean, they are, according to the program, the Guide (Rodrigue Preteau) and the Skywalker (John Gilbey), benevolent and malevolent respectively; the latter, despite his title, wears a miner's helmet and resembles an outsized glow-worm.

The show may not add up as narrative, but nobody will be going to it for the story anyway. It is astoundingly colourful, thanks largely to the costume designs of Eiko Ishioka, and wonderfully lit by Nol van Genuchten. The set, designed by Stephane Roy, features a steep and perilous staircase that threatens to ascend into infinity but in fact culminates in an especially daunting catwalk; surprisingly little is done with it but along with a wilderness of poles, it makes an imposing background for an expanse of beautiful space. The score, a world music stew concocted by Violaine Conradi, pounds not only excitingly but helpfully.

Varekai, we are told, is the gypsy word for "wherever," and the impenetrable tongue into which the performers occasionally break may conceivably be Romany as well; I prefer to think of it as Cirquish. Only two of the cast of 50 -- the Guide and the lady-clown -- are Canadian, but the show is still recognizably ours in spirit, or at least recognizably Quebec's; directed by Dominic Champgane, from Montreal, it belongs to the same luxuriant streetwise culture as Robert Lepage. Cirque du Soleil is now one of Canada's greatest exports (there are currently eight different shows running across the world), and in its welding of international talent into a unified whole, it represents us at our best. Traditional circuses are multi-ethnic too, but in them the various acts go their individual ways; here there is unity, if not altogether of theme, then of energy and goodwill. At the end the entire company join in the swing celebrations, and joy, like the hero and heroine, is unconfined. Anyone who does not break at least a dozen times into spontaneous applause while watching this show, and who fails to stand and cheer at the end, is not to be trusted.
