Icould almost feel grateful to Hardsell. Theatre criticism can grind you down into a state of numbed acceptance in which you begin to wonder whether you could truly love or hate a show ever again. In the past two weeks I have been twice rescued. The Last Days of Judas Iscariot gave me something to love. And now theres Hardsell, a show to hate.

That isn't because it lacks competence. It comes, after all, from the highly rated Necessary Angel Company, here spreading fresh wings. Daniel Brooks, the company's new artistic director, created the show, in collaboration with its sole performer, Rick Miller. Brookss direction is probably perfect, certainly slick; Miller emerges as a faultless mimic (which we already knew), a decent ventriloquist (which we didnt) and an accomplished clown. A clown is what he's playing here, name of Arnie and bearing a strong resemblance to Krusty from The Simpsons. Not a vocal resemblance -- this isn't a reprise of Millers MacHomer--nor much of a physical one, though he does appear in war paint. But hes like Krusty in his avarice, cynicism and venality. He's not as commercially successful, but he's certainly commercial. Which makes for bitterness. Which is what the shows about.

Arnie is, he tells us, Rick Millers brother. And Rick Miller is an idealist, but a compromised one. He contributes to causes, does charity gigs, has a wife and (Arnie repeatedly tells us, with a kind of barbed relish) two lovely daughters. But he also hosts Just for Laughs, a deeply commercial operation; drinks Coca-Cola, and we all know about them; and is generally in the merchandising business. Why, the very show we're watching is sponsored by CIBC. Everyone is selling, everyone's for sale.

Well, yes. I agree. Whatcha gonna do? Not much, seems to be the answer. This is another blast of showbiz self-hatred; the kind of thing that, on a larger scale, kept the late Bob Fosse going, all the way from the movie of Cabaret to Big Deal. You play tricks, tell the audience how much you despise yourself for playing them, then play some more. In a more sober and searching manner, Wallace Shawn has also explored liberal guilt in duologue (My Dinner with Andre) and monologue (The Fever). Shawn took his own rap. Miller, though, is triple-bluffing. Instead of offering a straightforward mea culpa, he shifts his angst on to an imaginary evil sibling. And if we retort that Arnie, after all, is only Miller in disguise, then he can still score points for honesty, but without personally facing any music. Its infinite regress. And its not as if the theme is all that interesting, certainly not when its supposed avatar keeps excusing himself. The show has little to say, nowhere to go; all Miller-as-Arnie can do is vent till ready. He's technically adept at many things (his instant James Brown impersonation, twice repeated, is nice), but he's unrelentingly unfunny. That could very well be the idea, but it isnt a rewarding one. Visually, the show is fairly striking. Arnies face is white, his hair a shock of red, and he inhabits a whitish universe (designed by Ben Chaisson and Beth Kates) that's heavy on the video. The press release says that Hardsell is theatrical irony at its nastiest." They got that right.

The Madness of the Square is not a show you can hate. It's too inept for that. Marjorie Chans play (Cahoots Theatre Projects, in cahoots with Factory Theatre) tries to tell the story of Tiananmen through the experiences of four involved students with, at least to start, varying degrees of commitment. It proceeds on the dubious assumption that Chinese students in 1989 would have thought, talked and behaved exactly like Canadian students now, or at least like Canadian students with the concerns and the vocabulary of a campus soap. Theres Fan-Ying, initially aimless both scholastically and politically, whose ability to speak English leads to her becoming a talking head for foreign media, which in turn brings her dangerous notoriety. There's Shen, who's there because he fancies Fan-Ying. Theres Ding-Ding, the frivolous rich boy who foots the bill at the noodle-shop and thinks hes a wow with the ladies. And there's Sun-Yi, who's the dedicated one and who declares late on that the freedom she believes in is sexual freedom; though she seems to have a narrow view of this since she, a lesbian, strongly objects to two of her heterosexual friends getting married. There are also a more seasoned activist, who can be quite compelling, and a BBC reporter whos an embarrassment. Of course, even revolutionaries have private lives, but the play doesnt seem able to get beyond them. Its principal method of trying is periodically to assemble its actors in chorus formation and have them shout Brechtian chapter headings ("What happened to Sun-Yi ...") as if this would somehow lend epic weight to a forlornly sentimental script. (To be fair, Brecht often played this game, especially when he, too, set plays in China.) These interludes apart, Ruth Madoc-Jones stages the play well, but she cannot disguise the banality of the text or of most of the performances; Karl Ang and JohnNg are the only actors who don't come off helpless. There's plenty of video here, too, most effectively at the end when images of the square after the tanks went in have us retrospectively putting faces to the carnage. For that much, I guess, you have to give the play credit. But it remains a hopelessly naive attempt at a tragic subject, like children's theatre on a bad day. - Hardsell plays through May 9 at the Berkeley Street Theatre.
