SHE LOVES ME

Royal George Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont.

The setting of She Loves Me is a parfumerie -- a cosmetics shop - - in 1930s Budapest. One of the items on sale there is a musical candy box, touted by one of the salesclerks as being "attractive to the ear, attractive to the eye and functional." That is also, as the authors must have known, a perfect description of the show itself, one of the best American musicals of the 1960s and surely the most charming.

A mild success on its first outing, the show -- whose ultimate source is a Hungarian comedy of 1936 -- has since established itself as a small classic. The operative word is small; coming from Broadway's brazen age (it opened the same season as Hello Dolly, which has similar Central European roots, but grew away from them), it's a singularly un-brassy piece. But it isn't whimsical, which is the usual American alternative. It's sweet but real and it requires acting and singing to match.

This makes it ideal for the Shaw Festival, which can cast pretty much from strength and can put the show into an intimate house without amplification. It fits perfectly not just into this theatre, but into this town.

Niagara-on-the-Lake is one of the few places where can you can still find pharmacies that resemble, in neatness and elegance, the one designed here by Patrick Clark. Though not even in Niagara should you expect this kind of service; the help down the street are unlikely to line up as they do in the show, carolling to each departing customer their gratitude for purchases past and their anticipation of those to come.

The great attraction of She Loves Me is that it isn't just a love story. It's the story of a store, and we follow the fortunes of Maraczek's Parfumerie through the changing seasons, culminating in a choreographed Christmas rush that's as wittily written as staged. We get the feeling of people working together, liking or tolerating one another, rubbing up against each other or rubbing each other the wrong way. This last is the case with Georg, the senior salesman, and Amalia, the newest recruit; they dislike one another almost on sight.

Since they are both fairly young, fairly presentable and single, it's a foregone conclusion that they'll end up together, and they have, in fact, been unknowingly corresponding: sending one another lonely-hearts letters that are always headed "Dear Friend" and that seem based on their shared taste for classic 19th-century novels. (Make them technology-literate rather than literature-literate, and it's a very contemporary situation.)

Georg realizes the truth at half-time; Amalia suspects it shortly after, but it takes the rest of the evening for them to act on it. Everything here is taut but nothing is rushed. It's very satisfying.

"Will wonders never cease?" asks Amalia, in the course of a show- stopping song of dawning recognition titled Ice Cream. With no break for dialogue, Georg bounds on stage to put precisely the same question by way of lead-in to the title song.

This has one of Sheldon Harnick's most agreeable lyrics: "She loves me" it proclaims, conscientiously adding "True, she doesn't show it/How could she/When she doesn't know it?"

Georg is Ben Carlson, who established himself last year in All My Sons and You Can't Take It With You as that rarest of commodities, a juvenile lead with backbone. Now it turns out he can sing as well. As a nervously ordinary hero, stolid without and impetuous within, he's a joy.

So, too, is Glynis Ranney, who conveys both Amalia's pertness and her loneliness, while managing some if not all of the high notes that made Barbara Cook a Broadway legend. Carlson and Ranney are recognizably from different schools -- he's drama, she's musical comedy with her charm a little knowingly sprinkled on -- but they meet very happily in the middle.

There's a secondary couple. Cameron MacDuffee hasn't the bravura for Kodaly, a Lothario among clerks, but as Ilona, whom he loves and leaves, Patty Jamieson is irresistible. She's also comparatively subtle, playing this soubrette role not (according to custom) as a cheerful nymphomaniac with occasional misgivings, but as a fairly experienced woman emerging from a hurtful relationship and embarrassingly liable to be drawn back into it. Watch her grapple hilariously with her feelings as the two of them dance a bolero.

As an assistant who will do anything for a quiet life, Jay Turvey is fine. So too are Richard Farrell as Maraczek lui-meme and, at the other end of the age scale, Mark McGrinder as an ambitious delivery boy, though both could use more vigour. I don't know the official tempo marking for the young one's big number, but it sounded slow to me.

Patrick D. Brown has a showpiece interlude as a proud head waiter, trying against all odds to maintain "a romantic atmosphere." This scene is one of several in Roger Hodgman's production to improve on a recent New York production, in which it was sent intolerably over the top.

As for the show's own romantic atmosphere: The program hints that it's the richer for being so fragile, what with our awareness of the Depression and the coming war. I don't think this is proven.

Certainly the characters are glad to have jobs, but Georg walks out of his without a qualm, and the store, dispensing mainly luxury goods, seems to have no shortage of clientele.

If Joe Masteroff, the librettist, meant us to brood on the historical context, he should at some point have mentioned it as he did, in spades, in his subsequent book for Cabaret. What matters here is the way in which his script supports and blends with Harnick's lyrics and Jerry Bock's crisp, versatile music -- both possessed of a shy warmth that expands under pressure.

On record, the score sounds nice but a bit studied. On stage it's magic. This may be the best romantic musical since My Fair Lady and the best musical production I've seen at this festival.
