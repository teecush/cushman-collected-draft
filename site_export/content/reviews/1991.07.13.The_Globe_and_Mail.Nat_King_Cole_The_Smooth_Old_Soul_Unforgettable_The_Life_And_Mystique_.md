Nat King Cole, the smooth old soul UNFORGETTABLE: The Life and Mystique of Nat King Cole

BY LESLIE GOURSE McClelland & Stewart, 309 pages, $29.95

Saturday, July 13, 1991

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Review by ROBERT CUSHMAN LESLIE Gourse starts her biography with an "Overview" that commends Nat Cole's "subtle swing" as a pianist, of which his singing style was initially just an extension. Of his ballad singing she remarks, justly and evocatively, "there was a humming quality to his sound that suggested the murmur of a bee flitting around a rose garden," adding that listeners were "touched by the kindness, tenderness and patience in his voice," while his friends and entourage found the same qualities in him as a man. She alludes to the "frightening racial clash in Alabama" when white racists jumped the stage and pummelled him to the ground. Gourse also points to the crucial influence on his career of his second wife Maria, "an unabashed snob" but "renowned for her shrewdness . . . and for her stylishness and taste." She was his unofficial manager, though his official one, Carlos Gastel, was important too; he "had a magic touch."

All this is fine, but I am not sure that any but diehard fans need to read the rest of the book, except perhaps for the indigestibly informative chapter-headings, the like of which I have never seen. They run from "Nat Begins Family Feuds with the Jazz Critics and His Own Jazz Trio" to "The Coles Adopt a Son, as Nat Searches for a Way to the Broadway Stage," with "A Chops-Breaking Ground-Breaking on Television" thrown in for light relief. I found the book hard going, in the way that Easy Listening can be hard going.

Cole's career certainly has significance even apart from his talent. He was the son of a preacher - from Alabama, ironically enough - who disapproved of the profane music that attracted Nat and his three brothers. Born, probably, in 1916, Cole became a full-time pianist in 1935. He scuffled in Chicago and Los Angeles, eventually formed a successful trio with a piano-bass-guitar format that has haunted cocktail lounges ever since. His singing career famously started when an insistent customer demanded a vocal on Sweet Lorraine. By 1950 he was an institution, and remained so up to and following his death from cancer in 1965.

He still suffered all his life from racial discrimination. The Alabama outrage was only the least subtle expression of it. He had his own TV series, but it was pulled for lack of sponsors. He was a very black black; and at one point - not mentioned in this book - they tried to lighten him up. They reckoned without his slanted eyes; he now looked sinister and, for some weird reason, it made his singing sound mannered.

Cole ran into serious tax problems; they were cleared up, but not before, Gourse says, "he had gone through the tortures of the damned." That is what she says; it is not what she makes us feel. She even fails to move us with her account of his death. She gets more caught up in his funeral, doubtless because it's better documented. She piles on the facts, but however much she tells us, she cannot get us interested in the man, maybe because she herself shows no great interest in the music. She characterizes herself as a jazz fan who has tried to take a "mellow" attitude to her subject's involvement with pop, but mellow, once past the overview, turns out to mean non-committal. Even the song that gives the book its title goes unremarked. So, more importantly, do some of Cole's best records, from his wackily exuberant Orange-Coloured Sky to his silken, definitive Stardust. During the 1950s, Cole's smoothness was a reassuring contrast to Sinatra's edge; in that sense he took over from Bing Crosby, who was ceasing to be a major recording presence at the time when Cole became one.

Sammy Davis, Oscar Peterson, Carmen McRae, Mark Murphy, Johnny Mathis and now daughter Natalie have all recorded tributes to the man and his repertoire. There have been few similar testimonials to Crosby and I doubt that there will ever be many to Sinatra. There is a recognizable, manageable body of Cole songs. Bing's portfolio is almost too vast to encompass, while Sinatra, who has had surprisingly few hit singles, is a National Theatre of song, doing the same numbers as everybody else, only better.

The Cole cult is also partly the result of his having died young; we feel robbed. Author Gene Lees thinks his life may have been shortened by suppressed anger. In The Singers and the Song he tells a story of Cole calling his record company, being greeted by the bright announcement "Capitol Records, home of the Beatles," and slamming the phone down in fury. None of that ever got into his music, which may be the poorer for it. The bosses and the public preferred him bland and so, it sometimes seemed, did he. Gourse, belying her prickly name, goes with the flow.

Robert Cushman writes and hosts Songbook on CBC Radio and Stereo.
