Joe Orton's Entertaining Mr. Sloane was his first play and, when staged in London in the 1960s, made him an instant celebrity. Gay when it was a crime, and fresh from a six-month jail term for defacing library books, Orton conceived the piece as a comic revelation of the hypocrisies and sexual rapacities of lowermiddle-class British life. Its a theme he was to expand in his subsequent, more elaborately farcical plays Loot and What the Butler Saw, both of which are, in dialogue and action, bolder and funnier than their predecessor. It does, however, have one quality that they lack, and that is superbly brought out in Soulpeppers first-rate new production. Orton would probably have scoffed at the phrase, but Entertaining Mr. Sloane has human interest.

Not that that makes it sentimental. Mr. Sloane himself is a fair-haired hunk of 20 or so, taken as a lodger by Kath, a lady twice his age who says she picked him up at the library. (Joe's in-joke?) Kath has designs on Sloane's body. So does her brother Ed, but hes too repressed to act on them, which no one, except perhaps herself, could say about Kath. despite her protestations of virtue ("I had an upbringing a nun might envy") shes all over him from the first moment he sits on her settee. Before the play is done, she will find herself, for the second time in her life, pregnant. The first time was when she was young, by a man whom her family forbade her to marry; at least, that's her story. The child was given up for adoption; and one rather expects it to be revealed that Sloane is her long-lost son. In a later Orton play, it would have been after she'd bedded him. Even as it is, she keeps calling him her baby.

Ed, meanwhile, a selfproclaimed businessman who keeps boasting of his high-placed and high-living contacts without letting on who they are, engages Sloane as his chauffeur and does his best to pry him away from Kath. Theres another member of the household, the siblings elderly, ailing but peppery dada (that's what Kath calls him) who suspects, correctly, that Sloane may have murdered his, dada's, former employer. Sloane has to shut him up, thereby putting himself at the mercy of brother and sister, and each of them at one anothers. The game of three-sided blackmail that ensues is clever, icy, hilarious and disconcertingly believable, like the entire play. Its also, like the entire play, overextended. We know everything about these characters, long before they've finished telling us. The wit of the dialogue, self-satirizing epigrams couched in tones as prim as their Wildean predecessors were fruity, isn't quite enough to compensate.

Brendan Healeys production is staged in the round, the acting area surrounded by billowy red curtains that part to reveal a lounge (genteel speak for living room) thats

the pared-down essence of suburbia; yannik Larivee's set cunningly gives us the basics without smothering the play in chintz, as has been its fate in some earlier productions. A happy corollary of this is that none of the characters gets patronized. The performances capture every shift in tone, every laugh and every twinge of horror or - believe it or not - pity; and the accents, allimportant in this play, are spoton. As Kath, Fiona reid is, not too surprisingly, perfect. undulating in her undies, she's a model of sensual gentility, and also of domestic pride. (The landlady-lodger relationship owes something to Pinters The Birthday Party; the master returned the compliment, not altogether successfully, in the more ruthless moments of The Homecoming.) But shes also a touching figure of desperation, not least when her claws come out. A moment at which she replaces her false teeth, dislodged in a squabble, and favours the assembly with a radiant smile, sums up the play and the character for all time.

Stuart Hughes is less obvious casting for Ed, and sometimes the effort shows. But he still commands the stage, rocking back and forth on his heels in agonies of inadmissible lust; and his timing and pacing are sharp as a tack, or as the checked suit that proclaims him a man of the world, no matter if the world's a small one. What Sloane's own sexual preferences may be remains a mystery; I think we can safely assume that he doesnt have any emotional ones. In later Orton plays, his chilly amorality would infect the entire world; here its his own personal province. david Beazley, whiny when cornered, otherwise casually cruel, flawlessly embodies it. Michael Simpson brings a surprising dignity to dada, whose (comparative) honesty isn't as mercilessly mocked here as it would be in Loot; while in What the Butler Saw honesty hardly enters the equation. They, as already stated, are probably more considerable, certainly more deeply subversive plays. But, no doubt about it, as presented here Mr. Sloane is very entertaining.
