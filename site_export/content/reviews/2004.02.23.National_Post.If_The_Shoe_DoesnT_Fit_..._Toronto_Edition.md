CHINA DOLL

Tarragon Extra Space, Toronto

Nora Helmers, the heroine of Ibsen's A Doll's House, slammed the door on her husband, her family and her whole past life in 1879. That of course was in Norway, but the sound reverberated around the world. By 1920 it was making a major contribution to the emerging feminist movement in China. The prostitutes in the teahouses of Shanghai took a particular shine to it, and were even referred to as "Noras." This is the kind of long-range, far-reaching effect that theatre buffs dream of. It makes us feel we may not be wasting our lives.

It sounds, in fact, too good to be true. But the facts are there, set out in the program for Marjorie Chan's new play China Doll, and justifying what might otherwise seem an intolerably cute title. Su- Ling, Chan's heroine, grows up during the last years of Imperial China and the first years of the republic, and her life is changed when a local draper with an activist past hands her a samizdat copy of the Ibsen play.

Su-Ling is not a prostitute, though she might as well be; she is a poor girl -- of, as they say, good family -- who is about to be purchased as the second wife (i.e., first concubine) of a local rich man. She has been brought up, very strictly, by her embittered grandmother, who is a servant in the house of the designated husband. The play charts the awakening of Su-Ling; everything in it is a flashback from her first line, which is "I am walking towards the future."

Those words have a particular resonance because Su-Ling has spent her life, since the age of five, with her feet bound. This traditional method of making a woman attractive to men was on its last legs, so to speak, at this time, but the news hasn't reached Su- Ling's community, where the custom is rigorously enforced.

It seems to work, too, at least in the sense of having turned Chinese men into a race of foot fetishists. Merchant Li, Su-Ling's benefactor and the only male we actually set eyes on, has an orgasm when measuring her for her tiny shoes -- and he's one of the enlightened. It was he who, long before making a Nora of her, taught her to read -- to the fury of her grandmother, who believes, with reason, that literacy lowers a girl's market value. The only other character is a servant girl with whom Su-Ling is forbidden to play when they are both children, and whom the grandmother bullies unmercifully when she turns up later as Su-Ling's bridal attendant.

Chan does a good job of suggesting the progress of history through the interaction of only four people, as does Kelly Thornton's clear and deliberate production. There is, however, a downside. The pattern can hardly help becoming schematic, and though the play is never boring, the slow pace gets oppressive. This is partly because the outcome is so predictable. We know, even without the opening line, that the show will end with the heroine having her own Nora moment, and we want her, for her sake and our own, to get it done and get it over.

We can't get too excited over the obstacles she faces, because they're mainly external. There is little complexity in Su-Ling or in anybody else, just the occasional faltering when the play feels the need of it. Ibsen always denied having any interest in women's rights, but that's his problem; the play speaks louder than its author. All the same, the best productions of A Doll's House are those that suggest the husband might also have a case, and -- most importantly -- that Nora has been partly complicit in her own oppression. There's nothing like that here.

We do encounter, though, at least one other literary analogue. At one point we hear a Chinese version of the Cinderella story, with special emphasis on the slipper. The foot motif actually looms larger than the doll theme, and is more deserving of a place in the title, but it isn't very satisfactorily worked out. The climax is Su- Ling's tearing the wrappings off for herself , and here the production, previously cool and open, goes melodramatic on us. Red lights flash and music jangles while the protagonist, behind her semi-transparent bed-curtains, is obviously up to something. What it is, we can't quite make out, and though we find out soon enough, the moment has passed. It's cheating.

Meanwhile, much has been made of the "Lotus slippers" -- ceremonial shoes, intricately designed -- that the bride herself must prepare and embroider, first for her betrothal and then her wedding. It turns out that Su-Ling is no good at embroidery and has to get the besotted Merchant Li to do it for her. She has, in other words, no stake -- not even an aesthetic one -- in the system she's abandoning.

We see nothing of her allotted husband, who wants to have a China doll that he can call his own, and that he is apparently prepared to break -- literally -- if she doesn't give satisfaction. We see far too much of the grandmother, whose function is to be either a bully or a nag, sometimes both at once, and who is very poorly played. Nothing is more theatrically lethal than an unsympathetic role, ineptly acted; it drains the stage. The actress, who seems too young for her role, is allowed a couple of vulnerable moments, and is tolerable when tearful. Chan herself plays Su-Ling, and her acting, whether downcast or perky, faithfully reflects both the charm and the limitations of her writing. John Ng's Merchant Li is likeable, but fuzzy at the edges.

As the servant, outspoken but chained to her social role, Keira Loughran brightens the scene every time she appears, and irradiates it whenever she opens her mouth. None of her colleagues has anything like her discipline or her attack. Maybe the contrast is intentional, meant to denote a difference in class, but it ends up seeming more like a difference in culture; she sounds, in her immediacy, far more North American than they do.

Loughran also makes a few ghostly appearances, most delicately executed, as Su-Ling's mother, who vanished into the work of the teahouses. This is another thread, the rebellion of the sexually exploited, that the play sets in motion but fails to pursue; its themes and characters go on and off like Christmas tree lights, rather than having the steady glow of Chinese lanterns. That, though, is a lot to ask. This is a first play, and an ambitious one, and it's worth attending. You learn something from it, and that's always a pleasure.
