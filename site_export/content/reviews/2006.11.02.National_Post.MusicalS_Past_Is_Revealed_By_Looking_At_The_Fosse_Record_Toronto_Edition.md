PIPPIN

Royal Alexandra Theatre, Toronto

- - -

There have been two Golden Ages of, or at least for, Stephen Schwartz. There was a point in the 1970s at which three hit shows for which he was both composer and lyricist -- Godspell, Pippin and The Magic Show -- were running simultaneously on Broadway. He is now experiencing, in Toronto at least, a more modest renaissance. Wicked, his most recent piece, is back in town for an extended run, and a revival of Pippin has just checked in at the Royal Alexandra Theatre.

There is one thing that all the above musicals have in common: They are terrible. (I have to take the awfulness of The Magic Show on trust, but it has been universally attested.) That doesn't necessarily mean the scores are; Schwartz at his best writes decent lyrics and better music, though they always come in tied to mawkish and/or pretentious entertainments. Pippin's score is his best -- in what was once considered a pop-rock vein but now sounding more like mainstream Broadway -- but Roger O. Hirson's book, and the show's entire concept, qualify for both the above adjectives, though "pretentious" predominates. Moreover, the current version (which originated at the Goodspeed Opera House in Connecticut, home and forcing-house of musical revivals) suffers from a fatal disease: de- Fossefication.

Bob Fosse directed and choreographed the original Pippin. It was part of the cycle that he began with the film Cabaret and continued with Chicago, Dancin' and All That Jazz. These were all self-hating productions that set show business against itself. The master showman used all his devices -- he gave us the old razzle dazzle, as they say in Chicago -- while ending up railing at his audiences for accepting them and himself for using them. Personally I found all this self-flagellation tiresome. If he despised himself so much for playing tricks, he should have either stopped playing them or shut up about it. The latter would have been preferable, since he played them so well, and never better than in Pippin.

Some shows can work when de-Fossefied; the phenomenally successful revival of Chicago is not, whatever people tell you, as good as the original but it's more palatable -- and more enjoyable - - precisely because its creator's obsessions are toned down and kept at a distance. Besides, the underlying material is strong and the story interesting. Pippin doesn't have those luxuries. Its hero is the son of the Emperor Charlemagne (whose chivalrous reputation, though the show makes no pretentions to historical authenticity, is thoroughly debunked) and a hopeless idealist. In the first act he tries such youthful pursuits as war, sex, parricide (revoked -- this is a musical) and benevolent rule on his own account, and finds them all wanting. In the second, in which the action and background of the first are almost completely forgotten, he pursues his quest for "a higher purpose" and finds it, to the surprise of everyone except the audience, in the love of a young widow and her son. This is not at all what the people telling the story had in mind. They are a garish troupe of strolling players -- Pippin himself seems to be with them but not of them -- who try to seduce him with lights, costumes, music and, well, razzle dazzle. (Once Fosse got an idea, he stuck with it.) Without these things, they jeer, he is just ordinary, and who wants to be that?

It's very thin stuff, but the original production made it work with staging that pinned your attention from the opening hallucinatory number, Magic to Do, in which a white-gloved corps leaped out from a black box. In the new version (directed by Gabriel Barre, choreographed by Mark Dundy) the gloves are still on but the box has opened -- the set's predominant shade is murky blue. In the battlefield sequence, where Fosse and his colleagues earnestly set out to inform us that War is a Bad Thing, there's another fleeting homage to the master: the reappearance of his trademarked Steam Heat trio -- a girl and two guys in derbies doing an insolent strut -- but no sign of his overall architecture. (In the original Pippin, the threesome became known -- rather tastelessly -- as the Manson Trio.) It's significant, not to say mendacious, that Bob Fosse's name appears nowhere in the program though this is a show that nobody would be reviving now if he hadn't done it first. It's still his idea but without his ceaseless inventiveness, which is all that made the idea fly.

Some of the show's language seems to have become more permissive with the years, and there is an apparent attempt to link the fable's temptations of celebrity glitter to the age of American Idol. To quote the title of Fosse's last show (his only flop): big deal -- though, in fairness, the ending here does seem harder-hitting than I'd remembered it. As for the performers, as the Leading Player who is meant to be a Satanic charmer, Cabaret's backdated emcee, Andre Ward, grinning a lot, is long on Satanism but decidedly short on charm. Joshua Park's Pippin compensates in the latter department, and has real appeal as well; he is always direct and never merely winsome. Mickey Dolenz brings off Charlemagne with the same bluff imperturbablity that got him through the even grimmer circumstances of Aida -- though for some reason he looks, when not wearing his crown, as if he had wandered in from Fiddler on the Roof.
