THEATRE REVIEW

The Lesson

Lower Ossington Theatre, Toronto

Two celebrated European plays from the mid-20th century opened in Toronto last week. In fact, they had their official premieres on the same night: Thursday if anyone's counting. Max Frischs The Arsonists and Eugene Ionescos The Lesson have something else in common: They have both been tagged as belonging to the Theatre of the Absurd, a catch-all category whose boundaries are so porous as to be meaningless. Or, in another word, absurd.

It certainly has nothing to do with Frisch, the action of whose plays is thoroughly realistic, despite being carried out in the main by characters generic or representative. But if there ever was an absurdist playwright, it might well be Ionesco, whose plays, of which The Lesson is one of the first and best, depict people saying and doing things unlikely to be said and done in everyday life. Which doesn't mean of course that they don't reflect and illuminate things that are said and done. They could even, though Ionesco himself might have had mixed feelings about this, be called political. And that does point us to some common ground between The Lesson and The Arsonists. The Arsonists is a transparent allegory on the rise of Nazism. In The Lesson a Professor, who has done something awful, is admonished by his housekeeper to don an armband as self-protection: a red armband whose business side is turned away from the audience but that might well feature a swastika. It figures, as he has momentarily transformed into a gibbering, goose-stepping parody of Hitler.

Or at least he does in this production. The armband is in the text; the Great Dictator routine isnt. Its an invention of Soheil Parsa, the Iranian refugee director whose Modern Times Stage Company is presenting the play. Ionesco's Professor flatters, intimidates and finally terrorizes his young female Pupil (Ionesco does generic names, too) by showering her with pseudo-academic gobbledygook. The use of language as a weapon has obvious political implications. Parsa brings these to the forefront with a battery of design, sound, lighting and casting effects that range from the impressive to the ridiculous. Broadly speaking, they work best at the start when they're establishing an atmosphere, worst at the end when theyre underlining points that the script and the actors are making powerfully well on their own.

The set is a bare chamber, furnished only with two high-backed office chairs on castors, uncomfortable enough to frighten any student (or prisoner), mobile enough to empower any teacher (or interrogator). The air is full of noises, strident, screeching and threatening. The opening bars of Beethovens Fifth are briefly transmitted to signal the Professor's approach; it sounds as if he's descending from an upper level with Beethoven as his elevator music.

The Pupil, mindful of how much she has to learn, awaits his approach with trepidation. However, when he appears, clad in this version in gown and mortar board like everyones favourite caricature of a schoolmaster, he is initially all kindness. He takes her through her mathematics, delighted by her skill at addition (she has no problem with one plus one), but disappointed by her inability to master subtraction (one minus one consistently defeats her). He moves on to philology, despite the warnings of his Maid, whos been here before, that "philology leads to calamity." It certainly leads, in the Professor's case, to prolixity and paranoia. On and on he rants, in a cascading spiral of meaninglessness, about Spanish and Neo-Spanish and some kind of Romanian, and the less the Pupil understands him - in effect, the less she submits to him - the more enraged he becomes. It's a truism that language is a weapon; its being brandished here without regard to any Geneva Convention.

David Ferry plays the Professor and Michelle Monteith the Pupil, and theyre as superb together here as they were two years ago in Blasted. Ferry's performance traces a seamless arc (barring a few understandable verbal stumbles) from pedagogue to demagogue, getting increasingly enflamed on the way, before subsiding into a grotesque crumple.

It's a brilliant display; but though I think I can see how he does it (if not how he learned it), I havent a clue how Monteith arrived at her sublime incarnation of the Pupil. At first shes the picture of demure, Alice bemused at her entry into Wonderland, where she does indeed meet the maddest of Hatters. Under his onslaught, she cries out that she has a toothache; she repeats this countless times, and the gradations of fright and helplessness that she's able, without any obvious changes in intonation, to infuse into that one line are extraordinary.

The tension generated by these two actors makes the fancier touches of Parsa's direction seem increasingly superfluous. They include having the Maid played by a man (Costa Tovarnisky) in drag, with a bald head and a thick Eastern European accent, and having him come on as a sinister, controlling apparatchik instead of a faithfully clucking domestic, cleaning up her masters messes.

Not that the performance isnt compelling in its own right, especially in its brief appearances in the apertures of an otherwise featureless set. Parsa has narrowed the play in presenting it as a primarily political parable, but he hasn't distorted it. If there is such a thing as the Theatre of the Absurd, then one of its achievements is to present us with power-plays in their purest form. Ionesco, one considered a back number, has had a resurgence in recent years and that's a reason why. He leads us, by fantastic paths, back to theatrical basics.

The Lesson runs until Dec. 1. For tickets and information, visit moderntimesstage.com.
