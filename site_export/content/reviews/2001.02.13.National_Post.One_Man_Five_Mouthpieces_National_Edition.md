VOICES

Theatre Passe Muraille Backspace, Toronto

Jeroen Willems is an amazing actor. He is Dutch, and he came to Toronto last week as the closing entry in the Six Stages festival in a solo performance.

With the exception of Paula and Karl, everything in this international mini-fest has been a one-person show and the standard has mostly been high. Also, blessedly, everything has been in English.

Willems has five voices in Voices -- five mouthpieces of international capitalism, gathered around a heavily laden dinner table in what seem to be the dying hours -- the liqueur, cigar and desultory dessert-eating hours --of a very fancy gathering.

Willems, who is both feline and forceful, passes from one chair to another, assuming a different voice and gait at each station. His physical grace in assuming and then maintaining each of these transformations is consummate. The vocal identities he bestows on each character are exact, his timing perfect, his inflections a joy, malicious and delicious. And he isn't even doing it in his native language.

Its possible his vocal virtuosity sometimes obscures the actual words he is saying, but I wouldnt lose sleep over it. The text, or most of it, is by Pier Paolo Pasolini, the late Italian film director, whose movies many of us for a few painful moments of the 1970s tried hard to persuade ourselves we liked.

It seems unlikely he meant the first four of this show's five monologues to function as parts of a play: They are separate character sketches, brought together here to illustrate the physical and intellectual structures that support multi-nationals.

There is a philosopher of sorts, distinguishing between those ordinary people who know they believe in nothing, and those equally ordinary people who believe in nothing without knowing it; there is a scientist; there is a manager. The attitude of each to the others is contempt, rising in intensity as we move around the board, though this is illustrated more in the staging than in the writing. At one climactic point, Willems gets to throw a drink in his own face, if you follow me; at another he gets to pinch his previous incarnation's helping of syllabub. (From where I was sitting, it looked like syllabub.)

What these characters say is often hard to follow intellectually; and even when it makes anecdotal sense, its hard to relate to anything else.

Pasolini, I suppose, is trying to condemn them out of their own mouths. But if mystification is what youre trying to attack, it hardly makes sense to add to it yourself, and it certainly isn't much fun to listen to. Also -- in a style I associate equally with 1960s Italian movies and 1970s English plays -- the piece seems to be presenting class warfare at the least interesting level. It's as if were being incited to revolution simply because the bosses eat richer food and have kinkier sex than the rest of us, but dont enjoy it very much. We're being asked to revolt against the privileged boredom of a mandarin elite, and it hardly seems worth the effort.

The last speech isn't by Pasolini at all, but by Cor Herkstroter, who gets program credit as "the chairman of the board of Shell International" and also -- in a fine display of barbed conscientiousness -- as co-author. (But is he getting royalties?)

So, for his last turn, Willems is impersonating Herkstroter, holding court among his sycophants and defending himself, and them, against the bad rap hung on them by the privileged mutterers of the West -- whose indignation at oil companies activities in the Third World has not, he notes, stopped them from driving their cars. He, too, of course, is meant to be condemning himself out of his own mouth. And if I read his speech in print, or heard him deliver it himself, I might well feel that he was.

But it doesnt work like that in the theatre. Herkstroter's ironies may be facile, but they are at least comprehensible, which gives them a distinct edge on Pasolini's. He transcends his intended role of devils advocate. And even if hes the real devil, he's preferable to the minor fiends around him.

The fact that his speech impales comfortable liberals (i.e. the audience) seems to have much impressed critics in Europe, comfortable liberals themselves, who seem to have found the show's format very novel and its viewpoint positively scathing. Essentially, though, its the same old, same old.

Except, of course, for Willemss performance, or rather performances. Starting out as the company's tame intellectual -- a daintily flapping logic chopper -- he moves through various degrees of camouflaged coarseness to arrive at Mr. Shell himself, whom he presents as unassuming but immovable, urgently courteous, suaver than suave. He strikingly resembles the smooth and fast gentleman salesman in the TV commercials for ING Direct.

His real set-piece, though, comes when he takes on the role of a lady diner, who conducts an intimate relationship with a pair of pantyhose and a high-heeled shoe that just happen to have been left lying about on the table among the brandy bottles. (It seems to have been quite a party.)

Willems gives a teasing performance of a tease, spinning out a detailed if obscure parable that does indeed concern diabolic temptation while the character's mind and body play out a separate drama of their own.

For a brief period, making louche use of a wig and a wine bottle, he is a man impersonating a woman impersonating a man: all the sexual levels you could want, with the added bonus of an assumed political significance ticking somewhere in the background.

The direction, by Johan Simons for Theatergroep Hollandia, matches the acting in world-class shimmer.
