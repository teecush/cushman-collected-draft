The Doctor's Dilemma continues in repertory until Oct. 29 at the Festival Theatre in Niagara-on-the-Lake, $25 to $75. Call 1 (800) 511-7429.

The Doctor's Dilemma, written in 1906 and one of Bernard Shaw's most durable plays, offers a rare example of a Shavian character actually dying on stage. The victim, struck down by tuberculosis in his early 20s, is Louis Dubedat, a gifted painter and equally gifted sponger. Shaw called the play a tragedy, but this must have been one of his jokes. Dubedat's end is not moving, except in the general way that death always is, and he isn't the central character; he enters the play late and makes his decisive exit early. The play's focus is on those who may kill or cure him: half a dozen medical men who among them make up perhaps Shaw's most extensive and entertaining portrayal of an English professional caste, and, through them, of clubman society as a whole.

The doctor in the title has just become a doctor with a title; he is Colenso Ridgeon, a bachelor of 40, knighted for his contributions to research. He has recently discovered a cure for TB; as yet, though, he can only apply it in a limited way, and his clinic is full. Jennifer, Dubedat's beautiful wife, comes to beg him to take her husband on; and Ridgeon falls for her. His dilemma -- complicated later -- is whether to save Dubedat in order to please the wife or to refuse him in order to please himself.

Few doctors, probably, have faced such a stark mix of personal and professional choices (though I bet some have). Nevertheless, the situation, outrageous though it seems, reverberates. The play is, between its lines, as sharp an attack on private medicine as has ever been mounted, but no system -- public, private or mixed -- is going to do away with the need to make choices. Money is the least of it; there will never be enough time or enough compassion. We can rob doctors of their godlike attributes and transfer them to administrators, but that only shifts the problem.

Christopher Newton's production is a first-rate ensemble showcase, full of rich comic acting and strikingly done up. Between the scenes, uniformed domestics shift the furniture to music, while a sextet of characters in simian masks cavorts in the foreground. Their faces are skulls; they hold their stomachs out before us in pot-bellied self-satisfaction. You feel that behind their masks, they may be blushingly conscious of their own absurdity. They are, of course, the doctors.

The inspiration for these interludes is apparently the Mexican celebration of the Day of the Dead, (though I only know that from reading the program so it doesn't count). It in no way interferes with the direction or performance of the actual text, but it gives the players a launching pad, and the designer, too. Released from stodgy realism, Sue LePage supplies a series of spacious environments in which the gallery that houses Dubedat's posthumous exhibition looks like an extension of Ridgeon's consulting room, with the artist's paintings looking, in long shot, unnervingly like the doctor's microscope slides. Especially striking is the design for a riverside restaurant -- a pattern of black branches on a green screen, lit by a broad yellow moon and a thin golden lamp. Dubedat's attic studio, with Jennifer languorously posing, is sensual enough to arouse any number of respectable gentlemen, if only to jealousy or disapproval.

These are largely what motivate Ridgeon, who must be the coldest of dramatic protagonists. He is not only the messenger of death, he is essentially dead himself, for all his mid-life stirrings; and it is this, rather than the disparity in their ages, that ultimately causes Jennifer to reject him -- a fact that Blair Williams brings out, at the climax of a fine and uncompromising performance, as he walks away rigid and broken.

His colleagues are the play's chorus, each with his own idee fixe or favourite cure, a device that Shaw overplays at the beginning, but orchestrates skilfully thereafter. Two of them stand out -- a wise man and a fool -- and it is not at all clear that the wise man holds any more of the truth than the fool does. They are both superbly played here. Bernard Behrens is Sir Patrick Cullen, the venerable physician who has seen medical fads come and go, and regards all medicines and all men with equal skepticism; he never says a kind word or performs an unkind action. His opposite number is Sir Ralph Bloomfield Bonington, "B.B.," doctor to the Royal Family, whose bland and mellifluous voice can itself make people feel better. (Shaw must have designed this role, prophetically, for that other Sir Ralph, surnamed Richardson, who unkindly never played it.) It is to B.B.'s literally tender mercies that Ridgeon fatally consigns Dubedat. Jim Mezon, whom I have never seen better, plays him splendidly, prowling distractedly around the stage whenever his vanity is pricked, deeply moved by his patient's death and saluting it with a patchwork Shakespearean soliloquy, and reflecting very persuasively that most people would indeed be "better dead."

The others, all admirable, are the surgeon Cutler Walpole (Lorne Kennedy, almost too incisive even for a man of his profession), Schutzmacher (Neil Barclay), a rich Jewish GP whom Shaw finds fun as a mouthpiece but then abruptly drops, and Blenkinsop (Guy Bannerman), a very poor GP, decent but dull, whose own case of TB gives Ridgeon the perfect get-out.

The doctors run the play, and the Dubedats are seen largely from their point of view. This makes Louis himself limited but manageable: a brilliant talent who borrows money and sex with equal unconcern. He spends most of his stage time shocking people and enjoying it; Mike Shara does this with wit if not quite enough spirit. Jennifer is defined by her blind hero-worship of him, which enchants some men and drives others mad; it gives her strength, and it does the same for Severn Thompson, who plays her like a mermaid: graceful, taunting, loyal and mysterious. There are notable small performances by Catherine McGregor as a chambermaid whom the painter once loved and left, and by Jennifer Phipps who, as Ridgeon's managing housekeeper, is perfect as ever; with her around, he's a baby for life.
