THIS IS OUR YOUTH

Berkeley Street Theatre

- - -

So, it's welcome back again to This Is Our Youth. Or This Is Our Youth Again. Or Sex and the City Goes Legit. The American play, seen in Toronto a year ago, has returned with two of its three roles recast with names, or sort of, from movies and television. The occasion has increased my respect for Kenneth Lonergan's script. If it can survive this cast, it can survive anything.

To recap: The play concerns three New Yorkers, all in their twenties and all with prosperous parents. Dennis has his own apartment and deals drugs; he is more or less permanently wired. Warren lives with his father, whom he hates, and seems to spend most of his time both hanging out with Dennis and hanging on to him; he's a human punching bag, to whom Dennis turns his attention when he's done with the actual punching bag that adorns his room. Jessica crosses their path as a possible date for Warren. As Dennis puts with his customary charm, she's the only good-looking girl his friend has had a shot at in years.

Lonergan's play is avowedly autobiographical (This Was His Youth), which explains why it's set in the 1980s. But, barring the absence from the characters' lives of PCs and CDs and the presence of hostile references to President Reagan rather than President Bush, it could be happening now. (This Is Still Our Youth, or a fragrant sample of it.)

Juvenile speech patterns, we realize, have stayed, like, remarkably unaltered down the decades. The characters talk in a mode that might be described as self-stylized, and within that framework the author's turn of phrase is impeccable. "You're really harsh, man" says a petulant Warren after Dennis has dealt him an especially vicious put-down, and the choice of word is perfect. Equally impressive is his skill at building scenes.

The peak here is the first encounter between Warren, unpractised lust trying to look cool, and Jessica, mild disinterest that feels obliged to act polite. With the help of some smoking and some dancing (to Tutti Frutti on vinyl, nostalgia squared, as it were) they adjourn to the penthouse suite at the Plaza -- though even now Jessica appears willing rather than keen. En route we learn about the death of Warren's sister, a topic the playwright puts to explosive use in the second act, as a partial and very persuasive illustration of Warren's angry, wounded relationship with his father.

The physical bones of Woody Harrelson's former excellent production are still clearly visible but the flesh has decayed to the point of decomposition. The two newcomers are devoid of theatre experience and they have the lack of technique to prove it. They both gabble and mumble; one of them has little idea of projection and the other has none whatsoever.

The former is Katharine Isabelle; a scene that requires her to get angry is totally beyond her, but otherwise she makes a reasonable stab at Jessica's well-intentioned blankness, as she oscillates between a permissive lifestyle and the desire to be back at her mom's before curfew. She's amateurish, but on screen this might be a passable performance.

The one given by Jason Lewis (the Sex and the City dude) would be intolerable in any medium. He speaks in an unvaried subterranean drawl, but his inaudibility is only the half of it. It's clear from the lines you can hear that he has no idea how to phrase his author's language and no appreciation of either its shape or its wit. The great cadenza in which Dennis suddenly develops the ambition to be a film director might as well not be there. He does admittedly look at home on the stage, even if he doesn't sound it; he slouches and pounces and punches as if he were enjoying it. Even so, one looks achingly back on the dynamic things Fabrizio Filippo did with the role last year, and especially on how he managed to integrate an enforced last-minute limp into Dennis' general jumpiness. It might take a full set of crutches to galvanize Lewis.

I don't recall Marcello Cabezas, the original cast's sole survivor, being as exaggeratedly high-pitched and hysterical last time around as he is now, but maybe he feels obliged to make up for the languor of his colleagues. (He is the moving spirit behind the whole production.) Having nobody to bounce off, and no rhythm to fit into, he is bound to seem forced, but he still transmits Warren's puppy-dog self-abasement -- he always seems to be presenting his bum for Dennis to kick -- and his dignity when, with nothing left to lose, he asserts himself.

Last year, Warren, because he has more stage time than anyone else, seemed to be the central character by default. This time he's the central character as of right. Between them, the trio run a gamut of incomprehensibility; but Cabezas at least has energy and focus. His colleagues just made me angry, as did the people -- Cabezas, under his producer's hat, included -- who hired them. What are they doing here? There must be any number of good young actors, in and around Toronto, who could use the break; there must, come to that, be any number of American actors whom one would welcome to chance to see. Why bring in rubbish?
