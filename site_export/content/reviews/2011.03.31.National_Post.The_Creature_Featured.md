THEATRE REVIEW

FRANKENSTEIN

National Theatre Live

Man, look in the mirror. Behold a Monster. In the British National Theatre production of Frankenstein, two young actors alternate in the roles of the eponymous scientist and his scarifying creation. Two weeks ago, courtesy of National Theatre Live, audiences in movie theatres worldwide had the chance to see Benedict Cumberbatch and Jonny Lee Miller as creature and creator respectively. Thursday, in the production's second international transmission, the roles will be reversed.

The production, which has been generally and for the most part justifiably acclaimed, marks the return to theatre direction for Danny Boyle. The adaptation is the work of the British playwright Nick Dear. They are rightly proud of having given Frankensteins monster his voice back. Most movie versions have presented him as a grunting, lumbering ogre. In Mary Shelleys original novel, though, he is intelligent, highly articulate and even, no thanks to Victor Frankenstein whose responsibility it might reasonably have been, literate.

In both the novel and the play, Victor, disgusted at the ugliness of what he has wrought, turns his back on it and leaves it to wander through GermanyandSwitzerlandfending for itself. It discovers three impoverished French swells in a hut, and through a combination of lip-reading and deduction, learns their language.

In the book, this is accomplished through a prolonged period of observation on the part of the monster. As a theatrical device, this could be limiting, so the Creature, to give him his program billing, is granted a prolonged relationship with the aged head of the house who, being blind, can't be physically revolted by him, and who gives him a crash course in Plutarch, Goethe and Paradise Lost.

As a created being, the Creature finds a kindred spirit in Adam; as an outcast, he identifies, far more strongly, with Satan. When he finally tracks down his own maker in his Genevan retreat, he's able to pose the pointed questions that man has always wanted to ask of God: If you constructed me -- and in your own image at that -- how can anything be my fault, and why am I suffering? And Shelley sees to it that Frankensteins answers are as evasive and unsatisfactory as religions have always been.

It's in bringing this debate and its attendant metaphor home that this version is at its strongest. The actual text isn't that wonderful. Some of it is authentic Shelley, which when spoken sounds stiff; the rest, meaning the most, is pastiche Dear, an uneasy compromise between ancient and modern. Theres nothing that good acting couldnt successfully navigate; but the standard of supporting acting here is far below what used to be the norm at the National. The lower orders drag out all the old rustic cliches; the nobility are dull or, in the case of Frankenstein pere, unforgivably bad. The only exceptions are Karl Johnson as the sightless tutor and Naomie Harris as the literal bride of Frankenstein, who comes to a nasty end on her nuptial night: the result of the Creature's taking revenge for Victor's welshing on his promise to provide him with a lady Creature to ease his terrible loneliness.

The monsters pain is palpable, and gets us on his side throughout. So what if he burns his benefactors in their beds, murders Victors kid brother and rapes Elizabeth, who's been anachronistically kind to him, before killing her? Cumberbatch achieves a looming, slow-moving, painfully eloquent triumph. So, by all accounts, does Miller, his alternate; though their movement director and make-up artists must surely share some credit.

The title character, at least as Miller plays him, is reduced to minor-character status, and overdone like the rest. When man and monster have their great confrontation, the monster is all focused anger and anguish; the man merely rants. Just in terms of stage time, the adaptation gives the Creature a head start over the Doctor, who never really catches up.

Technically and sonically Boyle's production is very impressive, although seeing it at second hand, so to speak, we find ourselves watching the effects for their own sakes rather than for their results; as was not the case on other, simpler, National Theatre Live occasions. It's tempting to compare the central duo to a Germanic Prospero and Caliban, but they come off here more like Othello and Iago: partners in crime, indissolubly knit. And they follow one another to the extremes. Like the novel, the play drives through to a finale in the Arctic wastes, and it summons them up with comparable intensity; though its new open-ended ending is more frustrating than stimulating. In this matter, Mary knew best.
