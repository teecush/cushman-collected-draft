William Saroyan's The Time of Your Life is a play that's impossible not to love, one that sends you out of the theatre with the heady sensation of wishing you had written it yourself. Soulpeppers production is one of the best things the company has done, certainly its finest new achievement since setting up shop in the Distillery. Written in the perilous year of 1939, and set in a San Francisco honky-tonk, the play presents a crowded stageful of American dreamers and, while fully acknowledging the odds against it happening, shows most of their dreams coming true. One of its minor but defining characters is Harry, an aspiring hoofer and comedian, played here by Jeff Lillico, a young actor whose stature increases by -- literally in this case -- leaps and bounds. After unrolling a stream-of-consciousness routine thats plainly ahead of its time, Harry declares I'm funny -- but they don't laugh." This line, in its blend of pride and frustration, just about sums up the plays view of the human condition. It also, to make the bitter even more sweetly ironic, garners the biggest laugh of the evening, a moment that doesnt so much rock the house as bond it.

Saroyan, the program tells us, died saying "I thought they would make an exception for me and the play's optimism seems, on the surface, just as unreal. It presents a world in which whores and innocents can find true love, preferably with one another (but then in Saroyan's world the whores are innocents) and in which strikers and strike-breakers lie down together like lions and lambs. The one unsympathetic character is an officious sadist from the vice squad, and when he gets struck down everybodys happy. At roughly the same time, a pinball freak whos been playing the machine the whole length of the play finally succeeds in making the Stars and Stripes pop up on it. This, the play implies, is how things should be; America should be standing up against Hitler, who in this perspective is a vice cop writ large. Saroyan knew something about genocidal tyrannies; his family was Armenian.

Large-cast plays set in bars traditionally inspire great ensemble work, and so it is here. They can also become merry whirls, an option that Albert Schultz's production, though rich in comedy, steadfastly refuses to take. It moves at a measured pace and a subdued volume that are initially disconcerting but gather increasing force, providing breathing-space for everybody and everything. Actors perform duologue scenes while those not involved but still on stage go into suspended animation, giving the whole thing, on Lorenzo Savoini's spare and mellow set, the look and feel of a genre-painting.

The play seems to be taking its tone and tempo from its central figure, a philosophical philanthropist named Joe. Joe spends his days and nights drinking champagne (a unique tipple among the habitues of this particular saloon) at a corner table, buying newspapers by the batch but not reading them, sending out for whatever takes his fancy and performing acts of random kindness. Joseph Zieglers Joe is another in his line of terrifyingly good performances, sad and witty and gentle, delightedly inquisitive about his fellowmen while personally unreachable, authoritative, even when sitting hunched over and moving with appropriate stiffness on the rare occasions he gets up. Joes attitude to the American Way is, like the play's, ambiguous; he takes great delight in such profitable national icons as toys, guns and chewing gum, while feeling guilty over the pile he himself has made from robbing the poor -- not, one suspects, illegally. There seems also to be a broken heart in his past, as he shares with a sympathetic stranger (Jennifer Villaverde) his memories of the woman he never married and his dreams of the children they never had -- the third one was my favourite." This is the most magical scene currently on the Toronto stage. The second most magical has Ziegler challenging Kevin Bundy's wholly delightful Tom, his protege and gofer, as to which of them can chew the most sticks of gum at one time. The contest, which coincides with major plot developments, is refereed by Stuart Hughes giving the performance of his life as a moccasined yarn-spinner, quick on the draw and slow on the drawl, whose most outrageous yarn is true.

The bar boasts a second guardian angel in the unlikely shape of its proprietor Nick, whom Derek Boyes makes likeable but too obviously soft-shelled. There are lesser roles, too, where the texture turns soggy: Oliver Dennis, too emphatically sententious as a cop with a conscience, and Martha MacIsaac, too emphatically tearful as an incipient war widow. Patricia Fagan, though, cuts sentimentality triumphantly off at the pass, being moving and even believable as the tart with a heart.

The text calls for a great deal of music, on mouth-organ or jukebox, and the production, as beautifully orchestrated aurally as visibly, picks him up on this and raises the stakes on piano. Jazz singer Denzel Sinclaire, making a good acting debut as a vagrant with an unexpected gift, does the playing; he also gets to sing Little Girl Blue, something that Saroyan didnt think of but should have done Everybody wants a job at Nicks; a newsboy (Benjamin Head) breaks out into When Irish Eyes Are Smiling, and gets a hand if not an engagement. More seasoned actors adorn some of the tiniest roles: John Jarvis and Jane Spidell as a pair of society slummers, and William Webster and Les Carlson as a pair of drunks. Webster has the play's first line: "This is a free country -- isn't it? The plays answer is an emphatic yes, with reverberating reservations. There can hardly be a Joe to look after every saloon in the land, and he makes a mysterious exit from this one, leaving behind him an air of hopeful melancholy. In his lyrics to Zip, from Pal Joey, Lorenz Hart asked, "Will Saroyan ever write a great play?" That was in 1940. On this showing, it's clear he already had.
