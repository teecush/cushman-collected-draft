It would probably be easier to draw up a list of plays that arent concerned with class and gender than of those that are. Still, some are more concerned than others; and the Shaw Festival has mounted a triptych of them. All, not coincidentally, are in some sense comedies. After Top Girls and The Twelve-Pound Look, the Shaw completes the panel with Pygmalion, the house sage's funniest play in a brilliant production by Peter Hinton.

Brilliant, but not always wholly convincing.

Shaw's story of the London flower-girl who infiltrates the upper classes when a professor of phonetics teaches her how to speak proper has had its time-setting advanced from 1914 to now. Theres a logical flaw here, and it spreads across the whole production. Accent in todays Britain does not determine status in the same way it did at the start of the First World War. Eliza Doolittle may learn, under Professor Higgins' tutelage, to speak what used to be known as BBC English but it's far from clear that she needs it, unless perhaps she aims for a place in David Camerons cabinet. In Hintons production the BBC itself, ironically enough, makes the point. Between scenes, we see excerpts from a TV documentary about Britain's new creative and entrepreneurial classes, none of them sounding as if they have plums in their mouths. None of them would be shocked by Eliza's cockney - or, in this dispensation, estuary - English; and neither would the people who hang around Higgins mother, who in this version is a fashionable dress designer with a very busy entourage.

Her showroom is the site of the famous teaparty scene in which Eliza, by saying the unsayable in her new cut-glass accent, shocks or titillates her fellow-guests. Or baffles them, though its hard to believe that anyone in 2015 would be unaware that to do someone in means to kill them. That line remains as it was, but others have been changed. The director's program note says that textual alterations have been limited to "money values and places" but they're actually a bit more radical than that. Elizas legendary not bloody likely has been saucily upgraded (or downgraded, if you prefer). Not all the changes work. "Them she lived with would have killed her for a hatpin, let alone a hat" loses all its flavour when its replaced by something about coats and linings. As for places: Eliza no longer comes from Lisson Grove (these days a rather trendy address, on the edge of Regents Park) but from Harlesden: Zadie Smith territory, as befits the ethnicity of Harveen Sandhu, who plays her. Trouble is, substituting a two-letter location for a three-letter one kills the rhythm. Shaw had a great ear; he knew about these things.

Humour - verbal humour, anyway - isn't what this production is best at. Eliza's insouciant verbal bombshells in polite company lose much of their effect when she delivers them roaming around the room rather than in sedate stillness. As her dad, proud spokesman of the undeserving poor, Peter Krantz seems more self-pitying than self-assured; when he comes into his fortune, his director has him rolling around on the ground, pursued by demons in the form of a reality TV crew. What does work, superbly, is the relationship of Eliza and Higgins, that intricate dance of approach and avoidance that every audience hopes will turn into a romance and that never quite does. Unless youre thinking of My Fair Lady, and even there the issue is left ever so slightly unresolved.

Doolittle senior isnt the only character whom this production keeps close to the ground; Sandhu's Eliza and Patrick McManus's Higgins, in their last long duet, hit the floor, and trail one another around it, in their desperation to get through to one another, or at least to have the last word. A scene that usually seems repetitious now yields up the secret of its length: neither party can bear to let go. Eliza at least has a shrewd idea of whats at stake between them; Higgins, emotionally oblivious all his life, cannot afford to. Hes one character for whom, in McManus's performance, the updating works perfectly. He lives surrounded by audio tapes and video screens; he takes his phonetic notes on an iPad; he rides a bike; he dresses with an ostentatious informality that verges on the insulting. In his relationships with women, and with society at large, he puts the pig in Pygmalion. One does wonder if, looking as he does, he would be granted entrance to the Savoy, even in the 21st century and even with Colonel Pickering to vouch for him. Jeff Meadows, by the way, makes the good colonel more of a person and less of a stooge than I have ever known before; other subsidiary bouquets are due to Donna Belleville as the professor's real mother and to Mary Haney as his substitute one, the housekeeper with, in this performance, an Irish accent her employer would surely love to cut with a knife.

Sets are by Eo Sharp, costumes by Christina Poddubiuk, and projections by Beth Kates and Ben Chaisson. Their work is uniformly sumptuous, though the lighting denies us a good look at Elizas probably gorgeous ball-gown, and the lovely waterside view from Mrs. Higgins place is puzzling; it has to be the Thames, since we're told her studio is in Chelsea, but if that's the case the river has certainly been cleaned up since I last saw it. Hintons production isnt a masterpiece like his Lady Windermere's Fan, which lit up every corner of the play. This one has its lapses and inconsistencies. But it's strong where his work, for all its gorgeous incidental qualities, is always strongest: in its emotional reality. Higgins and Eliza come so impossibly close; and the keyword is "impossibly." Or maybe not. A huge question-mark hangs over the end of the play: huge and stimulating.
