2 PIANOS, 4 HANDS

Elgin Theatre, Toronto

- - -

The best scene in 2 Pianos, 4 Hands remains the one in which the teenage Ted Dykstra, played by the present-day middle-aged Ted Dykstra, is told by an angry conservatory examiner, played by Richard Greenblatt, that he will never make a concert pianist. He has the talent, but he has squandered it through lack of discipline. Dykstra crumples at the keyboard. The audience, even those members of it who never took piano lessons, goes rigid in sympathy. The effect, I think, goes deeper even than the near-universal experience of having an adolescent dream crushed. It's about having a door slammed in your face at any time of your life, and knowing that the slamming was justified.

It rather queers the pitch of the succeeding, balancing scene in which its Greenblatts turn to shed years and Dykstra's to play the dismissive tutor. It seems that Greenblatt, who has also been a classical student up to now, has decided to play jazz, and he embarrasses his tutor, his audience and us by trying to sound hip, on and off the piano. He too gets his comeuppance, a funnier one than his partner's but less touching.

Returning to Toronto after having gone round the world for seven years, both with and without its performer-creators, 2 Pianos, 4 Hands remains a unique show. Comedy at the keyboard is a fairly common commodity, but its usually a solo proposition, and I certainly can't think of any other piece that welds a series of musical jokes and domestic sketches into a dual autobiography that resonates. Nor can I think of another that utilizes the same combination of talents. Versatility is not uncommon in Canadian theatre (it's almost a necessity for survival), but the accomplishments of Dykstra and Greenblatt -- each a respected actor- musician-writer-director -- are ridiculous. Here they get to show off all of it. (Andy McKim is listed as consultant director, which I assume means the other two prepared the production and he looked it over.)

They take us through encounters with intimidating or inspiring teachers, dead composers who make terrifying technical demands ("thanks a lot, Mr. Bach!") and parents who attack them for not practising enough when theyre young and for getting the whole thing out of proportion and practising too much when theyre older. The show, incidentally, is rather educational, especially about the naming of chords.

Much of the detail is plainly fanciful. I stand to be corrected, but I don't believe Dykstra and Greenblatt, a.k.a. Teddy and Richie, actually grew up together in Montreal and played together in duet competitions, at one of which Dykstra got humiliatingly lost in the Hall of the Mountain King. I wonder, too, whether their ambitions were as consumingly, exclusively musical as they would have us believe. Was theatre something that both of them just fell into later, as a last resort? (They've done very well if it was.) But the essence is believable.

The short, shock-headed Dykstra is the more resourceful and malleable clown of the two, especially adept at childish terrors and tantrums and at protruding from the piano stool at impossible, anguished angles. Greenblatt, taller and thinner, is a strict straight man with a touch of the Borscht Belt, a kind of thinking man's Bud Abbott. They are, as some of the world now knows, a great team. They end, after a couple of sharp scenes depicting the scuffles of the young almost-musicians, as middle-aged men reflecting on what might have been. They then play -- ostensibly for one another, really of course for us -- seriously and without interruption. Here, and especially in an encore rendition of Sheep May Safely Graze (thanks indeed, Mr. Bach) they convince us that they are indeed, as they claim, "two of the best players in the neighbourhood" and that it's a good neighbourhood.
