GEOMETRY IN VENICE

Factory Theatre, Toronto

I wish I had read more Henry James. That isn't a feeling I have very often. I find James -- as a novelist, short-story writer and essayist -- extremely resistible: socially narrow and esthetically prim.

From time to time, though, I am confronted by a decent dramatisation of one of his works. James -- who, despite repeated efforts, never wrote a successful play himself -- has nevertheless been the cause of good plays in others. And then I start wondering about the source, and about how closely it resembles the piece before me.

I wonder especially in the case of Michael Mackenzie's Geometry in Venice. This derives from a James novella entitled The Tutor and obviously has a complex relationship to it, one that extends to bringing James himself on stage as a character. The play was first staged 10 years ago. After its initial Toronto run, it was staged in French in Montreal, and ran there five years. It therefore ranks as a Canadian classic: a phrase that usually chills the blood, especially when it heralds an actual revival. A classic is a work that resonates beyond its own immediate time and place: Geometry in Venice is one of the few acclaimed Canadian plays that might conceivably do that. And Henry James, though I hate to admit it, must probably take part of the credit.

The action concerns a late 19th-century English family, upper- class and on their uppers -- i.e. broke. They have holed up in Venice, though in the course of the action they move to Paris, and their name is Moreen. (Only James would do a thing like that: You keep wondering why the lady of the house keeps calling her spouse Maureen, and why he, a peppery type, puts up with it.) Their source of income is uncertain but Mr. Moreen periodically sallies forth (his phrase) and sometimes, apparently, he returns with loot.

Mrs. Moreen is an apparently gracious lady with literary inclinations. They have a 23-year-old daughter, Amy, whom they are anxious to marry off, an anxiety Amy herself more than shares. They also have a pubescent son, Morgan, about whom they are deeply concerned. He was born with a heart-defect -- they call it "a flaw" - - and they worry about the effect on him either of over-exertion and overexcitement. Previously, his mother has attended to his education; now, they decide to hire a tutor, a young Canadian (the playwright's touch), also literary, and named Pemberton.

Teacher and pupil take to one another immediately once Pemberton recognizes Morgan to be exceptionally intelligent. That's putting it mildly: The boy seems to be a genius. Already conversationally fluent in Latin (though he knows, to Pemberton's dismay, nothing of grammar), he speedily masters Greek. From there he moves on to the sciences and especially to geometry, for which his tutor has a logician's passion.

The only snag is that the Moreens keep neglecting to pay Pemberton, and seem hurt when he raises the subject. Finally he presents them with an ultimatum, which Mrs. M. defuses by taking him into her bed. Here, I imagine, the play makes explicit what the story left decorously clouded. Certainly, when the Moreens' fortunes take a final tumble, the language unleashed is beyond anything any Victorian novelist, early or late, would have considered proper. It's convincing, though.

In its master-student relationship, not to mention its mistress- student relationship, in its sense of an ancien regime in decline, in its literary trappings, the play now seems strongly reminiscent of Tom Stoppard's Arcadia, though in fact it came first. (Did England's favourite playwriting son haunt the theatres of Ontario and Quebec a decade ago? Investigate.)

It, too, sets up an opposition between mathematical rigour and emotional chaos, though it's more talked about than felt. The play never fully justifies its title. It's not as elaborate a fireworks display as Arcadia, but it has its own wit and its own tension, each sharpening the other, and it rises to a poignantly ironic climax that seems both surprising and inevitable.

Ken Gass' production at the Factory divides, if you'll pardon the solecism, into two unequal halves. On the good side, illuminating everything and everyone it touches and leaving the audience most touched of all, there is Luke Kirby's remarkable performance as Morgan, a wonderful mixture of intellectual prodigy and emotional infant. Almost morbidly considerate of others, he clings desperately to his new friendship with his master; the homosexual overtones are finely shaded in. In these,

Graeme Somerville acts Pemberton with perfect sympathy and perfect tact. He is far less at ease, though, when he crosses over to the other part of the play to meet the Moreens, probably because they don't seem at home there themselves.

Graham Harley as Moreen repeats the oily robber-baron performance he gave this summer in Much Ado About Nothing. It was effective there, since it cast a new light on a character generally seen as benevolent, but here it plays like a cartoon. At least, though, it works on its own terms.

Nola Augustson as his wife tries hard for elegance but cannot reach it, and without that initial veneer, the cracks beneath go for nothing. Nor is pumping emotion into the lines the same thing as portraying intellectual passion. Juno Mills-Cockell's Amy (an underwritten role) has the same complaint on a smaller scale: There's no shock when she lets loose with four-letter words because there has been no composure to shatter.

Victoria Wallace provides a stark palazzo set, lit (by Rebecca Picherak) with excessive dankness, and demanding a more rigorous level of acting and staging than it gets.

I've left out James himself (Adrian Churchill). He appears as Amy's escort, of whom the family entertain vain hopes, then vanishes. Finally, he returns when all is ruined and is told by Mrs. Moreen (in one of the actress' finer moments) to get lost. As a person, he has aroused expectations he could not fulfil; as an author he has set in motion passions he could not control, and that his characters have had the gall to take more seriously than he ever could. That, at least, is how I read it, though maybe I'm projecting my own feelings about the novelist onto the playwright.

The eveming is dedicated the late and dearly missed Mira Friedlander. And, though imperfect, I think it would have pleased her.
