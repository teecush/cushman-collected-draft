THEATRE REVIEW BROTHER ANDRE'S HEART

Friday, March 20, 1992

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Toronto ONT -- WRITTEN BY LINDA GRIFFITHS Directed by Jim Millan with Linda Griffiths Starring Arsinee Khanjian At the Poor Alex Rating: 0 Linda Griffith's new play suggests a riotous black comedy but . . . Brother Andres Heart flatlines BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Toronto THERE are many ways of hiding from life, and being a compulsive watcher of Star Trek is one of them. That is the uncontroversial premise of Linda Griffithss new play, which introduces us to a couple of guys in Montreal who once had film-making ambitions but have given up on them, and on just about everything else. Scared by the state of the city, the country and the world, they rarely leave their apartment, and live only for their daily dose of sci-fi. You've heard of being spaced out. These boys are spaced in.

A lady friend, marginally more together than they are though much oppressed by memories of a Catholic girlhood, talks them into accompanying her to a local Oratory, where reside the remains of Brother Andre, a Jesuit and a possible candidate for sainthood. Her idea is that such a demonstration of piety will comfort her grandmother, who is old and near death. They go more than once, but these errands of mercy always take longer than they expect and when they return home the best they get from their TV set is a merciless rolling of the final credits. (What they really need of course is a VCR but obviously they can't afford it. Besides if they had one there would be no play, though that might not be such a great loss).

Driven to desperation they make off with the oratorys prize exhibit, Brother Andres formaldehyde-soaked heart. They spend the second half of the play wondering what to do with it and scared of being caught. The situation suggests riotous black comedy on the order of Joe Orton's Loot (which has a faintly similar set-up) but not much is made of it. Not much is made of anything. No mileage is extracted from the characters being failed filmmakers rather than failed anything elses, though their Trekkie fixation is exploited. It allows them to keep addressing one another as "Captain and Number One" and this keeps the script going for pages.

Grandma herself turns up, much healthier than we are led to expect. In fact she is crammed fit-to-bust with the life force. She talks to the others about her own past and their futures and turns them into better people. The meaning may be that we must work our own miracles, or that a holy relic is what you make it. At one point the audience is invited to get up on stage and inspect the heart for themselves. This is even more embarrassing than most audience participation, since it represents a total break in convention.

To give the author her due, she has handed one of the actors, Andrew Miller, a couple of off-handedly funny lines which he delivers very well. The rest of the time he is just fey, while his partner, Shawn Williams, is a hole on the stage. Debra Kirshenbaum has energy, more than the play can use. Arsinee Khanjian, as granny, has some stage presence but not enough. To get her inner youthfulness to really impress us, she would first have to seem convincingly old. Her arrival is meant to be the play's big surprise and it's certainly a jolt. There she is in the doorway, and as we catch our breath the lights go down for the intermission. But on the first night Jim Millan's production, by botching the blackout, gave us a large anti-climax.
