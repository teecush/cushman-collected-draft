Three Men on a Horse

Festival Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

- - -

Meek and mild Erwin Trowbridge of Ozone Heights, N.J., makes a living writing verses for greeting cards -- a living that's as modest as he is himself. He also has a talent, an uncanny and infallible one, for doping the horse races. He never bets -- he would never take such a risk -- but on one rare, rebellious day he wanders into a New York bar where he encounters three professional gamblers, who naturally regard him as a gold mine. These guys give Three Men on a Horse its title, though there is never any doubt that the protagonist is Erwin.

He is currently being given a sweet, lanky and altogether captivating performance by Kevin Bundy, and if everything else in this Shaw Festival production matched his charm and restraint, there could be unbounded joy in Lakeville. Before getting into why it doesnt and there isnt, I should supply a few details about the play itself.

It was staged on Broadway in 1935, very successfully, and is the joint work of John Cecil Holm, who first had the idea and wrote it down, and George Abbott, the master showman who knocked it into performable shape. Abbott's record at picking winners wasn't quite as infallible as Erwins, but it was impressive and phenomenally durable, and this comedy found him at his peak, an eminence that he occupied for at least four decades.

It isnt the wittiest comedy ever written or the most inventive; it spins out its one idea to dangerous lengths. But that idea is a solid one, and it connects to something beyond itself. Erwin, both as versifier and as tipster, is a creature of inspiration. He is in fact an artist (it doesn't matter whether or not he's a good one) and, like most artists, he combines total confidence in his gift with a terrifying awareness that it may disappear at any moment. Did I say he was modest? I really meant that he was humble -- a much finer thing.

The play fits into the favourite Niagara category of "classic American comedy" (a cupboard that must by now be dangerously close to bare), which has occasioned some of the best evenings on the Festival stage and some of the worst. The bad ones are those that blow the plays up to suit the supposed demands of the space and of its audience -- an audience that the management sometimes seems to consider remarkably undemanding. The nadir, a few seasons ago, was a production of The Man Who Came to Dinner that looked as if it had set out to undermine the play.

The current show, directed by Jim Mezon, isnt in that miserable league, but it exhibits some of its fondness for making jokes at the expense of the material. It features a running gag about the failure of the stage machinery to function properly. Thus, when the curtain first rises, it doesnt; it gets stuck halfway up. Even if you find this kind of gag amusing the first time, your later laughter is likely to be inhibited by the suspicion that some of the mistakes might really be mistakes.

I don't know how Abbott himself directed the play, but from all I've read about him -- and especially from the insistence of his star pupil, Hal Prince, that the famous Abbott touch was not about pace but about logic -- I am sure he would have hated the shenanigans here. They send out the most damaging signal possible for a comedy: the message that those concerned think that what they have isnt actually very funny.

The fault goes deeper than mere trappings. As an actor, Mezon never overacts -- the reverse, if anything -- but as a director he seems to be encouraging his colleagues to make up for his own lost time.

Some of the performances are embarrassingly exaggerated. Not all: Bundy, as previously noted, is a tower of strength, and not just metaphorically. Erwin does some spectacular worm-turning at the end, but even before that, he knows his own mind and shows it. This Erwin is no patsy. And neither is Patsy, that being the name of the principal horse-player. Simon Bradbury, pulling off a grand slam after his performance in Pygmalion, has a touch of Paulie Walnuts about him; generally genial, he turns speedily and frighteningly businesslike when he gets nasty. (These guys are not, as far as we can tell, mobsters, but they obviously have connections.) Peter Hutt, hulking, and Jeff Lillico, fresh-faced, complete an excellently contrasted trio.

As an observing barman, Peter Millard is almost too restrained (that sounds perverse, I know, but its true). As Erwin's bullying brother-in-law, Douglas E. Hughes keeps just about on the right side of caricature. The exit applause gained by Anthony Bekenn's tyrannical tycoon reawakened my suspicion, which I thought Id laid to rest, that the Shaw audience appreciates nothing so much as really bad acting.

The women, apart from Jillian Cooks cameo as a hard-boiled reporter, are horrific, though there are tantalizing signs that they don't have to be. Catherine McGregor, as Erwin's nagging but loving wife, is quite pleasing when she stops behaving like some kind of vibrating toy. As Patsys moll, a former showgirl, Glynis Ranney speaks in a voice that sounds like the product of a cheap tour through the continent of Europe but is probably meant to suggest a Jewish upbringing in one of the outer boroughs of New York City. Or maybe its a homage to Lotte Lenya. Anyway, it's an accent standing in for a characterization. Ranney, who has spent most of her career in musicals, seems to be working excessively hard to prove herself legit, and I suspect she could do far better with far less effort.

Erwin's specialty, at least as shown here, is writing Mothers Day cards, so Saturdays premiere had great, if necessarily fleeting, topicality. The appeal of Cameron Porteous' sets and costumes will probably prove more durable; they are solidly rooted in the Depression without being in any way depressing.

Erwin can only perform as a race-track prophet when riding the bus home; he tries to recreate the experience in the mugs' hotel room, but he cant, not even when one of them pipes up with an encouraging "fares, please." This is in the script, and its funny; I don't think it's enhanced by having the entire gang doing an elaborate mime. On the other hand, theres an addition to the texts last moments that's a definite improvement.

As in The Man Who Came to Dinner, a character written as black -- an elevator operator, in this case -- is played white, presumably so that nobody need feel demeaned. On the other hand, no black actors have been engaged to play parts that were written white but could plausibly be either. I would call this getting the worst of both worlds.
