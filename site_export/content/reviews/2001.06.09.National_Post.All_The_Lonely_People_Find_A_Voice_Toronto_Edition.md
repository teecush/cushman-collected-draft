LONELY NIGHTS AND OTHER STORIES

Factory Theatre

- - -

Theatre Columbus, who work up their shows in rehearsal through group improvisation, have a winner.

Lonely Nights and Other Stories is a kaleidoscopic entertainment that deals, as announced, with people who cannot get through to one another or, worse yet, cannot even find another to fail to get through to. Here are people who cannot get a date, or sit tongue- tied in the presence of those they do have. Here are parents in search of children and children of parents. Here are sad apartments and sadder hotels. Here are rabid environmentalists who cannot even get desk clerks to listen to them.

Here, though it may not seem too likely, is a great deal of fun and as much sweetness as sadness. All the lonely people: The important question, as the show realizes, is not where they all come from but where they all go to.

The play is set, apparently, in the near future, though there is little about it that could not be happening already. The main differences are that the squirrels seem to be taking over, appearing everywhere anyone looks, and that the corded telephone seems finally to have disappeared. Cell-phones rule, cutting themselves out and their users off, and occasioning a telling joke about the way people communicate in prisons.

The setting is a picturesquely grey townscape, of which half the architecture looks vaguely Shakespearean -- curtained entrances, like at the Globe -- and the other half Dickensian. At a high window on the Elizabethan side sits a 3 a.m. radio announcer, telling us more or less that there are a million stories in the lonely city. Apparently, it has been raining for weeks.

This is not too prepossessing, and an extended group section that follows, all fretful hustle and bustle and mostly in mime, would probably be more enjoyable if one were seeing it for the second time and could appreciate where it was leading.

There are five actors in the cast, and initially it is not too easy to tell when one of them is either assuming a new identity or playing a recurring character. The hopeful sign is that, as the characters establish themselves, we want them to recur. And they do, in shifting combinations; the show begins to weave a cat's cradle of fragmentary encounters, even of fragmentary relationships.

The turning point for me was the moment at which a gawky lady who had interrupted somebody elses drama by trying to sell her a vacuum cleaner (surely a dying breed) was glimpsed knocking on another door, about to take centre-stage in her own story. There is something here of the insouciant loopiness with which Monty Pythons Flying Circus used to link its separate sketches; the vacuum hawker could well have been on her way to or from the Ministry of Silly Walks.

The writing of the show is credited to the director, Leah Cherniak, and to the actors, who are all in varying degrees splendid. One, Maggie Huculak, is sublime. The tallest actress west of Vanessa Redgrave, she plays with extraordinary grace and economy: a few strokes, a few words, and a character is solidly there.

She is the gently flailing salesperson who, asked to describe rather than demonstrate her product, begins by saying that "nature abhors a vacuum," and she is a maiden schoolteacher, etched with a wonderful stern dignity, who is mortally ill and whose plight moved me more in two minutes than did the whole of Wit put together.

Oliver Dennis specializes in gentle grotesquerie; he is a sci-fi nut of a receptionist whose courtesy is so understated as to be spectral, and an enforced celibate who, when other men tell him of their troubles with women, snaps that they're lucky to have the women to be troubled by, and -- more crisply -- a small-time private detective, English apparently, necessarily specializing in lost causes. His timing is superb.

Ker Wells is both touchy and touching as an illiterate ex-con, believably out for good behaviour, and very funny as a self- obsessed actor who talks to his mother as though she were his agent.

Michael Simpson does gruff, eccentric and cheerily desperate, notably as a man phoning every long-ago acquaintance in his address book before giving in and settling for phone sex.

Martha Ross lacks the presence and precision of the others (her night owls' deejay is over-insistent) but is fine as a lonely-heart rabbit farmer and a mother trying to make contact with a daughter on the other side of the world

All these people deserve some happiness. What's more, they get it. Relationships get tentatively launched; vital messages, through tortuous chains of circumstances, get delivered. Even the rain stops. Everybody breaks into a celebratory little dance, awkward but a delight. The emotional resolution that The Full Monty busts several guts to bring off, this show actually achieves. It should stick around.
