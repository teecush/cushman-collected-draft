THE GAME OF LOVE AND CHANCE

Artword Theatre, Toronto

There is a dramatic subsection that might be known as the Theatre of Geometry, or perhaps the Comedy of Intersecting Lines. One of its prize exhibits would be The Game of Love and Chance, an implacably symmetrical pavane of assumed identities, choreographed by Marivaux, the foremost playwright of 18th-century France. Actually, any one of his long line of sweet-and-sour courtship comedies might have been given the title he bestowed on this one, but this might be his diploma piece; only 90 minutes long, it presents the game in its most concentrated form. The ground rules are traditional, but they have been refined and complicated in the authors own rigorous, and occasionally cruel, imagination.

The principal players are Silvia and Dorante, two young aristocrats who have been betrothed to one another, sight unseen, and are about to meet for the first time.

Silvia, like any good comic heroine, is skeptical about men in general and this arranged match in particular; her attendant Lisette, like any good down-to-earth comic maid, can't understand what she's making such a fuss about. Silvia asks permission of her father, Monsieur Orgon, to let her change clothes with Lisette, so that she may size her fiance up before surrendering herself to him; her daddy protests that he has never heard of such a thing (he cant have been going to the theatre much) but, being no tyrant, agrees. He then learns, again to nobodys surprise but his own, that Dorante has had exactly the same idea and will arrive dressed as his own servant, Harlequin -- and vice, naturally, versa.

So the game is on: a set of mixed singles between master and mistress, valet and maid, each with a mistaken idea of who the other is, while father Orgon -- knowing everything and telling nothing -- plays the genial umpire.

It sounds simple, but the fact that each player is both trickster and tricked gives every scene -- indeed every line -- a double layer of irony; matching the characters' awareness against our own can become quite a taxing, if stimulating, exercise. Of course Silvia and Dorante fall for one another, as do Harlequin and Lisette, so each one of them is faced with the prospect of crossing a social gulf that doesn't in fact exist. The playwright then tosses in a couple of additional hurdles. Dorante, thinking himself in love with the maid, is challenged for her affections by Silvias brother (who of course doesnt love Lisette but is in on the game). Dorante also reveals his real identity to Silvia, who however maintains her own disguise, so for a time the play becomes a French equivalent of She Stoops to Conquer -- which is what Silvia had wanted it to be in the first place.

For the most part, though, this, like all Marivaux' plays, is a very un-English work. About the closest our language comes to this rarefied and single-minded concentration on the codes of passion is in some of Shakespeare's comedies (As You Like It, especially) and even in those the outside world keeps breaking in. By the 18th century English comedy had become very genteel, at least sexually, but it still got its hands dirtier than the Parisians ever did; it ate and drank and talked about money. One permutation that this comic kaleidoscope noticeably denies itself is any encounter between the true master and maid, or the true mistress and manservant. This isnt a revolutionary play like Beaumachais Marriage of Figaro, from the same country and period; its social attitudes seem to be those expressed in HMS Pinafore by the First Lord of the Admiralty when it was suggested that he might marry an able seaman's daughter: "Love levels all ranks, but it doesn't level them as much as all that.

On the other hand: There is an implicit bitterness in the situation of Lisette and Harlequin, their fates and their feelings taken to be automatically subordinate to those of their employers. How often it breaks surface depends largely on the actors. The more we believe in them as people, the stronger, as well as funnier, the play becomes. Thats certainly the case here with Colombe Demers Lisette, a wonderfully vivacious performance, technically contained but overflowing in both joy and pain. Her opposite number is a tougher proposition; the name Harlequin stylizes him immediately, as the kind of sad clown (a Frenchified Italian) who figures in the paintings of Watteau.

It's not a tradition to which we have much access, and Alex Poch- Goldin has some trouble fusing the style with the feeling.

If the maid outpaces the man she also (and again perhaps traditionally) betters her betters.

Graeme Somerville's Dorante is vigorous but neutral, Amy Price- Francis Silvia confident but over-eager. As Orgon, Oliver Dennis is avuncular rather than paternal, and does not look happy in his wig.

Nobody, though, looks or sounds lost, which may have something to do with the director, John van Burek, also being the translator. His text tries hard for accessibility and mostly succeeds, though some of his attempts at modernity (cutie-pie" turns up a lot) actually sound dated, like an academic American translation trying to be racy. Nobody wants old-fashioned, but maybe the best thing to go for would be timeless. Its not as if this is a modern-dress production; it comes in the usual frocks and hats on a standard cut-price- elegant set of three pairs of arches in diminishing perspective. The production is virtuous pioneering work, in a field we are still discovering; it's only in the last 20 years that the English- speaking theatre has even discovered that Marivaux exists. (Though he's moving fast; he even made it to Broadway three seasons ago, as the source of the musical Triumph of Love).

It's a discovery worth making; the plays have a distinctive quality of disillusioned benevolence, the very essence of tragi- comedy. The emotions he isolates and dissects are real, and the technique is a pleasure in itself.
