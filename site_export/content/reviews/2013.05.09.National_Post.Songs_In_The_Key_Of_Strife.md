THEATRE REVIEW

Falsettos

Daniels Spectrum, Toronto

After Robert McQueen's superb productions of Light in the Piazza and Caroline, or Change we now have -for the same company, Acting up Stage - his equally fine version of a slightly older American musical, William Finn's Falsettos.

I call it a "version" because Falsettos has a complicated history. In 1981 there was March of the Falsettos, a musical comedy of manners concerning a Jewish New Yorker called Marvin, his wife, his son, his male lover, and a psychiatrist. In 1990 there was Falsettoland, a darker piece, in which the son prepares for his bar mitzvah and the lover dies of AIDS. In 1992 there was Falsettos, which consisted of revised versions of the two earlier musicals. The new Toronto Falsettos presents the same two shows, but goes back to their original texts. I did see the '92 production in New York but at this remove I can't identify specific differences, all I can say is that this staging seems a) a bit long and b) a lot better.

Patrick du Wors has designed a dual set for this dual show. Part One takes place on a bare, black-draped rectangle, Part Two on a white disc, clinically matched to the hospital setting in which the story reaches its climax. McQueen's other principal collaborators are carried over from his production of Caroline: Reza Jacobs provides impeccably sharp musical direction; Kimberly Purtell supplies beautifully concentrated lighting (how refreshing to see a show in which the actors aren't underlit); and Tim French contributes tight, witty and occasionally uproarious choreography. French's work is an especially vital component of the production's overwhelming virtue, its lucidity. Which in this piece, or these pieces, is a remarkable virtue to have.

Finn's ambition in the Falsetto shows - to musicalize complex relationships - is admirable. his realization of it is patchy. Officially Falsettos has a book (credited to Finn and his original director James Lapine); in reality, it's virtually through-composed. So all the work of story and characterization has to be done by the score, and especially by the lyrics. Finn's words, though, are often vague and fuzzy. Sometimes he uses rhyme as it should be used, to make or drive home a point; other times, he seems to be using it because it's expected of him; he rarely makes it sound effortless. "My father's a homo," sings Marvin's son Jason, "my mother's not thrilled." But then he's off, laboriously pairing "homo" with "chromo(somes)," while the music chugs off in pursuit, turning into formless recitative.

March of the Falsettos has two title songs. At one time the show was to be called Four Jews in a Room Bitching, and it starts with a song of that name, sung by the males in the cast; Trina, the wife, later joins in, upping it to Five Jews. (I should say at this point that Falsettos is presented "in association with" the Harold Green Jewish Theatre Company, and that it's their most distinguished association to date.) "Bitch, bitch, bitch" they sing "funny, funny, funny," which is a great abstract set-up, set to a pulsating tune, but one that the song never develops or particularizes. Nor does it herald the action that follows as accurately as it thinks it does. The official title tune March of the Falsettos, also by the four men is, especially as done here, a delight, very catchy and offbeat, but, again, what does it really mean?

It can't just be about being gay, because two of the characters are straight. It's set off, as is made unusually clear in this production, by Trina's complaint that there aren't any real men in her life. But the tone might be either abashed or triumphant with the words, apparently assembled on a free-association basis, giving no clue. The second half of the evening welcomes us to Falsettoland, which is even less defined. The cast is expanded to include "the lesbians next door," a doctor and a caterer. Both might be described as neurotic, but then who might not? On this showing, Falsettoland is a very inclusive locale - chances are, you don't even have to be Jewish - whose main function is to provide a bitter-sweet punchline.

A major reason that it's ill-defined as a collective is that its inhabitants are shadowy as individuals. There are actually three Marvin musicals (there was a prequel called In Trousers) so it's amazing how little we learn about him. We do gather that he has money, but there's no clue as to how he's earned it. We also find that he's a double-standard jerk who, after setting up house with another man, objects to his wife doing the same. he also strikes her, making him, at least for a moment, an ex-wife beater. Whizzer, his lover, is even more of a cipher; all we discover about their relationship is that Whizzer sucks at chess (Act One) and Marvin at squash (Act Two).

It's hard to get invested in them. But this matters less in the first piece, which is mainly comedic, and is buoyed up by McQueen's unyielding insistence on having each number flow logically out of what has preceded it and on having each character sing as if he or she were communicating something specific to someone specific.

It's also carried by two supercharged performances. Michael Levinson, the amazing young boy in Caroline, is equally phenomenal as Jason, puzzled and enquiring, taking refuge - at least until puberty strikes at the first act curtain - in his father's favourite board game. (Fair's fair: Falsettos is a much better musical about chess than Chess.) Darrin Baker is a tonic as Mendel the psychiatrist who, with a royal disregard for customary ethics, progresses from treating Marvin to treating and eventually marrying Trina to treating Jason; he even makes house calls. he's professionally and lasciviously anxious, shortish and balding, mole-like but expansive: imagine George Costanza as a shrink.

Stephen Patterson and Eric Morin do everything possible for Marvin and Whizzer, and Glynis Ranney, vocally more pungent than plangent, comes strongly through as Trina; her opening moment, exasperatedly pushing a vacuum-cleaner across the stage, gets the show off to the best possible start. Sara-Jeanne Hosie and Sarah Gibbons are fine as the second act cavalry, though McQueen's only misstep is not allowing Hosie, as the doctor, enough time and space for her ominous announcement that "something bad is happening," the first darkening mention of AIDS. It presages Whizzer's sickness and eventual death.

When the show was first done, he might have been moving just as a statistic; now, we need to know more about him and Marvin as people. Since we don't, the Falsettoland half of the proceedings seems drawn-out and sentimental. In the 10 years between the two original shows Finn's lyrics and music actually grew more skilful and sophisticated, but they don't have as much to be sophisticated about. The best numbers are, again, the comedy ones: the latest in the American musical's long line of baseball ensembles ("we're here to watch Jewish boys who can't play baseball/play baseball") and a bouncing, as in on the furniture, duet for Mendel and Jason entitled Everyone Hates His Parents. Jason, we notice throughout, finds it easier to confide in his parents' lovers than in his parents, which is a point worth pondering.

The staging of his bar mitzvah (what, no rabbi?) finally unites the two main strands of action in a manner that's become legendary but had better remain unspecified, just in case. Anyway, it's a great production, and most people don't seem to share my reservations about the piece itself, so you should go.
