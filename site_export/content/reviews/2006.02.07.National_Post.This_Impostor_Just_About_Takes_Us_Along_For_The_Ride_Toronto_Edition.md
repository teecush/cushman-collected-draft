THE GOVERNMENT INSPECTOR

Marilyn and Charles Baillie Theatre, Young Centre for the Performing Arts

- - -

Soulpepper have paired their production of Our Town, an ambiguous celebration of small-town life, with The Government Inspector, an unambiguous attack on it. If the first production is perfect, the second is hit-and-miss. The hits are ecstatic. The misses include some jokey references to the companion play and to the company's new theatre, which may be meant as self-mocking but come off as self- hugging. Soulpepper are neither young enough nor old enough to be begging us to love them.

The entire evening, though, is about self-conscious theatricality, and to determine why Morris Panych, directing his own new adaptation, should have taken this approach (other than that it comes naturally to him) we have to go to the end of the play and work back. Nikolai Gogols comedy, set in a particularly backward Russian backwater, is probably the most celebrated mistaken- identity joke in world drama. A washed-up young man from St. Petersburg is mistaken for an investigating official by the local authorities, who load him with bribes to conceal their misdeeds, which are legion. Its practically a modern myth. (Its descendants, as noted in the program, include a classic episode of Fawlty Towers.) As the play races towards its final curtain, it takes in two Textbook Great Moments; Panych has seized on these and built a whole style around them. In the first, the town's mayor, learning he has been tricked, goes ballistic and turns on the audience. "What are you laughing at?" he yells at them. You're laughing at yourselves. This really stopped them in their tracks in Russia in 1838. Whether it could have the same effect now I doubt, but Im certain that Panychs concept hasn't helped its chances. There is no shock in having a play suddenly go meta-theatrical on us, when it's been frantically meta all evening. A few minutes later the story concludes, with a reversal that turns the assembled councillors into a row of open-mouthed petrified dummies. This is a second celebrated moment that always has difficulty living up to its legend. To help it along, Panych has added a surprise coda. I cant, of course, reveal what it is, but I will say that in seeking to avoid an anti- climax he has come up with a bigger one.

The principal carrier of the shows theatrical virus is the impostor himself, Khlestakov, a penniless junior clerk with literary aspirations. At least that's how Gogol wrote him; in this version, he's a stranded out-of-work actor. I think that somebody who falls into playing a part, and comes half to believe in it, is funnier as an idea than someone who does it for a living. There are plays, including a famous Russian one called The Forest, in which actors are mistaken for the people they act, but The Government Inspector wasnt meant to be one of them. I cant push the point, though, because the role, concept and all, allows Diego Matamoros to emerge as the great farceur we always knew he could be. Some of the in- jokes actually help him; a riff on Panych's success with another Gogol adaptation, The Overcoat ("a play without words where everyone just ran around to music") certainly made me laugh. It's just a preliminary, though, to a scene that remains essentially as its author wrote it, in which the mountebank gets gloriously drunk on alcohol, flattery and his own unbuttoned fantasies -- a progress only impeded by the after-effects of the fish he was served at supper. Both vocally and physically, the performance becomes a series of triumphant swoops and shameful lurches, each shading delicately and with increasing velocity into the next. Earlier, broke at his inn, he has given us similarly sharp contrasts between misery and bravado; later, he greets supplicants while reclining on a divan, head resting on hands, like Groucho Marxs idea of sexy. Youre never sure whether Khlestakov is an innocent masquerading as a trickster or vice versa; and neither apparently is he. His manservant has been rewritten as his more level-headed fellow- actor; Oliver Dennis, the only quiet person on stage, is our old friend, the straight man who's funnier than most of the clowns.

Panych's adaptation falls between stools, at its best when it sticks closely to the original while sprucing up the chat a bit, at its worst when inventing unnecessary sub-plots. These include having the mayors dumpy daughter (Deborah Hay, delightfully twitching and squawking) dejectedly wooed by the local health officer (Ben Carlson, looking forlorn beyond the call of duty), and having the other functionaries, not so much bureaucrats as kleptocrats, appeal to the supposed inspectors generosity by making the town look even worse than it is. This goes nowhere, and anyway it obscures what the play is angriest about: Not that the characters try to paper over abuses but that the abuses exist in the first place. The purpose, of course, is to invite comparisons with recent Canadian scams, but rubbing the audience's noses in contemporary relevance is pointless; a play either resonates or it doesn't. Here the satire starts strong and then peters out, William Websters mayor whipping both town and play into shape with tremendous force in the first scene and then running on empty. Nancy Palk plays his wife as a towering lascivious virago; she does it well but I rather wish she wouldnt. The others merge into a group, apart from John Jarvis and Ted Dykstra who, as a matched set of capering gossips, are a two-man group of their own.

However, there seems never to have been a production of The Government Inspector, starting with the very first one, that wasn't criticized for farcical extravagance, so that element may, just possibly, be inherent in the play. Panych's actual staging is, on its own hectic terms, excellent. He arranges the characters, each of them costumed by Judith Bowden as a separate joke on Russian couture, into close-packed tableaux of greasy corruption; in between scenes he gets them dancing in the kind of frenzied gallop that usually serves to conceal a set-change but here distracts us from the fact that Ken MacDonald's design, a cartoon in steep perspective, is staying very much the same. I think audiences will enjoy the production very much; I'd like to think that, if some of the choices had been different, they would have enjoyed it even more.
