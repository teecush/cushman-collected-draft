BABES ON BAY STREET

Theatre Passe Muraille, Toronto

The title suggests a movie that Judy Garland and Mickey Rooney wisely forgot to make.

But Babes on Bay Street is a surprise: a smartly amusing comedy about the business of business, written by someone who's been there. Lilia Csorgo's day jobs have been in financial consultancy of one kind or another and this is her first professionally-staged play.

That makes it the second consecutive debut on the Passe Muraille main stage; it isn't in the same league as its predecessor, The Drawer Boy, but they make a lovely couple.

This one presents a short period in the life, and especially the conscience, of a young woman called Jane. Jane works for a company that works for other companies, moving intangible assets to untaxable places. She is halfway up the building (unable, as she points out, to make it either to the street or the roof in case of fire) and her position inside the organization is somewhat similar. She worries about her success, or the lack of it; and about her social usefulness, or the lack of it.

She is flanked, as in a morality play, by two friends: Brenda, who is moderately idealistic, and Jake, who is moderately cynical. Brenda has left the race, having acquired a prosperous husband and twins, but then -- as Jake, who was once her boyfriend, loves to point out -- she can afford to. Jake is thoroughly happy in and with the system, and especially enjoys observing the femininity that passes down Bay Street; as he more or less puts it, we're talking serious babe here.

There have been theatrical satires on the money-market before -- it was an unsurprisingly hot subject in the 1980s -- but I like this one more. It is cooler in some ways, warmer in others; its characters may be types but they are not automata, and we see them manoeuvring in a world where cupidity is a negotiable norm, rather than an unprecedented aphrodisiac blast. (If Jane has a love-life, she keeps very quiet about it.)

The action runs on well-oiled wheels: literally so in Michael Waller's quick-witted production where characters sit in their office-rollers and propel themselves from corporate cubby to chromium cafe to thoroughly nondescript apartment on a functional if decidedly unlovely unit set. God is also in the details: in Brenda's habit of dispensing moral advice in the same cooing tone with which she addresses her babies; in the pre-recorded phone message which informs Jane that her place of employment has been massively re- structured; or, climactically, in Jake's reaction to the suggestion of a spot of insider trading. There is, he insists, a pale, and this is beyond it. The play itself, of course, written from experience, might count as insider trading in a peculiarly benevolent form.

As Jane, Elisa Moolecherry ends up striding towards an unspecified future that seems, from the staging, to reside somewhere up the centre aisle of the auditorium. She negotiates her way to this point with sparkling eyes, crackerjack timing, and a fine bewildered turn of speed. Maureen Kirkpatrick oozes comfortable sensitivity as Brenda, and Jim Jones is first-rate as Jake, shiny but not oily and resembling, with his eager beam and cockatoo quiff, a young political pundit with all of the answers and none of the questions. Bob Nasmith, in a dual role, can't get a handle on Jane's boss who needs to be simultaneously bland and scary, but is admirable as a friendly street-person whom she uses as an unconscious moral sounding-board and who is called Frank.

The choice of names is unspectacularly perfect; and I don't think it's fanciful to suggest that this is one mark of a natural playwright, especially a natural comic playwright.

The author is mining familiar territory here -- familiar to her and, only by hearsay, to us -- but she makes it seem fresh and quirky. As Jake might say if he understood irony, we're talking funny here, seriously funny.

- Babes on Bay Street runs until May 2. Box office: (416)-504- 7529.
