BELLE

Factory Theatre

I have problems with the idea of Black History Month, since it suggests that the history comes to a stop for the rest of the year. Still, we take what we get, and this February has brought two first- rate pieces of theatre. For the kids, theres YPTs Mella Mella ; for the grown-ups there's Belle, Florence Gibson's dazzlingly- written new play about the aftermath of the American Civil War.

One of the few things most of us know about that period is that it was one of massive internal migration. With the Souths economy in ruins, huge numbers of ex-slaves moved north, there to discover a different kind of misery: the freedom to start poor and stay poor. So here we find Belle and Boleyn, a young married couple who come with their baby to the big city (I think I heard New York mentioned) to find work. She scrubs floors, he tries setting up as a cab driver, having spent eight back-breaking years in the fields saving up to buy a horse.

There also turns out, less expectedly, to be white refugees from the same region. There is Nance, who fled in disgust from her plantation-owning daddy but is still financially dependent on him. She now devotes herself to the fight for womens suffrage. Then we have Lackley, known universally and to his great indignation as Lackey, who is trying to ingratiate himself with the new Republican Party, mainly by drumming up votes. Each wants something from Belle and Boleyn: political support and -- at least in Nance's case -- moral support as well.

Nance first encounters Boleyn when she hires his cab. She is his first fare and his last; she is on her way to disrupt a convention, he is implicated and his horse is impounded. Calling round to apologize, Nance meets Belle and spies a prize: a woman who is vibrant and intelligent and also coloured: an all-round standard- bearer for the oppressed. Boleyn discovers a talent for oratory in his own cause; he also signs up with the Republicans who are promising voting rights for blacks - though only for black men.

A brief period of solidarity is followed by disillusion. Boleyn discovers that his new rights are hedged around with conditions that prevent him from exercising them; Nance, to placate her white constituency, drops black suffrage from her platform. She is remorseful where Lackey is cynical, but the practical results are just about equal. Meanwhile, with Boleyn both resentful and defensive that Belle is now the breadwinner, their marriage sags and eventually breaks.

The play begins with a speech in rhyme. It then moves into prose, but prose of a richness to which we are hardly accustomed. It is aglow with metaphor but it is also tense with conflict. Prose poetry in the theatre is usually an ornamental disaster: an invitation to the audience to forget the characters and admire the playwright. Here it's an actors weapon.

Gibson can on occasion be devastatingly funny. You should be President of the United States says Nance to Boleyn, whom shes just met; he is taken understandably aback. She can be brilliantly and paradoxically compact: when Nance rails against "those Southern bitches" in her own caucus, Belle quietly reminds her I'm from the South.

She can also make her images flesh: as in a scene where the two women, discussing the effects of colour on the way we think and the way we talk, paint one another's faces. She uses her four characters to teach us, without apparent strain, a remarkable amount of history. She can illuminate the present through the past, and politics through sex -- especially with Nance whose relations with the two men seem equally fed by resentment and desire.

There are certainly flaws. The action -- black-white, man-woman - - can hardly help being schematic, and this takes its toll on the later parts of the play, where the characters get stuck in repetitive grooves. Belle herself, standing as she does at the epicentre of oppression, becomes an emblem rather than a person: this despite a performance by Yanna McIntosh that manages to be both earthy and radiant.

There is a fifth character, Belles sister left behind in Georgia, whom Boleyn calls "mad" and who in childhood was used and impregnated by her master; she hovers wraith-like over the action, always slightly out of focus, and is none too convincing when she comes belatedly to life.

The play deserves a more imaginative staging than it gets from Ken Gass. The characters may be poverty-stricken but the show doesnt have to be. The set, composed of planks and sackcloth, is simultaneously skeletal and cumbersome. The play is composed in bold, even melodramatic scenes; one concerns an attempted rape that climaxes in a fire. Here the episodes melt awkwardly into one another; actors don't even get the chance to make clean exits.

Still, Gass and his actors are on top of the dialogue, whose rich currency they convert it into lines of force passing between the characters. Soo Garay's Nance reclaims the image of the Southern belle: a second title-figure in fact and maybe the one to whom the author has lost her heart.

Nigel Shawn Williams' Boleyn is his strongest performance to date; he can tremble -- with rage, guilt or embarrasingly -- as eloquently as any actor I have seen. Alex Poch-Goldin's Lackey is a wily emotional derelict, an underdog with a perpetual itch in his voice. Here it is at last: a play that sings.
