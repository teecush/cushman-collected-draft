The People's Choice Award, a sort of extramural Dora, is voted for, online, by people who, it is assumed, are neither theatre professionals, such as comprise the juries for the Doras themselves, nor critics. In fact, they are ordinary punters making their preferences known in the teeth of the self-proclaimed experts. This is healthy. Last Monday, the prize went, hearteningly, to Evil Dead the Musical, a show that had been nominated in four of the official categories but lost in all of them. With that off my conscience, let me tell you what I think of Evil Dead.

I think it's terrible, at least as far as its actual writing is concerned; the staging and some of the performances are excellent. By now, we have learned to dread any show that has "the Musical" in its title. The suffix can mean one of two things, except in cases like this one where it means both of them. One is that the show's source material has already been so thoroughly worked over that the latest version needs to come equipped with some kind of distinguishing mark. The other is that musical theatre itself is regarded as a debased sub-genre, only viable as a source of campy fun. The result has been a long series of shows, spoofing things that nobody took seriously in the first place. Low-grade horror movies are favourite targets. The cycle probably started with The Rocky Horror Show, to which Evil Dead pays explicit homage. It has a dance-number called Do the Necronomicon, whose lyric describes it as being like The Time Warp only better. The judgment is arguable; but when a derivative show has to make a defensive joke of its reliance on another derivative show (to pre-empt the audience's noticing for themselves) we have reached a very advanced stage of enfeeblement indeed.

Evil Dead started as a Toronto fringe show, and probably worked very nicely there. It has since been to off-Broadway and back. It derives from a couple of cult movies, neither of which I've seen. This may conceivably disqualify me from judging the show, but a piece of entertainment is supposed to be able to stand on its own feet. I did get the impression that the enthusiastic audience with whom I saw the show already knew the material backwards, and were getting their kicks from being reminded of it. Maybe the award was less Popular and more cult-fanatical than I'd first thought.

Anyway, those who know the story in advance have an advantage over the rest of us, because the show itself doesn't tell it very well. It starts out with five young people -- a department-store salesman (houseware), his girlfriend, his kid sister, his best buddy and a tall pneumatic sex-mad blonde whom the buddy has been lucky enough to pick up--off to spend a weekend in a deserted cottage in the woods. This information is dispensed in an opening number that's almost entirely inaudible, so you have to pick up most of the information for yourselves later on. Anyway, the cottage is spooked, and by evening's end, four-fifths of the quintet have been turned into Candarian Demons, whatever they are, each transformation being announced by the victim's cackling "look who's evil now." The only one left standing is the salesman himself, and he's had his moments. Converging on their story is that of the daughter of the house's owner, a frantic academic, and her ineffectual boyfriend whom she never lets finish a sentence. The daughter gets to sing a long country-flavoured (i.e., whiny) song whose title is All the Men in My Life Keep Getting Killed by Candarian Demons. Apart from being a candidate, musically and lyrically, for Worst Song Ever Written for a Musical, even a cult-spoof one, it leaves us no wiser as to what, if anything, distinguishes a Candarian from any other kind of demon. The show skimps on its basic mythology even more shamelessly than it does on establishing its characters. What it really cares about is gore, as humans and demons set about slaughtering one another. Our indomitable houseware hero loses a hand quite early on; this is ingeniously handled as indeed are all the effects, all credit to the directors, Hinton Battle (who's also the adroit choreographer) and Christopher Bond, and to the very droll sets by David Gallo. The blood spatters very inventively; though the point does seem to be quantity rather than quality.

When asked who were the authors of Oklahoma!, Cole Porter, who himself always wrote both music and lyrics, replied "Rodgers and Hammerstein -- if you can imagine it taking two men to write one score." The gods alone know what he would have made of Evil Dead whose music is credited to four composers --Frank Cipolla, Melissa Morris, George Reinblatt and the aforementioned Christopher Bond -- who have failed between them to come up with a single distinctive tune. Reinblatt also wrote the lyrics, to which the idea of building a comedy song, as opposed to throwing in a couple of random jokes, seems completely foreign. His book, though, has its moments. I liked the failure of the nerdy boyfriend even to announce that he, too, was evil now without being interrupted; and I loved the response of his lady to the news that the hero's girlfriend was the only demon with a habit of making bad puns: "And she's the one you let live?" she says, in tones of censorious wonder. The towering Sarah Cornell, who can be smart and wide-eyed at the same time, does this delectably, as she does the entire role; she's less successful as the blonde bimbo, but as this character isn't around very long, it doesn't matter. Ryan Ward does unblinking yeoman service as the protagonist from retail, and the rest (there were some understudies on when I saw it) are enthusiastic if not exactly subtle. The show itself doesn't matter; the trend behind it does. It consists of people who have contempt for musicals expressing that contempt by writing them.
