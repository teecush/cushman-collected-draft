WOMEN WHO TALK TOO MUCH AND THE MICROPHONES THAT LOVE THEM

Poor Alex Theatre, Toronto

In this racket, you try to keep an open mind. Still, the very title of Micki Moore's one-woman show places a severe strain on anyone's reserves of faith, hope and charity. The first line of her actual performance wipes them out. She comes on stage, purporting to be a faded film star desperate for a comeback, and moans, in an intercontinental sort of voice, "I no longer vant to be alone."

That's about as witty as the evening gets. Moore's Garbo impersonation (why, at this late date in the history of civilization, would anybody want to do such a thing?) reaches its climax when she reveals that her career foundered, as recently as 1939, when she lost out to Vivien Leigh, whom she accurately describes as "wasp-waisted," for the role of Scarlett in Gone with the Wind. Well, that's news for cineastes. As a kicker she offers her own Central European take on one of Scarlett's most celebrated lines: "As God is my witness, I'll never go through Hungary again."

Moore not only performs this stuff, she wrote it.

Her subsequent vignettes are a little more up-to-date but are no more enlivening. She portrays a series of women who make public speeches with lamentable results. A tipsy mother at her son's bar mitzvah reveals her true feelings about her husband. (First the wedding scene in Hello Muddah, Hello Fadduh, now this. We've suffered enough. Or, as Mort Sahl is said to have said when the movie Exodus wound into its third hour, "Let my people go.") A lady convict sings the blues, but I've blotted most of this one out. A Presbyterian fundraiser reveals an innocent addiction to pornography and tries to get her congregation to invest in same; she also carries on a peculiarly unhilarious telephone conversation with her broker. An English aristocrat talks about celibacy and how often she's taken it up. (She is announced as Lady Penelope the Third. Call me an Anglocentric snob, but I have to point out that members of the English nobility -- unlike reigning monarchs and certain American millionaires -- do not have numbers after their names.) What all these women have in common is a propensity for dropping supposedly titillating hints about their sex lives, physical or mental. This may be a theme, but it seems more like a tic.

After each monologue, captions inscribed on a screen tell us on just which terrible TV shows the various ladies continued to make their confessions. Lest we miss anything, this information is also read aloud by a deep male voice -- very slowly.

For her last turn Moore portrays a Southern belle who married well, came to Canada and changed her originally Jewish name to Micki Moore. So it's autobiographical, though not, I would guess, very closely. Moore's own career has mainly been as a journalist and broadcaster; she conducts seminars on How to Be a Television Talk- Show Host. (Hey, that might make a show.) Her theatrical experience seems to have been limited, so for her to undertake a solo stage performance might be considered brave. Taking as her subject matter a parade of female narcissists strikes me as foolhardy. Vanity, vanity: All is vanity.
