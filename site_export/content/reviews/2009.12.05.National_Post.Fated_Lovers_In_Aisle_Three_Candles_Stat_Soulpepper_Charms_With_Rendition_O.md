For Parfumerie, Ken MacDonald has designed the most appetizing set ever to adorn a Soulpepper production. It's scrumptious -- truly scrumptious, in fact. Its also businesslike, giving its actors space to manoeuvre and a helpful, believable environment in which to exist. This suits, since Parfumerie is set in a shop, one that mainly dispenses beauty products; and though it is most obviously a romantic comedy, its as much concerned with the day-to-day survival of the store as with the affairs of its employees' hearts. It's sweet, but with a tang.

Morris Panychs witty and charming production is the theatrical definition of a gift that keeps on giving. This, as it happens, is something that Miklos Laszlos play seems to have been doing since its Budapest premiere in 1937. Its author moved to America the following year, making him one among what have been called Hollywood's resident Hungarians." The play, though neglected in its original form, has given birth to two movies ( The Shop around the Corner, You've Got Mail), one musical ( She Loves Me) and one movie musical ( In the Good Old Summertime). Finally, we get to see the play itself, which in this English version by Adam Pettle and Brenda Robins, turns out to be as good as any of its derivatives and better than some.

Its the old Much Ado About Nothing story of two people who are obviously destined to be together but in the meantime cant stand one another. This time they're clerks in the store. He is George, very organized and snippy about it, and she's Rosie, disorganized and defensive about it, hence equally snippy. Both are lonely; both, for more than a year, have been engaged in passionate correspondence with an admirer they intend soon to meet. This, of course, is the old device that Youve Got Mail found to be electronically new again. Its greatly to the author's credit that he doesn't play it for any more suspense than its worth; we guess the truth early on, only a moment before he reveals it. George guesses it, too; and some of the plays delight comes from his attempts to juggle his own knowledge and remorse with Rosie's lack of them.

Part of the cunning is that, for a considerable time, the subordinates' story is itself subordinated to that of their boss. Mr. Hammerschmidt, a hitherto benevolent dictator, is tormented by the ( justified) suspicion that his wife is unfaithful. So he takes it out on his staff, and Joseph Zieglers biting fury at this point, as of a Scrooge whos regressed, is a wonderfully smouldering display. It's all show of course; and Ziegler, with a great actor's ability to convey volumes in a moment, can break your heart with the way he walks towards a telephone, girding himself for bad news. This good father -- which is what old man Hammerschmidt turns out metaphorically to be -- is a performance to rank with his bad dad in A Whistle in the Dark.

His drama reaches an early peak, but continues as a ground bass to the duet of Oliver Dennis and Patricia Fagan as the mail-matched lovers, neither of them glamourizing or sentimentalizing their characters but each becoming more endearing by the moment. Its an interesting counterpoint, Dennis agonizing as Fagan melts. Their final moment of mutual recognition is a beautiful contrivance, the more satisfying because of the transparency with which its engineered.

If I wanted to hunt for faults, I could say that Michael Simpson's anxious senior clerk, who never saw a troubled water he didn't want to oil, could afford a little variation in his harried benevolence; and that Jeff Lillicos ultra-keen messenger boy could tone down some of his exuberance, especially after he too gets a chance to boss someone around. But these are minor blemishes on performances that bring their characters to vibrant life. Lillico vibrates with especial virtuosity, and his development as Zieglers surrogate son is one of the show's treats. Kevin Bundy delivers a suave outline of the establishment's resident cad and Maev Beaty an entertaining one of its aspiring flirt, both roles that get abruptly cut off when apparently approaching their prime. She Loves Me did rather better by them by giving each a showpiece farewell number; in general though, one of the joys of encountering the play lies in discovering how closely the musical, which is one of the best, clings to its source, and how right that was. Several of the staff double as customers, a role in which Beaty is especially sharp. The retail routine hums with life, and also with taste; Panychs staging has all of his choreographic inventiveness and none of his excess. It also has a lovely tone; I never knew he had such beatitude in him, though, on reflection, I should have guessed. Brenda Robins plays the stores cashier, who appears to be keeping an eagle eye from behind her register on both the day's takings and her own script, and William Webster turns up in three roles, among them a solicitous private detective who, after destroying his employer's happiness, does everything but wish him a merry Christmas. I should have said that the play takes place during the holiday build-up, thereby adding to the stresses on all concerned. And of course, if Soulpepper is looking for an ideal alternative to its bi-annual revival of A Christmas Carol, here it is.
