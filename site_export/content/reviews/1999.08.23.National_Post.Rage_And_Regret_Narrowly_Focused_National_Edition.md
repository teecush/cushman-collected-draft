Anton Chekhov's Uncle Vanya may be the most frightening play ever written. True, nobody in it is killed or blinded or driven mad, and the title character's attempts at murder and suicide are fumbling, farcical failures. That is what makes it so harrowing. The dramas involved are not the huge ones that most people get to contemplate only from a distance but the small, encroaching agonies that eventually impale us all.

Each of Chekhov's major plays depicts this world of rage and regret. In Vanya, which has a smaller cast and a narrower canvas than the others, the focus is at its most intense. Living in a huge house in the vast and echoing Russian countryside, the characters are engulfed by space. Their chief enemy, though, and the play's principal villain, is time.

Uncle Vanya himself wakes up in his 40s to the realization that he has wasted his life. "I could," he cries out, "have been a Schopenhauer, a Dostoevsky." Obviously he couldn't (and it's typical of Chekhov that Vanya realizes the absurdity, as soon as the words are out of his mouth); still, it's true that there were opportunities in his past that he might have seized.

He might, for example, have proposed to the beautiful Yelena, who is now the second wife of Vanya's ancient brother-in-law the Professor, a selfish fraud to the management of whose estates Vanya and his niece Sonya have devoted their existence. Sonya, who is self- consciously plain, loves Astrov, the hard-working and hard-drinking local doctor. Astrov has a passion for forest preservation; apart from that he, like Vanya, only has eyes for the Professor's wife.

Jealousy and frustration drive Vanya to the attempted shooting of the Professor, whom he sees as having ruined his life. He misses, twice: a scene that, above any other in drama, is capable of provoking laughter and tears simultaneously.

It takes a great production to scale those tragi-comic heights, and the Shaw Festival's version -- directed by Ian Prinsloo, of Theatre Calgary -- is not that. It has, nevertheless, a respectable amount to offer. It employs a lucid translation, by John Murrell, that allows the characters to talk to one another as if they had some reasonable expectation of receiving an answer. It maintains a very even tone, with its characters, in the famous Thoreau phrase, all leading lives of quiet desperation.

An even tone, though, inevitably results in an uneven effect, since Chekhov's characters are in fact extremely volatile. This has nothing to do with their being Russian, despite the recurrent masochistic moans from English-speaking critics that our actors are incapable of interpreting the Slavic temperament. (I have seen the Moscow Arts Theatre, admittedly well past its glory days, playing Chekhov; and its productions were just the same as ours, only less good.) It has everything to do with their being human.

Michael Ball's Vanya may be the first opportunity this actor has had at the Shaw not to adopt an English accent. This frees him from his usual teddy-bear persona to present an acerbic, envious, liquorous uncle, deeper into his vodka that any other I have seen. At the end he subsides movingly into the realization that his life is every bit as awful as he had suspected it was.

But Vanya should be manic as well as depressive, and his break- out here is too measured to be credible. The production's deliberate sloth at this point infects everyone, even Bernard Behrens' generally admirable Professor, who seems to be running for his life in slow motion while screaming for assistance as if it were a second glass of tea.

Jim Mezon's Astrov had me likewise split between respect for the actor and disbelief in the character. He is very fine when he expounds his ecological ideals to Yelena, his burning enthusiasm progressively undercut by her obvious indifference. But he seems too soft-grained for the life he leads and the things he says; Astrov is as much a failure as Vanya, but one who has seen more of the world and in whom self-pity has been replaced by a corrosive cynicism.

Yelena, meanwhile, is afraid of emotion; that doesn't necessarily make her shallow but that is how Philippa Domville plays her. Shauna Black similarly takes Sonya too much at her own wallflower evaluation, though her final speech on the relentless future that lies before her has its usual searing effect.

The Professor, who has spent a comfortable existence "writing about things that intelligent people have known all along and the stupid ones aren't interested in anyway" (Chekhov's sobering verdict on critics), departs from the estate preaching the sobering virtues of work -- leaving behind those who really know what work is and are being both sustained and destroyed by it.

There are useful performances by Jillian Cook as Vanya's purblind mother and Blair Williams as an ineffectual neighbour, both of them playing convincingly above their own ages. Amanda Smith, however, as the even older nurse, can manage neither the age nor anything else about the character, which is unfortunate since the nanny's undeviating serenity is central to the atmosphere; part merciful, part cruel, like time itself.

The production's overall buttoned-down effect may partly be due to the production occupying the festival's smallest stage, though nothing can excuse the interpolation of three Tom Waits songs, whose appearance is largely inexplicable and wholly demeaning.
