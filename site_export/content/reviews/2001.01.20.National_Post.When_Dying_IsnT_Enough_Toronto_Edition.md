BURYING MICHAEL

Buddies in Bad Times

---

Michael was dead, to begin with. He died in Winnipeg, of AIDS. But he had expressed a wish to be buried in New Mexico; so Max, his lover, puts the body in the back of his car and sets off on the road with it, without telling anyone.

Max's earlier partner, a drag-queen named Albert, guesses what has happened and sets off in pursuit. Max is helped across the U.S. border by Frank, a young truck-stop attendant who deals with any kind of trouble by orally servicing the bearer. The car Max and Frank are driving is hijacked by Billy, a gunman who turns out to be harmless. The trio pick up Albert, who by now has been reduced to hitch-hiking; and they all live happily ever after.

Max, who has always led a pretty inhibited life in Canada, decides that this new mobile existence is what he really needs. He comes to realize that it is also what Michael wanted for him, and is probably why he made his deathbed request in the first place. My paraphrasing of the first line of A Christmas Carol was just a flippant way of getting this review started, but it now occurs to me that Ken Brand, the playwright, may indeed be offering a new post- seasonal version of the tale, with Max as a nicer Scrooge and the three other major characters serving, through their blithe disregard of approved social rules and roles, as the alternative-lifestyle equivalents of Dickens' cautionary ghosts.

Anyway, it is something to think about while you're watching the play. There isn't much else to occupy your mind.

About the most attractive features of the evening are the two road maps that hang throughout on the back wall of the set: one centering on Winnipeg, the other on Albuquerque and Santa Fe. A lot of us have fantasies of driving across America, and New Mexico seems as good a destination as any, even if we might not want to start from Manitoba.

Movies, though, are better placed than plays to bring the dream to life. The show feels amateurish, dependent on arbitrary bursts of energy from the actors. Terrence Bryant gives a credible performance as Max, casual and unforced but still focused; and he has been given some wryly contemplative speeches about his probable future in Winnipeg as "an AIDS widow." He does seem to carry a life with him.

Jason Jazrawy as Frank is a stagier figure but has plenty of bounce. The two are onstage together a lot, but though acting well individually, they never cohere; here and elsewhere, David Oiye's production flows about as well as a river of glue.

David Macniven makes awkward cameo appearances as some variously venal law-enforcement officers, before settling more happily into the role of Billy, the hold-up artist. Brian Sexsmith as Albert bears the bulk of the play's humour, and it seems a heavy burden. There is some momentary suspense in trying to work out whether he is giving an unconvincing performance of a woman or an unconvincing performance of a man dressed as a woman, but that of course is soon cleared up; he goes on to display all the warmth and humanity of Dame Edna Everage and none of the dexterity. He, too, improves as the play goes on; and he has the one moment that, catching me off- guard, made me laugh.

The main problem, though, is not these on-stage characters but the off-stage one. We are told nothing about the late Michael, except that Max loved him, and yet his name keeps coming up as if it were some kind of talisman.

Do I have to say this again? In the real world, a death from AIDS is horrible, though there are other equally horrific ways of dying. In the theatre it is, to put it brutally, only as good as the person who's dying it. It does not automatically bestow moral authority.

Since Michael doesn't exist dramatically, his plans for Max's future welfare are of minimal interest. And Max's own spiritual journey, which is what the play is meant to be about, is a subject raised and dropped as and when the author pleases.

If the other characters are meant to learn from him as he from them, that's even less substantial. As the story of a perilous journey, the play is flimsy, because there's no threat: little in the writing, less in the production. I suppose it preaches all- round tolerance; that isn't much to get for your time, but sure: God bless them. Every one.
