THE SONG

Theatre Passe Muraille, Toronto

Nestled demurely among the more thunderous books of the Old Testament is one of the greatest pieces of erotica ever written -- maybe the most beautiful of all if you get the right translation. The Song of Solomon is, among other things, a testimony to the inclusiveness of the Bible as a record of human experience. It isn't a story about sex, it is sex.

The Song -- which comes to us, roughly speaking, as the play of the book -- reflects its source: It isnt much on narrative, but its a sensuous feast. It gains its effects with rigorous economy from the first moment.

All of us, at some time or another, have struggled to put on a recalcitrant sweater. Alon Nashman, one of the show's two actors, takes this simple ordeal and makes a spectacular ritual of it.

Armholes appear where the head wants to go and vice versa; the garment, instead of draping itself snugly around the body, insists on emerging skewed, and its victim -- though he actually owns the thing, for God's sake -- has to start all over again.

This is several minutes of silent comedy denoting frustration that is also, through sheer skill in execution, beautiful. I should mention that the actor performs the entire routine while perched precariously on a narrow strip of platform that represents the only stretch of level ground on the stage. Between it and the audience stretches a steeply tilted floor, criss-crossed by a lattice of wooden slats. The whole design looks like an ingenious machine for inflicting cruelty on actors.

Nashman plays a character called Alan, and he is also the artistic director of Theaturtle, the company producing the show. It was written and directed, however, by Adam Nashman. (Concentrate.) Alan is an accountant cast into desolate confusion by the death of his wife, Rachel, two years earlier. The Song of Solomon is a catalogue of all the delights, spiritual as well as physical, of which he is now deprived.

Alan thinks and dreams of Solomon himself, and of Rabbi Akiva, the ancient talmudic scholar responsible for establishing the Song in the biblical canon. Akiva himself, it seems, had a wife named Rachel (the name game is compulsive, in and around this show) and had to endure long absences from her, while commuting between Judea and Rome.

We see him attracting the attention of the Roman governors wife, who first tries to seduce him then settles for Bible classes instead. All I see, she says of the Song, "is a sexy little poem." Akiva has a reply to this, but its less concise and harder to remember.

`All i see is a sexy little poem'

The play, apart from the actual quotations, which are extensive, is a lot less compelling verbally than it is visually, and the actors can't always kick it into life. It doesnt help that the story of Akiva, which is meant to be subsidiary, has more going for it dramatically than Alans psychological progress, which it's supposed to illuminate. The play's rather meagre present tense has Alan striving to address a number-crunchers conference in Banff, and finding it understandably hard going.

One scene has real fireworks: Alan on the line to a government office trying belatedly to track down some information about his dead wife. The acting Nashman performs is a double balancing-act, literally and metaphorically at the end of his tether. He is grappling with bureaucracy, generally a hopeless battle, and in every sense he is losing it.

He is also stretched up, down, and across the acting area, holding on to a phone with a very long lead while grasping for a glass of wine that, however he contorts himself, remains stubbornly beyond his reach.

Deborah Hay is the other half of the cast, playing Solomon, all the Rachels, and the governors wife, and is especially enticing in the latter role.

Both players, as they intrepidly climb the stage, slide merrily down it, hang upside down from it, or disport themselves at languorous diagonals, pay implicit tribute to the possibilities of the human body and the human imagination -- which presumably is their point even as it was Solomon's.

(There is a third presence: a mute male double to facilitate some quick changes from Alan to Akiva and back. Even allowing for his assistance, they are amazing.) By the end, Alan has progressed so far that he can take off his sweater in one graceful action. That's a climax; and I'm not kidding.
