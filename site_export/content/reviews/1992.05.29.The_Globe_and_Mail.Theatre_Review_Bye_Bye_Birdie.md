THEATRE REVIEW BYE BYE BIRDIE

Friday, May 29, 1992

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Toronto ONT -- WRITTEN BY MICHAEL STEWART, CHARLES STROUSE AND LEE ADAMS Directed by Gene Saks Starring Tommy Tune At OKeefe Centre Rating: Back in 1960, Bye Bye Birdie had a lot going for it, but time and a less than rigorous revival have stolen some of its charm Musical feeling its age BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Toronto HEY says Tommy Tune, favouring the audience with a winning smile that must have been honed by a year of touring, "its 1959." He was responding to a barrage of hisses, occasioned when the guy he plays, a turning worm, starts screaming at his girlfriend that a woman's role is to obey. The barracking was as idiotic as such displays usually are. For one thing, the character at this point is plainly seen to be ridiculous, and, for another, the show is full of sexist assumptions which the same house had swallowed with gurgles of enjoyment. Subtlety was wasted on them; so, apparently, was cartoon blatancy. Tune's reminder that the musical - first seen in 1960 - is a period piece, was apposite and charming. It also raised a lot of questions.

Bye Bye Birdie has two story lines. One concerns Conrad Birdie, a rock n roll idol who, Elvis-like, is about to be drafted. Teen-aged girls react to him as adoringly and hysterically as you would expect. But though the show is named after Conrad, more stage-time is given to his manager Albert, the Tune role here, and to Rose his secretaryfiancee who is far more competent than he is but nevertheless hangs around devotedly, carries his bags (a running gag), and waits patiently for him to marry her. Which, at the happy fade-out, he does.

Obviously the scenarios converge. To secure maximum hype from Conrad's exit (and therefore more money for Albert to marry her on) Rose dreams up a gimmick: have Birdie bestow One Last Kiss, duly publicized, on a random fan. The lot falls on Kim McAfee, a bobbysoxer from Sweet Apple, Ohio (pop. minuscule) and to Sweet Apple from the big apple the show goes. There, just enough complications ensue to keep things ticking over till curtain call.

Back in 1960 the show had much going for it, of which some still remains. But a lot has departed, some of it chargeable to time and some to this revival. Michael Stewart's book is slick, and sometimes funny, but nobody could call it rigorous. Developments are arbitrary, climaxes do not satisfy, and even within the separate plots characters wander off and do their own thing. Rose is especially good at this. I saw the original production when it came to London; and my memory is of two distinct entertainments. There was the show, and there was Chita Rivera, gloriously and repeatedly stopping it. Maybe in New York, where Rivera was co-starred with Dick Van Dyke, the balance was better. This time round it has tilted the other way.

This Birdie is being promoted as Tunes show, and he is certainly its most luminous presence: acting fair, singing excellent, hoofing unrivalled. But his role does not give him much scope, and he is wrong for some of what it does give. Albert is a comic neurotic, too nervous even to take a pill, and oppressed by a stock Jewish mother raised to a higher power. Marilyn Cooper plays her as the ultimate in doleful emotional blackmail (composite sample line: dont bother to drive me to my funeral, I'll walk") and, though any actress would play her that way, Cooper does it especially well. Tune's enormous height might theoretically make him gangling and unsure; but moving as confidently as he does, in a wardrobe that looks to have eaten half the budget, it actually makes him seem impregnable. Lenora Nemetzs Rose is all-round competent (and vocally a dead ringer for Rivera) but, whether vamping or vulnerable, she poses no threat.

Conrad himself is curiously underwritten; this was Broadways first encounter with a pop music it thought would go away, and there is nobody inside the sexy-sullen mask. Marc Kudisch wears it well, though. Susan Egan as Kim and Steve Zahn as her unsteady steady do equally good by the youth of Sweet Apple. Their elders are more of a problem, given sit-com sensibilities that Gene Saks's production seems unsure whether to milk or mock. Here, admittedly, it reflects the script, too much of the 50s to get a satirical bead on it. But that is what Bye Bye Birdie now needs: a stylish sense of period. (Hey, it's 1959). It could also do with a brighter tone and a less sluggish pace, from the overture on.

And, reaching back 30 years, I miss Gower Champions champion staging. The shriners ballet, involving Rose and a stageful of bashfully predatory men in fezzes, is much as I remember it though it means less without a teasing presence at its centre. The terrific opening number An English Teacher - that's what Rose thinks Albert should be - and its successor The Telephone Hour have both been truncated, depriving them of shape, sense and impact. Put on a Happy Face no longer has Albert going head to head with a pair of defiantly mournful moppets: a pity since partnering someone a fraction his size is one of the things that Tune (cf. My One and Only and the film of Hello Dolly) has always done best.

I don't drop those song titles idly. Anyone who knows them (plus Kids, A Lot of Livin to Do, Rosie and others) will recognize that what remains undimmed about the show is Charles Strouses music. It is one of the best- humoured scores I know, full of sweetness and juice. Lee Adams's lyrics are perky, too, though they fall apart when they go for sustained satire. Two new numbers, inserted to let Tune and Cooper strut their respective stuff, strike me as inferior; but they would, wouldn't they? Bye Bye Birdie plays until Sunday.
