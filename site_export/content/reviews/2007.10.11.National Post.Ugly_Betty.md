Watching the first two episodes of Ugly Bettys second season, I started wondering about categories. These are important in television, because without them no one would know where to send the awards. But they matter to viewers as well. Great art may confound expectations, but that's hardly the issue here. Most of TV is entertainment designed to fit into a box: literally and metaphorically. We like to know what kind of rise it is trying to get out of us.

I think Ugly Betty is a holy horror, but it has won plenty of prizes. And most of them have been for comedy. This is strange, because it doesn't have any laughs. Its title character, a perky but determinedly unglamorous young Mexican who lands a job as editorial assistant at a New York fashion magazine, is accident-prone; America Ferrara, who plays her, can take an endearing pratfall, and regularly does. But the humorous potential in that is limited, and besides, its offset by the characters all-around pluckiness in the face of a world dead set against her. She has to watch her boss's back while a perfidious and amoral rival plots to unseat him from the editorial chair, though not from the wheelchair to which he's currently been consigned. Shes lost her boyfriend to some promiscuous schemer (not the same one) though at close of play last week she seemed about to get him back. She was also, however, being blackmailed; her dad is stranded in the old country without a U.S. visa, and she can get him fast-tracked back if she stoops to a bit of personal betrayal. Agony. This last wrinkle might be a lost shard from some cop or spy or terrorist drama. Looked at another way, its like a daytime soap with production values. Or The Devil Wears Prada going on forever.

If it's comedy, it can only be so in the default sense: That is, that it's drama so weightless that we refuse to believe that anybody meant it seriously. The other elements include a transsexual amnesiac, an office flunky who has issues over her long-lost father and her all-too-present mangy dog and a very elegant convicted murderess on the run. Some of this is played for pathos, some for absurdity and some -- most -- for whatevers nearest to hand. Its overheated without ever coming to the boil.

Somewhere on Ugly Betty's trophy-shelf sits a Teen Choice Award for Best Villain, given to Vanessa Williams. There's specialization for you. And the lady has had to work hard for it. She plays Wilhemina, the ambitious bitch-goddess (her middle name might be Success) who schemes to marry the hapless editors proprietor- father and is making out with a hunky bodyguard. Thats as well as putting Betty through the wringer, though she's not above pretending to make common cause with her. ("I am black, you're Mexican.") When things threaten to go wrong for Wilhemina, we glimpse the flames of hell burning in the fireplace behind her; she then proceeds to the office roof where she takes out her rage by smashing a row of mannequins. For all that, she isnt really scary. The show wouldnt let her be.

Its survival is obviously due to something. It has a basic knack of narrative pacing, and it has Ferrara, who is the closest thing on it to three-dimensional. But the ugly duckling defying the spoiled beauties always strikes a chord and Ferrara too has won a Teen Choice Award -- for Breakout. This may be true in an ethnic as well as a personal sense; she's a rare Hispanic heroine, though this is hardly likely to count in Canada as it does in the States. And going from ethnics to ethics, she's had a scene in which she indignantly decries the idea of a beauty-shoot built around victims. This did have a comic build to it as well as a moral one. It was nicely off- hand. But it stood pretty much alone.

The wily Wilhemina has an assistant called Marc: a gay stereotype- -placed within the fashion world, hes a double stereotype without the saving grace of wit. The nearest hes come, after being attacked by a fake nun, is I don't think I'll ever be able to watch The Sound of Music again. Hes unscrupulous and sycophantic but, when he accidentally does something nice, hes capable of saying "I'm a good person -- no, I'm a great person", crowing like Peter Pan and meaning it. This is the kind of having-it-both-ways that has degraded the once noble concept of irony. I think Marc may have written Ugly Betty.
