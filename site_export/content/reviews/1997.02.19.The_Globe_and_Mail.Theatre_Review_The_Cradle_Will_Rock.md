THE CRADLE WILL ROCK

THEATRE REVIEW

THE CRADLE WILL ROCK

Wednesday, February 19, 1997

Directed by David Oiye

Rating: **½

Cartoon characters swarm cradle

Uneven performances detract from historic

musical of wit, passion and beauty.

Special to The Globe and Mail


  


THE mystique of The Cradle Will Rock has outdistanced the show itself. A political musical, allegorical in style and radical in content, its 1937 premiere passed into legend when its sponsors, the Federal Theatre Project, got nervous and cancelled the first night. Abetted by the performing unions, they locked the production out of its New York theatre. The performers, escorted by an ever-increasing audience, then marched through town to another house where the show went on: the author-composer Marc Blitzstein playing his score from the stage and the actors, officially forbidden to join him there, getting up and speaking or singing their lines from the auditorium. It didn't hurt the legend any that the director was a young prodigy named Orson Welles.

A decade ago Welles's assistant John Houseman was enlisted to tell the story as prologue to a notable revival in New York and London. Lacking Mr. Houseman's services, Equity Showcase preface their production with a brisk re-enactment of the events, or at least the discussions preceeding them; the really exciting bit -- the march -- is not readily stageable. The actor playing Orson (Steve Ross) manages a convincing look of the chubby boy-genius; the show's MD (Alan Moon) sits at the piano and announces the scenes just as Biltzstein did; and the first character, a moll called Moll, duly announces herself from the house. She plays her opening scene, with a predatory john and an even more predatory cop, at the front of the stalls. This episode is itself a kind of prologue; when the main action starts, the company gets up on the stage and -- as in Olivier's Henry V -- some kind of magical, seamless change in convention is deemed to have taken place.

But the preliminaries are either too much or not enough. Their main practical effect is to start the show 10 minutes later than advertised. There were great ironies attending the birth of Cradle ; a ferociously anticapitalist, pro-union show was nearly aborted by unions and a government agency, caving in to right-wing pressure: exactly the kind of spinelessness for which the piece itself arraigns the petit bourgeoisie. To explore all this might require the kind of wholesale rewriting favoured by Blitzstein's idol, Brecht. Equity Showcase, once past the starting gate, play the text straight.

It can stand the exposure. Frankly dated and frankly agitprop, it remains a work of wit, passion and beauty. (Besides, it's all true.) We are in Steeltown, fief of the all-powerful mill owner Mister Mister. Helping him run the place are toadying respresentatives of the church, the press, the arts, the universities and medecine; all of them co-opted on to Mister Mister's Liberty Committee, formed to opose the increasingly strident demands of organized labour. The latter are represented by the charming and incorruptible union organizer Larry Foreman. A series of vignettes shows how each of the sycophants have sold out, while the more innnocent prostitute Moll watches in amazement. If the show's view of union power now seems starry-eyed ( Larry, with his folksy wisdom and constant invocations of his off-stage Aunt Jessie, is very hard to take) it still looks better than the alternative.

David Oiye's production is smartly and inventively staged, stylishly constumed and unevenly performed. A large ensemble (only very rich or very poor theatres can afford to do this piece) is paraded in telling formations. Blitzstein had a gift for musical pastiche, and his Croon Spoon and Honolulu for Mister Mister's awful spoiled children are spot-on parodies of thirties pop archetypes. Jan Johanson's choreography accentuates the fun, laying on a swaying Hawaiin chorus of indetermninate gender. Similarly entertaining is the bitching duet of Dauber the painter and Yashua the violinist, a matched pair of parasites, biting the hand that feeds them for as long as they're safely begind its back, if you follow me. ("Oh, there's something so damned low about the rich . . . What can they do? Support you!") Blitzstein reserved his most withering fire for his own kind, and you could not accuse the show of preaching to the converted. Its original audience would have been the Liberty Committee and their friends: Mrs. Mister is a great, if simpering, patron of the arts.

Jay Turvey cuts a slick figure as Editor Daily, the one lackey to be allowed a modicum of professional integrity, and Robert Clarke finds the light and shade in Dr. Specialist, the only one with scruples. The others are cartoons, executed with insufficient force. The glaring lack is in Mister Mister himself (Andrew Scorer) who has no power and no presence. Gleamingly bald-headed, he resembles that even more trenchantly named tycoon Daddy Warbucks, and seems, unfortunately, even less of a threat. Among the goodies: Stefanie Roth carefully rescues Moll from the status of downtrodden symbol; her wistfully tough lament Nickel Under the Foot is touching, but not as piercing as it might be; he same goes for the threatening title song, an assault on complacency which, as delivered by Andrew Pifko's pugnacious punk Larry, sounds more like a pious hope than a prophecy. Nor does the angriest song in the score, Joe Worker Gets Gypped , realize its full devastating potential.

They still work, though; Blitzstein spent the rest of his career writing haunting scores for commercial projects that turned out to be uncommercial, but his music grows on you. His lyrics can be clumsy -- some of his scansion sets the teeth on edge -- but his ideas come through, sometimes to scorching effect. Amusingly, he was to achieve his greatest success solely as a lyricist, translating The Threepenny Opera ; his are the English words of Mack the Knife we've been singing since the fifties. It's fitting, because Cradle had actually been more Brechtian than anything Brecht and Weill themselves ever turned out. The first expressionist musical, its abstraction is what makes it live today; the techniques accentuate the show's period feel, but also cut through it. The Cradle still rocks.

At Harbourfront's Studio Theatre, York Quay, until Feb. 22.
