WALK RIGHT UP; SHADOWS

Studio Theatre, Stratford, Ont.

The double bills of new plays that have opened Stratford's new Studio Theatre have all fallen into the same pattern. Before the intermission we have had a more-or-less naturalistic, usually domestic issue-drama of the type of which we all profess to be thoroughly tired. It has invariably been followed by a free- wheeling experimental piece, the kind of boundary-bursting piece we all theoretically crave. And the tame old-fashioned offering has come out ahead every time.

That is certainly the case with the third and last of the season's two-headers. It starts with Walk Right Up, which is by a new playwright, Celia McBride, and which would be easy to dismiss as yet another illness play with a pat ending. It concerns Millar, who has had two strokes and is confined to a wheelchair, and his wife, Lily, an overpoweringly affectionate -- hence, controlling -- woman, who is now in the initial stages of Alzheimer's. These two have sinned against nature (certainly against credibility) by naming their son Brilliant and one of their daughters Poet. This must have been Lily's idea; she was once a writer, and one hopes that her books were less flowery than she is.

Poet has not become a poet. In fact, she is an actress, and has put her career on hold to look after her parents. She seems to have been something of a martyr about it, which may be why they have renamed her Pill. Anyway, she now has a movie job, and has handed over her caregiving role to her sister, who doesn't want it. The sister, whose name is Ella (the font of inspiration seems to have dried up when she was christened), in turn calls in Brilliant, a feckless pothead who initially evinces great enthusiasm for the task but who quits the house the moment somebody upsets him. Brilliant was once an actor but has long since given up the struggle, along with all other struggles. Ella -- we learn from a single line -- has a design business to run.

As you'd expect, much of the play is given over to recrimination. But it's barbed, focused recrimination, and McBride is very adroit at showing the sins of the parents reproducing themselves in the children, especially in a set-piece scene at the dinner table. The daughters are as aggressively protective as the mother, though Ella tries hard not to be -- self-consciously hard, in fact. As for the men, Brilliant's irresponsibility is itself a form of control. Millar, immobile and often silent, is the strongest person in the play, but his condition means this fact keeps taking us by surprise. His family treats him with well-meaning condescension and so do we; implicating us in this way is the author's smartest and most resonant device.

In Michael Shamata's production, Paul Soles admirably delivers the father's slurred speech and unimpaired feelings. Elizabeth Shepherd does finely by the intimations of his wife's crack-up, but lays on the smothering aspect too heavily. Kimwun Perehinec's Poet- turned-Pill lives rather too drably up to her second label, but Brenda Robins, with her quality of smiling edge, takes excellent care of Ella, and Damien Atkins (the author of Good Mother, last season's parental stroke play) gives his most incisive performance yet as Brilliant. Like Bereav'd of Light in the previous studio double bill, this play lets itself down with a platitude. At the end, Lily says, unarguably but unnecessarily, that "we all need help." It doesn't exactly match up to "reason not the need."

All the same, it was probably no accident Stratford opened this play on the same day as King Lear. It also has links to its companion piece, Shadows, which again shows us a group of artists, actual or potential, at the table, this time in the final stages of a dinner party. The host is Ben, a successful playwright, and the others seem to be his colleagues and acolytes. The play -- as its author, the late Timothy Findley, must have known -- is the acme of self- indulgence; unfortunately, it doesn't have the requisite audacity. It stops short at cheek.

Ben, a viperish piece of work, springs a party game; somebody has to reveal a dark secret that may or may not be true. Up to this point, not one of the characters has said or done a believable thing, and the game is incredible, too. But the author has a let- out; they're all playing another game. In fact, they're all would- be playwrights trying out ideas. This is offered as a surprise, and it is, but I'm not spoiling anything by revealing it. (Knowing it may even make the early stages seem better.) The winner will have his or her play performed on the main Festival stage; this is not, to be fair, the biggest laugh in the play but it is the most rueful. (Only one new play has ever been seen on that stage, and that was in 1961.)

Ben then proceeds to involve the audience. He orders the lights raised and asks a spectator to sip from one of the dinner glasses to see whether it contains real wine. On the first night, the chosen victim -- a fellow critic -- refused it as if it were a poisoned chalice, which in a way it was. His neighbour -- another fellow critic -- had fewer qualms and tossed off an unexpected response, with which Brent Carver, playing Ben, coped, I must say, admirably.

Anyway, it doesn't really prove anything. An author can play as many tricks as he chooses, and this play goes on playing them. It, too, ends with a cliche, though one spun out to greater length: a speech about how drama is composed of lies but that those lies may sometimes hit a nerve of truth. Undoubtedly: but this one never does, on any of the levels it keeps insisting on revealing. After so much smugness, this humility comes too late. And -- to reiterate a point made already this season -- it overrates the audience's fascination with the author's problems. The fact that all the characters are theatricals only makes it seem that much more incestuous.

The only convincing line anyone utters in this play is to the effect that Ben is a bastard, and since we have long lost track of who the speaker is or is pretending to be, or indeed of who Ben is or is pretending to be, it doesn't count for much. At different times, the characters invoke, by name, Noel Coward's The Vortex and David Mamet's Oleanna. Audiences are as likely to think first of Albee's Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf and later of Pirandello's -- well, anything really. They are not likely to think anything new has been added.

Dennis Garnhum probably does as well by the play as any director could, and Carver sports a new high-pitched voice with which he hits an appropriate note of sour mischief. He heads the bluest of blue- chip casts, including Robins (not as good as in the preceding play), Perehinec (much better than in the preceding play), Chick Reid, Karen Robinson, Gordon Rand and Stephen Ouimette, who is spectacularly wasted.
