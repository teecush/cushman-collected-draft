THEATRE REVIEW TABU

Friday, March 15, 1991

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Toronto ONT -- Created by The Canadian Stage Hour Company Starring David Findlay, Alisa Palmer, Shaun Phillips and Dawn Roach At The Stage Upstairs, 26 Berkeley St., Toronto The show Tabu, which is a lot more didactic and less exploratory than it would like to think, moves quickly and skillfully but in no direction Of Flowers and sexual stigma BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Toronto THE Hour Company's Tabu is billed as "a celebration of sexuality." It is a collective product, and one thing the collaborators discovered in their research was that in 1832 a Canadian infantryman was hanged for making love with another man. The victim's rank was private, and his name was Flowers. Private Flowers. The words fairly pulsate with imagistic possibilities.

The Flowers story is intercut with an invented modern one. Two young women are surprised in one another's arms. One of them, called Garten, has abuse hurled at her by her brother and is more gently intimidated by her boyfriend. When they call her a lesbian, she bridles, on one of three possible grounds: (a) the term is accurate but the straight world has made it sound derogatory; (b) she is actually bisexual; (c) her sexuality is her own business, and to slap any kind of label on it is to diminish her as an individual.

The evidence points to the third option. Private Flowers proclaimed on the gallows that his body was his own to do with as he liked, and the memory of his defiance in the extreme gives Garten the strength to proclaim her own. That seems to be the idea. Whether it is given concrete embodiment is another question. The show moves quickly and skillfully, but in no direction. Garten's words ("I'm not a lesbian") are the same at the end as at the beginning. Ritual repetition in the theatre generally does more for the actors than for the audience. We have not a character's journey of discovery but a parable proving that she was right all along. The show is a lot more didactic and less exploratory than it would like to think.

There are four actors - David Findlay, Alisa Palmer, Shaun Phillips, Dawn Roach - and two musicians, and the standards of acting, singing and miming are high. The use of a row of bunks to evoke the army life will win no prizes for originality, but it is done with great efficiency and dispatch. In fact, not just the acting but the writing of the historical sections is excellent. There is no individual author's credit, but whoever invented the words has done an impressive job. We are informed that authentic records of the Flowers case are non-existent. The gap is filled with period dialogue that rings true without pedagogy, and is executed with chilling economy. As was Private Flowers himself, who never had a trial.

The Hour Company will have it that he was hanged to save the skin of his partner, who was also his officer. I doubt if there is any direct evidence of this, but it plays plausibly. It does, however, make the peer-group pressures faced by the play's contemporary gays look mild in comparison; and they are the only pressures we are shown. I know we are meant to extrapolate and start thinking in terms of society and other evils, but on the theatrical evidence, why should we? The past gets careful writing and performance; the present gets slogans.

It gets a pretty good song as well: soft rock, with a nifty lyric, including the memorable couplet "when I'm in a crowded room" surrounded by the baby boom." We also hear, by way of warm-up, the old Eartha Kitt record, I Wanna Be Evil, arch but still amusing. At the end of the show proper, the company, in accordance with what are apparently their immemorial principles, hold an audience discussion, not even pausing for a moment to allow those who prefer the base role of consumer to make a discreet exit. It was then that I recalled the darkest step on Eartha's downward path: "And in the theatre I want to change my seat" just so I can step on everybody's feet." None of us, though, had the guts. Shaw had the right phrase for it: intimidated, that's what we were, by middle-class morality.
