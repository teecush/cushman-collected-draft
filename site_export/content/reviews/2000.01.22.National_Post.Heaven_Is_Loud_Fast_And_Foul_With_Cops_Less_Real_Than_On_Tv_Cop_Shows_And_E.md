HEAVEN

Canadian Stage Company

Stumbling out of Heaven, as befits a critic, I ran into my old friend Phyllis Stein. I was surprised to see her there. George F. Walker's new play is presented at Canadian Stages down-market premises on Berkeley Street, and Phyllis, who is a regular theatregoer and a CSC subscriber, is generally believed to confine her attendance to glossier offerings at the St. Lawrence Centre.

Were you shocked?" I asked her.

"No she said, sounding shocked that I thought she might be. Should I have been?"

"Well, I explained weakly. The management expected you to walk out. Thereve been articles in the paper about it. Previews. This is a dark personal vision, and it's not for the likes of you. There's even a sign in the lobby. It says that at this performance of Heaven there will be gun shots, and four-letter words, and sex, and violence, and strobe-lights, and all that jazz. By the way," I added, spotting a tempting tangent to go off at. "What do they mean, at this performance? Does it mean that tomorrow night, theyll be doing a cleaned-up version, and all this stuff was just put in on the press night to titillate the likes of us? Well, the likes of me, anyway.

She requested me not to be flippant. I said Id try but I couldn't promise.

I saw that sign" she said. "I thought 'um, everything I like.

And did you?"

Phyllis pondered. "Not really. I mean, it was all there. Well, maybe not the sex. People talked about it but they didnt do it. In fact, they talked about not doing it. They shouted about not doing it. They shouted about everything. Maybe there should have been a sign in the lobby about that.

It bothered you?"

"Well, yes it did. There's this first scene, and there are these two guys. One's a cop, and hes mad because his partner has committed suicide, after being sent down for killing a suspect: a black suspect. And the other guy is a sort of civil-rights lawyer, and he was responsible for the dead cop being convicted. And theyre both very angry, and every other word is fuck.

Maybe," I suggested, "that's understandable in the circumstances.'

Yes" said Phyllis, thoughtfully. "I realize that. But then theres this next scene, and theres this teenage girl, and she's trying to learn to juggle, and she keeps dropping the balls, and every time she does, she says 'fuck too. Well, yes, she would; I mean, I would too if it were me. I know you think Ive led a sheltered life but you should come around some time. And it's just a word, just a collection of vowels and consonants. But it does get monotonous. Especially when they shout.

That," I explained, trying not to sound patronizing because she gets enough of that, "is the Walker style. Especially when he directs his own plays. They shout, and they go very fast. Critics complain about it; I've complained myself. Now Im not so sure. I found myself thinking, how else could they do those lines? But it does leave them looking exposed. Youve got actors here with good reputations, you've got Ron White as the lawyer, and Wayne Best as the cop, and they're one-dimensional. One of them just bellows, and the other one just snarls.

I know said Phyllis. "I watch cop series on TV, and I see Homicide, and Cracker, and the cops there are so believable. You forget theyre actors."

I know, I said, with a worrying feeling that I was losing my vocation like a character in a George Walker play. "And the writing's usually better than what we're asked to admire in the theatre. But, look, maybe Walker doesnt want you to believe, at least not naturalistically. I mean, these characters have hang-ups like you wouldnt believe. The lawyer's a Catholic who took to fighting racism because of childhood guilt, and he married a Jewish girl to prove how liberal he was, and both the job and the marriage have gone sour.

And the cop turns into a serial killer, and he keeps hitting on the juggling girl, who learns to do it on stilts and a unicycle, which I think is a message of hope, and he also victmizes a young black drug-dealer. And the wife is in love with her rabbi, who must be the least Jewish-looking rabbi on record, don't you agree" (Phyllis agreed), but maybe theyre trying not to stereotype. Anyway, hes not as irritating as the funny rabbi in Patience.

"Being less irritating than a character in a Jason Sherman play is no defence" said Phyllis.

She had me there.

Anyway, I said, "you could probably authenticate all these things individually from the newspapers, but put them together and it's urban hell. And then they get to heaven, and that's urban hell, too. The murderous cop is privileged and the black boy is put into slave-clothes. Its one mans idea of organized religion's idea of what the world should be like. Very quirky. And there's fantasy: a dead woman comes down from heaven on a wire, and the audience goes wild, just like the people they despise would do for the helicopter in Miss Saigon or the chandelier in Phantom. Theres even a full- length song-and-dance number.

"I know, said Phyllis. Cheek to Cheek -- heaven, Im in heaven." (We hummed together, for a blessed moment.). "Wasn't it tacky? But they loved it. Don't these people ever get out? You know, I have a theory. People enjoy song-and-dance in a play just because its in a play. It seems sort of camp. And it puts a stop to all that talking.

Its my job to have the theories," I said stiffly, "but I think I'll steal that one. Anyway, you must have liked Ron White's stand- up routine at the end: God thinks Im funny -- and I think the same about him. It had real timing. Especially coming just after a scene the audience had applauded as if they thought the play was over. Rhythm is not Mr Walker's strongest point. But he does good one- liners.

Oh yes," said Phyllis, "I laughed a few times. And I can see he means it. But is that enough?'

Not really.

"And people say he's our best playwright."

"You know, the terrible thing is, that may just be true."
