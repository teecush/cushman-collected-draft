WHAT ABOUT LUV?

St. Michael's Theatre

So what about What About Luv?

It derives from a Broadway comedy by Murray Schisgal, called simply Luv, a title that sends both me and my spell-check into convulsions. The play, a cute-as-a button number about a trio of New Yorkers swapping partners and aphorisms, was a hit in the '60s and, in its own inbred terms, it worked fairly well It became a terribly constricted and frenetic film, and much later, in 1984, like every successful American play lighter in tone and lower in ambition than Long Day's Journey into Night, it became a musical. Which is what we have here.

Like the play, the musical has three characters and is set entirely on a bridge. (It's closed to traffic, so there are no intrusions by other people, which might be awkward and would certainly be expensive.) Harry Berlin, who might be a neurotic if he wasn't so crazy, comes to the bridge intending to throw himself off. He is prevented by the arrival of Milt Manville, an old college chum he hasn't seen in years.

In temperament and accomplishment Milt and Harry are poles apart; Harry seems to have no purpose in life while Milt is bursting with it. Milt is out to make money and, by his own account, is doing well at it. He also has a rich emotional life; "I'm more in love now" he says "than on the day I got married. Unfortunately my wife won't give me a divorce."

He has come to the bridge intending to throw his wife Ellen off. His encounter with Harry suggests a marginally less drastic expedient; he will introduce Harry to Ellen and the two will fall in- - well, you know.

Surprisingly -- at least it would be surprising if the script in any way resembled real life -- the plan works. Ellen comes to the bridge not, as far as we know, intending to throw anyone off, though she seems capable of it. She is delighted to find a man who can appreciate her powerful mind. Harry is delighted to find a woman who can appreciate him in any way whatever.

Act two dawns. A year has passed, to the day. The three characters reassemble on the bridge and, would you believe, they are not content. Milt's great romance has not worked out. The marriage of Ellen and Harry (this is a world of short divorces and shorter engagements) is a bust. Harry is only happy when hiding from the world with a paper bag over his head; this, and their total lack of any sex life, is driving Ellen crazy.

So Ellen and Milt, whose repertoire of dirty tricks seems somewhat limited, plan to throw Harry off the bridge so that they can be together again. The show's surface is wrinkled by Harry's disturbance at Ellen's leaving him, but there is no suggestion of real pain.

The Toronto production, judging from a single reference to the CN Tower, has attempted to make the show locally relevant, but essentially it still takes place in a nowhere zone of brittle feelings and glib talk that we have agreed to call New York. It isn't much like the real place, if only because its inhabitants seem able to maintain comfortable apartment existences with no visible means of support.

In the '60s, plays with actions this improbable and characters this thin were routinely described as Absurdist (cf. Edward Albee); now they just look silly, though they still persist in all media (cf. Friends). The musical respects and reflects the play's timelessness, and is probably indulging its own nostalgia for the brand of sweet-and sour, comic-romantic, off-Broadway musical epitomized by The Fantasticks.

Howard Marren's music (which probably sounded even more anchronistic in 1984 than it does now) tinkles competently, and has a taste for pastiche that it indulges whenever possible. Susan Birkenhead's lyrics are adroit, except when asked to shoulder too heavy a burden of exposition. The book, by Jeffrey Sweet, hangs together well; so well that one begins to wonder what could possibly have happened in the portions of text that have been cleared to make room for the songs.

Maybe they included some mordancy, or some needling wit. The show seems to think it's making some sort of statement about life and love and the purpose thereof, and its characters can say, do or contemplate cruel things. But since they're all bloodless, the result is just well-intentioned mush.

So at least it seems in this production, directed by Daryl Cloran, who is also currently responsible for the much superior I Might Be Edgar Allan Poe; his staging here is again competent but the level of performance is wretched. Julia Moore-Gammie's Ellen is devoid of personality, and her singing can't even fill the tiny hall. Christopher Wilson as Harry does have voice, and his lanky frame and lanky personality are right for the role, but his perpetual sickly grin is insufferable. Chaz Thorne's Milt is the tidiest of the three, but for a self-described go-getter he is singularly lacking in get-up-and-go.

The whole show in fact seems to be going through the motions of having energy.When Harry and Milt meet up, they go into an old school cheerleading song and they think, from the looks of them, that they're raising the roof, but not a rafter stirs.
