The Brigantine Room at Harbourfront Centre, generally a sober site for authorial readings or for devout question-and-answer sessions with Margaret Atwood, has been transformed into a cabaret. The patrons still sit at tables, but instead of merely sipping water, they can gorge on popcorn (free) and alcoholic beverages (not). They may also notice -- indeed they can hardly fail to -- a lady with a ripe French accent and a clown's red nose circulating among them, pre-show, and inquiring after their well-being. She turns out to be Rebecca Northan, originator and performer of Blind Date, which premiered at last year's World Stage festival, and has returned for this year's by popular demand and, if Tuesday's sold-out first night is any gauge, to popular approval. For the duration of the evening, she will be known as Mimi.

The performance proper finds Mimi onstage, at a table of her own, awaiting a prearranged companion for the evening who after two hours (she tells us) has proved a no-show. ( "We haven't even met and already he's insulting me.") So she seeks a substitute, and finds one among the audience. On Tuesday it was Scott, a cartoonist. He became in effect her costar, and he was very good.

"This is not" said Northan at the curtain-call, "a one-woman show." She can say that again (and probably does). The people she was paying tribute to at the time were the techies (who have to do their own improvisation), the ushers and a couple of supporting actors, one of whom gets to do a hilarious silent bit as an undertipped waiter. But her greatest debt was to Scott. All unrehearsed, he had to act out with her a story that took them from first date to going back to her place, and then on to marriage, pregnancy and even delivery. Game guy.

Scott emerged as sweet, funny and intelligent with some very quick come-backs. Expounding on her own mixed feelings about children, Mimi asked him what he does when, in the course of his work, he comes across children he doesn't like. "I draw them faster" he said. Either partner has the right to call a temporary halt to the action and retire to the side of the stage for a time out. "I wish" said Scott, on being told about this, "that life was like that." Right on. He called for one of these times out when Mimi started asking him about his ex-girlfriend who, it turned out, had come to the show with him. Should he act, he asked, as if he were aware or unaware of her presence in the audience? This raised all kinds of questions about the nature of theatre and the relation of fact and fantasy; and it seemed to me that Northan, protected by her persona, was rather flippant and evasive about answering them. I don't know how much the show varies from partner to partner; I would guess that Northan has a number of set routines and tries to steer the action toward them. But she still has to roll with a variety of punches.

An experienced Second City performer, she is a smart improviser. The show is enjoyable, but it's also rather unlikeable. Tina Rasmussen, World Stage's artistic director, says in a program note that Blind Date owes its success to "our desire to see people being real onstage." This strikes me as crazy. Scott and his fellows at other performances may be real; they don't have any choice. But Northan isn't; at least, I doubt that she goes through her offstage life with a red nose and a fake accent. Besides this, she has prepared, and she comes with a set of skills.

Two other entertainments passed through my mind while watching this one. One was the recent visit of Patti LuPone and Mandy Patinkin at which the closing ovation seemed to signify not just appreciation for a job well done but gratitude to such superior beings for doing us the honour of coming us. This struck me as a demeaning reversal of the proper order of things. After all, they were our employees; we (well, not me personally but some of us) had paid for our tickets. The other, humbler enterprise, was the Mysteriously Yours dinner theatre, whose presiding genius Jean Daigle invariably harangues and involves the audience in a Hercule Poirot accent remarkably lie Mimi's. The pattern of his shows is similar as well, from the pre-performance mingle-and-greet to the final adjuration to tell our friends if we liked the show and to shut up if we haven't. That's an old line of course, and the cheerful way in which Daigle & Co always deliver it is a virtual acknowledgment of its corniness. At the end of Blind Date it sounds more like a command.
