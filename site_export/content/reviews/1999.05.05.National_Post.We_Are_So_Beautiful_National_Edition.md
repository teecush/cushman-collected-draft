HELLO . . . HELLO

Factory Theatre Studio Cafe, Toronto

At the mid-point of Hello . . . Hello, a husband comes home from work to find his wife pregnant and cradling a dead rabbit. There seems to be no causal relation between these two circumstances, but everyone -- meaning he, she, and two choral figures who play everybody else -- behaves as if there is. They break out into a celebratory song -- "there's a new life flowing when a rabbit dies" - - designed to make us tap our feet and then feel thoroughly ashamed of ourselves. The show manufactures a coincidence and tries to make emotional and, for want of a better word, intellectual capital out of it. This is known as taking a bad idea and running with it.

It all takes place in a brave new ecologically impaired world where the seven seas have been reduced to six, the seafood dishes served in restaurants are dripping with mercury, and cod has been off the menu for 27 years. Artists, though dimly remembered, have disappeared, and so apparently have the poor and the ugly. All who remain are beautiful and affluent -- or aspire to be -- and there is a thriving service industry catering to their dreams, operating out of breathtakingly translucent Bay Street skyscrapers. These are described, at great length, but not seen; the actual set is a barely functional array of steps and platforms.

Our heroine, Cassandra, works in a boutique called The Abyss, which presumably is a yawning Gap. She encounters Ben, our hero, in a graveyard where each has buried a former partner. Cassandra likes to hang out there; it suits her temperament. She is named, of course, for the Greek prophetess of doom. Ben's name is less resonant, but it may signify benevolence; he certainly means well, toward her and the world at large. He works high up in one of those agencies as head of creative; the show's very best moment comes when he tells Cassandra this and she looks quizzical, as if expecting more. Dear old-fashioned thing, she thinks creative is still an adjective.

Anyway, they get married and Ben buys her everything she could wish, including -- a real luxury item, this -- a hospital bed in which to give birth. The baby turns out to be beautiful but extremely undersized. It stays that way; there is, we are told, a lot of it going about. And that's about it for plot. The show's real concern is to be a lyrical fantasia of the shimmering disaster that is tomorrow.

For approximately the 75th time this year, I am left wondering why a production (Chris Earle's, in this case) should go to so much trouble to persuade me of things that I already believe. To be sure, Hello . . . Hello is not your standard hectoring sermon. It is a small musical that wants to be simultaneously bright-eyed and cloven- hoofed. It alternates between coyness and the ironic exploitation of coyness, and it's very hard to tell the two apart, or even summon the energy to try. Narrative speeches, delivered in sickly sweet tones, hint archly at what we should be feeling and then back off with a sinister giggle.

Karen Hines, the author, also plays Cassandra, and her performance epitomizes the play. She devotes considerable skill, and a touch of the uncanny, to presenting someone very irritating, someone who specializes in delivering bad news with a cute simper. "There is a devouring blackness that has a hold on my imagination," she says, about 90 minutes after we've noticed it for ourselves. Like her classical namesake, she is right about everything, and therefore intolerable to have around.

The other actors -- David Jansen as Ben, Steven Morel and Teresa Pavlinek as the etceteras -- play with energy and good intentions, though Morel and Pavlinek are affected -- by text, direction, or both -- with advanced smirkiness. The author's lyrics are sometimes neat, and the music (by her and Greg Morrison) harmlessly tuneful. Occasionally, the intended chastening vision comes through, bringing with it hints that the piece might have been awfully interesting. Most of the time, though, it's just interestingly awful.
