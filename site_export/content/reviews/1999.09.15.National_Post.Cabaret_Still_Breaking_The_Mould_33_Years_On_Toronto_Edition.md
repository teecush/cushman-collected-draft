The new production of Cabaret is the re-invention of a classic musical, staged and performed with enormous verve and authority. You should see it. Moment by moment, it is hypnotic. Add the moments up, and doubts intrude.

First, the history. This is the latest incarnation of Christopher Isherwood's endlessly fertile Berlin Stories, which were based on the author's actual experiences of the early '30s; he witnessed the rise of Nazism and befriended a young night-club singer and all- around sex object -- English like himself -- whom he called Sally Bowles. In the '50s, Chris and Sally became the centre of a play, I Am a Camera. In the '60s, Cabaret arrived: a ground-breaking musical, directed by Harold Prince, with Chris transformed -- presumably for audience-identification purposes -- into an aspiring American writer named Cliff, and the Kit Kat club, Sally's stamping- and-vamping ground, transformed into the show's metaphor and nerve- centre. Then -- running with this idea and making many changes -- came the movie, directed by Bob Fosse.

The current version started small in London, triumphed in New York, and is now on the road. Its director, the British wunderkind Sam Mendes (the man of the moment in Toronto, with his American Beauty a success at the film festival and his production of Oliver! due to open at the Royal Alex) has stated that his aim is to seduce his audience with the delicious degeneracy of pre-Nazi night life, and then appall them with the more savage degeneracy that followed. That, as it happens, is what the original Prince production did; having hooked us with an opening number of incomparable seedy panache, it proceeded to deliver a trio of knock-out blows, each one small in itself but cumulatively horrific. A soaring musical salute to the approaching dawn was transformed into a Fascist anthem. A single brick was lobbed through a Jewish greengrocer's window. A floorshow routine showed the club's Master of Ceremonies dancing lovingly with a (presumably) female gorilla, ending by confiding in us "if you could see her through my eyes/she wouldn't look Jewish at all."

All those moments remain in the new production, but they have lost their impact. It has in fact been pre-empted. The hymn Tomorrow Belongs to Me is ended first time round with a Hitler impersonation, so the reprise has no surprise. The gorilla number lacks all lyricism, so its coda is no longer a slap in the face. Grace, indeed, has been conscientiously eradicated throughout. The chorus- girls -- and boys -- are mean and poverty-stricken. Sexy suspenders have given way to run-filled stockings, sly androgyny to sullen bisexuality; the joy of sex here comes not from doing it but from flaunting it. This may well be true to the period and it is also, as everybody keeps assuring us, very '90s, but it is also monotonous; the emcee starts the evening by grabbing his crotch, and from there he can only proceed to grabbing everybody else's.

The choreographer, Rob Marshall, has had some neat ideas for individual numbers, and the cast are as skilled as they are dedicated; they're musicians as well as actor-dancer-singers, and there is an unforgettable entr'acte in which they huddle inside, or spill out of, a giant tilted picture-frame while playing a Dixieland version of the title number. But, politically and sexually, the show has to keep upping the ante. The emcee moons the audience, to reveal a swastika tattooed on his buttocks. Finally, as Cliff's train pulls out of Berlin, we find ourselves transplanted to another railway platform, where our Master of Ceremonies again sheds his costume, to reveal a striped concentration-camp uniform, adorned with a yellow star and a pink triangle. Good taste hardly being the issue here, I should like to point out that at the Princess of Wales this is a bit deja vu; we have already seen The Pajama Game.

The serious point is presumably that this is the fate of those who think they can dance with the devil; Berlin clubland giggled at Hitler, instead of fighting him, and this is where it ended up.

The truth is actually more complicated; the more immediate objection is that the show hijacks the worst images of the century to underline what is already implicit.

To be fair, no one has ever known how to end Cabaret. (It transgresses the unwritten rule that you don't applaud shows about the Holocaust.) And even in its earlier scenes, the show is famously schizophrenic. There are the commentative nightclub numbers and there are the narrative book-show bits. This production tries to bring them together by setting the whole thing in the cabaret. Even in a big conventional theatre, this looks good; and Mendes and Marshall have bled the scenes into one another very cleverly.

But the styles still break apart.The emcee hovers over the whole action: echoing it, mocking it, influencing it. It's his brick that goes through the window. (So at this point he actually does represent Nazism? Very confusing.) One comes to resent his presence; Norbert Leo Butz preens and leers as expertly as could be wished, but he may be the first performer of this role not to walk off with the show. Perhaps because he's on stage so much. How do you steal something you already own?

Meanwhile the realistic scenes are working amazingly well; it's no great surprise that the John Kander-Fred Ebb score stands up so magnificently, but one hardly expected Joe Masteroff's book to equal it. But it does, and -- this production's real if unheralded trump- card -- it is wonderfully acted.

Best of all -- breathtakingly, undeviatingly so -- is Alma Cuervo as Fraulein Schneider, the Gentile landlady pressured into breaking off her engagement with the Jewish grocer. She is straight and clear -- even when temporizing or perplexed -- and unfusssily warm, and deeply moving. She also sings superbly, and her two songs of survival, one jaunty and the other desperate, are the best in the show. Nobody, not even Lotte Lenya who created it, can have played this role better. Hal Robinson, Drew McVety and Jeanine Morick are admirable as the other representative Germans, and Rick Holmes makes far more of Cliff than is customary.

Joely Fisher has got Sally absolutely right: an upper-class English schoolgirl gone off the rails and living, with tremulous bravado, on her nerves. Her voice is smoky, and she quivers; she does drugs but only admits to alcohol. You can see why Berlin would find her exotically provocative, and the staging of her Don't Tell Mama number takes full advantage. Her torch-song rendition of Maybe This Time is both touching and ironic. Sally is indeed the only character who bridges the show's two worlds, and the production comes closest to unity when she is on. Only the title song eludes her; it's done here for desperation rather than triumph, and she has already sounded that note to its depths.

It strikes me that the traditional affirmative reading of this number -- as exemplified by Liza Minnelli, and by Judi Dench who was the best of all Sallys -- is not only more immediately rousing but, given the ghastly historical context, more mordant. But I gladly concede that Cabaret, the musical that broke the mould 33 years ago, is still doing it.
