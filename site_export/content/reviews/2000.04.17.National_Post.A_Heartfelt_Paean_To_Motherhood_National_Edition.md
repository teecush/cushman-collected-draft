FOR THE PLEASURE OF SEEING HER AGAIN

Bluma Appel Theatre

Michel Tremblay's new play, much his mellowest, is a loving tribute to his mother. She died, nearly 40 years ago, too soon to see her son's career take off. As he tells us here, she was fascinated by drama; she would get engrossed in TV plays, caught up not only in their stories but in their mechanics.

What, she used to wonder, was going through the actors' heads while they performed, or indeed after the performance was over? She never got to go to a first night, much less attend a rehearsal, and the play is her son's attempt to make it up to her.

We can guess that she inspired some of the mother-figures in his earlier work. Now here she is in her own right, at the centre of a play that makes no bones about being a play. It's funny, and it's touching, but not quite enough of either.

Mother and son -- called here Nana and Narrator -- are the only characters. The play begins with the narrator outlining for us all the kinds of heavy drama -- from the Greeks to Beckett -- that are not in store for us this evening. (Several of them, as it happens, are currently available in Toronto on other World Stage stages. This play -- a co-production with the Montreal Centaur Theatre, is the Canadian Stage Company's contribution to the international festival but will run beyond it.) This speech is a piece of blush-making self- consciousness that the explicit theatricality of what follows does not excuse.

We then watch a series of remembered dialogues, scenes during which the son's age rises from 10 to 20; the consequent shifts in attitude are subtly captured by all parties.

We witness the son's teasing dissection of his mother's favourite romantic novels, and her passionately indignant defence of them. We hear her, who has spent her married life in Montreal, yearning for the plains of Saskatchewan, where she grew up. We listen to her describing and deploring, at length, the faults and foibles of her brother-in-law, whose conversation seems limited to descriptions of Fernandel films, and of his wife, whom she likes even less.

She muses on the problem of aristocracy; of which families have blue blood, and by what divine dispensation it got that colour in the first place. She accepts without fuss both her son's probable choice of career and his homosexuality.

Finally, we see her succumbing to stomach cancer and worrying how her family will get on without her. She acts everything out, with a zest for impersonation and a natural gift for language. She also invents and embroiders, partly for her own enjoyment and partly because she senses that this is the stimulation her boy needs; though she complains that as he gets older she "doesn't know what to make up any more."

Linda Gaboriau has translated the script from the French, and I suspect that the play, and especially the character of Nana, have lost something on the journey. There is a peculiar quality in French- Canadian writing and performance -- a sort of gnarled sophistication -- that generally keeps sentimentality at bay.

Nicola Cavendish as Nana seems to be having too obviously good a time; when she turns on the character's extravagances, when she doubles up with glee at her own comic turns, she is less a force of nature than a wind-up doll. She's better in the quieter scenes. Dennis O'Connor makes an admirable narrator, keeping the child alive within the grown man without any attempts at explicit juvenile characterization; but he, too, can be overly ingratiating. (Though it's nice to know that Tremblay can twinkle.) Gordon McCall's direction is efficient but sweet-toothed.

The play ends with the son contriving a theatrical apotheosis for his mother, a natural theatre artist who never got to set foot on a stage. As a joint tribute to the woman who nurtured his talent and the medium that let him express it, this should charm us off our feet; but it works a little too hard at it.

Tremblay tells us in his title that he wrote about his mother "for the pleasure of seeing her again." We can share some of that pleasure -- it would be impossible not to -- but not as much as he or we would like.
