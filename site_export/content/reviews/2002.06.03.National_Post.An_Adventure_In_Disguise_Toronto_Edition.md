THE SCARLET PIMPERNEL

Avon Theatre, Stratford, Ont.

- - -

STRATFORD, Ont. - Well, let no one say that the play choices at this year's Stratford Festival lack thematic continuity. The Threepenny Opera, which opened on Wednesday, ends with a foiled execution. The Scarlet Pimpernel, which followed on Thursday, begins with several spiritedly unfoiled executions. Welcome to the French Revolution, which as we all know consisted solely of the serial guillotining of inoffensive aristocrats (no middle-class counter- revolutionaries need apply) for the entertainment of howling, cackling and mainly female mobs. But fear not: the Scarlet Pimpernel, flower of the British upper class, is coming to the rescue, in an assortment of funny get-ups, and fewer heads will roll.

Those that do, roll most persuasively; that first scene is, in its comic-opera fashion, not bad. What follows is an atrocity, with only three mitigating features. One of these is the overall slickness of Dennis Garnhums direction, especially in the matter of scene changes. Another is the sets themselves, designed by Cameron Porteous, which not only cross the Channel but bob about on it en route; the guillotine frame, minus its blade, remains a unifying feature, and should be put into storage awaiting the production of a real play about the Terror: Dantons Death, perhaps, or -- to show there can be such a thing as intelligent melodrama -- an adaptation of A Tale of Two Cities. Finally, there is Peter Hutt, whose performance as Chauvelin, the perpetually thwarted French agent, is smoulderingly hissable. That, however, admirably though he carries it off, is the key to what is wrong.

The sound of modern theatregoers self-consciously booing the bad guy is on the same level of loathsomeness as The Sing-a-long Sound of Music; they both denote an audience that feels smugly superior to the entertainment on offer and a production that wants them to. They can patronize this presentation of Chauvelin not because he is nasty but because, as a villain, he is farcically inept. Even when he's theoretically winning he carries on like a loser, boastful and accident-prone. He never presents anything resembling a threat. And this is supposed to be an adventure story.

I like swashbuckling romance. I loved the Stratford production of The Three Musketeers. That had its tongue from time to time in its cheek, but never so far in that it lost the audience's respect for the story it was telling. It enthusiastically piled on the narrative, and it never stood still. It helped, of course, that it was based on a masterpiece; nobody would apply that description to Baroness Orczys sturdy old potboiler. But she did give us in Sir Percy Blakeney, the fop as superman, a hero whos become an archetype, or at least a franchise, and this first story about his exploits ought to work dramatically. Indeed, it often has; the Baroness herself turned it into a play before it ever got published, and I'll wager that her version was superior to this one, which is by the late English playwright Beverley Cross. He takes a simple tale --one with surprisingly few surprises -- and tells it haltingly, abetted by a production that is far more interested in masked balls than in people.

The main people here are Sir Percy himself and his wife, Marguerite, a French ex-actress of former Republican sympathies. At least they seem to be former; that part of the tale is left muddy as is the question of how the Blakeneys, who fear they have fallen out of love, ever fell in it. One thing that has always puzzled me about the Pimpernel: He seems to have adopted the pose of a terminally languid dandy as a cloak for his daredevilling. In which case, what was he like before the Revolution made this cover necessary, and why has nobody noticed the difference? And what stage was he at when he met Lady Blakeney? Still, the important considerations theatrically are that his affectations should seem to be second nature and that both he and we should enjoy them; and the casting of the unalterably rugged Peter Donaldson in the role makes all this difficult. A heavyweight fop is conceivable (witness Donald Sinden, who played the Cross version in London), but not a morose one. Donaldson's Percy lacks innocence and a dancing wit, though admittedly the latter quality must be hard to convey when your best line, addressed to a hunchback, is "rung any good bells lately?" (A professor of English quotes this in the program as the sparklng summit of the plays humour, and who am I to disagree?) His more transitory disguises, once he crosses to France and gets down to the business of being damned elusive, struck me as remarkably transparent; but, of course, the officers of the Revolution wouldnt have seen Donaldson on stage as often as I have.

It might have been different. Obviously you don't go to The Scarlet Pimpernel for political complexity, but one passage in the novel that has stayed with me down the years and is not in the play is an exasperated remark of the hero's to the effect that a couple of the French toffs he had rescued positively deserved the guillotine for their ridiculous prejudices. Donaldson might have made something of that. As it is, its only in a couple of tender lines at the end that he reveals anything of his real quality.

Sheila McCarthy, his real-life wife playing his on-stage wife for the second consecutive night, seems to have lost any period style she may have possessed; her Marguerite is vocally and emotionally choppy. A couple of dunderheaded assistant pimpernels are given performances to match, and are unlucky enough to be involved in a fight scene, choreographed to music for the maximum of giggles and the minimum of excitement. I dont know, or wish to know, what Ian Deakin is up to as the Prince Regent, but I liked Brian Tree's huffy little turn as Lord Grenville.
