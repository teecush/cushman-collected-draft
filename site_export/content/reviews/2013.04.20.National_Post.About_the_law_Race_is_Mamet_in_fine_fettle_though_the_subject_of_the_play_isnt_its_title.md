Nigel Shawn Williams, playing a lawyer in David Mamet's play Race, gives a tornado of a performance. The storm he raises reaches its highest level of force and velocity when, propelled by rage and frustration, he charges across his office, encountering a row of swivel chairs on his way, giving each of them a vicious shove as he passes, and setting them all spinning. It's a terrific image, electric in itself and a vital building-block in a relentlessly exciting evening.

Williams plays Henry, who is black; he shares the office and the firm with Jack, who's white, and with their junior, Susan, black. To them comes Charles, a rich young white man, accused of raping a young black woman in a hotel room. He claims innocence; says there was sex but it was consensual. The lawyers' initial response to that might be paraphrased as, "Sure. You would say that, wouldn't you?"

Henry has the first speech of the play: a summary of all the derogatory or patronizing things that white Americans might think about black Americans. Williams delivers it as a suave series of hammer-blows. "O.J. was guilty. Rodney King was in the wrong place, but the police have the right to use force ... Malcolm X was noble when he renounced violence. Prior to that he was misguided. Dr. King was of course a saint ... and you had a maid when you were young who was better to you than your mother." Charles protests that he would never say such things. Henry says of course he wouldn't; he wouldn't dare. He himself, being black, can, if only to make a rhetorical point. It's even possible that he believes them.

Charles says it isn't fair. Jack, speaking for the first time, says "It is neither fair nor unfair. None of us is immune. From a false accusation." He adds that if they take the case they will believe in his innocence, because it's their job. The play may have the wrong title. What it has to say about race is pungent and provocative, but it never really advances on that first speech. But if you go for portentous one-word titles then a better one here - Justice having been taken a century ago by John Galsworthy - might be Law. Jack and Henry delight in telling Charles how much his case, or any other, depends on performance, spectacle, manipulating the prejudices of the jury. "The law" says Jack "is an alley fight." He adds that "neither side wants the truth," or even knows what it is. "Each wants to prevail." None of this may be news either, but it's exhilarating to hear it expressed so nakedly and pursued so inventively. At the punchy, punch-line level - though not necessarily at all others - Mamet is writing as well as ever. And he follows it through, in words and action. Charles is cautioned against making any kind of confession to the press; they and the public would crucify him. There was a time when his class and colour might work in his favour; that time isn't now. Ken Burns' documentary on the Central Park Five, shown this week on PBS, reminded us just how recent that past time was. Both his film and Mamet's play perform a useful public service in reminding how far we've lost the meaning of the word "alleged".

Intertwined with race and law, especially race, there is of course a third issue, gender. Susan, though present, says nothing in the first scene, which is its own statement. The firm, for reasons you should discover for yourselves, takes the case; for a time, it even thinks it has one. Further developments are largely due to Susan, who is Jack's protegee, much resented by Henry who describes her as his white colleague's "science project." Henry volubly despises affirmative action, though it's perfectly possible that he's been the beneficiary of it: Susan sees him as an updated Uncle Tom. (Everybody, in or out of the courtroom, thinks they're innocent. We're all the heroes or heroines of our own melodrama.) There's a strong resemblance to Mamet's Speed-the-Plow in which the dreams of a couple of Hollywood shysters get derailed by a female with her own agenda. That was a slighter play, because it wasn't about much. Who doesn't know that Hollywood is a nest of bottom-feeders? And who in hell cares? (Mamet would put that more forcefully.) Race, by contrast, is about things that concern all of us. That, combined with its skill and drive, keep us on the edge of our seats. The only other current dramatist who can so consistently do that is Martin McDonagh. That he and Mamet may be our most misanthropic playwrights is probably no coincidence.

Mamet's two best plays, American Buffalo and Glengarry Glen Ross, have all-male casts, and he's often accused of not being able to write for women. Oleanna, whatever people say, proved the opposite; the trouble in Race is not that he hasn't written a good role for Susan - she has an interesting trajectory - but that he hasn't written her good lines. Cara Ricketts has to work hard to justify her character's actions, in both the verbal and the psychological sense. Matthew Edison, cast radically against previously unassuming type, shows as Charles that he can get powerfully angry. Jack is Jason Priestley, the production's presumed selling point, and also its weakest link; he hasn't been on stage in 13 years and it shows, in his limited range of both inflection and gesture. He doesn't actually distort the play but he lessens its moment-by-moment impact. Williams picks up most of the slack, though even he has his weak moments (about 5%, I'd say.) Mamet, we're told, sternly forbids any tampering with his stage-directions, but he doesn't seem to object to them being added to, and Williams' business with the chairs, which isn't in the script, is an inspired invention of the actor, or his director Daniel Brooks, whose production is a triumph of tautness, or both. It isn't a great play; you don't believe, as you did in Buffalo and Glengarry, that the characters have off-stage lives. But it's a searching and unsettling one, and its grip never slackens.
