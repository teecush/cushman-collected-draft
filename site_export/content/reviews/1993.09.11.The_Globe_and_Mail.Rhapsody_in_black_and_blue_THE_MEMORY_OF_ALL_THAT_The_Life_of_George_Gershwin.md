BY JOAN PEYSER Simon and Schuster, 319 pages, $33.95

Rhapsody in black and blue THE MEMORY OF ALL THAT: The Life of George Gershwin

BY JOAN PEYSER Simon and Schuster, 319 pages, $33.95

Saturday, September 11, 1993

ROBERT CUSHMAN

GERSHWIN REMEMBERED BY EDWARD JABLONSKI Amadeus Press, 181 pages, $17.95 Review by ROBERT CUSHMAN LOOK here upon this picture and on this. Joan Peyser directs our attention to two portraits of George Gershwin. One is a Hirschfeld drawing of a composer smart and self-confident, chin and cigar rampant, addressing the piano with one set of fingers elegantly curled and the other aggressively outstretched. The second picture, painted by Gershwin himself is, says the biographer, "melancholy, thoughtful." The world is still deceived with ornament, and it has accepted the picture of an extrovert Gershwin and ignored the pain within.

You could have fooled me. Does anyone believe that Porgy and Bess -or, come to that, such popular songs as The Man I Love, But Not For Me, They Can't Take That Away From Me, Love Is Here To Stay -could have been written by a man who was not at least "thoughtful" and probably, from time to time, "melancholy" as well? Even the most superficial, pot-boiling accounts stress a dark side. His film biography, Rhapsody in Blue, presents his life as a guilt-ridden struggle; he can never enjoy his commercial success because he keeps remembering the old music-teacher who wanted him to starve in the cause of art. It has also been soberly suggested that his death in Hollywood was due not to a brain tumour but to a broken heart, brought on by the studios' refusal to treat his music with the respect it deserved. That, of course, is ludicrously sentimental, but it does suggest that the Gershwin legend has never depicted an uncomplicated golden boy.

Joan Peyser needs to believe that it has, so as to bestow on her own shadowed portrait the grace of novelty. Her book is not precisely a hatchet-job. The Gershwin it presents is less villain than victim: mainly of his own inhibitions. It seems that back in 1917 the teenaged Gershwin missed some cues while accompanying a vaudeville comedian who responded by shouting abuse at him right in front of the audience. The humiliated prodigy later recalled that "I could have lifted the piano and hurled it at him." Naturally, though, he didn't. "Such internalization of hostility and aggression," says Peyser solemnly "is not good for one's health." At that rate, 90 per cent of us must be headed for brain tumours.

When not swallowing his pride or silently smarting over bad reviews, Peyser's Gershwin, like everybody else's, is the life of the party, monopolizing the piano to spin out countless variations on his own tunes: the Gershwin who prompted Oscar Levant to ask, "George, if you had to do it all over again, would you still fall in love with yourself?" Can it be news to anyone that a man who showed off like this must have had plenty of self-doubt to assuage? Come to that, has there ever been anyone in any of the arts who was not a bundle of insecurities?

Gershwin had plenty of affairs, but steered away from any real commitment. This too is well known, but Peyser does supply more names than we are used to. She also tells a story of Gershwin going to a Paris brothel with a couple of friends who spy on him there and are astonished both by his ineptitude and by his subsequent boasts about his prowess. Curiously, she seems to think it is Gershwin rather than his companions on whom this anecdote reflects badly.

The book's real punch, however, lies in its allegation that Gershwin had an illegitimate son named Alan, who is still living, and whose chorus- girl mother left him to be brought up in poverty by relatives. Gershwin continued to see him, but furtively and infrequently. Again a picture is crucial: the book includes a photograph of the grown Alan that certainly bears a resemblance to George, though hardly a clinching one. Peyser threads Alan's tale through the book, which is good human interest but bad advocacy. She never gathers the threads together to make a really convincing case.

There are two monsters in Peyser's story. One is Gershwin's mother Rose, who contributed to her son's problems by constantly decrying his music, while enjoying the money it brought in, and by a terrifying coldness. The other is Leonore, wife of George's lyricist brother Ira. "The most striking similarity," says Peyser, "between Rose and Leonore - apart from an imperious, demanding manner - was an obsession with diamonds."

Leonore's husband, on the other hand, has always enjoyed the reputation of a saintly teddy-bear, and here Peyser does break new ground. To begin with, Ira's talent is downgraded. There is no mention of the immense contribution made by his lyrics to the charm and quiddity of the brothers' songs; George apparently took him on in order to keep the royalties in the family. The author sets our pulses racing with the suggestion that Ira's lyrics constitute a coded biography of his brother, but in fact she produces few examples, none of them very illuminating. Meanwhile Ira and money become her idee fixe. References in Ira's letters to his earnings, harmless enough to the casual eye, are continually held against him. "When George began to immerse himself in Porgy and Bess, Ira, to keep money coming in, increased his collaboration with others." I suppose that's one way of putting it.

Indeed, Peyser's approach to biography seems to be to take the worst possible view of practically everybody, a task made easier by her apparent lack of a sense of humour. The Gershwins' father, known to musical anecdotalists for his pronunciation of Fascinating Rhythm as "Fashion on the River", was asked at one of their first nights how he liked the show, he replied 'What you mean how I like it? I HAVE to like it.' Peyser: "While many writers report it as another example of Morris Gershwin's wit, it is a hostile response by an uninformed and jealous man." Well, it's possible. But how does she know?

Peyser's own comments on Gershwin's music, though sparse, are sound and sympathetic. And in further fairness to her, it is true that a hagiographic Gershwin cult has always surrounded both the brothers. Biographer and musicologist Edward Jablonski has written several books about them that are invaluable resources but leave you longing to hear that the Gershwins wrote just one imperfect song, or behaved with less than impeccable propriety. Jablonski's latest offering, Gershwin Remembered, is a flavoursome collage drawn from the immediate reactions and later reminiscences of those who were there. No mention, needless to say, of Alan Gershwin or any other unacknowledged progeny; plenty of evidence, though, pace Peyser, of unhappiness and "internal conflicts." One figure whom both authors agree on demonizing is Gregory Zilboorg, a psychoanalyst who bullied his patients and failed to notice George's developing tumour. Peyser says that Gershwin's seeking this man out was part of his self-destructiveness. Jablonski, in a frosty footnote, says that he "was merely curious about psychoanalysis and himself, not a neurotic with a mother complex, as has been suggested." So you see how it goes. Robert Cushman is a writer and broadcaster, who hosts Songbook on CBC radio.
