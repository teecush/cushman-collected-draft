There is a recurring kind of bad acting that I seem to keep banging on about, and that I can best define as "passion in excess of the object." It usually manifests itself as nagging and pleading. Two characters, let us say, are having an argument. The actors turn the thoughts and emotions into grievances, to be stuffed desperately down one another's throats. There is a lot of this about, maybe because we have lost our feeling for the weight of words, and so try to colour them, garishly.

Bad acting is not the exclusive province of bad actors. Lucy, a new production at Can Stage, is the work of genuinely talented people. Damien Atkins' play stars Seana McKenna, who is more than talented; she is, quite simply, the best. She plays Vivian, an anthropologist who is also the mother of an autistic daughter, the Lucy of the title. Lucy, now aged 13, has been brought up by Vivian's divorced husband who wants some time off with his new girlfriend. So Vivian has to take care of the child for a year, though she has always shrunk from human contact and never wanted to be a mother, either before or after becoming one. But she eventually bonds with Lucy, deciding after a consultation with a knowledgeable but unsympathetic therapist that she and her daughter have a lot in common. In fact Vivian comes to believe that she is autistic too -- hence her lack of the conventional social skills and instincts -- and that autism represents the shape of things to come. Or rather, a return to the shape of things that were: Civilization was a huge mistake, and modern society is destroying itself by its overbearing insistence on notions like Feeling and Sharing and Community.

I don't know how much scientific basis there may be to this, or even how much Atkins himself believes it, but it's an undeniably provocative idea, even an appealing one, that runs refreshingly counter to today's mushy orthodoxies. The first act can't shake off the disease-of-the-week disease, but it still gives us an economically constructed, sympathetically observed view of Lucy's condition and Vivian's attempts to cope with it. We can see for ourselves the similarities between them, and we begin to think that the play is hitting us over the head with the irony of it all. It turns out that Atkins has far more in mind. When Vivian gets to state her case, her auditor and shocked antagonist is her former research assistant. In earlier scenes this lady has been a mere device, a foil whose function has been to ask the right questions at the right moment, and Philippa Domville, who plays her, has not been able to bring her to life. Now, faced with an idea that repels her, she becomes a raging fury, generating far more heat than light. She devalues her own case even as she tries to make it; which cannot have been the intention. The scene becomes a screamfest and even McKenna, usually the most fastidious of actresses, gets caught up in it. She gets off some wonderful sharp zingers ("You can decide for yourself, am I the future or am I a bitch?" encapsulates both the character and the argument very nicely, with wit working in the service of meaning) but too often the sense of what she is saying is swamped by the sheer belligerence of it. It all goes by too fast and too furious.

The earlier scenes offer McKenna good jagged writing to depict a good jagged personality, and she inhabits every twist, turn and hesitation of it. She plays her final desperation to the hilt, and does the same for some disconcertingly plain speaking: "You do this every day?" Vivian asks her husband incredulously, when first confronted with the specifics of looking after Lucy. Lucy herself is seen in double focus; she's a clinical study in the symptoms of autism (Meg Roe handles the remoteness and the ferocity splendidly) and she's a lyrical narrator, letting us know what she really thinks, especially about her mother. The two strands converge at the end of the first act, when Lucy creeps into Vivian's bed and Vivian finds that she can, first frighteningly and then joyfully, hear with her daughter's ears, see with her eyes. It's a tricky scene, to put it mildly, and it's done here with a lot of flashing lights and clangorous sounds that remain just tricky. Eda Holmes is a fine but uneven director, and this production has got away fromher.

Teresa Przyblski contributes a chilly unit set that does duty for everywhere without quite convincing as anywhere. Brendan Murray as the medic and Tony Munch as Vivian's ex are competent though Munch's compulsive arm-gestures are reminiscent of the seals in A Fine Romance who flap their fins to express emotion The last scene finds the two guys and the uptight assistant (whose business it doesn't quite seem to be) uniting against Vivian who wants to take Lucy to Africa, presumably to connect with something fine and primal. There's a paradox for you, a play and character that seemed to be coming out against fashionable touchy-feeliness ending up as touchy- feely as they come.
