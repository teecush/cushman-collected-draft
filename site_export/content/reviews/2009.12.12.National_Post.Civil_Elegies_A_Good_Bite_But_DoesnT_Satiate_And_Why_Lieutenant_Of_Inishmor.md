The dauntingly versatile Mike Ross figures in Soulpepper's Civil Elegies as actor, singer, pianist, composer and original begetter. The show grew from his enthusiasm for the work of Dennis Lee whose long poem of 1968 furnishes it with its title and much of its text. Ross recites it, or most of it, interspersing it with his own musical settings of Lee's comparatively lighter verse, the stuff that rhymes. These take very well to song form, and Ross has supplied them with plangent downbeat melodies that sometimes recall Tom Waits (Randy Newman, his other cited influence, not so much) though vocally he suggests a more melodious Bob Dylan, 1960s vintage, a sly and throaty prophet.

His unseen collaborators are Albert Schultz, who directs, and Lorenzo Savoini, credited as co-creator and designer. Old photographs of Toronto are projected onto a brick wall whose still-visible contours can make even a simple streetcar look ominous. There's also, more playfully, a video feed showing us what Ross's hands, when not hitting the keys, are getting up to on top of the piano; they're doing unexpectedly creative things with coffee and doughnuts. All this adds to his already considerable charm as a performer, which in turn leavens material whose nearest approach to optimism often seems to be a bruised wistfulness.

Civil Elegies the poem presents a young bard sitting in Nathan Phillips Square, meditating on a Canada whose colonized and colonizing past and compromised and compromising present have infected and dehumanized the cityscape. Forty years on, your thoughts may stray to Afghanistan and Dundas Square. Poem and songs reinforce one another's themes. "Skyscraper, skyscraper, scrape me some sky," sings the evening's first line, echoing a number from Fiddler on the Roof. The Americans want the country, says another song, "for a backyard barbecue." A rueful paean to William Lyon Mackenzie, entitled 1838, dovetails with an Elegy commemorating "the first spontaneous mutual retreat in the history of warfare." The most cheerful piece, celebrating the defeat of the Spadina expressway, could maybe use a more triumphalist setting than Ross has given it, if only for contrast. The components of Lee's vision sometimes seems strained, but it's a powerful one, especially when it turns to the failures of love.

For all the talent on display, the show doesn't satisfy. There's a monumental bitterness to some of the poems that Ross, lucidly though he delivers them, doesn't -- or doesn't yet --have in him; Lee may have been only 28 when he wrote them but one thing they don't feel is young. And there's the simple question of form. Most poetry since 1900 can be read aloud but it can't easily be listened to aloud; the words go by too fast and too knotted. Or, either the text is too dense or I am.

Kudos, I guess, to Rep 21, an outcrop of Canadore College in North Bay, for bringing us Martin Mc-Donagh's The Lieutenant of Inishmore. Shame on our more established theatres for leaving the Toronto premiere of a major piece by a major playwright to a barely professional company who have done it very badly. The play is a cruel Irish farce about terrorism. The title character, a splinter of a splinter-group from the IRA, takes time off from torturing a drug dealer to wreak horrible vengeance on the villagers --one of them his own father -- who have killed his cherished pet cat. Another faction lies in wait for him, and an aspiring gun moll completes the pattern. The action is a thoroughgoing reductio ad absurdum, but the casual attitude towards horrors seems glib and the tensions don't hold. Here, they don't stand a chance. I suppose it's reassuring that the one actor with an Equity card (Benjamin Clost) is the only one with any style, presence or timing; in a play where everyone's trigger happy, one dreads his disappearing too early. The rest are hung out to dry in a production (by Rod Carley) with no apparent sensitivity to tone or pace.

We mourn the death of Goldie Semple, a coruscating actress whose performances in The Royal Family and The Cassilis Engagement were among the Shaw Festival's finest.

- Civil Elegies runs through Dec. 24 at the Young Centre (416-866-8666); The Lieutenant of Inishmore closes tonight at the Berkeley Street Theatre. (416-363-3110).
