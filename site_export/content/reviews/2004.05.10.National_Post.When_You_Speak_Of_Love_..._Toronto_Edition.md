PYGMALION

Festival Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont.

- - -

THE IMPORTANCE OF BEING EARNEST

Royal George Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont.

- - -

The opening production of this year's Shaw Festival is a treat. One of the more delicious things about it is its unfamiliarity -- a surprise, perhaps, considering that Shaw's Pygmalion is generally reckoned one of his most popular plays and is probably still the one likeliest to crop up in schools. But I have only seen one previous professional production of it, though I must have seen half a dozen of My Fair Lady.

That, of course, is the reason; the musical has preempted the play. It has not, however, killed it off, which is what was done to the plays (name them if you can) that gave birth to Oklahoma!, Carousel and Cabaret. In this instance, there is room for both versions, and one wouldn't want to be without either.

Inevitably, the original now seems to be full of cues for song that nobody picks up. It's quite a jolt to hear Eliza Doolittle say, rather than sing, that she can do without Henry Higgins, and it's an electric shock to find him telling her in speech, rather than us in song, that he's grown accustomed to her face. Note, though, that the songs of which those lines remind us -- even the latter, a love song that never mentions love -- are among the tougher numbers in the My Fair Lady score; and that the play provides few pointers to the gentler ones. Nowhere in Pygmalion does Eliza fantasize about life being loverly -- certainly not with somebody's head resting on her knee -- and nowhere does her dustman father profess his faith in a little bit of luck or make a song and dance, even a purely verbal one, about getting to the church on time. My Fair Lady is a soft- edged romance; Pygmalion is a hard-edged one.

It is a romance in the sense of being a love story, despite Shaw's own protestations to the contrary. This famous play's actual title is now one of those meaningless, taken-for-granted labels (like Hay Fever); but it refers back to the Greek legend of Pygmalion, the artist who sculpted a woman, Galatea, so beautiful that the gods allowed her to come to life and marry him. Shaw's 1913 equivalent (not a very close one, come to think of it, but he's the one making the comparison) is the professor of phonetics who teaches a Cockney flower girl to speak like, and thus to pass for, a duchess. "Galatea never does quite like Pygmalion," said Shaw, flying in the face of myth. He said it, not in the play itself but in a narrative sequel affixed to the published text and explicitly designed to disillusion its readers. It tells how Eliza married, not Higgins, but the helplessly adoring Freddy Eynsford Hill. Nobody has ever wanted to believe this. I don't believe Shaw himself believed it. It's true that , in real life Eliza probably would have married Freddy. But she wouldn't have done it in the comedy Shaw actually wrote; as you can tell from comparing the play, which is effortlessly witty, with the epilogue, which is effortfully smirky.

Jackie Maxwell, responsible for the Niagara production, quotes the epilogue in her program note as one of her guiding lights. Frankly, I wouldn't have known if she hadn't told me, certainly not from the fine show she's actually put on the stage. It's a panoramic presentation, splendiferously designed by Sue LePage, and with lighting and sound, by Kevin Lamotte and Marc Desormeaux respectively, that make the opening in the Covent Garden rainstorm thunderously effective. The text incorporates some scenes that Shaw wrote for the 1938 movie; these afford us glimpses of two vintage taxis and of Eliza, in silhouette, forced into taking her very first bath by the professor's ruthlessly motherly housekeeper.

These extra episodes come in the printed version with an authorial running commentary, some of which the production retains and puts into the mouths of small-part players, representing the London poor who also seem to be moonlighting as scene shifters. They also speak some, though by no means all, of the original play's stage directions, in accordance with a strange convention that declares it late, departs early and seems arbitrary throughout. The director appears here to be operating on what Shaw himself might have identified as the Todgers principle: Reviewing one of Henry Irving's heavily cut Shakespeare productions, he likened Irving's treatment of the text to the culinary habits of the Dickensian Mrs. Todgers, "a-dodgin' around among the tasty bits with a fork and a- eatin' of 'em."

The tasty bits that Maxwell has picked out are socially conscious bits; they supply details of what it's really like to be poor. But they aren't necessary; the play itself -- through Eliza's mouth, and her father's, and even Higgins' -- tells us all we need to know of the constriction and deprivation from which the heroine's fairy- tale transformation allows her to escape. It's what was left out of the musical, and it's the real difference between the two. Poverty here isn't a jolly knees-up about the joys of beating the system; it's Doolittle senior, spokesman of the undeserving poor, accepting an unwelcome legacy because it's all that stands between him and the workhouse. That's a fairy-tale too, of course, and a hilarious one, but its implications are anything but.

Doolittle's prose arias are here superbly delivered by Simon Bradbury, with cock-sparrow gusto and a rare -- very rare -- instinct for the construction of lines, speeches and entire scenes. Rhythm: It's why Eliza's celebrated lapse into the vernacular is still bloody hilarious in the play, but why they could never get it to work on Broadway when they wedged it between choruses of the Ascot Gavotte.

Lorne Kennedy makes a dapper Colonel Pickering, and the genteel- poor family of the Eynsford Hills are nicely differentiated and also nicely connected, Jillian Cook's Mama having apparently bequeathed her mild snobbery to Catherine McGregor's unsteady Clara (in whom it ceases to be mild) and her gallantry to Adam Brazier's wistful Freddy. Nora McLellan's Mrs. Pearce is a fine figure of a domestic, and Patricia Hamilton's Mrs. Higgins is, in her grace and authority, the mother Shaw probably wished he'd had.

Jim Mezon endows her undentable son -- the male chauvinist who puts the pig in Pygmalion -- with the glee of a schoolboy, the passion of a pedant and the empathy of an elephant, which is just the right combination. Tara Rosling's Eliza resorts too easily, even after her elevation, to what Higgins earlier describes as "detestable boo-hooing" but is in all other respects enchanting, especially when, in standing up to her hooray Henry, she starts to penetrate his defences. The two of them chart a clear and exhilarating line through the long and potentially repetitive duet that constitutes most of the play's second half. And by the last line, which states precisely the opposite, you know by the end that Eliza will marry Higgins and not Freddy, as surely as if they were characters in a musical.

The festival's production of The Importance of Being Earnest is by contrast, or even without the contrast, appalling. As the work of Christopher Newton -- whose eye, ear, and taste are usually as fine as any director's in Canada -- it is almost incredible. What, given that Wilde's text depends even more than Shaw's upon absolute rhythmic certainty, are we to make of a production that allows Jack Worthing to interject a "yes, quite" into his first scene, and Lady Bracknell an even more unbelievable "uh-huh" into her last? Why, in a play whose characters live by a social code that permits them to do or say anything so long as it doesn't involve an obvious breach of decorum, does Algernon not merely grab Jack's plate of muffins but stomp on his foot and leave him unfunnily hopping? Why does Miss Prism keep pawing Canon Chasuble? Is she afraid that otherwise we wouldn't realize she fancied him?

In fairness to the director, he may have resorted to slapstick to compensate for his younger actors' inability to get their laughs on the lines. And in fairness to them, very few young actors can cut it. With tradition dead (though it should surely be alive at Niagara if it's alive anywhere) you have to work hard to acquire the technique; and of course by the time you've acquired it, you may no longer be young. Of the insecure quartet here, Evan Buliung's Jack comes off best; he can afford a certain heavy-handedness, since he is both the oldest and something of an outsider, his upbringing having been, so to speak, handbagged. He makes a fine thing of his surprise appearance in mock mourning -- or would if the staging allowed him to take full advantage of it.

David Leyshon's Algy is woeful, a supposed wit unable to feel his way to the end of an epigram. As Gwendolen, Fiona Byrne comprehensively violates the character's own dictum that in matters of grave importance style, not sincerity, is the vital thing; in place of self-absorbed hauteur, she offers aggrieved pettishness. Diana Donnelly's Cecily is a fine red-headed figure of a gal but too beefy in shape and manner for anyone to compare her to a pink rose. The tea-table scene, a usually infallible piece of situation comedy, falls flat because both young ladies are far too blatant in their rudeness. Certainly they should have their claws out, but the claws should be sheathed in the silkiest of gloves.

So should be those of nearly everyone in the play, though in the case of Lady Bracknell the gloves need to be lined with lead. Goldie Semple, like many recent Bracknells, is a looker, an idea with which there is nothing wrong. Not looking like a gorgon does not, however, absolve her of the responsibility of behaving like one; Semple's phrasing of one particularly famous line (it would be unfair to say which) is inspired, but she undermines herself by being both physically restless and vocally fussy.

Indeed, if the younger performers haven't yet learned how to do it, the older ones seem to have forgotten. Bernard Behrens' Chasuble has some nice scattered moments of scandalized propriety; Brigitte Robinson's Prism is not only cheap but forced. The best performance is given by Robert Benson as the manservant Lane; in the few moments he's on stage he creates both a credible past and an interesting present. In the even smaller role of the butler, Guy Bannerman melts charmingly into tears at the happy ending, and there are some excellent groupings as that ending is approached. But that is about all.

Wilde's masterwork is a farce of manners, which makes it a one- of-a-kind achievement. It's constructed of jokes, and even if you know them by heart, there can still be joy in hearing them perfectly told. To muff them is to destroy not only the author's aesthetic but his meaning. In Pygmalion, Higgins complains of high society that "the silly people don't know their own silly business." In The Importance, written 20 years earlier, they know it all too well, and if the actors cannot convince us that they know it, too, the play is pointless.
