THEATRE REVIEW

The Breathing Hole Studio Theatre, Stratford

What War Horse did for horses, The Breathing Hole does for bears.

The central character of Colleen Murphy's new play is Angu'juaq, a polar bear whose preternatural, not to say symbolic, existence stretches from 500 years in the past to 20 years in the future. He is brought to life in Reneltta Arluk's production in three separate incarnations. Before the play proper gets started, in the theatrical equivalent of a pre-credits sequence, he's a shadowpuppet, created by a couple of children. Then, as a cub, he's a hand-puppet, fondly manipulated by the Inuit woman who has saved his life and adopted him. Finally, and magically, he's a skeletal life-size puppet, very much in War Horse style but maybe even finer, inhabited by an actor (Bruce Hunter) and moving with an enchanting blend of delicacy and dignity.

A hush comes over the house whenever he appears. Particularly impressive are his fishing expeditions. He reaches down under the stage floor into the breathing hole, the food-source, of the play's title and comes up every time with a full-sized seal: another triumph for the design department.

The bear is also, in all his charm and majesty and significance, a triumph for the author's ambition and imagination. He holds together an uneven but, in its last moments, overwhelming play. The end is prophesied in the beginning: Hummittuq, the woman who, having lost her own children, will adopt Angu'juaq, looks down through the breathing hole into the waters of the Arctic and sees them turning black. The year is 1534, or so the program tells us; the play has no way of doing so. The other members of Hummittuq's community laugh at her and at her cub, but eventually come to value, even love him, as a mascot and as a hunting companion.

The play, a Stratford commission, is also the first at the festival to feature a plurality of Indigenous characters, played by Indigenous actors. The most experienced, Jani Lauzon, plays Hummittuq, and is excellent. She appears later on as, or inside, a second bear, the protagonist's mate.

The others in the play's opening scenes are hobbled by fake-primitive dialogue that seems inappropriate and rather demeaning. Why have people talk in what used to be called pidgin-English when they would not have been speaking English at all? Things get much livelier when we flash forward to 1845 (though again we have only the program's word for it). The British arrive, in the persons of Sir John Franklin's ill-fated expedition. They too encounter our bear. Some, frightened, want to kill it, but Franklin, an officer and a scientist, holds them back. He's a humane fanatic, given a lovely performance, both funny and dignified, by Randy Hughson. Thomas Mitchell Barnet and Jamie Mac stand out as junior crew members, jaunty and terrified, and much given to slipping on the ice. Juan Chioran is authentically wild-eyed as the first of them to anticipate starvation (and, it seems, the only one to dress appropriately for the climate).

The richest moments come when the explorers meet the natives: descendants, presumably, of the Intuit (or "Esquimaux" as Franklin punctiliously calls them) we met two centuries earlier, and played by the same actors. The non-violent culture-clash makes for wittier, more sophisticated writing than we have heard previously, and the performers rise to it.

And so to 2028 and then to 2033, dates at which fashions and technologies seem surprisingly identical to those we have now. In the penultimate scene, scientists and environmentalists politely oppose oil-drillers and tourism promoters. In the last scene, the bad guys have won and everybody is on board a Northwest Passage cruise ship, casually going where Franklin & Co tried and fatally failed to go.

The writing here descends to Ugly American cliche, far worse than the simplicities of the opening scenes which were at least an honest effort. There is the occasional bright spot: an Indigenous steward (Johnny Issaluk), asked what his mother would think of his present employment, replies that actually she got him the job. Most importantly there is the last stand of Angu'juaq, still swimming in the blackened waters his foster-mother foresaw so long ago. We first met him in a culture whose inhabitants revered all creatures, even while eating them. Now he has to contend with human fear, human brutality and human pollution.

The production throughout is an audio-visual treat, what with Daniela Masselis'set designs and Itai Erdai's bright Northern lights and Carmen Braden's soundscore. The final tableau, the previously immortal bear in an oil-drenched sea, is unforgettable. And though some of this may be put down to anthropomorphic sentimentality, it strikes deeper than that. There are plenty of plays that tell of climate change. This may be the first to find a way of showing it.

The Breathing Hole is in repertory through Sept. 22.
