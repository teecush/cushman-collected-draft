ASSEMBLYWOMEN!

Hart House Theatre, Toronto

Greg Robic is a Canadian playwright, scholar and lyricist who a few years ago had a substantial and well-deserved hit with an oddball satirical musical based on Aristophanes' The Clouds, staying faithful to the originals structure but replacing its comic choruses with puposefully anachronistic lyrics fitted to familiar (i.e. out-of-copyright) opera and operetta tunes. He followed it up with a similar show derived from the same old masters Lysistrata. Now the two of them are back with their third collaboration which Robic has called Assemblywomen! Aristophanes called it Ekklesiazusai, and though I have even smaller Latin and less Greek that Shakespeare was once credited with, I believe that Robic's title is a fair translation. The play usually turns up in English, on the rare occasions it turns up at all, as something like Women in Parliament. This was a pretty revolutionary notion in ancient Greece, and it might even have rattled a few ears in 1918 Canada, which is where this production, for reasons that remain mysterious, purports to be set.

This time around, Robic has felt confident enough to propose himself a vote of thanks. A first-act song, listing the requirements for success in civil society, admonishes us "make sure you can quote all the plays of Greg Rouc." All? There have only been three. This kind of supposedly charming self-aggrandizement (there's a lighting- change to underline the daring deliciousness of it all) is premature; and though many in the first-night audience obediently fell about, I didnt. Aristophanes himself may have gone in for this kind of thing, but he had a corpus; even his surviving plays get him into double figures, and there must have been many more that are lost. Besides, he invented comedy. More importantly: even at this early stage -- especially at this early stage -- it was obvious that unlike Clouds (I cant speak for Lysistrata) Assemblywomen! wasn't going to work.

In looking for someone to blame, we should first go back to the source. As Robic himself has pointed out, the kind of comedy Aristophanes invented isn't our kind; it wasnt even the kind practised by the next generation in Greece. Down through the millennia stage comedy, whether satirical or romantic, has been about logical plot-lines and boy getting girl. The older kind lives on in revue, cabaret and early Marx Brothers; Duck Soup may be a greater Aristophanic masterpiece than anything by Aristophanes. Its an honourable tradition, but hard to revitalize over a whole evening in the theatre. Aristophanes started with an idea, and then spun out variations on it. It could be a great and subversive idea: most obviously so in Lysisatra where the women go on sex-strike until their husbands give up war. But even that one isn't developed; it's just repeated or -- when the author gets tired of it -- forgotten.

The original Assemblywomen (sans exclamation-point) must have struck even the Athenians a as a tamer re-run of Lysistrata. Here, the notion is that the women, led by Praxagora, tired of the self- glorifying and self-enriching antics of the all-male legislature, steal their clothes and, thus disguised, induce the assembly to dissolve itself and vote in a female government instead. Its typical of the author that the men, supposedly hobbled by having only their wives dresses to wear, still make it to the assembly first; the cross-dressing tactic, elaborately set up, is then dropped and only resurrected for a single self-contained scene. By most standards this is slapdash dramaturgy, but of course Aristophanes wasn't operating by most standards. The trouble is that the satirical points he makes, here at any rate, aren't sharp or novel enough to make us see things his way. We merely discover that the conventional wisdom that politicians are corrupt buffoons was equally conventional (and equally wise) 2,500 years ago. It is not a surprise. And the 1918 seating is, literally, neither her nor there. All you can say for it is that it does show us the women wearing the trousers.

Even Aristophanes felt something was lacking, because in this -- his last extant play but one -- he goes soft on us, as if nervous about attracting a young audience. In his second half he offers something approaching a love-plot. Praxagoras reforms include the abolition of money, free food for all and -- the one idea thats developed -- complete sexual liberty except that the young and beautiful have first to satisfy the old and ugly. (This may be the earliest, and potentially most far-reaching, instance of positive discrimination on record.) Praxagora's husband, Blepyros, formerly the loudest mouth on the Acropolis, is now plotting against her regime. They meet, both in disguise (they have their reasons; they also have masks), and most heavily fall for one another. However, both of them are firmly in the Attractive camp, and I'm sure you can work out the complications for yourselves. The situation certainly has some piquancy but it is quite remorselessly spun out.

At this point Robic and his director Marc Richard have to shoulder the guilt, or, more appropriately perhaps, face the music. My happiest memory of Clouds is of a wonderfully irrelevant number lampooning megamusicals and somewhat reminiscent (to put it tactfully) of Ya Got Trouble from The Music Man. Apart from its comic and lyrical merits, it came as a great relief in its American breeziness from the sedate European airs that made up the rest of the score. There is no equivalent number here. (The revelation that Bring Him Home, from Les Miserables, is lifted from Puccini, is no compensation. We still have to sit there and listen to the bloody thing.) The music here is taken from Donizetti, Sullivan and such, and it is very well sung by all concerned. But the numbers take a long time to sing, they repeat themselves, and there is a laborious archness about them that infects the whole evening: writing, performance and staging. Robic has a great fondness for polysyllabic humour. It works well when set to music, or at least to this kind of music. Between songs it just sounds pedantic.

Elizabeth Beeler acts fairly wittily, and sings spectacularly. Leo Wigglesworth, mobile in face, voice and body, is the obstreperous Blepyros. Lawrence Cotton is agreeably grumpy, leading the chorus in the first act and subsiding into it in the second. There are a couple of very good laughs. But the whole evening seems to be spoofing itself without having a self to spoof.
