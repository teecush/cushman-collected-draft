Today is the centenary of the birth of Johnny Mercer who wrote the lyrics to Come Rain or Come Shine, I'm Old Fashioned, Skylark, Too Marvelous for Words, Moon River and Jeepers Creepers. Those happen to be the six songs whose sheet-music title-pages are reproduced on the back cover of his newly-published collected lyrics, but in itself it's an arbitrary list. One could just as easily cite Blues in the Night, That Old Black Magic, One for My Baby, Autumn Leaves, Something's Gotta Give and On the Atchison, Topeka and the Santa Fe. It would probably be possible to compile another half-dozen half-dozens without exhausting the catalogue of Mercer songs that are still at least moderately well-known. Throw in all the buried treasures and coterie classics and the resulting list would cover this page. And he was only 66 when he died: in 1976, of a brain tumour.

The Complete Lyrics of Johnny Mercer (Knopf ) runs to more than 1,200 songs. It also lists 230 composing collaborators, probably a record for any lyricist. The dozen songs named above include four with music by Harold Arlen, Mercer's greatest and most compatible partner; one by Jerome Kern, the dean of American popular song; one by Hoagy Carmichael, Mercer's fellow ruralist; one each by Harry Warren and Richard Whiting, masters of the Hollywood musical; one by that johnny-come-lately, Henry Mancini; one translation from the French; and one tune composed by Mercer himself (the insouciant Something's Gotta Give, his finest effort as a melodist). He wrote, in fact, with almost everybody. If one man sums up the Golden Age of American Popular Song it's Mercer. He covered all the bases. He wrote Broadway shows, though not many of them; he originally wanted to be an actor, but the theatre wasn't his metier. Rather, he was the genius of the one-shot song; he wrote hundreds of individual pop songs. In the same vein he wrote for movies, dozens of them. He founded Capitol Records. He was deep into jazz, especially Dixieland and swing, and put witty or evocative or celebratory words to instrumentals. He was part of the big-band era, not just as a writer but as a very good singer. Indeed he was the best of all singer-songwriters, ranking with Fred Astaire and Louis Armstrong among the great vocalists who are most famous for doing something else.

A Turner Classic Movies documentary produced by Clint Eastwood and entitled Johnny Mercer: The Dream's on Me (it's on tonight) is most valuable for the clips it includes of Mercer singing and, charmingly and wittily, talking. Somebody could compile a great TV show or DVD simply by stringing together all the appearances he ever made on TV specials and just letting them roll; in the documentary they're invariably and frustratingly truncated. (It also suffers from a neurotic compulsion to illustrate everybody, no matter how fleetingly they're mentioned.) But it covers an impressive amount of ground; you see Astaire's hard-driving movie performance of One for My Baby and doubt that anybody could do the song better, then you see a youngish Sinatra sinking deep into its melancholy bravado and realize that somebody could. And it brings out the things that made Mercer an anomaly among his colleagues: his double life as a performer and the fact that he, like Carmichael, was a Southerner in a predominantly New York Jewish profession.

It leaves untouched the great paradox of his talent: that, if he hadn't been a great lyricist, he might not even have been a good one. Like his peers, he wrote mainly love songs. Unlike them, he had nothing individual to say on the subject. Cole Porter was steamily intense about love, Lorenz Hart painfully self-mocking, Ira Gershwin humorously above it all. Mercer was content to take the standard Tin Pan Alley tropes of dreams and stars and moonshine and endlessly re-shuffle them. The results should have been hack-work. In fact they were inspired. At some level, he probably believed in the sentiments, but really what he loved was words: how they sounded, what they evoked. From When the World Was Young (another of his adaptations from the French): Ah the apple trees Where at garden teas Jack o'lanterns hung. Fashions of the day Vests of applique Dresses of shantung...

"Jack o'lanterns...shantung": you feel the images on your tongue. Quoting those lines gives me special pleasure, since they're left out of the not-quite-Complete Lyrics, a handsome and invaluable volume disfigured by typos and omissions. Mercer's oeuvre is so scattered that it must have been a nightmare to collate, but they should have listened more carefully to the additions and variations in his own recordings, which hold the great truth about him: He was possessed by words.

Especially long ones that propelled rhythms and surprised with rhymes. How about, in the ironically-titled Too Marvelous for Words: "The sweetest words in Keats' and Shelley's lyrics/Aren't sweet enough to be your panegyrics." Mercer was once asked if he and his contemporaries hadn't been in a rut, constantly reworking the one theme of romantic love. He replied "I think we were in a rut, but ours was a more attractive rut." He was right on both counts.

Still there are times when one wishes he would escape the temptation to turn every song into a love-song, as in Whistling Away the Dark where the initial detailed image of a frightened child on his way home from school turns out to be a metaphor for lonely lovers.

It does though exemplify a recurring theme in Mercer's work, one that obviously was personal, and connected to his Georgia roots: a nostalgia -sometimes tender, sometimes scary -for half-remembered childhood things. Its apotheosis of course is Moon River, a song whose familiarity (plus some sugary arrangements) has obscured how brilliant it really is: not just the famous "huckleberry friend" line but the gentle "there's such a lot of world to see", words that linger, implying more than they say.

There's mystery, though never obscurity, in many of his best songs; in One for My Baby, we never discover what the heartbroken drinker's story really is, and we don't need to. Mercer himself was by all accounts a mean drunk, though irreproachably kind and courteous when sober.

The sadness that surfaces in his later lyrics also illuminates his later singing; as a young man he'd limited himself to rhythm and comedy songs (he could be very funny, both as writer and performer) but in the 1970s he trusted himself with ballads, which he sang with a mellow wistfulness that was very moving. What a legacy. What a man.
