Dawn Greenhalgh and Megan Follows play very well together as mother and daughter, which, at the risk of being simplistic, may have something to do with the fact that they are mother and daughter. That is certainly the hook for the Soulpepper production of Marsha Norman"s 'Night Mother, a New York hit in 1983 and a repertory staple ever since.

Closely related the actresses may be, but their styles are very different. That isnt a criticism; generations styles are different. Greenhalgh, in this play at least, is flossy and chatty; Follows, stark and withdrawn and unnervingly logical. The women they play share a house in a small Southern town. The action begins with Jessie, the daughter, informing Thelma, the mother, that she is about to kill herself with her late father's gun. The rest of the play consists of Thelma trying frantically to talk her out of it, and Jessie declining to be talked. It's a contrast between desperation, which spills out of Greenhalghs Thelma, and despair, which Follows hugs tight to herself as an accomplished fact. Both modes have their disadvantages when it comes to communicating with the audience -- Greenhalgh tends to gabble and Follows to mumble -- but both are powerful

Its an artificial piece with the trappings of realism. I should just have written you a note, says Jessie to Thelma, giving up, almost with a shrug, on making her mother see her point of view. And in real life, if that's the appropriate expression, writing a note is just what she would have done; at least, I've yet to hear of a suicide confronting a putative survivor immediately before doing the deed. Typically, they depart, with or without written communication, leaving the survivors to worry forever after about what went wrong and how they might have prevented it. Jessie wants to spare Thelma that, but she only succeeds in giving it to her in concentrated form. And the playwright works in a similar way. The purpose of the drama is less to probe the unknowable--a suicides motivation -- than to show us the essentials of what went on between and around the two women. In this its successful, though on a less than triumphant scale.

Thelma had a loveless marriage, and resents the bond between her husband and her daughter. Jessie's own marriage failed more dramatically; she and her estranged (though apparently still caring) husband have a thieving, drug-taking son. She also has a married brother, whom both she and Thelma seem to regard with, at fondest, amused tolerance. Jessie is also an epileptic, though she insists that her fits have nothing to do with her desire to kill herself. And she has always been reclusive; it seems her mother engineered her marriage in order to get her out of the house.

"You're my child," cries Thelma, at her wits end for arguments. Im what your child has turned into, replies Jessie, with devastating calm. It's a searing exchange, but we really have no means of gauging what changes may have occurred in Jessie over the years. Even by her own account, the seeds of self-destruction were always there. There's a sense, too, in which both women are plain wrong. They have reached the stage where the child becomes the parent, exacerbated by the fact of their living together. Part of the reason for their conversation is for Jessie to instruct her mother in the details of day-to-day survival: a course that Thelma, who isnt exactly senile, resents as impertinent but whose necessity she tacitly acknowledges. The mother, in fact, is about to be orphaned, and dreads it.

The domestic details are splendidly summoned in Ken MacDonalds neatly-furnished kitchen-living-room set, and the play makes us powerfully aware of the restricted lives they both live; they're in a very narrow community. But again, though that may be part of the reason for Jessie's decision, were never really told so. Jessie makes sardonic references to "Red China" but thats about as far as the play gets in acknowledging the outside world. It's true to life, I guess, but it's constricting for the audience as well. Night Mother is one of those plays thats frustrating and compelling at the same time; one of those high-toned American dramas (they generally win Pulitzers as this one did) obsessively, not to say monotonously, focused on the domestic. And it doesn't tell us as much about the psychology of mothers and daughters as it thinks it does: less, actually, than an apparently more extreme and melodramatic play like The Beauty Queen of Leenane.

Greenhalgh's performance, respectably blowsy, seems genuinely rooted in the plays landscape. Followss doesn't: She comes on, not just as more intelligent than her mother but as belonging to another social and intellectual world altogether, and the play suffers from it. The tension, in Alisa Palmer's production, comes and goes; or maybe its just that the audiences absorption in it does; much of it seems, despite the intimacy of the theatre, to be going on a long way away. The moments when the actresses come downstage to get in one another's faces and in ours -- jovially as well as tearfully -- send the dramatic temperature mercifully but too infrequently up. That doesn't count the ending; weve been told its coming for an hour and a half but despite or perhaps because of that, it's a genuine shocker. Nothing surprises like the inevitable.
