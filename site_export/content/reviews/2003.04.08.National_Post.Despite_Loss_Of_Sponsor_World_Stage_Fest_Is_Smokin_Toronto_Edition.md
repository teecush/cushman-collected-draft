GLENGARRY GLEN ROSS

Premiere Dance Theatre, Toronto

A WOMAN IN WAITING

York Quay Studio Theatre, Toronto

- - -

I doubt whether fewer cigarettes will be smoked in Toronto in future merely because the duMaurier name, and with it the duMaurier sponsorship, have been forcibly removed from the World Stage festival. I doubt if many duMaurier customers will even change their brand loyalty. Apparently the state may continue, without hypocrisy, to profit from the sale of tobacco, while forbidding valuable cultural institutions to do so. And one of the most valuable will probably cease to exist. Somebody somewhere must be feeling very virtuous.

Meanwhile, for this year at least, the World Stage show goes on. It even goes on when one of its principal actors has to bail out. Last Wednesday, the first night of David Mamet's Glengarry Glen Ross, from Chicago's Steppenwolf company, was cancelled when one of the cast -- the lead, in so far as this paragon of ensemble plays has one -- had to return home for family reasons. Another Steppenwolf actor, Rick Snyder, was brought in next day to replace him. Steeped in the style but not in the role, he had to carry a script. By Sunday afternoon, when I saw the show, he was in flawless command of the text -- the immaculate succession of starts, stops, hesitations, overlappings and aggressive-defensive obscenities that make Mamet at his best both the supreme stylist and the most cunning naturalist among living playwrights -- but was physically reined- in. It was like seeing what would obviously be a fine performance, with a week still to go before opening night.

Glengarry is the third great U.S. play about that fundamental national figure, the licensed huckster. It completes the trilogy that was begun by Eugene O'Neill's The Iceman Cometh and whose centrepiece, obviously, is Arthur Miller's Death of a Salesman. Indeed, Mamet's whole play might be considered as both an extension and a refinement of a crucial scene in Miller's: the one in which Willy Loman, down on his luck, asks his boss for a break and winds up fired. Mamet's piece gives us four front-line sellers whose business is persuading the unwary to buy what we may safely assume to be worthless properties in Florida. Shelley Levene (a.k.a. the Machine, the Snyder character) was once the champ of them all, and is now the most desperate. But all of them both despise and depend on their office manager (who in turn depends on the unseen bosses above him, as they do on the even more shadowy figure above them).

Like Willy Loman, they all --even the younger, slicked-down hot- shot whose figures should win him a Cadillac -- keep harking back to old heroic days, when men were men and went out and found their own suckers -- sorry, clients -- without needing some jumped-up office boy to point them in the right direction.

A surprising amount of commentary on the play seems prepared to take them at their own valuation: This despite Mamet's own warning that we should never trust the things that people say; that language invariably lies for its own advantage, and the speakers' real feelings can only be glimpsed when they shut up. (Some silent despair at the end of this play makes the point very graphically.) The old order may be giving way to the new, and the new may be more soulless, but there still seems little to choose between them. These guys are in the business of cheating the gullible, and they always were. And however they may talk about old-school loyalties, they will cheat on one another as readily as they will on anyone else. We know why they do it; we don't meet their families as we did the Lomans, but we hear just enough to appreciate the pressures. We can even feel sorry for them.

But the most sympathetic character in the play is, fittingly, the least voluble: A terrified buyer, torn between fear of his wife and a touching loyalty to the man who wants to fleece him. He is beautifully played here by Peter Burns.

Oddly enough, this quintessentially American play was first produced, in 1983, by the National Theatre of Great Britain, which had earned Mamet's trust with its production of his earlier American Buffalo. Steppenwolf, though, has the U.S. patent on the play; and Amy Burton's new production, both aurally and visually perfect, shows why.

It has two settings. One is a strip-mall Chinese restaurant, where you can just about taste the MSG from looking; this is where the characters gather in pairs to bitch, scheme and do business. The other is the office itself, the morning after a break-in; someone has stolen the all-important client list. Seeing the place spectacularly trashed, we also register how it must have looked before, and the combination is explosively funny.

The cast includes David Pasquesi as the star shark with a bewitchingly literate line of patter, Matt DeCaro, Tracy Letts and Alan Wider, and there is no fault in any of them. Each, by the way, is a completely different physical and emotional type to his counterpart in the equally fine and believable English production, which argues a great richness in the play. Everyone's timing is a delight. Words may be as untrustworthy as Mamet says they are, but they can still give great joy; in this play, and in this production, every one of them counts.

- - -

The festival's substantial South African component began with A Woman in Waiting, conceived and written by Yael Farber and Thembi Mtshali. It's an autobiographical one-woman show, a monologue with some music, for Mtshali, a commanding and attractive performer who is in her fifties and who was a lead player in Ipi Tombi, the black African musical that went round the world. She is also a national TV star. Once though, she was a Durban live-in domestic,with no apparent future.

The cruelties of apartheid predictably give the show its greatest impact, hitting harder for coming after a few years of happily sheltered childhood. Perhaps the most moving part tells of the bonds that she could not help forming with the white babies she looked after, while not seeing her own child for a week at a time. (She prayed that, at least, none of her charges would grow up to be a policeman, brutalizing blacks.) She got her break when the daughter of one of her employers heard her singing in the kitchen, and insisted she audition for a show. It's truer than usual to say that the rest is history, and a lot happier than history usually is. Critics in other countries have complained that she slides over some important matters and that she devotes too much time to celebrating her own success. I can agree with the first complaint but not with the second. It seems to me she's earned the right.
