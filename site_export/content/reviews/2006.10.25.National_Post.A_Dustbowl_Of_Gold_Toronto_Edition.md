THEATRE REVIEW

OF MICE AND MEN

Bluma Appel Theatre, Toronto

John Steinbeck's Of Mice and Men, at least in its dramatized version, is one of those works that everybody knows but hardly anybody gets to see. Now, suddenly, two productions are upon us. CanStage have revived it as the opening production of their new season, and there is another revival scheduled next year at Stratford. The Toronto version, a co-production with Theatre Calgary, suggests that the best-laid plans can sometimes work out. Its excellent, and it looks magnificent.

Within the play itself, set among itinerant ranch-hands in the depths of Depression America, hopes are things that are born to be frustrated. They are also contagious. The dream shared by the duo at the heart of the story -- Lennie, the slow-witted giant who seems fated to crush everything he loves from his pet mouse on up, and George, his friend and protector -- communicates itself to almost everyone they meet. They talk, endlessly, about buying their own place; Lennie cant get enough of hearing George tell about the rabbits he'll be able to tend, and -- shades of Tom Sawyer and Huck Finn -- the circuses and ball-games they'll be able to slope off and attend. Candy, the fat crippled hand kept on to clean out the flophouse, overhears them and immediately wants in. Crooks, the black worker turned bitter and suspicious after years of being patronized and excluded by men in desperate need of someone even they can look down on, pours scorn on the idea but is still tempted by it. Curlys Wife, married to the bosss mean and jealous son and branded a tramp by all the men, has her parallel fantasy, one that likely shared by at least half the women in America; she dreams of being swept off to Hollywood and making it in the movies.

Steinbeck wrote Of Mice and Men as a novel and then turned it into a play. It was begging to be one -- the book's dialogue is insistently speakable -- and it took naturally to its new medium. There is no way that, without prior knowledge, you would suspect the stage version of being an adaptation. It is devilishly well- constructed, with all the seeds of ultimate destruction carefully sown and meticulously watered. The climactic encounter is between Curly's Wife and Lennie; they barely know one another, but by the time it happens we can practically prophesy how the meeting is going to go down. We can foresee its aftermath as well. But for all the authors control, and his rightful insistence that these men are what their era has made them, the characters remain people, not puppets; people who, in their isolation and their hopelessness, could easily be our contemporaries.

The play begins with George and Lennie on their way to their next job, and Dennis Garnhums production gives us the necessary time to get acquainted. George loves Lennie, feels responsible for him, and is frequently and inevitably exasperated by him; Shaun Smyth's edgy performance keeps all the shades in view, and moves naturally towards the acceptance of tragic necessity. Ashley Wright brings off a complementary balancing act as Lennie, reaching deeply and affectingly into the trusting recesses of a one-track mind. When they reach the ranch, they are confronted by a further roomful of good performances, most of them by actors new to Toronto: Sterling Jarvis brings a sardonic cackle to Crooks (the play isn't big on laughs but hes responsible for those it has), while Stan Lesks Candy amounts to a plaintive and appealing hill of flab. The scene in which he is pressured into allowing his sick old pet dog to be shot is the play's emotional signature, an emblem of what happens to the helpless, and the production wrings every drop of tension out of it. Candy's physical if not temperamental opposite would have to be the foreman Slim, the most sympathetic character -- in terms of both attracting and extending sympathy -- is played as such by John Kirkpatrick; in an atomized society he is the only person capable of charity. Even the devoted companionship of George and Lennie, which the others think phenomenal, doesnt extend beyond themselves. The only false notes are struck by Lisa Norton who seems to be playing the woman from the mens point of view rather than her own. She plays her character's anger at the expense of her hurt, and adopts a kewpie-doll voice presumably intended as her passport to Tinseltown.

The sets, by John Jenkins and Allan Stichbury, are spectacular: Vast expanses of golden corn (glowingly lit by Kevin Lamotte) majestically accommodate the play's hopeful first scene and its despairing last; in between we get an alternating series of timbered interiors. There's one scene that ends abruptly and confusingly; otherwise the constant changes offer bold statements rather than, as is usually when scenery gets shifted in full view, awkward apologies.
