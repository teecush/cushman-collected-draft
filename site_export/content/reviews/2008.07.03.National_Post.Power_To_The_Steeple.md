FUENTE OVEJUNA

Tom Patterson Theatre

---

The Golden Age of Spanish drama was roughly contemporary with the equivalent era in England, and Lope de Vega's Fuente Ovejuna is its most celebrated product. It's a body of work with a rarefied reputation outside its homeland, though, in my limited experience, when the plays are given in English, they always work. They come across as Shakespearean, only more so. Lope, in this play anyway, offers even more sheer story than Shakespeare, more blatant theatricality, including a lot of music and fighting, a huge relish for each passing moment, and a shifting social viewpoint that goes beyond Shakespearean ambiguity into a kind of populist absolutism. Fuente Ovejuna (literally, "the sheep well") is a play about a village whose inhabitants rise up against a local oppressor. It gives power to the people, and then has them surrender it to their feudal overlords.

The Stratford production, translated and directed by Laurence Boswell, who in Britain has been a pioneering Lope de Vegan for a quarter of a century, is uneven but exhilarating. It's 1476 -- about 130 years before the play itself was written --and a divided Spain is up for grabs; Commander Guzman of Calatrava persuades Rodrigo, the young Grand Master of the province, to throw in his lot with the Portuguese-backed forces of Castile rather than with those of the soon-to-be-celebrated and "most Catholic" monarchs Ferdinand and Isabella. Guzman lets slip that his soldiers are quartered in the tiny town of Fuente Ovejuna, and the second scene transports us there, to meet the really important people.

What we took for the main picture was only a frame, which will gradually dissolve to become part of the canvas. The villagers welcome the victorious Guzman back home with fulsome tributes; he, though, has his eyes on more tangible rewards. A notorious despoiler of peasant womanhood, he now has his sights set on the young and spirited Laurencia; he is only prevented from raping her in a field by the arrival of her admirer Frondoso, who has to flee for his life after rescuing her. He returns when the Commander is called back to battle, and marries Laurencia; but their wedding is interrupted when Guzman too returns, this time in snarling defeat and even more inclined than usual to exercise the Spanish equivalent of droit du seigneur. Frondoso is arrested, Laurencia abducted. She escapes with her honour apparently intact, but her clothes torn and her face bloodied. The villagers take collective action; armed with pitchforks, they storm Guzman's castle, and kill and dismember him. Their Catholic majesties send in a torture squad to investigate; the people outwit them by maintaining, even on the rack, that there is no guilty party: "Fuente Ovejuna did it." Or: It takes a village.

The play is an extraordinary, sometimes disconcerting, mix of comedy and drama. It applauds the villagers' steadfastness, but never seems to question the throne's right to have them tortured in the first place. They themselves may be heroic self-liberators or a vigilante mob. Boswell's staging of their summary justice as a slow-motion battle doesn't really work, but it does fit the play's try-anything spirit. On the other hand, he gets the full force, both frightening and inspiring, of the textbook-famous scene in which Frondoso and Laurencia (who, for reasons of dramatic convenience, have escaped the round-up) overhear their friends and neighbours, including a 10-year-old child, being interrogated. Jonathan Goad and Sara Topham play these militant lovebirds with a blend of playfulness and determination; this may be Topham's best performance, once past an over-emphatic discussion with her best friend, in which role Severn Thompson undergoes a similarly beguiling development.

The star villager, though, may be Mengo, a fat and self-serious lad whose attempts to protect his cousin from the Commander's depredations earn him a pitiless beating, and who goes through the rest of the play offering to bare his buttocks to show the results. "You're the best actor," somebody tells him; that may not be strictly true, but Robert Persichini certainly does superbly in a role that might have been written for him. The other locals range from James Blendick's sonorous patriarch, through Nigel Shawn Williams' easy-going poet manque, to Laird Mackintosh, one of several excellent recruits from Stratford's musicals division, as a bespectacled student who comes home to bemoan the invention of the printing press, or as this translation has it, "information overload." It leads, apparently, to books being published under the names of famous authors who didn't write them; here we may conceivably hear Lope himself disclaiming responsibility for some of the 2,000 plays with which he is credited. The greatest joy of this production may be the way in which it summons a community made up of distinct individuals; their council meetings, on a spare Peter Hartwell set of benches and floor grilles, are very compelling.

As their oppressor-in-chief, Scott Wentworth indulges in too much tooth-baring villainy but has subtler stuff in reserve. Stephen Kent shows nervous promise as a vacillating Grand Master. Stratford royalty Geraint Wyn Davies and Seana McKenna play the Spanish royalty, though neither performance is likely to add substantially to its owner's resume. (McKenna -- something I never thought I would say of her -- comes close to phoning it in.) The pair are depicted as benevolent autocrats, suggesting that Lope was sucking up to the dynasty that sheltered him. This production accepts that, putting a conservative spin on a play that can easily be interpreted as a blazing call to revolution. It isn't the very best show at Stratford this year but it may be the most encouraging. - Fuente Ovejuna runs until Oct. 4.
