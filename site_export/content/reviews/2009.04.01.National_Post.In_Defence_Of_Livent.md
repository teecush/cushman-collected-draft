For 18 months -- the last 18 months, really, of the company's active existence -- I was an employee of Livent Inc. (I came direct from there to the National Post, which may make me the only person to have worked consecutively for Garth Drabinsky and Conrad Black.) My official title at the Toronto-based theater production company was Director of Corporate Communications, which could have meant anything but eventually resolved itself into a cross between a speechwriter and a dramaturg. My time there lasted beyond the suspension of Drabinsky and Myron Gottlieb, but it was Garth who recruited me and it was largely him to whom I reported. And whatever others experiences may have been, I have to say that mine was positive.

I felt valued and appreciated; I worked alongside a phenomenal number of able --and likeable--people; and I believed, and still believe, that the company I worked for was producing good shows.

Its true, I realized early on, that you couldn't have a conversation with Garth that wasn't in some way a conversation about Garth. But once that was accepted, ideas were always welcome. I dont even recall being shouted at. The director of one Livent show, an American, told me that hed come to Toronto with some trepidation, having heard all the stories, but in fact found that he was being treated with nothing but courtesy and encouragement. He was still, he said, waiting for the other shoe to drop. I doubt that it ever did. People considered to be artists and/or intellectuals got considerate treatment.

In the wake of last week's trial verdicts, some pundits have taken to disparaging Livent-- not just in regard to the company's business practices, but its production of large-scale Broadway-style musicals. Starting with its mounting of The Phantom of the Opera from a [London/New York] template, said Rick Salutin in The Globe & Mail last week, Livent promoted theatre that wasnt "live in any meaningful way."

There is a fragment of a point here, but only a fragment.

Id date the international franchising of musicals to 1947, which is when Richard Rodgers and Oscar Hammerstein's Oklahoma! opened in London -- at the Theatre Royal, Drury Lane -- in a replica of its Broadway staging. As one historian has noted, R&H didn't just mail it over; they supervised it in person and insisted that it be done right. The show was enormously successful, and was followed over the next two decades by authentic remounts of other shows from the American musicals unfolding golden age. The best of them were, by general consent, among the most exciting theatre to be seen in London.

It would have been terrible if that had been all there was to the British theatre. But it wasnt. The same period saw the establishment of two major subsidized companies, the National Theatre and the Royal Shakespeare, and the

advent of unstoppable generations of new playwrights. And the same goes for the -- admittedly less distinguished --musicals that have been imported to Toronto, and everywhere else in the world, over the last 25 years: They haven't stopped anything else from happening, either new plays or new companies.

I know people talk as if the success of the megashows drained off attendance from smaller-scale homegrown Canadian productions, but I don't believe it. There isnt much overlap between the audiences in the first place, but those who are interested in both would find their way to both. In Toronto, Phantom ran at the Pantages for 10 years, so one visit would leave plenty of time over for catching other shows.

Personally,Id hate to see Phantom in anything but the original Hal Prince production. But I did want to see it, and all the other international hits before and since, if only to find out what all the fuss was about: a natural human impulse, I think, and not to be despised.

However, Phantom was only the starting point for Livent. Most of its subsequent energies went into the productions of brand-new shows, plus one major revival. These productions -- Kiss of the Spider Woman, Show Boat, Ragtime, Parade -- constitute a track record unmatched by any commercial producer, anywhere, in decades. (Fosse, the remaining show, wasn't in the same league.) Kiss, Ragtime and Parade reached a level of musical expressiveness and dramatic intelligence that leaves later lauded shows such as Wicked or Spring Awakening looking half-starved.

What of the criticism that these were all shows written and staged by Americans, on American subjects and with largely American casts, and all ultimately aimed at New York? Undeniably, Livent in general, and Drabinsky in particular, were dazzled by Broadway and wanted to be part of it. But that doesn't mean Canadian audiences couldnt identify with the shows and their characters. I might also point out that both Brent Carver in Kiss of the Spider Woman and Steven Sutcliffe in Ragtime are Canadian actors, and pillars of the community at that, but it would be disingenous. I too would like to have seen more Canadian actors, and some shows written by Canadians; some of the latter were in development when the company imploded, but I cant say how far they would have been developed.

But where, I wonder, would cultural nationalists draw the line? Would they disapprove of the foreign companies brought over by festivals like WorldStage and Luminato? How about the Stratford and Shaw festivals, where the actors are overwhelmingly Canadian but the basic repertoire is old European?

"Vertical integration" -- owning or building a chain of theatres and filling them with your own product, which was Livent's plan -- depends on generating a guaranteed stream of hits. This may have been possible for the Hollywood studios whose heyday was Livent's professed inspiration, but no theatre company can do it. This doesnt mean, however, that Livents actual shows were soulless or mechanized or developed according to the whims of focus groups. Yes, opinions were solicited (mine among them, if you must know), but then, to quote my favourite line from Gypsy, this is show business; everybody listens to everybody. The Livent process was not essentially different from the workshop method routinely practised by non-commercial theatres. It doesn't produce fail-safe results for them either.

Behind the criticism of Livent, I sense the familiar phrase "telling ourselves our own stories." The implication is that in Canada those stories should always be Canadian. But a story belongs to whoever wants to tell it and whoever cares to listen. Livent's stories, none of them safe or obvious choices for musicals, carried that conviction. The name meant something: Toronto theatre was livelier with Livent than without it.
