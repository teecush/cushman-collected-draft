HAMLET (SOLO)

Theatre Passe Muraille Backspace, Toronto

- - -

A few years ago Robert Lepage came to town with a one-man Hamlet that was very hi-tech and, if memory serves, made occasional surreptitious use of an extra actor. Raoul Bhaneja, offering his own solitary take on the play, has no truck with such extraneous aids. He comes before us, unsupported by tricky lighting or sound effects, a sensitive young man in a black sweater that takes us right back to the old existentialist days of Jean-Paul Sartre. To be, that is the question.

For setting, Bhaneja has a simple brick wall, against which, when one or another of the characters he plays feels specially intense, he bangs his head. In another sense, he's banging his head against a brick wall all night. The question arises, just as it did with Lepage: Whats the point? And the answer comes back the same now as it did then: There isnt one.

It's not as if Hamlet, the character, doesn't provide enough to keep any actor occupied. Even in a conventional production, we have to spend a lot of time in that actors company, and the experience can get very wearing if he doesnt click. Whether he does or he doesn't, one thing that usually makes the evening go faster is the presence of other actors in other roles, adding texture and promoting interplay.

In such circumstances, Bhaneja might make an interesting Hamlet; he is in command of the text, suggests an active mind and has a compelling line in instant hysteria. This surfaces to especial effect just after he's seen the Ghost, and later when he turns, with an unusual degree of spite, on his errant mother. He also offers the occasional new reading. The air bites shrewdly; it is very cold, uttered on the battlements as he awaits his ghostly father, becomes "is it very cold?" This Hamlet is apparently such a skeptic, he cant even trust his own senses. But its hard to see the character or to develop any feeling for him, because he has to keep stopping and turning into other people.

The best of his multiple performances is probably Claudius, who begins as a stereotypically jovial villain but then develops a tormenting conscience that he has to abandon when his talents for conspiracy, plus the need for self-preservation, prove too great to resist. But a man can only do so much, and Bhaneja begins to run out of voices. Some of those he rings in come from the British Isles, where he has in fact worked; his Polonius sounds Welsh while his Rosencrantz and Guildenstern -- Hamlet's fellow students, mark you - - are oafishly Cockney, like heavies from a Guy Ritchie movie. Horatio, by contrast, is so gentle he almost fades into the brickwork.

Visually, Bhaneja manages the instant changes very well. He and his director, Robert Ross Parker, have developed a graceful repertoire of movements and gesture that enables one character's action to merge into the next ones response. Especially striking is the sight and sound of Ophelia sobbing even, it seems, as Hamlet and company leave the stage. The aural hand-overs are trickier; theyre done at the same unnatural speed that a ventriloquist uses to switch voices with his dummy. The text has been keenly and ruthlessly cut (one whole soliloquy gone, and not a sign of Fortinbras) with an eye to comfort and clarity; I still wonder, though, if someone unfamiliar with the play would know what was going on. Probably the most commanding lines are interpolations: Bhaneja's brisk calls, in his own person, for lights, "intermission," and blackout The last carries an inescapable subtext of "There! I've done it." The production is a vanity project, but it differs from most in leaving the impression that the actor, as well as the audience, deserves better.
