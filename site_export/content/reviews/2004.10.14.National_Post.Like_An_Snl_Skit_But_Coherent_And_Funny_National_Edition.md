MATT AND BEN

Poor Alex Theatre, Toronto

- - -

This is a sweet surprise. Matt and Ben is a one-act fantasia about how two struggling young actors surnamed Damon and Affleck might just possibly have come to write an Oscar-winning movie script called Good Will Hunting that also, by some strange coincidence, provided plum roles for them both.

The play is itself a funhouse reflection of the story it tells. It was created by two American actresses, Mindy Kaling and Brenda Withers, who played it themselves at the New York International Fringe Festival and then did it again, very successfully, off- Broadway. The New York director, David Warren, has now re-mounted it in Toronto for macIDeas, the indie-commercial concern that wisely brought us This Is Our Youth, then unwisely brought it back. Two local ladies, Hilary Doyle and Jane Spence, now play the two guys, and play them very well. In fact text, staging and performance reach a collective level of accomplishment and delight that leaves every one of the season's previous openings in the dust.

The way the play tells it, the script for their breakthrough film fell literally into its authors' laps. There they were in Ben's Boston apartment (which, in Michael Gianfrancesco's colourful cock- eyed design, bears a suspicious resemblance to a playroom), working away on a screenplay based on The Catcher in the Rye. Their working methods consist of Matt's reading the book's dialogue aloud and Ben's transcribing it word-for-word on his much-prized computer. Matt expresses some doubts about this, on the lines of "If this is so easy, why hasn't anyone done it before?" But Ben isn't having any.

Then suddenly, down from the sky by way of the ceiling, there appears a typescript bearing the title Good Will Hunting with their own two names attached as authors -- Matt the thoughtful achiever being billed, in defiance of the alphabet, above Ben the blowhard slacker. It seems too good to be true. It also seems as if the play is trying to deny the real-life Damon and Affleck any credit for the work that made them; but, by a twist that it would be unfair to reveal (and that you wouldn't believe if I did, but that makes perfect sense at the time), it manages to have things both ways. Kaling and Withers make fun of Matt and Ben, but they don't actually denigrate them.

You could hardly call the play a satire of the celebrity culture, if only because it's quite explicitly part of that culture itself; I mean, the authors wouldn't be wasting their time writing about a couple of losers, right? Nor is it fair, though it seems popular, to denigrate the piece as an extended Saturday Night Live piece, since it is a) coherent and b) funny. It's a riff based on the assumed resemblances between the two men and the roles they played in the movie, though the play dramatizes this without ever becoming as maudlin or repetitious as the film. Its Matt is no charismatic genius, but he is energetic and ambitious. Its Ben is a goof, but one who has his own agenda and is capable of being hurt.

The play, like every crosstalk comedy written after 1955, has echoes of Waiting for Godot, and there's at least an acknowledgment, in the suggestion that the characters first met when getting up Vladimir and Estragon as a high-school drama exercise. (Matt, typically earnest, asks Ben if he can do a French accent. Ben offers to learn one from his brother Casey, who took French for a year.) It's very acute, and very entertaining, about the jealousies and the frayed but enduring loyalties between two young men who bonded as kids and have come to take their relationship for granted. It's done as cartoon; one of the best sequences shows us the teenage Ben creatively disrupting his buddy's painfully serious rendition of Bridge over Troubled Water. But that doesn't make it any less truthful.

The crucial element in the style is, of course, having women play the roles. Doyle, whose background is in revue, and Spence, whose background is in Shakespeare, have mapped out a perfectly proportioned middle ground that accommodates the best of both. Indeed you might say they pass on into one another's territory. Doyle's fair-haired Matt is the more detailed and inward performance, Spence's bumptious brunette Ben the more extrovert, outsize in enthusiasm, outsize in hurt, though never ballooned out of proportion. (I have a sneaking and possibly unreasonable respect for Affleck as an actor, based on the fact that I first saw him in Shakespeare in Love, in which he sported not only an excellent English accent -- presumably not learned from Casey -- but a real sense of style.)

Each of the actresses gets to play one other famous figure, while her principal character is offstage somewhere, either on an errand of mercy or having a huff. Spence appears as Gwyneth Paltrow, urging Matt to follow her own example and go for it, whatever it is. The funny thing is, you don't at this point think of Spence as a woman playing a woman; you think of her as a woman playing a man playing a woman. But then the last thing I saw her in was As You Like It.

Doyle's apparition is a terminally crotchety JD Salinger, apprising Ben of the extreme improbability of he or anyone else getting the rights to Catcher or to anything else in its author's legendarily slender oeuvre. This, I suppose, is where Ben grows up. "No wonder no one's ever done it before," he muses, or words to that effect. I found myself thinking what a blessing it is that nobody has ever been allowed to turn the book into a musical (I bet they've asked), since it would undoubtedly come out as You're a Good Man, Holden Caulfield.

Whether they're playing male or female, singly or together, the performers' timing is of exemplary crispness, as is the production as a whole. Its ending is surprisingly touching, and it only takes 70 minutes to get there.
