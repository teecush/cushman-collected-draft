THEATRE REVIEW

FACTORY THEATRE

---

Florence Gibson's new play Missing is based on an earlier radio play of hers that in turn took off from an actual incident: the 1988 disappearance, still unsolved, of a woman named Lois Hanna from her home in Ontario. Gibson has changed many things. The historical Lois was in her twenties and single but her theatrical equivalent, Evelyn MacMillan, is in her thirties, wife of a London-area farmer and mother of three. The time has been moved back to 1974, presumably chosen as a date when contemporary feminism was still in its birth-throes and relations between the sexes that much starker. Evelyn may have been abducted, perhaps killed; she may have chosen to leave a family setup she found insupportable. The play's emotional thrust is towards the latter conclusion; but we are never definitively told.

Alongside the aftermath on the farm and the attendant police investigation, the play explores related relationships in local locations. Carol Seaforth, the detective assigned to the case, is herself married to a schoolteacher, their still-young relationship being in a precarious place. Their most immediate disagreements concern which of them has sacrificed most and whether or not they'll have children, but these are aggravated by his jealousy over the time she's spending with the bereaved Trevor MacMillan-- who, as it happens, is at least as hostile as he is unhappy.

We also spend time in a roadside diner, presided over by Janine, a salty dispenser of eggs, fries and ancestral wisdom, whose sole prop and assistant is Freddy, an elderly man whom she habitually addresses as though he were mentally backward (which he probably is) and devoid of feelings (which he isn't). They are, though, fond of one another. Onto the premises wanders a young woman calling herself Elaine, whom Janine promptly engages as a cook. Elaine speaks vaguely of a possibly abusive boyfriend, but gives very little else away. She also keeps changing hair styles and colourings, and eventually has plastic surgery: all of which encourages the suspicion, never fully verified, that she may be the errant Evelyn.

Gibson writes exceptionally colourful dialogue, and has some piercing insights into her chosen theme of domestic entrapment (of both genders), how or whether to escape it, and at what cost But she may have set herself an insoluble problem. Asking why Evelyn left turns out to be as fruitless as asking why Godot never arrived, and without comparable compensations. If Evelyn isn't Elaine, we know nothing about her: Her nearest-if-not-

dearest give away more than they realize, but it's still second-hand. If she is Elaine, we also know nothing about her, since Elaine very much isn't telling. And detective Carol's increasing tendency to project herself onto one or both of them gets us very little further.

The actors occasionally line up as a chorus, chanting or murmuring local gossip. It's an awkward device that feels as if it were left over from the radio version (though I probably wouldn't have liked it much on radio, either). It hints, though, at a greater formality than either the play or David Ferry's strangely muffled production manages to encompass. What seems to be an abstract pattern of feelings and ideas is superimposed on a naturalistic piece of storytelling drawn, after all, from life; and the two modes defeat one another. There are gestures towards stylization in the direction. Doors open and close without anyone passing through them. (Could this be a homage to the offstage slamming door of A Doll's House, most famous of all plays about domestic departure?) Props are minimal, and one character gets handed two bottles of invisible liquid purporting to be Coca-Cola. Gillian Gallow's set is dominated by a spiral tree, the second Factory design (following on Toronto the Good) to feature an image of no practical use to the play. A fight is staged with punches visibly pulled. Probably less purposeful in its artifice is the lack of contact, erotic or otherwise, in the scenes between the cop and the farmer, who hardly seem in the same space. Carol cannot have been intended to be as cold and shallow as Fiona Highet makes her seem. As Trevor, Alan Van Sprang shouts a role that's improbably articulate to begin with, and loaded with confusing subplot, though he does come through for the play's best scene, a broadcast appeal for his wife to return "so everything will be the same" -- which of course would be the problem.

There's a pervasive belligerence about the performances: especially Emma Hillier's as the farmer's eldest daughter, another of those sullen, sulky teenagers who are becoming a stage cliche, and Shauna Black's Elaine, opaque however you take her. Kyra Harper has the warmest and wittiest writing as Janine, though she gabbles some of it, and is probably too striking and eccentric a presence to be the point of balance the play seems to require. Guy Bannerman, though, makes a pleasing Freddy -- who, in the spirit of equality, makes a brief getaway of his own--and Andrew Gillies is excellently bewildered, resentful and furious, in that order, as the teacher without a pet. - Missing runs until April 5.
