The Thrill

Studio Theatre, Stratford

Judith Thompsons The Thrill is a play that holds the attention while gradually forfeiting belief. It's stronger on speeches than on scenes, and has a large structural flaw. But it is extremely well acted.

Its protagonist is elora, selfdescribed "bedpan crip"; she's been in a wheelchair for the whole of what would otherwise have been her physically active life. In other ways, though, shes exceptionally active, indeed an activist. Shes a lawyer, and a crusader for the rights of the disabled. Top of her agenda are the right not to be sentimentalized or patronized, and the right not to be put out of what others might call her misery.

The first has led her to a years-long denunciation of Jerry Lewis and his telethons. The second leads her, in the play's present tense, to wheel herself onto the platform where a euthanasia advocate named Julian is making a book-plugging speech and to denounce him.

Elora is based on Harriet Johnson, a similarly handicapped lawyer and driving force of the Not dead yet movement, and the play is set in her hometown of Charleston, S.C.; though nothing specific, apart from the paying of respects and the occasional distant sound of the blues, is made of this. Julian is more loosely based on Peter Singer, an ethics philosopher who once shared a stage with Johnson at Princeton. They were, obviously, on opposite sides of the debate but they found themselves respecting and even liking one another. Thompson takes the relationship several degrees further. She has elora and Julian fall in love.

There are two other characters. One is Francis, elora's gay male nurse and devoted friend and confidant. The other is Hannah, Julians mother who is in failing physical and mental health and who, by convenient coincidence, is also living in Charleston, having arrived there by a circuitous route; she spent much of her married life in Ireland, where she bore three children including Julian, but she herself was born in england This distances Julian from the real Peter Singer, who is Australian; Julian also differs in not being a career academic but rather one who has chanced onto the college circuit by writing a single blockbuster book. The book was entitled Wheelbarrow, that being the vehicle in which his crippled younger sister was transported during her short, agonized life. He feels guilty for not having put an end to her suffering (and also probably guilty about feeling guilty). He has no desire, however, to abridge his mothers life; but why, even by his own professed standards, should he? She doesn't appear demented, merely crotchety and inclined to imaginary conversations. So Julian's double standards arent quite the irony the play seems to intend.

Elora and Julian find some common ground in their detestation of the institutions fate that she dreads for herself and her friends, and that Julian fears for his mother. That, though, wouldnt necessarily fuel a romantic relationship, and from the moment Julian, impressed by elora's intrepidity, shows up at her place and asks for a date, the play starts to feel forced and schematic. Both of them, of course, find their initial positions modified, though not by contact with one another, more by the ineluctable pressures of life. There's a suggestion, if only in the title, that what seems to be eloras sexual initiation hastens her decline. Appropriately enough in this Stratford season, she evinces a desire like Othello to "die upon a kiss"; theres a further connection with the other current studio play

Taking Shakespeare in that both present testy, indomitable, lonely women fighting impossible odds.

Except that elora isn't all that lonely. She has Francis; and each of them admits that the other is the most important person in their life, even though Francis has an offstage partner with whom he seems very happy. elora and Francis have a more credible and interesting relationship than elora and Julian; so, to a lesser extent, do Julian and his mother. The play is, on its own showing, skewed; livelier at its edges than at its supposed centre.

Lucy Peacock is terrific as elora, the hunched immobility of her limbs offset by the darting intensity and crackling wit of her voice, her mind and her eyes. Robert Persichini embodies unsentimental devotion as Francis who was once an actor and is now, professionally and otherwise, a moderately camp counsellor; acting and writing between them redeem what could easily be a stereotype. Patricia Collins' Hannah takes beautifully unforced of a last speech, chronicling the uneventfulness of her life, that shows Thompson at her best, pitiless and compassionate at once. everybody in this play gets to soliloquize; Julians monologue doesnt save him from being an authorial puppet, but Nigel Bennett animates him very well. dean gabourie's production welds the performances sensitively together, and also supplies some visual flash, which doesn't hurt.
