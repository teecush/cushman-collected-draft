Some things look too good to be true, or too attractive to come out right. Boardwalk Empire was one of them: all that booze, all that dancing, all those gangsters, all those 1920s fashions, all that Atlantic City, all that sex. The Borgias is another: all that poison, all those brawls and banquets, all those corrupt cardinals and guzzling grandees, all those Renaissance frocks, all that Rome, all that sex.

Boardwalk Empire, after a disappointing start, did eventually pull itself up by its bootstraps and become an approximation of what wed hoped it would be. The Borgias, which has the cords of innumerable ecclesiastical robes on which to elevate itself, may manage a similar improvement over the season. Judging, though, from its double-length series premiere and conventionally proportioned second episode, I can't say I'm hopeful.

Like Boardwalk Empire, which had Martin Scorsese among its producers, The Borgias has a famous film director in its corner. The show is Neil Jordans baby. Like Scorsese, he directed the first episode. Unlike Scorsese, he also wrote it, and the eight that follow it. His direction here is standard TV historical, very solemn and scenic; so, and rather more damagingly, is his writing. The smarts of The Crying Game are nowhere to be heard.

The show is advertised as the story of the original crime family and were meant to think of the Borgias, whose patriarch bribed and intrigued his way to the papal chair, as ancestors of the Corleones and the Sopranos. Those families' writers, though, were spared any stylistic battles between ancient and modern; they made the current vernacular poetic. The Borgias, ploddingly prosaic, tries instead to creep us out by telling us, over and over again, how recognizably evil these 15th-century priests and nobles were. And, look, they had sex, too.

We start, in 1492 (now, there's a year) with Pope Innocent VIII on his deathbed, lamenting that neither he nor the church he represents have lived up to his chosen name. Lurking round a pillar is Jeremy Irons as Cardinal Rodrigo Borgia; skulking round another pillar is Colm Feore as his arch-enemy Cardinal Della Rovere, whos nasty but incorruptible; and peering round a third is seasonpremiere guest star Derek Jacobi. He plays a cardinal more venerable than the others but no more trustworthy.

Borgia, a Spaniard, is an outsider in Rome and hated for it; he also, despite his vows, has fathered two grown sons, Cesare and Juan, and a growing daughter, the laterto-be-celebrated Lucrezia. But then, as one of his fellow cardinals, says, "Let he who is without children cast the first stone." (That, by the way, is as good as the dialogue gets.)

Irons, whos the best reason for watching the show, excels at the silky, ambiguous pronouncement, and at conveying, through pouchy cheeks and darting eyes, that he's burning with three conflicting emotions at the same time. When Irons is on fire, he glows. He hasn't, though, communicated that heat to the rest of the family. His sons (Francois Arnaud and David Oakes) are indistinguishable, despite one being a priest and the other a soldier; and with Lucrezia (Holliday Grainger) its too soon to tell. She may grow up to justify her, possibly spurious, reputation as the most lethal of the Borgias, but at the moment she seems no more than spoiled, though with an unhealthy attachment to her priestly brother Cesare. The old combination: incest and incense.

Theres also the usual complement of degenerate princelings, including the stone-deaf king of Naples and his mincing son. But nobody comes to life, though Sean Harris tries hard as a dedicated assassin who knows the value of hanging out with cooks.

It must all, what with sets and costumes and extras, have cost bundles; and I may go on watching, from a residual interest in the subject and in some of the actors. Joanne Whalley, for example, plays the ex-mistress, and I'm intrigued by the prospect of Steven Berkoff, who was once TV's best Hitler, as the blistering friar Savonarola, who cast all vanities into the fire and was finally cast there himself. But really, if you want literate and involving drama on a Sunday night, best to stick with A&E's Breakout Kings.

The Borgias premeries April 3 at 10 p.m. on Bravo!
