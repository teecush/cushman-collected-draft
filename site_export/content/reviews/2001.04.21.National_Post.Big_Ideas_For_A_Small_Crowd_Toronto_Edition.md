HOW IS IT THAT YOU WANT TO LIVE?

Canadia Dell'Arte Theatre

- - -

The question How Is it That You Want To Live? is posed by a blind architect to one of his lodgers. It is almost the only thing he says in the play, though he does also ask the same man how he would feel if he saw a hippo coming down the street. Most of the time, inscrutable behind his eye-shades, he sits immobile, surrounded by empty grandeur. That is, he doesn't seem to have much furniture besides his chair, but he does possess a Henry Moore, or anyway a sculpture with a hole in it.

He may remind the drama-Harries among us -- that's a phrase a friend of mine once coined for people like me who obsessively compare plays to other plays -- of Hamm, the sightless chairbound protagonist of Samuel Beckett's Endgame. Like Hamm, he has a male companion to -- for want of a better term -- interact with and two other dependents living in confined conditions.

Hamm kept his parents in dustbins. Balthazar, this play's master builder, is the unknowing host to a married couple who look to be in their thirties and have taken up residence in Balthazar's crawl- space, which surprisingly affords them room to cook, eat and sleep. This is Beckett with a socio-economic context; someone even mentions the possibility of getting a job at K-Mart, though without much enthusiasm.

The piece is written, directed, designed (quite strikingly) and one-quarter performed by Peter Reitzel, who heads the Canadia dell'Arte Theatre, a troupe who are certainly Canadian and definitely arty. They have their own space and, judging from my own three or four brushes with them, a very select audience. There were 10 people in the house on Thursday and, in my experience of the Canadia dell'Arte, ten's a crowd. They don't seem all that concerned about communicating, even with the audience they do have.

This is true on the most basic technical level; Dennis Frey as Balthazar puts little energy even into the few, presumably significant, lines he does have. The company describe their play as "not so much a comedy as it is a reality." They add, "it is a battle of the ego deciding whether to side with the superego or the id." This, though probably meant to be helpful, is alarming. All the same, they have something.

The stage is divided between Balthazar's room and the couple's, and what happens in the latter is interesting and, given the premise, believable. Mr. Reitzel plays Richard, who understandably doesn't like being called Rich and who at the beginning of the day has a job, which he speedily loses. His wife, Elizabeth, is played by Julie Reitzel, who I take is Peter's wife, and they convey a powerful sense of intimacy. She has literary pursuits -- at least she is constantly either reading or writing -- and the deal seems to be that he will support her and help her pay off her student loan.

Naturally, this proves progressively more difficult; Christmas Day finds them reduced to sharing a couple of pieces of bread. Affection and resentment chase one another 'round and 'round as he sinks into lassitude and she into bitterness; the slightest thing will set them off but nothing conclusively erupts. The two Reitzels manage the subdued tensions very well, though -- as seems to be the way of this company -- they crack whenever something more extroverted is required.

On the other side of the wall the legitimate tenant, Edward (Michael Chipman, whose performance is indeed chipper but never quite takes), tries to make some kind of contact with his landlord. He puts on a periwig and reads to him from what may be a Restoration comedy; the dressing-up aspect is wasted on Balthazar, of course, but Edward is probably doing it for his own benefit. He also has a long, and pretty good, speech about cocaine.

The upshot seems to be that society is a house built by a melancholy man who may have good intentions but cannot see what he is doing, and in which artists -- if that is what Richard and Elizabeth are -- can only hope to scratch out an existence in a corner, on sufferance and unobserved. If they can accept that, they may not do too badly; Elizabeth says she's happy.

Occasionally, the two worlds collide; the couple tiptoe past the unseeing Balthazar on their way in and out, and once Elizabeth pauses to bestow a kiss on him. There is a confrontation half-way through that I totally failed to understand; maybe it's a dream (the production boasts a phantasmagoric score).

The ending is quite remarkably abrupt: when we finally grasp that the proceedings are over, we drift out without being detained by anything as vulgar as a curtain-call. Like the company's 1999 production of Ibsen's Ghosts, this new home-grown piece veers between scrupulous naturalism and portentous vagueness. The former is much preferable, but I doubt if the company see it that way.
