THEATRE REVIEW THE BAKER'S WIFE

Monday, February 19, 1996

ROBERT CUSHMAN

WRITTEN BY JOSEPH STEIN AND STEPHEN SCHWARTZ Directed by Todd Stewart At the Studio Theatre, Harbourfront, until Feb. 24 Rating: Musical slight but roles charming BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail THERE is a category of Broadway show that devotes much of its energy to presenting cross-sections of some kind of enclosed community, real or fantastic. I think of it as the Merry Villagers Musical. It's an unruly category whose denizens, either sentimental or satirical, include Brigadoon, Plain and Fancy, Anyone Can Whistle, Lil Abner and The Most Happy Fella. The masterpiece of the genre, if you dont count Porgy and Bess, is Fiddler on the Roof, which owes much of its power to the staging of Jerome Robbins and, as is less often noted, to the supremely skillful libretto furnished by Joseph Stein.

In the 1970s, some 10 years after Fiddler, Stein wrote the book for The Baker's Wife, based on the celebrated 1938 movie with a script by Marcel Pagnol, the writer who for 20th-century popular purposes invented Provence. It's another village show, set in the 1930s. It echoes Fiddler (and quite a few other musicals) by starting with a company number thats three parts exposition, one part homespun philosophy. It describes the community and introduces its members, with particular emphasis on those, a majority, who are quarrelling with one another. These are people with hearty appetites: for liking and disliking, for drinking and especially for eating.

For some time they have lacked a local baker and their lives, it seems, have been desolate. Now, however, a new one is on the way. When he arrives he turns out to be an ungainly middle-aged man with a beautiful young wife. One of the local young men seduces and runs off with her. After a short period, during which the quality of the village bread supply declines precipitately, she returns. Her wounded but devoted husband still loves her, she really loves him. Thats all. It's touching, but it's throughly predictable and extremely slight.

When the musical was first conceived, it was planned that Zero Mostel would play the Baker. Mostel created the role of Tevye in Fiddler and as it turned out, it was another celebrated Tevye, the Israeli actor Topol, who actually got to play the Baker in the production that was aimed at Broadway. When the show was in trouble on the road, the producers brandished the ultimate out-of-town weapon: They called in Robbins. Presumably they wanted him to firm up the shows sense of place, to give it more of the strength of Fiddler. Despite his ministrations, the show never made it to New York; and structurally it must have been a hopeless case.

There is no way of expanding the central story, and the more you do with the supporting cast the more unbalanced the show seems. However hard you try, the ensemble of earthy Innkeeper, raffish Aristocrat, cynical Teacher, pious Priest, spinsterish Housekeeper remain exactly what those labels suggest: a collection of types. The more you try to flesh them out, the more insistently superficial they become. They can only come to life if they have a strong central drama to react to. Fiddler gives it to them in spades, personal and political; The Bakers Wife leaves them gasping.

Despite this, the show has acquired a cult reputation. It has the courage of its own innocence, which gives it charm, and it has the best score that Stephen Schwartz (Godspell, Pippin) ever wrote. His lyrics, though never painful, remain earthbound, as opposed to genuinely earthy which is what they are trying for, but his music has size and occasional sweetness; on the Broadway-to-contemporary scale it clocks in somewhere between John Kander and Andrew Lloyd Webber. One song, Meadowlark, sung by the heroine at the moment of temptation, has become an underground cabaret classic; I find it rather studied, but it does take the stage and enables the actress to do the same. Unfortunately, having sung it she sinks back into anonymity; her role is drastically underwritten.

Equity Showcase's necessarily miniature production at Harbourfront might seem the ideal solution, defusing false expectations. There are some nice performances and the rare treat of unamplified voices. But the piece seems barer than ever. It was written big, in intention at least, and it needs the help of size. It needs strings in the ballads instead of just piano and, to prove we're in France, accordion. It needs evocative sets and choreography thats more than standard-order jolly. It needs faces and voices that are older, stronger and more weatherbeaten than those on offer here. Nobody is really at fault, but its a no-win situation.

Not, however, a wholly unrewarding one. I was glad to see the show, and especially pleased with the performance of George Pothitos as the Baker. The character is a sensitive cuckold, a type that goes back at least to Moliere, but here transcends type; he is self-conscious about his love for his wife but absolutely strong in it, and in his reaction to the villagers' clumsy (and self-interested) attempts to help and comfort him, he becomes a hero, dignified and delicate, arousing sympathy without ever begging for it. Carol McCartney cannot bring his spouse to similar life, but she looks good and sings agreeably as, to a lesser degree, does Ian Simpson as her remarkably unlikable lover. And the show does take a firm hold on one of the primal passions; it is difficult, after all, to resist the aroma of good French bread.
