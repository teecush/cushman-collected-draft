One of Robert Lepages finest but least celebrated qualities is his humour. There are scenes in Needles and Opium that are the funniest in any Canadian play in ages. They owe their effectiveness, in roughly equal proportions, to Lepages writing, to his understated direction (the adjective isn't one he usually attracts, but it's appropriate) and to the performance of Marc Labreche, playing the role that the maestro himself created when the work first appeared, more than 20 years ago.

The role is that of a Quebecois actor, whose bilingualism has got him hired as narrator for a documentary film, shot in Paris, about the visit to that city in 1949 (just 40 years before his own trip) of Miles Davis and about the trumpeters affair there with the chanteuse Juliette Greco. We watch our hero, at the mike, expressing his frustration with both the script and the direction hes been given. We see him trying to place transatlantic calls from his hotel, and to get the lovemaking couple in the next room to keep the noise down, and being further frustrated by operators and receptionists on both continents. We see him visiting a hypnotherapist and being sidetracked into giving a hilarious summary of the history of his home province.

The reason for his seeking help, and for his being on edge generally, is that he's coping, or failing to, with a recent breakup; his lover has left him for someone and somewhere else, the somewhere being New York. This makes him feel a special and wrenching empathy with Davis who, we are told, broke up with Greco, and returned to the Big Apple, because he wanted to spare her the pains of an interracial romance. A second actor, Wellesley Robertson II, appears as Davis, and his story is acted out in counterpoint and in flashback. Those terms, though, hardly do justice to the physical and technological wonders that, more than the jokes, are the selling points of Lepage's work in general and of this piece in particular.

The narrator and his hotel rooms are encased, in Carl Fillions design, within a spinning cube that moves all over the stage, testing Labreches ability to keep his physical as well as his histrionic balance. Meanwhile Robertson's Davis prowls the streets of a Manhattan evoked by a stunning combination of documentary film footage, solid and perfectly dovetailed scenic elements, and his own recorded music is phenomenal. Labreche begins the play contemplating the merits of acupuncture; Davis, whose points of visual reference range from Grand Central to a pawnshop, seeks solace in the more lethal needles of heroin. Robertson also perfectly mimes his trumpetplaying; a coda finds him, cleaned-up and back in Paris, recording the improvised soundtrack for Louis Malle's Lift to the Scaffold.

So much for the needles. The opium is supplied by Jean Cocteau, on another feted postwar trip, this time from Paris to New York, and also played by Labreche. His scenes occasion their own share of visual marvels, re-creating some famous photographs in theatrical terms, but they arent as satisfying a fit, partly because Labreches French-Canadian imitation of a French-French accent lacks the casual grace, not to mention the comprehensibility, of the rest of his performance. The show's manner may in the end be more compelling than its matter, but we have a couple of superlative performances in a superlative production: one for which I return to that word understatement. One of the charms of Lepage's work is that it seems to take its own magic for granted. It has us gasping in amazement without gasping itself.

The Gay Heritage Project brings together three fine actors - Damien Atkins, Paul Dunn, Andrew Kushnir - who also have solid track-records as playwrights, to investigate the implications of their own title. They dont arrive at any final definitions of either "gay" or heritage (even "project" turns out to be problematic) but I doubt if they expected to. What, intriguingly, they present is an autobiographical show that (mostly) lifts the curse of self-indulgence. Each presents passages of his own life, including his own coming-out,

while also examining those of others. In one especially effective sequence, each in turn gets to depict one of his colleagues, while the one presented looks authoritatively on. (I think that this should come nearer to the end of the show than it does). The trio only perform together in song-and-dance interludes; otherwise the show is composed entirely of solo turns, though each actor may in that time play many parts. Kushnir, whose background is Ukrainians, conducts a dialogue with two gay men in the old country who can hardly understand the freedom he has attained in Canada. Dunn (Scottish-Irish) counterpoises his own experiences with the exponentially grimmer ones of homosexuals in Nazi Germany. But its Atkins (Australian, which I'd never suspected) who makes the most spectacular impression. In a spoof of The Wizard of Oz, he plays all the parts, Toto included, finally identifying the inscrutable Michel Foucault as the man behind the curtain; he details the frustrations of being typed as a gay actor, remarking that actors are never denied roles because they seem too straight (I'm not sure he's right about that); he finds himself, in what may be the most self-critical item of the evening, on board a metaphorical freedom bus, surrounded by members of minorities that overlap with his

own, thus raising the question of how anyone defines themselves. Atkins has become an actor of great versatility and extraordinary authority, most notably and recently as the hero of Angels in America, whose unsought quest might function as a supplement to the one he and his colleagues undertake here. Or maybe, since Angels is indisputably the larger work, the other way around.

Needles and Opium runs through Dec. 1 at the Bluma Appel Theatre, canadianstage.com; The Gay Heritage Project at Buddies in Bad Times through Dec. 8.
