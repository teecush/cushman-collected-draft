PATIENCE

Canadian Stage Company

At least Jason Shermans 20th century Job doesnt get boils. (I apologise for making the play, now revived by Canadian Stage, sound so dated, but it is all of two years old.) His physical health remains good throughout, but he takes other knocks that reflect those of his Biblical prototype.

He loses his job to corporate politics, his wife and children to divorce, and his brother to death. Not surprisingly, he spends a fair amount of time asking "why me?" The only answer he gets from the other characters, or from the play itself, is why not? Which is fair enough but, at the almost three-hour length at which we get to hear it, frustrating.

Reuben, the hero of Patience, is an executive working in, and often by, cellphones. He is shafted by his colleagues when he fails to push a new Korean deal through fast enough; he also seems to have diverted company funds to his own use. His wife throws him out when she discovers his 10-year-old love letters to another woman.

Around this fateful time he runs into his old flame's husband who is putting the rat race behind him and flying off to a new life as an independent movie-maker. The implication is that Reuben had better follow his own dream before it's too late. This subsequently turns out to have been a mystical encounter, since his friend has actually been dead for a year.

The Job parallel is neatly signalled in the plays title, but Im still not sure that I would have got the point if I hadn't read the publicity. However, since I have: one thing Reuben doesn't lose is his faith, because unlike Job he never had any. He does, late in the play, meet a rabbi who has an exhaustingly roguish sense of humour; asked the perennial question about the unfairness of things, he pretends to put in a call to the Almighty who of course isnt telling. (Theres been no mention of Reuben's Jewishness up to this point and there's very little after.) He is also the means of reuniting Reuben with his old love, demonstrating that life is governed by coincidence, though theyre not always the coincidences youd like.

This has been a sound theatrical principle ever since Oedipus struck a man to the ground without looking where he was going. In this play, though, it's linked not to an implacable divine plan but to a lot of talk about the uncertainty principle and chaos theory. Philosophically these are very plausible. They clash, though, with the structure of what is still, essentially, a morality play. Job knew whom to blame, even though his complaints got a dismissive answer. Reuben just flails around in a context in which anything can happen and does, over and over.

He is split dramatically between Mr Everyman and a specific businessman and husband. In the former incarnation he floats around between three women, a ruefully amusing embodiment of the masculine conviction that the grass is always greener.

He has a virtuoso speech in which he tries to describe his marriage and keeps tripping himself up, unwilling to sound either too negative or too positive.

In the latter he is drawn in the broadest and most unhelpful strokes. When his wife turns on him to list all his marital faults she could be right, she could be wrong. Since his marriage has never been dramatically there in the first place we have no way of telling.

We learn a little more of his professional life, mainly that he was a driven and ambitious bastard. We first see him playing squash with the man who will soon unseat him. Actually we don't quite see it; the actors are glimpsed in silhouette, on a screen. Its a nice fancy effect to start the show but it left me too busy trying to sort out who was who to pay any attention to what they might be saying. The hero, though, keeps referring back to it as a crucial scene, the one where everything started to go wrong.

Since the play is a long and ambitious essay on important- sounding themes, it is already being hailed as a masterwork. It does possess intelligence, and some wit.

Some of the jokes are irritating but others are funny, though the best, whose punch-line is craftily withheld for half an hour, seems to be traditional.

It also has some clever structural strokes, unexpected jumps between past and present. Joseph Zieglers production throws in a few self-conscious tricks -- one scene is played with cross-gender casting though if there was any point to this beyond a fashionable one I didn't get it. (It produces some very bad acting.) Otherwise it's a straightforward, somewhat laboured, naturalistic staging in unlovely sets.

A nice crack about Ikea is spoiled by furniture that looks nothing like.

Jim Mezon as Reuben starts out with a fine blend of innocence and brutality but then has to go on repeating it. The women are little more than male fantasies, played, in ascending order of conviction, by Brenda Robins, Brenda Bazinet and Nicole Libin. Victor Ertmanis doubles as Reubens nemesis and his surviving brother and hardly differentiates between them.

David Storch does three roles, with style. The play at least sends us away with something to hum: For All We Know (the 30s standard, not the '70s impostor) hauntingly summarizing that uncertain feeling: "For all we know we may never meet again -- tomorrow may never come, for all we know."

It confirms Dylan Thomas' opinion that all the wisdom of the world lies in the lyrics to popular songs. They do it much quicker too.
