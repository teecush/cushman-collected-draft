Alan Bennett's first play, Forty Years On, contains a detachable monologue about T.E. Lawrence. It describes him, in the anguished aftermath of the First World War, striding through London in his Arab headdress, flowing robes and scimitar, "in which uniform -- I quote from memory here -- he hoped to pass unnoticed in the Charing Cross Road." "Who am I? he would cry out in his despair. To which passersby would reply, You are Lawrence of Arabia. And I claim my (ps)5."

Some years before Bennett, another English playwright, Terence Rattigan, had given Lawrence a play of his own, a biographical piece titled Ross. The climax of that was the protagonist's capture and rape by the Turks, and his horrified realization that he had enjoyed it; horrified because it was not just his body that had been violated but his sense of his own -- to use the word fairly literally -- integrity. He had thought he was untouchable. The same incident is recounted, and given much the same interpretation, in a new Canadian play, The Oxford Roof Climbers Rebellion, by Stephen Massicotte.

The enigmatic, charismatic Lawrence is an obvious candidate for treatment on stage (and, of course, on film) but the narcissistic, masochistic Lawrence can also be a tough sell. Massicottes play shows us the postwar Lawrence as a fellow of All Souls College, where one of his fellow fellows is another young vet, poet Robert Graves, still in a state of intermittent shell shock from his experiences on the Western Front. The Oxford vice-chancellor, Lord Curzon, also happens to be the Foreign Secretary, so he is doubly in authority over them: certainly over Lawrence, who despite his protestations of disillusion and disengagement, is still involved with the British mandate in Arabia and Mesopotamia.

Graves is incensed by the mass sacrifice of his slaughtered comrades, Lawrence not so much by that as by his government's breaking of the promises he made to the Arabs whom he had roused to fight for them. In Lawrence's pre-war student days, he and his pals had climbed the highest, most dangerous towers in Oxford for the fun of it; now he and Graves (an experienced rock-climber) go climbing for a purpose. Lawrence goads and infuriates Curzon by hanging an Arab flag from the college spire. He and Graves also stage a pre- situationist protest involving a herd of deer, and issue manifestos appropriating lines from Gravess poems.

Or misappropriating them: That is the view of Gravess wife, Nancy, with whom he keeps a grocery store. Nancy is a more pragmatic progressive: socialist and feminist and suspicious of the possibly erotic bond between her husband and his friend.

The Graves's marriage is another line of plot; so are Curzon's attempts to get either man to deliver the address at what seems to be the first-ever Remembrance Day service. There is also Jack, Lawrences manservant, roused to murderous rage -- first at Curzon, then at Lawrence -- when his soldier son is killed in a Mesopotamian ambush. And looming behind everything, of course, is the birth of the current Middle East; in the later scenes words like "oil and jihad" and "insurgency and Iraq" are voiced, the last inducing a perceptible audience shiver.

That is a lot to cram into one short play. This one manages to get everything in on a narrative level, and seems in retrospect impressively shaped, but it doesnt carry much weight in actual performance. Scenes are rushed, issues crowded and characters rarely come to life. Here's Lawrence, striking attitudes, one of which is an acknowledgment that he strikes attitudes; there's Graves, having his meltdowns; the others just wander on and off as needed.

Nor does the plays style always match its ambitions. There are some barbed exchanges, as the setting and characters seem to demand, but some of the writing is sloppy. What does Curzon mean when he says that Lawrence "was veritably dismissed from the Paris peace talks? (Virtually," perhaps?)

Richard Roses direction doesn't supply much tension, and though Tom Rooney's Lawrence ("Ted" to friends) has the right blend of agony and mischief, his performance, which should drive the play, has a faltering rhythm. Jonathan Crombie does all that seems required of Graves, and Michelle Giroux, in a voice that's three- quarters Maggie Smith and the rest Emma Thompson, is superbly worried and imperious as Nancy. Victor Ertmanis and Paul Rainville fill in efficiently as Curzon and Jack. The title (singular, so Lawrence is the protagonist, even if he seems to share the limelight with Graves) suggests a sandbox revolt, an absurd gesture while the real action is going on elsewhere. Fair enough, but it makes the play itself seem out of proportion. The smaller action is too small, the bigger frustratingly out of reach.
