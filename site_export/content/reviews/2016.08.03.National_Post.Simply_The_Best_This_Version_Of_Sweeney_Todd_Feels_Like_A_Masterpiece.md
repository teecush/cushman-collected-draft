THEATRE REVIEW

Sweeney Todd

Festival Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

Jackie Maxwell's outgoing production as artistic director of the Shaw Festival is a big musical. Thats fitting, as it was she who introduced musicals to the Shaws main stage with her 2005 staging of Gypsy. It's also gratifying: Sweeney Todd: The Demon Barber of Fleet Street is the best musical production ever to grace the Festival Theatre stage.

The satisfaction starts in the orchestra pit. From the first dark notes of the introductory Ballad of Sweeney Todd, this musical thriller musically thrills. I had forgotten the quivering excitement that Stephen Sondheim's score can generate when played by an orchestra of the right size. The Shaws production restores the Jonathan Tunick orchestrations from the shows 1980 premiere, and has the resources to do them justice. The string figure that underlies the opening number sounds, as it should, like something echoing from the murkiest depths of the River Thames. Paul Sportelli's musical direction is, as usual, immaculate.

Nearly as impressive as the orchestral work is the choral. The Ballad, with its racing rhythms and crashing chords, is introduced by a succession of isolated solo voices. In Maxwell's production, designed by Judith Bowden, theyre hanging menacingly out in a huge derelict building. Suddenly, as the song takes a hairpin bend into its middle section, we behold the rest of the ensemble - which here means everyone except the shows two leads - invoking the vengeance-mad protagonist: "Swing your razor wide, Sweeney, hold it to the skies." It's the evening's second electric shock, built into the writing but seldom realized as forcefully as here. And we havent even gotten to the main ingredient.

Thats Benedict Campbell, giving what must be the performance of his life in the title role. What's remarkable about it is how firmly grounded it is, and how terrifyingly it rises from that ground. The show's ultimate source is a Victorian melodrama in which the demon barber slits his customers throats while his accomplice Mrs. Lovett turns the corpses into meat pies, neither of them for any discernible reason beyond fun and presumably profit. It isnt clear how Sweeney built up his clientele. It can hardly have been through word of mouth, since all the mouths had been closed. In the 1970s, an English playwright, Christopher Bond, adapted the original, supplying Sweeney with a motive: a powerful judge had raped his wife, stolen their daughter, and had Sweeney himself transported to Australia on a trumped-up charge. He has escaped and has returned to London, bent on revenge. Bond's story is the one the musical tells, its reach extended and its impact intensified by Sondheim's music and lyrics, encased in a script by Hugh Wheeler thats short on dialogue, but strong on structure.

Of all the Sweeneys Ive seen, Campbell's is the most believable barber. He isn't a flashy demon at the start; hes an artisan, proud of his skills but consumed, naturally enough, with a terrible brooding bitterness. As he learns more of whats happened during his absence, the rage turns to obsession; reunited with the tools of his trade - my friends he calls them in one of the music's most dangerously lyrical passages - he's both professionally and murderously elated. When the judge escapes his clutches, obsession becomes possession, as he embarks on a career of mass murder; "they all deserve to die" he proclaims, not excluding himself. The meat-pie proposal tickles his new-found nihilism; for the first time, he laughs. But the practical craftsman never disappears; the immediacy of Campbells transitions, the intensity with which he lives each moment, had me constantly on the edge of my seat. In his handling of a demanding score, theres scarcely any detectable strain. I have never seen a better Todd.

In the damnable equation that makes atrocity possible, Mrs. Lovett is the indispensable other half: the amoral functionary to Sweeney's deranged visionary. Corinne Koslo starts out shrill; placed too far upstage, her celebration of The Worst Pies in London lacks both verbal and visual clarity. After that, though, she's wonderful: sentimental on the outside, ruthless underneath, not least in the precision of her comic timing. Her By the Sea is a joy, the best rendition ever. The productions storytelling is first-rate, sped along by Valerie Moores purposefully grotesque choreography, clarified and punctuated by Alan Brodie's lighting. The set turns into a cage for the madhouse to which Sweeney's daughter Joanna is confined, and from which she escapes with a homicidal readiness that shows her to be a chip off the old block. The inmates swarm onto the streets taking over the city, in a sequence whose staging matches the astonishing confidence of Sondheims non-stop score.

Notable supporting performances include Jeff Irvings fresh-voiced, lovestruck sailor, Patty Jamieson's tragically crazed beggarwoman, Jay Turvey's oily beadle, and Kyle Blairs vocally and physically acrobatic Signor Pirelli, rival barber and blackmailing mountebank. Tobias, his illused apprentice, is played by Andrew Broderick with exaggerated grotesquerie early on, but with real poignancy later. Its a pity, though, that the production, like others before it, underplays his final rebuke of the herovillain: "you shouldn't hurt people, Mr. Todd." It's the shows saving moral gesture, and the only one it needs.

There are a few other flaws. Marcus Nances judge is finely sung but weakly acted. Sweeney's trick-chair, dispatching his victims to Mrs Lovett's bakery basement, doesn't function with the proper cheerful efficiency. There are no oven doors for what should be the story's ghastly climax. Otherwise the production on the first night roared its way to a standing ovation, unanimous and deserved. The show has always been a tour-de-force. This time it feels like a masterpiece.

In repertory until October 19.
