There have been some grievous losses in and around the theatre this year: William Hutt, Ed Mirvish, Bluma Appel. But the death of Richard Bradshaw hits especially hard. He was in his prime and had just begun what should have been a brilliant new chapter of his career. He gave Toronto its long-awaited, and long-despaired-of, new opera house, and presided over its triumphant opening season.

He didn't, of course, get the Four Seasons Centre built all on his own. But it's certain that without his charm, enthusiasm and dogged pertinacity it wouldn't have happened. (He told me that a CEO once offered a handsome personal donation, on top of what his company had provided. "Sorry," said Richard. "Opera is a very expensive business. I need much more than that." "All right," said his benefactor, taken aback but fascinated. "How much do you need?" And gave it.) The Canadian Opera Company now has a home that's a pleasure to visit in itself, and that serves, with superbly unfussy concentration, the art that it houses. It's no surprise that Bradshaw and Jack Diamond, the architect, became such good friends. They brought out the best in one another. And in doing so he made our city a better place to live.

But Bradshaw was already doing that, in the COC's less glamorous lodgings at the Hummingbird Centre. The complete Ring cycle was planned to give the new house a magnificent baptism, and it did, but three-quarters of it had already come into being at the old address, including the Gotterdammerung that was dramatically the high-point of the series. The huge conference-table in that production -- another gift from the business world, you might say, though this time in an imaginative sense -- was as powerful an image as the Toronto stage has yielded in years.

The COC's best shows have been as important theatrically as musically: the Ring, Elektra, the superbly integrated Lady Macbeth of Mtsensk that lit up this past season. That, of course, is how opera is supposed to work, and it is certainly what Bradshaw wanted; I have known few people as acute and enthusiastic about the arts in general and those of the stage in particular. His decision to let Michael Levine design all four Ring operas and direct one of them was a brave hunch, superbly vindicated. As a musician, of course, he led from the front, or at least from the pit, and many of the company's most exciting evenings were under his baton.

It will obviously take two people to replace him; at least I'm not aware that the world is swarming with candidates who could be both artistic director and principal conductor. It's probable that standards will be maintained; the coming season, after all, is already in place and, operatic planning being the long-term thing that it is, so is much that will come after. His shrewdness and geniality, though, are irreplaceable. He was, in addition to everything else, fun.

"Richard Bradshaw," said the minister at his funeral, "abhorred eulogies." He still deserves them.

From tragic business to Funny Business, the new musical promoted from the Fringe to the cabaret space at the Diesel Playhouse.

One man may seem incompetent, another not make sense

While some may seem like quite a waste of company expense.

They need a brother's leadership, so please don't do them in;

Remember mediocrity is not a mortal sin.

Those lines aren't from Funny Business. They're from a much older satire on office politics, Frank Loesser's How to Succeed in Business Without Really Trying, and I quote them, partly because it gives me pleasure just to type them out, partly because they show what the musical theatre has lost --lethal satire masquerading as bland acceptance. These days we get the reverse, as in Funny Business whose script keeps pulling the rug out from beneath itself and pulling its punches in doing so. How to Succeed impaled its characters; Funny Business lets them off all possible hooks.

A Toronto communications company is under pressure to improve its performance or reduce personnel. The kindly boss decides to mount a talent-show to restore morale. Head office (in Winnipeg) decrees that one of the four performers -- heads of marketing and sales, the receptionist, an intern -- shall be fired by the end of the performance. The intended entertainment is abandoned in favour of an elimination contest There's a further reversal at the end that I won't reveal except to say that it represents the ultimate cop-out. There's some bad-mouthing and some back-biting, but everything is directed at sending the audience home if not happy, then fuzzy. The office details are very vague.

Daniel Abrahamson's score includes one clever item in which the senior personnel jealously compare their finances, the numbers deflatingly displayed on computer screens. Otherwise we get cheery tunes with no profile and words to match That's another lost Loesser lesson: You can't have sharp lyrics without sharp music (which hardly anyone writes any more). Some ideas here are funny, but there's no way they can land. The cast work hard: Trevor Campbell makes the office junior a charming loser and Alison Wool-ridge is, aptly, a department head you wouldn't want to get caught under. Lana Carillo and J. Sean Elliott are respectively perky and smirky, both qualities written into their roles but overdone, and Chris Trussell charmless beyond the call of duty. Authors and producers are very young and have bold plans for rescuing the commercial theatre. And Frank Loesser was right about mediocrity.
