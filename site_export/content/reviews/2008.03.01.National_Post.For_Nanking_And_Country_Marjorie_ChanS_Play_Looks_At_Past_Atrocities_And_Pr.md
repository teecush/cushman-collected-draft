In 1937, some 300,000 civilians in what was then the capital of China were killed, and some 80,000 women violated, by the invading Japanese army. This atrocity, or collection of atrocities, is less notorious than it should be, but to the extent that it is known, its known as the Rape of Nanking. This circumstance alone suggests that the fate of the women has not been ignored in comparison with the whole, but the central character of Marjorie Chan's play a nanking winter is convinced that it has.

More precisely, she is the central character of the play's first half. a nanking winter ("title is all lowercase says the publicity -- why?) is divided into two ill-fitting sections. The first concerns Irene, a present-day Chinese-American woman whose book on the massacre is about to be published. She is based on Iris Chang whose The Rape of Nanking is the best-known volume on the subject and who subsequently committed suicide. Irene is herself subject to fits of depression, during one of which she was hospitalized; while she was away, her publishers, with the consent of her husband, took it upon themselves to change her title from The Nanking Holocaust to the supposedly more reader-friendly The Nanking Incident. This would make it the only known example of a sales-hungry publisher opting to make a title less sensational.

Irene is understandably angry about this, and she takes her anger out on everyone around her. She has an especially unlovable habit of putting words into other peoples mouths and then blaming them for it. She plays this trick most notably on her husband, who, for the sake of rhetorical effect, is himself a Japanese-American man whose grandfather may possibly have been among the perpetrators. She also gets mad at her younger sister, who, admittedly, is annoyingly perky, and, of course, with her editor and the firms lawyer.

All present are at least agreed that that a suggested compromise title, Nanking: The Other Holocaust, would have represented the worst of all worlds, implicitly downgrading Armenia, Rwanda and others too heart-breakingly numerous to mention. But the idea that the term holocaust" is, so to speak, already taken, sparks another tirade from Irene. "All right she yells, let's talk about the Jews." However, when she starts doing just that, she comes up with something both interesting and unexpected. It turns out that the man who tried hardest to protect the Nanking victims was German; not only that, he was China's ranking Nazi. He reported back to Hitler on the open slaughter, convinced that the Fuhrer would be shocked and would protest. The result, apparently, was merely to convince the regime that its own genocide had better be carried out, as far as possible, in secret.

Chans first act tries to fuse domestic drama with polemic, succeeding with neither. We then move, via a transition that struck me as both cheap and inept, backwards in time and outwards in scope. We are in Nanking, in a convent thats serving as a refuge for a remnant of the Chinese and that's seeking official Japanese recognition as a secure zone. It's run by an American sister, whose ally is the good Nazi, Niklas Hermann. (The historical names have been changed.) The action takes in the rendition, through trickery, of 200 female refugees to serve as "comfort women.

The five actors from the first part play more or less corresponding roles in the second, but it would take far more imaginative writing and direction than we get here for so small a number to evoke horrors so huge. The script reduces the events to prison-camp melodrama, and Ruth Madoc-Jones production (for Nightwood Theatre) falls back, both at home and abroad, on unmotivated emoting. Grace Lynn King and Ella Chan, who play Irene and her sister, reappear as two young Chinese women both named Mei, randomly selected by their author to stand in for thousands. King gets to play a suicide for one half of the evening and a survivor for the other, and her fate seems equally arbitrary both times. Brooke Johnson flails as both the publisher and the nun; Leon Aureus reduces the various Japanese characters to a row of apologetic zeroes. Stephen Russell comes off best, as Niklas, but then hes playing the most compelling character, the play's equivalent of Oskar Schindler or Paul Rusesabegina. Being neither villain nor victim, he has some freedom of choice. Besides, the good German" always attracts sympathy: not, I think, because we're all secret Fascists, but because we like to believe in the possibility of redemption. Not that this one seems to have had much to be redeemed from; he was thousands of miles from home when his countrymens excesses began and, we are told, was speedily singled out by the Gestapo when he returned. But after Irenes revelations about him in Act 1, we view his actions in Act 2 through a doubly tragic lens. We can practically hear his masters picking up unwitting tips from his reports, and can see him being plunged from one catastrophe into another. This may be my own ethnicity speaking, but the play is more successful at suggesting the nightmare yet to come than it is at evoking the one that is supposed to be its subject.
