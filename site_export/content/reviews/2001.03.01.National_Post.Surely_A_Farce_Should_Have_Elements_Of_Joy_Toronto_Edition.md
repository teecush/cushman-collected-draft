Until April 8.

A FLEA IN HER EAR

Elgin Theatre, Toronto

I know what's expected of me, says the practised roue in A Flea in Her Ear, preparing to entertain yet another married woman in yet another hotel room, "but I dont know how to make it new." His plight is that of all the erotically propelled respectable citizens who inhabit classic farce, and part of the genius of Georges Feydeau, the Parisian master of the genre, is that at moments only the characters get to look into the abyss. Underneath the fin-de- siecle frocks and the ormolu gilt, its a bleak, mechanistic world.

It's also the plight of the director since, though the plays may have dark implications, those are not what make them work, and not why we keep going to them. Laszlo Marton, in charge of the Soulpepper revival of A Flea in Her Ear, is too intelligent a director not to know that what's expected of him is to make it funny; and he must know, too, that the best way to make it at least seem new is to make it socially and psychologically real. Unfortunately the methods he chooses contradict one another. The evening is an intermittently entertaining debacle.

People who try to summarize Feydeaus plots run the risk of ending, like the author, in a mental asylum, but here are the basics: Raymonde, the wife of insurance executive Victor Emmanuel Chandebise, finds her husbands marital performance flagging and suspects that he is squandering his resources elsewhere. So she has a friend write him a letter that will lure him to the notorious Hotel Coq d'Or (which she says in John Mortimer's probably free but indisputably excellent translation is not the Christian Science Reading Room) in hopes of an assignation. She will then burst in and surprise him. Unfortunately, everybody else she knows or is related to has a reason for arriving at the same hotel at the same time; unfortunately, too, the hotel porter is a dead ringer for her husband. Everyone collides; everything, with awe-inspiring infallibility, goes wrong.

Marton begins by parading the characters before us. All well and good: We are being shown a tight society inhabited by recognizable, not especially glamorous people who will be driven by lust, jealousy and the fear of discovery to behave like demented automata. A piano accompanies their promenade, and the pianist remains to underline, silent-movie fashion, every turn in the action -- every suspicion, every discovery, every flight and pursuit. While he plays, the actors freeze or move in rhythm. The plays central act, in the hotel, is essentially one long chase, which is here played out in an endless series of short, cute, stylized bursts.

Presumably the idea is that these moments will set off, or crystallize, the surrounding realism -- and it does not work. It destroys rhythm, it destroys narrative and it comes close to destroying the performances. The actors are troupers, and the script is a masterpiece, but there are only so many times that they can pick themselves up and carry on. There used to be signs hung in saloons that read: "Dont shoot the pianist, he's doing his best." I'm sure this one is, but hes killing the play stone-dead.

His accomplices, who made me feel equally murderous, are Andrew Massingham, who designed the movement, and William Schmuck, who designed the sets. The Hotel Schmuck is mainly composed of stairs, with the crucial bedroom shunted off to one side. The actors spend a lot of time executing debilitating steps on debilitating steps.

The social angle resides meanwhile in Diego Matamoross Victor Emmanuel, an uncompromising portrayal of a timid, prosperous Parisian bourgeois -- man as calculating machine. It is, at least to begin with, a fascinating, desiccated performance, but it lacks charm. This actor's dazzling series of performances over the last couple of years have all been as essentially cold characters. But to laugh at Chandebise -- or at any farcical hero -- we need to suffer with him, and Matamoros shuts us out.

And even if this might work for the insurance man, it doesn't for his double: Poche the porter, alcoholic and happily put-upon, should have an innocence about him that seems within the actors comprehension but beyond his scope. There is no joy in the quick changes of his double role -- a joy that was certainly there a few years ago when Bill Carr played the part at the Wolfville Theatre Festival in a production that had Soulpeppers licked at practically every point. (I have other memories, but to compare either show to the British National Theatre production of the '60s, which established the play in the English repertoire, would be unfair; it was maybe the funniest evening I've ever spent in the theatre.)

If the husband is disappointing, the wife is an outright disaster. Madame Chandebise should drive the play -- the jealous flea is in her ear -- and Liisa Repo-Martell has neither the right comic energy nor the right comic precision for the job. Nor, most crucially, does she have the air of outraged propriety that should make the character's prurience hilarious.

Cruelly enough, Colombe Demers as her confidante possesses all these qualities -- qualities that enable her to transform a theoretically dull role into the flashing centre of the production. She is matched only by Tony Nardi, who plays her Spanish spouse, a homicide named Homenides -- an unquenchable conflagration of jealous pride who takes his own obsession with sublime seriousness and thus makes it funny.

There are other good performances: from Tom McCamus as a Narcissus of the boudoir, from Ric Reid as the family doctor (another rescue job on a functional part) and from Stephen Ouimette in the poetically conceived role of a young man rendered incomprehensible by a cleft palate, though the actor could afford to be braver than he is, less afraid that we as well as the other characters will fail to understand him.

The middle reaches of the cast consist largely of actors of proven talent being strangely ineffective, but the lower orders include a delectable couple of maids (the kind everybody should have) from Melissa Moore and Patricia Fagan, and some dedicated acrobatics from Cliff Saunders as a perpetually priapic Prussian. These get applause, as does some virtuoso swinging from a chandelier, but such isolated moments are no substitute for a steady flow of laughter derived from character and situation; rather, they draw attention to its absence.

The production, amazingly from this director and this company, doesn't seem to trust the play. Its miscalculations, and their persistence through rehearsal, do however reinforce the Feydeau world view, which holds that human beings, having once launched themselves upon a collision course, will allow nothing to deflect them.
