A few weeks ago at Stratford, Peter Hinton gave us a production of The Taming of the Shrew that rode wildly off in all directions. Now, in superb contrast, we have his take on The Way of the World, which turns out be ruthlessly concentrated and consistent. This makes it very funny and at times rather moving. Here in Canada, where we have very little tradition of playing Restoration comedy, it may be easy to underestimate just how magnificent an achievement this production is.

Written in 1700, The Way of the World is generally regarded as the pinnacle of the form. It still flopped with its first audience, and revivals in England have to this day been greeted with more respect than enthusiasm. The main stumbling-block is the plot which, though fairly simple in itself, is almost impossibly convoluted in the telling.

The author withholds vital information for ages, and then spills it out too fast to be followed. Hinton makes it clearer than in any other production I have seen. I understood everything until the last lap, in which two sets of characters -- one supposedly good, the other indubitably bad -- are engaged in competitive blackmail on remarkably flimsy premises. At least, I cant tell what they have on one another that's so terrible. But it doesn't matter much because here, and in all that leads up to it, the battle lines are so elegantly and forcefully drawn.

The production, a joint venture by Soulpepper (which supplies most of the actors) and the National Arts Centre (which supplies Hinton) unfolds on a compact stage, with the audience on three sides; we have them surrounded. We may be hazy on the factual details but never on the emotional stakes. We know precisely who loves, who hates, and who fluctuates.

Like all the plays of its school, indeed like most comedies from any school, The Way of the World is about money and sex. The pivotal character is Lady Wishfort, a widow, who has a lot of the former but hasnt been getting much of the latter. So shes a doubly easy mark, jealous of everyone (including her own twice-married daughter) and susceptible to most. Hinton and his fine designer, Carolyn Smith, pay a kind of visual lip service to the play's historical period with a mural serving as backdrop, depicting the beaux and belles of the time. But what goes on in front of it is dressed from the cusp of the 1950s and 1960s -- the London equivalent of Mad Men time. (A coffee-house wench has been transformed into a Playboy bunny.) It's a period, like Congreves own and most others, of rampant lust and desperate hypocrisy. It fits perfectly.

We start, emblematically, with two men playing cards, both hating to lose but hating even more to admit it. They are Fainall, the villain, and Mirabell, the hero. Both have designs on Lady Wishforts money; the moral difference between them is that Mirabell is capable of love. He seems actually to have made love, real or pretended, to every woman onstage, servants possibly excepted, but his genuine passion is for the lovely Millamant who keeps him, as she keeps most of London, on a string. She's playing an aggressive-defensive game as, in this world, she needs to. Mirabell and Millamant are supposed to be the summit Restoration couple, though it's sometimes seemed to me that theyve been refined almost out of existence, speaking jewelled prose but without much character. This time, though, I believed.

Millamant usually goes to a legend-in-her-own-lifetime actress: an Edith Evans, Maggie Smith or Judi Dench. Here shes Caroline Cave, young and breathtakingly lovely (beautifully gowned, too) with a sure sense of her own worth and grave doubts about others'. Some of the epigrammatic wit may be damped down (the line about dwindling into a wife, for example) but she finds a deeper comedy in the tension between vulnerability and surrender. The great "proviso" scene in which she and Mike Shara's Mirabell lay down conditions for a civilized marriage lives up to its reputation and beyond. Shara, in what is traditionally the duller role, really runs the play: a recognizable quite-spoken modern young man, watchful and testing and ambiguously decent -- even, at the end, compassionate.

The productions clarity is as much verbal as visual. Both aspects pay off brilliantly in Damien Atkins Witwoud, a kiss-curled fop whose every line is a killer, even when he himself is the victim. He and William Webster as his older chum Petulant push the time frame a few years forward; dressed in camp cast-offs from an English public school, they definitely suggest Carnaby Street. More broadly hilarious is Tanja Jacobs' Lady Wishfort, first seen doing game battle with a weight-reducer (and its vibrating belt), and later achieving pathos, though some of her words come out strangulated. (Or fail to.)

C. David Johnson is a powerfully malignant Fainall, a study in passionless jealousy while Nancy Palk, as his cohort Mrs. Marwood, plays jealousy saddled with passion (though she often seems marooned in a corner of the stage). John Jarvis is a bluff country squire, and the nicest man around; Randi Helmers plays a deliciously varied medley of maids; Michael Simpson is Mirabell's manservant, nefariously disguised as the Ladys servant, though hes at his best in his dainty delivery of the prologue.

The production gets in nearly every syllable of text, and has thought about every one of them. As a Soulpepper show, it's on par with Laszlo Marton's Chekhovs --and more like them than you might imagine. - The Way of the World runs through Aug. 2 at the Young Centre; 416-866-8666.
