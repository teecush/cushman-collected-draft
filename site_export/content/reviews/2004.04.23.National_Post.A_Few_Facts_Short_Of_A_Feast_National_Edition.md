COOKIN AT THE COOKERY

New Yorker Theatre, Toronto

- - -

Theyre still Cookin' at the Cookery, and I am still the spectre at the feast. I wouldn't want to rattle my chains too indignantly; the performances in this purported biography of the jazz, blues and cabaret singer Alberta Hunter make this an enjoyable evening. Jackie Richardson, though totally unlike the wiry and vinegary octogenarian who held court in the 1970s at the Greenwich Village boite that gives the show its name, is an experience in her own right. All the same the show, now transferred from the Bluma Appel for a commercial run, retains its original stunning indifference both to the facts of its subjects life and to the intelligence of its audience.

Maybe its ignorance rather than indifference. The song list in the program gives a small but significant clue. It includes a song titled My Handy Man Ain't Handy No More. There certainly is such a song -- a complaint about a partner's flagging virility so blatant it hardly qualifies as double entendre -- and the real Alberta very likely sang it. But Richardson, impersonating her, doesnt sing it here. She sings an earlier song -- a prequel, if you like -- that was certainly in Hunters repertoire, titled simply My Handy Man. Since this number, as you might guess, has precisely the opposite message, you would think someone -- writer-director Marion J. Caffey, perhaps? -- might have noticed the difference. This is in a show in which we are adjured, not once but twice, "to tell it like it was, if you're gonna tell it all."

It gets worse. Hunter was born in Memphis around 1895 (that's what she told The New Yorker), to a mother who apparently never saw her daughter perform, so distrustful was she of all music that wasnt church music. But this same mother is shown quizzing her daughter on who wrote the St. Louis Blues. Alberta answers, correctly, that it was W. C. Handy; she even throws in a reference to Handys Memphis Blues, thus getting extra credits for both musical knowledge and civic pride. Alberta seems to be about 10 at the time. Handy published St. Louis Blues in 1914.

The show isn't big on musical chronology; either that or, as I noted before, it's trying to credit its heroine with prophetic gifts. She starts her career, in the Chicago clubland of the teens and twenties, by treating the patrons to songs from the Thirties. (One of them is Swing, Brother, Swing, which belongs to Billie Holiday and no one else. It has little to do with Hunter stylistically, and nothing historically.) She teams up with Louis Armstrong to sing When the Saints Go Marching In, in a barefaced pilfering of a routine that Armstrong performed with Danny Kaye in a 60s movie.

The guiding principle seems to be that any old song will do, the operative word being "old." Hunter enjoyed a phenomenally long career, which naturally encompassed many different styles. Thats one reason why she's interesting. For this show, however, as for so many others, all pre-rock music is the same, and it makes precious little difference whether you're using it for purposes of derision (the old fashion) or nostalgia (the new one).

The non-musical scenes are crummy. Extended conversations between Alberta and her mother never get beyond platitudes. It then tries to use the relationship as a tear-jerker. Mama died while her daughter was off entertaining the troops in the Korean War. Obviously, Hunter would have felt terrible about this, but it wasnt her fault, and the show hasnt earned the right to lay a guilt trip on either her or us. The message seems to be that this is what taught her how really to sing the blues. It cheapens both the woman and the music.

The show makes a brief and justified fuss about the indignities to which black performers were subjected in the clubs and on the vaudeville circuit. It doesn't see fit to tell us anything relevant about Barney Josephson, the club owner responsible for Hunter's comeback. When Josephson, who was white, was in school, he got beaten up for befriending a black classmate. In the 40s he ran Cafe Society, the first truly integrated nightclub in New York (any patron making a racist remark was ejected), and took a lot of heat for it.

Barneys brother Leon, who had been involved in a plot against Hitler in 1934, was one of the first victims of the Un-American Activities Committee. The Hearst press went after him, one columnist implying that he was a junkie and concluding, with no supporting details, "and there is much to be said about his brother Barney."

Not in this show there isn't. He's presented just as an ancient Jewish gentleman, endearingly doddery if tight-fisted. I somehow feel that he and Alberta would have had things to talk about.

If the writing insults the career its meant to celebrate, the performances more or less make amends. Richardson, who acts as commandingly as she sings, is motherly in a way the childless Hunter never was; the real Alberta seems to have gone direct from daughter to universal aunt.

Richardson, whose voice is richer and rounder than even the young Hunters, does not, probably cannot and maybe need not affect the leathery parlando that was the hallmark of her subject's late period, which is the period that really matters; without it, it is doubtful anybody now would know or care about her. She also goes in for the kind of audience participation antics to which the real Alberta would never have stooped but that producers of these kinds of shows seem to think are mandatory. (I trust CanStage isn't planning to insert them in next seasons production of Aint Misbehavin'.)

She does, though, put a rich, authoritative stamp on everything she sings, and especially to those Hunter wrote herself, from My Castle's Rockin, sheer joy, to Ive Got a Mind to Ramble, sheer devastation. (There's also The Love I Have for You, which, I'm afraid, is sheer schmaltz, at least as presented here.)

Strictly speaking, Richardson and her co-star Montego Glover share the central role; Richardson plays the older Alberta and mother, Glover plays the younger Alberta and everyone else, to which latter assignment she brings a caricaturists edge. As lithe and winning as ever, she is less of a threat this time around. Its definitely Richardson's night.

A lot of performing also gets done by the folks out front who, on both occasions that I have seen the show, have been warmly demonstrative. It's amazing how enthusiastic a theatre audience will be, one that would never go to a jazz club, if you can persuade it that what it is hearing is jazz.
