Thomas Mann's short story Tristan has a delicate, fragile plot and a steely, ironic tone: a velvet fist in an iron glove. It tells of Gabrielle Kloterjahn, a rich merchant's wife who, in the early years of the 20th century, seeks treatment at an expensive Alpine spa for what turns out to be a case of TB. She attracts the attention of a fellow inmate, an author (of one book) named Detlev Spinell. Discovering that before her marriage she had played the piano, Spinell consructs a romantic fantasy of Gabrielle as an artistic princess snatched from her true vocation to become the wife of an unappreciative philistine and -- worse yet -- the mother of a rudely healthy child. Spinell is right about one thing: Gabrielle is a gifted pianist, as she proves when he inveigles her into playing selections from Tristan and Isolde. But the excitement brings on a fatal worsening of her condition.

Wagner's love music wreaks a subtler brand of aphrodisiac havoc in the story than it does in the opera. Physically, Mann's couple never come close. Their affair never happens, and is thus very hard to dramatize. It might perhaps work as a play of Chekhovian nuance, or as a chamber opera. The Shaw Festival's attempt has something of both, being a high-strung musical with a lot of dialogue. It's an honourable try, in which nobody's intelligence gets insulted; but the combination proves self-defeating. It's nearly an hour before there seems any reason for the show to be a musical at all.

Paul Sportelli and Jay Turvey take joint credit for the book, music and lyrics, though I imagine that Sportelli, the Shaw's musical director, is chiefly responsible for the sounds and Turvey, a long-serving Festival actor, for the words. The lyrics are sensitive and sensible, with a neat habit of cross-referencing one another -- though sometimes too audibly pleased with their own rhymes -- and the music has been excellently scored for a string quartet, just the right arrangement (or arrangements) for the tale's physical and emotional climate. It reaches an impressive height at the end of the first act when Jeff Madden's Spinell sings, thrillingly, of the One Face that has enthralled him. The title comes from a line in the original, but Mann's mordant dryness has been left a long way behind -- which might not matter if only the show were consistent about it.

Madden plays Spinell very well as a man who's dreamed of being in a romantic musical and thinks he's blundered into one. The show seems inclined to humour him, though it's hindered in this regard by Glynis Ranney's hausfrau Gabrielle, who obstinately refuses to radiate and who has been given songs that lower the musical tone from silvery post-Sondheim to leaden Lloyd Webber. Another obstacle is the lurking, unromantic spirit of Thomas Mann himself. When Spinell agonizes over a letter to Herr Kloterjahn, Mann observes, in words that must go straight to the heart of every scribbler, "a writer is a man who has a more difficult time writing than anyone else." That line, alas, has not found its way into the musical, but much of the subsequent confrontation between the two men is there, some of it spoken, some sung. It makes Spinell seem both naive and patronizing -- in short, insufferable. So he seems in the book, but the show then tries to rescue him. In the story, his principal antagonist is not Gabrielle's husband but their baby. This is obviously difficult to stage; the Shaw has already balked this season at casting 12-year-olds in Summer and Smoke, and a baby would certainly be pushing it. Spared this humiliating encounter, Spinell is able to emerge with some of his illusions intact, even -- something Mann never countenanced -- as the Tristan of his own imaginings.

You wouldn't think to go looking for musical-comedy energy in a supporting cast of sick people interspersed with the occasional hypochondriac, but the show does its best. A couple of characters have been invented; others have been enlarged from mere hints in the text. In theory, that's legitimate; in practice, it means that the central narrative gets crowded out. Mann has a single throwaway sentence about the sanatorium's housekeeper and her unrequited yearning for the doctor-in-charge. Here, their relationship is expanded so that it almost becomes a sub-plot but never quite makes it. A woman "with an ear disease" (Donna Belleville in a melodious reprise of her agreeably twittering performance from The Heiress) is elevated to a full-blown case of comic deafness. A retired general (a kindly performance by Neil Barclay) is also given jokes that don't work, along with regrets that do: A song called Happy, about the statistical improbability of ever being it, is one of the authors' best inventions.

But both the writing and Eda Holmes's direction are full of loose ends and unresolved opportunities. We're someway into the action before either of the leading characters gets a song (Gabrielle has actually been forbidden to sing, and the repressed Spinell hardly seems the type), so it ought to be a big deal when they do; in fact, it passes unnoticed. One patient (the dancing Jane Johanson) has been so traumatized that she can only utter the three words "isn't it strange." So what's she doing joining in a celebratory number about a sleigh ride -- a trip, incidentally, on which she isn't going? "Hey, it's a musical" really doesn't cut it. It is neat, though, that Isn't It Strange should be the title of Gabrielle's big song, and that Johanson should make a spectral appearance in it, just as she has made a celebratory one during the fateful piano recital. This involves a transparent, two-dimensional piano that lights up and wheels around the stage. That's nice, but it has nothing to do with the rest of Judith Bowden's design, which is lumpenly realistic, or with the production as a whole. - Tristan is in repertoire through Oct. 6.
