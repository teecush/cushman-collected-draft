The four outstanding shows of the 2002-2003 theatre season in Toronto were A Chorus of Disapproval, The Shape of Things, Sunday Father and Sweeney Todd. Of these, all but the first were produced by the Canadian Stage Company: a surprising fact, perhaps -- given that CanStage also puts out a lot of middlebrow junk -- but a fact nevertheless. Despite this, CanStage don't get no respect. This places them at the opposite pole from the Tarragon Theatre, which gets oodles of it and whose productions have snagged a record number of nominations for this years Dora Awards. With these circumstances in mind, let us look at whos up for the Doras, which are being handed out on Monday night.

We had better start in the General (i.e. big, or at least bigger) division, with the contestants for Outstanding Production of a Play, which is not the same category as Outstanding Direction of a Play, a fact that must have many ordinary theatregoers confused. The best productions are the ones that at least look the best-directed, and if you weren't at rehearsals, how do you know otherwise? Maybe the Production trophy should be renamed Best Wrapped Package, or just Most Enjoyable Evening, though I admit that neither of these has as high-toned a ring. My own nominations would naturally be the first three shows listed in my opening sentence. (The fourth is a musical.) In fact, Neil LaBute's The Shape of Things has been shut out, not only from this category but from all the others, while Sunday Father has done hardly better. Being American, The Shape of Things was ineligible for Outstanding New Play; it was, though, incomparably the most exciting new-to-Toronto play of the season, and it was also well done, with fine performances by the indigenous Allan Hawco and -- though some people would say but -- the imported Amy Redford. So what gives? Xenophobia?

A Chorus of Disapproval has been nominated, and obviously it would be my choice: a funny and truthful play, in an English style thats not too easy to crack, brought off triumphantly by nearly every member of a large cast -- Soulpepper at its best. What probably will win (on the Oscar principle that its been nominated for nearly everything) is Tarragon's Girl in the Goldfish Bowl; this wouldn't be a disgrace, though the show was a classic case of the whole being less than the sum of its parts.

Soulpepper got a second nod for its Absolutely Chekhov compilation; surprisingly, since, as one would expect of a bill of five one-act plays, it was uneven.

One of the remaining nominees, Spark Productions Romeo/Juliet Remixed, was R&J in a disco, literally. Out of tenderness for my ears, I avoided it, but I gather it was an event. As for the others, Tarragons Russell Hill and Factory Theatre's revival of The League of Nathans, I don't know how they got in there. Russell Hill, to be fair, had everything going for it except a play. Is this what "production" means?

Outstanding Production of a Musical should, of course, be Sweeney Todd, and its in with a chance. It has a huge advantage in sheer quality of material. It will very likely lose, though, to Little Mercys First Murder (Tarragon), which seems to be another of those board-sweepers. Otherwise we have the barrel-scraping that's customary in this category: Brookstone's A Dixie Gospel (I didnt see it; it was probably wonderful); Theatre Non Nobiss Snappy Tales, which had its moments, but still; and, I give you my word, Ross Petty's Robin Hood.

Outstanding New Play was Adam Pettle's Sunday Father, no question. It hasnt been nominated. Just to rub it in, the same playwrights respectable but lesser Therac 25 has been. (Because it's autobiographical? Because it's about cancer? Because it has fewer jokes?) Anyway, that being the case, good luck to Morris Panych, author of Girl in the Goldfish Bowl; he may need it, as hes up against Sonja Millss The Danish Play (autobiographical at one family remove, and with very few jokes), which has been wildly overrated in every department. Also up are Chris Earle's Russell Hill, which proves that jokes aren't enough, and Matthew Edisons The Domino Heart; if there were a "most promising" award, the latter would win it in a domino heartbeat, but a play composed of sequential monologues leaves me, at least, unsatisfied. Oddly, Susan Coynes Kingfisher Days, Tarragon's second-best new play (after the Panych) has gone unnoticed in all particulars, though my impression at the time was that everybody loved it.

Outstanding New Musical: We're talking about the writing here, are we? The prize will undoubtedly go to Little Mercy, though Snappy Tales probably has as good a score, at least in the bits that were composed by Allen Cole. In both shows, the music far outclassed the words. Greg Robics Assemblywomen! was also nominated, presumably by people who didnt see it. (As Frank Sinatra once sang, they have to fill their quota.) The year's best musical, far and away, was This Could Be Love, which doesn't qualify here since it came from the Independent (i.e. small) theatre sector -- which, however, has no separate category for musicals. You lose some, you lose some.

Outstanding Direction of a Play: This ought to go to Albert Schultz for A Chorus of Disapproval. Hes really blossomed as a director; not only was this the seasons best-acted and funniest show, it was also its best-staged. That apart, I wouldn't begrudge it to Morris Panych, for his own work on his own play, and would feel surprisingly cheerful about it going to Chris Abraham, whose staging of Russell Hill is responsible for whatever credibility this piece has. Also up: Brian Quirt for Through the Eyes, a one-man show, so you really can't tell, and Leah Cherniak, whose nomination for The Miracle Worker (at YPT) must be a pious gesture. This was approved, when Jim Guedos The Shape of Things or Diana Leblancs bold stab at The Maids or (if you want a kids' show) David S. Craig's Pinocchio werent? Oy, in such a world we are living.

Outstanding Direction of a Musical: This has to be between Eda Holmess handling of Little Mercy's First Murder and Morris Panych's handling of Sweeney Todds multiple ones. My heart is with Panych (such flaws as I noted on my first visit had mostly vanished or turned into virtues by my second), but my money is on Holmes. And she made an extraordinarily good impression, recently reinforced by her production of Blood Relations at the Shaw. If by any chance Panych does win in all four (you heard me) of his nominated categories -- well, theres nobody who could make a better job of all those acceptance speeches.

Outstanding Performance by a Male in a Principal Role, Musical: Sweeney Todd is the most demanding role in the musical repertoire, and I don't see how you deny the palm to anyone -- in this case, George Masswohl -- who makes a go of it. So he wasn't ideal; who, since Len Cariou, has been? Peter Millard was nice in Little Mercy, but theres no comparison. Graham Abbey is up for his Robin Hood, but he won last year, and for essentially the same performance.

Outstanding Performance by a Female in a Principal Role, Musical: This will go to Fiona Reid for her Mrs. Lovett in Sweeney Todd. Or there will be riots.

Outstanding Performance by a Male in a Principal Role, Play: Ted Dykstra in A Chorus of Disapproval. No contest. But there could be, since his perfectly gorgeous, or gorgeously perfect, performance was a) nearly a year ago and b) in a comedy. Also, Richard McMillan is up for Through the Eyes, a taxing solo turn, though not his best work. John Jarvis (Goldfish Bowl) and Ari Cohen (Sunday Father, its only look-in) were both splendid, and Im glad they're there, but no. Stephen Ouimette, pulling up his socks, or his Elizabethan hose, halfway through The Beard of Avon? Gimme a break. Most overlooked actor: Ben Carlson, the best thing in both Swollen Tongues and The League of Nathans, and, superbly, unshowily versatile.

Outstanding Performance by a Female in a Principal Role, Play: This will be Kate Hennig for her uncompromising (i.e. unnatural) performance in The Danish Play. Though it could just be Rosemary Dunsmore in The Domino Heart; she was impeccable and she was monologizing. I'm happy Brooke Johnson got a nod for her handsome and heroic work in The Gwendolyn Poems, and surprised that Nancy Palk and Martha Burns were passed over for their similar qualities in The Maids. They were both, in the end, too nice for their roles, but I wouldnt hold that against them

Outstanding Performance in a Feature Role in a Play or Musical (and, I might add, by a male or female; we get real indiscriminate at this point): This is going to be either Eric Peterson for what amounted to a comeback performance in Boy Gets Girl, or Joe Ziegler for his hilariously downtrodden lecturer in Absolutely Chekhov. I liked them both, though I liked them even more in The Blues and Kingfisher Days respectively. And what, pray, happened to David Storchs Autolycus in The Winter's Tale? Actually, I shall root for Bruce Dow's tonsorial mountebank in Sweeney Todd, since I underrated him at first and would like to make amends.

Outstanding Design (of various kinds): John Thompson is in line twice over, for his sets for Little Mercys First Murder and Russell Hill (that subway), and could justifiably win for either. So, probably, could Andrea Lundy, who lit both of them. Jay Turvey and Paul Sportelli are up for Little Mercys sound design/composition, but it's a musical and they're its composers: ditto with the Snappy Tales guys. As far as Im concerned, theyve had their nominations. How do you tell the sound-score from the score?

Outstanding Choreographer: Suddenly we have our own breed of director-choreographers, since Morris Panych and Eda Holmes (with Jane Johanson) are nominated in both categories: This doesn't make them Robbins and Fosse, but the footwork in Sweeney Todd and Little Mercy was excellent. Also named is Tracy Flye for Robin Hood. This has to be a gag; no Petty panto is ever going to feature awardworthy choreography. It's not what those shows are about.

Outstanding Musical Direction: Bruce Kellett (Sweeney Todd) should be a shoo-in. Though so too, working in a more modest musical context, should Marek Norman (A Chorus of Disapproval).

So much for the Generals. On the proliferating Independents I have, for the most part, to pass; I didnt see enough. Id like to see Krista Sutton get Best Actress, for This Could Be Love, and don't see why she shouldn't; the show itself should be in the omnibus Best Play or Musical section, but, like I said, it couldnt get a break. Best Actor, from what I saw, was Alex Poch-Goldin in Bully, with honourable mention for Matthew MacFadzean, another soloist, in richardthesecond, a play that he wrote.

Which reminds me: This years Dora host is the incontestably adorable Kristen Thomson, who two years ago won both author and actor prizes for I, Claudia, and our hearts along with them. I have fond hopes that she will bring out the beloved Claudia mask, and that she will make fewer jokes about her pregnancy than did last year's emcee, Diane Flacks. Are they going to make fecundity an official qualification?
