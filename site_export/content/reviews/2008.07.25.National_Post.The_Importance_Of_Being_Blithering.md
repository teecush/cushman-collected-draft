THEATRE REVIEW

BELLE MORAL: A NATURAL HISTORY

Court House Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont.

---

Ann-Marie MacDonald has a very good ear. I'm not referring to the aural appendage that sits in a jar of formaldehyde on the desk of Pearl Mac-Isaac, the amateur-scientist heroine of MacDonald's play Belle Moral. I mean the rhythmic sensitivity that enabled her to write expert Shakespearean pastiche for the otherwise feeble Goodnight Desdemona (Good Morning Juliet) and excellent conversational lyrics for the musical Anything That Moves. She is also a good actress and, in a logical world, her writing and histrionic talents would combine to produce good plays. But it hasn't happened yet.

Belle Moral, produced at the Shaw Festival in 2005 and now revived, remains a series of orations linked by whimsy: amusing for its first half-hour and progressively agonizing thereafter. (Shaw Festival programs always sport a quotation from GBS himself. An apposite choice for this one might be Saint Joan's "how long, O Lord, how long?") It's set on the very brink of the 20th century. Belle Moral is a stately house near Edinburgh whose owner recently died, willing the place to Pearl, his daughter, on condition she remain childless. Apparently he feared the perpetuating in the family of a strain of insanity, which may possibly be manifesting itself in Pearl's brother Victor, a madcap aesthete with suicidal tendencies.

Both siblings have a thing about dogs. The ear in the jar is a canine one, a present to Pearl from the local doctor who has his own designs on her and is encouraging her in her Darwinian pursuits; she hopes to use it to prove a genetic link between dogs and men, and thus perhaps a unity in all creation. By the end of the play the doctor has emerged as a villain and Pearl, a belle moralist on her own account, seems to have moved from science to mysticism. I say "seems" because all she does is make speeches; no process is dramatized. There are other elements: a mysterious painting executed by Pearl's mother, who died giving birth to her, and an even more mysterious woman who haunts the premises and whose identity is eventually revealed. But there is no dramatic traction. The plot's main practical effect is to break the play down into short scenes, necessitating trundling scene-changes that make the proceedings seem slight and ponderous at the same time.

In between the changes, Alisa Palmer's production is firmer than it was, and Fiona Byrne's performance of Pearl, always admirable, is now superb. Jeff Meadows' capricious Victor is less irritating and more contained than before, though the character isn't. Peter Hutt, the one major cast change, finds a diabolism in the doctor that his predecessor lacked, but falls foul of both the Scots accent and the play's web of ideas. When it comes to expounding philosophic positions -- something that Byrne does with exemplary fluency -- he has to take a breath after every couple words. Maybe he is trying to compensate for his character's being so relentlessly demonized; he remains a raging rationalist while all the others, in varying modes of fancy dress, line up for a photograph presumably intended to signify the supremacy of the imagination and the harmony of all creation.

Of the others in the lineup, Graeme Somerville, in a regrettably brief role as an honourable lawyer, comes closest to matching the leading lady for presence and sparkle. Donna Belleville, theShaw's first choice for maiden-aunt confidantes, does another one here, and in her case, the accent (what I'd call an Edin-burr) helps. Bernard Behrens plays, enchantingly, an ancient retainer who is referred to as Young Farleigh and has a strapping tall grandson greeted, even more hilariously, as Wee Farleigh. An earlier and very good laugh-line tells us that "you can't be pagan and Protestant -- or if you are you're a Catholic." But it leads nowhere. The play has virtually nothing to say about Christian sects, and very little about religion at all, except in the mushiest generalities. All very New Age-y, if 70 or so years before its time. I can only repeat what I wrote three years ago: There is no living connection between what these characters do and what they say. - Belle Moral: A Natural History is in repertory until Oct. 5.
