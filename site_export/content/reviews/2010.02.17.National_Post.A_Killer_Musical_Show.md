ASSASSINS

Theatre Centre, Toronto

---

One of the new joys of Toronto theatre is the abundance of independent companies, working to the highest standards: Company Theatre, Studio 180, Acting Up Stage and BirdLand Theatre, whose first couple of shows were shaky but who broke through magnificently with The Last Days of Judas Iscariot. They have now (in a joint venture with Barrie's Talk Is Free Theatre) mounted a production of the Stephen Sondheim-John Weidman musical Assassins that hits the same exhilarating mark.

Assassins is a show that alternately seduces and shocks, a pattern that's set within the first two minutes. In an especially dilapidated fairground, the proprietor of a shooting-gallery makes his pitch with a soothing, insidious tune: "Hey, pal -- feelin' blue? ... Hey pal -- I mean you. ... C'mere and kill a President." It's a funny line that blows up in your face. In both tone and meaning, it sums up the show. One of the clients who comes to the booth is actually called Booth. The show mixes and matches the stories of nine people who tried to kill the President of the United States including four who succeeded. It takes us from John Wilkes Booth to Lee Harvey Oswald ( "why" someone asks "do all these rednecks have three names?") and beyond. They aren't presented chronologically; they rub shoulders as in some carnival version of the Bar at the End of the World, goading and mocking and encouraging one another. The final scene finds Oswald in the Texas Book Depository, with the others past and future persuading him to shoot JFK rather than shooting himself. It'll make him famous. Look at Brutus. Look at Booth.

Booth, killing Lincoln to avenge the honour of the South, is the only one with a discernible agenda and a specific target. The others are desperate people, beaten-down or deprived or merely crazy, failures in a society that worships and promises success, lashing out at the most prominent symbol available. Each gets his or her own scene, own song. Some are moving, like Leon Czolgosz, the starving immigrant factory worker who killed Mc-Kinley. Some are farcical, like Squeaky Fromme and Sara Jane Moore, failing to kill Gerald Ford. The show's analysis of what drove them isn't especially original, and it may not even be especially profound, but it has the virtue of being true. Given America's current mood, it seems prophetically scary. (The show was first staged in 1990.) It does run the risk of monotony, but just as you think the show may be repeating itself, it explodes. A tempter-character billed as the Balladeer sings about the land of opportunity ( "the usherette's a rock star ... the mailman won the lottery"); the others respond with "but it's never gonna happen, is it?" and go into the most un-Broadway number ever written by a Broadway composer. ( Assassins didn't actually reach Broadway until 2004, and it didn't run then.) Most show tunes, most musicals, reiterate the promises. This one tramples them under its feet. And it doesn't pretend to feel virtuously good about it, either, though it's immensely enjoyable as a song. The commitment of the actors doing it is thrilling in itself.

Like Judas Iscariot, Adam Brazier's superbly lucid production offers one great individual performance after another, coalescing into an ensemble. Many of the actors double as musicians: a currently popular device but one that makes particular sense here, where they're all part of the carnival troupe, and where their instrumental contributions are an exciting part of the staging.

Weidman's book is the equal, in content and structure, as Sondheim's score, a miniature cavalcade of pastiche Americana, but all infused with an individual voice, from the strumming Ballad of Booth through the lulling, brooding Gun Song ( "all you have to do is move your little finger") to the 1970s throbber Unworthy of Your Love in which John Hinckley and Squeaky Fromme pledge devotion to photographs of Jodie Foster and Charles Manson respectively.

The cast, as you may have gathered, is superlative. Graham Abbey, in a Santa Claus suit, has a mesmerizing high-definition soliloquy as Samuel Byck, taping a message to Leonard Bernstein about his plan to fly a 747 into Nixon's White House while adjuring him to write more love songs. (He hums bits of the Bernstein-Sondheim West Side Story to illustrate. He also eats a banana. Is this Byck's Last Tape?) Mike Ross, in his finest performance yet, is heartbreakingly lost as Czolgosz, and Steve Ross boisterously unsinkable as Charles Guiteau; the two Rosses have a great brief scene together. Paul McQuillan has enormous Dixie style and command as Booth; Christopher Stanton is all pent-up boyish frustration as Hinckley; and Trish Lindstrom's Squeaky, a crop-haired bouncing automaton, makes a hilarious double-act with Eliza-Jane Scott's fretful housewife of a Sara Jane.

Then there are Jay Davis' Zangara, comprised of stomach cramps and an Italian accent; Martin Julien's proprietor, creepily grinning if vocally underpowered; and Geoffrey Tyler's chirpy Balladeer morphing ironically into the desperate Oswald. Reza Jacobs' musical direction is impeccable; Beth Kates' set design has the shooting-stall doing ingenious duty as a scaffold and an execution chamber. As no choreographer is credited, Brazier and the actors must have worked up the steps themselves; they are mainly furious stomps, and perfectly appropriate. The show is the apotheosis of Sondheim's long line of songs for the dispossessed, from Gee Officer Krupke to Sweeney Todd. It's our equivalent of Brecht and Weill, but better; it doesn't sit around, congratulating itself on its own cynicism. It doesn't condone its characters; what it does, far more disturbingly, is understand them.
