In New York, they have Mel Brooks; in Toronto, we have Kristen Thomson, and it is hard not to feel that we have the nicer deal.

Ms. Thomson, the author and sole performer of the play I, Claudia, won a Dora Mavor Moore award in both capacities, and while this may not be board-sweeping on the scale accomplished by Mr. Brooks's The Producers at the Tonys, it was still a heartwarming success.

She faced stiff competition in both categories (Seana McKenna in Wit would have been a shoo-in most years, and Zadie's Shoes was a fizzing new play), but the sheer attractiveness of both her and her performance -- not to mention the astounding fact of this being her first play -- had everybody rooting for her.

This has also been the year when a couple of operas divided most of the spoils for best musical, and justifiably.

The Canadian Opera Company's Billy Budd won for production, direction, lighting, lead male performance and -- inevitably -- set design. I must be the only person in Toronto who did not actually like Brian Thomson's hydraulic ship, but oddly, I still feel the award was justified, and it is hugely gratifying that Richard Bradshaw, the COC's dedicated chief, should have been rewarded for his inspired baton-waving.

Billy Budd, being old and British, could hardly win as a new Canadian musical. That prize went to the stirring Iron Road. I would have been happy for that show's very moving heroine, Zhenga Zeng, to have been acclaimed best musical actress, but Louise Pitre, in Mamma Mia!, was a reasonable choice.

The Irish actor Conleth Hill, superb in Stones in His Pockets, won over all the Canadian talent in Toronto, which must have been a nice consolation for losing out to Canadian Richard Easton in New York. Most gratifying-but-perplexing awards were given to Soulpepper's Platonov (supporting actor Diego Matamoros, and production). This being the very same wonderful show that won for best director for Laszlo Marton last year. The same director's brilliant School for Wives was not even nominated.

Other mysteries: Why did a play as poorly written as Ghost Train (a kids' show) get nominated for best play, when a play as brilliantly written and directed as Morris Panych's Earshot was shut out? In the independent (ie. small theatre) and young people's categories, the nomination of shows as weak as The Hats of Mr. Zenobe and as downright awful as The Complete Works of William Shakespeare (Abridged) suggest the Doras are still riddled with cronyism and/or sheer bad judgment.

The winning independent new play, rightly, was Chris Earle's Radio: 30. In all other independent categories -- acting, direction, design -- opposition was mowed down by a revival of Ibsen's The Doll House; it probably did not deserve quite as much as it got (though maybe it did when you look at the competition), but Shaw Festival actors Ben Carlson and Fiona Byrne were fittingly welcomed for venturing into Toronto.

The awards show itself, presided over and gently sent up by singer-comedienne Mary Lou Fallis and The Lion King's Jonathan Wilson, may have been the slickest ever.
