YOU CAN'T TAKE IT WITH YOU

Festival Theatre,

Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont.

- - -

Maybe You Can't Take It With You, but you can certainly bring it back.

This comedy by Moss Hart and George S. Kaufman was successfully revived at last years Shaw Festival. We now have a revival of the revival, and its wonderful.

I was surprised. Kaufman and Hart wrote a succession of hit comedies in the 1930s, of which Once in a Lifetime is the best and The Man Who Came to Dinner the best-known. Most of these pieces are knowing satrical extravaganzas with a show-business background. (Their greatest descendant is the Betty Comden-Adolph Green screenplay for Singin' in the Rain.)

This play is different. Neil Munro, its director here, accurately observes that its "energy threatens to explode into farce, but the inner warmth of the characters holds it back." Trouble is, I've always believed that farce, liberally spiked with wisecracks, is what K&H were good at, and that their inner warmth was fraudulently sticky. Left to myself, I still believe that. But for its duration this production had me hooked.

The play takes place in the home of the family Vanderhof, just around the corner from Columbia University, but dont go looking for it. I quote from the authors stage-directions, which are generally more quotable than the dialogue. Their tone, though, is much the same; they are funny, and endearing, and don't they know it. (They are also infectious.)

Grandpa Vanderhof, now 75, was once in business but sometime in his 30s he got tired and got out; he must have done all right though for he now lives in some comfort, if in considerable disorder. Certainly nobody else in the household is bringing home much bacon. Son Paul makes fireworks in the cellar, and daughter-in-law Penny has been writing unperformable and uncompletable plays for the past eight years, or ever since a typewriter got delivered to the house by accident.

Paul and Penny have two daughters. The elder, Essie, is a long- time and extremely unskillful student of the dance whose every move is a foredoomed pirouette and whose husband likes to print things, any things. The younger, Alice, is a link with the outside world; she is a secretary on Wall Street and is in requited love with her boss' son. There are also various unrelated residents and semi- residents; people who drop in chez Vanderhof tend to stick around.

Two sets of intruders appear in this Eden: one would call them snakes except that the family already keep snakes.

First is the IRS, demanding Grandpas unpaid taxes for the last 24 years. He replies that he doesnt think much of what they'd spend it on, and of course we cheer for him. (If we are liberals or worse we assuage our consciences with the thought that the U.S. circa 1936 was hardly a welfare state.)

Next come Mr. and Mrs. Kirby, Alice's prospective and very respectable in-laws, who are naturally upset by the Vanderhofs, especially when their visit leads to a night in jail for everyone present. All, I need hardly say, works out in the end.

A Marxist would describe all this as a cosy bourgeois rebellion, and a Marxist would be right.

The authors themselves were among the most successful Americans of their time, and the fact that they made their pile in showbiz hardly removed them from the rat race. They tailor their nonconformity pretty shrewdly to a middle American audience; meals in this play begin with Grandpa saying a home-made grace that begins "Well, Sir . . ." and could be called ecumenical, in that its two secular Jews idea of a Norman Rockwell Christianity. We might also note that, alternate nights on this very stage, the equally drop- out Shotover clan of Heartbreak House (to which this play owes a lot) are being arraigned as crypto-Fascists.

Heartbreak House plays like an improvisation. You Can't Take It with You starts that way, and then turns out to have joints of steel. Munro's direction honours this irresistible craftsmanship; he steadfastly refuses, true to his own diagnosis, to get frenetic and the rewards are huge. The scene in which the Kirbys meet the Vanderhofs, each side trying gallantly to accommodate the other, is a glorious passage of blissfully sustained high comedy. And when the explosions come, they explode.

The productions virtues are enshrined in Bernard Behrens Grandpa, less zany than his descendants, with shrewd eyes and biscuit-dry delivery: a treasury of underplaying. He is superbly complemented by Norman Browning as the dyspeptic Kirby, a mountain of repressions waiting to erupt. A definite bonus is that the young lovers (Ben Carlson and Catherine McGregor) are positive personalities rather than romantic stooges.

Actually everyone in the cast deserves mention, from Mary Haney's dishevelled determined Penny and Peter Millard's unobtrusively supportive Paul, through Camille James and D. Garnet Harding as the coloured help (somewhat loosely described by Penny as "like Porgy and Bess"), down to the one-scene wonders: George Dawsons vulpine taxman, Lynne Carlsons sublimely alcoholic actress, Jillian Cook's pinched and frustrated Mrs. Kirby, and -- a last-act epiphany -- Jennifer Phipps' exiled Grand Duchess reduced to dispensing pancakes at Child's, done with a shy glow as warming as any histrionic firework-display. (As it happens, the show has already set off some splendidly literal fireworks.)

Sue LePage has designed a massive rumpus-room of a set, in sub- marine hues with mysterious trailing bits. It seems the Vanderhofs have fronds like other people would like to have friends.
