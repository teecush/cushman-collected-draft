PRIDE AND PREJUDICE

Festival Theatre, Stratford

The first week of Stratford openings concluded, not with a whimper exactly, but with a genteel sigh. Christina Calvit's adaptation of Pride and Prejudice is an exhaustively -- and yes, exhaustingly -- complete account of the action of Jane Austens novel. It also attempts to capture the books ambience and its tone.

As staged by Jeanette Lambermont, it is a pretty affair in which massed scenes of small-town gaiety -- life for the gentry of Regency England seems to have been a ball, invariably followed by another ball -- alternate with rather more effective scenes of one-on-one confrontation, played across the full bare extent of the uniquely compelling Festival stage.

Time was when the first casualty of any stage version of a major novel was the author's narrative voice. This changed around 1980 when the Royal Shakespeare Company staged their unforgettable marathon dramatization of Dickens' Nicholas Nickleby. This introduced us to the idea of having members of the cast recite, in concert, some of the books passages of commentary and description.

Its proved a hard act to follow. Most shows don't have eight hours at their disposal, so the device doesn't have time to gather force; and besides, not every authors voice is as robust as Dickens. Jane Austen's irony is likely to come between the lines, and the choral routines in this Pride and Prejudice tend to expose not the writer's insight but the characters lack of it. Dressed in black and bearing umbrellas, they keep telling us in awkward unison how vital it is for the young women of impoverished families to make financially advantageous marriages. They also sneer heartily whenever one of these young women, or the mother of one of these young women, fails to bring this off.

In short, they are jealous snobs. It is necessary to our understanding of the situation that they should be, and that we should be made aware of it, but once the point has been made, its been made.

More prominent individuals are allowed to describe themselves in the third person, making areas of the play sound like Ragtime without the songs. What is sharp on the page can be arch on the stage, and only one character seems thoroughly at home with the device. This, thankfully, is Elizabeth Bennet, who is both the centre of the story and the nearest thing to an author's representative.

Lizzie, second of the five unmarried Bennet sisters, is justly reckoned among the wittiest and most attractive heroines in literature, and Lucy Peacock's performance is refreshingly up to this mark.

The other Bennet daughters, being so numerous, put nearly as much of a strain on the audiences concentration as they do on their fathers purse. None of them comes to theatrical life, nor do any other of the young ladies of the district, nor the variously eligible young men, be their intentions honourable (the wealthy Mr. Bingley) or the reverse (the penniless and rakish Mr. Wickham).

Most wooden of all is Geordie Johnson as Mr. Darcy, the proud and handsome landowner whose relationship with Lizzie -- his pride, her prejudice, mutual misunderstanding giving way to mutual love -- is supposed to be the heart of the story. Whatever his fascination in one medium, it proves untransferable to another; lacking any spark of wit, and too much of a gentleman to give way to passion, he is simply unworthy of his partner.

This leaves the comics, who come in two categories: dry and juicy. Driest of the dry is Mr. Bennet himself, whom Austen saw as a self-centred bookworm bored by his family and neglectful of their welfare but who, having the funniest and coolest lines, invariably comes off as the most sympathetic male character in any adaptation; thanks to Brian Tree's perfect world-weary timing, he strolls effortlessly off with this one.

Juiciest of the juicy, by far, is Steven Sutcliffe as Lizzie's unfortunate suitor, the Reverend Mr. Collins, tiptoeing about the stage in an ecstasy of self-satisfaction, at a loss to imagine why, with his prospects and his connections, anyone should find him less than irresistible. This is caricature so precise as to be truthful, in contrast to Lally Cadeaus scattershot attack on Mrs. Bennet, the lady whose tireless intriguing on behalf of her daughters propels much of the plot and whose fits of the vapours at those same intrigues and daughters should theoretically account for much of the humour.

Broad as she is, she is restraint itself compared to Patricia Collins Lady Catherine de Bourgh, a tyrannically complacent country chatelaine who here sounds like the current Queen of England as impersonated on late-night television.

The greatest evidence of Lizzie's wit and courage is supposed to be that she stands up to this woman; yet, faced with a figure so far divorced from any conceivable reality, her victory evaporates. So we lose out on the social conflict, and we lose out on the love.

It is hard enough to turn the tensions and values of a novel into those of a play without falsifying them at the outset. The show trades on the assumption that anything based on a masterpiece and bearing its name is automatically worthy of respect. It seems intended, consciously or not, for those who know the book by heart and enjoy singing along with it, and for those who have never read it and feel guilty.
