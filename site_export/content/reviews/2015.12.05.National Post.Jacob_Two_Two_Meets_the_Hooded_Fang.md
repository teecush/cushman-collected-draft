Two anniversaries meet. Torontos Young People's Theatre is 50 years old this year, and Mordecai Richler's Jacob Two-Two Meets the Hooded Fang is 40, at least in book form. Three years after the storys publication, YPT put it on stage. It has been, in various incarnations, one of their most reliably popular offerings ever since.

On the strength of the latest version its hard to see why. Jacob is the sixyear old boy - or, as he would say, the two-plus-two-plus-two-year-old boy - who antagonizes his elders with his habit of saying everything twice. This, he says (twice), is the only way he can get anyone larger than himself, meaning everybody, to take notice of him. Entrusted with his very first errand, he incenses a greengrocer with his repeated request for tomatoes, and is arrested on the dread charge of annoying bigger people. He is found guilty by a kangaroo court (though not, alas, one presided over by an actual kangaroo) and sent to the children's prison on the fog-shrouded Slimer's Island, whose governor is the dreaded Hooded Fang, a personage who, the production suggests, has haunted his dreams even in his pre-convict days.

The Fang turns out, however, to be a softie, an ex-wrestler trying to be tough. Jacob sees right through him, and in doing so robs the story of any chance of excitement. However nasty and threatening the subordinate prison staff may be, however heroic Jacob may be in encouraging his fellow inmates to rise up and escape, there really is no contest. Drama is further defused by the musical component, of which there is far too much. The talented team of Britta and Anika Johnson have supplied a score that is plainly meant to carry the story but is likelier to obscure it. This is partly a matter of sound-balance; in a show thats close to being through-sung, the music too often drowns the text. There seems to be a message about Child Power but its bafflingly muffled. Damien Atkins, doubling as grocer and Fang, does manage to cut through. Darrin Baker, as Jacob's court-appointed lawyer Louie Loser, granted one of the rare sustained passages of dialogue, takes advantage.

Toronto has a too-brief opportunity to see Canada's greatest King Lear since William Hutt. David Fox is playing the king in somewhat reduced circumstances; of his supporting cast only Hume Baugh, a super-intelligent sweet-and-bitter Fool, approaches the lead actor in bite and presence. Rod Carley has set his production in what his program note calls "the colonial aping of the British court in preconfederation Upper Canada in 1837." Well, all right, but there was never a king of Upper Canada, and I doubt if there was a Dover cliff either. The setting does nothing to illuminate the play (or vice versa). But it doesnt obscure the play either, and some things are very clever. The storm scenes are especially well staged, with touches at once domestic and titanic, and the relationship of the multiply disguised Edgar and his blinded father Gloucester is beautifully developed, with an onstage epiphany that isnt quite in the text but in no way betrays it. Above all there is Fox, capable of roaring passion at one moment and unearthly quiet simplicity the next; the transitions are thrilling and unpredictable. I am firm he says, banishing Cordelia in the first scene; he says it quietly, with the ironclad whimsy of a tyrant-by-habit. Once unkinged, or self-deposed, he develops a wry troubled humour about his situation. Decidedly un-humorous is the curse of sterility he lays on Goneril; it too is quiet, clinically detached and reasonable, a revelation. The humour, turned up a few twisted notches, modulates naturally into sweetly reasonable madness; the mock-trial scene is a heartbreaker. So, of course, is the ending. "Why should a dog, a horse, a rat, have life?' he asks over Cordelia's body, and the question has never seemed more agonizingly appropriate: for any death, anywhere. As for the famous repetitions: "howl, howl, howl, howl, howled from offstage, is a lament worthy of the Greeks; the five nevers" occasion an extraordinary, inspired modulation, vocal and physical. To describe it would be to spoil it; discover it for yourselves. But hurry: There are only a few performances left. Otherwise, pray that he returns to the role - maybe in a setting that retains this productions best ideas while dropping the others. (The battle should be left offstage as in most other productions, as should the original stage directions.) Lear admits to "fourscore and upward." Fox is 74. He has time.
