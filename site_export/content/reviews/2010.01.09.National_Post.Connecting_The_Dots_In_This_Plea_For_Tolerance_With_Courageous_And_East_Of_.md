Try this for a paradoxical situation. A male justice of the peace, Catholic and openly gay, is asked to marry another male gay couple, and refuses on religious grounds. Immediately before this, we've seen him presiding over the union of a heterosexual couple, barely past adolescence, whose chances of happiness seem very fragile indeed.

Those are the starting points of Courageous, Michael Healey's new play. Having set them up, he divides the rest of the action into two apparently independent halves. Act One follows the gay relationships and is very good. Act Two concerns the straights, and is even better. The acts are written in different styles, the first, a conventionally realistic Shavian debate, the second, presentational with one character stepping repeatedly out of the action and even playing with the audience -- which by now, of course, is equally conventional. Both work, the question is, how well do they hang together? The answer is, I'm not sure. Ask me again in a year's time. For now, I'm prepared to settle for the wit and passion of Healey's writing, especially as delivered in a first-rate production with acting that is almost unwaveringly superb.

Act One. One of the rebuffed would-be spouses, who happens to be a lawyer, insists that his hard-won Charter rights have been infringed and complains to the Ontario Human Rights Commission. We don't see the tribunal in process, but we do see a waiting-room confrontation between Brian, the very belligerent lawyer, and Tom, the mild-mannered JP, who proves, when goaded, to be equally passionate about his own convictions, however irrational and even self-harming they may seem. Nobody wins in this scene; alternatively, they both do. Meanwhile, Tom's lover, Arthur, an apparently imperturbable Sudanese businessman, endeavours to plead his case by chatting up Brian's contrastingly nervous partner Martin, and ends up seducing him. Further wrinkle: Arthur threatens to leave Tom unless they marry, the one thing Tom, agonized but consistent, will not, cannot do.

Act Two. "That was rough," says Todd, with special reference to Tom who seems to have lost everything. Todd is the guy who got married at the top of the play and who, immediately after the intermission, announces himself as our narrator for the rest of it. He's feckless --his lifelong philosophy, repeatedly proclaimed, is "what the f--k" -- and, at crucial moments from wedding to job interviews, tieless. His marriage almost got derailed when Tammy, his bride, discovered that Lisa, her maid of honour had given him a blow job the night before. Lisa nevertheless remains in their life, because they both need a friend. With Tammy having given birth and sinking into depression, and with eviction threatened, Todd goes to work, loading bottles of Fresca onto trucks. He has a friend, neighbour and co-worker named George, a Somali refugee, who soon outpaces him in all three departments. George also turns out to be religious, as is their boss (who's also an alcoholic: one more element than the play can use). Slowly and resentfully, though certainly with humour, Todd recognizes that he isn't the centre of the universe, even if he is the narrator.

The dots are all there, begging to be connected, even if the play, for better or worse, refuses to do it for us. Tom, in the first half, and George, in the second, leave the impression that the author -- I speak without personal knowledge--isn't religious but rather wishes he was. Certainly, he respects people who are, but then he seems to respect everybody; though Brian, pressing for a speedy marriage because a delay will upset his plans for lunch, is rather a hard case. He's the one character who doesn't seem driven by love (and whom we never see, at any length, with his lover). Politically, the play is an explicit plea for tolerance; for preserving the values that might make people like George want to move here. Healey writes plays for Canada like nobody else does; he's also, as a playwright, almost unfairly fair. Todd expresses passing shock when he finds himself the victim, and George the beneficiary, of affirmative action; the moment gets a laugh but it isn't pressed, and Todd, whatever his other faults, is nobody's racist. We have two African characters; a poor one who learns English preternaturally fast, and a rich one who revels in his own super-literate command of it. Arthur rocks the theatre by saying that people think he's nice because he doesn't -- sorry, does not -- use contractions. Maurice Dean Wint plays both black roles, endowing them with distinct varieties of preternatural poise.

And here begins the roll call. Patrick Galligan plays Brian with a fire-and-ice ferocity I've never seen in him before. Tom Barnett has a few wavery moments as Tom but is funny and moving when roused to rage and plain moving when abandoned to sorrow. As Todd, Brandon McGibbon takes complete control of character, stage and audience. Erin MacKinnon as Tammy is fine when angry and finer when lost. Lisa, the sketchiest role, is nicely filled out by Melissa MacPherson. Tom Rooney plays both Doug and the soft-drinks dispenser, and conveys miraculous amounts by the most economical of means. Richard Rose directs with infallible clarity, delicacy and force; on David Boechler's bare, triangular set, every moment is charged with energy. The human connections, if not the philosphical ones, are always crystal clear. Anyway, with this play on its main stage and East of Berlin on its third go-round in the Extra Space, the Tarragon can boast of having the two best new plays in ages under one roof.

- Courageous runs through Feb. 7 at the Tarragon Theatre. Call 416-531-1827 for ticket information.
