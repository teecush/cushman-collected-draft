Last week, I saw the British National Theatre production of Alan Bennett's play The Habit of Art, directed by Nicholas Hytner and starring Richard Griffiths. I am tempted to call it the best time I've had in the theatre since seeing the same team's collaboration on the Broadway transfer of The History Boys. Except that I wasn't having it in the theatre, or at least not in the same theatre that the actors were in. I was at SilverCity in midtown Toronto, one of the Cineplex outlets across North America to be receiving the latest offering in the series known as NT Live. I was vicariously experiencing what must, if I have the time difference right, have been a midnight matinee at the National. Just to make the theatrical experience even more meta, The Habit of Art is actually set in a theatre. And not just any theatre. The conceit, hinted by sly in-jokes at the start and made explicit in a major speech at the end, is that we are witnessing the rehearsal of a play in the National Theatre itself. The play they're rehearsing is about a late-life meeting between two English cultural heroes, W.H. Auden, poet, and Benjamin Britten, composer. The rehearsal is marked by all the battered camaraderie, farcical misunderstandings and bruised but resilient egos that theatrical preparation is heir to.

The National has done four of these simultaneous transmissions so far, with more on the way. I've seen two, the first being Phedre, a different but almost equally successful proposition. Many of the supposed distinctions between stage, film and television acting melt away, though the audible (and occasionally visual) presence of a live audience provides an overall perspective. This isn't the film-of-the-show (always a dilution, as the movie of The History Boys, even with all the original personnel, handily demonstrates) but neither is it a static recording of the play. The cameras move, probe and explore; wide shots establish the full stage picture, close-ups reveal how inward and detailed the best stage acting can be, even when its immediate destination is a large auditorium. Though a residual theatrical piety makes me wish I had been there in person, the only thing I really missed was the opportunity to applaud. Doing so, when you aren't in the same room as the actors, still feels silly.

The play is less than the sum of its mostly brilliant parts, though its title, a phrase of Auden's own, gives it a kind of unity. "Art" is something an artist does, by compulsion that's part psychological, part economic and eventually unshakable. This applies to Auden, to Britten and to the people we see trying to put on a play about them. Griffiths plays the actor who plays Auden, who worries publicly about not looking the part (much too tubby, and needing an uncomfortable mask to suggest the aging poet's tram-lined face) and privately about learning the lines. Auden is shown returning to Oxford after his long exile in America, and sought out by Britten who wants his advice about the opera he's trying to write: Death in Venice. The meeting is imaginary, but in much earlier years, the two men had really been friends, and had even collaborated; Auden had been Britten's librettist. He very much wants "Benjy" to ask him again, but Benjy isn't buying. It's a searching, touching scene, a clash of values, styles and personalities; both men are gay, and the raffish poet wants the uptight composer to come out -- in his music, if not in his life. In truth, everyone in Britain knew about Britten, but they didn't ask and he didn't tell.

Also in attendance is Humphrey Carpenter, who in later life wrote biographies of both men. For now though, he's just a budding journalist who's come to interview Auden in his grotesquely untidy lodging, but who gets mistaken for a sex-worker whom the professor of poetry has ordered up by phone, occasioning the deathless line "I'm not a rent-boy, I'm from the BBC." When the real rent-boy turns up, he's impressed by the culture around him, but only up to a point; he ends the play-within-the-play with a speech on behalf of all those who haven't acquired the habit of art and are ignored by those who have. This seems belated and dragged in, though his fictional author claims that he's merely riffing on The Sea and the Mirror, Auden's own riff on The Tempest, which gives the last very ornate words to Caliban.

His real author, Bennett, presumably agrees, though maybe he's just having fun. He certainly provides lots of it. Travesties meets Noises Off, with results that may be even more hilarious than either. Comic writers are supposed to slacken with age, but Bennett at 75 remains funny like nobody else, generous and forgiving but always surprising and scalpel-sharp. Griffiths, the world's most complicated teddy bear, has become his ideal mouthpiece. Alex Jennings' pettish Britten is equally fine, with both men scrupulously distinguishing between their on-and-off stage personae. Frances de la Tour, the voice of reason and tolerance, as she was in The History Boys, gives a perfect performance of the perfect stage manager. Bennett apparently devised the backstage framework as a painless way of feeding the audience information, but it's grown into irresistible comedy on its own account. Hytner's production is flawless; it restored my battered faith in high-end British theatre. Short of a plane ticket, this series is the ideal way to experience it, the best of most worlds. Next up, in June, and currently packing them in on the South Bank: London Assurance. Which might be a good overall title.
