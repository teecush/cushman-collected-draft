robertcushman@lycos.com; Enoch Arden closes tomorrow at the Theatre Centre, 416-538-0988. The Monster Trilogy closes tomorrow at Buddies in Bad Times, 416-975-8555. Banana Boys runs through Oct. 16 at the Factory Studio, 416-504-9971.

Alfred, Lord Tennyson wrote a narrative poem titled Enoch Arden, about three inhabitants of an English seaside town. Enoch himself was a mariner, eventually a shipwrecked one; Philip was his friend since childhood and a miller; and Annie, loved by both of them, married Enoch but then, despairing of his long absence, gave herself to Philip. Needless to say, Enoch returned and underwent what I can best describe as a crisis of renunciation. The story shows all three of them behaving with exemplary nobility.

Richard Strauss set this poem to music, a combination of disciplines that is technically known as "melodrama." Now comes Judith Thompson, who has utilized the work of both men to create a two-character piece set in the present day and titled Enoch Arden by Alfred Lord Jabba and his Catatonic Songstress. Jabba is a mentally disturbed man, possibly schizophrenic, who lives (this is Judith Thompson, after all) in a halfway house in Parkdale. His catatonic songstress, and fellow-inmate, is named Ciel. Jabba undertakes to perform the poem, partly, we suspect, in order to show off, but also, by his own account, to jerk his companion out of her stupor and into the redeployment of her talents as a singer and pianist. These turn out to be considerable; the two moments in the play at which Kristin Mueller's Ciel bursts into song are electrifying.

The original narrative obviously runs the risk of seeming melodramatic in the more familiar, non-musical sense. Enoch could fairly indulge in enough self-pity to float a musical by Andrew Lloyd Webber or Claude-Michel Schonberg. But Tennyson kept such indulgence at bay, Strauss disdained it and Thompson looks it in the eye and stares it down. There's no denying that Jabba jabbers. He claims that the poem is really his story, and says that he once held an audience spellbound with a complete recitation of it. (He confesses that many left at the end of the first hour, but the survivors liked it.) He identifies, we note, with Enoch or Philip, depending on who is the loser at any given moment, and he takes off on virulent rants at the winner. We can place him; and, having done so, we can sympathize without having to swallow everything he says. His motives seem confused, not least to himself, but at least the friendship he proclaims is real.

The piece has its flaws. You'd have to be Jabba, or maybe Thompson, to believe that the past and present characters display an inevitable affinity, and anyway we don't have time to take the poem on board before grappling with how it's being used. (Read it first; it's shorter than Jabba would have you think.) Maria Lamont's direction could hardly be more sensitive, technically or textually; the show's atmosphere grabs you from the start. It takes time, though, to appreciate the accumulating skill and speed with which Thompson alternates levels and styles. The same versatility and virtuosity characterize John Fitzgerald Jay's Jabba, as assured in measured Victorian verse as he is in frantic hyperactive contemporary prose (in his mouth they sound equally natural, which in fact they are) and able to switch instantly from one to the other without dropping a stitch. This is an amazing performance; where has this actor been all our lives? The show closes tomorrow; I imagine it will reappear somewhere but it's worth catching now at the Theatre Centre, the ideal space in terms of its location (way out west on Queen) and, despite recent face-lifting, its general air of unsettlement.

Also worth hurrying for (to Buddies in Bad Times), and also concerned with weird, is The Monster Trilogy by RM Vaughan. This started life, per the title, as three separate monologues, but in the current production (by Moynan King) they have been intercut. The governing one is for, and about, Susan Smith, the spurned South Carolina woman who murdered her two children by strapping them into her car and driving into a river, and is here seen in prison at work on a series of videos that she intends to send to Oprah, Springer and Barbara Walters, so that they and the world will understand. She is juxtaposed with two fictitious (as far as I know) "monsters," also female. One, who talks to us direct, is a burly cop who expresses racist sentiments, has probably warped the life of her son and is not getting much satisfaction from either her job or her husband. The other is a minister, who seems to derive a suspicious amount of pleasure from officiating at the funerals of teenagers, and who does her justifying to an unseen Monsignor. (Wouldn't that make her Catholic? Did the Church start ordaining women when I wasn't looking?)

The writing is notable for its wit: not just in the sense of being funny (though it is, very, especially when the policewoman is talking) but in how it probes into peoples' minds through the texture of their speech. Vaughan presents a frighteningly persuasive picture of Smith, who goes, before our very ears, from speaking in terms of what she has done to what has been done to her. She claims to have killed her children to spare them the pain of living without her, and then of course outlived them, to embark -- on this showing - - on a career as a successfully self-justifying victim. Kirsten Johnson, whose video image appropriately takes up more space than she herself does, inhabits her completely.

But is the author presenting a moral equivalence between this woman and the other two, or is it just that the show's publicity suggests that he has? All three characters may be guilty of sins against the young, but to equate verbal racism or even perverse parenting with actual child murder seems to me political correctness gone mad. I think I'd feel that way even if Ann Holloway's cop, miles the best performance I have seen her give, were less endearingly uproarious than she is.

On the third part of the puzzle I have to suspend judgment, since Caroline Gillis's Reverend is so monotonously artificial that I lost the ability to follow what she was saying. But I couldn't detect any implication that she had personally disposed of the young people she was burying, so her sins, too, seem of a lesser order: thought crime, perhaps. If the point is "you think these people are monsters -- think again," well, two of them aren't, not by comparison with the one occupying the cell downstage left -- whom Vaughan does make us understand but not, I think, forgive. I don't understand, either, why St. Teresa of Avila descends at the end; nor would I have known it was her if the program hadn't told me. Still, this is the best new writing I have seen at Buddies since I started doing this job.

- - -

Banana Boys, by Leon Aureus from a novel by Terry Woo, is about five young Chinese men growing up in Canada: second-generation immigrants caricatured, so the title reminds us, as "yellow outside, white within." The scenes presenting them as a group are rambunctiously enjoyable, even when the mood is desperate; it's notable that when kidding around they show themselves as adept as any whites at imitating blacks. The rub is that the two who are meant to interest us the most -- one who burns out on dope and a fast-track business career, one who's pressured into becoming a doctor though he wants to be a writer -- are the closest to stereotype, not ethnic but dramatic. The bonds binding them to their friends are frayed, to be loosened or tightened as suits the script, so the action winds ineluctably down. Nina Lee Aquino directs a strong, flashy production, with a high-energy acting quintet: Richard Lee, Derek Kwan, In-Surp Choi, Dale Yim and David Yee, who is the most incisive, as the youth most alert to, and angry about, racial slurs. He's also the one who looks the least Asian, and I wondered -- this seems to be my week for racial insensitivity -- whether this should be put down to Occident or design.
