A British TV drama series can usually be counted on to start with a bang. The first episode of The Whistleblowers is no exception; it begins with a virtually naked man running in terror down a London street. A young man and woman-- we subsequently learn that they're lawyers, colleagues and lovers -- are driving down the same street and narrowly miss running him over. They witness his recapture by a pair of strong-arm guys who advise them to go home and forget about it. Our couple, whose names are Ben and Alisha, obey the first half of the instructions but not the second. Fairly soon, they discover that the fugitive is a suspected terrorist and that his captors are holding him, a long way under the radar, for "enhanced interrogation."

It looks like the beginning of a series-long intrigue, a sort of anti-24, or a domestic successor to the BBC's brilliant Washington- set The State Within. In fact, though, the situation is wrapped up within the episode. Ben and Alisha drag this semi-judicial thuggery into the light, and get it stopped -- this example of it anyway. Flushed with success but unemployed -- their bosses, bowing to official pressure, have dispensed with their services -- they decide to open their own agency. This agency will provide counsel and support for lonely citizens eager to follow their example by blowing the whistle on all kinds of institutional malfeasance. Next week, we see them confronting a cover-up by a pharmaceutical firm that has carelessly released a deadly virus; the week after, they investigate the suicide of a boy pushed out of a rich school and into a poor one. The rest of the six-part series has them taking on "the immigration service, the drinks industry and an environment agency." (I quote from the press release.) There's corruption everywhere.

I don't doubt it, but in going after so much of it, the show has boxed itself in almost before it's started. It's the latest variation on the old private eye format: You bring us your problems, we'll put them right. It has, of course, grown up a bit; our heroes can expose symptoms, but there's no suggestion that they can heal the underlying rot; they're more Philip Marlowe than Perry Mason. The creator and prime author is Tony Marchant, who 20 years ago was one of Britain's most promising left-wing playwrights. What he's come up with here is reasonably compelling, but without much individuality. The storytelling is sometimes confused (some establishing flashbacks in the first show take forever for the viewer to process) and the characters are paper-thin. Richard Coyle and Indira Varma do what they can for Ben and Alisha without getting much beyond her being the plucky practical one and him being rash and whimsical. Their assistant, a semi-hard-boiled investigator named Kenny (Daniel Ryan) is implausible comic relief. Guest actors are the best: Douglas Hodge, Allan Corduner (Sullivan in Topsy- Turvy), David Threlfall and Josette Simon make up a welcome parade of British old-stagers.

Nearly everyone, though, gets done in at some point by the script's methods of exposition. "What's waterboarding?" asks Ben, all innocence; either he doesn't read the papers or he's trying out for U.S. Attorney General. Before too long he gets to see for himself. So do we; and though it's hard to watch, it's still one of the best sequences in the show. The victim himself is only there because he was implicated, also under torture, by an outsourced innocent who happens to be a citizen of Canada. So at least the writers read the papers, and have heard of Maher Arar. Considering how close to the bone all of this is, the story should be more gripping than it is.

The next two episodes do offer some variations on the format. We get a reluctant whistleblower and a fake whistleblower. The reluctant one is a microbiologist blackmailed by Big Pharma into covering up what they falsely tell him are his own mistakes. So far the villains in the series have been easily identifiable; they wear silk shirts, eat in expensive restaurants and speak in what used to be known as BBC accents. They also regard all other people as expendable. The fake whistleblower is a teacher, employed by one school but a mole for another. "Well, you try living on my salary," he tells the virtuous legal eagles; the author of this third episode, Steve Thompson, is an ex-teacher himself, and that line must have come from the heart. A boy gets sacrificed because his emotional problems might compromise the school's position in the academic league tables. But the show can't just leave it there. The school also has to be a bastion of the religious right, teaching creationism in science classes. Presumably this reflects the beliefs of the rich man whose millions are funding the place, but neither he nor the situation are ever explored. This is the best, most complex instalment of the three, but it's still hampered by characters who converse in sound bites. "If they can do this," says the bereaved mother, "if they can refuse to help my son ..." Ben completes the thought for her: "Then think about how many other kids may be in need." Worthy thoughts, wooden words. - The Whistleblowers airs at 9 p.m. ET on Wednesdays beginning tonight on TVO.
