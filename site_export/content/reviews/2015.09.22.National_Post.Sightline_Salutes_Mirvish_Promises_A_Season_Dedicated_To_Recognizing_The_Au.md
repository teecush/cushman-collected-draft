On Monday, at the Royal Alexandra Theatre, Mirvish Productions announced their upcoming season. Or rather, they celebrated it. Most of the 2015-16 season has been public knowledge for some time. But this was an opportunity for those involved to sing a few songs (most Mirvish shows are musicals), screen a few video clips and tell a few jokes.

The principal joker, in his capacity as host, was Luke Kempner, a British actor about to open here in his one-man show Upside Downton, a spoof of Downton Abbey in which he plays thirty roles: that's more characters than most people could probably name from having watched the TV original. Once upon a time parodying a pop culture phenomenon would have been the job of a revue sketch, but the trend now is to make it last an evening. A few years ago Mirvish brought us a similar entertainment that derived from Harry Potter (and that played at the same theatre, the Panasonic).

"Without you, the audience, we on stage are nothing," said Kempner. "That's why this season is all about saluting you." That's true, of course, though I'm not sure why it should be truer of this season than of any other. Still, it's nice to hear some acknowledgment of the people it's all being done for, the people who laugh, cry, clap and buy subscriptions. Subscribers were actually represented at yesterday's event, via videotaped testimonials.

However, the bulk of the promotional activity concerned the bigger shows to which subscribers are being asked to subscribe. A quartet of performers from the Tonywinning musical A Gentleman's Guide to Love and Murder (touring version) breezed through a four-minute version of that show's extremely complicated plot. Bailey Ryon, currently playing the title role in the Broadway production of Matilda, flew up to sing one of the songs. This is the musical, based on a Road Dahl novel, that started in Stratford-on-Avon, then conquered London, and then New York. These two shows have something in common with one another, and indeed with Downton Abbey. They're all fantasies about England.

Matilda has oppressed schoolchildren with magical powers, rising up against a schoolmarm so monstrous that she has to be played by a man. Gentleman's Guide is about an oppressed young Edwardian gent with intellectual powers, who rises up against the relatives who have done the oppressing, murdering each of them until there's nobody left standing between him and the family title. This may remind you of Kind Hearts and Coronets, greatest of all English film comedies. Indeed, show and movie both acknowledge a common source, a long-forgotten novel. Gentleman's Guide, however, makes no explicit claim to being Kind Hearts: The Musical, and I'm sure the fact that, in both versions, all the victims are played by the same actor is merely a coincidence.

The designated holiday attraction is Cinderella, with music by Richard Rodgers and words by Oscar Hammerstein II. This, Kempner's narration assured us, "took Broadway

by storm." Well, no wonder: Broadway hasn't had a new RH score since The Sound of Music in 1959. This one, of course, isn't really new. It was a live TV special in 1957, starring Julie Andrews and attracting, we were told, 100 million viewers, more than have ever watched Downton Abbey, Kempner sportingly acknowledged. This isn't the first time somebody has attempted to put this Cinderella on stage: a cut-price version came through Toronto a few years ago. Maybe the new incarnation will preserve some of the original's wit along with its nice songs; Alexandra Zorn, who plays Cinders, sang one of them to whet our appetites.

Just about to open is Motown, a title that needs no explanation. A theoretically more adventurous musical than any of the above is If/Then, which has a score by Tom Kitt

and Brian Yorkey, who wrote Next to Normal, a musical about mental illness. This one is about life-choices; its opening song is called What If ?. The heroine gets to choose between two possible paths, and we get to watch her follow both of them. Idina Menzel won plaudits and prizes for playing this split personality on Broadway, but if she's playing it in Toronto the producers are keeping uncharacteristically quiet about it.

The most intriguing item, though, in the Mirvish Main Subscription package is a play, from the 1930s: Gaslight, a psychological thriller - actually, a psychological torturethriller - that became a famous film and is still a famous title, but has fallen off the theatrical radar. Patrick Hamilton, who wrote it, was a master of atmospherics, starting with his taste in titles. The noted British actor Owen Teale plays the charming villain. There's also The Judas Kiss, a play about Oscar Wilde by David Hare, starring Rupert Everett.

"For those looking for something a little more quirky, a little more challenging" there's the Off-Mirvish season, which has its own subscription system. It features two recent American plays, in new Canadian productions. One is Seminar, by Theresa Rebeck, whose central character is a cruelly demanding creative-writing teacher, to be played by the esteemed Tom McCamus. Disgraced, by Ayed Akhtar, is in the great American line of explosive dinner-party plays. Four diners: a Muslim lawyer, his WASP wife, a Jewish art-dealer, and his black wife. Raoul Bhaneja stars, in a role played off-Broadway by The Daily Show's Aasif Mandvi. (Later, on Broadway, Boardwalk Empire's Gretchen Mol joined the cast.) There's also Bigmouth in which a Belgian actor, Valentjin Dhaenens, delivers political speeches from Pericles of Athens to George W. Bush, each in the original language. It's described as "the ultimate press conference."

And - standing apart from the subscription seasons - there's the ultimate long-runner, The Phantom of the Opera. Again. However, this time it is being announced as "a spectacular new production by Cameron Mackintosh." The wording suggests that Mackintosh is directing it, which would be a departure for somebody previously content to be the most successful producer in the history of musical theatre. In fact, though, this is a new staging by Laurence Connor, who previously re-directed Les Miserables and improved it, if not to the extent of making it good. I must admit I find it hard to imagine that any production of Phantom could improve on Hal Prince's original. Mind you, I thought the same about Cabaret. Mind you, I was right.
