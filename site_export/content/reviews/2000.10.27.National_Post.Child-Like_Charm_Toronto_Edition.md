YOURE A GOOD MAN, CHARLIE BROWN

New Yorker Theatre, Toronto

You're a good show, Charlie Brown, and I'd forgotten how good you are. Perhaps its because youre so well-meaning and mild-mannered and unassuming that you get overlooked.

I must stop this. There's no reason to feel sorry for You're a Good Man, Charlie Brown, which can never have been out of production since its premiere in 1967 (My press kit included a Peanuts chronology that omits that original staging, while listing the 1999 Broadway revival from which this new Toronto production derives. But thats producers for you: No sense of history.)

You might want to shed a few tears for the shows author, Clark Gesner, who is now dead, and even while alive never had another success, though he certainly did well enough out of this one. The U.S. critic Martin Gottfried once claimed there was a jinx on triple- threat writers -- those who do book, music and lyrics, as Gesner did -- since none of them ever had more than a single hit, and he may well be right. You might conceivably say the same of Charles M. Schulz, creator of the original Peanuts comic strip. He is also dead, and Peanuts was the only thing he ever did. But then, he was able to do it for 50 years, without ever growing stale, spinning out new variations on the same group of characters and the same idea.

Schulz's inspiration, very nicely captured in the musical, was to foist grown-up neuroses and a grown-up vocabulary on small children who in all other respects seem to be leading a conventional five- year-old existence: one that captures the 1950s and makes it seem a pattern for eternity. They go to school, fly kites, do homework (a book report on Peter Rabbit ...), play baseball, and have pets. (Or one of them does; others have fetishes.) When they suffer, as they often do, they do so very articulately. It's cute when you think of them as kids, and rather painful when you realize that you, the adult, are caught in exactly the same binds. The comic strip, every day another anecdote, gave Schulz the perfect controlled environment in which to carry out this unceasing experiment.

And the musical wisely makes no attempt to impose a plot. Its book is a series of sketches -- most of them probably transcribed directly from the cartoon -- and, like its source, it generally eschews punchlines, since its true to life, and life rarely provides a payoff. It just goes on, in a repetitive and compulsive kind of way thats both its comfort and its torment. Charlie Brown, nice though he is, will never win at baseball, kite-flying or romance, and will always have the feeling it's his fault; Lucy will always be unstoppably bossy; Sally will always be insistently self- justifying; Linus will always be super-bright but blanket- dependent; Schroeder will always occupy the musician's impenetrable space (him and his Beethoven); Snoopy will always lead a dogs life, secure, except at mealtimes, in his superiority to the humans among whom fate and the pet shop have unaccountably thrown him.

Especially telling is the contrast between Charlie Brown and Lucy -- the one more intelligent than he knows but fatally lacking in self-confidence and easily -- indeed, permanently -- punctured; the other dumb and loud and undeflatable. Watching her in her psychiatrists booth, charging Charlie Brown five cents for the privilege of learning that nobody loves him, including Snoopy, you wonder if she will really grow up to be a therapist or if (as seems more likely), she will compromise and become a CEO.

Charlotte Moore, who plays Lucy here, is conceivably the most accomplished performer on the New Yorker stage, but she is too knowingly brassy for the show: The style depends on keeping a delicate balance between innocence and experience, and she is weighted too far toward the latter. But Steven Gallagher is a perfectly articulated Charlie Brown and there is sweet-sour support from Daniel Murphy (Linus), Gab Desmond (Schroeder) and Cara Leslie (Sally), whose indignant and multi-faceted repudiation of the C grade she has received for her coat-hanger sculpture is the highlight of the show. (As she very pertinently says, if lack of talent is the issue, why should she be penalized for something that isn't her fault? Not to mention that her family's dry cleaning comes back on inferior coat hangers.) Brian Sills Snoopy is an engaging fantasist, finally taking off in a rendition of Suppertime ("bring on the hambone, bring on the bun/mamas little puppy loves every one") that's almost too insistently razzmatazzy for its context. The direction by Kevin Bowers (after New York's Michael Mayer) is as unobtrusive as direction in a musical can efficiently get.

The function of the songs is to drag into the light the thoughts and feelings that lie just below the characters surfaces, to make long musical lines out of captions. Sometimes what Gesner brings up is mush, as in the closing number, Happiness which exposes the sentimental cheeriness Schulz generally kept hidden. (Im actually a sucker for this, but it's a guilty pleasure.) Sometimes, as in Lucy's hectoring Little Known Facts or the title song or the glorious Book Report ("I work best under pressure/And therell be lots of pressure/If I wait till tomorrow" -- is Charlie Brown really a journalist?) its ace.

Gesner's original score has been supplemented by two new songs from Andrew Lippa. One, Beethoven Day, tries too hard; the other, My New Philosophy, gives a well-taken musical opportunity to the otherwise underserved Sally. Both numbers are anachronistically rocky for the company they're required to keep or the deceptively gentle world theyre meant to illuminate. Theres a second Peanuts musical, Snoopy, that has a better score and probably a sharper sensibility, but it doesn't have the charm of this one.
