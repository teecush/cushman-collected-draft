Some of the more revealing moments in the TV coverage of the U. S. Democratic National Convention have come on Larry King's show on CNN. He's had Republican pundits commenting on the day's proceedings. (He promises to have Democrats return the compliment at the Republican shindig next week.) I wouldn't say that most of the talk-show hosts and blond congresswomen he has assembled are notable either for subtlety or perspicacity; indeed, as time went on over the first two nights, Larry appeared increasingly bewildered that he'd invited them into his room. But on Monday night, Ben Stein, he of the money and on this occasion the brains, said something that struck home. He said that Michelle Obama's keynote about her husband's great personal qualities was a mass of cliches.

It hurts to say so, but he was right. On the other hand, what else did he expect? I can't think of any political system that puts such sentimental emphasis on a candidate's relationship with his or her spouse. What other democracy assumes that the voters are, in effect, electing a couple? It's as if the U. S. never got over the pain of separation from the British monarchy and still yearns for a pair of figureheads at the top. Maybe it still expects them to produce an heir. (Given the dynastic strain in American politics, that may not be so far-fetched an idea.) So there was Michelle, forced into telling the convention, and the world how much she loves her husband. As Ben said, that's nice, but really we could take it for granted. And as he should also have said, who cares? Not that his own pronouncements were so shiningly original. He expressed his disappointment (pundit-speak for delight that your opponents have done something you hope will damage them) that none of the Democratic speakers had expressed support for America's brave troops. That's a cliche in itself, and who could doubt that if the Democrats follow his advice during the remainder of the week, more cliches will result.

It's also a cliche to say that American politics, especially when televised, are a branch of show business. But nothing else makes sense. The videos that preceded Michelle's speech and Ted Kennedy's were exactly like the buildups given to lifetime achievement honorees at the Oscars: schmaltzy and, in this context, irrelevant. (Ted's was all about what a good uncle and yachtsman he is.) It says much for both speakers that they were able to rise above it when they reached the podium. Kennedy was irresistibly leonine; even Larry King's panel cheered him, metaphorically at least. (Well, Ben Stein did refer to the "disgusting" things Ted had done, presumably meaning Chappaquiddick, but we know by now that pols and commentators only indulge in these fits of morality to score points against their enemies, while keeping very quiet about the turpitudes of their allies.) Michelle, bromides aside, managed to make a prepared speech sound spontaneous, a talent that I, as an old broadcaster, greatly admire. And I believe that she meant every word of it. All the same, it was sad to see her, in effect, dumbing herself -- and her husband -- down. It's the opposite of what we've liked them for. And when her two daughters gave a cute hello to their daddy, brought to them live by video-link from Kansas City, with Stevie Wonder's Isn't She Lovely as their exit music, I wept; but not in the way I was obviously meant to.

Not that that would stop me voting for him, were I American, or will stop me rooting for him, as I'm not. But what is all this stuff about introducing us to the real Barack, and telling us that the Obamas are people just like us? By the time any politician gets to make a serious run for the White House, let alone actually secures his party's nomination, he's already way out of touch with the lifestyles of the poor and humble, or even -- to invoke both sides' favourite totem -- with those of the "middle class." (Nobody in America mentions the working class anymore. I wonder what happened to them.) Elitist? Michelle in her Tiffany blue gown looked pretty special to me. Besides, to repeat Jon Stewart's memorable comment, if you're running for the most powerful office in the world you'd better be better than the rest of us. If it comes to that, isn't that what John McCain's supporters keep claiming for him? Their default position is that he withstood four years' torture in the "Hanoi Hilton," a feat surely beyond most of us. I forget whether the talk-show jock who brought this up on the King show (and seemed to blame Obama for not having been through it) was or was not the same guy who objected to health care reform on the basis that an America that looked after its citizens from cradle to grave wasn't the American he grew up believing in. It didn't seem to be the possible cost of the plan that he objected to. It's the principle of the thing.

It's been wives' week. Hillary Clinton, resplendent in orange pantsuit, made the speech of her life, one that did sound prepared, but in a good way, hitting every point passionate and hard, with the cameras periodically cutting away to a beaming Bill, beaming especially bright at her (literal) shout-out to him. She commanded her troops to support Obama, and though she may, as the scoffers said, have been playing "the good soldier," I believed she meant it. It doesn't seem to have been enough, though, for some of her supporters. Larry, ramping up the incredulity that increasingly becomes him, interviewed a Democrat lady who refused to cast a vote for Obama because "he hadn't wooed [her] enough." (She presumably meant her vote at the convention, not in the actual election, though Larry disappointingly didn't press her on it.) So it wasn't about the country, it wasn't about the party, it wasn't even about Hillary, it was about her. Meanwhile the Republicans continue to use past quotes from Hillary in ads against Obama that disconcertingly punctuate the Denver love-in; if they think she's that wonderful, what were they doing in the other party? Meanwhile they counter the Democrat claims that McCain equals Bush by dissociating him from their incumbent leader. One wonders why the Americans bother to have political parties. Neither of them seems to stand for anything. Unless they're on the meat-market floor of a convention, in which case it seems they'll stand for everything.
