PPPEEEAAACCCEEE

Theatre Passe Muraille, Toronto

- - -

Darren O'Donnell, the talented creator of Boxhead and White Mice, wrote and directed pppeeeaaaccceee with Rebecca Picherak. The triple spelling is presumably connected to the fact that the show has three actors, playing three roles. It would be inaccurate, and maybe even insulting, to suggest that they are playing characters. Most of time they talk -- mainly among themselves, though sometimes they're looking at us -- about life Before, During and After the Revolution. We'll get back to that.

First though, they ask us to engage in "an imaginary group hug." They acknowledge that it may seem silly, but say that it's our last, best hope. An hour-and-a-quarter later it transpires that this ethereal love-in was meant to envelop the whole show. At the beginning they tell us to close our eyes. At the end they say we can open them again. Of course if we follow their instructions we won't see any of the show, so I doubt they mean us to take them literally. In any event, I strongly advise against it. The visual aspect of the production is much its strongest point.

Is there irony here? Maybe not, but there is whimsy, which amounts to a weaker version of the same thing. It permeates the evening. The precise nature of the Revolution is left undisclosed, but it seems mainly to have been one of consciousness. It probably involves the redistribution of wealth, but the trio seem more concerned with its effect on their egos, which it has diminished, and their love lives, which it has enhanced. The language in the early stages of the show is dialectical, or maybe metronomic. Before the Revolution there was X, after the Revolution there is Y, and Y is, always and axiomatically, superior.

Of course, it's possible they've been brainwashed. One thinks of the poor beasts in Animal Farm, willing to put up with anything rather than have Farmer Jones back. Orwellian satire, though, does not seem to be what the show has in mind.

From Vladimir Lenin to Mike Harris, the word "revolution" has acquired so much unsavoury baggage that you'd think people would be wary of using it, even in the theatre, without at least defining their terms. But here it is again, a mantra rather than a program. The most specific the show gets is when it starts taking about the theatre; it's very self-referential. Before the Revolution, the actors muse, there was Clapping and there were Bows. "What" asks one of them "was all that about?" They try going through the motions, to find out what it was like, and they can't continue; it's just too ridiculous.

Now this, apart from being thought-provoking in itself, creates suspense; it starts you wondering how, come the end of the evening, the actors will deal with their own curtain calls. Because they do take them, and the audience does applaud; at least, it did on the first night, and I think we can assume that it will be just as incorrigible throughout the run. The actors stuck to their guns; they didn't prostrate themselves in front of us. They resorted, instead, to one of the oldest cliches of new theatre; they started applauding us.

The first time I ever saw this done -- probably not the first time it ever was done -- was in Peter Brook's production of The Marat-Sade, that play about the lunatics literally taking over the asylum. It conveyed, very powerfully, the actors' contempt for the audience. Since then, of course, it has come to signify something milder: the actors' patronizing of the audience.

That isn't how it's intended, of course, but it's how it always works out, even in the hands (again, literally) of nice peaceful people like the ones doing pppeeeaaaccceee. Because consider this: The relationship between stage and auditorium is a power relationship. While the play lasts, they are in control. They are up there, in the light, doing their thing; we are in the dark, paying respectful attention, even when hardly understanding a thing that's going on -- as is very much the case with this show. (I'm generalizing, of course, from my own reaction, but the vibe I got was that most people were as baffled as I was.) Only at the end do we, the spectators, get to reverse the balance: to assert our authority as customers, sometimes even paying ones, by bestowing or withholding applause. When they start doing it, usurping our privilege, they aren't displaying sweet solidarity; they're trying to hang on to power. And, by the way, applause is not some toadying invention of bourgeois society; it's been going on for centuries.

Some lines in the piece (I am tempted to elongate the spelling, but self-control rules) are evocative, even scary: "The roots of my teeth have turned to liquid" is one that stays with me. The word rhythms are good.

The set (by O'Donnell and Naomi Campbell) is impressive, a huge billowing white drape that must mask a very solid stepped structure, since half-way through all three actors climb up the curtain and perch at its top, in a sort of crow's nest.

Intrepid though this is, it ushers in the most confusing part of the entertainment. The three are hiding from something, and they're still (they say) in a theatre. A caption informs us that is now "during the Revolution." The show, without sense and without warning, breaks its own time frame. Since its initial try-out last year (which I didn't see) the play has apparently made some concessions to conventional narrative, and this could be one of them. But it strikes me as ill-advised. The abstract portions at least have some charm. This bit, which hints that the revolution may not have been as bloodless as all that but which isn't letting on, manages to be both didactic and obscure, which is not an appealing combination.

The actors play together with dedication and what looks like confidence; among other things, they apply a fine sense of shape and variation to a long section in which they take turns proclaiming "I am an idiot," which may be an especially heartfelt form of Revolutionary Self-Criticism. Greg MacArthir is the most winsome of the three but not to excess, Maiko Bae Yamamoto is contemplative but worried, and Ngozi Paul is questioning but tough.

They are, respectively, caucasian, Asian and black, but there doesn't seem to be any special significance in that. The show distinguishes itself from the fiascoes that have so far littered the new season, in that the people on stage seem to have a very good idea of what they're about. What they don't do is communicate that idea to the audience.

Last Thursday I stated, wrongly, that there was no visible tribute to the late Bill Glassco in the lobby of the Tarragon Theatre. The theatre has, in fact, posted a blow-up display of its press release announcing his death. I apologize.
