THEATRE REVIEW

The Changeling

Tom Patterson Theatre, Stratford

There has been, in the last 60 years or so, a renaissance of Renaissance drama. The plays of Shakespeare's contemporaries and successors, long eclipsed by those of the man himself, have returned in force. The revival started in Britain when the advent of institutional subsidized theatres meant a demand on their part for product. So they naturally turned to a period that's loaded with it.

One of the first plays to benefit was The Changeling, a tragedy by the major playwright Thomas Middleton and the minor playwright William Rowley. It is a piece much lauded by scholars but unseen on the professional boards for three centuries. Once restored there's been no stopping it. It's become a staple of the British classic repertoire. In Canada, too, its star has risen; it now gets its second Stratford production.

The Changeling is a play with two stories, that barely touch as narratives but reflect on one another thematically. The principal plot (mainly Middleton) tells of a high-born young lady who, having fallen suddenly for a newly arrived young traveller, hires a servant to rid her of an unwanted fiance, and then finds that she is at the murderer's mercy. The subplot (wholly Rowley) is set in the Jacobean equivalent of a mental hospital. The owner-doctor's attractive wife is beset by gallants posing as patients. She too is blackmailed, by her husband's assistant who thinks he has caught her succumbing to one of her suitors. However, unlike her counterpart on the wealthier side of the tracks, she knows exactly where to tell the men in her life to get off. The elderly doctor is fixated on preserving his young wife's chastity; her unwelcome suitors go to ridiculous lengths to conquer it. This comic section of the play may be the more empowering but it's the other, tragic part that's the more powerful.

Beatrice-Joanna, our tragic heroine, will do anything to wed her beloved Alsemero, including putting herself in the power of a man, De Flores, who is equally fixated on her and whom she has previously treated with disdain and disgust, on account of his pimpled hairy face. "The next good mood I find my father in," she says in spoiled-child accents that ring amazingly true down the ages, "I'll get him quite discarded." That's before she decides she needs him; and before, the deed done, he names his price. He'll take her money, sure, but what he really wants, true to his name, is to deflower her. And to keep on doing it. The two achieve a kind of erotic complicity: true partners in crime.

The play is set in Spain, and Jackie Maxwell's production updates it to the time of the Spanish Civil War. Halfway through she gives us a street parade, whose centrepiece is a giant Franco puppet. Idle bystanders are likely to be swept up and swept away, presumably to the asylum whose textual population of "fools and madmen" has been expanded to include a new third category of "renegades." This seems to me a case of tailoring the play to fit the concept, to no particular advantage. It's true that Beatrice-Joanna lives in a garrison; her father is the governor of the fortress of Alicante. But the script makes no mention of the country being at war, with itself or anybody else, and dictatorship is not on its menu.

In its basics, though, this is a good production. Camellia Koo has given it a colonnaded set with arches that evoke a church or a castle as required. They never actually obscure the action but there were times that I wished they could magically disappear, leaving the stage to the actors. The great scene in which De Flores claims his reward needs a hard, unrelenting focus, every line should look a hammer-blow.

The essentials are here: Mikaela Davies'Beatrice, genuinely shocked and pleading modesty, Ben Carlson's De Flores scornfully, unanswerably, insisting that all that is irrelevant; she's now "the deed's creature." Davies sometimes falls back on generalized anger, but the trajectory of the character is there. Carlson plays De Flores'relentlessness at the expense of his vulnerability but finely captures the lines of thought, the opportunism, the amoral practicality: "a wondrous necessary man" as Beatrice comes to call him.

The action's later stages involve virginity-tests and bridal-bed substitutions; the production admirably keeps unwanted laughter at bay and actually gains in excitement as it proceeds. Cyrus Lane's Alsemero, an adventurer out of his depth, is a help here, and so is Mike Nadajewski as his friend Jasperino, a functional role that acquires an identity, cheerfully randy and halfway cynical.

The Bedlam scenes come off splendidly. The original calls for two intruders - one fool, one madman - but the production gives us only one, the mocksimpleton Antonio (officially the "changeling" of the title). Less here is definitely more, as Gareth Potter gives him a lovely quickchanging double identity: the amiable fool and the would-be wooer who is really the more foolish of the two.

Michael Spencer-Davis and Jessica B. Hill do well as the imaginary cuckold and his sensible spouse, and Tim Campbell, in an unaccustomed role, is outstanding as their employee Lollio, a brutal clown. The two plots do grow closer together as they unfold, and their eventual convergence is here very satisfying. By the standards of its era, this tragedy has a low body-count: a mere four down at the final curtain. But this very economy means that all of them hit home.

In repertory through Sept. 23.
