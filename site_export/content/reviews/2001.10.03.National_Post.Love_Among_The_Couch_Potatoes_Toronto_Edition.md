THE WALLS OF AFRICA

Theatre Passe Muraille Backspace

Not surprisingly, many shows written and planned long before Sept. 11 have had extra resonances thrust upon them in the weeks since. Some of these are quirky: I wonder, for example, how Soulpepper's William Webster coped with his role in The Bald Soprano as the fire chief who tells householders that a fireman is much like a confessor. Some are fundamental: The meditations on Hiroshima in Ota turned on their axis from anguish recollected in tranquillity to tranquillity invoked in anguish. And some now seem amazingly daring, like the opening sequence in The Walls of Africa.

This play starts with an apparently Muslim woman, swathed in black from head to toe, regarded by a seated man. For a long time he just sits and she just stands. Then she starts to move, to undulate and, with artful thoroughness, to strip. Just what are we in for?

Nothing, as it turns out, especially religious or even explicitly political. The stripper is merely one of several erotic visions encountered or invoked in the course of the play by Mr. Pym, a middle-aged professorial gentleman living in what we assume is London in the 1950s. (The play mentions neither the city nor the decade explicitly but it reeks of both, though the characters are universal.)

Every evening, Mr. Pym comes home and goes straight up to his room, there to read or study or let his thoughts wander. Meanwhile, his landlady, a widow called Mrs. Fields, stretches out on her sofa, ever more languorously, with a glass of sherry, and tries unavailingly to attract his attention.

That a shy man, set in his lonely ways, may be so wrapped up in his own sexual fantasies that he cannot respond to the real, available thing even when it's spread before him is no great revelation, and one starts to wonder if the author, Hrant Alianak, will have anything else to tell us.

He hasn't, but he extends his theme artfully and compels our attention. He is his own director and he has scored and conducted the piece meticulously. The early scenes are wordless, and the principals' first extended conversation is almost drowned out by music. Mr. Pym is a great concert-goer (at the Albert Hall, I fancy) and he seems to bring the repertoire home in his head. Terry Crack is credited with this production's sound design, which makes especially intense use of some searing passages for strings.

When we do get to hear the characters talking, they turn out to be pleasantly and poignantly amusing. They find they have things in common, in addition to their complementary solitudes. Both have fond memories of visits to Africa before the war. Both are interested in Tolstoy, though her acquaintance is primarily biographical while his is philosophical. He is reading A Confession (surely a shorter book than the tome he is lugging about, which looks like half of War and Peace) and speaks with apparent approval of its author's late-life devotion to celibacy. She finds this perplexing, especially when considered from the point of view of Countess Tolstoy.

There is a delectable sense of constraint about these episodes, since even when Mr. Pym is persuaded to join Mrs. Fields on the sofa, he sits there with his hands locked to his sides and his hat in his lap. There is, of course, no tension like erotic tension, and these scenes are loaded with it.

Even after the walls of Africa have, figuratively, come tumbling down, all is not solved. He can hardly bring himself to call her by her first name of Elizabeth, while she seems all too eager to address him by his, which is Timothy. (This, by my computation, makes him Tim Pym.) She feels rebuffed, he feels hemmed in, and both, in different ways, feel guilty. And the play gets yet funnier and yet more painful.

It is superbly played. Layne Coleman, currently best known as director both of plays and of this theatre, returns to acting with a beautifully subdued, bound-up Mr. Pym, happy in his inhibitions or at least afraid of being without them -- gifted, probably for the first time in his life, with emotional power over someone.

Tedde Moore performs an emotional striptease at least as revealing as the literal one with which the play begins. She shows us a gracious, contained woman who spends the first half of the play releasing the temptress within herself and the second balancing her need and her dignity -- a matter now, not of preserving a social facade, but of salvaging her self-respect as a woman.

There is a striking contrast in visual and aural rhythms between these two -- he staccato, she flowing -- and also of dynamics: he necessarily on a single level, she modulating between a whisper and a mild conversational volume that, in this context, has the impact of a shout.

We should see more of Moore, who has been absent from our stages for a long time. We see, obviously, a great deal of Nicola Pantin, who plays the various mental pin-ups; she is impeccably alluring as all of them. Steve Lucas' twin-levelled set is, on this agonizingly cramped stage, a virtuoso deal.
