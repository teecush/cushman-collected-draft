You know it when you hear it, but it's hard to put a name to. Its an area of song bordered on four sides by Broadway, Hollywood, jazz and the pop marketplace sometimes known as Tin Pan Alley. It held sway in the United States, and also conquered most of the Western world, during the first half of the 20th century. It was celebrated and chronicled in 1972 by Alec Wilder in a book titled American Popular Song that was immediately hailed as definitive, and has remained so. Tony Bennett, when asked about it, said simply, "Its the Bible."

Wilder's title, however, prompts a few questions. The urban style he celebrates wasn't the only kind of music before 1950 to be both popular and American, even if it was arguably the best, and certainly got the most attention and the most airplay. More recently, admirers have been referring to the the Great American Songbook, but that carries the implication that the book is now closed. There is also Classic American Popular Song, which is the title adopted -- with some misgivings -- by two writers, David Jenness and Don Velsey, for a new book (Routledge) that is an avowed sequel, and a less avowed supplement, to Wilders.

This is a book that had to be written, though one hardly dared hope that it would have been written this well. Its lively, intelligent and awe-inspiringly thorough. So was Wilder's, but his tone and approach were different. He was both a distinguished composer and a distinguished curmudgeon, and his very personal book (published by Oxford, and still very much available) has an engaging air of being made up as it goes along. Though neither Jenness nor Velsey is a professional academic, theirs is a more obviously scholarly work -- which doesn't make it immune to the odd factual howler. Several of these concern Duke Ellington, whose highly collaborative ouevre slips through their fingers as it did through Wilders.

The new boys have different axes to grind. Wilder, who subtitled his book The Great Innovators, 1900-1950, believed the world came to an end, musically speaking, in, at the latest, 1965. Jenness and Velsey, whose subtitle is The Second Half-Century, 1950-2000, think it still goes on, if in commercially diminished terms, and they make a persuasive case.

There is an overlap. Wilder ignored his 1950 cut-off date to pursue the later careers of Irving Berlin, Richard Rodgers, Cole Porter, Harold Arlen and their lesser-known peers. (Jerome Kern and George Gershwin were already dead.) Jenness and Velsey retrace these steps and amplify them. They are dead right, I think, in their assessment of the splendours and miseries of Rodgers and Hammerstein v. the earlier Rodgers and Hart. And theyre wonderful on Arlen -- the very first song they treat is his snappy, unknown I Love a New Yorker, which put me on their side from the start. They are also able to do justice to Wilder's own songs, which he, of course, had to ignore.

Like Wilder, they insist they are concerned with theatre songs only to the extent that they can be sung and understood out of context. This was all right for Wilder, who wrote of an era when theatre songs were pop songs, and were more or less interchangeable between shows. Post-1940, musicals had greater dramatic identity, and songs are more firmly tied to sources. They cannot be ignored, though, because they're too good -- as good, our authors claim, as their predecessors, and they have the arguments and analyses to back this up.

So they tie themselves in a few knots, claiming, for example, that comedy songs are outside their purview. They turn out to be inconsistent about this, though not, to my taste, inconsistent enough. We get discussions of West Side Story minus Gee, Officer Krupke, and of Guys and Dolls (Frank Loesser is rightly one of their heroes) minus Adelaides Lament. They do, however, persuade me of the merits of Ive Never Been in Love Before, the one song in the latter score that I'd always dismissed.

Among later theatre composers, they pay rapturous attention to Cy Coleman, whose superb jazz-influenced songs may never have received serious treatment in print before. (I wish he could have lived to see it -- he died in 2004.) They are also properly admiring of Coleman's much undervalued lyricist Carolyn Leigh. They deal more systematically with lyrics than Wilder did, and a few word men, such as Johnny Mercer, get sections of their own.

Stephen Sondheim gets a chapter more or less to himself, pointing out (apparently it still needs to be said) that his work isnt cold and that his music is as good as his lyrics.

Its also noted that in his later scores he's often abandoned the old compressed song structures. This, plus his frequently unromantic subject matter, makes him a distinctively contemporary figure, even outside the theatre.

Unlike Wilder, Jenness and Velsey don't hate rock, but they dont think it has much relevance to the tradition theyre discussing. They do admit the influence of the singer-songwriters, from Dylan on. The irony (they're politer about this than I would be) is that these writers have been praised for doing what Sondheim and a few of his theatrical successors have done better: without formlessness and -- since they're dramatists -- without the self-pity that too often gets equated with honesty.

As exceptions, with which Id concur, the book names Joni Mitchell and especially Randy Newman, a dramatist manque with dynastic ties to old Hollywood.

In a book that gets in nearly everything (I count myself an aficionado but was amazed at the cabaret songs I didnt know), one notices the gaps. The most glaring omission is Oscar Brown, Jr., who shone in the '60s and unites any number of traditions -- he came from jazz, he was a singer-songwriter, he worked in the theatre, he wrote both love songs and protest songs, he had links to rock and even to rap. When it comes to technical analysis, the authors devote more space to harmony than did Wilder, who was a melody man.

This makes their book harder work for a layman, but you can usually see what they're getting at. It helps, of course, if you already know the songs, but everybody knows some of them, even now. That is the greatest hope for the Classic Song: Its attracting young singers, young writers, young listeners. It will never again be the popular form, but it will survive.

There is little analysis, musical or historical, in Ken Blooms coffee-table book The American Songbook: The Singers, The Songwriters and The Songs, but there is plenty of fact and anecdote, lavish illustration, and the occasional quite biting opinion. (Nat King Cole did all his good work in the '40s; Bobby Darin had "absolutely no depth." Ear of the beholder.)

The book is arranged in three biographical sections. First come the singers, a long but inevitably incomplete list: The fine Johnny Hartman makes it into the blurb but not into the book and there are no entries for figures as smooth as Vic Damone or as quirky as Shirley Horn. Then there are the bandleaders, but only a few: My favourite, Woody Herman, is missing.

Finally, the real meat of the book, there are the composers and lyricists. Bloom omits most of the post-1960 show-writers on the grounds -- a plain man's variant on Jenness and Velsey -- that they had no pop hits. Interestingly, though, when he does write about them, he does so in terms of scores rather than songs. (He is, I suspect, more a theatre than a pop or jazz animal; he has an equally handsome companion-book on Broadway.) Im puzzled by his references to the title song of Bye Bye Birdie -- I didnt think it had one. Most fascinating are the minor figures: I never expected to read a whole page on Sidney Mitchell and Lew Pollack (They wrote At the Codfish Ball for Shirley Temple.)

The sidebars include lavish song lists, happily not confined to the obvious hits, and my favourite story, about a songwriter with a stump instead of a hand, using it to pound a man's head against a saloon bar. That's Harry Woods, said a friend when the cops came. "He wrote Try a Little Tenderness."
