Welcome to the graveyard.

You'll be amazed at how lively it is.

Spoon River is adapted, by Albert Schultz and Mike Ross, from Spoon River Anthology, a collection of mainly short poems by Edgar Lee Masters, first published in 1915. Schultz directed it; Ross composed, arranged and directed the music, which is omnipresent, and he is also in the cast. Its the fourth Soulpepper production for which Ross has set a body of verse to music, and by far the best. Masters poems, a pungent series of dispatches from the dead, are substantial enough to work dramatically, yet simple enough to be understood when sung or spoken rather than read. It's a further strength that many of them are spoken; Ross's musical settings are brilliant, and stylistically varied, but theyre all the more effective for not being continuous.

Spoon River is an imaginary small town in Masters own state of Illinois. Its deceased inhabitants regale us with what I can best describe as their own virtual epitaphs: What they would have liked to have had inscribed on their tombstones, if only tombstones told the truth. That truth, in the overwhelming majority of cases, is dark. The population of Spoon River seems to have divided between the exploiters and the exploited, social or sexual. Those last two categories, of course, frequently overlap.

The book gives us its characters and their utterances unadorned. The theatre version supplies a frame. We make our way to the auditorium through a long black passage, adorned with memorial portraits; blackclad attendants usher us along, and on our journey we pass a coffin. We are on our way to a funeral. The town cemetery is called The Hill and when we reach our destination, there it is, rendered on stage in all its unearthly moon-haunted glory. (All honour to Ken MacKenzie, who designed the set and the superb lighting.) The stage is filled with actors, all of them for the moment motionless. The one live presence is Diego Matamoros, who identifies himself, in the only prose speech we are to hear all evening and the only one not taken from the book, as the sole Spoon River citizen old enough to speak for the community but young enough to manage the climb. He's also a father recently bereaved of his son, though the corpse over whose burial he's presiding is that of a newly deceased young woman. He makes himself scarce, and the massed ghostly presences spring into ... well not into life, but into a rousing, swinging old-time country number, self-accompanied. There are 19 actors in the cast, ranging from company vets to academy recruits, and nearly all of them can play at least one instrument.

The set-up is reminiscent of an earlier benchmark Soulpepper production, Thornton Wilders Our Town, or at least of its graveyard-set last act. Its tempting to describe Masters' vision as a bracing antidote to Wilder's rosier view of small-town Americana, but Im not sure that would be true. The splashes of darkness on Wilders generally sunny canvas register all the more strongly through contrast, and his final message - that the dead forget - is bleaker than anything here, especially as we had got to know those dead people when they were alive. Masters' characters still have all their memories.

The poems sharply juxtapose those memories, especially where married couples are involved. Schultz's production finds a striking visual correlative; the successive husbands and wives appear on either side of a revolving upright tombstone. The first pair (Gregory Prest and Raquel Duffy) seem to have had a long and happy union, and they sing about it, beautifully. They turn out, though, to be thoroughly atypical. Far more representative are Mr. and Mrs. Benjamin Pantier (Prest and Nancy Palk), he an attorney who wound up living in his office with his dog, she lamenting that the only man you are legally permitted to have sex with is the very man that fills you with disgust.

The poems are, for their day, amazingly explicit: "The old, old problem: Should it be celibacy, matrimony or unchastity?" - so wonders a woman who could have been as great as George Eliot but instead married a druggist and had eight kids. Copulation thrives; some ruined girls leave for greener pastures; another is cast out by her husband when he discovers she was raped at age eight.

In the public world, Prohibition is the mark of virtue, drunkenness the norm. The effective ruler of the town seems to have been banker Thomas Rhodes, who also ran the church and the store; we meet an overworked, underpaid employee who might, since hes played by Oliver Dennis, be described as the anti-Cratchit; he dies of his own anger. In compensation, the same actor appears as Fiddler Jones, who died "without a single regret."

The most harrowing stories involve mothers. Duffy plays a seduced servant-girl whose child was raised by her employers as their own and who had to watch his subsequent rise to fame, wanting vainly to cry out, Thats my son. Even kindness can corrode; it's heart-rending. Even more so is Palk's quiet soliloquy as a woman whose son (Colin Palangio) went cheerfully to the Philippine war, and was first disillusioned, then killed. They buried him, too, in Spoon River with, he bitterly complains, a flag over his grave.

Stuart Hughes plays, among other memorable things, a Lothario invincible until he got too old or the women too young; Frank Cox OConnell is a looming deaths head presence; and there's the unexpected sight of Matamoros, in a funny hat, singing and playing bluegrass.

I should list everyone; this is one of those productions that comes over as a heartfelt gift from the actors to the audience, joyously offered, joyously received. Ross himself is an MD who leads from the front; he plays a thrusting guitar but denies himself a song until the end. Then he emerges as Matamoros's dead son, exhorting the just-buried girl, and everybody else, "Is your soul alive? Then let it feed." It builds to a showstopper. It might be accused of privileging one uncharacteristically cheerful thread from the original pattern, just as Masters own pessimism, taken in bulk, can sometimes seem facile. But thats show business.

And if one of the functions of an adaptation is to send you back to the original - well, this one did that for me, and what I found was richer than I'd remembered.

Spoon River runs through Nov. 15 at the Young Centre.
