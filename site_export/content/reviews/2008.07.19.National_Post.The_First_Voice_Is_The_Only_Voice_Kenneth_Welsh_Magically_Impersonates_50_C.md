To begin at the (real) beginning: When Under Milk Wood, Dylan Thomas play for voices," was first broadcast by the BBC, the First Voice -- that's how he was billed -- was Richard Burton. He shared the play's narration with a Second Voice, but he had the bulk of it, and he delivered the long opening speech -- the summoning of the South Wales village of Llaregubb at night, "starless and bible-black -- as if it were an incantation. In the 50 years since, most actors, in whatever medium, have followed his example. Kenneth Welsh breaks the mould.

In Ted Dykstras production, which originated at last years Luminato festival and has now been adopted by Soulpepper, Welsh is not only the first, but the only voice. He handles all the narration and impersonates all the 50-odd characters, vocally and to a great extent physically. His approach to the first, long, lush, adjective-driven overture is to treat the town and the night not as familiar entities, lying there just waiting to be evoked, but as buried treasure, whose constituent parts have to be revealed and enumerated with an archaeologist's sense of wonder and delight. Welsh has to climb down a ladder in the back wall of the stage in order to reach the place; once there, he sets about removing dust-sheets, metaphorical and literal. And that's the way he explores the whole day-in-the-life piece, less as a magician than as a scientist.

He is something of a magician as an actor, with a dainty roguishness of which archaeology probably knows little. He stretches out unself-consciously on the ground to depict female sexuality, blossoming or fulfilled. Thomas view of womanhood might be considered by the forward-thinking to be limited; the women of Llaregubb mostly divide into obliging wantons (Polly Garter, the late Rosie Probert), wannabe wantons (Gossamer Beynon, Mae Rose Cottage, BessieBighead, Myfanwy Price), harridans (Mrs. Pugh, whose sadistic shrewishness drives her husband to poisoning her, in cherished fantasies; Mrs. Ogmore-Pritchard, whose fanatical cleanliness drove both her husbands to poisoning themselves, for real) and malicious gossips. The only happily married men are a drunkard and a bigamist. The others are weaklings, interspersed with the occasional visionary: the blind Captain Cat, the Rev. Eli Jenkins who is the towns poet and historian. Welsh brings them all to life, with a different voice and stance for each. He is assisted by an onstage musician, Mike Ross, and -- most spectacularly -- by two onstage sound-effects artists, Jenna Dalla Riva and Brooke Graeff. They remind us that this was, originally, a radio play, though the BBC at its peak could never have competed with the actual smell of cooking onions that arises at a relevant moment. (And persists through others less relevant.)

In terms of performance, the only fault is that Welsh isn't actually very Welsh, and the lack of lilt makes some of the language less delicately witty than it could be. (This is especially the case with the censorious biddies in the grocery store, who sound less narrow than malignant.) The gentle, langourous voice that Welsh has conjured up for Polly Garter is delightful; it's also identical to the one that Rod Beattie uses to portray Mrs. Walt Wingfield, who as far as I know is pure Canadian.

I admired Welshs virtuosity, and I enjoyed being reminded of a text that I used to love but havent heard or thought about in years. But it is a text, rather than a play, and the solo treatment only underlines that. (And --nothing to do with this production -- I've come to distrust some of its easy rhetoric.) The problem with it is that it has no internal movement. Here it contrasts markedly with its most obvious analogue, Thornton Wilder's Our Town, which accepts that people dont change much but shows how time and events can bring them to agony and to ecstasy. Thomas was a greater writer than Wilder (or at least a greater wordsmith) but this, his only work in dramatic form, is as time-locked as its land-locked.

Its representative characters are Mog Edwards and Myfanwy Price, tradespeople who live at opposite ends of the village and write letters of undying love to one another but will never meet, on this day or any other. Other characters do meet, or at least collide, and the collisions keep Thomas' show on the road. The most celebrated moment comes when Polly Garter sings a contented song about all the men she has lain with, and the Rev. Eli Jenkins, sublimely unshocked or even uncomprehending, cries out thank the Lord, we are a musical nation." It's a lovely incongruous moment, a great first-act closer in productions with intermisssions. (This one is continuous.) But its impact is reduced when one actor, however skilled, is ventriloquising both roles. We lose the contrast and we lose the timing. There are similar difficulties when Mrs. Organ Morgan ("a martyr to music) talks about Polly Gs twin boys and her husband (its organ, organ all the time with him") thinks she's comparing Bach and Palestrina. It takes two to make a failure of communication.

If Under Milk Wood is any kind of play it's a comedy of humours: a parade of farcical obsessives, though without a farcical plot to sustain them. Its also sentimental, a quality driven home by Welshs and Dykstra's decision to ditch Thomas' ending in favour of the Rev. Eli's poem, "we are not wholly bad or good/Who live our lives under Milk Wood": a thunderingly obvious statement that can sound very nice in passing but hardly qualifies as terminally profound. This performance has its pleasures, even its splendours, but it adds a layer of cuteness to material that is quite cute enough to begin with.
