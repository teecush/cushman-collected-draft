CANDIDA

Festival Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont.

- - -

NIAGARA-ON-THE-LAKE, Ont. - The Rev. James Mavor Morell, Christian socialist, takes his duty to his North London flock very seriously, though he seems to regard them less as his parishioners than as his constituents. Morell is a tireless worker and an even more tireless orator; Blair Williams, who plays him at the Shaw Festival, wins our sympathy within seconds. Talking to his adoring secretary about all men being brothers -- everybody says it, nobody believes it -- he sounds witty and he sounds sincere. Then his curate arrives, and Morell changes the subject of his ongoing sermon; he is now preaching about the virtues of marriage in general, and about those of his own wife, Candida, in particular. Suddenly, he seems less attractive. He is still likeable but he sounds tactlessly, even tastelessly, boastful, and dangerously complacent. Our feelings about him remain in flux for the rest of the evening.

Jackie Maxwell's is the smoothest and most absorbing production of Candida I have seen, and the fluidity of Williams performance gives it much of its charge. Morell, who has taken his wife and her devotion for granted, is challenged for her love by Eugene Marchbanks, a hopelessly unworldly teenage poet. (The unworldliness is due less to his poetry than to his privileged position as the son of an Earl. He is an outcast from his family, but still.)

From the moment Eugene makes his move -- and its the husband he tells first, not the wife -- Morell is on quicksand: Not only does he have a rival but his own belief in himself, as his wife's protector and as a social activist, is called into question. Williams is younger than most of the actors who get to play this role, less sententious and more vulnerable, and he shows us a naturally resilient soul being harrowed. The close of the play finds him crouching, penitent and scared, on a touchingly low chair, like an overgrown schoolboy, which, of course, is what he essentially is.

Mike Shara's Marchbanks is a lanky faun, rolling on floors or hurling himself over sofas in ecstasies of adoration or self- abasement. This is absurd, but it is neither embarrassing nor unbelievable; it is done in precise rhythm with the characters emotions. Shaw had a peculiar idea of both poets and poetry, and much of what Marchbanks says is atrocious, whether hes inventing idylls of escape for himself and his beloved, or expressing horror (what Candida's father, with alcohol on his mind and breath, calls "the poetic 'orrors") at the thought of her having to chop onions. Morell points out that Eugene has no objection to menial tasks being performed by women with whom he doesnt happen to be in love; and here, as at many points of the evening, he is intellectually in the right. But of course, in Shaw, people in the right are always likely to get in wrong, and his greatest clowns are liable to be those with whom he is theoretically in agreement. It keeps the sparks flying.

What sets them alight is the fact that both Morell and Marchbanks are aspects of the author himself: Shaw the unstoppable talking and writing machine, and Shaw the platonic idolater of unattainable women. The problem with Candida is Candida herself, who is given little opportunity to justify the passions she inspires. She isnt even onstage very much. ("So I'm to be put up for auction," she says at the end, and it could be a resonant feminist line. But, to justify such pretentions, the play should have kept her around more. As it is, most of the energy goes into the two men squabbling over her in her absence.)

You can see why Morell clings to her; she runs his life. Marchbanks' infatuation is plausible on the assumption that shes the first woman who ever took an interest in him. But, theatrically, one wants more. In his extra-curricular pronouncements, Shaw was known to denounce Candida as an immoral woman who played with emotions she had no intention of gratifying. But that isnt really what he's written. He's given us an underequipped enchantress, almost begging an actress to fill in the gaps by coming onstage and radiating. Radiance is not really in Kelli Foxs armoury but she has charm and practicality, plus bags of intelligence, and they carry her the distance.

Candida, whatever its trimmings, is a conventional triangle comedy, confined to a single room within a single day. Those of us who enjoy Shaw for his craziness are apt to find its neatness tame. But Maxwells production reminds us that the well-made play has its own pleasures; there is a satisfaction in seeing characters arrive onstage at precisely the right well-motivated moment.

The set, designed by Sue LePage, is a very good space to be at home in, cosy and rambling at once. Bernard Behrens is slyly funny as Candida's ill-bred and impenitent dad, an entrepreneur who can always find the best excuses for underpaying his workers and with an eye continually open for a good deal; his daughter takes after him. He is the most Shavian character in the play, not so much because of his comedy but because he shows us, in the lightest way, what in the real world Morell is up against.

Laurie Paton is admirably sour and put-upon as the overworked typist (who decides, when finally given the chance to find out, that she has taken the pledge for beer but not for champagne), and Todd Witham is pleasingly ineffectual as the curate.
