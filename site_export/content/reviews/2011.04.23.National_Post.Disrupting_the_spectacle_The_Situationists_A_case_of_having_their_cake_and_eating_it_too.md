Sky Gilbert sometimes seems to me like the gay Steven Berkoff. Berkoff is the aggressive British actor-playwright-director who has done powerful work in all three departments but devotes much of his energy to proclaiming how wonderful his own work is and how worthless is that of everybody else. Gilbert is a more engaging figure, but his productions for his Cabaret Company always come adorned with a manifesto informing us that what we are about to see is something radically subversive that goes beyond conventional categories like "literature" and "drama." What we actually do see invariably falls into the long-established category of discussion drama, and is as "literary" as anything by Bernard Shaw or Tom Stoppard.

I drop those names because Gilbert himself does, in the course of his new play, The Situationists. He has his actors step out of frame to refer scornfully to playwrights like those two who, by his account, flatter their audiences by tossing ideas around and telling us what to think about them. The implication -actually it's more of a statement -is that this play is above all that. In fact, Stoppard usually leaves you in doubt about what to believe; Shaw was more dogmatic, perhaps, but he was also too incorrigibly mischievous to refrain from periodically dynamiting his own side. And though Gilbert's best plays can be funny (Bad Acting was very funny), Stoppard and Shaw are consistently funnier.

The Situationists isn't one of Gilbert's best plays, though it's possible to see why its subject attracted him. The Situationists were a group of armchair revolutionaries, largely French, who believed that a consumer society could be changed if people were made aware, by histrionic methods, of just what, practically and ideologically, they were consuming. They called it "disrupting the spectacle," though of course they would have had to create another spectacle to do it. And they did have some success. They are credited, not just by themselves, as a prime influence on the Paris uprisings of 1968. They may even have inspired my alltime favourite political slogan: "Je suis Marxiste, tendance Groucho."

The spirit of '68 lingers, but it was still a long time ago. The characters in Gilbert's play want to revive it, in Canada, now. We meet Jacques, a philosophy professor, and Lise, his student who reveres his ideas but can get pretty impatient with him personally. (Though, occasionally, it seems that her feelings may be the other way around.) Jacques, obviously and perhaps in deference to the movement's history, is French. And I don't mean French-Canadian. Gavin Crawford plays him with a spindly gait and an even spindlier accent. He is, for much of the play's first half, incomprehensible, especially given the author's decision to stage his play on an oblong strip down the middle of the auditorium. Your chances of understanding the professor are greatly affected by whether you're sitting on the side that he happens to be facing. Haley McGee, who plays Lise, practically runs the first act by virtue of being loud and clear and very forceful. The fact that the play features a male professor and a female student has caused the play to be announced as a parody of David Mamet's Oleanna, which suggests that some people have a very low threshold for what constitutes parody.

Rightly sensing that, left to himself, Jacques will never get anything done, Lise insists they enlist Yvon, a dropout student even more aggressive than she is, with anarchist views and exhibitionist talents. He is also a compulsive contrarian, expressing opinions on recycling that may coincide with the author's own, and on AIDS that certainly do. He's also very Gilbertian in seeing sex in everything, and in this company he may well be right. Jacques and Lise, of course, deny being attracted to him or to one another. Nevertheless, by the play's end, each of the three characters will have had an onstage orgasm.

It isn't clear whether Yvon, played by Gil Garratt on alternating notes of sneer and threat, is attacking the hypocrisy of ideologues in general or of Situationists in particular. But his methods do undeniably perk the play up: not the seductions themselves but their aftermath, in which intersecting jealousies finally bring the three-handed debates to some kind of comic life. As they advance their own plans for disrupting the Canadian spectacle -say, by planting gay or pretend-gay candidates in either of the major parties and then outing them -one thing begins to dawn on them: It ain't gonna work. It would take too long and probably nobody would notice anyway. The play also gets clearer because Jacques' accent gets lighter; and as Yvon remarks on this, we know that it's not due to the actor being careless. Indeed, his performance begins to acquire some subtlety, and we wonder if Jacques is about to be revealed as an ethnic fraud as well. The play, though, stops before that can happen. Or rather it jumps tracks. The house lights go up; and Gilbert starts disrupting his own spectacle. Only he doesn't do it very thoroughly, because though the audience is exposed, the actors are given the unfair advantage of remaining in character, Jacques' accent included. It's here that the play name-checks Shaw and Stoppard. It's also here that the actors enjoin any reviewers to refrain from giving away the final twist. So I won't, except to say that Gilbert had me fooled for a couple of minutes, and that it was only good professional acting (actually the best in the evening) that gave his game away. Probably The Situationists is trying to be more situationist than the Situationists, while simultaneously telling us that in an age where meta-theatrics are the norm, it's a non-starter of a technique. Jacques might call this having your gateau and eating it. It happens a lot in the theatre.
