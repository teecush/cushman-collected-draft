THEATRE REVIEW

The (Post) Mistress Berkley Street Theatre

Tomson Highway has been absent from the Toronto theatre for a long time. It's been a quarter of a century since Dry Lips Oughta Move to Kapuskasing moved to the Royal Alex. Since then we've had only a revival of his other major play, The Rez Sisters. He returns with a more modest work, a show whose principal revelation is that Highway is a first-rate piano player.

The (Post) Mistress is a cabaret act dressed up as a play for which Highway wrote both words and music. It's been performed in various forms and various places since 2009, but its present production, a joint venture of Pleiades Theatre and Theatre Francais de Toronto, is its first one in Toronto. It's double or triple tongued, presented in French with a bit of Cree, and English surtitles; or in English with a bit of Cree, and French surtitles. The former version is the one I saw; the latter is the one that will be performed for the remainder of the Toronto run.

Marie-Louise Painchaud is the post-mistress of the title in the northern Ontario village of Lovely, near the mining town of Complexity. Marie-Louise is middle-aged and married, for the second time and not, she tells us, very satisfyingly. She appears to know the contents of all the letters that pass through her domain, the show being set in the 1980s when people still wrote letters. At first we suspect her of steaming them open, like the village postal lady in Dylan Thomas' Under Milk Wood, but her methods turn out to be far more rarefied. Most of them are love letters, telling of steamy encounters in exotic locales like New Orleans, Buenos Aires and Rio de Janeiro. These provide Marie-Louise, who has never even been on a plane, with a rich fantasy life. They also provide the show with its songs; Highway has written an effective series of musical pastiches.

The post-mistress' identification with the illicit affairs that she conjures up explains the punning parenthesis in the show's English title. One of the more passionate couplings that she describes is so craftily set up, and then so passionately bumped-andground, that we may come to believe, or at least hope, that it really happened to her, but we're soon disillusioned.

Patricia Cano is exhaustingly roguish when speaking but comes to vivacious life in her songs. The staging is generally smooth though it's a pity that madame's desk, on and around which much of the action happens, is placed far to one side, making much of the show feel, and especially sound, remote. Teresa Przybylski has designed an attractively cavernous set, dominated by seemingly endless shelves of sparsely-populated pigeonholes. These turn out to have an extra significance in the evening's last section whose revelations I must not reveal.

The show's worst faults are that it's dramatically and psychologically monotonous, and - with an intermission thrown in - too long. The (Post) Mistress runs through Nov. 6 at the Berkeley Street Theatre Upstairs in Toronto.
