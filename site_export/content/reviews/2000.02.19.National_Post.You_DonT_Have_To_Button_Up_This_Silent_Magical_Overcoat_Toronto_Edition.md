THE OVERCOAT

Canadian Stage Company

Breezing in wordlessly but not silently from Vancouver, The Overcoat is in a category of its own. It isn't classical mime, since it's done to a full musical score, and it isn't ballet, since it's moved rather than danced.

Whatever it is, it's magical.

It takes its title and its nourishing idea from the appropriately hard-wearing story by Nikolai Gogol, which it moves forward, by about a hundred years, and also westward.

We are now in the 1920s in some unspecified, probably Central European, city in which people move about en masse like demented insects. In fact we are in the landscape of Franz Kafka who, roughly speaking, was Gogol without the laughs. Gogol took aim at a specific Tsarist bureaucracy; Kafka had it in for the capitalists as well as for the bureaucrats of his own time, but he seemed to regard oppression as a fact of life, hard-wired into the human condition. The Overcoat, as staged here, is a graphic illustration of the proverb about clothes making the man, showing that they can unmake him as well.

Our protagonist is a poorly-dressed clerk, that eternal victim- figure of bourgeois culture. Every day he gets up from what looks like a very hard and unwelcoming bed, avoids his predatory landlady (who seems to be demanding either his body or his rent), and commutes to his work-station, over which hang a couple of huge downward-pointing pens. His colleagues mock him, throwing his shabby coat on the ground (repeatedly) and him with it. Superiors either ignore or encourage the persecution. Desirable female workers troop by, carrying bundles of papers and with their noses in the air.

As a Gogol hero, the worm turns. As a Kafka hero, he doesn't have a prayer.

Tired of living off the peg, he gets a new overcoat made to order: a gorgeous purple creation with a collar of bristling ginger fur. His life changes; everybody swoons. Even his boss -- distinguished by an ample watch-chain and a head that's mostly skull -- takes notice; so does his boss's sycophantic brown-jacketed aide; so, even, do the women. He steps out into high society. Eventually, and predictably, he falls among good-time girls, sailors, and he loses it: loses the coat, loses everything. Police are aggressively unhelpful. He ends up in a mental hospital where, eased into a strait-jacket, he emits a beatific smile; it's a perfect fit.

Directed by Morris Panych and Wendy Gorling with great imagination, unflagging speed and perfect precision, the show unfolds to music by Shostakovich: music whose first bars plant a happy grin on the audience's face that subsequent developmentrs freeze into a horrified stare. It takes time; the full brutality of both the musical and visual score only become apparent when the victim visits the cops. Before that, our pleasure in the physical grace displayed takes precedence over any horror at what it's actually depicting.

The magic coat is created by five men working maniacally away at five sewing-machines, while a great wheel turns behind them. Maybe they're in a symbolic sweat-shop, but if that's what it takes, that's what it takes. Ken MacDonald's set is a double tier of square compartments each of which, craftily lit by Alan Brodie, can open up to reveal a new vista, threatening or seductive or both. A flawless, inventive company of 22 (surely the biggest ever seen on any of Canadian Stage's stages, and though some of them are students you wouldn't know it, except perhaps by their dedication) is headed by Peter Anderson who is billed as "The Man" and is indeed somewhat anonymous, but who is superbly skilled and resists any temptation to play cute.

I am sufficiently non non-verbal to register a few objections. Some of the ideas and the imagery here are familiar. How often have we seen ballets of automaton commuters strap-hanging their way towards another day of anonymity? The workplace routines recall the Bob Fosse of How to Succeed in Business Without Really Trying with added misanthropy. (On the other hand, this could be the great Canadian musical minus songs.) If Anderson sometimes resembles the Chaplin of Modern Times, he's a Chaplin without the saving grace of a Paulette Goddard. The scales are weighted against him from the start, and they never look like shifting. Like most expressionist nightmares, this one is too wrapped up in itself to resonate much outside. I think I felt more threatened by the somewhat similar episode of The Simpsons in which Homer got respect when he grew some hair and lost it when he lost it. But the captivating confidence of the whole thing is irresistible, and, with no language barriers to crash, there seems no reason for it not to conquer the world.
