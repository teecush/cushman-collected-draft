robertcushman@lycos.com; Romance runs through June 10 at the Berkeley Street Theatre Upstairs, 416-368-3110

David Mamet's Romance -- a title for which I cannot supply even the most fanciful interpretation -- has acquired a reputation for being verbally unbuttoned even by Mamet's standards. One sits through its first, very entertaining, scene wondering exactly why. We are in a New York courtroom where a chiropractor (not, whatever the prosecution may say, a chiropodist) is on trial for some misdemeanour whose nature is never disclosed but that seems to involve a trip to Hawaii. The accused is an expert at deflecting or not seeming to understand any questions that are put to him, while the prosecuting and defending counsel are constantly at one another's throats over points of order. The judge, meanwhile, is rather out of it, since his hay-fever medicine makes him drowsy, which means he can't remember whether he's taken it or not, which means he takes more. He orders an adjournment.

This is vintage Mamet, all misunderstandings and misdirection and overlapping staccato dialogue, and also all male. It's given a new focus by the legal setting, which just about mandates verbal duels and purposeful obfuscation. It almost seems as if the former poet of junk shops and real-estate scams has found his ideal metier, even if the formal atmosphere means that the language has to stay uncharacteristically clean.

But then, as the defendant angrily confers with his mouthpiece who never wanted him to take the stand in the first place, things change. The chiropractor, we suddenly learn, is Jewish, and all of a sudden the lawyer is calling him on it. "You people," he says, "can't order a cheese sandwich without mentioning the Holocaust," to which his client smartly replies, "we people don't eat cheese sandwiches." He also starts insulting the lawyer's own faith -- "an Episcopalian? What's that, a Catholic with a Volvo?" -- and soon the two are exchanging every racist jibe and obscenity in the book. It may not be too plausible, not in these circumstances and on such slight acquaintance, but it's certainly funny and oddly liberating. Besides, we tell ourselves, Mamet is dealing with ugly essentials, not polite surfaces. And to prove that uptight prosecutors can be just as hypocritical as uptight defenders, he takes us into the home of this one, who turns out to be a closet queen with a live-in boyfriend who resents him for a) keeping their relationship secret; and b) burning a pot roast.

Here comes the wider angle. Out on the streets a peace march is - - well, whatever is the opposite of raging. "So many people," says the judge, whom the protest has delayed on his way to work, "but I suppose that's the nature of a parade." It's occasioned by the rare presence in town of an Arab-Israeli peace conference. This gives the defence an idea; the chiropractor will give peace a better chance by relaxing the delegates' spines. Will the judge grant permission? Who, given the stakes, could say no? Especially as his honour has switched to another medication, one that releases all his inhibitions. Under its influence he reveals that the judicial life ("sentencing people to death and having a reserved parking space") isn't always as cool as it looks. He wants to do the decent thing, whatever it is, though it leaks out that he himself has been an occasional child molester and golf-course anti-Semite.

Indeed everybody onstage turns out to be racist or homophobic or both. Somehow the question gets aired of whether Shakespeare was gay or Jewish. ("No Christian," says the chiropractor, conceivably speaking for his author, "could write that well.") The only group that everybody ostentatiously refuses to slam is Muslims. The joke about political correctness -- and sheer bloody fear -- has some truth, I suppose, but it comes across as very heavy-handed. Nor is the play much helped by a closing spate of far-fetched coincidences. Not that we're supposed to take them seriously, but the sudden switch from intellectual farce to narrative farce suggests desperation. Anyway, the point seems to be that if chaos and prejudice prevail even in a moderately well-intentioned courtroom, what hope can there be for the world? And so it proves.

A wise man once wrote that you can laugh and laugh and still leave unsatisfied, and so it is here. But that's better than leaving unsatisfied without having laughed. And if the targets here are obvious, the jokes are mostly good and occasionally inspired. The Toronto production, a maiden directing effort by Irene Poole, has great brio, with Don Allison giving an especially joyous performance as the judge. David Ferry has a devilishly innocent twinkle as the defendant, and as the opposing attorneys Paul Eves and Stewart Arnott are a right pair of professional barracudas, falling privately apart at the seams. Brendan Gall plays the houseboy for pique, which seems to be what's required. Christopher Stanton adds to the throng of licenced maniacs in a last-minute appearance as a doctor, and Steven Manuel is excellent as the court bailiff, one of those rewarding roles that starts as a solicitous functionary and then sprouts wings of its own. As he sits reading the sports pages while Jew and Christian hurl abuse at one another, you wonder what, if anything, is going on in his mind. It turns out something is, and it isn't comforting.

DEAD AIR!

Mysteriously Yours

2026 Yonge St. - 416-486-7469

The plot and characters of the new Mysteriously Yours show are the same as the plot and characters of all their previous shows. But this time there's a new wrinkle; this, as its subtitle says, is a musical murder mystery that sharpens the element of parody, here directed at old-time network radio presenting cowboy stars in things with titles like Withering Heights. (A Brontesaurus?) The songs are neat and all the cast have voices, including Jean Daigle doing his customary interactive turn as both corpse and sleuth. Indefinite run.

OLIVER!

Festival Theatre

Stratford - 1-800-567-1600

Donna Feore directs a stage-filling production of Lionel Bart's epic crowd-pleaser of a musical, one that even manages in its opening scenes, and sometimes later, to sneak in some of the nightmare atmophere of Dickens' original non-stop chronicle of crime and child abuse. Bart, whose genius was instinctive if short-lived, spoke showbiz like a native, resulting in a self-believing score in which even a rank bad song like As Long As He Needs Me sort of works. Colm Feore revels in Bart's comical Fagin; Tyler Pearse's Oliver is splendidly acted and sung; Bruce Dow and Mary Ellen Mahoney are perfectly matched workhouse tyrants. In rep through Oct. 29.

HENRY IV,

PART ONE

Tom Patterson Theatre

Stratford - 1-800-567-1600

Richard Monette's production understands that the prose and verse parts of this great play are equally comic and serious. Adam O'Byrne's gangling, impetuous Hotspur is an especial revelation, witty in himself and the cause of wit in others (to quote Part Two). Scott Wentworth's sonorous King is both calculating and devout, two faces sporting one guilty conscience. Falstaff and Hal are less exciting, but there are many vivid minor performances and some brilliant strokes of staging. In rep through Sept. 24.

THE HEIRESS

Royal George Theatre

Niagara-on-the-Lake

1-800-511-7429

Tara Rosling gives an extraordinary performance, rising to tragedy, as Henry James's shy, plain heroine. Mike Shara, smooth and gauche at the same time, is the suitor who tantalizingly may not be faking it. Taut direction by Joseph Ziegler, superb design by Christina Poddubiuk. In rep through Oct. 7.

10 DAYS ON EARTH

Berkeley Street Theatre

26 Berkeley St. - 416-368-3110

In Ronnie Burkett's new puppet-play, a mother's love is felt, overwhelmingly, through its forcible removal. Darrell, a 50-year- old mentally challenged man, is orphaned but doesn't know it. The effect is heartbreaking, though the supplementary material, despite Burkett's technical virtuosity, feels like padding. Through June 24.

ARMS AND THE MAN

Festival Theatre

Niagara-on-the-Lake

1-800-511-7429

Jackie Maxwell's production of Shaw's anti-romantic comedy is cute as a whole row of buttons; this has its charms but ends up selling the play short. Peter Hutt and Patrick Galligan give entertaining performances, but the exaggerated Balkan sets and costumes dominate the evening. In rep through Oct. 29.

HIGH SOCIETY

Festival Theatre

Niagara-on-the-Lake

1-800-511-7429

The show of the movie musical of the play (that was also a movie). It never worked, and this busy production is particularly coarse. The augmented Cole Porter score is made up not of songs the characters just have to sing but of songs it's not quite impossible they might; it's dehydrating. Dan Chameroy and the sets are good. In rep through Nov. 19.

THE LORD OF THE RINGS

Princess of Wales Theatre

300 King St. W. - 416-872-1212

At its best, it's wonderful; at its worst, it's far better than doubters ever thought it could be. Scenically and technically, it's a marvel; and the adaptation of Tolkien's trilogy is outstandingly intelligent. Indefinite run.
