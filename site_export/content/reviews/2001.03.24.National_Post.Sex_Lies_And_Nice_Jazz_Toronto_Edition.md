THE BLUE ROOM

Poor Alex Theatre

Arthur Schnitzlers La Ronde, on which The Blue Room is based, is one of the few plays ever to have been banned by its author. Schnitzler, a doctor as well as a novelist and playwright, wrote it as a study of erotic behaviour in his native Vienna before the First World War.

Since every scene involves an act of copulation, it quickly became famous for what Schnitzler, who died in 1931, decided were the wrong reasons. He forbade performances in his lifetime and beyond. The play only became available in the early 1970s when the copyright ran out. At which point, of course, everybody jumped on it.

Some time after the deluge comes this version (billed as a free adaptation") by the English playwright David Hare, which updates the action to the present and to what the program describes as "one of the great cities of the world. The flavour of the dialogue suggests London, but the accents in this Canadian premiere place it in New York or possibly Washington.

As is well known, the play consists of 10 duologues. Two characters have sex, then one of them proceeds to another partner who in turn goes onto another, and the last one to appear rounds things off by getting together with the first.

Schnitzler probably meant each to be played by a separate actor but in Mr. Hares version, as in some others, two performers take care of all the roles. The play did sellout business in London and New York, due to the fact half the cast consisted of Nicole Kidman; even, for a couple of moments, of Nicole Kidman undressed. She was apparently sensational.

The Toronto production does not, of course, boast that kind of star power or that kind of eroticism; its two actors have their merits but they cannot provide the irreplaceable allure of Famous People Doing It.

Mr. Hare is not my favourite playwright but he has done a smart job here, and a snappy one. Updating the play inevitably means, though, depriving it of much of its original force. Schnitzler wrote of a society in which promiscuity was tacitly accepted but officially disavowed; advance the action to the 21st century and it no longer seems a very big deal. The play shrinks from an expose to an exhibition: a jaded display for jaded palates.

One tension in particular has gone: In Schnitzler's world the men always believe the women, barring a few obvious hard cases like actresses and prostitutes, to be more innocent than they are. There is a particular Schnitzlerian-type known, according to which translation you consult, as the Sweet Girl or the Little Darling, a charmer whom each lover believes to be available only to him.

The equivalent character in The Blue Room is a coke-snorting model; and the locale in which she gets herself seduced by a married man -- referred to by Schnitzler simply as The Husband and by Hare as The Politician -- has been moved from the private chamber in a restaurant so beloved of Mittel European drama to the standard hotel room. There is no sense of conventions being breached; the elegant cruelty of the Viennese version has evaporated.

The most obviously brutal scene is the first: a streetwalker has a taxi driver (originally, and more effectively, a soldier) against a wall. Subsequent encounters are more refined but less honest. The early scenes in Anthony Leo's Toronto production are very rushed; it seems remarkably easy to get laid. There are blackouts for the act itself.

The Blue Room is really a bitterly tolerant comedy about the lies people tell, to their partners and themselves, before and after sex; the one married couple we meet are the biggest liars of all. Of the duo here, both young, David Cook actually does best in older roles: as the husband/politician, equally complacent in both his aspects, and as a playwright consumed with authorial vanity: a sporting bit of professional self-satire on the part of both dramatists. Vanessa Hunt, by contrast, is best as Maid and Model, effortful when required to put on weight as the Actress and as the politician's wife, who should probably be played younger anyway; her old man certainly treats her like a child.

There is some beautifully moody inter-scene jazz sung by Melissa Stylianou; if she has a CD, I want to hear it. Her songs match the bittersweet final scene, in which an ineffectual aristocrat (delicately rendered by Mr. Cook) wakes up with the hooker and tries to persuade himself he did no more than kiss her eyes. It is the play's one romantic thought; she, however, is able to set him right.

The expense of spirit in a waste of shame": if you can imagine that great Shakespearean sonnet delivered with a shrug, you have the essence of the Schnitzler and, fitfully, of the Hare.
