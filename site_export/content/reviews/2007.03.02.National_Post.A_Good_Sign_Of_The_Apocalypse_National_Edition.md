THEATRE REVIEW

THE FOUR HORSEMEN PROJECT

FACTORY THEATRE

- - -

You might describe The Four Horsemen Project as an archival extravaganza, though that, like most other possible descriptions, would sell it short. Some years ago Kate Alton, a choreographer, and Ross Manson, director of the Volcano theatre company, heard a recording by The Four Horsemen, a quartet of 1970s Toronto experimental poets who were wedded to sounds more than to words, and whose works were intended to be heard rather than read. They meant their poems as visceral experiences. Alton and Manson, with a quartet of dancers who are more than dancers, have set out to expand that experience, using the Horsemen's poems as base. They have triumphantly succeeded.

The original Horsemen were Rafael Barreto-Rivera, Paul Dutton, Steve Mc- Caffery and BP Nichol who is now dead. He seems also to have been the most influential of the four, and one of his creations -- a pome is your body -- serves both as the show's finale and as its overall theme.

The mix also includes some cheeky animation, designed by Bruce Alcock. Nichol and company described what they did as "intermedia," or so a television show of the time assures us. We're more likely to call this idea "mixed media," though even that is beginning to sound a little faded.

Anyway, part of this production's strategy is to pit its own more advanced tools at the service of the old idea. We start with a clip from that sober old TV documentary projected on the stage's back wall. Then it's dragged down to the floor and the first performer to appear, the joyous Naoko Murakoshi, begins not so much tapping as rhythmically stomping on it. She manages to make what could be a gesture of dismissal into a tribute. She also talks, introducing us to the word-jazz and foot-percussion that will be the foundation of the show. She plays with the words "a drum and a wheel," becoming both as she whirls round in circles that may not be ever-decreasing but are certainly ever-accelerating. And the words themselves keep pace, scrolling in different formations all over the acting area. I don't think they're all that wonderful as literature, but that isn't the point. The writers seem to have been laying a perfect foundation for a show of which they are unlikely to have conceived.

The three other performers are Jennifer Dahl, Andrea Nann and Graham McKelvie; they, too, can use their voices as well as their limbs, and they all have stellar personalities. Nann and Dahl are the sophisticates -- "worldlings," the Elizabethans would have called them, though maybe here it should be "wordlings" -- to Murakoshi's gamine. McKelvie, who appears older than his female colleagues, often regards them with beguiling bemusement; he works through it, as he also gets to share an erotic duet with Dahl.

The most enchanting things about the show are its cheek and its colour. Alton's choreography comes from all over; there is even a Hawaiian number with ukuleles. Manson's direction, much helped by Itai Erdal's lighting, knits everything together, makes everything flow.

Some sequences outstay their welcome but the show as a whole doesn't. Toronto has recently been inundated with tributes to the Beat poets who were the Horsemen's precursors. Here the beat really goes on.
