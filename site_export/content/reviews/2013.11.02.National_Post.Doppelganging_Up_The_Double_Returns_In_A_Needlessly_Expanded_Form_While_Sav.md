In seasonal flight from anything involving zombies or vampires, I arrived at the Downstage Theatre, underneath the Magic Oven restaurant on the Danforth. The play is Savage in Limbo, an early work by John Patrick Shanley, later celebrated as the screenwriter of Moonstruck, which deserved its success, and the playwright of Doubt, which didn't.

Like Doubt, whose characters were mainly priests and nuns, Savage in Limbo belongs to the new school of American Catholic drama, not so much lapsed as haunted, and most stimulatingly exemplified by Stephen Adly Guirgis (The Last Days of Judas Iscariot, Jesus Hopped the A Train). It also fits into the great American saloon tradition, in which the playwright corrals his characters in a symbolic watering-hole and watches them ferment. In Shanley's play, three of the bar-flies are 32-year-old Jersey girls who once went to convent school together. One of them is Denise Savage, who is a virgin and aggressively bitter about it, and about most everything else. Her opposite number is Linda Rotunda, who's far from virginal (three illegitimate births and counting) and is more of a single issue malcontent, the issue being that her boyfriend has recently ditched her, declaring that he wants to be with someone ugly, which Linda certainly isn't. These two seem to be regulars at the bar; the final member of the trio, April White, who once wanted to be a nun and whom the others initially fail to recognize, must practically live there, knocking back all the booze that a gruff but protective proprietor will allow her to pour into herself. She's unhappy, too, but in a more sodden and resigned way than the others.

Rounding out the personnel are the barman, name of Murk, and Tony Aronica, Linda's ex, who arrives to explain that his taste for ugly girls relates to a recent stimulating encounter with a woman whose pillow talk consisted of a two-hour lecture on Soviet Russia. (The play dates from 1984.) In other words he, like everyone else onstage, wants more out of life than he's been getting. That's relatable; and Shanley's dialogue zings, though it's unclear what his overall point is, beyond the reiterated one that we'd better do something with our lives - maybe love one another or die - while we have a chance. Denise, the titular savage, is especially insistent about this, but the least equipped to put it into practice. As starved for friendship as for sex, she suggests setting up house with the other two women, but their other options and her own abrasiveness inevitably get in the way. The limbo she inhabits, and that Murk's bar embodies, must be one of the antechambers of hell. Whether it's escapable depends on who you are and who's there to help you. Denise looks defeated on both counts.

Sarah Kitz's production, with the audience on three sides and at close quarters, is an entertaining, well-orchestrated workout. Diana Bentley as Denise is initially trapped on a sustained note of angry grievance; she improves considerably when the script allows her to modulate. The play may advertise her as its central figure, but it's really an equal opportunity piece, and all the parts here are well-taken. Melissa D'Agostino's Denise sizzles and sparkles in a leopard skin-printed top, and Nick Abraham is puzzled and pugnacious as Tony, the only member of the quintet with an imaginable offstage life. Caitlin Driscoll is both sad and funny as April whose thoughts walk but don't run towards suicide. Tim Walker as her drink dispensing guardian angel is given a really surprising surprise entrance to start things off, followed by a nifty passage of mime, which seems excessive at the time but feels justified in retrospect. He spends much of the rest of the play fixing the others (and us) with an uncanny gaze, compounded of control, compassion and a determination that everyone should pay for their drinks. He also, in the evening's stand-out sequence, gets to dress up as Santa Claus. The play still feels promising, even if the promise was made 30 years ago.

A mere two years ago I raved over The Double, the extraordinarily creative dramatization of a Dostoevsky novella created and performed (them's the credits) by Adam Paolozza, Arif Mirabdolbaghi and Viktor Lukawski. Paolozza directs and plays Golyadkin, the prickly and timorous government clerk (archetypal figure in Russian fiction) whose paranoia is both inflamed and embodied by the appearance of a lookalike namesake who possesses all the social graces he wishes he had himself. Paolozza is if anything too personable for the man whose heart, according to his author, had the habit of "beating on all other people's staircases" and who, on festive occasions, "almost always turned up inappropriately." Technically, though, he's a marvel, whether running on the spot in a desperate attempt to escape from himself or locked in ventriloquial combat with his other self. The same goes for his production, not least in the way it uses Andre du Toit's marvellous lighting to simulate a stage curtain. Lukawski plays the extensive role of Everybody Else, meaning all Golyadkin's persecutors, including most spectacularly a glutinous nightclub MC in a scene you won't find in Dostoevsky but that fits perfectly here.

Mirabdolbaghi plays double bass (and you can bet the pun is intentional) and narrates, doing both superlatively. He's also the figure in Golyadkin's mirror, reflecting our hero's chronic uncertainty over what to do next, and the possessor of the week's second uncanny stare, in this case incorporating a satanic grin. Smaller stringed instruments represent Golyadkin's shapely beloved: a mocking, vestigial romantic interest that is largely the play's own invention. The piece has changed since I first saw it, largely by getting too long; we're on the brink of a needless intermission when the main theme emerges. And though the casual updating works perfectly, the local references (Gordon Lightfoot?) are just too cute. The occasional up-chatting of the audience also worked better in the show's original cabaret setting. These, though, are tiny flaws in a haunting, hilarious whole.
