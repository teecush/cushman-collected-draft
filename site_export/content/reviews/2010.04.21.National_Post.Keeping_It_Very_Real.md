The first time I saw Daniel MacIvor perform one of his self-penned one-man shows, I was knocked out by his vocal and physical virtuosity, by his tight control of the same, and by the equally precise and frequently scary array of light-and-sound effects with which Daniel Brooks' production surrounded him. Subsequent encounters found me still impressed but left me increasingly unsatisfied. All that technique was adorning sinister urban fables that didn't amount to much beyond "look at all the lonely people." A couple of years ago, MacIvor forswore solo performance and dissolved his company, da da kamera. Now, under the aegis of Brooks' company, Necessary Angel, he's back in solitary, having renounced his renunciation.

This Is What Happens Next, a title for whose relevance see above, differs from its predecessors in being the only one in which MacIvor appears initially as himself. He comes onstage with nice unexpectedness, through the auditorium, clutching a Starbucks cup and claiming to have been delayed by the demanding customers of an inexpert barista. It's good stand-up stuff that keeps promising to be more sidesplitting than it actually is. It's also dynamited a little later on, by the performer's pouring scorn on the idea that his opening routine could possibly have been either truthful or spontaneous. The admission hardly needs making but it's there as an early warning example of the second-guessing -- or, in technospeak, deconstruction --that typifies the evening.

MacIvor tells us a story, through the mouths of its various participants. First off, we meet Warren, who's recently come through a gay divorce and is off to reclaim some belongings -- including, he stresses, a John Denver album--from the home of his ex. This apparently is a reflection of MacIvor's own recent marital history, which I had probably heard about but had forgotten. Really, it shouldn't matter whether you knew about it or not. But the show keeps nudging us that it does matter, and then backing away again.

One thing leads to another, sort of. Warren mentions a possible appointment with his friend and lawyer Susan, one tough broad with two problem daughters. We meet Susan, though not her daughters, and find that if Warren breaks their date then she can keep one with Erin/Aaron, a semitransgendered astrologer who in turn tells us about his/her seven-year-old nephew Kevin, whom we eventually meet too but not before we've encountered his alcoholic father. We've been given early warning about Kevin really being the centre of things; we've been told that the author's experience figures here as well. Semi-autobiography keeps shuffling its feet and hedging its bets. Why should we care?

In between impersonations, MacIvor reverts to being himself, or as much of a self as anybody can be onstage or indeed off it; his persona is, obviously, another construct. He tells us of another character, called Will. This is a name young Kevin bestows on a giant who figures in his fantasies as a substitute for his dad. He's also The Will, as described by Schopenhauer who gets mentioned anecdotally, but doesn't appear, though he's the one person involved about whom I wanted to hear more.

Anyway, my impression is that the play is MacIvor's attempt to escape his own Will, the one that dictated the dark-glassed plays of his past, by giving us something personal and optimistic. But of course the Will will have its way. The story that gets told has a shock-horror conclusion, though it's one that, despite a child being involved, fails either to shock or to horrify. Set up as accidental but inevitable, it simply feels contrived. And that's even before the rug gets pulled from under it by a couple of alternative endings. But we know that narrators are unreliable and fictions fictitious. What's more, we always have. Breaking the fourth wall can be fun, but there's no point in getting solemn about it; nobody believed in it in the first place.

I found myself remembering The Blonde, the Brunette and the Vengeful Redhead, the Australian play of intersecting monologues that Lucy Peacock performed at Stratford a few years ago: a more conventional piece, but a far more satisfying one. That goes for performance as well as script. Peacock played people; MacIvor plays rhythms, and they aren't that various. He can be very impressive, especially when switching between tones in a fast furious staccato with his head moving in sympathy like a demented tennis umpire, and underneath it all he's likeable. But all he has to say is that he has nothing to say, which he says repeatedly. The show relies heavily on the audience's regard for its creators' previous work. It isn't a case of the emperor having no clothes, but his wardrobe does seem limited.
