WELDON RISING

Alumnae Theatre, Toronto

Phyllis Nagy is an American playwright based in Britain, and this play, her first, received considerable acclaim when it was mounted at the Royal Court Theatre eight years ago. Either time has robbed it of its sheen, or its first Canadian production hasn't done it justice, or it wasn't that good to begin with. Or probably all three.

The play revolves around a murder. Natty Weldon, the protagonist, is that theatrically familiar oxymoron, a sad gay. He lives with his lover, Jimmy, on East 12th Street in New York, right in the meat- packing district. It is Jimmy who is murdered, knifed by a prowling young homophobe who has been taunting Natty. Natty flees the scene, leaving Jimmy to bleed to death. The killing is also witnessed by Jaye and Tilly, a lesbian couple in an upstairs apartment; they call the police but they don't go down to help. And crouching on the street corner, trying desperately and uncharacteristically to avoid being noticed, is a transvestite hooker named Marcel. All these onlookers, obviously, are afraid; and the point being made is that what they are really afraid of is their own sexuality.

There seems to be a rather basic confusion here. We know to our cost that witnesses to violence don't want to get involved. We know, for example, that when Matty Baranovski was killed in a Toronto park, there were people driving by who heard the sounds and didn't stop, and we know their inaction was due to a mixture of indifference and physical cowardice. We also fear that in similar circumstances we would behave equally shamefully. But however well or badly we acted would not, I think, have much to do with our sexual orientation or with how comfortable or otherwise we were with it. The play takes an untenable idea and works itself into a frenzy over it.

It makes slightly more sense with Natty himself than with the other characters. After all, it's his lover who was killed, and he ran away. What's more, Jimmy died trying to protect him; it was Natty who kept asking to be left alone; and in trying to face up to the killer and to set his own partner a good example, Jimmy ran right into the knife. Now, in the aftermath of the crime, Natty sits around in his shorts -- we are in a heatwave of mythical proportions -- and agonizes about not being the man Jimmy wanted him to be. He has gone through life trying to keep his homosexuality as quiet as possible, and Jimmy wanted him to come out.

Meanwhile, the two women pursue an apparently destructive, alcohol-filled relationship, and Marcel works the street, though with very little to show for it. Marcel therefore has a lot of time to talk, largely about the contrast between his flamboyance and Natty's repression. Marcel always speaks of himself in the third person. Marcel becomes very tedious. The four witnesses offer startlingly inconsistent descriptions of the murderer, though when he is actually produced each of them is able to identify him on sight.

This wildly improbable sequence is apparently meant to prove how self-hating they all are: Each has imagined this gay-basher as someone to whom they might be attracted. Here again, the author wills an image into life, and then builds on it as if it were a proven dramatic fact. The killer then speaks his own piece about how incurably full of hate he is, and very trite it sounds.

At a certain point, when the temperature has soared to heights unmentioned in the Guinness Book of Records, the characters are presumed to have talked and sweated themselves into a state of grace. A golden car cruises along out of nowhere (and just off stage, alas), perhaps allowing Marcel to turn the trick of his dreams.

Jaye and Tilly, currently in possession of a shopping cart loaded down with beer, give up needling one another and take to expressing their love, tenderly and physically, on the street. Jimmy returns to Earth to lead Natty into the sunset. And Natty, proud at last, goes with him: Weldon rises. (This is some clumsy title.)

He has learned that life's not worth a damn till you can say, 'Hey world, I am what I am.' I quote from La Cage aux Folles, Broadway version, and I can't see that the play expresses those admirable but generalized sentiments any more subtly than the musical did.

Besides, maybe what Natty seems naturally to be is shy. And maybe that's an index of repression but it looks here like he's being asked to exchange one stereotype for another.

The play's first five minutes promise good things, and Nagy does appear capable of caustic or evocative dialogue, even if it falls dully from the lips of this cast.

The actors (with the possible exception of Brendan Wall in the necessarily brief role of Jimmy) seem to be explaining or displaying their roles rather than embodying them. Hume Baugh, as Matty, at least does this on a fairly high technical level; the others -- Tori Higginson, Melee Hutton, Scott McCord, Mehron Paul -- clock in at various stages between strident and tentative.

Jordan Merkur's direction, meanwhile, drifts aimlessly between styles. The women's room is placed in realistic relation to the view it's supposed to command, but everything else seems to be happening in limbo. Natty sells lighting fixtures for a living, and he appears to have moved the shop counter into the open street.

This has been touted as a comedy but there are few laughs here. The director has even compared it to Seinfeld, on the grounds that it's about self-absorption and is set in New York (though in a very different part of the forest).

A show as good as even the best TV sitcom is not much of an inducement for audiences to leave home, but the comparison is presumptuous.

Seinfeld is, or was, not only funnier but more perceptive, and infinitely better constructed. It had one episode that featured a character who, like Marcel, always referred to himself in the third person; there, though, it functioned not just as a tic but as a plot device, and one that revealed character. A plausible action is the sine qua non of any kind of drama. Without it, characters are puppets, and ideas merely notions.
