OEDIPUS UNMASKED

CBC-TV, 7 p.m. tonight

For reasons presumably known to somebody, CBC is tonight airing a long promotional video for a Stratford Festival production that came and went two years ago.

Oedipus Unmasked is, of course, meant to be something more than a promo. It masquerades, if that's the metaphor they want, as an arts documentary; and the gods know it has a promising subject. In 1998, Douglas Campbell, who as actor and director has been associated with the Stratford theatre since its inception, directed Sophocles' Oedipus Rex with his son Benedict Campbell in the title role. The staging, featuring tragic masks and platform soles, was modelled on a much earlier one; the first (and for several years the only) non- Shakespearean production at Stratford, Tyrone Guthrie, the festival's originating genius, directed it in 1954.

Make a note of that date, because the TV show gets it wrong. It tells us that Guthrie brought Campbell over to Canada with his family in 1954, and cast him the following year as Oedipus. In fact, Campbell was one of four British actors whom Guthrie brought over in '53, the year the festival started, and he was the only one who stayed. He did indeed play Oedipus in '55, but that was in a revival. In '54, Campbell had played the supporting role of the messenger from Corinth; Oedipus was James Mason, of whom you may have heard. Whether these facts have been fudged through ignorance or design I would not presume to guess.

As everyone knows, Oedipus was a legendary king of Thebes who unwittingly killed his father and married his mother, and who blinded himself when he discovered what he had done.

Oedipus Unmasked has barely started to unwind before we hear the narrator, Paul Gross, intoning about the Oedipus complex. Unfortunately, Oedipus himself didn't have one, and the text gives the actor no room to pretend that he did; he's too busy dealing with other matters, like the plague that's ravaging his city when the action starts.

I once had a theatre history student who twitched his way through a reading of one of this play's scenes. When asked why, he replied that he was trying to demonstrate the Oedipus complex. I nearly threw something at him. This was wrong of me, but one thing the play does show is that we all do impetuous things under stress. Oedipus killed his father, whom he had never previously met, because the old man wouldn't move his chariot out of the way. He is the man who invented road rage.

The film's thesis seems to be that even if the play itself is not Oedipal in the popular sense, this latest production just had to be. A father was directing his son in a role the father had once played. This circumstance, we are told, offers "a unique opportunity to see how an actor's skills are passed from one to another against the Freudian backdrop."

This idea, however, is decisively scuttled at the outset by Campbell pere, who says that he's never had any Freudian feelings about Ben and that it's "a lot of nonsense anyway -- not what the play's about." (It's a shame that he doesn't say what it is about, but conceivably nobody bothered to ask him.)

Ben himself seems less sure: "I've had the blessing and the curse of being very like my father ... we've had an up-and-down relationship. All kids blame their parents -- 'I'm like this because of you.' I'll be getting it from my own kids." Very true, but irrelevant to the Sophoclean Oedipus, who never had a chance to forge any kind of filial relationship, and not especially Freudian, either.

From the very brief glimpses we get of rehearsal, the Campbells could be any director and any actor, except that the directing Campbell, padding robustly around the periphery in shorts, seems unfashionably ready to offer the acting Campbell (and all the other actors) line readings.

It's a shame, because there are things to be explored here. If you want to extend the father-son idea, you might look at Campbell Sr.'s relation to his mentor, Guthrie. You might look at Guthrie himself, who is always worth looking at and listening to (he combined the frown of a dictator with the voice of a choirmaster); we see footage of him rising to his feet to tell the first generation of Canadian Stratfordians, "I do wish that all of you would get rid of the idea that acting is some kind of ..."

Some kind of what, we never learn, since he is faded out in favour of a voice-over. I mean no disrespect, but I would have been more interested in hearing Guthrie's ideas about acting than the ideas anyone else here expresses about anything. His Oedipus, judged by all who saw it to be a towering achievement, was filmed, and we see a few clips, but nothing that lets us measure it against the new one, of which we see even less.

Nobody asks why Tanya Moiseiwitsch's original, awe-inspiring costumes and masks could not have been used again, rather than redesigned. Nobody considers the different approaches to staging Greek tragedy, or why the plays have suddenly become so prevalent; a contrastingly naturalistic Medea is the high point of the current Stratford season, while in Denver, Peter Hall and John Barton, who in London have pioneered directly opposing methods of staging the Greeks, are joining forces on a 12-hour cycle.

Apart from its psychological top-dressing, this could be any superficial film about any show, down to the sound-bites of emerging first-nighters telling us how wonderful it was. Why did CBC bother? And why did Stratford let them?
