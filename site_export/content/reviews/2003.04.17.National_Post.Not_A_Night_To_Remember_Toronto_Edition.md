100

du Maurier Theatre Centre, Toronto

- - -

This year's World Stage season, now at its halfway mark, has brought us some stunning evenings of theatre: far better, I can tell you, than the World Theatre seasons that became legendary in London in the '60s and '70s, but were often simply boring.

Don Shipley, buyer-in-chief for Toronto's international bazaar, has stocked it with exceptional taste and skill, but he, like everyone, has his lapses. One of them is an apparent willingness to be impressed by any show that has won prizes or praise or both on the fringe of the Edinburgh Festival. The Edinburgh fringe is an overstuffed annual jamboree that distributes its favours with indiscriminate enthusiasm. The agreeable but hardly overpowering Gagarin Way got raves there, and not just because it was Scottish. Now we have 100, an English show which comes bearing a Fringe First (Edinburgh's equivalent of an Oscar, and about as trustworthy) and which plays like an unwitting parody of everything denoted by the dread phrase "experimental theatre."

All the elements are in place, all the signs that have denoted the avant garde for 70 years and counting. It is presented by a company that calls itself theimaginarybody. The eschewing of capital letters has long been taken to signify virtue (I've never understood why), and this lot climbs further up the sacred mount by abjuring spaces between words as well.

There are five actors, dressed in different varieties of white smock, and there are cubes for them to sit on. One of the actors carries a piece of fruit. The stage floor is shiny black, though it does assume other shades, including an interesting dappled one, when the lights change. And the action, as you might have guessed, is set in limbo.

It's set there literally, which I guess is a point in its favour. I mean, we're not just dealing in arty metaphor here. Of the five characters, four have just died. The fifth is an old lag, there to show them the ropes. He tells the newcomers that they must each select a memory from their lives, to stay with them always. And he means always. Everything else they will forget. If they either cannot or will not come up with such a key moment their fate, though unspecified, will be awful. One imagines they will join the truly lost souls, neither good nor bad, hot nor cold, milling hopelessly about in the ante-room to Dante's Inferno.

Three of the four deceased are from London; this seems a bit skewed any way you take it, but the idea was presumably that the company would draw on the world it knew and that the audience would respond accordingly. Two of the three knew each other in life; in fact they were lovers who died together in an accident. The third is an executive high-flyer whose career was cut short by a brain disease.

The lone foreigner has a French accent, but that's probably just the actor; he comes from an unspecified African village where he antagonized his fellows by telling them the world was round. It may be irreverent of me to be reminded of Michael Flanders' Reluctant Cannibal who got clobbered by his peers when he tried to persuade them that eating people was wrong -- it may even be racist -- but I can't help it.

All the candidates act out scenes from their pasts, with the other actors dropping their immediate identities to help out in supporting roles. These episodes can take place in offices or parks, or on underground trains, or at speed-bike races. Whatever the setting, it's summoned up with the aid of some bamboo sticks, which apparently are standard issue on the doorstep of eternity. These implements are unquestionably versatile -- it's even possible that in jungle scenes we are watching bamboo canes playing bamboo canes - - but that's because they're featureless, and I groaned every time the actors started twirling them. It's like watching a drama school exercise going on for 60 minutes.

Sometimes the proffered memories fail to pass muster with whoever the authorities may be, while a successful application is signalled by the shining, in the audience's eyes, of a battery of bright lights. It seems unlikely, though, that we're being offered a whole new mythology of the afterlife. Rather we're being told, none too subtly, to make the most of our earthly lives while we have them -- to Seize, in fact, the Moment.

A suffocating air of piety, earnest but not earned, afflicts the entire enterprise. People wrote plays with similar settings and themes in the 1930s; there's an especially ineffable English version called Outward Bound, in which death is an ocean liner. This new treatment, with several decades of the group-creation ethic at its back, is even sicklier.

The writing is credited to Neil Monaghan, with input from Christopher Heiman (director) and Diane Petterle (producer). Between them they have achieved a remarkable level of watery banality: not a character, not a situation, not a single line comes to life. One doesn't care how these people have spent or misspent their lives. One is only aware of the show's creators hovering over them, tut- tutting.

The show comes with one of the most touchingly overbearing press releases I have ever encountered. It's an orgy of name dropping. The production team invoke Chekhov (Michael, not Anton), Lecoq (there is some mime), Stanislavski (who would have taken to his bed had he seen the sloppy generalities that pass here for acting) and Gabriel Garcia Marquez. The director, in his program bio, carries this quest for glory by association to even more tenuous extremes, informing us that he studied acting at "Cygnet Training Theatre (Patron Peter Brook)." Gosh. The actors, we are told, have between them worked with Peter Hall, Trevor Nunn, Mike Alfreds and Katie Mitchell. So have many: What I want to know is, would any of these directors work with any of these actors again?

The current script, though, may not be a fair test. It offers its players nothing to bite into, especially given that the cast we see is not altogether the same as the one that first worked the show up. But what they come up with is very forced. It's possible, listening to their back-slapping line readings, to conclude that they must have been hired for their physical skills, but it's not as if any very extraordinary demands are being made on these. It's no big deal for actors to suggest a subway ride by swaying from side to side while hanging on imaginary straps; it's been done.

I spent a lot of time resenting Lawrence Werber, who plays the hellish host, for acting the nag when he should have been acting implacable. There turns out to be a reason for this (it's the show's one half-decent surprise) but I still think he could have used more variety. He, by the way, is saddled with the device that gives the play its title; his charges have to make their decisions before he can count up to the magic number. I can't explain why this seems to me to epitomize the evening's quality as nothing else could, but it does.
