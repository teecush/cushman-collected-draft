Two days after the U.S. election, I was in the lobby of the Public Theatre in New York. It was crowded with people, many of them milling around a buffet table at the side. Eventually, the milling stopped; the crowd formed itself into a circle, stretching right around the room. Then, one by one, the people in the circle identified themselves. They were all, it seemed, members of the Public Theatre's staff. I, standing on the periphery peering in, was the only civilian present. When it came to my turn to declare myself, I had to admit that I was only a member of the Publics public.

Into the middle of the ring stepped Oskar Eustis, the Publics artistic director, to rally his troops. Nothing", he said, "that happened Tuesday night changes what we do. It just makes it more important. It was a great morale-building moment.

One of the things the Public is currently doing is a play called Women of a Certain Age. The play, written by the long-serving American dramatist Richard Nelson, is the last part of a trilogy whose overall title is The Election Year in the Life of One Family. The family name is Gabriel, and its members live in upstate New York. The assembled women of a certain age are the wife, ex-wife, mother, sister, and sister-in-law of a recently deceased novelist and playwright. This final instalment takes place on Election Day itself, but since it obviously had to be written well before that date, the story tactfully stops at 7 p.m., before any results could be in. The exwife is an ex-actress, who is rehearsing a solo tribute to Hillary Clinton. All the Gabriels seem to be Hillary voters, though none of them appear terribly enthusiastic about it. One tells of her son emerging from the polling station, literally holding his nose.

Most Broadway theatres were closed on Election Day, not wanting to compete with the cliff-hanging action outside, but this play, off-Broadway, actually opened that night. The performance, with nobody knowing the real-world outcome, was apparently a cathartic experience. The Canadian Stratford veteran Roberta Maxwell is in the cast, and she gives a magnificent performance as the grandmother whose body is frail but whose words are acerbically strong. Maxwell told me that the actors had emerged euphorically for the first-night party, only to find joy draining out of them, as the news came in.

That must have been the whole city's story, and not just that of the arty crowd. When I first arrived in New York, a cab-driver informed me: The Trump party is over at the New York Hilton ... and our future president is holding hers at the Javits Centre." New York, of course, isn't America. That lesson has been driven home. But what was notable about Women of a Certain Age was its premonitory elegiac quality. When I saw it, two days after the premiere, the explicit references to Trump and Clinton had become the least persuasive things about it; they felt, in fact, like sore thumbs. But the play as a whole tapped into a national mood.

The Gabriels wouldnt qualify as working class, but theyre white middle class and they're struggling. They're dyed-in-the wool Democrats but they have the same economic concerns as the most rabid Republicans. The late Thomas Gabriel apparently made a success of his writing, but his first wife has had to give up acting for teaching, while her successor, a retired doctor, sees no way of returning to her former job and will also have to teach. Thomass brother is yet another teacher, of piano; hes also a cabinetmaker and greatly resents some of his richer clients.

His wife has been working in catering, part-time, but is about to commence work as a hotel maid; the grandmother informs her that is the same job, in the same place, at which her own mother had worked a century ago. Grandma is responsible for the family's overarching fear; she had been sweettalked into taking out a reverse mortgage as result of which the

Gabriels are about to lose their home.

Many of the fears and prepccupations it presents overlap symptomatically with those of another family drama The Humans, currently the most lauded play on Broadway. I saw the two shows on successive nights, and sometimes felt as if I were watching the same play twice. The one at the Public, though, is clearer, subtler and less pretentious.

I don't know what Donald Trump would make of it. Its common knowledge, though, that he has been much exercised, even though he hasnt seen it, by another show that started its life at the Public, before transferring to become a Broadway phenomenon. I refer of course to Hamilton: An American Musical. Mike Pence recently attended and was roundly booed by members of the audience.

Then, after the show proper had ended, Brandon Victor Dixon, who now plays Aaron Burr, stepped forward, on behalf of the musical's creators and his fellow performers, to express the hope that the vice president elect had taken its message of inclusiveness to heart. (Hey, an actor playing a past VP addressing a real-life future one. How's that for fourthwall-breaking.) Pence seems to have taken this in good part, even commending it as an example of free speech, but his boss was greatly offended by it, tweeting that it was "rude and insulting and that there should be an apology.

Im torn on this. The purist in me feels that the show should have been content to make its own statement, which is powerful and, I would have thought, unmissable. This is the musical in which a cast of black and Latino actors stake a claim to their countrys history by playing America's founding fathers. It's the show in which the line immigrants, we get the job done," uttered jointly by Hamilton himself and the Marquis de Lafayette, was already raising the roof when I saw it, a week into its Broadway run. On the night of the Pence visit, it stopped the show. Its been argued that the tradition of criticising living politicians from the stage stretches back to Aristophanes, who in one comedy had his chorus step out of the story to attack the demagogue Cleon and in another put him on stage as a character. But these were parts of the plays as written; they werent extra-dramatic add-ons.

On the other hand: Dixon's speech was very far from being insulting. He actually began by asking the audience to cease their booing, and his words were reasonable and respectful. A man about to ascend to the second-highest office in the land should be able to handle public disagreement, as indeed it seems Pence was.

Trump, undeterred by this, further tweeted that he had heard that Hamilton was "much overrated which, true or not, is startlingly irrelevant. It's also petulant, of a piece with his spoiled-child characterization of the protests that followed his electoral victory as unfair;" How dare they rain on his parade? Meanwhile, at a performance by Hamiltons Chicago company, the "immigrants line was greeted by a man in the audience getting up and shouting: We won! Get over it!" That really was rude, to put it mildly.

Unlike some, I cant pretend to know what goes on in the president-elect's head. My guess is that he believes every word he says - however selfcontradictory - at the moment he says it. Perhaps it's the secret of his success. I suspect that he will have to get used to being attacked from the stage; sooner rather than later, there will be off-Broadway plays in which he is a character.

Hes already inspired theatrical comparisons. In The New York Times, a few weeks before the election, a leading Shakespeare scholar was comparing him to Richard III. This, I think, was unfair to both parties. Theres more to Shakespeare's Richard than Trump with a hump. And, nobody has accused the Donald of murdering his relatives to get to the throne. There is also the fact that Richard has a great line of ironic self-deprecation, which includes casting doubt on his attractiveness to women. That does not sound very Trumpish.

The great Trump analogs come from a dramatic source less exalted and more recent. It's been pointed out that, 16 years ago, The Simpsons showed a future President Lisa inheriting the office from a disastrous President Trump. It seemed like an incredible flight of fancy at the time. But there was a more acute if unconscious prophecy in another Simpsons episode, the one in which Sideshow Bob runs for office against Mayor Quimby. His platform is that Quimby is soft on crime; his proof is that Quimby let Sideshow Bob out of jail.

This sounds uncannily like Trump claiming that he was the right man to fix tax loopholes because he himself had so thoroughly exploited them. That was also the episode in which Bart and Lisa, like reluctant Clintonians, campaigned with signs reading "Vote for Quimby. This time he really is the lesser of two evils." The more skeptical of Richard Nelson's characters might have empathised. Oskar Eustis would, I think, have recognised the spirit of wit as part of what he and his team intend to preserve at the Public, even if this example came from California.

I have been visiting New York for 50 years, and have never fallen out of love with it. Right now, I love it more than ever.
