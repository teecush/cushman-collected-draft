THEATRE REVIEW THE PHILADELPHIA STORY

Monday, December 04, 1989

ROBERT CUSHMAN

LONDON, Ont. -- Written by Philip Barry Directed by Robin Phillips Starring Nancy Palk and Joseph Ziegler Phillips tells an elegant probing Story BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail LONDON, Ont.

The audience at the Grand Theatre clapped when the curtain rose on The Philadelphia Story, and for once the applause was justified. Elis Y. Lam has designed a drawing room that is a symphony in pearl-grey. With its mirrors, windows and double doors, the whole thing is a touch larger than life but not so as to be overwhelming. Abstract enough to be esthetically respectable, it is still a solid machine for acting in.

The visual pleasures extend to the staging. Nothing in Robin Phillips's production is obviously spectacular, but it is a tonic to see a show in which everybody seems to be in the right place at the right time without having to resort to violence to get there.

As expected from Phillips, the craft supports a probing, elegant view of a play that, if only by default, ranks as an American classic. The Philadelphia Story is Philip Barry's best-known portrait of what one jaundiced outsider describes as the privileged class enjoying their privileges. A Broadway success in 1939 with Katharine Hepburn in the lead, it has reached millions in the film version that teamed Hepburn with Cary Grant and millions more when musicalized in 1956 as High Society. That title, by the way, denotes a certain democratic unease. The accompanying slogan, as I remember it, was "the low-down on high life," suggesting that the rich were there to be goggled at. Barry, born to the money himself, had taken them on their own terms.

Or nearly. One of his lowlier characters, a reporter, does put the surly question: "What right has somebody like Tracy Lord to exist?" thereby raising all kinds of awkward considerations. The immediate answer is that if she didn't, the play couldn't. Tracy is our heroine and the play is her fable: the thawing, in the play's reiterated imagery, of an ice-maiden. A young lady with exalted standards, she disapproves of her father for philandering, and of her first husband for reasons unclear, but she seems to find him lightweight.

We encounter Tracy about to take on a replacement: a foursquare, not to say stuffy, young man who has worked his way up from nowhere to be something big in coal mining. Of course, he is quite wrong for her, and the play's objective is to reunite her with C. K. Dexter-Haven, her number one spouse and social equal. However, before reaching the desired Haven, Tracy has to go through the mud. She (a) gets drunk and (b) imagines that, while under the influence, she has gone to bed with the reporter. This humanizes her.

There is a dispensable major character and a confusing subplot involving blackmail, but your attention is captured from the outset and never released. The dialogue is beguilingly smooth, part of a confident Broadway tradition. At least it seems confident; there are tell-tale glitches. Tracy calls her father "Father," which is reasonable, but he calls her "Daughter," which suggests a straining after style. This, all too obviously, is comedy with heart. It puts up a sophisticated front, but it goes to bed early.

Then there is all the chat about class, issuing in the comfortable conclusion that there is good and bad at every level. A true comedy of manners would ignore the issue entirely or would bore from within, no moralizing. Barry exemplifies the embarrassment of being earnest.

This solemnity can trip Phillips's production up. At times he digs amazingly deep. Nancy Palk as Tracy makes it clear that her frigidity is more than a matter of style; her sexuality frightens her, and the revelation of that fear disturbs her even more. Hers is a good, serious performance with the appropriate rangy looks. But it lacks fizz. Barry, bastardizing the American screwball tradition, assumes a sparkle if he doesn't actually write it, but this Tracy gets drunk without a trace of madness. And this uncertainty bedevils the entire show. Give this cast a line that's actually funny and they will hit it good and clean, but tripping blithely through the barren bits between is beyond them. It may be a lost art.

The most surprising performance is Joseph Ziegler's Dexter. He seems so young: a dark-glassed drifter in a style 20 years junior to the play, and hardly in a position to lecture Tracy on life or love or anything else. But, through sheer wit and panache, he imposes himself, indeed, makes excellent sense. This is a Dexter who resembles a reed but proves a rock. As opposed to George, the intended, whom Peter James Haworth presents as a splendidly complacent hunk, a man who gravitates naturally to platforms. The drawing room has a dais, and he lives on it.

Peter Donaldson's journalist is another reappraisal, not a James Stewart charmer but a roughneck intellectual who looks like Andre Previn redrawn as a prizefighter. Dixie Seatle as his camera-toting partner is thoroughly traditional and a thorough joy, the infallible hard-boiled gal with a heart of silver. Lewis Gordon and William Webster are reliable elders, respectably raffish and responsible. At the other extreme, Tracy's kid sister, played by Marguerite Pigott, seems old enough to be her twin, which has an unfortunate effect on some passages of text that have staked their all on being bewitchingly precocious.

I regretted, too, that economy has dictated the omission of Mac, the nightwatchman, "a prince among men," whose absence severely damages the second-act curtain. All the act-endings, as it happens, are staged with surprising tentativeness. And bringing on balloons and confetti at the curtain-call is no compensation; neither play nor production has been that kind of festive. Besides, on the first night, Friday, some of the decorations descended three whole acts ahead of their cue, which disconcerted everybody. I suspect that the whole show is going to gain in confidence.
