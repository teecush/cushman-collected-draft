OLEANNA

Young Centre for the Performing Arts, Toronto

---

Twenty years after its premiere, David Mamet's Oleanna can still have an audience literally gasping. It certainly does so in the production that kicks off Soulpepper's American year. Not that it's been officially announced as such, but it's hard to see how else to describe a season that includes Mamet, Miller, Wilder, Williams, Simon, Saroyan and The Fantasticks.

Laszlo Marton's production of Oleanna is powerful, accomplished and unbalanced -- the last being a quality that isn't nearly as inherent in the play as popular mythology might suggest. There are two characters: John, a university professor on the verge of tenure, and Carol, one of his students. There are three scenes, all of them set in John's office.

In the first scene, Carol arrives to protest a failing grade that -- judging from the one sentence we hear of her paper --she deserves. Her complaint is that she doesn't understand: what John says in class, what he's saying now, and what's in the books. He offers to scrub her grade, start again, and give her some private tuition. He also makes the mistake of putting a comforting hand on her shoulder. Next thing he knows, he's being accused of sexual harassment. In the second scene, he asks her to withdraw her complaint to the tenure committee. Things get worse. The committee makes its decision. In the couple's last interview -- the only one that, in this production, takes place with the door closed --all hell breaks loose.

The visceral reaction the play evokes has to do with the apparent unfairness of it all. Carol gets very articulate. Terms like "rape" get thrown around in a context to which they do not, if words mean anything, apply. As John, Diego Matamoros brings him superbly to life, tying himself in a tighter knot, the harder he tries to make himself clear. John spends a lot of time on the phone, negotiating the purchase of a house befitting his new station. Believable telephone conversations are among the rarest things in drama, but Matamoros actually persuades us that there are real people on the other end of the line.

With fiendish persistence, Mamet uses the constantly ringing phone to advance the plot and to delay understanding between the characters. The tension here is raised very high, with Sarah Wilson giving an equally fine performance as Carol. As a matter of fact, she gives two equally fine performances. The usual knock against this play is that the mousy student of the first scene is unrecognizable as the aggressive spokewoman of the rest. I've never believed this; it seems to me that Carol starts as a blank slate who then gets written on. Wilson, though, pushes the contrast to extremes.

First, she's a bewildered naif, whose breakdown is truly alarming. Then, she's an impervious, unyielding, sternly spoken, self-appointed symbol. What gets lost, though, is that Carol actually has a case: a general if not, in this grotesque instance, a particular one. When she talks about the disadvantages and humiliations that she's had to overcome to get here, there's no reason not to believe her. And she's dead on when she criticizes John's privileged pride in his "maverick" status.

The irony, in a play that's loaded with them, is that she, the apparent revolutionary, is the conservative one. She doesn't want to hear the education system mocked, because she has too much invested in it. She attacks John for misusing his "power," and proves her point by abusing her own, thereby provoking those audience gasps. She goads him too far, and in his response he effectively destroys his own life.

It's a wonderful ending, but three things conspire against it here. First, there has never been any previous sexual tension. I don't mean that John actually has designs on Carol, but when he says he wouldn't touch her with a 10-foot pole, we should at least feel that he's protesting too much. Second, Teresa Przybylski's stark, white set starts doing crazy things meant to illustrate the denouement but comes close to destroying it. Third, and minor: John in his fury uses the phrase "political correctness," which may be meant to show him again resorting to cliche but sounds more like the author doing it. He writes a mesmerizing play, and then puts a bumper sticker on it.
