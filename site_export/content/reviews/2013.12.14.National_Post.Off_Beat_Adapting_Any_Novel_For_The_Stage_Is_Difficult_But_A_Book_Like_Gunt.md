Adevice that may work in a novel can seem like a thin literary conceit when transferred to the stage. That is the principal problem with the bold attempt by the independent company Unspun Theatre to adapt Gunter Grass's The Tin Drum. Oskar, the central character of both book and play, addresses us from the German mental hospital to which he's been voluntarily confined sometime in the 1950s. Oskar is 30, and he stopped growing - physically, not mentally - at the age of three. This was another conscious decision, taken to avoid the pangs and responsibilities of adulthood. These would have been especially onerous for Oskar since he grew up, or didn't grow up, in Danzig, the polyglot city whose disputed Polish or German identity was a flashpoint for the outbreak of the Second World War.

Oskar's own identity recapitulates that of his hometown in, so to speak, little. His father Alfred is German, and an early convert to Nazism; his mother's family is Polish, one of them being a cousin, Jan, who's also her lover and quite possibly Oskar's real father. This situation, which seems to be tacitly accepted on all sides, doesn't stop Alfred and Jan from being card-playing best buddies, but it finally brings death and destruction. There seems no particular reason for Oskar to blame himself for this, but the novel appears to think he should. Or at least the play does. Or at the very least the program-note does. It asks us to consider "what kind of burden do we bear when we watch history unfold but do not intervene?" By this reckoning, Oskar's indifference to the dramas in his own house (and a convenient hotel) are a dress rehearsal for his opting out of the greater cataclysm that engulfs his whole community. All he cares about - and here the story does begin to build a case - is his drum.

Or his drums. He goes through a lot of them, having been given his first, as a kind of pacifier, when he was three. Beating them, furiously, becomes his primary method of communication; his secondary method is a scream that really can shatter glass. Both these talents come in useful in his subsequent young-adult career as a professional entertainer. As a child, he's acquired his instruments from a friendly storekeeper, obviously Jewish, by whose eventual fate he is quite unconcerned. When the Danzig post office mounts a last desperate stand against the invading Germans, Oskar's greed for the city's last available drum causes the death of cousin Jan, who has been reluctantly conscripted into heroism.

The post-office siege is one of the most powerful sequences in an uneven production by Chris Hanratty, who's also one of the two adapters. An earlier pogrom is similarly effective, at least visually; in both scenes the noise, ingeniously created through the pounding of found objects, tends to drown out the dialogue and hence the narrative. Storytelling, though, is hazardous throughout, the difficulty being not so much making the events clear as making them count. As often happens with novels dramatized, we get one thing after another, rather than one thing leading to another. The adulterous triangle in Oskar's home takes up more time than it justifies. There is also the unstageableness of Oskar himself, played by a full-grown actor (Jesse Aaron Dwyre) who makes no pretence of being anything else, in a staging that isn't fanciful enough for us to make the necessary imaginative leap. We have to keep reminding ourselves that he's supposed to be a midget, a task made more complicated by scenes in which, temporarily deprived of his drum, he is said to have grown to full size. Played with some charm but little individuality, this Oskar is a more unreliable narrator than may have been intended. We're told that he's an omnivorous reader, much affected by early exposure to Goethe and Rasputin. But what they meant to him, and how they influenced him, is never explored. He remains opaque.

The general performance level is variable, very. Cyrus Lane, an actor of great sardonic power, plays Jan, indifferently at first but then quivering with terrified life in the face of death. He's even finer, later, as a liberated concentration-camp inmate saying a determined, demented Kaddish over the body of a man whom he doesn't know, but may possibly suspect, to have been an enthusiastic anti-Semite; it's the high point of the evening. (The word "Jew" is never uttered in the play, though the word "synagogue" is; decide for yourselves whether this represents subtlety or coyness.) There's much doubling by everyone but Oskar; Courtney Ch'ng Lancaster is good in all her guises, especially the shy ones, and Shira Leuchter (the other adapter) blossoms when she dons an Italian accent. She does this when playing a circus psychic, Oskar's lover (he's sexually active, in all his dimensions) and one of a troupe of selfstyled freaks who school him in the wisdom of remaining aloof: wisdom that comes back to bite them. Him too probably: but, despite a scene of choral guilt-tripping that attempts to tie everyone and everything together, you never really know.

Repetitive Strain Injury, from another indie group that calls itself Company Kid Logic, has already received some of the rudest reviews in memory, and Rob van Meenen's play does indeed live all too faithfully up to its title, especially the adjective part. But its second half does contain a couple of half-decent if essentially free-floating scenes. In one a married couple conduct a believable row, even though the issue that gives rise to it is well beyond belief. In the other a non-married couple seem to be approaching an interesting rapprochement: until, that is, it transpires that one of them is playing a trick not only on the other but on us. The two relationships do nothing to illuminate one another, and are tied together as narrative only by the thinnest pieces of string. These include a fifth character whose story and function I cannot bear to describe, except to say that she's a supposedly wise figure who exemplifies the play's wild erratic swings between failed comedy and attempted tragedy. It would take acting and direction of exceptional deftness to keep such a script afloat. It doesn't happen here.

The Tin Drum runs through Dec. 14 at the Daniels Spectrum; Repetitive Strain Injury runs through Dec. 15 at the Factory Studio.
