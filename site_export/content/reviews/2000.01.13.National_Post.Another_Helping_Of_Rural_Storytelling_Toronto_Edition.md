PERFECT PIE

Tarragon Theatre, Toronto

Nancy Palk is an actress whose lean rangy build and alert glittering eyes usually lead her to be cast as high-style women in high-style plays. She also carries with her, though, a relaxed earthiness of which we got a rewarding glimpse last year when she played one of the mothers in Our Town. Her role in Judith Thompson's new play, Perfect Pie, summons the same quality, only deepened and enriched. It may be the best performance she has given.

She plays Patsy, who owns and lives on a farm outside a small Ontario town. She inherited it from her parents and now runs it with her husband, and its doing well for them. (Theyve recently been offered a million for the land.) She also, as the title indicates, bakes very good pies -- the talent was bequeathed her along with the farm -- and she begins the play by sending one of them, along with a taped greeting, to a girlhood friend whom she hasn't seen in years. The friend, in fact, left town, apparently never to return, following a grievous accident that put Patsy into a coma lasting weeks.

The two had come from opposite sides of the tracks, a metaphor given striking life in Sue LePage's set, which is dominated by a railway line curving up and round the stage in steeply diminishing perspective. (The publicity department has also got in on this act, describing the girls relationship as derailed.)

Patsy had been a loved, comfortable girl with no more than the regulation quantity of teenage problems, and she has grown up accordingly. Her friend Marie had been dirty, dishevelled, somewhat abused and chronically unpopular. Now she has resurfaced in Patsys perception as a svelte, successful metropolitan actress with TV credits ranging from Hedda Gabler to commercials for flavoured coffee. Her manner has changed, and her hair-colouring, and her name.

Patsy invites her to visit, and one afternoon, en route to an engagement in Montreal, the former Marie -- now Francesca -- materializes in Patsy's kitchen. They catch up, reminisce and compare; and as they do their younger selves appear, re-enacting episodes from their shared past.

The play started life as a monologue, performed on television by the author, and in essence it may still be that. There are four characters (or, if you like, two characters each split two ways) and they all have a lot to say, but the whole thing may be going on in Patsy's head.

Her friendship with Marie was certainly real, and there presumably is an actress out there called Francesca, but Patsys identification of the one with the other may be an illusion, and their reunion may be an extended piece of wish-fulfillment. It enables her to match what she values in her own life -- her home, her husband, her three sons -- against what she has missed out on: travel, glamour, sexual adventure. Francesca has had three husbands and, presumably, numerous affairs, but is childless and currently alone.

Patsy, as Palk presents her, has a roguish, round-voiced quality: unprudish but shockable. She can say "eh?" with more point and humour than anyone this side of the Royal Canadian Air Farce, and the surprise on her face can expand into a grin of amazement or freeze into a mask of horror. Marie used to have fits; since her departure, Patsy has had them, too. It is, she wryly comments, the only instance on record of epilepsy as a communicable disease.

If Patsy is fantasizing, an otherwise predictable structure -- joyful meeting, re-enacted trauma, acceptance, dying fall -- begins to seem more inevitable, less glib. (The play suffers the problem afflicting any drama hingeing on the delayed revelation of events known to the characters but not the audience; however horrific the details, its always an anti-climax.) We can also worry less about Marie/Francesca's self-conscious agonizing about her two names and two identities, and about the total disjunction between them. This, making all allowances for the thoroughness of show business makeovers, is incredible; there is no continuity of appearance, thought or speech patterns.

Even at face value, though, the play has a lot to offer. It creates a world; maybe a world and a half. Its two timelines grip as they converge, and their mutual dependence is increasingly persuasive. There is a rhythm, too, to the individual scenes.

Some of Thompson's more lyrical flights are hard to take; or maybe its the careful way that the actors, under her direction, deposit them directly in the audiences laps. (Pausing for effect, after harmless conjunctions like and or "like" is, categorically, bad acting. People in real life, other than the grossest poseurs, never do it. Ever.)

Otherwise, the performances are very strong. Sonja Smits as Francesca is glossy without and craggy within. (She also bears a noticeable resemblance to Joanna Lumley, which, what the old-girls- together theme and one of them being called Patsy, makes the whole thing seem, on occasion, Absolutely Fabulous.)

Of the juniors, Tara Rosling as Marie provides a fiery variation on her disturbed hitchhiker from See Bob Run: in the former play she delivered a verdict on runaway youth; here she provides the evidence. Liisa Repo-Martell's young Patsy, all emphatic concern, is a tumultuous prelude to Palk's luminous account of her older self.

This is undoubtedly the best new play set on a farm and reaching back into the past since The Drawer Boy; it's also the best new play of any kind, and leaves you wondering why rural Canadian plays should at the moment be so much more accomplished and affecting than urban ones.
