THE ATRE REVIEWS

Cuisine Confessions Princess of Wales Theatre Acquiesce Factory Theatre

The Seven Fingers company are a peripatetic, multi-ethnic, multi-disciplinary group based in Montreal. Their skills include acrobatics, music, storytelling and - for the purposes of their show Cuisine Confessions - cookery. They arrive in Toronto as a kind of culinary Cirque du soleil.

The acrobatics are the most important ingredient. They execute their manoeuvres with charmingly casual grace, and some of the feats are breathtaking. They put the audience in a pleasurable trance. We know in advance they can do these things, because otherwise they wouldn't be doing them.

Least important is the cooking; omelettes and pasta and banana bread are prepared on stage in real time and even served to the audience, but the food is really just there to provide a pretext and a framework. A juggling act acquires an extra piquancy when it's done with whisks.

The setting is an exceptionally spacious and wellappointed kitchen: the sum total, it seems, of the performers' own culinary memories and experiences. This is where the confessional part comes in. The cast-members tell us in turn what preparing food and eating it meant to them, especially when they were growing up.

There is some audience participation, too. On the first night, a young lady was enticed on stage to be wooed with an omelette and with the thought-provoking compliment, "the less I know you, the more I love you."

While little of the talking here communicates much (and neither does most of the singing), when Sidney Bateman and Melvin Diggs, both from the United States, take turns jumping through hoops at increasingly impossible angles, we're transported to a magical world. They're helping one another, they're competing, they're egging one another on (that seems the appropriate phrase) - they make it look both easy and impossible.

There is one sequence that overshadows all the others, one autobiographical monologue that is not so much a confession as a bearingwitness. Matias Plaul, from Argentina, tells of how, when he was a boy, his father was abducted, tortured, killed. To illustrate, Plaul climbs up what the program identifies as a Chinese pole, stretching from stage floor to somewhere out of sight. He then abruptly slides down, presenting an image of death as heart-stopping as I have seen in a theatre. As he goes on to tell how he and his family coped with the loss, he performs other feats on that pole, some of which cannot be believed even while they're happening; they defy gravity.

At the end of the show, pasta is served from the stage. I didn't brave the crowds to partake, but I did pick up some chocolate-chip banana bread on the way out and it was good.

David Yee, accomplished actor and playwright, combines the two roles in his new play, Acquiesce, the opening production in Factory Theatre's season of plays by authors of colour.

Yee plays Sin Hwang, an Asian-Canadian summoned to Hong Kong for the funeral of his father. Sin is a writer. We first meet him on the outward flight, trying to sleep while the man in the next seat badgers him with questions - first flattering, then insulting - about his one successful novel, also called Acquiesce. This is a funny scene, but our hopes for more are dashed when Sin arrives and is informed by his cousin Kai that jokes don't go down well in Hong Kong. A later scene involves a Buddhist monk who's a pretty fair joker himself and proud of it, but most of the play is heavy, or certainly feels so in Nina Lee Aquino's portentous production.

Sin, who is very Westernized, must come to terms with his Chinese heritage and also with the legacy of his father whom, as he keeps protesting, he hardly knew. Dad walked out when Sin was six, and was physically abusive while he was there.

Abusive to a six-year old: that's a thought to pull you up short, but the play doesn't dwell on it, or tell us anything at all about the rest of Sin's upbringing. His mother isn't even mentioned.

We gather the father's behaviour was learned from his own childhood, and that Sin fears he may have inherited it and therefore wants no children of his own, causing his girlfriend to leave him. But all that is just words. We're asked to respond to a father-son relationship that hasn't been dramatized. What we're finally told about it seems arbitrary and inconclusive.

Sin does learn his father suffered from hallucinations, and it seems this, too, may run in the family. Sin's flight companion asked him to explain a dream sequence in his novel, and the play goes on to give us dreams and flashbacks and even flashbacks of dreams. One of them involves a talking Paddington Bear, with a surprisingly rough London accent. Yee's presence as his own protagonist is a plus. He has strength and humour and an appealing wariness, a built-in psychological shrug: very useful when Sin is called upon to ritually wash his father's body or to deliver a eulogy in Cantonese, which he doesn't speak. But he acquiesces.

Richard Lee plays cousin Kai who is fastidious, or - in Sin's eyes - bossy, but who melts eventually. Yee and Lee build an initially amusing, finally touching relationship.

Two other actors play everybody else: John Ng provides a bright series of cameos, culminating in a surprise appearance as the deceased; Rosie Simon is defeated as the girlfriend, who exists only in unreliable memories, but is very entertaining as a funeral parlour attendant who walks and talks like a cartoon geisha.

Yee has written better plays: Paper Series and Carried Away on the Crest of a Wave. Or, since this one is apparently a revision of an early discarded work, you might say, more positively, that he has gone on to write better plays.
