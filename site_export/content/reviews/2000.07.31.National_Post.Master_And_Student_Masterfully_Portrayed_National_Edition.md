COLLECTED STORIES

Tom Patterson Theatre, Stratford

STRATFORD - The lights go up on an elderly, grey-haired woman sitting at a desk in her apartment, scribbling and frowning. From a semi-visible kitchen, there comes the sound of a kettle whistling. Since the lady seems to be getting somewhere with her work, she resents the interruption. On the other hand, she does want her tea. (We discover presently that she doesn't drink coffee.) Not a word has been spoken, but every nuance of her inner conflict has come across, in a perfect moment of silent comedy. We have also met a character -- complete at the moment we see her, but still open to exploration and even to growth.

The play is Collected Stories by Donald Margulies, an American playwright who has achieved great success in his own country over the last 20 years but is still little known outside it. The actress, recreating a role that she originated in New York three years ago, is Uta Hagen, at 81 the doyenne of American acting. She plays Ruth Steiner, a much-honoured author of short stories, who also teaches creative writing at a New York university. The play concerns the relationship between Ruth and a young woman named Lisa Morrison who, at the start of the actions six-year span, is Ruths newest student. Later she becomes her assistant. Later still she becomes -- at least as Ruth sees it -- her rival and possible supplanter.

The first scene is a tutorial, fetchingly written, with Ruth a stern but appreciative taskmistress, and Lisa a bouncingly eager pupil. Ruth has to rein her in; she is especially critical of Lisa's habit, endemic to young American women, of ending each sentence on a rising inflection, as if it were a question. (Or, as if it were a question? And you thought that was just a Canadian disease.) Lisa also, you know, says like a lot; Ruth doesn't pull her up on that one, but its notable that both mannerisms have disappeared by the second scene, Lisa having now become an official part of Ruths life. The play settles down into a study of an adoptive mother- daughter relationship, thereby becoming more commonplace but still engrossing. It finally comes to rest on the subject of literary appropriation; having made her own mark with short stories, Lisa tries her hand at a novel, something that Ruth has always avoided. She bases it on intimate details of Ruth's past life. Ruth is livid; battle is joined, an irreconcilable combat in which, as Margulies very fairly shows us, both are right and both are wrong.

As an imported text of less than classic stature, Collected Stories might well sit better at CanStage or Tarragon than at Stratford; though it's a far better play than, say, The Gin Game, the atrocious Broadway hit that was once shoe-horned into the festival as a vehicle for Jessica Tandy and Hume Cronyn. Hagens performance, though, makes quibbling irrelevant; this is seamlessly great acting that we would be lucky to see anywhere. (And great acting, though it cant be guaranteed, is presumably part of what Stratford is for.) Everything about it is implicit in that opening silent sequence; it sets a rhythm that can be varied but is never broken. At one point she makes satirical mention of the U. S. Cavalry and puckers her lips to suggest a bugle; that was her only moment that struck me as in any way forced or excessive. Her delightfully ambiguous facial play at the start finds a darker reflection later on when she learns that Lisa has placed her first story without her mentor's help; pleasure and pain fight a visible battle on her countenance. Returning from a triumphant public appearance Ruth announces "I was just acting a part -- the feisty old lady who can get away with saying anything." It's a part that, despite her protestations, she believes in, and its sheer comic gusto carries the play along, and us with it. But beneath it lurk an increasing frailty, that she resents, and a capacity for meanness that visibly shocks her but that she cannot control. Hagen communicates great complexity by the simplest means; the line of her performance is extraordinary. Thirty-five years ago she was the legendary Martha of Whos Afraid of Virginia Woolf: another aggressively witty woman who turned out to be jelly within. I remember that performance -- the only previous one of hers Ive seen -- as trumpeting and swashbuckling. The years have obviously shrunk her -- vocally and, I think, physically -- and the present performance is pitched very quietly But there's great variety in her pianissimo -- it can accommodate anger and suffering as well as wit - - and unquestionable truth, and, I might prosaically add, total audibility.

Lorca Simons' Lisa stands up to her very well, as actress and as character. Lisa, too, has to find a part to act and the one she develops -- and that we can see her working at -- is that of the bright-eyed, well-meaning, debutante, talented but humble; she goes from being innocently gauche to purposefully gauche. The persona (which is quite sincere) blossoms in the one scene that takes place outside Ruths Greenwich Village pad: Lisa, in the populist- cultural precincts of the 92nd Street YMCA, offering a preview of her novel. (It sounds absolutely dreadful, by the way, though Im not sure that was the intention.) The final showdown is very honestly handled; we don't doubt that the two characters are essentially through, but we know that they will continue to meet on occasion, and that their unbreakable bonds will continue to chafe.

The vice of the play -- like that of most American drama below the John Guare-David Mamet level -- is that it's self-conscious, without being truly self-aware. These characters speak, even for writers, in implausibly complex sentences, loaded with subordinate clauses; and they draw our attention to things, like the implicit, mother-daughter relationship, that are already in plain view. But Lisas placing of herself and her mentor ("you had that rich, wonderful Jewish life to draw on -- Ive only got WASP culture") is dramatically justified, if only for the fierce riposte it gets from Ruth. Hagen really does seem to have walked out of the New York Jewish intelligentsia; and it adds to the texture that she herself is as celebrated a teacher as she is an actor. If that's cultural appropriation, I'm for it. Im far less comfortable with Margulies device of making Ruth the fictitious lover of an actual dead writer: the alcoholic poet and critic Delmore Schwartz; since he hasn't taken the creative risk of actually putting Schwartz on stage, this strikes me as glory-by-association, and verisimilitude at one remove. It also makes Hagen, by strict count, 10 years too old for her role. But there's no point in worrying about chronology; what we have here -- living, breathing and making us part of -- is history. She never appears to trade on it, but that is what the actress brings on stage with her: that and surpassing accomplishment, honed and shining. The play itself is not especially moving but I was still in tears at the end.
