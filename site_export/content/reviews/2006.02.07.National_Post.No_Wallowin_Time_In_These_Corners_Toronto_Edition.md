OUR TOWN

Marilyn and Charles Baillie Theatre, Young Centre for the Performing Arts

- - -

Good morning. Its a bright and early day in the city of Toronto in the province of Ontario. At least it is for those of you who live there and are eating your breakfast while you read this; and the rest who live some place else or just plumb got up late, well, youll have to use your imaginations. Which is what Mr. Thornton Wilder would have wanted you to do.

Wilder wrote this play, Our Town, back in 1938; it uses a bare stage to present three days, spread some years apart, in the life of a place called Grover's Corners, N.H., even further back in the 20th century. We're talking about horse-and-buggy days, moving on to early automobile days. Wilder wanted us to laugh and cry and wonder, and maybe hold our breaths our little, over people bein born and fallin in love and marryin' and dyin', sometimes ahead of their time. All the satisfaction of it and all the futility. And you know what? He brings it off, just about completely.

Of course, he doesnt do it all by himself. He has the best help. Some of us are still chokin up and feeling the glow from last week when that old Soulpepper company -- they're only eight, historically speaking, but they're already part of the landscape -- opened their new theatre with this very play. Its very warm and practical and inviting -- the theatre and the Young Centre complex its part of and the Distillery complex it's part of -- and it's in a nice situation. You can walk out the back door and see clear down to Lake Ontario; least you could if some public benefactor would take a stick of dynamite to that darn Gardiner Expressway.

Now this first night of Our Town ended with a standing ovation, which wasnt at all surprising. Thing is, it began with one as well, and you know that aint usual. Here's how it happened. The play is narrated by a Stage Manager who talks directly to the audience, very much like I'm talking to you now. Hes played by Albert Schultz, whos Soulpepper's artistic director and the man most responsible for getting them to where they are today. Literally. So we file into the theatre, and there's Albert, which is how everybody refers to him in these parts, alternately movin bits of furniture around and just standin and lookin' out at us. Then the lights go down, and our eyes meet his and everybody stands up and cheers. And it's not so much him saying "Welcome to my theatre, which you might expect, as us welcoming him.

As for the rest of the evening; well, you cant say fairer than it not only lived up to that beginning, it topped it. Same goes for Alberts own performance. Everybody says Our Town is sentimental. Well, it can be if you don't do it right; but, apart from having enough discreet charm to make the entire bourgeoisie turn green, he keeps steering so we don't get a chance to wallow. When hes asked whether people ever realize their lives while they live it," he says "the saints and poets, maybe." I always thought that was kind of a phony line, but here its all in the maybe. He does it as a sort of rigorous shrug.

Well, that's Joseph Ziegler's production all over, a real model of tact and control. He directed this play for Soulpepper at the start of their second season, and it was like a testimony that they were here to stay. Its even better now, thanks in large part to Martha MacIsaac, who tore all our hearts out last year in The Wild Duck and now lacerates them all over again. She plays scholarly young Emily who pairs up with George, the baseball champ next door. She and Jeff Lillico make the scene where they plight their troth over mimed sodas in a minimalist drugstore, sad and funny and touching like it always was. She makes the last act, where shes one of a bunch of dead people all sittin' on chairs to represent a graveyard, devastating like it never was but was always supposed to be. Young Miss MacIsaac gets very earnest about lovin' and bein loved, and her disillusion when she revisits her past and sees everybody taking everything for granted is terrible.

Sittin next to her in that cemetery is Miss Nancy Palk, who plays her mother-in-law and has never been better. We know from the start that she's going to die, and the knowledge makes her delicacy and her enthusiasm very moving. She has modest ambitions that won't be fulfilled; she makes us feel delight in small pleasures and grief that they cant be larger. Then theres Oliver Dennis as her doctor- husband, all upright and caring; while as the other parents there's John Jarvis, a newspaper editor who's a bit pompous and a lot shy, and Jane Spidell who struck me at first as over-brisk but who justifies it when she gets to that flashback in Act Three. And thats not the half of it because in the outfield --doin their bit in the bit parts, you might say -- are such folks as William Webster, with a bitterness carved out of granite, Charmion King shedding easy tears at a wedding and leaving the hard ones for us, and old Diego Matamoros readin' us a geology lesson, and some terrific kids and more.

It's the authors opinion that, not only are our lives the only ones weve got, they're the only ones the universe has got. He wrote this play just before a war, about a time just before another one. The crazier and more fragile the world gets, the more poignant the play seems: poignant and maybe terrifying. But not depressing; in fact, in this production, which is as good as this play is ever gonna get, it's positively uplifting. Some lady actor, posin as a member of the audience, asks whether our town has any culture or beauty in it. Well, our town does, and this is part of it. Seems like the city of Toronto and the province of Ontario ought to be tellin people about it. You have a good day.
