The new Second City revue, Facebook of Revelations, begins in time-honoured fashion with a musical number. Second City programs never list the titles of anything, be it sketch or song, but I think we can take it that this opener is called Safe Bet: The Musical. It's a spoof of all the We Will Rock You-Mamma Mia shows that hang "songs you already know" on the thinnest or most pretentious of storylines. (Sometimes on both.) Every cliche of the genre is in place, paraded at whistle-stop speed. It's a joke that needed to be made, and the six Second City writer-performers make it very well.

They then start taking swipes at environmentalism, both outside (a sketch about a road trip) and in (a scene about a dinner party). God knows, environmental self-righteousness, like any other kind of self-righteousness, is a ripe subject for satire. And the Second Citizens, who are probably satirizing themselves, seem prepared to take it to the limit; the second item has a very tough finish. The scenes, though, are cleverer than they are funny, and this turns out to be a problem with the entire first half of the show, culminating in an extended routine, largely mimed, about a baseball rookie whose beaming incompetence brings colleagues, fans, and even the team mascot on to the field in protest. I'd probably find it more hilarious if I knew or cared more about baseball, but I can see that it's very well done. Still, after the sustained and no less acute hilarity of the last two Second City shows, this one is a letdown.

Things improve considerably after the intermission; it can't just have been that by that point I'd had more to drink. Marital breakups and Afghanistan are beautifully skewered-- both, remarkably, in the same number. Dad, trying desperately to explain to the children over breakfast, why Mom is no longer around without actually saying a word against her (he does drop his guard to the extent of saying, ruefully "oh, yes, Mommy yells"), tells them she has gone Over There and embarks on a wonderful non-stop rodomontade about our conflicted reasons for being There in the first place and the probable outcome of same. It's like Tony Kushner's play Homebody/ Kabul only much, much shorter; and, I'm inclined to think, better. As a bonus, we get the sight of two full-grown actors (very full, in one case) getting to play the kids whose moods swing between terror and oblivion, tearful and cheerful.

And then, there is a number about two screw-sorting robots who fall in love with one another on the job. (According to the male robot, you can tell how a female is going to age by looking at her motherboard.) The idea isn't the newest in the world but it's done here with great invention and finesse, and is even a little moving; the lady robot asks her minder please not to erase her memory for the day, and is greeted with a bland, patronizing "Whatever would you want that for?" So, what we have just seen must happen every day. Talk about bittersweet. It seems appropriate that this sketch should be followed immediately by one about human office workers who behave like robots. They are planning to murder their boss. He doesn't seem to have done anything to deserve it. But you know how people are once they get an idea in their heads.

The star performer here (and a veteran of those two great predecessor revues, Bird Flu Over the Cuckoo's Nest and Tip of the Melting Iceberg, is Lauren Ash. Only she isn't so much a star as a terrific featured player who has honed her talents to the finest edge. Dark-haired and comfortable-looking, she does everything with razor-sharp economy, whether playing a love-sick computer, a homicidal drone or a pharmacist censoriously refusing to dispense a morning-after pill. Ash does grievance very well indeed. Jim Annan, another Second City familiar -- short, fair and maniacal -- doesn't have enough to do, but is always welcome when doing it. He has his best moments here when lurking on the fringes. Scott Montgomery is a tall guy with a gift of the non-stop gab, some of his tirades being scripted (the Afghan one), others seeming at least partly improvised. Marty Adams is the most prominent newcomer, if only because of his pronounced chubbiness. He is both the coy male robot and the rambunctious child performing prodigies of slow-motion naughtiness, but his finest hour comes as an oleaginous call-in host who never gets any calls. Except tonight, when members of the audience are drafted to play the callers, thereby imposing on the cast the necessity of being somewhat spontaneous. (Even if they already know where they're going, they have to work out how to get there.) This is one of the best-conceived items in the evening. Darryl Hinds and Karen Parker both have their moments, but have some way to go The show's title declares itself in a gospellish closing number whose theme is "Jesus wants you in his facebook." It returns us to the realm of bright ideas that don't really go anywhere, but it's enthusiastically put over by the ensemble and it sends us away reasonably happy.
