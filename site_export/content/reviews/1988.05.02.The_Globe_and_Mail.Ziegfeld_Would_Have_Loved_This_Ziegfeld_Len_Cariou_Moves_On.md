Ziegfeld would have loved this Ziegfeld Len Cariou moves on

Monday, May 02, 1988

ROBERT CUSHMAN

London ENGLAND -- BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail LONDON Len Cariou is playing another obsessive. The actor from Manitoba, whose career has included Coriolanus at Stratford and Sweeney Todd on Broadway, made his West End of London debut last week at the world-famous Palladium in the title role of Ziegfeld, a musical biography of the United States most celebrated showman.

Florenz Ziegfeld was known for the opulence of his decor and the quality and quantity of his chorus girls, and London producer Harold Fielding has spent $8-million in an attempt to emulate him.

To get back this kind of money, a show has to appeal to the coach-and- tourist trade. No one would claim Ziegfeld is attempting to compete with Shakespeare or even with Sondheim. So, for a leading actor who has triumphed in both, what was the attraction?

"Ziegfeld was a very interesting character. The way we're doing it, he tries to tell the story of his life as if it were one of his own shows. And for most of the evening he gets away with it," Cariou said between rehearsals. However, nobody's life really resembles a spectacular Broadway revue from the palmy decades before the Depression, so in the end reality catches up with him. In Carious words, Hes told, okay, you've done all right, you've more or less stuck to the truth, but thats enough.

The show keeps Carious Ziegfeld on stage almost throughout. "He's interwoven so intricately with the show, the role's about as long as Sweeney Todd. Its also like reading your own obituary."

The historical Ziegfeld went through money and mistresses relentlessly, but the show passes lightly over the financial problems, concentrating on the women - a course that Ziegfeld, whose self-proclaimed mission was Glorifying the American Girl, would probably have approved.

He also did a certain amount to glorify the American songwriter. Jerome Kern, Irving Berlin and George Gershwin were among those whose services he commanded, although he was somewhat cavalier in the way he treated them. (One Broadway historian said: He must have had a taste for good writing, just no interest in it.) The show Ziegfeld draws eclectically on the music of the period. It allows Cariou to exercise his lyric baritone on numbers ranging from A Pretty Girl is Like a Melody - the theme song of the Ziegfeld Follies - to Im Always Chasing Rainbows, which has no Ziegfeld connection whatsoever. Of course, the real Ziggy is unlikely ever to have sung a note, but there is such a thing as poetic licence.

There were rumors that the almost operatic demands of Sweeney Todd had destroyed Cariou's voice, and they certainly took a toll. "The only way to rest your vocal cords is to rest them - completely - and there's no way I could do that."

He was heard to croak in his subsequent Broadway musical, the short- lived Dance a Little Closer, but he has recuperated. The rumors of my voices death, he says, choosing his words with dry deliberation, "have been greatly over-produced."

This is just as well, since there are many who would agree with Hal Princes estimate that Cariou is the best leading man I've ever seen as far as musicals are concerned. Certainly he ranks with Alfred Drake and the late Robert Preston. Prince, Broadway's leading producer-director, paid Cariou that compliment in person after witnessing a run-through of Applause, the show in which Cariou made his Broadway debut opposite Lauren Bacall.

Cariou was gratified, of course, but as Prince hadnt introduced himself it was some time before the importance of the accolade came home to him. "Somebody said, That was Hal,' and I said, 'Hal who? " That was in 1969. Cariou heard nothing more from Prince until 1973, when he was asked to audition for a subsidiary role in Princes production of Stephen Sondheim's A Little Night Music: So he's a man of his word anyway, even if he does take his time. He was then offered the male lead, a 50-year-old lawyer. When he protested that he was too young for the part, Prince said crisply, "Youre an actor, arent you?"

I get tightened up like a corkscrew at auditions, Cariou says, who these days refuses to do them. Having become, as he puts it, "an international twinkle-twinkle," he no longer has to. These days, if somebody wants to know if I can sing, I say, 'Send them an album.'  His role in the Prince-Sondheim Sweeney Todd was, he believes, written with him in mind. "Hal told me it was. Stephen has never said."

The legendary barber of Londons Fleet Street became in Sondheims version a figure of tragedy rather than melodrama: a wronged man whose justified grievances against society turn him into a creature of homicidal madness. Cariou's feeling on approaching the role was, Thank God I've done Lear.

That of course is the other side of Carious career. Americans regard him as a musical star who regularly takes inexplicable salary cuts to play the dramatic classics in regional repertory theatres. (When he left Applause after 13 months, he says, "Betty Bacall couldnt understand it. She said, 'Why are you leaving this job?' ") Canadians, though, think of him as a classical actor who makes occasional lucrative forays into song and dance.

Historically, the Americans may be nearer the truth. Cariou began as a singer. I was born a singer. My mother was a singer. All the Irish side of my family were musically involved. I started in the musical theatre, but I quickly went into legit. I discovered the classical theatre.

Winnipeg, where he grew up, was a good place to discover it. "In the late fifties," Cariou recalls, there were not an awful lot of professional actors there. But John Hirsch wanted to make a professional theatre there, and he founded the Manitoba Theatre Centre. Cariou "got the bug, and jumped in. But Hirsch, who was my mentor, also told me, Dont ever give up doing musicals. You have a natural bent for it.' " Under Hirsch's influence, he progressed in the early sixties to Stratford, Ont., where he joined the companys operatic arm and spent a pleasant, relaxed season in a single show. It was, he remembers, "the first time in my career I had the leisure to play golf."

Since 1966, his career has been U.S.-based. Douglas Campbell was running the Guthrie Theatre in Minneapolis in the sixties. He had seen Carious work at Stratford, and asked him to join. Cariou muses on how easy it was for actors to cross the border in those days, and how hard it would be now for an actor who was less than an international star.

His last role was Teddy Roosevelt in the musical Teddy and Alice, which opened and closed in very short order earlier this year. His own notices, though, were excellent, and the invitation to play Ziegeld followed immediately.

It means a chance to work with Joe Layton, the top director and choreographer whose successes include two previous showman bios, George M. and Barnum. Layton believes in giving the old razzle-dazzle and Ziegfeld is very much his show. For Cariou, it's a high-kicking step into the West End.
