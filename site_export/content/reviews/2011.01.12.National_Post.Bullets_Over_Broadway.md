ASSASSINS

The Theatre Centre, Toronto

---

'All you have to do is move your little finger, move your little finger and -- you can change the world.'

The Toronto revival of Assassins opened to the press on the day of the Tucson massacre, making a show that already seemed scarily prophetic horribly immediate. All the elements that seem to have been in play on Saturday in Arizona, and then some, are given dramatic form in John Weidman's book and Stephen Sondheim's music and lyrics. The sense of grievance, justified or otherwise. The almost mythological fixation on a scapegoat. The personal craziness enflamed by an apocalyptic political culture -- by a rhetoric of destructiveness that has always been with us, from both sides, but now has a more sophisticated media system than ever before to amplify it. The sense -- indelibly conveyed in the show's climactic number, Another National Anthem -- of being left out, of being cast as a failure in what's supposed to be the American success story and of realizing that, whatever the promises, "it's never gonna happen, is it?" Easy access to guns doesn't hurt either.

As The Gun Song, quoted above, goes on to say, "when you've a gun -- everybody pays attention." It's crooned, in beautiful lullaby harmony, by three killers of U.S. presidents: Leon Czolgosz who shot Mc-Kinley, Charles Guiteau who shot Garfield and John Wilkes Booth for whom no identification is necessary, which is part of the show's point. There are six other principal characters, each of whom tried to assassinate the president of his or her day. Five of them failed. The sixth is Lee Harvey Oswald.

Even without the fresh context, the remount of Assassins, as jointly produced by Birdland and Talk Is Free, would be a stupendous evening. It was the best thing to happen in the Toronto theatre last year and it's every bit as good on its return. Adam Brazier's production is an ensemble triumph, with the added kick that the ensemble is made up of star performances. That's part of the reason that Another National Anthem is so shattering. It's a chorus number that ends with everybody singing the same words, stomping the same steps. (Cheers by the way to Linda Garneau, the choreographer, whose contribution I failed to recognize last time.) Their impact as a collective is intense enough; it's multiplied as you glance from one to the next and realize that each is coming from the same individual place that's been so powerfully established earlier. I don't think I've encountered anything quite like it before in a musical.

Along with the shooters themselves, there are two other characters present: a carnival proprietor, taunting them into greater despair, and a Balladeer who does the same thing by dangling false hopes in front of them. They turn on him, and in effect make him one of themselves. He becomes Oswald, the only assassin we haven't met yet, persuaded to kill the president instead of killing himself, and so earn a place in a historical line that even he has heard of. "Brutus assassinated Caesar -- what? -- 2,000 years ago" says Booth, the tempter, "and here's a high school dropout with a $1.25 an hour job in Dallas, Texas, who knows who

he was. And they say fame is fleeting." Weidman's writing is as weighty as anything in the American theatre, musical or dramatic, in the past 20 years. It's stark and ironic and often very funny. But it's never giggly or demeaning or smug; it restores the tarnished honour of black comedy.

PaulMcQuillan'sBooth, with his flashing eye and patrician drawl and exquisite singing, is even more imposing than before; likewise, in a different key, Steve Ross's self-inflating Guiteau, cakewalking his way to the gallows. Graham Abbey's Samuel Byck, paranoia in a Santa suit and a toy car, was hardly susceptible of improvement; his monologue, in which pleasantry gets increasingly soaked in hate, remains a marvel of both phrasing and feeling. Geoffrey Tyler's Oswald and Christopher Stanton's John Hinckley are fine fragments of social and psychological wreckage. Newcomers include Alex Fiddes' haunting Czolgosz; Lisa Horner's Sara Jane Moore, hilarious hippie housewife; and Whitney Ross-Barris, who movingly sings Something Just Broke, the show's nearest approach to an epiphany.

Actors double as instrumentalists, the reed section making especially witty fleeting appearances in Unworthy of Your Love, a '70s country ballad more poignantly sappy than the real thing, sung by Hinckley and Squeaky Fromme to an absent Jodie Foster and Charles Manson respectively. Sondheim has furnished one of his most apparently forbidding subjects with one of his most ingratiating and accessible scores, one whose journey through Americana may even surpass Follies as pastiche composition with a steely double edge. The show ranks among Sondheim's masterpieces, alongside its immediate predecessor Into the Woods. Two very different approaches to that most abused of words and subjects, community.
