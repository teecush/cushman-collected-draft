THE CD COLLECTOR Show tunes underscored BROADWAY MUSICALS

Saturday, February 06, 1993

Robert Cushman

Robert Cushman IN a haphazard way, Broadway cast albums have figured among CD reissues since the beginning. There were predictable releases of the most famous titles; there were also surprise appearances of the most obscure. (Would you believe Whoop-Up?) Now, however, the companies are becoming more organized: Capitol, Decca and RCA - or their corporate descendants - are all inaugurating systematic show-album policies.

Leading the field, as is only fitting, is Sony, inheritors of Columbia which, from the late forties to the seventies, recorded more musicals, better, than any other label. The expected My Fair Lady, West Side Story et al are now being supplemented by a marvellously conscientious series called Sony Broadway which has restored and remastered - impeccably - many scores that had been unavailable for years. Each comes with a booklet providing synopsis (in four languages) and background material from one of three writers: Ken Mandelbaum who is formidably sensible and informed, Marc Kirkeby who is witty, and the producer of the series, Didier C. Deutsch, who is at any rate enthusiastic.

The first 18 sets cover shows from 1949 to 1978. Most momentous is The Most Happy Fella (S2K 48010), which on two discs gives virtually the complete show, Frank Loesser's score being of operatic breadth if not depth. I have never seen it on stage and the excerpts I had heard made me nervous; it sounded stickily mock-Italian, being set among immigrant wine- growers in California. Heard in extenso, though, the story of a shy elderly man and his young mail-order bride is touching, except when it tries too hard as in the attempted aria, My Heart Is So Full of You. The melodrama is gripping, and there are refreshing injections of Loessers own city smarts. Take the wonderful opening in which Susan Johnson, a peerless soubrette, sings Ooh My Feet, the plaint of a weary waitress and originally earmarked, if thats the word, for a similarly afflicted cop in Guys and Dolls.

From possible opera to (intentionally) risible operetta. On The Twentieth Century (SK 30589) found librettist-lyricists Betty Comden and Adolph Green on home ground, affectionately satirizing the sacred monsters of Broadway and Hollywood. Cy Coleman's smilingly ornate melodies and rhythms combine with the faux-naif lyrics and overripe performances to put the Arch into Archetypes. It is all of a piece, and a favourite of mine, right down to its Art Deco poster, reproduced in time-honoured cast-album fashion on the CD logo. Madeline Kahn apparently found the prima donna role too taxing to sustain on stage, but she gives her all on the record whose sound has been not merely remastered but revitalized. A few restored cuts help too; the conductor on the 20th Century Express who suddenly bursts into song with the announcement that he has written a play (as has everybody else the actor-manager-hero encounters) is a mere snippet but sets the tone of the whole show.

Gentlemen Prefer Blondes (SK 48013) is formulaic but pleasing. Carol Channing's innocent-vamp act on Diamonds Are a Girls Best Friend is still the best monument to her peculiarly metallic charms; other numbers in Jule Stynes score vary from breezy to routine.

Irma La Douce (SK 48018) is a French musical - an almost unheard-of commodity in the 1950s - that became a major West End hit in an adaptation by Julian More, David Henker and Monty Norman (who separately or together wrote many of the most successful British shows of the time) and a production by Peter Brook who wasn't always the ascetic experimenter he is now. A gently cynical story of prostitution and petty crime in Montmartre, it fitted right in with a contemporary British vogue for low-life musicals: social significance without tears. It then went to New York with its three British stars, Elizabeth Seal, Keith Michell and Clive Revill, and had a more modest success. The Broadway album, though proficient, has far less atmosphere and punch than its London predecessor. It does though have the signal advantage of being available.

The same goes for Wonderful Town (SK 48021). This 1953 Broadway smash was given a cast recording by Decca that has yet to make it to CD. Sony's version features a TV cast that was largely identical, and above all had Rosalind Russell repeating the only stage musical role she ever needed to play. The score sparkles; the 1930s' New York setting inspired Leonard Bernstein to create some of his most buoyant musical pastiches, and the lyrics show Comden and Green at their comic peak. The first time around Russell dispatched them with a matchless economy and panache; in this reprise she tends to spread herself - a familiar phenomenon most notoriously exemplified in Rex Harrison's steady decline through the successive versions of My Fair Lady. Wonderful Town used to be truly wonderful; here it is merely terrific.

There is much more. Tune in next week.
