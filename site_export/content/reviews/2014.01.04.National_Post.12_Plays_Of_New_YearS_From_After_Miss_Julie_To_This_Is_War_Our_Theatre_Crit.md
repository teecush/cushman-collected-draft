Here, alphabetically, are some of the shows that stood out for me in the year just departed. I couldn't get it down to a Top 10 so here is my Divine Dozen (with others associated).

After Miss Julie Strindberg has rarely been so exciting or made so much political and emotional sense as in Patrick Marbers British update, masterfully directed by David Ferry with perfectly pitched performances by Claire Armstrong, Christopher Morris and Amy Keating. "Wow!" murmured one member of the spellbound first night audience and the feeling was unanimous. The tiny Storefront Theatre must take some credit for the intensity of the experience: one of a new wave of makeover sites for independent companies (the Red Sandcastle is another).

Angels in America Soulpepper had a peak-and-valley kind of year, but the peaks included a couple of mountain ranges: three evenings of The Norman Conquests, and two of Angels in America, Tony Kushners comic-cosmic-fantastic epic standing up superbly in Albert Schultz's production, with a breathtaking roster of major performances from the company's senior, junior and intermediate generations: Damien Atkins, Diego Matamoros, Nancy Palk, Gregory Prest, Mike Ross, Troy Adams. Other Soulpepper highlights: the aforementioned Conquests, with Fiona Reid relishing in her element and a lovely debut by Laura Condolln; Rosencrantz and Guildenstern Are Dead, with Ted Dysktra especially delightful; and the much underrated and very imaginative La Ronde.

Arcadia The Shaw Festivals revival of Tom Stoppards house-andgarden possible masterpiece might have filled one of their larger spaces, but Eda Holmes' production made clever use of the Studio space, while the demanding text was beautifully handled by Gray Powell, Diana Donnelly, Patrick McManus, Kate Besworth, others.

Lady Windermere's Fan At the opposite extreme of scale, Peter Hinton reclaimed Wildes society melodrama in a production that was a visual (designer, Teresa Przybylski), musical and emotional masterpiece. And funny too. Other Shaw highlights included Major Barbara, Jackie Maxwells intimate revival of what seems increasingly likely to be the house sage's best play.

The Little Mermaid Well, why not? Ross Petty's "family musicals" (or made-over English pantomimes) have become a tradition Toronto can call its own, and this may be the best of them. Also, you couldnt find a musical performance this year to top Chilina Kennedy on skates and tail.

Mary Stuart Antoni Cimolinos first production as Stratford's artistic director could not have been happier. A powerful and meticulous staging of the greatest female double-header ever written, with Lucy Peacock and Seana McKenna radiating in isolation and coruscating in conflict. And with a luxurious line-up of supporting males: Ben Carlson, Brian Dennehy (his best Stratford performance ever), Ian Lake, Geraint Wyn-Davies.

Needles and Opium Lepage, in glory. Ditto his two actors.

Once The year's best musical. Aladdin came in second; while among revivals there was Falsettos (Acting UpStage/Harold Green), a flawless presentation of a flawed work.

Othello With Romeo and Juliet, Measure for Measure and The Merchant of Venice, Stratford had its best all-round Shakespeare season in recent memory, but the crown was Chris Abrahams conquest of the usually unyielding Avon stage: red-hot designs by Julie Fox and performances by Dion Johnstone, Graham Abbey, Deborah Hay ... again, the list goes on. A great year altogether for what has recently seemed the most intransigent of the tragedies; in London, outgoing director Nicholas Hytner staged a marvellous production at the National Theatre; a scene from it can be glimpsed in the Nationals 50th birthday gala, well worth catching when it comes around again on NT Live. (Check your multiplex). Back at Stratford: special commendations for Sara Topham's Juliet, Scott Wentworth's Shylock and Tom Rooney and Stephen Ouimette in Measure; the last two were also terrific tramps in Godot, with Randy Hughson a revelatory Lucky.

Pig Not the greatest play, but a chance to pay tribute to Brendan Healys transformative directorate at Buddies in Bad Times. A mesmerizing production, fearlessly performed by Bruce Dow, Paul Dunn and Blair Williams. Fearless was also the word for Dow, earlier, in Of a Monstrous Child; while Dunn, later, teamed impressively up with Damien Atkins (possible actor of the year) and Andrew Kushnir in The Gay Heritage Project. Healy, meanwhile, also scored with Soulpeppers Entertaining Mr. Sloane.

This Best Play from Away, no contest. (The author's Canadian born, but this play and her career are essentially American). A bold staging by and at Canadian Stage, who later gave us Venus in Fur; again not the greatest play, but Carly Street was electric and Rick Miller a surprise.

This Is War By Hannah Moscovitch.

Best Play from Here. Again, no contest - including a couple of others from the same author. Finely observed, deceptively well constructed, and an especially gripping and wellacted production by Richard Rose.

Additional: family connections prevent me from including Passion Play on the list, still: It united the talents and resources of three of Toronto's independent companies (Convergence Theatre, Outside the March, Sheep No Wool) backed by a fourth (Crows Theatre), it was creatively site-specific in two separate sites, it brought us an impressively ambitious American play (by Sarah Ruhl) and it had eye-catching performances by Cyrus Lane, Mayko Nguyen and Andrew Kushnir, not to mention Maev Beaty as Elizabeth I, Hitler, and Ronald Reagan, the last of whom she devastatingly outacted. And, though its not strictly my turf, Canadian Opera Company's Dialogue des Carmelites and Peter Grimes were among the great theatrical events of the year. Dramatic companies should consider poaching.
