THEATRE REVIEW

Miss Julie: Freedom Summer

Bluma Appel Theatre

Katharine Hepburn famously ascribed the chemistry of Fred Astaire and Ginger Rogers to him giving her class and her giving him sex. August Strindberg's Miss Julie, the world's most famous intermissionless play, gives us that formulation with the genders reversed and the partners irredeemably out of step. Strindberg's Julie, virgin daughter of a 19th-century Swedish count, has class in spades and is not about to forget it, though she sometimes acts as if she'd like to. Jean is her father's valet and thus socially far beneath her; but he does have sex, or at least sexual experience, and in the course of the play, which unfolds on a hot midsummer night, he literally gives it to her. Their coupling is combustible and its aftermath dire.

For the second time this Toronto season we have a version of Miss Julie that updates and transplants the original. Last year, Miss Julie: She'mah situated the production in 1920s Canada, with a white Julie and a First Nations Jean. Now comes Stephen Sachs' Miss Julie: Freedom Summer, an American piece that comes to us as a coproductionby CanStage and the Vancouver Playhouse, and that turns up the heat by placing us in a Mississippi mansion in 1964, the turning-point year of the civil rights movement. Julie is a white judge's daughter; Jean, now John, and his fiancee Christine are of course black, she being the family's cook and he the chauffeur. I can't believe that nobody has yet dubbed this adaptation Driving Miss Julie, but if they haven't, then let me be the first. Anyway, it's the best production of Miss Julie, either straight up or with a twist, that I have seen.

I'm suspicious of revivals that implicitly suggest they're doing their originals some kind of favour by making them "contemporary." But this doesn't pretend to be the original play; it's a new text that follows the old one closely, but not exactly, while grabbing at what's most vital about it. Strindberg meant to shock with frankness, and the core of his play still seems brutal. But the conflict of aristocrat and servant no longer packs the same automatic punch; we understand it but we don't really feel it. Add race to sex and class, and you have a new dimension that reinforces the old. And by choosing the precise date that he has, Sachs gives us the sense that not just the central couple but the whole country is in heat.

Caroline Cave's Julie is white-hot from the moment she comes on stage, as the other characters observe and as the play requires. (Slow builds, I sometimes think, are wasted on Strindberg.) Slim and proud and desperate, acting with a treasurable combination of intelligence and sensuality, and with whiplash velocity, she encompasses both the self-assertion and the self-abasement of a woman who tempts and taunts and surrenders, sometimes in the space of a single sentence. Even actresses native to the territory can sound exaggerated when playing the Southern belle -- as can actual Southerners who aren't acting at all -- and Cave has her moments, but she always rescues herself. Inevitably we think of Tennessee Williams, and there is an astonishing correspondence between the baleful gentility of his heroines and that of Cave's -- and Strindberg's -- Julie. It's awfully plausible that, dressed up to elope, she should sorrowfully command John to execute her pet bird who's not wanted on the voyage, and then hysterically berate him for his inhumanity. She immediately sees herself as a slaughtered bird, and nothing could be more Tennessee than that. The equation only fails to balance at the end. Julie, in her own quaint way, chooses death rather than dishonour; a Williams heroine might talk like that but would never actually do it. (Blanche DuBois herself, according to her creator, would speedily have seduced some kindly stranger into releasing her from the New Orleans asylum.) Sachs is his own director, and I mostly relished the speed of his production, but its conclusion seems arbitrary when it should be ineluctable. Cave is more riveting with her hair metaphorically and literally down, rather than demurely up as she wears it at the end. But this is a still a whirlwind of a performance.

Kevin Hanchard matches her as a John with complementary hang-ups: sure of himself as a man, but still jumping shamefully to attention at the ring of the master's phone (a particularly effective update). He's cruel without being naturally callous; he has just as much compassion as he can afford. He's nettled and shamed not only by Julie but -- Strindberg's notorious misogyny being on hold in this play --by Raven Dauda's spirited and sensible Christine who, all dressed up in her Sunday-go-to-meeting, gives the play's religious imperatives an unaccustomed authority. She also, in an uncommonly handsome kitchen, attacks her chopping-block with an implacability that foreshadows the dispatching of Miss Julie's pet. Some of the scene setting is heavy-handed; John reads about Lyndon Johnson's signing of the Civil Rights Act and the murder of the freedom riders Goodman, Schwerner and Chaney in the same edition of the daily paper, dated the Fourth of July. And The Real Inspector Hound should have killed off the device by which, whenever someone turns on the radio, it's right at the start of either a song or a news bulletin. But most of the details fit beautifully into place. The brief shared dream of opening a jazz-club in Chicago--John as manager, Julie as hostess, Christine as cook-- rings true for all three of them; they must all have seen the same movies. The play Miss Julie is supposed to have the tension of an erotic nightmare. For once it does. - Miss Julie: Freedom Summer is at the Bluma Appel Theatre until March 7.
