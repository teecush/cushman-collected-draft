This week, Toronto welcomed tree plays on momentous topics, all presented with a high level of skill - and all suffering from a lack of focus.

Waiting Room, by Diane Flacks, begins silently and brilliantly. A married couple are in the hospital area of the title, nervous and impatient, doing everything but talk to one another. We wonder which of them is the patient and which the support. When they start talking, though, they refer to an unseen her, who turns out to be their baby daughter, who has brain cancer.

They will spend the play torn between hope and despair, getting mad at each other, and even madder at a pediatric surgeon with the worst bedside manner imaginable. This part of the play is movingly written, directed (by Richard Greenblatt), and acted; Jordan Pettle combines doggedness and impetuosity as the husband, while as the wife Michelle Monteith is devastating, naked in her pain, volcanic in her anger.

We've come to take Monteith's intensity almost for granted, but here she has the advantage of at least starting from normal, normal being something that she also does well when she gets the chance. Her character has forged a fortuitous bond with another mother whose son has been in and out of the same establishment and whom Jane Spidell plays acidulously well.

Meanwhile, on the other side of the stage, the doctor is having his own meltdown. Self-diagnosed with Alzheimers, he self-medicates, with an unlicensed experimental drug, enlisting a junior pediatrician to monitor his progress and also to keep it a secret; she has misgivings, but goes along because shes both a dedicated researcher and her boss's lover. (Ever wondered why doctors keep you waiting? It's because theyre having sex in the office.)

Trying to inject human emotion into an impossible situation, Ari Cohen and Jenny Young inevitably end up over-acting. Cohen does, though, become very moving later, even suggesting that his aloofness comes not from caring too little but from caring too much. Alone in the cast Cohen and Monteith are allowed monologues, suggesting that their roles are meant to illuminate one another, but it doesnt work out that way. The two stories overlap structurally but hardly ever emotionally. We seem to be watching two

different plays, one of them all too believable, the other anything but.

Boom, Rick Miller's new solo show, has come to the Panasonic Theatre, which I guess makes it a Panasonic Boom. The title actually refers to his parents' generation, and his show begins with brief filmed interviews with them and with a male contemporary of theirs whose relevance is kept a secret until the end. They all seem to be likable people; and, the clips over, Miller spends the rest of the show impersonating them, together with all the cultural figures who most influenced them during their Baby Boomers lifetime. Thats a lot of ground to cover, even given that, having started with VE-Day, he stops inexplicably short at the end of the 1960s.

The end result is scattershot and superficial; maybe if Miller had concentrated on his mother as a representative Canadian Boomer, his story might have had more coherence. As it is, the choice of events seems encyclopaedically arbitrary.

Technically, the show's a wonder. Miller, who directed it himself, spends most of inside a cylinder on a platform, fading in and out of view, constantly changing costume and position, seamlessly merging with David Leclerc's nonstop video footage. And of course he does all the voices. He isnt as accurate imitating real people as he once was when doing everybody from The Simpsons; his Winston Churchill and Harold Macmillan are approximate, while his Perry Como is a travesty. Hes more successful when spiritually closer to home; he does a succession of persuasive rockers, including an amazing Janis Joplin. He finishes with David Bowie, emerging to great effect from his cylindrical cocoon to do "Space Oddity." After which, and for no detectable reason, the show ends. It's true that it's touched on the space race, among many other things, but that hasnt exactly been a guiding theme.

Small Axe, from Project Humanity, is also an interview piece. Andrew Kushnir, writer, and Alan Dilworth, director, talked to gay Jamaican-Canadians; and five actors deliver, very well, verbatim excerpts from the conversations. Kushnir himself presides, talking through a microphone which he discards, as if sacrificing his own position of privilege. He, too, is gay, but white, and of Ukrainian heritage; and his efforts to establish common ground with his subjects results in his being told that it isnt about him. Fair enough; but the play continues as if it is.

Kushnir is a talented and sympathetic performer, and an autobiographical piece about his growing up and coming out in Canada might be very interesting; it's already happened, to an extent, in The Gay Heritage Project. Here, though, his self-punishment seems both excessive and distracting.

Waiting Room runs through Feb. 15 at the Tarragon; Boom through Feb. 1 at the Panasonic Theatre; Small Axe through Feb. 1 at The Theatre Centre.
