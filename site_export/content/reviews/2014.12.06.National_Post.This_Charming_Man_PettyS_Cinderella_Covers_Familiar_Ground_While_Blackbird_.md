In Ross Pettys new Cinderella, the heroine is the proprietress of an organic vegetable store in St. Lawrence Market, whose evil stepmother keeps getting in her enlightened way. Her Prince Charming is a universally adored pop singer and environmental crusader whose fanatical female following keep getting in his enlightened way. This is not unlike - no more weasel words, its very like - the situation in last year's Petty extravaganza, The Little Mermaid, in which the romantic couple bonded over their shared dedication to keeping Toronto's lake free from pollution.

So this Cinderella is a re-tread: an entertaining re-tread certainly, with some great performers, but less fun than it was the first time. To be fair, pantomime itself - the traditional British panto on which Petty has been putting a Canadian spin for nearly 20 years now - is all about theme and variations. You take a traditional story, and mess with it. The more popular sources keep coming around at the Elgin like the more popular Shakespeares at Stratford. Topical jokes have always been part of the recipe, but in recent years theyve become more pointed. The shows are no longer set in some anonymous fairyland but in a fairyland called Toronto. Its quite a radical shift, and a welcome one. But this year's show, compared to last year's, suffers from there being too much theme and too little variation.

Cinderella, in accordance with the independent-heroine tradition that came in with Disneys Beauty and the Beast, is a lot less oppressed than she used to be. I mean, here she is, running her own business, quite successfully it seems, and with a whole singing and dancing chorus as her employees. Shes come a long way from the days when her only companions were mice. She does still have a wicked stepmother, one Revolta Bulldoza - played of course by Petty himself - who, to give the plot some traction, has the legal power to take over the market and remake it in her own evil image, so long as she can seal the deal before Cinderella turns 21. So, when none of the good characters are minding the store, she fills its shelves with an addictive product called Hypno Chips. The property they do have is that of rendering anyone who consumes them a zombie slave to Revolta's will. This is a fate that eventually, if temporarily, overtakes nearly every member of the cast. It obviously makes life easier for Revolta. It also becomes a narrative crutch, and a tiresomely repetitive one, for the show itself. The best part of the story turns out to be the most traditional: the transformation scene, which is suitably magic and involves a pair of real ponies to take Cinderella to the Eligi-Ball (nice one) at which Max Charming, on the advice of his manager, will choose himself a bride. (Or maybe not. The rules are a bit vague.) This sequence is a long time coming, but no more so than in more traditional versions. It makes it all the more effective when it happens.

Danielle Wade is a delightfully wholesome Cinderella and Jeff Lillico a charming Charming. The standout performance though comes from Dan Chameroy, playing his accustomed role of Dame Plumbum, but with a difference. Previous Plumbums have been minor villainesses, sharing the stage with Petty's major one. This Plumbum is a goodie, in fact the Fairy Godmother. This is good economy; no show needs two vain and malevolent drag queens. (Im not even sure it needs one. Pettys best performances have been as Captain Hook and the Sheriff of Nottingham. This time around he's reduced to coming on in a frock, leering and berating the audience for not booing loud enough.) Chameroy's Fairy Plumbum is a delectable creation, accident-prone and easily distracted, but coming triumphantly through in a crisis; the pantomime dame may be a coarse creation but here shes most delicately realized. Shes also a whiz at the ad libs, as is Eddie Glen, who was born to play Buttons and does. Unlike previous Buttonses he isn't lovelorn; another departure is that he gets to accompany Cinders to the ball as her footman, in which capacity he's renamed Zipper. His opposite number Dandini (aka Dan Dean), accurately described as Max Charmings clever little characteractor friend, is played by Reid Janisse, whos also the show's writer; and if you think I've slipped that information in too casually, you should see how modestly hes billed in the program.

A great season for independent theatre in unaccustomed venues continues with a new production of David Harrowers play Blackbird. David Ferry directs, and also plays Ray, a man approaching 60 who once had an affair with a 12-year-old girl and went to jail for it. Now, 16 years later, his victim Una tracks him down to his new workplace; he's apparently rebuilt his life, and has certainly changed his name. His new surname, Trevelyan, has aristocratic connotations of which Una is especially scornful; scorn and aggression are, initially, the dominant notes in her register; surveying the "pigsty" (his word) in which he works and which he seems intent on clearing up, she queries whether he is in fact the janitor. Apparently he isn't, but now that shes appeared, he dreads losing what position he has. Ferry plays him, brilliantly, as a compound of fear and shame, his face illuminated, in an especially striking moment, by the pale glow of a floor-lamp. Striking, too, is the desperate way he talks of "these people" in an attempt to distance himself from your run-of-the-mill pedophile. We blame him, but we find it increasingly hard to despise him. At the same time we watch Sarah Booths Una displaying new layers of vulnerability; at one point she's left alone in a darkened room, and her cries - adult cries as helpless a child's - are indelible; we lose any sense of an actor acting. From this point the play shifts into a different mode; the author says its a love story, and it seems he wasnt kidding. And then it shifts again. It's never obscure, but it's honestly ambiguous: not about facts but about feelings. The intensity of both performances sometimes slips into inaudibility (paradoxically a greater danger in a small space than in a big one) but this is a far better balanced production than the one a few years ago by Studio 180. Like its predecessor, it gives this British play a Canadian setting, or at least Canadian accents: no harm, though some of the video accessories are unnecessary. The setting is uncredited but then it's practically non-existent, which makes it bleakly perfect.
