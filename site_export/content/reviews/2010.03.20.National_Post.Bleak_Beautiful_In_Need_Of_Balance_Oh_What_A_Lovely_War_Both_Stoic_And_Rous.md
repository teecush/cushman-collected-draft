The Soulpepper production of Oh What Lovely War picks up steam when it pulls into Waterloo station -- 1914 is approaching its end, and the first British troops from the First World War are returning home, some wounded, some blind and some on stretchers. They're singing that grandly disgruntled traditional chanty were here because were here. Transport is available for officers, but no arrangements have been made for other ranks; eventually some lorry drivers offer to ferry them to hospital on their lunch-hour. From there we proceed to the famous Christmas Eve truce in the trenches; the British and German troops exchanging carols ancient and profane, and lobbing eatables at one another in place of shells. The effect is at once heartwarming and heartbreaking: like the Waterloo scene, only more so. This is almost the end of the show's first half, but the momentum is pretty much maintained through the second. It's a story told in sorrow and in anger, and it provokes both feelings in its audience.

It still isnt quite what it might be; or, to declare my own prejudice, what it was. Lovely War is a musical/scrapbook/ documentary in which mostly cheerful songs are juxtaposed with mountingly horrendous casualty figures. A bewildering number of writers and creators are acknowledged in the program, but the show is basically based on a BBC feature by Charles Chilton, a great radio producer who was a master at rendering history by song; and then given inspired stage form by Joan Littlewood and her Theatre Workshop troupe. That was in 1963, and I saw it at the companys theatre in East London. It was incredibly funny, incredibly moving: one of the three or four formative theatrical experiences of my then-young life. The Soulpepper revival, the first I've seen, is good but not great. It falls short in two apparently contrasting directions: It's too bleak, and its too beautiful.

The shows framework is that of a seaside pierrot show. It starts upbeat, in an attitude of patriotic certainty, one that gets progressively blown to bits. In this trajectory it resembles Cabaret, a show much influenced by this one; at least it resembles Cabaret before Sam Mendes decided to make it dark from the get-go. Albert Schultz does the same thing with Lovely War; the stage has the traditionally comforting curtains and footlights, but visible behind them is a threatening brick wall. There ain't much warmth. Proceedings start with a mordant overture that gives away too much, musically and dramatically. The score divides, broadly speaking, into two: rousing music-hall numbers, recruiting pamphlets in song, in the first half; stoical disillusioned barrack-room ballads in the second. Here, the latter work better than the former. The cast is largely composed of members of the Soulpepper academy, and the boys at the front, young men playing raw young men, take more naturally to their posting than the theatrical seductresses who send them on their way.

Here's where the beauty part comes in. As I remember it, the Theatre Workshop cast included just one trained singer plus a lot of actors who could "put over a number." The academy actors are intensely musical; they all play instruments and, under Marek Normans expert direction, they execute intricate vocal harmonies. This is especially evident in a wonderfully grotesque chapel scene, in which the troops interrupt the padres platitudes with their own mocking lyrics to the old hymns. Originally, the show's emotional highlight was a despairing nurse's singing of Keep the Home Fires Burning, evoking tears and rage together. Here its just another song, one partly defused by having been instrumentally forecast, twice.

Some things work better than Id remembered, notably a revue-sketch meeting of international profiteers on the grouse moors. There's a stunning staging of the title song in which four pianos, each with its own pianist, are strung diagonally across the stage. Assorted detritus cunningly suggests the trenches, though it does cramp what should be a devastating last scene: French soldiers advancing "like lambs to the slaughter," baa-ing defiantly at the audience.

Two Soulpepper vets do lovely work: Michael Hanrahan, switching between a dainty, raunchy MCand a bullheaded Field Marshal Haig, and Oliver Dennis in a dizzying succession of roles, but happiest in the exploiter-and-officer class. Mike Ross alternates beguiling cheeriness with haunting blankness, and Ins Choi tosses off an array of accents. There is plenty to be pleased and stirred by, especially for those unburdened by memories.
