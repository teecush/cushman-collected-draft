HIGH LIFE

THEATRE REVIEW

HIGH LIFE

Saturday, March 1, 1997

Directed by Jim Millan

Written by Lee MacDougall


  


Starring Brent Carver, Clive Cholerton,


  


Randy Hughson and Ron White


  


Rating: ***

A metaphor for a circular existence

In High Life, four free-floating characters

are forced into a tight scenario and a big part of the

attraction for the audience is getting the chance to watch them wriggle.

Special to The Globe and Mail


  


ASSURANCE is always attractive in the theatre, and High Life has bags of it. Some of this stems from its knockout success at last years World Stage Festival, but a lot seems built in. The play now returns to Harbourfront with its original cast and production. It looks, in its second coming, like something less than The Second Coming, but it is possible to see what its original enthusiasts were going on about.

Lee MacDougall, an actor writing his first play, has an enviable flair for spinning dialogue and the rarer ability for harnessing it to a plot. There is remarkably little fat on the play, even though it only lasts an hour-and-a-half. Its four characters are all morphine addicts. Addicts are by definition monomaniacs (though to be fair these guys smoke and drink a lot as well) and our quartets chosen path to the next hit is robbing banks.

They have a bright new scam hinging on the use of stolen bank cards and requiring seamless and unselfish teamwork: the one thing that these characters, if not these actors, are least equipped to deliver. They are constantly, and often literally, at one another's throats. Only one of the four seems to have any qualms about constantly shooting up and his, if you'll forgive the expression, are skin deep. The play derives its comedy from the fact that none of them has, or can afford to have, any convictions, other than strictly criminal ones; three of them have done time, plenty of it, and they look with considerable suspicion upon the one who hasnt.

The plays great attraction is that it gives four gifted actors the chance to fill their own space. Jim Millan's production boasts what one would call, if mixing metaphors were not as dangerous as mixing drugs, a crack cast. The junkie-ensemble play and the bank-heist play (although up to now there has generally been only the bank-heist movie) are two established genres that go surprisingly well together. It's a mixture of opposites: free-floating characters are forced into a tight scenario, and we watch them wriggle.

Brent Carver is a virtuoso wriggler. He plays Donnie, a laughing boy with a mean streak of self-pity, disturbed by what his habit has done to his body (hes down to his last kidney) but not enough to kick it. The play, for all its humorous veneer, is short on wit -- the lines are nimble rather than resonant -- but Carver is loaded with it. Donnies mind moves slowly -- he greets each new development wih a broad smile and a blithe, "Oh, yeah" -- but his body compensates. He nods his head repeatedly to take in bad news while his weaving hands seem to be pushing it away. Pervading the whole performance is a fey innocence. You wonder initially if a creation so bizarre, so obviously built by the actor, could exist outside the theatre; after five minutes you give in and enjoy.

His polar opposite, immediately credible, is Randy Hughson's Bug, a hoarse hulk in whom violence and the bottle serve as a nervous system. Clive Cholerton pays Billy, the smooth, new boy with a scarcely-concealed ambition to be top kid on the block; he's the most dangerous of all, not least to himself. And the kingpin is Dick, who hatches the scheme and tries to keep everyone in line. He has the shell of authority but not, being as hooked as any of them, the substance. This makes him an acting problem, and one that Ron White, though perfectly watchable, fails to solve. The writing traps him; he has lines that sound like failed Damon Runyon and, unlike the others, is given no real chance to implode.

The staging moves easily from Dicks room, where the four plan their caper, to the car from which they execute it. This last scene is a paradox; its absorbing but almost totally lacking in suspense. We relish the actors, and we appreciate the situation, but we couldn't care less what happens to the characters. Their own insensibility seems to have seeped into the writing. By the end, two of them have taken a dive while the others are back exactly where we found them; it's undeniably elegant, an apt metaphor for a circular existence, but no more than that. There seems to be nothing beneath the plays surface that really matters to the writer -- which, in a first play, is a considerable surprise. Its a diploma piece. It can hardly be called promising, because it's not fulfilled. And it offers no clues whatever to its author's future.

The Crow's Theatre production of High Life is playing at Harbourfront Centre in Toronto.
