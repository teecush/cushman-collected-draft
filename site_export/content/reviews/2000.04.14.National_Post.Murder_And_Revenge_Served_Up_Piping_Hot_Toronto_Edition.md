THE ORESTEIA PART TWO: THE DAUGHTERS OF DARKNESS

Du Maurier Theatre Centre, Toronto

In its 40-year existence, Britain's National Theatre has staged two separate productions of Aeschylus Oresteia. In the early 80s, Peter Hall directed a version that was aggressively stylized: everyone in masks, an all-male cast and a translation that sounded like Anglo-Saxon. It was impressive, in a numbing sort of way. Now - - at the opposite extreme and imported to Toronto for the World Stage festival -- comes Katie Mitchell's production, modern in dress and in technological trappings, and as close to psychological realism as any staging of a Greek tragedy is likely to get.

Mitchell's approach, which I find far the more congenial, comes most into its own in the trilogys central section -- the play that Aeschylus, or his English translators, called The Libation Bearers and is billed here as The Oresteia Part Two: The Daughters of Darkness.

This is the section with the most action, the one closest to the familiar tragedy of revenge. In Part One we saw, or at least heard, Queen Clytemnestra of Argos and her lover, Aegisthus, murdering Clytemnestras husband, Agamemnon. Now they are themselves dispatched by Orestes and Electra, Agamemnon and Clytemnestra's surviving children. Orestes has been living in exile; Electra has stayed home, nursing her hate. When Orestes returns, Electra spurs him on to vengeance -- not that he needs much spurring. He does have momentary qualms about killing his mother, but his sidekick, Pylades (who is otherwise entirely mute), reminds him that they are acting under orders from Apollo's oracle, and he goes ahead. No sooner is the deed done than Orestes begins to feel the consequences. He has a vision of the avenging Furies, who are to dominate the proceedings after the intermission.

I enthused yesterday over Ted Hughes translation, the finest and most speakable dramatic verse heard in English since the same poets adaptation of Seneca's Oedipus, which Peter Brook directed (also for the National) in 1968. His lines make up a superbly flinty armoury for actors -- a set of weapons to be hurled across the family dining table, which is where Mitchell sets the murders. First we see Orestes, arriving in disguise and getting past palace security to be admitted through a vast and clangorous metal door. He announces his own supposed death, to the delight of the tyrant Aegisthus (played by Sebastian Harcombe as a smooth-fingered piano strummer and tango dancer).

The delight proves short-lived, as does Aegisthus, leaving the siblings free to deal with their mother. While she pleads (with a half-accepting desperation, in Helen Schlesinger's performance) and Orestes hesitates, Electra sits in silent, hysterical expectation. The reason shes silent is that her author never actually wrote her into the scene. Its hard for us to accept, but Aeschylus -- unlike Sophocles, Euripides and dozens of moderns who've built plays and operas around her -- wasn't much interested in Electra; after shed served her turn by recognizing Orestes, he dropped her.

Theres quite enough in the lady's one scene for Lilo Baur to get her teeth into; she paints a powerful picture of a gnawing, obsessive daddy's girl to the last, and she has no trouble maintaining it through her subsequent silence. (Silent, that is, except for her scream when she hears her mother being killed offstage. This is surprising, and a bit sentimental. Wouldnt she have gloated?) But it does point up a problem. Its thrilling to hear scenes written 2,500 years ago responding so readily to the modern actors' touch. But though these characters live very vividly in the moment, they don't accommodate much digging. Paul Hiltons Orestes establishes himself from his first line as a quivering neurotic who commits matricide in order to acquire an identity; he then has to go on striking the same note, and the effect is grindingly monotonous. (This is perhaps the place to point out that, for all its daring and all its excitements, this ambassadorial production does not present English acting at anything like its current best.)

The chorus in this episode are subsidiary figures: Trojan slave girls, confederates of Electra, who assist in the conspiracy and can easily take a naturalistic treatment. The last instalment, though, is the chorus play. They are the Daughters of Darkness, though some of them seem to be sons. More familiarly, they are the Furies, who subsequently change into the Kindly Ones, a.k.a. the Eumenides (that being the title by which this final section of the trilogy is generally known). Mitchell's visual presentation of these vengeful wraiths, as a troop of menacing nannies, is the least ludicrous I have seen, but she cannot solve the problems of the play's narrative, of which the chief is that it doesnt have one. What happens is that Orestes stands trial in Athens, the Furies attack him, and Apollo defends him. The jury is deadlocked, but the goddess Athena has the casting vote and she acquits. She then offers the Furies a consolation prize: They can have a new life and a new identity in Athens, presiding over the transformation from a culture of bloodlust and vendetta to one of peace and mercy. One of my old textbooks called this "the apotheosis of patriotism," and its still possible to be stirred by it. Aeschylus, who had lived through his country's wars with Persia, cannot have been too starry-eyed about human justice. Hughes certainly wasn't, and Mitchell obviously isnt. But its an ideal to be striven toward, and it has never been more nobly stated. It's worth remembering, next time you hear someone sounding off about "Judeo-Christian values" as the be-all and end-all of Western civilization, just how much we owe to the Greeks.

The Furies' transformation was the one point at which Halls hieratic approach worked better than Mitchells. But what really lets her and us down, despite the dignified lucidity of Joy Richardson's Athena, is the legal buildup. The arguments advanced in Orestes' favour are specious, not to say misogynistic; and as counsel for the defence, Michael Goulds sneering Apollo (a corduroyed flying doctor) does his client very little credit. One is humanely glad that Orestes gets off from eternal torments, or whatever it is the underworld social workers have in mind for him, but its difficult to feel much joy. It's an honourable failure, though; and where it succeeds, the show is electric.
