CORIOLANUS

Festival Theatre, Stratford

- - -

Shakespeare's Coriolanus hates being called a boy, though his enemies have good reason for doing it to him. For a man with a large and unashamed expanse of bald, shiny dome, Colm Feores Coriolanus makes a remarkably convincing boy. The boyishness is psychological rather than physical. This least empathetic of heroes is an invincible Roman soldier -- a human war-machine -- who despises anyone not born into the officer-class and reserves especial venom for the poorer and hungrier of his non-combatant fellow citizens. They in turn divide our sympathies by being both fickle and shrewd. They certainly have the protagonists number; right at the start they attribute his military exploits to his wanting to please his mother and to be proud. (I didn't actually hear them do it. A chapter of accidents made me miss the first couple of scenes of the Stratford Festival's opening production, but I cannot imagine that these vital lines were cut.)

Feores Coriolanus actually -- and accurately -- combines intolerable arrogance with agonizing modesty. His body language comes in two dialects. Upright and unforgiving on the battlefield -- which can include his native senate-house when people disagree with him -- he goes gawky, gangly and weak-at-the-knees when obliged to listen to speeches in his honour. (What he says, roughly paraphrased, is "I was just doing my job.") He also, unlike some Coriolani I have seen, has a sense of humour, though its a very circumscribed one. When forced to put on a ragged gown to solicit popular votes for the consulship, he feels as ridiculous as he looks, and he turns the joke against himself but also savagely against the people. Most of his playfulness, though, is reserved for his mother Volumnia, the controlling matron who made him what he is. She is as arrogant as he, but unlike him she has the instincts of a politician. (It's currently tempting to liken Coriolanus to Stephen Harper, but it's impossible to imagine him belonging to a party, let alone leading one. He may be aloof , but he isnt ideological about it.) She knows, as he doesnt, how to temporize; and when she tries to get him to do it, it puzzles him. For her part, though her pride in him is absolutely genuine, she enjoys her power over him; indeed, in Martha Henry's performance, she revels in it. And since mother and son have been playing these games for years, there is a shared joy in their mutual dependence.

The crisis comes when Coriolanus, banished from Rome, makes common cause with the national enemy and marches back to destroy the city. All pleas for mercy fail, until his family is sent to plead with him. And Volumnia gets through to him by appealing not, as earlier supplicants have done, to his pity, but by making him feel on some level ridiculous, like a helpless child. She doesn't, in this performance, seem to care that much about Rome, but she cant stand her sons disobedience to her. And he can't stand disappointing her. In breaking him down she, knowingly or not, signs his death warrant. She also, drawing on her last maternal reserves, exhausts herself, spiritually as much as physically. Whatever the reason, at her last supposedly triumphal appearance she's shaking like a leaf. Its the most powerful moment in the play.

Nothing Feore does quite matches it. His characterization is outstandingly lucid and intelligent, and his classical chops are in excellent shape. He is fine in moments of lip-curling irony; hear him repeat the words must and "shall," as if incredulous that anyone should have the impertinence to make such demands on him; fine too in moments of prophecy since this Coriolanus, once banished, does to a certain extent grow up, developing a wry sense of fatality. Its the moments of regression that lack their proper scale; the outbursts of volcanic rage, fuelled by pride and heedless of danger, that breach all dams and bring, in inexorable succession, disgrace and exile and death. Maybe Feore really is too mature an actor to play this boy. I don't mean he's too old. I mean hes too watchful.

Antoni Cimolinos production achieves the same partial success as its hero. It conveys a sense of history on the move -- moving fast too, with one scene merging seamlessly into the next on a Festival stage reduced to its barest and most satisfying essentials. Crowds look good on it and solitary figures look even better, with help from Gil Wechsler's lighting and from Santo Loquasto's costumes, which have an original, sculptural line. There is also a fair, or unfair, amount of generalized hubbub; relationships are well stated, but they dont put down roots. This is especially true of the heros protracted dance with Aufidius, his rival, ally and nemesis; and especially frustrating since Graham Abbey, making clearest sense of some of the most convoluted passages in Shakespeare, sounds ready for anything. Paul Soles is ingratiating as the wily old Menenius, and especially fine in his mixture of pique and pain when his errand of mercy is slighted. Robert King stands out as the toughest and wittiest of the Roman crowd as do a pair of senate officers, for similar reasons, and another pair of enemy sentries. Best of all the supports is Bernard Hopkins as the oilier and more articulate of the two demagogues who engineer the hero's enforced exit and get bitten by it. He and the title character barely deign to speak to one another, but there's a real current between them and between the things they stand for.
