WILLIAM AND JAMES

Theatre Passe Muraille

- - -

One historic function of the stage, much neglected in these penny- pinching times, is to provide spectators with an image of the kind of luxury in which they would all like to be living.

Theatre Passe Muraille, which can't be exactly flush with cash, has nevertheless put out nobly with its set for William and James. We gaze at the Victorian bedroom of an English country gentleman, done out in gilt, plush and crushed red velvet, plus a really hot- looking fireplace, tantalizing with the promise of even richer apartments, just out of sight.

David Wootton is the designer, and his work here is sumptuous, soothing and decadent, the latter in the same sense as a dozen boxes of President's Choice cookies placed end to end. Still, we cannot live just by gawping, and the sweetmeats here are less fun than the wrapping.

James is the master of this house, and William the young man he has picked up at a London party and brought home with him. James is married, but his wife lives conveniently far away, in one of two other establishments that he has dotted about the land. (One hopes, for the sake of James's credibility as a gentleman, that they are equally grand.) William, who looks pretty and sounds smooth, is carving out a career as rough trade and one night stand. He refuses in advance to stay longer than a night, to the dismay of James, who has decided, even before they go to bed together, that he wants love.

The morning after, James makes a proposal. William will stay in his house -- as friend, companion and sexual partner -- for a period of two years, at the end of which James proposes to die (of what cause remains unclear), leaving William an inheritance. William, hardly surprisingly, accepts. There are ups and downs in the relationship; at the end of the two years William, revealed as a far needier person than he would earlier have admitted, is also predictably in love.

I don't imagine that Robert Tsonos, the author, is telling us this story for its own sweet sake. But it's no great news that Victorian sexuality, homo or hetero, was a lot more complicated than its novelists and other official chroniclers were ever at liberty to disclose. Or that sexual relationships are power relationships, with money one of the greatest power sources available. That was true 150 years ago, and it is true now; but the choice of setting illuminates neither period.

It connects, perhaps, in a small way, when we learn that James, upholding the best of his society's values, likes to give to the poor, and that William, anticipating the worst of our society's values, says that it will simply spoil them. He adds that William only does it to make himself feel good. Unfortunately the obvious answer, which is "so what?" never gets made.

Otherwise, the play is escapist without being entertaining. Along with the stage-pictures we get the assurance of a well-staffed household (like My Fair Lady but with the chorus offstage) and the promise of elegant chat. Here Tsonos falls down; he does hit a consistent and acceptable tone, weighty without being antique, but it has no depths and the attempts at epigram are self-conscious.

To make a comparison the play itself seems to invite: Oscar Wilde's wisecracks, at their best, tell us something about the assumptions of the people making them, Tsonos' seem to be there because it's expected of them. A recurring debate about the meaning of a painting that the master has hanging on the wall of his room is especially dull; it's obviously meant to reflect on the characters themselves, but they're too flat to reflect anything.

Worst of all, perhaps, is the drawing out of the narrative. As a vignette, the piece might pass. Protracted over two years (real time) or 75 minutes (stage time), it really drags.

Mark Caven has a fine fit of anger when James pulls rank and declines to be a pushover; this comes late though, and one wishes that he had more backbone earlier. It's not so much that the character needs it as that the play does.

Michael Schultz, as William, preens as requested, both physically and vocally, but has exhausted his resources before his time is up. The director is George Pothitos.
