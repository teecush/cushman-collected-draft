THE BATTLE OF STALINGRAD: A REQUIEM

Du Maurier Theatre Centre

- - -

Georgia, Georgia, no peace I find. Rezo Gabriadze, a Georgian puppet-master and stage designer seeking a metaphor for the war that wracked his homeland in the 1990s, alighted on the devastating siege and defence of Stalingrad in the Second World War. The resulting piece, involving half a dozen puppeteers and a long list of recorded voices, has come to Toronto as the last offering in this year's World Stage Preview series.

Officially described as less a history than a lament, the show takes to extremes an honourable tradition of presenting cataclysmic events as glimpsed in flashes from the sidelines. Stendhals account of the Battle of Waterloo, in The Charterhouse of Parma, belongs in this line; and so does practically all of War and Peace. There was a celebrated dramatization of the Tolstoy novel that anticipated Gabriadze to some extent by using toy soldiers to demonstrate the Battle of Borodino.

In The Battle of Stalingrad, the events are twice distanced (thrice, perhaps, if you count the surtitles); not only are they acted out by marionettes but by marionettes who, for much of the time, represent farm animals. The inspiration here is apparently a war report about the landscape of Stalingrad having been covered with dead horses. The implications of that are very poignant, though not perhaps as poignant as the prologue of this production, which shows us a human body: a tiny, naked puppet, appallingly frail and vulnerable.

There is a matching epilogue, not quite as resonant, in which an ant mourns the number of his progeny stamped out in the campaign. We are meant, I think, to accept him as truly ant-like, rather than anthropomorphic, though obviously his plight has resonances for the fate of humans trapped in the same way.

Other times, though, the animal characters seem to be directly standing in for humans. So Alyosha, "a stage worker and transport specialist" and the nearest thing we have to a hero, is played by a horse, overloading a metaphor and diluting tragedy with whimsy. To put this in perspective: I am not a fan, either, of that American cartoon strip which presents the Holocaust in terms of mice. I love Animal Farm, but that has a consistent satirical tone; you know which are the animals and which are the humans, and why.

This show, too, uses both; you are expected to laugh indignantly at the humans and be sorry for the animals; which means taking a sentimental perspective and imposing it on the audience. Its one thing to concentrate on the marginalized characters who don't make it into the history books, but they need to live as characters.

I found the show visually beautiful, sometimes moving, but also confusing. It seems to be trying to be an epic and a collection of fragments, both at once, wavering between the sardonically mournful and the traditionally patriotic. Alyosha's relationship with Natasha, an actress, is hard to take hold of or to care about. I couldnt tell why the action shifts to Berlin before the war for a satirical look at intellectual cafe life. (I presume its satirical, since among the artists supposedly present are Bertolt Brecht and Franz Werfel, both of them well out of Germany by 1937, which is when this is said to be happening.)

The view of Stalin himself, and of the Russian brass in general, is very ambiguous. We feel the devastation, in a generalized way, and we also applaud the ingenious presentation of a train journey. We also note the presence of a Jewish wedding, complete with canopy, signifying, apart from its place in the plot, that life has a habit of going on. We assent to the proposition that war, any war, is hell, and that the helpless always suffer.

So Gabriadze has his metaphor. But it would have to exist more fully in its own right for us to take it to heart. It isn't just the use of puppets that makes it all seem small.
