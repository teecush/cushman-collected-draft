THE GREAT WAR

Cameron House, Toronto

---

The First World War keeps on being fought. Soulpepper has revisited it twice this year, with Oh What A Lovely War and with Billy Bishop (who will be going to war again next year). Video Cabaret, working its way through its mordant comic-strip history of Canada, have now reached 1914-18 for the second time. Or really the third, since The Great War, first staged by Video-Cab in 1993, was revived earlier this year and was such a success that it's now been re-revived.

The success is deserved. I've been known to carp at VideoCab's reliance on caricature, not because I doubt its skill or even its accuracy, but because an evening of sneering and sniggering can, and sometimes has, become monotonous. This instalment begins as if it might tempt that same old law of diminishing returns, but it bucks it. The basic technique is the same: seven unbelievably quick-changing actors in a succession of brief black-out sketches. But the subject-matter, and the concentrated time frame it inhabits, give the production a unity that some of its predecessors have lacked. Michael Hollingsworth's direction is as pointed and inventive as ever, Andy Moro's lighting as devastatingly precise, Astrid Janson and Sarah Armstrong's costumes as over-the-top gorgeous. And Hollingsworth's script may be his best ever. It's certainly, in my experience, the most layered.

It starts on the Canadian home front. The Duke of Connaught, Governor General of Canada, surveys the coming carnage from the privileged point of view of everybody's uncle. England's king, Russia's emperor, Germany's kaiser: they're all his relatives. Sobering to realize that all those lives were sacrificed in Europe's last dynastic struggle -- "a family quarrel" as the Duke blithely observes. The Duke's Duchess is German, and makes no secret of her sympathies. In fact, her role consists of chanting "Deutschland! Deutschland!" in an aggressively manic way that eventually leads to her being discreetly deported; I was bothered at the time by characterization that seemed simplistic even by VidCab standards but, on reflection, maybe not. Canada does its colonial duty, under the auspices of Prime Minister Sir Robert Borden, while Henri Bourassa does his apoplectic best to keep Quebec out of it. Everybody acts (I'm referring to the characters, not the performers) self-centredly, and many of them appallingly: par for this particular course.

And we meet the private soldiers who suffer for their superiors' mistakes and who are presented, also as expected, with far greater sympathy. What distinguishes this production, however, is that there's a middle ground. It's occupied by two young officers who start out rivals for the same woman and who then, after she's chosen one of them, are comrades at Ypres, Vimy, Passchendaele and Amiens. One (Paul Braunstein) loses his previously permanent smile when he gets his face blown off. The other (MacFyfe, who also, and in complete contrast, plays the Germanophile Duchess) starts out as a sardonic commentator and becomes steadily more so. Both these performances punch above VidCab's usual moral weight. And so, but even more so, does Rick Campbell as the Canadian commander Sir Arthur Currie, trying to preserve his and his country's dignity while sending his men to the slaughter at the behest of a succession of incompetent and vainglorious British generals. When peace finally comes, he finds the colonies' contribution relegated to an official footnote. A new Canada stirs: a point touched on in Billy Bishop but explored more suggestively here.

The other ranks are represented, poignantly but unsentimentally, by Johnnie (Dylan Roberts) who loves his mother and ends up crying for her while crucified, and Dave (Anand Rajaram), a sweet-natured boy from the farm who, after being shell-shocked, turns into a killing machine. Rajaram's other roles include Sam Hughes, a war minister who's not only outstandingly clueless but outstandingly neckless; he's built like a playing card. This actor's versatility was established in his Second City days, but this may be the first time he's been allowed to go through an evening without playing anybody who could conceivably be Asian; he does the whole show in white-face. But then so do all the other actors; it's part of the style.

Also a part of the style is an almost total absence of scenery, there being neither the time nor the space. We do get a single glimpse of barbed wire, and it may be the most memorable image in any Video Cabaret show. Terrifying too are the anonymous gas-masked, almost science-fiction figures, wreathed in smoke, who fall upon the empire troops from behind. Back home, a couple of scenes in a munitions factory contrast the heedless relief a war-wife feels when the dreaded telegram is for someone else with the heart-rending collapse when it's for her. As in Oh What a Lovely War, the evening gets more powerful as the casualty figures get more overwhelming, though on the one occasion on which it strays into that show's territory (a rendition of Pack Up Your Troubles) it suffers in the comparison. Otherwise, it's nearly as powerful. The difference between this and some other plays in the cycle is the difference between making a joke and making a point.
