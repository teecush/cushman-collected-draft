A young man and woman, probably early twenties but maybe late teens, confront one an-other in front of what looks like a garbage dump, though it sports a sign saying that no garbage is allowed. Ain't that just like life. The pair are bobby and Tina, who got together, against the odds, in George F. Walker's play Tough! and whom we now meet again in a sequel, Moss Park, which reaches us two decades after its predecessor though the couple themselves have aged by only three years. but then the poverty trap, which grips them both, is ageless.

Bobby and Tina aren't married, or even living together. They do, however, have a toddler daughter; and, thanks to a one-night stand reunion, another child is on the way. bobby learns this from Tina in the course of the, mostly crackling, 60-minute conversation that makes up the play. bobby wants Tina to take him back - he's always wanted that - but she has serious doubts, as he's repeatedly proved himself incapable of holding down a job. Always, as he tells it, that's somebody else's fault, though it's doubtful that he's managed to convince even himself of that. He certainly hasn't convinced Tina, who seems both smart and practical. The ways of the heart, and of the body, are notoriously and perhaps mercifully strange, so it's easy to accept that she still loves him: less so, perhaps, that she might be prepared to build a life with him. bobby, it seems, has known success in only two areas of his life. Tina is one of them; the other is acting as lookout for a small-time break-and-enter gang, one of whom, a close friend, is now in jail.

One thing that together Tina and attention-deficient bobby have in common is they both come from, and are currently living with, colourfully dysfunctional families, whose antics sound quite funny when we hear them described, until we stop to think about what these situations actually entail. The strength of Walker's writ-ing here is his ability to depict

deep pain with a light touch; I actually prefer him in this mode to the more elaborate Gothic of his Suburban Motel plays. The comparative simplicity of Moss Park must owe a lot to its being designed for a young people's (not a children's) audience; it's staged here by the Green Thumb company of vancouver whose mandate that is. There's one plot-and-character twist that's hard to take - it's a relief, in more ways than one, when it turns out to be a false trail - but in general Walker spins his situation out resourcefully, making especially good use of crossed cellphone calls from Tina's dis-approving buddy and bobby's enabling one.

Despite its brevity, the play does run out of variations, as do the basically good perform-ances in Patrick Mcdonald's production. Graeme McComb's bobby is consistently the over-grown class clown. Haley McGee's Tina coruscates, not least when most desperate, but you do start wondering how somebody so smart and witty and strong got stuck in such a no-win situation. The play tells you why; the performance doesn't quite bear it out. Still, you root for the couple, hope the best for them, fear the worst; which I think is what the play wants of you. both actors, by the way, have great timing.

Timing is something nota-bly missing from Where's My Money?, with which Toronto's independent theatre scene continues its exploration of the plays of John Patrick Shanley. This one is billed as a Halloween play, and it does involve ghosts: deceased entanglements haunting, literally, the troubled marriages of a couple of couples. Staging these apparitions is a problem, not solved in this production which combines spooky lighting with awkwardly matter-of-fact appearances. Still, the ghosts are the play's trimmings rather than its heart. That resides with the spouses who converse in the brittle comic manner that, on broadway and off it, is the conventional mask for hatred and heartbreak. It isn't a style I especially like, but it can be effective if competently done. Here, whether through tone-deafness in the director (Jodi Sprung-boyd) or inexperience or simple incapacity in the ac-tors, nobody is suave, every-body shouts. In a play that introduces its characters one by one, the acting gets progressively more leaden; every time you think you've hit bot-tom, somebody worse comes on. The first to appear, Emily Coutts, is actually pretty good (and the most interesting character as written) but her role proves brief. After that, the only break in the depress-ing pattern is Michael Goldlist who's terrible in his first scene but improves thereafter. The play itself says some interest-ing things about dependence and co-dependence, but its people are puppets, and they need really skilled animation.
