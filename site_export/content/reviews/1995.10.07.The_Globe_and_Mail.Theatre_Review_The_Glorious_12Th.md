THEATRE REVIEW THE GLORIOUS 12TH

Saturday, October 07, 1995

Robert Cushman

Written by Raymond Storey Directed by Bob Baker Presented by The Canadian Stage Company At the St. Lawrence Centre in Toronto until Nov. 4 Rating: 1/2 Play promises vicarious thrills Raymond Storey's two-tiered structure doesn't sustain this work about bigotry Reviewed by Robert Cushman BIGOTRY is a perennially fascinating subject, affording readers or spectators any number of vicarious thrills. If we don't share the prejudices on display, we can enjoy being shocked by them. Raymond Storey's play The Glorious 12th offers a double helping, being concerned with the interaction of the Orange Lodge and the Ku Klux Klan.

To take the first first: When the Protestant William of Orange - William III of England - defeated the Roman Catholic James II at the Battle of the Boyne in 1690, a new era of sectarian vindictiveness was loosed upon the world and has stayed loosed. Ireland has been the area most obviously infected, but the fever also took root in Scotland and, we are now reminded, in Canada. It is 1927, and the members of a small-town family are preparing for the Orange Day Parade, their annual gesture toward keeping the Pope out of Ontario. Head of the house is Dad, a scythe-wielding patriarch (Father Time, perhaps) who doesn't believe in patronizing Jewish stores. His son, Dalziel, is a war-hero whose principal peacetime activity seems to be keeping his wife pregnant; she is overworked, under-understood, and generally distraught. There is also a 14-year-old grandson who is being groomed for membership of the Lodge and whom we hear taking his initiatory oaths: a blood-chilling litany of curses against Catholics.

Still, there are degrees of hate, as the Orangepersons discover when Dad's doctor son-in-law introduces a white-suited visitor from Indiana who is part snake-oil salesman and part snake. He plans, in effect, to assimilate the Lodge to the Klan; he wants the Canadians' membership and he especially wants their money. He and the lady he travels with - a manic Bible-spouter - also engage in furious amorous wrestling whenever their hosts' backs are turned. It is hard to accuse a writer of vilifying the KKK, but, by making its representative a fraud even by his own dubious lights, Storey seriously weakens the conflict and the play. Nor is that the only problem.

The play has been planned as a two-Storey building, with a personal level and a public one. Dad has two daughters. One is the doctor's disaffected wife; the other is a singing-teacher whom the family regard as slightly crazy. This turns out to be a cover for their disgust at her having had a relationship with an Indian: a friendship brutally destroyed by brother Dalziel. One can see that the two levels were meant to reinforce each other, but in practice they amount to two plays delivered in alternating sections. There seems no connection between the cruel Dalziel of one scene and the comparatively tolerant Dalziel of another, nor between that one and the Dalziel who comes out for the Klan at the end of the play. Dad, meanwhile, takes a stand against the intruders, but whether it is his sense of decency that has been wounded or just his amour propre is never disclosed; the part is cruelly underwritten. In another sense the entire play is overwritten. "Everything about you falls so squarely, so completely into place," says the doctor's wife, brandishing adverbs as if they were hatchets. "You can't" runs a climactic and excessively detailed admonition "carry hate around - it's like acid, it'll eat the inside of the container." The play manages to titillate us with reminders of prejudices past, and it implicitly reminds us of their connection to prejudices present, but that's all it does.

Bob Baker's production looks splendid, beautifully lit by Kevin Lamotte and spaciously set by Sue LePage who starts things off nicely with the silhouette of what, in another locale, would be a perfect little house on the prairie. But the acting goes in fits and starts, especially fits. It seemed unconvinced and unconvincing. Mary Ann McDonald and Nancy Palk do best as the sisters, granted a determinedly lyrical and virtually free- standing scene of mutual confidence. John Bourgeois is flounderingly underweight as the Klansman; Brenda Robins's oratorical excursions as the evangelical Southern belle are best described as valiant. Ron Hastings as Dad grabs what opportunities he is given, but his hair and eyebrows look very false. Marjorie Campbell as the unwilling Earth-mother plays in a comic convention unbroached by anyone else on stage and got exit-applause after delivering a turning-worm tirade: a sign, as it nearly always is, that the actor has dragged the audience out of the play.
