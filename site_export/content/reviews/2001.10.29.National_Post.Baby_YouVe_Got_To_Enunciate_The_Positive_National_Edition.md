DIANA KRALL

Hummingbird Centre, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - Diana Kralls first two numbers on Friday night were a summary of her career to date. She began with a breakneck novelty, accompanied by her own prickly piano plus guitar, bass and drums. Then the 40 musicians seated behind her picked up their fiddles and horns and escorted her through a lush, slow ballad that she voiced in something between a murmur and a moan. So far has Canadas local girl come: from the West Coast pianist-turned-singer plying her solitary trade in, presumably, Nanaimo bars, to the high-priced siren whose promoters will spring for a symphony orchestra in every town.

Along the way she has evolved, or someone has created for her, an Attitude. She now comes on like the Gap-ad idea of a jazz singer: long black dress sliding off the shoulder, pouty lips, open-toed shoes with precipitous stiletto heels that made her initial traverse of the huge Hummingbird stage, from wings to piano, look decidedly perilous. Her image, however carefully fostered, doesn't sit quite comfortably on her, which is rather endearing. When she talks between numbers, which she does sparingly, it is usually to tell us on whose album she first heard the song she has just sung. Dispensing this information, she hovers between aloof and confiding. One half of her says, I'm just a beginner, learning from the greats"; the other half says, "Im a star.

Obviously what we are seeing and hearing is the result of a huge, and hugely successful, commercial operation. Critics are supposed to deplore this kind of thing, but actually the makeover has worked to Kralls advantage, and I don't just mean financially. True, she plays less interesting piano than she used to -- or maybe just less piano since her big new arrangements require her just to noodle against the strings. But it has given more character to her singing. I always used to find her a generalized vocalist; all the songs came out alike. Her up-tempo interludes with her trio still don't have much character; she had nothing new to say about I Love Being Here with You (the opener) or the now hopelessly shopworn Fly Me to the Moon.

The fans yelled for her to sing Peel Me a Grape, Dave Frishbergs cartoon of a femme fatale, with which she once had something of a hit. She refused, which was tasteful of her because her version of that song is horrific. She hasnt the humour for it; there is a difference between mock-deadpan and dead-deadpan. She did sing the more straightforwardly sexy Do It Again, but by eradicating any trace of demureness from the song and rendering it as a straight come-on, she lost all its tension.

That was among the stringy ones, and the orchestra helped kill it. But when they accompanied her on ballads, either lost or yearning, then something did emerge. It first peeped through on Maybe You'll Be There, which is not on the Sinatra album to which she assigned it but is a rare enough song to catch the ear.

It came more clearly into focus on Cry Me a River, the old Julie London anthem. It's a vengeful song and it suits Kralls sullen- goddess persona very well. She takes it harder and faster than London did and actually makes it more powerful and persuasive. There is also something dreamlike about her performance -- as if she were indulging a fantasy of retribution -- and this tranced quality turned out to work for happier songs as well -- sometimes in quite unlikely circumstances, as in her bossa nova treatment of the Gershwins 'S Wonderful, in which she seemed to be testing out the adjectives, or in I've Got You Under My Skin, which, after all, is about a kind of possession. She claims to have learned this one, too, from Sinatra, though her version has nothing to do with his; it might have come from an album called Songs for Swinging Catatonics.

Most of the arrangements are by Claus Ogerman, and heard in bulk, as on her new CD, The Look of Love (Verve), they are soporific. In smaller doses, with the singer actually there, they are quite compelling. Her vocal timbre is still off-putting; she either has or uses very little breath, so the words come out in short groups whose duration bears very little relation to their meaning and helps reduce the lyrics to mush -- a state to which they have already been brought by her apparent aversion to consonants.

Occasionally she does bite into a word -- as on Im not alone in the night" in Love Letters -- and the effect is contrastingly arresting. (It also helps that she does this song complete with verse.) Her showpiece encore of Joni Mitchells lugubrious A Case of You was almost completely unintelligible; all that came through was the title line, admittedly the best in the song.

The best -- or at least the best-showcased -- of her sidemen was the intent guitarist Dan Faehnle. Alan Broadbent, a superb pianist in his own right, conducted the band, whose physical presence, unlike Krall's, seemed oddly superfluous. The charts they were playing were the ghostly kind that seem to have been written to be piped in.
