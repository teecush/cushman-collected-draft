robertcushman@lycos.com; Snow White and the Group of Seven runs through Jan. 8 at the Elgin Theatre, 416-872-5555.

They call it a family musical but its still a pantomime. Just four years ago Ross Petty's panto subject at the Elgin was Snow White and the Magnificent Seven. The seven were small and therefore sort of traditional, even if they did take their group description from Hollywood, with a distant nod to Japan. Now the same story has come round again, this time thumbing a couple of noses at tradition, with a double dose of Canadian content.

Snow White and the Group of Seven pays homage on its backcloths to the local painters on whom it puns, but the people who live in the cottage where the heroine finds refuge aren't artists, at least not in that sense. Theyre a pop group, and three of them (Ryan Malcolm, Gary Beals, Billy Klippert) are -- Ill fess up, I only know this from the publicity -- alumni of Canadian Idol. Six of the seven are brothers -- the characters, I mean, not the performers -- and the seventh is their dad. He's played by Alan Frew, who was a Canadian idol before there was Canadian Idol, and who presides over the pack with an air of exasperated authority and in an accent of ripest Caledonian.

Well, why not? The original story of Snow White is bare-bones simple: Mirror, Dwarfs (not Dwarves, cf. the pedantic Prince of Into the Woods), Apple, time to go home. Something has to fill out the evening, and pantomime has always been cheerfully eclectic in its choice of filling. Despite what you may have heard, there is nothing revolutionary about having the cast break into current pop songs rather than show tunes. In the UK, where the panto form originated, they've been doing it for years, grabbing at whats middle-of-the- road topical in music as much as in humour.

But, here the music becomes part of the plot. The Group have rented what used to be the Dwarfs cottage in order to re-hone their act. This could mean that they're woodshedding in an actual woodshed. When Snow White moves in she becomes their manager and sends them out on the road, this being the cue for a miniature pop concert in which the boys show off all the different styles they can imitate. Needless to say, it's while theyre away seeking their fortunes that Snow Whites evil stepmother comes to call, bearing the poisoned apple. Only this time it's an apple-flavoured cookie, and the queen is disguised as a Girl Guide. O Canada. By the way, Snow White's fondness for apples has been carefully set up earlier in the show. This is something new: a pantomime with exposition.

Even newer, its a pantomime with deconstruction. At the climax, through some postmodern jiggery-pokery that went right over my unsophisticated head, the traditional fairy-tale ending is declared inoperative, and the forces of good and evil do battle with contrasting rock songs as their weapons The jury that decides the victor is composed, of course, of young members of the audience, and they have far more to do than most children who get pressed into panto service. They have, for one thing, to be hectored by Sean Cullen, whose role in the story is unclear (he keeps emerging from behind the mirror) but who works around it as a narrator-and- commentator figure. By the way, the voting is rigged and all ends happily. I dont think that's a spoiler.

David Finley's script is more cheerful than funny, and it can also be rather cumbersome. It gives Snow White a group of animal buddies who are so attentive that her official septet of protectors seem superfluous: How many friends can a girl have and still claim to be persecuted? But the shows heart is in the right place, and the recruiting, for the second year running, of Ted Dykstra as director gives the proceedings a renewed shot of energy and purpose. Hes ruthless with the conventions. There is no initial gambolling on the village green; there's no village green. Instead there is, courtesy of Tracey Flyte, choreography worthy of the name. And the inevitable singalong is down to just one chorus, with no competing comics setting the house against itself. Make a quick exit and you can miss it altogether.

One of the production's reforms has long been a feature of Pettys pantos. It consists of making the romantic leads interesting. They, this year, are another pair of ex-Idols. Elena Juatcos Snow White is a capable, sensible heroine of the kind pioneered in Disney's Beauty and the Beast (the film, not the show). Jamie McKnight is endearingly helpless as Prince Willie Wanderoff, who keeps getting lost. He even looks lost, though his beloved doesn't, when the two of them break into a duet, but then he has some cause to be -- the words that come out are only loosely attached to the situation, even by pantomime standards.

Apart from doing good musical service, the group act, at least as well as is called for. (Of course, playing wannabe pop stars is something at which theyve had practice.) Eddie Glenn manages to be endearing as the Princes comic sidekick, despite being handed some ropy material. And then there's Petty. As ever, he plays the villain, who in this case has to be the wicked queen. But this is one place where tradition strikes back. The pantomime dame (middle- aged comedian in drag) has always been a sympathetic character, definitely on the side of the goodies, and combining the huggable with the hissable doesn't work. The updating, too, seems to have left Petty behind. Some of his humour is whiskery (do the kids really appreciate a reference to Seinfeld's Soup Nazi?), and though he throws himself into his glam-rock number with admirable ardour, I suspect that his heart, like mine, is still with the show tunes.
