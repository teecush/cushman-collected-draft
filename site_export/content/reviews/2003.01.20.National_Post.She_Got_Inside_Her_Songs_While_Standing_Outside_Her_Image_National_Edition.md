Peggy Lee

The Singles Collection (4 CDs)

EMI

Peggy Lee spent most of her best years recording for Capitol, and since EMI owns that catalogue, it's the products of that association that fill most of this box. There are also some token cuts from her fruitful intermediate period with Decca, and a couple from her apprenticeship as band-vocalist with Benny Goodman; the opening track, Lets Do It, reveals a Lee far more forthrightly and conventionally sexy than the veiled seductress were used to. Subtlety sets in on, of all things, The Atchison, Topeka and the Santa Fe, with Bob Crosby, and once she starts recording under her own name, the familiar Peggy with the subtle beat and the eggshell- finish voice -- at once soft, warm and unattainable -- arrives pretty much full-blown. But though she's consistent -- colonizing everything from pseudo-Latin novelties to '70s soft rock on her own unshakeable terms -- shes never monotonous; she thinks every song. For the first 10 years of her career, all her records were singles, so this can hardly be a complete accounting, but its an intelligent selection. The obvious things are here (Why Don't You Do Right, Manana, Fever, Is That All There Is) but so are some bewitching rarities: Alec Wilder's wistful Goodbye John and A Shade on the Blue Side, an otherwise unknown Hoagy Carmichael tune that allows her to be rapt and rhythmic at the same time. She gets inside her songs while standing outside her own image as a singer. Sinatra (of whom she's the nearest female equivalent) went for the dead-centre of the standard repertoire, but she weaves supply around its edges. The voice is misty, but the sensibility is made of steel.
