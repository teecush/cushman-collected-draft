Singapore runs until April 16. Box office: (416) 504-9971.

SINGAPORE

Factory Theatre, Toronto

I doubt that I could pronounce any verdict on Singapore more damning than the one the performers themselves seemed bent on handing down. On the first night they gabbled and mumbled and shouted and gesticulated and swallowed words and acted past one another; the bare-bones production doesn't have much furniture, but if it had they might well have completed the traditional litany of actors sins by bumping into it. They behaved, in short, as if they were deeply embarrassed to be there, and were taking all possible measures to prevent the word, as well as the words, from getting out.

Initially, their efforts are successful. For the first half-hour of John Palmers new comedy, directed by himself, it's impossible to work out what's going on. You can tell that everybody onstage is meant to be venal and lascivious, but what else is new? The fog then lifts somewhat for a couple of bearable, even enjoyable, scenes. In one of them, Sam, a withdrawn young man in his thirties, shares some moments with his father, Felix, an extrovert with Alzheimers, whose favoured apparel is shirt and underpants. They are sitting in Felixs basement, which is where he is kept confined by the woman who is about to become his second wife; and they are going through some decade-old phone bills on which Felix thinks he can get a refund. They also discuss Sam's unfulfilling love life, in which Felix takes a commendable fatherly interest, despite the fact that he's aggressively straight while his son is reclusively gay. This leads them to consult Felixs list of his old girlfriends, and even to plan an excursion to one of them; this plan, though, is nipped in the bud by the girlfriend upstairs, who rightly suspects Sam of trying to take his dad to a lawyer in order to frustrate her own plans to gain control of his property.

We then listen in while the fair schemer herself, whose name is Marjorie, conducts a rebarbative phone conversation with her mother, explaining what she hopes to gain from her approaching marriage, how fast she intends to get out of it, and why this doesnt make her a gold digger.

Tanja Jacobs as Marjorie plays the first part of this scene stretched out on top of a table, and the second crouched underneath it (I lied a little about the furniture); she is occasionally interrupted by her young lover, but most of the time she has the stage to herself, which, in a production so uncertainly timed and choreographed, is lucky for her. She works hard at being shamelessly funny, and succeeds; she is the nearest thing to a reason for seeing the play.

Neither Felix's old flames nor Marjorie's mother has any subsequent part in the action. The play tries to place self- contained comic routines within an accelerating farcical action, which is a tough act if youre not the Marx Brothers. Palmer seems to be attempting a black satiric comedy on the lines of the late Joe Orton, whose comic specialty was to have his characters say and do outrageous things with perfectly straight faces. He brought great verbal skills, and more erratic structural ones, to the elaboration of this basic joke, and made it the vehicle for a sustained comic vision: an outsiders view of a society built on lies.

Palmer may have the same vision, but he doesn't, on this showing, have the skills. Also Orton never dropped his guard, never suggested that, underneath it all, he had a heart. Palmer is apt to go sentimental on us, even bringing down his curtain on some additional bonding between father and son in the geriatric ward, with Felix dementedly recalling his own dad, whom he believes to be not dead but in Singapore (in case you were wondering about the title). Since the characters are Jewish, this seems like an abbreviated relay of a subject currently being treated at great length in The Offering. Orton was insouciant; you never caught him trying to shock. You catch Palmer at it all the time. He starts his play with Sam's elderly lover stepping naked into a bath and trying, ineffectually, to open a vein. This is to stop Sam from walking out on him. He fails, but spends the rest of the play in hot pursuit, lunging at any piece of male flesh within groping distance.

Theres probably a way of making this entertaining, but it doesnt happen here; you keep blotting out the character's existence and groaning when he reappears. Louis Negin plays the role with an enthusiastic grossness born, I sincerely hope, of desperation.

Also on hand is Felix's divorced first wife, who is by no means reconciled to her situation and says of her ex, "I cant forget him like I did the Armani sunglasses I lost on the train to Montreal."

You can tell the type she is. You can also tell what constitutes the scripts idea of biting epigram. If you have to start dropping brand-names to make satiric points, you've either lost it or you never had it to begin with. Dawn Greenhalgh rattles through this entire role as if she, too, had a train to catch, and I found her actions baffling. Aron Tager as Felix starts similarly flustered and befuddled, but quiets down and warms up. Sam, who spends most of the play wishing he were somewhere else, is played by Alon Nashman, who seems to know the feeling only too well.

Shaun Benson plays Marjorie's boyfriend, who is brought to realize the advantages of marketing his gigolo talents to both sexes, and the cast is completed by James Harkness as a biddable rabbi whom Marjorie imports to officiate at her wedding.

The set is most kindly described as functional; walls keep sliding apart to reveal a new scene, even more crabbed and anonymous than its predecessor. For the wedding, the grand farcical climax on which all the characters converge in pursuit of their goals, a staircase is added. That brightens things, though not for long; the staging of the physical comedy achieves prodigies of awkwardness seldom seen on a professional stage.

The trouble with plays that try to wow you with their bad taste is that, when they fail, all you're left with is the taste.
