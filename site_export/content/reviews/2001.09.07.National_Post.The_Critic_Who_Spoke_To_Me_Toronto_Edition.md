Robert Cushman is the National Post's theatre critic. His review of Saturday Night Fever appears on page B14. Obituary of Pauline Kael.

Pauline Kael was the first film critic I ever read -- and almost the last -- who seemed to be speaking to me.

By that, I dont just mean she had a button-holing colloquial style, though she obviously did. Most writers on film seemed to be operating from a realm of theory, to which I had no access. Some were purists who apparently admired only silent pictures; others revered European directors -- this was the heyday of Resnais and Antonioni -- whose films about the boredom of existence bored me as, I discovered to my delight, they did her.

None of them seemed to have anything to do with the experience, or at least with my experience, of enjoying a movie. None, for a start, seemed to have any interest in performance. They might occasionally analyze actors as icons, but they would never describe or evaluate what they actually did.

What immediately made me her fan for life was her demolition job on the film of West Side Story and her comparison of it to good musicals -- Singin in the Rain or anything with Fred Astaire.

Along with this went an exaltation of the best American "trash" (His Girl Friday, Duck Soup, Citizen Kane) over contemporary art films, along with an enthusiasm for the good new things that were managing to come out of Hollywood -- The Manchurian Candidate, Lolita, Ustinov's Billy Budd, eventually Bonnie and Clyde. Which didn't stop her slaughtering most other Hollywood movies, especially the piously well-intentioned ones, or liking Truffaut.

All this was before her tenure at The New Yorker, where she spawned a school of imitators who, she said, copied her style but not her opinions.

She was wrong there. They parroted her opinions as long as they knew what they were. When she wasn't around, you could see them hilariously hopping about, trying to second-guess what Pauline would have thought. You could never quite be sure because she was a merciless empiricist, too intellectual for show-business and too showbiz for the intellectuals: a very healthy if perilous position, and one shared by my other favourite critics, Kenneth Tynan and Walter Kerr.

She was always ready to lambast either the boobs or the snobs, and she could jump either way. Despite her breezy style, she was seldom funny. She is the only reviewer who has kept me reading compulsively without making me laugh. But you sensed that, beneath the unrelenting burrowing intelligence, there was an exasperated good humour. As in fact there was.
