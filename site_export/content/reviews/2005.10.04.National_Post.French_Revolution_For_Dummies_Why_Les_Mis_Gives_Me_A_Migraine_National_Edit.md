With the customary acknowledgements to Letterman: The Top Ten Reasons why Les Miserables is the worst musical ever written. Well, all right: the musical with the least forgivable ratio of commercial success to artistic bankruptcy.

1. I DREAMED A DREAM The name of the show's most prominent song is itself a case study in redundancy. What else would you do with a dream? Note that this show, which started life as a French song- cycle drawn from the Victor Hugo novel, was hammered on to the stage by the Royal Shakespeare Company, whom you would expect to have some sensitivity to the English language. They approved that lyric, the rest of which is as vapid and generalised as its title. They also approved the accompanying melody, which is reprised, with minute variations, whenever something emotional seems called for, and which must constitute about 50% of the score. Claude-Michel Schonberg, the composer, is no marvel of melodic fecundity.

2. MUSIC The tunes he does write are grindingly repetitive in themselves. They come in two flavours, moaning (the ballads) and bullying (the choruses). Neither is equipped to explore the characters or to forward the story; each song endlessly repeats things that we already know. This score, copyright 1985, sets musical dramaturgy back 40 years. This being a through-sung musical (meaning there's no release), the plot, of which there is much, has to be carried by those even whinier mini-melodies that think they're recitative. Any lyricist setting them is bound to end up writing doggerel.

3. LYRICS Not that that obstacle should let these writers off the hook. The English lyrics are by Herbert Kretzmer, a decent craftsman (as is just observable in Master of the House, the evening's one gesture towards wit), but one who seems to have no horizon beyond show business. A story that needs to be freshly imagined is told through stale images ("no song unsung/no wine untasted") that derive from memories of other shows. ("Additional material" is credited to James Fenton, a respected poet who was fired as original lyricist; apparently some of his work remains, but Fenton slumming is indistinguishable from Kretzmer soaring.)

4. CHARACTERS There aren't any. There's Jean Valjean, the stolidly saintly reformed convict who, in the immortal words of Forbidden Broadway, "significantly changes France by being an all- around nice guy." There's Javert, the policeman who hounds him through the decades (and eventually the drains) and whose implacability is matched only by his incompetence; the arm of coincidence being longer than that of the law, he keeps running into Valjean but never manages to nab him. Both men are monochrome. Youth is represented by an extraordinarily lifeless love-triangle.

5. ONE DAY MORE At the end of a very long first act, and in blatant imitation of the Tonight ensemble in West Side Story, all the cast sing about how they have things to do tomorrow. Don't we all?

6. REVOLUTION Much of Act Two is devoted to Parisians mounting the barricades; actually just one barricade, which seems unrealistic of them. This (Forbidden Broadway again) is "not the big, famous French Revolution but a little later, one you didn't think you knew anything about." Not, I suspect, that most of the audience does know, even when it's over. We are never told what the issues are; the only Establishment figure on view is Javert, so the people seem to be revolting against one malevolent cop. They might as well be the Parisian rabble of The Vagabond King, about to break into a rousing chorus of "to hell with Burgundy," except that this bunch wouldn't know any tunes that good. This is a show for people who like to drool over revolutions, especially doomed ones, without being bothered by anything as sordid as politics.

7. KIDS, ESPECIALLY THE ONE WHO'S THE LOGO That would be Cosette, the waif with the sunken eyes, who, as an orphan, sings a song that makes Oliver! seem like searing social realism. But then so does the whole show. Even more shamelessly, there is Gavroche, chirpy child who sings about little people and who waves a little banner while his elders wave a big one. Ain't that cute? His principal function is to wring our hearts by being slaughtered on the barricade. This isn't as stomach-turning as the use, in the same authors' Miss Saigon, of real footage of real orphans, but it's on the way.

8. STAGING This hurts. The show was directed -- 20 years ago but it's still their production -- by John Caird and Trevor Nunn, who were previously responsible for the RSC's Nicholas Nickleby, the peerless model of how to stage a novel while making a glowingly humane theatrical statement. Nunn, who has proved himself a great director a dozen times over, here gives us stereotyped groupings, a revolve that spins as if someone had lost control of the controls and no human content. The story is clearly told, but that's all.

9. Stars Somebody, for some reason, sings a song of that name, and the sky starts twinkling. This happens twice.

10. STUPIDITY See 1 to 9 above.

Notwithstanding clause: The touring version that's arrived in Toronto is in pretty good nick. Randal Keith is a ringing Valjean (his song Bring Him Home is, let's be fair, a cut above everything else in the score, both melodically and dramatically) and Norman Large is amusing as Thenardier the scoundrelly innkeeper, more Dickens than Hugo but that's a blessing. Most of the singing is excellent, though the composer's addiction to long, high endings does nasty things to the vowels. The sound-design (by Andrew Bruce) has always been first-class; you can hear every word, whether or not you want to.
