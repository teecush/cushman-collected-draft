There's nothing wrong with tampering with Shakespeare, but there is with doing it inefficiently, leaving loose ends from the original lying about for the cast and audience to trip over.

There are some lively things in Jeannette Lambermont's production of Twelfth Night. One is the life-sized portrait of Countess Olivia that hangs in the chamber of her admirer, Duke Orsino. It actually consists of the actress playing Olivia standing motionless behind a large frame. It has a double effect, since it also looks from the audience as if Olivia herself is gazing into a mirror for extended periods.

Since narcissism is a major concern of the play - what with Olivia indulging herself in an extended period of mourning for a dead brother, Orsino indulging himself in his hopeless infatuation with Olivia, and the steward Malvolio explicitly diagnosed as being "sick of self-love" - this seems very neat and very apt. I'm not at all sure, though, that it is intentional. The show has other things on its mind.

The production starts with a lot of prologue. The storm that lands the twins, Viola and Sebastian, on the coast of Illyria, is energetically mimed. Before we get even that far there are songs welcoming the audience to "our fairy tale," songs actually drawn from the introductory verses to Lewis Carroll's Alice books. When the play itself gets going, it seems to be partially set in Wonderland, filled as it is with outsized props such as Alice might have beheld after sampling one of the cakes marked "Eat Me." Olivia's portrait is one of them. A monstrous green bottle that follows Sir Toby Belch around looks capacious enough to quench even his thirst. The ring that Malvolio delivers to Viola is the size of a tambourine. Most entrancing, and most Wonderlandish, of all are the huge pink flowers that shelter the plotters who spy on Malvolio while he reads the letter that ensnares him; Maria, the scheme's prime mover, has a similar bloom growing out of her hat. Danny Lyne's set and costume designs are gorgeously sophisticated by the standards of most open-air productions, and admirable by any standards whatever.

The program contains a heap more Alice quotes - of the "you're mad, we're all mad" persuasion - and a synopsis that tells us that "Viola, with the help of Feste the conjurer, disguises herself as a young man." Here the synopsis, if its writer has read the play, is lying. In the play's text, Viola's helpmate is an anonymous sea captain, and that is how she addresses him - even in this production, where his lines have indeed been taken over by Feste. Textually, she doesn't encounter Feste - who is actually a jester, not a magician - until several scenes later. When she does, it seems odd, in view of their intense earlier relationship, that she doesn't recognize him; especially since this clown, with his white death's-head face, shaven skull, magnetic presence and haunting singing voice, is not easily forgettable. It's strange too that Feste, who hovers over every scene in this staging, still gets the twins confused like everybody else.

I probably sound hopelessly pedantic. My objection is not to tampering with Shakespeare, but to doing it inefficiently, leaving loose ends from the original lying around and then tripping over them. Twelfth Night is not The Comedy of Errors; its use of the mistaken-identity motif is sparing and carefully motivated. Nobody in it is mad; a couple of people are falsely suspected of madness, but the point of that is lost if you subscribe to half- baked ideas about everybody being crazy. And it is not a fairy tale. It is part romantic comedy, with passion and skepticism in perfect balance, and part social comedy, tart and class-conscious. Over it all hang the matters that fairy tales exclude: time and mortality. It is probably a tribute to the director's better instincts that by the end of the play her initial concept has evaporated.

That does leave us, though, dependent on individual moments, individual performances. Fortunately, there are several to cling to. Alex Fallis's Feste, described above, is one of them; Ellen-Ray Hennessy's Viola, a good sport with an engaging sense of wonder about her, is another. She too sings well (and Phyllis Cohen's music is exquisite). Lynne Cormack is an interestingly skittish Olivia; Patrick Galligan makes a devastating exhibition of Orsino's self-pity. Below stairs is dominated by Michael Hanrahan's Malvolio, the actor as relaxed as the character is uptight. Benedict Campbell's Sir Toby is conceived as an effete habitue of one of the less-particular London clubs: valid enough in theory, but not very resourceful in practice. Since he is playing in the same register as his dupe Sir Andrew (Steve Cumyn), their scenes together quickly grow monotonous.

There are also two varieties of cross-gender casting. The sea-dog Antonio is played by a woman as a man; and you would have to go to Penzance to find a less convincing pirate. The possibility of Antonio's devotion to Sebastian being homosexual is effectively neutralized; thus does one kind of liberation drive out another. More defensibly, the servant Fabian, a notoriously indeterminate character, is played by a woman as a woman; you do wonder, though, why Maria, grimly hanging on to her position as Toby's intended, isn't jealous of his new confidante; it's another example of the production not looking where it's going. Still, for pastoral Shakespeare, this is well above average, beguiling even before darkness falls to work a magic far more infallible than any conjuror's.
