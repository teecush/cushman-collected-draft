THEATRE REVIEW

PALACE OF THE END

Berkeley Street Theatre

---

Arrange the following three atrocities in order of their atrociousness: (1) An American female soldier at Abu Ghraib is photographed pointing at a pile of naked Iraqi prisoners arranged in a pyramid, and she and her male colleagues subject them to other forms of sexual humiliation; doubtless you've heard about it; (2) A gentle Baghdad bookseller, his wife and two daughters are murdered by American soldiers claiming to be looking for insurgents; the elder daughter, aged 13, is also raped; (3) A pregnant Iraqi woman, a communist (like everybody, she says) is interrogated by Saddam Hussein's torturers, who want to know the whereabouts of her husband; she is beaten and raped in front of her 15-year old son; when she refuses to talk, her other son, aged eight, is tied to a ceiling-fan and spun round and round at increasing speed; when this too proves ineffective, he is placed naked on the snowy roof of the building where his mother can hear him coughing until he dies.

It's a tough call between (2) and (3), but I'd vote for (3). That also seems to be the opinion of Judith Thompson, who has written a monologue around each event and has arranged them in the order cited. The overall title, Palace of the End, derives from the Baghdad torture-fortress that is the setting for the last story. That narrative is as harrowing as anything I have ever heard in a theatre, though if the evening as a whole is intended to be anti- war (or anti the U.S. war in Iraq; everyone's anti-war) then it backfires. The emotional effect is to make you feel that anything-- even the cruel mess the Americans have made-- would be preferable to leaving the country in Saddam's power.

The effectiveness of the three individual plays is not in direct proportion to their horror-quotient. In fact, it's the reverse. This is accentuated in David Storch's production, which boasts, in order of appearance, one superb performance, one that is fine, and one that is passable.

The first benefits enormously from being the only one told by a perpetrator rather than a victim. This immediately adds complexity. The woman identified in the program as "An American Soldier" is of course Private Lynndie England, who took the fall, along with her boyfriend, for the Abu Ghraib abuses, and can actually be seen as a kind of victim herself. In fact, that's how she sees herself, though not for the OK political reasons. She's incensed at the messages about herself that she finds posted on the internet, especially the ones that describe her as ugly white trash. ("I think if I was an actor, I would have to call that a bad review.") Hell, she'll have us know, she was never ugly, and even back home in the South there were girls worse-looking than her. She's very defensive, though. It's plain that being posted as a prison guard made her feel more desirable than she'd ever felt in West Virginia. She felt she had to prove herself to her male buddies, and she could only see the prisoners as means to that end. At home she'd needed someone (specifically, an unfortunate crippled girl) to feel superior to, and to join in humiliating. Thompson is brilliantly successful in getting inside her character's mind, understanding her without excusing her. So is her actress, Maev Beaty, playing here triumphantly against type: She's fleshy, querulous, allowing us to connect the dots that the woman herself is hardly aware exist.

The second play features "A British Microbiologist and Weapons Inspector." He's Dr. David Kelly (unlike Lynddie, he gives us his name) who committed suicide after outing himself as having "sexed up" the Weapons of Mass Destruction report for the benefit of Tony Blair. The bookstore victims were his friends, and -- at least in Thompson's telling--it was learning of their fate that turned his mind around; he passed from thinking the invasion justified, and therefore worth a little falsehood, to killing himself for having helped make it possible. We meet him just before the moment of death in the peaceful English countryside. Julian Richings plays him very quietly and very sympathetically, but the play is out of focus; unlike its predecessor, it can't make the narrator as compelling as the horrors he narrates.

This brings us to "An Iraqi Mother," who also has a real-life analogue, though one likely to be unknown to
most: Nehrjas Al Saffarh, who survived her torture under Saddam only to be inadvertently killed by the U.S. (Her home was bombed in the first Gulf War.) Arsinee Khanjian is bland and monotonous when setting her character up for us, but develops considerable intensity when describing her actual ordeal. She has the misfortune of being handed the play's most pretentious
element: a mystical dream of future peace and reconciliation that hardly measures up to the hideous realities that have gone before. The ending also suffers from a forced attempt by author and director to draw all three characters together; the first two are mute audience to the third though their reactions remain inscrutable. Having them all talk about their respective journeys "through the looking-glass" doesn't do much for artistic unity either. These are three shards, wrenching ones, from the tragedy of Iraq. There is no way that they, or any other three, can add up a to a full picture. - Palace of the End runs until Feb. 23. For tickets, call 416-368-3110 or visit canstage.com.
