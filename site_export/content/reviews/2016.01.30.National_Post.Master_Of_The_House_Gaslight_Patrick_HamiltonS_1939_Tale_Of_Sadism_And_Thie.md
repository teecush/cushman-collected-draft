Gaslight, by the English playwright and novelist Patrick Hamilton, is a 1939 thriller with a split personality: half fresh, half stale. Appropriately enough, it's been filmed twice; MGM, who made the second and more famous version, the one with Ingrid Bergman and Charles Boyer, apparently did their damnedest to have the earlier, British movie destroyed. They weren't successful, though their film was. It might be claimed that in their attempted ruthlessness, the Hollywood guys were merely mimicking the behaviour of their principal character.

This is a Mr. Manningham who lives with his wife in a big and luxurious house in late Victorian London; the play was a period piece, and a very effective one, even when it was new. Mr. Manningham, a smooth tyrant, is waging psychological war on Mrs. Manningham, who is timid and desperate to please. Mrs. M's mother died in an insane asylum, a fact of which Mr. M never tires of reminding her. He keeps accusing his wife of losing things, things of sentimental and practical value; in fact, he's removing them himself and hiding them. He varies the cruelty with tantalizing, not to say patronizing, shows of kindness; he promises to take her to the theatre, then withdraws the offer on the pretext that she has slipped back into her old careless ways. He further increases the pressure by varying the intensity of the gas-lamps that illuminate their home; the lady never knows when she will be plunged into semi-darkness. And, this being 19th-century London, it's foggy out.

It's perfectly possible, for most of the play's first act, to believe that the master of the house is motivated by an innate sadism, reinforced by traditional ideas of male superiority. Taken that way, the play works disturbingly well as a study of a respectable cruelty that its victim has been conditioned to accept. It turns out, however, that the gentleman has other motivations and that they are, by detective story standards, disappointingly commonplace. When he steps out, as it seems he does every evening, another man steps in. This is Inspector Rough, a retired policeman whose mind is still working on an unsolved case and who has found the clues to it in this very house. It involves that staple of stately home melodrama, missing jewellery of inestimable value. As a storyline it's perfunctory, though it takes the inspector a long time to expound it. He does succeed, though, in restoring the morale of Mrs. Manningham, who needs it.

The good in the play outweighs the bad, and the current Toronto production, which comes to us from England, is mostly excellent. Flora Montgomery gives an especially fine performance, quivering with life, as a woman delivered from an all-too-believable state of terror. We feel for her in her bewildered attempts to make sense of a senseless situation, and we root for her to escape. In a way she's a case of Stockholm Syndrome; she hasn't been kidnapped or abducted, exactly, but she has been separated from her family and she clings to her tormentor as if he were her protector. Mrs. Manningham may be trapped, but she has the theatrical advantage of being an original creation, struggling in her chains, unpredictable in her reactions. Mr. Manningham may be her jailer but he's the play's prisoner: the period's too. Owen Teale never actually twirls his moustache but he can't quite escape the stereotype of the gentleman-cad. He fills it out though with grim, sardonic presence, underplaying whenever possible; in his early sallies he might almost persuade you, as he does her, that he's acting from principle. He's snarlingly good at the end when the mask is torn off, when the gentleman vanishes and the cad remains, still trusting in his own charm. Ian McElhinney plays the oddly named Rough, who's actually rather gentle, as devoted to comforting the afflicted as to afflicting the comfortable, indeed in this performance rather more so; he lays on the whimsical charm excessively thick, playing a "character" rather than the character, and slowing the play down in the process. Both these actors are Game of Thrones regulars, and if the production is counting commercially on attracting that show's fans, then it made a good bet; on the third night, the house was packed.

The Manningham menage runs to a cook, unseen, and two maids, contrasted and highly visible, both admirably played. Victoria Lennox is the sympathetic older one, definitely in her mistress's corner; Emily Head is the flighty, flirty younger one, very much in her master's, or rather trying her best to lure him into hers. (He certainly has no objection; sexual humiliation, all but overt, is another of the crosses Mrs. Manningham has to bear.) David Woodhead has designed a handsomely menacing drawing-room set that manages to be spacious and claustrophobic at the same time. The production doesn't always sound as good as it looks; the actors are unreliably and unnecessarily miked, with distortion at the top end; when Teale, cornered, tried for a shout, all that came out the other end was a hollow boom. The director, David Gilmore, also reveals a taste for corny underscoring, as if he didn't trust the play and the actors to scare us on their own. Worst of all, he destroys the play's signature effect by accompanying the dimming of the gas with a chamber-of-horrors soundscore; the sound drowns out the sight. For compensation, though, there's Montgomery's handling of her last scene, in which the former worm turns. I have a hunch, completely unverifiable, that the author originally meant the play to end more drastically than it actually does, and that either he or his original producers got cold feet. As it is, the spectre of an alternative finale haunts the stage, the actress teasing both her stage partner and the audience, all of us squirming on tenterhooks.
