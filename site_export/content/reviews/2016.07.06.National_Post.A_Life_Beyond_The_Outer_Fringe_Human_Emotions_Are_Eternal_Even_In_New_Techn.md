THEATRE REVIEW

Cam Baby Life After Toronto Fringe Festival

Two productions arrive on this year's Toronto Fringe Festival as winners of the festival's awards for Best Play and Best Musical. Giving prizes to shows before they have even opened is a new phenomenon. It turns out, though, that both pieces deserve their honours.

The play is Jessica Moss's Cam Baby, which begins as another comedy about the Internet. Clara and her boyfriend Tim, neither of them glamorous, are indulging in their favourite pastimes of texting, viewing and recording themselves. They don't know that Andrew, Clara's ineffectual landlord, and Matabang, his controlling self-described friend, have installed cameras in the house and are making videos of the couple, naturally including their most intimate moments.

They've made a small business out of it; they share their products with a select circle, and some of them go less selectively viral. Clara, who is overweight, is a great hit, or gets a lot of hits, on Ugly Porn. One of her admirers turns up in person, which leads to Clara finding out and being, naturally, very hurt and very angry.

Moss first burst on the scene as a writer-performer, doing hilarious monologues about, what else, relationships. It seems that she can write just as wittily and perceptively for other performers.

Her earlier scenes strike funny sparks off people's relationships to their toys; Matabang

in particular would likely die without his. But she goes on to cut deeper. For one thing she observes the Chekhovian rule that you should never bring a revolver on stage unless you intend to use it, though in her case the weapon is a hammer.

Most importantly, she lets nobody off the hook. Clara and Tim are sympathetic, especially Clara, but they're also complicit. They're victims of a culture in which they themselves partake. And it isn't something that just happened with the Internet. We're dealing with human qualities - cruelty, greed, loneliness, exhibitionism, indifference

- that may have found new technological outlets but are really eternal. Charlotte Gowdy directs an excellent cast. Clara is played by Ashley Botting, an ornament of Second City who is expectedly amusing here, but is also both touching and scathing. The others are Andrew Cameron and Brandon Coffey as two varieties of helpless; Christine Horne, the best known of the actors but playing the least rewarding role, one that's mainly plot-mechanical; Beau Dixon who doesn't so much forward the action as explode it; and Karl Ang, who conveys Matabang's self-admiring smoothness but delivers it too hard and fast for clarity. Actually too many words get lost throughout; casualties, yet again, of a hollow echoing stage.

The same problem bedevils the musical Life After, probably through no fault of Brandon Kleiman who designed both shows and is obviously a good-luck charm. The words here are especially precious, since Britta Johnson's lyrics are amazing; she knows what a rhyme is and how it can sharpen both feeling and meaning.

She also wrote the book, which is minimal, and the music. This doesn't quite make her Lin-Manuel Miranda, as she doesn't act in the show, but she does musically direct the music. And by playing piano, she's also half the orchestra; the other half is Kevin Wong on, of all things, viola. The only fault to be found there is that the accompaniment sometimes drowns the singers; voices without microphones are a blessing, but it must be confessed, a mixed one.

The life of the title is that of Alice Carter, beautifully played by Anika Johnson, the composer's sister, and what it comes after is the death of her father, killed in a car accident when he should have been attending Alice's sixteenth birthday. We watch

her coming to terms with the loss and with the suspicious circumstances surrounding it; though it's a mark of the show's honesty that what seems to be the big reveal turns out to be something that can be lived with.

Father himself is a ghostly or flashback presence, sung by Tim Funnell, appropriately haunting. He is, or was, a motivational speaker, though the show itself is refreshingly free of fake uplift. Alana Hibbert gorgeously sings Alice's mother, whose survival strategy is to re-decorate; Kate Blackburn is her tactlessly cheerful friend, and Janet Gigliotti plays her teacher whose friendship carries an unintended sting; and Kaylee Harwood is excellent as her older sister, wondering how much to tell her. A fine cast all around, under a director, Robert McQueen, who takes full advantage of the score's best feature: its capacity to make every song a piece of the action, physical or emotional or both.

That's a Sondheim quality, and we're in his territory in the musicalization of group scenes and in the probing of contradictory feelings. Musically we're more in the slightly later world, plangent and nervous, of Next to Normal or Fun Home, though I much preferred this show to either of those. It's more intelligent, less portentous. Its last scene had me tearing up.

These two shows must have a life beyond the fringe; ideally in more forgiving surroundings, though there really is very little to have to forgive.

The Toronto Fringe Festival runs until July 10. Cam Baby is at the Factory Theatre; Life After is at Theatre Passe Muraille. Check schedules for dates and times.
