THE BUNDLE

Equity Showcase Performing Space, Toronto

Edward Bond's The Bundle, first seen in London 20 years ago, was described at the time as having "the force and the onward movement of a tidal wave." That's a good phrase, if I do say it myself; and I did say it myself, when reviewing the first production. My words, quoted in the publicity for this new Toronto staging, have come back to haunt me.

I stand by half of them, the half about the movement. The Bundle is a fable play, whose narrative extends over many years and many episodes, and Bond paces himself brilliantly. Every scene is clear, every scene has weight; and Bond's carved, tempered prose -- always cool, sometimes startlingly witty -- is a perfect medium, often imitated, never equalled.

The story begins with Basho, Japanese poet of the 17th century and master of the haiku, leaving his village to seek "enlightenment." Refusing to pay his ferryman ("I go to be reborn. Does the midwife charge the child?"), he spies an actual baby lying on the riverbank, abandoned by its poverty-stricken parents, but refuses to rescue it and risk spoiling his quest. After a token display of reluctance, the ferryman -- who calls himself a bad man but is regarded by everybody else as a saint -- takes the child in, and he and his wife raise it as -- since it's male -- their own son. They call him Wang.

Later on, the boy -- desperately crying, "Buy me, buy me" -- sells himself into nine years' servitude in order to protect his parents. Most of this time he spends in the house of the poet, who's now a judge. Thus educated, Wang joins a band of outlaws whom he turns into guerrillas. Their purpose is to expel the local landowner and to reform the economy, this by taming the river whose capricious floodings have hitherto kept the population at the authorities' mercy. They succeed; and at the end of the play everybody -- except Basho, who is still moaning about enlightenment -- seems dreamily happy, everybody left alive, that is.

Yes, there is a price to be paid. Bond dramatizes it by having Wang, like Basho, abandon a baby by the river just before taking flight. Basho is to be blamed for his action because his motives are selfish; Wang is to be endorsed because he's off to start a revolution.

Bond, in his voluminous commentary on his own plays, says that his purpose "is to demystify the use of moral argument." In other words, he's saying that the end justifies the means, which is something we shouldn't have believed even 20 years ago; writers as unimpeachably anti-capitalist as George Orwell and Albert Camus had exposed it halfway through the century. Anyway, Bond doesn't have the theatrical courage of his convictions. That baby on the bank is no test for an audience; it truly is a bundle, a prop made out of rags.

There are flesh-and-blood people who get killed in the play -- Wang's parents and one of his comrades -- but that is all the fault of the establishment that is chillingly, and very convincingly, nasty. Wang's own revolutionary hands are left amazingly clean. The play cheats.

I had the same intellectual doubts about the play's force when I first saw it, but at least the force was theatrically there, projected in a first-rate production by the Royal Shakespeare Company in one of its golden ages. Equity Showcase can hardly compete. Their new acting space, in the bowels of a church, is wide and bare and acoustically tough. The director, Dean Gabourie, puts his actors in monkish cowls and has them lie down end-to-end to simulate the river, which is striking, and he has a mobile raft, which is useful. He also has a habit of staging long, important scenes at the furthest reaches of the stage, which is infuriating. The brigand scenes are a dead loss, performed in an incomprehensible mumble, though the fault here is partly the play's; my admiration for Bond's style goes into abeyance when he gives Wang's merry men pidgin English to speak.

The company is large and unpaid and mostly inexperienced, which might seem right but isn't. The characters, in the style of a Brechtian or indeed a mediaeval morality play, have one dimension apiece but that is not a licence for monotony; if the psychological canvas is limited it needs to be all the more vigorously coloured.

Jovanni Sy's Wang has some youthful appeal but little ability at communicating thought processes. Christopher Kelk as Basho and Ed Fielding as the ferryman establish their basic qualities securely enough -- smugness and innocence respectively -- but can't put them to much subsequent use. There are some lively performances in small parts: from Richard Alan Campbell, very fluent as a smooth merchant; from Adrian Griffin as a secret policeman who twirls a sinister fan, and from Quyen Lee as a sly and desperate water-peddler.

Bond's group scenes of fear and mendacity are excellent; he may seem fuzzy about what society should be but he is mercilessly accurate on how it is.
