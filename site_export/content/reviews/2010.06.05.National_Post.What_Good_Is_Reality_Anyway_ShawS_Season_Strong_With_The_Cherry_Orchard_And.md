The two best productions in an impressive opening week at the Shaw Festival are of Chekhov's The Cherry Orchard and Mary Chase's Harvey, both of them domestic comedies, staffed by obsessives. They are also both about the urge to escape reality, an impulse that, in defiance of their cultures' respective reputations for dreaminess and practicality, the Russian writer deplores and the American enthusiastically endorses.

The Cherry Orchard is directed by Jason Byrne, the Irishman celebrated in Toronto for his productions of Festen and A Whistle in the Dark; his compatriot Tom Murphy, author of the former play, provided the easy-flowing adaptation of this one. Like those other shows, this one unrolls freely but compulsively. It starts with a perfect evocation of the half-awake early morning as Lopakhin, the peasant turned profiteer, and Dunyasha, the sentimental maid, await the return from Paris of Madame Ranyevskaya and her abbreviated retinue. The second-act prelude is similarly evocative: The domestics' criss-cross patterns of loneliness and flirtation, at once aimless and desperate, are as compellingly executed as any I recall. Much the same is true of the mopped-brow partying that starts the third act, and of the hurried, empty packing-up that starts the last one. The final dying fall, Al Kozlik's superannuated Firs, alone and forgotten in the deserted house, is equally haunting, equally precise and unstressed.

Within these multiple frames is a production that seems to have no specific agenda except to render every moment as truthful and spontaneous as possible. It doesn't beg us to laugh or to grieve at its characters; their saving grace as people is that they can do those things for themselves, often simultaneously. Laurie Paton's Ranyevskaya is a mix of soft-hearted and hard-headed; she knows what she is doing to herself, and to those around her. The only thing I can fault her for is an apparent absence of anxiety as she waits for news of the auction of her estate; she may not care as much as she claims about the fate of the cherry orchard, but she should care something. Jim Mezon may never have done anything better than his gravely ridiculous Gayev, the self-proclaimed "man of the '80s" trying to find himself a place in a new and hostile world. His ode to the family bookcase is exquisite, though delivered from a corner that, from my seat at the side of the house, rendered him almost invisible. However, Byrne doesn't believe in set blocking, so at other performances he may well declaim it from centre stage. The tearful farewells of this brother and sister, consumed with too-lateness, will be deeply affecting, wherever they play it. Benedict Campbell is extraordinary in Lopakhin's falling-down-drunk account of how he bought the orchard, pride and shame erupting from him in alternating violent spasms; it's the culmination of the love and resentment that he feels towards his exasperating genteel neighbours. Gord Rand's emaciated Trofimov, the perpetual student and apprentice revolutionary, is his perfect foil and perfect goad; a mellower sequel to these actors' recent confrontation as Hamlet and Claudius. Severn Thompson does fine pent-up emotion as Varya, Neil Barclay is irresistibly shameless as the Micawberish Pishchik and the servants, as I've hinted, are excellent. Julie Martell's Dunyasha is the most finished among them, but Mark Uhre gets at the springs behind the valet's callousness, and Gabrielle Jones is strikingly bold as the circus-bred German governess. She is hardly the play's central character but, with her conjuring tricks and her fear of destitution, she may be its emblematic one.

Harvey, the 1944 hit, emerges here as a good play, suspenseful and hilarious, not just a star-encrusted legend. Elwood P. Dowd, Denver's most lovable drunk, calls his invisible six-foot rabbit companion Harvey because he'd never known anyone of the name and therefore "had such high hopes for it." James Stewart, re-creating his Elwood in London, sounded depths of hopeful melancholy with that line that Peter Krantz at Niagara cannot reach. Stewart, though, never succeeded in making me see Harvey for myself, while Krantz, in Joseph Ziegler's lovely production, succeeds completely. Elwood is a one-note role; Krantz hits his note--sweet undentable wistfulness -- exactly, and sustains it impeccably. The real motor here, though, is Mary Haney as Elwood's sister, driven to her own kind of distraction -- some farcical, some dead serious -- by her brother and by the psychiatrists to whom she commits him. Just the way she says "festooned" in an opening phone conversation brings the house down; she also has it on tenterhooks at the end as she battles herself on whether to do the right thing. Also sublime are a brace of shrinks: Norman Browning's senior clinician, haughty and haunted; and Gray Powell, a precision instrument of an actor, as his unluckily confident junior. Diana Donnelly is pleasing as an adoring nurse. This is a huggable production that never quite lets you forget that this (like Blithe Spirit) is one of those wartime plays that Never Mentions the War but is still soaked in it. Both these Shaw shows have fine sets. Peter Hartwell's for the Chekhov is spare and suggestive; Sue LePage supplies for the Chase one of those comfy American drawing rooms you would swear the festival keeps in storage, except that this one can turn quite spectacularly into the shapeliest of sanatoria.
