HELLO ... HELLO

Tarragon Theatre, Toronto

This unbearable musical is set in the future, and the future is in polka dots. They are sprinkled remorselessly across the back of John Thompson's set, which is otherwise shiny and silvery, like a nightclub trying desperately to happen. It could give you a headache, but then so could the show. The design never tells us where, in what the script calls "a megalopolis," we are supposed to be but, in terms of glitz and emptiness, the material and its staging make a perfect match.

Hello ... Hello seems, rather confusingly, to have been conceived as a satire on glitz and emptiness, maybe even as a lament. Its moving spirit -- as author, lyricist, performer and (for want of a better term) assistant composer -- is Karen Hines, who may be best known for a series of solo clown shows, about a young woman called Pochsy; the latest, Citizen Pochsy, has yet to reach Toronto. Pochsy's specialty is the bland acceptance of the intolerable; she dispenses coyness laced with doom. Hines' persona here is very similar, and it proves unexpectedly more monotonous when she's teamed with other players than when she's at it on her own.

She plays Cassandra, who works in a boutique called the Abyss. (After the Gap, there was nowhere to go but down.) Visiting a graveyard to mourn her late husband, she meets Ben, who is paying similar devotion to his deceased girl-friend. With all this in common, plus a shared tendency to sing flatly, they fall in lust, and even in love. In the second act they have a child. Cassandra learns of her impending motherhood through some kind of pregnancy- test involving a dead rabbit. I couldn't follow the details, which are buried somewhere in the most protracted musical number of what comes to seem like an endless series. I can testify, though, that in this song the author attempts to rhyme "suspects her" with "trimester." We have all been so beaten down by lyrical incompetence that these days we hardly bother to wince when mere monosyllables are mis- mated. But failed attempts at trick-rhymes are intolerable.

Ben is very high up in a company that manufactures expensive baubles for the delectation of people who have been persuaded they need them, and it's just as well he has the money and position he does. In this society, medical care is only available to people who are very rich or who appear on television. It is also a society that has abandoned all the things that are pleasant without being profitable. Their absence is marked in a recurring litany -- "I remember deer," "I remember bananas," "I remember artists" -- spoken by characters who may indeed remember but can only go through the motions of regret without the comprehension. We are being presented, in fact, with Our Own World If We Don't Watch Out. Unfortunately the satire is more joyless and sterile than anything, now or to come, that it could conceivably be satirizing.

Cassandra, whose ex was an artist and who may have shared his fatal sensitivity, withers away in this climate, but has nothing to wither away from; she mopes, in a high-pitched monotone, from first to last. Her moments of joy are especially alarming, flashing artificial brightness like the teeth in a dentifrice ad. (This may well be intentional; that doesn't make it good.)

Ben, played by Peter Oldring with a rampant quiff and a modicum of boyish charm, apparently survives better because he feels less.

All other roles are played by Aurora Browne and Steven Morel, the latter a veteran, in one capacity or another, of most of the better small musicals of the last few years. In fact, though, there aren't many other roles; these two actors mainly function as a chorus, filling in every inch of the narrative before, or sometimes even while, it happens. Alienation was surely meant for bigger things than this. They also deliver a lengthy prose-poesy prologue, so ornate as to be impenetrable. I could hardly wait for it to stop, and the first song to begin. Then the first song began, and hope died.

The melodies, composed by Greg Morrison ("with Karen Hines" says the program), fall like water on the brain, though the synthesized harmonies -- Morrison plays the entire score solo on an electric keyboard -- supply some astringency that helps a bit. Chris Earle's production hits, I imagine, precisely the note of glossy self- satisfaction for which he and his collaborators were aiming.
