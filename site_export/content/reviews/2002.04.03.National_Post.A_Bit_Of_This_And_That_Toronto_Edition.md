IN ON IT

Buddies in Bad Times Theatre, Toronto

Daniel MacIvors In On It is another play about the impossibility of writing plays. This is the strategy. You write some crummy naturalistic scenes about the fallout from a disintegrating marriage, then acknowledge their crumminess in the hope this will prove disarming. You surround them with marginally less crummy scenes about the interaction of the two people who are presenting the first lot, then trust they will look good by comparison.

In On It is less arid, or at any rate less long, than such previous MacIvor works as The Soldier Dreams and You Are Here; and it does have its pleasures. One of them is a brief moment of mistaken identity that catches up actors and audience together in a blissful mental double take. Another is an opening speech, delivered by the author himself, tracing the external steps that can lead ineluctably to a fatal car accident.

MacIvor, who is also the director, is half the cast; he plays This One, the author of the play-within-the-play. Sharing the stage with him is That One, played by Darren ODonnell, another actor- playwright-director who here sticks to acting. The onstage relationship therefore mirrors the offstage one.

This One and That One are also lovers, though their relationship seems at the point of dissolution. They alternate roles in This One's play, about a man getting a fatal diagnosis and being left by his wife on the same day. That One objects that all This One's female characters seem to be drunks. Thats the least of the problems.

The stricken hero is given scenes with his grown-up son, who doesnt want to hear about his father's illness, and with his father, who is senile and doesn't recognize him. Later he meets the former partner of his wifes new lover and her young son. Its funny when the protagonist turns on his doctor, saying the resentful things we would all like to but never do. Other sections are less amusingly implausible. People can certainly be selfish and they can be shallow, but the behaviour here is shallowly shallow. I have been in some of the extreme situations presented here and I recognized no truth in the depiction of any of them. Perhaps the writing is meant to be bad, but that is no consolation when you're listening to it.

It's not as if all the soap-opera episodes illuminate, or are illuminated by, the predicament of the two presenters, unless you believe that one troubled menage is the same as any other. We learn how the guys met, and thats faintly amusing. This One has a thing about opera, which That One dismisses as cultural hypocrisy. Thats all. Otherwise, all we get are winks at the audience, as when the twosome hold up the poster of the show we are watching and start critiquing it. Or when we are told how people love a full-circle ending, and are duly given one.

Why bother? We all know theatre isn't real. We all know it can only tell a partial truth, so MacIvor is puncturing illusions that nobody holds anyway. Art about art doubtless has its place, but it's a small one. If plays cant be about anything else, then we may as well stop writing plays. The only immediate implication to be gleaned from this one -- or from This One -- is that homosexual authors have no business writing about heterosexual characters. I dont believe in this kind of ghetto aesthetics, and I doubt MacIvor does either. Certainly history is against it.

MacIvor's direction is fine, on a wide, bare, flat stage that concentrates the mind as wonderfully as, and more cheerfully than, the proverbial death-sentence. His acting, except when he is soliloquizing, is self-effacing to a fault, but O'Donnell, a fine figure of an actor, compensates in the focus and energy departments. The two do a delightfully daffy dance to the Lesley Gore record of Sunshine, Lollipops and Rainbows that recalls the sign-off of a Wayne and Shuster special.

I'm all in favour of plays that send you out humming. On the other hand, the same song was used to greater comic effect in the Thelma-and-Louise episode of The Simpsons. It makes you wonder why professedly experimental theatre should automatically get more respect than commercial cartoon television. But maybe it's unfair to invoke genius.
