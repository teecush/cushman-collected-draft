THE KABBALISTIC PSYCHOANALYSIS OF ADAM R. TZADDIK

Theatre Passe Muraille Backspace

Adam R. Tzaddik would not call himself an observant Jew. But he is a studious one. For four years he has lived hermit-like in his apartment (what he has lived on is not revealed) immersed in the Zohar, an ancient Hebrew text that purports to explain that most insoluble of riddles, how God created himself.

However intellectually stimulating this may have been for Adam, he has not found it spiritually fulfilling. He claims, indeed, to feel deeply empty, if that isn't an oxymoron, or even if it is. So he signs up for therapy. In The Kabbalistic Psychoanalysis of Adam R. Tzaddik, the playwright Anton Piatigorsky presents us with 60 minutes worth of excerpts from Adams confrontations with his shrink.

Almost the first thing Adam says to his unnamed Doctor is that, since all psychiatrists are necessarily atheists, his must really despise him for his religious leanings. The Doctor doesn't exactly answer that (he doesn't exactly answer anything) but it does become clear that he sees Adams belief system as a challenge to his own.

In the plays most resonant moment, he proclaims works such as the Zohar to be what the world had to make do with before it discovered psychiatry: The author of the Zohar was actually analyzing himself, and Adam, in quoting the book, is effectively becoming its author. This equivalence is made easier by the fact that the author (I rely here wholly, of course, on what the dramatist tells me) is riddled with male-female, father-mother symbols. Pretty soon, this being a short play, Adam is dredging up his own most agonizing memories which have to do with incest, real or imagined.

The trouble with this kind of thing as drama is that, however painful the analytic routine may be for the doctor and the patient, it always looks easy for the playwright.

He is telling us things about his characters that he knew all along, so why not just tell us in the first place? Theoretically, the process of discovery has its own suspense, but in this foreshortened form it seldom works out that way. Reviewers here and abroad (the play has been done in New York and Montreal, as well as previously in Toronto) have compared this piece to Peter Shaffer's Equus but that play, with all its imperfections on its hoof, succeeded by putting the therapist at the centre of the play and letting us see what it meant to him. It was glib, but it was still affecting.

And it is also unfortunate for Piatigorsky's play that it arrives in the immediate wake of Terry Johnsons Hysteria, which makes far more moving and entertaining use of the psychiatric process. I couldnt care much about either Adam or the Doctor, and what we learn about them is much less interesting than what we always seem on the verge of learning about the Zohar; I put it that way because in performance the information flashes by too fast to be absorbed. And just in case we are about to follow it, the production drowns out the text at the end of scenes with appropriately anguished neo- Hebraic music, composed by John Zorn.

Chris Abraham's direction is accomplished, in a flashy high- style manner. The clinical relationship of desk and couch is liable to cause serious sight-line problems, so both characters here are in chairs. They do not however, face one another; the doctor sits behind Adam but at an angle to him, so they look like a detached pair of airline passengers. (Business-class; they have plenty of leg- room.) Each is bathed in a pool of light, a device of which Abraham - - as demonstrated at length in his Montreal production of The Possibilities -- is extremely fond. It's a striking effect, but the note it strikes is never varied. You long, however unreasonably, for someone to get up, and/or for the participants to look one another in the face.

The two sedentary performances are excellent, negotiating an extremely tricky script with great intelligence and unerring precision. Paul Fauteux, looking and sounding like a buttoned-up Albert Schultz, has all the scholarly shame, the defensive pride, of Adam. Terrence Bryant is a silkily impenetrable Doctor, professionally ruthless about winding up the session -- and indeed the play -- on time. He may, though, be more unfeeling than necessary, making a show of concern and implicitly acknowledging his own hangups, but still emerging as insufferably knowing.

That game-playing tone pervades the entire enterprise. Whatever the private feelings of those concerned, they come over all superior.
