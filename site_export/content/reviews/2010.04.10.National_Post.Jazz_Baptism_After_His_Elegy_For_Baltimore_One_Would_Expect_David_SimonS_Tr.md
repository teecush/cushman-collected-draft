First thing you see on Treme is a title, on a black screen, that says "three months after." David Simon's new series is set in New Orleans, so there's no need to ask "three months after what?" The show is about people getting their lives and their city together in the wake of Hurricane Katrina, and in the first episode they seem, for the most part, to be making a good and hopeful job of it. The episode ends with a funeral procession -- not for anyone we know--and, as we've always been told about such events, it's a joyous, jumping occasion: also a businesslike one, as many of the show's characters are musicians. You end up thinking that if Simon's The Wire, set in Baltimore, was an elegy for a city, Treme is a celebration.

That, though, is just Episode 1. The next two instalments reveal that things aren't that simple. Nor were they, indeed, on The Wire, at least some of whose characters were survivors. A difference between the two shows is that on The Wire, hope, however often dashed, was the prerogative of the young. Here it stretches across the generations, largely buoyed by the music. We've all heard of the French Quarter of New Orleans, but the Treme is a different district, shabbier and less celebrated but claimed as the true birthplace of jazz. Simon and Eric Overmeyer, his chief collaborator as writer and executive producer, planned it in part as a tribute to the music; I suspect that by the time the season is over it will have done more for jazz, and made better dramatic use of it, than anything in the history of television. As in The Wire, and indeed in all the best HBO shows, the music is come by honestly. It's always there as part of the story. There's no manipulative underscoring. The first tumultuous scene shows musicians assembling for a parade, one trombonist short. Then the missing man arrives: Antoine Batiste, bulky and broke, haggling with the taxi driver who brought him. This becomes a running gag; Antoine takes three cabs in the course of the hour, and he's always arguing about being overcharged. Wendell Pierce plays him with the same world-weary ebullience he brought to Bunk in The Wire, though his heartbreak look, when deprived of both his 'bone and his livelihood, is something that the earlier show never required of him.

Antoine takes his place in the second-line parade, playing, marching, chanting with the others "I feel like funkin' it up." The sequence is superbly staged and shot by the episode's director, Agnieszka Holland. It's defiance, recognized as such by a bespectacled, and currently naked, young man watching from a window. "It is," he says, "the first second-line since the storm." He is Davis (Steve Zahn), the kind of white boy who's more deeply, or at least more selfconsciously, into black culture than most blacks. A manic, self-destructive fixer, fired successively from jobs as a radio DJ and a hotel desk-clerk (he looks hilariously smart in a tie), he gives the show a lot of energy. Watching him disenchantedly from bed is his sometime girlfriend Janette (Kim Dickens, in a role not dissimilar to her Joanie in Deadwood), a restauranteuse who's having difficulty meeting her bills. Nobody says anything about the flood until, suddenly, we find ourselves watching Creighton Bernette, a Falstaffian English professor, being interviewed at the canal-side by an oddly unsympathetic BBC man and excoriating governmental neglect for decades before the disaster. We relish his wit and his passion, but what we enjoy most of all is the fact that he's played by John Goodman. The best acting duet in the first three episodes comes when Goodman's irony bumps up against Zahn's frenzy. It's like jazz.

Creighton's wife, Toni (Melissa Leo, alternately charming and splenetically frazzled) is a civil-rights attorney, and the nearest thing to a unifying character, since just about everyone in the cast needs her services; Episode 3 finds her, time after time, bailing victims out of jail; the recurring motif of part 2 is people asking their parents to bail them out of debt. Toni is trying to locate the brother of Antoine's ex-wife, LaDonna (Khandi Alexander) who vanished from a prison when the apocalypse came; she meets official intransigence that may have been exacerbated by recent events but was obviously always there; here the show is at its most Wire-ish. We also meet a pair of street musicians, one of them played by Lucia Micarelli, a professional violinist who turns out to be a bewitching screen presence and a natural actress. Finally, there is Albert, a building contractor who's also a part-time musician and head of a tribe of Mardi Gras Indians, a singular black New Orleans institution. Clarke Peters, the laid-back Lester of The Wire, is here almost equally compelling in a completely different mode; ferociously dedicated to re-building his house and re-uniting his tribe, he screws up his face in obdurate determination, and has frightening reserves of violence. When he puts on his feathers, whooping and summoning, he's extraordinary. He has a son, Delmond (Rob Brown), a saxophonist who has moved to New York to play bop, reminding us that there's a world, including a jazz world, outside New Orleans, but who can't escape tradition.

Is it as good as The Wire? Not quite or not yet. Baltimore was representative while New Orleans is singular; so it's harder to identify. The Wire, a cop-and-crime show however much it ramified, had built-in narrative drive; Treme ambles. There is, to be frank, something soapy in the measured way it keeps moving from one storyline to another, with no dominant character or theme, though the latter may well emerge. It's rich, and it teems, and there is, naturally and omnipresently, the music. This is a milieu in which even the strippers perform to live accompaniment. Some star players (Allen Toussaint, Dr. John) appear as themselves, without seeming cheap; Elvis Costello adds to his already Alpine credibility just in the way he gazes from beneath his fedora at the resident guys in a club. The same respect that the show pays to the music, it also grants its viewers and itself. I don't think I've heard the word "Katrina" spoken, but it does appear on the screen, at the end of Episode 3. A bus, advertising Katrina Tours, pulls up in the Treme, disrupting another funeral procession--and this time we do know the corpse; we saw it discovered, rotting, in one of the devastated houses. The mourners angrily send the bus on its way, and even the driver acknowledges the impropriety. The procession re-forms, but this time we don't accompany it. It's a sign that the show, too, is moving on.
