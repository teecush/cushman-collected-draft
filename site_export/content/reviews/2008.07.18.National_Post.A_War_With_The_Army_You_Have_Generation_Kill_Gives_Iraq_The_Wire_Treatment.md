In the first sequence of Generation Kill, a U. S. Marine, stationed in Kuwait just prior to the invasion of Iraq, laments to one of his buddies that the local population "still haven't cleaned up their trash from the last war." (Ive cleaned up his language a bit there myself. You cant write about Generation Kill in a family newspaper without doing that to almost every line.)

After this, the conversation gets more philosophical. A soldier reflects on the achievements of the ancient civilization around them and on all the killing that has gone on in and around it. It's destiny, he drawls. "White man's gotta rule the world." He himself is black.

Racial tensions flair within the battalion whose members are the subjects of this HBO series, the creation of David Simon and Ed Burns who gave us The Wire. I was going to say this is that shows long-awaited successor, though Generation Kill seems to have arrived in very short order. So do sexual tensions; at least, being gay is a favourite accusation that these guys fling at one another, though nobody seems to take it very seriously. This kind of hazing is just a standard way of passing the time.

As far as their reaction to the job at hand is concerned, the men seem gung-ho for combat, though this may be an act. Theres a memorable scene in the first episode in which the guys read the fan mail that the kids back home are sending them: not their own kids, but boys and girls who must have been told to write to them as a school assignment.

Some are straightforward bloody-minded hero worship; others sympathize with them over the terrible responsibility of killing people who after all are equally children of God. That prompts one guy to improvise a reply about how he's actually a killing machine who just can't wait to get himself as many Iraqi scalps as possible.

How much of this is bravado, how much is real? Now youre asking.

Anyway, kids letters aren't want they want from home. They want copies of Hustler. And then there comes among them, for embedding purposes, a journalist who used to write for that very publication (Boy, says one of his new compadres, "we are in the presence of greatness") but who is currently employed by Rolling Stone, which America's warriors naturally despise as a bastion of liberal wussiness.

He is a surrogate for the real life Rolling Stone correspondent Evan Wright whose book is the basis of the series. I welcomed his arrival, as a possible point of reference among a mass of soldiers whom I was having difficulty sorting out.

He hasnt served that purpose, though its been fun watching him alternately shocked and intrigued by the occupants of the vehicle to which he's assigned when the wagons finally start rolling. The call to action has been heralded by the unwonted arrival at the gates of Fort Matilda, Kuwait, of a long line of trucks from Pizza Hut. Prior to this the men have been largely cut off from the outside world, only roused to interest by a rumour, spreading like wildfire, of the death of J. Lo.

Among their other troubles is a sergeant-major, continually on at them to conform to the military code by shaving off the facial hair that's resulted from a battalion-wide moustache-growing contest. My sympathies, here, are rather with the sergeant-major. The facial decoration makes the characters even harder to tell apart than is usually the case in war films.

It isnt only masturbation fodder and palatable chow that the troops are lacking. Armaments and electronics are in chronically short supply. (A sign, we know, of what is to come. Or not to come.) Some of this seems familiar, from previous wars, or from books and films about them.

Were in a Catch-22 world of disgruntled grunts and incompetent or eccentric commanding officers. Or, keeping it in TV terms, this might be a new M*A*S*H, but one in which the characters we like stand a chance of getting killed. (There aren't any I'm attached to yet, but I assume that will change.)

The battalion commander, known as Godfather, has to rely for most of his information on the BBC. Finally he gets to send his men into Iraq proper with the following encouraging words: The general has asked this battalion to be Americas shock troops, and Godfather cant tell the general, 'We don't do windows. 

Godfather speaks in a hoarse rasp, and his resemblance to Marlon Brando presumably earned him his nickname. Rolling Stone guy finally plucks up courage to ask him how come. "Throat cancer" is the answer. You a smoker? inquires the scribe. "No", says Godfather. Just lucky, I guess.

Generation Kills dialogue, as I trust has been made clear, is up to Simon-Burns standard. So is the underlying mordancy, with an extra touch of the absurd.

The first Iraqi we see, as the troops start rolling, is a solitary child gazing at the caravan from the roadside. When a squad of Iraqis surrender, they have to be "unsurrendered" because HQ can't think of anything practical to do with them that falls within the provisions of the Geneva convention: a decision that causes disbelief in the ranks.

This doesn't seem to be as fertile ground for its authors as The Wire was; they may well feel constraints about making any of the characters too engaging. The situation, of course makes the Baltimore streets --geographically closer but less historically immediate -- seem escapist by comparison. But even The Wire, though I can hardly believe it now, took its time to draw me in. We'll see.
