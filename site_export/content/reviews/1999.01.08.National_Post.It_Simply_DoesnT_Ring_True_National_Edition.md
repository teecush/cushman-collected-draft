IT'S ALL TRUE

Tarragon Theatre, Toronto

Jason Sherman's It's All True is a clever, occasionally brilliant, evening with a fast pulse. It is not, however, anything more than that, and at some crucial moments it's less.

It's a play about a play. It takes off from one of the most celebrated first nights in theatrical history, and is part tribute, part expose.

In 1937, a new political musical, The Cradle Will Rock, was about to open in New York, sponsored by Roosevelt's Federal Theatre Project. Just before the announced first night, the FTP, rattled by allegations of left-wing bias, put the production on ice -- cancelled it. More surprisingly -- since the show was practically a hymn to organized labour -- the actors' and musicians' unions reinforced the ban. There was a rivalry between the two unions, and the script happened to be on the wrong side of it.

The Cradle was written and composed by Marc Blitzstein and directed by Orson Welles. Blitzstein was a virtual unknown -- an adventurous musician and a committed Marxist, greatly influenced by the Berlin classics of Bertolt Brecht and Kurt Weill. Welles was Welles -- at 22 a major force in theatre and radio and a genius in everybody's eyes, not the least his own.

With their theatre padlocked, they and their colleagues took guerrilla action. They marched their company, plus a couple of thousand fascinated spectators, through the streets to another theatre, and hey, kids, they did the show right there. Denied sets and musicians, Blitzstein sat on stage at a piano, played the score, and announced the scenes. The actors, forbidden to appear on stage, sang their roles (Cradle is virtually through-composed) from the house. It was, of course, a howling success; subsequent productions have all been modelled on that thrilling impromptu premiere, rather than on the expressionist spectacle Welles had originally devised.

It's All True takes us from the announcement of the ban to the climactic moment at which a frightened actress gets up in the auditorium and delivers her first line. This being the theatre, the legend of quick-thinking artistic solidarity dissolves on examination into a viciously diverting war of egos. In addition to Welles and Blitzstein, there are John Houseman, Welles' pernickety patrician producer, and Howard da Silva, a belligerent leftist actor. Then there are the women in their lives -- a fitting phrase here since the men all treat them as appendages.

The casting drives this home. Four actors get to stretch out in a character apiece; meanwhile, two actresses divide half a dozen roles between them. Tamara Bernier and Melody Johnson are the ladies, and their quicksilver doubling is one of the great pleasures of Richard Rose's staging. (This is a co-production between Tarragon, the host theatre, and Rose's Necessary Angel company.) By a sharp modulation of voice or stance, either of them, with no change of wig or costume, can propel us into a whole different phase of the action.

The play doesn't confine itself to the crucial day; it delves, through interlocking flashbacks, into the writing, casting, and rehearsal processes, and into what lies behind them: the backbiting, the agonizing, the artistic and sexual seductions. Like the performance it commemorates, Rose's production roams all over the auditorium as well as the stage, but through all its ramblings in space and time it never loses its way.

Neither does Sherman's script -- at least not in terms of narrative coherence. The opening scene -- a three-way quarrel when the axe first falls -- is done with great zest, as a staccato battle of unfinished sentences, revelling in its own stylishness. The backstage revelations are fun. So is the history: Welles has just done Doctor Faustus, and a congressman has asked, "This Christopher Marlowe, was he a Communist?" The contemporary resonances -- subsidies remorselessly whittled away, artists divided if not conquered -- are very pertinent. But the centre of the play is hollow.

Richard Binsley's Houseman is an amusingly elegant cartoon; Martin Julien's da Silva a feisty if rather monotonous Brooklyn whippersnapper. But the main burden lies on Blitzstein and Welles. Both are haunted by women who died on them: Welles' mother, Blitzstein's wife. It seems very pat. Citizen Kane's Rosebud, twice warmed over.

We are told that Blitzstein's wife gave him a) a temporary refuge from his homosexuality and b) all his political ideas. Unsure of his own talent (like everybody) he worships Brecht but snipes at Weill, whom he calls a facile crowd-pleasing melodist. (It's certainly true that nobody could ever have levelled that accusation at Blitzstein.) Tom McCamus doesn't have much room to manoeuvre but he sounds impeccable and, in derby and neatly rumpled brown suit, looks it, too.

Victor Ertmanis' Welles, by contrast, looks and sounds entirely wrong: a flabby, grumbling bully. Welles may well have been a bastard, he was certainly a megalomaniac, but he was never charmless. Welles at 22 was the classic boy wonder, and you have only to look at the early scenes of Kane to get the message. (Ertmanis, oddly enough sounds more like the real, and oily, Howard da Silva.) "It's All True is not all true, says the author in the program, and fair enough, but this Welles convinces neither as documentary nor as an original creation with the same name.

And there is the little matter of The Cradle Will Rock itself. At one point, Welles and Houseman dismiss it as "sentimental agitprop." Well, they're right, it is. It's also a passionate and original work, with clumsy words but powerful music. But we never get to hear it. Maybe it was an artistic choice, maybe they couldn't get the rights, but the brief excerpts we hear aren't Blitzstein, but somebody's approximation of Blitzstein. (And talk about imitation Weill: the opening vamp sounds as if it's going to turn into Pirate Jenny.) So we never get to feel what the fuss has been about. Imagine if, at the climax of Shakespeare in Love, Tom Stoppard had decided to indulge a passion for blank-verse pastiche. Coupled with the author's apparent dislike of his characters, it makes for a chilly evening.

When Welles the theatrical conjurer appears at the end to murmur "magic," it may be meant as ironic self-deprecation. But the joke rebounds.
