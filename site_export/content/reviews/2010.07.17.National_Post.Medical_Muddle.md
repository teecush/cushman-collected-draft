The Shaw Festival this year has taken its sweet time getting around to doing Shaw, but it's been worth the wait. Morris Panych's production of The Doctor's Dilemma is as funny as this play, with its perambulating chorus of physicians, each obsessed with his own miracle procedure, usually is. It also carries an emotional clout that isn't usual at all. Following on his charming mellow Parfumerie at Soulpepper, it suggests that Panych is reaching a new maturity as a director.

Shaw called The Doctor's Dilemma a tragedy, but, as the man who pointed out that "life doesn't cease to be funny when someone dies any more than it ceases to be serious when someone laughs," he was at least half-kidding. Someone does die in this play, and mortality hovers over all of it, often to comic, or at least satiric, effect. Mentioning death, says one of the corps of beknighted, and benighted, specialists, is "a dastardly advantage to take of a medical man." The dilemma of the title is the one faced by the newly ennobled Sir Colenso Ridgeon, who has discovered a cure for TB at a time when it was a killer. His clinic is full but he agrees to squeeze in one extra patient, the genius young painter Louis Dubedat, whose case has been pleaded by his bewitching wife, Jennifer. Two snags emerge: Dr. Blenkinsop, an old classmate of Ridgeon's, now a penurious GP, turns out to be another sufferer, while Dubedat turns out to be, by the doctors' respectable lights, a rat; he cheats on his wife and also on anyone whom he can persuade to write him a cheque. So Ridgeon, in a play set 40 years before England gained a national health service, has to choose between saving a talented scoundrel and a virtuous mediocrity. The issue is complicated by his having fallen in love with Mrs. Dubedat, and hence having a vested interest in seeing her widowed. This may weaken the play as pure Socratic debate, but enriches it -- in fact makes it possible--as drama.

Ridgeon's motives aren't all dis-honourable, and Patrick Galligan does a good job of portraying a sheltered man who's grown up believing in his own decency without ever having it put to the test. He and Krista Colosimo as Jennifer play chillingly off one another in the play's sardonic epilogue, while earlier on she's burningly believable in her protective love for Louis, both in and out of his presence. Dubedat charms the doctors too, initially, though Jonathan Gould makes him too obvious a snake for this to be credible; he's more effective, though still casually anachronistic, when running insouciant rings round them later, and is very good at dying, in their embarrassed presence, in his own studio.

The most powerful moments of all come from Ric Reid, one of the Shaw's best and most taken-for-granted actors, as the penurious Blenkinsop, proudly refusing to sponge on his friends (as opposed to Dubedat who'll sponge on anyone). The friends themselves are a grand collection, with Michael Ball courteously implacable as the voice of conscience, the sage who's seen medical fashions come and go, and Patrick McManus, an appropriately incisive surgeon. The medic to whose tender but unskilled mercies Dubedat is consigned is Sir Ralph Bloomfield Bonington, consultant to rich families, with his mellifluous bedside manner and his unvarying determination to "stimulate the phagocytes." (A phrase that became proverbial; at least, my mother used to quote it.) Thom Marriott savours the role like the rich plum it is, though he short-changes the patchwork of Shakespearean misquotations with which BB salutes his patient's death; it should be so ludicrous it's moving, but here it stops at ludicrous. A couple of minor roles are misjudged, as well as being assigned to actors too young for them, but there's a notable small performance, poignant without being mawkish, by Cherissa Richards as one of Dubedat's discarded loves. Ken MacDonald's set designs feature giant X-rays at Ridgeon's place and a giant paint-brush suspended over Dubedat's; medical and artistic images merge until we arrive at Louis' posthumous exhibition, which consists entirely of portraits of the play's doctors. The doctor's dilemma becomes the artist's revenge.

One of Shaw's doctors gets a laugh by claiming a friendship with his fellow-playwright J.M. Barrie, for whom this is proving to be quite a year. Stratford has his Peter Pan, and the Shaw has uncovered a one-act gem called Half an Hour. The title refers to the time-frame in which a society lady may or may not put her life back together. She's walked out on her husband, after the latest of many rows, but her plans for escape go cruelly awry. Like Peter Pan, this production gives us Barrie as quizzical narrator, utilizing his own irresistibly garrulous stage-directions, which make Shaw's seem laconic; Michael Ball, perfect again, does the honours and also throws in one of his sublimely offended butlers. A program note suggests that the lady's a bitch, but Diana Donnelly plays her sympathetically and very involvingly; it's her best work yet. She has two fine foils in Gord Rand as her broken lifeline and Peter Millard as yet another doctor: one who enters the play as a mere functionary and by the end has become inexorable Fate, weighing its options. Gina Wilkinson's production squeezes the tension very tight, and makes highly imaginative use of dust sheets and a trap door. Deadline plays -- think of The President -- seem to suit the Shaw at lunchtime very well; they go like clockwork.
