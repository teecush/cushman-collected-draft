The WorldStage festival, left for dead a couple of years ago, has recreated itself this year in minimalist form. Every show has been a solo performance of some kind. This can only have been a financial choice, even if dressed up as an artistic one. Still, there have been some interesting events.

The best I encountered was My Arm, written and performed by a shaven-headed Englishman named Tim Crouch. Some of its appeal lay in the games it played with the idea of confessional theatre. Crouch tells his story in urgent though unhurried tones that convince you that he is being autobiographical.

Gradually, you come to realize he can't be. He is in far too good physical shape. The man whose story he tells has done horrible, irreparable things to himself. He tells us he lost a finger. We count. Crouch has 10 of them. Also he talks about his hair, of which he has none.

The person he talks about seems, as a boy, to have been on the vulnerable end of an intense sibling rivalry. As the climax of an escalating series of attempts to gain attention, he raised his right arm in the air. He never lowered it again, leading in time to the breakdown of his whole body. But he did get the notoriety he craved, and more. When he moved to London he got taken up as an art object; a whole exhibit was devoted to him, under the title Man- I(n)festation. A cultural entrepreneur acts as his producer, and is said to be at the back of the theatre, checking up. It was, I think, this detail more than any other that persuaded me for the longest time that the guy was for real.

Crouch, an experienced actor, has created a character, even while making no attempt to imitate him. This aesthetic runs through the whole show. For props, he uses objects he borrows from the audience. But he casts them, so to speak, against type; any thing -- a credit card, say -- can stand in for any other thing, or for any person. He trains a camera on these objets donnes and they appear, enlarged, on a video screen, still stubbornly themselves but also convincing emotional correlatives for the ingredients of his story. The use of video is indeed extraordinary. The narrator mentions his mother's death; she did not, as far as we can gather, drown, but he shows a clip of a woman wading out to sea, and the sense of loss is overwhelming.

The show has its limitations. Its commentary on the art scene gets nowhere in particular. Crouch does, though, come up with a one- line mantra that works both as satire and as common-sense affirmation: "Art is anything you can get away with."

Laurie Anderson, in my heretical view, doesn't get away with it, at least not in The End of the Moon. This is the fruit of her unlikely tenure as artist-in-residence at NASA, and is a series of anecdotes and observations linked, as far as I could tell, by free association. Some of them are delivered from an armchair, others from the podium on which she plays her electric violin. The sound is pleasant, but it all fades into a single blur.

At one juncture she announces a 30-second pause, which duly happens. This is cute, though hardly provocative. And her use of video is not nearly in the same imaginative league as Crouch's. Where she falls shortest, though, is in her delivery, which is even in pitch and tempo (like Crouch's) but without an edge (unlike Crouch's).

It all comes at you through a mic, which depersonalizes it even further. The total effect is undeniably hypnotic, as presumably was the intention. But the trouble with hypnosis is that it puts you to sleep. I felt, especially when the ritual cheers rang out at the end, that I was watching an unclothed empress.

The other shows I saw lacked even Anderson's sense of occasion. Bombshells was a set of four monologues written by Joanna Murray- Smith and performed by Caroline O'Connor. The characters are girls or women in states of quiet or noisy desperation: a bride who marries "for the sake of the dress" (is this credible any more?) and goes on the day from exultation to despair; a "yuppie mum" overwhelmed by everything (this is credible, but it isn't fresh); a widow who experiences an unexpected awakening (the best of the bunch); a teenager in a talent contest (grim caricature masquerading as affection). Every one of them is a stock figure, and the writing is rarely funny or sympathetic enough to compensate. O'Connor, an actress-singer-dancer of Anglo-Australian-Irish extraction, gets to use all her skills and all her accents, and she is a trouper. Rosebud, another U.K. import, gave us Orson Welles waxing autobiographical, its structure apparently determined by the availability of recyclable anecdotes. A scissors-and-paste job, without the ghost of an original viewpoint, Mark Jenkins's script told us little about the work and nothing about the man. But what else would you expect of a piece that takes its title from the Citizen Kane conceit that Welles himself dismissed as "dollar-book Freud"? Christian McKay's Orson came to life just once -- when his voice broke in the middle of a list of abandoned projects.

- - -

There is some fine cosmpolitan theatre, from closer to home, in Moliere's Le Bourgeois Gentilhomme, as presented by Theatre Francais de Toronto. This is famous as the play whose parvenu protagonist learns from his newly engaged philosophy teacher that he has spent his entire life speaking in prose.

Guy Mignault, a radiant beanpole, makes the discovery a blessed event, and he is scarcely less engaging when receiving dubious instruction from his fiercely competitive tutors in fencing and dancing. Mignault's Monsieur Jourdain goes through the entire play being duped, and you feel happy for him. His folly, born of joy, is nobler than other people's wisdom.

As often in Moliere, the play's first half, where the hero's foibles are exhibited as in a revue, is more rewarding than the second, in which the plot grinds into gear. In Diana Leblanc's production, though, everything glides along wittily, smoothly and even subtly. There is good work from Marie-Helene Fontaine's Madame Jourdain, exasperatingly sensible, and from Paul Essiembre and Jessica Heafey as scheming or saucy servants. In Sarah Balleux's design, the actors are sartorial centaurs, 17th century below the waist and 21st above. It works, as does nearly everything else.
