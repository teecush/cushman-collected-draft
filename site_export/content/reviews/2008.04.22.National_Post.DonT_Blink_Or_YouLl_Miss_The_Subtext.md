Sky Gilbert's self-described "very gay little musical" starts with a song en-titled We're Much Too Drunk To Write a Musical, which is very disarming of it. As it turns out, Happy is a show that needs all the disarmament it can muster. It's not without its charms, but it deconstructs itself at such a rate that not only does it leave nothing standing, it hardly gets around to building anything in the first place.

Even the one-word title is a snare. It's presumably a joke on the more traditional meaning of "gay:" It also seems to be ironic, in that the story concerns a married male couple who think they've found a contented life together even if their author is positive that they haven't. (Sky Gilbert, meet Michael Coren. Different premises, same conclusion.) And that's before we get to the subtitle, which both mocks and reaffirms the belief that the musical is an inherently gay medium. (Doesn't work for Guys and Dolls, to name but one.) And this musical is certainly "little;" it has a duration of an hour and a quarter, a cast of four, pre-recorded piano accompaniment that frequently drowns out the unamplified singers and a score containing just three numbers. The rest of the evening is dialogue, though that's just as well since dialogue remains what Gilbert does best.

Now concentrate. That opening song is performed by Bob, a playwright and lyricist, and Sue, dramaturge and (again self-described) fag hag. Later they're joined by Dave, the composer who is Bob's personal and professional partner, and then by a young man who gains entry by claiming to be selling subscriptions for something called the Evening Star. Once in, he announces (in song) that he's AIDS. Not that he suffers from it: that he is it. Then there's a blackout, during which posters for the Xtra, the gay Toronto weekly, and Follies, the apogee of cult musicals, disappear from the walls of Bob's apartment. Camp, in other words, has been struck. We realize that Bob and Sue and Dave are writing a musical about Bob and Sue and Dave writing a musical, and that the musical they've been writing is the one that, up to this point but no longer, we have been seeing. What we're now seeing is a musical about the musical (about a musical) that they've been writing.

And the musical they were writing is about gay marriage, and it glorifies the union of Bob and Dave as being devoted but chaste. Only the real Bob is a hypocrite because he's been carrying on with the Evening Star boy who now reappears in his own person rather than as a symbol for AIDS. Now you might think that if Bob and Dave have found an arrangement that works for them, even with loopholes, that's a good thing and nobody's business but their own. Their author, however -- and this time I mean Gilbert -- seems more inclined to upbraid them for forsaking the promiscuity that he seems to think synonymous with homosexuality. Admittedly, this is less evident from the piece itself that from the surrounding literature and from some first-night guffaws from clued-in members of the audience.

Micah Barnes' music is traditional show-tune, jolly but undistinguished. Gilbert's lyrics are hard to judge since they mostly can't be heard. None of the men can sing them, and even Sharron Matthews, usually a musical-theatre dynamo, sinks beneath sound level, at least until she gets to belt out a solo, a sadder-but-wiser number in the line of The Ladies Who Lunch. Where Matthews really surprises is in her acting; she handles her speeches and her reactions with unsuspected ease, force and even subtlety. David Tomlinson captures the two sides of Bob, the prig and the slob; Jamieson Eakin makes something touching of a lost-boy stereotype, and though Jason Cadieux has little to do as Dave, he seems quite nice.

Part of Gilbert's purpose seems to be to blame gay authors of musicals, especially successful ones, for not dealing with gay subjects. He also has a go at fellow playwrights, taking potshots at Brad Fraser for depicting just such a lifestyle as he himself seems to be advocating. No, wait, that's in the play-within-the-play -- the hypocritical one -- so maybe it doesn't count. Either way, a piece as flimsy as this is hardly a solid foundation from which to attack other writers. In this show -- it isn't true of all his plays -- Gilbert doesn't seem to like anyone very much, including his own characters. As Bertolt Brecht so very nearly said, Happy is the show that needs no heroes. - Happy: A Very Gay Little Musical runs at the Buddies in Bad Times Theatre, 12 Alexander St., until April 27, 2008.
