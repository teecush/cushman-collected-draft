To June 16. (416) 872-1111

THE ISLAND

Bluma Appel Theatre

- - -

To begin with, some history: In 1973 in Cape Town, Athol Fugard, the white South African playwright and director, collaborated with two black actors, John Kani and Winston Ntshona, on a play they called Sizwe Bansi Is Dead. It dealt with one of the most oppressive aspects of apartheid, the pass laws, and was surprisingly a comedy: it showed a black man beating the system by the desperate means of assuming a dead mans identity.

That same year the production travelled to the Royal Court Theatre in London, where its humour, anger and extraordinary authenticity knocked everybody out. It quickly returned in harness with a second, grimmer piece, The Island, about two prisoners in the notorious high-security fortress of Robben Island. The two plays then went on to conquer New York and, in 1977, even received major public production in New York.

Now, with apartheid gone, it is The Island that is staking a claim to survival. It was revived with the original performers at Peter Brooks Bouffes-du-Nord theatre in Paris in 1999, went from there to the National Theatre in London, and has now arrived in triumph in Toronto.

Sizwe Bansi was a protest play; The Island is a history play. The terms overlap, of course, but the latter is the more durable. Bansi was about a specific issue; The Island takes on an institution, one that still stands and, even if it crumbled tomorrow, could stand in imaginatively for political prisons everywhere. Robben Island is where Nelson Mandela was incarcerated, but even without that (and the play never mentions him) it would have the force of a myth.

The Island places another myth within the myth. It shows us two prisoners rehearsing their own version of what might be called the original protest play, Sophocles' Antigone.

That idea, probably Mr. Fugard's, may seem high-flown but is based on fact; there was an actor imprisoned on the island who attempted just that. Messrs. Kani and Ntshona themselves did time under apartheid, though in less extreme circumstances, but its probable that any black South African would know in his bones what the experience would be like.

The two appear here as cell-mates actually called John and Winston. John is the prime mover, the ex-actor who wants to do the play at the prison concert; Winston is his wavering collaborator, grumbling but loyal. Cast as Antigone, he more or less says, like Flute in A Midsummer Nights Dream, let me not play a woman." People, he rightly says, surveying his false breasts and straw- coloured wig, will laugh. Yes, says John very wisely, they will, but they can't laugh forever and then they'll listen.

Its hard to be sure after nearly 30 years but my impression is John has put on weight in the interim and Winston has lost it. Their mind-and-body double act has the same dynamic but different physical expression. Their acting is, if anything, even richer; physically, vocally, facially, each is complete to the core, while their rapport, after all these years as colleagues, goes beyond teamwork; they seem to breathe in symphony.

They begin the play with a 15-minute mime (not fun to watch, but necessary) of hard labour on the island beach: a special detail, even worse than the customary work in the quarry, aimed as much at their minds as their bodies. Then we find them in their cell where they can talk, quarrel, be human. Perhaps the most terrifying thing about the play, though, is the immediacy with which, when a knock comes at the door and an unseen guard confronts them, they revert to their officially sanctioned role as terrified automata, cringing at the double; its an act but it's real.

They don't spend all their time arguing about Antigone. They have a nightly ritual by which each of them alternates in the task of entertaining the other. One night Winston will tell John the plot of a movie (suddenly were reminded of Kiss of the Spider Woman, another two-prisoner piece, presented by the same Canadian producer, Garth Drabinsky, on the same stage); on another, John will act out the fantasy of placing a phone call home, inquiring after friends and family. Here, in the plays subtlest and most wrenching moment, John passes from amusing Winston to desperately reassuring himself as he asks about the wife who has stopped writing to him.

John is called to the governor's office and told his 10-year sentence has been commuted to three; he'll be out in three months. Winston, in brilliantly acted silence, tries to conceal his jealousy; then, gradually, as he describes Johns homecoming in cold, blistering tones we havent heard from him before, he lets it slip.

I used to think this passage too literary; I no longer do, though I could do without Winston telling John that he has the stink of freedom -- we've got there before him. It's probably unfair that were not told how long Winston is in for, and its not made quite clear how arbitrary the regime was; that two people could be given drastically unequal sentences for the same supposed offence.

The two men rally and we see their performance, noting that when John introduces them he mentions, according to form, not only their cell number but the fact that they're on "Diet G." Their take on Antigone destroys the Sophoclean balance, making Creon the racist tyrant, Antigone the unequivocal freedom-fighter. It's inevitable and stirring (one wonders if it will cost John his freedom) and not, even for a cushioned audience like us, sentimental. The actors -- who risked a lot to create the show -- have earned it, even if we haven't.

Performed by other actors, The Island would not be a great play and, outside this and Sizwe Bansi Is Dead (which I would like to see them do again, just to be sure), these may not be great actors.

But the play and the actors together are great, doing honour to the theatre and to the world. It is everything we were promised.
