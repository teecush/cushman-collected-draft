THEATRE REVIEW

Between the Sheets

Tarragon Extra Space, Toronto

Nightwood Theatre's Between the Sheets is a gripping new play, terrifically performed. The rest of this review may constitute a spoiler, but I can't see any way of discussing it without.

Jordi Mand's play, her first, is set in a Grade 3 private-school classroom. It's the end of a parent-teacher evening. Teresa (Christine Horne), the young teacher, is just about to pack up and go home when Marion (Susan Coyne), a mother, appears to claim her interview slot to discuss her son Alex. This comes as a surprise to Teresa, since Marion hasn't made a booking, and anyway all Teresa's previous discussions about Alex have been with his father, Marion's husband, Curtis.

Marion is well aware of this, and is resentful about it. Well, more than resentful. Alex is a special-needs child, with what sounds like a serious case of ADD, and Marion doesn't like being left out of his loop. She certainly doesn't like Teresa's declaration that as Alex's teacher she spends more time with him than his career-woman mother does, and therefore understands him better. But what she likes least of all is her recent discovery, revealed after some initial thrust and parry, that her husband and Teresa are lovers.

That, plus Teresa's occupational paperwork, accounts for the failed pun of the title: the only clumsy thing about the play. The running time is a bare packed hour; we're a good 10 minutes into it before Marion drops her bombshell, and it's a measure of the author's skill that though we're not really surprised by it we're still shocked.

The same holds true for the other disclosures that come, thick and fairly fast, from one woman or the other for the rest of the play. About the unseen Curtis's incurably restless nature. About the plans he and Teresa seem to be making. About the economic pressures that keep Marion from her son while providing the money that sends him to school. About Teresa's own professional dedication, about her childlessness, and about her health.

Mand, her director Kelly Thornton, and their two actresses take the familiar technique of retrospective exposition, and imbue it with remorseless emotional logic. Each revelation is a revenge. Think Ibsen for long-range technique; Strindberg for short-haul intensity; and, for kinship of setting and subject, that other seat-clutching classroom duet, David Mamet's Oleanna. Like Oleanna, this play shows two people locked in mortal combat on the edge of a cliff. Also like Oleanna, it ends with one of them falling off, courtesy of an act that cannot be undone.

Mand, though, may be subtler than Mamet; she keeps the balance of power and of sympathy constantly shifting. Also unlike Mamet, she's charitable. Her two characters do have their moments, presented with tact and without sentimentality, of rapprochement. But then, as two women fighting over the same man, they would be likely to discover things in common. One telling detail is their varying use of one another's first names; it can signify patronage, guilt, sympathy or - the last time it happens - a horrific combination of all three.

The play might, as one psychological scar after another is laid bare, be accused of piling things on. But every person comes with issues, and every relationship automatically doubles them. Or, if you throw in this play's two offstage male characters, quadruples them. They interlock, and Mand makes each of them fresh and individual, with the possible exception of Marion's conflicted feelings over her enforced superwoman status; the situation is believable but the actual writing feels like an undigested magazine article.

The production can be wrenching without ever playing for pathos. There's a kind of hush all over the house while it's playing: the sound of an audience caught, held, rapt. It isn't something that you hear too often.

Between the Sheets runs until Oct. 7. For tickets and more information, visit nightwoodtheatre.net.
