Play is a feat of comic writing and acting

Sky Gilbert's new play is called Bad Acting Teachers, a title thats less straightforward than it looks. It leaves you wondering at just whom Gilbert, who these days is a theatre professor himself, thinks he is taking aim. His targets could be bad teachers of acting, or people who teach bad acting on purpose (there could be quite a market for that) or teachers of acting who are also bad people. Gilbert, the most vociferous gay activist in our theatre, if not our culture, is also quite the moralist, and that last definition turns out to be the one he has in mind. This is a play about power trips.

It isnt an especially profound play about power trips, but it is an entertaining one, the best-sustained feat of comic writing, acting and direction to appear in Toronto in ages. The structure could hardly be simpler, which means that bringing it off is a tour de force. A young actor, seeking to improve on his already decent professional credentials, seeks help from three charlatans in succession. A silent opening sequence tips us off. It's a trailer, composed of significant moments from the encounters that make up the main play. The first teacher punches our hero in the chest, the second slaps his face and he flees the presence of the third, who is showing ominous signs of executing a panther-like spring.

Number One is Finn Gunn, a cowboy-booted veteran of the alternative theatre of the '70s, nostalgic for the days of people putting on plays in tiny spaces without any money (so whats changed?) and of "speaking to farmers about farmers." Or, in Hollingsworths case (that would be Michael Hollingsworth, now of VideoCabaret; Gilbert isn't afraid to name names, or at least to drop them), there was "good old-fashioned decadence, not like the fancy decadence you get nowadays."

Finn, put over by Jason Cadieux with hilarious faded swagger, seldom has a drink out of his hands. His notions about acting are difficult to make out through the resultant haze, but they seem to involve the student getting in touch with his inner man, plus a massive amount of wrong-footing, physical and psychological.

I can't work with you, he says, "if youre afraid of me," an ultimatum that on the intimidation scale must come out just ahead of act naturally. He then tops it by throwing what appears to be an epileptic fit. I have seen few funnier sights recently than that of Jefferson Guzman, as the Actor whose name we never learn, attempting to escape the room by creeping turtle-like across the floor, while his mentor lies writhing a few feet away from him. What he most wants to do is leave without paying, which really gets Finn where he lives.

Number Two is Ismene Popoff, who commiserates with the Actor over the violence hes suffered from Finn. If Finn, for macho reasons, preferred working with men, Ismene, rendered to life by Ann Holloway as a would-be Earth mother from an even earlier cultural era, likes to interact with women. She believes in "mortifying the flesh," which she finds easier with women because, if I'm deciphering my notes properly, they're vaginas.

Our boy, lacking that genetic predisposition, isnt too sure he wants his flesh mortified but, after a wittily played round of cat- and-mouse, he consents, on condition that it wont hurt too much. When it does, and he protests, she accuses him of wanting to rape her. He counters that this is unlikely, since he's gay, but this cuts no ice with her. (The hierarchies of victimhood can be damnably tricky to establish.) He makes his second precipitate exit of the evening, pursued by cries of "you rape me with your privilege." It's a little disappointing to see her turn into a stereotype of the resentful feminist, and more so that the characters never really engage, but were dealing with cartoons here, and this is a funny one.

Number Three is Reginald Architruc, who commiserates with the Actor over the violence hes suffered from Finn and Ismene. If Reginald has theories about acting, he keeps them to himself, along with his hands, at least to begin with. What Reginald has, or says he has, is contacts -- he seems to be as much agent as teacher -- and he uses them to get his young male clients into bed. He's a snake in snake's clothing, if snakes wear pink. He can even promise Hollywood if, amusingly enough, his prey will conceal being gay. He asks what man, with a couple of token British exceptions, has ever succeeded in being both out and a star, and the Actor is abashed to discover that he cant name one. So, probably, is the audience.

Gavin Crawfords Reginald sports an English accent himself -- whether it's meant to be natural or affected, I can't say -- and is altogether a fawning, preening, dangerous person. Finn, in the first scene, rails about Stratford; Reginald manages, in a single paragraph, to bitch about the festival and about Finn for being fired from it. (Poor Stratford; gets it coming and going.) Reginalds own name-dropping is of the sleekest brand. His prize anecdote concerns fellating Edward Albee. It seems to be his favourite seduction gambit. You neednt, he tells the Actor, think of me as a predatory teacher (I paraphrase slightly) because I'm so obviously behaving like a predatory teacher. So it doesn't count. As a comment on the debased postmodern idea of irony, that cuts pretty deep.

It spurs the Actor finally to assert himself, on behalf of all students, acting or otherwise, who have ever served as scapegoats for their instructors' hangups. It's probably too explicit a statement to be believable, but it is undeniably satisfying, not least because it holds true for many relationships besides pedagogic ones. Most of what goes before is undeniably slight and parochial, but enjoyably so. Gilbert writes great witty parabolas of wounded self-aggrandizement, impeccably delivered by three of his cast and sympathetically suffered by the fourth. Everything is phrased and timed well; placed well, too, on a wide set, helpfully stripped of all but essential furniture. Appearances can be deceptive, but on this evidence Gilbert himself is rather a good acting teacher.
