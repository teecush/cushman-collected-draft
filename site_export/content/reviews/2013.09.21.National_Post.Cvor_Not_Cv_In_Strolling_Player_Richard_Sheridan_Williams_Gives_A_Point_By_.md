Richard Sheridan Willis is a nicely grandiloquent name for an actor. Its owner-as he tells us in his autobiographical show Strolling Player - is not only an actor but the child of actors. He was born in Stratford upon-Avon, England, though not, as you might expect, because his parents were members of the company there. They had in fact made a special pilgrimage to Shakespeares birthplace so that it might be their son's as well. Biblical parallels come to mind.

The treatment took. Willis speaks Shakespeare very well, and his show is dotted with speeches from the plays, mostly the famous ones. His early theatrical experience, though, was far from Stratford, in terms of both geography and prestige. His father ran a company in Guernsey, one of the Channel Islands, in a period - the late 1960s - that must have been among the last days of weekly rep. Willis gives a vivid account of a childhood spent taking tickets, helping sets get built and dismantled and presumably playing the odd juvenile role. The repertoire was Agatha Christie, farce and the occasional Shakespeare or Ibsen to keep the actors from mutiny." The ambience, of both stage and auditorium, seems to have been an intriguing mixture of gentility and sweat.

From there, Willis progressed to drama school and then to a professional career that's been split, like that of most actors, between stage, film and TV; and also, unlike that of most actors, between three countries: the U.K., the U.S., and Canada where he now lives, having taken a Canadian wife. (Shes Heidi Reimer, an author, whos his co-writer here.) It's been, from what we gather, an up-and down life, symbolized perhaps in the outfit that he wears: a nice, cream-coloured suit in an extreme state of disrepair, one leg cut off at the knee. He has entertaining stories about fighting to retain his death scene in an episode of Dr. Who; understudying Antony Sher's marathon role in the West End production of Harvey Fiersteins Torch Song Trilogy (but never going on); marvelling at the ferocious power of an unknown young repertory actor called Daniel Day-Lewis; and falling incongruously off the stage while playing a suave man-about-town in an Oscar Wilde society drama. He also does a killer impersonation of Richard Burton, who figures in an anecdote with an especially good punchline.

Whats lacking is much sense of how these isolated incidents fit together. The show was apparently inspired by the exhaustive Canadian immigration form on which Willis was required to fill in his entire biography since turning 18: a daunting task for anyone, and especially for a self-described "strolling player. Having gone to all that trouble, he's turned the results into a performance.

A bureaucratic inventory can do without connective tissue; a play, though-or, if you prefer, a theatrical event - really can't. The Shakespeare extracts are plainly intended to serve this purpose, but they dont, good though they often are in themselves. Kicking off with all the worlds a stage" seems too flamingly obvious, but Willis won me over with the unusual sardonic emphasis he gave to the line about "the bubble reputation. It may even be meant to key into his own career, though we're never told what his own professional reputation has been: where, at any given moment, he has fit into the pecking order of British actors, or how often, on his own turf, he was able to put his love of Shakespeare into practice. I don't know what the inclusion of the kings repentance - or attempted repentance - speech from Hamlet is supposed to illustrate. This confession of a brothers murder here oddly prefaces an account of his father's funeral. The best integrated of these excerpts, and one of the best executed, is an anti-romantic speech of Benedick from Much Ado About Nothing: a role he happened to be playing while falling in love.

There's a lot about the actors love life, and its either too much or too little. Falling in and out of love and even of marriage is something that happens to most people, and if we're to be interested in one person's experience of it rather than anothers, then we need some specifics. Delicacy may well forbid him from naming names, but since he raises the subject one would like to know more about his first marriage and why it fell apart. Its aftershocks are felt in a longish section about his paparazzi-stalked relationship with a young actress (again unnamed but easy to detect) in Peter Halls production of The Master Builder, starring Alan Bates. This production, by the way, followed its London run with a season at the Royal Alex in Toronto, though it predated Willis' move here by several years. In the meantime, he seems to have spent much of his working life in America. Again one would like to know how this came about - green cards don't grown on trees - but theres a delightful account of giving a Shakespeare recital in the White House before President George W. Bush. (I liked him," says one of his fellow-performers afterwards; "its very confusing.) Indeed it seems to have been in the States, and especially in Chicago, that Willis' Shakespearean career has blossomed. Looking him up on the web, I found an interview that goes into far more detail on the theory and practice of playing Shakespeare than the show ever does, though I think the show would be better for the inclusion. It's a pleasant experience, though, one that left me wanting to see Willis in a play. Or in another play, since I did see that Master Builder when I myself was new in Canada, though I can't claim to remember much about it, except a feeling that Bates was too likeable for his role. At one point Willis describes himself as painfully shy and reticent." That's not uncommon among actors, even those doing first person, one-man shows. It would be another paradox worth exploring.
