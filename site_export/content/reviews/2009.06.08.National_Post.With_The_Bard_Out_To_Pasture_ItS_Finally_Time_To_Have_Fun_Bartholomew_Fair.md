THEATRE REVIEW

Tom Patterson Theatre, Stratford, Ont.

Ben Jonson's Bartholomew Fair is a play that seemingly sprawls and sprawls before revealing itself to be quite cunningly organized. It's a poetic documentary, though written entirely in prose except for a few songs and a sustained burst of doggerel. Its setting is a shyster's carnival, and its cast divides into two groups: those, a mixture of cheats and fools, who visit the fair; and those, cheats all, who operate within it. Together, these make up as bulging a list of important characters as in any play ever written. They are assembled in groups, mixed up and finally brought together as the audience of a play within the play, this being a show literally in miniature as it's performed by puppets. The reunion is also, as so often in comedy, a mock-trial. Only the final judgment here is that there's no judgment: We're all human, all frail "flesh and blood." It is, depending on how you look at it, either a deeply cynical world-view or a joyously tolerant one.

Antoni Cimolino's Stratford production (the play's North American premiere, 395 years after its British one) takes the tolerant view which, especially in the theatre, has to be the right one. Jonson, notoriously censorious in his earlier plays, became surprisingly mellow and accepting in his late ones, of which Bartholomew Fair is the first and greatest. Maybe the retirement of his friend and rival William Shakespeare had something to do with it; the market in festive comedy was no longer cornered. Not that the two of them ever spoke the same dramatic language; Jonson's, here more than ever, is topical, satirical, aggressive and obscene. He could be lyrical, too, but never for long and never here. His style may be tough, but it swings, and the great attraction of this production is that all of the performers in it seem to relish what they're saying and whom they're playing. There's a real sense of discovery here, on both sides of the house. The limitation is that hardly anyone seems quite rightly cast.

That sounds dogmatic, but Jonson was a dogmatic writer. He's notorious for practising "humours" comedy, meaning roughly that his characters are obsessives, and he's still at it here. The obsessions have to be hit hard, fast and clear, which is partly a matter of temperament and partly of technique. Like last year's Love's Labour's Lost, this production is largely cast from Stratford's conservatory, with a top-dressing of older actors including some of the best there are. Neither side does as well now as it did then, partly because no director gets to acquire the inside-track knowledge of a Jonson play that Michael Langham had of Love's Labour's, partly because the juniors then were dealing with a play and characters essentially as young as themselves. The stand-out here is Ian Lake, who gives the neatest performance of the evening as Trouble-all, an amiable madman with a thing about judicial warrants. His appearance halfway through is the show's turning-point -- which, to be fair, it always is because it's through his crazy agency that the play's succession of vignettes begins to shake down into a plot.

There is a sort of title character, Bartholomew Cokes, a rich young booby who visits Bartholomew Fair upon Bartholomew's Day and undergoes his own massacre, being happily robbed of everything he owns, including his fiancee; Trent Pardy gives him some sweetness but not the ecstasy of innocence that should possess this most gullible of gulls. The prime role among the elders is Adam Overdo, the local judge, working undercover to expose "enormities" and getting everything wrong; he is beaten, insulted and put in the stocks but persists through all humiliations and three separate disguises. Tom McCamus gives him great sidelong charm but is never credible as the occupant of even the humblest bench; he has no height to fall from. Juan Chioran thunders wondrously as Zeal-of-the-Land Busy, baker turned Puritan, who goes to the fair to eat roast pig and denounce idolatry including that of the puppets by whom his hypocrisy is decisively exposed; what he lacks is a sense of place and position, say a cockney accent. Brian Tree gets the surliness and pugnacity

of Wasp, a servant with the shortest fuse on record, but not the volume. Lucy Peacock, spectacularly padded at all conceivable points, is chuckle-some casting as Ursla the pig-woman, who's practically the fair personified, but once she's established the character's girth and vulgarity she has no place to go.

There's satisfying work from Jonathan Goad as a gambling gallant who loses one heiress and gets another, older but richer (note that, in a comedy without a love story, he's the closest thing to a hero); from Randy Hughson and Victor Ertmanis as a pair of pimps who put a couple of respectable women on the game, right under their husbands' noses; from Jesse Aaron Dwyer as a conscientious cutpurse; Michael Spencer-Davis in the minute role of an officer; and Cliff Saunders who runs a great puppet-show. The show itself is a production highlight, and though some of the fairground jollity is run-of-the-mill, there is also some good surprise staging and an unusually intelligent placing of actors in the auditorium. Jonsonian comedy, combining a thick text with the action of a farce, is a tough act; this is an encouraging start at cracking it. - Bartholomew Fair is in repertory through Oct. 2.
