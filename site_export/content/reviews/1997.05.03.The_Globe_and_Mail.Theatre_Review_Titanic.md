TITANIC

THEATRE REVIEW

TITANIC

Saturday, May 3, 1997

Titanic awkward but moving

ALL jokes about the musical Titantic were made when the project was first announced; my own favourite was Linwood Barclay's prediction in the Toronto Star that it would be like "Show Boat but with greater depth." There were technical problems during previews; one audience had to be sent home with a happy ending since the damn ship just wouldn't sink . Anyway, the show opened; and despite its promise and some intemperate reviews, it is not such a spectacular disaster. In fact -- and this is the trouble -- it is hardly spectacular at all.

It is a decent-spirited, hard-working show, defeated by the magnitude of its subject, and by lapses in its execution: lapses of imagination, finesse and possibly finance. It starts all right with the doomed liner's architect singing proudly about his "floating city" being the early 20th-century's equivalent of the Parthenon and the Pyramids, an artistic and technological achievement that represents the era at its best. Then we're at Southampton, and passengers and crew are embarking on the Titanic's maiden and only voyage. We meet a representative selection, and are furnished with names and salient characteristics to be filed away for future reference. Finally everyone enters in a long line from the wings, and mounts the gangplank. It's a stirring moment; it brings home to us just how many and how varied lives are involved, thousands of them. The line should stretch out till the crack of doom, or at least to the end of the scene. But they run out of people, and can't find any way of disguising it.

It's moving, since we know that most of them are going to die. It's also predictable, since we know that most of them are going to die. That double bind works throughout the evening. It would take rare incompetence to make this story ineffective. It would take even rarer genius to bring it off completely. We get something between. Much is made of the segregation of classes that operated snobbishly during the voyage and fatally in the selection for the lifeboats. (Though we're never shown in detail how this happened; one thing the show seriously runs out of is time.)

People are always telling us, as if it were a new discovery, that class had a lot to do with the tragedy of the Titanic, which is seen as the symbolic ending of 19th-century privilege. This show, with a politically conscious stoker as one of its heroes, is no exception. We meet the captain, ironically relishing this as his last voyage before retirement, and the head of the shipping-line, a melodramatic villain all round, who is constantly urging increases in speed so that his product will have the edge in future advertising. (It's his fault there aren't enough boats; he wanted the deck-space in order to provide additional first-class luxuries.) In Richard Jones's production, Stewart Laing's sets go elaborately but not very excitingly up and down to reveal different cross-sections of the ship and its population. His costumes fall someway short of elegance, the seams metaphorically showing. When the ship finally hits its iceberg, the convention totally changes, the set disappears, and we watch a model ship cheerily steaming toward its doom. As a way of rendering the climactic moment, this is insufferably cute.

Maury Yeston's copious score includes a couple of neat ragtime-era pastiches but otherwise goes in for elaborate and extended underpinning of the action. It works well in the first half for premonition; in the second act, it starts to get in the way. Music, we are always told, can speed things up. But it can also slow them down, especially when words are sung that could just as well be said. Yeston's lyrics, much inferior to his music, are plodding and prosaic; once the crisis is under way, they can be maddening. Song titles such as Wake up, Wake up! and Dressed in Your Pyjamas in the Grand Salon , as the stewards rouse the passengers, should give you the idea. As the boats are lowered, those who are leaving and those who are staying find time to salute each other with an inspirational chant called We'll Meet Tomorrow . This is Broadway striking with a vengeance; and it strikes again when a Jewish couple who have elected to stay and drown together toast one another in a last bottle of vintage champagne, the man concluding their duet by smashing the glass beneath his feet as in a wedding ceremony. Doubtless Titanic had to be a musical to reach the Broadway stage at all. It didn't have to be that kind of musical.

Peter Stone's book carries the story with dignity and economy, and allows a stageful of good performances from an ensemble cast. (Alphabetical billing and all that jazz.) To particularize; John Cunningham keeps his beard wagging bravely as the captain, Michael Cerveris's architect is all neurotic sympathy, ending his life scribbling plans for a new craft free of all social barriers, as around him furniture slides along the tilting floor. Jennifer Piech rivets eye and ear as a sparky young Irishwoman, off to America in steerage to find a father for her unborn baby. In second class -- whose occupants seem livelier on the whole than either their betters or their inferiors -- Judith Blazor and Don Stephenson gleam with fun and mischief as an eloping couple. Everybody sings well. I laughed occasionally, cried quite often, but never held my breath.


  


Titanic is at the Lunt-Fontanne Theatre, New York, for an indefinite run .
