The end of last year was darkened by the death, shockingly early and sudden, of Gina Wilkinson. Wilkinson, who was 50 and seemed younger, had a triple career as actress, playwright and director. Her acting was typically, as she was herself, forceful and vivacious, a combination that served her well in Shakespearean comedy; I remember her most as Beatrice in Much Ado About Nothing, and especially as Rosaline in Loves Labour's Lost, both of them at the much-missed Festival of Classics in Oakville', Ont.s Coronation Park.

She wrote two adventurous plays with lively, speakable scripts: My Mothers Feet and Whistle Me Home, the latter a monologue in a bath, brilliantly performed by Tom Rooney. (Wilkinson and Rooney had been partners for the past 10 years; they had long planned to marry, and in fact did so shortly before her death, in the hospital.) But it was as a director that she seemed likely to make her greatest mark. Indeed she was already doing so.

After making her debut on the Toronto Fringe in 1997, she worked her way up at theatres all over the country (plus one in Germany), and had a decisive

breakthrough with her Shaw Festival production of Born Yesterday in 2009. She took this over at short notice, and demonstrated complete command of the Festival's daunting main stage. The following season her lunchtime production of J.M. Barrie's Half an Hour was less spectacular but at least equally impressive, and she was due to return to the Shaw this season to direct Candida.

Last year she had two very different shows on at the Young Centre in Toronto; Marcel Aymes Clerambard (for George Brown Theatre School), a French classic in the literary-whimsical tradition by which she professed herself amiably bemused, and for Soulpepper, Brian Friels Faith Healer. She was in Winnipeg in November, directing another Irish play, Conor McPherson's The Seafarer, for the Manitoba Theatre Centre, when she received a diagnosis of cervical cancer. If ever an artist was cut off in her prime, she was.
