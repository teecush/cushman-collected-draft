When We Were Singing runs at Buddies in Bad Times, 12 Alexander St., until Oct. 15. For tickets, call the box office at (416) 975-8555.

Three of the characters in When We Were Singing are female, one is male. Two of them are gay, two are straight. Three are actors, one is a writer. The quartet (even I can do that much math) are friends, and enjoy the kind of exclusive, not to say incestuous, relationship that only exists in plays and television sitcoms. They meet regularly over drinks, alcoholic or caffeinated, to exchange confidences and consolation. When at home, they spend much of their time telephoning one another, bitching about the third or fourth party. They dont seem to know anybody else, at least not closely. They have sex with people outside the circle, but that doesnt count.

When We Were Singing is, not surprisingly with that title, a musical, the first one to be written by a promising composer- lyricist from Vancouver, Dorothy Dittrich. (She's also the musical director, and a good one.)

It's a talented but very unfulfilling show. It unfolds over a single weekend, probably in the authors hometown. It starts with Abby, an unemployed actress, lamenting the imminent (and, shortly, the actual) loss of her latest boyfriend. We then move on, cross- cutting as we go, to Jenny, a documentary director and screenwriter, lamenting the loss of her latest girlfriend; to Les, actor-in- waiting and also acting waiter, lamenting the more brutal loss of his lover, to AIDS; and to Belinda, who, despite a lucrative career in commercials, just laments. Abby, heretofore heterosexual, finds herself in bed with Jenny, to their mutual surprise, though not apparently to that of the two others. End of story, though not end of show. There is a whole act of regrets and readjustments still to come.

Satisfying plays have been made of less, though Im not sure that satisfying musicals have. If a show is all about feelings and relationships, then it needs to dig into the people who are having them, and though song can be an admirable method of doing this, it cannot easily do the job unsupported. When We Were Singing might more aptly be titled When Aren't We Singing?, because the characters never stop; Dittrich has written a sung-through musical, just at the moment when the Andrew Lloyd Webbers are giving it up. To her credit, she never descends to the Webber level of quasi-recitative; she writes songs, one after another, in a variety of styles: not varied enough, though, to ward off monotony.

We learn that the characters are discontented with their professional and personal lots, but that hardly makes them special. They are, in fact, commonplace, without being representative. Writing of urban angst among the rapidly ageing young, she inevitably recalls the Sondheim of Company (but that had a book, if not a wholly satisfactory one, and a larger and more varied cast) and the Maltby and Shire of Closer Than Ever (but that was a revue, every number a new beginning). I'm not saying that what shes after is impossible. Ive known it to work, once: in William Finn's Falsettoland, which has a strong plot and solidly realized characters. His earlier March of the Falsettos is structurally diffuse and textually fuzzy, problems that beset Dittrich's show as well.

It starts on a promising level of acerbic wit, which it doesnt sustain. It goes far too easy on its characters, and it doesnt tell us enough about them. Nor does it always make their feelings very clear; the lyrics can be vague, and the music of Dittrich's more languid ballads is not much of an incentive to concentrate. The four characters share a nostalgic yearning for a level of luxurious ease they are never likely to attain; they sing a concerted number about escaping to the seaside, a pretty song beautifully harmonized and also (by Jeff Logue) beautifully lit. But the same note is struck too often elsewhere, both before and after. After all, none of us in the audience are likely to attain La Dolce Vita either, so while we can briefly sympathize with their frustrations, it gets irritating when we're asked to take them seriously.

Dittrich also writes compelling blues (blues in the theatre are usually compelling) and neat patter numbers. She also supplies what I can only heartlessly describe as the obligatory AIDS anthem. (Its also the title song.) We are, surely, past the stage of being moved by the news that the disease exists. If we are to sympathize with the bereaved, we have to know specific things about the relationship and the show doesnt tell us. And that is symptomatic of what's wrong generally; merely being told, to music, that people are suffering-but-surviving is not enough to make us care. Not even their recurring bouts of self-mockery are enough to do the trick.

The show has been lucidly and sympathetically staged by David Oiye and the performers are excellent. They all sing like birds, and they act, too. Marc Richard transcends some fairly routine writing as Les, defusing self-pity by nailing every bit of humour that comes his way. Jennifer Rayner is fresh-faced and bewildered as Abby, and Alison Woolridge melts agreeably in what seems to be the central role of Jenny; I assume that she's central because she gets the biggest of the big ballads. Paula Wolfson is both plangent and pungent, though she has trouble supporting her role description, which is that of a self-hating unloved alcoholic; this, in stage terms, should make her more fun than the material allows her to be. (She also, if youll permit me a bit of gratuitous stereotyping, seems to be wrongly christened. Belinda is a cuddly name; the character strikes me as more of a Jackie. Im really not kidding; we need these things as footholds.)

Note, by the way, the past tense in the title: a reference to a past, perhaps illusory, time when the characters were happy. But if characters are now, metaphorically, past the time of singing, then writing a musical about them, especially an all-sung musical, has to be an uphill job; ditto responding to it. Here's to the author's next.
