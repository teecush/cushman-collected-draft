Anna Chatterton and Evalyn Parry, known collectively as the Independent Aunties, are two performers who appear, like so many others, to be better than their material. Sympathy is tempered by the fact that they write the material themselves.

They are appearing in the Stage 3 season at Theatre Passe Muraille, in a quintuple bill, in which four of the five plays occur after the intermission. The one that comes before, obviously the longest and theoretically the most substantial, is titled Frances, Mathilda and Tea. Frances, who is short and in black, and Mathilda, who is tall and in red, are genteel middle-aged ladies of the Victorian era, and tea is what they are taking: a birthday tea in honour of their mother, who is dead. It is somewhat surprising to find them as part of an avowedly experimental season on a contemporary Toronto stage, and dispiriting to find them living up so completely to received ideas about Victorian womanhood. If the tea table itself isn't enough of a tip-off, we find them to be repressed, spiteful and generally lacking the advantages of more enlightened generations. They spend this day, and presumably most others, playing mutually exploitative mind-games.

Mathilda likes to taunt Frances with allusions to her own happy and productive days as a memsahib in British India, though even if she's fantasizing, which seems likely, its strange that she should feel obliged to identify her late spouse as "my husband Charles" every single time she mentions him. Frances likes to trap Mathilda into playing physically humiliating party games. There is a revelation at the end but the characters have been so little explored, their milieu so much taken for granted, that it is difficult to care about what has been revealed. The best you can say is that both actresses, especially Parry as Mathilda, are good at the voices.

Their cartooning skills are further deployed in the succeeding playlets, collectively billed as The Mysterious Shorts. They arent all that mysterious, and they don't all seem short, but the first one qualifies on both counts. It gives us Gertrude Stein and Alice B. Toklas, conversing in brusque non sequiturs drawn from Stein's own writings. It goes on just long enough to establish the ladies identities and then abruptly stops, which is probably appropriate.

Next they play female reporters from a 30s or '40s newspaper movie, bereft of their scoop and also of any wit or point that might carry the sketch past the initial pleasure of recognition. Parry at least manages a super parody of Rosalind Russell (the program credits both His Girl Friday and The Women) at her toughest, with a touch of Katharine Hepburn thrown in, a matter not so much of vocal tones as of vocal angles. Then, even more protracted and less amusing, come the 1960s, which are really cartooned, giving us Chatterton as a frustrated housewife named Petronella and Parry as a dubious emissary of liberation known as SuperStacked.

The last segment, pegged to the 1980s and titled A Drop Dead Gorgeous, Tanned, Thin Sweet Valley Adventure, gives us, at last, a good joke: only one, and it's pretty much given away in the title, but weve learned to be grateful. A golden generation of complacent California girls (and a few boys), having gone to sleep one night truly content, wake to find that all their charms have been stolen. They have, in fact, Lost Their Adjectives.

The breathless tone of the eras teen fiction is, I am going to assume, accurately captured, though the story doesn't make sense. The thief turns out to be not some dumpy and dumped-on outsider but a girl whom our twin heroines (identical, we're told, but with nothing else in common -- all right, two good jokes) had always regarded as a serious threat; which she couldnt have been unless she had Adjectives of Her Own. Taken as a whole, the evening suggests that women were oppressed but inwardly rebellious in the 19th century; achieved style, if not actual independence, from the 20s to the '40s; and frivolously blew it in the '60s and after. It also indicates that white gentiles are as vulnerable as any other of the ethnic groups represented in Stage 3 to stereotype and to self- stereotype.

The Alchemy Theatre, a small-scale operation specializing in the classics (which seems, in practice, to mean just Shakespeare), offers a Julius Caesar that is most successful with the funeral scene in the Forum. This, considering the lack of resources, may appear surprising, but in fact the enforced economy here works better than many other productions half-hearted attempts at spectacle. Half a dozen actors, clustered in the auditorium wearing beaked masks, make an excellent symbolic crowd, and their few spoken lines function as they should: as brilliant shorthand for the shifting emotions of a crowd.

Brutus (Kyle McDonald) unpacks his oration with logic, and with a palpable self-satisfaction in that logic. Antony (Chris Cocoluzzi) then wipes him off the popular radar with a delivery of the friends, Romans, countrymen speeches that superbly blends genuine passion over Caesars death with a ruthless skill in manipulating his own and his hearers' emotions. I really didn't want him to stop. Alone in the cast, he has a real sense of pace. Even more important, the rhythms of his speech match those of the characters thoughts. He is less successful in his subsequent scenes, in which it seems the actor rather than the character is dangerously pleased with himself.

Brutus is played with intentional smugness, which is justifiable up to a point, but should not constitute the whole performance. Signs of humanity -- toward his wife or his page -- are ruthlessly obliterated, or maybe were not noticed in the first place. True, this is a political play from which nobody comes out well -- an honest political play, in other words -- but the characters are complex men, not bugs.

Simon Michellepiss nondescript production hardly lives up to his company's high-sounding slogan ("Classical Theatre for the Modern Mind"). It consists of lines spoken more or less intelligently but without much depth, and of bodies shunted rather awkwardly around a confined space.

The black drapes, off-the-peg togas and inexperienced cast are all economically understandable, but they take a toll. So does the textual editing that has the minor conspirators, an ignoble bunch, returning as Brutus's and Cassiuss staff officers, who are the opposite. (And, in one case, commit suicide to prove it.) The most ingenious rewriting is applied to Casca, the number-three conspirator and, textually, the number-one assassin. In this version he doesnt get to stab Caesar in the back or anywhere else, but he does change sides, ending up as one of those men whom nobody likes.

Still, that central scene is really exciting, far more so than it usually is. Or perhaps I should say than it used to be, because Julius Caesar, in theory one of the most popular plays and one of those we all studied in school, is these days hardly ever done. I don't know why it's slipped so far down the charts. Its certainly not for lack of relevance. I think its because of all the tragedies it offers the least scope for sex and violence. Or anyway for sex.

- - -

It's fitting that John Mighton should have won this year's Siminovitch Prize. His Half Life has created more ripples than any new Canadian play since The Drawer Boy. Equally gratifying is the plays current success (in its original production) in Glasgow. Its rare for a play to cross the ocean, in either direction, and to be applauded with the same seriousness on both shores.

- Francesca, Mathilda and Tea and The Mysterious Shorts run through Nov. 6 at Theatre Passe Muraille, 16 Ryerson Ave., 416-504- 8980. Julius Caesar closes tomorrow at the Alchemy Theatre, 133 Tecumseth St., 416-998-7503.
