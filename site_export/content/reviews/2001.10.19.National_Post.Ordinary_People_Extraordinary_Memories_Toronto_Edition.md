ELISA'S SKIN

Tarragon Theatre Extra Space, Toronto

Carole Frechette wrote Elisa's Skin as the product of an exchange scheme between writers from Montreal and Brussels; the condition was that each author from one city write something inspired by the other. Inspiration comes in unpredictable ways, but for an outsider it is difficult to discern anything in Frechette's play that needs to have sprouted in Brussels, apart from a profusion of references to local landmarks. There is nothing in it that could not happen equally well if not anywhere in Canada, then certainly in Quebec. It does imply a European-style cafe culture.

Not that much in the play does anything so vulgar as happen. It is largely a monologue for Elisa herself, who sits nursing a drink at a patio table and talks about her love affairs. Two discernibly different men figure in her reminiscences, and while we are still trying to work out whether these relationships would have been sequential or juggled and whether they might still be going on, the author hits us with something else. Elisa starts talking as if she were the male half of a similarly remembered romance, although her voice and attitude remain the same. It is all very mysterious and the mystery, at least for me, diverts attention from what she is actually saying.

All is revealed when Elisa, speaking -- we may be tolerably sure - - in her own person, tells of a purely social encounter with a Young Man, who promptly manifests himself onstage. It is he, it turns out, who has put her up to this: to sitting around in public places and pouring out her emotional history. When she protests that she has only a limited amount of history to pour, he tells her to adopt other people's; it will be a kindness to them and therapeutic for her. So this is what she does: becomes a conduit for the extraordinary memories of ordinary people. Once we know this, her speeches become a lot less puzzling and rather more gripping.

The reason Elisa needs therapy is that she has skin problems. She constantly invites us to inspect her knees, her neck, her elbows, and see for ourselves whether there is just not too much skin there, falling in folds. (There isn't, by the way.) The cause, she learns, is that there are all these stories inside her, pushing to get out. This, plainly, is not a medical drama about middle-aged sag. It's a metaphor for something. I think it's about the writer -- I mean the writer in general, not just the author of this play -- about the need, the obligation, to give inarticulate people a voice; if you have the talent, don't use it -- you will explode. Taken literally, it just seems sentimental. Either way, the conceit tells us little about Elisa herself, and hers is the presence we are stuck with.

The writing of the play seems to be excellent; certainly John Murrell's translation is a string of pearls. There is a feeling here for the erotica of the everyday, coupled with a sensible regret that it doesn't actually happen every day. The incidents are believable, and so are the feelings that go before and after them. But it is still only one idea, repeated. The emotions Elisa reports are essentially disembodied, however physical the details; and Elisa, endlessly parading an unspecified sensibility, comes to seem like a Marguerite Duras heroine, only with laughs. Not enough of them, though: Like most solo plays that aren't very funny, this one, scarcely more than an hour long, becomes first monotonous and then boring.

This is not, as far as I can tell, the fault of Jackie Maxwell's production, which seizes on every piece of wit that comes its way. It has a prettily exaggerated little-town-square design by Ken Garnhum and mockingly moody lighting by Andrea Lundy. Tanja Jacobs matches them in style with a bold performance of a shy person. In a red dress with short, bunned hair and emphatic earrings, she seems constantly amazed and accepting.

She admits to being wide-hipped; she is also wide-mouthed and wide-eyed, and the eyes have a habit of migrating to the corners of their sockets and staying there, peering out in expectant disbelief. This happens especially when Patrick Galligan appears, as Elisa's occasional interlocutor; he shares with her a talent for pouncing, on both words and pauses, and though he obviously has far less opportunity of displaying it than she does, they are equally effective with it. Their few moments of duet and interaction are blessed ones.
