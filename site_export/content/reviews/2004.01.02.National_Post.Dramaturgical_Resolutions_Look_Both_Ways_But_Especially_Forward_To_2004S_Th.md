Kristen Thomson, already in line for being Toronto's favourite actress, clinched the title in March, 2001, when she appeared at the Tarragon Extra Space in the solo piece I Claudia. In this she gave three fine performances and one enchanting one, the last being as the title character, a lonely and indignant schoolgirl. She also -- who knew? -- wrote the piece herself, subsequently carrying off Dora Awards in both her capacities.

The first night was the most joyous I remember attending in Toronto, local girl making better than we could possibly have imagined; since then she has performed the show in Victoria, in Hungary and at World Stage, and she is now back with it again at the Extra Space (Dec. 30 to Feb. 8). We'd like of course to see her do something else, and even write something else. (She's also taken time off to have a baby. How selfish can a lady get?) Meanwhile, I Claudia remains a treat that everyone should enjoy. It lifts the heart just to see Thomson put on Claudia's mask: an icon already.

She is just one of the heavier hitters descending on Toronto theatres after the holiday lull. Talk about versatility: Ronnie Burkett -- actor, dramatist, director, singer and, oh yes, master puppeteer -- brings his latest piece, Provenance, to CanStage's Berkeley Street Theatre (opens Jan. 9). Burkett has just won the annual Herbert Whittaker/Drama Bench Award; this is administered by the Canadian Theatre Critics Association, and we done right. He is also, after Robert Lepage and the Cirque du Soleil, the Canadian theatre artist whose name means most abroad.

In the Tarragon main space, Judith Thompson directs her own new play, Capture Me (Jan. 6). The cast includes Soulpepper's Nancy Palk and the husband-and-wife team of Tom McCamus and Chick Reid. At the Passe Muraille Backspace, the admirable Randy Hughson and Jane Spidell make up the company of Tom Walmsley's Blood (Jan. 22), in which they play a brother and sister who are, apparently, more than usually tight-knit. Kate Lynch directs.

Martha Henry, first lady of Stratford and maybe of Canada, makes a rare Toronto appearance in Michael Frayn's Copenhagen at the Winter Garden (Jan. 7, at last.) Her colleagues in this multiple- award-winning English piece about war, nuclear physics and moral responsibility, are two Shaw Festival luminaries, Michael Ball and Jim Mezon, and Diana Leblanc directs. This show is a co-production from Ottawa's National Arts Centre and Halifax's Neptune Theatre, and won high praise in both places.

In Ottawa itself the NAC, under director Marti Maraden, stages Hamlet (Jan. 8). Tom Rooney, one of the young actors most esteemed by his peers, plays the prince, while a strong supporting cast includes Fiona Reid and Ben Carlson, two actors who to date have played criminally little Shakespeare, and -- with enough experience in that vein for seven -- Stratford's veteran of veterans, Douglas Campbell.

Campbell started his career long ago in Britain. This month brings to Toronto three great Britons (an appearance by any one of whom would be an event) in a single show: Vanessa Redgrave, Ian Richardson and Donald Sinden. Their vehicle is The Hollow Crown (Princess of Wales Theatre, Jan. 27); the anthology-chronicle of jovial skeletons in royal closets that has been a Royal Shakespeare Company standby since -- are you ready for this? -- 1961. (Which reminds me, the Toronto production of The Mousetrap closes this month.)

Restrospecting for a moment: I apologize for omitting Adam Pettle's Sunday Father from my survey of 2003, the more so since it was one of the best plays of the year. I should also have shed an angry tear over the enforced demise of duMaurier World Stage. This festival's last lineup included some work that was inept and some that was agonizingly boring, but it also brought us masterpiece productions from Poland, Belgium, South Africa (The Mysteries) and the United States (Glengarry Glen Ross).

And I have to pay a tribute to the late Alan Bates who, apart from his fine film career, was one of the best of the post-war generation of English stage actors. (He managed, in a peculiarly British fashion, to steer an even course between movie star and jobbing thesp.) Canada didn't see him at his best; he was miscast in a dull production of The Master Builder that came to the Royal Alex a few years ago, and -- at Stratford in 1967 -- seems to have been a low-key Richard III and (in The Merry Wives of Windsor) a forced Ford.

He was a great ironic actor -- savage or tender, depending -- rather than a heroic one, and also a man with great historical timing -- he was in the original productions of no less than three epoch-defining British plays. In John Osborne's Look Back in Anger he was Cliff, the stoical best friend; in Harold Pinter's The Caretaker he was Mick, the flash, menacing brother; in Simon Gray's Butley he was, finally, the protagonist, the seedy academic falling apart. He was even better as the shiny-smooth protagonist of Gray's subsequent play Otherwise Engaged. Like Albert Finney, his classmate (the word "class" applies here in both its senses) and contemporary, he kept getting better as he got older. He won a Tony for his last stage appearance, in Fortune's Fool, after Turgenev. I wish we could have seen it.
