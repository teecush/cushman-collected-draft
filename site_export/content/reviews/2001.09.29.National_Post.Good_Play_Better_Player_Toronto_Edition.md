SKYLIGHT

Tarragon Theatre

- - -

David Hare's plays are routinely praised for seamlessly blending the personal with the political. It would be nice if that were true. What actually happens is that he tears the two spheres into alternating strips, which then get pasted on to his characters.

In Skylight, which is certainly one of his best plays, there are only three characters, and two of them -- ex-lovers and political opposites -- get most of the stage-time. They are Tom, a restaurant mogul, and Kyra, 20 years his junior, who was once his employee and a virtual member of his family, and had a six-year affair with him. It ended when Tom's wife found out, at which point Kyra disappeared from his life. She now lives in a bleak North London flat and teaches at a bleak East London school, with a long, bleak daily commute between.

Both characters -- the powerful, perversely attractive man and the idealistic woman -- have their prototypes in earlier Hare plays; she, in particular, has been a recurrent figure. (Canadian content note: She used to be known in London as the Kate Nelligan role.)

Kyra is a more engaging figure than some of her predecessors, partly because it is only in the second half of the play that she starts haranguing anyone. We meet her on a snowy evening on which she receives two visitors from her past. The first is Tom's son, Edward, in his "gap year" between school and university, who tells Kyra how badly his father is handling his grief over his wife's death from cancer. The second is Tom himself, who, of course, wants Kyra back and is baffled by her current choices.

In the play's first, and better, half, Hare handles with remarkable skill the job of filling us in on the characters' past lives (it seldom sounds as if they are telling one another things for our benefit) while moving the new dramatic situation forward. We want to know what happened then and we want to know what will happen now.

It is all done on the arc of one continuous conversation, itself integrated with the ongoing domestic detail of Kyra's life. She is trying to cook dinner, and in Dennis Garnhum's fine Tarragon production, this is done with superlative realism. Food proves important in this play, and when Kyra eventually sets two places at table, it is a major climax.

One of the wonderfully fortifying things about Yanna McIntosh's performance of Kyra is the businesslike concentration she brings to all this; it makes you believe in her. Another is the quiet, sad, amused demeanour with which she watches Tom striding (physically and verbally) around her terrain, and the wry, laconic, perfectly calibrated way in which she replies to him; she never puts a foot or a word wrong.

Tom, whatever emotional wounds he may be bearing, has the implacable confidence of the self-made rich. Hare is a clever writer rather than a funny one, but he makes you smile at Tom's attack on that sloppy word "spiritual" and laugh hard at his declaration that a personal driver, whatever Kyra's conscientious qualms on the subject, is paid (and well paid) not to drive but to wait.

Thus far the personal and political -- which is mainly implicit - - run pretty much in tandem. And, by the way, the attraction between these two people is not only possible but extremely likely.

In Act Two, things come apart. Kyra delivers -- though McIntosh doesn't just deliver them, they flow out of her -- passionate defences of her work and blistering attacks on "the self-pitying rich" who mock her for doing it. (This play's six-year delay in reaching Toronto hasn't hurt it any.)

Like Shaw's Major Barbara, who worked in the identical part of London, she challenges Tom to come and see her at it. But by now the play has slipped its emotional moorings. It's as if the relationship were invented just to set up the speechifying. We find we have little idea of who she used to be and how it relates to who she is now. We may believe in the possibility of the six-year menage a trois in Tom's house, but we have no emotional purchase on it. We don't believe in the wife, from whose last days the play derives its title-image.

And we don't believe that Tom bleeds. Joseph Ziegler, stalking the stage with head and body cocked (and bearing a spooky resemblance to the author), has a sardonic edge but not an emotional one. There is no sense of his being a wounded bull who has fought his way up; Ziegler's is a second-generation swagger. The play is partly an exercise in class-reversal but it's equally hard to believe in McIntosh as the daughter of an ultra-conservative country solicitor. And yes, her being black has something to do with that.

The play is insistent that Tom and Kyra are in love but I couldn't care by the end whether or not this couple got together again, or why. I believed, though, in Tom's offstage relationship with his son; Brandon McGibbon's awkward Edward is immensely appealing and potentially infuriating. John Thompson's set looks too Canadian-comfortable, but the atmospherics (the occasional brush of snow on the window) are perfect. All faults allowed, this is still the best dramatic writing currently in Toronto, and McIntosh's is incomparably the finest performance.
