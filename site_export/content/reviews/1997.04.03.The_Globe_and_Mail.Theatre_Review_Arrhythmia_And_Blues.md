ARRHYTHMIA AND BLUES

THEATRE REVIEW

ARRHYTHMIA AND BLUES

Thursday, April 3, 1997

Directed by Jim Warren

Written by Howie Wiseman


  


Starring Andrew Massingham, Rena Polley,


  


Catherine Hayos and Steven Morel


  


Rating: *½

Arrhythmia could leave you blue

Trying to get laughs from this source material

would give anyone a heart attack.

Special to The Globe and Mail


  


THE title, a forced and feeble pun, gives almost too much warning. You expect the worst. Arrhythmia and Blues is not quite that. But it is pretty bad.

There is a screenwriter called Leah (Rena Polley) who is now scripting her first TV sitcom. Her modest concept is an updating of the Book of Job, with Job recast as a woman, and God -- to judge from the brief excerpts vouchsafed us over the theatre sound-system -- as a Jewish mother. (Satan remains male. Wouldnt you know.) Trying to get laughs from this source material would be enough to give anyone a heart attack, and that is what happens to Leah. She checks into the emergency ward of a Winnipeg hospital. For the rest of the action she stays there.

Sharing the space with her are Mel (Andrew Massingham), a hypochondriac of awesome proportions with a special fixation on his bladder and bowels, and two members of staff. These are Dr. Margolis (Catherine Hayos) and Robert (Steven Morel), a nurse, both harassed and overworked, both -- as far as we can tell -- efficient and conscientious but humanly imperfect.

What is supposed to bind the quartet together is hard to make out. True, they're all under pressure, but who isn't? Leahs impending deadline and impatient employers push her further into trauma; at one point she tries to discharge herself, aided -- for inscrutable reasons -- by the omnipresent Mel. Presumably we are meant to see Leah as the female Job she is trying to write about. The story of Job depends for its effectiveness on two things. He was unusually happy and prosperous before his downfall, and he fervently believed in the God who tested and tormented him. Leahs precariously lucky break in television-land doesn't compare with Job's accumulation of family and livestock, her personal life consists of an ex-fiancé of whom we know nothing, and her thoughts and beliefs are a blank. When her boss fires her immediately after sending her chocolates its a sobering moment -- the only one in the show -- but it does not rank as a biblical catastrophe. It doesnt rank as anything.

The program describes the piece as written by Howie Wiseman. This "written by" credit -- as opposed to the simple traditional by -- is one I have learned to distrust; it suggests a plea of diminished responsibility. Certainly the writing here is weightless; loose ends trail, relationships fail to relate. Dr. Margolis may have been responsible for a patient's brain death. The patient is still on the ward, playing percussion. I thought for a while that this was just an atmospheric effect, but no, we actually get some hurried glimpses of him, beating out that rhythm on a drum. This is not, to put it naively, credible. And what's a chronic patient doing on an emergency ward?

The doctor is also pregnant; why? (No smart answers, please.) The actress doubles as the voice of God in Leahs sitcom, so maybe shes the Old Testament deity about to give birth to the New. And Mel plays the voice of Satan, so perhaps that's why he tempts Leah to leave the hospital, thereby courting certain death. On the other hand, most of the time, Mel is a victim, and I wish I hadn't started trying to make sense of all this.

Jim Warrens direction is pretty slick in the matter of moving beds and screens around, but it doesnt impose much coherence on the play's story or even, as things get increasingly frantic, on its geography. Stiletto Theatre Company, who present the show, have won praise in the past for their visual skills, and the actors here all look their parts to perfection, but they don't seem good with words. Each exists in his or her own bubble, with none of the pace or flexibility with which people, on stage or in life, actually talk to one another. Andrew Massingham, doing Mel as a gentle comedy turn, comes nearest to turning this liability into an asset, but even he gets wearing. Both the women are thoroughly wooden. Admittedly the dialogue doesnt give them much help but since they, along with the writer, are credited with "creating" the show, its difficult to feel too sorry for them.

One more thing. The hospital is closing down. The piece was apparently conceived before the official assault on Ontario's health-care system turned murderous, but there are obviously lines that now resonate unbearably (and I imagine a few have been added). The show is on the side of the angels, but that isn't enough. In fact, it gets to seem like unearned income. The giggly pretentiousness in the title makes for an uncommon combination, but in this case it's all too appropriate.
