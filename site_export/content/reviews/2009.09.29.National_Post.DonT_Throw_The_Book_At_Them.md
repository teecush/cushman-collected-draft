THE BOYS IN THE PHOTOGRAPH

Royal Alexandra Theatre

---

It's amazing how much better Andrew Lloyd Webber's music sounds when you don't have to spend the whole evening listening to it. The Boys in the Photograph (Mirvish Productions via the Manitoba Theatre Centre) is a re-working of The Beautiful Game, which was staged in London in 2000. The game is soccer; the boys are a teenage Catholic team in the ravaged Belfast of 1969. This makes it the first Webber musical, with the possible exception of Evita, not to be set in some kind of fairyland. Most of his shows have had a stateless sound; there are a couple of ballads in this one that felt as if they'd been written before, but most of the music gains enormously, in flavour and in backbone, from being rooted in a real place at a real dire time. It gains even more from not having to do all the work; this is a book musical with dialogue, not one of those pretend-operas in which the story gets told mainly through droning recitative. The result is the freshest Lloyd Webber score since ... well, probably since Joseph and the Amazing Technicolor Dreamcoat.

The built-in hazard of a book musical, though, is that it has to have a book; and the one that Ben Elton has written and directed has enough holes to admit a whole fleet of British Army tanks. It does one important thing right; it resists the temptation to make the show a Protestant-Catholic Romeo and Juliet story. The principal lovers here come from the same side of the tracks. He is John, the team's star player with professional potential and no strong political feelings; she is Mary, a peace activist. Brutality and deception, from both factions, put their relationship under siege; John is framed, and does time in Long Kesh prison, from which he emerges the fanatic he had previously refused to be: an irony at least as potent now as then.

The show never downplays the grimness of the situation, but it can't find a consistent way of presenting it. That's not just because it now, in an effort to reverse its comparative failure in London, has a happy ending. This feels tacked on, but it might be excused as being in tune with a newly hopeful

Northern Ireland. But both the tone and the structure are unfocused, split between gag-writing and platitude-spouting.

If Elton's book-writing is amateurish, his lyrics are dreadful: "Football is the only reason/God Almighty gave us feet": Those are good lines, but they wave forlornly from a muddy field of formlessness, rhymelessness, and -- worst of all -- pointlessness. The songs never tell us anything we don't already know, either as action or commentary. In the second act, you dread the sound of the orchestra starting up because you know that the story, which has gathered some steam, is going to be stopped in its tracks for yet another stentorian meditation on how terrible sectarian warfare is and how we should all love one another. It's even worse when the band doesn't play. Erica Peck as Mary has to preach the score's climactic sermon a capella, meaning the music has no chance of drowning out the words; I was embarrassed, not by her but for her. When the lyrics do take a stab at developing story and character, you wish they wouldn't. As a writer, Elton likes to end a scene with a punchline; as a director, he leaves his actors to slink off after the punch has landed. The staging of more literal violence is remarkably unthreatening. The show begins with rival gangs dancing their antagonism on the streets. This can hardly help recalling West Side Story, though I'm not sure if Tracey Flye's choreography counts as imitation or parody. The production in general looks drab and trundling.

After all of which, it's awkward to have to admit that the show is always involving and ultimately moving. Credit goes to the history, to the music and to the fact that the creators, however much they fumble, do seem genuinely involved with the subject. The cast helps, too: Richard Harte, Brandon McGibbon, Tracy Dawson and Jacquelyn French give pungent if oddly uneasy accounts of team players and team fans, while Richard McMillan as priestly team coach takes some stock jokes and good intentions and makes a man of them. But it's John and Mary who really lump our throats by letting us partake in their growing up. Tony LePage is lithe, sympathetic and, when bitterness is called for, powerful. Peck is superb, confirming what she promised in We Will Rock You: that she's a first-rate theatre singer who's also a charming and economical actress, forceful without forcing and capable of projecting a passionate moral strength. It's a strangely admirable show. Let me put it like this: It's often dumb, but it's not stupid. - The Boys in the Photograph is at the Royal Alexandra Theatre until Nov. 1.
