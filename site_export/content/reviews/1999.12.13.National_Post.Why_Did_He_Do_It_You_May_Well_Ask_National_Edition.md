Carols and seasonal novelties are playing as the audience enters the theatre, and there is a tree in the centre of the playing area. A young man is making some desultory attempts at decorating it. James Harkness' Pills, a success on the Toronto fringe, has been brought to the Factory for Christmas, but nobody is likely to walk into it expecting a happy holiday show

The young man is sitting on a small trunk, for lack of anything more comfortable, and he is surrounded by whisky and cigarettes. He is a former mental patient who has decided to give up on his medication; hence the play's title, though of course it ramifies.

He is called Jason. (At least I think he is; the characters have been christened in the program but the dialogue doesn't know from names and, since the actors are listed alphabetically rather than in order of appearance, I may have got them mixed up.) He is soon joined by his boy-friend Bill, who is older and a car mechanic. Love is in the air, but is greatly outweighed by desperation. It's a familiar message: this may be the season of goodwill but it's a wilderness out there.

One way for the audience to track a path through the jungle is to regard the action as the chronicle of one man's efforts to get laid. Bill tries to get it on with every other member of the three-person cast in turn and is thwarted every time. He is about to have sex with Jason, when the latter spies a dead mouse in the corner of the room, poisoned apparently by the hated woman in the flat upstairs. He is distressed and the mood is broken.

Bill wanders out into the park where he encounters Ron, a middle- aged teacher. They seem to be happily picking one another up, when suddenly Ron turns on Bill and savagely beats him.

Lurking in the shadows is Marie, Ron's colleague and sexual companion ; she circles the two of them, looking on with great fascination, and you wonder whether she will offer Bill succour or further torment. As it turns out, she gives him both.

Bill staggers home, to find Jason even more bloodied than himself. The boy has gone berserk and turned murderous. He is also conscience-stricken; and he ends the play by doing what might, in the circumstances, be called the decent thing.

Why, he keeps demanding of nobody in particular, did I do it? It's a good question, one the audience might well ask about everybody in the play. We are interested enough to want to ask, but not enough to care what the answers might be. (And of course the author isn't giving any.) We can tell that at least three of the characters are to some degree disturbed, and we can see -- if only because a string of other plays have subjected us to almost Pavlovian conditioning -- that they are the signs and victims of an alienated economically driven society.

There is a sort of effectiveness in taking the contrast between poverty and platitude to extremes by setting it at Christmas; but it's not as if nobody had ever thought of it before.

It's an easy irony: factually accurate, and excellent material for charitable appeals, but needing to be thoroughly re-imagined if it's to be the basis of a play. Here it reduces to a few lines of text and the choice of music to usher the audience in and out with.

Harkness has a talent for lean dialogue, and for depicting the bonds between characters. As directed by John Palmer, they work better as pairs than as individuals.

This is especially true (perhaps because we spend less time with them) of the two pedagogues lurking in the park; this seems to be a regular rendezvous for them, though whether gay-bashing forms an invariable part of their agenda is one of the things left uncertain.

Anyway, they move entertainingly from glum shop-talk ("done your report-cards yet?") to erotic banter ("dirty old man!"; "dirty older woman!"). When their prose turns pompously purple ("maybe we'll continue to thrive on sugar and fat while the rest of the rest goes hungry" -- I trust I got the last word right, the actor's delivery went very muffled) we can hope that they are either satirizing themselves or being satirized.

The couple give great scope for ambiguity -- who knows if they are even, in any orthodox sense, lovers? -- and the two actors run or amble with it: Sky Gilbert displays a considerable gift for subdued looming menace, while Caroline Gillis suggestively bobs and weaves.

The play's politics seem surprisingly conservative: Teachers are monsters and Jason wouldn't have gone so completely off the rails if he'd kept taking the tablets. Gil Garratt brings him to full febrile life, while Tony Nappo carries off Bill's largely reactive role in a quiet vein of sympathetic smouldering. Jason's teasing of him -- "I'm your dirty little secret -- that's exciting") and his reaction to it are among the play's more successful hints at hidden depths. A comparatively happy Christmas to all of them.
