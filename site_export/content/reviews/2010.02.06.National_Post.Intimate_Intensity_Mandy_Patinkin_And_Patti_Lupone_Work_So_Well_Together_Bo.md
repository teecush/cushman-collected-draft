According to Patti Lu-Pone, a very smart booker approached her agent and said: "We have Mandy, how about Patti?" According to Mandy Patinkin (I spoke to them separately, in two different calls), someone a few years ago said, We have this new theatre in Dallas, we have Patti, how about Mandy? However it happened, this was the start of Patti and Mandy, the show that they have been doing together "on and off for about three or four years," across the United States and in Australia and New Zealand, and that they are about to bring to Toronto.

Its a reunion. Thirty years ago, LuPone played the title role in the Broadway production of Evita, with Patinkin playing Che. She was a known musical performer, even star, at the time; he wasn't. He thought of himself as an actor, not a singer; still does in fact: I'm an actor; sometimes the words I act are set to music. When his agent, after the Evita auditions, called and told him hed got the part, "I didnt know what part."

The two of them hadn't worked together since, but they'd stayed in touch. When the idea came of doing an evening of songs together, they were adamant that it wouldnt be just an evening of songs. Patinkin had always those shows where one performer comes out and does his stuff, then the other comes on and does hers, and they only meet for some token duets at the finale. So they put together a program of, in LuPones words, showtunes that tell a story. A love story, naturally, because what else would you do with showtunes?

It was Patinkin who did most of the putting-together. LuPone didn't have the time or, one suspects, the inclination. According to Patinkin she said, presumably in her gum-chewing voice, "go ahead, doll." When the two of them got together they started, as she says, tweaking the lineup: a lineup that ranges from I Really Can't Stay (a. k.a. Baby, Its Cold Outside) to Everybody Says Dont, the latter an aggressive song from the Stephen Sondheim cult flop Anyone Can Whistle. There is quite a lot of Sondheim in the show, as you might expect. Both performers are widely associated with his work, though in fact Patinkin has only appeared in one of his musicals (he created the title role in Sunday in the Park with George, and had to search his memory to confirm that really was the only one) and LuPone in one-and-a-half, both revivals: She was Mrs. Lovett in Sweeney Todd and Rose in Gypsy, for which Sondheim wrote the lyrics. Patinkin says of his association with Sondheim that "for me, he's William Shakespeare. They both have an uncanny sense of human nature -- I think they both work from elements of darkness that they illuminate for us." Given both writers' unerring capacity for finding the right words, that isnt an extravagant assessment.

Theres also a lot of Rodgers and Hammerstein. We start with South Pacific," Lu-Pone says, "and end with the Bench Scene from Carousel -- that's the big love scene built around If I Loved You. Occasionally in the show, one or other of them is alone onstage, but mostly even if it's just one of them doing the singing, the other is right there reacting. Patinkin calls it the story of two souls"; also, less grandiloquently, "a balls-out entertaining evening, thats nothing but fun, though he also adds we dont tell jokes -- we're not comics, we're actors." They also dance a bit: Ann Reinking has contributed what Patinkin calls "age-appropriate choreography.

Patinkin is known as an intense actor and especially singer, both in his love-it-or-hate-it performing style and in the volume and variety of work he takes on. Patinkin was in New Haven when I called, working on a new play called Compulsion, which takes its title from the American novel by Meyer Levin that was based on the Leopold and Loeb murder case. It isnt, though, based on the book; its the story of Levin's fight to get his adaptation of Anne Frank's diary onto the stage and of the plagiarism suit he launched against the authors whose version was used. (He even sued Otto Frank.) Patinkin plays the Levin character; asked if he thought the affair had destroyed Levins life, he first said that it did, then thought and amended it to it defined his life; it didnt destroy it." The show opened at the Yale Repertory Theatre last week, then was put on hold for a week so Patinkin and LuPone could fulfill their Toronto commitment. Later this year Patinkin will be re-united with Hal Prince, who directed him in Evita, for a new musical called Paradise Found. He plays the Shah's Grand Eunuch though it isn't an Oriental story; its set in Vienna, which explains why it uses music by Johann Strauss II, fitted with lyrics by the greatly underrated Ellen Fitzhugh, and with a book by the American playwright Richard Nelson. Susan Stroman co-directs with Prince, and the cast includes a half-dozen other Broadway luminaries. It opens in New Yorks favourite new try-out place, London.

LuPone is also known as an intense performer, and I asked her whether this accounted for the onstage chemistry between them. She says no ( "I have equal chemistry with Boyd Gaines, and he's very laid-back), but that Mandy and I have a combination onstage of sexual energy, personal energy, work energy. You can intellectualize but ... it's just our chemistry." She, too, sees herself as "primarily as an actor, led by lyrics, and adds that shes actually done more plays than musicals. Which is surprising but, as she also says, musicals have a higher profile." Patinkins take on their collaboration is that "it completely rests on our pleasure in each other, our love for each other; if you like being onstage, it doesn't get better than doing it with Patti. Which would be the best possible place to end, if it weren't trumped by Lu-Pone's definition of theatrical spontaneity: Theatre is an accident waiting to happen."
