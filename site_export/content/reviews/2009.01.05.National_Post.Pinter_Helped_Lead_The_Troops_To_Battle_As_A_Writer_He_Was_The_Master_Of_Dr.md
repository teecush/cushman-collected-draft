Maybe, if you're thinking of the 1960s as the British decade, your first stop should not be the Beatles. but Harold Pinter. He was in at the start. It was in April, 1960, that The Caretaker opened in London, and immediately became the Play To See. I doubt that any play since, British or other, has achieved the same instant and unquestioned cultural cachet. And it wasnt just a fashionable thing: The play has endured.

But in fact the thing -- the Pinter phenomenon -- had already happened; and not in the theatre but on television. Just a month earlier there had been a triumphant TV production of his previous full-length play The Birthday Party, a play that had famously crashed and burned on the London stage just two years earlier. There had, though, been a groundswell of critical and professional support that had ensured the author a reputation; and hed re-surfaced with a couple of one-act plays and some revue sketches. These had established his talent as a writer of dialogue that was pungently funny and also hung disturbingly in the air. He could conjure tension from words.

What they hadn't been able to show was that he could assemble these tensions into a good story. But this was what The Birthday Party, released into the living rooms of a nation, explosively proved. Admittedly it was a story from which bits seemed to have been left out; we never found out exactly why the jovially sinister Goldberg and Mc-Cann were terrorizing the reclusive mother's boy Stanley or where they were dragging him at the end. But that didnt matter; in fact in the context of this play, designed to tap into everybodys insecurities and everybody's evasiveness, it helped. That isn't always true -- sometimes, including in some of Pinters later plays, obscurity can just obscure -- but it is true here. It certainly held my family spellbound: "You didnt know what was going on," said my father, but you couldn't take your eyes off it. Or your ears: I remember my dad's astonished laughter when Goldberg rounded off the toasts and congratulations for Stanleys birthday with the traditional Yom Kippur greeting: "And well over the fast." The timing -- always Pinters greatest strength -- was uncanny; it came out of nowhere and was completely right. You could say the same of the play itself. After this firework display, the triumph of The Caretaker, a more contained piece with no rough narrative edges, was a walk.

Pinter claimed that his crosstalk mix of veiled threat and inconsequential defence derived from his East End youth, walking and talking his way out of trouble with anti-Semitic gangs. (Goldberg, the Jew as gangster and Pinter's only overtly Jewish character, may be his revenge. He's certainly drawn from life. Pinters father was a tailor. Goldberg, Ive always felt, had a previous career in gentlemen's outfitting.) The tramp and the two brothers in The Caretaker are avowedly based on people he met at a later period, as a struggling actor and writer. Davies, the possibly Welsh tramp who claims service in the colonies (I was over there--I was one of the first over there) is a tragi-comic creation worthy of Dickens. The English director Mike Alfreds claims that he's the last great character in British drama, and that the theatres subsequent decadence can be traced to its failure to come up with anything comparable. I think hes right, and not just about Britain. The Birthday Party has great lines; The Caretaker has great speeches. And always the words serve the action. Mostly they are action.

The Caretaker got Pinter tagged as "a kitchen sink dramatist," a label that stuck -- oddly, since, as he moved up socially, his plays did too. The Homecoming was the transition play: An East End family are visited by their son, now a successful academic, and his wife, who takes over; she has all the men on their knees. It's a brilliant, chilly piece, though not always credible: I imagine that Soulpepper, who are working their way through the Pinter canon, have it in their sights. I wish they, or someone,

would do The Collection, his most neglected play, first written for television and one of the very few TV plays to have transferred successfully to the stage. It's one of his most trenchant studies of sexual jealousy, the form of territorial warfare that most concerned him in his middle period. It was also the first of his plays to be directed by Peter Hall, whose icily elegant approach crystallized whats known as the Pinter style. Its a style from which subsequent productions have had to free themselves. Interestingly it's a style with which he himself as an actor had little to do. Playing a tyrant-boss in a revival of his early play The Hothouse he gave a brutally enjoyable performance, noticeably devoid of pregnant "Pinter pauses." But then, even as a playwright, I don't think he used pauses more than anyone else; he just notated them more.

Pinter did cultivate an image of inaccessibility, refusing to explain his plays to journalists or even actors. Theres a famous story of him directing one of his own plays and telling the cast, Of course, we cant be sure what the author meant here. Though to balance that, there's another story of him helping out an actor in a play of his that he wasn't directing by giving him the blessedly helpful note, "Well, he just wants to f---her, doesnt he?" I met him myself about a dozen times, unable on the first occasion to believe that it was happening: not because he was intimidating -- he couldnt have been friendlier -- but because he was such an established cultural fact. It was like being approached by T. S. Eliot.

To call Pinter a major talent is almost insulting. He was the greatest master of dramatic weaponry to appear in the last half-century. He had an innate feel for verbal and psychological violence: All plays are battles, but his are more nakedly so than most. That doesn't mean he always produced major work. The late short political plays are diminished, though they're recognizably the work of the same man; their torturers and interrogators are descendants of Goldberg and Mc-Cann and horribly believable. Maybe he could have written their scenes in his sleep but the fact remains he did write them, and nobody else could have. He was accurate -- and prescient -- in his attacks on American exceptional-ism, which he summed up as "Were American, so whatever we do is right," though his own counter position, amounting to "Its American, so it must be wrong," was just as simplistic.

Even the middle, and middle-class, plays strike me as inferior to the great early ones, with their cockney roots. The characters' idiom is impoverished and so, theatrically speaking, are they. For one very important thing, they aren't as funny. But thats a matter of degree: The heart is still there. Listening to John Gielgud reeling off the prose arias of the trickster-poet in No Mans Land, or hearing William Hutt deliver them 20 years later -- both actors plumbing the depths of the man's humiliation while letting us relish the way he tries to skate over them-- was to know, again, what drama is about.
