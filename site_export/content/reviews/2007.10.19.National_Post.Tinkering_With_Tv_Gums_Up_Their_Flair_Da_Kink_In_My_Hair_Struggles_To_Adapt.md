When Trey Anthony's 'da Kink in My Hair was a stage play, it was a breakout event: first on the Toronto fringe, then at the small uncommercial Theatre Passe Muraille, then in the big and commercial Princess of Wales, where its run kept getting extended, then in London, and tomorrow -- the world. It was by far the most successful show ever to have been written, staged and performed by and about black Toronto women, or indeed black Canadian women. It broke out in other ways, too. By the time it arrived at the Princess of Wales, it was a combination of play and musical. It was also a show whose characters addressed the audience in person, breaking right through the fourth wall.

So it's disappointing, indeed dispiriting, that the new TV version of 'da Kink should have stepped right back again. The characters remain within the frame, which itself seems to have shrunk. The TV series, like the play, is set in a Toronto hairdressing salon operated by two sisters, originally from Jamaica. The proprietor, Novelette, a.k.a. Nettie (Ordena Stevens-Thompson), is ambitious and efficient; her sister Joy (played by Anthony herself ) is a freer or at least more feckless spirit, who has come to Canada to help out. Next to them in prominence is Starr, a trainee, played by Anthony's co-writer, the striking Ngozi Paul. There's also a male stylist, Nigel, who fancies himself big-time and can't understand why Starr, at least at this writing, doesn't feel the same.

Joy put in time in Jamaica raising Nettie's son Dre, now an teenager facing the usual problems of fitting in at a new school -- added to the ongoing struggle over whether he is, spiritually speaking, his mother's son or his auntie Joy's boy. The fact that two of the show's five principals are male suggests a capitulation to received ideas of what a TV series, especially a comedy series, is supposed to be about. So also, to use an appropriately conservative phrase, does the new focus on the family. In the stage show, the spotlight was very much on the customers; their lives and experiences were explored through the things they said while sitting in the stylist's chair, or just by how they liked to have their hair done. ("The hair," says Nettie in the TV show, "tells you everything.") A picture of a community was built up. That hasn't happened yet in the new medium. All that domesticity is getting in the way.

To be fair, the new Kink is one workplace show in which convincing work gets done. We learn about the hours, the scheduling, even the actual techniques. We also believe that everyone involved is either good at the job or will be very soon. Well, it makes a change from The Office. Unfortunately, although the authors seem to have been at pains to make the concept less theatrical, they've actually made it more stagy. It feels like a filmed play, even though it isn't one. Make that a videotaped play; film is slicker. 'Da Kink is billed as a comedy, but it plays like a soap -- and nothing as sophisticated as shampoo. A daytime soap at that, with the same leaden pacing, both of writing and performance.

"Everyone relax, Nigel has arrived," says Nigel, arriving. Starr's status is announced by her being told "gentle now, trainee." A posh lady in the first episode was hardly through the door before telling the room "my daughter is -- miracle of miracles -- getting married." "Oh yes," said Nettie, anxious that nothing should be missed, "the society wedding of the year." Talk about exposition.

Well, all right, it's tough setting things up. It might be easier -- on the viewers at least -- if the actors could make less of a meal of it. Anthony herself, continually whirling about to show off Joy's vitality, is the worst offender. She was fun onstage, but she hasn't adapted. I've seen three episodes of 'da Kink (the second airs on Sunday), and though I've sometimes sensed the texture of humour, I haven't laughed once. Episode 3 involves Joy taking the day off to sell jerk chicken at Caribana, and running into trouble for not having a permit. It does seem sort of basic. Also, Nettie meets a man who is personable and divorced and can fix the air conditioning. I wonder where that will lead.

The show does offer Torontonians the pleasure of name-checking; Nettie is addressed as "Empress of Eglinton Avenue." And even viewers further afield may appreciate the bits involving Dre's troubles at school, scorned by a girl he likes for wearing Nikes whose logo includes a tell-tale extra 'e'. As Starr says, remembering her own shy schooldays, "making friends is torture" -- for both sexes. Glad to have that equality recognized.

Dre has also been caught by his outraged mother with a girl in his room, though as tolerant Joy says, "It wasn't a full panty- dropping episode." Well, it makes a change from Weeds -- though both shows have involved jokes about: "coloured people's time" i.e. being late.

There has so far been nothing in the TV show to match the searing monologue about child abuse delivered on stage by d'bi.young. Maybe there won't be; 'da Kink seems to be after tamer game. But, either as comedy or drama or something in between, it needs to get hot to be hot. As hot, in fact, as it once was. - 'da Kink in My Hair airs Sundays on Global
