Its common knowledge that the American plane that dropped the atom bomb on Hiroshima was called the Enola Gay. It's not so well known--at least it was news to me -- that the name was that of the pilot's mother. Sweet. More ominous is the news that one of the two accompanying planes -- the one charged with taking pictures of the operation -- was called Necessary Evil. The questions remain, how necessary and how evil.

Hiroshima, a first-rate documentary from the BBC, leaves no doubt about the evil, but is tight-lipped about the necessity. It restates the familiar conundrum about the bomb ending the war, saving thousands of lives at the cost of thousands of innocent others. It tips its hand only towards the end when it points out that the moral turning point for many people was not the immediate toll in lives but the radiation sickness that overtook the initial survivors -- some of whom had drunk acid rain to quench a raging thirst -- to an extent far greater than anyone had foreseen.

The film, written, produced and directed by Paul Wilmshurst, mixes archival footage, first-person reminiscence and freshly acted scenes. The mix, as always, is jarring; we get imagined dialogue among U. S. politicians, U. S. troops, and the Japanese high command who converse via subtitles. The most effective reenactments are silent scenes of the survivors stumbling and crawling to some kind of safety, with their older (real) selves narrating in voiceover.

The linking narration is spellbindingly lucid, and delivered (by John Hurt) with exemplary sobriety. Hiroshima was selected for sacrifice because it was a major military base, but also -- we learn in one of the more pungent lines of dialogue -- because "Kyotos out -- Stinson likes the temples too much." There are built-in ironies, horrible or hopeful. We get the expected shots of inhabitants obliviously pulling rickshaws or taking the subway to work on the morning of Aug. 5, 1945. The sighting of a reconnaissance plane leads to a bomb warning; people take shelter. Then theres an all-clear, and they go back to being sitting ducks. A doctor tells how his life was saved because, the night before, he'd been called to the bedside of a boy in a village some miles from the city. Cycling back the next day he encountered something like a body stumbling towards him. Trying to take its pulse he found there was nothing to hold on to-- "no skin."

One of the crew confesses that he didn't believe until the very last moment that the damn thing would actually go off. We hear few regrets from that quarter, though one of the men from Necessary Evil says that, looking back, the Japanese had been so demonized that nobody really cared. We are left with the last ironies: that Hiroshima is a thriving city again and that the most warlike nation has become the most pacific, as a direct effect of the bomb. But there isn't much moral accounting or much about afterthoughts. Early on, when dealing with the tests at Los Alamos, we get a freeze-frame on a figure identified as J. Robert Oppenheimer, accompanied by the familiar quotation about becoming Death, the destroyer of worlds. But he's never mentioned again. Maybe there should be a sequel.
