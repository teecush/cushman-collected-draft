Challenging the play commonly known as Marat/Sade in the long-form title stakes is We Are Proud to Present a Presentation About the Herero of Namibia, Formerly Known as South-West Africa, From the German Sudwestafrika, Between the Years 1884-1915.

Also like Marat/Sade, We Are Proud... is a play about the production of a play, a play moreover about blood-soaked historical events. The Herero were an African tribe, wiped out by their German overlords during the period denoted by the title, after which the future Namibia was taken over by the British. The proud presenters assume, rightly, that this is a subject about which most of us know nothing, and they are here to educate us about it. The show they produce has the air of being something worked up in rehearsal by the actors themselves. In fact, it's a formal script by an American playwright, Jackie Sibblies Drury, brought to us now by a Canadian cast who - it's something of a shock to realize - are playing Americans. Or: they're playing Americans, playing at being Germans and Africans.

So they begin, if you're still with me, by providing us and themselves with a potted survey of the territory, historical and geographical, though it takes them time even to get started on this, as they keep interrupting and second-guessing themselves. For all I know, Drury's script intends these introductory skirmishes to be as hideously jokey and awkward as they appear in Ravi Jain's production. After a while, though, things begin to tighten up. By the end of the evening, they are very tense indeed.

A female director-figure is in nominal charge of the proceedings. She addresses her cast as White Man, Black Man, Another White Man, Another Black Man, and Sarah. The first major glitch comes when Black Man accuses the showin-progress of concentrating on white people's problems: more precisely, of basing its text on letters sent by German soldiers to their wives back home. (That, collectively, would be Sarah.) White Man counters that these letters, which skirt around the subject of slaughter, are the only documentation we have. This struck me as a straw-man conflict, the kind of scene that makes the audience want to bang the characters' heads together and tell them to stop shouting. There must be some record of the genocide, or nobody would have known it happened. Which everyone is agreed it did.

Anyway, the performers try other tactics. They work up a scene of a German soldier shooting a refugee African, even though he'd prefer not to. Black Man sees this too as, for want of a better word, whitewashing; and, to bring the real atrocity home, he plays an African brutally executed by the whites. Only the scene that transpires doesn't seem to be happening in South West Africa at all; it's the image of a lynching in the American South.

What seems to be in question here is cultural appropriation, the ultimate straw-issue. History and humanity are there, to be turned into art by whoever wants to try; the only criterion is how well and honestly it's done. The play, or the production, seem to acknowledge this when Another White Man (Brandon McMurtry-Howlett) gives the outstanding performance of the night as an African grandmother, a character different from himself in age, race and gender. The final lynching sequence, though, seems to be arguing otherwise. These American actors (let's not get into the fact that they're actually Canadian) are the prisoners of their own history. The scene is very well staged and it sends the actors into apparent shock: shared by the audience, the infallible proof being that we don't know whether or not to applaud. The play, again like Marat/Sade, plays games with us and with its own ideas. The final irony, I guess a conscious one, is that it obscures the actual fate of the Herero just as much as it accuses everybody else of doing.

The Immigrant, presented by the Harold Green Jewish Theatre, is a benevolent musical based by its librettist, Mark Gorelik, on the life of his grandfather, who came from Russia to the small town of Hamilton, Texas, and began his life there by selling bananas door to door. One of the doors belongs to a local banker and his Baptist wife; conquering their initial prejudices, they take him into their home and under their wing, and help him become a prosperous department-store proprietor. He brings his own wife over from the old country; she too has suspicions but she conquers them, mainly during the intermission. So we have four nice people getting on together, which is humanly warming but dramatically tame. When strife does break out, it's another of those manufactured conflicts that could be defused if everyone took a deep breath. It does, though, occasion the only number in which the show justifies itself as a musical; otherwise the score (Sarah Knapp, lyrics; Steven M. Alper, music) does nothing the script doesn't do better. The evening is salvaged by Robert Mc-Queen's firm unfussy direction and by a very high level of performance, musical and dramatic; the four actors (Sean Arbuckle, Tracy Michailidis, Kathryn Akin, Steven Gallagher) are strong, poignant and, on occasion, funny.

The most gorgeous theatrical experience around is Talking Heads, two alternating evenings of monologues written by Alan Bennett for BBC TV and transferring perfectly to the intimate setting of Campbell House, one small room per playlet. All are set in Bennett's native Yorkshire. I saw Program B, which begins with Deb Filler as an antiques dealer, superbly supercilious, who can't enter a sickroom without appraising the bedside table and is eventually hoist with the invalid's petard: irony shot through with unsentimental pathos. Then we have Richard Sheridan Willis, riveting as a youngish man, gay and repressed, living with his somewhat ailing mother and threatened by the arrival of an admirer from her distant past. Finally there's Fiona Reid, in the most detailed, most revealing, and most hilarious (or maybe it's just her) of the segments, as a vicar's alcoholic wife who finds brief erotic bliss with an Indian grocer, with whom she learns "what all the fuss was all about" and also about his god: "a god, not the God, not the definite article." After beginning as the picture of glum, she is transfigured - though this may owe something to the production's adroit use of the on-site lighting. John Shooter's direction is excellent throughout. Bennett's major plays (Kafka's Dick, The Madness of George III, The History Boys) have been neglected around here. These sketches are excellent appetisers and a meal in themselves.
