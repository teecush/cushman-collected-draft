Knowing things that perhaps we ought not know FORBIDDEN KNOWLEDGE: From Prometheus to Pornography

Paradox of knowing is at heart of bold critical journey

Knowing things that perhaps we ought not know FORBIDDEN KNOWLEDGE: From Prometheus to Pornography

Saturday, December 14, 1996

By Roger Shattuck

St. Martin's Press, 370 pages, $35.99

'ARE there things we should not know?" asks the very first sentence of this book. To which the only answer can be another question: How do we know until we know them? By which time, of course, it's too late.

I waited with some impatience to see whether Roger Shattuck would acknowledge this paradox at the heart of his grand undertaking. Eventually, on page 301, he does. Reaching back past that opening sentence to his title, he says, "If we are familiar enough with any entity or domain to call the result 'knowledge,' then we already know too much about it to apply the adjective forbidden ." But he still finds the phrase useful, in many ways, and it's certainly suggestive.

As is indicated in his subtitle, From Prometheus to Pornography , Shattuck casts a very wide net. He's a respected literary critic and a professor of modern languages, with interests and experience far beyond his specialties. At one revealing point in this book he compares his feelings on reading the Marquis de Sade to his reactions on first witnessing surgery, and to the sensation of going into combat in the Second World War. So he himself has acquired a great deal of knowledge that is painful if not actually forbidden.

The operating-theatre experience he presumably went in search of, as an amateur scientist. He writes with ease and authority about astronomy and about research into recombinant DNA. The theatre of war -- again I'm presuming -- was wished on him. He was a pilot in the Pacific and believes it probable that his life was saved by the dropping of the atom bomb on Hiroshima. Later on, of course, he began to have qualms. The bomb and its successors are perhaps the classic example of knowledge-we'd-be-better-off-without, with J. Robert Oppenheimer, the physicist who later claimed to have "known sin" and to have blood on his hands, the central character in a tragic drama of Faustian dimensions. Except that the evil here is not in the knowledge but in its application.

I don't think I'm quibbling. "Knowledge," in Shattuck's book, turns out to be an extremely slippery concept, sometimes just a synonym for "experience." We begin, as advertised, with Prometheus, who in Greek myth stole fire from heaven and presented it to mankind. Zeus was furious and chained him to a rock, there to have his liver perpetually devoured by a visiting vulture. As further revenge, Zeus gave Prometheus a sister-in-law, Pandora, to release upon the world all the evils that our flesh and spirit are heir to. (Plus Hope, to keep us coming back for more; Shattuck leaves this last redeeming detail out.)

From there it's a skip across to the equivalent Judaic, and subsequently Christian, legend of the Garden of Eden, definitively fusing, as Shattuck says, "the themes of knowledge, curiosity, sexuality, the origin of evil, and immortality." Pandora and Eve, with their fatal proclivities for opening boxes and picking fruit, stand revealed as the first exemplars of what Shattuck calls "the Wife of Bath effect," after the Chaucerian character who declared, "Forbede us thyng, and that desiren we . . ."

The biblical creation story may have licenced millennia of misogyny, but that "we," at least as Shattuck uses it, means all of us; we all desire forbidden things. It's basic human nature, therefore presumably God-implanted, so it seems unfair that Adam and Eve should have been punished for it.

SHATTUCK writes that upon tasting the apple "straightaway, their innocent love changes into the dalliance of guilty lust." Does that mean they'd had sex before eating the apple but now it was somehow different? It's difficult not to feel that the Eden story has a lot to answer for. The Greeks, for whom the gods simply represented the way things are (i.e. unjust), were grateful to Prometheus for saving the human race from extinction and sorry for him afterwards. The Jews, as Shattuck points out, forgot the Eden story as soon as they'd written it; it is never alluded to in the rest of the Old Testament. For the Christians, though, it was central: The fortunate Fall, to be deplored but also praised for having made possible the rest of human history including the Redemption; indeed, it is precisely what was to be redeemed.

The double bind persisted. The Christian Middle Ages deplored too much curiosity, but could hardly help glorifying it at the same time. Dante's Divine Comedy is a huge apologia for orthodox thinking, and one of the lost souls of his Inferno is Ulysses, damned for voyaging in search of forbidden lands. But there is terrific grandeur in this portrayal of a man sailing over the edge of the world.

In the Renaissance, Montaigne, a tireless self-deprecator, kept proclaiming the limitations of the human mind but couldn't stop his own from working overtime. He virtually invented a new subject: human psychology. The self-proclaimed Age of Reason thought it had things in perspective. Alexander Pope in his Essay on Man (1734) famously proclaimed that heavenly speculation was futile and "the proper study of mankind is man," but that's not as safe as it sounds. Man was uncharted territory, and exploring it could get you into trouble.

Studying human biology certainly could, as Darwin discovered a century later. The Darwinian T. H. Huxley said that though he had evidence for his scientific beliefs, he had none for any religious views, and therefore was not prepared to declare any; he did the English language a major service by inventing a new word to describe his position. The word was "agnostic," and as Shattuck says, it's difficult to believe how long we got along without it. (At least I think it is; I don't really know.) As he also says, it's a shame that a term originally coined to denote honest ignorance has shifted to mean an aggressive rationalism. Faith in reason: That's an Inferno of a paradox.

GOETHE'S Faust , written across 50 years, shows what happens when the intellectual centre shifts. It's a dramatization by a man who didn't believe in damnation of a legend that depends on it. It's a brilliant mess; however shabby much of Faust's behaviour in pursuit of personal freedom, Goethe couldn't bring himself to condemn a fellow scientist and ends by letting him off the hook. Another Romantic, Mary Shelley, married one of those questing spirits. She wrote Frankenstein , the classic warning. Women, despite Eve and the Wife of Bath, often figure in Shattuck's account as restraining agents. There is Mme de La Fayette, whose novel La Princesse de Cleves (1678) glorifies the avoidance of passion, or at least of its gratification; not on prudish grounds, but rather to avoid hurt and disillusion. Emily embraces renunciation in her poety and in her life.

Shattuck as a critic is at his best on Dickinson; I found him revelatory. He seems deeply drawn to the ascetic esthetic. When he turns to science he applauds biologists who voluntarily imposed a moratorium on certain forms of DNA research. But vastly different kinds of "knowledge" are involved here, so different that the word begins to seem inadequate and ineffectual. Dickinson's denial was private and individual; the scientists' was public, collective and professional. It was also unique and, I would say, purely symbolic. If you can look far enough along a road to foresee its dangers, you have in essence travelled that road already. It isn't just that what is thought can never be unthought -- so that we would be living with the bomb even if every nuclear device on earth were dismantled -- it's that every step inevitably entails its successor.

Finally, Shattuck comes to pornography. He examines de Sade and finds him, rightly, appalling. No, he does not believe he himself was harmed by the encounter. Nor can he quite make up his mind that the sex criminals who read de Sade -- the Moors murderers in Britain, Ted Bundy in America -- were decisively influenced by him; at most, he may have suggested specific techniques. I doubt myself if de Sade's being "a classic author" had much to do with his appeal in this area, but Shattuck is right to be angrily ironic about the literary critics who, in the sacred cause of shocking the bourgeoisie, have bestowed that status on him. Answering a question once posed by Simone de Beauvoir -- "must we burn Sade?" -- he answers no. He thinks we should probably do something (moral health warnings, like on cigarette packets? Counter-productive, I'd say). But burning -- or banning -- would be ineffective and wrong, since "we should not deliberately destroy any human life or accomplishment." I find this conclusion disconcertingly flabby, the more so as I share it myself.

The book is graced with great lucidity and occasional wit. (He writes of Don Juan that "he seems to elude our grasp by dodging in and out among the several masterpieces that have brought him to life.") Half the time he is talking not about knowledge, but about action, and the book is crazily ambitious -- which means that the author is, in a way, the subject.

"The time has come" he says at close, "to think as intently about limits as about liberation." I take it he means intellectual limits. If he means limits we may impose on ourselves, then we have always had that right and sometimes perhaps that duty. If he means limits imposed by others, he's wrong; there's nobody we can trust.

Robert Cushman is a Toronto writer, critic and broadcaster.
