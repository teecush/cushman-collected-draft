TALK TO ME LIKE THE RAIN AND LET ME LISTEN / THIS PROPERTY IS CONDEMNED

Solar Stage, Toronto

Here, performed twice daily at lunchtimes in the bowels of Scotia Plaza, is a pair of early sketches by Tennessee Williams on his perennial themes of fantasy and defeat. Each is a duet, and the same two actors appear in both.

Talk To Me Like the Rain and Let Me Listen unfolds in a seedy hotel room in (one assumes, since it's Williams) New Orleans. A young man lies on the bed and murmurs, I wonder if I cashed my unemployment, an admirably economic way of clueing us in both to his social and his mental state. He also has recent memories of waking up in a bath full of ice cubes.

While he seems pretty much content to go with the flow, his female partner projects a more active discontent, a forcible though probably doomed desire to escape. "Ive been passed around," she complains, like a dirty postcard among people in the city. She says it twice, which is once more than the image will take. As so often happens in Williams, the lyricism walks a fine line between the hypnotic and the absurd.

This Property is Condemned, a more substantial and better-known piece, presents a boy with a kite and a girl with a doll. Hes playing hookey from school, down by a deserted stretch of railroad track. She's playing hookey from life, teetering along the track in a tattered parody of elegance, saying things like "I'm glad I didnt wear my silk stockings," and Wheres my diamonds? The boy sagely doubts her possession of either.

She is spinning yarns about her family; about her sister, who seems to have been a popular attraction locally, and about her own very different existence. "I don't have time for inexperienced people . . . I'm going out with popular railroad men."

The boys buddies have told him different, but -- like the heroines of The Glass Menagerie and A Streetcar Named Desire -- she fights fiercely to maintain her shell. The doomed property of the title is her grand old family home (which may or may not have ever existed), or her own life, or the whole decaying edifice of genteel Southern society.

In both plays its the female who does most of the talking (like the rain, if that means persistently) and the male who listens -- or is, at least, sceptically there. In Martin Hunter's modestly atmospheric productions, Peter van Gestel fills his double subordinate role admirably. Marjorie Chan gets the lioness' share of the taut, lazy rhythms and seductively quotable lines, and does very well by the angry aimlessness of her first character; she doesn't find enough variety in the more obvious vulnerability of the second. But she gets the drawl right, thereby setting up the mood of gentle desperation on which these plays depend.

- Runs until Friday at 12:12 and 1:11 daily. Box office, (416) 368-8031.
