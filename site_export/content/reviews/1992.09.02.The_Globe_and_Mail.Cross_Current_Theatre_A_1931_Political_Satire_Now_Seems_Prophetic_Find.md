CROSS CURRENT THEATRE A 1931 political satire now seems prophetic, Robert Cushman finds

Wednesday, September 02, 1992

ROBERT CUSHMAN

ROBERT CUSHMAN EVERY four years, come U.S. Presidential election time, I fall to wondering why nobody revives Of Thee I Sing. There may well be very cogent reasons why they don't, but for the moment please allow me my fantasy.

Of Thee I Sing, staged on Broadway in 1931, was the first musical comedy ever to win a Pulitzer Prize. It had music by George Gershwin, lyrics by his brother Ira, and a book by George S. Kaufman and Morrie Ryskind. Kaufman famously - too famously, since it's one of the most over- quoted of all apothegms, and here I go with it again - defined satire as "that which closes on Saturday night." But he disproved his own dictum since Of Thee I Sing wound up the longest-running musical of the thirties. And it was a satire on presidential politics: the story of John P. Wintergreen, his road to the White House, and what he found there.

I used to think the satire of Of Thee I Sing pleasant but toothless. Now it begins to seem prophetic. The show begins with the ultimate in campaign songs: unambiguous in its message (Wintergreen for President) and vigorously ecumenical in its supporting statement ("Hes the man the people choose"Loves the Irish and the Jews.) The torchlit marchers who sing it carry banners inscribed in a similar spirit: A Vote for Wintergreen Is a Vote for Wintergreen" and, my own favourite, "Vote for Prosperity and See What You Get. There is also Turn the Reformers Out," pungent but a little odd, seeing as Wintergreens party are actually the incumbents. Maybe, though, they were having trouble with a gridlocked Congress; the spirit behind the slogan is unmistakable, and I recommend it to President Bush.

Behind all this public confidence lies, as so often, private panic. The party doesn't have a program, and its credibility with the people suffered a grievous blow when it sold Rhode Island. In search of a platform, the party bosses ask their hotel chambermaid what she most cares about. "Money, she says, an unhelpful answer given the economic circumstances; the show is set, of course, during the Great Depression. (Maybe I should clarify that and say that is set in the last Great Depression.) Allowed a second try, she shrugs and suggests love." And there they have it. An issue. "Something that everybody is interested in, and that doesn't matter a damn.

Love" as an issue has the same weight, and fulfills the same evasive purpose, as "family values today. And it seems to carry the same connotations, right down to the cookie connection. Wintergreen pledges to marry the winner of a national beauty contest, and to make their courtship his platform. But he reneges and marries Mary Turner, whose main attraction is that she can bake corn muffins. A taste of these muffins is enough to put the moulders of opinion on his side. And when he has been installed, and impeachment threatens over his earlier breach of promise, Mary has merely to get pregnant to win the country over. She then clinches the matter by bearing twins. And when a White House pretender arises, Mary can squash him by pointing to his suspiciously unmarried state: A selfish old bachelor he!" (The style can get very Gilbert and Sullivan.) Im ahead of myself. This last wrinkle does not come from Of Thee I Sing. It belongs to the sequel, Let Em Eat Cake, which appeared in 1933 and was a failure. Wintergreen, despite banners that read, "The Same Promises as Last Time," loses the next election to a President Tweedledee, and can only oust him by staging a military coup and becoming dictator. Dislodged himself by extremist supporters he survives by turning counter- revolutionary. Mary, as ever, shows him the way.

One or two numbers from these two shows achieved independent popularity as love songs. In context, though, they are shamelessly self-serving: displays of emotion for public consumption. The only character with any thought beyond self-interest is a comic butt: Vice-President Alexander Throttlebottom, accident-prone and incurably low-profile. (Well, half of that could be Dan Quayle.) For everybody else, politics is a matter of diverting attention from what is actually going on and from their own inability to deal with it. They are so frank about this as to be almost lovable. After all, nobody, in any era or any country, expects politicians to know what to do about the economy. We don't really mind this. What we object to is their pretending that they do have the answers.

Now admittedly the shiftiness of today's leaders outdoes anything that these 1930s humourists dared imagine. We have watched a U.S. president get through a whole convention without mentioning the most important event to have occurred in his own back yard all year, the L.A. riots, while simultaneously taking credit for happening to be in the White House as societies crumbled on the other side of the globe. John P. Wintergreen, challenging the League of Nations to a baseball game for the amount of their war-debts, double or quits, seems a visionary idealist by comparison. But his creators undoubtedly had the right idea.

Fashions in satire grow fiercer, though not necessarily more accurate. Of Thee I Sing, thought in its time to be acute good fun, now seems a little quaint, while Let 'Em Eat Cake, once thought to be unconscionably virulent, now seems like acute good fun. I would like to see them cut down and combined into a single show. If we start now we should have them ready for 1996. Nothing much should change in the interim. Not even if Clinton wins the victory that his opponents have worked so hard to make him deserve. Robert Cushman is host of Songbook on CBC Stereo.
