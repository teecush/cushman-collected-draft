PICNIC

Court House Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont.

Back in the 1950s, Kenneth Tynan, in an essay on the Broadway theatre, told of attending the Boston tryout of a new play, and then repairing to the director's hotel suite for the first-night party. There he found a committee of impromptu play doctors, rewriting the piece and then rewriting the rewrites, according to which overnight review they had read last. These desperate surgeries apparently worked: The play, Tynan reported, was "a tremendous success." It was also, he said bluntly, "a bad play." Tactfully, he refrained from mentioning its title.

A few years ago, Tynan's diaries, or bits of them, were published, and all was revealed. The play proved to be William Inge's 1953 Pulitzer Prize-winning Picnic, and you could have knocked some of us down with a feather. This, after all, is a famous play, one that has endured despite taking some other hard critical knocks on the way. A Broadway production a few years ago showed it to be unembarrassingly revivable, and Jackie Maxwell's adroit staging at the Shaw Festival confirms that verdict.

Picnic has even been called the best American play of its decade, an estimate that may take your breath away in the other direction until you pause to consider the competition. (The Crucible? Cat on a Hot Tin Roof? They're the work of better playwrights but they're not better plays.) The '50s were a great period for American musicals but a dreary one for American plays -- not that subsequent decades have done any better.

Clearly Tynan's blanket dismissal of Picnic was wrong. Picnic is not a bad play; it is in fact the best example of its genre. He was right about the genre, though, and about its inherent limitations. It's what he called a "mood" piece, the mood being "a sort of steamy, twilit ambience of pubescent yearning."

It all takes place in a small town in Inge's native Kansas, on and around a porch; the script actually calls for two porches but the tiny Court House space means that the lady next door has to live offstage and constantly seems to be bustling in from nowhere in particular. (What Christina Poddubiuk's set does show us, it shows excellently.)

On the porch, and presumably, though less believably, in the house behind, live Flo Owens and her two teenage daughters, beautiful Madge and clever, artistic Millie, each jealous of what the other has and she hasn't. The family has a lodger, Rosemary, a self-described "spinster schoolteacher" who proclaims so emphatically that she doesn't need a man that, by all known dramatic rules, she has to be lying.

Into their lives on the day of the town picnic drifts Hal, a well- muscled young hobo whom the author craftily contrives to have play the entire first act stripped to the waist. ("Can't you put on a shirt?" somebody finally snaps, in what may be the best line of the play.) He turns out to be a former student -- his football got him into college, his academic dumbness pushed him out of it -- and a fraternity buddy of Alan, the respectful and respectable young man whom everyone expects Madge to marry.

Naturally, Hal lures Madge away from Alan. Naturally, Rosemary is attracted to him, too, but masks her jealousy by getting drunk (illegally, Kansas being in every sense a dry state) and spitting vitriol at young people in general.

Give Inge credit for some subtlety; he does not, as you might expect, allow the stud to seduce the teacher, or even vice versa. Rosemary's pent-up feelings instead throw her more decisively than ever before into the arms of Howard, a local tradesman and her long- time semi-reluctant suitor. The two couples -- Hal and Madge, Rosemary and Howard -- brush up against one another, pass one another, but never collide. And this is dramatized in a long, virtuoso sequence of impromptu dancing -- superbly sustained in this production, which features some great jitterbugging to very appopriate music.

Neither the audience nor the characters (who by now have better things to do with their time) ever get to go to the actual picnic; after this scene, we don't need to. All the same, wouldn't you love to see a play in which the nice guy didn't finish last, or in which the spinster lady refused to be toppled from her lonely perch?

Apart from the dance scene, a real challenge, the play is probably foolproof, not that I've ever seen fools put it to the test. You cast it well, you light it well, you step back and watch the ingredients interact. The play connects with everybody's feelings of regret, and somehow -- despite the fact that it is set when it was written -- it has the comforting air of nostalgia.

When Tynan took in that Boston party, he told the director that the play's third act seemed to have come from some other play. The director, the redoubtable Joshua Logan, said that was probably because he'd written it himself. Doubtless he exaggerated; the last act seems of a piece with the rest, though Inge later felt he had copped out by supplying the approximation of a happy ending.

But even if he had insisted on frustration rather than fulfilment, that would not have turned him into Chekhov. There isn't enough at stake. Chekhov's characters are quirky and they sometimes die, or at least lose their cherry orchards. This play is thoroughly and professionally unboring -- something to be grateful for, especially these days -- but it is pat.

Patness infects the acting, too. Goldie Semple cuts a striking figure as Rosemary (she looks as I imagine Rosalind Russell did in the film, especially when wearing a hat) but when she goes through her drunk and jealous routine, a routine is what it remains. Fiona Byrne as the struggling Madge is correct without being exciting, as is Wendy Thatcher as her mother, suffering tight-lipped at the prospect of her daughter repeating her own mistakes, something we only fully realize at the very last moment. There's well-made drama for you: revealing and frustrating at the same moment.

Mike Wasko is fully alive in Hal's bravado and in his shamefacedness, and Mike Shara barely falters as the hapless Alan (originally played by Paul Newman -- the last time he ever lost the girl).

Lisa Norton wins all hearts and tickles all funny bones as Millie, the kind of awkward-age prodigy who seems to collapse time; i.e., when she grows up she will probably write the play we have just been watching.

Two older players bring worlds on to the stage. Jim Mezon, though maybe too insistently uninsistent, is Howard, the small-town sensual man, to the white-suited brow-mopping life. Jennifer Phipps is the neighbour whose existence -- a one-day marriage and subsequent enslavement to a bedridden mother -- is a distorted mirror of the other women's experiences and who lives, vicariously and with deceptive cheerfulness, through them. Effortlessly she reveals depths when nearly everybody else seems to be speaking lines.
