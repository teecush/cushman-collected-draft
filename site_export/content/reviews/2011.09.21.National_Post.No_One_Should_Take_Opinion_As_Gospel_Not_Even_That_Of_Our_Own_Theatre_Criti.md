Soulpepper's production of Arthur Miller's The Price received a standing ovation on its first night. These days that isn't such a rarity, anywhere you go. But the cheers and claps on this occasion were unusually prolonged, to the point that they took the cast and stage management by surprise; the actors staggered on for their last call with the house lights up, and looking uncertain whether to stay or go. The audience, of course, lapped up the confusion, laughed in delighted sympathy and clapped all the harder.

Among "the audience" I include myself. I thought the enthusiasm wholly justified, participated in it and subsequently wrote a rave. My colleagues (that's journo-speak for the other critics) mostly didn't, which surprised me. Never mind the demonstrations at the end: Hadn't they noticed how the audience was held and excited by the soul baring and illusion stripping, not to mention the humour, that had gone before? Apparently not. I was shocked.

Which was probably silly of me, and is certainly ironic, because I've been in the identical situation to those reviewers on numerous other occasions, grimly keeping my head while those around me were vociferously losing theirs. Just this year, there have been at least three occasions -- Billy Elliot, Hugh Jackman in Concert, Come Fly Away -- when I've returned a negative verdict, in the face of apparently universal delight in the audience, reflected almost (though not always quite) unanimously in the other reviews. The reaction I aroused was predominantly one of resentment. I have the emails to prove it.

I'm not complaining. I even sympathize; my own feelings in the case of The Price weren't that different. When somebody fails to appreciate something you yourself have deeply enjoyed, it's hard not to feel personally affronted. This isn't logical -- why should your own pleasure in something be lessened by somebody else failing to share it? -- but it's human. You may feel that your whole value system is under attack. The feeling can be especially acute when the person who disagrees is someone you love. Though that may not be relevant to this discussion; after all, as more than one playwright has inquired, who loves critics?

We do, though, get questioned on other grounds. All three of the shows on which I've recently been called out have been large-scale commercial musicals, and several of my correspondents have questioned my credentials for pronouncing on musical theatre at all. I don't know how to answer that, because I don't know what the appropriate credentials would be. (I've seen a lot of musicals, if that helps.) Obviously the only credential that counts for most people is that of agreeing with them. It's also true that music affects us even more viscerally than drama, so feelings about it are apt to be more passionately expressed. In addition, a big musical has a big publicity machine behind it, and though ideally this shouldn't affect audience response, in practice it does, especially if the show in question has been a hit elsewhere; now it's come among us and we want a piece of the action. Then, too, patrons may enter the theatre with a built-in devotion, say to Hugh Jackman as a performer or to Billy Elliot as an emotionally loaded subject. Musicals give us more opportunity to applaud -- in a real sense, to participate -- as the show proceeds. And the tickets cost. Audiences are, in every sense, invested. These feelings are, of course, accentuated on an opening night, when the pitch can be feverish and there are plenty of interested parties present. Though, in all fairness, this can apply just as much for a play at Stratford or Soulpepper or the Tarragon as for a musical at the Canon or the Royal Alex or the Princess of Wales. The involvement may be different in degree but not in kind.

An accusation that frequently gets hurled at critics is that we may be spoiling, not our correspondents' fun, but that of hypothetical future audiences whom we are discouraging from seeing the show. It's an interesting point but I don't think it's a very serious one. I doubt, truthfully, that any of us locals have that much power; Toronto isn't New York where The Times is supposedly all-powerful. (Supposedly, not actually, even before the arrival of the Internet and its multitude of bloggers.) That's healthy; Toronto doesn't have as many critical voices as London, but it has several, and between us we're likely to cover the spectrum of possible responses towards a given show. Nobody should take the opinion of a single critic as gospel. Not to mention that, if we take the accusation literally, a negative review of any show has the potential of limiting its audience. It can't just apply to shows that the complainant happens personally to like. I doubt if anyone wants each of us to give raves to everything, but that would be the logical conclusion. In the case of the shows I've mentioned, the implicit appeal -- often the explicit appeal -- is to the verdict of the cheering crowds. Well, if word of mouth means anything, that verdict will make itself heard beyond anything a critic might say. (As, in the case of Billy Elliot, it demonstrably has.) I've noticed too that, as years go by, shows tend to acquire a "critical reputation" that may have little to do with what the critics actually said. As Arthur Miller might have said, as one who suffered his share of critical ups and downs and continues to after his death, it's the Price.
