A saving spice of self-mockery APPRECIATION / Despite his long list of plays, plus four adaptations, a film script, and two volumes of autobiography, John Osborne remained the man who wrote Look Back in AngerFriday, December 30, 1994ROBERT CUSHMANBY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail JOHN Osborne had a peculiar relationship with history. He made some, and was made by it. He was its beneficiary and, inevitably, its victim. In 1956 he was the playwright who transformed the English (and, by extension, the English-speaking) theatre. He had to spend the rest of his days living up to that initial achievement. Impossible expectations were followed by implacable disappointment.Impressively, he never let up. His publishers list 26 of his plays, plus four adaptations, a film script, and two volumes of autobiography, A Better Class of Person (1981) and Almost a Gentleman (1991). But he remained, as his obituaries earlier this week bear witness, the man who wrote Look Back in Anger. He himself came to accept that, however much he may have resented it. His last play brought back the two leading male characters of Look Back, 35 years on, pairing them with two younger women bearing the same names as their predecessors in the earlier piece. It was part sequel, part (large part) self-parody, and part autobiographical fantasia. It was called Dejavu.What the admirers of Look Back said at the time remains true. It put contemporary life on the stage, and thus brought the stage back into contemporary life. Doubtless somebody else would have done it if Osborne hadn't, but his method of doing it was bracingly direct. He set his play in the kind of dowdy one-room flat familiar everywhere except in the theatre, and put in the middle of it a young man volubly at odds with everything in his world.As social realism it was sketchy but its feeling was contagious. It was a game that everybody - under a certain age, anyway, and even that barrier could be breached - wanted to join in. As an incipiently stage-struck 13- year old, I felt it myself. I didnt, of course, see the stage production, but I did catch a TV relay of the first act: a broadcast, incidentally, that made the plays fortune, since even Kenneth Tynan's landmark rave review had so far failed to fill the house. From the opening moment when Jimmy Porter, the check-shirted protagonist, flung the smart Sunday paper to the floor I was hooked. When he said I've just been reading three columns on the English novel - half of its in French, I was entranced.The last revival I saw confirmed my impression that the play worked largely because it was very funny. When the author felt obliged to wheel on a plot things began to fall apart but a lot of it was just Jimmy talking, doing a routine. It was exhilarating free-form theatre. Osborne had been an actor, at a now inconceivable time when there was theatre, however tacky, all over the country. He had a sense (shared in different ways by his peers and successors Harold Pinter and Peter Nichols) of what worked.His next play, The Entertainer, took this point to extremes. There was the failed comedian Archie Rice doing his routines, wonderful in their awfulness. THere were the naturalistic scenes in Archies family, which were deadly. It became conventional critical wisdom that Osborne wrote great star roles - a point proved by Laurence Olivier in The Entertainer, Albert Finney in Luther, and most of all by Nicol Williamson in what is probably his best play, Inadmissible Evidence -but lousy ensembles. As the 1960s wore on even the protagonists began to fade; the presence of Paul Scofield in Hotel in Amsterdam and Ralph Richardson in West of Suez ensured respectful notices, but the evenings were pretty boring.Osborne himself was in a bind. People had wondered whether Jimmy Porter was based on his creator. It seemed likelier that Osborne came to base himself on Jimmy Porter. He was expected to find new targets to lash out at, and inevitably passion gave way to bewildered tetchiness. One of the few good jokes ever published in the satirical magazine Private Eye depicted a successful playwright called John "Osbore" complaining there must be something wrong with a society that can produce me. Look Back had been one of the first plays presented at the Royal Court, a London theatre dedicated to new playwrighting; the writer and the theatre established one another, inaugurating a stretch of theatrical history that still just about continues. For 40 years the Court has remained identifiably left- wing, but Osborne certainly didn't. After doing his share of anti-nuclear protesting (and feeling, to his credit, pretty silly on a demo) he became increasingly, if idiosyncratically, Blimpish. He seemed incensed that anyone should have the cheek to be younger than he was. His plays invoked, but did not dramatize, things called "love" (an emotion apparently felt only by his favoured protagonists) and England.Of his later plays I liked best A Sense of Detachment, a much-vilified piece that was free-form with a vengeance: a debate on the awfulness of everything and the impossibility of shocking anyone. In a rather similar way, the autobiographies are irresistible; they put nearly everybody down but disarmingly, and are surprisingly free of self-pity. If Osborne's real talent was for monologue, these were the best he ever wrote. (A wonderful line about Beckett: "Uncle Sam had the monstrous good fortune of actually looking like one of his own plays.") Unhappily the autobiographies only get as far as 1966, though a notorious passage in the second flashes forward for the sole purpose of vilifying the suicidal actress Jill Bennett, last but one of his five wives ("this whole rotting body of lies and invention which was her crabbed little life"). Admittedly she had become one of the actresses one most dreaded seeing, but this was largely because during the nine years of their marriage she seemed always to be playing smug complaining roles in his plays.I have no idea how much of his work will survive. Look Back, presumably, as an unusually lively historical artifact. Inadmissible, probably, though so far it's proved unworkable without Williamson. But the life and the work together make an achievement, carried off with style and chronicled with a saving spice of self-mockery. Lines from the autobiography keep turning up in Dejavu, as if he were wryly admitting that for practical purposes Porter had become Osborne. It might have been calculated as his last play; he had become, as he himself said of Noel Coward, his own contribution to the century. It was not the most comfortable of gifts; its ultimate value remains to be seen.Word count: 1144
