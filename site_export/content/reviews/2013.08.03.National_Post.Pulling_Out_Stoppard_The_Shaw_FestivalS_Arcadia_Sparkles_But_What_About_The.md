When the Shaw Festival announced, some years ago, that it was expanding its programming policy to include plays written later than gBS own lifetime, the first author who came to most peoples minds was Tom Stoppard who, in his wit and his fascination with ideas, is obviously the most Shavian of all contemporary playwrights. It was not to be, at least not for a long time. But now, at last, the Shawfest has brought us Arcadia, the play generally regarded as Stoppard's masterpiece, and has given it, by way of further amends, a sparkling production.

If you detect a guarded note in that phrase "generally regarded," you are right. I don't myself find Arcadia as intoxicating an experience as Stoppards earlier intellectual extravagances Jumpers and Travesties; while The Invention of Love, the play that followed Arcadia and that still awaits its Canadian premiere, contains what are for me his single funniest joke and his most wrenching emotional moment. It would also, being set very much in Shaws lifetime, have fit perfectly into the festival's revised mandate, while Arcadia only qualifies under its re-revised one. However, I've been going on about The Invention for a dozen years now, so I should probably shut up.

No doubts, anyway, that Arcadia is the most perfectly organized play of an author whos always been distinguished as much for his tidiness as for his audacity. Any number of conceptions, artistic or scientific but generally both, chase one another around in it, but the governing one is the Second Law of Thermodynamics: The universe will eventually run out of heat and die. This ultimately terrifying but comfortingly far-off eventuality is intuited in the play by Thomasina Coverly, a precocious mathematical genius who is 13 when we first meet her and 16 when we say goodbye. Thomasina is ahead of her time in more ways than one, as she comes up with her idea in 1812, a full 40 years before its official formulation. Shes the daughter of the aristocratic Croom family, in the schoolroom of whose English stately home, known as Sidley Park, both portions of the play are set.

About those portions: We alternate between the 19th century and the present day (1993, at the play's premiere) when the house, still in the same family, has been invaded by a couple of literary scholars, each with an axe to grind. Bernard Nightingale, a gaudy media don, is intent on proving that Lord Byron was once a guest in the house and that, when there, he duelled with and killed a very minor poet named Ezra Chater whose wife he had seduced. Hannah Jarvis, a far more reserved author, is interested in the Sidley Hermit who, around the same time, took up residence in the Park's capacious park, which had just been elaborately converted to what was taken to be a state of nature, i.e. a wilderness, naturally - or unnaturally - complete with hermitage. Hannah sees the hermits fate as emblematic of the decay of romanticism, she herself being a dedicated Classicist and (again in more ways this one, this being Stoppard) anti-romantic. Both Bernard and Hannah are wrong in their suppositions, but Bernard is the more recklessly and spectacularly wrong of the two.

Byron, as we know from the plays alternate sections, was a guest of the Crooms, and he did have sex with Mrs. Chater. (But then who, apparently didn't? As her husband, more than willing to trade her favours for a good review, puts it, there is nothing that woman would not do for me.) Other assumptions are due to the modern characters' misreading of the few surviving documents; we see for ourselves which evidence has been preserved and which destroyed; some things can disappear forever, even without the heat-death of the universe. It should be stressed that all characters in the play are fictitious, apart from Byron who doesnt appear. We do, though, meet his friend Septimus Hodge, Thomasinas tutor in mathematics and drawing and literature and everything else including, platonically and somewhat reluctantly, love. Septimus shares some of the Byronic temperament, and much of the cool Byronic wit. He is in fact the kind of character whom Stoppard most seems to enjoy writing, and to whom the audience certainly most enjoys listening; asked if sexual congress is the same as love, he replies: "Oh no, it is much nicer than that." In Eda Holmes' effervescently fast and intelligent production, gray Powell makes a capital Septimus, alert and amusing with an undertow of sorrow, while Kate Besworth is delightful as a Thomasina whose emotional development is catching up, rapidly and poignantly, with her intellectual.

This delectable couple has its latter-day counterpart in diana donnelly's prickly and practical Hannah, her best performance yet, and in Patrick McManus Bernard, whos wonderfully, self-satisfyingly pink, in costume and demeanour (I don't know about his politics); he's his own fireworks display. He in turn has his own fine foil in Martin Happer as Valentine, gauche present-tense scion of the Crooms and admirer of Hannahs, a quietly dedicated scientist who also finds his own findings anticipated by Thomasinas. Only she only had algebra and limited time; he has a computer and so, theoretically, infinity. Every idea in this play, indeed almost every line and especially the jokes, connects with another. It isn't true that Stoppard's plays are bafflingly intellectual; he explains the ideas as he goes along. But he does tend to wrap them in epigrams, and unwrapping them without spoiling them requires care. This production mostly supplies it, though Nicole underhay makes disappointingly heavy weather of Lady Croom (1800s edition) who stands sexually to the absent Mrs. Chater as Septimus does to the absent Byron and who also has most of the lines about landscape gardening, which are here so gabbled as to obscure one of the plays themes and one of its major plot developments.

Everything comes together in the plays famous last scene, a scenic and temporal overlap in which the classical and the romantic join hands, literally, while time is magically, heartbreakingly, suspended. At least, that's the idea. I get it, but I've never been as moved by it in practice as Im obviously supposed to be. I think the production might hint more powerfully than it does at Thomasinas eventual fate. However, the choice of Leonard Cohen's Dance Me to the End of Love as closing music is inspired (music is another of the play's crucial elements; sorry not to have mentioned it before) and the final use of Sue LePage's set is audience involvement at its very best.
