THEATRE REVIEW THE ESCAPE

Saturday, September 23, 1995

Robert Cushman

Written and performed by Abel and Gordon At the Tarragon Theatre until October 8 Rating: -1/2 The Escape suffocated by winsomeness Reviewed by Robert Cushman ABEL and Gordon are very good at what they do. Dominique Abel is a sweet-faced Belgian harlequin who, when he perches on imaginary ledges 13 imaginary storeys up, can make you share his vertigo. He can pull the oldest trick in the book of mime - feeling his way along an invisible wall and convincing you it's solid - and render it new. Fiona Gordon, who has the absentmindedly sultry air of an elongated Carol Burnett, boasts a body that bends in all the recognized places plus a few on which the patent may still be pending. She is superb at jumping into bed. That may not sound like much, but her bed is fixed at a steep diagonal to the floor and is some five feet tall at its highest point.

It's what they do with what they do that's unspeakable. That may not be quite the right word, since they use their voices as well as their limbs. In fact, it's their dialogue, much reinforced by cute addresses to the audience, that does most to create the show's overall effect, which is one of suffocating winsomeness. (A new esthetic theory: The only thing more unbearable than mime is mime that talks.) Abel plays Modiste the Ripper, who accidentally stuck a fork into somebody's head in a restaurant and is now doing time. However, he escapes, by unravelling the bars of his prison window in a fashion known only to pantomime artistes, and is soon being chased through the streets by his co-star who is playing a Keystone Kop with a ginger mustache. (Naturally, the chase is in slow motion, but at least we're spared strobe lights.) Our hero then invades the apartment of a lanky female insomniac who is briefly alarmed but soon recognizes him for the harmless paragon he is. When the police interrupt their idyll, Modiste takes refuge in the fridge. When his beloved opens it he has disappeared. He subsequently explains that he was hiding in a can of sardines; and if that doesn't sum up The Escape for you nothing will.

Logic - the essence of nonsense - is sacrificed for whatever piece of whimsy comes into the performers' heads, as is suspense; why should we be concerned for someone who can do the impossible? The show's publicity blasphemously invokes Buster Keaton, who did admittedly specialize in chase-sagas shared with ditzy heroines. But Keaton always played, hair- raisingly and implacably, by the rules, and he never begged for our love; that was the great virtue of his famous stone face. Abel's bids for sympathy make Charlie Chaplin, Harpo Marx and even the super-masochistic Marcel Marceau look as misanthropic as W. C. Fields. At one point he reads the instructions on a bottle of sleeping pills, and pronounces them unfit for children, adults, cats, dogs and giraffles (I swear.) Gordon does look a bit like a giraffle, and she manages to appear coy while wearing a dress that starts off-the-shoulder and then plunges, which is quite a trick.

Two other recommendations: The audience bravoed, and it's all over in an hour-and-a-quarter.
