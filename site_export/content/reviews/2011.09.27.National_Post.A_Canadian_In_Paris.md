THEATRE REVIEW

Private Lives

Royal Alexandra Theatre,

Toronto

Just to refresh memories: Elyot and Amanda used to be married. They fought all the time, so now, meaning at the beginning of Act 1, they're divorced. Theyve also remarried and, honeymooning with their new partners on the Riviera, they find themselves booked into adjacent rooms. Thrown together across their respective terraces they realize, inevitably, that they are still in love and they re-elope to, almost as inevitably, a flat in Paris. And there for the bulk of the play they remain, still loving, still fighting.

The play, of course, is Private Lives, Noel Cowards most concentrated report on the sex war. It comes to Toronto, en route for Broadway, in a production that's already been a success in London, with Kim Cattrall as Amanda. For the North American run, she's been joined by Paul Gross as Elyot, a combination that is tempting to describe as Sex and Arrows. That might not be such a bad summation of the play itself; though when it comes to weapons, Elyot and Amanda favour blunter instruments.

They start their new relationship fully aware of their past mistakes and determined not to repeat them. The audience knows these promises cant possibly be kept and waits, with varying degrees of schadenfreude, for them to be broken. That really is all the suspense the play has; and Richard Eyres production is at its best in mining it. There's enough giddy romance in the first act's evening air for us to wish the couple well; Coward even composed a song, Someday Ill Find You, to oil the reunions wheels. "Strange how potent cheap music is" as he famously and with shameless effrontery has Elyot remark.

Cattrall gets to sing it, twice, with more charm than accuracy, in which she probably resembles the first Amanda, Gertrude Lawrence. She may also resemble Lawrence in projecting a kind of guttersnipe sophistication. This suits Amanda, who is never as sure of herself as Elyot is, a quality that dramatically works to her advantage.

For all her bravado, physical and otherwise, Amanda seems the more vulnerable, not only to Elyot but to Victor, her discarded stodgy second, to whom she shows some sympathy, even tenderness. Which is more than Elyot ever does for his newer model, the tearful fribble Sybil.

The play's central scene, bringing the central couple from embraces to words to blows, is a tour-de-force of psychological warfare in which the dialogue is the plot (no wonder Coward liked Pinter), played out here in an unnervingly spacious Parisian pieda-terre whose furnishings amount to a lot of bed and very little board. The sex is there all right, and the regret, but the wit has mostly gone missing.

Coward's lines dont call for epigrammatic elegance, but they do need charged crispness. Cattralls delivery is often flustered, and she underrates the adjectives. Gross is effectively hushed rather than conventionally clipped, when assuring his newly rekindled old flame that her body holds no secrets for him, which is not only the character's key moment of erotic surrender but the play's. Hes admirably tactful, too, at delivering Elyots credo of heartfelt hedonism, which has the potential to be very embarrassing. These moments apart, he doesn't come into his own until the last act when fate, in the shape of their temporarily lawful spouses, catches up with the duo and has to be faced with a desperate insolence that gives the serene smile Gross has been wearing for most of the evening a whole new purpose in life.

Bad news though it may be for Elyot and Amanda, the arrival of Sybil and Victor is balm for the audience.

Anna Madeley, all pert sweetness when we first meet her, develops under pressure an aggressive shrillness that's as funny as its alarming. Simon Paisley Day, by contrast, gets more sympathetic as he goes on, his dogged devotion to the woman whos deserted him becoming rather touching.

Caroline Lena Olsson completes the cast as a haughty and cold-ridden French maid, breathing unhygienically over the brioches. I could not, though, however hard I tried, warm to the apartment that Rob Howell has designed for her to clean, an Art Deco jape, with ducks on the walls and goldfish in three tiers of bowls, and in its cavernous lack of coziness, enough in itself to drive Elyot and Amanda to blows.

I suppose it's possible the goldfish are a metaphor for their relationship, and I'll admit that they (the fish, that is) occasion a first-rate and unmetaphorical visual gag; some of the other physical comedy, though, is strained. I'll end with a non-spoiler and a question: The play concludes with Victor and Sybil at one another's throats, just like their exes. Is Coward trying to tell us that all relationships are like this? Or did he just want a nice symmetrical final curtain?
