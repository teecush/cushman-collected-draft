THE BEAUTY QUEEN OF LEENANE

Bluma Appel Theatre, Toronto

---

When he wrote The Beauty Queen of Leenane, Martin McDonagh was 23 and, according to all the publicity, had hardly seen a stage play in his life. His ignorance is our bliss. No one seems to have told him that in the contemporary theatre, character is kept strictly subordinate to ideas while narrative is at best ignored and at worst despised.

Before coming to Toronto, The Beauty Queen has conquered audiences in its native Ireland, in London and in New York; and it has done so primarily by telling a riveting story. This makes life hard for critics. Even on a second viewing the play radiates suspense, but its initial impact depends largely on narrative surprise, and it would be unfair to spoil that. It is also full of psychological shocks; people behave in ways that initially seem incredible but then, after the next revelation, make perfect sense.

It takes place in a cottage in present-day Galway, the remote county in which McDonagh's parents grew up, though he himself, a Londoner, knows it only as a visitor. Here, amid constant rain and perpetual poverty, live Mag, a 70-year-old widow, and Maureen, her 40-year-old unmarried daughter.

Mag is a tyrannical and very cunning hypochondriac, existing largely on a diet of tea, porridge, dietary supplements and boil-in- the-bag fish, all of which she depends on Maureen to prepare. Among her endearing habits is that of emptying her chamber pot in the kitchen sink.

Maureen, who on the face of it is considerably more intelligent, loathes her existence but has to put up with it; the reason why is the first of the traps that the play keeps opening beneath our feet. When not attending her mother, Maureen looks after the livestock that presumably helps support them. ("It's at the chickens she is," explains Mag to one of their rare visitors. A spoof of the play in a recent Forbidden Broadway has one actor yelling at the other "Stop reversin' your syntax, you old cow.")

McDonagh brilliantly captures the dynamics of a strained mother- daughter relationship, carried to the bitterest extreme. There is mental cruelty and there is physical cruelty, and the former seems far the worse; indeed one of the play's disturbing aspects is the way in which, as in a revenge tragedy, the audience finds itself cheering on acts of violence.

This may explain why people, including the author himself, keep comparing him to Quentin Tarantino, but there really isn't much resemblance. The violence in Pulp Fiction is presented as amoral, impersonal and cool; in The Beauty Queen it's the work of amateurs, the fruit of agonized frustration and white-hot hatred. Only one of the play's four characters could reasonably be described as nice, but each has a point of view and each is fully dramatized.

The nice one is Pato, who works on the building sites in London (where he's mocked and exploited) and whose appearance as a suitor on one of his regular trips home presents Maureen with her one chance of escape. He is only the third man in 20 years that she has as much as kissed, though her mother declares this to be too many; one of the overriding ironies is that, though Maureen seems far the more sexual of the two women, it is she who is the virgin, a fact that Mag is not about to let her forget. It is Pato's presence that turns their antagonism into a fully fledged duel.

Beyond the personal, the play acts as a deft and unforced metaphor for a depressed rural Ireland: its culture dulled by television (principally Australian); its young people forced off to England or the U.S.; and its weather, apparently, terrible.

It is also extremely funny, and not, despite the bleakness of the tale, in any sniggering black-humour way. McDonagh's dialogue is as rich as Irish talk is traditionally supposed to be, but it never -- unlike the language in Synge, O'Casey and even Brian Friel -- threatens to swamp the play. Everything here is economical, and McDonagh is a master at preparing his ground. Thoughts, people, objects -- from a poker to a package of biscuits -- are mined for long-range comic or dramatic effect: often for both.

Garry Hynes, who has directed the play with Irish actors from Galway to Broadway, does it again here with Canadians (a double coup for Canadian Stage) in a production equally rich in theatrical tension and human truth. Fiona Reid is a superb Maureen, a character whose trajectory is faintly similar to that of the actor's last role of Blanche DuBois. (What with Lady Bracknell and How I Learned to Drive, she has had quite a year.)

Blanche, though, was a performance in which you could see the wheels going round; her Maureen is complete and alive from the moment she steps on stage, a flaxen-haired countrywoman, and begins snapping at her ma. She snaps, indeed, in all kinds of ways, and an increasing edge of disturbance both colours the role and holds it together. This is a ruthless, pitiable account of a ruthless, pitiable woman.

Joan Orenstein's Mag, a less monumental termagant than Anna Manahan's original, is still commandingly sly and cantankerous. Oliver Becker plays Pato with a gentle dignity that flowers in a long monologue in which he writes and recites two letters: a scene that breaks the play's convention without injuring it. In fact it enlarges it. All their accents are convincing, at least to my ear.

The only actor who has trouble here is Matthew Edison, whose performance as Ray, Pato's brother and the play's tragi-comic messenger, is indistinct in both voice and personality. Still, his second-act scene with Mag, in which for her own purposes she tries to get rid of him by boring and frustrating him to distraction, remains a minor masterpiece of atmosphere and construction. That makes it an apt image of the play itself -- though, things being as they are, it may not prove to be all that minor.
