The confidant to whom Garbo opened her soul GARBO Her Story

Saturday, August 18, 1990

ROBERT CUSHMAN

BY ANTONI GRONOWICZ General, 476 pages, $34.95 REVIEW BY ROBERT CUSHMAN HERE is something new in as-told-to autobiographies, though I am unsure about the exact nature of the frontier it crosses. It might be a triumph of imaginative ventriloquism or it might be the nadir of exploitative chic. Or cheek.

Antoni Gronowicz was an expatriate Polish writer who, in 1938, was invited to Switzerland as the house-guest of his musical countryman, Ignace Paderewski. One day the host took himself off, promising in a strangely English manner to be back in time for tea. He really wanted to give Gronowicz a fair crack at another guest, who happened to be Greta Garbo.

Gronowicz and Garbo got on famously. They walked through the woods, and engaged in profound conversation: She: The sun is shining the same way on flowers and on mud. . . . A man gets married because he is in love.

He: "But a woman?"

She: A woman pushes herself into love because she wants to get married.

He: "Let's say openly what were thinking about instead of playing with abstractions."

Of course, Gronowicz wasnt taking notes, and probably remembered the conversation as more stilted than it actually was. Maybe he'd seen too many Garbo movies. Anyway, it seems to have worked for him, because very soon (by page 20, to be exact) he and the Swedish Sphinx are in bed together. This is good for a few paragraphs of light-breathing prose (Although she didn't show a special ecstasy, I felt that she gradually began to like my lovemaking, which lasted minutes or hours). But Gronowicz was not just after her body. He wanted her memoirs.

Over the years they talked and she reminisced, on the conditions that he take no notes and that nothing appear in print until after her death. In fact, he died first. Still, she finally fulfilled her part of the contract, and out the book has come.

On the copyright page the publishers advise that "the author wrote much of this book using the first-person literary device to emulate the voice of Greta Garbo. The words are those of the author, based on his recollection." But, leaving aside the self-aggrandizing prologue, why did he not maintain this literary device throughout? There are sections dealing with Garbos youthful emergence from embittered poverty in Stockholm which are written in the third person, but which seem no less reliant on the subjects own memories. If Gronowicz went to Sweden and conducted additional interviews, he makes no acknowledgements. Maybe he grew tired of his own "literary device." Maybe he just wanted to move the story on a bit.

There is actually no discernible stylistic difference between Gronowicz-as-Garbo and Gronowicz-as-Gronowicz. Both rely heavily on reported speech. This is not of the crassly informational variety usual in showbiz sagas (Judy, do you remember those fun-filled days back in 1932, when we turned out eight two-reelers at RKO and moonlighted on a couple of features at Paramount, one of them directed by Eddie Frankovich?). The Gronowicz style is more loftily unspeakable. "Here we are locked in a cage which races through states and which does not allow us to do anything but eat and sleep." That, supposedly, is Garbo talking to Mauritz Stiller as they cross America by train.

Stiller shares much of the limelight in this book. He was an apparently brilliant director, and expert con-artist, who discovered the teen-age Greta, changed her name from Gustafsson to Garbo (It's the Polish word for the process of making leather, he tells her, after she has spent several years wondering), and brought her to Hollywood anticpating fame and fortune for them both. He started as her Svengali and ended as her Norman Maine. She made it, he didn't. He even - like Maine, the outclassed husband of A Star Is Born -tried to end it all by walking into the sea. He survived (unlike Maine), returned to Europe and died young: the Only Man She Ever Loved.

Far less, however, than she loved herself: that is made very clear. "From the very beginning of my career I believed that with my talent and my capacity for work I could play any role better than anyone else." I despise people. . . . My legend is everything to me now. "Ambition and egoism drove me to achieve fame and money."

Her honesty does her credit, but how do we know for sure that it is her honesty? By his own account, Gronowicz wrote up their conversations from memory. Nobody talks like Gronowicz says Garbo talks. He could have misreported her as well. One ends the book disliking its subject intensely, not for being egotistical but for being boring. Then one remebers to transfer ones dislike to the author. An actor, after all, is under no obligation to have something to say. But a writer is, especially a writer with pretensions. Nobody asked him to write Garbos biography: least of all, one gathers, Garbo herself. But then, it was notoriously difficult to get a striaght answer out of her about anything.

As movie history, the book is useless; the transition from silents to talkies, crucial in Garbo's career as in everyone else's, barely registers. All we learn about the films she made for MGM - her only studio -is that she thought nearly all of them unworthy of her. The numbness induced by this reiterated information is an accurate reflection of the torpor with which one greets the prospect of watching any of her pictures now.

If you think that is harsh, you should read the afterword affixed to this volume by Richard Schickel, movie critic for Time magazine. He thinks that her only bearable picture is Ninotchka. His trenchancy is a welcome antidote to what precedes it, but even I think he is unfair. Trapped into seeing Grand Hotel or Camille, I can goggle at the one and be moved by the other, smothered though they are in the upholstery that was MGM's idea of style.

Schickel points out that she could have jumped ship; there were other studios in Hollywood far more attuned to the pulse of American life. But she stayed, and trusted to her legend to sustain her: I chose as a weapon complete silence. You can use this weapon very successfully if you have enough money to maintain absolute privacy.

Her career ended after the failure of Two-Faced Woman in 1941: "My 24th film in America. And I have no regrets that it was the last film of my life." Whether she meant it to be at the time remains ambiguous. But she was locked into her self-created role. A legend can hardly ask for work, least of all a silent legend. Ahead lay 50 years of emptiness.

The book shows her fiercely asserting her own greatness while deploring its misuse, taking some blame but dishing out a lot more. It is an unattractive posture. But the woman Gronowicz presents must be partly his own literary creation. Maybe he had a phenomenal memory and the words really are all her own. But if she was self-serving, he was still more so. Robert Cushman is a Toronto writer and critic.
