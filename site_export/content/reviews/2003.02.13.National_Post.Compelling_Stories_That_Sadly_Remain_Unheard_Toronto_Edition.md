THE PIANO LESSON

Du Maurier Theatre Centre, Toronto

Obsidian, Toronto's new black theatre company, bowed in last year with The Adventures of a Black Girl in Search of God, a show in which the performance outshone the text. In this, their follow-up production, the situation is reversed.

The Piano Lesson is one of a sequence of plays in which August Wilson has sought to dramatize the African-American experience of the 20th century, decade by decade. Wilson is a major American playwright whose plays are hardly ever seen in Toronto -- a shameful omission that it should not have taken the formation of Obsidian to repair. It would surely not have been beyond the capacity of CanStage or Tarragon -- two managements that pride themselves on showing us notable U.S. plays -- to assemble a group of actors to perform them.

Wilson's plays drive very hard. The Piano Lesson is his 1930s play, set in Pittsburgh in the home of an elderly man named Doaker, who shares the house with his niece Berniece and her young daughter, Maretha. Up from the South come Berniece's brother, known as Boy Willie, and his friend Lymon, with a truck full of watermelons. They plan to unload these and to return with the old family piano, of which Berniece and Boy Willie are theoretically equal owners; Boy Willie wants to sell it and use the proceeds to buy a piece of land, back where his grandparents were slaves. One other family member drops in from time to time: Wining Boy, a cheerful drunk who speaks of himself as a record star, and did in fact cut a couple of sides many years ago. And there is Avery, a preacher who is Berniece's suitor, and who finds himself pressed into service as an exorcist.

What the family has to exorcise is its past, represented by the ghost of a white oppressor who years before fell down a well in mysterious circumstances. He only manifests himself upstairs, out of our sight, but Berniece and others encounter him, and we hear their reactions. Much of the family's bitter history is enshrined in a set of carvings on the piano, with which Berniece is fiercely unwilling to part; you might say she treasures it for unsentimental reasons. So the action resolves into a duel between her and her brother.

Structurally, this is a solidly traditional, indeed old- fashioned play, on the Ibsen model -- the kind that confines itself to a single room full of people telling us and one another about their pasts. However, it keeps bursting those bounds, as indeed Ibsen himself did; it takes his title of Ghosts only a mite more literally than he did, and it shares his taste for conjuring symbolism from a nominally realistic setting.

Wilson's range of styles, though, is bolder. He is apt to forsake dialogue -- which in his hands can be rich, snappy or occasionally tender -- for monologues; and these range from lyrical reminiscence (Doaker recalling the sheer reliability of a career on the Yellow Dog railroad) to vaudeville schtick (Wining Boy on just about anything) to white-heat obsession (Boy Willie at the end of his tether).

And on top of all this there is, of course, music, which divides and defines the characters. Wining Boy still sings rural blues, the ambitiously contemporary Boy Willie shows Maretha how to play boogie- woogie, and Berniece, reaching far into the past, reveals an unsuspected affinity for religious chants that go back beyond gospel.

There's a thick texture here; when the characters talk about their family experience, it effortlessly takes on the density of American history. When Boy Willie talks about wanting land, the words are simple but the passion is palpable.

That, at least, is how good I think the writing is; I can't be sure, because in this production only snatches of it come through clearly. An acoustically unfriendly space is made even more hostile by a yawning, topless set, shabbily under-painted and grudgingly furnished. The play may transcend naturalism, but it needs to be natural before it can be transcendent.

Alison Sealy-Smith's production is awkwardly moved and sloppily spoken; in a play that depends on storytelling of one kind or another, the story is often very hard to follow. And the all- important music is ragged, and not in any Scott Joplin sense.

There is one shining light among the actors, and another that's modestly glowing. The shiner, giving a performance that rings clear of the surrounding mush, is Yanna McIntosh: Maybe too fresh and insufficiently careworn to be an ideal Berniece, but still wedding economy of means to accuracy of intent. The glow comes from D. Garnet Harding as Lymon, gauchely intent on finding himself a big- city woman. These two, blessedly, have a scene together -- one that shyly brushes against the edges of affection -- and it's much the best part of the evening. Suddenly there's rhythm, tone and contact.

Admittedly, the last exorcism in the play, involving Boy Willie, seems to be a case of a playwright gnashing his teeth and hoping for the best, and I doubt that any production could bring it off; this one, in which even McIntosh runs out of resources, hardly begins.
