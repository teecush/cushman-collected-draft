A couple of caveats. I gave a talk on Noel Coward and Blithe Spirit immediately before seeing a performance of the new production at the Segal Centre, which I don't think counts as a conflict of interest, but still had better be declared. More importantly, the performance I saw was the first preview -- a matinee at that -- at which the show was plainly still finding its feet.

The surest feet it found belonged to Blair Williams and Kelli Fox as Charles and Ruth Condomine. Theirs are the least showy of the principal roles but -- even more than in the Soulpepper revival a few years ago -- their relationship here underpins the whole play. Charles is the novelist who, for research purposes, arranges a seance in his English country home and finds that Madame Arcati, the local medium, has inadvertently conjured up the spirit of his dead wife, Elvira. Ruth is his current wife, who has always been vaguely jealous of Elvira's memory, and has a lot more cause when her equally jealous rival actually materializes. Charles professes to have been worn out by Elvira's passionate playfulness and to have greeted Ruth's comparative placidity with relief.

Outwardly, Charles and Ruth have a good, companionable marriage. Underneath, a bored resentment seethes. For the first two of the play's three acts, Williams and Fox, expert barbed comedians, cruise the polite surface and dip toes into the embittered depths with equal adroitness. Williams, one of our smoothest stage sophisticates but with a gift for suggesting trouble within, plays host to the audience as well as the characters; it's through his eyes that we see the play. If his grip slackens in the last of the three acts, that's because he doesn't in fact have much to hold on to. It's hard to disagree with John Gielgud's criticism that the play spins out a good comic idea beyond a reasonable length.

Charles isn't one of the more individualized of Coward's comic heroes; he's the quarrelsome Elyot of Private Lives and the suave beleagured Garry of Present Laughter, mixed and diluted. Ruth is absent from most of the last act, only reappearing (this would be a spoiler alert, if the play weren't so well known) in the evening's second ghostly form, a device that should double the farcical effect but actually succeeds in halving it. Charles ends by bidding a not-very-fond farewell to both his exes, plus all the previous demanding women in his life from his mother onwards; this burst of unregenerate misogyny might be quite bracing (as it is in Coward's one-act Fumed Oak) if only the characters and the situation hadn't already outstayed their welcome.

The repetition is emphasized by the slack pace of Marti Maraden's production, though it may very well have tightened up by now. When I saw her, Clare Coulter's Madame Arcati was effective only by fits and starts. Coulter excels at playing people who live strictly by their own rules, and her blend of dottiness and practicality would seem to be ideal for what is, by far, the most distinctive role in the play. But what should have been a seamless blend of hearty and wispy was still jerky. Gemma James-Smith has an unusual take on Elvira, whom she presents as upstart guttersnipe rather than throaty socialite. This seems to be playing not so much against the text as against the context. In fairness, I've yet to see an Elvira who justifies the claims that the play implicitly makes for her; as with some other screwball heroines, we have to take her enchantress qualities on trust. In this production, she does actually look like a refugee from Restoration comedy, as she returns from the dead wearing a voluminous blond wig. This seems to be regulation gear in the beyond as the revenante Ruth also sports one, though on Fox, one of nature's brunettes, it doesn't look so good.

Diana Donnelly gives a spirited (to put it mildly) account of the maid Edith who, it turns out, has inadvertently caused all the trouble; she's excellent when terrorized into taking the rap for it. Every time I see the play I hope to understand how Edith, who never even knew Elvira, could have been responsible for her re-appearance, but I never do. The set, by John C. Dinning, is wonderfully spacious and detailed, providing a fine environment for actors while summoning up both the comfortable place and the distinctly uncomfortable time: provincial England in the middle of the Second World War -- a fact that, in this comedy about death, is never mentioned but always present.
