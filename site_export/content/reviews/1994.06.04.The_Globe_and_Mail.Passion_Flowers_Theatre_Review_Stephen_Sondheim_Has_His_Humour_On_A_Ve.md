Passion flowers THEATRE REVIEW / Stephen Sondheim has his humour on a very tight rein in his latest musical, a comparison of different kinds of loveSaturday, June 04, 1994ROBERT CUSHMANNew York NY -- BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail New York STEPHEN Sondheims new musical Passion (Plymouth Theatre) has no separate musical numbers listed in the program. That might suggest that he has succumbed to the fashionable lure of Broadway opera, but this is not a through-sung work; the book, by James Lapine, has a lot of dialogue. All the same, the songs it separates do coalesce in memory into one continuous threnody, and there is something about it that might loosely be called operatic. The setting is 19th-century Italian, and the music is taut, plangent, yearning. Passion (which has been nominated for 10 Tony awards) is a love story, or a comparison of different kinds of love. Sometimes, describing a relationship that is over-confident and probably shallow, music and lyrics relax their grip and become suspiciously sweet. At other times, they grow spartan; the music tightens, the lyrics avoid easy images like the plague. What we get, in fact, is passion.It's a peculiar kind of passion, and I would like to think that the show is sympathetically examining rather than whole-heartedly recommending it. The pivotal character is Fosca, a tolerated resident in the quarters of her cousin, an army colonel. She is wasting away from a fatal disease, and is physically ill-favoured as well: crushing disadvantages in the culture she inhabits. (Or, to be honest, in almost any culture.) Into her environment comes a young and handsome captain, who is polite to her and respects her considerable intelligence. She flings herself at him, relentlessly, with no inhibitions about embarrassing him or humiliating herself; in one virtuoso number she dictates from her sickbed the letter she would like him to write her, and he helplessly complies. In the end, her devotion gets through to him. It compares favourably to the more conventional affair he has been enjoying with a beautiful married woman who, when the crunch comes, refuses to leave her husband.The show is based on a film (by Ettore Scola) which I have never seen but have heard described as black comedy. A spirit of irony hovers about the show as well - most explicitly in the choral comments of the captain's uncomprehending brother officers - but it never takes over. The mood is claustrophobic: in Lapines elegant but unspectacular staging, and in the construction of the score. There is much use of letters whose contents may be sung by the sender, the recipient, or the ensemble; everyone, knowingly or not, is caught up in the same emotions. Sondheim has habitually made his most serious points through wit, but in this show he has his humour on a very tight rein. There is less to savour joyfully in these lyrics than in any he has written. But there is also nothing that sounds unskillful or false; which suggests that, as usual, the words are doing their job.There is a tremendous performance by Donna Murphy, who quietly (a rare word to use in connection with Broadway musicals) imposes herself as both suffering martyr and relentless fury. Her voice, especially in its lowest reaches, is almost an additional instrument in Jonathan Tunicks dark, string-laden orchestrations. The hero (Jere Shea) is mellifluous but rather bland: unfortunate since this is at least as much his story as hers. He is the one in the centre of the relationships: the one to whom things happen. (She is the one who makes them happen, which is far more rewarding.) I am skeptical of claims that the piece marks an emotional breakthrough for Sondheim; he has written soaring love songs before, and only in a very special sense could it be claimed that this show allows its lovers a happy ending. Not that it matters. There are quite enough affirmative ballads in the world, and most of them are awful. Arthur Miller, now aged 80, has a new play on Broadway: cause for congratulation, especially as it's quite a good one and another Tony candidate. The title of Broken Glass (Booth Theatre) summons the German Kristallnacht of 1938: in retrospect, the real beginning of the Holocaust. The play revolves around a New York Jewish woman of the time, who seems alone in recognizing the horror of the situation, as depicted in newspaper photographs. Especially oblivious is her husband, a specialist in Jewish anti-Semitism; he votes Republican, and takes a nervous pride in being the only Jew employed in a Gentile firm, a position from which, in the course of the action and after mishandling a real-estate deal, he is fired. (At long last, you might say, Willy Loman has come out.) Either because of her unsatisfactory sexless marriage or her historical intuitions or both, his wife has suffered a hysterical paralysis. The sympathetic family doctor, also Jewish, never quite decides; but then his feelings toward her are not entirely professional.Only one of these themes is resolved, and powerfully; the husband has a heart attack and his wife walks. That sounds crude, but in context it's powerful and credible. Nothing else quite coheres (what, for example, are we meant to make of the doctors own marriage, to a Gentile?) and the plays most resonant point begins to count against it. As the wife senses, the really important and frightening things are happening in Berlin, but the play goes on happening in Manhattan. John Tillinger's production is not ideal. Though individually effective, each of the three leads - Ron Rifkin, Amy Irving, David Dukes - seems cocooned from the others; she in particular is warmly persuasive until she hits the line "I'm a Jewish woman" at which she goes into sing-song and never gets out. The WASP boss (George N. Martin) seems more ostentatiously callous than is absolutely necessary. And there must be a smoother way to handle the constant scene- changes than the trundling revolve in evidence here, even if I don't know what it would be. One line pleased me immensely: "So you have a sense of humour after all." It sounded like a wry self-tribute. Miller is not now and never has been Neil Simon, but he can be as funny as he wants when he wants. This is his most entertaining play since The Price.Word count: 1083
