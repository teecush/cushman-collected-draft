There was Half Life, and there is still Orpheus Descending, but the most moving play in Toronto, as well as one of the most delightful, is Danny, King of the Basement at the Lorraine Kimsa Theatre for Young People.

It's another welcome revival of a recent success. David S. Craigs play, mounted by his Roseneath Theatre company, turns up on the former YPTs main stage four years after making its debut in the same theatre's studio. In the interim, it has been on numerous Canadian tours and has been seen in other productions abroad. It's new to me, however, and though I had gathered that it was a play about homelessness and child poverty" that never became journalistic or didactic (there isnt a social worker or activist in sight), nothing had prepared me for the dazzling economy of the writing.

Within five minutes of curtain rise, and without being force-fed a thing, we have ingested nearly all the salient facts about young Danny Carter and his single mother, Louise. In one short, fast scene we have seen them executing an early-morning flit from their latest home and Louises latest boyfriend. (We don't meet him, just hear him snoring, but Louise's "he didnt turn out to be Prince Charming, did he? tells us all we need to know about that relationship, all three sides of it.) We have seen them take the St. Clair streetcar -- charmingly represented, like all transportation in this show, by a cut-out on wheels -- to their new home. This is not, as Danny -- who has now moved eight times in two years --expects it to be, a shelter. Its a flat: 92B Clinton St., B for basement. We have discovered that Danny, having had to grow up fast, has a close and playful relationship with his mom. His encyclopedic memory for subway stops and the order they come in helps her find her way about town, which is sort of cool; she calls him Mr. Banker" and actually lets him take charge of their cash, which sort of isn't.

Louise is the only adult we actually see, though we hear plenty about others. The two other visible characters are kids. We get three social levels placed side by side, with problems that reflect off one another. Penelope is the daughter of the woman who lives in the rest of No. 92 and, in the aftermath of a divorce, rents out its basement; as Danny learns, "rich people can think they're broke. Penelope goes to ballet classes and acting classes and is very clothes- and- status conscious. She resents having been taken out of private school, and she especially resents her neighbour and public-school classmate Angelo. Angelo does have two on-site parents, but his father is out of work and his resentment over this has turned into him a monster who yells at his son for being unable to score at hockey. Dannys assessment is that he sounds like a dinosaur," and thats the shape in which we glimpse him through his window, shadow-play style. It's no great surprise that the three kids, initially hostile, quickly become friends, or that the catalyst for their doing so is Danny's unstoppable imagination. In his fantasy life, which proves very infectious, hes a "game agent who reports, gathers information, hangs out." In his game, Louise is an agent posing as his mother. (Angelo objects, "Shes your real mother. Danny, worried that his friend might crack under interrogation, replies, it's probably safer for you to think that.") Danny has had a lot of practice in imagining things; he has had to imagine a winter coat and winter boots. He also believes, or wants to believe, that his father is in the Rocky Mountains, fighting fires. Eventually, some of his dreams, plus what he regards as a shameful secret, crash in on him, and he becomes for a time truly homeless.

Craig isn't one of your sadistic playwrights, and there is, of course, a happy ending, at least for these characters. Still I doubt if any adult is going to sit through this play without crying. (Children, officially the target audience, will likely be more resilient.) Richard Greenblatts production is lovely in its speed, fun and ability to distinguish between compassion and sentimentality. It also looks great, against a colourful set by Andjelija Djuric that amounts to a witty reduction of downtown Toronto, with the OCAD building putting in a whimsical appearance on the skyline. The ace up the shows sleeve, though, is its casting of grown-up actors as the kids. This device could be, and often has been, embarrassing, but here it proves liberating. Actors, by definition, play; that part of the child stays alive in them longer than it does for most people, and this piece, whose motive force is imagination, lets them tap right into that. They make an emotional connection that's direct, unforced, and totally unpatronizing.

In fact, Jacklyn Francis, as grownup Louise, seems to be playing at a disadvantage; she places so much emphasis on the mother's conscientious cheerfulness that we lose sight of the desperation beneath it. The juvenile roles, though, bring out the best in three of the best. Yanna McIntosh has a gift of directness that she has compromised in some recent roles by trying too hard or doing too much, but shes in top form as a perfect Penelope, focused and complete whether being impossible or being concerned. And also when being hurt; Penelope has two cellphones, one for each of her parents to call her up and abuse the other. Caught between them ("Im just a kid, says Ms. Hardboiled. Talk to each other."), she throws both phones into the trash; it's hard to conceive of a more wrenching image of the child as battleground. Cliff Saunders, last seen as Bilbo in Lord of the Rings, provides a private kick when Angelo has to read in class from The Hobbit; throughout he has a hotline to pre-teen bewilderment and pre-teen strength. Freshness and alertness are Jordan Pettle's stock-in- trade, and they find a wonderful conduit in Dannys progression from King of Moving (his own phrase) to King of the Basement, a better thing to be.

Dannys last traumas seem rushed in the writing, and hence in the playing; this is the only part of the play that made me wonder what it would be like with real kids. Otherwise, no complaints. Stanislavsky said that acting for children should be the same as acting for adults, only better; he might have said the same about writing and here is proof of both. I forget who said that if your play has a message you'd better not let your characters know what it is but, whoever he was, he gets vindicated, too.

Talking of kings and basements: In the COC's production of Gounods Faust, I really liked the climactic descent to Hell, via instant trapdoor, of Faust and Mephistopheles, while the wronged Marguerite did the hangmans job for him and achieved instant apotheosis, going literally through the roof. I know the music critics have been rough on this production, and I agree that having Faust and Mephisto exchange identities is too clever by half. Again literally: man into fiend makes sense but the reverse doesn't. Still, it seems to me that by bringing salvation and damnation back into play, this Faust restores some of the gravitas that belongs to the legend and that the opera sacrifices by reducing the whole thing to a lachrymose love-story. If all Faust wants to do with his magic powers is seduce one village maiden, then his pact with the devil seems like a waste for him personally and an irrelevance for the audience. Or, to put it another way, the end of the show forgets the beginning. Admittedly, you could say the same of Goethe's Faust, or a least of the Part 1 that was Gounods inspiration, but Goethe came back with a Part 2 that gives the hero bigger fish to fry. He also gets to escape, though Ive never been quite clear from what; Goethe was too much the philosopher to have believed in damnation, and that's enough to make any treatment of the Faust story problematic. Gounod's music does believe, but only at the unquestioning level of a 1950s movie epic. (I confess: coming to opera from musical theatre I cant help hearing Marguerites Jewel Song as a parody of Leonard Bernstein's Glitter and Be Gay, rather than the other way around.) Anyway, I found this production, directed and designed by Nicholas Muni, visually thrilling with its evocation of, and constant return to, Faust's cabalistic study. The new opera house continues to be one of the best places to go for exciting stagecraft.

- Danny, King of the Basement runs through Feb. 25 at the Lorraine Kimsa Theatre for Young People, 416-862-2222; Faust is in repertory through Feb. 25 at the Four Seasons Centre for the Performing Arts, 416- 263-8231.
