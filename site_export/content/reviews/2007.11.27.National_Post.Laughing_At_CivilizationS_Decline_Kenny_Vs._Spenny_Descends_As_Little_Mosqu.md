(From The National Post, November 28, 2007) Kenny Hotz and Spencer Rice were assigned each other's names in a photo caption yesterday in Arts & Life. *****

According to the sheriff in No Country for Old Men, the world started going to hell when people stopped addressing one another as "sir" and "ma'am." He may just have meant Texas, but I think he was including the world. He may well have had a point, one that stretches as far north as Canada. Here, the decline of civilization is being hastened by the weekly gross-out contest known as Kenny vs. Spenny.

I said the decline of civilization, not the decline of television. The show can be pretty funny. (Well, maybe scratch the pretty part.) By presenting these supposed friends in state of perpetual competition, it implicitly invites us to pick sides. I had better admit that I, true to critical type, identify with Spenny, witheringly dismissed on the show's Web site as a "neurotic adult- type," rather than with Kenny, pegged by the same source as "a witty wise-cracker and brilliant schemer." I think Kenny must have written that, always supposing of course that Kenny can write. Spenny is the subtler performer, for what -- in this context -- that's worth.

Last week's episode had the duo vying to create the best viral video. As an exercise, this may have been a trifle superfluous as all their shows are viral videos in the first place; they look cheap, and the two participants are bidding against one another for audience approval even when no vote is taken. Last week, one was taken and Kenny won, despite Spenny's inventive Cute Extravaganza, for which he mustered a whole floor full of rabbits, puppies and uncooperative babies, the mother of one of whom was heard to declare "this is the stupidest thing I've ever signed him up for." Even Spenny's desperate attempt to lower himself to Kenny's level by, in the dainty words of the synopsis, "consuming excrement," availed him nothing. Kenny gained his viral victory by having himself filmed "on top of Paris Hilton": i.e. posing on a roof against a cut-out of the Eiffel Tower. Spenny thought this was cheating, but the landscape of these two isn't a country for old or young men. The most hilarious thing about this show may be the ritual warnings about its being "suitable for adult viewing only."

Tonight we find out who can eat the most meat. Kenny starts out by concentrating on tongue and Spenny on turkey, both true to character. Which reminds me: Is turkey, meat, strictly speaking? There are some who would consider poultry a separate category. Sorry I brought it up. Which also reminds me ... no, I won't go there. But you can, if you watch the show. I won't, of course, give away who wins, but it's probably no secret that both guys get to craving a change of carnivorous diet. Kenny, after crooning to some strips of bacon, fries them up before our very eyes. ("My rabbi's gonna be pissed.") There's also a scene in which he has to make out with an old lady. You can't help feeling that she's the one who's lowering her standards. This is no country for her, either.

As has been reported here and elsewhere, Kenny vs. Spenny is our latest export, with the U.S.'s Comedy Central using our boys to fill the space vacated by striking writers. It's certainly one of those shows programmed to connect with every American's inner frat-boy. But if it keeps getting more strenuously outrageous that may just be because of the desperation that overcomes the majority of sitcoms as each begins to exhaust its initial premise. And Kenny vs Spenny is a sitcom, and as crafted and artificial as they come, despite its pretense of being reality TV (which of course is also a pretense). Despite, too, its primitive production values, which make The Sarah Silverman Program look like vintage Carol Burnett.

Not all sitcoms degenerate. Little Mosque on the Prairie has come along very nicely, outgrowing the giggling folksiness signified by its title in favour of a raised-eyebrow jollity that occasionally erupts into a genuine hoot. Tomorrow night's episode has Baber stricken with fear of what may be the Evil Eye, and is called "Best Intentions." That could almost be a generic TV comedy title (though it wouldn't do for Kenny and Spenny). I haven't seen this episode, but I would like to pay tribute to last week's episode, which had Samantha Bee as a guest star, proving that she can be just as funny when she's restrained as when being extravagantly wide-eyed and incredulous on The Daily Show. No, funnier. She fitted into the situation; she reacted to other people's lines and delivered her own as if she believed in them. In short, she acted. The show's three statutory plot lines were nicely rhymed, with a satisfying pay-off. An especially pleasant strand involved the efforts of Carlo Rota and Sheila Mc-Carthy's mixed-married couple (would this make McCarthy the Muslim equivalent of a shiksa?) to work from the same home, with predictably nonproductive results. Rota's unclassifiable ethnicity (English accent, Italian name, Canadian passport) has take him in the last few TV seasons between being unnervingly all-knowing at a reception-desk in At The Hotel, tortured and then self-torturing as Chloe's errant ex on 24, and funny and unruffled here. Through it all, he's stayed urbane. And he always seems to get the smart, funny girls. - Kenny vs. Spenny airs at 10 p.m. tonight on Showcase; Little Mosque on the Prairie airs Wednesdays at 8 p.m. (8:30 NT) on CBC.
