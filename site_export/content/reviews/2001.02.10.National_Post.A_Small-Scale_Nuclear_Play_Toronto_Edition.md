Mordechai Vanunu is the Israeli nuclear technician who gave photographs of his country's secret nuclear weapons program to The Sunday Times. The Israeli government kidnapped him, tried him for treason and sentenced him to 18 years behind bars. He is now in his 13th year of solitary confinement.

I Am Your Spy, a one-man play about Vanunu, is a joint production of two Vancouver companies, Rumble Productions and NeWorld Theatre, brought to Toronto as part of the Six Stages festival.

It is billed as a mixed-media production, a term that always seems to make those who use it feel very important and cutting- edge.

Here it simply means the show incorporates two working video screens, which today hardly seems a big deal. The smaller of the two screens shows us clips of talking heads, filling in some of the political and historical background of Vanunu's actions. The larger is used to present a blown-up image of the man himself, in his cell, as he would appear through prison surveillance-cameras.

Between the pictures is Vanunu himself, played by Camyar Chai, who also wrote the play (with Mara Coward), pacing a narrow stage- space which, according to the program, is exactly the same size as Vanunu's actual cell. We watch him trying to stay sane.

The effect is modestly hypnotic. Vanunu's first concern, chronologically speaking, is that his meal has not been served to him on time, depriving him of one of the psychological props that he needs in order to keep a grip on his day.

His next concern is whether or not he will receive a promised visit from his brother, his principal link with the outside world. We are left in no doubt that he is being held in conditions that are inhumane, if not actually inhuman. The same would of course be true of any prisoner in these circumstances, whatever his offence; but the play would not exist if Vanunu were any prisoner. All the same, it is only toward the end of the 65-minute piece that Vanunu starts talking about what he did and why he did it.

It amounts to a tiny treatment of a huge theme. The ethics of betrayal, and of the crazy-house world of nuclear armament, are more complex than the play ever comes close to acknowledging. The televised pundits make the most dramatic contributions of the evening; they reveal among other things that Daniel Ellsberg, author of The Pentagon Papers, learned via President Kennedy that if the U.S. were to bomb all the targets theoretically available to them, they would kill 600-million people.

This, it is pointed out, would make the Holocaust look like a minor conflagration; it would also, as another speaker says, destroy not just the present but the future.

The tone, though certainly more measured, is like that of Bertrand Russell's notorious denunciation of Kennedy and Macmillan as "more wicked than Hitler." Few would deny the Pentagon is a paranoid and self-righteous organization, but there really is a moral difference -- not to mention a practical one -- between a murderous scenario and a murderous reality. Hitler crunched more than numbers.

There is, after all, a case to be made for nuclear preparedness but nobody on that screen is ever allowed to make it. More crucially, the play has no mechanism for questioning Vanunu himself. He is alone on the stage, and the best the play can do by way of supplying a conflict is to have him put imaginary arguments into the mouth of his father, who has disowned him, and then answer them.

The only suggestion that there may have been some personal mixture in his motives is the acknowledgement that, as a Moroccan Jew, he felt in Israel like a second-class citizen. Probably the sharpest comment he makes is also the most flippant: If the purpose of the Israeli nuclear arsenal was to frighten the enemy, then wasn't he doing the government a favour by blowing the whistle and giving the enemy something to be frightened of?

The script, which combines extracts from Vanunu's own letters with the author's imaginings, is pedestrian; and though Chai's performance is perfectly competent, it is generalized: another nerve- wracked man in another prison drama.

I left the theatre perfectly prepared to sign a petition for Vanunu's release but far from sure he was right. (I actually felt more sympathy for him -- intellectually as well as emotionally -- at the time of his arrest and trial than I did watching the play.)

The production's tone is uncomfortably reflected in a jargon- ridden "writer's disclaimer" in the program: "Fiction is a double- edged sword. On the one hand, it allows the usurpers of the world to imprison history. Yet fiction also permits the artist to break free from the constraints of propaganda." In other words: It's all right if we do it. The Pentagon, from its own standpoint, might say exactly the same thing. Talk about double standards.
