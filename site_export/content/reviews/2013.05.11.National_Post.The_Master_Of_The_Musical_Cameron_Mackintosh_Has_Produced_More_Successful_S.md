In 1998 in London, the royal Family in attendance, there was a gala tribute to Cameron Mackintosh, the most successful of musical theatre producers then, now, and conceivably ever. It was indeed called Hey, Mr. Producer! Perhaps its most memorable item was a pretaped duet between Stephen Sondheim and Andrew Lloyd Webber, two composers who, whatever their dissimilarities, share two things: a birthday and a track record of having their shows produced by Mackintosh. For this occasion, Sondheim wrote a parody lyric to music that alternated his own Send in the Clowns with Webber's Music of the Night. The moment that went viral, as people probably weren't saying in those days, was the one in which Sondheim sang "God, but he's rich!" to which Webber replied: "richer than me!"

Well, he ought to be. He's produced the longest-running of Webber's own musicals (Cats and The Phantom of the Opera)as well some comparably successful shows by other composers: most spectacularly, of course, Les Miserables. He also produced the movie version of that show, Blu-ray edition now available. Since this was his first film, ("apart", as he says, "from filming a few concerts") I was curious to know whether his had been a hands-on involvement. On the phone he replied, emphatically, that it was.

Not from the start, apparently. "I went into it" he said, "just as an executive producer. But Les Mis is my baby, as it were. I've been its catalyst since the beginning." He can even take some credit for being its inspiration. It turns out that Claude-Michel Schonberg and Alain Boublil, its French composer and lyricist, had been much influenced by seeing Mackintosh's revival of Oliver! in London. So the much-remarked similarity between their Parisian street-urchins and Lionel Bart's London ones was no fluke. Having seen their work as a song-cycle in Paris, Mackintosh took it to Trevor Nunn and the royal Shakespeare Company, and the rest is history, if not perhaps in a sense that would be recognized by scholars of 19th-century France.

When the movie came around, a quarter of a century after that London opening, Mackintosh found himself "in a very unusual position for a producer; I represent the authors. I didn't realize, originally, that I'd be there for 99% of the filming. But I was. I also worked a lot on the editing. It was incredibly complicated." Also, it seems, amazingly fast. "This time last year, we were just about to start filming."

As for how he feels about the result: "I'm thrilled and relieved. We achieved what we set out to do. We took the story apart - a theatrical, full-blown popular opera - and put it together as a movie. It was a challenge, but Claude-Michel was up for it."

Has it whetted Mackintosh's appetite to do more films? "Not hugely. If I was 40, I'd be jumping up and down." (He's 66, though he still looks comparatively boyish.) Besides, he has a theatrical empire to take care of.

"We're in an extraordinary situation. So many of our shows are in their third or fourth decades. And they're being done in new productions." This, at first blush, is unusual. Of course, plays and musicals get new productions all the time, but generally only a certain time after the original has closed. It's unconventional for a show to get a complete facelift during what is still its initial run. (Though come to think of it, Agatha Christie's The Mousetrap, which has been running in London since 1952, making the Mackintosh musicals seem like new kids on the West End block, has regularly brought in new directors with completely new casts.) In fact, Nunn's and John Caird's production of Les Mis, and Harold Prince's of The Phantom will continue in London. But everywhere else will see new stagings. That includes Toronto, where a re-vamped Les Mis is due at the Princess of Wales in October.

Meanwhile, "the new version of Les Mis is breaking box-office records on Broadway. The 25th anniversary production is in rehearsal in London ... thank God I can now do casting through video. Then I go to Japan at the end of April. I'll be in Australia for a new production in 2014. There's a new staging of Phantom in Birmingham."

Personally, I can't imagine Phantom without the Hal Prince staging, or maybe it's just that I don't want to, but Mackintosh says that "the new stagings work incredibly well. They're done by a different generation of people. They have a different energy. They're both darker and grittier than the originals."

The worldwide success of the Mackintosh generation of musicals is unprecedented. He advances two explanations. One is that, despite some demurs from critics and other skeptics, they have "universal subject matter. Miss Saigon is about the sacrifice of a mother for her child. Phantom is about a love that can't possibly be realized. you can't get a ticket for Les Mis now. Kids in schools see the truth in the characters." And kids, apparently, do their own productions. "We've released Les Mis for use in schools; Phantom, too, though it isn't done as much; the technical requirements are more daunting. I'm very attracted to shows where ordinary people triumph over their circumstances. My shows are nearly always powered by great stories; I don't come up with the ideas, but when somebody else presents one, I dive in. If I don't like the characters, then I don't want to work on it."

I suggested that Cats didn't quite fit this sociopolitical story-driven description; though, to be fair, its characters do represent the cat in the street (or the alley) rather than the cat on the lap in the drawing room. Mackintosh says "it wouldn't work - Andrew would agree with any lyricist other than Tom Eliot. There are wonderful allegories in their poem." They initially tried to impose a more complicated concept but eventually, "Trevor said we just need a simple story. We didn't impose a premise. The simple story of an outcast being redeemed is what made it work."

Mackintosh's second explanation for his shows' longevity is that "the musical started going global in the '80s, when cheap airfares started to happen. It put the shows in dozens of countries. Now it's cool to see musicals." I think that depends on your definition of cool, but there can be no arguing with Mackintosh's statement that "theatre was always a major business - now it's one of the biggest industries in the world."

And it's no longer a principally American business: not, at least, in terms of origin or subject-matter or - most importantly - sensibility. Mackintosh points out that the last comparably dominant musical-theatre brand - the Rodgers and Hammerstein shows - "were written for a 1950s American sensibility." And their success was parochial by comparison. "The musical of theirs to achieve the greatest worldwide success was The Sound of Music - their most European show."

It also, of course, owed much of that success to its film version, which in turn has sparked successful stage revivals that are essentially based on the movie. One myth that has been exploded - the one that kept the r and H shows off the screen for years - is the belief that making a musical available on film, let alone on video, would kill its chances with live audiences. The opposite has proved to be the case. "Films," Mackintosh says, "open up shows to a whole new audience." It's also, of course, led to attempts to short-circuit the process by turning successful films into stillborn shows: Mackintosh agrees that Shrek, for example, "doesn't have a reason to sing in its own right."

Mackintosh also owns seven London theatres ("I thoroughly enjoy being a landlord") only one of which currently houses one of his own shows. (Les Mis, at the Queen's.) But he has other people's musical hits (Mamma Mia, Jersey Boys) and, perhaps more remarkably, "four or five plays doing stonking business. The London theatre currently has a Pounds 10 to Pounds 15-million advance - the highest it's ever been in my lifetime." recession or no recession, "London hasn't been healthier for a long time."
