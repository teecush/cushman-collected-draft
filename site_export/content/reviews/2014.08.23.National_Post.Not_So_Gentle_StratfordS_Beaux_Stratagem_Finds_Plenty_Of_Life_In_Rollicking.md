There are many, many treasurable moments in Stratfords production of The Beaux Stratagem, but the one to treasure most may be the appearance of Martha Henry with breastplate and broadsword, repelling the burglars who have invaded the stately home that she shares with her son, daughter and daughter-in-law. Though maybe even more delicious is the smile, at once surprised and gratified, that she shares with the audience when a gallant pays a call on the house and, discovering its three female inhabitants, asks in apparently genuine perplexity, "which is the old lady?"

Henry's character, who's far from being the central one in the piece, is a benevolent naturopath by the name of Lady Bountiful; and its notable that, in this 1707 comedy, the name has none of the patronizing implications that it has since acquired. This Lady may be eccentric (certainly in Henrys, to put it mildly, vigorous performance) but she is genuinely concerned for the well-being of the surrounding countryfolk who flock to be treated with her herbal remedies.

The reason her home is being burgled is that it's the biggest one in the district, and thus a natural target for a gang whose more usual game is highway robbery. They know about the place because they've been tipped off by a local innkeeper with whom theyre in the habit of dividing their spoils: His current guests include a master and servant who seem suspiciously free with one another, and whom their landlord suspects of being rival highwaymen in disguise. In fact they are a pair of gentlemen, the beaux of the plays title: younger brothers and hence impoverished, touring England in hopes that one of them will attract a marriageable heiress whose fortune they will then divide.

They are called Aimwell and Archer, and Aimwell, playing the master on this stage of their journey, finds his target in Lady Bountiful's daughter Dorinda. He then, of course - this is a comedy - falls genuinely in love with her. Archer, meanwhile, finds himself in a more complicated relationship with Dorinda's sister-in-law Mrs. Sullen, whose name denotes not her own nature but her husbands. This married pair - she a creature of the salon, he of the saloon - cannot stand one another but, the laws being what they are, and continued to be, seem irrevocably yoked. The beaux intentions, honourable or otherwise, lead to their presence in the threatened house just when they're most needed.

It all hangs together, more tidily than we expect in Restoration comedy, of which this play might, if it were less lively, be described as the last gasp. It was almost literally the last gasp of its author, George Farquhar, who died at 29 just after completing it, and who was the saddest early loss to English drama until the death of St. John Hankin (coincidentally on the bill at this year's Shaw Festival). Farquhar was a very audience-friendly and actor-friendly playwright, preserving much of the cynical wit of his predecessors, but infusing it with an unsentimental warmth and also with an awareness of political reality. Theres a war on, and if their scheme doesnt succeed Aimwell and Archer will be cannonfodder. The end of the play practically invents no-fault divorce. Some legal and financial machinations enable the Sullens to split up and the lady to be united with her new lover. In real life, we're left feeling, things wouldn't be so happy. It was another 200 years before another major playwright touched this subject, when Shaw wrote his rejected last act to The Philanderer, currently reinstated in another Niagara production; our two major festivals might almost be conspiring.

Antoni Cimolinos Stratford staging is joyous, relishing the characters, telling the story clearly, and exploding into periodic bouts of high farcical invention. It only falters at the end, when Mrs. Sullen and Archer dance off into happiness. We should feel just as happy for them, but the ground has been only half prepared. Colm Feore is brisk and adroit in Archers fluctuating role as cheeky servant, and in his flirtations with the innkeeper's daughter (Sara Farb, delightful). He actually seems more involved with her than with Mrs. Sullen. Lucy Peacock gives us all that lady's acerbic defensive wit, and also her pain, but the moment at which Archer should truly, and perhaps to his own surprise, connect with her never happens. Hes still, for all we can tell, the fortune-hunter.

Aimwell, on paper the less lively of the two, becomes another in Mike Sharas line of second bananas who emerge at the top of the tree. His scene of feigned injury is hilarious, but equally impressive is the conviction he brings to his determination to do the decent thing by Bethany Jillard's Dorinda. The list of rich supporting performances is almost endless, a credit both to players and play: Scott Wentworth's Sullen giving depth to a type, Robert Kings ripely expository innkeeper (who disappears abruptly from the action, perhaps because his author found it difficult to reconcile his joviality with his villainy), Victor Ertmannis highwayman prophetically named Gibbet, Gordon S. Miller a real comic gem as a timorous butler in frustrated pursuit of an aggressively unresponsive chambermaid, a role in which Tara Rosling is almost unrecognizable. She in turn leads us to yet another compartment of the plot, one occupied by a couple of French POWs, billeted at the same hospitable inn. One is an aristocrat, whom Evan Buliung makes subtly funny, the other an Irish (don't ask) chaplain whom Michael Spencer-Davis makes broadly funny; both have their place.

Both also fold very nicely and usefully into the main action; of its kind, this is an exceptionally well-made play. It's certainly a well-made production, with Patrick Clarks sets and costumes making us feel at home in two alternating locations and Berthold Carrieres music easing us through the journeys between them.

The Beaux' Stratagem is in repertory through Oct. 11 at the Festival Theatre, Stratford.
