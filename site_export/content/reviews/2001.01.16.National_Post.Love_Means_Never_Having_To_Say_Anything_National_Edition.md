First seen nine years ago in Theatre Passe Muraille's backspace, Jump returns on the main stage: a stage that now looks more main than it used to and has been fitted out with -- of all dear old- fashioned heartwarming things -- a proscenium arch. The medium suits the message, since Jump is a 60-minute lampoon of the conventional marriage ceremony, which has been around for some time and which the show presents as something endearingly antiquated. Well, as something antiquated. For a one-joke affair, Jump is fairly ingenious.

It was written by Daniel MacIvor, though not much putting down of words on paper can have been involved; there is no dialogue. MacIvor devised a scenario, which the current director, Mark Lonergan, has reactivated; having missed the original, I can't say what changes he may have made.

Anyway, the conceit of the present show (though I probably couldn't have worked it out if I hadn't read the publicity) is that some bureaucratically minded government has redefined and codified the wedding ceremony, and renamed it a Jump. The traditional elements of signatures, vows, and gift-giving are streamlined, and all lead up to the Jump itself: The bride and groom are each required to climb a ladder (impressively revealed at the back of the stage and stretching out of sight) and demonstrate their commitment by leaping from it into one another's arms. Or not. So a regime intent on stemming the tide of unlicensed co-habitation by reasserting the sanctity of marriage has tried to make the ceremony more attractive by turning it into a combination of circus and Kafka. It doesn't make sense.

Of course there are those who would claim that marriage already IS a combination of circus and Kafka, and the show could perfectly well be a satiric cartoon based on that premise, using a theatrical vocabulary that has been common ever since the 1920s, or when experimental theatre started to call itself experimental.

The high-water mark of this kind of thing was the Absurdist movement of the 1950s, and this show often seems like Ionesco without words. The character billed as Man in Grey Suit (Clinton Walker, looking and leering like Eric Idle at his most jovial- sinister) might be a figure of Orwellian prophecy; he might equally well be a caricature of any present-day master of ceremonies. The bride, a.k.a. Woman in White (Severn Thompson), looks tearful, and the groom, or Man in Red Jacket (the very agile Scott McCord), looks out of it: No surprises there. The groom does take us somewhat beyond the norm; he finishes -- not to give too much away -- in a heap. It's a fitting conclusion to the day he's had.

For example: Inveigled into a dance with two kilted lasses who I suppose are bridesmaids (Girls with Dark Hair in Plaid Skirt 1 and 2: Aviva Chernick and Sarah McDonald), he ends up doing the splits, and is stuck like that for the longest time. The remaining characters are Woman with Purse (Anne Anglin), who is the bride's conventionally formidable mother and who gets to snuggle with the emcee, and Man in Overalls (Brendan Wall), a handyman who presumably comes with the hall.

A high point is the presentation of the presents. Everyone, it seems, has given a toaster; I don't know whether this is a rule under the new dispensation or whether it merely denotes a lack of imagination, or at least of co-ordination, among the happy couple's friends and relations. Either way, it's a tired joke in itself. But it's brought to life here by the sight of the bride trying to balance four toasters in a pile on her lap, while a cart laden with other toasters stands by her side. All then adjourn to watch a video of a toaster. The picture remains totally stationary, but they sit there gazing at it anyway. Finally, their attention turns to other matters, and so does ours, and just when we're least expecting it, the onscreen toaster pops. It's a neat moment.

Overall, though, the show falls into a species of comedy I find unendearing; treat your characters like automata and then mock them for following prescribed patterns. Marriage may well be a strange ritual inaugurating an even stranger relationship: All the more reason to explore why people sign up for it. (Or, if you want to extend the argument to gay couples, why they should WANT to sign up for it.) The ceremony is only as interesting as the people undergoing it; and each pair have their own history and their own motivation. If we don't know that, the spectacle becomes boring. Not to mention that it's a mime. (The presenters claim that it's something else, but nobody is going to believe them.)

STONES IN HIS POCKETS:

I made some large mistakes in yesterday's review of Stones in His Pockets (Winter Garden). I attributed the superb performances of the two actors, Conleth Hill and Sean Campion, to their standbys, Declan Mooney and Stevie Ray Dallimore, whose names are given almost equal prominence in the program. (Hill was on the left in the picture that accompanied my review, Campion on the right.)

I also, for reasons I cannot explain, misremembered the names of a couple of minor characters whose names are not in the program at all: Lewis is actually called Clem, and Victor should really be Simon. And the name I misheard as Ashley is really Aisling. My apologies to everyone, and -- to Messrs. Hill and Campion -- my renewed (and this time, I hope, accurate) congratulations. --R.C.
