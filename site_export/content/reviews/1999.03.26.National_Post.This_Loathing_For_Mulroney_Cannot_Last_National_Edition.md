THE LIFE AND TIMES OF BRIAN MULRONEY

Factory Theatre, Toronto

Michael Hollingsworth's VideoCabaret is a company devoted to making theatre look and sound like television. This is more fun than you might expect; and technically, in terms of both performance and presentation, it can be enormously impressive.

The action unfolds within an oblong frame, as if behind an invisible screen. There is much use of projections; an actor playing, let us say, Brian Mulroney may be seen in three forms simultaneously -- front and back as a video-image and centre-stage in the flesh. Performers -- there are six in the current show, handling forty-five roles between them -- scamper tirelessly about while still maintaining absolute precision. Somehow every element, human or mechanical, arrives in the right place at the right time.

The Life and Times of Brian Mulroney is the twelfth and last instalment of VideoCabaret's comic-strip history of Canada. Its tone, like that of its predecessors, is remorselessly sarcastic; it's like an episode of The Royal Canadian Air Farce at four times the length, or -- less genially and more precisely -- like the British series Spitting Image, only with people instead of puppets.

The show presents Mulroney being groomed for the premiership, principally by Ronald Reagan and American Express, and then chronicles his terms of office. It also hints at the aftermath with a chilling little scene in which Prime Minster Jean Chretien promises the paymasters that he will stand by free trade and the goods and services tax despite anything that they might chance to hear him say during the election.

Meanwhile, the former PM has been portrayed as endlessly venal, selling out national liberties right and left, but especially left; and also as endlessly vengeful. (His response to any rumble of opposition is: "I'm gonna get that guy" -- or, as the roster of challengers begins to proliferate and he needs to distinguish between them, "I'm gonna get that guy.") He is eventually undone by his failure to secure a consensus over Quebec. Having initially seen Meech Lake as a golden opportunity to divide and conquer ("When I get through with Canada, it'll be 10 little dwarves in search of a master -- mark my words."), he finds himself faced by incompatible interests that not even he can smooth over. He finds himself haunted, in an especially vivid scene, by the spirits of Jean Chretien, Lucien Bouchard, and Preston Manning: an especially threatening trio of ghosts since, of course, they happen to be alive. His wife also takes to rubbing her hands a great deal.

These two cases aside, the Mulroneys are no Macbeths; no tragic grandeur is permitted here. When Brian goes down, he goes into financially cushioned oblivion.

I believed it. But believing something is not necessarily the same as enjoying it.

My one previous brush with VideoCabaret was Trudeau and the FLQ, two shows ago. This had the benefit of a good story about hostage- taking and a protagonist who was credibly something of a hero, something of a clown. Hollingsworth and company obviously had a sneaking admiration for Trudeau.

Mulroney they merely loathe. Although hatred might conceivably provide enough fuel for a full evening's theatre, loathing -- a far colder emotion -- runs out of gas. Despite Michael Copeman's best and sharpest efforts in the role, Mulroney does not emerge as either funny or interesting. There may, of course, be excellent reasons for this.

Ray Landry's Reagan -- a figure who seems to have existed principally in order to be impersonated -- is funny, and his John Turner is both funny and rather touching: ditto for Michael Carley's Joe Clark. Drama loves a loser -- which, the world being what it is - - is greatly to drama's credit.

As comedy the show suffers --certainly it did on the first night - - from the presence of a complicity captive audience, willing to lap up every line. This breeds complacency. The show improves the darker it gets, which is to say the second half is better than the first.

There is a subplot about the effects on three working Canadians of the New World Order (which, the Americans explain, they prefer to call free trade since everyone loves the word free). This starts out as a definite drag, but as its characters are thrown out of work and through the cracks, it does take on some power. And there are always the toys to marvel at.

The show runs until April 18.
