Kristen Thomson's The Patient Hour turns out not to be what it seems. I have mixed feelings about this since, pardon my grammar, I like what it seems. I admire the audacity with which the play moves into unexpected territory, and in retrospect, I appreciate the cleverness of the hints dropped earlier on, both in the writing and in Chris Abrahams production. The first two-thirds of the play are funny, touching and engaging. The last section varies between the provocative and the pretentious.

We are in a hospital room: almost literally, since the patient, unseen, is presumed to be somewhere in the middle of the auditorium. She is an elderly woman, in critical condition after a stroke. The other characters -- her son, her daughter, the night-nurse and a mysterious female patient claiming to be from a room down the hall -- speak to her unresponsive form, therefore in effect to us, as well as to one another. This is the place to say that any doubts that lingered about Thomsons writing after her triumphant and self-performed I Claudia are here dispelled. She can write for performers other than herself, and she can write dialogue as well as monologue. It's true that her people often don't seem to be paying attention to one another, and that they often talk past one another. Thats clearly intentional, and after all, it worked for Chekhov.

Also obviously intentional is the pun in the plays title. Hospital vigils are doubly disconcerting, in that they're predominantly uneventful while the events that do happen are frequently alarming. They're typified by forced smiles and frayed nerves, and the play gives us full and believable measure of both. They can also exacerbate family tensions, and that seems to be the pattern here. Charles and Laura, brother and sister, havent communicated in years. Much of this is ascribed to Lauras having been in prison, or somewhere, for shoplifting: a farcical-sounding episode that Waneta Storms, who plays her, relates with great biting humour and aggressively rolling eyes. This is a tough, grimly amused performance from an actress who was everywhere in this city's theatres 10 years ago and has been much missed since. Charles, it seems, never visited her; neither did their mother, Dawn. Charles is an auto mechanic, a compulsive jokester driven by insecurity. Pushing 40 and never married, he contemplates making a pass at June, the nurse, who has been married and wishes she hadn't. The territory occupied by these two, each both hoping and dreading that the other will make a move, isnt exactly uncharted, but its sweetly explored. He makes clumsy attempts at flirting; she, flashing a semi-discouraging smile, makes awkward gestures at being flirted with.

Charles is played by Todd Thomson, who is the author's brother and bears a strong resemblance to her, both physically and in the warmth and wit of his acting. He's likely to hop up and squat on a chair to attract attention or to make a surprise entrance as an accident victim. Liisa Repo-Martells June, who had a career in pottery before switching to health care, offers a quieter, more businesslike study in shyness. The scenes between the two of them give what may be the most thorough workout ever given to the Canadian upward inflection, that vocal tic whereby every statement becomes both a question and a plea for approval. It works. As the most fully realized characters in the play, everything we hear from or about them rings true.

Patricia Fagan plays the unplaceable, and troubled, Young Patient who keeps wandering in to confide in the quiescent Dawn. She has a harrowing speech about having given birth to a dead child, a trauma from which she has not recovered. She doesnt seem to fit with what one knows about hospital routine, and the disconnect is reflected in the production. Julie Fox's set is impeccably, if economically, realistic, but there are strange discontinuities in the action, especially in the matter of exits and entrances. The director, though, absolutely knows what he is doing, and nearly --not quite--everything adds up by the end. Some things from the past are revealed at just the right telling moment, notably the fact that Dawn once worked in a prison. Her name, too, is probably significant; the play is full of cosmic metaphor (even the puzzle game that Charles brings to entertain his mom is called Planet Earth) that, I confess, struck me as less astronomical than astrological. In other words, it lost me. The parts of the play that I found most persuasive, either as pain or pleasure, are those grounded in experience rather than philosophy. There's a fair amount of what I would call wishful spinning. But compassion -- for the living, the dying and the dead -- comes through clearly and for the most part unsentimentally. The acting is unwaveringly superb. And frankly, everyone in or around Toronto theatre is in love with Kristen Thomson; this play will keep all those flames burning.
