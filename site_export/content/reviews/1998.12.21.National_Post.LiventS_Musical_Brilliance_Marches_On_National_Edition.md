Parade

Vivian Beaumont Theater,

Lincoln Center, New York

A show about racial prejudice so virulent that it leads to murder. A show about the American past whose score is steeped in Broadway tradition but not in nostalgia. A show directed by Harold Prince that ends with Brent Carver getting executed.

Parade, a Lincoln Center co-production that officially opened on Thursday in New York after a long run of previews, is unmistakably a Livent musical.

It belongs, that is, in the tradition of Kiss of the Spider Woman, Ragtime, and the revival of Show Boat. None of those shows possesses all of the characteristics I've listed, but each of them some of them. There is a definite family likeness. Fosse, Livent's fifth original -- already seen in Toronto and opening on Broadway next month -- stands to one side of the tradition, being a retrospective revue of the work of one choreographer; by definition it too is steeped in the history of the musical but in a more nakedly obvious sense.

The four major shows seem to me to constitute a very impressive track record. Or -- if the most funereal predictions about the company's future prove true -- an impressive legacy. Either way, I can't think of any commercial producer in North America in the last decade with a body of work to touch it. (How many producers have ever developed a house style?) I have always loved musicals -- which doesn't mean I like all of them, far from it -- and I especially love American musicals; so I admit to a bias in favour of Livent's shows, which adhere devoutly to the American book-musical tradition.

The company's parallel role as importer of the complete works of Andrew Lloyd Webber seems to have had no effect on its own creations.

I also have a more immediate interest: For eighteen months -- from the spring of 1997 until the bitter downsizing of three weeks ago -- I was, very happily, an employee of Livent. (The group of people assembled there were the best I have encountered in any organization. Their dispersal is tragic.) I was director of corporate communications. Apart from my responsibilities as an in- house writer, I attended workshops of the new shows --

Parade, Fosse -- and gave feedback. Kiss, Show Boat and Ragtime were all done deals long before I joined; in fact, as a critic, I'd reviewed them.

It's probably fortuitous, but there has been a progressive concentration of the historical focus in Livent's shows. Kiss paints a recognizable if unspecified landscape of oppression in modern South America. Show Boat follows an array of fictitious characters across half a century; both time and space are clearly denoted, and the inherent epic quality is accentuated in Hal Prince's production.

Ragtime, still epic, moves over fewer years and fewer miles, and it brings in a handful of historical figures as symbolic markers.

Parade zeroes in on a single factual incident: the railroading in 1915 of a Jewish factory supervisor in a small Georgia town, on a charge of murdering one of his workers, a 13-year-old girl.

If Parade is the most intimate of the four musicals, it is also the most challenging. This is not because it ends with the accused, Leo Frank, being lynched. The same actor got himself tortured and shot at the end of Kiss. And if you want to talk about dark finales, you can't beat Cabaret, which sent its audience home on the rise of Nazi Germany. What's disturbing about Frank is not his death but his life. He's cold, he's aloof, and he's awkward; if we are going to sympathize, it has to be with his predicament, not his personality.

(There's a curious parallel here with Prince himself: the work, not the man. Because they don't gladhand the audience, his shows have been called cold.)

Brent Carver's particular brilliance as a musical actor is to find the rhythm of a character and carry it through speech, song, and movement. As the gay window-dresser in Kiss he was lithe, warm, and hopeful. As Leo Frank he's dainty, prim, and -- for a musical hero -- amazingly quiet.

It's nice to beat the americans at their own game

It's not just that he acts spooky; he acts guilty. When the cops first come for him, he volubly protests his innocence before anyone has accused him. I don't suppose Prince or his book writer Alfred Uhry actually believe that Frank killed the girl, but they never get around to saying explicitly that he didn't. There's a teasing vein of ambiguity in the show, and beyond that a scary truth: He may be guilty, he's probably innocent, but from the town's point of view it doesn't matter. They've all made up their minds, from hatred or opportunism or both; just as, 80 years later, congressmen and columnists have lined up for or against Bill Clinton entirely according to their a priori political opinions.

Ironically, almost the only character who seems to doubt is Mrs.

Frank, played with wonderful poise by Carolee Carmello. She almost deserts Leo at his trial; then, after he's been found guilty, rallies to his defence, wins his pardon from a conscientious state governor whose career is thenceforth ruined, and rediscovers herself and her marriage in the process -- at which unbearable moment the lynch mob moves in.

When I first heard about Parade, I was told that it was basically a love story. That, I thought skeptically, is what they would say.

In fact, when I saw the workshop, I found that they were right and that it worked beautifully; all the same, it has been strengthened by a couple of new songs. More important, though, are new numbers that fill in what was once a patchy racial and political background: a sardonic black ensemble about being for once not the most downtrodden people in town, or a savage spiritual that unites all the haters. (By the way, despite all the glib remarks in the press about corporate theatre and focus groups, musicals were workshopped at Livent in exactly the same way as they are everywhere else.)

There are still some loose ends: some characters abruptly introduced or dropped , some connections left shadowy or unexplained. The lynchers are individuals and full of misgivings; but since, for the most part, we haven't gotten to know them, the scene can't capitalize on this, and it's strangely ineffective. The staging lacks the burnished glamour of Show Boat, the sweep of Ragtime, or the visual jazz of Kiss (still my favourite Livent musical).

But Parade has its own stark power, and a highly encouraging score from a young composer-lyricist, Jason Robert Brown. This affirms, as did Ragtime, the wit, variety, and dramatic power of American song after all the constricting theatrical years of europop. It benefits too from not being asked to do what dialogue can accomplish better. Alfred Uhry's book, like Terrence McNally's for Ragtime and Kiss, is exceptionally adroit and intelligent, but perhaps the basic function of any musical book -- we've learned this the hard way over the last 20 years -- is to stop the score from sounding silly.

The 1990s have not been good for musicals. Even admirers of the British and French non-stop music machines would have to admit that in this decade they have been dying on their feet. (Sunset Boulevard? Martin Guerre?) There have been a mere handful of substantial American shows: Rent, Titanic, Passion (my least favourite Sondheim, but Sondheim nonetheless). There is The Lion King, but there's something dispiriting about plonking a film musical down on the stage, even if it's done with bravado. And there has been nothing funny.

I can't claim that the Livent shows have solved all these problems -- they haven't even begun with the last -- but they have recognized how much potential still resides in the language of the Broadway musical, how exciting its best practitioners are, and how both the art and the artists can leap at the right subject. The American musical's historic function is to show America to itself; and it's a pleasing irony that it has taken a Canadian-based company to reaffirm this. It's nice to beat the Americans at their own game.

Our nationalists may have other words for it, but I see no evidence that big musicals have been taking up space, physical or psychological, that would otherwise be occupied by Canadian plays. I think it's exciting that fine Canadian actors -- Brent Carver, Steven Sutcliffe, and others -- have held their own with the best on Broadway, and I would hope that in time there might be more: writers and directors too.

Finally, after years of hard labour as critic and audience, I acknowledge the brute fact that good theatre is always to be welcomed, whatever colours it sails under. If Livent does disintegrate, somebody had better pull up the slack. And fast.
