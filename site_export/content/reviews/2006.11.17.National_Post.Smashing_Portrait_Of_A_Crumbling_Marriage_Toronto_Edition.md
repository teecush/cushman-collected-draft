INSOMNIA

Buddies in Bad Times Theatre

- - -

The name of Daniel Brooks on a theatre bill promises, if it doesn't always guarantee, an adventurous production, one thats visually and sonically mesmerizing. This seems to be the case even when Brooks himself isnt the director. Chris Abraham has staged Insomnia -- which Brooks wrote 16 years ago with Guillermo Verdecchia and in which he also acts -- in a manner that might pass for Brooks' own work and was confessedly inspired by it. Banks of downstage lights flash in the audience's faces between scenes; other lights bathe strips of stage in red, making the affected parts look like lines of carpet, along which characters nightmarishly hurtle. Humming, screeching sound effects further shatter our nerves (lighting and sound are by Andrea Lundy and Richard Feren respectively, though at this stage in the game it seems almost superfluous to say so). None of this is gratuitous. The production reinforces the text with absolute precision.

That texts immediate subject is a breaking marriage, and it carries complete conviction. The larger subject seems to be contemporary society, all of it, and thats a lot chancier. The protagonist, John F., is a blocked writer, receiving little consolation from his wife Gwen, because she no longer has any to give. Maybe she never did; she knows it's over, even if he can't accept it and even though they have a child.

The pleadings and recriminations are starkly depicted in a series of very sharp, very short scenes. There arrives from California Johns brother William, an executive with Disney. At one point John, desperate, asks him for a job, only to be told that to work for the Mouse would require his becoming a completely different person, i.e. it isnt going to happen. John is indeed an insomniac (the condition drives him to take baths at 5 a.m.) though the title might also apply to Gwen, since his being awake keeps her awake. William's wife, Kate, though, is a narcoleptic, which would seem to be completely the opposite. William's own sleep patterns appear normal, which is suspicious in itself. John is attracted to Kate, and William appears to seduce Gwen, though around here the play slips beyond my literally minded ken. William announces that he has a plan to change his brothers life; his motives may be kindly or malevolent, but they involve him hypnotizing Gwen, and everyone but John going off to Japan. John, left behind, rediscovers his politically activist self and makes a long speech, one requiring a virtuosity in acting and performance that it doesnt actually get, in which he demolishes the entire spectrum from Noam Chomsky to Ann Coulter. (They are, he points out, essentially the same.)

He then smashes some windows -- I think -- and goes to jail. There is an ironically reassuring epilogue, whose point may be that you can paper over the cracks but the cracks will still be there. Which is all well and good, but somehow the device seems tired.

I'm not persuaded by the title image either, though it's undeniably effective in inducing critics -- including this one -- to affirm that this Insomnia will certainly keep you awake. The best parts of the script and all the parts of the production just about enforce it. Brooks himself, after a long break from performing, returns to the stage to play John; he may be only a journeyman actor, but his own belief in what hes doing ensures ours, especially in the early scenes where hes misery incarnate. Randy Hughson is not the most obvious choice for William, who is Mr. Success, but if he doesn't have the sleek profile he certainly has the mental measure, and its power; he also keeps us hoping against hope that, being Randy Hughson, he may really be a good guy and that, in its way, is helpful. Fiona Highet's Gwen, a bleak survivor awaking to agony, and Colombe Demers' Kate, a beautiful sphinx, are irreproachable.
