THE HOUSE OF BERNARDA ALBA

Court House Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont.

- - -

NIAGARA-ON-THE-LAKE, Ont. - The Shaw Festival production of The House of Bernarda Alba has "brave try" written all over it.

Federico Garcia Lorca's last play is one of the great unseen modern classics. The year of its creation, 1936, was also that of its author's death. A Spanish Republican, he became a Fascist victim during the early days of the Civil War.(The play remained unproduced in Spain for another 30 years.) Its subject is repression, and it has become a critical commonplace to regard the shuttered all- female household it depicts as a metaphor for the country itself, buttoned-down and beleaguered. I doubt, though, that it really strikes anyone that way when watching it now, especially in translation. It stands in its own right as a tight, inexorable march toward disaster -- an authentic tragedy.

Bernarda Alba, one of the more prosperous women in her Andalusian village, has five grown, unmarried daughters and has buried two husbands -- the second of them immediately before the start of the play. She now, with grim relish, declares an eight-year period of mourning. No men are allowed to cross the threshold, though prospective suitors are permitted, in accordance with tradition, to speak with their intended at her window.

A certain Pepe el Romano pays court in just this manner to Angustias, the eldest and (by virtue of her father's or stepfather's will) richest of the quintet, but it seems he has been conducting a more active dalliance with Adela, the youngest and most rebellious. Pepe is also desired by an intermediate daughter, Martirio, who is crippled and -- even more than her sisters -- embittered. All this we are told; Pepe himself never appears, nor does any other man.

The conflicting passions, and conflicting spites, of these three drive the play. (The remaining two daughters function mainly as chorus. They are Magdalena, dubbed "the hyena," though apparently just for the sake of the rhyme, and Amelia, whose only distinguishing feature -- at least in this production -- is that she has red hair.) They take up more stage time than their mother. Nevertheless, it is her play as well as her house. She controls the narrative. She, of her own choice, is doom.

Tadeusz Bradecki's production looks good, on a rich white courtyard set designed by Teresa Przybylski, and it clearly marks out the initial scenes of sibling rivalry. These get, intermittently, quite exciting, and the last act, which takes place in the early hours of morning and in which Fate gets seriously down to its appointed business of closing in, exerts a real ominous grip, much enhanced by Kevin Lamotte's lighting as it charts the pitiless onset of dawn. But, whatever the spatial limitations of the Court House stage, Bradecki was surely wrong to make such copious use of entrances from the auditorium. The daughters, when summoned by their mother, have to waste precious time climbing the steps from the lobby, when they should be gathering instantly like so many crows. An explicitly claustrophobic drama becomes diffuse.

Mainland European artists are commonly supposed to have access to depths of emotion unplumbable by the uptight British, a category that includes the Anglo-Canadians who dominate the Shaw (and most other Canadian theatres). This is probably a myth; and it is difficult to see any logical reason why a Polish director such as Bradecki should be more at home than a Canadian with a hot-climate play like this one. Certainly, some heat is missing here.

One might want to blame that on Richard Sanger's translation, but in fact it sounds direct and speakable, and if it lacks lyricism then so, by its author's own proud testimony, does the original. Lorca, a poet as well as a playwright, was insistent that in this play he had abandoned poetry for realism. Unlike such florid, and often static, Lorca plays as Blood Wedding, this one moves, purposefully and economically. There is poetry but it is the stripped-down poetry of action.

Though her daughters are the ones who scream or moan about their frustrated sexuality, the play's real passion resides in Bernarda herself. It is a passion, though, for the denying of passion, her own or anyone else's; and that makes her an almighty acting problem.

Glenda Jackson gave an atrociously externalized account of the role in London a few years ago, as a sort of walking ramrod. Nora McLellan at Niagara (in a performance nobly shorn of mannerisms) attempts something subtler and initially more sympathetic: a woman who is a victim of her own upbringing, a tyrannical tradition and a couple of no-good men. She does, in short, what has been done to her. But the play still demands that she do it with conviction; and the director hardly helps her by sending her on anguished walking tours of the stage, undermining her authority when she should be standing firm.

At the end, when the character turns into a mixture of avenging angel and hanging judge, the actress has to fall back on stonewalling; she simply cannot summon the grandeur.

She shares many of her scenes with an old servant on whom she leans for advice and who both fears and despises her. Not being one of the family, this woman serves a link with the outside world, bringing word for example of a girl who has been caught having non- marital sex and is about to be lynched for it -- the best news that Bernarda, whose own household may be running out of chastity belts, gets to hear from one end of the play to the other. Patricia Hamilton delivers these bulletins with some power, but there isn't a character there. Her relationship with her employer is seriously skewed; a confidante is not an equal, but this one is played almost as a superior. In caste-ridden pre-war Spain this is not credible. (It might be interesting to see a modern adaptation of this play, set, say, in Nigeria or Pakistan.)

Lynne Cormack's angular Angustias stands out among the daughters, as also does Susie Burnett's Martirio, a skilled tormentor of herself and others. Fiona Byrne's Adela, the only character in the play possessed of erotic pride, is unfortunately to the English Channel than to the Mediterranean. The Casa Bernarda also boasts a crazed grandmother who periodically emerges from her room cradling a lamb, as if in mockery of her enforcedly barren descendants; Jillian Cook plays her with most delicate dementia, but she suffers more than any of the others from huddled exits and entrances, as if the production were as concerned as her family to keep her out of sight. It is good, however, to see generally underused actresses such as Cormack and Cook given substantial roles. For all the inadequacies, there is the feel of a great play here.
