WAITING FOR THE PARADE

Royal George Theatre,

Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont.

- - -

This seems to be Kelli Fox's week for being incandescent. The night after opening in Rutherford and Son, she appeared, peroxided almost out of recognition, in Waiting for the Parade, giving an equally striking performance in a totally dissimilar role.

She is one of a quintet of wives, mothers and daughters doing their bit on the Calgary home front in the Second World War. Fox's character has a husband serving abroad and reported missing. She takes up with another man, to the strong disapproval of some of her friends and the envious tolerance of others. Conflicted and fuelled by alcohol and music (she likes Elmer's Tune, though it's a controversial choice), she takes off into a great tirade in which affirmation dukes it out with despair.

Throughout the play, she is a vibrant, practical voice to whom the audience instinctively warms. Once again one admires this actress's energy. I don't mean that she shouts or runs around a lot, but that she zeroes in on her character's emotions and projects them with maximum intensity and minimum fuss. She is, as far as I can tell, absolutely in period. And it's refreshing to see an actress who has specialized in being solitary or rejected getting her man: two of them, in fact.

Those men, like all of their gender, remain offstage. The five women have the play to themselves. Just about every notable Canadian actress seems, at some time or another, to have appeared in John Murrell's play, which was first staged in 1977 and has been constantly revived ever since. It belongs to the snapshot school of Canadian drama; a historical situation is researched, representative characters are devised and given group scenes and solo scenes that it is hoped will accumulate into some kind of meaningful mosaic. The technique now seems old-fashioned, though it is still in frequent use (vide, most recently, Unity 1918) and is seldom as well-handled as here.

Murrell's greatest assets are his sympathetic ear and his resourcefulness at mixing and matching. Four of his characters work, in uneasy alliance, at a voluntary organization whose name and precise purpose are unspecified but which seems devoted to entertaining the troops, chastely, and bolstering the morale of any passing civilians. (Qualifications seem to include the ability to sing harmony.)

The fifth woman is a ringer, a German immigrant, non-Jewish, whose father has been interned. She is an object of suspicion to most of her neighbours, and the play's means of demonstrating this are initially glib; when she's heard singing Lilli Marlene, the Canadians, who apparently were unaware of the song's origins, immediately remove it from their playlist. Complexity dawns, however, when some of the other women begin to welcome her as a friend, with losses that parallel their own. Others don't, or they vacillate. Battle lines aren't drawn, exactly, but there are stimulating skirmishes.

If the play is meant to make a larger statement about women and war, as the author's program note suggests, then Linda Moore's prosaic production hasn't brought it out. The interest remains anecdotal. But they are involving anecdotes, and the performers are afforded at least the opportunity to dig beneath familiar surfaces. (How many male playwrights have written five good parts for women in one play?)

Laurie Paton matches Fox's performance with her quiet, dark and witty work as the German. Donna Belleville gives a brooding, peremptory account of an older woman who loses one son to war and another, a conscientious objector, to peace. Jenny L. Wright plays a young wife tethered to an ageing slob of a husband, and is lively if somewhat cartooned; hers is the one off-stage relationship that never comes clear in our minds, but then this character, with her reiterated worship of Leslie Howard and her uncritical trust in Mackenzie King, is something of a caricature.

The women's institute, or Calgary canteen, or whatever it is, is run by a bossy, self-important and self-consciously elegant person who is patronizingly beastly to the German, and raises her colleagues' hackles as well. "I don't like that woman," says one of them, repeatedly, apparently voicing the general opinion.

Unfortunately, it seems to be shared by Helen Taylor, who plays the hate object and delivers a flat performance that never suggests she might have a valid point of view, or even feelings of her own. It's true that the dramatist has left the character out in the cold, but it's the actress's and director's job to bring her in again.
