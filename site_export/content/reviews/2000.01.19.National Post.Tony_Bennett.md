TONY BENNETT

Roy Thomson Hall, Toronto

Whenever Tony Bennett appeared on my family's TV screen in the 1960s, my father would invariably describe him as the least likely pop singer he had ever seen. He looked, according to my dad, like a cross between a tycoon and a prizefighter: both, come to think of it, useful accomplishments in the vocal game.

He looks much more like a singer now; or maybe, as a great survivor -- less emotionally involving than Sinatra or musically than Torme, but still the last of the giants -- he has redefined his milieu, so that we expect singers to look like him. He still moves with engaging awkwardness -- he ambles where others would strut -- but his high, broad frame and silver mane (surely one of the most persuasive hairpieces in the world) give him the look of an amiable lion.

His singing, too, is mostly lean and leonine, especially on his current tour, on which he's accompanied by an unequivocal jazz quartet, headed by his perpetual pianist, the venerable Ralph Sharon. Sharon really does seem to get better with age; he took a couple of delicate, beautifully economic solos, which sounded like Count Basie waxing lyrical, and his mere presence was a blessing. He and Bennett climaxed the night with a dreamy, gently rocking duet on S'Wonderful (not usually either a dreamy or a gentle piece) on which the singer seemed to be backing the instrumentalist.

Intimate settings bring out the best in Bennett. (I'm talking about music, not venues. Nobody could call Roy Thomson Hall intimate. One would love to hear him in a club.) Shouting with a big band, he can sometimes be exhilarating but is often messy. Small groups let him concentrate on what he does best, which is to swing in a whisper.

Others, like Peggy Lee and Blossom Dearie, have done that, of course, but they are miniaturists by nature; Bennett, warm where they are cool, offers the attraction of power kept voluntarily in check. He takes this to extremes with his now-statutory practice of singing one song unmiked. It used to be Fly Me to the Moon; this time, in commemoration of Martin Luther King, he moved to a different part of the heavens and sang Kurt Weill's Lost in the Stars. It wasn't the best way of exploring the lyric, but the suddenly frail voice in the huge hall was its own metaphor for a song about man adrift in the cosmos.

We expected, on the basis of numerous interviews, to hear a lot of material from his new Duke Ellington CD, but it seems that, like Bogart in Casablanca, we were misinformed. He did only three Ellington songs, of which one -- It Don't Mean a Thing -- was mainly a showcase for his drummer, Clayton Cameron: a spectacular musician who went from a masterful juggling routine with sticks and cymbals to a rapturous solo with brushes.

Bennett came into his own on a sumptuous Mood Indigo, for which he supplied the mood while the lighting boys poured on the indigo. An unexpected absentee was his eccentric re-casting of I Got It Bad, which he sings on disc as She Got It Bad, at some expense to the rhyme-scheme and -- in its assumption that only women get the blues - - to the principle of equal-opportunity suffering.

Otherwise, his program was remarkably similar to the one he gave us last year. It derived from his previous half-dozen albums, all of them, as he kept reminding us, Grammy winners. Winning the prize for Best Traditional Vocal, or whatever patronizing title they give it nowadays, has become a running boast for him, just as losing it has become a running gag for Rosemary Clooney.

Incidentally, though the Grammys could hardly matter less, can anyone explain, fashion apart, why neither Bennett nor Clooney has ever made the crossover to a nomination for best vocal per se, while Diana Krall has? The songs may have been the same but much of the singing was better.

Another Weill song, Speak Low, which I thought over-dramatized last year, was beautifully controlled this time; even the studied descents into hushed parlando seemed to work, as a literal fulfillment of the song's injunction. Similarly effective -- though he should have found a different way of doing it in the second chorus -- was his stabbing isolation of the word "need" in People; he is the only singer to make this song (abstracted from his Here's to the Ladies CD, where it honours Barbra Streisand) sound good: important instead of self-important.

The selections from his Fred Astaire disc were suitably rapturous; they included, on the slimmest of historical pretexts, a superbly sly Old Devil Moon, in which Bennett sat out the kicker- phrase about "razzle dazzle" and let the drummer supply an aural equivalent. (This time the stage went gold.)

He did a no-nonsense hour-and-40-minutes with no warm-up act, no intermission and a minimum of chat. Most of the songs were done in a dancing, casual 4/4, supplying maximum opportunity for overall good humour and flexibly conversational phrasing; the latter quality helped People a lot.

He stiffened up only for the inevitable San Francisco (give him credit, he still sings it like he means it) and for his closer, How Do You Keep the Music Playing, a better-than-usual lyric by the incorrigibly earnest Alan and Marilyn Bergman, in which the soft concern that fills the outer edges of his voice builds to the ringing affirmation that he likes to summon from its centre.
