Stephen Sondheim, America's pre-eminent musical dramatist, turned 80 this year, and New York has been paying tribute. There has been the expected round of gala celebrity concerts. Perhaps less predictably, Bergdorf Goodman has turned its display windows, the most celebrated in Manhattan, into a showcase of Sondheim memorabilia: costumes and props from his various shows, photographs, a glorious bumper collection of Hirschfeld caricatures, and -- most attestingly --scrolls of microfilm, elegantly draped, each bearing the complete lyrics of a particular musical. And a theatre is being named after him: the former Henry Miller, on 43rd Street.

All very fitting and deserved. But the best way of celebrating a dramatist is to stage his work. There hasnt been a new Sondheim musical on Broadway since Passion in 1994 but the stream of revivals has been constant, especially if you count the two perennials, West Side Story and Gypsy, for which he wrote only the lyrics. A revival of the former is now into its second year, but the only music-and-lyrics Sondheim on offer is A Little Night Music (Walter Kerr Theatre), an acknowledged classic but here receiving its first major New York production since its original staging in 1973. It comes, like many other recent revivals of American shows, from a small theatre in London, and its a beauty.

The director is Trevor Nunn, whose record as a stager of musicals has been mixed; his Oklahoma! was fine, his My Fair Lady and Anything Goes stuffed to overflowing with mostlymechanicalexuberance. Night Music, though, hardly lends itself to endless production numbers; it demand wit, elegance, bitter-sweetness and a sensitivity to text. Nunn, a great director of Shakespeare, treats the songs here with the same attention to the subtle flow of feelings and ideas that he would bring to a Shakespearean soliloquy. The seriofarcical complications of Hugh Wheeler's book -- painful to the characters, funny to us -- are handled with exemplary wit and finesse. A show that can sometimes seem like the idea of a high comedy, is here the thing itself. The second act, where the intrigues thicken and where the lines between the principals and the observing chorus progressively blur, is especially strong in holding the elusive balance between action and commentary. It's summed up by the maid Petra (Leigh Ann Larkin) alone on stage, singing the most powerful, abandoned version of The Millers Son: Its at once her own clear-eyed erotic credo and a summary of the entire action.

A more elaborate onlooker is the great Angela Lansbury as the ex-courtesan Madame Armfeldt, fragile but imperious in her wheelchair, working her way magisterially through her big song Liaisons, and triumphantly despatching the script's more laboured epigrams by turning them into barbed conversation. She has the show's great ending: She dies, contentedly. As her daughter Desiree, Catherine Zeta-Jones has the mannerisms of a 21st-century British actress, rather than the manners of a 19th-century Swedish one; but Alexander Hanson, a first-rate actor and singer, and the one survivor from the London staging, is exemplary as her co-protagonist, the lawyer Frederik. As a middle-aged man, to be rescued from thralldom to a virginal young wife, he negotiates every twist and turn of self-aware absurdity. Self-betrayal is what the show, like much of Sondheim, is about, and this production thrives on it.

Along with the Sondheim revivals go Sondheim anthologies. The birthday offering, Sondheim on Sondheim (Studio 54), gets its title from the filmed presence of the composer himself, who has always talked illuminatingly about his own work, and here intersperses numbers with commentary, autobiography and occasional technical analysis; I could have done with more of the last, but I may not be a typical audience member. James Lapine, a frequent collaborator, devised and directed the show, and it looks very smart, with video screens arranged and re-arranged in scrabble-board patterns against Beowulf Boritts fine stairway set. There are three billed stars: Vanessa Williams, whose looks and moves are beautiful but who lacks humour; Tom Wopat, who seems consistently on the verge of delivering something memorable, but never quite does; and Barbara Cook, Broadways definitive ingenue of the 1950s and a concert and cabaret

legend ever since. (Sondheim, Lansbury, Cook: This is a great season for octogenarians.) She, like the show itself, takes time to hit her stride, but she finds it in a sequence of songs from Passion, a dark vulnerable show that here sounds more affecting than ever before. A supporting ensemble of five all have their moments. Songs from Merrily We Roll Along come off appetizingly well; those from better-known shows tend to leave you homesick for their original contexts: more of a problem here than it was with earlier Sondheim samplers, when the material was less well known.

David Edgar, speaking for all British playwrights, recently wrote that none of them could write a word that wasn't influenced in some way by Harold Pinter. The same holds true for Sondheim and subsequent writers of musicals. It certainly holds for the Tony-and Pulitzer-winning Next to Normal, by Tom Kitt and Brian Yorkey, which emulates Sondheim not so much in style (it's more like imitation William Finn) than in ambition. It's a self-consciously "serious" musical about mental illness. A bipolar woman struggles to come to terms with her husband, her daughter and her son -- the last of whom, we discover, died in childhood. The discovery wrecks the show, since he hangs around in memory, haranguing everyone. Everyone else screams right back; this is a rock score, virtually devoid of nuance. Not every subject has to be a musical; not every sentiment gains from being rhymed. If there is one lesson Sondheim and his collaborators have to teach their successors it's how, and when, to make use of song. Most of them have yet to learn.
