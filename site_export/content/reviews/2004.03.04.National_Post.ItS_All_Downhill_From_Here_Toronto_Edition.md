RUNE ARLIDGE

Tarragon Theatre, Toronto

As a comparatively new Canadian, and one wholly indifferent to sports, I had no idea until this week who Roone Arledge might have been. Thanks to kind friends and helpful press packages, I have now learned of his fame as a sportscaster and TV executive, and I could even understand the single sidelong reference to him in Michael Healey's Rune Arlidge, whose title character, a woman, was named after Mr. A. by a father who was a fan. What I don't understand is why Ms. A. is the title character, because she never properly takes her place at the centre of the play. Neither, unfortunately, does anything or anybody else.

This is a three-act play, a long-neglected form well worth resurrecting. The action begins in 1994, and ends 15 years in the future, a time about which the play, mercifully, bears no apocalyptic tidings. It seems that life then will be much the same as it is now; even print journalism still hangs on. The people the play depicts have gone even further downhill than they had when we first met them, but that was to be expected.

The scene throughout is a dilapidated Ontario cottage on what one suspects to be a fairly stagnant bit of lake. Here, when we start, lives Mrs. Frances Arlidge, whose husband left her, and who is taking this and other things out on the world and especially on her two early-twentyish daughters, Michelle and Rune.

The three of them have an abrasive relationship. Frances usually communicates with the girls by swearing and moaning at them, and they give at least as good as they get, at least in the profanity department. Frances is also given to delivering long retrospective monologues about, for example, how she and her ex came to buy the cottage and what a terrible idea it turned out to be. These narratives are often funny and always fluent: "How do you speak in paragraphs?" Rune asks her, and even while enjoying them its possible to share her incredulity.

According to Frances, a lack of empathy is what causes relationships to fail; she should know (and, to be fair to her and her author, she knows she should know). Also according to Frances, Michelle is tough and takes after her, while Rune is sensitive and vulnerable and needs protecting. Frances may be right, but the difference doesnt register dramatically. In the first act, we perceive the sisters just as a double-act up against their mother, and the play, lacking a foundation, never recovers.

Things do happen: Michelle, doped-up and promiscuous, is revealed to be pregnant, while Rune, currently a student, is visited by her boyfriend and receives a surprise proposal, which does not work out. Act Two, which takes place now, is almost wholly uneventful. A series of strokes has robbed the once-vocal Frances of speech; she can only grunt. She gets very little sympathy. Michelle has a 10- year-old daughter with whom she is already replicating the relationship she had with her own mother. Rune has a new, and startlingly boorish, fiance. The ways of the heart are strange, especially when fuelled by desperation, but it's impossible to believe these two could ever have got together: Not that the guy isn't well-meaning -- he may, come to think of it, be the best- intentioned person on stage -- but still.

Act Three shows us Michelle more like her mother than ever, while finally bringing Rune herself into something like focus. Re- encountering her oldest flame, who is still burning, she delivers some wise words about how knowing the kind of person you are, and why, is no help at all in changing it. There is a Chekhovian sense of boats having been burned, mainly while nobody was looking.

Its Chekhov perhaps mediated through the homelier tragicomedy of, say, Alan Ayckbourn. (I once quoted a friends description of Ayckbourn as speeded-up Chekhov; Healey, in this play, is slowed- down Ayckbourn.) There are recurring images to bind the piece together -- leeches, canoes and toast are prominently featured -- and the self-perpetuating mother-daughter dynamic is wincingly recognizable whenever it appears. It's what we grab on to. But it's also a bit obvious, and since Rune herself is supposed to stand somewhat outside it, it pulls the plays focus.

Leah Cherniaks production is strong on mood (thanks are due to Charlotte Dean's set and Kirk Elliott's sound design) but less so on pace and coherence. Good actors abound. Fiona Reids phrasing of Frances fretful arias is expectedly expert, but more impressive is her silent pain later, her face and hands expressing her longing to have another vengeful go. One still never knows who this woman is, especially socially.

One does know what Rune does; she's a newspaper columnist, but there's no particular dramatic reason for her to be so. Jane Spidell is excellent in the role, and at moments extraordinary, the moments being the gentle ones in which she exposes a capacity for love combined with an incapacity for letting it happen. Julie Stewart is probably louder, not just in decibels, than Michelle needs to be, but I wouldnt fault her too heavily for it.

Severn Thompson is a believable terror at 10 years old, and a sultry, incisive one at 25. (Her role includes a near brush with incest, horrific because casual.) Rick Roberts is charming as Runes loser of a suitor, and Ari Cohen works very hard as his loudmouth successor who has to carry far too much of the middle movement. The same act concludes, however, with a lovely speech, a stumbling evocation of the might-have-been, beautifully delivered by John Dolan. It suggests that the play's real subject might be time. But then, of what long play could that not be said? Healey's previous plays have marked him as our most humanely intelligent playwright; nothing here makes you doubt his talent, but structure and content are way out of whack.
