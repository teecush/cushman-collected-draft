In the first scene of Soulpepper's The Royal Comedians we meet Moliere, played by Diego Matamoros, giving a virtuoso display of crosstalk and pratfalls to acknowledge the presence of the most powerful and distinguished of patrons. The setting is Moliere's own Palais Royal theatre in 17th-century Paris; the patron is King Louis XIV, direct from his own royal palace. As he seems to like the show, this could be the great actordramatist's proudest hour. The last scene will find him in disgrace and despair, literally dying on stage while playing his own Malade Imaginaire, an ending too appropriate to be true, except that it apparently was. How did he get from there to here?

Mikhail Bulgakov's play, written in Moscow in 1936 but censored out of performance for decades afterwards, offers a variety of possible explanations, none of them conclusive. Moliere has offended the clergy by writing and performing Tartuffe, the great satire of religious hypocrites, and they are out to get him. A fire-breathing captain of the musketeers is also out to get him, preferably on the point of his rapier, having been duped by those same priests into believing that he is the original of Moliere's licentious and blasphemous character Don Juan. Moliere may have contracted an incestuous marriage, since his young wife Armande, whom he took to be the sister of a former flame, is actually her daughter. He suspects Armande of being unfaithful to him, principally with a young actor whom he first found hiding in a harpsichord and whom he brought into the company and called his son. These threads do, theoretically, tie together; the Archbishop of Paris who's also the arch-enemy of Moliere uses all of them to discredit him with the fickle-favoured king who, beginning as the playwright's apparent protector, ends by abandoning him. But in practice there's no propulsion; the domestic components in particular have disappeared long before the end. We grasp that Moliere feels angry and deserted, but there is no logical line leading to his death, which plays as a picturesque but autonomous event.

I have seen two productions of this play (the first in England, where it was titled Moliere), and in neither has it registered as more than a succession of scenes, some vivid, some vague. There's something intractably woolly about it. The most fascinating thing about the play is its background. It was Bulgakov's own remarkably daring commentary on the Stalinist regime under which he lived; and which he only survived because the dictator had, as Paula Wing's program note says, a soft spot for him. Stalin played the same kind of cat-and-mouse game with Bulgakov as the play's Louis does with Moliere. Their relationship has in turn been dramatized this year at the National Theatre in London in Collaborators, a new play that may be less distinguished and is certainly less risky than Bulgakov's, but is more rewarding to watch. It also makes use of scenes from The Royal Comedians just as that play makes use of scenes from the collected works of Moliere.

Those scenes are the highpoints of Laszlo Marton's Toronto production. He may have made more of them than Bulgakov envisaged, but he's done his author a favour. Moliere's uxurious vulnerability is illuminated by a passage from The School for Wives in which he's the farcically anguished cuckold, while his wife and her lover play, more or less, themselves. Then we get what is almost (not quite) the best performance of the Tartuffe seduction scene that I have ever seen, with Matamoros offering a superbly sanctimonious expose of the horny hypocrite, and Raquel Duffy a delightful mix of the practical and the provocative. The Imaginary Invalid finale is given the full comedieballet treatment, all slapstick and grotesquerie and music, that its text demands and, at least in English language productions, hardly ever gets.

The rest of Marton's staging veers from exciting to exasperating. Lorenzo Savoini has designed a whispering gallery of a set that serves for both varieties of palace and whose multiplicity of doors are put to use alternately farcical and sinister, the latter aspect much reinforced by Richard Feren's scary sound-score. Its other acoustic properties are questionable. A remarkable proportion of the play is hard to hear. Even the Soulpepper stalwarts in the cast sound muffled, while among those less experienced, inaudibility runs riot (if you can have a quiet riot). They do especial damage to the scenes in which Moliere gets busted by the church's secret police, for which the production goes into full 1930s NKVD mode, replete with black uniforms and threatening searchlights. Meant to reinforce the play's implicit metaphor, this merely succeeds in shortcircuiting it. Rule of thumb: When a period play has modern dialogue, it doesn't need modern dress as well.

I've now seen two virtuoso actors fail to bring this play's protagonist to life. Antony Sher in the British version was so hyper-kinetic that I wanted to yell at him to keep still and let me see the character; Matamoros, equally adept with limbs and lungs and more controlled, left me feeling that there was no character to see. Certainly there are no relationships, other than the self-abasing one with Gregory Prest's Louis, who almost justifies his more vocally indolent moments with some electrifying transitions into absolutist authority. Otherwise, even as a lover, this Moliere is a loner, whether intentionally or by default. He does have friends, or at least loyal employees, both of them actors with other responsibilities on the side, and both well played here: William Webster gives the most humanly detailed performance of the evening as a nervous and censorious candle snuffer; Michael Simpson as the 17-thcentury equivalent of a company manager also has to function, to no helpful end, as a narrator. On the other side of the social and psychological tracks, there are stage-seizing performances by Stuart Hughes and Michael Hanrahan as the lords temporal and (theoretically) spiritual. They express their contempt for one another with a spitting contest that captures the grotesque satirical spirit for which the whole play may conceivably be striving. The idea is that all the characters are royal comedians, professional or otherwise. The younger performances, mainly by members of the current Soulpepper academy are, with an exception and a half, lamentable, the exception being Daniel Williston as the king's tame shoemaker who's also his jester, and the half Paolo Santalucia, who has his moments, though not enough of them, as the boy from the harpsichord. That whole business is obscurely and confusingly presented. Marton's finest productions have been notable for their physicality and their clarity. The first is more in evidence here than the second.
