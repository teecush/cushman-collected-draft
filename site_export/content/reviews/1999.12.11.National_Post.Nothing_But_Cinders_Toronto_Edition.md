I'm sorry to say it but Sears aint got no class. Sears presents Cinderella, the program says, all big, bold and italicized. They seem to have taken their participation as a licence to plug their wares.

Most shows at least have the grace to spread the commercials around a bit. Here the backers even have their logo emblazoned on the toy sleigh that descends from the flies bearing presents for the intrepid kids who have climbed on stage for the mandatory sing- along sequence. The company may think this makes them look like Santa Claus. I think it makes them look like Scrooge.

It is also stretching things to describe this Cinderella as a contribution to the arts. Its the latest of Ross Petty's attempts to mount an English-style pantomime in Canada, an endeavour that peaked a few years ago in the memorable Robin Hood and has been in steep decline ever since. Petty, previously the fruity villain of his own pieces, has this time remained off stage as adaptor (from what?), and director.

Much of his staging is not so much traditional as antiquarian -- not since the '60s can a panto have begun with half-a-dozen merry villagers doing what might be charitably described as a dance while favouring us with a hopeful chorus of A Wonderful Day Like Today.

The old story has been given a few new tweaks, too. Baron Hardup, Cinderellas dad, has not yet taken his second wife, he is merely engaged. We never do meet his intended, but her daughters, the Ugly Sisters, have come to stay with their future in-laws. Even by pantomime standards, this raises serious problems of credibility.

If the sisters have just arrived and have no legal status in the household, why does Cinderella put up with their bullying? Why doesnt she just appeal to her father, who seems to dislike them anyway?

He is a puzzling character altogether.

Though apparently eligible for an invitation to the ball he a) stays home; b) is pressed into service, along with the faithful Buttons, as one of his daughter's coachmen; c) turns up anyway in his own person and gets an official welcome. The baron is played by Ernie Coombs, the veteran children's entertainer (Dressup as Hardup), and he won a little of my heart by trying less frenetically to be funny than anyone else on stage.

Cinderella (Laura Bertram) is a Disney heroine with glimmerings of a conscience. She disapproves of blood sports and hangs out in the forest with her furry friends singing Talk to the Animals.

Prince Charming (Laird Mackintosh) is thoroughly repulsive, making his first appearance swinging across the stage and back on a trailing vine, and then announcing, "My name is Prince. I swing both ways."

The Ugly Sisters are played by a couple of refugees from our classic festivals, Stratfords Juan Chioran and Niagaras William Vickers. Chioran, very tall, has the more originality and authority, and Vickers, rather short, the more music-hall spirit.

The production, whatever Sears' investment, looks as cheap as it sounds. Even the palace staircase lets us down, being poky and twisting and destructive of grand entrances.

There are a few good moments, such as Vickers singing, comically but also sweetly, Round Midnight. This must be the first time Thelonious Monk has made it into a pantomime. On the whole, though, if you want a show for Christmas, go to Oliver!

CINDERELLA
