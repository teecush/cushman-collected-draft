HOTEL LOOPY

Tarragon Extra Space, Toronto

- - -

Here's a health to Rick Roberts, actor of this parish: recently returned to it, in fact, after a spell working in the United States, a fact that has earned him a surprising number of column inches in the local rags.

He justifies them all in Hotel Loopy, a show for which he is one of seven writer-performers and in which he has one solo scene that must be the funniest thing in town. It involves him speaking into a phone, a long red one to match the noses worn by everyone in the cast. (Hotel Loopy, it had better be confessed right now, is a clown show.) He tells his friend at the other end -- who is unheard, unseen, unidentified and, let us face it, non-existent -- that the audience isn't laughing.

This, of course, immediately starts the audience laughing, nervously at first and in isolated pockets. Roberts then describes the quality and quantity of this laughter, thereby provoking more of it. Certain words in his commentary are especially likely to set us off. He remarks on this fact, thereby ensuring they set us off all over again.

Interestingly, the laughter -- at least on the night I went -- never becomes full-throated or unanimous, but some individual peals are hysterical. This creates its own tension; every one of us feels alone in the actor's grip.

Audience reactions are, I suppose, fairly predictable, but Roberts must have to improvise his routine to some extent. Whatever the case, his control is cast-iron. Indeed, I was probably wrong in describing the scene as a solo; it's a duet played with the house, or perhaps a symphony in which he, with a maestro's affected negligence, conducts us. It's the most creative use of audience participation I have ever seen.

Hotel Loopy is indeed set in a hotel. It resembles Garbo's grand one in attracting famous guests who like to parade down the staircase, and John Cleese's Fawlty Towers in being in a state of dilapidated chaos. The latter resemblance extends to the sudden death of one of the guests and the staff's subsequent efforts to dispose of the body.

Roberts is the bellhop, with a Manuelian habit of dropping the guests' bags. His audience-involving habits declare themselves quite early in the show, when he crosses into our space, promising not to embarrass us by singling out individuals, and singling out individuals just to impress on them that he won't embarrass them. This, he reassures us, is not a kids' show. It has a fourth wall. He knows, because he's just cleaned it.

The show's strongest point is the deftness with which it demolishes its own pretensions; its weakest is the pretensions themselves. Theatre Columbus, the company responsible, is a long- established comedy-and-movement troupe with an uneven track record, and this is an uneven show.

Its credentials warm the heart. The cast includes some of Toronto's best-loved actors, and some of its most notable playwrights. (In a couple of instances, these are the same people.) All, as already noted, are in clown makeup and costumes. For those playing the hotel staff this involves striped pyjamas of a distinctly jailhouse cut.

The producers quote the mime artist's spiritual mentor Jacques LeCoq: "When the actor comes on stage, wearing a small red nose, [he is] entirely without defense ... The clown is the person who flops, who messes up his turn." These are resonant sentences, but the clown's vulnerability, his scapegoat quality, is much diminished when everybody is somebody's fool.

Michael Healey presides as the hotel manager, greeting every untoward thing that happens by looking, as is his wont, amazed but not surprised. Dropping and then hanging in at the hotel piano is Scott McCord as Louis the lounge act, hell's answer to Billy Joel, breathing lascivious-sounding greetings into his microphone and whispering his way, as imperturbably as incongruously, through what he claims is his trademark rendition of Rhinestone Cowboy.

Both of these actors are unforced fun. Less so is Marjorie Campbell as (I think) the proprietor's wife, interrupting illicit trysts with her frenetic cleaning, as if to prove that a vacuum abhors Nature.

Crossing over to the guest list: Marthas Ross also seems to be trying too hard, or her portion of the script does, as a self- proclaimed famous writer who claims to recall assignations with the manager on the beaches of their youth. Kristen Thomson, as a visitor very rich and very easily provoked, has surprisingly little to do except, at the end of the show, give birth. (She has looked convincingly pregnant earlier on.) She does get, all too briefly, to intone a blues to Louis' accompaniment, including a line that sounds like "I'm not Judi Dench." Maybe she isn't, but I can't think of a Canadian actress who comes closer.

The prize turn on this side of the register is Alon Nashman's; he plays Tom Cruise, or at least plays an actor playing Tom Cruise, who in turn does not wish to be recognized, despite having adopted the thinnest of disguises, or at least it would be thin if Nashman bore the slightest resemblance to Cruise in the first place.

You're lost, aren't you? So was I, and I'm not convinced that all this blurring of lines and confusion of roles (where's that fourth wall again?) is justified by the amount of laughter it raises. In his own right, though, Nashman is very appealing, whether descending the staircase, singing along with the Sammy Davis record of What Kind of Fool Am I without quite knowing the words or at least the phrasing, or grandly taking on Louis to play "the Soundtrack to My Life."

His antics lead to a kind of showdown but it doesn't count for much. Nor do Healey's head-scratching reflections (he always sounds like he's scratching his head, even when he physically isn't) on humanity being divided into those who find life too short and those who find it too long. Nor does the (possibly related) fact that the play ends with both a baby and a corpse in view. The show prides itself on being inconsequential, but even inconsequentiality has consequences, the chief one being that it's difficult to keep it funny over a space of nearly two hours. (An alternative might be to make it menacing, but Leah Cherniak's production hardly tries for that.)

There are longish periods when Hotel Loopy becomes, to clean up one of its own jokes, Hotel Droopy. When it is funny, though, it's sublime.
