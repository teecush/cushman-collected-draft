BOOK REVIEW CATS OF ANY COLOR: Jazz Black and White

By Gene Lees Oxford University Press, 246 pages, $34.95

Friday, February 24, 1995

Robert Cushman

Essays that explore race and riffs Reviewed by Robert Cushman IN a feature on racism in the current issue of Jazz Times, Gene Lees is quoted as saying, "If I was a black, I would hate white people, no question in my mind about that." Lees himself is a Canadian who grew up during the Second World War with a pantheon of black American heroes. ("Fifty years ago," he recalls, "it appeared that Canadians had never accomplished anything.") He has spent his adult life in the United States writing about and around jazz. The essays that make up this book provide plenty of grim evidence as to why blacks might hate whites, but are also full of heartening testimony from those who refuse to.

Louis Armstrong supplies the epigraph: "It's no crime for cats of any colour to get together and blow." Clark Terry tells a horrific story about being forced, as a young man, to hide under the mud of a building site after his friend had accidentally jostled a white woman in a Southern store. He then tells of how, decades later, his attempt to start a street- kids' band in Harlem was sabotaged when he brought in a white teacher. "Man," he was told, "we don't want Whitey teaching us about our music." A disgusted Terry insists that a note doesn't care who plays it, as long as it's done well. A third and much younger trumpeter echoes him: "Music is music."

That's Wynton Marsalis, discussing his own determination to prove that a black jazzman could also play classical. For Marsalis, though, this traffic seems to be on a one-way street. Such, at least, is Lees' contention in his last and most spirited chapter. He excoriates Marsalis, as director of the jazz program at Lincoln Centre, for excluding white composers and musicians. His real target, though, seems to be Marsalis's mentor, the critic Stanley Crouch, whom he accuses of rewriting history to marginalize white musicians such as Bill Evans and to downgrade blacks - including Miles Davis - who played with them.

Wars between jazz critics are bloody affairs, and it's possible that some old scores are being paid off here. But Lees documents his charges, sometimes to devastating effect. Having quoted Marsalis as claiming that improvisation was "invented" in New Orleans, he responds with a quick catalogue of improvisational European music, going back centuries before jazz. In more detail, he demonstrates that black swing bands were as eager to hire white arrangers as white bands were to "exploit" black ones. None of this cancels out the brutal discrimination suffered by black Americans in and out of the music business. But Lees' conclusion is unassailable. Either jazz is the protest-vehicle of a single race, or it's a major musical form and, as such, bound to be explored by anyone interested.

The rest of the book is made up of easy-going profiles: Of bop pianist Horace Silver, who says fairly and decently that if their talent were equal he would hire a black musician over a white one but does not believe that you can rectify an old injustice by inflicting a new one; of trumpeter and scam artist Red Rodney and masterful expatriate bassist Red Michell; and of Dave Brubeck, whose part-Indian descent allows Lees to highlight a racial component of jazz that has really been marginalized. His subjects all come out sounding the same - an occupational hazard familiar to all writers-up of interviews - though Lees reserves the really awkward lines for himself. " 'When and where does the black participation begin in America in what we imprecisely call classical music?' I asked." Did he really?

He is also keen to tell us how he used to hang out with Miles or to throw in a reference to "my friend Oscar Peterson" in a context to which their friendship is totally irrelevant. The dedication to Dizzy Gillespie is vintage Lees, shifting the spotlight firmly from the subject to the author: "I miss you Birks./ I thought you would be there forever / like the Matterhorn, and the Aurora Borealis / and Polaris." The comparisons sound like rejects from one of his lyrics. He has written many, including the tenderly awestruck words to Evans' Waltz for Debbie and Jobim's Someone to Light Up My Life, but their uniform solemnity keeps them out of the front rank. (He also performs them well; if you can imagine a critic who sings.) Much the same is true of his prose; if his last chapter here is the best, it's partly because in the excitement of polemic he almost forgets to be self-admiring. Robert Cushman is a Toronto critic who sings.
