THE BEWITCHING OF MAX GUNTER

Theatre Passe Muraille Backspace, Toronto

Sky Gilbert is such an endearingly tireless self-publicist that it seems fair to review his new play with as much reference to what he has written about it as to what he has written in it. The Bewitching of Max Gunter has a subtitle tied to its tail: this AIDS play explodes ... Everybody duck.

"Everyone," says Gilbert in his press release, knows the typical AIDS play, and he goes on to list its constituent parts: the HIV- positive man awaiting death in his hospital bed, surrounded by relatives and friends. But, he asks, "what if HIV wasn't the cause of AIDS? ... What if men with AIDS werent guilty of anything? What if they werent victims? What if they were survivors & thrivers who got up from their hospital beds -- happy, promiscuous, & free of disease?" The answer, it seems to me, is that that would be very nice, even nicer than the moon being made of green cheese; and that the protagonist of such a drama, having arisen, would maybe do a little dance and then go home, as would the audience. It really wouldn't make much of a play. Oddly enough Gilbert seems to agree, because he hasn't written it.

In the first half he does seem to be sticking to his contract. We get Max, the bed-ridden hero, and his promised companions. Their encounters dont much resemble those in any AIDS play I remember seeing, but they do correspond to what might be called the platonic idea of an AIDS play -- an idea that most of us will gloomily recognize and that Gilbert has much fun lampooning. He isnt averse to a bit of gay stereotyping when it can help the show along, and his characters tend to be amusingly world-weary; Mark Christmann, playing Max, does this especially well.

Then there are the perfectly named Liz (Tracy Wright), Max's loyal woman friend, and a black hospital volunteer (Ryan Hinds) who puts his head cheerily round the door to ask how everybody is doing, and there are two other figures who require more detailed explanation. I'm not sure I can give it, but Ill try; the author- directors own published comments may help.

In the typical AIDS play, says Gilbert, there may or may not be a lover figure, the dreaded person who gave the homosexual AIDS. If such a character appears, the dying homosexual usually accuses his lover of killing him. That's news to me, but maybe I've been seeing the wrong plays. Anyway, Maxs lover, Thomas, does appear and is welcomed and doesnt get blamed for anything.

He does offer to perform oral sex on Max beneath the covers (Max declines) and then launches into a long speech about how Max should be out of bed eating plenty of vegetables and AIDS is just something invented by doctors and Max isn't sick at all. (In which case, we may wonder, why did he go to the doctors in the first place?)

Up till now, the actors have been reading from scripts, to demonstrate that this is just a play, for which the alleged dramatist takes no intellectual responsibility. When Thomas goes into his big number, however, he puts his book down, to show that he is speaking for his author. So it's unfortunate that Thomas, at least in Gil Garratts bright and shiny performance, seems more of a caricature than any of the supposed stick figures around him.

With one exception: Maxs monster-mother. To quote for the last time from Gilbert's typical-AIDS scenario: "[The patient's] parents come to visit, and though they are good-hearted people, they have prejudices, and find it hard to understand their sons disease"; he then adds, rather inconsistently, we know ... his parents are going to curse him. No we dont -- certainly not if they're the good- hearted people Gilbert has just claimed they are. Mother Gunter, however, (dad is dead) is not good-hearted and apparently never has been. She is selfish. and self-pitying. and does she ever have prejudices, and yes indeed she curses -- not so much her son as everyone else, especially everyone who isn't white. Her racism appears more rampant than her homophobia, though theyre clearly cut from the same cloth; in fact, her diatribes cause the black actor to leave the stage in protest. (Just what convention were meant to be in at this point is anybody's guess.)

Liz, trying to placate her, says, "Please calm down, Mrs. Gunter," or words to that effect, at which the old lady screams, Call me Goody Gunter! and the lights change and so does the parodic style (this must be the promised explosion), and suddenly we are watching a pastiche of The Crucible. Goody Gunter rolls around on what was once the hospital bed kicking her legs in the air and accusing people called Elizabeth and Mary and Tituba and Hecuba of having sent the Devil into her, and all we can tell for certain is that Ann Holloway, who plays her, seems to be having a good if exhausting time. (For some reason she never manages the Irish accent that her son says she has, and that he puts on when he's mimicking her.)

But it is possible to deduce that Mrs. Gunter is demonizing those she says have demonized her and that 17th-century persecutors of witches are being equated with modern haters of gays, not only in their intensity but in their effect. Homophobes have invented a disease and persuaded homosexuals to die of it.

As conspiracy theories go, it's remarkably simple and remarkably unpersuasive; it has too many deaths to account for. In a bizarre new twist on the old "my mother made me a homosexual" joke, we have "my mother made me a homosexual victim." Whether the theory extends to other AIDS fatalities -- to drug addicts or the children of drug addicts or to heterosexual victims, especially in Africa -- Gilbert doesn't say, inside the play or out.

Which is a shame, because though on this occasion he can neither think nor construct (his Ayn Rand play of last year, The Emotionalists, was far better) he still has a good ear; he writes very speakable dialogue, far better than that of some other, more highly rated playwrights. All he gives us here, though, are a few good jokes, a prolonged self-righteous scream and the opportunity to be out of the theatre within an hour, all posing as something daring and revolutionary. Sky, as they say, is the limit.
