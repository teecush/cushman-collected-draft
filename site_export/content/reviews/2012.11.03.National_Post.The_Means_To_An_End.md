The fabled swish of the curtain is a sound seldom heard in the theatre these days. It's especially not heard in Soulpepper's revival of Endgame despite the fact that this is the rare modern production that actually has a curtain. The noise it makes as it rises to kick off the evening is a combination of creak, whirr and rattle. It's both scary and funny, the perfect overture to a play and production that are both those things in spades.

Daniel Brooks directed Samuel Beckett's doomsday phantasmagoria in Soulpepper's second season back in 1999; and though I can't claim to remember that production in much detail, I do remember being very impressed by it. He's now revisited the play with the same design team (Julie Fox, set; Victoria Wallace, costumes; Kevin Lamotte, lighting; Richard Feren, sound) and together they have created a bleakly witty environment for the blind and chair-bound Hamm to await the end of the world. The end of his world, anyway; he doesn't much care what goes on outside, though he would prefer that nothing did. We may well be in a post-apocalyptic situation; when Clov, Hamm's shuffling manservant who can no more sit than his master can stand, laboriously climbs his set of steps to peer through one of the two impossibly high chamber windows and inspect the world outside he does just once see a sign of life, but it's soon snuffed out.

"Not an underplot!" prays Hamm, who tends to think in literary-dramatic terms ("haven't you ever heard an aside before? ... I'm gearing up for my soliloquy") when not picturing himself as a chess master, about to execute a grand checkmate. Somehow, though, his endgame never ends. He hopes for stasis but gets, at best, infinitely suspended motion. That at least is what I take from the final tableau which, even though Beckett and spoilers would seem to be notions from separate universes, I shall not describe. I will reveal though that the curtain comes down as bone-creakingly and as definitively as it went up.

What's extraordinary is that a play that seems to crave death should be so full of life, that dialogue so apparently barren should be so fertile. A script crammed with bookish references somehow flourishes as a sustained crosstalk act, with subsidiary turns for Hamm's parents whom, in the play's most celebrated image, he keeps at the side of the stage. Under Brooks' direction the tension, in a play that on the face of it has little to get tense about, never snaps.

It's a fine production but not a wholly satisfying one. The discontent stems, most unexpectedly, from the joint presence of two of Canada's finest. At text level, Joseph Ziegler's Hamm and Diego Matamoros' Clov are exemplary; not a nuance gets past them. But in the end neither seems ideally cast. Ziegler is an actor who exudes empathy, even when (as in The Crucible, A Whistle in the Dark and the pre-conversion parts of A Christmas Carol) it's negative empathy. Hamm is a monster of uncaringness, of not needing; and though Ziegler faithfully explores all his self-pitying grandiloquence, he never really embodies it.

Matamoros played Clov in the 1999 version and knocked me out, but then I spent that whole season being knocked out by the varied brilliance of an actor I had barely registered before. He's great at the physical comedy, the constant painful toiling over objects that he has always left in the wrong place, but something snapped for me when Clov, pulling himself together after a lapse, says "Now I'm intelligent again." That should seem like self-deception, but Matamoros is always intelligent; he can't avoid it. My disappointment may have something to do with my anticipation of seeing these two working as a team. Beckett doesn't really allow for teamwork; he writes competing solitudes.

On the other, stage-right, hand, Eric Peterson and Maria Vacratsis do wonders as the bin-bound parents, even importing some reciprocal tenderness or the memory of it. Beckett slams the trash-can lids down on the idea, but it's too late. We'll remember it well.
