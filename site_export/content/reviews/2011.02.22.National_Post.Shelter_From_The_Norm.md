The onstage methods of The Middle Place could hardly be simpler. The impact is very powerful, and also surprisingly complex.

Set in a homeless youth shelter in Etobicoke, it's "verbatim theatre," a new name for a form of documentary drama that's been with us for a long time now, but is here distilled to what is likely to be its purest form. How pure that actually is, we are never likely to know.

The script of The Middle Place derives entirely from filmed interviews conducted on-site, with inmates and staff. But of course, even if none of the words have been changed, they must still have been selected and edited. And they aren't being spoken by the subjects themselves; they're being delivered to us by actors who, however talented and dedicated they are (and some of the performances here are remarkable), cannot help but have their own agendas. The very fact that four out of the five of them play multiple roles means that we spend some of our time admiring their virtuosity. Alan Dilworth's direction, though technically spartan, is precisely calculated for maximum theatrical effect.

The above, by the way, is compliment rather than complaint. The Middle Place started when Project: Humanity, an artists' group focused on community projects, commissioned Andrew Kushnir, an actor and writer, to write a play for Youth Without Shelter, a Rexdale centre where they had already run some drama workshops. Kushnir decided that the centre's inhabitants had more to tell him than he had to tell them. Hence the interviews and hence, later, the play, which itself has gone through a long gestation process.

It was staged, triumphantly, at SummerWorks 2009. It's now returned for a double run: at Theatre Passe Muraille at the end of last year and at Canadian Stage now. It's the first example known to me of a co-production by two companies within the same city. I'm all in favour of good work being given the most exposure possible, but I can't avoid the feeling that both of these theatres attract essentially the same audience.

Dilworth's program note contains the following fascinating-- actually mind-blowing-- information: "Neither I nor the actors listened to or watched any of the audience footage." So, coming back to "purity:" we're getting something that's impure as documentary but thoroughly pure as theatre, interpretation rather than impersonation. It's a choice that obviously respects the actors and more subtly respects the people they're playing, which in combination means that it respects the audience.

In truth, one of the actors must be thoroughly familiar with the footage, since Kushnir appears as himself, mainly in the auditorium and behind our backs. His four colleagues -- Akosua Amo-Adem, Antonio Cayonne, Jessica Greenberg, Kevin Walker--stand on a tilted white disc (lit brightly, sometimes cruelly, by Kimberly Purtell) that covers most of the stage. They step off it, and to its side, when playing members of staff, in which roles they communicate an overall sense of careworn optimism. They make you feel that, considering the alternatives, the middle place isn't such a terrible place to be.

When they step back into the ring, their perspective changes. None of their clients actually challenges the shelter's implicit claim to be preferable to some others; some of them even project a determined cheerfulness, whose very relentlessness seems suspect and is usually the product of drugs or mental illness are both. The comment, though, that resonates the strongest comes from Amo-Adem as the most articulate and also the most belligerent of the inmates, and is to the effect that once you've entered the shelter you're already in a pit from which it can be almost impossible to climb out.

Her performance, which was overwhelming at SummerWorks, has lost a little in vocal focus in its new and more sprawling surroundings, but she remains spot-on as the shelter's equally articulate and forceful director. The common ground between the two roles reinforces the sense of there-but-for-the-grace-of-godliness that permeates the play, and occasionally gets explicit statement.

The most haunting performance is Walker's, playing a variety of disturbed young men with bleached-out voices, one of them looking forward with unnerving serenity to impending fatherhood. Nearly as unsettling in its own way is Greenberg's aggressive cheerfulness as the self-named Nevaeh. That's "heaven" backwards; another character chooses to be identified as Brussels Sprout.

Most of them are aggressive-defensive, towards their interviewer and, in the stories they tell about shelter life, toward one another. It can sometimes be hard to distinguish which of his or her allotted roles a given actor is playing, but they all do justice to the vigour and wit of the lines with which their impromptu authors have blessed them. The way these kids talk can give you hope. Like them, the play makes painfully but not depressingly clear, we need it.
