Ross Petty continues to reinvent a classic genre

This Christmas, not for the first time, Ross Petty will produce Aladdin in Toronto. But this year, for the first time with this or any other of his shows, he fulfills a long-cherished dream of taking the show to other places as well: to Vancouver, Edmonton, Calgary, Regina, Saskatoon and -- after the Toronto run -- Ottawa. Petty's holiday entertainments are commonly known as "pantomimes, but he never gives them that label. The reason is that, after all this time -- and Petty has been involved with these shows, as producer and/or performer, for more than 20 years -- people hearing the word pantomime" still say, "Is it Marcel Marceau? In North America, pantomime" is just a longer synonym for "mime. But in Britain, which provided the inspiration for Pettys shows, pantomime is very verbal. It comes with words that are spoken, sung and -- in the mandatory sections involving audience participation -- improvised.

You take a familiar story: It could be European (Cinderella), Eastern (Aladdin), or even English (Robin Hood). You tell it, with as many diversions as possible in the shape of comedy routines and popular songs. You cast as many well-known, or at least well-loved, performers as possible. Your comic female lead will be played by a man (the pantomime dame) and your hero by a woman (the principal boy). That, pretty much, is the panto as it exists right now in Britain, though not all the conventions are cast in stone; the principal boy seems to be dying out, though the dame remains immutable.

Petty has been adapting and transforming for Canadian audiences for more than 20 years. His first encounter in 1982 consisted of seeing his future wife, Karen Kain, appearing in Aladdin at the Royal Alexandra Theatre in Toronto, in what must have been the twilight of theatrical colonialism. Kain was the token Canadian" in a show that otherwise made no concessions to where it was playing.

"Id never, says Petty, bemused but smitten, seen anything like it."

The following year Paul Elliott, the British producer, cast Kain and Petty, now married, in the most English of panto subjects, Dick Whittington. They played, literally, opposite one another: She was the Good Fairy, he was the villainous King Rat, the natural prey of Dick Whittington's famous Cat. Elliott, Petty says now, was "testing the waters -- if he had a show he would bring it over, but it was all hit-and-miss. Petty became his co-producer in 1986, partly because I wanted to have control of my life, instead of waiting for the phone to ring." And once he'd taken over the whole operation, he set about turning the shows into "a different hybrid than British audiences would be used to seeing.

In its way, though, the Petty panto has been a logical development of the British prototype. It, too, uses local talent and local topical references. It still has a singalong near the end of the second act, with kids brought up from the audience and given prizes. It has been, on the whole, more businesslike about the actual storytelling. It has persevered with the dame (sometimes in Pettys own person) but has dropped the principal boy -- the latter being a convention that has always had audiences outside Britain thoroughly perplexed.

We really came into our own," says Petty, "with the arrival of Ted Dysktra. He has an actors sensibility. He works well on his feet.

He also has what Petty appealingly calls the lunacy factor." Dykstra is the actor/writer/director probably still best known for being one half of Two Pianos, Four Hands and with a penchant for the rock 'n' roll bizarre; he starred in the Toronto production of Hedwig and the Angry Inch, and will direct The Rocky Horror Show for CanStage next year. (He also gave one of the best comedy performances Ive ever seen in Soulpeppers A Chorus of Disapproval.) Dykstra himself credits the beginning of the change to another actor/director, Jim Warren, who "began dragging Ross's shows across the pond and getting rid of the sexist and racist jokes. The texts they had were beyond repair, and Jim began insisting that they write new stuff. Ross was scared to change but Jim pushed him.

When Dysktra was asked to succeed Warren, he'd never seen an English panto, and he too was scared; he then decided that fear had always produced his best work and accepted. His own inspiration was closer to home.

I thought of The Simpsons as a template. They did all these things at first. They take contemporary culture and apply it to old stories, ideas and relationships. I was doing a cartoon series at the time called Bobo Roach. We hired a writer from there, Dave Finley."

Before Finleys advent, Pettys programs had been notoriously coy about the shows' authorship. Some of the scripts might have been folklore, and indeed played that way. Two years ago Dykstra and Finley did Aladdin, the show they're reviving now (with some new jokes; they seem especially proud of having one about Svend Robinson).

Last year it was Snow White and the Group of Seven, a title that gives the clearest pointer to the new Canadianizing of the form. Though the heroines non-dwarfish friends werent painters, but a boy band, made up of alumni of Canadian Idol. In Aladdin the singalong number is If I Had a Million Dollars.

"And you can't says Dykstra, get more Canadian than that." Except perhaps by casting wrestler Bret "Hitman Hart as the genie. This idea of Dykstra's turned out to be a real coup.

I have," says Dykstra. "walked down the street with a lot of famous people, but Ive never seen the reaction Bret Hart gets. Lawyers, homeless people, everyone.

One piece of casting thats remained constant is Petty himself -- still invariably the villain, and loving it.

As a performer, I'm a stage animal. This gives me a chance to get in front of a live audience again. And the response here is an experience you couldn't get anywhere else. Getting hissed as soon as I come on: you really need the energy of the audience." (In Snow White, of course, the baddie is the Queen, so playing the villain entailed playing the dame as well, even though Petty had never had "any great desire to play the dame. And indeed, the combination didnt really work; you cant be lovable and hissable at the same time.) The energy inspires Dykstra as well: You get the greatest feeling of accomplishment, to sit through this and make everyone feel that they've had a good time."
