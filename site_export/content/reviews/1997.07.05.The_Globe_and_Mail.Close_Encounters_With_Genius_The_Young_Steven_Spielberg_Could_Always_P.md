Close encounters with genius

The young Steven Spielberg could always persuade his friends and his three sisters to take part in his home-made movies, which from the start evidenced extraordinary sophistication and invention. STEVEN SPIELBERG: A Biography

Saturday, July 5, 1997

By Joseph McBride

Simon & Schuster, 528 pages, $39.50

THERE was a boy, born a year after the end of the Second World War, who spent his earliest days in an intensely Jewish suburb of Cincinnati. His mother was loving and chaotic, his father affectionate but strict and consumed by work. He was doing well in the new computer industry and hoped -- vainly -- that his son would inherit his passion for electrical engineering.

In pursuit of father's career the family kept moving: first to New Jersey, then to Phoenix, Ariz. In Phoenix the boy -- who from an early age heard Holocaust stories from relatives -- had what he later claimed to be traumatic encounters with anti-Semitism.

The boy was a loner (the word nerd crops up in the reminiscences of relatives and acquaintances) and was driven further into himself when his parents divorced. In one area, however, he showed great powers of leadership. He could always persuade his friends, and his three sisters, to take part in his home-made movies, which from the start evidenced extraordinary sophistication and invention. (He was inspired less by theatrical films than by television, whose U.S. golden age coincided with his childhood.)

The family moved again, to California, and he went to college there; the department of radio and television at Long Beach State, the chairman of which recalls that the student "knew more about cameras than anybody in the department . . . he could teach the department." He began hanging out at Universal Studios, making friends and contacts, learning by watching. Soon he was directing for television. At 24, he made a wildly exciting TV film, Duel , a chase picture in which the antagonists were a man and a monster truck. That took him straight into real movies, beginning with a more elaborate and psychologically layered chase picture, The Sugarland Express . Though thoroughly gripping, it was not a commercial success; people werent sure who to root for. It did not, however, interrupt the momentum of its directors career. For the next thing was Jaws .

According to Spielberg for the first 12 weeks of Jaws , people were thrilled by it. Six months later they were saying that no film that made that amount of money could be that good. This has been, as abundantly documented in Joseph McBride's excellent biography, the story of the rest of his life.

Actually, Spielberg understated the case. People were saying that no film that made that amount of money could be any good. And as for the man who made the film, and of course a good percentage of the money . . .

And that was only the beginning. I was surprised, reading this book, by the extent to which I had forgotten Jaws ; not as a movie but as an event in its director's career. There have been so many blockbusters since: Close Encounters of the Third Kind , E. T., the Indiana Jones trilogy, Jurassic Park , even Schindlers List earned substantial profits.

It also earned its director reviews that spoke not merely of professional and artistic advance, but of the justification of a life. David Denby wrote in New York magazine: "Its as if he understood for the first time why God gave him such extraordinary skills." Janet Maslin proclaimed in The New York Times: Mr. Spielberg has made sure that neither he nor the Holocaust will ever be thought of in the same way again. The italics are mine, to point up a judgment, and a juxtaposition, that I find embarrassing -- as I imagine Spielberg himself would. It is, however, preferable to some of the negative comment that McBride exhumes. He says that he was prompted to write the book by "the disdain of the self-styled intellectual elite for this great popular artist," and some of the reaction to Schindler's List amply justifies him.

Spielberg has suffered from an especially odious kind of the ad hominem reviewing rampant in North America. Its characteristic tone is patronizing; its underlying message is that all artistic practitioners are charlatans who must be slapped down in case they get above themselves and put something over on us. Spielberg, it was ludicrously asserted, had made a feel-good movie about the Holocaust; although Thomas Keneally told the identical tale in his highly praised novel, whose visceral impact -- it's a real page-turner -- is much like that of the movie.

McBride makes a lot, as has Spielberg himself, of the film returning him to his Jewish roots. (He had already denied that E. T. was a Christian parable: What would my mom say? She has a kosher restaurant on Pico and Doheny in Los Angeles.) Doubtless theres truth in it, but I think they both protest too much. McBride also emphasizes, but not strongly enough, that the film was not a conversion for Spielberg but a progression. He was a fine narrative director faced with a tremendous story; it should not be surprising that he told it so well. As for his humanity, that had already been demonstrated in Close Encounters and E. T. , science-fiction though they were.

McBride seems more sensible than most movie critics, but he still falls prey to director-worship, praising Spielberg for "penetrating the twisted psyche of Goethe" and quoting lines of dialogue to prove it. But those lines are in the script. Why should Spielberg get the credit? The director himself seems to have bought into the fallacious artist-entertainer dichotomy: Hes quoted as saying, I want to go back and forth from entertainment to socially conscious movies, though there is no reason why a "socially conscious" movie (note the Hollywood jargon) shouldn't be entertaining. Anyway, since this book went to press, Spielberg has broken his box-office records with The Lost World , which is admittedly as socially unconscious as a film can get, unless you're a dinosaur. One is supposed to deplore this, but I see no reason to.

There have been dark passages in Spielbergs career, mostly connected with his proliferating role as producer of other directors films. He distanced himself when two children were killed on the set of the Twilight Zone movie he produced, and his quoted pronouncement (it's made me grow up a little more) hardly seems adequate. He squeezed out the nominal director of Poltergeist ; one wag who visited the set remarked, "Well, now I know what the executive producer does. I've always wondered. He sets up the camera, tells the actors what to do, stands back, and lets the director say, Action! " Penitent, he gave subsequent directors a far freer hand.

The infantilization of Hollywood, for which Spielberg has so often been blamed, is in part the result of his presiding over pictures that have tried to be fun without the benefit of his wit, flair and intelligence. (I was pleased to find confirmation of my hunch that the brilliant casting of Sean Connery as Harrison Ford's dad in Indiana Jones and the Last Crusade was his acknowledgement of the link between the action heroes of two generations.)

Spielberg's best films are all adventure stories, Schindler included, and there are other kinds of films. But he is the best presiding genius Hollywood is likely to get, and it obviously couldn't have happened to a nicer guy. There are nicer guys, but it couldn't have happened to them.


  


Robert Cushman is a Toronto writer, broadcaster and critic.
