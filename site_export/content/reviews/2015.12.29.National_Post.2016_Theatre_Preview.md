You've heard of feasts and famines: also of coincidences. One of the most compelling, and most surprising, Toronto theatre productions of 2015 was Blood Wedding, mounted at Buddies in Bad Times by the Modern Times company, an enterprising independent outfit who come among us at unpredictable intervals. Blood Wedding, by the poet-playwright Federico Garcia Lorca who was one of the casualties of the Spanish Civil War, is a lyrical tragedy much honoured in textbooks but seldom staged, at least in English, possibly because it's very hard. In decades of theatregoing I'd never seen it. Now I'm to get a second helping, with a different flavour and at a more settled address. The same play features in the 2016 lineup at Soulpepper, in a new version by the company's head of playwriting Guillermo Verdecchia, announced as "reset with the rhythms of rural Ontario," so presumably taking place there; though in what period has not been revealed.

Blood Wedding is one of two mainstream classics in Soulpepper's new season. The other is a far more familiar piece: the proto-feminist, and unshakably resonant A Doll's House, directed by Laszlo Marton, the recurring Hungarian maestro responsible for a healthy proportion of the company's best work, including one previous Ibsen, The Wild Duck, which was among the best of the best. A Doll's House is being presented in a translation by Frank McGuinness, noted playwright from Ireland, though presumably not set there.

Arthur Miller is a Soulpepper favourite (Death of a Salesman, The Crucible, The Price) who stubbornly refuses to go out of fashion; his 60-year old A View from the Bridge is currently the sensation of New York. His later Incident at Vichy has been neglected, though I remember being riveted by its London premiere with Alec Guinness as a European nobleman caught up in a roundup of Jews in Nazi-occupied France. Diego Matamoros has the princely role at Soulpepper in a production by Alan Dilworth, who gets to follows his thrilling Twelve Angry Men with a comparably tense play about eight terrified men.

A more recent American play - a 2015 prize-winner in fact - is the one billed as Father Comes Home from the Wars (Parts I, II, III). (There are other parts to come, though they haven't all been written yet.) Following in the footsteps of Eugene O'Neill, whose Mourning Becomes

Electra retold the story of the Oresteia by setting it in the American Civil War, Suzan-Lori Parks gives the same period treatment to the Odyssey, focusing on the country's racial divide as O'Neill didn't, at least not in that play. In Soulpepper terms the production would seem to continue the radical American-epic tradition of Angels in America.

Another company tradition, and a good one, is that of reviving - or keeping alive - successful productions. 2016 will offer three of these, all from the middlebrow or frankly populist, section of the repertoire: The Odd Couple (a re-revival), the backstage comedy Jitters, and, at the top end, those dozen angry guys. The current Family Festival, which continues to Jan. 3, is made up of such favourites; and it's fun to see old shows with some new faces. In A Christmas Carol, Oliver Dennis now alternates as Scrooge with the role's creator Joseph Ziegler (who has restaged Michael Shamata's original production, making no changes that I could detect, because none were necessary); Dennis's Scrooge is closer to the miserly profile of Dickens' illustrators than Ziegler, equally moving as he learns the truth about himself, less transportingly joyous in the conversion where Ziegler seemed to leap almost out of his skin. The show remains a delight, funny and honestly tearful, as does Parfumerie, another fruit of the Hungarian connection, with Gregory Prest and Michelle Monteith a new and beautifully detailed pair of mail-order lovers, Sarah Wilson a delectable acquisition as a Judy Holliday dumb blond, and the surrounding ensemble stronger than ever in an adaptation and production that seem wittier than ever.

Antoni Cimolino's personal production slate as artistic director at Stratford seems to have settled into a pattern: one Shakespeare tragedy, one non-Shakespeare comedy. Last season it was Hamlet and The Alchemist (the latter one of the festival's three best shows, along with The Taming of the Shrew and The Physicists); this time it will be Macbeth and The Hypochondriac, the latter being a Moliere adaptation by Richard Bean whose immortal One Man Two Guv'nors will presumably be seen in Canada sometime, somehow; Stephen Ouimette, farceur supreme, has the title role. This year's Macbeths are a youngish couple, Ian Lake and Kristin Pellerin: not an unprecedented choice but an unusual one. Stratford too has its Ibsen and its Miller; the latter All My Sons (Ziegler and Tim Campbell, father and son in Soulpepper's Salesman reprising the relationship in the play that preceded it); the former the great, late John Gabriel Borkman, one of the few plays to offer three walloping roles for mature actors, here Scott Wentworth and - in a re-match of their great Mary Stuart duet - Lucy Peacock and Seana Mc-Kenna. And talking of re-matches: I look forward to Stratford's studio premiere of a new Hannah Moscovitch play Bunny, fruit of one of her many commissions, starring Maev Beaty who first knocked me out a Moscovitch solo piece called USSR.

There's no Ibsen at the Shaw Festival, which spoils the classical symmetry, but there is Strindberg: The Dance of Death with the formidable couple of Jim Mezon and Fiona Reid in a marriage that makes Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf look like a model of happy domesticity. And there's Chekhov: Uncle Vanya, in a production by outgoing director Jackie Maxwell, with Neil Barclay, an actor who seldom gets such breaks, in the title role. And there's Sondheim: Maxwell directs Sweeney Todd (Stratford has A Little Night Music. So that's symmetrical). There's Lewis Carroll: our theatres can't keep their hands off Alice, and - in what publicity is featuring as the festival's flagship production - Peter Hinton will give us his take on Wonderland, which will undoubtedly be imaginative, whatever else. There's Gilbert without Sullivan: a rare and thoroughly sardonic comedy called Engaged. Oh yes, and there's even some Shaw: a dramatization of the story The Adventures of the Black Girl in Her Search for God, and one actual play: Mrs Warren's Profession, last seen, or so it seems, only yesterday. When, he asked yet again, are they going to do Androcles and the Lion?

And ... and ... and: Crows Theatre, in cahoots with CanadianStage, are bringing Chimerica to Toronto. This is the 2013 play by the British playwright Lucy Kirkwood that shuttles between Tiananmen Square, 1989, and New York 2012. Large scale, large cast, and Chris Abraham directs. It's hard not to feel excited.
