Luminato is celebrating the bicentennial of Edgar Allan Poe with two separate productions. One of them, unsurprisingly entitled Nevermore, Ive yet to see, though I hear good things about it. The one I have seen, also good on its own terms, is A Poe Cabaret, and it's exactly what it says it is. Two shows in one festival strikes me as excessive, whatever the birthday, but then I've never found Poes combination of Gothic horror and romantic sickliness very appealing. It seems to fit the Zeitgeist, as it seems to have fitted most others.

The show is subtitled A Dream within a Dream. The outer dream is presided over by Mike Ross, as composer, singer, musician, reciter and host in a clown suit: striped-shirted, check-trousered, white-faced. He also devised the evening in collaboration with Lorenzo Savoini, best known as a designer but here functioning also as a very sharp director.

Moving among the audience, Ross discovers, seated at a table, a svelte and smart Patricia OCallaghan, whom he draws into joining him on stage and whose appearance and demeanour get less demure, though no less incisive, as the evening progresses. She's a superbly pointed, technically dazzling cabaret singer. He's a contrastingly eccentric vocalist, who favours jangling tones and has an individual line in wide-eyed menace. Separately and together, they sing Rosss settings of Poe poems (a Poepourri?), principally to string-quartet accompaniment. At one point, they join forces on a Weimar waltz. Poe, the show seems to be saying, was not of his age but of one particular later time; the evenings recorded warm-up music is ironically cheerful '20s jazz.

Well, though they do it, I doubt the effectiveness of setting poems, which already have their own rhythm, to music. It's like putting one melody-line on top of another. Even speaking a poem over music here proves hazardous. The inner dream -- the part of the show not directly involving Ross and OCallaghan--includes Tom Allen giving a rather rushed and inexpressive recitation of The Raven, with musical interjections from harpist Lori Gemmell and The Penderecki String Quartet (the evenings permanent backup group). They just get in one another's way.

Admittedly, nobody will ever be able to compete with Homer Simpson's performance of this perennial party piece, but I was never able to take it seriously even before he got to it. Its too obvious that the poet only gave his lost ladylove the unlikely name of Lenore for the sake of the rhyme, and every time it comes round I chuckle. The Masque of the Red Death is the inspiration for Conte Fantastique, a long-neglected instrumental composition by Andre Caplet, which the Pendereckians resurrect; lines from the story precede and succeed it, and OCallaghan walks across the stage in the middle of it, personifying the Red Death (at least that's the colour she's wearing), but any more precise correspondence is confined to both pieces sounding sinister.

The evenings most resounding success is sung prose: The Tell-Tale Heart (another piece with Simpsons connections; it helped rob Lisa of her diorama prize) is performed with virtuoso range and control by Sean Robert Clark. Its the story of a mad killer protesting his sanity; the music, composed and directed by Lance Horne, mocks and echoes him all the way. This is music giving new form to words, rather than just going along with them.

From Poe to Breau: or, Brault on Breau. 5 O'Clock Bells, named after an album, is a one-man show by Pierre Brault about Lenny Breau, the phenomenal Canadian guitarist who came out of country into jazz -- without, apparently, much encouragement from his parents. (They don't know who Oscar Peterson is. Isnt that a crime in Canada?)

As depicted here, Lennys dad, a country singer called Lonesome Pine, let the boy into the family act as a novelty attraction and was distressed when he started hearing bop chords behind his vocals. ( "One flat fifth and I'll knock you down.") Pine was already having family problems, with his wife turning out to be a more popular and accomplished singer than himself. Eventually, it seems, he drank himself to death. Lenny, naturally, inherited his addiction problems, though, also naturally, with him it was drugs. His mother begins and ends the story, waiting for him to come home. Behind her on a screen we see what appears to be a man falling to earth but turns out to be a body at the bottom of a pool, Sunset Boulevard style.

The production comes from the Sleeping Dog Theatre, Ottawa. Brian Quirt directs stylishly and (how else?) economically, and Brault of course plays all parts, male and female, stepping brightly from one to the next. The famous people include Chet Atkins, the Nashville king who on hearing Lenny declares himself to be now only the second greatest guitarist in the world, and our own Don Francks, rather reductively portrayed

as a hipster Sinatra wannabe. There's also Lennys teenage sweetheart and first wife, and Jewel, the Christian singer who seems really to have done for him.

The only person for whom Brault fails to find a distinctive voice is Lenny himself, whose talk is kept to a minimum. Offstage snatches of his playing dont really compensate. He never rises above the familiar figure of the doomed jazz musician, exploiting the people who love him as regularly as the business exploits him. It's like The Chet Baker Project of a few years back -- like this one, the work of a fan trying to find the key to why the music moved him. Brault's piece is better but, like most plays about musicians, especially jazz musicians, it fails to find an equivalent for its subjects verbal shorthand. Its like plays with phone calls; people keep telling Lenny things he already knows or repeating back, for our benefit, what we presume he's just told them. It's biographically interesting but, like most fan literature, its stuck on the outside looking in.

For all their merits, both these shows seem slight for a festival with Luminatos pretensions. Today, however, I'm bound for Robert Lepage's nine-hour Lipsynch which should be as much size as anybody needs.

- A Poe Cabaret played at Buddies in Bad Times Theatre; 5 O'Clock Bells closes tonight at the Berkeley Street Theatre Downstairs .
