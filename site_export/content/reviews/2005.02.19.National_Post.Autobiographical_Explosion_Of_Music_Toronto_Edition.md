Poor Alex Threatre, 296 Brunswick Ave., box office 905-881-6546. Until Feb. 26.

Tick, Tick ... Boom! driven by its songs rather than story

Certain theatres, even some that are strictly for rent, with no overall artistic direction, develop identities. They have good or bad karma. The Poor Alex, cramped and intimate, is one of those with good. I dont recall ever seeing a bad show there. Tick, Tick ... Boom! keeps up the standard.

The show is a fascinating fragment, the fascination deriving from its proximity to a much better-known work by the same writer. Jonathan Larson is the lyricist and composer who wrote Rent. He devised this piece, in 1990, while waiting for that break, or for any break. Its autobiographical, and it's all about his fear and frustration at the prospect of turning 30 without having got anywhere. As all the world knows, he died on the very day he seemed meant to get there, the day Rent was due to open. The rest is history that he never got to see. Watching Rent with that in mind is poignant enough. Seeing Larson's own story is doubly or triply so.

Tick, Tick ... Boom! (the title refers to the countdown to an explosion threatening within the protagonists head) is not what youd call a sturdy piece. Larson wrote it for himself to perform, just songs on a linking thread of monologue. After his death, David Auburn (the playwright of Proof) reshuffled the bits and turned the show into a three-hander. There is still the hero, Jon, trying to blend Broadway and rock in a new musical that would do justice to both; meanwhile, wouldn't you know, he waits tables. There is now Susan, his girlfriend, a dancer turned dance teacher, who wants to leave New York and take Jon with her. There is also Michael, Jon's boyhood friend and cold-water flatmate, who has given up an acting career for something in marketing. With a new apartment and a BMW, he seems well on the way to becoming one of the yuppie scum who are excoriated in Rent, though hes treated a lot more sympathetically. For one thing, hes gay, which in a Larson show is enough in itself to put you on the side of the angels (or at least of the Angels in America).

What happens between and around these three is thin and predictable, but there is an overall impetus deriving from the impending workshop of Jon's musical, Superbia (in fact, an unproduced Larson title). It happens, it's liked and it leaves its composer wondering what now, which is very much like life. (The show is pronounced too rocky for Broadway, too big for Off). But at least he has something to celebrate on his 30th birthday. In real life, Rent is still six years off.

What makes the show, though, is the songs. The tunes, with a couple of exceptions, are at least as driving, infectious and melodic as anything in Rent, and the lyrics are noticeably better. For one thing, they have humour, something lacking in Rent (except perhaps for Tango: Maureen). For another, there is no portentous recitative. The script may be slight, but it serves to keep the songs from falling over one another. There is a lovely, sexy thing titled Green Green Dress, and another titled Why that Jon sings about himself and Michael, which is poignant, witty and specific: "When I was sixteen/Michael and I/Got parts in West Side/At White Plains High./Three oclock, went to rehearse/In the gym./Mike played Doc -- who didnt sing --/Fine with him." Note how easily and pointedly the rhymes fall. There aren't many rock scores you can say that about.

The reference to West Side Story, lyrics by Stephen Sondheim, is no accident. Sondheim, Jon's idol and mentor as he was Larsons, is the shows fourth character, though he remains offstage. His voice, the genuine article, is heard on an answering machine toward the end, and it's touching. He's also all over the score, in the form of homage and plain, though maybe unconscious, borrowing. A song titled Sunday is a hilarious spoof of its namesake anthem in Sunday in the Park with George, though its not about Art but about Brunch. A ballad starts off like Send In the Clowns; a tongue-twisting argument number recalls the virtuoso Your Fault in Into the Woods, but then goes off at breakneck speed in its own direction, which happens to be Country.

And the whole birthday motif in the plot is reminiscent of Company.

That doesnt make Larson a copycat; he just knew to steal from the best. One dialogue scene finds Jon wandering among the alien corn (i.e. Les Mis posters) on Broadway, and lamenting that theatre music is still 60 years behind the times. This isn't true. For better or worse, there's no new music on Broadway now that could have been heard there, or anywhere, in 1930 or even in 1950. That very much includes Sondheim, however traditional his craftsmanship. That craft was his greatest gift to Larson, who never equalled it but might have done, and meantime had his own energy. He was especially good, as in the opening numbers of both his shows, at fury.

The Toronto production, by a new group called Getting Up, isnt great. Mario DAlmonte's direction is sluggish, and the design unimaginative. Dean Armstrong is initially too pixieish for Jon's desperation, but he grabs hold of it in the crucial workshop scene and never lets go. Daphne Moens is miscast, physically and vocally, but Michael Dufays has charm as Mike.

The songs still sound good, though they sound better on the New York cast CD. Larson at his death was one year younger than George Gershwin when he died. They both brought outside forms, rock and jazz, to Broadway, in bastardized form, of course, but the theatre is always a bastard. Im not about to compare them in terms of quality, but the quantity bears thinking about. Think of the dozens of shows Gershwin put out in his lifetime, and the one that was all Larson could manage. The system has broken down: Thats really what Tick, Tick ... Boom! is about. Anyway, to adapt John O'Hara, Jonathan Larson died on Jan. 25, 1996. I don't see any way of not believing it, but I still feel cheated.
