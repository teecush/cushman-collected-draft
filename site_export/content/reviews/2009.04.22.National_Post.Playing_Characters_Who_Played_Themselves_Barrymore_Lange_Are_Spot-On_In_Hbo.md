In HBO's Grey Gardens, Jessica Lange and Drew Barrymore play, respectively, Edith Beale and her daughter, also Edith Beale. Grey Gardens itself is their house in the Hamptons, which progresses from grandeur to acute decay to a kind of reclamation. One of the best scenes shows Lange as the elder and now aged Edie, sitting up in bed, singing along with an arrangement of Tea for Two that had been specially written for her in her younger days. Arriving at the last stanza, she forgets a line and comes in too early on the next one, the one about the sugar cake. So she has to ad lib her way through, so as to finish at the same time as the accompaniment, which she does with true upper-crust aplomb.

What I didn't know, when watching last Saturday night, was that Lange was reproducing, and reproducing exactly, the performance of the same song in the same circumstances given by the real Edie in the documentary film, also called Grey Gardens, made in 1975 by Albert and David Maysles: same cracked but accurate voice, same gaffe, same carelessly indomitable recovery. I didnt know but I soon found out, as HBO showed the Maysles film immediately after showing its own. This was a lot of semi-patrician eccentricity to take in one evening, but it was worth it. The Beale ladies were fascinating sociological and psychological phenomena, something between facts and artefacts. The same description might hold for the performances of Lange and Barrymore, both of whom immersed themselves in all the available biographical material, including of course the documentary, and both of whom emerged giving, with invaluable assistance from cameramen and makeup artists, skin-tight impersonations of the originals. But not just impersonations. These are performances that start from externals, which are certainly striking enough in this case, and then find rigorous justification for them. Acting often works like that.

The Beale women -- Big Edie and Little Edie-- were Jazz Age party girls who could never bring themselves to admit that the party was over, even though their later years were one monstrous hangover. That's the situation captured in the Maysles' documentary, which shows them reminiscing, recriminating and reconciling with extraordinary abandon -- though what remains unclear is whether they forgot the camera was there or enjoyed themselves playing up to it. (Some of both, probably.) The dramatized movies not-so-secret weapon is that it can show us the events and people that the documentary could only talk about. We are of course substituting one kind of unreliable narration for another, but we do get some kind of external purchase on the relationship. There are two eternal dynamics at work here: mother-daughter and ambitions-regrets. Either can yield powerful drama; together, theyre explosive.

Past and present alternate. Present here is the Maysles brothers coming to call and staying to shoot. Past takes us back to the 1930s; Big Edie is married to a rich businessman (Ken Howard) who commutes from New York and is increasingly appalled by her behaviour; nursing dreams of being a singer, she entertains lavishly (and apparently continuously) and has her own live-in young male pianist. Little Edie also has theatrical ambitions, and when daddy moves definitively out, she goes with him. It even seems she might make a go of it; at least she flirts and wheedles her way into an audition with a prominent producer. But an affair with a married man (Daniel Baldwin, whom one hopes is reconciled to roles that seem designed for his brother Alec) blows up in her face, and she hightails it back to mother. And there she remains. It's a perfect setup for two lifetimes of mutual blame, tempered by unbreakable, exasperated love. Why did Little Edie come home? To look after her mother, she says. Because she was scared to do anything else, her mother says. Both are obviously right.

Debt and dilapidation take over the premises, as also do an army of pet cats and scarcely less domesticated raccoons. (So that's why they filmed in Toronto. Though along with the raccoons, they got the services of top Canadian actors like Kenneth Welsh and Ben Carlson.) Luckily the Beales have a rich cousin, the former Jacqueline Bouvier (Jeanne Tripplehorn), who vacationed with them as a child and now, midway between being a Kennedy and an Onassis, is able to wave a magic wand and have the house restored to a semblance of its former status. And in her wake come the Maysles duo, who cant promise much in the way of monetary reward (as they put it, "the words documentary' and 'profit seldom occur in the same sentence") but who do end up bringing the two Edies something of the fame theyve always craved. There's a moment of apotheosis that's both funny and touching.

One of the minor felicities of Michael Sucsys script and direction is the balance held between the Maysles exploitation of their subjects and their affection for them. Balance indeed is the word throughout. The film keeps a high-comic tone, and its evolving period feel is immaculate (the music's great, with Lange doing a lot of singing and Barrymore a bit) but it never loses its grip on two women, each of them devastatingly clear-eyed about the other while being -- mostly -- a fantasist about herself. The human condition, in other words, carried to extremes; and these two can laugh at themselves as well as one another. Lange plays with a debutante drawl that grows into a grand, craggy snarl: the voice, as we know, of her original. Barrymore's tone, equally authentic, is Katharine Hepburn on ice, whether viperish, imperious or just riffing on her own frivolity; she's still a flirt. She addresses her mother, always, as "Mother darling," but there are no Joan Crawford overtones; it sounds natural and overbearing, but sincere. These are two superb performances, in an exceptional production. - Grey Gardens airs again today at 1 p. m. and 8 p. m., as well as on Sunday at 8:50 a. m. on HBO Canada.
