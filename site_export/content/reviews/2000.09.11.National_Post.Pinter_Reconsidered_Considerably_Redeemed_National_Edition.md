BETRAYAL

Soulpepper Theatre Company, Premiere Dance Theatre

When Harold Pinter's Betrayal was first staged in London in 1978, the local critics received it disdainfully. (I know -- I was one of them).

Since then, words have been feverishly eaten, and the play has become one of its authors most-admired and most-revived. The new Soulpepper production takes it a step further along the road to canonization.

One reason the play was scorned at its premiere was that it concerns English middle-class adultery, and we had all had quite enough of that. Let me rephrase that: It was generally believed that, at one quite recent time, all British plays had been about the anguished bed-hopping of the bourgeoisie and nobody wanted to see those dark, constricted days return.

Pinter, besides, was felt to have forsaken his roots. The man who had made his name writing about psychotic tramps and seaside boarding houses was devoting himself in this play to the emotional entanglements of the urban literati, i.e. the world in which he himself now moved. (How dare he flaunt it, seemed to be the feeling.)

Worse yet, his characters werent even real writers, just prosperous hangers-on: the people in Betrayal are Jerry, a literary agent; Robert, his best friend and a publisher; and Emma, wife of the latter and mistress of the former.

Also, we were tired -- justifiably -- of what had become known as the Pinter style: the icy-smooth, deliberate manner that Peter Hall, for many years Pinter's regular director, seemed to employ as a form of elegant intimidation. Every word, every movement, came loaded with significance, even though nobody could be sure what they signified. This, though, was part of the deal; the plays themselves usually had narrative glitches that made them seem, in a rather antiquated way, experimental.

Betrayal, though, didn't even offer the consolations of obscurity. Its story happens to unfold in reverse chronological order, but apart from that, its development is straightforward. Or at least straightbackward. Things that may seem puzzling in one scene are invariably clarified in the next.

The plays first scene shows Jerry and Emma meeting for a drink, some years after the end of the affair. We gather that Jerry would quite like to rekindle the relationship, and that Emma wouldnt.

The last scene shows Jerry declaring his passion for the first time and Emma responding. The mood, which survives even the sudden appearance of the about-to-be-cuckolded Robert, is one of delicate tenderness.

In hindsight, the beginning of their romance seems like its high point. Once consummated, it seems to have declined very quickly into a routine of lying, logistics, and regular rendezvous in a rented flat in an unfashionable quarter of London. (Probably only an English actor could pronounce the name "Kilburn" with the curdled contempt it requires.)

Like all Pinter's plays, but more nakedly and subtly than most, the piece takes shape as a series of psychological power struggles. In any given scene, two of the characters will share some piece of knowledge of which the third is ignorant. So each of the trio is at once both victor and victim: It depends which way the light is shining.

From one viewpoint, Jerry seems to hold all the aces. He has the woman he desires, and none of the attendant responsibilities. (His own marriage and family are kept off stage.) But he still has to live with the fact that Robert has first claim on Emma, and he is in constant fear of losing her. Robert is the deceived husband, but he has been enjoying infidelities of his own. Besides, about halfway through the action, Emma tells him about her affair, thereby strengthening the bond between them and making Jerry even more the outsider in their marriage.

Emma, meanwhile, is pointedly -- even brutally -- excluded from the camaraderie of the two men, symbolized by their regular games of squash, though these seem to be more talked about than actually enjoyed. (As also, from what we gather, seems to be the case with sex.) Still, Emma has had both men, and has moved on to a third: The play has a fourth, unseen, character, a writer named Casey whom Jerry represents and Robert publishes. They live off him but he walks off with their woman. It is, perhaps, the author's revenge.

The relationships pulsate, unspoken words conveying invisible words. (The spoken words are, as usual, brilliant.) As in any good play, all the characters have areas of unknowability. Some, however, are more unknowable than others. And, as often with Pinter, the woman is little more than an indecipherable, much-coveted Sphinx.

Susan Coyne gives her an air of chilly practicality, but little else; maybe thats all there is. Albert Schultz is admirable as the bemused, trying-to-be-cool Jerry whom we first meet, but as the onion gets unpeeled the layers beneath seem identical. (In a recent London production, it was Jerrys passion that drove the play.)

It is Diego Matamoros' Robert who predominates, showing us Pinter at his most uncomfortable. Robert, who cheerfully admits to hitting his wife and who has grown to hate his job, is a virtuoso put-down artist. He responds to the news of Emma's adultery with the kind of elegantly wounded barbs that Tom Stoppard lampooned in The Real Thing. Its the way that betrayed husbands behave in "civilized" plays, but in this text and this performance it rings true. Matamoros makes the cruelty irresistibly entertaining; he also manages to suggest that the man inflicts pain because he suffers pain.

All three performances are technically immaculate and they mesh beautifully under Daniel Brooks production, which, on the simplest of sets, cuts to the bone.

Not the least of its virtues is its enthusiastic pace: You will wait in vain for the notorious Pinter pauses. There are moments of silence, of course, but no more than in any other play in which the characters are not impossibly glib. (The author himself has recently returned to acting. And, even in his own plays, he performs with an exhilaratingly coarse barrelhousing energy that couldn't have less to do with the Pinter style. So we've been had.)

There is a good sound score. We still wait for actor-musician Ted Dykstra to make his onstage debut with Soulpepper, but in the meantime he has furnished this play, as he did Twelfth Night, with haunting incidental music.
