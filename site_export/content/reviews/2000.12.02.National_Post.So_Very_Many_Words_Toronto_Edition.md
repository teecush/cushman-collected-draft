SO MANY WORDS

Theatre Passe Muraille's Backspace

In So Many Words, a young woman pursues a celebrated writer to his hotel and -- after a great many well-chosen words -- goes to bed with him. That makes the second consecutive night on which I have seen this situation played out. The set-up in this play -- in which the man, the woman and the hotel are all up-scale and the two parties met that day on a plane -- is more plausible than the fantastic premise on offer just round the corner in the aptly named Science Fiction; but it isn't as dramatically real.

That doesn't matter too much since, unlike Science Fiction, Roger Rueff's piece isn't about story; it's about dialogue and ideas. It takes in place in Washington where the male protagonist -- a novelist whose works are better known for their intellectual than their emotional qualities -- has arrived to collect yet another award. His agent is waiting to greet him and in the first scene we watch her trying, unsuccessfully, to get rid of the preternaturally cool and determined young lady who has come straight from the airport to claim her own prize. In the second, this visitor finds herself up against a more formidable opponent, the novelist's wife. She discovers, at least to her own satisfaction, that the marriage is dead; the wife discovers that the intruder, who talks like a philosophy student, is an actress and lingerie model.

Next, our hero having finally checked in, comes the seduction. His admirer, after much probing, gets him to confess that he doesn't love his wife. She also reveals how much else she knows about him, including the well-guarded fact that he has a second career as a writer of high-class porn. (We have to take her word for it that it is high-class.) She offers -- and this time it is in so many words - - to make his fictional dreams come true. Either he confesses or his wife finds out, because the next sound we hear is that of her demanding a divorce.

Some uncomfortable truths come out about their relationship, with especial reference to his coldness both as a man and a writer. A coda brings back the agent who may love him in her way, and has the percentage to prove it.

The opening and closing scenes present us with brittle badinage on a high level: words used as weapons, offensive or defensive. The seduction is fun to watch, but seems to be acting out its own, rather common, fantasy: the one that says a rumpled middle-aged author will be an irresistible magnet to an amorous woman 20 years his junior. The marital battle cuts painfully deep, as does the expose of the author's psychological ruthlessness. It turns out that he has hugged to himself a hideous secret that gives him the energy to write.

I think the playwright overreaches in his choice of secret, and I don't believe he has properly calibrated the effect on the wife when it is revealed. (It would destroy her.). I'm willing to believe almost anything of writers, especially novelists, but not quite this.

To revert to my initial comparison: the characters here are more immediately recognizable than those in Science Fiction but they don't have the same theatrical blood in their veins; nor can they beguile us with long juicy speeches. The demands on the actors are severe; they have to maintain their poise and their pose to articulate the text and mine the subtext.

The staging is by the Actors Repertory Company, whose one previous show, Strindberg's Creditors, was another triangle piece conducted in duologues. What the script describes as "a well- appointed room" has been economically realized by the designer James Cameron, and the production, directed by Paul Lampert, is reasonably enjoyable; but the performances don't really cut it.

Both Brenda Bazinet as the wife and Jennifer Wigmore as the self- propelled sex object keep letting down their guard; Bazinet sometimes neglects even to raise hers, while Wigmore doesn't always sound as if she knows what she's talking about. Robin Ward, playing the most passive of the trio, has more legitimate cause to relax occasionally, and is, given the built-in implausibilties of his role, believable. Tedde Moore has the agent, a creature of lunches and reassurances, down 100%; 100% if you figure in her commission.
