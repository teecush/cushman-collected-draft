You want timeliness? Greg Nelson's The Fall involves a politician who's renowned for his probity, caught up in a call-girl scandal. You want coincidence? The play is three years old; it was first produced at SummerWorks in 2005. But really, if you want this kind of thing to be topical, all you have to do is wait awhile. It'll come 'round.

Anyway, The Fall has more to offer. It is, in its expanded version at Tarragon, a tense, intelligent and economical play, very well presented, whose only weakness may be an excess of ingenuity. It's a twohander with four characters. We're in an Ottawa conference room: one long table, a chair and the audience on two sides. Despite the furniture, the people on stage rarely sit down; they have too much on their minds. First off, we meet Kate, a legal scholar, and David, a lawyer who has come direct from delivering a tear-inducing oration at the funeral of his father, Harry, who was a judge. A quarter-century ago, Harry was one of the prime backroom boys in the drafting of the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms. Kate and David were college contemporaries. They even once dated. (Apparently, it was a disaster.) A more pressing memory for Kate is that of her very first job as a clerk in Harry's office. She emerged from it in possession of memories, ethical dilemmas and an incriminating file.

And suddenly, with just a change of lighting and the removal of David's glasses, we're back in time, in the same office, to find Harry confronted by Jane, a journalist, who has some goods on him -- or, more precisely, on one of his bosses, the minister responsible for pushing through the legislation in which Harry so passionately believes. Drugs are involved and, as aforesaid, an escort. It may all come out. To stop this from happening, Harry may have to violate the spirit of the very Charter he longs to establish.

It's a pretty problem, echoing across two generations. Added to the mix is that, whatever eulogies David may have recently delivered, in life he and his father did not get on. They were estranged for years. And, maybe because of this, David is a drunk.

The play keeps us on moral and theatrical tenterhooks. Both, though, are somewhat blunted by the number of levels involved. To put it another way, the storytelling is immediate but the issues are removed. Harry might move us more if he were covering for his own misdeeds, rather than somebody else's. And his agonies might be more agonizing if we saw them happening present-tense rather than in flashbacks. It works both ways; the fencing matches of the younger generation seem less urgent once we realize that they're only a series of feints for the real drama.

Which isn't to say that the fencing isn't effective in its own right. Nelson gives very good cat-and-mouse -- all the characters carry secrets that they only give up under pressure -- and he's very well served in this production. Jennifer Tarver, a director known for fine work on more abstract plays, proves equally adroit at adjusting the nuts and bolts of this one. She makes it weighty but not ponderous. Two of Toronto's best younger actors make up her cast. Sarah Dodd, in a pin-striped pantsuit with hair centre-parted and in a bun, makes little attempt to differentiate between the two women she plays, but the play neither asks nor permits her to; both are capable people in terrible binds, and Dodd, terse and wounded, does fine by both of them. Ashley Wright is squishier as the judged son than as the judging (and self-judging) father, but he is really offering us complementary halves of one riven man. The play tells us that, when you're in a tight corner, blackmail comes easy -- emotional blackmail if no other kind is available. Having griped last week about the recent standard of new plays in town, I have to say that The Fall is a definite rise.
