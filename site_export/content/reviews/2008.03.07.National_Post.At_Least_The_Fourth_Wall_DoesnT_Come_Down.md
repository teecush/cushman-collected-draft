THEATRE REVIEW

Democracy

Tarragon Theatre, Toronto

West Germany, under the 1970s chancellorship of Willy Brandt, is considering rapprochement with East Germany. A member of Brandt's cabinet wonders aloud whether "these new friends" can be trusted. A minor functionary in the government office, one Gunter Guillaume, is asked his opinion. "Half of me," he replies, "wants to say one thing. Half of me wants to say another." Brandt himself observes that that is "the position of the electorate as a whole on almost everything."

Democracy, the title of Michael Frayn's play about Brandt's rise and fall, is also its governing metaphor. It's about balancing contradictions: within states, within governments, within individuals. Half of me wants to say that it's a subtle examination of the political process and the people within it; the other half has to acknowledge that the production actually on view at the Tarragon is amazingly dull.

It's amazing because Frayn is a humane, witty and unpretentiously intelligent writer, skeptical without being cynical. He's also a self-aware craftsman constantly setting himself technical challenges and usually solving them, whether he's writing a farce about a farce ( Noises Off) or making discussions about nuclear fission dramatic ( Copenhagen). Democracy works on three levels: as a psychological study, a political intrigue drama and a spy story.

Gunter Guillaume has good reason for his confessed ambivalence; he's been sent from the East to worm his way into the chancellor's confidence. He succeeds, though without ever making Brandt like him -- the reverse, if anything. He himself, though, comes to practically worship the worldly, witty, charismatic Brandt, a condition he shares with a large proportion of the population. Guillaume is altogether an unusually benign kind of spy; if Brandt's intentions towards East Germany are truly positive, then East Germany is interested. Guillaume's job is not so much to undermine his quarry as to shore him up. Ironically, it's the detection of Guillaume's identity -- by a remarkably clumsy Secret Service -- that contributes to Brandt's eventual unseating. The wall, as we know, will come down anyway, the glory going not to Willy but to his less lovable successor Helmut Schmidt.

Much of the play is seen through Guillaume's eyes: When he isn't reporting back to his control, he's giving us one-sentence updates on his state of mind (the two conventions are sometimes hard to tell apart). Here, I think, Frayn falls into the same trap that ensnared him in an earlier play, Benefactors, in which the characters were always compelled to tell us what they felt. This compulsion to tell rather

than show destroyed Benefactors, and it damages Democracy. You keep wondering when the real play is going to start. Frayn was a novelist before he was a playwright, and both these plays read better than they act. At least, Democracy reads better than it acts in Toronto.

I was struck at first by the pace of Richard Rose's production; then I realized that what we were offered was not pace but mere speed. Complicated speeches are rattled off at an impressive lick by actors who seem to know what they're talking about, but after a while it all blurs together. This is a play that keeps cutting not just from one scene to another, but from one mode to another and there is no weight to any of them and no distinction between them. Visually, it's dispiriting: Guillaume describes his new workplace at the Palais Schaumburg as "a 19th-century ironmaster's dream of the aristocratic life," but it looks here as if he might have well have stayed home. Charlotte Dean's set is an arrangement of drab office furniture, which consigns some vital scenes to cramped quarters at the back of the stage. What really does the show in, though, is the casting. The Willy Brandt one remembers from newsreels was a fleshy, smiling charmer. R.H. Thomson's Brandt is as gaunt and dour as his surroundings. This isn't just a matter of reproducing history; the play itself keeps insisting on Brandt's humour, his knack for playing the crowd and his surface warmth. It also shows that there was darkness beneath: Brandt's rootless refugee past, his feeling of being unfathered, his doubts on just about everything. But there's no interest in penetrating beneath the mask when there's no mask.

It's Brandt's loneliness that helps draw Guillaume to him -- the two men turn out to have discontents in common (they're both in dried-up marriages for one thing, though Willy naturally had more access to consolation than Gunther). Guillaume seems to have been the kind of sad spy patented by John Le Carre -- he certainly comes in from the cold, without ever feeling at home in the warm. Alon Nashman gets Guillaume's plaintive externals right but can't ring many changes on the inner man. He doesn't grow on the job. With so little tension between the two central figures, the play becomes an affair of undifferentiated minor guys scurrying around in suits, hatching plots.

Graham Harley is the best of them, as a dedicated hack who's painlessly converted from Communism to Catholicism. With his English accent and air of spiritual dandruff, he might be on loan from Old Labour. Alex Poch-Goldin has the featureless and therefore thankless role of the German Democratic Republic spy-master who spends the play sitting at a cafe table downstage left receiving confidences. This points to the play's biggest technical problem: It wants to be fluidly impressionistic and massively specific. Half of me blames the production for not solving it. The other half thinks it's insoluble.
