THEATRE REVIEW

FOLLIES: IN CONCERT

Festival Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

---

I am lucky enough to have seen the original production of Follies in 1971 and unlucky enough to have seen it only once. It marked the zenith of any number of talents: composer-lyricist Stephen Sondheim, librettist James Goldman, director and producer Harold Prince, co-director and choreographer Michael Bennett, designer Boris Aronson and many others, including maybe the finest collection of actors, singers and dancers ever assembled on Broadway. The performers weren't all equally good in all departments, but each was superb where it counted, with the acting in particular the best I had seen in any musical up to that time.

There were 51 in that original cast, which is one reason why the show can never be properly revived, which in turn is why we fortunate ones alternately gloat and grieve over our memories. Follies' afterlife has been in cut down, sometimes extensively re-written editions, including one devised for concert performance: actors at music-stands and some token staging. This is the version that the Shaw Festival is mounting for four performances this season. Its limitations are obvious and inescapable. All the same, this is the most satisfying Follies, the most enjoyable and the most moving, I have seen since that first one.

Follies is actually about two kinds of follies: Ziegfeld and human. A theatre that served as home for one of those inter-war series of spectacular revues is about to be torn down, and the former producer is throwing the closing-night party to end them all. Former stars and former showgirls turn up in force:Among them two chorines from the 1940s, Phyllis and Sally, and their respective husbands (and former stage-door johnnies), Ben and Buddy. Ben is "the famous Benjamin Stone," writer and politician; Buddy is Buddy, travelling salesman for an oil company. Before their marriages, Ben had a fling with Sally, and 30 years later she is still fixated on him. The strands and implications of this cat's cradle are revealed in a retrospective song, Waiting for the Girls Upstairs, that even for Sondheim is a tour-de-force of accumulated evocative detail set to an accelerated soft-shoe rhythm; the tour becomes even more forceful when, halfway through the number, the characters' younger selves appear to act out their memories. Most of the characters, major or minor, have these youthful doubles, as past and present, nostalgia and disillusion, rub up against one another. Valerie Moore's Shaw production brings out these interactions very well -- I'd forgotten how powerful the moments are when a protagonist's old self turns on his or her young one, each blaming the other for past mistakes and present betrayals.

Another wonderful memento is the orchestral sound: Paul Sportelli has a full complement of musicians on stage (the pit would be too small) to render this score as it was meant to be heard; indeed, of all the show's original participants, the one best-served here may be orchestrator Jonathan Tunick, whose work is ear-tingling. He had of course an extraordinary score to work with, one in which contemporary expressions of angular angst are juxtaposed with word-and-note-perfect tributes to the old Broadway masters, each given a dramatic or psychological tweak. (The difference between the period pastiches of Chicago and of Follies is that those in the former never advance beyond the initial joke while those in the latter explore.)

Moore is a choreographer, but there's no way in these circumstances that she can begin to equal the point or the excitement of the original dances. Who's That Woman, the "mirror number" that just about sums up the show, is virtually meaningless. Even in the solo songs, there's a limit to how much someone can move around at a lectern. But some of the limitations are made to work: Static staging-- the participants in a scene a whole stage apart, points up the emotional distances that the piece is about.

There's one performance that's a musical and dramatic triumph: Glynis Ranney's Sally, affectionate, vulnerable, disturbed and offering the best rendition of In Buddy's Eyes I have ever heard. It's a shame that this version (like all the revisions, though less than some) softens the book material, especially at the end, which should find Sally catatonic. George Masswohl's Ben is authoritative and intelligent, Jay Turvey's Buddy proficient but lightweight. As Phyllis, Melanie Janzen is over-parted, lacking the woman's New York smarts and depleting Could I Leave You, the most devastatingly sardonic show-tune ever written, of its wit, its shape and its anger. Anger, however, makes an unexpected and very effective appearance in Goldie Semple's delivery of the show-stopping survivor's anthem I'm Still Here.

Her role as a perennial ex-star now consists entirely of that song; this text cuts all her dialogue and, here as elsewhere, every cut hurts. The show as conceived may be a third kind of folly, of the grand architectural kind. It needs its size to drive home its over-arching metaphor in which the death of old show business signifies the death of the American dream. You may not quite believe it (did audiences in the '30s really take the everlasting-love-at-first-sight stuff as words to live by?) but you have to believe the show believes it. What we have at the Shaw isn't enough, but it's a lot. Whatever your memories or lack of them, enjoy. And suffer. - Follies: In Concert closes tonight. Call 1-800-511-SHAW for information.
