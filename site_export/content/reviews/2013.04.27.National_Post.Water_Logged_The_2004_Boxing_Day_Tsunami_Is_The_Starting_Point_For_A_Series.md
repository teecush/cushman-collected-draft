David Yee's Carried Away on the Crest of a Wave is a talented piece of work - but it nonetheless comes close to drowning in its own ambition. Appropriately, it unfolds on a waterlogged stage.

The water is impressive and also vital since, though the title might suggest an ancient musical comedy, it actually refers to the Asian tsunami of Boxing Day, 2004. The play is a series of vignettes, designed to show the aftershocks of that catastrophe, across huge expanses of time and space. Some scenes take place as physically far from the epicentre as Toronto and Salt Lake City; some are as temporally far as, well, now. I think, though I'm not sure, that the events depicted are shown to us in chronological order. Each one presents us with an entirely different set of characters. The moral, emblazoned on the back page of the program and expanded in a long speech in the last scene, is that "we are all connected, and we are, none of us, alone."

It's no criticism to say that we've heard that before: from John Donne in one of the world's great one-liners; from Stephen Sondheim and James Lapine in a key song from Into the Woods and, more importantly, in that show's whole structure. It lived its meaning by taking four disparate stories and tying them inextricably together. Yee's episodic approach means he has to provide a separate coincidence - a shocking revelation, a long-delayed reunion - in each scene. The tsunami and its aftermath must of course have been full of such things; Yee's play isn't a documentary, but he's based it on interviews with survivors, whose stories he has freely - sometimes, by his own account, very freely - adapted. Some of them carry dramatic conviction; some don't, but there's no cumulative effect. It makes, inevitably, an uneven evening.

There are a few gestures towards continuity. Figures whom we think we may recognize from earlier scenes can sometimes be glimpsed gesticulating from behind a scrim. It may signify something that two separate characters are called Thomas; or that another makes a not-too-convincing reference to Samuel Beckett, while Beckett is the name of the character who delivers the play's opening monologue, a speech about imperceptible but devastating shifts in the Earth's balance that introduces us to the suggestive notion of "leap-seconds." The concluding scene introduces to another scientist, a meteorologist consumed with guilt at not having delivered an early warning. So that may close a circle. There are also two contrasting and consecutive scenes, one on either side of the intermission, featuring a prostitute and a client.

The set is a pool, with curtains behind it and a deck in front. Designed by Camellia Koo and lit by Michelle Ramsay, it's suggestive and efficient. Only one scene is actually set in a flood; physically, it's the highlight of Nina Lee Aquino's production. We see two brothers, designated the Swimmer and the Runner, perched on the roof of a doll's house, jettisoning their family possessions in an attempt to stay afloat; when this seems fruitless, Swimmer sets off to readjust the Earth's position to its proper place on the back of a turtle. We then switch to a different brand of religious belief. A Catholic priest is informed by an architect, who happens to be Muslim, that there's no scientific evidence that he and his flock were spared by having hung garlands on their statue of the Madonna; the Father is resistant, at least initially, to the suggestion that their survival is wonderful enough, and that he should thank Heaven for small miracles.

That's one of the play's best scenes. Another shows a Canadian DJ resolved to play his own song about the disaster, deemed by the higher-ups to be in bad taste, barricading his studio door, perhaps as if trying to hold back a tsunami. This segment is a bit over-stretched, but it's full of good lines. Indeed Yee writes many of them; perhaps the best is the suggestion in the second of the brothel scenes that a prostitute would regard the idea of love as unfair competition. That's another of the good scenes. Its predecessor, though not without tension, is one of the least believable, with a confusing shock-ending, feebly staged (though an apparent missed lighting-cue on the first night may have been partly to blame.)

An overall failing in Aquino's production is that it ain't got rhythm. The actors are too often left to flounder. Some can take care of themselves; Richard Zeppieri, who gives a trio of strong and very varied performances as intrusive North Americans, certainly can. So, most of the time, can Kawa Ada, especially as the imperturbably reasonable architect. Mayko Nguyen, the one woman in the cast, is fine when required to be cool and impassive, as when playing Second Sex Worker. But she flounders and gabbles as the Beckett-quoting Utah housewife, who gets an accusing visit from an FBI man (Richard Lee, unhelpfully stolid) with a literary background of his own. Whatever plane of reality this encounter is supposed to be occupying, the acting and direction fail to establish it; there's no pacing, no light, no shade. Unfortunate, because the basic situation, though strange, is not implausible. There's similarly no reason for Ash Knight to deliver the last big speech so over-emphatically that one loses the will to listen.

But there's delicacy on hand as well. Nguyen may be the only grown woman in the cast, but there is also a little girl, Eponine Lee, whose bio lists under upcoming engagements "being a first grader this coming September." She plays an orphan rescued by a gruff but compassionate man (John Ng, excellent) who knows from experience what it's like to be an orphan. There's a bond of sorts between them, however tenuous and temporary. And it's firmer on her part. Her every look and gesture, completely unstrained, communicate less fussily and more effectively than the verbalized efforts of some of her elders. At the end of the scene her uncle, as previously arranged, comes to collect her and doesn't even say thank you. This doesn't seem likely. It would also seem to undercut the theme of solidarity. But then that theme never establishes itself, and the play's own qualities undercut it. It's a special variety of curate's egg: good in unconnected parts.
