MAN AND SUPERMAN

Festival Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

- - -

"I wish says Enry Straker, Cockney chauffeur from the days when automobiles were automobiles and only the select and wealthy few could afford to own them, that I had a car that would go as fast as you can talk, Mr. Tanner." On Saturday at the Shaw Festival, every reviewer in the house must have rushed to copy that line down, while the response from the audience was uproarious. Enry had expressed perfectly what we had all been thinking. Ben Carlson plays Jack Tanner, self-described Member of the Idle Rich Class and author of The Revolutionists Handbook, and the exhilarating velocity of his delivery is only matched by its clarity, intelligence, charm, passion, self-mockery and wit. This is a wonderful performance, an individual creation that is a walking textbook on how to act Shaw.

Its the driving force of a tremendous production, one that was greeted at its premiere with an ovation of a fervour I have never encountered before at this festival and rarely anywhere else. (For an audience to stand up and cheer is standard politeness these days, but it's still unusual for it to refuse to shut up and leave when the lights are raised so that the actors have to take their calls all over again.) The volume of the applause was commensurate with the volume, in another sense, of the performance. This production is a six-hour affair, including a generous interval for lunch. It includes not only the basic comedy of courtship that constitutes the standard acting edition but the long tangent, in the form of a dream, that Shaw stuck in halfway through and that is generally known as Don Juan in Hell. In this, John Tanner takes on the identity of his namesake and supposed ancestor, Juan Tenorio, the lecher of Seville. Remembering that The Importance of Being Earnest is also in this year's repertoire, you might say he is Jack in this world and Juan in the next. This infernal interlude is crammed with orations that are among the longest, and mercifully among the best- turned, that GBS ever dreamed up.

Though the production is running through the season, the complete version can only be viewed this month. My best advice is to beat the doors down now, perhaps shaming the management into bringing the whole thing back next year. The shorter version will no doubt continue to be excellently performed, but it is unlikely to have the same impact.

It may also look rather odd. The director, Neil Munro, and his designer, Peter Hartwell, seem to have planned their staging on the sensible principle that a play which has to incorporate a scene in Hell can hardly afford to look realistic. So, though the costumes reflect the plays Edwardian origins, the settings could be anywhere. The first act, in particular, looks as if its going on in a rehearsal room; it's lined with benches and filled with chairs and tables that can be arranged to give the actors all the support they absolutely need and not a sofa or bookshelf. At the back is a screen on which the characters, who are, after all, myths-in-waiting, can cast their shadows before making their entrances. Tanner's motorcar gets similar skeletal treatment, and delighted applause, in the next scene. Shaw being notorious for his love of paradox, it seems appropriate that this season there should be a realistic car onstage in Pygmalion, which doesnt demand one, but not in this play, which does; and that both should work perfectly.

Munro and Hartwell delight in playing games with our expectations. When we get to Hell, we find four bare chairs side by side and dread that we are in for a static four-sided debate; in fact the chairs are only once occupied, and then as a joke. Most of the scene finds the actors roaming around a plush red operatic Hades, complete with yawning smoky grilles, but roaming always with purpose. Each of them seems ideally placed at every moment; the visual orchestration matches the verbal, and helps make it possible. And so it is, too, in the more conventional scenes. The play is a duel between Tanner, the self-proclaimed rebel against convention, and his ward, Ann Whitefield, who is set on marrying him. In each act they have a duet; each time they start on opposite sides of the wide stage and come gradually together, grudgingly or, at the last, passionately. Ann is that recurring Shavian figure, the respectable woman whose virtue allows her to be both shameless flirt and ruthless predator, but whom we cheer on because we know shes made the right choice, and that he will be the better for it. Fiona Byrne plays her very prettily and guilefully, if without the magnetic power that Shaw envisaged for this vessel of what he was pleased to call the Life Force.

And what has all this love play to do with Don Juan in Hell? Not much, actually; or, at least, the connection hangs by a thread. For centuries, poets and dramatists have been re-casting the legendary womanizer as a philosopher; Shaw, being Shaw, makes him a Puritan as well. The Devil (Benedict Campbell, courteous and implacable) is a sentimental hedonist, whom we have glimpsed on Earth as a Spanish brigand; backed up by the Statue who was Juan's earthly nemesis (David Schurmann, courteous and very placable, known in the waking world as Roebuck Ramsden, pillar of moderately enlightened society) he makes horribly irrefutable speeches about mankind's essentially destructive nature. Juan nevertheless refutes them, claiming, whatever its lapses, humanity strives for perfection; evolution continues to evolve. Ann, present as Dona Ana, grasps from this that her purpose is to help the process by finding the best father for her child. In other words, the whole gigantic digression provides the unconscious motivation for her behaviour in the main action. But, basically, what we have here are two plays for the price of one, and I personally am not complaining, especially given how well they are both being done.

I admit that the barebones production tends to maroon the older female characters, who need a society to belong to: Sharry Flett, good actress though she is, is in no way credible as Anns browbeaten mother. (If its any consolation to her, she looks far too young.) But there is a phalanx of good men. Evan Buliung may be too modern, too aware of his own absurdity, as Ann's hopeless poet- suitor, but his is still a delightful performance, and the herald of a future star. Straker is a gift of a role, but one that a surprising number of actors fumble; Patrick Galligan nails every thought and every syllable. There is solid work from Graeme Somerville and Guy Bannerman as a pair of Irish-Americans who serve to people a sub-plot, along with Lisa Norton who plays Ann's opposite number with unflinching unpleasantness.

We also get a glimpse in Hell of Jennifer Phipps, who plays Ann as an older woman and finds as much wit in five minutes as her colleagues do in five hours. The production knows what it's doing at every moment, down to when it starts lowering the curtain, with one vital speech still to go. Carlson, with whom this review must end as it began, unabashedly detaches himself and delivers it down front, marvellously. Earlier he has won applause in the middle of a scene with his magnificent delivery of a speech composed entirely of antitheses. I have never, in the non-musical theatre, felt an audience to be so completely with an actor; and never can the solidarity have been more deserved.
