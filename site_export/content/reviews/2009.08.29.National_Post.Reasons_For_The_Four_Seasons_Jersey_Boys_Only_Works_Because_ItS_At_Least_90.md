(From the National Post, September 2, 2009) Jersey Boys' book writer Rick Elice's name was spelled incorrectly in Saturday's Weekend Post. *****

Bob Gaudio was in Toronto last week for the first-anniversary gala performance of Jersey Boys, the musical based on the lives and songs of The Four Seasons. Gaudio was the Season who wrote the songs and the one who, if the show is to be believed, was the most diffident about performing them. I met him at his hotel that was, naturally, the Four Seasons.

According to Gaudio, the show is to be believed. "I was a more than reluctant performer. Who says you have to go out on the road and perform?" Beyond that, Jersey Boys isn't some outsiders' attempt to cash in on the fame and music of a legendary group. "It was homegrown; it was our idea," More specifically, it was the idea of the two most iconic of the original Seasons: Gaudio himself and lead singer and falsetto extraordinaire Frankie Valli. They had had "lots of TV offers," but the theatre beckoned, certainly for Gaudio himself: "Frankie and I thought, 'If we do a TV movie, it's a quick run and then it's over; we'll give the theatre a shot.' So we started to go back, to open doors."

Somewhere in the course of their door-opening, Gaudio saw Smokey Joe's Cafe, the roof-raising show based on the songs of Jerry Leiber and Mike Stoller, and thought, "Wow, look at that audience reception." That show was a revue with no connecting story. Then, even more successfully, came Mamma Mia!, which hung ABBA's hit songs on a newly invented storyline to which they had no particular connection. "I thought, well, where do we go from here? Well ... biography. When Rick and Marshall got involved, they jumped all over it." Rick Ellice and Marshall Brickman (the latter best-known as Woody Allen's screenwriting collaborator) were the show's book-writers. Des McAnuff, the show's director, now of the Stratford Festival, had a similar reaction. He's said that he had little interest in directing "another jukebox musical," but according to Gaudio, "his eyes lit up when he heard the backstory."

So, it seems, did the audience's. In October 2004, when the show opened at the La Jolla Playhouse in San Diego, where McAnuff was artistic director, Gaudio and Valli sat there "very apprehensive; how would a show about little old New York go down in Beach Boy territory?" It was perhaps a little late historically to worry about that; after all, nobody supposes that a show about the Beatles would only be of interest to people from Liverpool. Still, "we had mixed emotions about how it would all be dealt with. We had this upscale $300,000 audience. But it was stunning. We said, almost in unison: 'If this ever gets to New York...' "

Which, of course, it did. And it's still there. It does appeal to local pride; when I saw it in New York, the audience was full of present-day Jersey boys and girls, and their parents and grandparents, plus Valli himself, which delayed proceedings somewhat. But obviously the attraction goes far beyond that, as witness the success in Toronto, in London and all over the world. Gaudio says that keeping up with it and keeping an eye on it "is consuming most of my life." (He'd just got back from Australia.) So what are they responding to? According to Gaudio "at least 50% of the success is due to the staging, the story and how it's crafted."

At this point, Rick Ellice, one of the two men who crafted it, walked into the bar and joined the conversation. Gaudio, in his sixties, is tall, quiet-spoken and rather scholarly. Ellice, decades younger, is far more Manhattan and considerably more flamboyant. But he corroborated Gaudio. "We couldn't do Mamma Mia! Someone had already done Mamma Mia!. I thought I could do a turn for my friend Marshall Brickman to write it with me. I said 'Marshall, you wanna write a Broadway musical?' Marshall said 'no'. I said 'Marshall, it's about Frankie Valli and the Four Seasons.' " This apparently changed Marshall's mind. Ellice: "It's such a ripping yarn. That they led the lives they led. We were very lucky to have stepped in as good listeners."

And, Gaudio insists, they did lead those lives. Or at least "I'll go along with Frankie. He thinks it's 90% to 95% accurate --not chronologically perhaps, but most moments are the real deal." Even the things that seem too good to be true, like a teenage Joe Pesci being around as a gofer when the boys were scrabbling in Jersey, actually happened. And Gaudio writing their breakthrough hit Sherry 15 minutes before rehearsal -- that's true, too.

It's the breakthrough song in the show as well: The moment when the audience gets to hear what it came to hear and goes wild. But, as Ellice says about the show in general, there's no false drama attached to it: "Nobody sings 'oh Sherry baby' to a girl named Sherry." What it is, as Gaudio says, is an enormous release of tension: "You draw back a slingshot and, 45 minutes into the show, you finally release it. The audience shares in that reality." Part of that reality, as the show now stands, is the accuracy with which the actors mimic the sound and even the look of the original group, and especially the phenomenal Valli. Apparently, though, this wasn't the case in La Jolla where the actor, though talented, was far from a Valli soundalike: The scene still took off. These days, though, they insist on verisimilitude; "Frankie candidates have to go to Camp Castelluccio," Francesco Castelluccio being Frankie's real name.

I have a favourite theory about the Four Seasons' establishing hits, Sherry and the even better Big Girls Don't Cry, which is that with the extreme and abrupt contrasts between their high highs and low lows, they're funny. Well, that's how they sounded to my generation in London, where we couldn't have cared whether they came from New Jersey or New York or New Orleans. I tried my theory out on Gaudio, but he wasn't having it. Well, he did admit that "some of our songs have been quite humorous," and he did admit that the bass voice owed something to the cartoon sound of Yogi Bear, who at the time was even more popular than Sherry. But, he says, "we don't allow parodies of any kind. We wouldn't want to make light of songs that have moved people."
