MANGIACAKE!

Artword Theatre

- - -

Drama is a tough medium, though we seem to be losing sight of the fact. More and more we are asked not to experience a play but to empathize with some narrator as he turns the pages of a family snapshot album.

This week in Toronto, three solo performers on three consecutive nights have offered us their own or somebody elses memoirs in shows that have ranged, in strict order of appearance, from the sublime to the iniquitous.

On Tuesday, Kristen Thomson in I Claudia went to the trouble of creating four characters, none of them obviously herself, and one of them, the title figure, a possible classic. On Wednesday, in So Its Come to This, Emmett Walker told us more than we needed to know about how it felt to be Emmett Walker, but compensated by imparting some first-hand observations on the more interesting figure of Joseph Papp.

On Thursday in Mangiacake! (a term denoting assimilated Italian- Canadians who are popularly believed to eat cake rather than bread), Charly Chiarelli spent two-and-a-half hours filling us in on the great experience of growing up as Charly Chiarelli.

So why should anybody care?

All this frantic confessing or, in Mr. Chiarelli's case, frantic exulting may conceivably be traced to the old culture wars on the topic of Appropriation. We were told that for white writers to write about blacks or for men to write about women was to appropriate the other person's experience, and not to be endured.

If you took both the race and the gender warriors seriously (admittedly few people did, and they themselves never seemed to), then you had to go all the way and accept that only a black woman was entitled to depict the experiences of a black woman, and that she had better not include a black man or white woman, even as a walk-on. Nor, logically, should you stop there: since the identity of each one of us can be broken down into any number of criss- crossing subdivisions, obviously the only kind of a play anyone is entitled to write is an autobiographical monologue. And it had better be performed by the author, since bringing in an actor can only send the appropriation to radioactive levels because actors always have their own agenda and theyre such egotists.

This seems to be the position at which Messrs. Foster and Chiarelli have arrived. And maybe theyre right, but it does seem to deal a death-blow to the idea of theatre as we have traditionally known it.

Mr. Chiarelli grew up in Hamilton, the son of Sicilian parents. He begins by telling us of a trip he took to the old country as a young man in the company of his new Irish-Canadian wife. He then flashes back to his boyhood and doesn't return to Sicily until after the intermission. He is a cheery, emphatic performer with a stocky build, flashing white teeth and matching hair, and he does not underplay. Describing a sadistic schoolteacher as a mean, cruel bitch," he draws out the words and illustrates each with a separate grimace.

He intersperses his chat with songs, which he performs in a pleasantly modified Vegas croon while accompanying himself on blues harmonica. (It's like listening to Tony Bennett and Sonny Terry taking alternating phrases.) One of the songs, called Crazy in Love with Italy, is your standard Tin Pan Alley travelogue ("Florence is divine/Michelangelo and history), the kind traditionally turned out by writers who had never been to the places in question. It somehow doesnt help to know that Mr. Chiarelli actually has.

What really makes the evening unbearable is its unrelenting positivity. Mr. Chiarelli wins every round. True, he gets beaten up at school by kids who call him a wop; but he gets his own gang to inflict violent retribution on his tormentors and even ends up as president of his school council. (I confess his election-slogans -- Put a wop on top" and "If youre a Jew, Chiarelli's for you" -- made me laugh.)

The second act has a rollicking section concerning his adult bout with testicular cancer but this, too, he beats, enduring the side- effects of radiation by smoking marijuana: a happily topical touch in view of this week's news.

There is supposed to be an overriding theme: Mr. Chiarellis acceptance that he is, in his scripts terms, a mangiacake rather than a wop and that this goes double for his kids. But the play takes shape as a mere series of anecdotes. Those set in Italy only left me longing, even more urgently than usual, for the next episode of The Sopranos.

Dramatists naturally draw on their own experience but that experience has to be mediated: invent some characters, hire some actors, put yourself in a bad light, change the names. Before he earned the right to compose Long Day's Journey into Night, Eugene O'Neill had spent three decades being Eugene O'Neill, and I mean being the playwright, not the man.

Of course, for all I know Mr. Chiarelli may have invented the whole thing, but it felt like he was telling the truth, and I mean that as an insult. Am I being too harsh? Well, it has been a long, lonely week, and Mangiacake! was the longest as well as the worst thing in it.
