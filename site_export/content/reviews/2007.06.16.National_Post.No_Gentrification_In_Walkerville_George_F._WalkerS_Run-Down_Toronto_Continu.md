The Luminato festival focused heavily on the plays of George F. Walker, and two segments of that Walker Project are still around, playing in tandem. The Factory Theatre has revived its last year's production of Escape from Happiness and has added to it a new staging of the play to which that play is a sequel, the ironically titled Better Living.

Or is it ironic? My feelings about Walker's plays continue to bounce around. They have a style and a vision that are unlike anybody else's, they have a casual, eccentric wit that keeps me engaged and listening and, whether set in run-down Parkdale houses or suburban motels, they create a section of Toronto --call it Walkerville--full of people on the edge, living just inside or just outside the law. The problem is I don't believe in them. I don't mean that I don't believe such people exist, but that the ones Walker puts on stage rarely seem more than their author's puppets. Which makes the plays, for all the things that happen in them, seem weightless and ultimately unsatisfying.

Better Living, which is now 21 years old, is directed by Ken Gass with exemplary zip and precision, and it boasts an unbeatable cast. Nora, who, with eccentric determination, is renovating the basement of her East End house and would like to renovate her family along with it, is played by Clare Coulter, a past mistress at presenting a kind of batty integrity that almost amounts to secular sainthood. She's in her own world, though so far inside it that the messages she sends the outside world can sometimes, not for the first time with this actress, be indecipherable. She has three daughters. Elizabeth (Irene Poole, playing emphatically on the nose) escaped the house to become a lawyer; returning to it often makes her feel angry and frustrated. Mary Ann (Sarah Manninen, who's very funny) is the mother of a child whose whereabouts, as well as whose father's name, she keeps forgetting. Gail (Lisa Norton, leaving Shaw Festival gentility way behind her) has what seems to be mutually satisfactory sex with her boyfriend Junior (Brandon McGibbon, agreeably goofy), though when she moves out temporarily, he stays put.

The catalyst for the comings and goings is the return after several years of Nora's husband, Tom, an abusive ex-cop who left home in the wake of his family's self-defending attempts to kill him. You have to love a man who can take a hint. Now, however, he wants to bring order into their lives, so that they'll be able to defend themselves against the plundering hordes of people less fortunate than they are. Such do exist, and they have a nasty habit of being foreign. Fascist Tom may be, but, as played by Ron White, he attracted much of my sympathy simply by virtue of being the only person on stage who was neither shouting nor twittering. Well, almost the only one; his blessed calm is shared by Oliver Becker as Nora's singularly unshockable priestly brother. The two men have little else in common; in fact, brother, or should that be Father, Jack was a prime mover in Tom's planned demise.

Anyway, White's sleek performance develops a healthy decibel level of its own, as the residents and visitors line up with or against him. Some of them alternate, especially Elizabeth; the ferocity of Poole's performance may partly derive from the frequency, and the unaccountability, with which she is required to change sides. Nora, too, begins to warm to her ex, but only by refusing to acknowledge that he is her ex. This cognitive- dissonance device is repeated in Escape from Happiness, and it's no more persuasive here than it is there. All the same, Nora's stubborn refusal to see evil, even when she used to be married to it, has its own strength and it stands at least a chance of prevailing.

It's tempting to describe Walker's plays, with their casual violence, as black comedies, but it might be more accurate to call them sunny tragedies. They have an underlying benevolence: better living indeed. He deserves his place as the foremost Canadian playwright of his generation, but I wish there were more competition.

Tough!, a slightly later Walker play given a complementary production in the Factory Studio, does have believable characters: believable to a fault. It's a play about teens, for whom Walker turns out to have an excellent ear. Bobby has got Tina pregnant and has to decide whether to do the decent thing or carry on being happily promiscuous. Tina is going to keep the baby but has no intention of forcing Bobby into anything. Jill, Tina's friend, has never liked Bobby and is aggressively on his case and in his face.

A straightforward three-actor production would, I suspect, seem grindingly obvious: the sort of thing that gets toured to schools for sociological uplift. Gass has had the inspired idea of dividing each role four ways among a multi-cultural cast. So each character becomes an archetype compounded of individuals. Technically, too, the show gets terrific mileage out of overlapping dialogue, choral speech, and surprise movement. Nina Lee Aquino is associate director and her choreographic skills are well in evidence.

The 12 young performers (down to 11 on my night) worked exhilaratingly together, with Bethany Jillard, blonde and proud, outstanding among the Tinas. The assorted Bobbies ran a delightful gamut of feckless resentment while the various Jills gave an uncompromising joint account of one of the least sympathetic characters in contemporary drama. The show has closed, but you can bet it will be back.
