THEATRE REVIEW JESUS CHRIST SUPERSTAR

Thursday, August 09, 1990

ROBERT CUSHMAN

By Andrew Lloyd Webber and Tim Rice Directed by Tony Stevens Starring Stephen Lehew At O'Keefe Centre Jesus Christ Superstar is an up-to-date Sunday school treat with an impressive score and lyrics that are fresh and intelligent The Passion story as rock oratorio BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail THIS is the way it goes. A lot of street-life is happening in old Jerusalem, a territory with a hitherto unsuspected incidence of dry ice and late sixties tribal bopping. A token military presence, composed of young men in tunics and gleaming ironmongery, polices the quarter in the desultory fashion referred to in the training manuals as crossing from stage right to stage left and exiting; but as soon as this manoeuvre has been accomplished the locals return to their customary avocations. The righteous among them go looking for prostitutes to insult; the free- thinkers spring to the ladies defence; and there is a violent outbreak of Choreography. Then a long-haired young man in off-white Biblical togs appears atop the flight of steps that is the places principal architectural feature, and everything and everybody goes very quiet.

And the age-old question arises: is He or isn't he?

I may be the only theatregoer on the planet to whom, on Tuesday evening, all this was news. Until then I had never seen Jesus Christ Superstar.) Of course I had heard lots of it, and heard lots about it, and I approached it with certain prejudices. Many of these were positive; I wanted confirmation of my suspicion that Andrew Lloyd Webber's best shows were those he wrote with Tim Rice. On the other hand I distrusted rock musicals (remember rock musicals? nobody writes them now but they were quite a thing in the seventies) and though this may be because I distrust rock music anyway, I like to think that there are purely pragmamatic, theatrical reasons connected with lack of flexibility, and inaudibility of lyrics.

I remain as helplessly agnostic as the show itself. It is the Passion story, told as a set of reactions. The most famous of these is Mary Magdalenes chirpy professional response, cast as the prototypical early Webber ballad: the one that goes I Dont Know How To Cry For His Memory, Argentina. The others express various shades of skeptical bewilderment and human unworthiness. The Roman governor does his conscience-stricken hand- washing act: automatic Pilate. The High Priests are cardboard villains. Except in the taut Last-Supper-and-Gethsemane sequence, nobody interacts. At its inception, the show was tagged as rock opera but in its stasis it is more like rock oratorio.

Two characters, though, stand out. The authors, like many before them, reckon that Judas had a lousy deal: damned for carrying out a preordained divine plan. They give him a cogent case: disillusion with his leader's increasing intimations of immortality. He comes back for an 11 o'clock number - the original Superstar song - to ask Jesus a number of jivily sardonic modern questions. Does he believe in himself? Wouldnt his message have stood a better chance in an age of mass communication?

The second question history has answered. The final tableau - stars descending on the Crucifixion, like a Hollywood logo - graphically demonstrates the endurance of Jesuss legend. The audience cheered, partly out of nostalgia for the musical styles of 20 years ago but also out of a more deeply-rooted atavism. Ours may be a godless society but it has taken a lot of religious trappings on board. There is always a market for Biblical epics. Jesus Christ Superstar is an up-to-date Sunday school treat.

It doesn't have to be that way - Monty Python's Life of Brian proved that - but it is hard to avoid. Rice and Webber failed to avoid it perhaps because they had no fundamental interest in the subject. They pick it up, shake it about a bit with some smart points and exciting noises, and put it back precisely where it was.

On their own terms, though, they wrote an impressive score. Webbers ballads may be bathetic but his more barbaric noises - most notably the sardonic Hosanna chorus - are more than literally electric, and are offset by a recurrent haunting use of Mexican guitar. Only the pounding title phrase, like its pattering successor in The Phantom of the Opera, sounds risibly reductive. Rices words are often themselves reduced to doggerel by the insistent rhythms, but their concepts if not their execution are fresh and intelligent.

The Jesus of Stephen Lehew and the Judas of Milton Craig Nealy are powerfully sung, with tireless and rabble-rousing falsettos. Performers in this kind of show don't play the character, they play the style. That may be rock's legacy to the musical theatre.
