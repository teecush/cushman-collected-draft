The white politicians and entrepreneurs are back doing what they do best: swindling, land-grabbing and oppressing the native population. The native population are doing what they do best, resisting fiercely while squabbling among themselves so as to make victory even less likely. And Michael Hollingsworth, an artistic director of Video Cabaret and author and stager of most of their works, is doing what he does best, which is making vicious fun of the lot of them. At least I have to assume it's what he does best, since in 10 years or more of watching his work Ive never seen him do anything else.

The History of the Village of the Small Huts, Video Cabarets cartoon history of Canada, has now reached its seventh instalment, The Saskatchewan Rebellion. It's 1884, and that new-fangled railroad contraption is continuing with its mission, adumbrated in the preceding Canadian Pacific Scandal, of crossing the continent, spike by spike, encroaching and disinheriting everything that stands in its way. Doing a show about it may count as performing CPR. This time the victims are the Metis of Saskatchewan, whose struggle is led by Gabriel Dumont and the returned, and possibly deranged, hero Louis Riel, and the Cree, under Chief Big Bear. The grasping Donald Smith heads the Company; John A. Macdonald, as complacently power drunk, or just drunk, as before happily -- well, more or less happily -- supplies, at public expense, the massive infusions of cash so grandiose a project requires.

History, in short, repeats itself; and so does Hollingsworth. Much of what he does as a director certainly bears repeating. After all this time, one takes his black-box staging methods for granted, but there's nobody else who does what he does, at least nobody who does it with live actors rather than puppets. (His actors, or at least the people they play, may resemble puppets, but thats another thing.) On a tiny stage in the back room of a pub -- Hollingsworth, in a welcoming speech, called it a "cosmic dollhouse" -- seven actors playing an average of four roles apiece perform prodigies of quick change in gorgeously exaggerated costumes (designed by Astrid Janson and Sarah Armstrong) and a positive forest of wigs. Andy Moro supplies lighting of pinpoint precision and awesome lack of spillage, and the performers achievement in invariably hitting their marks deserves a round of applause of its own. The scenes are innumerable and, of course, short. This, as I've noted before, prevents both players and spectators from getting closely involved with any of the characters. Despite the proximity of stage and auditorium, and the clownishness of the performance style, which you'd think would be ingratiating, Video- Cabaret thrives on keeping everything at a distance. Its live theatre pretending to be canned.

Theres good and bad to this, and The Saskatchewan Rebellion offers both. The political intrigues are hard to follow, not I think, because they're especially confusing as narrative, but because the characters executing them are so bloodless. We know they're despicable, even before theyve opened their mouths, so we stop being interested. This goes, by the way, for the Metis leader Gabriel Dumont, who is decidedly bloodthirsty, as well as for the white men; the show spreads its satire around, though it reserves its contempt for the Caucasians. Video- Cabaret shows have, Ill swear, a special sound: a sneering acoustic. Words come out slow and curdled, surrounded by dead air. This show, though, has slightly more emotional content than most. When the military and the Mounties set about starving the Cree off their lands and into reservations, it's moving as well as horrific. Some of this is due to the performance of Michaela Washburn as the Cree chief, a tiny figure -- not so much Big Bear as Baby Bear -- of piteous dignity, especially when trying to be crafty in the face of firepower. (Washburn is the only woman in the cast, and only one of the roles she plays is female.) The other outstanding turn comes from Mac Fyfe as Riel. If the show uses video techniques, then he is the only character permitted a close-up. We see him right downstage, his face to us and his back to the judge, as he is tried, convicted and condemned to hang. He is also on his knees, though this fact is concealed from us until he gets up; the lighting is that good. Earlier, as a charismatic leader, he has teetered brilliantly on the edge of insanity, a religious zealot who seems to be his own God. Fyfe, uniquely, plays just the one role, and he's the Riel thing. The script goes in for some low-rent anachronisms: the victors declare that theyre going to party like it was 1885. Far more agonizing is when John A., at a temporary low point and reaching for the bottle, sings "my kind of town, Oblivionville is"; it doesnt even scan, and he does it over and over again In related news, it's the rich what gets the pleasure and the poor what gets the blame, throughout.
