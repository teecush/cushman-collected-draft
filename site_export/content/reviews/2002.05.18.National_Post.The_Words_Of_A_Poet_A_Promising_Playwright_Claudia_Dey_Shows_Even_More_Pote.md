Until June 9. 416-504-9971

Honour a theatre that stands by its writers. A couple of years ago Factory Theatre, in its studio space, took a chance on Beaver, a new play by a new playwright, Claudia Dey. It was savaged by the Toronto critics, including this one. Undeterred, Factory has now given us a new Dey, this time on its main stage. In the meantime, Beaver has been staged off-Broadway, where it has had a much warmer reception.

Beaver struck me as the work of a talented writer whose gift for language far outran her instinct for construction. It sprawled unbearably, though the New York staging may conceivably have made it tighter.

Dey's new piece, The Gwendolyn Poems, is even better written and rambles less, though it still lacks focus. It does, though, have the built-in discipline of biography, being the latest play about the poet Gwendolyn MacEwen. (They seem to come round once every two years.)

The play gives us MacEwen's deathbed reminiscences, or hallucinations; she died in her forties of complications from alcoholism. The framework, once established, is easy to forget, and most of the time we could just be watching a series of random flashbacks, featuring the more damaging people in Gwendolyns life. These include her photographer father, loving in a distant kind of way but separated from her, first by the disciplines of the darkroom and then by those of divorce; her mother, crazily embittered; and her first husband, Milton Acorn, fellow poet and about 20 years her senior.

His star wanes as hers waxes, and you half expect him to walk sacrificially into the ocean, or at least into Lake Ontario, like Norman Maine in A Star is Born. Getting wet, though, isnt his style; he has serious bathing issues.

The first half of the play shows us Gwendolyn facing these demons with considerable resource. The second half shows her falling apart, unhappy when drunk and worse when sober and a prey to DTs. In theory, one can understand how the later woman would develop from the earlier; in practise, we just don't see it happen. We gather that Gwendolyn is/was essentially life-affirming. Well, she would be, wouldn't she, being a poet and a woman and all.

There seems no pressing reason for most of the scenes to happen in the order they do, but individually many of them are a pleasure to listen to. Deys dialogue has real stage presence: heightened and electrically charged without ever seeming false. This is not, blessedly, an and then I wrote" evening; when poetry is called for, Dey writes her own rather than quoting her subjects (or so I am told; I am hardly a MacEwen expert). It works very well.

Words and feelings are brought superbly to life by Brooke Johnson, a hugely expressive actress who can change age and temperament in the flicker of an eyelash, and sometimes less. She has a comic-heroic presence that breathes sanity, making it all the more moving when she loses it. Barbara Gordon is brilliantly infuriating as the mother, forgetting and remembering things as it suits her.

I'm beginning to wonder if theatres that hire David Fox and Jerry Franken (together again after their joint appearances in The Drawer Boy and Stratford's Trials of Ezra Pound) get a rate. I am usually delighted to see them, either individually or as a team, but they got on my nerves here. Foxs Milton Acorn is a caricature of the eccentric artist, Quasimodo in a fright wig and a red-check shirt, and largely incomprehensible. Franken is surprisingly grey and mumbly as dad, though he compensates with an excellent performance as a prosaically kindly doctor who wants to save Gwendy (her mothers name for her, which tells you a lot about her mother) but is dubious about his chances.

Not all the best scenes are due to MacEwen's nearest and dearest being monsters; indeed, some of the best scenes are with her best friend, a sensible poet, played with refreshing solidity by Tamsin Kelsey.

Tony Nappo completes the cast as Gwendolyn's second spouse, an amiable Greek (his home country impresses his wife as being far superior to the Danforth) and also as Lawrence of Arabia, a life- long obsession of MacEwens who makes a sheik-like personal appearance just prior to the final curtain. This doesnt work, but then neither do any of the periodic references to him earlier on; this does seem to be a case of the author getting hung up on what she's read in the books.

The sky-cloth turns a welcome shade of deep blue for this last encounter; up till then, everything in Eda Holmes' production has looked dingy. This makes factual sense, Gwendolyn having lived for the most part in underlit basements, but it doesn't help concentration.
