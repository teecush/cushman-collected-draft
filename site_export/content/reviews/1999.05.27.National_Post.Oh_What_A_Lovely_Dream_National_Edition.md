HEARTBREAK HOUSE

The Shaw Festival

Bernard Shaw's Heartbreak House begins with a young woman waking up. It is the midpoint of the First World War, and Ellie Dunn is visiting the strange, ship-like house of the octogenarian Captain Shotover, situated -- appropriately enough -- on the English seacoast. Waiting for someone to notice her, she has fallen asleep over a book.

Tadeusz Bradeckis production, which gets this years Shaw Festival off to an uncommonly beautiful start, is not the first to treat Ellie's abrupt awakening as if it were a descent into a deeper level of slumber; in effect, to present the whole play as her dream. But it goes further than any other that I have seen. Bradecki frames the action by showing us Ellie approaching the house at the beginning and departing it at the end.

There seem, as well, to be dreams within dreams. One character is sent into a hypnotic trance, and the experience sends him into jerky, marionettish staggers. Another spends the entire last act in his pyjamas. The captain himself, who combines the roles of absent- minded inventor and angry sage, slips constantly between levels of consciousness. All this is played to the hilt.

Intellectually, this cuts very little ice; it is hard to interpret the dreams of people whom you have never known to be awake. Atmospherically, it is very effective, culminating in a Zeppelin raid that the characters ecstatically welcome (up to now, none of them has even mentioned the war) and for which the sky turns apocalyptically red while thunderous strains of Mahler fill the air.

Peter Hartwell's set is less obviously ship-shape than most; it has a somewhat Japanese air, with screen-like walls and a floor marked out in alternating squares of black and white. It is the kind of geometrically satisfying design on which everything and everybody looks good.

I said the production was beautiful; I didnt say it was totally satisfying. It takes very good care of the play but loses some of its endearing untidiness. This, according to taste, is Shaws richest play or one of his most wayward. (I pass.)

Probably, it's two plays. One of them is a savage arraignment of a bunch of drifters, exploiters, and ne'er-do-wells who deserve the fate they seem so enthusiastically to accept. Shaw said that Heartbreak House was a metaphor for cultured, leisured Europe before the war, but he seems really to have had something narrower in mind, and closer to home. "Learn your business as an Englishman," rails the captain, "navigation . . . neglect it and be damned," and in England, lines like that seem perennially topical. Im not sure how well they travel. (Closing your eyes and thinking of Canada doesnt quite do it.)

At the same time we have an enchanting bunch of talkers who make excellent jokes, flirt incessantly but passionlessly, and seem to be doing no harm to anyone. As one of them says, they have no hearts to break. Shaw was incapable of disliking his characters (the converse is that he couldn't love them either), even when he disapproved of them.

There is a supposed villain here: Boss Mangan, a middle-aged capitalist who has ruined Ellie's harmless father and now wants to buy her in marriage. But he never stands a chance; he is victimized and talked down by everyone. We spend most of the time feeling sorry for him. By the time its revealed that his millions are illusory anyway, its just another Shavian paradox, at which we merely shake our spinning heads. Seen in this light, the play's finale is just Shaw's way out of a tight corner.

Bradeckis production keeps the play firmly within its judgmental frame, while losing some juice and some jokes. This bothered me less, though, as the evening progressed and, looking back the morning after, I wonder at my own ingratitude. Bradecki can orchestrate actors as well as effects. Individually, I have seen most of these roles better played (except perhaps for the housekeeper, brought to fire-breathing Irish life by Patricia Hamilton); collectively, they make a very impressive ensemble.

Douglas Rains Shotover is a partial problem: impeccably phrased, but in tenor rather than bass (Shaw, music critic, was precise about these things), and physically slight, both as farceur and prophet. Shotover is Shaw's Lear, complete with daughters. One, Hesione (Sarah Orenstein, eye-flashing, eye-catching and warmly easy on the ear) is a gypsyish enchantress, married to a moustachioed ladykiller (Gordon Rand) who is tired of the role, and who acts as mouthpiece for Shaw's erotic despair; he and Hesione claim to have children, but I dont believe it.

Daughter two is Ariadne (Kelli Fox, imperious) married to a colonial governor who -- perhaps because he is kept offstage -- is the one character for whom his authors loathing rings true. Ellie, though unrelated, counts dramatically as daughter three, and is played by Fiona Byrne with appropriate cool detachment; her dad (David Schurmann) is very nice, in all senses.

Jim Mezon does Mangan, and has a sporting try at being simultaneously ogre and dwarf. There is also a languid toff (Kevin Bundy), similarly mocked before he even opens his mouth; and a logic- chopping burglar (Barry MacGregor) who seems to have wandered in from some more run-of-the-mill Shavian play.

A tot of rum -- or better yet champagne -- to all hands. (But don't tell Shaw.)
