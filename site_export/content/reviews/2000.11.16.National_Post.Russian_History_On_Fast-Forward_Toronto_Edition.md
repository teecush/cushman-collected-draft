SLAVS!

Tarragon Theatre, Toronto

Tony Kushner's much-feted double-decker Angels in America was a trenchant little piece about No. 1 ratfink Roy Cohn, adorned with some very good jokes and embedded in hours and hours of New Age whimsy that were tiresome at the start and unendurable by the close.

SLAVS!, his new play -- well, comparatively new, its been around in the U.S. since 1994 -- is, physically at least, a smaller-scale work; it plays for 90 intermissionless minutes rather than seven hours. It still manages to pack within those confines three acts and an epilogue, and its subject could hardly be larger. Its about Russia, during and after the last years of the Soviet Union. The mixture, though, is proportionately much the same, though this time the good parts, rather than being teasingly parcelled out through the entire work, come in a lump at the end.

Also at the very beginning: The show could hardly get off to a better start. Marked out on the stage floor, on John Jenkins' set, is a large white hammer and sickle. It turns out to be made of white confetti, and two substantial and impeccably Russian-looking women come on stage to sweep it off. While they work they debate the principles of dialectical materialism and their continued relevance or otherwise to the international working class.

The two babushkas (so identified in the program) are played by Maria Vacratsis and Randy Hughson, the latter of whom is a man. So this introductory scene is formally halfway toward the Monty Python sketch in which Eric Idle and Terry Jones (I think) appeared as two housewives with scarves on their heads who sat around in the laundromat, discussed existentialism, and finally put in a call to Jean-Paul Sartre.

The humour, though not up to the Python level, is still pretty funny, and it's abetted when more confetti falls from above to simulate snow. (Though when this effect is repeated later on, the law of diminishing returns kicks remorselessly in.)

The babushkas are chased offstage by a posse of apparatchiks who proceed to make speeches. These are the days of Gorbachev, and one speaker, seemingly one of natures filibusterers, makes a plea to stand fast against the tide of what calls itself reform. Another, by contrast, advocates that they should all leap vigorously into the future, and himself starts leaping all over the stage.

With only six actors in the cast, doubling and trebling come with the territory, and there is more cross-gender casting here. Brenda Robins and Maria Vacratsis deliver their tirades with much stomach- patting and squeaky baritoning, just in case were not sufficiently amused -- or sufficiently tipped off -- by the characters having names that sound like a bad translation of Gogol: names like Aleksii Antedilluvianovich Prelapsarianov. Both the orators fall dead -- one from too much talking, the other from too much jumping -- and that is Act One.

Act Two takes place in a room where the brains of old revolutionary heroes are kept, pickled. A party functionary (Ashley Wright) has wangled a post as guardian of these exhibits for a woman whom he would like to seduce. She, however, refuses his advances (though not the job) on the truthful grounds that she is having a lesbian affair with a doctor.

The doctor is a still-idealistic socialist; the sympathies of the guard (played with sardonic gusto by Kelli Fox) are, if anything, peasant and pre-Marxist. We take notice of this, and of the fact that love unites what ideology might put asunder, and of the surrounding visual symbolism.

It still seems very scrappy, and unfunnily cartoonish, and it doesn't account for a sequence, punctuated by blackouts, in which the two women get progressively (if those are your politics) drunker and more amorous.

By this point, I thought the play, considering the author's reputation and the amount of performing talent involved, to be a disgrace. Then the third act came along and knocked me over.

Its set in Siberia, under Yeltsin. The doctor from the previous episode, fervently played by Brenda Robins as a practical idealist whose disillusion has stopped miraculously short of cynicism, is ministering to the hopeless victims of radiation; we see one, a mute little girl.

In an earlier scene we have met a quietly smooth politician, given by Hughson the most restrained and effective performance of the evening thus far. He now reappears, and the childs mother, tremendously played by Maria Vacratsis, asks him what he -- not some abstract government, he -- is going to do about her situation.

It's a great wish-fulfilling moment: the kind of thing we have all, even in far less drastic circumstances, ached to ask of authority. The man, of course, has no answer -- except that he turns out to be a Russian ideologue of the newest and most frightening type, blaming everything on foreigners, financiers and Jews.

Kushner is a Jew and a leftist (he has said that coming out as a socialist is harder these days than coming out as gay) but to his credit, he doesn't have the woman reject fascist ideas in the name of proletarian principle; she merely says coldly that she is a Lithuanian, and that Russian nationalism is of no interest to her. Its left to the doctor to speak for the author: to say that socialism, whatever the crimes committed in its name (and she makes no bones about the impossibility of something like Chernobyl being permitted in the West), is still preferable to the alternative.

I hope shes right, but I'd feel happier about the play's position if it didnt (as the left, especially the theatrical left, still routinely does) sentimentally invoke Lenin, who may not have killed as many people as Stalin but was no teddy bear, either.

The epilogue reintroduces us to the two deceased politicos, who are in heaven and better company dead than alive; they are joined by the sick girl, now able to talk. She tells them, clearly, unemotionally and -- in Adanya Dunns performance -- with spell- binding fluency just how and why she died. Here the play finally achieves the tone of mordant, aching irony for which it has striven all evening.

It doesn't justify everything; the play's generalizations about the Slavic temperament -- all that vodka and desperation -- amount to no more than a series of literary gags. (A line about wanting to go to Moscow got the loudest laugh of the evening from the theatrically hip first-night audience, myself included.) But the good outweighs the bad, and Dennis Garnhum's production, uniting fidelity and flair, plays both to the hilt.
