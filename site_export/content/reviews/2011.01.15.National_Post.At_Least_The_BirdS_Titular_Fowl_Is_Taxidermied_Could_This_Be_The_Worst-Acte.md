Noel Coward once wrote of The Seagull, or it may have been The Wild Duck, that he hated plays in which there was a stuffed bird sitting on the bookcase shrieking "I'm the title! I'm the title!" The bird in The Bird is also stuffed and even more irritating. At least Ibsen and Chekhov gave us wildfowl who served a specific purpose in the lives of their plays' fully developed heroines. In Sonja Mills' new play, the significance is spread around and hopelessly diluted. In prosaic terms, the bird owes its presence, and its present stuffed state, to the fact that one of the characters is a taxidermist, half of a lesbian couple who live high up in a luxurious-looking Toronto condo. They're throwing a party whose action makes it "finally clear that we, all of us, are no better off than a bird who has hopelessly smashed into the 40th floor window of a skyscraper, unaware there was any obstacle in its path at all." That's even though some of us live in nice spaces and have good jobs.

The quotation above comes from a plot-and-theme synopsis that was issued to the critics and for which I am profoundly grateful, since I certainly couldn't have worked any of it out -- the thematic part anyway -- on my own. Ordinary punters presumably have to do without, though they can read a program note in which the producing company, Union State Theatre, announces that its mandate (from whom?) is to present work "which integrates text with movement." I thought that was what all productions try to do, unless they're either mime or stationary recitations. I did notice a certain awkwardness in some of the blocking, but I'd put it down to the director, Ruth Madoc-Jones, being defeated by some of her author's more improbable contrivances regarding entrances and exits. But I now see that it was a conscious stylistic choice, and that the show has a "movement director," Clare Preuss, to prove it. (And on the subject of programs, what's wrong with the good old tradition of listing the characters' names on the main page alongside those of the actors, rather than having the audience hunt through the cast bios to discover who's playing whom?)

Even with two directors, The Bird is the worst-acted play to have arrived on a Toronto stage in a long time (and, no, I'm not forgetting Soulseek). I spent some time wondering whether the script might have sounded more credible if the players had delivered it with a bit more naturalness and a lot more nuance, or if they were forced into strident artificiality by a text that consists entirely of people bitching. Quite possibly all concerned got helplessly locked into the same spiral. Anyway, Mia the bird-stuffer lives with Kate who's in marketing, and they've invited Kate's new assistant, Petula, a.k.a. Petty, over for drinks and chat. Petty brings her husband, Gord, and also her brother, Boo, and his wife, Donna. All these pairings are acutely dysfunctional, both intra-and extra-murally. Kate suspects that Petty in fact may be Mia's new pet. Donna is acidly, and continuously, contemptuous of Boo, who's in turn convinced that she's out every night cheating on him, quite possibly with Gord. It doesn't even stop at sex. Boo is a bank manager, and Kate one of his customers, each complaining that the other has been extremely rude. This disclosure drives Boo to tears and Kate to a level of abrasiveness that may be even higher than the one she started the play with, though it's hard to tell. There's a seventh character, Mia's heavily pregnant sister, Spencer, who seems to be there mainly so the others can discuss the pros and cons of smoking (either tobacco or weed) in her presence; just for a laugh, she herself is the heaviest puffer of them all. When all other avenues have been exhausted, the play throws in some remembered incest. To be fair, August: Osage County employed the same scattershot approach and it got a Pulitzer. But at least it had some wit, and characters with a modicum of depth. The people here are dummies, set up merely to knock one another down. Towards the end, there's some attempt to broaden the focus. Boo complains that because he's a banker, everyone blames him for the recession, whereas marketers like Kate, pushing useless goods, are equally responsible. My impression is most people at the moment hate both professions equally, and the play possibly agrees. It seems to want to implicate all the other characters as well, selfish things that they are. It also seems to want us to feel sorry for them, without giving them enough humanity to warrant either reaction. Throw in a few lines about the economy, and you get marks for sociological concern. Add a symbolic dead bird, and you also get credit for compassion and poetic imagery. It's a fair summation of what's wrong with Canadian playwriting.

To be fair, again, Mills' previous, and slightly better, work The Danish Play seemed to me equally forced and artificial, but everyone else loved it; it got raves and awards and revivals and everything. There, too, unnatural writing seemed to breed unnatural acting; it had one good performance (and not the one that won the raves and awards). I don't see any of the present team romping home with Doras, but again, what do I know? Astrid Van Wieren gives the keynote loud performance as Kate; it isn't so much that she's worse than the others, it's just that there's more of her. The most tolerable is Bruce Hunter as Boo, perhaps because he gets to show some variety. Before he becomes hateful, he's allowed to be pitiable; he keeps saying he wants to kill himself, and it's hard to resist that. But, really, it's for the birds.
