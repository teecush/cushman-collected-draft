The Roar of the Greasepaint, the Smell of the Crowd

Jane Mallet Theatre,

St. Lawrence Centre, Toronto

Between 1961 and 1972, the team of Anthony Newley and Leslie Bricusse turned out three musicals with long titles and grandiose ambitions. The first, and the most successful, was Stop the World -- I Want to Get Off. The last, and the least, was The Good Old Bad Old Days. The Roar of the Greasepaint, the Smell of the Crowd came in between.

Reprise is a Toronto company dedicated to presenting "great musicals" (well, of course they're great; this is show business, baby) "in concert" (this means the actors carry scripts, but the technical and performing standards are high). Roar, which played over the weekend, is their second production, an enterprising choice since the show has hardly been seen since its Broadway run in the mid-1960s. I am grateful for the chance of collecting it, and they did it well. But it is a truly terrible show.

It's a musical morality. Its principal characters are Sir, who is fat, and Cocky, who is starving. Sir wears a top hat and Cocky a bowler. Cocky staggers about carrying Sir's suitcases. Sir doesn't actually drag Cocky around on a rope, but in all other respects they might be Pozzo and Lucky, limbering up for the musical of Waiting for Godot. (You mean you hadn't heard?) Sir even oppresses Cocky with bogus Latin tags.

They spend their days playing "the Game," a sort of snakes-and- ladders with humans for counters. This is actually a ritual devised by Sir for the humiliation of Cocky, who is systematically deprived of food, drink, cash, and -- despite the tormenting presence of a sizable female chorus -- sexual gratification.

However, since Sir can change the rules at will, and since he treats Cocky the same even when they're not playing, it is all crashingly monotonous. There is some variation in the second act, when a character billed as The Negro comes on and declines to be oppressed, giving Cocky the inspiration for a revolt of his own; it's surprisingly easy when you know how. But even when the tables are turned, it's the same two dummies on either side.

Newley and Bricusse are English but even in England, even thirty years ago, the class war was subtler than this. Newley always starred in his own shows (he directed them, too), and Cocky does occasionally exude his personal brand of scary underdog humour, but the efforts to harness it to satire are embarrassing. They were even worse in Stop the World, a loathsome show, but at least in that one the protagonist functioned as both villain and victim, so there was some complexity.

But, of the two shows, it's The Roar that has the score. Nine of the songs became popular (a stunning number for a musical by that time, especially a British musical) and a couple have remained so. The best-known is Who Can I Turn to?, which has a hauntingly dark tune but a lyric so generalized as to be virtually meaningless. It's a shock to discover that, despite the song's generous use of the word "you," it isn't actually sung to anybody; Bricusse -- insiders generally credit him with the words and Newley with the music -- was already on his way to his current Jekyll and Hyde status as author of Broadway's most vacuous lyrics. The songs have a theatre sound but a pop mentality, and in their day their generic quality helped them get around; A Wonderful Day Like Today was every Vegas singer's favourite opener for a season or so, and Look at that Face has charm. To be fair, both songs in context have sardonic second choruses.

The show's abstraction goes well in a concert setting, and Tom Diamond directed it smartly on a stage marked out in three adjacent circles. Marek Norman's four-piece band sounded like more. Michael Fletcher embellished Sir's complacent pronouncements with extensive hummings and hawings of his own. If he slowed the show, Mark Cassius, with an impassioned reading of the Negro's anthem Feeling Good, outright stopped it, which is a far, far better thing. Steven Sutcliffe, the magnetic Younger Brother of Ragtime, played Cocky with a vocal infection that impaired his dynamics, but not his dynamism. Raging or suffering, he was in complete control.
