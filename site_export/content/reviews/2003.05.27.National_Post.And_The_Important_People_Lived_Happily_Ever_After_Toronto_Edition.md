WIDOWERS' HOUSES

Court House Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont.

I know people who can't stand Shaw, but who make an exception for Widowers' Houses. It was his first play, originally staged in 1892, and in some respects it's a crude one, but its crudity is bracing. It is free of the sound of the author congratulating himself on his own cleverness. Widowers' Houses is an "issue" play, but like all the best of its breed it transcends that label and becomes a moral play, though not -- to echo a distinction made by one of its own characters -- a moralizing play.

The issue here is slum landlordism. The deeper themes are poverty and responsibility. Harry Trench, a young London doctor on a holiday jaunt to Germany, falls in with Sartorius, a self-made gentleman of mature years and ample income, and his daughter Blanche. Harry and Blanche are mutually attracted, and he asks for her hand. Her father is glad to grant it, since Trench, though of modest personal means, is aristocratically connected.

Back home, Trench discovers that his future father-in-law's wealth derives from buying up decayed properties, letting them decay further, and squeezing rents out of impoverished families who have nowhere else to go. Trench, revolted, wants nothing to do with the business or its proceeds, until Sartorius points out to him that he is already profiting from it; he holds a mortgage on one of the properties. By now, however, Harry has alienated his fiancee by asking her to live on his salary. She, who has a temper to match her father's and is far less adept at controlling it, has him shown the door.

In the play's last act, the climate changes. Sartorius discovers there is more money to be made by improving his houses, thereby upping the compensation when railways and town planning call for their demolition. He is given this tip by Lickcheese, formerly his highly efficient rent collector but dismissed for authorizing some desperately needed repairs. Lickcheese has since become adept at insider trading, and is now a figure of oily prosperity himself. For the scheme to go through, Trench's consent as mortgagee is required, and he sullenly refuses to give it. But he is talked round, the lovers are reconciled, and the drama concludes with all proceeding in to what will undoubtedly be an excellent supper. There is a happy ending for all the characters except the unseen ones: Sartorius' tenants, who are still destitute and will shortly be homeless as well.

Many of Shaw's plays conclude in this way, with a festive finale beneath which the relevant social evils continue to fester. However, as the plays get progressively more talkative and diffuse (and also, to be fair, funnier and wider ranging), so the bonhomie tends to overwhelm the indignation. Here there's no mistaking it. The lines are drawn halfway through the evening, when Sartorius sets about demolishing Trench's illusions. Like all Shavian capitalists, he makes a very good case. People need somewhere to live; any improvements he put in would be stolen and burned for firewood; the situation is regrettable but it cannot be helped. Lickcheese, while railing at Sartorius' meanness, says much the same thing. Trench, convicted of fecklessness for never having examined the sources of his own income, can find no answer. He refuses to play the hypocrite by swallowing Sartorius' late conversion to the cause of slum clearance, but sex -- as powerful a lure as money -- destroys even these defences.

Joseph Ziegler, director of the Shaw Festival production, says in his program note that Shaw "offers us no hint of a solution." This isn't quite true. Before catechizing Trench, Sartorius says, "I assume that you are not a socialist, or anything of that sort."

"Certainly not," says Harry, indignantly. Shaw himself, of course, was a socialist, and he is here offering us the glimpse of a loophole, one that his characters immediately close. In other words, this is the best kind of propaganda play: the kind that keeps the propagandists offstage.

The roles have been cast largely against type, with varying results. Trench, an unthinking young hearty in Shaw's description, is played by Dylan Trowbridge as slight, intense and idealistic -- "boyish," as the stage directions prescribe, but in a more vulnerable way. It works, though, and Trowbridge's alternating bouts of passion, shame and confusion give the production much of its energy. There is a further injection from Peter Millard's Lickcheese, less cringingly Dickensian than his name and some of his lines might suggest, but solid and biting -- occasionally too much so, since he steps out of character to underline a few of his points. (Shaw isn't Brecht.) His insidious geniality in the last scene is very good.

Jim Mezon, with massively domed brow, curled grey moustache, trim beard, and walking stick rampant, brings his familiar quiet authority to Sartorius, and commands the stage as usual. I don't know why he goes sentimental instead of stern when reminding Blanche of his own mother's poverty, or why he relapses into Cockney while he's doing it. Or rather, I think I do know why, and I also think it's a mistake.

Blanche herself is a problem, partly because though she enriches the play, she also overflows it; everybody else is in a bitter parable while she's in Hedda Gabler. Her tantrums seem to have been inspired by one of Shaw's own lady friends. Her furious relations with her father and with her intended are more than the main action requires, but need more space if they are to register in their own right. (She also seems to be conducting a sado-masochistic relationship: a minute role, admirably filled out by Susie Burnett.) The production errs by bringing her final seduction of Harry down almost to the level of farce. Their last clinch needs to be passionately sustained if it's to bear the weight the author places on it. Lisa Norton has to strain hard, and mostly in vain, to reach the full dimensions of Blanche's passions, or indeed of her snobbery. If Sartorius has the vestige of a social conscience, she ("I hate the poor -- if they must be provided for, let somebody else look after them") has none, as he ruefully recognizes. It isn't her fault -- few things here are anyone's individual fault -- but she comes as close as anyone to being the play's villain.

Christina Poddubiuk has designed a versatile setting that blossoms especially in its third and last incarnation as a lamplit drawing room, a cosy bastion against the outer darkness.
