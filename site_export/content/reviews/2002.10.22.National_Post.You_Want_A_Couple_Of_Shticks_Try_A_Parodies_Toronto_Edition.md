HELLO MUDDAH, HELLO FADDUH!

New Yorker Theatre, Toronto

Years ago in New York, I sat in on some auditions for what turned out to be a legendary flop musical, an item called Dance a Little Closer: lyrics by Alan Jay Lerner, who was absent that day, and music by Charles Strouse (Bye Bye Birdie, Annie), who was present. One applicant impressed everyone with an unknown song titled Juggling that lived up to its title in the dexterity with which it sustained its metaphor for the plight of a philandering husband. It turned out to be a cut song from an un-legendary flop musical called The Fig Leaves Are Falling, whose lyrics were by Allan Sherman. Upon hearing this information, Strouse slapped his thigh and said, Every lyric by Allan that Ive ever heard has been brilliant.

Sherman is better known, of course, as the author of comedy lyrics set to pre-existing tunes, the most famous being Hello Muddah, Hello Fadduh, which lends its name to the anthology of his songs that has now arrived in Toronto after two separate runs in New York. Juggling, alas, is not included in this show, but three other songs from Fig Leaves are thrown in toward the end, notably one of those what-has-it-all-been-for ballads, Did I Ever Really Live? It has an admirably terse lyric that steers clear of melodrama, and it might well be powerful in the right context.

It isn't in the right context here. Hello Muddah has been equipped with a story -- a man's life from cradle to within sight of the grave -- but that life seems to be so extraordinarily free of major trauma that the song seems pointless. Did he he ever really live? Not dramatically he didnt.

The show was put together by Rob Krausz, who also directs, and Douglas Bernstein, a skilled lyricist in his own right. If youre talking about parody lyrics, Bernstein once co-wrote a spoof of one of the songs from A Chorus Line that is funnier than all the editions of Forbidden Broadway put together. The script they have provided here is admittedly and intentionally the slightest, a succession of gag-filled pretexts for moving us from song to song.

But it's still a book musical, and that genre comes with its own built-in rules. Even if the book is just there to provide a framework for the songs, it should provide one that allows them to make sense. Alarmingly, Hello Muddah fails to do this even for the familiar numbers that are its reason for being.

Our hero's name is Barry Bockman, and he delivers his first song from his high chair. Its addressed to the little girl he will eventually marry; both she and the song are called Sarah Jackman, the tune being that of Frere Jacques. The song sends deadpan greetings to all the members of Sarahs very extended Jewish family. It may sound cute coming out of the mouths of babes, but it doesn't sound right.

Then there's the title number, that deathless musicalization of a boys desperate letter home from summer camp. Thats destroyed, too. "I've been here one whole day" wails the lyric, but here it's preceded (and interrupted) by dialogue suggesting that not only is it not young Barrys first day at the dreaded Camp Granada, it isnt even his first year. Some of the original's best lines have been cut, and the punchline -- a perfectly poised ending to a perfectly constructed lyric -- has been defused.

One thing that's unavoidably missing is the authors own voice. The song became a hit, 40 years ago, in Shermans own recording. He conveyed the boy's sensibility perfectly but made no attempt actually to impersonate him. Obviously, this option is not available to an actor, but without this kind of distancing effect the songs, which are slight, tend to wither.

It's even worse with numbers that were written to reflect no sensibility other than their authors; the spirit, that is, of an observant Borscht Belt comic, happily immersed in both domesticity and show business but wryly aware of their follies. These are smart songs, and they cannot convincingly be sung by dumb people. Shermans setting of the Mexican Hat Dance, tongue-twisting and devilishly rhymed, is here murderously entrusted to the hero's monstrous shopping fiend of a mother-in-law, on the pretext that she has just returned from a holiday south of the border. (A built-in hazard of this kind of show, for those who know the catalogue that's being plundered, is that you can see the songs coming from some way off. Sometimes you see them coming but they fail to arrive, leaving you to feel haunted by the ghosts of numbers that perished on the road.) This show came before Mamma Mia!, which uses the same cut- and-paste technique, but it hurts more here because Shermans songs, unlike Abbas, aren't junk.

There are a few occasions on which the numbers and the narrative actually match, and they feel like blessings. Grow, Mrs. Goldfarb (think glow, little glow-worm) was written in character as a husband's admiring commentary on the eating habits of his spectacularly overweight wife, and thats how its done here, with gentle precision, in contrast to the many numbers in which the staging, the acting and the sound system appear to be in a conspiracy to upstage the material. Also good is Crazy Downtown, in which a mature Barry and Sarah lie awake in the suburbs waiting for their teenage daughter to get home. The Ballad of Harry Lewis (who was "tramping through the warehouse where the drapes of Roth were stored") is a rousing closer, if you can accept a finale tribute to a character we haven't met before.

But other songs die on the vine. When Sherman rewrote Shine On, Harvest Moon as Shine On, Harvey Bloom, an ode to an astronaut, all the fun was in the pun. He might just as well have stopped after the first couple of lines (as he memorably did in such squibs as hello, young lovers, you're under arrest). Turning Mr. Bloom senior into Barrys employer, just so he can be dragged on stage to sing the whole song to nobody in particular, is an act of desperation.

It isnt the only one. The tone of the script is set early on when Barry and Sarah go to school. Their teacher refers to that great book The Canterbury Tales "by Cantor Barry." Apart from being a lousy philistine joke, this plunges us into a world of, to be polite, fantasy that has nothing to do with Sherman, who got his laughs from exaggerating reality. It also suggests that, contrary to all those rye bread ads, you do have to be Jewish.

Actually, I have nothing against ethnically stereotyped humour, as long as it's funny. The authors do come up with a few neat lines, but much of the show is stifling, especially a grossly protracted wedding scene. (The first-night audience lapped it up, but I don't think that does them any credit.) If this is meant to be the story of Barrys life then surely, somewhere in the course of it, he must have met at least one Gentile. Ghetto comedy loses its edge when the characters are no longer living in a ghetto.

Barry himself is given a charmingly tousled performance by Darren Baker, and Sandy Winsby offers an object lesson in the virtues of dry understatement. (He plays an accountant, and looks like one.) Everybody else tries far too hard; Jimmy Spadola, playing a relative whom even his relatives find irritating, almost makes a virtue out of shamelessness, but not quite.

Probably the best part of the show is a curtain-call medley, in which the performers step out of character and simply sock the numbers across. This is invigorating, and allows you to appreciate the songwriters gusto and craftsmanship, as well as his flair for attaching himself to catchy tunes. Charles Strouse was right: Sherman was a first-rate lyricist, born to write musicals, and it was Broadway's loss as well as his own that, after the fig leaves had fallen, he never got another chance.
