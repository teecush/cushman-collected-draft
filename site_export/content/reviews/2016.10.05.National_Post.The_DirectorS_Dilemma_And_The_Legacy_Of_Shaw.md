You can be replaced."

Those words were spoken this year to George Bernard Shaw, on one of the stages at the Niagara theatre festival that bears his name. To be precise, and to be obvious, they were spoken to an actor impersonating him. GBS was drafted as a character in a play based on, or at any rate inspired by, his short prose parable The Adventures of the Black Girl in Her Search for God. And if Shaws shade was looking down from wherever it is freethinking playwrights spend eternity he may well have reflected, ruefully, that his dethronement seemed all too likely.

For at the only theatre in the world ostensibly dedicated to him, Shaw's plays have recently been conspicuous by - well, not their absence exactly but their modesty. Or the festival's modesty on their behalf. This season, only two plays have earned him any kind of authors credit. The Black Girl was a highly-praised lunchtime show at the small Court House theatre; Mrs. Warrens Profession, the year's one actual Shaw play, was presented at the slightly larger Royal George.

These last few seasons, Shaw has indeed looked like being replaced by his contemporary and fellow Irish expatriate Oscar Wilde. Wilde's Lady Windermeres Fan had a memorable production on the main Festival stage a couple of years ago, and this year the same big space has housed his A Woman of No Importance.

Wilde duly features on the bill for the 2017 season, the first under the festivals new artistic director Tim Carroll. But now it's his turn to be the subject of an adaptation and to occupy the lunchtime slot. Under the title Stories for Children, four of his tales have been dramatized by actress-playwright Kate Hennig, who achieves a rare double. She has a new play going on at the Stratford festival as well. Meanwhile, Shaw himself is making a comeback. Two of his plays are being presented, both directed by Carroll himself who has never directed a Shaw play before, and one of them in the big theatre.

This is Saint Joan, which Carroll, who made his reputation in Shakespeare, was drawn to by its "poetry and lyricism ... its shamelessly ecstatic language. The play presents an unconventional portrait of a saint, a Catholic martyred by other Catholics, and one who in Shaw's dispensation often sounds like an inspired Irish peasant. The play used to be thought of as Shaws masterpiece, perhaps because its subject made it a suitable choice for schools. More recently, it has slipped in esteem. In London, the National Theatre recently staged a production: rather grudgingly, it seemed, because the entire Shaw canon, once a prime source of both subsidized and commercial revivals, had fallen out of favour.

Saint Joan of course is a history play. So, in its own peculiar way, is Carrolls other choice, Androcles and the Lion, which takes the old legend of the man who won a lion's undying gratitude by removing a thorn from its paw and transports it to ancient Rome. The hero becomes an early Christian about to be thrown to the lions himself. There are no prizes for guessing how the story ends. But on the way the play has many scintillating things to say about religion: more, I think, than Saint Joan. It's always been one of my favourite Shaw plays (it was the first I ever read) and this will be only its second production at the festival. Carroll himself commends its open-endedness ... the questions it raises about faith and power ... it conducts a two-way conversation with the audience." Theres a lot in the 2017 lineup that should intrigue: some plays, like Androcles, that I have always thought the Shaw should do, others that I could kick myself for never having thought of. High among the latter is the seasons big musical Me and My Girl, a big London hit in 1937 and an even bigger one in 1984 when it was revived and extensively re-worked by Stephen Fry before he was famous. With its story of English toffs infiltrated and invigorated by lovable cockneys, it should play right to the festival company's accumulated strengths in both high and low comedy.

There's a prime piece of Canadian theatrical history about a prime piece of Canadian political history - 1837: The Farmers Revolt - its authorship jointly credited to Rick Salutin and Theatre Passe Muraille, dating from that theatres glory days. Salutin was known as a playwright before he was known as a journalist, and the play is generally cited as a key text in the flowering of theatrical nationalism. Carroll approached it warily, thinking it might reflect "the insecurity of Canadian theatre but found it to have none of that - it feels happy and ebullient. The audience is opened up by comedy and then punched in the gut. It's like Middletown."

Carroll is referencing another play on next season's bill: a response by the American playwright Will Eno to his own countrys classic Our Town, so a kind of sequel to this years Shaw production of the Thornton Wilder play. From closer to our own time and place, there's a new play about Joe Clark by Michael Healey, who's already done Stephen Harper.

Then theres Alan Bennetts The Madness of George III, about the English king who's currently enjoying great theatrical celebrity, courtesy of Hamilton in which he has the funniest numbers. Bennett got there first, however, with a play that was a success in London, New York and on film, but unstaged in Canada.

The Shaw's strength, like Stratfords, has always been its comparative permanence; and Shaw, which prints a complete list of its ensemble in every program, may even have the edge here. Certainly both as companies exceed anything to be found in the U.S. or even in the U.K., where the National hasnt had one since the end of Laurence Olivier's directorate 40 years ago.

You can definitely tell the difference. There's a twoweek training session at the beginning of each Shaw season, known as the Mandate Intensive, a chance for experienced actors to pass on their accumulated skills to new ones. Beyond that there are, says Carroll, "certain actors who are fitted by nature for this kind of work, able to pull off the level of wit in Shaw or Wilde or Stoppard."
