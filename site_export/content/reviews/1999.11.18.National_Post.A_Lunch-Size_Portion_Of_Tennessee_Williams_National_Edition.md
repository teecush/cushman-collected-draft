A PERFECT ANALYSIS GIVEN BY A PARROT

Solar Stage Lunchtime Theatre, Toronto

Two mature Southern ladies, of dubious but carefully-preserved gentility, sit in a St.Louis bar, nursing their beers and wondering where their youths have gone. They also wonder where their middle- aged guys have gone; they are supposedly here with a couple of good ol' boys from one of the lesser-known fraternities. The Memphis Sons of Mars are in town for their annual convention, and these two hopeful daughters of Venus are, in every sense, hanging on.

It sounds like the archetypal set-up for a Tennessee Williams play, and that in fact is what it is; except that this piece only lasts half-an-hour, so the set-up is the play, a one-act vignette that Williams gave the world in 1958. It harks back to one of his best earlier dramas The Rose Tattoo, whose minor characters included an eager pair of harpy-groupies, given, like these, to saluting their beaux with a rousing chorus of Mademoiselle from Armentieres. This plays Flora and Bessie are the same women given a little time to rest their feet, contemplate their troubles, and delicately needle one another.

"Complacency is one thing," says Flora, the trimmer of the two when her friend reflects on the attractiveness of her own spreading figure, optimism another.

Williams is in fine verbal form in this dialogue, beginning with the girls displeasure at the seediness of their surroundings: "Waiter," they say, commenting on the room's lack of the entertainment promised at the door, this place is just a tissue of lies.

With that settled, they fall to recalling the more glamourous conventions of 20 or 30 years earlier, to lamenting their late husbands (granted one line apiece), to extolling or denigrating the restorative effects of golf and hair-styles, and to discreet boasts or disclaimers concerning their respective sexual availability. "Nobody's got any pride where men are concerned," says Bessie, who claims to be the more obliging of the two. Thats how it is, so lets face it.

Flora tells of having her character read by a parrot; you pay its owner a dime, and the bird brings you a piece of paper in its beak. Flora's particular fortune cookie reads "you have a sensitive nature and are frequently misunderstood by your close companions" which is archetypal Williams, both in its quivering pain and in the robust way that it burlesques that pain. (Williams himself read the role of Flora, to Maureen Stapleton's Bessie, at the premiere 40 years ago, and the play seems as likely as any of his others to be a coded gay lament.) Bessie laughs at this, thereby proving its truth, and Flora does the same thing back.

There isnt much else to the play, but you end up with a very good sense of these womens past and present, and can shudder sympathetically at their likely future.

There is probably more bite and shape to it than appears in Bernadette Jones' production, which plonks two distinguished actresses down at a table and lets them get on with it. Heaven forbid that I should appear ungallant towards Southern womanhood, but Williams describes his characters as "in the late afternoon of their youth" and these ladies seem to have advanced well past the happy hour. This, strangely enough, makes the characters less rather than more disturbing; instead of a desperate clinging to something only just out of reach, we get a good-humoured remembrance of times thoroughly past. They are a ringing pair of Dixie belles, played without grotesquerie. Charmion King as Bessie could actually afford to be blowsier than she is. Barbara Chilcott as Flora wields a sharp and fancy verbal stiletto

By way of prologue we get a glimpse of the two missing Martians whooping it up in another hostelry. Williams didn't write this, and the dialogue isnt up to his level; but its sufficient, and the men, heartily embodied by A. Frank Ruffo and David Dunbar, spend most of the time singing and pounding the piano anyway. (Baby Face is their ballad of choice.) Then, at the very end of the play proper, they reappear, to sweep the girls off for a good time.

I assumed that this was a director's interpolation, and a bad one; Bessie and Flora seemed all set for a defiant prowl of the city's best and second-best hotels, perspiring beneath the paint. It turns out that it's in the script, but I think Williams meant the encounter to seem more menacing in its automatic gaiety than it does in the rushed finale we get here. The characters may think they have a happy ending, but the production doesn't have to indulge them.
