Hannah Moscovitch's In This World begins with a fight and ends with an embrace. It's a two-character play, both of whose characters are teenagers. Bijou lives in Rosedale, Neyssa lives at Jane and Finch. The play is very Torontospecific, though it was commissioned and produced by a Montreal company, Youtheatre, which has done strikingly well by it.

For most of the action, the two girls sit on opposite sides of the stage, each perched on a chair at the top of a dauntingly high ramp. There are traces of red on the slopes between them. They're high schoolers and they're in detention, waiting for a teacher to come and sort out the brawl that we witnessed at the start. Neyssa hit Bijou in the face, drawing blood. Neyssa is black and Bijou white, and it's a few minutes before we can tell whether this is just a fact of casting, or a factor in the play.

It turns out to be the latter. Race figures, and so does class. The school is in Bijou's very rich neighbourhood; Neyssa's grades have got her there. The fight was no random thing. The girls, it's gradually revealed, were friends, maybe best friends. Bijou is dating Neyssa's cousin; Neyssa has got involved at a party with an ex-boyfriend of Bijou's. Their relationship has combusted, across lines of class, race and morality. Neyssa calls Bijou a slut, saying that no Jamaican girl would behave the way she does. Bijou comes close to calling Neyssa something else. The intake of breath from the teenage audience with whom I saw the show was far sharper over the racial epithet than the sexual one. It's rather brave of Moscovitch's play to have almost used it.

The issue at the heart of the play is date rape. But it's another tribute to the writing that, however it may actually have been planned, the characters seem to have spawned the subject, rather than the other way about. (The title's rather vague, though.) The actresses, in a post-performance Q&A, paid tribute to the "tightness" of the writing. This isn't absolutely true in terms of narrative development -- there is a certain amount of repetition, even in a brief space -- but the dialogue is certainly tight to the characters. It's tense, and it's believable. Moscovitch doesn't ventriloquize; she seems to find an authentic voice for everybody she writes about. That, on the face of it, is what one would expect of any playwright, but I can't think of any other current Canadian who does it so consistently.

The play's lay-out is schematic, maybe excessively so. Bijou lives in a house with seven bathrooms, and isn't even the richest kid on the block. Her mother's a lawyer, her father's constantly away on business and communicates with his daughter mainly through hurried phone calls from airports. Neyssa's father is abusive, though not towards her; her mother is enormously proud of her having made it to a posh school. (Which made me wonder how Neyssa could contemplate, as she seriously does, the prospect of being expelled; it would surely break her mother's heart, but the question is never addressed, though Bijou, at least, has every opportunity and every reason to do the addressing.) The contrasts are hammered home, but they also strike home. Quarrels are probably easier to write than reconciliations, but when the two begin to draw closer together, they manges to avoid becoming mushy or programmatic.

Their movements towards or away from one another are given bold, literal illustration in the staging. Michel Lefebvre's direction, Veronique Bertrand's design and Renaud Pettigrew's lighting are a perfect match for the play; they provide a stark, stylized frame and fill it with fruitful detail. Both Sharon James' Neyssa and Hannah Cheesman's Bijou suffer from a certain young-actorish self-righteousness; James doesn't find much variation in her character's justified anger and Cheeseman lays on the defensive priggishness a bit thick. But these are both strong, truthful performances that connect with the text and with one another. I've seen three productions in this year's LKTYP lineup -- The Princess and the Handmaiden, Hana's Suitcase (which I caught on its initial run, a few seasons ago) and this one--and they've all been first-rate.
