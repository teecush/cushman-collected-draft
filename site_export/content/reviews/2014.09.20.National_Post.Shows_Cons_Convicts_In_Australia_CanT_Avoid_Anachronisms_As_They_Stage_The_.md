George Farquhar, last of Englands Restoration playwrights, is having a bumper colonial year. His final play, The Beaux Stratagem, is a hit at Stratford. The one that came before it, The Recruiting Officer, was, 200 years ago and counting, the first play ever to be presented in Australia. And that production, with a cast composed of transported convicts, is itself the subject of Our Country's Good, by the contemporary British dramatist Timberlake Wertenbaker, now touching down in Toronto for the second time.

The first time was some 20 years ago, when we were visited by the original London production, directed by Max Stafford-Clark, one of Britain's best, and mounted by his company Out of Joint. The same director and company are responsible for the new production, adding another referential layer to a play that already comes loaded with them.

Wertenbakers play (itself based on a novel by Thomas Keneally, The Playmaker) is a confessedly free interpretation of an actual event. The convict production of The Recruiting Officer really did happen, in 1789, and presumably was part of the comparatively liberal governing policy of Arthur Phillip, Royal Navy captain and first governor of New South Wales. Ralph Clark, the young lieutenant whom Phillip puts in charge of the production, certainly existed but theres no evidence that he plays the part assigned to him here. It is in fact the leading part; Nathan Ives-Moiba, who gives a highly sympathetic performance of Ralph, is the only actor in a cast of 10 who isn't required to play two or more roles. He also, though the point isn't stressed, has the leading part in the play-within-theplay; when the prisoner cast as Captain Plume, the recruiting officer himself, fails to turn up for the first rehearsal, Ralph reads in for him, and hes still playing the role when the show goes up. Hes fallen in love with the theatre. He has also, despite having a muchmissed wife back in England, fallen for his leading lady and she for him. Altogether, Ralph is shouldering quite a burden for a first-time director.

"Director" is what everybody calls him, even though the term was not to come into general use in the British theatre until well into the 20th century. The point may seem pedantic, but in fact it's indicative of a flaw that runs right through the play. Wertenbaker's characters are continually saying and doing whats convenient for their author, rather than what rings organically true. Its hard to find the right tone for a modern play in a period setting. This script does include one speech made up of authentic 18thcentury thieves' slang; it's rich and fruity but largely incomprehensible. Obviously you cant write a whole play like that. But there doesnt seem much point, either, in being obviously anachronistic. Or rather there is a point, or several, but they feel imposed.

The naval officers running the colony are presented frankly as types. You have your enlightened governor, continually quoting classical philosophy; your chaplain hoping that the proposed play won't condone immorality (he can't have read much Restoration comedy); your sadistic and sarcastic disciplinarians who think flogging and hanging to be far more effective therapy than drama and who take delight in interrupting rehearsals and humiliating the actors. Those convict actors also come off as types; the play works hard to make them individuals, but it only succeeds in flashes. When we feel for them, its as a group, reprieved from hanging in England for mainly petty crimes, only to be consigned to brutal and effectively permanent exile. The women are given the most time, all but one of them proudly hard cases who, initially at least, treat the amateur dramatics with a mix of incredulity and resentment. Why, asks one cast as a ladys maid, can she not have the fine speeches the lady herself has? It's a good 21st century question, but in the 18th it comes off as the character saying what her author politically thinks she should have said, rather than what she would. In fact Wertenbaker's play neglects the politics that are in Farquhars. The Recruiting Officer is a play whose amorous intrigues are set against scenes of poor men being tricked into enlisting or effectively press-ganged by heartless justices. Those episodes would surely have resonated with a cast (and audience) sent similarly overseas. But we hear nothing of them. What we see rehearsed are fragments of love-scenes that must surely baffle anyone who doesnt know the original play. On its previous visit here, Our Country's Good was given in rep with The Recruiting Officer, but that enjoyable homework isn't available this time.

What does work, very well, is the sense of all the imprisoned actors, against terrible odds, getting caught up in the process of putting on a show, and us getting caught with them. Stafford-Clarks production, cunningly designed by Tim Shortall and beautifully lit by Johanna Town, gives us a stage upon a stage; the last scene, done with infectious urgency, ends with curtain going up on the convicts first, and only, and apparently triumphant performance. At the curtain-call we hardly know whom we're applauding, the real actors or the pretend ones. It's a good feeling. But Im not sure that the-showmust-go-on feelings are much different from those generated by 42nd Street or, to raise the cultural bar presumably higher, Shakespeare in Love. And, immediately effective though it is, the abrupt ending leaves a lot of questions hanging: about the fates of individual characters and of the entire colony, which weve been told is facing starvation.

The actors seem rather tentative when playing the officers, not least when trying to be bold. They're much better as the rehearsing convicts, though - miked in the plush confines of the Royal Alex - they still often sound loud but muffled. When Ralph tells one of his players not to shout, I wished the whole cast would heed his instruction. There's a recurring note of over-insistence, but there are fine moments from Jessica Tomchak, Kathryn OReilly, Simon Darwen (a petty thief with literary ambitions and even talents), David Newman (a stage-struck pickpocket) and Richard Neale (a hangman, to escape being hanged). Sam Graham plays a guilt-ridden, lovelorn, guilt-tormented, middle-aged midshipman, very strong and clear when establishing himself, less so when required to go to pieces and have hallucinations, in scenes that sound written by numbers. Hes meant, I think, to be a bridge between the play's two worlds, but it's a bridge that doesnt hold.

Our Countrys Good runs through Oct. 26 at the Royal Alexandra.
