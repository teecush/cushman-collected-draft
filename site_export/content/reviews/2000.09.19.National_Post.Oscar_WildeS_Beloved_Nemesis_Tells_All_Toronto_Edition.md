OSCAR REMEMBERED

Tom Patterson Theatre, Stratford

The story of Oscar Wilde -- his fame and his folly, his imprisonment, and his endurance while in durance -- has been told on many occasions, several of them forming part of the Wilde Celebration at this years Stratford Festival. The familiar material gets another going-over in Oscar Remembered, the twist being that we get to hear it through the mouth of Wildes beloved nemesis, Lord Alfred Douglas: Bosie to his intimates, and to posterity.

The late Canadian actor and playwright Maxim Mazumdar put this piece together, and he performed it at Stratford some 20 years ago. His problem is the same as that which afflicted the American writers who once had the bright idea of making the Dauphin the protagonist of a musical featuring Joan of Arc. The audience's attention keeps straying back to the story's real hero and -- in both cases -- martyr.

Its aggravated here by the fact that Mazumdar uses the characters original words, and those words are far likelier to be Wilde's than Douglas's; more of Wildes have been preserved -- whether as letters or repeated anecdotes or formal literary offerings -- and they are certainly more memorable. They are funnier and they are more passionate.

Michael Therriault, the young actor who does the show now, is required among other things to read the eloquently bitter letter that Wilde addressed to Douglas from Reading Gaol. He needs to convince us that he has just got it in the mail. So he has to deliver it tentatively, as if it were all new to him, while also signalling his own shock-horror reactions as recipient. This is on top of conveying to us the sense and feeling of the letter itself, which is written in Wildes most fluent oratorical manner. (There is pain in the substance, but enjoyment in the style.) This amounts to a double, if not a triple, acting problem, and it is no wonder that Therriault fails to solve it.

You may add to this that Bosie, even when allowed to state his own case, remains a stubbornly unlikeable apostle: Judas crossed with Peter in his denying phase, and with a vein of self-pity and self-dramatization all his own. Pretty much by his own admission, he used his relationship with Wilde as a means of getting back at his father, the monstrous Marquess of Queensberry, and he eventually sacrificed Wilde to this vendetta. You feel that even Queensberry, who at least had the courage of his own prejudices, deserved better than this son, sniggering at him from a distance, and eventually fleeing the country while his lover stood the trial into which Douglas himself had goaded him.

He also seems, on a smaller scale, to have thrown a nasty fit when Wilde dedicated a volume of Salome -- the overripe Biblical drama that he wrote in French and that Douglas translated -- to somebody else. (Strangely the play itself -- the partners' one joint effort -- is not quoted in Mazumdar's text.)

In Bosies defence: He had to stand around at parties and listen to Oscar getting all the laughs. This cant have been fun, however much he may have professed his admiration for his friend's wit. (Did he ever remark that he wished he'd said that, and did Wilde ever reply "You will, Bosie, you will?" Well, now Bosie does.) He apparently helped provoke that wit to its finest flowering by suggesting that Wilde knock off a comedy about cucumber sandwiches, thereby earning the gratitude of playgoers and tea drinkers everywhere. (Cucumber sandwiches are a recurring motif in this piece.) He visited his imprisoned idol in Holloway, under atrocious conditions, if not in Reading.

Perhaps most telling of all, when the ordeal was over (and the tale remains appalling, however often you hear it), Wilde resumed their relationship. This, I think, is less a tribute to the power of love than an acknowledgment of human frailty and need, but it was Wildes own choice, and it should be respected.

At this last stage, Douglas didnt let him down. The play's most poignant remark is to the effect that Wilde could no longer write because one cannot write about life; one can only live it, which is what he had now been forced to do, in spades. That may not be a universal truth, but it applies here. Unfortunately it is, again, Wilde's perception, not Douglass; Bosie seems fated to be the permanent understudy.

So here he is again, soaking up somebody elses limelight; so, at a further remove, is the author of this piece; and so, at yet a fourth, is whoever plays it now. At this point, however, things begin to even out. I have to confess my own prejudice here; I once saw Mazumdar, in another of his own shows, and I thought him a wholly derivative writer and a thinly narcissistic performer.

Therriault is a far more talented actor, one of Stratford's genuine discoveries; he is currently excellent in Fiddler on the Roof as -- all together now -- the tailor Motel Kamzoil; and last year he was an impressive Ariel to the Prospero of William Hutt, who coincidentally directs him here. (The year before that he was unbearable as Mordred in Camelot, but it's an unbearable role.)

As Bosie he seems to be talking partly to an invisible Wilde, represented by an empty armchair, with a green carnation on a side- table; partly to us; and principally to his mirror. The production isnt very rigorous about these distinctions, but theres probably no way it could be.

Therriault delivers intelligently, moment by moment, and cumulatively suggests an overbred puppy dog with a grievance, one whose bite is worse than his whimper. Almost the last thing he gives us is a rendition -- truncated but still substantial -- of Wilde's last work, The Ballad of Reading Gaol; he gets very actorish here, fastening in a self-accusing manner on the famous repeated lines about each man killing the thing he loves, and elsewhere painting on pathos while neglecting the verse form. (Or, in layman's terms, he keeps emphasizing the wrong words.)

This may be a subtle comment on Bosie's incorrigible insensitivity, but somehow I doubt it. It also shows the evening decisively up for what it is; an unsubtle pretext for regurgitating, yet again, Oscar Wilde's Greatest Hits.
