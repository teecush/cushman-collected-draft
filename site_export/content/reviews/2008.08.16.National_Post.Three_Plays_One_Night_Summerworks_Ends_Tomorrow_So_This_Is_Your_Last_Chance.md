Three shows from Summer-Works, two of them outstanding in their own right and also, as it happens, internally linked.

Erin Shields If We Were Birds (Theatre Passe Muraille Mainspace) reinvigorates a dramatic tradition that goes back to the Elizabethans. Shakespeare and Company often drew on the classical myths narrated by Ovid, sauciest of the Latin poets, in his Metamorphoses; indeed the Chairman of the Board drew on the same legend as Shields when writing the blood-bolstered Titus Andronicus. The story here is that of the rape of Philomela, a princess ravished by her brother-in-law, Tereus, who cut her tongue out to stop her from telling. Procne, Tereus' wife and Philomel's sister, nevertheless found out about it, and took a revenge ghastly even by mythological standards. (Shakespeares Titus, whose daughter had been subjected to similar treatment, swore to outdo Procne in vengeance, but even he couldnt quite bring that off. As an avenger, Procne is Titus plus Medea. Let's call it a tie.)

Erin Shields' re-telling of Ovids re-telling brings in an appropriately tragic chorus: five women from our own times who -- from Nanking 1937 to Bosnia 1995 -- have been subjected to other, thoroughly documented violations. This could seem like a crude bit of nose-rubbing relevance but, in fact, it works magnificently, due to the authors mastery of tone and the ease with which she can slide from one to another, a quality beautifully reinforced in Alan Dilworth's production. We have the girl-talk, pubescent and post, of the sisters sharing a bath, a scene written and staged with delicious tact. We have the urbane realpolitik of their sisters' sly father King Pandion, bumbling but ruthless (and played by David Fox in a manner not too far removed from his Donald Rumsfeld in Stuff Happens), bestowing the hand of his acquiescent elder daughter as payment to a military ally. We have domestic comedy from the domestics. And we have the stark narratives of those same servants as they tell how, and how brutally, they came to be enslaved, in a powerful mix of solo and unison speech. This is what the chorus in Stratfords Trojan Women should have been like -- though its true that Euripides offers less immediately grabbing material. Shields also supplies a binding metaphor: Like the classical Philomela, who was turned into a nightingale, these modern rape-victims become birds.

The sisters are perfectly cast, with Tara Rosling an inquisitive, trusting and finally heartbreaking Philomela, and Philippa Domville (in her best performance since the Chet Baker play Time after Time) a sensual, sophisticated and finally implacable Procne. Geoffrey Pounsett makes a reasonable stab at Tereus, but seems to be holding the horrors -- both those he inflicts and those he suffers --at arms' length. The production is a terrific event, and Shields is clearly a major talent.

She also acts: not in her own play but in Fewer Emergencies (Factory Studio); this is presented "in association with" Groundwater Productions, who mounted If We Were Birds, though in this case the principal producer is someone or something called Mitch. The vaunted British dramatist Martin Crimp wrote the play. Or maybe the plays: Fewer Emergencies is a three-piece suite, whose components form a pattern, one that's thematic and, up to a point, narrative. But the stories are told scrambled; theyre recalled in fact by a central female figure, prompted and/or interrupted by two -- or, in the middle section, three -- male interlocutors. They pull her up on nearly everything she says. (Did she mean this? Are these the right words? Is she serious?) Crimp may be the most piercingly precise stylist in the British theatre since Pinter -- Martin McDonagh, being Irish, doesnt count -- and he has put his own talent on trial here. In Brendan Healy's production, the actors sit on stools and speak through microphones; as in some nightmare talk-show. All the exchanges are precisely calibrated, everything brittle but impeccably conversational, nothing forced. Shields, who bears the brunt, proves as much a virtuoso at speaking another 's words as at writing her own. She plays a woman anatomizing a marriage -- we assume her own, though everythings third-person -- that she declined to leave, even though it was too perfect to be happy. The middle section involves a school massacre, meticulously described, and here the play does seem to me to stray into sensationalism, the author showing off how tough he is, in a way that If We Were Birds never does. Or maybe its just that such an event is literally closer to home than carnage in Africa or Central Europe, however contemporary. The men are Andrew Pifko, Ken MacKenzie (who's also the designer, a very rare double), and, briefly, Vahid Rahbani. Among the accomplishments required of the cast, and duly delivered, is the ability to harmonize in scat like the Swingle Singers. These two shows might make a fine, if searing, double bill.

Another multi-tasker, Claire Calnan, functions in Raising Luke (Passe Muraille Mainspace) as author, director and -- on the night I went --last-minute actress. The play, set in a convent, may need a Catholic audience, lapsed or current. The crises of conscience and vocation it presents seem specialized, and in a huddled and muffled production, remote. Nor does the staging make much sense of a convoluted time-scheme. Martha (well played by Maja Ardal) switches between being an ill-adjusted novice and being the mother of a troubled son (the titular Luke) with a highly guessable secret. Calnan herself played a more outspokenly sceptical nun who yet manages to survive within the order; even with a script in her hand, she was excellent.
