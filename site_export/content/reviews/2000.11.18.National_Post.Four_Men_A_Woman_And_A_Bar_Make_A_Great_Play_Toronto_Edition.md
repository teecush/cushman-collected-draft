THE WEIR

Bluma Appel Theatre

Conor McPhersons The Weir, like many other Irish plays, takes place in a bar. In this case its a run-down pub -- a snuggery that looks more like somebody's dilapidated living room than a place of public entertainment -- in what we gather is an equally run-down village in the west of the country.

It is almost a private club, and the members get through a lot of beer -- bottled, to their disgust, since the tap is out of order.

But if the setting is conventional, the dramatic structure is not; at first, indeed, the play does not seem to have one. It unfolds principally as a sequence of stories told by the characters, and only later do we realize how craftily this succession has been graded.

There are five people onstage: the barman, a couple of regulars, an occasional and a newcomer. They tell these stories as monologues, as ways of showing off, getting comfortable, or just getting acquainted. The first tale we hear is a simple, and rather fragile, fairy tale, only mildly threatening, whose only immediate relevance is that it is set in the local community; it is in no way autobiographical.

The second is scarier, and though it doesn't relate directly to the speaker, it does concern people he and his friends know.

The third, which concerns a graveyard, comes straight out of the experience of the person who relates it and is disturbing; the only escape-clause is that the events apparently happened when the protagonist was running a high temperature, so he may have been hallucinating.

The men who spin these yarns are Jack, a garage-owning bachelor in his 50s; Jim, who works for Jack and gives him good racing tips, and who is somewhat younger and lives with his mother; and Finbar, who is married and prosperous and runs a hotel in the nearest town. He is making a rare visit, in the company of Valerie, a woman in her 30s recently arrived from Dublin, and the others suspect that he is making a play for her and that his intention is not so much to show her around as to show her off. They deprecate such behaviour, but that doesnt stop Jack, who has advance notice of Valeries arrival, from putting on a suit for maybe the first time in years.

Valerie, who seems shy but pleased by all this attention, also has a story to tell, and it's her own. It is about bereavement, and it is understated, riveting and deeply painful.

This is the second time The Weir has played in Toronto -- the original London production came to a WorldStage season -- and on neither occasion have I been able to look at the stage as Valerie's story reached its climax. There are elements of the paranormal here, too, but they are dwarfed by factual human tragedy. The mood in the pub naturally changes; whatever amorous designs the men may have entertained melt away, and their only concern is to make fumbling verbal gestures of comfort.

There is yet a fifth story, and Ill get to it. First, though, I must salute the cunning sympathy of the writing, the ease and economy with which life inside and outside the bar are brought before us; and the vitality of the language, which has the richness and muscle we expect of Irish drama but not the quaintness or the excess. The literate fluency is traditional, but the time is very much now.

The setting (by Francis OConnor) is from the bar at one side to the fireplace at the other, by way of the rain glimpsed through the dank window.

The acting, under Jackie Maxwell's direction, doesn't match the practised ensemble ease that we saw in the authentic Irish cast at Harbourfront, but it is still fine, warm, touching and funny. Barry MacGregor, an actor to whom bluffness comes natural, supplies a broad outline for Jack and then fills in the mans loneliness with a touchingly clumsy delicacy. Jim (generally referred to, even in his presence, as "the Jimmy fella") is Robert Persichini, who has a deliberate delivery, a slow-burn timing and a solid grace that are all his own.

John Jarviss Finbar, hands jangling in his pockets, shifting from one leg to the other as he wonders how and whether to play the seducer, is the picture of saloon-bar sophistication returned uneasily to its public-bar roots. Ann Baggley nicely sketches in Valerie's initial uncertainties, but gives a rather shallow account of her big speech, full of carefully planted pauses; it can hardly help being affecting, though.

Oliver Becker completes the quintet as Brendan the barman, quietly and humorously solicitous, still fairly young, and the only one who doesn't tell a story. Hes the audience; ultimately, it is for his benefit -- or so it increasingly seems to me -- that the stories are being told. He is the only one of the men with a chance of changing his life.

The last story is an encore for MacGregor as Jack, and it is soberly realistic. (The adverb is appropriate, despite the amount he and the others have put away.) He tells of his own foolish selfishness regarding his girlfriend of 30 years ago and how she slipped away from him when she moved to the city, of how he attended her wedding, of how he made a fool of himself at the service, of how he was touched by an act of kindness from a stranger, and how he still wakes every morning with, to put it less gracefully than he does, the memory of that girl in the room. The weir of the title is a local landmark, only once referred to, but it must be an image for the habits of a lifetime that can drown us all. Jacks story ends the progression by shaking off the supernatural. The moral seems to be that fairy tales are fine, but clap hands if you believe in people.

Apologies to Mairtin O'Carrigan, an authentic Irish actor whose performance in Jitters (as another authentic Irish actor) was misattributed in last week's review.
