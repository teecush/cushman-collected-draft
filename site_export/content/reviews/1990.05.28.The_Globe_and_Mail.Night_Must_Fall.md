NIGHT MUST FALL

Monday, May 28, 1990

ROBERT CUSHMAN

NIAGARA-ON-THE-LAKE, Ont. -- By Emlyn Williams Directed by Randy Maertz Starring Peter Krantz, Sharry Flett and Jennifer Phipps Murder a natural theatrical thrill BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail NIAGARA-ON-THE-LAKE, Ont. Heralded by a rather alarming advertising campaign (the irresistible charm of the smiling psychopath - where else but the Shaw Festival?) Emlyn Williams's Night Must Fall duly descended on Niagaras Royal George Theatre Friday afternoon.

Director Randy Maertzs program note asks questions too: "What is the morbid fascination we have with murderers?" Well, part of the attraction for Williams the playwright back in 1934 was the chance of writing a smashing part for Williams the actor. As for the rest of us, the answer is obvious: murder is the extreme act. It affects us that way, just as citizens. As playgoers, the theatre being above all the place for extremes, it positively attracts us. We don't condone it, but we want to understand it. Some of the best playwrights have catered to our prurience. Shakespeare's most compelling psychological study is Macbeth.

Williamss protagonist, a Welsh boy called Danny, is the runner-up among English dramas Celtic killers. A page boy in a hotel near London, he has before the play begins disposed of most of an importunate lady guest at the bottom of someone's garden, reserving only her head, which he keeps in a hat box. This is not to give anything away; it is obvious by the end of the first act that Danny is guilty. The suspense lies not in discovering who done it, but in anticipating who is going to get done next.

As a sideline, our boy has impregnated a parlormaid and he now insinuates himself into the household and the affections of the girl's employer, a cantankerous and hypochondriacal widow. As everybody else sees through her, she naturally warms to Dan, who plies her with sympathy and chocolates. The one person he apparently cannot charm is the old ladys niece and companion; and she, it turns out, is the most deeply affected of all. She suspects him from the outset and is hostile. When suspicion turns to certainty she is captivated.

A police inspector drily remarks that this is only the beginning; when Danny stands trial he will be deluged with proposals of marriage. This seems to please the lad no end. The point Williams insistently makes is that murderers crave the attention we so freely bestow on them. Its a mutual contract. This may not hold true for all cases, but for a psychopath like Danny it seems plausible. Certainly it carries dramatic conviction. The sexual bond comes out very clearly in this production, with an uncompromising Sharry Flett who finally melts with alarming abandon.

One presumably unintentional symptom of stress is that she loses her English accent - rather a rare fault at the Shaw Festival - and while Peter Krantz never forfeits the Welsh lilt that Williams, for reasons of theatrical charm as well as native convenience, wished on Danny, he does sometimes seem oppressed by it, as if the voice were playing him. He still gets across all the salient points: orphaned resentment, spiralling fantasies of omnipotence, the total confidence of the charismatic dissolving into black terror when things misfire, and by the way, the perfect nannying tactics to disarm his victims.

The relationship takes time to come into focus and until it does, the production has the fidgets. Excellently made though it is, the play is cast in yesterday's mold, the hardest of all to bring to life today. It is a three-act one-set job and the tiny Royal George stage is unkind to it; even by 1930s' standards "the sitting room of Forest Corner" seems hideously cramped and cluttered. Characters stare fixedly at what is presumably meant to be the fourth wall, and sometimes press their noses up against it. Or they stand in straight lines, reacting by numbers.

They also make a lot of noise. Jennifer Phippss chair-bound tyrant is exaggeratedly coarse-grained and the rest of the household must take her tone or perish. She does noticeably improve when Dan has softened her up.

The other characters are stock, vigorously dispensed. Lorna Wilsons Cockney cook leads the field, but Ian D. Clark's stolidly beamish suitor and Helen Taylor's permanently gawping mother-to-be bring up a creditable rear. The play grips. And if it falters you can beguile the time calculating which of the two leading ladies sports the more revoltingly improbable wig.
