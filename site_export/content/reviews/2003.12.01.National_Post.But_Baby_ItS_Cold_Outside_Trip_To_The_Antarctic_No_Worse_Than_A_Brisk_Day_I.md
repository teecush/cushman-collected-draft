SUR

Artword Theatre, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - There is a musical sequence in Sur that sums up the enterprise as well as anything could. It comes a short way into the second half. There is no listing in the program, but the song seems to be called Six Little Sparrows and it's an elimination number, on the order of Ten Green Bottles, in which each verse announces the disappearance of another character. It's performed here by half-a- dozen South American ladies of the early 20th century, who are on their way to discover the South Pole. (I'll get back to that.) Accompanied by music with a vaguely Latin pulse, they all skip to the centre of the stage, converge, clap hands and skip back again. They repeat this routine six times, without variation, and at the end of it every one of them is still standing. There is a complete disjunction between the words and the staging. And so it goes, throughout.

There is also no connection between the genteel perkiness of the song and the toughness demanded of these women to get where they're going; and this too is a constant. Sur (Spanish for south) is adapted and directed by Ronald Weihs, from a short story by the veteran American fabulist Ursula K. Le Guin. It's set in 1909-10, after Captain Scott's first failed expedition to the Antarctic but before his legendarily disastrous second. "If Captain Scott can do it," says one of our heroines, in what the playwright declares to be his favourite line, "then why can't we?" (She chivalrously refrains from mentioning that Scott couldn't do it.) The idea, having once taken root in her mind, spreads from country to country across the South American continent as if by bush telegraph, in a sequence that stands out from the rest of the evening in possessing a certain amount of dramatic reality and even a little wit. Having pried themselves free from protective or disinterested menfolk, our six recruits take ship with a kindly if patronizing captain, who deposits them on the outer shores of Antarctica, trusting they will have a nice holiday and get down to some serious knitting. The sea - voyage through decreasing degrees of latitude is charted in a song, one with the temper of a nursery rhyme and the tempo of a dirge, whose refrain, endlessly repeated, is "there's land to the South, so I've been told." One reason for welcoming this show's intermission is the hope that the performers might use their time out to learn another tune.

Despite the songs, Sur is not a musical; it seems to be using music because, in group theatrical creations drawn from either literary or documentary sources, that is one of the things one does. The show has been in preparation for half a year, and has gone through two stages of workshops: a fact that suggests -- not for the first time -- that good intentions and long rehearsals are no guarantors of authenticity. One thing that "workshopping" (a word we could probably do without) is supposed to achieve is a high standard of physical reality. To put it at its most basic: In this show about polar exploration, you would expect the participants to look, sound or act cold -- even to the extent that we feel it ourselves. The way this lot behave, and the way they're kitted out, evoke nothing more extreme than a brisk day in Southern Ontario.

When two of them actually reach the Pole (the others having stayed at base camp) they do try to summon the appalling climactic conditions, but by then it's too late. They also refuse to leave any markers to show subsequent explorers that they made it. The story's sly conceit is that while it can't be proved that a couple of women succeeded where Scott and Shackleton failed and where Amundsen had yet to venture, it can't be disproved either. This tale and its characters are avowedly fictitious, but who's to say that something like it couldn't have happened?

No one, that's who. But the what-if style of narrative is far easier to sustain on the page, where the author is in full command of the tone, than on the stage, where everything -- certainly on this occasion -- shrieks out that the whole thing is impossible. However much the text may go on about women's endurance and adaptability and about their superior knowledge and skill in matters of diet and housekeeping, nothing persuades us that it could have been done --not by these women with their finishing-school manners, aptly reflected in a school-play standard of acting.

Actually, that's unfair. I have recently seen two high-school shows that, for inventiveness and sophistication, left this one choking in the dust -- or, as I suppose I'm obliged to put it, gasping on the ice.
