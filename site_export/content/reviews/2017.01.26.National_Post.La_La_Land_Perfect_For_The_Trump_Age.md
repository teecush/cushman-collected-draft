So its official. We're all in La La Land. Well, strictly speaking, it's only semi-official. The shiny movie musical may have received a record 14 Oscar nominations, but nominations arent victories. Still, a juggernauts a juggernaut, especially in Hollywood, and nobody is betting against this one. The Golden Globes have already pointed the way. La La Land won there as Best Comedy or Musical, which was fair enough; what else was there? But it also won Best Screenplay, and that's incredible.

It's being hailed, especially in the wake of the American election, as a Film for Our Time. It does indeed have something for both sides. Anti-Trumpers can find solace in its beautiful colours and in its obvious desire to please: the boy, do we need it now syndrome. Pro-Trumpers can point to it as a tangible example of Making America Great Again, if that means, as seems to be the case, taking America back to the 1950s. La La Land is an acknowledged tribute to the Hollywood musicals of that decade. When Ryan Gosling self-consciously fingers a lamppost before going into his dance, were obviously meant to think nostalgically of Singin in the Rain. In the number that follows, it's probably better if we don't.

My own feeling about La La Land is that I liked it best when it wasnt pretending to be a musical. But for most of its length, it is. And in reflecting a national mood, or moods, it is following historical precedent.

There have been two great periods of American musical film. The first was the 1930s: the Age of Consolation. The musical offered two modes of dealing with the Depression. They could soar magically above it all, as in the Fred Astaire-Ginger Rogers pictures, or they could confront it, as in the grittier backstage stories with the vast militarized production numbers, masterminded by Busby Berkeley. 42nd Street is the famous title, but the masterpiece is Gold Diggers of 1933, which begins with the ladies of the chorus singing Were in the Money ("we never see a headline 'bout a breadline today"), a number abruptly terminated when the producer tells them that the show they're rehearsing is cancelled, due to lack of funds.

The finale, Remember My Forgotten Man, is actually about breadlines; its a song depicting destitute ex-soldiers whose march-rhythm and lyric idea borrow shamelessly from the previous years Brother, Can You Spare a Dime, and yet is still very powerful. It's cynical too: the protest-number is presented as part of a show that's going to be a great big hit. "Doesnt it get you?" says the producer. This time, he will be in the money.

Artistically, though not commercially, the musical went into a slump when war came; the pictures were glorified recruiting posters (and the clothes were really ugly). But it revived at the end of the 1940s and into the 50s. This was the Age of Celebration: the musical as a goodnatured reflection of national confidence. It was the period of Singin' in the Rain, The Band Wagon, It's Always Fair Weather. It looked back (the songs were often from previous decades) but through a perspective of wit.

Then came the end of the studio era and with it the end, effectively, of the American film musical. The talent - a staggering amount of it, in all departments - dispersed.

You can feel that loss in La La Land. It isnt just that Gosling and Emma Stone arent Fred or Ginger, or Judy or Gene. They can't even compare to the endless line of second-string players who buoyed up the old musicals, good or bad. There seems no need for them to sing, either as actors or as the characters they're playing.

Both these characters are in fact performers; shes an actress, hes a jazz pianist. Neither of them are presented as singers or dancers. They do it, apparently, just because they're in a musical. Well, Astaire could get away with that. When his numbers weren't presented as stage routines, when they were just part of the story, we believed them because, naturally, he would move like that: he was Astaire. Generally, though, Hollywood, afraid of looking silly in a naturalistic medium like film, liked to have a quasi-realistic excuse for bursting into song: hence the curious fact that so many film musicals are about the theatre. There are few whose characters arent performers; the ones that arent, ironically, are mostly adaptations of stage shows.

La La Land has new songs that try to sound old, but you forget them even as you're hearing them. It wants to get the best of both ages: to celebrate and to satirize at the same time. It falls between any number of stools. To its credit, it seems to realize this. Boy takes an intolerable length of time to get Girl, but after he does, when the relationship starts to fall apart, things get interesting - believable, even. This happens without benefit of song.

We're offered virtually a choice of endings, happy or otherwise, while being left in no doubt as to which is the likelier. To that extent its honest. It isnt quite the uncritical glorification of Los Angeles that it's been taken to be. The title is as much mocking as approving. A musical that isn't a musical, a romance that isn't a romance: something for everyone but not enough for anyone. A divided film at a divisive time. No wonder it's going to clean up.
