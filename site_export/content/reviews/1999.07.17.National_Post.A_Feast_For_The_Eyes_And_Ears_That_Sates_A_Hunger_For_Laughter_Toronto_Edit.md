THE PLAYS THE THING

Soulpepper Theatre Company

Three cigarettes glow in the dark. Then the lights go up on a trio of dinner-jacketed men in what appears to be a drawing-room, one sparsely, but elegantly, furnished and dominated by a great gilt mirror. The men, who seem to be theatricals, speculate on the difficulties of getting a play started; if this were a play, how would they convey the situation to the audience?

By way of answering their own question, they embark on some bald- faced exposition from which we learn that the room is in a chateau and that its inhabitants are two middle-aged dramatists and a young composer who are collaborating on an operetta and are here to present its script to their leading lady, who also happens to be the composer's beloved.

It's a magnificently cheeky opening, though it may make you wonder whether the author can possibly keep it up. He can.

The Plays the Thing was written in the 1920s by the Hungarian grand boulevardier Ferenc Molnar; the English adaptation is by P.G. Wodehouse, whose dialogue here has all the buoyancy and wit of his best stories plus a sharper edge. (When Wodehouse, who was also a first-rate lyricist, opted for prose fiction, the theatre may have lost a great comic playwright.)

The chateau turns out to have this wall; our heroes can hear their star, in the next room coquetting with a former lover apparently unwilling to settle for being former. The adoring musician, vulnerable and idealistic, is prostrated; and his colleagues are alarmed. Not only is their friends happiness at stake; so, if he should do something desperate, are their careers.

Turai, the senior partner in the playwriting firm, has an idea: He will pretend that the overheard assignation was the rehearsal of a play. All he has to do is write the rest of the script, which he accomplishes in a couple of hours before breakfast, and blackmail the guilty pair into playing along, which he managers in a couple of minutes after it. The full rehearsal of his play pretty much constitutes the last act of our play, and it is dazzling: a consummate piece of figure skating that sounds the occasional deeper note as the characters' inner and outer roles intersect. The dog- eared (and much overrated) themes of art-and-life, reality-and- illusion, are here accorded precisely the amount of mocking seriousness they deserve.

This production is the Soulpepper company's third venture, following Don Carlos and Our Town, into the territory of famous plays we hardly ever get to see; and it is a triumphant as its predecessors.

The actors have engaged a Hungarian director, Laszlo Marton, and he has kept them on an exhilaratingly tight rein. The play presents an absurd action, executed with the utmost suavity, and it demands the same degree of pose from its players; the balance here is almost perfect.

The evenings predominant flavour is the dry sparkle of Diego Matamoros Turai. Contemplating disaster with one hand in his pocket and the other cradling his specs, he is the drawing-room playwright as god: a controlling presence who never raises his voice.

William Webster, playing pompous, and Mike Shara, playing naive, make two fine foils; while Michael Hanrahan as a Magyar major-domo (a transplanted Jeeves, perhaps) almost matches the maestro in imperturbability. Their scenes together are a contest in cool.

The only performer who falls short is Karen Robinson as the diva of Budapest, trying hard for an authority that must come effortlessly or not at all; in her presence, Matamoros is reduced to playing straight man to a non-existent fireworks display. (The lady's sincere moments are fetching, but there isn't much call for those.)

There is gorgeous compensation, though, from C. David Johnson as Almady, her overweening paramour, an ageing matinee idol forced mercilessly into acting out his own follies; his humiliation may seem a little protracted, but both the text and the actor keep uncovering new abysses, new reserves. They even provide a moral, if morality is your thing.

There is also a gem of a small performance from Jim Warren as Mell, the castle secretary, temporarily and very conscientiously in charge of props.

The production is masterfully staged, every visual and verbal joke precisely timed, on an economically splendiferous set by Julie Fox. I have said that Turai tosses off his impromptu masterpiece before breakfast; the meal is served on-stage and its a corker, lovingly itemized and confirming my long-cherished belief that the Central European drama is the only drama that knows how to eat. It figures; the play is a feast.

- In repertory at Premiere Dance Theatre until Aug. 28. Box office: (416) 973-4000.
