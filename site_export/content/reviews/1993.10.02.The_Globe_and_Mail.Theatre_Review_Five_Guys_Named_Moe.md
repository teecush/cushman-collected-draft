THEATRE REVIEW FIVE GUYS NAMED MOE

Saturday, October 02, 1993

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Toronto ONT -- WRITTEN BY CLARKE PETERS Directed by Charles Augins Starring Kevin Brackett, Doug Ekew, Milton Craig Nealy, Jeffrey Polk, Kirk Taylor and Kevin Thorne At the Royal Alexandra Theatre Rating: One guy named Louis Jolly, but lacking the true wit and musicality of the master BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Toronto LOUIS Jordan, the 1940s king of rhythm and blues, is commonly held to be the missing link between jazz and rock 'n' roll. Actually, the more you listen to his records the jazzier he sounds. None of the fifties rockers, with the possible exception of Chuck Berry, could come anywhere near him in wit or musicality.

Five Guys Named Moe, which comes from London via New York, is an anthology of songs he sang and, in a few cases, wrote. (He got co-author credit on many of the numbers in his repertoire but the selection here is stacked against them.) The title derives from one of his earliest hits, about a fictitious jumping band whose members were all namesakes: crazy but it makes as much sense as singing sister acts. Here the five Moes emerge from a radio - in a puff of smoke, natch - to offer comfort and advice to Nomax, a young man whose girl-friend has left him: apparently with some reason though the details are left vague. By the end of the evening his visitors have taught him the error of his ways and he is all set to be a better lover and a better man. This is an interesting switch on the actual Jordan, whose only recorded advice to his listeners was that they should Let the Good Times Roll and who, when he sang What's the Use of Getting Sober (When You're Gonna Get Drunk Again), sounded as though he meant it.

Actually, the show is engagingly frank about its own inconsistencies. Most of its second half is devoted to a nightclub sequence in which the Moes drop all educational pretences and just do numbers. Then, with surprising effectiveness, it wrenches itself back to base for a medley of lovelorn ballads, including Jordan's classic Is You Is or Is You Ain't My Baby and the preternaturally haunting Don't Let the Sun Catch You Crying. The vocal style is Broadway-souls: usually the ghastly mating of two kinds of musical excess but here pared down to something spare and affecting.

None of this has much to do with Jordan's own style. For one thing, as a soloist, he never had to bother much with vocal harmonies. For another, most of his numbers - including the original Is You Is -were, in some way or other, comic. Here the show has a problem. It doesn't really have a sense of humour. It has a sense of good humour; it practically assaults the audience with jollity. But what wit there is comes between songs; the comedy in the lyrics is never released. Somewhere in the show's promotional literature is the claim that Jordan didn't shy away from addressing problems of black life in his music. That's probably true, but you wouldn't know it from what happens on stage. The show wouldn't dream of taxing its audience with anything as demanding as historical context.

In fact, a lot of the music is homogenized. Jordan could even make Calypso hip, but the show makes it an excuse for an excruciatingly extended sequence of audience participation: one of several. This kind of thing used to be confined to kiddie-shows, and its spread prompts two thoughts: 1) if spectators are going to be roped in to provide the entertainment they should start a union; 2) I have never seen a show that was so professional in its execution and so amateurish in its tone.

As for the executors: Kirk Taylor's downbeat defensiveness as Nomax makes him an increasingly sympathetic foil to the aggressive exuberance of everybody else. He even gets specific with his songs, as does Milton Craig Nealy (Four Eyed Moe), who not only dominates as performer but gradually and irresistibly takes over as host: a role initially filled by hulkingly genial Doug Eskew (Big Moe). Kevyn Brackett (Eat Moe) has a lustrous sequence of flash acrobatic tap. Charles Augins's choreography is first rate throughout, always moving, never forced. The sets are stylized-dingy, but the costumes are a symphony of coloured shirts and suspenders.
