Inherently and inescapably, theatre is artificial, and never more so than when it tries its hardest to be real. Such is the case with verbatim theatre, the form whose scripts are based on interviews whose subjects' words are taken down and then used in evidence. However scrupulous the transcription, there are always choices and selections to be made; however self-effacing the actors may try to be in reproducing the voices they've heard in the street or on tape, their own talents and personalities will keep breaking through. These paradoxes are exhibited, explored and exalted in London Road, a fascinating British piece given a momentous and fabulously cast production by Canadian Stage. It is, amazingly, a verbatim musical.

The road of the title isn't in London; it's a street in the medium-sized Suffolk town of Ipswich. Five women, all prostitutes, were serially murdered on this street in 2006, and the killer was subsequently discovered to have lived there. Alecky Blythe, a British playmaker, visited the place when the case was still under investigation and the neighbours in shock. She returned before, during and after the trial, discovering at the end of two years that the street's inhabitants had become a community. An impromptu organization that had started defensively as a Neighbourhood Watch had evolved into a beautifying project intent on reclaiming itself in the eyes of the world and in its own. Floral contests and displays were a major feature, though they'd been mentioned as a morale-building possibility from the very start. They are indeed the subject of the first and most beautiful of the show's musical numbers.

Ah, yes, the musical numbers. Blythe is known in Britain as a verbatim practitioner par excellence; she likes to reproduce not just her subjects' exact words but their every hesitation, repetition and nonverbal exclamation. The National Theatre brought her together with a composer, Adam Cork, to whom it said, more or less, "set that to music." And so he did, finding musical rhythms to match speechrhythms, not changing a word, counterpointing the characters when they're in disagreement, supplying choral settings for their moments of unanimity and doing especially exciting things when they're all saying the same thing but in different words. It's an extraordinary score. There are spoken passages too, but essentially this is opera, demanding from its performers (and here receiving) operatic precision and commitment, if not traditional operatic range.

That makes it of course very artificial; people in real life don't sing in impromptu chorus, any more than they talk in blank verse. But theatrical writing from Shakespeare to Sondheim has found a special thrill, and a special truth, in making artificial forms sound natural, in making the conversational sound inevitable (and vice, necessarily, versa). It draws out the heart of a situation, in concentrated form. That is what happens here when Deborah Hay and Glynis Ranney fearfully agree, each echoing and reinforcing the other's words, that with the killer at large, "you automatically think it could be him" of every man you encounter. A nervous keyboard accompaniment underlines their feelings and amplifies their fears; I don't know if Reza Jacobs, the show's MD, is conducting his own arrangements or as has inherited them from the National production, but he and his combo do superb work either way.

Hay is best known for her work in plays, Ranney for musicals. In fact, though, both can do both, and so it is throughout the cast. The director, Jackie Maxwell, has miraculously corralled eleven first-rate and first-ranking performers, allowing us the potent pleasure of seeing each emerge powerfully and pointedly from the ensemble and then merge seamlessly back into it. Each plays a named member of the Committee, and they also account between them for, by the program's count, 52 other roles. Nobody plays the murderer; he's revealed to be a truckdriver who resided on the street for eight months (there's no suggestion of any miscarriage of justice) but all we learn of the trial is the street's reaction to the news coverage. Nor do we hear any individual details about his victims; though Hay, Michelle Fisk and Fiona Reid share a memorable trio as three of their surviving colleagues, no longer able to work this particular street. The street's own reaction to this can be disconcerting, especially when voiced by Reid in her best house-and-garden mode as the horticultural lady-in-chief. Then we have George Masswhohl as an avuncular committee chair, caught making an illphrased promise about "tarting the street up"; Ben Carlson as a saloon-bar wiseacre insistently reiterating that his fund of knowledge about serial killers doesn't mean he is one; Damien Atkins hilarious as a TV reporter tripped up by the similar names of prosecutor and accused and also by scheduling-restrictions on the use of the word "semen" (this goes remarkably well into song); Sean Arbuckle, Julain Molnar, Steve Ross, Shawn Wright: they're a community in themselves. The evening is a carousel of knockout performances, beautifully contained in Maxwell's visually simple and logistically complex production; to which, considering the variety and accuracy of the accents on display, Jane Gooderham, the dialect coach, must have made a major contribution. As must whoever coordinates the myriad changes of Judith Bowden's costumes.

The show invents a format and makes a dazzling case for it. I am less sure what else it has to say. Maybe just that these things happened and that these people matter, but it cannot avoid at least the appearance of commentary. It ends with the street en fete, reprising its celebration of flowers. In the middle of this finale, there's a momentary freeze. We hear, briefly, the voices of the actual interviewed prostitutes (nobody here says "sex-worker" but, equally, nobody says "slut"): their real voices, not actors'. So here for a moment is reality, not artifice: in what is presented, though not of course in the choice of where. It's a shock effect that makes the merrymakers on stage, with their prizes and their lottery tickets, seem smug and insensitive. For most of the evening the show has been presenting a balanced view of its people, people who could easily be us: a view both critical and admiring. The end, though, feels less like complexity than like having it both ways. It's a significant blot but it hardly cancels out the glories of what has gone before.

A rather different kind of community musical is Once on This Island, the show that the Ragtime team wrote before they wrote Ragtime. The revival, by Acting Up Stage and Obsidian, hardly measures to their previous collaboration on Caroline or Change; Nigel Shawn Williams' production lacks the helpful bazazz of the Broadway original, and as simple storytelling - in a show that explicitly celebrates storytelling - it doesn't really get going until its final poignant and tender third. In this Caribbean companion to The Little Mermaid a peasant girl from the French Antilles falls in love with a scion of the Grand'hommes, the lighter-skinned and impenetrably snobbish people from the richer side of the tracks. She nurses him back to health after an accident and he, in his fashion, loves her: not enough, though, to throw over his arranged bride for her sake. She dies at his gate, but the gods take belated pity and work an arboreal transformation on her: girl gets boy, girl loses boy, girl turns into tree, becoming a posthumous emblem of reconciliation. Stephen Flaherty's music has grown on me over the years, and at least one of Lynn Ahrens' lyrics - the hero's blithe apologia for his own caddishness - is a treasure; it's called Some Girls, as in "some girls you marry, some you love." As she he loves Jewelle Blackman looks enchanting, acts with ardour, and hits some startling low notes in her singing; some of her high notes are startling as well, but in a less agreeable way.
