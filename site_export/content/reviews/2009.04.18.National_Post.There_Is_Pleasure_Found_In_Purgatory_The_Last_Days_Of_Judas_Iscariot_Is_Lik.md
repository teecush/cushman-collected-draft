The beginning and the end of The Last Days of Judas Iscariot are that Judas had a mother. At the start, she talks about the love that suffused him when he was born and about his burial in a potters field. At the close, she sings about them. The song isn't in Stephen Adly Guirgis' original script, but with Louise Pitre playing the role in David Ferrys Toronto production, musical additions are more than justified. An ambitious, provocative and furiously funny text translates into a magnificent night at the theatre.

Pitre proves as powerful a talking actress as she is a singing one. Another fine musical transplant is Adam Brazier, who plays Butch Honeywell, foreman of the jury at Judas trial. Judas is in Hell, but some people feel that after a couple of millennia he could use some clemency. So a trial is convened, in Purgatory, which here seems to be a suburb of Middle America. Butch has the play's last major speech, delivered to a catatonic Judas in his infernal cage, and it is as beautifully paced in the acting as it is in the writing. It's a mournful account of the sins in his own life, whose upshot is that we all betray. In doing so, Butch lost the person he loved most. So he has a fellow-feeling for Judas.

The trial is presided over to his extreme reluctance by Ted Dykstra, stupendously alarming in dark glasses and a Southern drawl. For Judas defence, there is an all-purpose feminist progressive (Janet Porter whos forceful, if sometimes too much so); for the prosecution, a toadying Egyptian riotously played by Morris Panych, who communicates infectious joy at being on a stage for the first time in years and untarnished skill at occupying the space.

Guirgis is transparently Catholic. He gives all his characters a case, and the space and energy to state it. I doubt if he approves politically of Mother Teresa, but she still emerges, in Aviva Armour Ostroff's performance, with dignity fiercely intact. Judas' story is examined from every conceivable angle. Caiaphas (Dykstra in a heavy beard, and very troubling) resonantly refuses to hear the Jews blamed yet again for the death of Christ; though Pontius Pilate (Philip Akin, massive as a four-star general), playing the antiterrorism card, is disposed to do just that. Various disciples declare Judas "a dick," but not a traitor. Great thing about a court of the dead: You can call anyone. Both sides call Satan who makes both the most powerful and the most disturbing case for the existence of God; the devil is Diego Matamoros at his smoothest and most terrifying.

Judas, condemned for carrying out a divine plan, got a raw deal. Hauntingly played by Shaun Smyth, he shuffles periodically onstage, the last time for a writhing encounter with a Jesus (Jamie Robinson) whose love he still cant accept but who also seems disinclined to accept his, at least on his terms. The court gives a verdict; the play doesnt. My own is that this is the most exhilarating play to come out of America, or maybe anywhere, in years. Here's the only bad news: The show is off already. Economics, and maybe casting logistics, made a run of more than 10 performances (in the Distillery's Fermenting Cellar) difficult. But the production is a revival of a show seen even more briefly four years ago. So, perhaps it can rise again, phoenix-like. Especially since the producing company is Birdland Theatre.
