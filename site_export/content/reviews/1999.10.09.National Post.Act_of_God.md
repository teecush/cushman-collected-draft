Theatre Review

ACT OF GOD

Theatre Passe Muraille

Just what I needed: another monologue.

Lights up on a dentist's chair, front and centre of an otherwise empty stage. Enter James OReilly, author and performer, with a loping gait and even a loping voice, to discourse on the terrible state of his teeth.

Apparently he hasnt been to the dentist since he was a kid, when his parents told him that he didn't need those expensive braces, whatever the man said; after all, why preserve your teeth at the cost of an arm and a leg? (Note these parents down for later.)

So here he is, for major mouth surgery. He wonders why your teeth can't just drop out suddenly instead of slowly decaying like, say, the man in your apartment building who just quietly keels over and dies. He embarks on a full-blown fantasia about this guy, taking his tone from the common belief that extended whimsy is the same thing as comedy: pile on the quizzical inflections and somebody will laugh.

That was the prologue, now comes the play. The anaesthetic unleashes -- as he knew it would, having had plenty of non-medical experience with drugs -- a whole kennelful of memories. (Is he having a general or what Duke Ellington called, appositely though in another context, a Happy-Go-Lucky Local?)

His mind wanders over his early years, spent variously in Cleveland, Vancouver and Toronto; over his father, gamely refusing serious pain-killers after extended intestinal surgery; over arguments with contemporaries on the demerits of heavy metal versus progressive rock; on being pushed onto the streets by his folks to sell boxes of greetings-cards before graduating to a proper paper- route.

Everybody whom he quotes addresses him as Jim or Jimmy and the authors names is James, so we assume that what he is telling us is genuinely autobiographical; certainly there is no sense of a distance being kept between performer and material. It all seems random, and of a lot more interest to him than it could possibly be to us.

Shamefully, I found my mind taking off on its own excursions, and even asking some barely related questions. (How many plays begin in a tooth-clinic? Shaws You Never Can Tell, obviously. There must be others.)

In retrospect there does seem to be a shape, though that's not much consolation while you're actually listening. The protagonist circles around, finally zeroes in on, a specific youthful memory: a friend was struck by lightning and died, while he himself survived. (They had been under a tree; Jim had selflessly insisted on his friend having the best spot.) He recalls the nightmare ride in the ambulance. He feels guilty.

The shows title, Act of God, neatly points up its major flaw. An accident, however sad, is of no dramatic value; nobody is at fault and its unfair to treat the audience as if it were your insurance company, or -- if you feel really badly about it -- your shrink. We have been watching a self-indulgent play about self-indulgence.

The most effective sequence, by far, reaches back to a time when both Jimmy and his younger brother were small. They had been fighting for hours in front of the TV and their mother lost it. They were ordered to pack their bags (I was eight says O'Reilly pointedly, "my brother was six") and their father took them on a long drive to, he said, an orphanage. This understandably traumatized them, perhaps for life. It is suggested that the experience was ultimately responsible for the lightning incident, which makes no sense, or conceivably for our hero's reaction to it.

Maybe all of life is a guilt-trip, an especially vengeful act of God. Ultimately, though, it all seems like an excuse for the performer to get up and riff.

As a performer, he is fairly accomplished; he is not, as far as I can see, an actor (i.e. someone who builds a character and fits it within a context composed of other characters) but then he isnt pretending to be. Another James, James Thomson, perches in a gallery at the side of the stage, and supplies musical punctuation, witty and pertinent; they make a primal jazz duet, voice and bass, and OReilly's delivery is somewhat reminiscent of Ken Nordine, the 1950s word-jazz man, who is having something of a revival. It is dry and twangy, alternating between screeches of protest and murmurs of self-deprecation, varying the beat to emphasize the points. There are plenty of these but the sum total still seems pointless. Susan Potvin is the director and, apparently, muse. (Her credit in the latter capacity reads "dramaturged by"; now there's an ugly verb.) I wish theatres, and even some journalists, would stop promoting solo plays as if they had access to some special realm of confessional purity, when their real attraction is that they're cheap.

- Act of God runs until Oct. 24 at the Theatre Passe Muraille Backspace, 16 Ryerson St. Call 504-7529.
