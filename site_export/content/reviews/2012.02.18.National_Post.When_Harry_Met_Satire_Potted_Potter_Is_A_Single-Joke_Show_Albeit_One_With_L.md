Fifteen years ago, TVOntario took its cameras into a Toronto school library in an attempt to find out what the children were reading. One 10-year-old girl spoke enthusiastically about a book she'd just received from her uncle in England, a book that was already very popular over there but had yet to be published anywhere else. It was about a boy called Harry Potter. "And who," the interviewer asked, in words destined never to be uttered again in a public forum, "is Harry Potter?"

I can vouch for the authenticity of this story, as the young lady in question was my daughter. So I can boast that, where North American Pottermania is concerned, my household was way ahead of the curve. My Potter credentials, though, pretty much end there; I never got around to reading any of the books, and I only saw the first half of the first movie. (It was on the VCR, as we called it in those days; I got called away and never returned.) However, the performers and producers of Potted Potter claim their show can be understood without any knowledge of the source material, and I can proclaim, from the full depths of my ignorance, that they're right.

Not, of course, that anyone can really remain ignorant of the Potter universe. When Daniel Clarkson and Jefferson Turner drop names like Voldemort and Dumbledore, they don't need to explain who those people are or what they stand for. Though, actually, they do. Their show, which is subtitled "The Unauthorized Harry Experience - A Parody by Dan and Jeff," presents itself as a 75-minute synopsis of all seven Potter volumes. It's Dan and Jeff themselves who need an introduction. So here it is: They are a couple of young British writerperformers who've been working this material up for several years now, and whose style of academic knockabout is fifth-generation Monty Python. It's also, and more fundamentally, in the venerable tradition of comic and stooge. In this case Dan, the taller, is the comedian and Jeff, the shorter, is the straight man. As usual, the seeming authority figure gets progressively undermined by the feckless zany to whom he is shackled for the duration.

Clarkson and Turner are especially close to the great British team of Morecambe and Wise, whose standard procedure on their TV shows was as follows. Ernie (that was Wise) would proudly announce that he'd written a play, based on some literary or historical theme and Eric (Morecambe) would proceed to ruin it. Similarly: Jeff is a self-proclaimed Potter expert, and this dramatized speed-read is his idea. He will also play Harry. Dan will play everybody else, despite the fact that he hasn't read any of the books. He has to play everybody else because he has taken the budget with which he was supposed to hire actors from the Stratford Festival and blown it all on what he promises Jeff will be a truly amazing dragon. To be fair to Dan, we do get to see the dragon. To be fair to Jeff ... no, it would take too long. Anyway, it finally dawns on Jeff that Dan is having all the fun and that Harry, in these reduced circumstances, is not much of a role, is in fact the straight guy to end them all. This is a history-making departure; never before have I known the sober half of a duo to act jealous of the fact that his partner is getting all the laughs. An ancient comic secret is being dragged into the open.

And indeed Clarkson, who has the air of innocent solicitude characteristic of havoc-wreaking clowns, can be very funny indeed. He's funniest, as is the show as a whole, when it gets furthest from its presumed premise: most of all when he takes charge of both stage and auditorium to involve the audience in a game of Quidditch. Some of what he does counts as Potter parody, as when he remarks of the rule concerning the Snitch (all new to me, of course, but they make it easy) that it renders the rest of the game completely pointless. But what's really fun is the glee he injects in some very traditional routines: dividing the house into competitive halves and getting two of the smallest spectators onstage as their representatives. He can be surprisingly, and refreshingly, impolite about all this, and he really does seem to be thinking on his feet.

The most striking of his instant impersonations is his Ron Weasley who sounds remarkably like Ali G. The duo gets surprising mileage out of an oft-repeated and extremely irritating prophecy of Dumbledore's and they do indeed provide a comprehensible, if not comprehensive, rundown of the books, with a sidelong glance at the movies' one-for-the-priceof-two rendition of one of them. All the same, there's a depressing air of cashing-in about the whole occasion. A joke that would be good for one revue sketch is made to serve for an entire show. It's a longish ride on somebody else's broomstick.
