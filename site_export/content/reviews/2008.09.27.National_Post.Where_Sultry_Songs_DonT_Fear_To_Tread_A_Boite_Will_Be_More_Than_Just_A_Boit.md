I feel some trepidation in writing this, since I'm personally involved, but I'm greatly looking forward to next week's Canwest Cabaret Festival at the Young Centre. I've spent some of the happiest evenings of my life watching and listening to singers and songs in intimate surroundings, preferably with drinks available. It's an experience that's been hard to come by in Toronto in recent years; it exists, but you have to go digging for it. So it's heartening that there should be four days of the stuff, featuring some of Canada's performers, in a prime downtown location.

For 19 years now there's been an annual event in New York called the Cabaret Convention, a series of concerts mainly devoted to what's become known as the Great American Songbook: golden-age Broadway songs, Cole Porter, Gershwin, that brand of thing. (It's named after Mabel Mercer, the New York singer who epitomized that kind of repertoire in that sort of cafe setting.) During the first Convention, I sat on a panel on which we tried to determine what cabaret actually was. It became clear that for a lot of people, it meant mainly the title of a film; this was before the show on which the movie was based came back in revival glory. And beyond that, it meant, and continues to mean, the whole legend of louche, satirical Berlin nightclubs that the musical, somewhat freely, evokes. Well, that's if your fancy is German; if it's French the word "cabaret" is likelier to evoke Edith Piaf, Charles Aznavour and Jacques Brel. In the United States, and I think in most of the English-speaking world it's come to mean that Broadway catalogue and its extensions. In England, obviously, Noel Coward. In Canada, some of the later troubadours writing in the enigmatic confessional mode. Joni Mitchell, perhaps. Or Leonard Cohen.

Cohen (Oct. 2-3) is one of three composers featured in the Cabaret Festival's Songbook evenings: a trio of tributes, playing two performances each, alongside the less formal solo shows that make up the bulk of the programming, and covering between them quite a few waterfronts. Cohen, with his man-in-black mordancy, connects with the poet-as-singer traditions of Berlin and Paris, but also with the North American pop maestros of the '60s and after. In pop, but not quite of it, although -- like so many of the older American songwriters -- Jewish. With a twist: I don't know how many American songs there are called Hallelujah -- hundreds probably -- but there can't be any others this sardonic. Patricia O'Callaghan will be singing it, along with (among other things) one of those oddly yearning Cohen songs, Take this Waltz. Steven Page (one singer-songwriter saluting another), does the famous Famous Blue Raincoat and -- what may be the ultimate uncompromising title -- A Singer Must Die. Major Cohen love songs on parade include Dance Me to the End of Love from Maryem Tollar, and I'm Your Man from Sarah Slean. (I was surprised too.) Andrew Whiteman (Apostle of Hustle) rounds out the bill with some of the more biblical titles: Waiting for the Miracle and Waters of Babylon.

If there's a place where jazz intersects with cabaret, and even with show tunes, it's in the songs of Duke Ellington (Oct. 5). Ellington's Harlem was its own cabaret: metaphorically and, in the first years of his bandleading career at the Cotton Club, literally as well. Ellington, whose wide-ranging musical showmanship deserves the appellation of "total theatre" that he himself bestowed on Frank Sinatra, ran in effect his own repertory company, bringing out the compositional best in many of his instrumentalists as well as in himself. The Duke's arranger and alter ego Billy Strayhorn was responsible for at least two of the most achingly lovely tunes in the Ellington book, Something to Live For and Chelsea Bridge, both to be sung (though I don't think the latter has an official lyric) by Ada Lee. Strayhorn also wrote the startlingly precocious saloon song Lush Life, to be sung by Mary Margaret O'Hara, and the Ducal theme-tune Take the "A" Train, to be done by singer-pianist John Alcorn, along with the Ellington-Strayhorn Satin Doll.

There are plenty of songs, though, with music by Ellington alone: the matched pair of Don't Get Around Much Anymore and Do Nothin' Till You Hear from Me, both instrumentals are fitted out with flavourful lyrics. They're to be sung by Don Francks, a Canadian cabaret institution, along with I Got It Bad, conceivably the most famous Ellington song and one of the few that actually originated in a show -- as, surprisingly, did the zany Bli-Blip, to be sung by the evening's emcee, Andrew Craig. One of my own favourites, Take Love Easy, also originated in a show, albeit a different one. The song is being done by the great Jackie Richardson and it's from Beggar's Holiday, a Broadway update of The Beggar's Opera.

Which brings us to Kurt Weill (Oct. 4), whose Threepenny Opera was the greatest of such rewrites and who may sum up the world, or worlds, of cabaret more completely than any other composer. In his collaborations with Bertolt Brecht, he was Weimar Berlin. In the 1940s, he was Broadway, at its most adventurous, collaborating with writers from Langston Hughes to Ira Gershwin via Ogden Nash. In both those modes (and in a Parisian period between), he bequeathed whole sheaves of songs to a breed of intense recitalists whom Brecht's daughter once described to me as "all those ladies in black." Did the German and American Weills have anything in common? Well, to be superficial about it, they both wrote showcase songs about ladies called Jenny: the baleful Pirate Jenny, with Brecht, giving way to the cheerfully admonitory Saga of Jenny ("don't make up your mind") with Gershwin. Patricia O'Callaghan will sing both, as well as the Brechtian Bilbao-Song and Alabama Song, the latter in duet with Mary Margaret O'Hara who'll also perform the haunting Lonely House from Weill's Broadway opera Street Scene. Mike Ross salutes the Broadway Weill with two songs from his Nash collaboration One Touch of Venus, the famous Speak Low and the less famous but terrific I'm a Stranger Here Myself. The French Weill gets more of a look-in than is usual on these occasions from Sara Slean who also sings Lost in the Stars, a song that's practically become an anthem. Once again the emcee sings, in this case John Millard, presenting Weill at his most savage (Army Song) and most mellow (September Song). And there is every possibility that Albert Schultz, the festival's overall director, will reprise some of his Macheath numbers from Threepenny.

One more thing: Cabaret is a notoriously ephemeral art, literally flying by night. So if any of these promised songs fails to materialize, sue me. But it won't hold up in court. - Robert Cushman will be hosting the Songbook evenings at the Canwest Cabaret Festival.
