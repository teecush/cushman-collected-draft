I am intrigued by the prospect of Not the Messiah, the oratorio based on Monty Python's Life of Brian and composed by the Spamalot team of Eric Idle and John Du Prez, which, it was announced this week, is coming to us next year as part of the new Toronto arts festival, Luminato. Though I don't know about these names. Why can't they just call it the Toronto Festival? It doesn't seem to have hurt Edinburgh.

Anyway, I'm taken by the oratorio idea because I've always preferred Life of Brian to Monty Python and the Holy Grail. So I am pleased that, in the wake of Spamalot, we will be getting the sit- down version; indeed, not only getting it but originating it, with the Toronto Symphony conducted by its very own director, and Idle's very own cousin, Peter Oundjian.

I'm much taken, too, by Idle's promise that it will be "funnier than Handel, but not as good." The world may well need a funny oratorio, though it's hard to be sure since it's probably never had one. Anyway, it seems a safe prophecy, on both counts, and a very Idle thing to say. Or a very Python thing to say. Or, to come right down to it, a very British thing to say. It ties in with Idle's first-night speech at the Canon when he thanked Toronto audiences for "opening your arms -- and your legs" to the Python team when they still were a team and toured here in the early '70s.

There's always something refreshing about the demeanour of British performers at showbiz junkets. You see it particularly at the big award ceremonies. They rarely, when victorious, thank God or their mothers, and they always seem somewhat amused by the whole enterprise. The balance of arrogance and self-deprecation is hard to bring off, and Americans, though they sometimes try, can never manage it.

I'm never sure that Americans get British toughness or even, at the root, British humour. (Canadians? They're in between. Like honest brokers.) It doesn't stop them rhapsodizing over British entertainers. Idle himself has said that the Pythons owe their cult status to America, but that may be because the Americans thought them more whimsical than they actually were. It's like the Beatles. For many of their countrymen, the Beatles' defining moment had little to do with music. It was when they appeared at the Royal Variety Performance, and John Lennon told the audience in the (comparatively) cheaper seats to clap; "the rest of you," with a look at the Royal Box, "just rattle your jewellery." That kind of cheerful disrespect -- which wasn't that cheerful underneath -- didn't travel, not in the way that lovable mop-toppishness did. And when the group grew spiritual pretensions, they became perfect transatlantic exports. It's tempting to blame America for bringing out the worst in the Beatles, and, ultimately, for ruining them.

America didn't quite have that effect on the Pythons. (After all, John Cleese went on to write the misanthropic farcical masterpieces that were Fawlty Towers. Years later there was an attempt to produce an American version, which the networks felt compelled to enrich with "human interest." I'm sure they didn't set out to desecrate the originals. They just couldn't help themselves.)

But I suspect American fans embraced the silliness of Python, while ignoring the intellect. What really made the TV shows was the shamelessly intelligent way in which their creators mixed their surrealist skills with their university education. Your typical Python sketch found most of the cast in drag as housewives at a laundromat, trying to put a phone call through to Mrs. Jean-Paul Sartre to check on a detail of her husband's philosophy. I'm not claiming that that's as hysterical as the dead-parrot number, but it's as characteristic. It isn't, though, something that the fans keep quoting.

I was surprised when Idle told me that Holy Grail had always been more popular than Life of Brian, but I shouldn't have been. Brian isn't just a Biblical send-up, as Grail is an Arthurian send-up; it has something to say about human gullibility. (I remember Cleese being emphatic about this in a TV discussion with Malcolm Muggeridge, the English cultural mandarin and late-life religious zealot, who wearily dismissed the movie. Wearily dismissing things was his trademark.) So maybe it was born to be an oratorio, and especially one with that double-edged title, which manages to spoof Handel while quoting the film's most famous line. It's almost as if they'd planned it, decades in advance.

And maybe, by the same token, Spamalot -- whose title is probably only a coincidental spoof on the Lerner and Loewe Camelot, but is a purposeful homage to the processed-meat product that Python has kept fresher than any refrigerator -- was born to be a musical. After all, there were, just as in The Producers, already two musical numbers in the movie waiting to be staged. One is the minstrel's song of the Brave (and Multiply Mutilated) Sir Robin, which makes it on to the stage unscathed, unlike its subject. The other is Knights of the Round Table, a small mock-production number in the movie and a big mock-production number on the stage. The size and the mockery, though, cancel one another out. In the film the number is effectively cancelled out by having Graham Chapman's Arthur say, immediately after it's over, "No, let's not go to Camelot; it seems like a silly idea." That's funny, and very Python. But you can't do it in the theatre, not with the audience's applause providing a buffer zone. That's Broadway. That's America.

With the rage for putting famous movies on stage, I have to wonder if anyone is planning to turn the Marx Brothers' masterpiece Duck Soup, the last word on war, into a musical. I hope not, but I have to admit that it is, well, a sitting duck. It, too, has two numbers already in place, and they could easily be supplemented with other songs by the same composers. (Bert Kalmar and Harry Ruby, if you're wondering.) And the Marxes themselves aren't strictly inimitable. And you can bet that, however good its intentions, any stage production would forfeit the movie's anarchic inconsequentiality, and probably its satiric bite as well. Tame the Marx Brothers? But it's already been done, by MGM: the studio to which the brothers moved after their early classics at Paramount (which culminated in Duck Soup) and which fitted them out with hearts of gold and romantic subplots. The audiences bought it, too. It isn't just the British to whom American show business can do it. It can Americanize Americans as well.
