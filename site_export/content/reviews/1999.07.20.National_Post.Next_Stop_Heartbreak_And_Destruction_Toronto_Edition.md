A STREETCAR NAMED DESIRE

du Maurier Theatre Centre, Toronto

Like Hamlet, A Streetcar Named Desire is full of quotations. Tennessee Williams' play contains, in fact, an unfair proportion of the most famous lines in mid-20th-century drama from Blanche du Bois sometimes theres God so quickly" as she melts in the arms of the first man in years who seems likely to respect her, to her final, miraculous "whoever you are -- I have always depended on the kindness of strangers. It's always a shock to hear those much- parodied words in context, and to realize that Blanche speaks them to the doctor who is about to cart her off to a madhouse.

It is an overwhelming finale: the inevitable denouement to a long, tough and compassionate play. In Diana Leblanc's new production for Soulpepper -- thanks to magnificently counterpointed work from everyone on stage, both the involved and the indifferent - - it draws tears. It is also, as tragedy should be, appallingly ironic: Blanche, the faded Southern belle adrift in the lurid squalor of New Orleans, is being incarcerated as a fantasist and a liar. She has, indeed, been both, but what dooms her now is that she has told a truth that nobody wants to believe: that Stanley Kowalski, her brother-in-law, has raped her.

Blanche, on her tram-ride to destruction, says some other memorable things. There is her exhortation to her sister Stella, Stanleys wife, to have some regard for civilization, for art and literature, and not to hang back with the brutes" -- meaning Stanley. There is her declaration to Mitch, her admirer who has requested to see her in the light, that she doesnt want realism, she wants magic, "and if that is a sin, then let me be damned for it." These passages, the second especially, are often taken to be Williams' credo as well as Blanche's; and actresses have accordingly given them the works.

This can be unfortunate since, when isolated, these speeches sound overripe and embarrassing: the progenitors of far worse things in Williams later plays and in American drama in general. One of the great qualities of Fiona Reids Blanche is the way she folds these arias into her characterization; she races them, she argues them, making them means rather than ends. Put another way: She says them, urgently, to the other characters, not to us.

Everything in this Blanche is implied from the moment she sets foot on stage: a primly, fussily elegant overdressed figure wandering into a crowded, noisy, jazz-haunted slum, organdy adrift in a world of blue jeans. This is a fastidious performance of a fastidious character -- or of one so deeply convinced of her own gentility that she can black out any evidence to the contrary. She lives on her nerves and on her very considerable wits: the child of a vanished ancestral home, the bruised survivor of marriage to an idolized husband who killed himself when she discovered his homosexuality, a teacher dismissed from her school for seducing a 17- year-old.

Not all the more lurid aspects of this Blanche's past seem credible at the moment we hear about them; but they fall into place at the end. (Besides, if life and drama teach us anything, it is not to make assumptions about other people's sexuality.) This is a performance alternately feisty and flirtatious, coy and aggressive, one that is sometimes very funny (when she puts on her glasses and lectures people, she takes on a bizarre resemblance to Dame Edna Everage) but is not very moving, not until the very last moment, when it is overwhelming.

The play is in large part a duel between Blanche and Stanley, who suspects and resents her pretentions from the moment she crosses his threshold. Stuart Hughes Stanley is hardly the graceful gorilla immortalized by Marlon Brando (in a performance that has cast a longer shadow than any other in a modern play except, perhaps, Laurence Oliviers in The Entertainer); he is more of a preening, malicious chimp, a relocated Dead End Kid.

The central antagonism is very credible; so are Stanley's outbursts of violence against both his and her sister. But the actor doesn't have the vocal reserves for his more extended scenes -- most crucially, for his relishing revelations of Blanches nymphomaniac reputation in her home town -- and it doesnt help the play's balance that he looks shorter than she does.

Kristen Thomson's Stella, though, is absolutely perfect: practical, sensual, alternately careless and caring -- torn between sympathy for Blanche, passion for Stanley, and loyalty to both. She pretty much sums up the play in her own person. (Hers are the choices her sister never made; she prefers realism to magic, and is saved by it.) Oliver Dennis, though decently shy and well-meaning, finds less in Mitch than his author has provided -- until the climax when his silent disgust makes a powerful contribution.

Invaluable throughout is Maria Vacratsis as the woman upstairs whose marriage parodies, if such a thing is possible, that of the Kowalskis. She is part of the atmosphere -- which in this production, and in Astrid Jansons blue-hued set, is overpowering: lyrical, and austerely compassionate.

I didnt see Leblanc's production of Death of a Salesman but this must be its twin: another sub-section of that mythical post-war America where a person has to dream because, to quote again, it comes with the territory. And, by the way, according to an off-the- cuff remark of Williams' own, Blanche charmed the doctors and was out of that hospital in three weeks flat.
