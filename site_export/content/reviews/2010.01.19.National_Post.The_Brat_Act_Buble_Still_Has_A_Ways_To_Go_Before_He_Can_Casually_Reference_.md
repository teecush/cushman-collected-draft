M ichael Buble, answering a question from the audience, expresses huge admiration for Elvis Presley because, somewhere inside himself, "he realized all the screaming wasn't real; hed mastered self-deprecation." Elvis, of course, had a self -- and a position at the very top of the hill -- to be deprecating about. He didnt have to work at it. Buble, still a newcomer, has to work very hard, both at being a hyperactive performer and being ironic about it in between numbers. It's very early for him to start sending himself up: early in his career and early in tonight's TV show. In Michael Buble at the Concert Hall (taped at the Masonic Temple, Toronto) he starts in on the roguish Q&A after having sung a mere two numbers.

Those two opening songs, All of Me and Georgia on My Mind, happen to be the two on the program closest to what are usually known as standards. All of Me was a favourite starter for Sinatra himself, and Buble does it in Sinatra style, at Sinatra tempo, and with Sinatra lyric-changes: "Cant you see Im just a mess without you." And so with the next tune: Georgia, I said Georgia, he interpolates, just like Ray Charles said Georgia. It seems to me that, if you want to breathe new life into old repertoire, it would help to go back to the original texts, rather than thoughtlessly reproducing what other singers have definitively done with them. Influence is one thing, imitation is another.

Original interpretation, though, isn't what bubbly Buble is about. He's one of a crop of neo-crooners, male and female, who are credited with rescuing the Great American Songbook from neglect, even though scores of jazz and cabaret performers have been exploring it for decades with far greater imagination and dedication: singers like Daryl Sherman, Wesla Whitfield, Eric Comstock, Justin Hayford. (Yes, I know you havent heard of any of them. Thats my point.) His initial success seems to have landed him with an identity problem. He's intelligent enough to know that being a "Sinatra knock-off" (his words) lays him open to mockery, so he tries to get in first. He does all his own announcements, even the ones promising that he'll be back with untold goodies after the commercial break. The idea is that he plays at being arrogant, while trying to disarm us by admitting it. Asked why he chose his career, he says frankly that he did it to get laid, then says that, of course, the official pious answer would be that he felt blessed to be able to carry on the legacy of my idols. This ought to come over as a welcome refusal of showbiz sanctimony, like Ricky Gervais hosting the Golden Globes, but the smile with which he accompanies it is suspiciously sickly.

Thats about the last we hear of his idols, or of their legacy. He does invoke Dean Martin before going into Youre Nobody Till Somebody Loves You, a lyric from whose sentiments he disassociates himself before starting (as well he might); as he does it, it's very Vegas but doesn't actually swing. Otherwise, he mainly sings his own compositions, for which he goes very pious, even while kicking his legs. He does a lot of that, also a lot of knee-bends, and his suit is very shiny. The kinetics, though, never seem to have much to do with what hes actually singing. At the end, he thanks us for accompanying him on this "journey": rather a grandiose term, surely, for a half-hours worth of performance. As it happens, though, the last stage of the trip is the best. His closer is Save the Last Dance for Me, a song nicely poised in date and quality between the classics to which he can't do justice and his own soppy originals. He gives it the right lightness of touch, and it can use both his moves and his urge to mingle with an adoring female audience. He may be shaping up less as the next Sinatra, or even the next Bobby Darin (not enough edge), than as the next Engelbert Humperdinck, but I'd take his last dance over Engelberts last waltz, any time.
