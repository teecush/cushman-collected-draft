A SHORT HISTORY OF NIGHT

Factory Theatre, Toronto

Johannes Kepler was the 17th-century astronomer who discovered how the planets move. Tycho Brahe was the older astronomer whose work, though less momentous and less durable than Kepler's, paved the way for him; he was jealous of the younger man but nevertheless gave him a place in his royally-funded observatory and thus allowed him to pursue his research.

I have derived this information from the program notes to John Mighton's A Short History of Night, where it is set out rather more clearly than in the play itself.

The play begins with the infant Johannes being inspected by his father, who playfully inserts a knife into the crib in the foredoomed hope that his boy will grow up to be a soldier like his dad. It ends with Kepler instructing his 16-year-old daughter to stop thinking analogically; the habit of comparing things to other things, rather than accepting them for themselves, and which has apparently been the root of human ills. I can see no connection, other than a random biographical one, between these two bookends. The play does not seem to be about the march of the generations.

The action has certainly been chock-full of human ills, among them the brutal interrogation of Kepler's wife on a charge of witchcraft; one minor character does keep asking her what the Devil is like, but this sounds like gossipy curiosity rather than the expression of a fixed intellectual habit.

The Keplers are Protestants, most of the other characters are Catholics, and the European religious wars are raging. But the conflict implicit in the first scene between the scientific and military lives is never pursued, and it isn't as though anyone ever tries to push the grown-up Kepler into uniform. The war is often mentioned, but its presence is never dramatically felt.

In fact, very little in the play is felt. The actors playing the rival scientists stand around dropping the names of their favourite theories -- one declares that "all movement is caused by suction," to which another opposes the virtues of "straight flying" -- but if they know what they're talking about they don't communicate it to the audience.

The play, in the fashion of modern historical drama, is written in a series of short and supposedly sharp scenes, full of edgy anachronistic jokes and casual horrors; the intention is to keep us off-balance between then and now but the effect is to drain conviction from both. A lot of important stuff seems to happen in the breaks; reading between the lines may be stimulating, reading between the scenes is just frustrating.

Brahe's observatory, where most of the story happens, is peopled by a collection of Gothic clowns (including the regulation dwarf) who behave like rude overgrown schoolboys, especially when Mrs. Kepler is around.

Presumably they feel her sexuality as a threat (her husband certainly seems to), and the end result of this is the Inquisition, represented here by a 19-year-old prodigy of a witch-hunter who ought to be chilling but comes off just silly. Maybe I just mention the sex theme because it's in the air, much as one assumes the play is about the Renaissance clash between science and superstition simply because so many other plays, starting with Brecht's Galileo, have been there already.

This play was first seen in 1991 when it won three major awards. Assuming that these were deserved (they aren't always) Ken Gass' present production has done the piece a disservice. It is lax and scrappy; a woman greets the news that she is about to be subjected to severe physical torture as if she had just been reminded of an imminent dental check-up.

Kepler (Alon Nashman) is a persnickety little fellow with a suitably earnest demeanour; but what he believed, or why it mattered to him, were matters that left me baffled and finally indifferent. What bluff old Brahe (Gary Reineke) thought about Kepler was even more opaque, as indeed was what bluff old Brahe thought about anything, other than the loss of his nose in an early and unusually acrimonious scientific dispute. (He seemed to get over it surprisingly quickly, though people in later scenes said that he was still touchy about it.)

Most of the others stand around being competent in short bursts, though a couple aren't that good. It's very drab.
