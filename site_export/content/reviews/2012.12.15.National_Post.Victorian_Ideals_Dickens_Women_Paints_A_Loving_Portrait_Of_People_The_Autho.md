My favourite line in the whole of Dickens concerns a very minor character in his very first novel, The Pickwick Papers. A junior barrister is momentarily singled out for notice during the breach of promise trial of Bardell v. Pickwick, and tried to look as if he didnt know that everybody was looking at him: an endeavour in which no man ever succeeded or in all probability ever will. Beat that for an insight into the human condition. It seems to me that if you cant enjoy Dickens as a gag writer, you're unlikely to enjoy him at all.

It's significant, too, that it happens in a law court: From first almost to last in Dickens a symbol of societys organized - or sometimes casually disorganized - injustice. I often wondered, in the days when Margaret Thatcher and her adherents were preaching about "Victorian values," just which values they had in mind. I especially wondered if theyd ever read any Victorian novels. The novelists of Victorian England produced the greatest, most enduring works of their time; and none of them could be said to be in tune with the governing ethos of their age. Implicitly or explicitly they denounced it for its cruelty, its complacency, its greed and its vindictiveness. None of them, of course, did this more persistently, more penetratingly or more hilariously than the one whose 200th birthday has been celebrated this year.

This week, the Young Centre is the site of a last-minute bicentennial celebration. Among other events, it plays host to Miriam Margolyes and her one-person show, now on the last leg of a world tour, Dickens' Women. That, she disarmingly announces after completing her opening item, "was Mrs. Gamp from Martin Chuzzlewit, and I'm Miriam Margolyes from Clapham." The workhouses and the Poor Laws are only an implicit presence in this recital, except in one scene. Departing slightly from the premise of her title, Margolyes gives us, from Oliver Twist, the courtship of Mrs. Corney, the workhouse matron, by Mr. Bumble the beadle, whose philosophy is that you should give the paupers what they dont want so that they wont keep coming back ungratefully for more. The exigencies of the scene confine Margolyes to rendering Corney in her coy rather than her later ruthless aspect, but she gives us, physically and vocally, the full Bumble, greasy, self-important, respectably avaricious and cautiously lecherous. She doesn't, I suspect, care much for Corney, but she loves Bumble, even while loathing and despising everything he represents. In all these respects she would, I am sure, have had her author with her.

That doesn't in itself make Dickens a misogynist. Plainly he adored Mrs. Gamp, the combination midwife and deathbed attendant (very similar functions, as she herself says) who so loved the human race that she would have laid all of them out for free if she could. He loved her, with her gin-tippling and her fantasy monologues, even while hating the economic conditions on which she battened. Margolyes revels in her, but in a very disciplined way. Mrs. Gamp doesnt only begin the show; she apparently began the idea of the show. Mrs. Gamp had very little neck and, in a book accompanying the show (and sold at performances) Margolyes reveals that "having very little neck ... I could ... use some of my unfortunate physical qualities to bring her into the theatre." I wouldnt have put it quite like that myself; still, Margolyes is a small but ample lady. She's also, though, a very dainty one, very precise and selective in her movements. As Mrs. Gamp she waddles. As Flora Finching, the decayed and ridiculous former beauty of Little Dorrit, she resembles a bloated Dresden doll.

Flora was Dickens' rather cruel revenge on Maria Beadnell, the pretty and shallow girl who broke his heart when he was young. In her linking narration (which is admirably informal and informative) Margolyes traces the relationships of the women in Dickens books to those in his life: the genteel, poverty-stricken mother who sent him to work in a blacking factory when he was a child; the sister-in-law he idealized and who died young in his perfectly platonic arms; the actress he fell for in his later years and who probably tortured him as much as Maria had. She also details his appalling treatment of his wife, whom he banished from their home while managing to regard himself as the victim, If he hadnt made us laugh so much, she says, speaking for herself and her director-collaborator Sonia Fraser, "he'd have made us very angry."

But then Dickens probably made himself angry. Though Margolyes claims that Miss Havisham, the avenging, forsaken woman of Great Expectations, was a Dickensian self-portrait, I think it's likelier to be true of Miss Wade, the confessed self-tormentor of Little Dorrit, the lesbian in thrall to a merciless younger woman. Margolyes, her face in profile on an otherwise blacked-out stage, brings her harrowingly to life: a brilliant contrast to the gossips and harridans who occupy most of her attention.

These she portrays with a wonderful appreciation of her authors genius for the evocative, crazy, inevitable word. Occasionally I felt that an irony might bite harder if it were faster or drier, but her taste, her relish and her rhythms are infallible. There are of course sins of omission. She reveals in her book that a gentleman in India once rose from his seat to demand of her, "Where, oh where is Betsey Trotwood?" I was glad to read that because Id been silently asking the same question myself: Betsey (from David Copperfield) is one Dickensian lady who is both richly eccentric and a force for good. She is, admittedly, almost the only one. Dickens' view of women was, to put it gently, selective, and this show makes no secret of the fact. It won't make you angry. But it will make you laugh, and wonder, and love.
