Verve's diva series covers Ella, Billie, Blossom and more

Divas are the opposite of singers. Let me try to put that in a more conciliatory way. People who enthuse about "divas seem to adore them for qualities that have only a tangential connection with music; for the amount of noise they make, or the degree of hysterical intensity they can inject into a song, irrespective of its text. The cult originated among the soprano-fanciers of opera, and found a natural home in the more melodramatic areas of pop, starting in Motown. It's dispiriting, though, to find that even in the saner world of jazz, everybody is trying to get into the act.

But thats how it goes at Verve, which calls the latest recycling of its catalogue of female singers the diva series." Recent convulsions in the record business have made that catalogue even more impressive than it used to be. The Universal Music Group has swallowed up not only Verve but Decca and Mercury, who, from the 30s to the '60s, recorded between them hordes of jazz or jazz-pop singers. The resultant riches are almost too embarrassing to contemplate.

For an extreme example take the figurehead of the series, and maybe of jazz singing in general, Ella Fitzgerald. She spent 20 years at Decca and another 10 -- her greatest -- at Verve. George Evans, the Toronto vocalist who compiled her volume and most of the others, can hardly have known where to start. In fact, he starts with some of her early, inescapable tracks (A-Tisket, A-Tasket, Mr. Paganini and what is probably her most beloved scat extravaganza, Oh Lady Be Good) and ends with two even more familiar warhorses from her famous Berlin concert, Mack the Knife (the one where she forgets the words) and How High the Moon. In between comes what inevitably seems a random sampling from her Songbook years; those in which, under the tutelage of Verve's founder, Norman Granz, she worked her way through the best songs of the best American composers, creating not so much definitive performances as definitive collections.

These tracks are as soothing or as invigorating as ever, but they dont mean as much cut loose from their original moorings. The standard complaint against Ella in this context has always been that she neglects a songs lyrics. She does not, perhaps, dig very deeply into them, but she doesn't obstruct them either. The sheer loveliness of her voice, and the unpretentious joy that she takes in the music, keep you listening and let you construct your own interpretations.

Also, there's a wry humour in her voice thats often overlooked and that works beautifully as long as she or her producers trust it. It comes out very clearly in the latest track on this disc, a song called I Said No, which has a typically witty Frank Loesser lyric that she sings perfectly, first time around; she then does a second chorus that she seems to have written herself and that leaves you wondering whether she understood what she was singing about in the first place.

The disc will certainly get a lot of play in coffeehouses, but so much of this material has been repackaged before that it seems anodyne. If you want to give Fitzgerald a serious listen, youd do better to pick up one of her Verve albums (they're nearly all on CD) in its entirety.

Sarah Vaughan's voice matched Ellas for range, precision and flexibility, though Ive never warmed to it as much. Its beauty is grandiose rather than approachable, and if Ella's approach to lyrics might be described as neutral, Sarah's sometimes seems positively destructive.

Along with the grandeur goes a disconcerting coyness of tone; when, in Misty, she compares herself to "a kitten up a tree, it sounds all too apposite. It can even grate when she isnt singing words: Ellas scatting on How High the Moon is exhilarating however often you hear it; Sarah's sounds sterile. We know she can do it; there's no sense of surprise.

Shes at her best singing with small groups, but Mercury (for whom she recorded) more often backed her with big orchestras, and this selection reflects that. Theres nothing wrong with that format, but the combination of big band and big voice is overpowering. There are Vaughan performances (Embraceable You, for example) that take my breath away, but none of them are here.

Carmen McRae, now: an unfailingly intelligent musician, she was capable of both sabre-toothed precision and amazing warmth, sometimes within the same song. You can hear her move from one to the other and back again on her disc's first track, Falling in Love with Love. Then comes a melting I Only Have Eyes for You, followed by a Speak Low that cuts right into the song's curt, ominous rhymes (our moment is swift / like ships adrift"), which in turn gives way to a hovering Midnight Sun that is, in the songs own words, "icy- white and crystalline," with a zany Comes Love waiting further down the block.

These early Decca sides of McRaes (she was in her thirties) are her best work; she was given great repertoire and inventive arrangements, and the tight format of what were meant to be commercial albums kept her from self-indulgence. Also, her voice was pleasanter then; later, she sounded jagged for jaggedness' sake. This is the best volume in the series.

Billie Holiday's entry draws on her careers middle period, self- aware while avoiding the masochistic depths (at least as relished by her more ghoulish fans) of her final husk-voiced recordings. Some of her best work is here, the serene Easy Living and its bitter counterpart Dont Explain, along with Good Morning Heartache, a Speak Low even better than McRae's, I Wished on the Moon, and the inevitable-but-welcome God Bless the Child. (Though the final track, Lady Sings the Blues, is an odd choice unless it's trying for some kind of name recognition.)

The anthology treatment has never hurt Holiday -- indeed she sounds better in the context of other singers. So many of her instinctive innovations have become common coin that you need to hear her successors to realize how good she was.

Anita ODay, Fitzgeralds main rival at the real Verve, sang a similar standard repertoire, usually in looser settings and with wilder results. She's undervalued as a lyric interpreter (like McRae she can be acerbic or even disconcertingly tender) but you really listen to her for high-velocity madness; there can't be many singers who are at their best in Tea for Two.

This is a good collection, though the inclusion of Peel Me a Grape is a reminder that nobody should be allowed to sing this song except Blossom Dearie. Its mysteriously missing from Blossoms own bouquet, though we do get her original unrivalled performance of Dave Frishberg's other early masterpiece I'm Hip. Dearie, at once wide-eyed and pungent, sings (and plays piano) with understated wit, as in They Say Its Spring, an obscure song that once heard refuses to leave your head. She can also, out of nowhere, touch you deeply with Someone to Watch Over Me, a song I had thought I wanted never to hear again.

Dearie whispers; Astrud Gilberto murmurs. With her the moment is more important than the music, so she of all of them may have the best claim to be a diva. True, divadom is supposed to be about attitude, and Gilberto sounds too modest to have one, but that can be an attitude in itself. Anyway, she remains identified with the Brazilian music that did us all a favour in the 60s, and since it still sounds enchanting, so does she. On some of her later tracks (I Haven't Got Anything Better to Do) she sounds good in her own right.

At the opposite extreme of force and guts, Dinah Washington's disc is a joy, as she bends a wide variety of songs to her own will and leaves both them and us the better for it. Nina Simone is more problematic but she, like most of her peers, is heard here at her most disciplined and rewarding. The lineup includes her classic, naked reading of Porgy, the self-descriptive I Put a Spell on You and Wild Is the Wind, a movie tune that she makes sound like the folk song it's trying to be.

There's an extra album, The Ultimate Diva Collection, which samples all the above singers (in, wastefully, some of the above songs) alongside others, like Peggy Lee and Shirley Horn, who deserve representation in any future batch, as indeed do several men -- Mel Torme, Bing Crosby and Fred Astaire are all in the inventory - - if there should ever be such a thing as a Divo Collection. There are brief, hurried-seeming liner notes by the ubiquitous, in these latitudes, Will Friedwald, and the artwork is uniformly hideous. Maybe such exaggerated portraiture goes with the title.
