The upshot of Boxhead seems to be that God is dead, and that, although this is on the whole a good thing, man is having difficulty shouldering the added responsibility. We begin with darkness, and a voice telling us about a certain Dr. Thoughtless Actions, a young geneticist who woke up one morning to find a box on his head. A light comes up on the boxed scientist, who seems uncomfortable in his situation, but disinclined to change it.

Anyway, he goes about his business, propounds theories and attends conferences (I suppose it's crassly literal to wonder how he manages to eat) and, at a certain stage, encounters another young geneticist in a similar condition, whose name is Dr. Wishful Thinking. The two men have an instant rapport, fall in love and even decide that they would like to have a child. This last ambition is thwarted, though, by the revelation that they are, in fact, the same person: Wishful thinking and thoughtless actions inevitably go together.

Both doctors are endowed with eager, squeaky cartoon voices, like speeded-up tapes. Their stories are told to us by two contrastingly deep and languid voices that claim to live inside the characters and to have other voices living inside them. They are omniscient narrators and -- by explicit analogy -- they are God. (If God, according to philosophers, died in the 18th century, the death of the narrator was postponed until the 20th, but this play capsizes time.)

The voices are tired of both roles, although they are not above engaging in competitive cross-talk routines about the extent of their power. At times, one gets the impression that they would be quite happy to cede all authority to the geneticists, who have presumably been singled out because of their own professional propensity to play God. Most of the time, though, the boxed pair are the objects of sadistic games played by both their on-stage and off- stage creators. You might say that each of the two teams has, for its own pyschological purposes, invented the other.

There is a lot of the play -- notably the doctors' chat about the cloning of time -- that I didnt understand. There is also a certain amount thats neat and funny. But 90 minutes is a long time to spend attending to mind games, especially when the characters don't have minds, only reflexes, and have silly names, and are wearing boxes. The author treats them with contempt, so the audience can't take much interest. He also, of course, places a great technical burden on his actors. However, the playwright, Darren ODonnell, cannot be accused of asking the performers to do anything he wouldnt do himself. He appears as one of the doctors. Paul Fauteux is the other; and they perform perfectly (actors in this kind of show are always perfect, or else they're dead) in a style that seems to owe an immediate debt to the Canadian clowns Mump and Smoot, and a more distant one to the Marx Brothers' mirror routine in Duck Soup. They also, through the wonders of technology and sound-mixing, play the narrators. The action, in Chris Abrahams sharp production, takes place behind a transparent curtain, with the narrators presence denoted by percussive sounds and flickering lights. This is, appropriately enough, black-box theatre.

There is also, as you'd expect in so self-conscious a piece, some explicit involvement of the audience, which is referred to as the people in the room." Humanity, we learn, can always be fooled if you divert it with some "stupid object of no intrinsic value; and the creators are far too smart not to intend an allusion to their own show. At one point, the gods divert Dr. Wishful by taking him to a disco (he accepts the invitation gratefully because Dr. Thoughtless has been neglecting their social life recently) and the music is Dancing Queen, which to a critic fresh from Mamma Mia! seemed like cruel and unusual punishment.

Sometimes, the show comes down from its metaphysical perch to engage in social comment of, considering its pretentions, a rather parochial kind. Somebody or some entity says that nowadays only lard-brained, golf-club swinging, f--k-headed morons" get to be leaders. Not least of things to be held against Mike Harris is the clodhopping level of satire that he inspires. n
