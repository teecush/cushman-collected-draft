A very good Stratford season concludes with a grand production of one of the best plays from the Shakespearean period not to have been written by the house playwright. Ben Jonson's The Alchemist is, along with the same author's Volpone, among the classic English comedies of scam.

We're in a house in London in 1610, during one of the city's recurrent bouts of plague. The owner has fled to the country, leaving his butler in sole possession. He teams up with a con-man named Subtle and a prostitute with the enchanting name of Dol Common. Subtle's racket is to pose as a "cunning man", one whose supernatural knowledge can help his clients to financial or social success. The butler's function is to roam around town, reeling in likely dupes; he adopts the persona of a gamester and soldier, and calls himself Captain Face.

Most of the victims are small-timers, for whom Subtle functions as a glorified fortuneteller. But there is one outsized exception: Sir Epicure Mammon, a knight with fantastic dreams of monetary and sexual glory. For him Subtle assumes the role of alchemist, purveyor of the Philosopher's Stone, the magical substance whose touch can turn base metals into gold. Since Mammon has a lot of base metal already on hand, and is happy to turn it over, he and the gang seem made for one another.

The play begins with the thieves falling out, Subtle and Face both claiming to be the one who does all the work. Dol keeps telling them to calm down and stop yelling, so the volume-level in Antoni Cimolino's production is probably justifiable. Certainly, too, the quarrel is pungently staged and enthusiastically acted. Still, it's a relief when they quiet down and agree to compete over "who shall shark best" that day. The whole action is confined to that one day and that one house: a classical economy unique in Elizabethan/Jacobean drama. The concentration makes for brilliant farce. The play's unique in another way; of all the raucous comedies set in the London of the time, it's the only one to be written entirely in verse. The language is a heady mixture of lyricism and slang; Cimolino's production does an excellent job of keeping the words clear, the action furious, and the characters distinctive, compelling, and extremely funny.

It's hard to say which of the two male leads does in fact shark best. Stephen Ouimette's lank-haired Subtle is the purer charlatan of the two, so ascetically intent in his doubletalk that you almost suspect him of believing it himself. He can certainly be piously indignant when challenged: not that this prevents him from being the dirtiest of fighters with his own colleagues. Ouimette's timing, physical and verbal, is a thing of wonder. Jonathan Goad's Face paints with a broader and more cynical brush; he's a shape-shifter, a man of many Faces, switching between backslapping barfly and scuttling lab assistant at a moments' notice. His energy is unflagging; impressively, he never tries to ingratiate himself with the audience, not at least until the epilogue in which he, the tricksters' trickster, enlists us as his coconspirators. Brigit Wilson's Dol is the voice of exasperated common sense when mediating between the two men, and an enticing decoy when let loose on the more susceptible male customers.

It's when these customers start overlapping that the fun and the desperation move into the highest gear, as the trio wildly improvise to keep their stories straight. We've already had time to size up the visitors, as each in turn makes an introductory appearance. By their clothes we know them; Carolyn M. Smith's period costumes help individualize them, as well as giving us an agreeable window onto a past world. Antoine Yared's Dapper, a lawyer's clerk with gambling ambitions, is defined by his barrister's wig, presumably borrowed and slightly askew: a definition that the actor fills out with properly pedantic insistence. Steve Ross' delightful Abel Drugger, humble pharmacist, wears an apron and a shy smile, one that gets bolder the more thoroughly he is swindled. I felt, as never before in this play, just how these fellows are drawn in, how inevitably one step leads to the next; it's easy, without any modern-dress nudging, to recognize and even identify with them.

Scott Wentworth's Mammon is something else; in the most bloated bloomers ever fashioned, he rhapsodizes over the riches he'll disperse and the harems he'll furnish. There's a luxuriant grandeur to the visions of this Epicure, even a trace of public spirit. When his dreams go up in (literal) smoke and onlookers commiserate on the losses he's sustained he replies, sorrowfully, "not I: the commonwealth." As his skeptical friend Surly, who tries to cheat the cheaters and pays the penalty for his (comparative) honesty, Wayne Best displays an unwonted flair for sardonic humour, first as its mouthpiece, then as its victim. Jamie Mac, fresh-faced and pugnacious in the role of Kastril, the "angry boy" from the country, anxious to learn how to quarrel "by the book," a book, needless to say, in which Subtle is eager to instruct. Indeed, he probably wrote it.

Jonson's is a measured madness. The only characters with whom he reaches Shakespearean heights of spontaneous craziness are his favourite targets, the covetous puritans. Here, however, they fall short: Rylan Wilkie is far too reasonable for the perpetually angry deacon Ananias ("I hate traditions; I do not trust them"), leaving Randy Hughson in the awkward position of playing straight man to a straight man as his superior, Tribulation Wholesome.

The play itself isn't quite perfect; it blows its stack a little too early, condemning the alchemist himself to a rushed, inglorious exit. Or maybe it just seems that way because Ouimette, unlike some Subtles, has earned his titular status, the centrepiece of an explosive, hilarious show.
