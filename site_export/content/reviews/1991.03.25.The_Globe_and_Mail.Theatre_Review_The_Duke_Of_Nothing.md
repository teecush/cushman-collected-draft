THEATRE REVIEW THE DUKE OF NOTHING

Monday, March 25, 1991

ROBERT CUSHMAN

WRITTEN AND DIRECTED BY ALAN WILLIAMS Starring John Jarvis, John Dolan Clare Coulter and Patty Jamieson At Tarragon Extra Space, Toronto Theatre emerges as just another weapon in the inter-cultural war between the two main characters here - one English, the other Canadian Firing cultural salvos in a war of egos BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail ALAN Williams first came to Canada 10 years ago with the improvisational English theatre company Hull Truck and he's still here. My abiding image of a Hull Truck show is of a roomful of people, making like students though clearly well past the age, sitting around drinking, quarrelling, making hopeful"hopeless sexual passes, and generally wondering where all the fire went.

The room always looked like a bedsit, but never was; the characters could afford a full-sized flat, but were reluctant to advertize the fact. Their heads had been shaped in the '60s, and the increasing brutalization of society affronted them, especially when they were doing well out of it.

In his new play The Duke of Nothing, Williams has updated and transplanted all this to Toronto. It is now the '70s that the characters are lamenting - the time when they had some chance to put their ideals into practice. Those ideals are mainly theatrical.

Reg is the artistic director of a small Toronto theatre. Kim is a young actress he lives with. Robertson is a social worker and first-time playwright, whose first-time play Reg is producing. Mary came over from England and is now teaching other first-timers how to write plays, having admitted that she can't do it herself. Also English is Andre, director and all-round loud mouth.

Theatre, says Andre, dictatorially, is a way of saying the unpalatable and making it palatable. Williams's play is very palatable, though I doubt if what he has to say would be all that hard to stomach even if his style were less ingratiating than it is. This is, in fact, the most hilarious play I have heard in ages. It is less strong on structure, in fact it comes off lopsided. They keep talking about theatre, but that never focuses as the real subject; it seems more a play about guys scoring off one another while women wait angrily or resignedly to pick up the pieces.

The play begins and ends with Reg, but Andre steals much of the limelight and most of the thunder. This is not because he is more likeable. Reg may be weak - he has a history of failing to defend his own corner - but Andre is an unlovely mixture of abrasion, conceit and self- pity. He is only just off the plane but, at an hour when any decently sensitive chap would be paralyzed with jet-lag, here he is knocking back the local beer, slagging off the local men and chatting up the local women.

Andre has an infinite capacity for aggression. He can switch targets in a moment from his own country ("I wish I could tell you how much I hate it") to somebody else's ("Like a lot of English people when they arrive in Toronto, I thought, Wow, it's great to be near America.") Introduced to Robertson, who is an Indian (a term he prefers - "people have dreams about Indians"), Andre is lecturing him within two minutes on how he should be writing about "the whole situation." He also takes it as a personal insult when Robertson, an alcoholic, refuses a drink.

"Indians" says Robertson, immortally, "hate to see it when white guys don't like Canada. We think, Gee, another dissatisfied customer." Williams, after ten years here, has a lot to say about both places, much of it scintillating, and he orchestrates his jokes into a war of egos. Neither Reg nor Andre can resist a jab at their own expense, or at anyone else's; conversation becomes a matter of keeping one's cynicism in perfect equilibrium. When the war winds down, so does the play.

Theatre emerges as just another weapon in the inter-cultural war. Often it's a good one; there's an irresistible moment when Andre, surveying the Toronto listings notices that The Mousetrap, eternal in London, has also been running for 12 years here. "I expect," he says "it's performed with much bitter post-colonial irony."

John Jarvis's Reg goes beyond self-effacement into amorphousness. John Dolan as Andre starts with high energy and ascends into one continuous shout. As moral judgments on the characters, these readings may well be perfectly valid; as performances to sit through they quickly become monotonous.

The real strength in the show (and, I suspect, the real sympathy in the writing) comes from Clare Coulter as Mary, a permanently displaced person, witheringly aware of her own deficiencies and everyone else's. She and Kim (Patty Jamieson) sit silent through one prolonged scene between the men, and the silence says a lot. Clayton Odjig's Robertson is, by any orthodox standards, an unskilled performance, but it unquestionably has presence.

So does the play. "Generally," says Reg in one of his best aggressive- defensive moments, "our shores have been untroubled by talented British people - present company excepted, of course." Of course.
