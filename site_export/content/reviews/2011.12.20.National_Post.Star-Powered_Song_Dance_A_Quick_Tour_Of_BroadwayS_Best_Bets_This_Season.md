The movie franchise may have run its course but Harry Potter and his almost-nemesis Severus Snape continue to confront one another, though for a limited time and only along one New York street.

At the Al Hirschfeld Theatre Daniel Radcliffe stars in a revival of the classic musical How to Succeed in Business Without Really Trying. At the Golden Theatre, one block down on West 45th, Alan Rickman has the dominating role in a new play, Seminar. Each of those two actors, it's safe to say, is his shows principal selling point. And both are very good in their roles, though Radcliffes success is the more unexpected of the two.

It's remarkable to find a British actor, barely into adulthood and with limited stage experience, taking such complete command of a big Broadway musical. Not only does he sing and dance with captivating enthusiasm, he is the only member of the cast to get the show's comic-satirical style right. Almost everybody else overplays or underlines or both.

As J. Pierpont Finch, conniving his way up from window washer to chairman of the board at World Wide Wickets Inc., Radcliffe draws a deft, smiley bow-tied outline that he can both inhabit and comment on. His Finch is outrageously, boyishly earnest, daring anyone to doubt him.

Rob Ashfords choreography is altogether uneven; many of the routines are crass and coarse, making you long for the resurrection of Bob Fosse. But there are two wonderful numbers. The first, Grand Old Ivy, spoofing college rah-rah, pits the diminutive Radcliffe against his boss, John Larroquette, whos approximately twice his height and at one point steps right over him. The stature-differential is more spectacularly exploited in the eleven o'clock rouser, Brotherhood of Man, a barbed paean to corporate togetherness in which everybody towers over Radcliffe, who still seems to be both leading and relishing the dance; he's obviously hardworking but without seeming effortful.

A compartmentalized set tries for satire and succeeds at being ugly and constricting, but Frank Loessers snazzy tunes and incomparably funny lyrics are a wonderful reminder of the days when people expected Broadway to be smart.

In Theresa Rebecks play Seminar, Rickman plays Leonard, a once-famous novelist teaching a private writing course to a quartet of twentysomethings. The man is patronizing, vituperative and unshakably aggressive, but this is gradually revealed to be a mask for self-hatred and - of course - passionate devotion to literature. Rickman is masterful with the snark, and in beautiful control of his aria of self-revelation.

Indeed all the actors are good, especially Lily Rabe and Hamish Linklater as the most gifted of the students, at least if we are to believe Leonard's instant judgments; he seems to be a phenomenal speedreader. He has to be, to stop the audience getting bored, but the device contributes to an overall impression of glibness. It's one of those instantly entertaining but quickly forgettable American plays that toy with ostensibly serious subjects.

The biggest hit on Broadway is, of course, The Book of Mormon (Eugene ONeill Theatre) and deservedly. Its become famous for its profanity, which in truth is fairly mild. The real and pleasant surprise is that, apart from being hilarious and well-performed and a true non-adapted original in an age where most musicals are conscienceless recyclings of pre-sold properties, it's about something.

Two recently graduated Mormon missionaries are sent on their probationary assignment to a village in Uganda, which of course has its own problems and couldn't be less interested; nevertheless the boys achieve something. It may not be realistically believable but as somebody says, in one of the shows finest moments, its a metaphor." The two young Elders are a highachieving favourite son, given a virtuoso straight-arrow performance by Andrew Rannells, and a plump and gormless acolyte, an accumulation of giggles and malapropisms that Josh Gad somehow stops from being irritating.

Book and score are by Trey Parker and Matt Stone (of South Park) and Robert Lopez (of Avenue Q); musically and lyrically, the three of them together don't add up to one third of a Frank Loesser; but apart from an interminably campy item set in hell, the songs do their work.

The show manages to make jokes about warlords and poverty and disease without making light of them. That's an achievement. It also pays tribute to traditional Broadway without being patronizing or parasitic.

A crowning attraction is the presence of Nikki M. James, Stratfords Juliet and Cleopatra of three years ago, as the sweetest, toughest, and most delectable of heroines.
