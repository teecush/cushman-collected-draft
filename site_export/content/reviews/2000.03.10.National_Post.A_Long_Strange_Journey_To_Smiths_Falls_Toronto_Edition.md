IF COWS COULD FLY

Artworld Theatre, Toronto

It seems that being the young Allan Merovitz meant growing up in the 1950s at Smiths Falls in the Ottawa Valley as a member of the third generation in a family of Jewish immigrants. Jews were a small minority in town but this never bothered Allan until a squabble with a gentile schoolmate led to some name-calling that had him running home in angry tears to his mother.

The grown-up Merovitz, self-described as "actor, singer and storyteller" (among other things) uses this incident as a springboard for an evening of autobiographical reminiscence. The title, If Cows Could Fly, resembles the proverbial expression about the unlikelihood of pigs having wings; its the kosher version. The show is a friendly but rambling affair that could use some tight editing and a firm decision on what it's supposed to be about.

The best of it comes early on, when Merovitz tells of his grandparents and of their coming to Canada. His maternal grandfather fled Poland at the beginning of the 20th century, travelling by hay cart and railway, to escape conscription. He had a spell in the Antwerp jewellery district, an atmosphere he found stimulating but - - after his previous life as a shoemaker in his native village -- grimy and constricting.

He then fetched up in London where he met his future wife, a refugee from Latvia, already the mother of two daughters and on the run from a husband rendered abusive by fits of epilepsy. Together they embarked for Canada where they found themselves in a tiny, remote fishing village in Nova Scotia, the only Jews the place had ever seen.

This collision of two cultures, each of them about equally remote from a modern metropolitan perspective, provides the most fascinating part of the show; you can sniff the sea-air, and taste the omnipresent fish, cooked every which way. Grandfather Abraham ends up making greatly improved boots and shoes for most of the population. Eventually the family moved on to the wider horizons of Antigonish, and from there to Montreal. We are told that they found it a blessing to arrive in a place where everybody wasn't speaking English.

It was in Montreal that the Waxmans, one side of our narrators family, met the Merovitzes. We learn less about this paternal branch, who had escaped from Russia during the czarist pogroms, but we are told that they managed to remain both prosperous and charitable after the 29 crash, the reason being that grandmother was running a gambling den in her parlour.

Hereabouts, though, the story starts to run thin. We aren't told how Allan's parents got together, or why they moved from Montreal to Smiths Falls. We do hear about other people, as the show succumbs to the fallacy that anything will be dramatically relevant just as long as it happened to one of the authors relatives.

There is a long self-contained sequence about the experiences of Allans uncle as a saboteur working for the British army. This ends the first act, literally with a bang (he blows up a German dam) but dramatically with a whimper.

Act two thrusts us into Merovitz's own boyhood, and gets still more diffuse. There are occasional encounters, triumphant or bizarre, with residual anti-Semitism, but nothing you could hang a theme on. There is a moderately entertaining bit about one of the narrator's aunts, carrying on the family shoe store tradition and terrorizing customers foolish enough to ignore her recommendations.

There is information, fascinating but misplaced, about a band of Jewish soldiers who came up from South America to help defend Canada -- well, English Canada -- in the 18th century. There is a really boring segment about hockey and there is a lengthy and mysterious digression about a friend of the authors who operated a home-made radio station devoted to country music.

Its mysterious because this buddy usurps the hero's own position as centre of the action. He even gets to sing the final song: a self- penned composition, or so we are told. Checking in the program we discover that Merovitz wrote it himself, so maybe his pal is really his alter ego. But it's confusing and a strange way to end.

However the music, and Merovitzs performance of it, are the strongest parts of the show. Most of the singing is in Yiddish, and some of the tunes are traditional chants. Some are klezmer, that beguiling Hebraic anticipation of jazz. But some of the music is outright American; we hear celebratory Yiddish swing and even an angry burst of Yiddish rock n' roll. That's not counting the Nashville stuff.

Its all done with great energy and authority; both in speech and song Merovitz seems to have every Canadian and U.S. idiom down pat.

He has two attendant musicians (who also occasionally, and awkwardly, stray into speaking roles). One is Anne Lederman, a tower of strength on piano, fiddle and musical direction. The other, strumming guitar and mandolin, is Ronnie Weihs, who is also the plays too-indulgent director.
