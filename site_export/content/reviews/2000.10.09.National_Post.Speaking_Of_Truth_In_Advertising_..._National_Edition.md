Till Oct. 22. Box office (416) 531-1827.

RADIO: 30

Tarragon Extra Space

---

In the immortal words of the late George Burns: "The secret of acting is sincerity. If you can fake that, you've got it made." The same principle applies to allied trades, such as commercial voice- overs.

Radio: 30, written and performed by Chris Earle, takes us inside the head of one of its practitioners.

His name is Ron; we meet him, alone in his recording booth, on a day when he's taping a tribute to a burger restaurant.

It's also -- this being a play -- the day on which things go crucially wrong. It's hardly his fault. He's a skilled and conscientious worker, known for the low-key urgency, the buttery persuasiveness with which he can infuse any sales pitch: what's known in the business as "giving it that Ron thing." Today -- as presumably on most days -- he is no sooner inside the studio than he begins running through his script, trying out different inflections and noting the best in the margin. He goes for a take, and the recording engineer -- whom we hear but never see -- can hardly contain his admiration; he calls Ron a machine and a one-take wonder.

However, the sponsors -- who of course are sitting out there in the control room, with powers of life-and-death over the session -- ask for one tiny adjustment: will Ron make one phrase sound slightly more "quirky"? ("A little bit wry?" Ron himself helpfully supplements, and notes it down.)

And then there's discovered to be a slight factual error in the copy, so they have to adjust for that, and -- gradually but inexorably -- the whole thing starts to unravel.

Part of the problem is that Ron, in the intervals between takes, has let his mind wander. He has started to think. He has thought about his previous career as an actor, which was much harder because "it's not just your voice -- you have to lie with your whole body."

He has pondered the paradox of advertising, in which everybody claims not to believe but for which the clients still go on paying. He has reminisced about his personal life, which was brilliant until, as he says, he slept with his best friend's wife.

The implied link between the public and private values of people in the advertising game may seem a bit glib. The convention by which Ron talks to us when not talking into the mic is awkward at first. But the show easily overrides these objections.

The script is very accomplished, the action moving tensely and steadily forward while the speech proves full of echoes and parallels and verbal trap doors that fall open to reveal more about the speaker than he would want anyone -- especially himself -- to know.

"This is good work for a shy person," says Ron, and "shy," in his case, could also mean "remote" or simply "cold." The privileged solitude of the booth, and the inhabitant's heightened relationship with his own voice, are captured with an ease that I, as a sometime practitioner, found eerily recognizable.

I hesitate to congratulate Earle's performance on its truthfulness, but it's 100% convincing. Ron, youngish but no longer quite young, comes off ingratiating and well-meaning and not quite as shallow as he might want to be. He is troubled; he knows that he is only as good as his last performance, and that the Ron thing may go out of style; he has seen it happen himself, to older voice-over talents. He lives always with the knowledge that the last polite "thank you -- that's it" may mean that they think he's wonderful, or it may mean that they've decided to pay him off and get someone else.

Crucial to the show, and superbly executed, is the off-stage characterization of Mike, the guy at the controls, by Robert Smith. Mike has worked many times with Ron and is a fervent admirer of his skills; he may even be a friend. Or he may be an enemy, plotting with the eternally paranoid sponsors. Or he may just be chillingly neutral.

Shari Hollett directed the show, and also worked on the script. And, as a collector of unorthodox credits, I also note the presence of Caroline Gillis, noted actress, as "Girl Friday." I don't know what she did but I'm sure, given the general level of proficiency, she did it well.
