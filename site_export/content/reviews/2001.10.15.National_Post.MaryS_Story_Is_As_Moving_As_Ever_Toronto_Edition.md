THE SECRET GARDEN

Lorraine Kimsa Theatre for Young People, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - The Lorraine Kimsa Theatre, a name I rather doubt will catch on, is what used to be known as the Toronto Young Peoples Theatre. It commences its first re-christened season with one of the most dramatized of Victorian children's classics, Frances Hodgson Burnett's The Secret Garden.

This is the one about Mary Lennox, a spoiled but unhappy child of the British Raj, whose parents have died in a cholera epidemic and who at the age of 10 is sent back to England to live in her uncle's house on the Yorkshire moors. Here, she learns about love and nature and growing things. The rambling manor and its all-important gardens dominate this production, directed by the aptly named Jim Warren: aptly named since Glenn Davidson's elaborate revolving set is very warren-like. We are spun from rooms to passages, from indoors to out. Sometimes we can follow the progress of characters from one area to another; at others, actors, abandoned at the end of the scene, are left conscientiously miming in character as they are whisked away from us. They do their best to look happy about it.

Even when the scenery is standing still, its constant motion seems to have infected the production, which is chronically disinclined to linger over anything. As a result, crucial moments go for nothing. When Mary first meets her new guardian, the humpbacked and reclusive Mr. Craven, it is an occasion not of shock but of mild embarrassment, as if somebody had jumped a cue and turned up on the wrong bit of set. The redemptive ending, rather movingly set up, is rushed abruptly and gracelessly through, as though everybody on stage had a train to catch.

Still, some of the performances are strong, and the story is infallible. In India, Mary had servants to jump to her every command, and she has become a little horror in consequence. In Yorkshire, she is bucked out of that by Martha, the maid, who cheerfully refuses to dress her every morning. Bidden to play in the grounds, she makes friends with a robin (here a cheerful red- breasted puppet) and then with a crusty but good-hearted gardener. She discovers a deserted garden, whose door has been locked ever since Mr. Craven's much-loved and green-fingered wife fell from a tree to her death there. With the help of Martha's brother Dickon, who has a way with both animals and vegetables, Mary restores the place to its former glory. (The progressive greening is very well staged.) They used to call her Mary, Mary, quite contrary; now just see how her garden grows.

She next turns her attention to the stunted life indoors. Right from her arrival, she has heard strange cries, which Mrs. Medlock, the disagreeable housekeeper, dismisses as the sound of the wind "wuthering." (Both house and fable seem to have been designed by the Brontes, working in committee.) Eventually, she finds that these are cries of pain, coming from Mr. Craven's young son Colin, who never leaves his room and whom everybody, including himself, believes to be mortally sick. He is fretful and petulant, but Mary, who has first-hand knowledge of such things, bullies and cajoles him into life. Soon, she and the ever-obliging Dickon have him out in his mother's garden; and when his father, who has been hiding out in Italy, comes home it is to find the place simply bursting with symbols of regeneration. He allows his son back into his previously fenced-off heart and all ends happily. It could be unbearably mawkish, were the children not so unsentimentally believable.

Paul Ledoux's adaptation, which YPT first staged in 1991, is much better than the dark and confusing musical version that played on Broadway a decade ago, but it has, unavoidably, had to leave some things out. These include a melodramatic but helpful subplot concerning the family physician, Mr. Craven's cousin, who has his own financial reasons for wishing Colin's death; here he just seems to be an unaccountably bad doctor. (It doesn't help that both the Craven gentlemen are played, presumably through financial necessity, by the same actor. It cramps both their styles.)

The children are played by young adults, and played well, with a helpful leavening of humour. Ramona Gilmour-Darling makes no bones about Mary's initial awfulness -- in fact, she revels in it -- and shows too how it connects with her later role as a ministering angel. She always wants her own way, but her way becomes a better one. Derek Aasland is sure-footed as Colin, incorrigibly the young master, and Richard Harte makes Dickon likeable, which, given his alarming reserves of natural wisdom and practical charity, is an accomplishment. Kate Hemblen is a direct and charming Martha, though both her role and the story's social structure have been impoverished by the cuts. The grown-ups playing grown-ups are generally less impressive, with Tamsin Kelsey uncertain from one moment to the next whether Mrs. Medlock is an inhuman monster or just a human one; faced with the rejoicing at the end, she gamely changes character and smiles anyway.
