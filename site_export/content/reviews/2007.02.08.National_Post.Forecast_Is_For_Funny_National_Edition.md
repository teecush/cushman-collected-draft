COMEDY REVIEW

Tip of the Melting Iceberg

Second City, Toronto

Many years ago, children-- so many that I can remember neither the date nor the title of the show -- there was a Toronto Second City revue that struck me at the time and strikes me still as one of the funniest things I have ever seen. One of the items that have stayed with me is set in a TV newsroom, where a report about the world coming to an end in five millennia was misread on air as "in five minutes," leading to much consternation and dropping of defences. The weatherman was especially affected. You guys, he said tearfully to the two anchors, "are the only friends I've ever had." Actually, Jack, one of them replied, "I never liked you much."

I was reminded of this last item by a scene in the new and excellent Second City show Tip of the Melting Iceberg. It picks up the same comic principle of people revealing themselves in extremis and goes deeper with it. Three guys, old friends, have come to their favourite meeting spot. One of them is about to get married, and has asked one of the others to be his best man, leaving the third feeling rejected and resentful. The chosen one reveals some truly shocking news about his own prospects, thus putting a crimp in everybodys plans. After the initial, and surprisingly slight, intake of breath, the audience watches in delight as the trio go into competitive paroxysms of self pity, self-absorption and plain old selfishness.

It could be that selfishness is at the root of all comedy. Its certainly at the root of farce, and revue-sketch humour is farcical almost by definition; it doesn't have the time to be anything else. This Second City show, smartly directed by Steven Morel, is especially well-equipped in this respect since, as its title suggests, it is from time to time about the environment. (We've learned by now that its only from time to time that Second City revues bear any relation to their titles. Weve also learned to live with it.) So it can satirize the things we do and the ways in which we ignore or excuse them. The opening number depicts a man juggling cellphone calls while driving through a built-up area and eventually hitting something in his path. The closing number, a song, sounds for a distressing moment as if the company is about to go earnest and preachy on us, but it soon regains its poise and reverts to being cynical, which is far more effective, even as propaganda. Somewhere in between there's an apoclayptic weather report from the near future; it doesn't cover Quebec, which has independent weather, and is disquietingly evasive about just what has happened to Halifax.

The five writer-performers are great. Anand Rajaram keeps shyly and uncharacteristically in the background for the first 20 minutes or so; this turns out to be a strategy since he first makes his mark as a worker in an office so nervous about racial profiling that it wont even allow him to recommend a good Indian restaurant. Rajaramis himself a one-man -- sorry one-person-- commentary on colourblind casting since he appears to equal effect in numbers where his ethnicity couldnt matter less and in numbers where it couldn't matter more.

Jim Annan is perhaps the most inner-directed of the team, untouchable within each of his personae, whether as a Canadian border guard applying for a gun like the Americans have (he'd prefer an AK-47) or a male panda in a zoo whod rather chew bamboo than help perpetuate his species though, push come to shove, hes prepared to settle for doing both at once. Annan, who is squat, has a Mutt-and- Jeff thing going with Scott Montgomery, who is lanky, and who shines especially as a corporate bozo trapped at a reception in the AGO, reeling off descriptions and interpretations of the exhibits at virtuoso speed while proudly maintaining an attitude of philistine contempt.

There are two ladies present. Lauren Ash specializes in outsize voices, reserving the deepest, slowest and lonesomest for a girl with multiple physical afflictions and an imaginary boyfriend whose invitation to take her to the prom she has, for obvious logical reasons, to refuse. (He's devastated; he thought he was real.) Leslie Seiler is a smooth blond, presenting Air Canada's new itemized-billing scheme; as she sweetly explains to a client, if you're going to order all those tasty in-flight drinks, then you'd better reserve bathroom privileges as well.

This, then, is a fast-moving, good-looking (designs by the feted Camellia Koo), quickthinking and, on occasion, rather courageous show. The theatrical funniness in Tip of the Melting Iceberg builds, so that you laugh till you cry.
