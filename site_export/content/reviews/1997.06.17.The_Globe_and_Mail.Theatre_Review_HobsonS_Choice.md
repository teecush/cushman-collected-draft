HOBSON'S CHOICE

THEATRE REVIEW

HOBSON'S CHOICE

Tuesday, June 17, 1997

Impeccably constructed tale bubbles with comic spirit

THESE are the principal characters of Hobson's Choice , currently onstage at the Shaw Festival and one of the few genuinely infallible pieces in the English-speaking repertoire:

Henry Horatio Hobson, abusive, alcoholic proprietor of a 19th-century shoe store, a tyrant to his employees, a monster to his family, and a monument of stinginess to all; Maggie, his eldest daughter, dauntingly capable and businesslike, riding roughshod over everybody's wishes in the successful pursuit of her own; Willie Mossop, her father's best workman (whom she stampedes into marrying her), a genius at bootmaking but a repressed worm in all other respects, blossoming under her tutelage into the most unbridled egotist on a stage that groans with them.

Taken one by one, they are a remarkably unlovely trio. Thrown together in the presence of an audience they magically generate warmth.

First seen in 1915, Hobson's Choice looks back thirty years to the thrift and promise of late-Victorian Lancashire. Even in its own day it had period charm, a burnished quality that never denies hardship but carefully distances it. It's one of the Manchester school of plays -- hard-headed but warm-hearted -- that gave birth to the regional repertory movement, itself the foundation of British theatre for the next half-century. There are other plays in the group that deserve an outing, but this is the only one to be regularly revived -- and the only work by which its prolific author, Harold Brighouse, is generally remembered. He keyed into legend here, invoking elements of King Lear and Cinderella and every Victorian manual on self-improvement. Maggie rescues Willie from her father's domain and they set up in business together, successfully draining trade away from the old firm which is left fruitless and -- almost literally -- bootless. Old man Hobson spends all his time in the pub; his two younger daughters are greedy, ineffectual snobs waiting for eligible young men to carry them off. The difference between their selfishness and Maggie's is that hers is constructive and -- beneath it all -- loving. When father's liver gives way, the only ones who will return to look after him are Maggie and her Will . . . at a price.

More of a tale than a closely knit intrigue -- built, that is, more like a novel than a play -- the piece is still in its own terms impeccably constructed. Every line tells, for character, action, ambience or all three, and though there are no quotably funny lines the text bubbles with comic spirit: a frank relish of human frailty and human resource.

Christopher Newton's staging is keen and unobtrusive with one or two humane and individual touches. Traditionally, the play's wedding scene has ended with Maggie dragging Willie by his ear into the bridal chamber. In this production he goes, after some initial hesitation, of his own accord. This fits the arc of Simon Bradbury's performance, which is of steadily increasing assurance. Even at the beginning he is nobody's fool; by the end, taking steely measure of his father-in-law's property, he is progressive capitalism on the march. This makes the character less endearing than usual but more credible. Michael Ball's Hobson, by contrast, is altogether too endearing: a fuzzy, teddy-bearish figure and something of a hole in the play. Instead of flourishing his vices as weapons against the world, he hugs them to himself like the minor foibles of a black-sheep uncle. He presents no threat, and his fall is no surprise.

Still, he times his laughs well, and he has one virtuoso sequence: a long, agonized toying with his tea cup before his final capitulation. Corrine Koslo's Maggie is a short, chunky dynamo whose determination is bred of past endurance -- she has winced under the title of "old maid" -- and tempered by a virtuous desire to inflict no more suffering than is absolutely necessary. (The play is, among other things, a hymn to the English Noncomformist conscience.) At the performance I saw Koslo was overpaced and underenergized -- a cycling accident had nearly put her out of action -- but she was still, as she should be, the heart of the play.

As a good repertory playwright, Brighouse hands opportunities to everyone, and the supporting cast take them, for the most part, ably if not spectacularly. Barry MacGregor has the gift role of the Scots doctor who terrorizes Hobson into abstinence and he gobbles it up, more voraciously than he might with a more assertive antagonist. The production adds a character, a clog-dancing delivery boy, dropped in presumably for local colour. It isn't necessary; the play breathes its origin in every line, and William Schmuck's designs -- a series of chintzy or stony interiors backed by a vista of mill-country smoke stacks -- positively belches it forth.


  


At the Shaw Festival Theatre until June 28 .
