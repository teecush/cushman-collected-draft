ARCHER

Theatre Centre, Toronto

Once there was a kind of play for which the stock critical adjective was "civilized." It especially flourished in France in the middle decades of the 20th century, and it was a mainstay of high- end British radio back in the days when broadcasting was broadcasting.

It was written in ornate, epigrammatic prose that aspired to the poetic and it drew its narrative inspiration from Greek mythology. Sometimes it left the stories in their original settings; sometimes it tried to update them with much feverish application of makeup compacts and philosophical smoking of Gauloises. Philip Shepherd's Archer is a late, Canadian entry in the same tradition.

Time-wise, it has things both ways. It tells the story of Odysseus ( Ulysses, if you're a Latinist), who functioned at the siege of Troy as the Greek army's one-man think-tank. It takes him from the last night of the 10-year war, crouching with his fellows inside his personally designed wooden horse prior to getting out and massacring everyone, to his return home after a further 10 years of wandering, just in time to pick up his mighty bow and save his wife Penelope from her obstreperous bunch of unwelcome suitors by shooting them full of arrows.

That's half the play. The other half, running in parallel with it, concerns Archie, who leaves his home on Toronto Island to retrieve his daughter's Barbie doll from the mainland home of his wife's sister. We, apparently, have Barbie like the Greeks had Helen; and it's unclear whether the purpose is to show how far we have fallen, or to cut the old myth down to size; to shed some borrowed glory on our own times, or merely to do a little light dumping on everything. Missing the boat home Archie takes refuge in a hotel; he, like Odysseus, runs into surprises and temptations before getting home, 23 hours later, to his wife whose name is Penny.

Shepherd is not the first writer to have tried cramming the events of Homer's Odyssey into a single modern day; James Joyce once had a go. But not even Joyce tried to tell both the new and the old stories simultaneously. The trouble with Shepherd's version is not so much that one tale undercuts the other as that the author doesn't seem aware of it. The story of Archie even undercuts itself. It is mainly told to us in the third person -- by Archie's wife, who acts like a harpy, and by his sister-in-law, who acts silly. It's ages before we meet Archie himself, and by the time we do it's too late for us to take him seriously, even though it now appears that the author wants us to.

Meanwhile we have had the Greek story, presented as a series of recitations. The actors -- there are three, plus a musician -- tread carefully through the language, which is all-enveloping. Occasionally they say something really interesting: as when the nymph Calypso, who entraps the hero for years on her idyllic island and can't understand why he wants to go home, confesses "I'm afraid of pain, all the gods are." But they discuss feelings rather than express them, and much of the writing simply washes over you; anyway, it washed over me.

The goddess Circe has a long speech about Odysseus' proposed descent into Hades ("the underworld is hunger itself and would scarf you down") that itself descends into high-pressure obscurity, and stays there a long time. The actress, Alison Lawrence, climaxes this aria with some light, rhythmic tapping on her throat: one of the more hypnotic visual moments of the evening.

The producing company calls itself Phyzikal Theatre; and the director, Steven Rumbelow, boasts in his bio the direction of 100 music videos and of "one of the world's foremost physical theatre companies, Triple Action Theatre." He gets his actors here to do a fair amount of arm-waving, but nothing really out of the ordinary.

The finest effect is the presentation of the Cyclops: a crouching figure swathed in a black cloak with a light-bulb in front representing the monster's single eye. The cloak is then thrown off, to reveal the maid at Archie's hotel, giving him a scare with some furiously noisy vacuuming.

Clever though this is, it sums up the show's essential misconception. The maid who wants to clean your room is not as menacing as the ogre who wants to eat you: nowhere near. Archie's uncomfortable night on the town is not in the same league as the travels and travails of Odysseus. The worst thing that happens to Archie is being trapped in an elevator wth a lady lounge-singer, also called Calypso. The sexiest thing is being invited to her room when they're released, an offer that he primly rejects.

Leanne Dixon plays the Cyclops and the various wives. The author plays the twin-protagonists. He has an appropriately taut bow- string of a voice that he seems to enjoy flexing; to cast yourself as a guy whose wife waits faithfully for 20 years while nymphs and goddesses across the Adriatic just throw themselves at you must be quite a trip.

The play has all the literary pretentions and ironic double- bluffing of the old school of classics revisited; the production adds in the more recent conceit that an old myth will fill the stage with dark primal energy, something that somehow never seems to hapen. Also in there is a quite sensible metaphor about humanity taking responsibility of its own fate and declining to be a target for the gods, or fate, or whatever. So it isn't all that bad; just sort of terrible.
