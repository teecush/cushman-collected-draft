THEATRE REVIEW THE DIARY OF ANNE FRANK

Saturday, May 11, 1996

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Written by Frances Goodrich and Albert Hackett Directed by Leah Cherniak Starring Waneta Storms At Young People's Theatre in Toronto until May 26 Rating: Flawless acting unlocks Diary BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail EIGHT Jews hiding from the Nazis in an attic in Amsterdam, remaining there just over two years, finally betrayed and captured, herded off to concentration camps, only one of them surviving. Those seven deaths are the tiniest fraction of six million but the truism remains true: The only way to get some emotional purchase on the Holocaust is to remember that each statistic represents an individual agony, the tragedy of one innocent, terrified, defenceless person.

Add in the romance of the young girl's journal, miraculously preserved until after the war, and it's no wonder that The Diary of Anne Frank has become a classic. The dramatization by Frances Goodrich and Albert Hackett - two otherwise undistinguished Broadway and Hollywood writers - was first staged in New York in 1955, and it bears some marks of its time and place. It compresses its narrative into half-a-dozen shapely scenes, where later writers would probably have gone for something starker, more episodic, and a lot harder to sit through. But the new ways are not necessarily better; and Goodrich and Hackett, probably writing the only way they knew how, have given us an all-American family drama gone frighteningly askew.

I see nothing wrong with making soap opera out of the Holocaust, so long as it's honest soap opera. I don't want distinguished writers pirouetting over this subject, telling me how sensitive they are. We can all identify with these characters - partly from life, partly from convention - and that is what the play is for. That's the reason for putting it on a stage and making a shared experience of it. In Berlin in the fifties it was a nightmare of collective guilt; audience members couldn't look at one another or, in some cases, at the stage. For us, it's an imaginative opportunity to remember history or to learn it.

I have never seen the play before, but Leah Cherniak's production at Young People's Theatre must be as good as it can get. (And after my recent strictures on YPT it's a pleasure to say that.) She opens the play up a little, framing the stage with blown-up photos; and the final arrival of the Nazis, unseen but very heard, is a more elaborate aural nightmare than I imagine the Broadway of the fifties would have tolerated. Essentially, though, she gives us the characters, in flawless performances, flawlessly merged.

In most dramatizations of this subject the interesting characters are the persecutors and the onlookers, because they alone have some freedom of choice. Here, though, we can concentrate on the victims, because, however stressed, they refuse to act like victims. The point of the play is that, for as long as it's allowed to, life goes on; and it's a messy and occasionally joyful business. Anne herself (Waneta Storms) is adolescence in a pressure cooker, exasperating and occasionally charming her elders, nearly as much as she charms and exasperates herself. Her father (Jerry Franken), the only character who seems somewhat idealized, is still credible as a decent businessman and natural leader. Her quiet, bewildered mother (Robin Craig) finally erupts when her own unwritten rules are transgressed. Sister Margot (Marjorie Campbell) is unwaveringly, almost unbearably, sympathetic.

The more colourful family with whom they share their refuge are the Van Daans: father (Jim Warren) a powerful-looking man who is jelly within; mother (Tanja Jacobs - the showiest performance, and that is not a putdown) flirtatious, possessive and desperately clinging; their son (Derek Metz) a wonderful picture of hunched, calf-like, growing boyhood. A solitary late arrival (John Jarvis) is a petulant permanent outsider. Then there are the benefactors: the factory owner (Jarvis), as worried as he is kind; the secretary Miep (Missy Christensen), the link with the outside world, who is not above tingeing her devotion with silent exasperation when her charges' demands become extreme. (Her real-life original appeared at this year's Oscars, where she was justifiably acclaimed as "a real heroine.") Charlotte Dean's set is a fine picture of enforced neatness overlaid with rebellious clutter. There is very little sentimentality here; these are, or were, real people. As were all the others. And so, as this most hideous of centuries trundles to its conclusion, do they continue to be.
