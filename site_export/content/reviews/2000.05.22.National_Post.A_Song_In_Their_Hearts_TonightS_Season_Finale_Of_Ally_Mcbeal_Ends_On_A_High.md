In the season finale of Ally McBeal (tonight at 9) everybody sings. Well, nearly everybody. The cast, we are told, will burst into a dozen Randy Newman songs (one of them new), plus one from Madonna (Hanky Panky, yet) and a couple of more innocent tunes from The Music Man. All of it, apparently, ties in with the plot.

This isn't the first time a TV drama series has gone musical. A couple of years ago there was an all-singing, all-dancing Chicago Hope special, directed, as it happens, by the same man, Bill D'Elia, responsible for tonights Tin Pan Ally. This, according to one report, will be different: "[The Chicago Hope episode] was akin to a classic movie musical, in which a tune stops the action. Ally McBeal, however, treads ground broken by the British miniseries The Singing Detective. DElia himself is then quoted as saying, Characters sing what they were saying, and it moves the comedy or drama along as it would have been had they been speaking."

It's unclear whether it's DElia or the reporter whos responsible for the Singing Detective comparison, but it reads like typical ignorance. In the first place, the characters in The Singing Detective didn't sing what they were saying; in fact, they didn't sing at all -- they mimed, to old pop records that were playing in their heads and that in some way echoed their present state of mind. Far from forwarding the action, they halted it, often enjoyably, in its tracks. The same author, Dennis Potter, had used the same technique in his previous series, Pennies from Heaven, so if he did break any ground, that was where he broke it. Second and more importantly, telling the story through song is what stage, and to a lesser extent, film musicals have always done. What else, to take the most obvious example possible, is Gene Kelly doing when he starts singin in the rain?

The fact that Ally: The Musical is not a death-defying leap into the dramatic unknown need not, of course, prevent it from being fun. If Calista Flockhart has any previous track record as a song-and- dance queen its escaped my notice, but her colleagues include at least two performers who have in their time stopped shows on Broadway: Jane Krakowski (Grand Hotel) and James Naughton (City of Angels and much else). You can find the same kind of talent in almost any regular TV lineup, drama or comedy. American theatre breeds great all-rounders. Maybe not all American actors can sing, but a huge percentage seem to have a kind of musical know-how. Musical performers can generally act, and since a full-time career in musical theatre is now impossible to sustain, many of them gravitate to TV.

Chicago Hope's musical excursion was of course spurred by its having Mandy Patinkin, a bona fide musical star, even if many people find his singing unbearable. And if a singing surgeon seems even more risible than a singing detective, what do you make of a singing shrink? Kelsey Grammer, a closet musical star if ever there was one, can do more than warble stylishly over the closing credits; he's sung Sondheims Sweeney Todd. Bebe Neuwirth made her name if not her fortune in musicals; her Second Murderess in Chicago was the only full-throttle star-quality performance I have seen on Broadway in a decade, and you can hear echoes of it in her great recurring hatchet job on Frasier, playing Grammers former wife; the producers of his Macbeth surely missed a trick by not casting her as his Lady.

Elliott Gould, now a daddy in Friends, started out in musicals (and was once, I believe, married to an actress who did the same). Seinfeld had in Jason Alexander the most accomplished musical- comedy man of his generation, though it resisted the temptation to let him, or anyone else, sing a note.

Such asceticism, though, is heroically rare. Most TV comedies like to squeeze in a musical interlude when they can; and now, as we see, dramas are following suit. A friendly editor at the Post suggested to me that this may be a sign that musicals are coming back. I think it's likelier to be the reverse. I think it's a sign that theyve gone, maybe for good, and that people enjoy playing with the memories. I think its camp.

To say that musicals have died may seem perverse considering that in the theatre they're making more money and drawing bigger audiences than ever. But none of the recent crop of stage successes are truly new; they're all revivals or built round old songs, or -- most depressingly -- adaptations of brand-name movies. The last trend does have an ironic appropriateness. All the legendary stage shows owe their real fame to their presence on film, and there is a sizeable, though not inexhaustible, list of original movie musicals with similar or greater resonance: Top Hat, The Wizard of Oz, Singin in the Rain. The line, though, has long since died out; in the last 30 years there have only been two attempts to revive it: Peter Bogdanovichs At Long Last Love and Woody Allen's Everyone Says I Love You, both of which used great old songs and both of which demonstrated a) that the charms of non-singing singers have their limits and b) even if you can carry a tune, it won't carry you if you dont have a style or a story.

Television itself has no tradition of original musicals. There were a few in the 50s and 60s, usually by old Broadway hands. They were mostly forgettable though one, Rodgers and Hammersteins Cinderella,was a classic; it was ritually desecrated a couple of years ago in a routinely philistine network revival. On TV, as in movies, the only recent evidence of musical style has come in cartoons.
