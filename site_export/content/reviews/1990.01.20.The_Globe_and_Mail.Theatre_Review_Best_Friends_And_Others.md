THEATRE REVIEW BEST FRIENDS AND OTHERS

Saturday, January 20, 1990

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Directed by Scott Burke Written by Edie Pistolesi Starring the Harbinger Theatre company In the centre of a dispiriting tradition BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail THE HARBINGER Theatre is a new company, and what it is harbinging is an era of new Canadian drama, building on the best traditions of the past three decades. That, at least, is the substance of its opening manifesto, though the ringing phrases tail off into a few lines of special pleading on behalf of Harbinger's first show, which happens to be written by an American.

Edie Pistolesi's Best Friends and Others is not, truthfully, a script worth breaking one's inaugural vows for. It is a collection of seven vignettes, some of them too slight too qualify even as one-act plays, all standing smack in the centre of a dispiriting tradition. Call it the cult of good-hearted domesticity. However dark it gets, it excludes the larger world. One does not want playwrights to spend their time self-consciously addressing issues - that it is the kind of thing that has been stifling British drama - but one would like to see people on stage with some believable connection to a society that penalizes its poor, lays waste its cities, and finds the damndest ways of exporting democracy. David Mamet and Sam Shepard, neither of them political dramatists in the glib sense, show us such people. Nobody else does.

Certainly Edie Pistolesi doesn't. She is a young playwright, showing some evidence of humor and observation, and may be capable of devloping them. She cites Harold Pinter as an inspiration but leaves out his cruelty, substituting a vague melancholia. Two of her pieces are female monolgues. One woman compulsively devours M & Ms; the other's life revolves around her ironing-board. Well, what's it matter as long as they're happy? And it would be perfectly possible to take the two playlets as defiant assertions of a person's rights to her own simple pleasures. But Pistolesi herself declares that she thrives on subtext. (It is probably unfair to use an author's extra-dramatic statements against her but, given the opportunity, who can resist?). If the text says somebody's happy, the only subtext can be that she isn't. Maybe it's the way we've been programmed, but the message all these plays seem to be booming out is: Oh, look at all the lonely people.

In the neatest sketch, Waiting, a young woman professes herself to be waiting, not unhopefully, for just about everything but especially for Mr. Right. But when he, or an approximation of him, arrives, his timing is off. This is amusingly revealed to us in a blacked-out bedroom scene; climax for him is frustration for her. (This must be a breakthrough; premature ejaculation is usually portrayed, if at all, as the man's problem.) "Damn it," she cries, "why couldn't you wait for me?" That's funny.

Two other segments, like Mr. Right, promise more than they can perform. In one, a waitress is goaded to desperation by a regular customer who takes no interest in the day's special, the salad dressing, her undressing, or anything except the book he is reading. The build is excellent, but then the man turns out to be crazy. (How, exactly, must not be revealed.) But since there is no attempt at exploring him - in this show women are subjects, men are objects - it is a cheap random pay-off.

In the other, a wife and mistress find that their experiences of their common mate are very similar. This is amusing if not very startling, but then it turns out that mistaken identity has been committed, leaving us to infer not just that one man is alike but that they all are. And then farcical melodrama takes over, or tries to.

That leaves us with two unre deemed clinkers. In Spitting Image, a woman, searching endlessly for the son she abandoned at his birth 20-odd years ago, meets a young man searching endlessly for the mother who, and I refuse to complete this sentence.

Supermarket is the title and setting of the finale piece, and the only one to deploy the acting company at its full five-fold strength. A man and woman fall in love over still-frozen chiffon cake, consummate in the car- park and marry in the aisles. Meanwhile, two women compare shopping-lists, the honors going to one whose purchases are dictated by the spirit of Elvis Presley. At the end, these two fantasies coalesce. But we know that supermarkets are not churches, and that their managers are not empowered to conduct weddings, and it would take more wit and imaginative power than this writer can muster to persuade us otherwise. What comes through is that these poor people, symbolizing millions of others, are just shoppers drugged by the mart. It may be meant as a celebration but it plays as a whimsical sneer.

Carol Sinclair, as a manic frump who shops by the alphabet, could get laughs with a jab of her elbow and a gleeful push of her trolley. And since, in Scott Burke's production, this section is largely staged as an angular ballet, she does. Earlier, she plays the importunate waitress, escalating from wounded goodwill to erotic frenzy, and the progessively disillusioned mistress; and she conveys the pain of both without ever losing her comic balance. Strangely, she makes least impact in what should be her tour-de-force as the soliloquizing ironist (i.e. one who irons) but she has an invaluable gift for aerating dubious lines by gently deflecting them.

Nora Sheehan is more inclined to bash them over but she has her moments. Kate Johnston, the one non-Equity player, lacks defintion. Michael Howell, playing the older men, has little to do except beam with varying degrees of self-satisfaction, and Doug Curtis, playing the younger, has less.

If Harbinger needs the excuse, there is nothing here that need seem alien to Canada. The condition of soap-opera, after all, is that everybody can identify with it. Condescension masquerading as compassion is an infallible formula. The clinical description is sentimentality.
