Bernard Shaw set just one major play in his native Ireland, and the split loyalties he must have felt between that country and his adopted England are reflected in its guarded, off-centre title. John Bulls Other Island is an untidy piece, suggesting that Shaw was cramming into it all he ever felt about Ireland, regardless of its immediate relevance. Not coincidentally, its also, or so it seems in Christopher Newton's production at the Shaw Festival, his angriest play. It contains at least two transfixing eruptions, one of them emanating from a minor character and one from a major.

The minor one is Hodson, a valet in the employ of the Englishman Tom Broadbent who, with his Irish business partner Larry Doyle, embarks on a trip to Larry's old home town of Roscullen. Hodson, accompanying them, is all deference until he finds himself alone with a native Roscullener, indulging in the familiar local custom of bemoaning the luck of the Irish. At which point Hodson loses it; dropping his vocational toffs accent for his native cockney, he tells his companion that until hes experienced London poverty, he doesn't know what suffering is. The switch from gentleman's gentleman to workers worker is blistering, especially as executed here by David Schurmann, who is himself, under normal casting circumstances, the most gentlemanly of the Festivals actors. You can feel the release.

Not that the play denies the Irish their grievances. Its justly celebrated last scene confronts Broadbent and Doyle, who are in the development game and intend to play it in Roscullen, with Peter Keegan, a defrocked priest whom the locals still persist in calling father and who is something of a Hibernian St. Francis; we first meet him communing with a grasshopper, and he is later moved to passionate indignation over the fate of a pig whom he calls "our brother." He also speaks of the Earth as Hell. He knows exactly what the visitors, with their grandiose schemes for hotels and golf courses, are up to, and his excoriating summary of it is the evening's second fit of transcendent rage. He then proceeds to a vision of Ireland as a country where the State is the Church and the Church the people and of the world as "a godhead in which all life is human and all humanity divine." Among lapsed Catholics, he's on a plane of his own, and he inspires his author to a visionary prose unique in his work. Jim Mezon, who can do marvels with quietness, plays him as fiercely gentle, a tormented soul physically and spiritually apart, and also funny. This, after his work in A Moon for the Misbegotten and The Cherry Orchard, completes a triptych of performances alike only in -- and I try to use the word sparingly--greatness.

Keegan may get the glory but the plays heavy lifting is done by Tom and Larry, whose approaches to Ireland are, respectively, enchanted and embittered. Benedict Campbell and Graeme Somerville give strong anchoring performances in the roles, though both are unduly self-conscious. Somerville goes into periodic vocal trances, as if Doyle were turning his sarcastic lyricism on and off like a tap; Campbell suggests not only that he sees through Broadbent, whos as bouncingly and bone-headedly and victoriously English as only a Shavian Englishman can be, but that Broadbent sees through himself. Which is going a bit far.

Among the native prizes Broadbent carries off is Nora, the local heiress and an old love of Larry's, whom Severn Thompson plays with enchanting romantic circumspection. Thom Marriott stands tall among her neighbours as a bigoted priest, frocked, whom Sean O'Casey might have drawn. OCasey might also have got more uproarious fun than Shaw does from the fate of the pig, taken for a disastrous ride in Broadbents car, though he, too, would probably have left us wanting to see it for ourselves rather than just hearing about it. Mary Haney provides an upright centre to a fine bunch of town rowdies, debating their country's future while obsessing over their own feuds; they include Guy Bannerman, Patrick McManus, Jonathan Widdifield as a terrified youth, and Ric Reid as two identical stage Irishmen named Haffigan, the first one mocked as a fake, the other presented as the genuine article: Shaw could be shameless. He was also, unmistakably, an urban Irishman patronizing the peasantry. But he had a tragic sense, and though the play was written and set a decade before the official Troubles, it's still troubling enough. Structurally and emotionally its all over the map, but the map has the authors blood on it.

Another deracinated Celt is on view in Do Not Go Gentle, an ambiguous celebration of Dylan Thomas, apparently set in Purgatory and performed by Geraint Wyn Davies, another actor who has, in the last few Stratford seasons, blossomed extraordinarily. He does finely by the staple Thomas poems, especially the titular exhortation to his dying father, which here concludes the first half and seems directed more at himself. The relation to his father isn't very central; indeed there's little discernible focus to the piece, which was concocted by another fine Welsh-Canadian actor, the late Leon Pownall. Wyn Davies presents Thomas, with considerable charm, as rather a bashful boyo, despite his boasted amatory and alcoholic prowess. He also recites a lot of Shakespeare, with whom Thomas, on slender biographic evidence, is said to have felt a lifelong and of course hopeless rivalry: especially hopeless in this context, since Shakespeares stuff, unlike Thomass, was actually written for the theatre. He gets to do "fear no more the heat o' the sun" as an 11 o'clock number: Beautiful, of course, but it leaves the supposed subject of the evening definitively upstaged.
