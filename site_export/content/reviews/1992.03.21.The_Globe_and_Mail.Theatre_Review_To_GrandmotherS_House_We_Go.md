THEATRE REVIEW TO GRANDMOTHER'S HOUSE WE GO

Saturday, March 21, 1992

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Toronto ONT -- WRITTEN BY JOANNA M. GLASS Directed by John Wood Starring Rita Howell, Marion Gilsenan and Joyce Campion At the Bluma Appel Theatre Rating: This old-style family play, and a very accomplished one, contains just one surprise, whose detonation is superbly timed Grandma, what big ideas you have BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Toronto ON Wednesday we had Brother Andre's Heart, in which a grandmother pays a call on the young folks. On Thursday it was Joanna Glasss play, which gives us, as promised in the title, the same situation in reverse. Who says the Toronto theatre lacks variety?

The grand-matriarch of the Glass menagerie is an 81-year-old New Englander. Her house is large, handsome, and a brute to manage. She lives in it with a slightly younger brother who is a whimsical ex-academic, a widowed daughter who paints, and a supernally devoted Irish servant, everybodys nanny. The daughter's own children, one male and two female, are now in their 30s. Each has had a failed marriage, and each is limping through the resultant wreckage. The family home is their haven, often deserted, always taken for granted. When we meet them they have returned in force for Thanksgiving. All have plans to stay longer, and to fill the house with their own children. Grandie, as they call her, quails at the sacrifices that are still expected of her. But, in good patrician fashion, she grits her teeth and agrees to accommodate yet another generation.

Grandie is no saintly pushover. She is an impoverished chatelaine, caustically outspoken about modern manners, and none too forbearing with her contemporaries. Her polar opposite in the play is her grandson's new girlfriend, vigorously up-to-date about everything from finance to food. (Her habit of chewing her bread without first breaking it drives Grandie to distraction.) The play extracts comedy from the prejudices of both age groups. In general, though, we laugh with the old woman and at the young one. Glass, who apparently married into just such a family herself, writes as an outsider but as one who has been taken over.

This is as much a matter of technique as of substance. This is an old- style family play, and a very accomplished one. Its narrative contains just one surprise, whose detonation is superbly timed. The presentation matches the text. Patrick Clarks set, which lays out the all-important battleground, is enough to make you purr with pleasure: so detailed and so beautifully proportioned in its clutter, an infinitely desirable residence, fraying at the edges. John Munros lighting is exquisite, especially when streaming through the windows of a morning. John Wood's direction balances the scenic and human elements with immaculate skill.

The play neither sentimentalizes nor caricatures. There are no epiphanies or sudden reconciliations. The juniors may be more blinkered and objectionable in their selfishness, but they have no monopoly on it. Even the faithful domestic turns out to have her own agenda, while to the widowed daughter, whose efforts at the easel are either patronized or ignored, goes the best speech in the play, one in which she flatly and finally refuses to adapt herself to anyone else's convenience. This is a worm-turning moment to inspire any actress; and Marion Gilsenan, an admirably amorphous mass in her earlier scenes, chisels herself into dignity and independence before our eyes. You can feel the audience reflecting with mingled guilt and admiration on how it has treated its own mother.

She deserved her exit applause. All the same, I wished that the scene had not been so obviously structured for her to achieve it, just as I resented the similar response exacted by Grandies anticipation of the behaviour of her great-grandchildren, who will fill her house with videos "full of extra-terrestrials who explode at the end. That speech, too, explodes at the end, and people do not talk like that: not even, I would hazard, in Connecticut. I have no quarrel with Rita Howells performance of the old lady, but does the character have to make such an emphatic claim on adjectives like feisty" and "indomitable"? And does the nanny have to be Irish? And do the grandchildren have to fall into high-pitched speech rhythms that have less to do with life than with the actors' recollection of similar performances in other plays? The well-made drama, of which this is a rare late example, always seems to demand belief and almost always frustrates it. Similarly with the well-made comedy; I recognized that I was in a climate of wit, but I laughed only once because only one comic moment caught me off guard. We are dealing with an articicial genre that never acknowledges its own artificiality. But it has its pleasures, and this time they are worth collecting. To Grandmother's House We Go continues at the Bluma Appel Theatre, 27 Front St. E., until April 11.
