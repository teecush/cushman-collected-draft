THEATRE REVIEW

Of a Monstrous Child: A Gaga Musical

Buddies in Bad Times Theatre

Staged and performed with immense style, Of a Monstrous Child is a musical-intellectual collage that centres on, or more accurately swirls around, the figure of Lady Gaga. Its title is a line from Michel de Montaigne on the subject of deformity, a line quoted by Stefani Germanotta in her pre-Gaga days as an art student in New york. It seems, from the sentences we hear quoted and see projected, to have been a very creditable student essay. And obviously it bore fruit. One thing the show firmly establishes is Lady Gagas intelligence.

She talks very sensibly about her audience being able to read into her anything they like. She also talks pretentiously about offering herself up to them as kind of saviour, living rather than dying for their sins. (The pretentious bits tend to come later in the show.) She's played here by a performer with the wonderfully appropriate name of Kimberly Persona, who functions beautifully as a walking Rorschach test. As a kind of epiphany, at the end of the show - and I mean really the end, after the curtain call - she gets to sit down at the piano and sing a whole song. This is a notable event, the kind of bonne bouche you might expect at the end of a jukebox musical.

Which up to then the show, as its author and director Alistair Newton has been insisting, very much isn't. (The production comes from Newtons group ecce Homo, and its their most ambitious and impressive to date.) What music we hear, and there isn't an enormous amount of it, isn't necessarily Gagas own. The most impressive number, the one that actually communicates something, is from way before her time; its the '50s show tune, turned jazz standard and gay anthem, The Ballad of the Sad Young Men. It's sung by three performers including Bruce dow, whose role is that of Leigh Bowery, a celebrated Anglo-Australian performance artist and sometime fashion designer (of whom I confess I had never heard) whom, far more than Gaga, the show is about. Though much of the script is made up of other peoples opinions of its heroine, Bowery doesnt have any, for the very good reason that he died, of AIDS, in 1994.

What the show seems to be telling us is that Bowery was a precursor of Gaga's, without her physical advantages and also without her reticence: an odd word here, perhaps, but where Gaga keeps everybody guessing, even when she's supposedly being frank, Bowery on this evidence was verbally unable to contain himself; he comes over as witty, blistering, a self-inventor like Gaga but also, completely unlike her, a self-destroyer.

According to Wikipedia (which the show invokes, so I dont see why I shouldnt), "As he got larger he used his costumes to exaggerate his size, and the effect was frequently overpowering and unforgettable for those who encountered him, the more so because of his confrontational style." dow certainly captures all of that; a performer whose previous stock-in-trade has been ingratiating ebullience, he is here ice-cold and diamond-hard - which, given his technical command as both actor and singer, actually makes him more likeable than ever before. For most of his appearances he's in drag; his last, though, is a long, spellbinding monologue delivered in the unforgiving, unapologetic nude. It caps a superb, noble performance.

One of Bowery's missions in life, he tells us earlier, is to redeem the concept of irony. To show how the word has been debased, the show gives us a very funny interpolated scene (written by Jacob Richmond, of Ride the Cyclone) in which a couple of hipsters compete in describing the awfulness of the music they prefer to listen to, rather like the flat-capped northerners of Monty Python, swapping memories of childhood deprivation; you might call this sport one-downmanship.

Gavin Crawford contributes a series of brilliant lightning impressions of figures who may have influenced Gaga, or have thoughts about her, including Bjork (putting the dead in deadpan), Cher (excessively gravelly, but never mind), Andy Warhol (very interesting), Marina Abramovic (a performance artist I have heard of, and indeed admire) and Camille Paglia, who declares the Lady to be a rip-off of Madonna, though I cant see theres much to choose, except that Madonna got there first.

Madonna herself is played by Chy Ryan Spain, as are Grace Jones (his best turn) and yoko Ono, while Kyle Travis young, one of the few guys in the cast who only plays guys, does especially well as a sniffy Boy George whose loftily approving comment on Gaga basically amounts to, Hey, I was her. Tyson James does extremely well as a Gaga-worshipper, the saddest of sad young men, who sees her as his salvation from suicide and speaks reverently of her music, without actually saying much about it.

And here lies a paradox that the showpokes at but doesn't illuminate. With Lady Gaga, as with Madonna, we've reached a time in which the success of pop singers has minimal connection with what theyre actually singing. Of course pop has always been about image as well as music, but the imbalance has never been as severe as now. It strikes me that Newton may have been canny as well as high-minded in declining to do a jukebox musical; he wouldnt have had a score.

I also think - though here I'm taking issue with his program note rather than his production - that Newton is off-beam in claiming that informer times "the star remained aloof, self-absorbed and unattainable, the fan vulnerable, devoted and available." Judy Garland? Billie Holiday? They were anything but aloof; they bled all over their audiences. Meanwhile, on Newton's own theatrical showing, Gagas fans are as vulnerable and available as fans have ever been.

How important this is, I dont know; I doubt that the world in general is as obsessed with Gaga as the show would have us believe. (A thought to live by: everything is a minority interest, except the weather). Anyway it's a beautifully wrapped package; and on the first night it broke - twice - the previously immutable theatrical rule that critics should be exempt from audience participation. Talk about transgressive.

Of a Monstrous Child runs until May 26. Tickets and info at buddiesinbadtimes.com.
