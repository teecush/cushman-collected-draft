Each week in our All About series, we introduce our subject on Tuesday, feature a first-person essay on Wednesday and open up the pages to our readers on Thursday. This week we look at TV snobs. Are you ashamed of what you watch? Send your thoughts to allabout@nationalpost.com

Dr. Rob Buckman, oncologist, author, medical educator and comedian, recently reconnected his television. Before that, he and his family had passed through a two-month period of disconnection (from the TV, not from one another). The fault, he says, was not in the programs, but in him. "I've got a seriously addictive personality. If the TV is on, I can only memorize what's on the TV."

His children were similarly hooked, as children tend to be. Most of the family "found it easier to watch cartoons" than to concentrate on schoolwork, housework or just work. The exception in the Buckman household is his wife, also a doctor, who, he says, "can read, talk on the telephone, make notes and watch television all at the same time. Whereas I can't even do one of those things at a time."

He did rent DVDs , during the period of abstinence, but this proved a worse situation. The Buckmans found, like so many before them, that they had a pile of discs that they could never find the time to watch, all stacked up and reproachful. So, three or four weeks ago, they rejoined the world; Principally, Buckman insists, so that his wife could watch CNN. ("I do, too" he claims.) But they try, he says, "not to watch repeats of cartoons and sitcoms. We have a rule: My wife has the remote."

Surprisingly, perhaps, they have only signed on for the basic service. They don't, for example, get HBO. Buckman acknowledges that this means missing out on some good shows, at least until they come out on DVD or reappear on the networks. He especially likes Dexter, which he feels has "conflicts beyond most characters on TV, even The Sopranos." (We differ here. Seriously).

He doesn't find any conflict between abjuring most television, and himself pursuing a TV career which, he says, "is going on quite nicely" with regular guest-expert appearances on TVO, Citytv, CTV "and so on."

Only he doesn't regard it as a career. He calls it "an explaining sideline: part of my general shtick of trying to explain complex stuff simply."

So Buckman's first abandoning, then rationing, his TV watching is "a reflection on the household, not on the medium. We needed to go into rehab, and then get back."

He is not a TV snob, or rather he isn't an anti-TV snob. In fact, he doubts if many people are any more. "That attitude of 'I wouldn't have one in the house' is very '50s. Like saying now that you wouldn't have a computer. It would be a bit of an embarrassment to people."

Buckman was a kid in London in the 1950s, as was I, and back there and back then, that attitude was certainly widespread, if only because a television was still a comparative rarity.

My father was in the retail trade so we were among the privileged ones and it seems to me, looking back, that nobody had the right to be snobbish about TV, if only because in England it didn't banish radio. (It still hasn't.)

Television was a large part of my education; seeing a BBC children's version of A Midsummer Night's Dream, when I was seven or eight, was probably the formative cultural event of my life. There was a lot of drama on TV in those days -- first adaptations from the stage and then, increasingly, original scripts -- and it was taken for granted that they'd exist and be good; unlike today when the single play, on both sides of the Atlantic, is a rare and much- trumpeted event, surrounded by a general presumption that it's going to fail.

I have a friend in New York, a major theatre and movie buff, who confines his TV watching to three events a year: the Oscars, the Tonys and -- perhaps by way of expanding his horizons -- the Miss World contest. Otherwise he despises TV as a hopelessly inartistic medium; The screen is too small.

It is, though, the long-form drama series that seems to have rekindled interest in TV among the cultural classes. One noted Canadian actress, who describes her watching as "selective but very loyal," says that her impression of TV was certainly affected by The Sopranos, which she agrees was "the nonpareil," though she personally preferred Six Feet Under ("about the best thing television had ever produced") and The West Wing.

She was turned on to the latter by her teenager, which must be a very common phenomenon; My own viewing over the last two decades has been guided by my children, from Sesame Street to Jon Stewart. And she does know people who won't have a TV, or will only watch the public stations: "I can't even get them to watch the Shopping Channel." So she's eclectic. But professional standards come into play here; She rates the Shopping Channel's presenters very highly indeed.

The only person I know in Canada who doesn't own a television is the actor and Soulpepper stalwart William Webster. At least I thought he didn't own one. It turns out he does -- he inherited it when a relationship ended -- but never turns it on. "I didn't want to waste time, as I had before, switching channels, trying to find a political debate that wasn't degrading, rude and uncivil." (Good luck.)

"I was becoming interested in poetry; Having spent 40 years reciting it, I wanted to understand it. So I'd rather spend my time reading good books, the occasional newspaper, trying to make sense of what's in The New York Times or The New Yorker."

He's very thorough about this, maybe confirming Buckman's diagnosis: "I live in the 19th century. I don't have a computer. And when I did see something on TV, I used to hate the bad acting, like bad vaudeville. I thought I'd rather go to a play. I'm very snobby. I'm sure I'm missing a lot. I've never seen The Sopranos. I did see an episode of The Office-- the U.K. version -- and I thought, 'I like this' -- the sense of eavesdropping on all the banalities and horrors of group existence, the intrigue and gossip that we all love as human beings."

But it's nature programs, which he used to enjoy, that prompt his most sweeping denunciation of the medium. "I thought 'if I have to see one more alligator chomping down on a bloated hippo somewhere...' It's all about the excesses of American capitalism. It's all about the food chain."
