CINDERELLA

Elgin Theatre, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - Certain marks on the body of Cinderella at the Elgin lead me to believe that dramaturgy has been committed. For a start, the program, for the first time in my experience of Ross Petty's annual holiday extravaganzas, lists an author. In fact it lists a dozen; Malcolm Heenman gets the principal credit for "script adaptation" (adaptation from what remains a murky question) but he seems to have had the assistance of the director, Jim Warren, and about half the cast. No doubt about it: This pantomime has been in a workshop, and I don't mean Santa's.

This, by the standards usually prevailing in these parts, is a very thoughtful show. We all know that Cinderella went to the ball in a coach magically made from a pumpkin. This version seizes on that fact, and works back from it. Cinderella's family, the village they live in, and -- as far as I could make out -- the kingdom that surrounds it, are all economically dependent on the pumpkin crop. But there seems to have been either a devastating blight or a profligate Halloween; and in order to send Cinders off to meet her Prince, her Fairy Godmother has to commandeer the only remaining pumpkin in the neighbourhood. It's supply-side economics: Subsidize the upper classes and their posh parties, and the benefits will filter down to the community, or at least to the pretty and deserving. For once it seems to work.

Not only is there thematic unity; there is also motivation. It's traditional for Cinderella to make a cheerful, sparkling entrance among the merry villagers in the opening scene -- which, considering the way she's being treated at home, makes no psychological sense. In this version however, Cinderella has no initial reason, apart from the threat of universal starvation, to feel unhappy about anything. Her father, Baron Hardup (his traditional name in English panto), has gone off to repair the family fortunes. No sooner has the first number been cavorted through than he returns, accompanied by a newly acquired wife, whom he must think is rich because she's immensely tall and dresses flashy, and her two dowdy and ungainly daughters. The new arrivals take one look at their beautiful new step-relative and decide they don't like the competition. So the heroine's persecution at their hands is not a given; we actually see it evolving. And if you want to know why her loving dad doesn't intervene -- well, it's partly because his every word is a malapropism so nobody would understand his protestations if he made them. But it's mainly because he spends most of the action in a debtors' prison, for non-payment of pumpkin tax. And if you want to know why his daughter allows herself to be domestically exploited, it's because she's being blackmailed; her stepmom won't allow her to pay prison visits unless she toes the line.

Jennifer Gould's Cinderella needs all this rationalization, because she's an unusually spunky and sensible heroine who backs down for no one. She hasn't even been nourishing any particular ambition to go to the ball, though when somebody mentions the idea she thinks it quite a good one. (She'll be able to ask the king to pardon her father; the show is not just conscientious about narrative continuity, it's obsessive.) About all she has in common with the regulation pantomime ingenue are good looks and a radiant singing voice. Her romantic interest is similarly non-traditional. In the old days across the sea, he would have been played by a strapping female with handsome legs and powerful lungs, and would have been known generically as the Principal Boy. That variety of pantomimic cross-dressing tradition having lapsed, the role has to be differently conceived, and the new Petty convention, increasingly evident over the last few years, is to make him funny. Thus, Adam Brazier's Prince Jack (they tell him to act charming but it doesn't take) is a moon-faced conservationist with a good heart but no social graces.

Turning the romantics into comics undoubtedly makes their scenes more interesting, but it does make life tough for those other performers who would seem to have being funny as part of their job description, and to whom the script and direction are a lot less helpful.

In most Cinderellas, the star role is Buttons, the page-boy clown who adores the leading lady, helps her with the chores, and wins all hearts when royalty steps in and makes off with his happiness. Cliff Saunders, possibly the first balding Buttons in theatrical history, is given coarse mechanical routines, and if his heart is breaking he keeps it to himself. He's quite easily bought off by being made Mayor, an occasion for topical jokes that the show, most uncharacteristically, denies itself. Meanwhile, Dandini (Jonathan Wilson), Buttons' opposite number in the Prince's suite, has been given more opportunity than usual, and slightly better material, and is a definite threat.

One regular attraction of the Petty pantos is the spectacle of some Stratford or Shaw Festival luminary, letting his hair down. This time it's Don Harron, a leading Stratord juvenile as recently as 1955, who plays the tongue-twisted Baron and is expectedly endearing. The Fairy Godmother is played by Erin Davis, famous radio and TV host. She gives, I suspect, much the same performance live as she does canned; that is, she's very assured, very enthusiastic, and speaks to the other actors and to the audience as if she were conducting a phone-in.

The producer himself plays the evil stepmother, thus combining his usual function of hissable villain with the less accustomed one of pantomime dame. The two don't go well together. You see, children, the Dame -- the more durable aspect of the transvestite tradition -- is meant to be a sympathetic figure; when the show is Aladdin or Dick Whittington, he/she is the hero's feckless but loving mother, man-hungry but harmless. She's also supposed to be dowdy. Danny LaRue, Britain's premiere drag queen, once played the Dame and it didn't work at all; he looked too good in the frocks. Petty has the same problem; svelte and sneering, he crowds the pitch for his ugly offspring (Mary-Ellen Mahoney and Karen Skidmore) who fight back gamely but with diminishing returns.

This production has more comic talent than it knows what to do with, and that is not a compliment. When mama leads her brood in a morale-boosting rendition of Everything's Coming Up Roses, we have no idea how to take it. Is it a character number? Is it a spoof? Are they hoping for a show-stopper? Or are they just hoping?

It also stands out uncomfortably by virtue of being a good song. The other music is bullying shlock, of the Lloyd Webber persuasion or worse. Pantomime (the show bills itself as a musical, but it's kidding) has always interpolated the popular songs of its time, but this of course means that its numbers just get worse. I suspect that Petty knows this, and that it's the Roses song that actually reflects his own taste, which is why he makes such a big deal of it.

As for production values, there are real ponies in the transformation scene (a first here, I think); and, as far as I could tell the dancers from the dance, the corps, especially its male members, seem capable of more than is asked of them. This isn't the best of the series that I've seen -- that would be the last Robin Hood -- but it's better than the last few.
