The stage show London Road was one of a kind: a verbatim musical, the actual spoken words of actual people, taken down and sung. There'd been nothing like it before, and theres been nothing like it since - though admittedly theres hardly been time yet for it to have any followers. It opened at the National Theatre in London in 2011, and was a great success. It's had only one major production elsewhere: a very fine one by the Canadian-Stage company in Toronto.

The show depicts the aftershocks of the murders of five prostitutes in the English provincial town of Ipswich. London Road is the street on which it happened, the street on which the killer lived, and the core of the piece is his neighbours' reactions, both before and after the arrest.

Now its been filmed. Rufus Norris, who directed the National Theatre production has directed a movie, with an augmented version of the original cast. Its playing at this year's Toronto International Film Festival, and Norris came over for the occasion, along with the two authors of both play and film, Alecky Blythe (she did the interviews, transcribed and edited the words) and Adam Cork (he set them to music, a fearsome task, nearoperatic in sheer duration).

Norris, a trim and friendly 50-year-old whose rise to the summit position in British theatre has been a steady progression through acting and directing, readily admits that "adaptation from stage to screen is very dangerous - especially when you're doing it with the same people. The verbatim musical is theatrical enough, so everything else needs to be questioned. There can be no direct address in the film and there can be no doubling."

London Road on stage has a cast of 11, all playing multiple roles; part of the pleasure of the Toronto production was keeping track of the actors. Thats a strictly theatrical device that can only work on screen if the film is consciously winking at the audience. Otherwise, it would just be confusing. So, London Roads London cast are all in the movie, but as one character apiece. Other roles are played by other people, the best-known newcomers being Tom Hardy in a small part and Olivia Colman in a big one.

We were trying, Blythe told me, "to make the film as active as possible." She also said that the film feels darker than the show. It certainly begins differently. The stage version starts by plunging us into the middle of the story; the householders of London Road meet to form a residents' association, to counter the bad rep they're getting in the media. The instigator of the meeting welcomes the others in song, haltingly but warmly. It sets up the convention of the whole piece, and the community at its centre.

The show then flashes back to the murders, or rather to the streets reaction to the murders. Thats where the film starts, with dialogue sequences: interviews that could pass for documentary footage. The approach, chronological and gradual, may have seemed logical, given the almost inescapable realism of film, but it means that when the singing does start, it seems artificial: even more than the average musical, because it's trying so hard not to be. There are some beautiful moments; when, with the murderer still at large, the women sing "you automatically think it could be him" of every man who crosses their path. Cork's choral setting, catching speech rhythms in music, is a wonderfully effective method of conveying a group reaction in song. The effect is mordantly enhanced in the film when the sung suspicions extend to a dummy in a menswear store. Too often, though, the singing seems to be a long-winded way of conveying information that might just as well be spoken (and, in real life, was).

London Road has the same problem all film musicals face (and one that would also affect original film musicals, if anyone made them any more): people breaking into song seems odd. And the more seriously the story appears to be taking itself, the odder it seems. It wasnt always a problem. When musicals were common currency, nobody minded; habit is its own convention. It helped of course that there were stars who created their own vocabulary. Fred Astaire, Judy Garland, Gene Kelly made singing and dancing seem as natural as talking. Nobody today gets to have that kind of career in movie musicals. But then very few of the classic film musicals were based on shows, even when they claimed to be. When the studios bought Broadway musicals in the 30s and 40s, they usually kept only the titles and a couple of hit songs; everything else got censored out. Religiously faithful, though still somewhat expurgated, films of shows only arrived in the 50s, when everything from Oklahoma! to West Side Story got the treatment.

Some of them (Kiss Me Kate, The King and I, The Pajama Game) were pretty good, but none captured the excitement of their stage originals, or even of a good stage revival, and many floundered helplessly between stools. Like the movie versions of straight plays, they performed a public service; they brought the material to audiences who wouldnt otherwise get to see it, and provided starrier, though not necessarily stronger, casts than any theatre production would be likely to afford. Pauline Kael once decried the cinematic purists who condemned filmed plays as if they'd got on the screen illegally. They were, she sensibly argued, among the many possible kinds of film, and should be accepted as such. But she seldom claimed that they were great movies. She did make an exception for Sidney Lumet's film of Long Days Journey into Night, and it contains superb performances by Ralph Richardson and Katharine Hepburn. But there have been productions of Eugene ONeill's play at the National Theatre and the Stratford Festival that moved me more, and in which the drama's confinement to one room seemed liberating rather than oppressive.

Were all familiar with the groaning sensation of a play being "opened up" for the camera; it sacrifices concentration without gaining anything much in return. The extra locations almost invariably seem gratuitous. Filming a play is a very different exercise to filming a novel; a play is a dramatic machine to begin with. Though Ill confess that I came out of the movie of Driving Miss Daisy feeling that I had a very good time and that I had no desire to see the play. I didn't want to spend an evening watching two people being lovable in an on-stage automobile. This is one case where opening up was a blessing.

Of late, the traffic has started going the other way, with the odds of collision even higher. Famous movie musicals have been put on stage, 42nd Street being the most successful example. But even that show is a pale, if loud, shadow of its original, with all the grit taken out. And why would anyone want to see a stage version of Singin' in the Rain? Its perfect as it is, and its still there. The public service argument hardly applies here. Stage productions are ephemeral. Films are forever, setting impossible standards of comparison. Why not just rent the movie?

There are exceptions, or at least there are ironies within ironies. The movie of Cabaret was good, but the original stage production was better. All the same, recent stage revivals have essentially been adaptations of the film. One of the great American plays, The Front Page (1928), became one of the great American films, His Girl Friday (1940), a wholly different though still related animal. The trick was changing one of the male leads to a woman, decades before gender-neutral casting became a thing. Then, in 2003, the National Theatre (and later the Shaw Festival) put His Girl Friday on stage, hoping for the best of both worlds and, predictably, getting the worst. The wheel didn't come full circle; it stuck in the middle.

A staged film that actually worked - triumphantly in Toronto, and also apparently in London - was the dramatization of the Danish film Festen, which was a very special case; even as a film, it was practically a play. As Rufus Norris says "it gives you permission." He directed the London production, which subsequently went to New York, where it quickly closed. As he says, it didn't have name recognition. And thats the problem with plays into movies, and even more with movies into plays. People arent buying entertainment. They're buying a name.
