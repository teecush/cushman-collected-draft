JEAN AND DINAH WHO HAVE BEEN LOCKED AWAY IN A WORLD FAMOUS CALYPSO SINCE 1956 SPEAK THEIR MINDS PUBLICLY

Artword Theatre, Toronto

The calypso referred to in the full title of what I had better just call Jean and Dinah may not be as world-famous as all that. At least I don't remember hearing it. But its certainly good and pungent. A big hit in the 1950s for the calypsonian Mighty Sparrow, it is sung from the point of view of Trinidadian men contemplating the departure of the Yankees from the island who have been monopolizing their women. It especially celebrates the fact that the formerly high-priced prostitutes -- girls called Jean and Dinah and Rosita and Clementina -- will now be available to them, cheap.

Tony Halls play -- a great success in Trinidad, Jamaica and the United States, brought to Toronto to coincide with Caribana -- looks at this situation from the viewpoint of Jean and Dinah themselves, some 40 years on. Jean has come by Dinah's place to persuade her to come out, as the two of them have done every year, for carnival. Dinah, the older of the two and half-blind, is reluctant to go. Besides, the two of them, as you would expect after all this time, have issues.

The patois, the accents and the thick local and historical allusions made a fair amount of the play incomprehensible to me. Around me, though, everybody was exploding with laughter and, making a few allowances for hospitable first-night enthusiasm, I think it's safe to assume they were right. Certainly the two actresses are magnificent, and you can feel the dialogue giving off sparks as Jean and Dinah survey their pasts, the men they have let themselves get involved with, the children who have been fathered on them (Dinah is at pains to point out she was a married woman and a nightclub hostess, not just a whore like Jean) and the vagaries of politics: "I never liked those Peoples National peoples," says Dinah of the nationalist government that came to power about the time the Americans left.

For all that, their conversation, which extends over the whole first act, does seem to go on a very long time. In Act Two, the convention changes, and we find ourselves actually at the carnival; not a specific carnival, past or present, but a distillation of all of them. Jean and Dinah play out a series of sketches in traditional masquerade roles. As a pair of sailors, mocking the shore-leave behaviour of the U.S. fleet, they seize the opportunity to hit on the men as they themselves have been hit on. (There is a certain amount of audience particpation here.) Dinah assumes the character of the Midnight Robber, an outsize gangster. Most pointedly, Jean plays Baby Doll, a role that allows her to accost any passing man and identify him as the father of her child. This is a role that strikes uncomfortably home, for both parties, and it melts into a painful scene about Jeans own son, ironically heralded by Dinahs crooning of another famous calypso, Brown Skin Gal, Stay Home and Mind Baby. (I do remember that one.)

Finally, we return to the present and the two women as they are now. By this point, however, the narrative thread has frayed, and a central question -- was Jean in any way responsible for the accident that robbed Dinah of her sight? -- seems especially inadequate to the weight it is meant to carry. Sentimentality, which has threatened all evening, finally senses a vacuum and moves in.

For most of the time, though, the saltiness of the language, and especially of the performances, keeps it at bay. Penelope and Rhona Spencer (cousins in real life, and too young for their present-day roles, but who cares?) are spectacular, intimately intense, audaciously exuberant. Penelope, who seems to have the more varied talent of the two, plays Jean with a wonderful squirrelling attack; Rhona, playing much of the piece from her bed and behind dark glasses, has sassy, somnolent authority as Dinah. Both chain-smoke throughout, but it doesn't seem to hurt their energy level. They share the stage with, and sometimes infectiously groove with, a percussionist, Richard "Popcorn" Cumberbatch, who is billled as Rhythm Man.

Overall rhythm, as opposed to individual performing rhythms, which are abundant, may be what this show, directed by its author, lacks. Even apart from that, it could use more music. But it is unquestionably joyful.
