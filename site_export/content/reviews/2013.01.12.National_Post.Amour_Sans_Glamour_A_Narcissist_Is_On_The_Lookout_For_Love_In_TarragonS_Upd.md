The theatrical year in Toronto looks like being a real Wiener-schnitzler. Soulpepper will be reviving La Ronde, probably the best-known play by Arthur Schnitzler, Viennese playwright and novelist of the late 19th and early 20th centuries. Meanwhile, at the Tarragon, we have Morris Panych's adaptation and production of Schnitzler's Anatol, its title extended to The Amorous Adventures of Anatol, so as to leave no doubt about the subject matter.

That of course is something that it shares with La Ronde, along with its episodic structure. There are differences. La Ronde is a linked series of sexual encounters, a theatrical daisy chain in which the first partner eventually reappears as the last. Anatol focuses on just one young man about town, showing him with a succession of different women, one per scene. Something else: the popularity and/or notoriety of La Ronde partly derives from the fact that in the course of all but one of its 10 scenes, the couple concerned actually do it. By contrast, Anatol's conquests are much described, especially by him, but never explicitly shown. His alliteratively amorous adventures consist of him being rejected or, less often, doing the rejecting himself. Somehow the stars never seem to align so that both parties get disillusioned simultaneously. Which, I guess, is life.

The first scene sets the tone. Anatol, partly to show off to his friend Max, puts his latest prey under hypnosis to determine whether she loves and is faithful to him. (His own fidelity doesn't enter into it; he may not have invented the double standard, but he flourishes it like his own personal banner.) The answers he gets seem satisfactory, but he can't quite be sure. He's doomed to frustration. Schnitzler apparently wrote this scene as a self-contained sketch, and it still feels sketchy. But he persisted, and found a remarkable resilience in the idea.

In the following scene, Anatol's doing his Christmas shopping (the staging recalls Panych's production of that lighter continental treat, Parfumerie) and encounters an older flame, a married or conceivably divorced or widowed woman - anyway a Mrs. Something - who proves jealous of whomever he's buying presents for now. It ends in a stand-off. And so it goes up to a final scene which finds Anatol about to set off to get married, but delayed by the presence of a hot date from the night before: A development that shocks even the unshockable Max. ("Oh, no, not LIONA").

Watching the play is a curious experience. It begins by being lightly entertaining. It then threatens to become tiresome: How many times do we need to be shown that Anatol is a narcissist who thinks he idealizes women while worshipping only himself, and is terrified that they will tire of him before he tires of them? But the repetition starts to justify itself, even to take on not just satirical edge but moral weight. It ends - in this version anyway - with the promise that Anatol's marriage heralds the revenge of all the furious women he has ever scorned.

The super-prolific Panych, mounting his first production in at least two weeks, has come up with a fluent, sometimes witty text (the laughs are surprisingly few but reassuringly good) and given it a fleet, crisply timed staging in a set by Ken MacDonald, whose back wall resembles a giant, elegant filing-cabinet, with one drawer for each chapter of Anatol's past. It's actually used that way in one scene, though it's typical of our hero that he can't file them under names, probably because he's forgotten what they are.

Mike Shara, who can shift from one foot to another more meaningfully and more energetically than any other actor I know, captures both the exterior Anatol, confident roue, and the interior one, nervous boy eternal. It's never revealed exactly how he maintains his apparently luxurious lifestyle. His lady-friends, or some of them, do have jobs: Dancer, actress, circus-performer - it seems that in old Vienna there was no business like show business. We meet seven of his partners altogether, all of them played by Nicole Underhay and nearly all of them (with handsome help from Charlotte Dean's costumes) dazzlingly differentiated. The first quick-change, from bouncing ingenue to wounded woman of the world, is especially striking; two opposite characters, fully inhabited. She's delightfully self-absorbed in her circus-town, off-handedly informing Anatol that she's ditching him, while tucking in to a restaurant meal at his expense. (Every play of this kind has one. Central European drama is the only drama that knows how to eat.) Her resources aren't quite inexhaustible; her jealous transports in her last scene closely resemble her jealous transports from earlier on. But both are dead-on in their own right.

Robert Perscichini makes a suitably tactful Max, alternately congratulated and upbraided by Anatol for not understanding women, though it's clear in at least one scene that that is another instance of Anatol's self-delusion. (Max is a psychologist, by nature and vocation; Schnitzler, a doctor as well as a writer, seems to have split himself between the two men.) Adam Paolozza plays a whole flock of flunkeys, to telling if generally silent effect.

Schitzler was one of the most penetrating of playwrights, and it would be good to see a Toronto production of one of his more elaborately constructed plays: Undiscovered Country perhaps, which Tom Stoppard has translated and which Chris Abraham directed, very well, some years ago in Montreal.

The Amorous Adventures of Anatol runs through Feb. 10 at the Tarragon Theatre.
