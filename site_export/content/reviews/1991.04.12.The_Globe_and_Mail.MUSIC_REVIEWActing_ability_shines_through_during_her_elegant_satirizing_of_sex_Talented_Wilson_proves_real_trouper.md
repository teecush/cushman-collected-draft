MUSIC REVIEW"Acting ability shines through during her elegant satirizing of sex Talented Wilson proves real trouper

Friday, April 12, 1991

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Toronto ONT -- BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Toronto JULIE Wilson, the regent of Manhattan cabaret, began her opening-night set Tuesday at the Top O' the Senator with Most Gentlemen Don't Like Love, a Cole Porter song that Sophie Tucker introduced on Broadway back in 1938. Not unnaturally, she employed Tucker's haughty, staccato tones for the purpose. These tones persisted, however, on songs that Tucker never had anything to do with. Wilson has borrowed creatively from her predecessor to develop her own style.

Like Tucker, Wilson is largely in the business of satirizing sex. She does it, though, from a very different standpoint, and from within a very different body. Tucker was dumpy and gemutlich; Wilson, now in her 60s, is tall, lissome and a lady, a slightly swacked lady, incredulous at some of the scandalous things she finds herself singing, but elegant forever. She is also an actress.

She is maybe more an actress than a singer. Cabaret singers traditionally lack voice, but most of them cultivate an easy going naturalism, gliding informally between the notes. They also tend to hide behind pianos as their own accompanists. Wilson commands the stage, and adorns it, in a diva's red dress, a white gardenia, and alternating boas of black and rose-pink. Her delivery is conversational - she is a mistress of phrasing - but not informal. She has slightly more voice than the late Soph - who by the end of her career was just talking to music - but like her she orates her songs.

Wilson's program was mostly Porter, as a centennial homage. Porter also satirized sex, when he wasn't celebrating it. Sometimes he did both at once, and Wilson included a generous number of these ambiguous comic classics, songs like Let's Not Talk About Love, or But in the Morning, No! an epically libidinous celebration of erotic hangover that, if Wilson's patter is to be believed, even succeeded in shocking Ethel Merman, who first sang it. Another Merman number, Down in the Depths (on the 90th Floor), was a Wilson triumph, luxurious heartache simultaneously mourned and mocked; her navigation of the treacherously simple Miss Otis Regrets was all but impeccable.

Sometimes her theatricality undoes her; in ballads she can dwell on her emotions instead of simply letting the song move us. All the same, she managed to freshen that most venerable of standard warhorses, Night and Day. She strayed occasionally from Cole to Coleman, Cy Coleman, that is, whose Don't Ask a Lady (from Little Me), raucous erotica to contrast with Porter's brittle, tore the place up. It showed her in a new light; a warm woman at a refreshing angle to the cool of most of her material. She is a trouper. She must come back and show us her Arlen, Weill and Sondheim programs. Her accompanist, William Roy, occasionally lifts his voice with hers: modest but true, and surprisingly legato. They match wits beautifully.
