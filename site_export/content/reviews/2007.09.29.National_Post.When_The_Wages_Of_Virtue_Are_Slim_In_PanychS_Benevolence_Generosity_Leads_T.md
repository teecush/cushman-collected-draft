The last Tarragon Theatre season began with a play called Generous. The new one starts with a play called Benevolence. It seems unlikely, but the Tarragon may be setting itself up as the home of the good guys.

Or maybe not. In Michael Healey's Generous, there were good deeds that went unpunished but not very many. In Morris Panych's Benevolence, the wages of virtue are even more dubious. The particular act of benevolence that sets things off is performed by Oswald Eichersen, an uptight orthotics salesman. One impulsive day he gives a $100 handout to Terence Lomy, a homeless man who's been sleeping outside his apartment for weeks. The gift arouses Terence's ingratitude or his vindictiveness --it's never quite certain which. Maybe it's just direction-less opportunism. Anyway, Terence takes it upon himself to rescue Oswald from his humdrum, hemmed-in existence and bring out the free spirit that declared itself in that first uncharacteristic gift. Not that Oswald isn't usually nice; it's just that he isn't usually that decisive.

Terence uses their new acquaintance to enrich himself at Oswald's expense, in more ways than financial, and to enmesh Oswald in a life of crime. He isn't your everyday tramp, though he may be your everyday literary tramp; he has a vocabulary and a range of literary references that are at least the equal of his victim's, and he has criminal connections.

Terence manages to estrange Oswald from his girlfriend, who's frigid and domineering (and a real estate agent, if you need further proof ), and introduces him to a friendly hooker who is cut out for a higher calling. The various transactions and encounters occur in a porno movie theatre that used to be an art house.

Panych directs the play himself and, as usual, he gets a superb set from Ken MacDonald. When we first glimpse it, through a scrim before the play commences, we think we're looking at a mirror image of the auditorium in which we are seated. When the curtain rises and the lights kick in, we find ourselves in what looks to me like an exact reconstruction of one of the two surviving movie theatres in my own neighbourhood though these, I'd have you know, are art houses. (Well, some of the time.)

There's a beguiling tone to the play, and to the production, but the overall feeling is thin. In the first half the writing seems to be aiming toward an airy funniness that it reaches only intermittently, while in the second, the action, most of it offstage and reported, gets increasingly random. Indeed, the world outside hardly seems to exist. The play seems to be preaching a liberationist message not unlike that of Panych's Feydeau adaptation, Hotel Peccadillo, but it twists and turns confusingly. The upshot, I guess, is that Oswald is victimized by his opponent (whom his girlfriend, Audrey, in a rare moment of insight, pegs as his alter ego) but is still the better for the experience. But, though the dialogue keeps harking back to the initial act of charity, it never establishes a causal connection of any weight.

It was once said of Peter Ustinov that he wrote not so much plays of ideas as ideas for plays, and that is what Panych, who somewhat resembles Ustinov in wit and whimsy, has come up with here. The abstraction emblazoned in the play's title hovers above the evening without ever being seriously examined.

Tom Rooney gives a lovely performance as Oswald, delicate even in humiliation. This actor really is one of the good guys, able to hold decency up to a comic light without ever becoming either harsh or sentimental. (The orthotics business seems right for him; his life pinches him like, well, a tight shoe.)

Stephen Ouimette brings the necessary bravura to the loamy Lomy but cannot make him real. It's hardly his fault that his early departure makes the play seem even more inconclusive, or that while he is there his role depends excessively on the incongruity of hearing a derelict talking about Kurosawa. He also pontificates on Oswald's own failed attempt at screenwriting: one of the play's loosest ends.

Jennifer Wigmore's role as Audrey amounts to one compelling scene of self-discovery, sandwiched between two bouts of caricature. Gina Wilkinson seems to have decided, probably rightly, that on the role of the tart subtlety would be wasted.

The cast is completed by Colin Heath, playing a succession of furtive patrons embarrassed, inconvenienced or just plain scared by the events going on around them. Or maybe he's playing the same patron on successive visits; I wasn't sure. Anyway his performance, almost entirely silent, is very effective, as befits a former cast member of Panych's The Overcoat.

The director's choreographic sense, at least, is as strong as ever. Visually and dramatically, everyone here is put in the right place at the right time.

- Benevolence runs through Oct. 27 at the Tarragon Theatre, 416- 531-1827.
