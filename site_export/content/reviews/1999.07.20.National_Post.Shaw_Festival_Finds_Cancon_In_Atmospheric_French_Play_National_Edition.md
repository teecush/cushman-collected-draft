In repertory until Sept. 25.

S.S. TENACITY

COURT HOUSE THEATRE, Niagara-On-The-Lake

Nobody would accuse the Shaw Festival of being nationalist, but like all this country's cultural institutions, it must occasionally feel some obligation to go digging for Canadian content.

Given the festival's commitment to plays written between 1856 and 1950, this can be a tough prospect, but they have found it this year, most unexpectedly, in a French play from 1920. S.S. Tenacity isn't set in Canada, but it concerns two young men who want to get there. We meet them in a waterside cafe in Normandy, waiting to board the eponymous vessel that will take them across the Atlantic. Its departure has been delayed, and they while away the time drinking with the locals and getting to know the attractive young lady who waits on tables.

The play's author, Charles Vildrac, was -- according to the Festival program, which I take as gospel -- an early proponent of the Theatre of the Unspoken, a movement that arose in Paris after the First World War as a reaction to the rhetorical excesses of earlier generations. This school was to reach its apogee in the plays of Jean-Jacques Bernard in which the drama became so unspoken as to be, to my ears, practically inaudible; and it made something of a comeback in the '50s and after, in the agonizingly rarefied dramas of Marguerite Duras. Vildrac, being a pioneer, is a vulgar exhibitionist by comparison with these later quietists; we have learned to take subtext inour stride, and his storytelling no longer seems especially subtle.

From the moment that our heroes, Segard and Bastien, wartime buddies about to emigrate together from a still-stricken country, set eyes on the fair Therese, it is obvious that they have both fallen for her. When Segard, the shyer of the two, spends a whole scene with her not mentioning it, he might as well, from the audience's point of view, be shouting his passion from the rooftops.

There is an attenuated charm to the play, best captured here in Catherine McGregor's Therese, a sensible but vulnerable girl, swept off by an unexpected passion.

Her suitors receive (from Craig Erickson and Jason Chesworth) somewhat callow performances; there is some poignancy in the outcome, since she chooses the one the audience is less likely to have been rooting for, and rather more when the loser must decide whether to stay on in Normandy, return to Paris, or travel alone to what is continually referred to "as the wilds of Manitoba" -- a choice, we are made uncomfortably aware, between three bleaknesses. At this point the play passes into the keeping of a weather-beaten barfly named Hidoux, whose musings -- both here and earlier -- on the situation and on life in general transport us from the Theatre of the Unspoken to the Theatre of the Positively Garrulous. He tells us that some people get to choose their destinies, but most are just carried along and, if I followed him rightly, that the important thing is to cleave to your fate with conviction. In other words, the S.S. TENACITY is the ship of your life, whether it takes you to Canada or just stays put. And don't think that the winners in love are, over the long haul, necessarily luckier than the losers.

Dennis Garnhum's production is pleasantly atmospheric; he plays both the first and the last moments in French, which gives the evening a much-needed pinch of salt, an intriguingly exotic frame, and a sense of life going relentlessly on according to its own rules and its own rhythms. The cafe seems authentic; you can almost smell the Gauloises.

Al Kozlik enjoys himself rather fulsomely as the bibulous Hidoux but as the kindly, taciturn patronne, Jennifer Phipps is a rock of believability.
