This is the 30th year of Toronto theatre's Dora Awards, and a celebratory book is being published. One winner from each year reminisces about what a thrill it was (I especially relished Daniel Brooks comment on awards ceremonies in general, which I roughly paraphrase as "theyre all pale imitations of the Oscars") and there's an overview of how great it's all been in promoting the profile of the community (I also paraphrase, but not by much). Nobody grapples with the recurrent complaints about eccentric choices of winners and nominees, but you wouldnt expect them to.

As for this season, the 2008-09 one: Im struck, looking back on its second half (i. e. this year) by how many of the most interesting shows were Doraineligible, either because they were revivals of previous productions or because they were only semi-professional or because they didn't run long enough: City of Wine, Seven Jewish Children, the revival of The Last Days of Judas Iscariot, the revival of Kristen Thomson's I Claudia ...

Hang on: It turns out I Claudia is eligible after all. Its been nominated for outstanding production of a play and also for outstanding direction. The first might just scrape by on a technicality since the play is, as they say, under new management: Crows Theatre instead of Tarragon. But the director is the same Chris Abraham as before; and if he's allowed a second kick at the same can, then why not Thomson herself, not as author (a play can't be new twice), but as actress? And what about the stunning replacement performances in Judas Iscariot-- by Morris Panych, Ted Dykstra and others? Replacements dont count? Then why is Jeff Madden up there (I dont doubt he deserves it) for his takeover job in Jersey Boys? The head, as ever, spins.

As it happens, I think Thomson's been robbed: not over I Claudia, which, much as I love it, shouldn't be there at all, but over The Patient Hour, which, though flawed, was the most interesting new play of the year. Of those plays that are nominated, Id have a hard job choosing between Andrew Moodies Toronto the Good (messy but compelling) and Joan MacLeod's Another Home Invasion (flawless but a monologue). The smart money, though, must be on Agokwe, a play I didn't see. It must have been spectacular because its gathered nominations for its author, leading actor (same man: Waawaate Fobister), producer, director, and set, costume, lighting and sound designers.

In the best-actor stakes, Diego Matamoros has been nominated (as he nearly always is and deserves to be) for Travesties. I thought, when I saw it for the second time at the last matinee, that this Soulpepper show was the most enjoyable production of the season. But I didnt think it the first time, so I can't really complain at its exclusion. Otherwise, the season's three stand-out events were Company Theatres Festen and Soulpeppers A Raisin in the Sun and The Way of the World. The last, which should have won nominations for its director (Peter Hinton) and at least three of its actors, has been completely shut out. The others are well-represented, with Philip Riccio's best-actor nod symbolizing, I imagine, the whole superb ensemble. This may be the place to register my bemusement at the nomination of Hrant Alianak, certainly a worthy pillar of the community, for giving one of the less-bad performances in the atrociously acted House of Many Tongues -- this in a year when Eric Peterson has been overlooked twice. I'd hate to choose between Jason Byrnes direction of Festen and Weyni Mengeshas of Raisin, but I'd probably go for Mengesha. And I'd certainly go for Alison Sealy-Smith in Raisin as best actress; this was a self-transforming performance.

Tastes differ, and mine are as eccentric as anyone elses, but one point on which all seem to agree, except the Dora people themselves, is that its lunacy to cram all the supporting performances -- play and musical, male and female--into one category. If there's a rational reason for this, could it please be made public? This year, there are five performances nominated, all by women. (Jordan Pettle in Travesties, anyone? Todd Thomson in The Patient Hour?) One actress, Raven Dauda, is running against herself, for a good performance in Miss Julie: Freedom Summer and a shallow one in Doubt. I'd like Brenda Robins to win for Ring Round the Moon, but Im not holding my breath. As for musicals: Jersey Boys will probably win, and I shant repine if it does, but I also carry a small banner for A New Brain, and especially for Daryl Cloran's direction of it. Elicia MacKenzie ( The Sound of Music) will undoubtedly romp home for solving the problem called Maria, twice. In the independent (i. e. smaller theatre) division, David Ferry is another one running against himself for best actor, and he is indeed twice worthy. All the same, I think that R. H. Thomson, in one of the same plays, has the edge. Best independent actress is split between three superb performances, one good one, and one that was terrible; gallantry forbids me from being more specific. Best independent play should be Anton Piatigorsky's Eternal Hydra, even though I didn't enjoy it much. But The Gladstone Variations, which took place over several ingeniously synchronized evenings in the eponymous hotel, deserves the palm for best production just for, so to speak, showing up.
