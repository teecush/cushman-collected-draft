BLUE/ORANGE

Berkeley Street Theatre, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - Joe Penhall's Blue/Orange was first staged in 2000 by the National Theatre in London, where it won every available award. On the evidence of the new Toronto production, it deserved them. As usual, its CanStage that has brought us the play; as usual, we wonder why it had to take so long.

Playing at the downstairs stage at Berkeley Street, Blue/Orange starts in remarkably similar fashion to This Is Our Youth one flight up. Two young men are squaring up to one another loudly, and one of them, ceaselessly on the move, is demanding coke. Only he means Coca- Cola -- not that he doesnt have a taste for some harder stuff as well -- and his antagonist is warning him off it, along with all other sources of caffeine. The hyperactive one is Christopher, of African parentage and now living in Shepherd's Bush, West London.

He's just completed 28 days in a mental ward and is entitled to go home. He wants to, or at least he thinks he does, or at least he thinks he does most of the time. Bruce, the junior psychiatrist in charge of his case, wants to keep him; he thinks Christopher could still be a danger to himself and others. He has Chris tagged as a case of Borderline Personality Disorder.

Enter Dr. Robert Smith, Bruces superior and supervisor, who points out that this is a very vague diagnosis: Borderline of what? Robert wants to send Chris home for a variety of reasons, of which the least controversial is that the hospital is short of beds. Obviously, though, he has his own agenda, as (less obviously) does Bruce; and the play develops into a power-struggle.

Chris has real problems. When he cuts open an orange -- any orange -- he pronounces its inside to be blue. He also claims to be the illegitimate son of Idi Amin; Robert seems inclined to take this possibility seriously, though Bruce cant, and neither, I imagine, can most members of any conceivable audience. The play does not go very deeply into questions of race, though all three characters play the race card when it suits them. Chris, whose feelings of persecution may or may not amount to clinical paranoia, is likely to describe all who cross him as fascists -- including his neighbours who, Bruce points out, are blank. Robert advises Chris to go home and listen to some reggae; Chris, of course, is not Caribbean, and the remark would be patronizing even if he were.

Penhall, like other young British playwrights, is an avowed fan of David Mamet. In the program for the National production of Mamet's Edmond, he describes the play as a response to all those worthy drones bemoaning the end of socialism. It was the end of worthiness. He and his peers have apparently turned their backs on the sermonizing of the David Hare-Howard Barker generation, just as that lot rejected the soft-centred nostalgia of John Osborne and Arnold Wesker, who also thought they were rebels. Each generation thinks it's the toughest until a new lot comes along, at which point the incumbents turn all sensitive and rebuke the intruders for their lack of finer feelings. Its pathetic, really.

For the moment, though, Penhall and company are writing political drama that isnt doctrinaire; please note that failing to make the expected liberal noises is not the same thing as being right wing. For what they're doing, Mamet, with his electric sense of personal confrontation on society's margins, is the best reference. Theres a brief bit in Blue/Orange, celebrating an offstage characters cooking skills, that recalls one in Glengarry Glen Ross; maybe it's an homage.

Structurally, the play shapes up like a three-sided role- shifting Oleanna, with a similar final movement. A character -- Bruce, in this case -- finds that what he thought were casual remarks have been taken out of context and used as evidence against him.

It's a continuously exciting play, building conflicts that never let up in dialogue, that is both stinging and believable. This too is our youth, not just in London; and numerically more representative of it than is the play upstairs. Its also skewed. Most of it is a struggle between the two physicians, and the audience is inevitably going to take the side of the younger; hes up against authority, therefore vulnerable, and he also seems to be talking sense. At least he isn't talking nonsense on the scale of Robert, who is still quoting R. D. Laing on madness being the real sanity and who also seems grammatically impaired. (He recites a line by Allen Ginsberg: "Human is not a noun, he is a verb.") I have no desire to knock Chris Abraham's production, which is very tense and, within its limits, visually striking; note the use of the sets single doorway, especially in relation to the perpetually hovering senior shrink. But it does accentuate the imbalance. In the first scene R. H. Thomsons Robert is a psychiatrist as one of the Monty Python team (probably the late Graham Chapman) might have presented; his beard, cords and sports jacket are impeccably realistic, but he seems to be on speed: tic-ridden, manipulative, and a bully. He's the paranoid, obsessively defensive about his own position and his own research. Is the play really telling us that the lunatics have taken over the asylum? It seems, at this date, a bit simplistic (though it might ironically confirm some of Robert's own wilder theories.) In itself its a riveting display, and Thomson does quieten down considerably in the later scenes, but the damage has been done; we simply dont credit a thing he says. Except one thing: He speaks of the damage Christopher will suffer simply by being locked up with older and undoubted psychotics. And after the play is over, a few more of his arguments feel more convincing than they did at the time. It's to the play's credit that it nags at you like this, but we could, I think, have had more of that at the time.

Kevin Hanchards Chris is as real as acting gets. His accent, for starters, is dead to rights (credit to Ned Vukovic, the dialect coach, for the sound of all three performances) and he manages a role that is one outburst after another without ever losing his own control. He is febrile, obviously, and tortured, and surprisingly funny; apparent innocence is mixed in him with a residue of slyness, and however much he contradicts himself he always seems, at that moment, to be telling the truth. Darren Keay as Bruce has the unenviable task of playing straight guy to a couple of crazies (Im speaking theatrically, not clinically) but he does it with increasing confidence and also with increasing ambiguity; he suggests, by the end, that he can, when cornered, be as petty as anyone else. This isn't a perfect show, but it's alive.
