The greatest performance I have ever seen was given by Michael Redgrave in the title role of Chekhov's Uncle Vanya. I find it hard to put a date to it since I saw it, and the equally great production, directed by Laurence Olivier, of which it was a part, three times over a period of three years. In 1962, Vanya was one of the three plays in the opening season of the Chichester Festival Theatre in the south of England. It was revived there the following year, and was then taken to London as part of the inaugural season of the National Theatre, of which company, as of Chichester, Olivier was the first artistic director. I hitchhiked from London to see the two Chichester versions and then, having started university, hitchhiked to London for the National Theatre one.

So my memory of Redgrave's Vanya is a composite. I think the performance grew over time but obviously it was my first exposure to it that had the greatest impact. I discovered in later years that I hadn't been alone; that many people who had seen it at much the same impressionable age as me thought it not only a great performance (people of all ages were agreed on that) but a transforming experience. One contemporary whose choice of career it influenced was Alan Strachan, a British director and writer who was to work with Redgrave in his last years, and whose excellent biography of him, Secret Dreams, has just come out in paperback (Orion, $19.95).

The core of the character and of the play -- the greatest moment, I think, in all modern drama -- comes when Vanya, the frustrated and exploited estate manager, tries to shoot the Professor who has proposed selling the family home from under him, and misses. "It was heartbreakingly moving," Strachan writes, "but also side- splittingly funny." That's absolutely accurate, though I think I'd reverse the order; one laughed first, especially at Vanya's anguished cry of "missed again," and then registered the abyss of tragedy beneath the farce. But in fact the two reactions came so close together as to be virtually simultaneous. We talk fairly glibly of being made to laugh and cry at the same time, but this may be the only occasion on which I have known it to happen.

What we saw was a man wasted; even sadder and funnier than that, a man self-wasted. Redgrave, a tall and handsome man with a superb voice, heroic in other roles and potentially glamorous even in this one, laid the groundwork early. When he first comes on, Vanya describes the Professor as a buffoon who has spent his life regurgitating ideas about "realism" and "naturalism" -- "things that intelligent people have known all along and the stupid ones don't care about anyway." It's a great line, one of my all-time favourites, but it tumbled out of Redgrave's mouth just a little too fast, unstable, as if he were compensating for something. Vanya is in love with the Professor's beautiful young wife, Ilyena, curses himself for not telling her when there was time, fantasizes about what their life might have been like and then curses himself again for having such ridiculous thoughts. Redgrave's Vanya was continually catching himself out in his own folly. He brings Ilyena flowers, and finds her kissing his contemporary, the local doctor -- a prelude to the greater humiliation of the failed murder attempt. The last act finds him hugging his own failure, refusing to hand over the drugs with which the doctor, Astrov, fears he may kill himself.

The rest of the cast was all-round fantastic: Rosemary Harris, Joan Plowright and the legendary Sybil Thorndike were all in it, and all at their best. Harris and Plowright, playing a brief scene of happiness, cruelly dashed, seemed to be living rather than acting. You'll understand that when, a few seasons ago, I described Soulpepper's Uncle Vanya as the second-best I had seen, I was giving very high praise. I had always assumed that it was the product of a group of star actors who had arrived at ensemble by mutual consent; Strachan's book persuades me that I had greatly underrated the force of Olivier's direction, and he reminds me how superb was his staging of the shooting scene, all over the open stage.

Olivier himself played Astrov, and though he was seldom my favourite actor I vividly remember the weariness ("I shall have to perform your autopsy -- do you think I shall find that interesting?") with which he signalled that the two men were brothers under the skin, both worn out before their time. And overlaying that, of course, was the mutual respect of two great actors. Twenty-five years earlier, Redgrave had played Laertes to Olivier's Hamlet; Redgrave's eldest daughter, Vanessa, was born during the run, and Olivier famously announced the event at the curtain call with the words "A great actress has been born" -- speaking, Strachan notes, "with what then must have seemed emotional hyperbole but which now seems extraordinary prescience."

Olivier wasn't always that generous, and Redgrave's first and only season at the National was unhappy. His memory began to fail him, a painful circumstance that it took years to diagnose as the onset of Parkinson's disease, a condition that slowly but inexorably removed him from the stage and even from the screen. His Vanya turned out to be not only the summit of his career but its climax, and it's possible to see its overflowing nervous energy as both a herald of his illness and an index to his psychological condition. Strachan, whose book is exemplary in its linking of its subject's personal and professional life, identifies Redgrave's twin demons as drink and guilt over his bisexuality -- not the condition itself but the pain it must have caused his wife, the fine actress Rachel Kempson. They stayed married, though, and devotedly -- her late career blossomed as his faded -- and they did, after all, found an acting dynasty. It may, unfairly, be what Michael Redgrave is now best known for. When we think of "the Redgraves," we think of Michael's son, Corin, and his daughters Lynn and Vanessa and their offspring, though in fact both of Michael's parents were actors, and the family tradition stretches back even further.

Vanya, a frightening portrait of a failure, might be called the great anti-heroic role. But Redgrave also played all the heroic ones; and though I never saw his Lear, Hamlet or Antony, I think I may well have concurred with those who thought them the best of our time. He also, of course, had a major film career that stretched from The Lady Vanishes to The Go-Between by way of The Stars Look Down, Dead of Night and The Dam Busters. Strachan reminds me that in the '50s it was customary to speak of Redgrave, along with Olivier and John Gielgud, as one of Britain's three greatest actors. By the time of his death he had been supplanted in that pantheon by Ralph Richardson, though, actually, all four belonged there. Redgrave also seems to have suffered from being dubbed "an intellectual actor"; if that means "emotionally cold," then he wasn't -- he was the most moving of them all, far more so than Olivier. He was certainly an intellectual man: university-educated, a former schoolteacher, a great reader and a fine writer. He wrote probably the two best English books on the craft of acting (The Actor's Ways and Means and Mask or Face), a nightmarish novella on the same theme (The Mountebank's Tale), a couple of unmade screenplays, including an Antony and Cleopatra that sounds as though it would have been wonderful and a first-rate dramatization of Henry James's The Aspern Papers -- natural-born Shaw Festival material if ever I saw it. He must indeed be the best documented of all modern actors, both by himself and others. British critic Richard Findlater wrote an excellent study of him (Michael Redgrave, Actor) in the '50s. Redgrave himself, with Corin's assistance, wrote a rather disappointing autobiography (In My Mind's Eye), and now Strachan has both completed the story and enormously filled it out. The result is the best biography of an actor since Simon Callow's of the similarly conflicted Charles Laughton. And though Strachan is a less graceful writer than Callow -- or than Redgrave -- his intelligence, his sympathy and his wide-ranging sense of theatrical history could hardly be bettered.
