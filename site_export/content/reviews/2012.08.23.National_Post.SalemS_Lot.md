THEATRE REVIEW

THE CRUCIBLE

Young Centre for the Performing Arts, Toronto

Soulpepper now has two plays running in which a 20th-century dramatist digs back to the 17th to furnish a parable for his own time and for a regime under which he himself suffered.

After The Royal Comedians, Mikhail Bulgakov's searchlight on Stalinism transposed to the age of Moliere and Louis XIV, we have The Crucible, in which Arthur Miller holds up the Salem witchcraft trials as a mirror to the House Committee on Un-American Activities of the 1950s. Bulgakov's satiric farce is, I guess, a more daring piece than Miller's melodrama, stylistically and indeed physically given that even Joe McCarthy wasn't quite Joe Stalin. But there can be no doubt which play packs the more powerful punch, both as a piece of writing and in its current production. The Crucible has its audience on the ropes.

I doubt if any modern play offers more visceral excitement than this one, especially in the third of its four scenes. This provides the thrills of a trial scene, and of a scene of demonic possession, at the same time, topping them up with all the satisfactions of moral outrage. We watch John Proctor, the Salem farmer determined to expose the fraud by which a clutch of vindictive and/or hysterical girls hold a community to ransom, and in doing so to save his own wife from the gallows. We see her seal his fate by telling the only lie of her life: denying that he has committed adultery with the girl who now accuses her of witchcraft, and thereby providing us with the additional theatrical satisfaction of whiplash irony. It all adds up to magnificent melodrama.

That is not a putdown. Melodrama is a legitimate, indeed a hallowed, theatrical form that can be put to serious use. Miller was masterful in fashioning the historical record into a coherent story, illuminating both the period he wrote about and his own. Others, too: When Deputy-Governor Danforth, the top Salem judge, proclaims that "a person is either with this court or he must be counted against it," he wakes any number of contemporary echoes, and not just from south of the border. And though the United States has never again been quite the theocratic society it was in 17th-century Massachusetts, where ploughing on Sunday could be cited in court as proof of bad character, we're still waiting for a time when a candidate can dare run for election without claiming God as his sponsor.

Albert Schultz's Soulpepper production has tremendous grip, much of it exerted by Stuart Hughes, who as Proctor communicates a throaty, sweaty honesty, enflamed with anger and drenched with shame. All these qualities explode together in his final courtroom outburst of mingled despair and defiance ("I hear the boot of Lucifer, I see his filthy face" - he seems momentarily to have broken into verse). He also manages to keep the embers glowing through the play's protracted final scene, which requires him to be more conscience-wracked than seems strictly justified: We appreciate that he feels sexual guilt, but why does he accuse himself of failing to confront evil? We've seen him do nothing but. His quibble over his "name" (he'll make a false confession, but he won't sign it) also sounds like Miller working through his issues with Elia Kazan. Through all of this Hughes charts a coherent and ultimately moving course

He's matched in impact by Joseph Ziegler's Danforth - something of an actor's dream role, I imagine, since he has the first half of the evening off and then swoops in and takes over. Miller later believed that he had gone too easy on Danforth; but in fact there is dramatic value, including suspense value, in making him, as Ziegler plays him, an apparently suave and reasonable man, morally undone by superstition and by a tender regard for his own authority. The less tenable his position becomes, the more fanatically he upholds it.

Poised between these two poles, indeed in some ways the fulcrum of the play, is John Hale, the minister who comes into Salem as a witchcraft expert and gradually sees through the fraud. (He wrote a book about it afterwards, a remarkably perceptive one.) Oliver Dennis is excellent in his initial pastoral visit, brimming with good intentions while modestly flourishing his credentials; when the scales fall, though, he lacks the sheer decibel power to compete with Hughes and Ziegler, and the action falls short of its full crescendo.

Patricia Fagan's Elizabeth Proctor is also a little muted, but she does convey both the love and the distance of the woman whose kindness, in her husband's memorable phrase, "would freeze beer." An especial virtue of the production, especially in its first scene, is the way in which, on Lorenzo Savoini's spare but summoning set, it navigates the cross-currents of a village population where rancor thrives on closeness.

Once the witch hunt has started, there are many reasons for keeping it going, all vividly depicted. For Reverend Parris (Derek Boyes), a paranoid with enemies, it's an insecurity so deep he almost becomes sympathetic. For the rich Thomas Putnam (Michael Hanrahan), it's coveting his neighbours' land, now suddenly available; for his wife (Raquel Duffy) it's the need to blame someone for the loss of her children in infancy. On the other side, Nancy Palk, superb in serenity, makes you believe all the good things her neighbours say about the aged and saintly Rebecca Nurse.

As the even more aged but less saintly Giles Corey, William Webster strikes a fine line between the intransigently sublime and the obstinately ridiculous; "it were a fearsome man, Giles Corey," Elizabeth says after his death, though the way in which she says it here, amused rather than awed, has the unfortunate effect of casting him retrospectively as comic relief.

There are a couple of odd casting choices among the other elders, but the production only really falters with the girls. Abigail, their ringleader, is a role more showy than substantial, and her abrupt disappearance weakens the play's last scene. That's history's fault, not the actress's, but while she is around Hannah, Miller never makes manifest either her fascination with Proctor or her power over his friends. She does, though, lead them compellingly in their massed transports at the trial, which are superbly staged and performed.

For Abigail, it's coldly contrived; for the others, who knows? We do see Mary Warren, the one among them who was about to tell the truth, being partly terrified, partly hypnotized into compliance; Courtney Ch'ng Lancaster, a standout among the younger generation, here makes our hearts break, both for Mary and for those who will now be her victims.

I don't know what Schultz is up to in his final tableau (I prefer Miller's) but otherwise this is a truly stirring show.
