THE MEMORY OF WATER

Winter Garden Theatre

Shelagh Stephenson's The Memory of Water comes bearing garlands. It was a success at Torontos Tarragon Theatre a couple of years ago and is now being restaged commercially with the same director and blue-chip cast. In its native England it has just won the Olivier Award for best comedy of last year. It was its authors first play (there has been another since and a third is pending) and it is a recognizably promising and intermittently accomplished piece of work. It holds attention throughout.

But after all the fanfare it appears overblown, not especially funny, and essentially false.

It's another play about three sisters, and they break down -- in order of seniority and in strict accordance with tradition -- into a frayed and responsible one, a bitter and sophisticated one, and a romantic and loopy one. Contemporary Britain being smaller and somewhat more travel-friendly than, say, Chekhov's Russia, the two younger siblings have the resources to get out of their native small town and go to Moscow, or in this case, of course, London.

Mary, the middle sister, has a successful life there as a doctor, and also a long relationship with a married male colleague. Catherine, the youngest, has gone further, cutting a wide and increasingly unsatisfying swath through the continent of Europe. Puzzled by the frequency with which she has been dumped she exclaims, to the accompaniment of a massed raising of eyebrows, that she must be able to relate to men because she has slept with 78 of them.

That leaves the oldest, Teresa, and being left is something about which Teresa frequently complains. She has stayed home to run a health-food business (or, as she vociferously insists, a dietary supplement business) and to look after their widowed mother, who was stricken with Alzheimers and who has just died when the play begins.

So everybody is home for the funeral, and for the consequent bouts of reminiscence and recrimination. We are in Alan Ayckbourn territory; literally, since the play takes place in Ayckbourns own rain-swept high-altitude corner of the north-east English coast. It also shares his pained but amused concern with the middle-aged middle class, urban but not metropolitan, financially all right but emotionally disconsolate. What it lacks is his instinct for the humour of character and situation -- a humour that lies beyond and beneath single lines -- and for a governing and illuminating technical device.

As free-standing characters, the members of the sisterhood are fully drawn, or certainly well enough to provide chewable roles for actresses. In Jackie Maxwell's production, Martha Burns stands out as Mary, the career woman who longs for a child (it doesn't play as cornily as it sounds) and can get neither that nor any other kind of commitment from her lover. The play takes place in the late mothers bedroom, and the sight of Burns sitting on that bed in the later scenes -- smart, cropped, and devastated -- is the evenings most abiding image.

Vegetarian Teresa, who lives what she sells, is a tightly reined pull-yourself-together-for-God's-sake kind of woman until she takes a drink and lets everything hang out, which she does at great length. Nancy Palk is finely angular when she's sober and rather forced when shes drunk, but then so is the writing.

Kristen Johnson, so good last year as the placid Stella of A Streetcar Named Desire, goes indulgently wild as Catherine, who waits in hysterical agony for a call from her flame in Spain, and whom she plays as an escapee from Absolutely Fabulous. (There are times when the play seems to need the added dimension of a laugh track.)

The play is written, naturally, from the womens point of view, but the two men on hand are shrewdly and not unsympathetically depicted; both have arrived cold, wet and weary, and both are inclined to keep as clear as possible of the emotional whirlpool.

Peter Cockett is blandly believable as the gentleman doctor, pointedly referred to by Mary's sisters as the married boyfriend. Randy Hughson, especially good, is Teresa's husband and business partner, Irish by the sound of him, and mortally sick -- as he tells her in the plays most resonant line -- of travelling abroad to sell stuff he doesnt believe in to people who don't want it.

There is a sixth character, the mother: Dead and not only not forgotten but manifesting herself, though only to Mary, which seems unfair to the others and frustrating to the audience. The whole play hinges on, or at least keeps referring to, the baleful and continuing influence of one generation on the next, but it's never suggested that it is any more important for Mary than for her sisters, nor does the idea get any further than a generalized restatement of a familiar theme about mothers and daughters.

It connects, just about, with an abrupt revelation about a traumatizing episode from Marys youth, but thats a melodramatic device that her character could do perfectly well without. The deceased is described as flighty and repressive, but that's only what we're told; when she materializes, Corrine Kozlo can't provide her with much more than a dead-on North country accent.

Her daughters dress unpersuasively up in her clothes for a costume parade at the end of Act One, and her coffin dominates the stage during much of Act Two, and these are plainly meant to be striking images, but they are just effortful. So the play ends up both structurally and psychologically unsound.

There is also the question of tone. That "best comedy" award is probably misleading; the barriers between comedy and drama fell down long ago, and best comedy is now usually a consolation prize for something that didn't get best play. This one is plainly not trying to be a laugh riot. We know at the end, as in much diluted Chekhov, that the characters are going to survive, and we also know that they are going to go on being unhappy.

But a lot of i is designed as barbed banter; someone is arbitrarily described as having "an ego the size of Asia Minor," and that kind of one-liner -- not specific enough to be witty, too calculated to be natural -- is typical. There is a lot of this going around, and I have certainly heard worse. But I expected better.
