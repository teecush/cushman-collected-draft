THEATRE REVIEW LET US TALK CARIBBEAN

Friday, March 27, 1992

ROBERT CUSHMAN

WRITTEN AND PERFORMED BY MARVIN L. ISHMAEL Directed by Damir Andrei At Canadian Stage, Berkeley Street Rating: Storyteller Marvin Ishmael tries to keep his audience hopping with too many vignettes; compasses keep needing resetting with nothing much to steer by Caribbean journey, a strain BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Toronto MARVIN Ishmael stands before us, casual and collected in a floral shirt that might give even Joseph some amazing technicolor nightmares. As well as standing before us, he runs around quite a bit. The movement amounts, according to the program, to a Caribbean storytelling journey, but I would settle for describing it as a series of vignettes. Or just as a good old-fashioned one-man show.

During a warm-up thoughtfully designed to sensitize us to his speech rhythms, he tells us that he is addressing us in mid-Caribbean. Midway, that is, between Trinidad and Jamaica: a dialect that must be spoken mainly by fish. Ishmael himself is Trinidadian, and has no need to worry about being understood. He has invented an alter ego called Trini who is accident-prone but a survivor. "Trini wanted to be single - independent - have his own apartment." In pursuit of this ambition, he goes to Canada, under strict instructions from his relatives to tell Harry Belafonte to stop singing Yellow Bird. (A forlorn hope, of course, but one thanks them for the thought.) Trini's mistakes and misadventures when faced with his first airline seat belt or his first North American breakfast are not record-breakingly droll, but Ishmael, by serving his hero up in alternating layers of wide- eyed innocence and smouldering indignation, keeps them appealing. Every so often, too, he whips up a really big laugh. I would have been happy to stay with Trini for the duration (about 90 minutes) but Ishmael keeps changing tack, spiriting us back to Trinidad for a ghost story, or sashaying into Jamaica to be ambushed by indigent Rastas. An appeal to their sense of brotherhood gets a stony response, something along the lines of "Okay, man, your money is mine and my poverty is yours."

Keeping your scene and your audience hopping is, on the face of it, a reasonable way of warding off the monotony that always threatens a solo performance. In fact, though, it imposes a strain; we have to keep resetting our compasses with nothing much to steer by. The subject changes but the mood remains the same, since Ishmael is no quick-change virtuoso, physically or psychologically. In this sense, perhaps, his billing is accurate and he is a storyteller rather than an actor, in which case it would be more pleasant to stay immersed in one story. When we do return to Trini for a final Christmas story, he has turned all mawkish and Miracle on 34th Street. The total is sometimes charming, ocassionally very funny, and a bit monotonous. Let Us Talk Caribbean continues until April 12.
