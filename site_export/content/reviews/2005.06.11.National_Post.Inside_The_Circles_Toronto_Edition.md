In Jonathan Coe's wonderful new novel, every major character takes a turn at being the narrative consciousness

THE CLOSED CIRCLE

By Jonathan Coe

Alfred A. Knopf

384 pp.,

$35

- - -

Jonathan Coe's new novel is wonderful: You can't wait to get to the next page and dread arriving at the last. It will, however, and despite the customary publishers' claims that it "stands on its own," make no sense to anybody who has not read its predecessor, The Rotters' Club. Which is no hardship whatsoever, as The Rotters' Club is also a terrific book. The Closed Circle, though, is better.

The Rotters' Club is about a group of bright, awkward teenagers growing up in the 1970s in Birmingham, then one of the dourest of British cities. It's also about their parents, and both books are, among other things, virtuoso exercises in the alternation of points of view. Every major character, with a few significantly opaque exceptions, gets his or her turn at being the narrative consciousness.

The spectacular finale of The Rotters' Club is a 30-page continuous sentence in which a painfully sensitive and socially hopeless boy named Benjamin Trotter expresses his joy and wonder at the world after his first sexual experience with the girl of his dreams. Many thought that the author went too far, but I disagree. It's a perfect expression of the character's state of breathless ecstasy, and it has one hell of an ending. Avert your eyes if you don't want to know this: The couple meet a man in a pub who prophesies that they will have a long and happy life together and that Mrs. Thatcher will never be Prime Minister.

Coe is very good at dropping these bombshells from the real world. He does it again, more literally and even more shatteringly, when Benjamin's older sister and her boyfriend, at the height of happiness, go out for a night at a Birmingham pub, which is then blown apart by an IRA bomb in the worst terrorist act yet to have happened on the British mainland. The sequel, set 30 years later -- today, in other words -- makes similar use of public events. The more domestic ones, such as the threatened closing of the city's BMW works, are splendidly used to bring the characters and the times into focus. The Iraq war, by contrast, simply (and not very satisfactorily) looms in the background. Tony Blair is almost a character in the book, often invoked but never actually present.

Remember Y2K? The Closed Circle begins with accountants and musicians -- Benjamin is both -- rushing to back up their files. Ben, whose contemporaries all expected him to turn out to be a literary-musical genius, has in fact produced nothing; he has also settled for a companionable, childless marriage. He has never forgotten his first love, and has made no effort to.

Of his male contemporaries, two are now journalists. One is a London high-flyer who married rich (he always wanted to, despite being the son of a shop steward) but whose ambitions are dashed when he's promoted to literary editor, not what he was hoping for. The other, still in Birmingham, wants to write an analysis of Britain's new Far Right, linking it to the advent of New Labour. But the pieces won't stay in place. Blairism, with its fondness for international capital, epitomizes much of what the neo-Nazis detest. Things are complicated. Everybody's instinct is to run for cover.

The latter scribe's ex-wife, still his and everybody else's friend, is haunted by the memory of her elder sister who disappeared in the earlier novel. She herself is currently on the rebound, at one point hooking up (incongruously, and she knows it) with a millionaire executive who has bankrupted one company after another, never failing to let a handsome compensation package hit him on the way out.

There is also Benjamin's younger brother, Paul, who used to lurk in his room reading Friedmanite economics and gloating over "the death of the socialist dream." Now he's a Labour MP. Privately, he disapproves of the war; publicly, he sits on the fence; eventually, he votes for it -- for strictly non-political reasons.

In the first book, he was seen only from the outside. In this one, we're allowed inside his head. Coe, being a humane and humorous writer, allows us to sympathize with him. That's not the same as approving of him. All the other characters despise him (even his girlfriend acknowledges that "his sense of humour was surgically removed at birth") and their author seems intellectually on their side. But even in The Rotters' Club, Paul performed the nearest thing to a heroic action: He saved someone from drowning. Here he proves as accident prone as his brother, falling recklessly, almost self-destructively, in love. Obviously, it can happen to anyone.

The Rotters' Club was at times confusing. It was hard to keep track of the characters, or at least of the links between the generations. In The Closed Circle, everything comes clear. The title refers in a minor jokey way to a right-wing think-tank (Paul's a founding member), and in a major serious way to the resolution of everything in the plot that had previously been left hanging: People and incidents we might have forgotten are brought cunningly back to mind and their significance revealed. This isn't coincidence, or not very often.

The new movie Crash deals in coincidence, and it's sometimes brilliantly effective. The Closed Circle trumps it by dealing in logical surprise. The crowning revelation is so masterfully sprung, so immaculately timed, that I laughed aloud in sheer joyous satisfaction.

Coe and his younger compatriot Zadie Smith have got me reading novels again. Both fuse sharp comedy (though hers is wilder) with formal ambition (his is more controlled) to create panoramic social satire with pain beneath. As a stylist, Coe hardly puts a word wrong.

Really, of course, The Rotters' Club and The Closed Circle are one big novel, with the second half making final sense even of what seemed the most strained device of the first -- the introduction as a framing device of a boy and girl from yet a third generation. They close the circle and then open it again: "the two of them," in a miraculous closing sentence that I think I can quote without spoiling anybody's fun, "wanting nothing more from life, at that moment, than the chance to repeat the mistakes their parents had made, in a world that was still trying to decide whether to allow them even that luxury."
