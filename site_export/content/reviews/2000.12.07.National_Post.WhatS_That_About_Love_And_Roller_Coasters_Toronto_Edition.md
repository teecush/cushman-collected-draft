SALMON MIRANDA

Artword Theatre, Toronto

I have to break the news that Salmon Miranda is about a pair of young lovers called Sam and Miranda. It seems to me that if the girl were named Patty, we might have had a title that made more gastronomic sense, and I don't think the author was wise to bring the title that she does have explicitly, and whimsically, into the dialogue. Still, the play, especially in its earlier stages, is tasty.

Salmon Miranda has been announced, with an understandable blend of defensiveness and bravado, as a romantic comedy. Miranda, a non- graduating university student who works in a bookstore, spends the night with Sam, a waiter and -- at least by his own account -- actor, whom she met that evening in a bar. This is most unusual behaviour for her, but apparently standard for him.

We start the play in Miranda's apartment the morning after; the author, Leah Davidson, and the director, Sarah Armstrong, evoke a wonderfully fluid and convincing sense of post-coital confusion. Davidson herself, playing Miranda, is extraordinarily natural as she veers between nervous self-consciousness, stern efforts at practicality, and renewed surrender. By the end of the scene, the couple have agreed to extend the one-night stand into a relationship.

There could hardly be a play if they didn't, but there seem to be other good reasons. They are temperamental opposites who seem guaranteed to stimulate and enrage one another just about equally. Sam is the dynamic one. In the course of their first week together, he treats Miranda to karaoke, bowling and a much-increased alcohol consumption; she only demurs when he suggests they spend the weekend scuba diving. His restlessness has begun to get to her; and also, I may say, to me. At least I wished that Christopher Morris, who plays Sam, could find some way of letting up a bit -- in volume if not actually in energy -- since, even for an actor playing an actor -- indeed even for an actor who may be fantasizing about being an actor -- he seems excessively actorish.

Also, while Miranda just talks, Sam makes speeches; he seems to be a manic depressive, though we only get to see the manic side. He tells a long story about an exciting childhood experience riding the roller coaster at Canada's Wonderland. Miranda isn't too keen on roller coasters; she says she prefers merry-go-rounds. This, apparently, is the index of their differing temperaments, and the two of them then proceed to bat these handy metaphors all around the dialogue well into the second act. In a play whose best feature has been its overheard conversational quality, all this self- consciousness becomes a pain.

Act One ends with Miranda throwing Sam out; and that was all there was to the play when it was seen on the Fringe last year. In its new second act it gets more solemn, though it doesn't necessarily go deeper.

A year has passed; Sam comes back, of course, and they debate whether to start again. This time it's Miranda who wants to; Sam who has doubts. Miranda admits to having told lies of her own; Sam is revealed as a full-blown mental case. This is too much freight for the play to carry. Comedy as courtship-ritual is a necessary part of theatre, much neglected recently, but if it's going to work we have to like the characters concerned and believe they would be good for one another. I liked Miranda, but I decided that she would be better off without Sam. The play works hard toward the end to persuade us otherwise, and sending us out of the theatre to the strains of Ella Fitzgerald singing Let's Fall in Love was a great help.

All told, the play itself is likeable, and much preferable to the glum pretentiousness generally available elsewhere. These are real people, not -- at least not most of the time -- ciphers pressed into service to make a point. Morris is a proven good actor, even if I couldn't warm to him here.

My only doubts about Davidson as an actress are those that always occur when you see someone apparently perfectly matched to her role: Can she do anything else? (She did occasionally seem to be living beyond her technical means.) Anyway, both as actor and writer. she's warm, funny and a find.
