THE IMPORTANCE OF BEING EARNEST

Avon Theatre, Stratford, On.

---

The recent stage history of The Importance of Being Earnest has not, among high-grade Canadian companies, been a happy one. The new Stratford production doesn't just break that jinx; it treads it, rejoicing, beneath its several pairs of dainty but implacable feet. It gets everything right.

It makes the dialogue work and it makes the plot work. Perhaps the greatest satisfaction of Brian Bedford's production is the joy it dispenses at seeing the absurd symmetry of the action play itself out. In most stagings, the story of Earnest is ignored, perhaps because it's assumed that everyone knows it, while the lines get the wrong kind of attention: the laboured kind. Everybody falls in love at first hearing with this play's endless procession of jokes, but once you've heard them, you've heard them. If they are to keep on delighting us, they not only have to be impeccably delivered, they also have to be impeccably motivated. That's what happens here. Every line is keyed to what its speaker hopes to get out of it, or what he or she hopes to conceal with it. This goes even for the obvious, apparently detachable epigrams; they're counters in a non-stop game of one-upmanship.

As everyone knows, Oscar Wilde's play, like all English comedies and most English dramas, is about class, the ongoing joke being that the conflicts are kept invisible while being fearfully present in everyone's minds. Its first quotable line is the one about the lower classes owing it to their superiors to set a good example (otherwise, "What on earth is the use of them?"), and even before that we've been treated to a subterranean duel between master and man over the merits of the former's piano playing. Indeed, before a line has been spoken, Robert Persichini's manservant, wincing at each misplaced offstage note, has clued us in that this is to be an exceptional evening.

The program notes, including Bedford's own, make great play with the word "subversive;" and it's true that Wilde, on the eve of his own downfall, took great barbed pleasure in having aristocratic society condemn itself out of its own mouth. That doesn't exactly make it The Communist Manifesto; it's unlikely that even in 1895, when the barbs were fresh, they led to any acts of what Lady Bracknell calls "revolutionary outrage." As it happens Bedford, who himself plays Lady B without a trace of pantomime dame, creased me up more violently with that line than with any other of the evening. It's not merely that it was immaculately phrased in itself, though Bedford's entire third-act performance is a sublime comic concerto; it's the way in which it embodies, in its appalled dismissive-ness, the lady's entire mindset. She summons a spectre and then crushes it with the vocal equivalent of a glance. No wonder Jack Worthing compares her to a Gorgon.

Lady Bracknell may be the play's legendary role, but it's the two young men, Jack and Algy, who have to do the heavy lifting; they run the first act together and the second by turns. Their casting here is unimprovable. For a start, the actors' natural pulse rates --Ben Carlson's brisk, Mike Shara's languid -- complement one another as perfectly as they suit the characters. Both of them are leading double lives, but it's the respectable Jack who has the most to lose; his propriety begets anxiety, and Carlson, furtively hearty, plays both to the hilt. He's the production's motor. I've read of actors provoking hysterics when Jack, in mourning for an imaginary brother, produces a black-bordered handkerchief, but I've never experienced it before. Jack is the one character who knows his conduct is ridiculous but could never admit that he himself is. He really is earnest. Shara, in a waistcoat that Wilde himself

might have envied, is enchantingly shameless, a duck's back to his friend's moralizing water. And though this may be the least sentimental play ever written, the revelation that they are in fact brothers comes within a few yards of being touching.

The two girls match them, with Sara Topham's impregnable hauteur as Gwendolen balanced by Andrea Runge's demurer steeliness as Cecily. As Reverend Chasuble, Stephen Ouimette, a visual symphony of limbs and gaiters, wafts phantasmally through the manor-house garden; Sarah Dodd's Miss Prism melts delightfully though always respectably in his presence. I thought at first she overdid her suburban asperity towards Cecily, on whom she is after all dependent, but on reflection she makes an excellent point. Prism, though personally as blinkered as Lady Bracknell, is the play's social conscience, prevented as a governess from aping the aristocracy as the butlers and valets do. And she has her unwitting revenge. One wouldn't want to throw the baby out with the bath-water, but leaving it in a handbag is an outrage as revolutionary as Aunt Augusta knew in her threatened bones that it would be. - The Importance of Being Earnest is in repertory through Oct. 30. 1-800-567-1600.
