It's taken a long time, but the third season of Prison Break has begun to get exciting in the way one took for granted with its predecessors.

Last week's episode was a double, which in itself is usually enough to ratchet up the tension. And as it went into its second hour, we actually got an escape attempt. This week's "fall finale" - - how did we get suckered in to using these absurd names? -- was even better. And it ended with our hero, Michael Scofield, actually being escorted out of Panama's Sona prison by the authorities, presumably to somewhere worse, though it's hard to imagine what that might be.

We, the viewers, are probably not free of Sona yet, as most of the supporting cast remains locked up inside there. Sad for them, and also for us, because as hellholes go, Sona is boring.

It's also been looking progressively less hellish, and on a sunny day it resembles a holiday camp with generous visiting hours. It's boring because it isn't believable. This makes it a marked contrast to the comparatively credible penitentiary of Fox River, Chicago, where the first season happened.

That setting had a strange double effect; we were perfectly happy to stay there for the duration and observe the daily round, but also invested enough in the escape team to wish desperately for their plans to succeed.

Season Two gave us the escapers on the outside and was even better. It was fun to see the team continually splitting and re- forming; and since they were on the run and in constant danger of re- capture, the escape theme was still playing full blast -- even though it depended improvisation rather than Michael's genius for long-range planning.

He seemed, by the end, to have defeated his enemies, saved his brother who had been framed for murder and sentenced to the chair, and united with the woman he loved. But then there was a cruel twist, with Michael south of the border and back behind bars.

Back there with him were three of his worst enemies: one fellow fugitive and two highly compromised fellow-pursuers. This seemed like it might be fun, but it hasn't worked out that way.

The show seems at its wits' end about what to do with most of its personnel, and it has to contend with our resentment about the people it's got rid of.

And then there's the hoosegow itself, one in which the prisoners were so unruly that the authorities left them to run their own lives, only swooping down from time to time to make an example of some unfortunate inmate. I don't know if this variation of the lunatics taking over the asylum has ever happened in real life, even in Central America, but it sure doesn't ring true here.

So Michael has nothing real to fight against. Lechero, the kingpin felon whom the guards left to impose his own reign of terror is actually, in comic-book terms, quite an engaging figure, though very hard to understand.

Basic comprehension has always been a problem with this show; the downside of Wentworth Miller's glamorous intensity as Michael has been that most of what he said came out as a mutter. Now, with half the cast sporting exotic accents, it's worse.

So what awaits, after the fall (finale)? Or does anything? Prison Break, like every other network series, is about to be grounded by the TV writers' strike, which means that Michael and his scapegrace brother Linc (ironically on the outside while Michael's been in), and those remaining few they hold near and dear, may be in limbo forever.

The upside of this is that we can invent our own developments. I vote, first of all, for Dr. Sara to be brought back to life. I know the producers and the actress have both denied this will happen, and that Linc actually saw Sara's head in a box. But we didn't see it, and Linc can sometimes be dumb as an ox.

Michael is a lesser figure without his constantly-deferred love interest. Ex-prison officer Bellick, who's shown signs of moral rehabilitation, should be encouraged to pursue them; he's been through enough south of the border to warrant redemption, and his humiliation long since ceased to be funny.

T-Bag should be given something to do, or at least something amusing to say. And the all-powerful Company, whose shadowy machinations were a great counterpoint in the first two seasons, should become less shadowy.

We clutch at straws, those of us who struggle to retain our loyalty to shows that used to be great. There is a good reason. The long-form thriller serial -- the pulp relative of the TV novel created by HBO -- is a form still young and worth praying over.
