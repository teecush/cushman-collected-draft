THEATRE REVIEW

One Thousand and One Nights The Joey & Toby Tanenbaum Opera Centre, Toronto

There are scenes in One Thousand and One Nights when we're watching a story within a story within a story within a story. (There may be even deeper ramifications but the human brain can only take so much.) The show, which is both exhilarating and exhausting, is a two-part, six-hour reduction of the massive volume commonly if erroneously known as the Arabian Nights. Its also the first theatrical production that Torontos Luminato has commissioned from scratch, in association with Dash Arts, the company run by the British director Tim Supple. The current show is performed in a mixture of English, French and Arabic, the last two with surtitles, by a company of 19 Arabic actors.

The story from which spring all the others is the worldfamous one of Shahrazad, who offered herself as bride to a king whose habit, after being betrayed by a former wife, was to take a virgin to his bed every night and behead her in the morning. Shahrazad, possessed of fabulous narrative skills, knew that she could keep herself alive by spinning the king a yarn too complex for one night and trusting in his desire to find out what happened next. And then next. Shahrazad invented the cliffhanger.

She starts the king off easy, with the tale of a fisherman who unwittingly releases an angry Jinn from a bottle and saves his own life by daring the demon to creep right back inside his prison. The story is charmingly acted out and we, like the king, are captivated. But that, says Shahrazad, is nothing compared to what happened to the porter who was the fisherman's brother; and we, irretrievably and inextricably, are off.

The porter, helping a lady home with her shopping, finds himself in the house of three beautiful sisters and their two bitches. (No, really, lady dogs.) In the course of a sportive night it is announced, in my favourite line of the entire entertainment, that there are three one-eyed Dervishes at the door. They are quickly followed by three merchants, all fully sighted. It becomes necessary for each of the visitors to explain what brought him there, and for each of their hostesses to explain why they prefer to live as they do, shunning men and marriage. Many of these stories involve people telling other stories. One of the visitors turns out to be the Caliph Haroun Al-Rashid in disguise and under his chairmanship the thing becomes a kind of cutting contest: Tell me a story funnier or more improbable than the one I've just heard, or take the consequences.

This progression, going full blast at the end of Part One, resumes immediately at the start of Part Two, but to lesser effect. This is largely because it is Part Two; we continue to be buffeted by one discrete narrative after another. For a couple of them, I simply tuned out, knowing that everything would be much the same when I tuned in again. Also, though Supples inventiveness and the charm and agility of his cast are considerable, they are not infinite; the show, staged on a three-sided platform, begins to repeat its effects. But theres also a change of tone.

These Arabian Nights aren't kids' stuff; no one told us before, for example, that the king raped Shahrazad before listening to her. The show is violent, more in report than in action, and very erotic, in ways both cruel and tender.

Theres still a lot to feast on, though, not least in the acting. Shahrazad (Houda Echouafni) is beguilingly expressive in Arabic, if stilted in English. Assad Bouab plays the captives captive audience, plus a succession of other disarmingly boyish potentates. Falah Ibrahim is ideally solid as the fisherman and many others, and Ramzi Choukair wins all our sympathies, and many laughs, as the porter, who eventually gets to play Sinbad the Sailor in a tale of burial alive that's the most darkly compelling part of the second instalment. And look, be grateful: We're getting in two nights what cost King Sharayah an additional 999. But of course he did get the girl as well.
