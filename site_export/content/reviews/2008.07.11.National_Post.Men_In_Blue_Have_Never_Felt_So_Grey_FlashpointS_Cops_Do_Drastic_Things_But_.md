Good cop, bad cop, new spin. The central characters of Flashpoint, CTVs new drama series, are Greg, whose job is to talk people out of situations mortally dangerous to themselves and others, and Ed, who, when Greg cant make them see the errors of their ways, shoots them. Ed, a champion sniper, heads a team at Toronto's SRU, one of those dashing television acronyms, here standing for Strategic Reserve Unit. Greg seems theoretically to be his second-in-command, but in practice -- judging from the first two episodes, anyway -- it is he who makes the running, and he who deserves to.

There are other regular team members -- eight actors get over-the-title billing -- but we have yet to discover much about them. There's time for that, though; they all have back stories, or so says the media release, which comes in the form of a dossier and is written in the kind of prose that could make even Mad Men or The Wire sound bad.

Flashpoint isnt in their league. For one thing it follows the crisis-a-week formula, rather than telling a developing story. Like CBCs The Border (and so much else), it's a series rather than a serial (like the public broadcaster's Intelligence and so little else). But, on the evidence available, its better than The Border. The conflicts within the organization seem less contrived, those within the individual characters a little deeper.

Flashpoint brings the action show to the place the "adult Western" reached before that genre crawled into the desert and died. That is, the cops do drastic things to people, physically or psychologically, and then feel bad about it. Its the kind of situation that 24 is always promising but never gets around to delivering, and that even The Wire tended to shy away from. Ed's marriage seems to be in jeopardy, due to his involvement in his work (and maybe his inability to discuss it at home); Greg's has already broken up, depriving him of any contact with his son. At the end of each operational day, they take their guilts home and deposit them in what the promotional material, recklessly mixing its metaphors, describes as their over-stuffed closets.

The first two episodes give them plenty of food for self-examination. The first, inspired, like the whole series, by an actual Toronto happening, is a fairly basic hostage drama. An immigrant man shoots a (presumably) immigrant woman who refuses to let him back in her life; he then grabs a bystander and holds a gun to her head. Greg, on the ground, asks him to put down his weapon; Ed, on the roof, gets ready to ensure that he will. The group also includes a pixieish sniperess, but she takes a wrong turn on the way up, so Ed has the action to himself. We learn that the code-word for shoot! is "Scorpio"; we wait to learn whether Scorpio will rise or will be averted. The options keep changing, and the arrival of the gunmans son raises the stakes, especially the psychological ones for Ed. Casting notes: Stephanie Morgenstern, one of the shows two cocreators, plays the (understandably) silent hostage and Dragana Varagic, one of Toronto's finest but most under-used actresses, is the initial victim, who is very voluble while she lives, but speaks only in Croatian. So does her killer. This makes it hard, both for the SRU and for viewers, to get inside his head.

This doesn't hold for Episode 2, which is told very much from the viewpoint of what the SRU would call the subject. He, well played by Henry Czerny, is Jack, a widower whose young and beloved daughter has been called into hospital for a long-awaited heart transplant. But theres been a computer glitch, and an older man is first in line to receive the new organ. Father and daughter are told to go home. Whereupon Jack seizes a gun and the contested heart and insists that it be inserted according to his specifications. Our boys (and girls) speed to the scene. Ed stands ready, along with a tiresome young stud of a new recruit who would plainly prefer to shoot first and, if necessary, afterwards. But this is really Gregs scene. As a kind of bereaved father himself, he has a direct line into how Jack feels and he uses it, both compassionately and ruthlessly.

I said that Flashpoint is formulaic. Its formula is to plunge viewers into the crisis at the start and then flash up a "two hours earlier" title, so that we can learn how both sides got there. It works better in Episode

2, the situation being more detailed and more special.

The first episode is decently exciting; the second is, I'm tempted to say, indecently tense. Anything involving a sick child, especially with a distraught parent thrown in, has built-in emotional force. Not only did I want to know what happened, I wanted to know how the writers would get out of the corner into which they had painted themselves. I don't think they do. (The ending is very American, network rather than cable. Once we would have called it "Hollywood." I hope thats cryptic enough not to spoil anyones fun.) But I bought the feelings, if not all of the actual narrative, and Enrico Colantoni is excellent as decent, troubled Greg. So, with less to go on, is Hugh Dillon as less decent, more troubled Ed. So, I'm sure, will be all the decent, troubled others, once they get the chance.

- Flashpoint premieres tonight at 10 on CTV.
