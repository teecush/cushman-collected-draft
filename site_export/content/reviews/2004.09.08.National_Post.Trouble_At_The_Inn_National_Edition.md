MIRANDOLINA

Harbourfront Theatre Centre,

Toronto

- - -

The Italian play we know in English as Mirandolina is actually called La Locandiera, which translates roughly as The Female Innkeeper. Mirandolina, the heroine of Carlo Goldoni's 1753 comedy, does indeed keep an inn, one in Florence inherited from her father, and keeps it very well. Her male guests adore her, or most of them do; the smitten ones we meet are a rich Count and a poor Marquis. The one holdout is a man referred to in Soulpepper's program as the Cavaliere and in the actual (uncredited) text as the Knight. It's nothing personal; he resists Mirandolina because he resists all women. So she sets out to humble him.

She'll make him fall in love like any other man and maybe then he'll know what kind of fool he am, er, is. And that's exactly how it works out. This task accomplished, Mirandolina turns her back on the encircling gentry and -- this is not a spoiler -- bestows it on Fabrizio, her only visible member of staff, to whom she had promised it all along, this arrangement being something else bequeathed to her by her departed dad.

Mirandolina is a play with a slender story, beautifully put together. Its story could well seem very conventional: Attractive working woman has fun with the nobs and snobs, puts them properly in their place, and ends up with her true love, who is of satisfyingly humble birth. That's one way of looking at it, and it may very well be the right way. But it isn't the only way, and Laszlo Marton's Soulpepper production tries for something more complicated.

As in many Shakespeare comedies, there are awkward bits left over at the end, and a production has the choice of sweeping them merrily under the carpet or leaving them exposed and throbbing. In Marton's version, there is pain on view. Mirandolina arouses in the Knight a passion that neither of them can control. He ends up wounded and she ends up scared and more than a little guilty; when she falls into her potboy's arms, it's not because he represents love, it's because he represents shelter. She's settling.

I quite enjoyed the show, but it frankly doesn't work. This is surprising, coming from the director who showed us all the dark comedy in Chekhov and Moliere, but this production is listless. The central fault is not in the interpretation but in the casting, or at least in one piece of it. Sweet or bitter, if the play is The Taming of the Misogynist, then that man's humour had better be there from the beginning, a fact in the play, upfront and kicking. Stuart Hughes was the best thing in last year's Soulpepper production of the faintly similar She Stoops to Conquer, but there he played a paralytically shy man rendered bold in the presence of a social inferior, preferably female. The Knight he plays here seems creditably devoid of class consciousness, and has no noticeable inhibitions whatever. He is presumably meant to be a hard-bitten soldier, but as Hughes presents him he is, at most, a cashiered cadet with a dissolute air. He does his best to play the roaring boy, and he has some good howling moments when his defences crumble, but it's hard to understand why his hostess should regard him as such a challenge.

This of course rebounds on her performance as well as on his. Mirandolina is supposed to be a woman everybody falls in love with, and there could hardly be better casting for that than Kristen Thomson, for looks, personality and a sense of all-round capability. It's the way she runs her house, as much as anything else that attracts the men. The production's best scene shows her seducing the Knight by cooking and serving him the perfect ragu: Ah, those Italians. Her smile and her decolletage don't hurt, either.

Marton's visual inventiveness (or the physical inventiveness he inspires in his actors) remains intact; the production's abiding image is of Thomson draping her shawl over her own head and the Knight's, then withdrawing, leaving him lost and floundering. It sums up their relationship. But things are seldom that precise. Thomson is firing on all cylinders, but indiscriminately, and it's hard to follow what's on the woman's mind or why. She's plainly meant to be more than a tease, but her actions are too cruel, too relished, for us to take her as a defender of sensible womanhood. She only gets it together at the play's epilogue, a routine farewell to the audience that she endows with half-a-dozen shades of meaning, flashed out in dazzling succession.

The whole cast goes off in all directions; not as unhappily as in Marton's production of A Flea in Her Ear, but with similarly jerky results. Diego Matamoros' Marquis is physically extreme but vocally subdued: an odd combination, and one that prevents the central joke of the impoverished aristocrat from coming through clearly enough to be funny. (There's one like him, boasting of his lineage and terminally cheap, in every Goldoni play; he may have disdained earlier stock figures, but he wasn't above inventing his own.)

Michael Simpson does better at the Count, especially when his pride flashes out and drives him to a duel. Richard Zeppieri makes a loutish, possessive Fabrizio, but I suspect that's director's interpretation rather than actor's choice, and there's nothing wrong with the way he does it; plus, as you might tell from his name, he's ethnically right. Also passing through the inn are a couple of penniless actresses, posing as noblewomen, and battening off each of the men in turn. One is played blowsily and well by Diana Leblanc, and the other sleekly and even better by Jennifer Gould. Paul Manz is charming as the Knight's man, who, in a delightful touch, is as gone on Mirandolina as everybody else.

The costumes are non-specifically modern, thereby compromising one of the play's principal attractions, the view it affords us of a distant society complete in itself. Astrid Janson's set, though, is timeless, a high chamber whose altitude accommodates a huge quantity of fully burdened laundry lines. A breeze blows enchantingly through the sheets as the play starts; a rainstorm, delightfully summoned through Andrea Lundy's lighting, is the cue for everything to be brought inside. This gives us a link to the play's most celebrated scene, in which Mirandolina does the ironing while fending off the Knight's advances. Which, he asks plaintively, means more to you, that linen or me; and Mirandolina, practical as ever, tells him.
