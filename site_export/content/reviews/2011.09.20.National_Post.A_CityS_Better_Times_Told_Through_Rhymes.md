THEATRE REVIEW

The Tale of a Town

Theatre Passe Muraille, Toronto

It isn't really accurate to say that The Tale of a Town happens at Theatre Passe Muraille. Taking its name literally, and perhaps reverting to its roots, TPM is here the embarkation point for a walkabout tour of the immediate surrounding area of Queen Street West, with an ultimate destination that's just a couple of blocks away, at least if you happen to be a crow. It's a mix of two kinds of theatre, promenade crossed with site-specific.

The audience gathers outside the theatre building, there to be greeted by a white-faced mime whose performance seems to be a commentary on the gentrification of the neighbourhood. (The accompanying song is Rise Up.) This turns out to be the merest prelude to the main event, though it does establish the theme. Our attention is rudely commandeered by an excitable lady in a fright wig (blond) and a fright dress (red) who will be our guide on the magical mystery tour. She talks in rhyming couplets whose main effect was to make me, for the first time in my life, nostalgic for the mime.

Lisa Marie DiLiberto is the lady doing the talking while leading the walking. She's the artistic director of Fixt Point, whose co-production this is, and is the show's principal creator and sole actress, though with some assistance from musicians and what the credits call "shadow play performers." She appears later in more agreeable guises than her first, but it's hard to forgive the initial impression.

Why do people think couplets are so easy to write? Perhaps they are, if you have no regard for scansion or even, on occasion, to what a strict critic might regard as rhyme. It's a tough discipline, which if the rules are obeyed can have great potential for wit. If they aren't, it has even greater potential for being excruciating. The latter being the case here, I'm not sure whether it's good or bad that the doggerel being screeched at us in the open air as we jostle for an advantageous position is often inaudible. We seem, though, to be asked to admire the transformation of what was once a bohemian neighbourhood, and to be asked to buy into it. ("Two years ago you had the foresight to surmise / Your new condo was going to rise.") Life colluding with art, we walk right past a brand-new Winners.

All this of course is "deeply ironic." Our destination, when we reach it, turns out to be the loft floor of a recently burnedout building, which for the purposes of the show is being advertised as the Champagne Flaming Feather Eco Boho Legend Lofts, ripe for conversion at astronomical prices. (For $610,000,000, says a projected legend on one of the walls, you get all the amenities, including two phone jacks.) And in fact, and presumably at far lesser expense, the production team has done a great job of converting it into a performance space. We keep being ushered in to scenic areas we hadn't realized existed, and the videos, props and sound effects mustered under Varrick Grimes' direction are impressive, both in themselves and in the precision with which they punctuate the narrative, which does exist, even though it comes to us in the form of retrospective monologue.

It's the self-told story of Jane, whom DiLiberto plays with comparative (and merciful) restraint, who for many years ran a vintage-clothing boutique on Queen West but had to close when her landlord, with many regrets left on her voice mail, sold her out. She reminisces about the good times, including her romance with a rockabilly musician who drugged himself to death. Well, maybe you had to be there.

The story takes a long time to come into focus, and at this distance the scene doesn't sound all that attractive, or all that missable. Extrapolate as you will, it hardly amounts to the tale of a whole town. The most resonant line in the show is Jane's admission that when she started her business, she herself must have been displacing someone else. That bit of perspective apart, the show's journey (the appropriate term for once) is from clumsy satire to high-tech sentimentality.
