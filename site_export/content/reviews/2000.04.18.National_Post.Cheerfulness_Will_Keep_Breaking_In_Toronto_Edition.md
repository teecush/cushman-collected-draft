FOOTFALLS/KRAPP'S LAST TAPE

Harbourfront, Toronto

OTRA TEMPESTAD

Buddies in Bad Times, Toronto

Do you remember last Saturday? It was lovely inToronto. How long ago it seems.

It must have been on just such a day that somebody in Ireland remarked to Samuel Beckett on how fine the weather was. Beckett, we are told, agreed.

"It's the kind of day," his friend continued, pressing his advantage "that makes you glad to be alive."

"Oh," said Beckett, "I wouldn't go as far as that."

Two of Sam's short plays -- Footfalls and Krapp's Last Tape -- are being given at Harbourfront as part of the World Stage season, and I emerged from them into the Saturday-night balm, just in time to collide with other spectators who had been spending a day at The Oresteia in the house next door. There we all were, grave but resilient, joint witnesses to two-and-a-half millennia of human tragedy.

With Aeschylus as with Beckett, cheerfulness will keep breaking in.

The last part of The Oresteia, after all, dramatizes the birth of a humane legal system -- the moment at which trial-by-jury replaced the more primitive process of trial-by-Fury.

There is no guarantee, though, that humanity will not slip back into its old vengeful, warmongering ways -- not with those capricious and extremely provisional Greek gods in charge. Beckett, working on a far narrower canvas, similarly gives us brief glimpses of happiness before proceeding to stomp all over them.

Footfalls, which is late Beckett, has always appealed to me as the author's sour joke on his audience. It came after a succession of plays in which the characters -- immured in dustbins or sandpiles or funeral urns, or just crouched and immobile -- had been progressively denied the use, and even the sight, of their limbs. Audiences (and possibly actors) were getting restless. May, the one visible character here, is seen, however, at full length and -- my God! -- she even gets to move about. In fact she paces ceaselessly. She doesn't, though, seem to enjoy it much.

"And that," you can hear the author saying, "is what movement gets you. Don't ask me for it again."

May is pacing because she has to attend to her bedridden mother, whom we hear but do not see, and the routine has turned her into a resentful automaton. But then, it seems, she always was that way inclined. She also has a very Beckettian habit of regarding herself as a character in a literary text.

The bare-bones situation is similar to the one depicted in a more recent Irish play, The Beauty Queen of Linnane, with the difference that these characters don't hate one another.

May's mother, indeed (especially as delivered here, in the deep, silver tones of Jennifer Phipps), is enduring, and even solicitous. She worries about her daughter -- as well, given Elizabeth Shepherd's performance, she might. Shepherd's physical aspect as the one proud walker is magnificent: her face is a tragic mask, with enough grief packed into it to furnish an entire Greek chorus. She matches it, though, with a slow, unvarying, agonized delivery, as of a rusting gate. The look is authentic but the voice sounds painted on.

This portentous way of doing Beckett derives, I think, from the vocal mannerisms of the late Patrick Magee, the momentous Irish actor who was one of his favoured interpreters and who, among other things, was the first to perform the earlier Krapp's Last Tape.

This monologue is an unqualified masterpiece: a work that illuminates a human condition (if not the human condition) through a single, transfixing image that is also both whimsically and sardonically funny. It's surprising the play hasn't been done in Canada before, since one of its principal early exponents, on stage and records, was Donald Davis of this parish.

It is also, even more than Footfalls, a realistic piece. You want to know where Krapp is? He's in what passes for his study. You want to know what he is? He's a writer whose potential, both personal and professional, has been savagely unfulfilled. You want to know what he's doing? He's replaying one of a set of tape recordings he has made at yearly intervals, as an aural diary, reflecting on his past and present. Krapp at 70 listens to Krapp at 40 vowing to cut down on his unhealthy consumption of bananas (he hasn't) and lamenting the end of a love affair that, it seems, he destroyed because it was going too well. He makes a new tape, castigating his own past sentimentality, but the pain in his eyes shows he still feels it.

John Neville plays him superbly at both stages: walking with an arthritic trudge, taking his last-ditch pleasures -- of peeling his bananas and enunciating his favourite word, "spool" -- with sybaritic relish, and generally reminding us how fine a character- actor he can be when not giving hollow displays of the voice beautiful.

An even finer vocal instrument, that of the 80-year old William Hutt, was celebrated on Sunday afternoon at the Winter Garden. With Diane d'Aquila as compere, this occasion was more fluent and mostly less sticky than such tributes often are. Colleagues and admirers paid homage, told stories, even sang songs (Mad About the Boy, with an additional male lyric previously unheard.)

There were videos of Hutt himself, revisiting past homes and enjoying his present one. He finally took the stage himself, to respond to what had been said with a presumably imprompu wit that vaildated the anecdotes. Much was justifiably made of his appearance on a Canadian stamp, in his last great role of Prospero.

So it was appropriate to repair to Buddies in Bad Times for Otra Tempestad, presented by Teatro Buendia from Cuba, and probably the first production of The Tempest in which the first character named in the program is Hamlet. Macbeth and Shylock also appear; and so, apparently, in an earlier version, did Othello, though he seems to have been lost in transit.

They share the stage, and the Afro-Caribbean island, with a bearded dictatorial Prospero, a lusciously bare-breasted female Ariel (well acted, too, by Giselle Navaroli), and a Miranda who falls for Caliban though her father wants her to marry Macbeth.

It's Shylock who snitches on the lovers, and he's sneaky throughout. He also chants, wears a prayer shawl, and is generally more regrettable than his Venetian prototype. Fidel-Prospero has his faults but, as yet another Shakesperean character once put it, "I fear there will a worse come in his place." Macbeth, who deposes him, turns out to be a cannibal.

Throwing Shakespeare wide open can be a fruitful idea, and the local colour and local music make Flora Lauten's production fun, but there seems little point to doing it beyond doing it. There is invention but no true inventiveness; and the audience, primed by scene-by-scene projected synopses, merely waits for each episode to give way to the next.
