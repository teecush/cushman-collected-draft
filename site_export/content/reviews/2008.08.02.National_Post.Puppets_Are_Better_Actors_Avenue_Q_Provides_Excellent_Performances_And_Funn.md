Note: Avenue Q runs through Aug. 31 at the Elgin Theatre. For tickets and information call 416-644-3665.

There are ways in which Avenue Q is like Chicago. Dont get too excited. Chicago was -- well, still is -- a musical that shamelessly revels in what it calls razzle-dazzle while affecting to decry and expose it. Avenue Q presents itself as a grown-up Sesame Street; it uses tropes and characters from the children's TV show to present a harsher view of the world than preschoolers should ever be exposed to. When the crunch comes, however, the stage musical turns far more preachy and sentimental than the thing it parodies. Indeed, even to say that is to be unfair to Sesame Street which, unless it has greatly changed since my kids were kids, has never been either of those things.

It's also, I confess, not wholly fair to Avenue Q, a musical with a good score, an OK book and -- in this touring version of the 2003 New York original -- some excellent performances. Its storyline concerns Princeton, a young man who comes to the fictitious street of the title because he cant afford lodging on any thoroughfare higher up the alphabet. He is, in fact, broke. "What," he sings, do you do with a BA in English? a question some of us are still asking, and a first line thats so good that the authors hardly bother to write the rest of the song. They do, though, hang in long enough to let Princeton declare that he still has a feeling that he "might make a difference to the human race." (You can see where this is leading. It's leading to a song called Purpose.) He knows he's among friends when the locals join in a competitive chorus entitled It Sucks To Be Me.

Among those whom it sucks to be are Brian, an unbooked stand-up comic; his -- careful now -- Asian-American fiancee, Christmas Eve (I a therapist but I have no clients); and a pair of male roommates called Rod and Nicky. The landlord is Gary Coleman, real-life former TV child star, whose fame is now so far in the past that the joke passed me by completely, though the writers think its a good one, judging by the number of times they repeat it. (Just to make it more confusing, Gary is played by a woman. His voice hasnt broken yet?) Perhaps most importantly, there is Kate Monster, a teacher's assistant, with whom Princeton embarks on a tentative relationship, because they're both rather tentative people--especially him.

Brian, Gary and Christmas Eve are played by real, full-grown actors. All the others are played by puppets, voiced and manipulated by other full-grown, and fully visible, actors -- just like on Sesame Street where the population is divided between Muppets and others. Here, though, the only reason for the divide seems to be to remind us of the source. Some of the parallels are rib-nudgingly close. Kate has a semi-namesake (but not a relative) called Trekkie Monster, who is all furry and growly. And as for Rod and Nicky: If you ever wondered whether Bert and Ernie were gay, well it turns out they were. Or at least Bert was. Or rather, Rod is. And closeted, and votes Republican. And though outing him may count as a fearless expose, it seems a thin exchange for the rich cross-talk act that Jim Henson and Frank Oz used to provide (and must still do, in reruns). This is despite the fact that Rods furious-denial song about My Girlfriend Who Lives in Canada ("Her name is Alberta / She lives in Vancouver") is rather funny.

The highly promising Robert Lopez and Jeff Marx wrote the shows music and lyrics, whose mock-educational content owes something to the actual Sesame Street but whose style recalls the off-Broadway musicals of the '60s: gentle and tuneful with the merest hint of a rock beat. (The lyrics also work in some subliminal Sondheim homages, while one song -- he made me a mix tape sounds like an update of the Ice Cream number from She Loves Me.) Kate's Theres a Fine, Fine Line is a fine -- if not quite fine, fine -- ballad thats searching as well as touching. Most other numbers are comic list-songs that depend on how inventively they can fill out their premise. Everyone's a Little Bit Lacist (that's how Christmas Eve sings it) does this very well; so does Schadenfreude, whose examples include "football players getting tackled / CEOs getting shackled." But The Internet is for Porn, Trekkie Monsters credo, just repeats itself, which, I suppose, fits the subject.

Princeton is winsomely played by Robert McClure, who doubles as Rod, and Kate is delightfully played by Anika Larsen, who doubles as Lucy the Slut; both bring off a different kind of doubling by acting alongside their puppets as well as through them, and always making the feelings match. I also liked the two Bad Advice Bears who get Princeton and Kate into bed. (So thats bad advice?) Brian is played by an actor called Cole Porter; he must be used to remarks on his name, so here's another one: How neat to have the only Jew in the show played by the only goy among the great songwriters. Jason Moore did the neat direction, and Jeff Whitty wrote the book, which has some good laughs but succumbs to uplift. Princeton goes through the show looking for his Purpose, and the show finally gives him one, in the shape of altruism. It then tries to make a joke of it and then goes sappy again. Like Chicago, it wants to have it both ways. And like Wicked (though Avenue Q's a better show) it hitches a ride off a far superior, and actually more adult, original. This isn't adaptation, or even thoroughgoing parody. It's freeloading.
