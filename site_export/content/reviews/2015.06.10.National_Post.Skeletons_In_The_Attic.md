Theatre review

The Diary of Anne Frank

Jillian Keiley's production of The Diary of Anne Frank is very much a Production. It incorporates a number of devices not seen in any previous staging of this piece. They don't, with one possible exception, harm the play's impact. But I'm not persuaded that they help it either.

The overriding intention seems to be to change the conventional focus of the play's title. The title character is no longer the diarist but the diary. Innovation Number One has all the actors taking turns at reading excerpts from it between scenes. Innovation Two (I'm not listing them chronologically) has the cast lining up at the top of the show to share with us their own memories of first reading the diary, or of hearing about the Holocaust, or simply to tell us about things in their own childhoods that somehow connect with that of Anne Frank, who experienced all the normal strains of adolescence in the decidedly abnormal setting of the Amsterdam attic in which she and her Jewish family, plus four others, spent two wartime years in hiding from the Nazis.

Each of the actors tells us which character he or she will be playing: a sort of counteralienation effect that accords the experiences we are about to witness an additional layer of respect. By acknowledging that the people on stage are merely players, it paradoxically frees us to respond to the real people they represent.

There are 10 of these people: the eight fugitives plus their two Gentile protectors, Mr. Frank's former business partner and his female assistant. They are not, however, the only participants in the prologue. There are half-adozen extras, billed collectively as Chorus. This brings us to Innovation Three, the dubious one. At various moments during the play proper, the chorus hide behind a scrim, and indulge in a capella humming, so that we'll know which bits of the story to be moved by. Most of the effect is subliminal, but when it's noticeable, it's annoying. It feels as if the theatre is belatedly catching up to an idea that the more intelligent TV series have abandoned, and that feels increasingly superfluous in movies.

The production unfolds on a slatted set by Britta Gerecke that isn't especially claustrophobic but does helpfully map out the different compartments into which the prisoners, as they effectively were, divided their living space. Sara Farb is an intelligent, unsentimental Anne; Shannon Taylor is a sympathetic mouse as her sister Margot; and Joseph Ziegler has all the strength and gentleness required for their father. Yanna McIntosh and Kevin Bundy give splendid performances as the senior members of the attic's second family, the Van Daans, she impatient and protective, he impatient and pitiful, continually quarrelling and making up as they must have done in the real world. The scene in which he's caught stealing from the community's tiny supply of bread is a highlight. (It always is, but it's higher here than usual.)

McIntosh also makes the most memorable contribution to the prologue, when she tells us that learning about the Holocaust as a child made her promise herself that in similar circumstances she would have defended her Jewish music teacher until it occurred to her, being black, she wouldn't have been too popular with the Nazis herself. This gets a nervous, sympathetic laugh, whose memory lends a special intensity to her presence in the play proper; it's nontraditional casting given a frame, best of both worlds.

In the last scene of the play as written, Mr. Frank visits his two benefactors and tells them of the fates of the other seven. This rather clunky device is replaced here (Innovation Four) by something more straightforward: Mr. Frank appears alone to give us the facts, ending by handing a member of the audience a copy of the diary, to keep remembrance alive. So we have a new epilogue to balance the prologue; Ziegler, who was the first to bear witness at the beginning, is now playing the character rather than playing himself, but the two personae effectively blend. At any rate, nobody could deliver the news more simply and movingly than he does. He completes the play's process of putting agonized human faces on statistics almost too appalling to comprehend. That's the play's primary purpose, and the production carries it out. It doesn't, though, do it any more effectively than have some more conventional productions, notably the previous, exceptional Stratford one directed by Al Waxman. And no production, really, can hope to match the devastating effect of the one in Berlin in the 1950s, as unforgettably described by Kenneth Tynan: "After the interval the man in front of me put his head in his hands and did not afterwards look at the stage. He was not, I believe, Jewish."
