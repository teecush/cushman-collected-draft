Stephen Sondheim died on Friday at age 91. And the world - or the part that cares about musical theatre - is in mourning.

My own devotion to him began in 1970, in London. A friend was raving about the cast album of a new Broadway musical called Company.

I liked the title. I also liked the fact that the score was by Stephen Sondheim, whom I already admired as the lyricist of West Side Story and Gypsy, and the lyricist and composer of A Funny Thing Happened on the Way to the Forum. So I bought the record and listened. And listened.

I was knocked out. There was something to savour in nearly every lyrical line. And the music, beguilingly astringent, was the perfect complement.

Id always loved musicals, but this one was different. It's about a 30-something New York bachelor who had lots of married friends (company) but is still alone. At one point he asks one of them, "are you ever sorry you got married?" To which the friend replies, in song, You're always sorry, youre always grateful, developing the theme through a litany of vivid, concise illustrations: "Youre scared she's starting to drift away/And scared she'll stay."

Company has a later song that does the work of an entire scene. Our hero, Robert, is in bed with a flight attendant.

Come morning, he asks her where shes off to and she replies with the title of the song: Barcelona. Entreating her to stay, he gives out with an impassioned, Youre just a very special girl, June! Which would be lovely except that her name is April. I sometimes think that "June," so precisely placed in the song, is the funniest line in all of Sondheim.

A composer, Richard Rodgers, may have said the essential thing about Sondheim as lyricist: Steve has a curious way of making people sing as though they were talking.

There's actually a bit more to it than that. Broadway lyricists had been writing conversationally since the 1910s. Later, they went beyond having characters saying unambiguously what they were thinking and had them admitting to doubts. It was Sondheim who took the next step, to deal in halftones and unconscious self-betrayals.

There's a lot of irony in his shows but it comes not so much in the words the characters sing as in the gaps between what they sing and what they are.

I wanted to meet the composer and to write about him. The next year I got my chance: Company was announced for London, and I had a hook. In the meantime another Sondheim show had opened in New York, Follies, and I was able to catch both of them before doing my interview. Company by this point was on its last legs (the original cast had decamped for the U.K.), and perhaps I had played the record too often. Follies by contrast came up fresh, and magnificent.

Set partly in the present and partly in a remembered showbiz past, it was gorgeously staged and better acted than any musical I could remember. The flashback songs were perfect pastiche, verbally and musically, but they were also dramatically resonant. The up-to-date songs cut like knives: They were ruthless about human failure without implicitly congratulating themselves on how daring they were being.

The man himself, when I knocked by appointment on the door of his elegant mid-Manhattan townhouse (neighbouring Katharine Hepburns), was friendly and courteous but not, by the high-pressure standards of U.S. show business, effusive. I knew he was the child of a broken New York marriage and that his life, by his own account, had been saved by his virtual adoption into the Oscar Hammerstein family. When I asked, I hoped artlessly, how big an influence the lyricist Hammerstein had been on him, he replied "Oscar had a complete influence on me." Not, he readily agreed, in terms of style and outlook, the older mans being folksy and optimistic, but of musical storytelling, musical dramaturgy.

We agreed to meet again in London, when Company was in preparation there. We had three conversations in the back of the stalls at Her Majesty's Theatre and were both more relaxed. I think I won his confidence when I described a certain hit show as the musical I had most hated in recent years. His face lit up as he said it was one of the two musicals he had most hated.

My eventual article began: Every so often it becomes possible to say that the American musical lives. At the moment it lives chiefly in the person of Stephen Sondheim, who writes music that is alive and lyrics that are incomparable.

I sent him a copy, and was overjoyed when he wrote back: "Dear Mr Cushman I loved your article. Partly it's the flattery involved ..." but apparently there were other reasons as well. He disagreed, though, with my praise of I Feel Pretty from West Side Story: No Puerto Rican girl would sing quite so much like Noel Coward, I fear.

And so began a friendship: regular letters and later emails (he was a meticulously punctual correspondent, something he said he learned from his father), Scotchfuelled conversations in that townhouse whenever I was in New York. By now I had become theatre critic of The Observer and he was eager to discuss whatever I had seen: plays, I noticed, more than musicals. His opinions, pro and con, were emphatic. ("I like Charles Strouses music a lot" - practically pounding a Manhattan restaurant table - not a little, a lot.) He was 41 when I met him and looked younger. Boyish, in fact. When, a few years later, he grew a beard, he said it was because he had become self-conscious about looking like a Peter Pan. But in 1970 he was at a turning point in his career. His 1957 debut with West Side Story, one of the most prestigious of all musicals, gave him a boy-wonder status, and his following work on Gypsy and Forum both confirmed and deepened his talents. These shows, plus a couple more that failed, made him a cult-figure, especially as a lyricist. (In New York. He was less recognized outside, though I like to think that, as a critic in London, I did something to change that.)

He was recognized as one of the best of a good new generation of Broadway songwriters. But it wasnt until Company and Follies that he was recognized as the best of them all - in fact, in a class of his own. Both of these shows were produced and directed by his lifelong friend Hal Prince (another great and good man, who had died nearly two years before Sondheim, and at the same age of 91). The combination of Prince's showmanship, Sondheim's craftsmanship, and their complementary intelligence and ambition, dominated the musical of the 70s. The list includes A Little Night Music, a romantic show that seemed at the time a little tame for them but has proved very durable and that gave Sondheim his only pop hit, Send In the Clowns. Then there was my own favourite: Pacific Overtures, Broadway out of kabuki, about the forcible "opening up" of 19th-century Japan, inspiring Princes most dazzling staging and some of Sondheim's loveliest music and most biting lyrics. (Hal said of his collaborator "he may not come in political but he goes out political.") The show was Prince's idea. The next, Sweeney Todd, was, unusually, Sondheims.

It was also the first Broadway first night I ever attended, complete with after-party at Sardis. Despite this, I thought the show a tour-de-force rather than a masterpiece. But it too has grown on me in revival (though I've never seen a Sweeney to match the original, Canada's own Len Cariou) and it certainly touches greatness in its depiction of the avenging barbers murderous obsession - obsession being a, well, obsessive theme in Sondheim.

The Sondheim-Prince alliance ended with Merrily We Roll Along, a flop, but with a score too good to disappear. It sent Sondheim into a prolonged period of wound-licking.

But he emerged from it to find a new partner, the playwright and director James Lapine, with whom he created Sunday in the Park With George and the fairy tale musical Into the Woods. Then came a reunion with John Weidman, librettist of Pacific Overtures, for Assassins, a collage about the people who have tried, sometimes successfully, to murder U.S. presidents. (This time, I think, he went in political.)

For me, the twin masterpieces are Into the Woods and Assassins. Assassins understands its characters, losers excluded from the American dream, without condoning them. Its climactic song, Another National Anthem, may, in giving full-throated voice to failure rather than success, be the most un-Broadway big number to be written by a Broadway master.

Into the Woods spends its first half ingeniously melding three traditional fairy tales and one invented one, and its second half remixing them to investigate what happens after happily ever after. Its an initially lighthearted show that turns dark and then works its way back into the light. It's also the one show in which Sondheim preaches. He's earned the right and the song, Children Will Listen, at once warning and celebration, is golden. Ive twice felt that a Sondheim show was speaking directly to me. The first was Company, which I saw when I, like the protagonist, needed to stop being lonely. (His name didnt hurt either.) Into the Woods I first encountered when I was a new and very nervous parent. (I never told Sondheim this. I wish now I had.)

I also owe to him the biggest live audience I'm ever likely to play to, when I interviewed him for Mirvish Productions at Toronto's Princess of Wales Theatre in December 2010. People said it was as if we were chatting in his living room. Not surprising, since wed been doing precisely that for 40 years.

He was a musical dramatist, and Im not alone in thinking him the best U.S. playwright of his generation.

I'd actually go further.

I felt nervous the first time I called him a modern Shakespeare, but I'll stand by it.

Like Shakespeare, he took an artificial form - song or blank verse - and made it sound perfectly natural and also, precisely because of those formal constraints, absolutely inevitable. And like Shakespeare he had the gift of imagining himself into practically any character, and submerging his own identity into it. He was a gay man with piercing insights into heterosexual marriage, a childless man who wrote lovingly about parenthood. In duets and ensembles, he could take all possible sides. He could be very moving, and was often hilariously funny.

He was the heir to a great tradition that he enriched and expanded. I don't see anyone replacing him.
