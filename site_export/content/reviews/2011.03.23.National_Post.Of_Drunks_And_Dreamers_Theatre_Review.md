THE TIME OF YOUR LIFE

Young Centre for the Performing Arts, Toronto

I had one of the times of my life at The Time of Your Life when Soulpepper first revived it three years ago. I had an even better time now that they have revived their revival.

William Saroyan's 1939 play is set in a San Francisco honkytonk saloon populated, like most of the great theatrical bar rooms, by a collection of drunks and dreamers. (The latter, surprisingly, outnumber the former. Compare it with the play's monumental anti-type, Eugene O'Neill's The Iceman Cometh, in which the two categories are almost synonymous.)

Albert Schultz's impeccably rhythmed production unfolds on Lorenzo Savoini's genre-defining set, between a series of fixed points. Upstage is the bar, where Nick, the owner, dispenses alcohol and a humorous tolerant brand of authority; Derek Boyes' genial performance in this role has strengthened over the years, and his momentary embrace of his young daughter (Alyson MacFarlane) is now a piercing emotional highlight, suggesting a world elsewhere.

Downstage right is a pin table to which a fanatical player (Ins Choi) devotes almost the entire length of the play. On the wall stage left is a phone, the conduit to many of the characters' hopes and despairs. And downstage from there sits Joe, who drinks only Champagne, because he can afford it, and who benevolently arranges some of the other characters' destinies, because he can afford that, too.

JosephZiegler'sperformance of his namesake is the magnetic, magnificent centre from which everything else radiates. Joe has made a pile in some unspecified business, which he says involved stealing, "and not from the rich," like all legalized money-making. We also learn that he never married or had a family, though he can imagine both. Ziegler's performance is the embodiment of still waters running deep; it summons the memory of great pain, both suffered and inflicted, and the desire to atone for it.

Particularly delightful is his relationship with his gofer Tom -a perfect performance by Kevin Bundy, naive and enthusiastic and on the edge of goofy -whom he sends out to buy toys and newspapers, and with whom he indulges in a gum-chewing contest, gleefully presided over by Stuart Hughes as a self-proclaimed old frontiersman, some of whose tall tales may even be true.

This scene, extraordinary enough in its own right, also helps forward Tom's blushingly ardent courtship of Kitty Duvall, a streetwalker who may or may not have a heart of gold but who does have the traditional supply of life-support illusions. Her name may be one of them. Karen Rae's Kitty is too much stolid, too little vulnerable; all the same a scene in her apartment, in which the play breaks its own implicit commitment to unity of place, turns out to be one of the best things in the production: tense and affectionate and beautifully inserted in its own pool of light.

The transition is also eased into by having Denzel Sinclaire, a fine singer-pianist in the Nat Cole vein, preface it with a dreamy rendition of Little Girl Blue. His actual character, very well played, is a dishwasher whose talent gets him promoted to inhouse entertainer and whose ill-treatment helps precipitate the denouement. Partnering him is Jeff Lillico as a hopeful -indeed, super-optimistic -hoofer-comedian whom the play declares to be both brilliant and terrible. His dancing brings down the house; his monologues provoke Oliver Dennis to advance on him with handcuffs.

Dennis plays an intermittently introspective cop who finds he has less in common with his buddies than with the old school pal whose longshoreman's strike he is supposed to be breaking, and to whom Brendan Wall gives a powerful supply of baffled bitterness. Gregory Prest is delightfully confused as the play's second hopeful wooer (mainly via that phone) whose true love (Krystin Pellerin) finds herself tearfully confronting the likelihood that she'll lose him in the coming war: still two years off if you were American, but the play knows.

Nearly everyone in this play is nice; even a snobbish society gentleman (John Jarvis) resentfully indulging his slumming wife (Brenda Robins) has his moment of protesting at cruelty. The necessary devil. unredeemed and irredeemable, is Blick from the vice squad, to whose nastiness Michael Simpson, in a startlingly improved performance, now brings complete conviction. A friend on the way out told me, rightly, that I should mention every performance; there isn't space but I should squeeze in William Webster's drunk with his reverberating rhetorical questions ("This is a free country, ain't it?"), Mike Ross, a mournfully oracular fixture at the bar ("no foundation, all the way down"), and Lyndon Hanrahan as a newsboy who breaks into When Irish Eyes Are Smiling and whom Nick, atypically, won't hire.

Everyone gets a chance, one of the defining joys of this kind of play and this kind of production. Saroyan's greatest subsequent success seems to have been writing the lyrics for Come on-a My House, the novelty song that made Rosemary Clooney a star but that she practically had to be forced to record. In this play, though, he managed somehow to be both wide-eyed and clear-eyed. It gives feel-good drama a good name.
