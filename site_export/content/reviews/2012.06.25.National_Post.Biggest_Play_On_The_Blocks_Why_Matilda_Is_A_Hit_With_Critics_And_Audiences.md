The musical hit in London at the moment, in fact the biggest in years, is Matilda, based on the classic children's novel by Roald Dahl.

It started as a holiday show presented by the Royal Shakespeare Company at their home base in Stratford-upon-Avon. Now installed in the West End, its attracted unanimous critical raves, sell-out audiences and a record number of awards. In other words, its overrated.

The best thing about it, though, is the staging. The show is directed by Matthew Warchus and, as designed by Rob Howell, it's a fluid, colourful and inventive production that unfolds on a set whose main building blocks are building blocks: outsized versions of the alphabetical aids by which schoolchildren have been taught to read. This suits a story that's set in a school, even though the heroine is already preternaturally literate, a devourer of Dickens and Dostoevsky and a formidable storyteller in her own right.

These accomplishments earn her the hatred of her parents, the Wormwoods; Mr. W, a crooked used-car salesman, wanted a boy anyway (and insists on referring to Matilda as such) while his wife regards motherhood as a tiresome interruption of her commitment to ballroom dancing.

As punishment for her incorrigible intelligence, five-year-old Matilda is sent to a school whose motto is children are maggots and whose principal, a Miss Trunchbull, acts vigorously and sadistically on that principle. Shes a teacher for whom phys ed is the only ed that counts. She confines recalcitrant pupils to a closet lined with nails, and is a world champion at throwing the hammer, though if no hammers are available, children will do.

If Miss Trunchbull can move humans with her muscles, then Matilda, at least in this version of the story, can move less animate objects with her mind. These extraordinary powers, acquired disconcertingly late in the action and just as suddenly dispensed with, allow her to do battle with her oppressor-in-chief and to champion the meek but kindly teacher who seems to comprise the whole school staff.

In fact this teacher, Miss Honey, gradually emerges as the shows real heroine, which leads to a damaging split in focus. This can't be blamed on the performers. There are five rotating Matildas, and the one I saw was excellent; so were all the children in the cast, and so were Haley Flaherty as Miss Honey and Melanie La Barrie as a friendly librarian.

In fact, and unexpectedly, the sympathetic characters come off better than the villains. The senior Wormwoods do their blowsy best, but have been handed ham-fisted material, the wrong kind of caricature. Mr. Wormwood's lecture to the audience on the virtues of TV is an especially low point.

At least, though, these two are cartoons of actual parental abuse. Miss Trunchbull is another matter. Yes, this is a fantasy and yes, there have always been sadistic teachers in English schools, but any remotely like this one would have been closed down; it isnt the 19th century any more.

Matilda herself, as a reader of Dickens, would be able to point out the difference between Mr. Squeers, a monster drawn from life, and Miss Trunchbull, a monster for the sake of having a monster, and therefore no real threat. The show itself unwittingly illustrates this by bringing in, just before the end, some representatives of the Russian Mafia who are funny but just about believable; or rather, funny because theyre just about believable.

Miss Trunchbull, by contrast, is an excuse for a thin-lipped drag act; the actor who plays the role, Bertie Carvel, has been rewarded with praise, but I couldn't love hating him. Ross Petty could do better and, come the Canadian production (the show's slated for Broadway next year, but will get here eventually) he probably will.

As for the score, music and lyrics are by Tim Minchin: It starts well, with an ensemble song about how wonderful parents think their children are, or how wonderful children think their parents think they are; given how the story develops, this is touchingly ironic. Thereafter, the simpler numbers are the best; the ones that try for wit or wordplay arent as clever as they think they are. Still, as with Billy Elliot, those anxious to proclaim the next "great British musical" arent bothered.

The RSC were also represented in London by Written on the Heart, a contrastingly sober piece about the birth of the English Bible, which is a more combustible subject than you might expect: literally so, given the fiery fates of some of the pioneer translators of the 16th century.

David Edgar has written an honest, sober if sometimes bafflingly detailed piece, served up with equally honest, sober acting in the principal roles and some either stiff or colourless among the supports.

This places it ahead of The Duchess of Malfi at the Old Vic, in which most of the performers seemed to be imitating, at a distance, some imagined style thought appropriate to Jacobean tragedy and basically consisting of not seeming to believe what you say. That doesn't apply to Eve Best, one of the most highly regarded of younger English actresses, though probably best known here as Edie Falco's tall doctor sidekick in Nurse Jackie; her choices were consistently fresh and thoughtful, though she overdid the haughtiness and was unexpectedly hard to hear.

For the rest, and especially remembering the last Stratford production of the play, it was hard not to feel that we generally act the classics better, the difference probably being not one of talent but of tradition. Or, to put it more prosaically, practice.
