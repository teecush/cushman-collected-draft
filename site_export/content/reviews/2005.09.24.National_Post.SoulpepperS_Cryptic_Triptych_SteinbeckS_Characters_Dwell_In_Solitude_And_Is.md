E.Y. Harburg, the lyricist of the Depression anthem Brother Can You Spare a Dime, used to say that until he wrote that song there was no Depression. John Steinbeck might have made the same claim for such emblematic novels as The Grapes of Wrath and Of Mice and Men, and also, it seems, for the short stories with which he began his career in the early 1930s.

I say "it seems, since I have never read them. However, I have now seen a dramatization of three of them, titled The Long Valley, presented by Soulpepper as the last production in its seven-year tenancy at Harbourfront, and the characters in all three seem very depressed, though not necessarily depressing.

They aren't on the poverty line; few of them are even near it. But the cold winds of a devastated society are blowing around them and have worked their way inside. These people have gone beyond loneliness and into isolation, a self-willed solitude rendered more intense by the fact that most of them have legal partners. The Long Valley, a locale as much psychological as geographical, takes us back to a time in which folks had, as the saying goes, to make their own entertainment, and the ways in which the women in particular choose to amuse themselves can be disconcerting.

In the first tale, a farmer's wife devotes herself to growing chrysanthemums and makes a present of some, in a nice pot, to a passing tinker; hes strangely reminiscent of the peddler from Oklahoma! (the musical, not the state) though the lady is no wanton Ado Annie. But this fleeting contact is enough to arouse a new feeling in her that her husband, driving her to an unaccustomed night on the town, can register but not explain.

The second shows us a woman getting her kicks by buying a snake from a biologist and watching it eat a rat.

The last concerns a well-to-do married lady who has turned her garden into a bird-sanctuary; her most loved pet is a white quail, and she orders her husband to kill a cat whom she thinks is threatening it.

This cryptic triptych is played by, respectively, Liisa Repo- Martell, Ann-Marie Kerr and Colombe Demers, who also make up a group calling itself This Is a Bird. Under this umbrella they have adapted and, it seems, staged the stories. (No director is listed, though Chris Abraham is named as dramaturg.) This collective credit has led to allegations that the show doesnt really count as a Soulpepper show; in other words, that This Is a Bird is a cuckoo in the nest. I can't see why, though I will admit that the play seems a strange choice as an end-of-chapter production, since it bears little resemblance to any previous Soulpepper production except The Mill on the Floss, which it's much better than.

Its a frail, uneven but haunting piece that faces two old- established problems at once: the challenge of literary adaptation and the curse of the multiple bill. The acting is naturalistic, at least at speech-level, but there is also much mime; the work bears some resemblance to that of Theatre Smith-Gilmour (with whom several of the cast have done time) and though it doesnt have that company's shaping visual imagination, or its editorial skill, it does have a more affecting emotional core. It has been well designed by Julie Fox, who places sheets of what look like corrugated iron at the back of the stage, and lays another on the floor to simulate -- very convincingly -- a creaky old jalopy on a bumpy rural road. It's also been well lit and even better sounded, if thats the word, by Jan Komarek.

The leading ladies vary. Kerrs orgasmic snake-fancier is acting by numbers. Demers has a commanding swagger as a Lady Macbeth of the aviary (playing opposite an actor, David Jansen, who was in fact her Scottish thane a few seasons ago) but this episode too remains book- bound, a possible black comedy in a state of suspended animation. It's the first one, the least obviously eventful, that works best.

Steinbeck's dialogue in Of Mice and Men always struck me as the most urgently speakable I had read in a novel, and the language here has something of the same charge. Youre nice," says Jordan Pettles farmer, trying to locate the change that has come over his wife, then "You're strong"; and the change in adjective is very revealing.

Repo-Martell is a quietly quirky actress, and Pettle a casually downbeat actor, so they don't exactly set off fireworks, but they do create a landscape, their own personal dustbowl. The men also fit into a pattern, one of destruction born of frustration: The tinker smashes the pot, the scientist throttles the snake, the husband shoots the quail. In the last story, two of the women act as ghostly doubles for the third, and the three wind up in an aggrieved enigmatic group. This works, perhaps better than it has a right to; what might have been an evening of fragments falls into a whole.

- - -

One of Soulpeppers more underrated productions was of Arthur Schnitzlers La Ronde, that celebrated daisy-chain from old Vienna. The same author also wrote Anatol, another sequence of heterosexual encounters but cut to a different pattern, depicting just one man and his apparently inexhaustible succession of women. It's less often done, at least in its original form, though in the early 1960s it inspired at least two musicals.

One, a Broadway confection with a gorgeous score and an unworkable book, was titled, with unfortunate lack of foresight, The Gay Life. The other, which I actually saw, was a very arch entertainment called Parasol, which should tell you something, done on British television but starring -- I'll give you 100 guesses -- Canadas own William Hutt.

Both those adaptations of course were more about setting than substance, more sachertorte than Sacher-Masoch. Schnitzlers play, though very witty, is as sobering as most studies of the Don Juan myth; eternal cynic or, as here, eternal romantic, Lothario leads a lonely life. This may or may not be true to life, but it's certainly very consoling.

It's a tribute to Schnitzler (and to his translator, the all- around theatrical genius Harley Granville Barker) that in its new production by a company called Glastonbury West, who have re-named it The Affairs of Anatol, the play still seems good. The odds against it are heavy. There are a few interesting things in Sue Miners production; the stage-floor (designer, Jackie Chau) looks as if Gustav Klimt had got constructively down on his hands and knees, and Anatols ladies are allowed to watch one another's scenes, as a mocking or pitying chorus; they, like their sisters in The Long Valley, end up in a mutually protective group, probably with more reason.

Individually, though, they range from the ineffectual to the actively embarrassing, with the notable exception of Kathryn Winslow, who is both subtle and supple as a respectable old flame wondering what life might be like on the other side of the tracks.

As Anatol himself, John O'Callaghan, gives a performance of simpering inadequacy; the guy may be a loser but he isnt supposed to be a stooge. It may partly be the contrast but as his pal Max -- who has a thinner address-book but probably a more fulfilling life - - James S. Murray seems to get more engaging by the moment. A young actor named Daniel Abrahamson makes his professional debut as a waiter and a valet; whether this is the start of a great career I couldnt say, but he is very good at looking professionally pained.
