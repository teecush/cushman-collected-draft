RICE BOY

Berkeley Street Theatre, Toronto

Sunil Kuruvilla is a young Canadian writer of Indian descent. He studied at the Yale Drama School, and his plays have been done in the United States before being seen here -- circumstances that have made him a thing of wonder in Canadian theatrical circles. That may be unfair, not least to him. On the evidence of last season's Fighting Words and now of Rice Boy, he is one promising local playwright among several.

The strengths of Rice Boy are its tenderness and its occasional wit. Its weakness is its fragile, overloaded construction. It's about a boy in a tree. Tommy is a 12-year-old from Kitchener whose parents have periodically taken him on trips back to their relatives in India. On one of these, his mother drowned; his father has never recovered from the loss. That's in the play's past. Much of its present is devoted to a more recent visit. This throws Tommy into contact with his slightly older cousin Tina, who has crippled legs and is about to be given away in an arranged marriage. The marriage of Tina's own parents has been in a state of suspended animation ever since her father discovered his wife's infidelity with the local doctor. The household, whose women are considerably stronger than its men, is completed by a grandmother and a serving girl; the latter is the former wife of a fish-peddler who still comes to call, for professional and other reasons. This last thwarted relationship is probably one more than the play can take.

These Indian scenes are framed by, and interspersed with, scenes in Canada. In India, Tommy would climb to get a view of the neighbouring town, and would report on it to Tina, whose condition prevented her from leaving the house. Back home, Tommy still spends time aloft -- partly in memory of Tina, with whom he fell into a kind of love, and partly, it seems, scanning the horizon for some place in which he might belong. Both as a Canadian in India and as an Indian in Canada he feels displaced. (His father, a math professor in India and a pizza worker here, feels the same but articulates it less keenly.) Tommy goes so far, having read the description of a missing boy on a milk carton, to offer himself as a substitute to the bereaved father, a meter reader in St. Jacob's, Ont. It works for both of them, for about a day. It's a surprisingly touching scene, of a kind one has never seen before.

This is a play of hopes and regrets -- Tommy's hopes, most everyone else's regrets -- much of them over lost or inaccessible partners. Tina is caught in the middle, with a family able to build up her dreams and slap them down again within a single sentence. (Her mother, preparing her for marriage, says, "Eventually you will be unhappy but not in the beginning.")

The word "Chekhovian" comes to mind, and with it the reminder that the evanescent feelings in Chekhov's plays are encased in symphonic structures, interweaving themes and characters within solid, act-length scenes. Kuruvilla, like most current dramatists, writes in short breaths. His brief scenes do have a theatrical identity. They don't look, as so often happens, like displaced fragments from a film or TV script. But knitting them together is a challenge, especially with the double time-scheme added in, and Micheline Chevrier's production doesn't meet it. The overall pace is limp. One scene baldly stops, another as baldly starts, when one wants them to melt into one another.

Some end on a confrontational note that may be intended as defiance but comes over merely as petulance. This is especially the case with Tommy himself. In the hands of Zaib Shaikh, an actor too old in years and too young in technique, he remains a literary construct. So do most of the others: Granny, played by 90-year-old Zohra Segal, is more impressive as a presence than as a characterization. Sanjay Talwar as the cuckolded Uncle reiterates his mastery of the mild and the accident-prone. In one of the play's best scenes, he and his brother, Tommy's father, return drunk and gloomy from a meeting with Tina's future spouse and in-laws, trying intently to persuade themselves that all will be for the best: so intently that they fail to notice Tommy, following behind them, has fallen down a well. When they do discover it, they take it in their stride, even if he's failed to. For them, it seems to be an everyday occurrence.

The play abounds in telling patches of local colour, but they play as isolated effects. We learn, for example, that Indian television companies trawl the city streets and, having found the prettiest girl, make her newsreader for a day. Or, to be more exact, for a half-hour. It seems to be the summit of Tina's ambition. Imali Perera plays her attractively, capturing all the impatience, physical and emotional, of a girl apparently doomed to the opposite. She is the most compelling figure in the play, but she still gets lost in the shuffle. Her journey ends in a question mark, but the pressure is so low that it seems more like a comma.
