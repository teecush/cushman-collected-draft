MAJOR BARBARA, THE AUTUMN GARDEN

Shaw Festival

The last, and very memorable, image in the Shaw Festival's Major Barbara is of Ben Carlson as Adolphus Cusins, professor of Greek, gazing with mixed feelings at a pile of explosives. They are, thanks to an agreement he has just entered into, part of his inheritance. He is to be groomed for succession to the armaments firm of Undershaft and Lazarus. Current proprietor: Andrew Undershaft, millionaire. Its a double connection since Adolphus is about to marry Undershafts daughter Barbara, who, as an officer in the Salvation Army ministering to the poor of East London, gives the play its title. Cusins shares Barbara's charitable values but not her religious beliefs. He abhors Undershaft's beliefs, which boil down to selling guns to anyone who can pay for them, but develops a grudging respect for his values, of which the chief is that the most corrosive of all crimes is poverty. No wonder hes conflicted.

The play, first staged in 1905, still resonates, what with its discussion of where exactly charity begins and its prophetic view of the military-industrial complex. Undershaft tells his son Stephen, who wants to go into politics, that he will end up passing whatever laws best suit his fathers interests. The arguments are as brilliantly marshalled as in any Shaw play, though it remains true that once you've heard them, you've heard them. The paradoxes may dazzle the first time around, but on subsequent encounters you greet them as old friends -- congratulating yourself and the late author whenever they seem to strike particularly close to home. The characters are sharply etched, but they offer the actors little opportunity to take us by surprise. It can happen; it happens in that last moment of Carlsons, which occurs, significantly, after the last words have been spoken and the play is officially over. But mostly one judges the performances not on what hidden depths they may uncover, but on how well they phrase the lines.

The play is a three-sided contest, with a large corps of kibitzers. In Joseph Zieglers mostly admirable production, the major flaw is the major. Whatever disagreements they may have with her, Barbara's father, her fiance and practically everyone else find her inspirational. Diana Donnelly's Barbara, though, seems less a saint than a prig, lacking both in intellectual passion and in the confident wit with which she -- an earls granddaughter -- bosses the working classes into obedience. This spoils the plays second and most obviously actable act, set in the Army shelter. A series of ironic reversals, culminating in her colleagues' decision to accept tainted money in order to maintain operations, pulls Barbara's rug, or her prayer-mat, out from under her. She ends in (temporary) despair. Here it hardly registers.

That matters, though, less than it might, since Shaw -- whatever lip service he may pay to his heroine -- is actually more interested in the men in her life. After some preliminary bouts in the East End, they come to grips in Undershafts model factory -- a veritable garden city -- where the tycoon convinces the poet to work from the inside and "make war on war." (The play almost persuades you that its that simple.) This scene, which could be just endless talk, is here riveting. The iron fist of Benedict Campbell's Undershaft is so well concealed within his velvet glove that it sometimes threatens to disappear altogether, but his is a fine confident performance nonetheless. Carlson's Cusins is a model of mischievous lucidity, infused with racking doubt.

The supporting chorus is split between drawing-room and slum. Mary Haney as Undershafts estranged wife commands the former, with an entertaining portrayal of an imperious woman pretending to be meek. She is supported by Evan Buliung as an endearing chump with a talent for blurting out the emabarrassing platitudes in which everyone secretly believes, and by David Leyshon as the ineffable Stephen who, with his serene confidence about family values and the difference between right and wrong, seems destined for success, if not as a politician, then as a political columnist. The East Enders vary in quality, but Patrick Galligans Bill Walker is a first-rate bit of rough, Sharry Flett screeches and wheedles to great effect as Rummy Mitchens and Jenny L. Wright is unobtrusively accurate as a junior Salvationist. The library and the shelter are gifts to any designer with imagination and a budget, and Christina Poddubiuk accepts them gratefully; she surpasses herself though in the trickier last scene, an observation deck commanding vistas of vertiginous prosperity

The late Lillian Hellman is best known theatrically as an author of melodramas, good (The Children's Hour, Montserrat) or bad (The Little Foxes, Toys in the Attic). So The Autumn Garden, a mild success on Broadway in 1951, comes as a surprise, and a pleasant one. Crammed as it is with characters in potentially explosive relationships, it might be described as melodrama that fails to happen, and is all the more truthful for it. There are two marriages on stage, both unhappy but both destined to endure. There is a long- standing friendship that is never going to develop into anything else. There is an engagement and that isn't going to go anywhere either. A wise critic once said the tragedy in Ibsens plays is that things turn out not to be what they seem, while the tragedy in Chekhovs is that they turn out to be exactly what they seem. By that criterion, The Autumn Garden is Chekhovian.

It is so in more superficial ways as well, since it presents a stageful of characters interacting in an enclosed setting that, like them, has seen better days. We are in the living room of a house near New Orleans that survives by taking in summer guests, all by now old acquaintances. This could be the more decorous side of the Southern discomfort currently running riot in Stratford's Orpheus Descending. The widowed owner awaits the return from Europe of an old flame, a raffish painter whose own wife has grown accustomed to his seasonal infidelities. Another widow watches in distress the involvement, unacknowledgedly homosexual, of her son with a predatory (unseen) writer, and is watched in turn by her own martinet of a mother. The son, meanwhile, is affianced to the niece of the house, who has been charitably imported from France and would like nothing more than to go back there. A retired general announces his intention of divorcing his wife, who acts as if she hasn't heard. Few of these people are nice, none are happy and most are tormented by what might have been, but all are interesting, if only because of their waspish (or could that be Waspish?) tongues.

Martha Henrys direction errs, I think, in ignoring the plays original three-act structure, so that its most obviously dramatic scene doesn't so much land as thud. Otherwise she adroitly maps the play's shifting emphases and unexpected confrontations, and lets us relish the sharpness of its dialogue. She has an A1 cast. Only David Schurmann as the general seems miscast; he goes through the motions of agony but his civilized facade refuses to crack. Wendy Thatcher, though, does an unflinching job on his spouse, while Patricia Hamilton is a splendidly pointed matriarch. Sharry Flett, Goldie Semple and Laurie Paton offer a trio of touchingly fastidious studies in distressed gentlewomanliness; Flett plays the central role of the chatelaine, which, on the heels of her Cockney pauper in Major Barbara, qualifies her for this years Niagara medal for versatility.

Mike Shara is a blushing juvenile and Jim Mezon a wine-and- weather-beaten commentator, but the most spectacular performance comes from Peter Hutt as the returned prodigal, a wonderful study in self-hatred perpetually deferred. He knows hes no good, as husband, lover or artist (his pictures are perpetually unfinished, and God knows how he maintains his cosmopolitan life-style) and he compensates by jovially patronizing everyone he meets. He's also the drunkest person on stage, which is saying something. He finally intrudes on the sleeping-quarters of the emigre niece, thus precipitating the nearest thing to a show-down. The girl, whom everybody takes for stupid though she's probably the wisest person around, is played by a striking newcomer, Charlotte Gowdy; she's tall, less gauche and more beautiful than the author seems to have envisaged, but that does no harm at all.
