THE ADVENTURES OF A BLACK GIRL IN SEARCH OF GOD

Du Maurier Theatre, Toronto

- - -

THE LOST BOYS

Berkeley Street Theatre, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - Djanet Sears uses space superbly. The Adventures of a Black Girl in Search of God, of which she is both author and director, unfolds on a deep, bare stage covered in what looks like, but probably isn't, baked sandstone. This design, by Astrid Janson, is wonderfully useful and suggestive, with Paul Mathiesen's lighting magnificently varying the contours. Scene changes, of which there are many, are immaculately handled, as are the movements of a cast of 21, including a singing-dancing chorus. Everyone and everything is in the right place at the right time. The show marks the debut of Obsidian Theatre, a bold new black company; and the overall sense of disciplined, but still spontaneous, conviction is very satisfying.

The play itself, which takes its name but nothing else from Bernard Shaw, is less impressive than the production, though it certainly aims high and wide. Its title character is Rainey, whose only child died at the age of eight and who is, understandably, still reeling. Once a religious seminarian, she turned to a career in obstetrics, then lost that vocation, too. She is divorcing her husband, a minister, though it is obvious they still love one another. She also has to cope with her father, a former judge; he is mortally ill but feisty, and now heads a squad of elderly urban guerrillas whose mission is to snatch demeaning black figurines ("lawn jockeys") from white property and repatriate them.

Down at the front of the stage is a trench, into which the cast periodically and joyously descends. It functions as a mass baptismal font and also stands in for Negro Creek, the Ontario area in which the play is set and whose black population -- recently, historically and successfully -- lobbied against a proposed change of name. The judge sees his own campaign as a continuation of that struggle. He also wishes not only to die at home but to have his funeral there -- an ambition with which Rainey, who is in denial where death is concerned, has considerable trouble.

There is, in fact, a lot going on here, some of it seriously serious. Rainey asks the age-old question about how, if God exists, He can permit evil and suffering. She is no more successful than anyone before her at getting an answer.

Exactly halfway through the play, she tells us the problem is not that she doesn't believe in God, but that she does -- a resonant line, but there is no dramatic progression either toward or away from it. Her husband, on the other hand, does not believe, but he continues in his job as a way of fighting for his community. This irony, once stated, remains unexplored; as does an additional late- blooming plot line concerning racist slogans daubed on his church, not to mention the revelation of an unspoken romance between the judge and one of his team.

Nor do we ever really discover how Rainey finally feels about her father's mannequin-rescue project. She starts by thinking it silly, but finds herself helping out with their most audacious project, a raid on the local museum. This occasions a rather delightful set- piece scene, but it exists purely as family comedy; it doesn't actually get us anywhere. Indeed, all the scenes involving the judge's gang are played for cuteness; they strongly resemble the old British film comedies in which senior citizens conduct lovable heists on fur dealers and jewellery stores.

The only hope of focusing all this is through Rainey's consciousness, but the writing doesn't do that; and neither does Alison Sealy-Smith's businesslike but opaque performance. Both author and actress fail to find a convincing mode for the character's frequent soliloquies. (Is she talking to us? Are we overhearing her? Or what?) All we are left with is a warm, fuzzy feeling when she (I think) finds God by accepting the people around her, including the fact that they die.

Walter Borden and David Collins are excellent as father and husband respectively, and Jackie Richardson makes a boisterous lady activist while also lending her gorgeous voice to musical interludes that range from gospel to scat.

The chorus (directed by Alejandra Nunez, choreographed by Vivine Scarlett) are similarly stirring, though their contributions seem increasingly a diversion from the drama rather than an illumination of it. Sears resembles Peter Shaffer, of all playwrights, in that she imagines scenes better than she writes them. She goes Shaffer several better, though, in staging them herself; and in that respect she realizes her vision to perfection.

There is another downstage trench in The Lost Boys, also designed by Astrid Janson; this one signifies the dugouts of the First World War. Four of R.H. Thomson's great-uncles perished in that fighting - - a fifth survived -- and in this one-man play Thomson tells us their stories, largely through reading their letters home.

There are some obvious objections here: We can hardly be expected to be as interested in his relatives as he is, and 2 1/2 hours (The Lost Boys clocks in slightly shorter than Black Girl) is a long time in which to listen to a single actor. But, for about four-fifths of the time, he surmounts them.

He doesn't help himself by dragging in obscure parallels with relativity theory or with his search for the death site of his own father, killed in a car crash. But he gets considerable assistance from the elaborate symphony of sound, film and lighting effects that has been orchestrated by his director, Jonas Jurasas; only toward the end does this become excessive.

What really counts, though, is Thomson's endless charm and resource in creating his characters, keeping them distinct and establishing his own commenting voice without overimposing it. As an actor with other actors, Thomson can sometimes seem guarded and academic; here he never strikes a false or unfinished note.
