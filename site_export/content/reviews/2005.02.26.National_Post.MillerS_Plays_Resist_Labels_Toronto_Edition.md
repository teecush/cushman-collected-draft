Some years ago I decided that, what the hell, I should spell out my standards as a critic. This is what I wrote then, and what I hold to now: I want the theatre to make me laugh, cry, tap my feet or hold my breath. I think that covers everything."

Arthur Miller, who at the moment of his death two weeks ago was the most honoured playwright in the world, never wrote anything that challenged admission in the foot-tapping category. He wasnt best known, either, for raising laughs, though in The Price he created one character, an old Jewish dealer, who convulsed the audience practically every time he opened his mouth.

Miller could certainly make us hold our breath, both for the scope and depth of what he attempted and the skill with which he brought it off. In The Crucible he brought a historical period to life, used it to illuminate another and -- a very rare feat in the modern theatre -- told a remorselessly gripping story. He showed the same narrative flair in All My Sons. In After the Fall he laid bare a marriage with an honesty that transcended autobiography (and incidentally redeemed an otherwise hideously long-winded play.) In the one-act A Memory of Two Mondays -- a play thats been forgotten since a short piece with a big cast has trouble finding a home -- he painted one of the great dramatic genre-pictures. The late plays (post-1970) are of interest only because they're by the man who wrote the earlier ones.

It all comes down, though, to Death of a Salesman, the play that makes everybody cry. Technically, it's an amazing achievement. I dont believe any play up to then had made such extensive use of flashbacks to denote overlapping states of memory and consciousness, and Im not sure any has done it as successfully since: all this while maintaining an inexorable forward momentum. It's also, and this is what really brings on the tears, the best modern play about the dynamics of being a family, about the way in which its members tear at one another, and try to reassure one another, and then tear again, because they just don't agree or they just dont understand.

Think of the choreography at the end of Act One. Willy Loman, the disintegrating salesman, and Biff, the son on whom hed pinned his hopes, are at their usual loggerheads. Happy, the other son, comes up with a business plan that sounds tempting. Biff is intrigued and Willy is over the moon. So is Linda, Willy's wife, but she expresses her enthusiasm a bit too forcefully for Willy, who feels crowded. So he yells at her, for agreeing with him. Biff, incensed for his mother, yells back. Willy, ashamed, backs off, and it all blows over. Till the next time.

As an undergraduate, I directed Salesman with a cast of other undergraduates. We were all young, all English, and yet we latched on instinctively to this American family of whom even the youngest was 10 years our senior. I was amazed by how little I needed to say about these scenes. They simply clicked into place, and they moved like express trains.

I was less enamoured of Miller's preface, with its insistence on the plays status as a tragedy. It seemed enough to me that it was a very good play, very moving (I used to sit in the audience and feel those sobs welling up around me) and obviously universal. Bringing in academic categories was, I felt, opening an unnecessary can of worms, and research revealed Miller had done just that.

Right from the start, there were those who couldnt forgive him for being a Broadway playwright with aspirations.

The suspicion persists, or so I judge from Barbara Kay's article on Miller in the National Post. She finds Salesman "more pathetic than tragic, largely because she thinks Miller blames Willy's fate on a capitalist society of which she herself approves. Well, it wouldnt bother me if Salesman were an anti-capitalist play, the salesman victimized by the hollowness of his own value system. But it isnt. Miller himself pointed out that the most decent character in it is Willy's neighbour Charley, a businessman whose values are not essentially different from Willy's. He just happens to be better and more cautious at putting them into practice.

Oddly enough, much the same criticism has been levelled at the play from the Left as from the Right. Kenneth Tynan, who revered the play, thought it couldnt be a tragedy because Willys fault lay not in his stars but in the fact his employers didn't have a proper pension plan. It was left to English playwright John Whiting to point out that Willy dies not because of the flaws or even the merits of the system he works under but because he is a weak and deluded mortal man. His job does indeed encourage his fatal belief that you can get by on a smile and a shoeshine. But if he were denied that, he would have found another way of screwing up his life and his family. He's like Chekhovs Uncle Vanya (in one of the few plays thats comparably affecting), who liked to believe that, dealt a better hand, he might have been a Schopenhauer or a Dostoevsky. The play knows better.

Chekhov can make me laugh and cry, sometimes simultaneously. His plays, not tragedies, are better than most tragedies. They're better than Miller's. But Salesman is a tragedy, even if it defies the so- called laws of tragedy," which in any case arent immutable. Theyre just theories, devised by humans to describe plays written by other humans. They don't even fit all the plays they're supposed to. Aristotle, from whose Poetics they all derive, confined himself to analyzing just one play, Sophocless Oedipus Rex, and even that defies the formula in a rather important respect: Its hero doesnt die. (Sophocles later killed him off in another play.)

Kay tells us that "in a Shakespearean tragedy, the protagonist, prompted by a 'tragic flaw,' offends against the moral order of things. That sounds all right, but what exactly is Hamlets tragic flaw"? That he fails to kill his uncle? He seems to be more the wrong man in the wrong place. (A victim, really. Like Willy Loman.) And if "suicide is cheating, dramatically speaking (take that, Willy, you wimp), then among the cheating plays are Othello, and Phedre, held to be the very model of a French classical tragedy, and Antony and Cleopatra, a double suicide. It really doesnt leave very much.

It does leave King Lear, and you can indeed trace a straight line from Lear's terrible mistakes in the first scene to his death in the last. But I doubt if anyone watches that ending thinking it was really all Lear's own fault and at least the moral orders now been restored. I dont want to be flippant, but I suspect the restoration of moral order" in Shakespeare is mainly about the necessity on an open stage of leaving someone in authority to order the removal of the bodies. Tragedies don't tell us that things are all right "as long as hierarchies are observed, though I can see why conservatives would take comfort in thinking so. They tell us that things go wrong. Always. That's why we need them.

I think now that Salesman, whose heros fate is shaped by his society but not determined by it, belongs among them. I certainly dont hold it against Miller that he failed to celebrate America's virtues and strengths." That wasn't his job. Its never been any dramatists.

Shakespeare, of course, covered himself by never writing about his own time and place, but reading between the lines of Lear or Troilus and Cressida, I don't sense a man in love with his own world. Euripides was run out of Athens, and Ibsen never stopped telling his fellow Norwegians how much he despised them. Even Chekhov said that the purpose of his plays was to make his fellow countrymen look at themselves and see how empty their lives were. Perhaps, as Kay says of Miller, in doing so "he weakened his claim to timelessness. It's really too early to tell. So far, though, they both seem to be doing pretty well. The academics say that comedies aren't just plays that make you laugh" and that tragedies aren't just "plays that make you cry." But nobody has come up with any better definitions.
