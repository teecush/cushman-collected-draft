A middle-aged man in Quebec City wins tickets to a production of Chekhov's Three Sisters, and seeing it changes his life. That's the situation in And Slowly Beauty ... by Michel Nadeau, brought to the Tarragon from the Belfry Theatre in Victoria. It's an appealing play in an appealing production, sometimes funny, sometimes touching, but finally lacking in teeth.

According to the program, Nadeau's protagonist is called Mr. Mann, though I don't think that this dire circumstance is ever mentioned in the play itself. Mr Mann seems to be a middle manager in some vaguely benevolent organization that's currently undergoing restructuring. An early scene in which he addresses his troops, who reply in high-pitched gobbledegook that makes them sound like speeded-up tapes, suggests that we're in for an evening of corporate or bureaucratic satire, but the suggestion is never subsequently taken up.

We do gather that Mann's office life is harried and unfulfilling and so is his home life. Mrs. Mann (or would that be Ms. Womann?) is a real estate agent whose career is sputtering; their teenage daughter is a multilingual highflyer; and their teenage son is, or seems to be, a contrasting underachiever.

The family communicates largely through Post-It notes, the reading of which occasions the play's most chucklesome scene. None of them has time to go to the theatre with him, so he goes alone, and finds that Chekhov's characters are speaking to him: so much so that he returns.

A couple of years ago, the Shaw Festival revived Drama at Inish in which an entire Irish village was exposed to an Ibsen play with similarly discombobulating results. The events there were more farcically frantic than those here, as perhaps befits the difference between the two classic playwrights. Or perhaps not. One scene here has Mr. M purchasing a copy of Three Sisters, with Uncle Vanya thrown in, from a second-hand bookseller who declares the plays "bourgeois" and urges him to buy Chekhov's short stories instead. Why, he asks, do the three sisters not just go to Moscow? Why don't the owners of the cherry orchard just sell it? He's repeating the familiar complaint that in Chekhov, nothing happens. In fact, a lot does: duels, suicides attempted or successful, adulteries, enforced auctions. Nadeau obviously isn't going into competition, but Mann's troubles seem trivial by comparison, which may be part of what irks him but isn't very satisfying dramatically.

Michael Shamata's production is smooth and pretty, and Dennis Fitzgerald excellently presents both the protagonist's busy exterior and his inner turmoil; if he seems increasingly to be hitting the same note, that's the score that he's been handed. He's certainly very likeable. Anyone concerned with the theatre, professionally or otherwise, is going to warm to the idea of a play having a transformative effect on somebody, even if it's always somebody else. For what it's worth, the nearest I've come to it myself was, as a teenager, seeing a truly great production of, yes indeed, Uncle Vanya, and feeling, between my laughter and tears, terrified at the thought that life might actually turn out to be like that. (It did.)

Catching up with the much-praised new musical Do You Want What I Have Got?: A Craigslist Cantata, I find myself, not for the first time in such circumstances, wondering what all the fuss was about. It does have an unusually good score; Veda Hille writes expertly and amusingly in a variety of modes, even managing to make a heavy metal number seem theatrical. Bill Richardson's lyrics also have their moments, though for a show that seems to be aiming at a sharp revue style, they can be sloppy about rhymes. The numbers have the Ride the Cyclone disease of going on after they've made their points, with the difference that here the points aren't all that strong to begin with. The show is a song-cycle based on notices placed on Craigslist - real ones or parodies, I don't know; a few of them are recited, between the songs, and they sound funnier, not to say shorter, that way.

Finally, the off-Mirvish season at the Panasonic is presenting Studio 180's Clybourne Park, the least sentimental of recent Broadway successes, restaged with its excellent Toronto cast from last year and highly recommended (by me).
