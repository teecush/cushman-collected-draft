MRS. WARREN'S PROFESSION

THEATRE REVIEW

MRS. WARREN'S PROFESSION

Tuesday, March 25, 1997

Directed by Sarah Stanley

Written by Bernard Shaw


  


Starring Colleen Williams


  


and Deborah Lambie


  


Rating: *

Mrs. Warren needs some work

For a woman who made it rich as a prostitute and madam, Kitty

is rather downtrodden and her daughter Vivie is rather smug.

Special to The Globe and Mail


  



  


B


  


ERNARD Shaw called his first batch of plays "Plays Unpleasant," but they are among the most palatable things he ever wrote. He had yet to construct his own myth or believe fully in his own publicity; his trick of stating hard truths or outrageous paradoxes with a sweet smile had not hardened into mannerism; and his concentration on specific social abuses kept his wit barbed and focused.

Mrs. Warren's Profession , the third and last in the group (it was written in 1894), is about prostitution and society's complicity in it. As a girl, Kitty Warren turned from a life of near-starvation as a domestic or barmaid to a successful career as a prostitute, then to a madam. With a chain of brothels all over Europe, she has put her daughter, Vivie, through college on the proceeds. (Vivie, like her mother, is brilliant at figures.)

Mother and daughter have seen very little of each another, but now, over a couple of days at an English country cottage, Vivie learns the truth about her mother's past (which she doesn't criticize) and about her highly profitable present. Vivie rejects her mother's support, peremptorily ends a romance with the son of the local clergyman and takes off to London to become a partner in a friend's actuarial business.

The first half of Sarah Stanley's production looks Edwardian, as much as it looks anything. In the second half, things begin to slip a bit. Some costumes look modern; a character reads Time Out, the London listings weekly; and Vivie's office desk sports a laptop computer. The nudging is gratuitous -- you would have to cast this play with inaudible actors to stop it from ringing loud contemporary bells -- and it's far too fragmentary and unimaginative to constitute a style.

This play about the sex trade hardly mentions sex; the furthest even Mrs. Warren herself goes is to refer to her "turn for pleasing men." (The reticence may have been Shaw's as much as his characters'; he could flout the censor's taboos while leaving his own intact.) To compensate, the director has young Frank, Vivie's intended, push his hand up her skirt after receiving an exaggerated, unmaternal kiss from Mrs. Warren.

Despite that, the main erotic energies are homosexual. Mrs. Warren has an artist friend called Praed who's meant to represent the sentimental assumptions of the average audience; here he's a dear old queen (or, in Greg Kramer's performance, a mincing caricature of one) who appears to have an understanding with Vivie's young man. Vivie herself practically turns a double somersault when freed from her entanglement, and is plainly about to settle for domesticity with her business partner, Honoria, who is this production's off-stage heroine. (The producers call themselves The Honoria Project.)

Well, it's a point of view. It might even, if consistently and skillfully carried out, enrich the play. What makes this production so monumentally crass is not what is put in but what is left out. It lurches from one painfully bright idea to another. The director thinks she has a melodrama on her hands and self-consciously spoofs a moment of coincidence that is not, in fact, especially far-fetched. Actually, the play is what used to be called a "strong" society drama, in which one painful revelation inexorably gives way to another; as such, it is gripping, morally and dramatically.

But this director seems unable to orchestrate action or dialogue. The two women have two great scenes: one in which Mrs. Warren tells all (well, some), the other in which Vivie casts her off. These, by any reckoning, are traumatic encounters, but the actresses never make contact with their own emotions or with one another. They never seem purposefully to avoid it, either; they just wander gracelessly around the stage awaiting the next cue.

For a woman who got rich on her charm and guts, and who her daughter calls "the strongest woman in England," Colleen Williams is amazingly dowdy and downtrodden, likelier to be cleaning the house than running it. It also doesn't help that she's much shorter than her daughter. Deborah Lambie as Vivie is livelier, but much of her spirit comes out as spite, and her self-confidence as sheer smugness. Despite what they keep telling us, they don't seem to be going through anything.

John Blackwood plays Sir George Crofts, a tweedy club man who has profitably invested in what he likes to call the Warren hotel business. He's too lightweight, probably too young, not nearly coarse-grained enough, and, in general, comes over as a pantomime villain rather than a repulsive fact of economic life. Given all that, he isn't bad.

The performer who comes nearest to making the show work is Jim Jones as Frank. Initially, he seems to have decided that a feckless young gentleman who spouts period slang must, by definition, be a ninny out of P. G. Wodehouse, but he develops some brains and a dandyish sense of irony. His wit is fussier than Shaw's and takes up a lot more time, but it does have presence.

Whereas presence, for the first three-quarters of the evening, is totally lacking in Wendy White's sets, which are barren. Turns out they were being saved for the last office scene, which has a cunning sense of elevation. For the most part, however, this is amateur night.

Mrs. Warren's Profession runs at the Tarragon Extra Space until April 5.
