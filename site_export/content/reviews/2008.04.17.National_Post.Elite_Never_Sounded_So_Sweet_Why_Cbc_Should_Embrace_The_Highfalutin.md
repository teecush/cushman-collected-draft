Jon Stewart said something on Monday night that I had given up hope of hearing on television. He said "I always thought elite meant good." He had been running the usual barrage of mind-numbing clips denouncing Barack Obama's "elitism." He then added, unanswerably, "If you don't think you're better than us, what the [bleep] do you think you're doing running for president?"

And when I hear a Canadian comedian come up with a line as smart and as heartfelt as that, I shall retract my criticisms of CBC's attempts at topical satire.

CBC itself has come under fire recently both for being elitist and for not being elitist enough. Last Thursday's Globe and Mail featured directly opposed columns on this matter by John Doyle, TV critic, and Russell Smith, pundit. Smith's article was the summation -- to date, anyway -- of his attacks on CBC radio for cutting down on its quota of classical music. Doyle has been responding by saying a) that any threat on that front is dwarfed by the censorship proposals implicit in Bill C-10, and b) that there's quite enough classical music available anyway. He's right about a), especially in light of the bill's ugly little phrase about art that's "contrary to public policy"; the response to b) must be that it depends where you live.

Anyway, Doyle went on to counter what he identified as "the widespread belief ... that CBC has come under fire both for being elitist and not elitist enough television isn't culture, it's lowbrow rubbish" by saying that, if you look beneath the surface, a popular show like The Office "illuminates the human condition" (indeed, and you don't have to look very far beneath) and that even ER instructs us that doctors have "very human foibles when it comes to human relationships." Well, yes, it does; so has every medical drama since Dr. Kildare and probably beyond, and it's hard to believe that most people didn't know it anyway. Complex drama means more than acknowledging that people can have jobs and sex lives. Doyle has a point, but when it comes to men in scrubs on soaps, he's pushing it. Smith's article pounces on the question of complexity. Complex art, he writes, has been regarded, traditionally and rightly, as more serious, longer-lasting, than simple art. Therefore, classical music has more right to subsidized airtime than the popular music with which the CBC proposes to replace some of it.

A problem here is that the phrase "classical music" muddies the waters. When we talk about a classic novel, or a classic play, or a classic painting, we're not talking about a particular kind of piece; we merely mean one that has been around long enough to have proved its survival value. More bluntly, we mean something old. "Classical music" can be new. It's a genre, hard to define but possible to recognize, if only by the negative qualities of not being pop or jazz and of being performed in concert halls and opera houses.

I have long nurtured the suspicion that when we say we want art that's "demanding" or "challenging" or even "offensive," we really mean we want it to be those things for other people. We ourselves have already met the demands, faced the challenges, swallowed the offence. I also think that what music you're comfortable with is very much determined by where, chronologically, your ears came in. I do agree that classical music, old or new, should have the greatest presence on CBC Radio 2, but I don't see why pop or jazz should be excluded as long as they're of the kind you can't readily hear elsewhere. As long, too, as they're presented in an illuminating way; they should be given the kind of context for which most commercial stations wouldn't have the time. And the management shouldn't be spooked by the idea that they might be catering to a minority audience. Minorities can be quite large. Even sport is a minority interest, hard though some people may find it to believe. In fact, with the possible exception of the weather reports, there's no type of program that interests everybody.

In this week's National Post, Marni Soupcoff made the case for abolishing all government funding for the arts. But her argument is historically flawed. There's always been subsidy. The great Florentine painters were subsidized by the Medicis, who were the government. The Athenian theatre festivals, on which the whole tradition of Western drama depends, were vast civic events. I want the CBC to get more money, not less. I want CBC radio to remain commercial-free and -- wild flight of fancy-- CBC television to return to being so. (The BBC has managed it, and for all its compromises it remains a beacon. British culture would be a wilderness without it.) I'd like to see a mindset in TV drama that glories in real achievements, rather than one that turns down Slings and Arrows, frets over the ratings for The Englishman's Boy and professes disenchantment with the narrative serial -- the one thing television can do better than any other performing medium. I would be happy to see the banishment of "reality TV." Just as -- to return to Stewart on Obama, and to Obama himself--I welcome broadcasters and even politicians who can express reasonably complicated thoughts, even if this lets their opponents thrash them in sound bites. Stewart actually offers the perfect wordplay for the CBC. It doesn't need to be "elitist." It does need to be elite.
