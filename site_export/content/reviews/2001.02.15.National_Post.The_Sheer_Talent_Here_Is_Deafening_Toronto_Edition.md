EARSHOT

Tarragon Extra Space, Toronto

There's an old critical theory, best expounded in Edmund Wilsons essay The Wound and the Bow, that holds that art is basically a hard- luck story. The creator has some kind of sickness, mental or physical, and exploits it. Its a romantic idea (or, more strictly, a Romantic idea), and these days we shy away from it. But it's a notion both exemplified and brilliantly vindicated in Earshot, a one- character play written and directed by Morris Panych. Randy Hughson performs the play, Ken MacDonald has designed it, and both their contributions are beyond praise, but everything stems from the author: The piece is a superbly mounted Panych attack.

Panych has apparently suffered all his life from over-acute hearing, an affliction he has bequeathed -- in, so to speak, amplified form -- to his protagonist, whose name is Doyle. (So says the program; I don't recall it being actually spoken.) A pain to Panych, the condition is a torment to Doyle. He lives in a spectacularly squalid one-room apartment, agonizingly aware of every audible detail in his neighbours lives -- and it seems that most details are audible if you concentrate. His complaint, he explains to some imaginary interlocutor, isnt deafness -- it's DEAFENING. The play's metaphor is similarly deafening in its obviousness, and quite magnificent in its effectiveness.

We like to think that the psychological barriers that separate each of us from everybody else are a curse; Doyle shows us that they are a blessing. He is forced to be super-sensitive to those around him and, even though he loathes most of them, he gets caught up in their lives to the extent that he hardly has one of his own. He rarely goes out. He was always, one suspects, a prey to inertia and he is now in its grip almost completely. He has no job, no friends, no prospects and only one ambition: to shoot himself. The plays title is double-edged, or rather double-barrelled.

Suicide is what he does, or wants to do, for love. Above him lives a paraplegic writer. Doyle recognizes the resentful rotation of the wheelchair and rejoices in the constant crinkle of rejection slips. Below lives a laid-off worker, whom Doyle judges, from the amount of time he spent on strike, to have been a longshoreman, and whose razor could dearly use a new blade. Offstage left is a senile woman calling for her dead husband and incessantly playing a 78 of, wouldnt you know it, Whispering. He hates the sound of all of them. But, offstage right lives Valerie, who works at a cosmetics counter and has a various but unfortunate love life and with whom Doyle is thoroughly and graphically obsessed. Tenderly, too: He rhapsodizes that there is nothing between them "except this wall" and that her skin is too delicate to be touched even by a sponge -- only the word sponge, the thing would not be soft enough.

She doesn't know he exists, so he is writing -- and mailing -- a suicide note to inform her. (He will, of course, be able to hear when she receives it.) We find him, at the start, composing this letter and excising the words "paradigm shift" with a shudder; shift reminds him of the only job he ever had (it lasted two nights) while "paradigm" sounds like 20 cents.

He is, in fact, quite a wit, and a cultured one -- incongruously so, in these surroundings. The wit falters somewhat in the middle sections of the play, but everything comes triumphantly together at the end, which presents a wonderful series of tragi-farcical reversals, culminating in Doyle's grudging performance of maybe the first charitable act of his life.

His living quarters are rendered by MacDonald (Panychs regular collaborator) in stunningly exaggerated perspective: life-size at the front of the stage and a virtual dolls house at the back. The furnishings are few and archaic; the phone is rotary, the portable record player might have adorned a teenage party of the 1950s. The bed -- wedged against the wall on the Valerie side of the room -- reveals naked mattresses whose foam seems about to break through some decidedly makeshift bonds; since the play has something of a Samuel Beckett form and flavour, it's tempting to christen these fragile fastenings Krapp's Last Duct Tape.

Doyle also has an endearing habit of fishing his used tea bags from the mug and throwing them at the shaving mirror, to which they stick; by the end there is quite an accumulation of them.

Unlike Beckett, Panych plays directly for our sympathy, but I find that honest. Certainly Hughson, a specialist in resilient derelicts, makes Doyle likeable, even in his eclectically crabby politics. ("I dont support welfare. I just collect it.") A giant on that foreshortened set, he plays the physical comedy to the limit (it seems possible that he may have to go through the whole play in his underpants, but propriety prevails) while vocally exploring the full range of the characters bemusement, anger and pain -- and his enjoyment of all of them. There is not a moment of dead air in his performance, and he has remarkable transformative powers; his face, pink and pouchy, sheds years when he harks back to his childhood and the memory of an operation that cured him of tinnitus and left him with something worse.

It's only February, and this, after Zadie's Shoes, is already Hughsons second virtuoso performance of the year; add in Slavs from last fall, and its his third of the season. At this rate, we won't need any other actors.

Panych's visual and verbal choreography of his own text is masterly. The visual, after The Overcoat and Hysteria, I would have expected; the verbal is an exhilarating surprise. The sound was designed by Derek Bruce, and it speaks, at appropriate volume, for itself.
