Albert Jackson was Torontos first black postman, beginning his rounds in 1882. His beat covered a few streets in what was to become known, just a few years later, as the Annex. The Postman is a Panamania site-specific production that tells Albert's story by having us follow, literally, in his footsteps. Scenes from his life and the lives of his family, liberally interspersed with songs, are acted out on a succession of porches. At least, that's how its been happening at most performances. The run has now been extended, and some of the shows are being given in a sit-down situation, in Margaret Fairley Park (really a parkette) on Brunswick Avenue.

I saw a park performance, which I suspect represented the play at less than its best. It may seem counterintuitive but a strolling production, with audience and actors adjusting to each new location, might be more concentrated than a stationary one, in which the story gets spread out across a single space. Or rather the stories, as The Postman has several narratives going on, in alternating chunks. Theres the story of Albert's fight to perform his job in the teeth of racist colleagues. And there are the stories of his father, a free black American who died of despair in his native Delaware, and of his enslaved mother who brought Albert and his siblings to Canada via the Underground Railroad. And there are various political shenanigans. Some moments are stirring, some moving, but the overall feel is amorphous.

David Ferry conceived and directed the show, and he also collaborated on the script with a phalanx of other writers, some of them members of the cast. Some of the speeches are taken direct from documents of the time (or if they aren't, someone on the team is a whiz at period pastiche). The music, performed by a half-dozen member marching band, is similarly eclectic; some of the songs are traditional, heavy on the spirituals; some of them are new; and most of them are good; a proud song from and about the Jackson women is great. There are, however, too many of them; they become both a crutch and a distraction.

When Albert, in full uniform, first reports for work, his fellow mailmen, after greeting "the inferior Negro" with blows and insults, hand him a broom and tell him to get on with it. Its a scene to make you angry even now.

Laurence Dean Ifill as Albert responds with dignity, while still conveying that underneath he seethes, implicitly tracing a straight line from postmen to presidents.

Dapper dignity, though, is about all hes given to play. He is allowed to add a note of wariness to the mix in a succession of scenes with a sympathetic white woman (Claire Armstrong) who lives on his route; her solicitude presumably awakens fears of what the repercussions could be if something similar were to happen down south. The fact that he's now allowed to do the job he was hired for is due, we learn later, to his cause having been taken up by John A. Macdonald (Layne Coleman, craftily ambiguous) who supports Jackson in his occupation as a means of getting out the black vote for his second term. But even here Albert himself is not given much of a role; the deal is clinched, in what is now the Royal York, by his two businessmen brothers.

The other side of the political coin is spun by a bigoted mayoral candidate (Sugith Varughese) with a rich line in vocabulary problems; he delivers a rabblerousing speech that starts out as a mere malapropism and ends up positively Joycean. Flashing further back, we see Mama Jackson (Karen Glave) in flight with her children, agonizing over whom to trust and which way to turn. Her terror strikes home, though even in a show as loose as this one, having her problem solved by the ghost of her husband (Maurice Dean Wint) is an unconvincing breach of convention. Even a kaleidoscope must have its limits. Still, as I've said, I didn't see it under optimal circumstances. Walk the walk, and it could well be worthwhile. And if you reflect that postmen, of any complexion, are now an endangered species, it may gain an extra level of poignancy.

The Postman runs until July 26 at selected porches on Brunswick Avenue, Major Street, and Palmerston Boulevard, with additional performances at Margaret Fairley Park. Ticket holders will receive an email on the performance day informing them of the audience meeting point.
