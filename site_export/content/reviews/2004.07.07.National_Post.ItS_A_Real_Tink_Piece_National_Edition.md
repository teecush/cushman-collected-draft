THE TINKER'S WEDDING

Court House Theatre,

Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont.

- - -

John Millington Synge wrote one famous full-length play, The Playboy of the Western World, and several that are lesser-known and shorter. The Tinker's Wedding is one of these, and is being given a lunchtime production at the Shaw Festival.

Like most of Synge's plays, this one is set among the peasantry of backroads Ireland, who are depicted as a voluble set of cunning loons. Young Sarah Casey has taken it into her head to marry her companion, tinker Michael Byrne. The priest in the County Wicklow village where they abide agrees to perform the rites, on payment of 10 shillings and a freshly made tin can. Michael's mother, Mary, who dearly loves her pint, sells the can to buy booze, and some hell breaks loose. The priest washes his hands of all three vagrants, and they assault him. He begins formally to anathematize them, and they run away in terror.

Synge was one of the founding writers of the Irish dramatic renaissance of the 1900s, and Dublin's notoriously shockable press and public were regularly scandalized by him. The Playboy caused riots, just apparently by using the word "shift." The Tinker went unstaged in its author's short lifetime, but it was published and, as expected, vilified. This time, though, it wasn't sex the critics were on about but religion.

They seem to have been unperturbed both by the probability that Sarah was already living with her intended, and by the gynecological implications of her marital yearnings coming upon her, as her man puts it, "since the moon did change." They ignored what they elsewhere denounced as Synge's slurs on Irish womanhood; what infuriated them was his view of the Irish priesthood. The parson here is venal and stupid, though no more so than the tinkers.

The common view is that the play is on the side of the latter, as being in tune with the natural world, and it's true they seem to have more notion than the priest of how to have a good time -- at least, old Mary does. But really each side is as bad as the other, and the play makes no bones about it. The great strength of Synge's plays is their total lack of sentimentality.

Their greatest weakness, though to be sure it's often reckoned a strength, is the elaboration of their style. He writes fine and flavourful, when he confines himself to simple sentences, as when the priest tells his tormentors, "Let you leave the road, and not be plaguing me any more."

But it's seldom he does so confine himself. "Is it raving mad you're going, Sarah Casey," asks her putative mother-in-law, "and you the pride of women to destroy the world?" The second phrase, pungent as it is, blunts the direct impact of the first. Words in plays are weapons, and should be, and these are too frequently muffled.

So the first duty of a production is to rescue the conflict, and to turn the lines of text into lines of force running between the characters. This is something that Micheline Chevrier and her cast only occasionally accomplish. Most of the time we are conscious of a decorative noise going on, without much specific meaning. The delivery is in a style that it's impossible to resist describing as Synge-song. (And it isn't a matter of accent, it's a matter of phrasing.)

Nora McLellan comes off the strongest as old Mary, with her face, as the playwright himself might put it, as black as a chimney- sweep's that's just after comin' down half-a-dozen flues one after the other, the way she'd be rollickin' about the stage with her arms flailing, and the smell of the drink upon her voice as clear as it might be upon her breath, and she a good 20 years too young for the part, with the vigour of 10 women inside her.

Trish Lindstrom as Sarah is fierce but undecipherable, and the way the two women are going on at one another, you know one is hammer and the other is tongs, but you couldn't say which is which and what they might have on their minds. David Leyshon is refreshingly laconic as the tinker, and he a mere pawn in the game, despite having his name emblazoned in the title in full view of the whole Niagara region and the blessed tourists besides.

The priest is described, just before his entrance, as "a big boast of a man with a long step on him and a trumpeting voice." So it's a grand surprise when he turns out to be none other than little William Vickers, and he less a boast than a modest understatement with a prim, pettish sort of walk and a voice to match. And though it's long we've known him to be a fine, funny actor, he carries no overtones here of the princes of the church, the way he'd rather be handing out condolences than maledictions, and any angry woman might snap her fingers at him and devil the consequences. He puts up, in short, not much of a fight.

The play is a tricky one, and some of the tricks are its own fault, but there's more to it than gets a proper airing here.
