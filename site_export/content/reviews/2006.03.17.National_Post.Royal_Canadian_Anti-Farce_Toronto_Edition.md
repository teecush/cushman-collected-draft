THE INNOCENT EYE TEST

Royal Alexandra Theatre

Michael Healey's new comedy switches between being a sex-farce, a political homily, a meditation on art and a rogue episode of 24. None of these elements work, partly because they get in one anothers way, but somewhere in the plays confused heart are some thoughts on how Canada should be conducting itself in the world.

Set in a swish Tuscan hotel, the show has a multi-national cast of characters -- they seem indeed to come from practically everywhere except Italy -- but the protagonist is Canadian, and one of his fellow guests keeps calling him Canada. This happens so often that we too start to think of him as Canada, and by the end he finds himself taking what amounts to a national stand on international relations, especially regarding the United States. The upshot is that it's bad for us to sell the Americans plutonium, a stash of which is kicking around one of the plot lines, but good to sell them paintings. Good for our pockets and very good for their souls.

These are worthy thoughts but they arrive late and in a hurry. The part about the painting has to do with Samuel Kneck, the man who is Canada, being an art-dealer. He is in Tuscany at the bidding of Darryl Plate, a Las Vegas entertainment mogul who is opening a museum back home and wants to acquire a Mark Tansey painting whose title, The Innocent Eye Test, is also that of the play. The painting depicts a cow in an art gallery gazing enchanted at the picture of another cow. Darryl, who had bid for the painting sight unseen, is enchanted when he actually sees it. He, the plain man smitten, identifies with the cow. This is the best scene in the play. But it doesn't relate to anything else, and Darryl himself has nothing to do with the nuclear-enrichment thread thats going on elsewhere. Thats the trouble. The play may aspire to be an anti-farce, purposely running its jokes into the ground, but that's hard to bring off. We're left with the trappings of the form, without the unifying energy.

Also present are an Irish married couple who may or may not be terrorists (or Irish, or indeed married); a young woman who has psychic powers and for whom Samuel falls heavily; and a Ukrainian, carrying a briefcase identical to Sams. We expect the two bags will get confused, and when they dont we feel half cheated, half relieved. A cliche has been avoided. Then they do get confused, but only half-heartedly, and it feels like the worst of both worlds.

Up till now Healey's writing, like his acting, has always been funny whatever else; but here, where that gift is vital, it seems to have deserted him. There is some charm in the character of Daryl, who envisages an art gallery where a moving walkway whisks the spectators from picture to picture, finally depositing them in the gift shop. Sounds to me like the logical gallery experience; I can't see why Sam, with his pesky Canadian conscience, has anything against it. Tom McCamus, cast against type as Daryl, does a very good job of whisking on his own account; he, like his character, seems to be doing his damndest to get the show on the road. Gord Rand, a boundlessly surprising actor, as the man from Ukraine, lugubrious but hopeful, does a remarkable job of acting in his underpants; and C. David Johnson has himself a nice little comic cadenza in the second act. But nobody else is much good. Kevin Bundy, usually fine with lovable losers, is merely bland as Sam; Lisa Norton is a blank; and Tanja Jacobs seems to have given up, especially on sounding Irish.

Accents are a problem all around, especially as half the characters are pretending to be from somewhere theyre not. One of them is juggling three nationalities simultaneously. This creates a problem with audibility, exacerbated by the Royal Alex acoustics and the open spaces atop and around David Boechlers sets. The first act, by the hotel pool, looks hideous. The second, in Sam's suite, looks hideous and unbelievable. Its back wall is adorned by what appear to be the eyes of the Mona Lisa; it seems you don't have to go to Vegas to get your art dumbed down. The same uneasy compromise between God and Mammon characterizes the show as a whole. Mirvish Productions, doing their bit for Canadian drama and one of its best writers, get an inferior example of both, while author and interpreters seem to be aiming at some coarse idea of "commercialism" and missing. The designer and several of the actors are veterans of the Shaw Festival, and Christopher Newton, whose production has pace but not much else, is that theatre's distinguished former artistic director. The whole thing does resemble a bad night at Niagara-on-the-Lake, posh theatre letting its hair down when it had much better keep it in place.
