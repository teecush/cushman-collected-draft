The first-act curtain of Fire really lights one. Two, actually. On one side of the stage, there's Ted Dykstra, as a first-generation rock 'n' roller not unlike Jerry Lee Lewis, shaking the theatre as he pounds out Good Golly Miss Molly on voice and piano. On the other, there's Rick Roberts as a Bible-belt minister with resemblances to Jimmy Swaggart, finding his feet and his voice on his first venture into a radio studio. The real Lewis and Swaggart were cousins, but this piece makes them brothers, and renames them Cale and Hershel Blackwell. (Acknowledgments, I guess, to Otis Blackwell who cowrote Lewis's Great Balls of Fire and many of Elvis's hits.)Whatever, they turn out to be kin in talent as well as in blood. Both are natural, consuming -- and self-confusing --performers.

The point is made again at the start of Act 2; Roberts's Hershel, now thoroughly in his preaching groove, is as wild, as physically and vocally hyper-kinetic, as purposefully thrashingly hysterical, as Dykstra's Cale: perhaps more so, as Cale is already beginning to lose it. There's a musical as well as a psychological validity to this: rock -- Cale's brand of it anyway -- came out of the South, out of the church, and when Cale, in an attempt at reconciliation with his long-estranged brother, sings and plays some gospel, very affectingly, it's like he's coming home. The boys' father is a preacher and a musician, though he disapproves of the ways in which each has used his inherited gifts. The brothers also share a wife (sequentially, I should add: Cale first, though Hershel was the first to confide that he loved her, or lusted after her); she's named for that Miss Molly who sure loved to ball. She spends the evening trying to resolve her feelings for the two of them and to define her faith as a Christian, somewhere between Cale's diabolism and Hershel's bigotry.

Fire, with a script by Paul Ledoux and David Young and a score that's half pre-existing and half Ledoux originals, is a better show than it is a play. But to deal with the good part first. Dykstra played Cale 20 years ago and won a Dora for it; he's since said that he was too young for the part back then. He's strictly too old for its first part now, for all his tousled boyishness, but he has the heart of the role firmly nailed down. Versatility is one thing and this cast is loaded with it, but Dykstra, a goodness-gracious medium-sized ball of fire, is in a league of his own. Apart from the quality of his musicianship (he's also the MD), there's its energy, and the conviction of that energy, and the tact and delicacy with which it's dispensed. In the first act, he's both a natural star and a boy out of his depth. In the second, he's grown and grizzled and sunk. He's starved of opportunities post-intermission, as if a rock star's decline was pro forma and didn't have to be gone into, but he sinks his teeth into what he's got, whether drying out in jail or prostrating himself before his inflexible sibling who by this point has moved, profitably and disreputably, into politics.

Roberts's Hershel, pre-ordination, is stiff and unrevealing, but once the spirit's got to him, he's possessed and thrilling. Nicole Underhay gives herself over as fully to Molly who, what with having the first and last speeches, emerges as both chorus and protagonist. The actress can carry it; the character can't. Simply, it isn't about her.

Anyway we have three electric performances. Make that four, since Richard McMillan is absolutely fine as the minister-father, a fundamentalist who's allowed to be decent. When a character gets to do a number, spoken or sung, the authors give him good material. I have purist misgivings about a show that mixes old and new songs, especially when the subject is semi-biographical, but Ledoux comes through beautifully with Cale's gospel song. But though the script devotes too much time and effort to the brothers' earlier relationship and too little to the later -- there's a lot of Tom Sawyerish stuff (in which Molly also partakes) about swearing oaths of eternal devotion with blood cut from fingers -- but it never means much.

If Cale's decline is taken for granted, Hershel's becomes repetitive; politically, he ties himself to the coat-tails of an aspiring senator who happens to be both Molly's pa and Cale's ex-manager, and who is given a hesitant performance that weakens this plot-line considerably. James Macdonald's production is played out on a constricting set that seems mainly inspired by the railroad tracks where the Blackwell boys go for their youthful confession sessions but doesn't really evoke much of anything.

The actors and the music make the evening; when they're in command we have a tale of sound and fury, signifyin' somethin'.
