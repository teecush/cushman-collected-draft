RUTHERFORD AND SON

Court House Theatre,

Niagara-on-the-Lake

- - -

The title Rutherford and Son suggests one of those early 20th- century dramas about power struggles in some family business in the North of England. The suggestion turns out to be accurate. The play is set in a big house on the Yorkshire moors in 1912, though its not as you might expect about trouble at t'mill, or even about trouble in t'insurance company. Rutherford and Son run a glassworks. And despite the patriarchal title, which accurately reflects the principles on which the firm has been run, the strongest and cleverest characters in the play turn out to be women.

As was the author. Githa Sowerbys play, a success in its own time, fell into neglect until 1991, when it was reprinted in the same anthology of New Woman Plays as last years Shaw Festival choice, Diana of Dobson's. That turned out to be an attractive but unsatisfactory piece. Rutherford is grittier and more compelling.

It starts deceptively. John Rutherford, the self-made master of the glasshouse, runs his home as dictatorially as he does his factory. He storms, for example, if he is kept waiting for his dinner, though he thinks nothing of keeping everybody else waiting until he is ready. So the play's first movement shows us the rest of the family hungrily anticipating his arrival. There his Ann, his spinster sister, stiff and resigned. There is John junior, who was being groomed as heir to the business (it would presumably save re- painting the signs) but showed no taste or aptitude for it and fled to London; he has now returned accompanied by his wife, a former office worker, and their infant son. There is Richard, Johns younger brother, who like the proverbial fool of the family, has gone into the church; and who has been cowed by his fathers bullying into acting more foolish than he really is. And there is Janet, their sister, unmarried like her aunt and, from the initial look and sound of her, as rigorous and puritanical as her father. They all have grievances and frustrations; and, as they gloomily unload them upon one another, it's hard not to be reminded of the old Monty Python sketch in which two flat-capped Northerners competed furiously over which of them had less to eat as a child and which had to dig more coal before breakfast.

When Rutherford senior arrives and calls the clan to order, the play and the characters break their bounds. He is in a professional and emotional bind; the business is in financial trouble, and though he claims, apparently sincerely, to have built it up for the sake of his children, he actually despises every one of them. Son John claims to have invented a new manufacturing process, one that might save the firm from ruin. He wants to sell it to the highest bidder; his father is determined to get it for free. Meanwhile, daughter Janet turns out to be a lot less repressed than everybody took her to be. There are awkward joins in the construction -- the author seems at her wits' end to find a place in the plot for Richard -- but the action rolls over them. You are agog, as in all the solid plays of this time, to know what happens next.

There are two major outsiders. One is Martin, the Rutherford foreman who becomes, by a powerful irony, the emotional trustee of three conflicting family members, and who buckles under the strain. The other is Mary, Johns wife, to whom old Rutherford has hardly deigned to speak since her arrival. By the end of the play, he has to. It might have been better had the author refrained from bringing this fact explicitly to our attention -- wed noticed it for ourselves -- but it's a superb device anyway, signalling a radical shift in the balance of power, and expertly prepared.

Jackie Maxwell's production makes a strong case for the play, with a couple of large lapses. Early on, an expository duet between John and Mary has been played on an exaggeratedly high pitch, as if both participants knew they were supposed to be upset, but had no idea why or how much. Even in the play at large, neither of these performances is quite right. John is a difficult role, a weakling with genuine abilities, self-absorbed and also self-destructive; the elements are all there in Dylan Trowbridges performance, but uncertainly dispensed, so one isnt always sure if it's the actor or the character who's vacillating.

As Janet, Kelli Fox is stunning, in two successive modes. As the black-clad housekeeper of the opening, she is a one-woman Wuthering Heights, barbed and boding. Then the mask comes off: She has to face her father, with both the accumulated bitterness of decades as an unpaid servant and the reality, already compromised, of her one chance at happiness. The griefs and the accusations come pouring out, white-hot; and the actress never indulges, never lets up, never hits a false note. And thats without reckoning the complexities of her last appearance which is composed about equally of erotic devotion and stony devastation, and which finally resolves into a silent declaration of independence. In The Return of the Prodigal a few seasons ago, Fox finely played a woman who was in a similar position but resigned to it. Here she gets to fight the battles and, from the audiences point of view, to win even those she loses.

Peter Krantz makes an arresting figure of Martin, torn between loyalties, though it's hard to believe in the feudal strength of the bonds to which he finally surrenders; you keep wanting him to stop being Frankenstein's monster and turn into D. H. Lawrence. Mike Shara adds a leaven of hapless humour as the reverend Richard.

Rutherford himself is Michael Ball, an actor who has been regularly cast as tyrannical capitalists and has often, as in Hobsons Choice and The Matchmaker, endowed them with unsuitably soft centres. Those plays, though, were comedies, so they may have seemed to be asking for it. This one gives no scope for lovability; and Ball, though he can never quite seem as if he were made of granite, rises finely to the occasion. Rutherford is not all monster; there is shrewdness, even occasional tolerance, in the way he deals with people to whom he is not related; his passion for the family firm is real, however destructive of his actual family. It ends up being both his downfall and, in a woundingly ironic reversal, his salvation. You can see it coming; which makes it all the more satisfying.

His accent wanders between north and south, which is conceivably intended to make a subtle geographical point but is in practice confusing.

HITCHES

There is a scene in which an aggrieved village-woman invades the Rutheford home and takes improbable possession of the living-room; she is played (by Donna Belleville) as if she were, accent part, an avenging banshee from an Irish play of the same period.

(This kind of confrontation, in which both parties aim at passion and only achieve petulance, seems to be a recurring weakness in Maxwells work; there was a scene just like it, and involving one of the same actors, in last year's Coronation Voyage.)

Nicole Underhay as his bride has even more trouble with the idiom, so much so that she often sounds simply inadequate; but the shape and meaning of the character are working away inside her, and they are in large part the shape and meaning of the play.

It may not have been the author's intention that this turn into a play about Janet, but she certainly provided the ammunition.
