Old plays came to Toronto, new ones debuted, and faces new and old took to the stage

The more it changes. I've been glancing at my summary of Toronto theatre in 2001, and my first thought is that I could almost reprint it, changing only the names, and by no means all of them. At least I think my headings and links can stay the same. Lets see if it works.

AS FOR NEW TORONTO PLAYS ... THE YEAR BEGAN INVIGORATINGLY ... THINGS DECLINED PRECIPITOUSLY ...

Right. 2002s best new play was its first. Michael Healey's Plan B took the author of The Drawer Boy from the meta-theatrical to the sexually political. It unrolled less smoothly than its predecessor but ranged more widely and more deeply, and it spoke, sharply but humanely, in our theatre's most identifiable comic voice. I mean that almost literally, since my memory keeps insisting that Healey himself played the leading role, even though he didnt. (Peter Donaldson did, very well.) This may be due to Healeys having been so enjoyably in evidence later on, in the role of the craven critic in Carole Corbeil's In the Wings. He also wrote, or adapted, the most memorable segment of Soulpepper's Absolutely Chekhov. Both as writer and performer (I think of him as a performer rather than an actor) he is a master of verbal slapstick, a deceptive bumbler whose peregrinations always wind up on target.

After that, though, there was nothing. Well, almost nothing; Morris Panychs Girl in a Goldfish Bowl, the years second-best Canadian play, was likeable -- Panych writes very well for actors, and is also a superb director -- but unco-ordinated, a letdown after his previous triumphs with The Overcoat and Earplay.

In the Wings was a moderately endearing mess. Otherwise, on the Tarragon-Factory-Passe Muraille axis and beyond, the year yielded a handful of smartly staged mediocrities, a couple of ambitious disasters, and several numbing atrocities. Somewhat off the main drag there were three first-rate youth-oriented pieces: Adam Pettle's probing Misha, presented as part of Theatre Direct's Buncha festival; Leslie Ardens enchanting The Happy Prince, brought into what we used to call (some of us still do) the Young People's Theatre; and Joan MacLeod's The Shape of a Girl, from Vancouver to the Tarragon Backspace.

WELCOME IF BELATED IMPORTS ...

The Canadian Stage Company, despite its name, has a terrible record with Canadian plays but an excellent one with plays from abroad. Tom Stoppards Indian Ink was, strictly speaking, the best new play of the year. New to Toronto, that is: On British stage and radio it goes back about 10 years. It was given a superb wide-angle production by Richard Cottrell, dazzlingly designed by Susan Benson. Visually, this production bore the same relation to everything else seen on our stages as did Julie Taymors Frida to everything else seen on our movie screens.

As for American plays, these admittedly not so belated. Proof, which arrived all Tonied and Pulitzered, turned out to be nothing special (no more did the comparably garlanded and slightly superior Side Man at the Tarragon). Neil LaBute's The Shape of Things was electric; for thrills and surprises that were more than tricks, it was the most exciting evening of the year.

SOULPEPPER, STILL TORONTO'S BEST HOPE ...

I dont know how critics in Toronto stayed sane before this company came along. (Maybe they didnt.) Soulpepper's fifth year was an especially good one for its director, Albert Schultz. As Astrov in Uncle Vanya he gave probably the best performance of his career, while his production of Alan Ayckbourn's A Chorus of Disapproval was simply the finest thing seen on a Toronto stage all year. (Another contender for best new play, though this one, apart from being English, was 20 years old.)

Apparently it got into the season through a lucky accident. Soulpepper needed a companion to its concert revival of The Beggars Opera, and Stratford had snagged the rights to the more obvious Threepenny bit. The Beggars Opera, incidentally (I didn't review it at the time), turned out to be too insistently, or at least too fussily, musical. The few songs that worked were the simplest ones. But it did suggest that Patricia O'Callaghan, a magnetic Polly previously known only as a singer, might become a musical-theatre star.

In truth, all of Soulpeppers shows, apart from Chorus and the revived Vanya and Christmas Carol, have to rank as near misses, The Winters Tale being the nearest. But the sense remained of zest and commitment and of windows being opened onto territories we hardly ever see.

STRATFORD HAD ITS STRONGEST SEASON IN YEARS, GRATIFYINGLY RICH IN SHAKESPEARE ...

Yes and no. Stratford's 50th season suffered somewhat in comparison with its 49th, but the Shakespearean standard held. Christopher Plummer's King Lear surpassed expectation, which was running high. It had all the anticipated grandeur, more than the anticipated pathos, and a knack -- unleashed at stimulatingly irregular intervals -- for applying electric shocks to the text. Jonathan Millers production had a helpfully specific sense of time and place, but its supporting players seemed to be operating on a sink-or-swim basis. Like most critics I was rough on the young actress playing Cordelia; it occurred to me later that she might have stood more of a chance if her director, apparently distrustful of the plays redemptive element, had cut less of her role.

There was more detailed textual exploration, and richer company acting, in some less heralded productions: in Miles Potter's Romeo and Juliet, which found craggy contours in the Capulet household while playing bravely but fairly with a tragedy that for most of the time behaves like a comedy; in Leon Rubin's invigorating two-part Henry VI; in Martha Henrys Richard III, which was almost done in by a smothering set but which had a host of interesting people nestling in its crannies; and, on the edge of the canon, in David Lathams account of The Two Noble Kinsmen, a rewarding showcase for graduates of the Stratford conservatory.

Apart from Richard Monette's handsome staging of My Fair Lady, non-Shakespearean work in the main theatres was below par. The compensation was the opening of the Stratford studio with a season of seven new plays. Most of these would have looked standard on the Toronto circuit but there were a couple of exceptions: Paul Dunn's compact High-Gravel Blind and, at the opposite extreme, Peter Hintons sprawling The Swanne, first instalment of a promised trilogy. This is still impossible to judge. It seems foolhardy to build an elaborate critique of Victorian society on an incident that not even the author believes actually happened, but the size and energy made you want to come back for more. And it made a great workout for the company.

THE SHAW FESTIVAL HAD ITS WEAKEST ...

Not this year, though it was uneven. Christopher Newtons farewell season was distinguished by his superlative Return of the Prodigal (itself a return from the previous season) and by his moderately dark and very funny Hay Fever. (An insight into how Coward can be immoderately dark and sublimely funny was provided by the recent London production of Private Lives, starring an immaculate Alan Rickman and a wounded, shimmering Lindsay Duncan, which I caught at the end of its season in New York.)

Newton's successor, Jackie Maxwell, directed the only enjoyable Candida I have ever seen. Neil Munro's production of Detective Story was one of the best of his large-scale American genre pieces, and Munro also rounded off his Granville-Barker cycle with His Majesty, which demanded patience but amply rewarded it.

BIG MUSICALS, USUALLY IN TACKY TOURING VERSIONS, WERE DEADLY ...

As before, except Contact couldnt reasonably be accused of being tacky.

THE GENERALLY MORIBUND COMMERCIAL THEATRE DID WELL WITH PLAYS ...

Make that singular; the Mirvishes gave a wider showing to Zadies Shoes. Otherwise, the producers are waiting for The Producers. So are we all.

THIS HAS BEEN A GREAT YEAR FOR ONE-PERSON SHOWS ...

There were at least two this year, both female, that I can hardly bear to think about.

I now depart from last year's template with a few observations peculiar to this one.

UNDERRATED PERFORMANCES ...

Amy Redford in The Shape of Things; she attracted inexplicable animosity for being American and a film star's daughter, but in fact conveyed the character perfectly. Jennifer Paterson in Proof; similar, she was unquestionably immature technically, but that actually helped. And -- amazingly -- Ted Dykstra in A Chorus of Disapproval; nobody denied he was good, but nobody said that he gave the best performance of the year, which he did.

OVERRATED PERFORMANCE ...

Kate Hennig in The Danish Play, itself a strong, though certainly not unrivalled, candidate for Most Overrated Play. Actually, she wasnt half bad. But she wasnt half good, either, and I find the idea that the bad parts were a bold stylistic choice hard to swallow.

BEST PERFORMANCES OTHER THAN THOSE ALREADY MENTIONED ...

An incomplete list, but just to show where I'm coming from: Fiona Reid in Indian Ink; The five family members in The Return of the Prodigal and everyone in Uncle Vanya; David Storch and Susan Coyne in The Winter's Tale and Chorus; Liisa Repo-Martell in Chekhov Longs; Ben Carlson in Swollen Tongues; Sharry Flett and Mary Haney in His Majesty; Graham Abbey and Scott Wentworth in Romeo and Juliet; Seana McKenna and Diane DAquila in Richard III; Edie Falco in Frankie and Johnny in the Clair-de-Lune (on Broadway, but youve seen The Sopranos, so you know); Jennifer Phipps in The Old Lady Shows Her Medals; Patricia Fagan in Therac 25; Jenny Young in The Shape of a Girl; John Jarvis, Oliver Dennis, Wiliam Webster -- three ultra-reliables -- in everything they touched. Happy new year.
