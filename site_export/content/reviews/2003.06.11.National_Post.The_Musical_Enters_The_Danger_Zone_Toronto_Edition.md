TOP GUN! THE MUSICAL

Factory Theatre, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - For a measure of how far civilization has declined, recall that 50 years ago Alan Jay Lerner and Frederick Loewe wrote My Fair Lady. They did not write Pygmalion -- The Musical. They had enough respect for their source material and for themselves to offer an interpretation rather than a rip-off. Since then, of course, the musical has dwindled from a genre to a suffix, to be tacked on to any property for which audiences might conceivably feel nostalgic. The creators of Top Gun! The Musical have noticed this phenomenon and are out to satirize it. Their source is the 1986 Tom Cruise film, but their show is not actually a musical of the movie. It's a musical about people who are attempting to put on a musical of the movie. So really it's Top Gun! The Musical! The Musical.

This may sound interestingly complex in theory, but it's rotten in practice. The production was a box-office smash at last year's Toronto Fringe Festival, proving yet again that in those hectic circumstances, audiences (not to mention critics) are liable to drop both their guard and their standards. Now that it's been remounted in its own right, its deficiencies gape.

They start with what the show apparently thinks is its smartest idea: the fact that Top Gun itself, being about aerial warfare, is the dumbest possible basis for a musical. Now, it's quite true that in the real world similarly hopeless projects are attempted all the time. I wonder if the authors realize that their title is only one syllable removed from something that actually happened, rather briefly, on Broadway in 1990 and that was called Shogun: The Musical. The question is: How much time do you want to spend making fun of this? We all treasure The Simpsons' immortal Oh, Streetcar, and some of us recall a movie called The Tall Guy, starring Jeff Goldblum and Rowan Atkinson, that involved a musical version of The Elephant Man. ("Somewhere up in heaven, there's an angel with big ears.") But the numbers in these cases never took up more than a few minutes of screen time, which is just the amount they needed in order to be funny. Beyond that a bad song is a bad song, whether it happens accidentally or on purpose.

Denis McGrath (book and lyrics) and Scott Wise (music) presumably realize this, which is why they've given their spoof a backstage frame. Unfortunately, the songs in the "real" story are no better than those in the fictitious one: worse, if anything, because they're sincerely turgid. Of course, it's possible that they're meant to be spoofs as well. There's one, a solo for a leading lady in need of a break, that is strongly reminiscent of the Music and the Mirror number in A Chorus Line, though it is not, as far as I can see, being played for laughs. In fact, I don't know how seriously the show's creators mean us to take their outer narrative; and, what's more, I don't believe they know either. I think they just drift from moment to moment, grabbing at whatever effect seems within reach. But you can hardly expect to keep an audience's attention for 90 minutes without offering them a foothold in something.

I admit my credentials for judging Top Gun! The Musical may be precisely the wrong way 'round -- that is, I have never seen Top Gun but I have seen a lot of musicals. The show may have been filled with satiric jabs at the movie that I totally failed to appreciate; though, in all honesty, the full Saturday-night house I saw it with didn't seem to be laughing all that much either. (They did clap at the end, though. I don't want to rig the case.)

I do know, because the publicity has told me, that the show wants to bring out the film's homo-erotic subtext, and there is one character, a gay actor, who's very insistent that Iceman, his role in the musical, is Maverick's real love interest. (I assume you know who these people are, even if I don't. Anyway you, unlike me, are under no professional obligation to confess your ignorance.) A once- male character, named Goose, has been recast as a woman. But the show-within-the-show is so obviously doomed, by incompetence as well as intractability, that we can't give a lick what they do to it.

Four weeks from opening, the creative team discover that they've been denied the rights to You've Lost That Lovin' Feelin', which they were planning to interpolate; "let's just say they're the Unrighteous Brothers," quips the director, in a fair example of the script's level of wit. It turns out not only do they not have the rights to that song, they don't have the rights to Top Gun. The producer who brings this news wants to push on anyway because it will be great propaganda for the war in Iraq; he's a fanatical U.S. vet, maybe still a practising General. Up until his arrival, I'd assumed the scene was Canada -- how else to explain the knowing references to David Mirvish and Garth Drabinsky, which, in substituting name-dropping for humour, had served early notice of the kiss of death?

The producer sings a song about putting asses in seats, but since the actor playing him has a trick of gabbling not only his dialogue but his lyrics, I couldn't tell whether he was hoping to achieve this end by filling his show with right-wing content or by eliminating all content whatsoever. Whichever it is, he manages to rile the director, who has ideals. This is surprising, because earlier on he has sung a very similar song called Forget the War. At politically charged moments like these, you wonder if the show wants to be Smoking Gun, The Musical.

The director is conflicted anyway because he's just come off Apocalypse Wow, which apparently lived up to the first word of its title but not the second. There is also a leading lady who arrives two hours late and puts the moves on the director, while demonstrating none of the charm or glamour necessary to get away with such behaviour. But why go on? Colin Viebrock's production, apart from being visually untidy, is extremely slow; every joke is given plenty of time to die. There is a cast of seven, of whom surprisingly few can sing. Dmitry Chepotevsky can (he plays Maverick) and he is also agreeably goofy. Alison Lawrence is more solidly appealing as the faithful stage manager who does most of the work (this is believable) but she is defeated when obliged to sing a generic blues that appears to think "biscotti" rhymes with "latte." One critic has compared this dreck to The Drowsy Chaperone, which, whatever its limitations, had excellent lyrics. When will they ever learn?

Lurking behind all this there may well be some talent and good intentions. Some of the tensions within the company are well observed. But consider: The cast sends us away with a reprise of Asses on Seats. Is this neo-Brechtian cynicism, or what? Everybody knows that, other things being equal, a show with songs is less boring than a show without songs, but it's time to stop trading on this circumstance and calling the results "a musical." It happens at every level, but at least the old mega-musicals (remember them?) had stories that they genuinely wanted to tell us. I never thought it possible, but Top Gun! The Musical made me nostalgic for the high professionalism of Aida (The Musical).
