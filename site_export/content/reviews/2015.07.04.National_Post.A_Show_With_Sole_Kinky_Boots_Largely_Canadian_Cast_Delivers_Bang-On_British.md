I have to admit that I never expected to see a musical set in Northampton. One of England's more featureless towns, it's mainly been renowned as a city of shoemakers. Kinky Boots follows the fortunes of a shoe-firm that has fallen on hard recessive times, and that restores those fortunes by finding a new niche market: manufacturing bright red, strongly supported footwear for men who like to dress as women. It happens because Charlie Price, the initially reluctant inheritor of the family business, has a chance encounter with Lola, a London drag-queen. Charlie engages Lola as his expert designer and the company goes on to success as, to adapt a venerable British phrase, a load of new cobblers.

Kinky Boots, following Billy Elliot and The Full Monty, is the third recent musical to be based on a movie with a British bluecollar setting, and it's much the best. It has a debut theatrical score by Cyndi Lauper and an expert and sometimes very funny book by Harvey Fierstein (with obvious resemblances to his script for La Cage Aux Folles, only better) but its real hero may be its director-choreographer Jerry Mitchell. This may be the first musical to be set in a factory since The Pajama Game, and the employees are ingeniously employed. So is their environment. The first act ends, joyously, with the new boots rolling off the assembly line and the workers rolling with them. You never know what, or who, is going to come along the conveyor belt next. It's the most inventive number I've seen in ages.

It gets part of its effect from the fact that the people doing it are, as individuals, so likable and even believable. This Broadway show is being done here with a cast that's almost entirely Canadian, and they are, if the original cast recording is any indication, far better at capturing the British idiom than their New York counterparts. The one American in the company is Alan Mingo

Jr. who plays Lola, and who is a dazzler. He's commanding in his floor-show routines; that includes his shop-floor routines, with which he bemuses the factory staff when he first comes among them, in full vocal and physical strut, with his six-strong support group in attendance. He's more subtly impressive when, boyish and suddenly shy, he reports for work in more conventional attire - which paradoxically arouses even greater hostility from the more boorish of the locals.

"I'm Simon from Clacton" he announces to Charlie, Clacton being a seaside town marginally more glamorous than Northampton but not much.

The confession - that's how it comes out - rounds off what is probably the show's best song, certainly its most moving, "Not My Father's Son." (One recurring phrase, about having "the strength of Sparta and the patience of Job," is quite beautifully set.) Lola/Simon bonds with Charlie over their shared father issues, both having wanting to please their dads while taking different paths from the ones laid down for them. Their relationship - the program calls it a bromance - is, obviously, the emotional spine of the show.

Graham Scott Fleming does a first-rate job as Charlie, puzzled but determined, growing into a job he says he never wanted but becoming possessed by it.

AJ Bridel captivates the house as Lauren, promoted from the floor to the office ("I been exec-utized") and trying, in a show-stopping solo, to resist the temptation to add Charlie to her "History of Wrong Guys." The beat may be new, but the lyric's in an old and honourable sadder-but-not-wiser Broadway tradition. (It's hardly a spoiler to reveal that the temptation gets resisted and the man gets got.) Daniel Williston gives a splendidly uncompromising account of the resident beerbellied reactionary, and Vanessa Sears is eye-and-ear catching as Charlie's fiancee, a somewhat unrewarding role as she has not one but two competitors for his affections, both of them given more stage time and more songs.

The first half has irresistible momentum. The second starts with an extended sequence about a boxing match that feels like filler; and proceeds to give in to one sentimental temptation after another. There's the heavy who sees the light and saves the day; there's the tearful surprise family reunion; there's also, twice, the team that threatens to walk off the job but rallies round in the end. And there's a breast-beating number for Charlie, into which Fleming puts his heart and soul and which predictably raises the roof, but that is far in excess of both the person and the situation. As so often in rock-era musicals, there's a total disconnect between how the characters talk and how they sing. That goes for both the writing and the performance. In the earlier numbers (the first one is especially good) Lauper writes with real theatrical flair, pointed and colloquial, but as it goes on the score gets generalized. The book, meanwhile, has given up any pretence of realism; all the characters arrive in the same place (definitely not Northampton) at the same time, with no regard for motivation or price of fare. They all join in an inspirational closing number that could come from, or go into, almost any show. But they've built up tremendous goodwill and we, more or less happily, give in.
