In repertory until July 14. 1-800-511-SHAW

LAURA

Royal George Theatre,

Niagara-on-the-Lake

- - -

NIAGARA-ON-THE-LAKE - Laura, says the song, is the face in the misty light, footsteps you hear down the hall. She is also, you may remember, the voice that floats on the summer night that you can never quite recall.

That Johnny Mercer lyric, more evocative than any of the lines in the play Laura, was written to fit David Raksin's indelibly haunting theme-music -- some of us in Niagara were humming it as we walked into the theatre -- for the celebrated 1944 movie. It was never sung in the film, but if you listen hard you can hear Woody Hermans vocal version playing behind the curtain calls at the Shaw Festival.

You also, in this atmospheric production, get to hear the tune rendered instrumentally by Charlie Parker and to listen, over and over, to John Coltrane playing Duke Ellingtons infinitely seductive In a Sentimental Mood.

There is also some older jazz on hand. Laura, the beautiful young copywriter whose murder in her own Manhattan apartment we are assembled to investigate, was a fan of "hot music," Louis Armstrong and Jelly Roll Morton: a taste deplored by several of her acquaintances, ranging from her permanent-bachelor friend Waldo Lyndecker, writer and self-styled connoisseur, to her janitor's wife, who claims her teenage son was being corrupted by Laura and her record collection.

In all, Laura reminds me of Lee Wiley, a sultry Southern singer of great distinction who was doing some of her best work at just that historic moment; bop was coming in, swing remained, but her heart and her rhythm were in New Orleans. People fell, and still fall, in love with the teasing promise in Wiley's voice, just as Mark McPherson, this plays splendidly named detective, though no necrophiliac, falls in love with Lauras portrait hanging over the mantel.

Laura began life as an unproduced play by Vera Caspary, then became a novel, then another play (the present one, written in collaboration with George Sklar) and then of course that film.

So any stage production comes weighted down with legend, accruing not only to the property itself but to the period from which it derives: the haughty 1940s, which have displaced the '20s and '30s as prime sources of American myth.

Both play and production are stronger on mood and even on character than they are on suspense. There is one diabolical twist in the first act but its very audacity lets the air out of the situation; it is hard, thereafter, to care very much who committed the crime, though there is a reasonable choice of suspects: five people seem to have had a motive.

What counts is the revelation of the number of men attracted to Laura like -- Im sorry, theres no other image -- moths to a flame: her dumb Dixiecrat fiance (this is the second Shaw Festival play in two days about a strong woman betrothed to a man unworthy of her), Waldo, McPherson, the boy downstairs. That, and the image of Manhattan high life, all smart talk and hard liquor. Good eating, too: Laura's success in the advertising racket apparently enabled her to employ a full-time cook.

Neil Munro's production cunningly hints the whole thing may be the smitten detectives dream. It is sensuously lit by Ereca Hassell and finely designed by Yvonne Sauriol, whose sets and costumes set off each other beautifully. McPhersons Dick Tracy yellow works especially well with the prevailing shade of dull ochre.

Ben Carlson, in a first-rate performance, wears the coat and the character most sympathetically. Jane Perry, playing the mysterious woman at the play's centre, has the heavy task of activating a period icon and is not equipped for it; her style is laboured.

Waldo, too, needs the high-strung ministrations of film noir music and lighting to achieve his full measure of eunuchoid menace (it also helps if he's Clifton Webb), but Michael Ball gives him a useful mixture of delicacy and bulk, and its a pleasure to watch him pattering across the floor or sliding along the sofa. The character seems to have been based on the waspish critic Alexander Wollcott, who was also the inspiration for The Man Who Came to Dinner, a role Ball is to play later this season: such a double comes seldom.

Mary Haney is superb as the cook; this is luxury casting in a stock part, as -- even more spectacularly -- is the triumphant deployment of Al Kozlik in the one-word role of a second cop. Stephen McQuigge also does well as the janitors kid besotted with the glamorous older woman. She gave him his very first kiss. That was Laura. But she's only a dream.
