Most of Toronto's theatres have sailed into the new season with all guns blazing: Buddies in Bad Times has disinterred Here Lies Henry, the Tarragon has been Generous, CanStage has delivered itself Of Mice and Men and the Factory has fed us with Apple. Its fallen to Theatre Passe Muraille to lower the tone with a new play from Tom Walmsley, the aptly titled Descent.

I wouldnt want to be vicious about this; the play is seldom actually dull but it's vague and apparently aimless. The descent of the title is executed by Susan, a young woman whose sexual life has been, by her own account, comparatively sheltered and who is just about to get wed; indeed she gets the show off to a misleadingly spirited start by singing Nellie McKay's I Wanna Get Married. First, though, she wanna treat herself to what she describes as the female equivalent of a stag night, with two guys at once. Her chosen partners are a pair of heavy drinkers, both named Randy and chiefly distinguishable by the fact that only one of them has pretensions to being a poet.

But in treating Susan as the central character, I am drawing less on the play itself than on the publicity, which says she is, and on the shows logo, which is a striking portrait of Susan herself, or at least of the actress playing her. On stage, though, she seems no more important than any of the other four characters, all of them male, all of them at some point or to some degree lusting after her. Apart from the two Randies, in whose very basic pad the first act is set (the set is drastically minimal, presumably by design), we have two crumbs from the comparatively upper crust: Robinson, a publisher for whose patronage the poetic Randy half-heartedly hopes, and James, his assistant and acolyte whom everyone else despises for reasons that arent terribly clear. The action, or at least the talk, unfolds in a series of short, sharp bursts, punctuated by blackouts. There is humour but, at least on the second night when I saw it, hardly any laughs.

The most irritating thing about the play is that it takes for granted characters and relationships that it hasn't established. The second act, which is set in an abandoned church, seems at first to be picking up exactly where the first left off. (There's a car theft in both.) It then looks as if it might be happening in Hell; most of the characters have religious yearnings.

Eventually it transpires that we are still on Earth, after a lapse of eight years during which all these people have kept in touch, which, since they barely existed in the first place, is hard to credit. One of them seems to be dead, though he unfortunately wont lie down. There are some half-hearted farcical developments, on the Joe Orton model, concerning the robbing and moving and dumping of a corpse. Susans marriage has happened, but it hasn't worked out, though by the end of the evening she has managed to realize at least half of her eight-year-long fantasy. She gets to make some embittered speeches from the pulpit. All the characters engage in monologues and they are, by and large, the best things in the play.

Indeed, the whole play, despite its various voices, feels like a monologue. It's a journey into its authors personal Skid Row; theres no self-pity in it but it does rely heavily on the awareness, on both sides of the curtain, of Walmsley's own alcoholic past and late Catholic conversion; also on the guilty liberal conviction that the worse off a play's characters are, the better we will be for looking at them.

Kate Lynch, whose last Walmsley production (Three Squares a Day) was rather good, cant lend any focus to this one. The performances are casual to the point of shapelessness. Deborah Hays Susan is the best, especially in her later, jaded diatribes, which have real style. Brendan Murray gets some chipper conviction into the poetic Randy (who defines himself, accurately enough, by not writing like Blake). Christopher Morris as his buddy and namesake is likable enough but looks as if he would like somebody to throw him some motivation. As the rich and reviled Robinson, John Blackwood is thoroughly at sea. Paul Fauteux as James swims in his wake with more authority but no more persuasiveness, but then he is playing one of the more tenuous characters in modern drama.

Peter Cockett, who directed the three plays in the recent Shakespeare and the Queen's Men project, described himself during a post-performance Q&A session as less a director than a facilitator." By his own account, he set some ground rules and then let his actors get on with it, just as their Elizabethan forebears, whose rehearsals were rushed and rudimentary, would have had to do. The results -- the modern ones, I mean -- were always lively and often impressive. They are unlikely to have been exact replicas of the original interpretations; indeed, the whole idea of "interpretation may have been an intrusion, though an unavoidable one. What we bring to these plays of the 1580s is, inescapably, irony.

I am thinking especially of The Famous Victories of Henry V, the most enjoyable of the three shows and the only one I saw in the Queen's Men on Tour" section of the enterprise. (The touring date was the Tranzac Club, whose ale-drenched atmosphere was thoroughly appropriate. The audience thronged around the fit-up platform stage like Elizabethan groundlings, though a few of us softies managed to secure chairs.)

The Famous Victories has attracted academic interest as a source not only of Shakespeares Henry V but of the two parts of Henry IV that precede it. It covers breezily in about two hours the ground that we are accustomed to traversing in nine. Unsurprisingly, there isnt much subtlety. This Prince Hal is first a merrily unrepentant highway robber, then, after being rebuked by his royal father, turns instantly into a storybook hero who, on becoming king himself, leads all the lads off to victory in France. There are none of the heart- searchings and transitions you find in Shakespeare; and, though we still get the scene when the new king cuts loose his former cronies, there is hardly a hint of a Falstaff.

This Henry's attitude to all things and people French is unabashedly contemptuous and acquisitive. It must have warmed every patriotic Elizabethan heart. We now see things a little differently, and Paul Hopkins, playing the role here, could hardly avoid winking with us at his own outrageousness. His was altogether an irresistible performance, bold and fluent and charming. (At a couple of points he helped himself to the audience's beer.) He went, as his remote predecessors must have done, with the flow; only now it was a different flow.

The production revealed, too, that crudity can have its own complexity. There is a scene (echoed in Shakespeare, though he spread it out among various episodes) in which soldiers, pressed into service, take leave of home and loved ones, the latter represented by one alternately heartbroken and lascivious wife. Its part pathos, part broad farce; the actors played both modes, full out and simultaneously, and the results were both rich and instructive.

The new Queens Men also followed the old in relying heavily on typecasting; thus Don Allison played the king in all three plays -- in Famous Victories, he played two of them -- and always, I'll swear, wore the same crown. Similarly, Alon Nashman was always principal clown, and a virtuoso one, though he sometimes seemed to be playing in a different rhythm from the others, with more curlicues and pauses. But I daresay Richard Tarlton, the original star and the first great comedian in English stage history, did the same. I don't know how much this project taught scholars about how plays were done then. It certainly showed the rest of us a lot about how they might be done now.
