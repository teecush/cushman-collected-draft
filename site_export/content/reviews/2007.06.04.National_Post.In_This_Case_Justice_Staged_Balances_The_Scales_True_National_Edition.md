TO KILL A MOCKINGBIRD

Avon Theatre, Stratford, Ont.

- - -

You can blame it on my age or on the educational system that raised me, but I have never read Harper Lee's To Kill a Mockingbird. I've never seen the movie, either. Nor, until Wednesday, had I seen the stage version. But I knew that the story involved an upstanding white lawyer in the Deep South defending a black man railroaded on a rape charge. I entered the Stratford theatre expecting something corny, but gripping.

What do you know, I was right. At least, I was on the mark when it came to the trial scene. For my fellow ignoramuses: It's 1935 in Macon, Ala. Atticus Finch has been nominated to represent Tom Robinson, accused of violently assaulting 19-year-old Mayella Ewell. It's obvious that her real abuser is her father Bob, a vicious guy who represents white trash at its trashiest, and Atticus easily establishes that Mayella led Tom on and that he, justifiably terrified, got out of the place as soon as possible.

But it's the Deep South, and Tom has already narrowly escaped a lynching. We have all the suspense of courtroom drama, plus the adrenalin of feeling that we are on the side of the angels.

Or, with Peter Donaldson playing Atticus, of the archangels.

Donaldson does a magnificent job of humanizing a man who, except for one brief moment of excess judicial fervour at the end, is always in the right. He fights passionately in a case he knows he's likely to lose. His final appeal to the jury's sense of decency is superbly sculpted, oratorical acting at its finest, alternating moral fervour with lashing irony. He can see the point of view of the people he's fighting.

A widower, he's a model father to his two young kids, leaving them in no doubt of where righteousness lies while admonishing them to be tolerant even of the crazy, bigoted old lady next door. And, despite being out of practice, he can bring down a mad dog (canine variety) with a single rifle shot. I can't believe that Gregory Peck (Atticus in the film) could have been as effective. Peck was Mr. Decency. With Donaldson, there's always a dark side lurking, throwing the light into relief.

The court proceedings also benefit from David Francis the scrupulously upright judge, and from Steve Ross, a freebooting prosecutor with no apparent scruples whatever. Dion Johnstone's Tom is pitiable with no loss of dignity, and Dayna Tekatch's Maryella pitiable with no dignity whatsoever, only a stubborn brattiness that's the residue of having been continually beaten down. How much she believes her own lies is unknowable, at least in this adaptation, but she desperately needs someone she can look down on. If she avoids caricature, Wayne Best as her pappy falls right into it; there's no sense -- in writing, acting or direction -- of what might make him tick.

Unfortunately, To Kill a Mockingbird (hate that cute title) isn't just a racial-prejudice drama. It's an out-of-the-mouths-of-babes drama. Much of it is seen through the eyes of Jem and Scout, Atticus's son and daughter, and of their friend Dill, who comes and goes for reasons that may make sense in the novel but are a timewasting drag on the stage. The story is told by Scout's older self, who presumably stands in for the novelist.

Christopher Sergel's adapatation is a lazy, unimaginative piece of work, dumping things on stage just because they're in the book; and this narration is the worst of it; it reduces the actress, Michelle Giroux, to bestowing long looks on the scene of her childhood and intoning languid prose about it.

Whatever the script and production pretends, this isn't Scout's story; she's just in it. The kids have their mischievous side, but they have all the right instincts; Dill, at the trial, comments on all the "contempt" there is in the world. They are appealingly played by Abigail Winter- Culliford (Scout), Spencer Walker (Dill) and especially by Thomas Murray as Jem, but they slow the play down. Child actors tend to be weak on timing unless dynamically directed which, in Susan Schulman's production, they are not.

The climax, ineffectually staged in the half-light, depends on the intervention of a hermit-like figure we've heard about but have not previously seen: Call him a recluse ex machina. This may work on the page or on the screen, both of them are better suited to atmospheric foreshadowing; on the stage, it's just flat.

The show is decked out with authentic-sounding backwoods music and a gospel choir. You couldn't have a play about the South without them.
