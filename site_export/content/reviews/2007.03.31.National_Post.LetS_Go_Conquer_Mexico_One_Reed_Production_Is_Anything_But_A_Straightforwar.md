If you go to see a play knowing only that it has something to do with the European conquest of the Americas, and if the first word of text you hear is "1492, then I think you might be pardoned for imagining that you are in for something concerning Christopher Columbus. This is the first point but far from the last at which One Reed Theatre Ensemble, creators and presenters of Nor the Cavaliers Who Come with Us, will turn out to have wrongfooted you.

It seems that 1492 was also the year in which the Holy Roman Emperor Charles V, formerly Charles I of Spain, discovered he was broke, having shelled out all he owned to buy his imperial title from the Pope who had it to sell. So, to replenish his coffers, Charles looked west. And the conquistador with whom the play is concerned is not Columbus but Cortez, conqueror of Mexico.

One Reed is a company composed of four actors -- Frank Cox O'Connell, Megan Flynn, Marc Tellez and Evan Webber -- who trained together at the National Theatre School, and discovered a shared interest in Mexican history and its ramifications for the present. They approached it from different angles: Tellez comes from a Mexican-American family; O'Connell was, in his own description, more the guilt-ridden white guy, trying to find the roots of my power." On graduation, they shared their research materials with a director, Daniel Mroz, and this show, developed over the past two years, is the result. It had a successful Toronto run at last years Summerworks, and is now being re-staged, in a slightly extended version, at the Glen Morris Studio on the University of Toronto campus. I saw a final run-through, which happened also to be the first run-through in this space. There were, though, no signs of uncertainty. It was a very accomplished performance.

Nor the Cavaliers is anything but a straightforward historical drama. More than half of it is concerned with a present-day figure billed simply as the Tourist (Webber) who journeys to Mexico to test what he describes as his own secular socialist principles, which he has found falling out of favour even in his native Canada. It seems odd in one way, fitting in another, that he should choose to visit a country whose traditions are the opposite of secular. Weve learned that it owes much of its present and historical form to the demands of a grasping church. We already know, and the play further informs us, that the soldiers who conquered it were also missionaries; they plundered and killed under the banner of Christ. But they came up against the natives' loyalties to their own gods, loyalties even more passionate and, for the invaders, profoundly disorienting.

The show switches backwards and forwards between past and present, but the present predominates. This, as far as I was concerned, was if not a mistake then a source of regret. I wanted to know more of the history and was frustrated, not just by its being constantly interrupted, but by its being relayed to us in gobbets that are written and acted in a style that I can best describe as intense but secretive. A central figure is a native woman who makes common cause with Cortez, spurring him on to greater excesses. She operates by incantation. " 'I will murder all the men and rape the women -- say it, she commands him, and he does. He says it, that is, but presumably he goes on to do it. We are dealing with her ancestral inter-tribal hostilities; she is a Mayan avenging herself on the Aztecs. Thats interesting, but I confess I got the information not from the performance but from talking to the cast and director afterwards.

Back in today, the Tourist finds refuge with a horse doctor in whom many of the old habits of thought live on. He talks about the lie of equality" and gives as his model of society the relation between horse and rider; together they are stronger than either could be alone but one, the rider, has to be in unquestioned control. These sentiments don't stop him happily cohabiting with a liberal-minded lady whose pet cause is the release of "women in chains, but he regards her activities with patronizing benignity. The Tourist is shocked by his host's attitudes but doesnt have much of an answer to them.

Atmosphere is provided, and changes in time and space effected, by a liberal and inventive use of homemade actorly effects. The evening all but commences with some piercing flamenco-style chanting by Flynn, accompanied by a complex rhythm of hand-claps from the men. Actors seem literally to somersault from one role and scene to another. Sand is strewn liberally across the stage; at one point Flynn, as the horse-medics domestic, stirs into an ever- increasing circle, a magical image. The performers are very good at all the devices they employ, but the air of a post-graduate drama- school exercise is still very much present; technique predominates over substance. In the first quarter of the show, Flynn told me, we share the tools that we have, we ask the questions so that you can come up with an answer." I couldn't find one.
