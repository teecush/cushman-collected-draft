Sammy Davis Jr.'s early career will be remembered with affection A black performer who succeeded on white terms

Friday, May 18, 1990

ROBERT CUSHMAN

BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail FOR THE first 30 years or so of his career - and remember he started right out of the cradle - Sammy Davis Jr. was strictly a North American phenomenon. Davis, who died Wednesday, was a Broadway star in 1956 in Mr. Wonderful, but the show, though a hit, was ephemeral, best remembered for a hit recording of its title song by Peggy Lee. Over the years, she probably did better out of it than he did. Anyway the show was essentially a blow-up of Davis's night club act, and American club acts have never really been big anywhere outside America. The informed bought his Decca record albums, but to most people he was another U.S. singer, covering the same standard repertoire as everybody else. Some said he did impressions.

He went world - I think it was in 1959 - at a Royal Variety Performance in London. The bill was crammed with American performers - Nat King Cole and Eartha Kitt, both much better known, were on it - but Davis was given the top spot. He looked disconcerting, with his diminutive frame and crooked face: "like a gnome" somebody said. He also, to British ears, talked funny. ("This song was one of our first hits, and therefore will always be a great favorite of ours." Royalty was surely meant to be in the audience, not on the stage.) Limited to an eight-minute spot, he sang, danced, clowned and impersonated in dizzying quick succession. He was overwhelming.

Later we found that he could stretch out as well. He did a couple of BBC television specials, just him and a band. It was his Civil Rights period, and we were all with him. "Back of the bus, indeed," he muttered, and we were delighted; in those days middle-of-the-road performers, black or white, didn't say such things. We warmed to him, even more, when he suddenly added gunslinging to his repertoire. After a few quick draws and rapid fires he favored us with a broad ingenuous grin. "Gosh," he said, "I wouldn't do it unless I was good at it." That was his compact with us. What we wanted from him was energy, versatility and personal communication. "Look," he said, delaying the next scheduled program for five minutes while he did his encore, "Perry Mason you know about, he wins every case. I'm only here tonight." Doubtless it was all scripted and rehearsed, but we loved it.

The early sixties were really Davis's years. The Beatles had yet to turn show business upside down, and Las Vegas still seemed like the entertainment centre of the world. Frank Sinatra started his own record label, and Davis and Dean Martin signed with it. In the antics of The Clan, which seemed fresh and amusing in those days, Davis was our representative. Sinatra was too unreachably talented and Martin too impenetrably laid-back for us to identify with, but Davis was the feisty little guy, setting off firecrackers, asking questions, and always doing more than any of the others.

As Sportin' Life, a role he had always longed for and was probably born to play, he periodically electrified the moribund movie of Porgy and Bess. Nobody else could have led on those Catfish Row picnickers with such loose-limbed enticing abandon. It was at this time, too, that he did his best singing. Not a jazz singer, he could still swing with ingratiating hipness in the right company. He learned as much from Mel Torme and Buddy Greco as from Sinatra. All his influences seemed to be white: Fred Astaire, Buddy Rich - and Mickey Rooney, with whom he shared a theatrical energy and intelligence in the reading of lyrics.

Being black, though, he had a more personal stake in Kennedy liberalism than any of his colleagues; and the second Kennedy assassination ended an era for him. He was in London again when it happened, opening a revised and improved version of his second Broadway hit, Golden Boy. The killing and the premiere coincided and for several nights he claimed to be unable to go on. This may have been self-indulgent and professionally dubious. But I was lucky enough to have tickets for the night of his return, and it was unforgettable. The show was an expert, and surprisingly tough, musical updating of Clifford Odets' thirties play about a young Italian - now of course a young Black - struggling out of poverty and into disillusion through the boxing ring. Odets' hero was granted a sentimental death: Davis, having killed an opponent, turned on us and sang "what became of me? and what became of you?" These seemed apposite questions, and Davis, if a limited and unsubtle actor, was a powerful one. And here, by the way, at the heart of commercial theatre, was a show that for once genuinely upset and disturbed its audience. The people who had booked to see Sammy Davis in Golden Boy were not expecting this.

It was quite a decline to his next and last stage show: a revival of Stop the World - I Want To Get Off. He had been singing its songs for years, and seemed to have persuaded himself that Anthony Newley and Leslie Bricusse were the contemporary Rodgers and Hart. This though was a symptom of the musical confusion that plagued all but the best singers of his generation when their citadel crumbled. He was after all an old vaudevillian; and when he stopped performing standards he lost much of his identity. If Sweet Gingerbread Man was ingenious and catchy, Mr. Bojangles was maudlin. He lost his political identity, too (as he more or less confessed when his second autobiography was published last year) and the basics of his act began to age; how late in the twentieth century could a man go on doing James Cagney? It was good, though, to see him on film and TV going back to his roots and upholding the dignity of tap.

As a kid in the army Davis had lived out his own version of Golden Boy; having grown up in an all-black world, he had to fight his way to acceptance and even survival. This part of his life story reads like something out of The Young Lions or From Here to Eternity.

In later years, he sang with feeling Stand Up and Fight, Carmen Jones answer to the Toreador Song. That he succeeded as a black performer on white terms is undeniable: also, as far as I can see, admirable, good for the world if not altogether for him. On Entertainment Tonight he was seen regretting that he had reduced admirable sentiments like "peace and love" to glib showbiz slogans, and it would have been good to see a real interviewer probe further into his reality and his image. That the image, in later years, turned many people off is undeniable and understandable. But those of us who were around for Davis's first impactcould never lose our affection for him.
