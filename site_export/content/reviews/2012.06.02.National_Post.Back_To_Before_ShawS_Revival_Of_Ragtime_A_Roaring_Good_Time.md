It may be hard to claim Ragtime as the great Canadian musical. It's an adaptation of an American novel, with an American composer, lyricist and librettist, and it was originally staged by an American production team with mostly American principal performers, and its very subject is America. Nevertheless, it was first produced by a Canadian company, Livent Inc., and its conceivable that its choice for revival at the Shaw Festival may have been influenced by those Canadian origins. Whether or not thats the case, it's regrettable, to put it politely, that the copious Shaw program makes only the barest small-print mention of Livent, and none at all of Garth Drabinsky, without whom the show would never have existed.

As it happens, Jackie Maxwell's Shaw production can stand comparison with the Livent original. It uses the Festival Theatre stage superbly; Sue LePages skeletal mobile sets and variously beautiful costumes and Alan Brodies superlative lighting make it both spare and spectacular. There's a surge of excitement right from the great opening title number that sets up the three groups of characters in pre-First World War America: the middle-class whites of New Rochelle, the emancipated but still submerged Negroes (the word at the time), and the European immigrants who escaped poverty and starvation to find, initially at least, more of the same. There are at least two occasions on which Valerie Moore's choreography betters my memories of the original; one is this opening, which she saves from looking like an imitation of the concentric Tradition number in Fiddler on the Roof, and the other is the vaudeville number in which glamour girl Evelyn Nesbit burlesques the trial of her husband for murdering her lover.

Evelyn is one of the historical figures in E.L. Doctorows novel and in the musical; others are Harry Houdini, Henry Ford, Booker T. Washington and Emma Goldman. Theyre the illustrative background figures in a tapestry whose principal characters are representative fictions from each of the social and racial groups. Terrence McNally's book still seems a miraculously clever reordering of the original, keeping faith with Doctorow's dry impersonal narrative tone while clearing space for Stephen Flahertys music and Lynn Ahrens lyrics to supply the emotional amplitude both inevitable and essential in a musical. Sometimes this spills over into sentimentality; it's notable, but not surprising, that the numbers that raised the loudest cheers on the Niagara first night were the most bombastic and simplistic: Wheels of a Dream, the hymn of ragtime pianist Coalhouse Walker to his new bride Sarah and his new Ford Model T; Back to Before, in which the unnamed Mother of the white family sings all too consciously about her intellectual liberation; Let Them Hear You, the last message of Coalhouse, turned avenger (some would say terrorist) after the vandalizing of his car and the killing of his wife. What does Coalhouse want us to hear exactly? Because he's just singing platitudes, that ride roughsod over the complexities of a situation in which hes a killer as well as a victim.

Far better are the sharp comedic songs, of which my favourite is Henry Fords brisk celebration of himself and his assembly line. Better still are Our Children, a duet for Mother and Tateh, the immigrant street vendor turned movie director, a love song that's all subtext; and He Wanted to Say, which is actually about subtext. Then there's The Night That Goldman Spoke in Union Square, simultaneously stirring and searching. Best of all is New Music, in which Coalhouses ragtime arouses deep feelings, rapturous and troubling or both, in all who hear it: Sarah, in a speechless heart-stopping moment, gives herself to him; Father, Mothers husband, just returned from Admiral Peary's expedition to the North Pole, understands enough to know he doesn't understand.

Benedict Campbells Father is the one performance that surpasses its Livent predecessor. He becomes the pivotal figure in the piece, despite being the least obviously sympathetic - or perhaps because of it. Father is hearty (at least as Campbell plays him) and hidebound; he senses the changes around him, goes grudgingly along with some, resists more, has a final cry of outraged humanity, that echoes Coalhouses from an act earlier. Campbell portrays him in broad strokes that still accommodate complexity.

The obvious star role, though, is Coalhouse. Thom Allison has been a star in my book for years, illuminating his roles with a Satanic gleam that he moderates in the first half of this show to a charismatic glow, augmented by his ringing singing. Vocally, he's just as powerful in the second half, but not dramatically; he doesn't make a convincing brooder or fanatic. In a show that covers a lot of narrative ground very fast, the surprise love interest of Mother (Patty Jamieson) and Tateh (Jay Turvey) fails to fulfill its proper weight; Turvey is delightful in his transported shtetl rags, but he cant reconcile his later dual role of romantic lead and foppish pioneer of spectacle. Kate Hennigs watchful Emma Goldman is very fine, as are her lightning-sketched historical peers: Julie Martell's delectable Evelyn, Peter Millard's Ford and Kelly Wongs Houdini. One bit of the show, though, that never works are the Little Boys repeated admonitions to the Great Escaper to "warn the Duke." He means the Archduke Ferdinand, whose murder at Sarajevo will end the era of ragtime for ever. Houdini doesn't get the message and neither, until far too late to be of theatrical use, do we. All one can say is, it worked in the book, by which I don't mean it stopped the war. The musical Ragtime does so much, it's unfair to expect it to handle its era's demise as well. Did Doctorow ever consider writing a sequel called Jazz?
