PROVENANCE

Berkeley Street Theatre, Toronto

Ronnie Burkett has a bad habit of making the rest of theatre look unnecessary. Not only is he a virtuoso puppeteer, he is also a playwright, actor and director able to stand comparison with any in the country. All these talents are on abundant display in his new piece, Provenance, which is uneven but unmissable.

Burkett continues to do wonderful things in the string-pulling line; he can make his marionettes waltz, skate (ice or rollers) or - - it sometimes seems -- blush. There is a bewitching delicacy about these puppets, even in repose; they have been carved for character. He gets some of his best and tenderest effects, though, when he abandons the strings and simply cradles his creations in his hands. There is a third convention, one in which he assumes a character by wearing him, her or it as a headband, but this is too remote, physically and emotionally, to be effective. There is an overall sense that, as far as simple puppetry is concerned, Burkett has been everywhere, done all of that and is now after other things. Or maybe it is we who, taking his mastery for granted, are looking for the other things. Either way, Provenance challenges both creator and audience simply as drama.

It even attempts poetic drama. He begins by setting three of his characters on little chairs and having them deliver intercut monologues in verse. This is asking more of us than is reasonable. It's the start of the show, we haven't yet adjusted to the physical scale, and its hard to focus on the words when you arent sure of the bodies from which they are supposedly issuing. Only one member of the trio grabs us from the outset, though all three prove crucial to the subsequent action. At least I think they do; the reappearance of one of them is so long delayed that I could only identify him through a process of retrospective elimination. I had almost no memory of what he had said.

The figure who springs to life proves, fortunately, to be the heroine. She is Miss Pity Beane, raised in a small Canadian town by her gay father and his companion. The latter, whom she calls Uncle Boyfriend, seems to have been her formative influence. Pity is a creation to rank with Mrs. Edna Rural, the sheltered housewife of Burkett's Street of Blood. Both of them are superficially inhibited ladies who keep surprising us and themselves with their reserves of strength and daring. Pity has grown up self-consciously plain and worshipping beauty, which she identifies with a painting that she found reproduced in a school library book. It's in pre-Raphaelite mode, and depicts a young man (androgynous in aura, like many of Burketts inventions, but technically male), bound to a tree in a glade like Saint Sebastian awaiting the onset of the arrows.

Pitys fixation on this picture leads her to art-history college, where she gives a presentation on it, fiercely defending her ideal of beauty, and indeed the whole unfashionable notion of beauty itself, against the scorn of the academy. This speech (in prose) is as good and funny as theatre writing gets, an overflowing torrent of intellectual passion mixed in with a kind of desperately innocent scurrility. "I'm not, she screams at her professor, even interesting enough for you to dismiss as beautiful"; think of all the warring ideas that line gathers up. (The unseen professor's name, by the way, is Turcott, which I take to be a mischievous homage to Iris Turcott, dramaturge both for Burketts Theatre of Marionettes and for CanStage, which is presenting the show.)

Pity, aided by a travel grant from the late Uncle Boyfriend, sets out to track down the original painting and determine its provenance: its physical and intellectual history. She locates it in a brothel in Vienna, whose proprietress is a glamorous aged lady named Leda. Leda is another of the soliloquizers we met when we came in, a woman whose sexual experience is as vast as Pitys is limited. She is even more loquacious than Pity and a lot less entertaining, affecting a style of Euro-worldliness Burkett embraces with excessive enthusiasm and at excessive length.

The provenance of Provenance seems to be an examination of the whole mystique of bodily beauty. Pity's virginity qualifies her for a single night as a sex worker (one client, specialized tastes) from which she emerges physically unchanged but emotionally enriched. Encouraged by Leda and others, she learns that ordinary can also be beautiful, which is just as well since it's what most of us have to get by on. Its a cheering conclusion, and -- as Pity more or less said to the professor -- I couldnt care less if it's sentimental. But I don't quite follow the steps by which we have arrived at it, and I dont understand, except in the vaguest way, what Leda herself has to do with it.

So Professor Turcott, or his literary-dramatic equivalents, would probably judge the play a failure. And so might I, if it werent for all the great stuff that crops up on the way. There are engaging subordinate characters, like Leda's tolerant husband (I was less keen on the philosophical monkey to whom he seems closely related), and an old American customer at the whorehouse, enamoured for decades of a black lady who looks like a Josephine Baker figurine and has the jazz-dancing chops to justify it. Pity has another tremendous speech that begins as an uproarious demolition of pretentious high-school dramatics and modulates into a searing account of pain and humiliation. I know that people have accused Burkett of wallowing in or exploiting violence, but they're wrong. He is, of course, by the very terms of his trade, a manipulator, but he is not manipulative. Compare Provenance to the recent Scaramouche Jones; that dealt in second-hand atrocity, this gives the real, newly imagined thing. Pitys pitiful story is matched by a long speech for a terrified young soldier in the First World War. This returns us to verse, a pounding verse, doggerel in metre but not in content, and delivered by Burkett with relentless power, horrified and horrifying.

He is of course onstage throughout, and his energy and concentration are amazing, keeping the show going even as he lovingly deposits its dramatis personae in spacious closets at the side of the stage; puppets, you feel, never had it so good. Burkett also sings, though I feel that the showbizziness of the numbers he did in Street of Blood suit his voice better than the dour morsels of Continental cabaret he has dreamed up here. (You probably have to dress up to sing songs like these; probably in what I cant stop myself from calling liederhosen.) Since there is no separate design credit, he must also have painted the painting and created the dollhouse-against-a-landscape that constitutes the set.

I would guess that Burkett attracts more passionate enthusiasm than any other theatrical figure in English-speaking Canada. I would also guess that it's justified.
