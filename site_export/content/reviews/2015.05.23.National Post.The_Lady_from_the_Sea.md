When the Shaw Festival is good, it's very very good: see The Lady from the Sea (and, with a couple of caveats, Sweet Charity). When it's bad, see, or don't, Peter and the Starcatcher.

The good first. The Lady from the Sea is a fairly rare, fairly late Ibsen that reaches us here in a streamlined new adaptation by Erin Shields, one that gets through the original's five acts in a mere 90 minutes without seeming to have left anything out. Meg Roe's production features outstanding performances from its two leads, Moya O'Connell and Ric Reid. Theirs is the deepest, most connected acting - connected to one another and to the depths of their own characters - seen at the Shaw since the same Ric Reid played opposite Corinne Koslo in Come Back, Little Sheba, another play about an agonizingly troubled married couple.

The agony in this one comes from their terror that the love between them, which is genuine, may not be enough to keep them together. Ellida, the marine lady of the title, was a lighthouse keeper's daughter. (Send your limerick entries now.) She grew up in the far Norwegian north where, as her husband says of her, "the sea seeped into her bones." She plighted her troth to a sailor who duly sailed away and was presumed drowned. So she married another man, a widowed doctor, and came to live with him and his two grown daughters, in a more temperate part of the country, but one still affording ample opportunities for bathing. But then her old lover reappears, summoning her back, and she feels powerless to resist him.

On the face of it, her situation is a no-brainer, She has to choose between that nice Dr. Wangel, who adores her, and a ghostly figure whom the castlist refers to as The Stranger and who identifies himself as Freeman, which seems hardly less bullyingly symbolic. But the name and the memories call up in Ellida her own yearning for freedom, something whose importance her husband comes, painfully, to recognize. The play presents this struggle on two levels - mythical and psychological - and the production makes us accept them both. Camellia Koo's set is dominated by a large rock;

as the play starts we see O'Connell's Ellida clambering onto it after her morning dip, stretching out naked, the magical picture of a mermaid. She's naked for the rest of the play too: emotionally naked. She makes us feel all the passions, the regrets and the longings that are shaking her, taking every moment to the extreme without ever seeming forced or losing our sympathy. Reid performs comparable wonders as Wangel, desperately sensible in his quest to keep hold of her, until he realizes that common sense isn't enough. The play, you might say, is about the importance of uncommon sense.

There's a gap between the fluency and fluidity of these performances, and those of the rest of the cast, who are doing Character Acting, playing their lines rather than what comes between them. The most satisfying is Neil Barclay in a role that's essentially a monologue anyway: a parttime painter and full-time everything else, who speaks unknowingly about the necessity of "acclimatizing" oneself (Ellida's problem of course) and who likes to paint mermaids. The most elaborate is Kyle Blair as a younger, full-time artist whose career, unknown to himself, is about to be cut short by tuberculosis; in the meantime he gives himself all kinds of self-important airs, in a manner so thoughtlessly egocentric as to be disarming. The woman whom he casts as his Inspiration is Ellida's younger stepdaughter Hilde, who will grow up to be the horrific heroine of The Master Builder and is well on the way now. Her rude behaviour is said to be the result of her unrequited adoration of Ellida, but Darcy Gerhart gives no sign of this; though, to be fair, the play doesn't give her much opportunity. Her elder sister Bolette (Jacqueline Thair), longing to leave home, completes the pattern of couples by accepting the advances of her former tutor (Andrew Bunker): a May-October alliance to which the production, in its closing moments, appends a stimulating question-mark, but to which neither performer brings much definition earlier on. Mark Uhre's Stranger is deep-voiced and bodeful, which is all that's needed.

Also at sea, in more ways than one, is Peter and the Starcatcher, which offers a back-story to Peter Pan. It's an American piece adapted by Rick Elice (Jersey Boys) from a novel by Dave Barry and Ridley Pearson, which I can only hope is less relentlessly and mirthlessly jokey than the stage version. Peter here is an initially anonymous Victorian orphan whose heroic qualities emerge after the ship that's transporting him is captured by pirates and whose new name is given him by semisupernatural figures somewhere in (I think) the Caribbean. The evening begins with some extended group narration (a program-note blasphemously invokes the RSC's great Nicholas Nickleby); when it finally ventures into drama, it proves incapable of taking itself seriously, though it seems to want us to. To summarize its tone and method: every reference to Queen Victoria (and there are many) is followed by a cry of "God save her;"

Act Two features a band of semicannibals whose vocabulary derives entirely from Italian cuisine; and what passes for humour depends entirely on stepping cutely out of context. The only ingenuity comes in the way the story finally dovetails with J. M. Barrie's original; the only emotion piggybacks shamelessly on that same source. In these respects it's like Wicked, but probably worse. Charlie Gallant makes a good impression as Peter. Kate Besworth is the Wendyish young lady who rescues and inspires him; it's a repeat of her performance in Arcadia, which I've certainly no objection to seeing again. The boy discovers leadership but the girl is always ahead of him; we get it already. So did Barrie. Martin Happer is the future Captain Hook, prone to accidents and anachronisms; he gives it a good shot but Ross Petty would do it more natural. A large cast pitches enthusiastically in; Jackie Maxwell's production relies heavily on standardized methods of suggesting waves and storms, and on the supposed outrageousness of Shaw Festival actors dressing up like mermaids (them again) for a musical number. The Broadway reviews made the play sound interesting: fooled again. A plague on all prequels, say I. robert.cushman@hotmail.com

The Lady from the Sea is in repertory through Sept. 13 at the Court House Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont.; Peter and the Starcatcher through Nov. 1 at the Royal George Theatre.
