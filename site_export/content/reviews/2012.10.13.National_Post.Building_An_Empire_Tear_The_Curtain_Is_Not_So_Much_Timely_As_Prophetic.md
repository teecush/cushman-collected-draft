Mixed media, multimedia, whatever, usually comes off as pointless and self-indulgent, an excuse for theatre directors to play at being movie directors without giving up the day job. Tear the Curtain!, which has come from the west coast to open the new season at Canadian Stage, is a different world. Its a seamless blending of stage and film, in the ironic but somehow fitting service of a story in which the two media are literally at war. The script isn't as good as the presentation but it's good enough, and the total effect, under Kim Colliers direction, is stunning.

Its a co-production of two Vancouver troupes - the Electric Company and the Arts Club Theatre - and it was first staged at the Stanley Theatre, a live house that was once a cinema. (This may sound like a reversal of the natural order, but it happens.) The Stanley opened in 1930 with a screening of Lillian Gish's first talkie The Swan. This factors into Tear the Curtain! itself, which is set in that same year and whose protagonist, name of Alex Braithwaite, is shown in the first scene arriving at another theatre for a performance of the play (by Ferenc Molnar) on which the movie is based, and being dangerously smitten by its leading actress. Our hero, in what may be a dramaturgical first, is a theatre critic, whom Jonathon Young (actor, co-author and co-director) endows with the intellect, charm, devotion to duty and suave good looks generally known to be characteristic of the trade. He has, naturally, a devoted and adoring secretary, Mavis, whom we see driving him to the theatre, dropping him off alone, and then going her winsome uncomplaining way. I should add that this whole episode is shown on film: film projected on to a theatre-sized screen, none of your video monitor nonsense. Not till Alex enters the imaginary theatre do we switch to live action in the real one, and the switch is so smooth that we hardly feel the join. We shuttle between the two modes for the rest of the evening, in a dazzling but strangely easeful display not just of synchronicity but of style. Collier's direction is visually flawless in both formats. Her design team comprises David Roberts (a versatile and hauntingly hued split-level set), Nancy Bryant (costumes), Alan Brodie (lighting), and Brian Johnson (cinematography), plus Peter Allen (music), Miguel Nunes (sound) and Alex Craig (editing). Their work is uniformly brilliant. The Bluma Appel, for which the show wasnt even conceived, has never looked better.

Theres also a story, quite a complicated one. In this 1930 Vancouver, which bears a strong resemblance to 1920s Chicago as depicted in things such as The Front Page, both the old theatrical empire and the budding cinematic one are controlled by gangsters, who are waging a turf war over a promising piece of real estate. Changes in culture are affecting the newspaper business too; Alex is appalled to learn that his free-standing theatre coverage is to be absorbed into an all-encompassing entertainment section. (Well, those sure were the days.) He's also absorbed in a different way by the history of the Stanley Theatre itself. In this telling there really was a Stanley, a visionary theatre director now dead. Except that someone claiming to be him, a derelict who lives in a tent in (naturally) Stanley Park, waylays Alex at his apartment, paints a portrait of him that might have been done by a drunken Modigliani, and charges him to spread the Stanley gospel. This, like most theatrical gospels, involves shedding all pretense and presenting Truth. Stanley's own group was called the Empty Space, a premature homage, perhaps, to Peter Brook (though I dont think Brook, a showman at heart, was ever that naive). Anyway Alex follows through, with a big think-piece called Tear the Curtain, which takes him weeks plus a lot of morale-building from Mavis, and which sets the town alight. All the factions want a piece of him, alive or dead, including a cadre of neo-or post-Stanleyites, who call themselves Liberty. They have seen the future and it works. They even show it to us, in a hilariously downbeat coup-de-theatre. Though that might not be the right term as what they present is, in the long run, anti-theatre.

It has to be confessed that if the medium here is the message, the former is far more crystalline than the latter. Theres a famous story about Raymond Chandler being quizzed about one of the plot twists in The Big Sleep (which he wrote) and confessing that he couldn't explain it either. Tear the Curtain! is comparably confounding as a narrative, even without the metaphors and metaphysics piled on top. Not to mention anachronisms; the film-noir style that Collier and her authors, Kevin Kerr and Mr. Versatility Young, have principally chosen to lampoon, is about a decade younger than the period they are ostensibly depicting. But they do it very well, and they have one continuing thread of suspense that sees us and themselves through. As Mavis keeps leaning physically and emotionally closer to Alex we ask ourselves, is the lucky idiot finally going to kiss her? I'm not kidding; it works, with great emotional effect. This is largely due to Dawn Pettens enchanting Girl Friday performance, tender-hearted and steel-willed, beautifully consistent (and consistently beautiful) from stage to screen and back again.

Laura Mennell also does well as the all-purpose siren, blond-tressed onstage, brunette-bobbed off it, who seems to be in with every faction in town. The rival moguls are Gerard Plunkett, promoting theatre but more convincing on screen than in the flesh, and Tom McBeath, the reverse. With the repurposing of theatres so much in the news, this play, which premiered in 2010, seems not so much timely as prophetic. As for its final import, I would say that it ends up championing not just illusion but escape, and blowing us away with it. As the man (not Stanley) incontrovertibly said, thats show business. And inescapable
