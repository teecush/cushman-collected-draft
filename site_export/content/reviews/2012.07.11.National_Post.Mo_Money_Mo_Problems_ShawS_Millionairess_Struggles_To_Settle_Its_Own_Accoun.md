THEATRE REVIEW

THE MILLIONAIRESS

Court House Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

Nicole Underhay, playing the title role in George Bernard Shaws The Millionairess, is miscast. To be fair, it's hard to think of an actress who wouldn't be. In this 1934 comedy, Shaw tried to create a heroine who was both monster and charmer, and he made the first ingredient far more convincing than the second.

Epifania Fitzfassenden - her married name, of which shes trying to rid herself when we first meet her - was born with money and with the talent for making more of it. She believes these attainments make her superior to everybody else, and gets furious when this opinion is challenged. She might say, like Popeyes Olive Oyl, that strong men are her weakness, though she can sometimes be mistaken about which they are. Unlike Olive, she's as selfish in love as in everything else.

All in all, she suggests Ayn Rand with a sense of humour; or rather - since she repeatedly insists that she doesn't have one - Rand envisioned by an author who did.

Epifanias maiden name is Epifania Ognisanti di Parerga, and its a pleasure to hear Underhay roll all those syllables around on her tongue. It's also possible to enjoy her whiplash timing, always useful in a comedy even when your character is not supposed to get any of the jokes, and to admire her looks and her clothes sense. Cameron Porteous, this production's designer, has endowed her with a preternatural capacity for dressing perfectly for every room she enters.

The plays first scene is done up in red, its second in blue, its third in green, its fourth and last in gold; and Epifania has an outfit for all of them. The colour coding, plus some ingeniously versatile furniture and a strong supporting cast, contribute to an overall stylishness in Blair Williams production. But the fact remains that the leading actress is temperamentally (and maybe physically) too small for her role, and is overcompensating.

Required to play a force of nature, she forces. That means a vocal delivery that substitutes volume for tone, and a physical demeanour that involves much twirling of her fur stole, and much hurling of herself around the stage. She hurls other people as well, and that's far more satisfying. Indeed, Underhay comes through when she's given something specific to do, whether its falling in love or taking care of someone elses business. Or rather taking it over, which is what Epifania really is good at, and where the play becomes interesting.

It takes a goodly time to do so. Shaw's powers were far from exhausted when he wrote it, but they were flagging, and the first scene and a half go round in circles. We start in the office of Epifania's solicitor, which as aforesaid is all red, right down to the incumbents desk and - I kid you not - his ukulele; talking or strumming, Kevin Bundy plays him with suavity and charm.

Epi wants to make her will before committing suicide, for motives that seem totally arbitrary and are soon abandoned for reasons equally unclear. They are joined by her estranged husband, a boxer huffed and puffed by Martin Happer, and his new squeeze who, as played by Robin Evans Willis, makes ordinariness seem very appealing.

In the next scene Epifanias current swain, a fashionable loafer rejoicing in the name of Adrian Blenderbland, says something tactless about her late dad, for which she breaks his bones. Later on, he will claim damages for his damages, and gets hysterical when informed that she will beat him in the courts because she's both rich and a woman. Steven Sutcliffe doesn't exactly underplay Adrians agonies, but he does them very well.

Meanwhile, Epi has met an Egyptian doctor who sees right through her but is enchanted by her pulse rate. Kevin Hanchard plays him with appropriately smooth serenity. It turns out that her father and his mother laid down similar conditions for their offsprings marriages: that the partner of each should demonstrate an ability to take a small amount of money and make it multiply. He fails, through being too charitable; she of course succeeds.

She goes to a sweat shop, and blackmails the owners into employing her. Here it's worth remembering that the play was written in the '30s, and this scene was set in Londons East End. Williams production tries to expunge period references, and he has the actors, in a possible Shaw Festival first, speaking in their own Canadian accents, but the specific traces of time and place are ineradicable.

The underlying issues, of course, are still painfully pertinent, and Michael Ball and Wendy Thatcher give searingly real performances as the exploited exploiters. Epifania does them good and she does them harm: a point elaborated in a later speech by Mark Uhre as a hotel manager whose rundown family business she has also appropriated.

He details the vast improvements she has made, and the human wreckage she has left in their wake. He's elegantly non-committal about it. But the matters are not pursued because Epifania makes another speech, about marriage. It's a fine one, but it has almost nothing to do with the rest of the play.

If its suggesting that Epifanias cruelty can somehow be redeemed by her having found the right man, then it's delusional. Shaw was far more plain-spoken in his preface than he is in his play: A society which depends on the incentive of private profit is doomed."
