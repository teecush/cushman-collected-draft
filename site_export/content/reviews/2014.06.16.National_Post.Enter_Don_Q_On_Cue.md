Theatre Review

Man of La Mancha Avon Theatre, Stratford

'Facts" says Tom Rooney as Don Quixote "are the enemy of truth". I've managed to avoid Man of La Mancha for the last 40 years or so, but my memory of the original production is that when the actor came to that line he drew himself up to his full height and declaimed it, as if daring anyone to contradict him. Rooney says it quietly and modestly, as befits an actor of taste and intelligence lumbered with a remark of outstanding stupidity. And don't go thinking that the show is here gently satirizing the don's crazed delusions; Dale Wasserman, the librettist, once went on record as saying that the line represented not just his hero's opinion but his own. The news must have gladdened the hearts of Holocaust deniers everywhere.

Cervantes' Don Quixote is of course a satire, aimed initially at an early 17th-century vogue for romantic stories about knights errant. Its hero is a Spanish country gentleman whose head has been turned by reading too many of these books and who sets out on his own quest, famously mistaking windmills for hostile giants, innkeepers for friendly lords, all in the chaste and devoted service of a country girl whom he had once glimpsed from afar and whom he never actually meets. He is constantly humiliated but can always find ways of explaining away his defeats.

It's a long and many-layered book, and it does far more with its protagonist than laugh at him. It can certainly have us wondering whether his folly may not be preferable to all the worldly wisdom, cynicism and exploitation he meets on the road. He develops into a moving as well as a comic figure. The musical, though, is too soft-hearted, not to mention soft-headed, for such complexities; in fact it emphatically spurns them. The book ends with the hero on his deathbed repenting all his follies; in the musical he gets up and sings a final idealistic chorus.

The principal action is presented as a story told by Cervantes himself, a playwright (as he historically was), imprisoned by the Inquisition (as he also historically was), and putting on a show in order to keep his cellmates from roughing him up before his interrogators get the chance. It works: They don't much like the darker parts of the tale, but they go all mushy at the close; as an audience, they're clearly ahead of their time.

A program note suggests that the musical (vintage 1966) is a throwback to the idealistic Rodgers and Hammerstein shows of the 1950s. Not really, unless you're thinking of R&H at their Sound of Music sappiest: "Climb Ev'ry Mountain" and you reach "The Impossible Dream." In its mating of flower-power with Broadway sententiousness, Man of La Mancha is very '60s. But it also looks forward: to the pop operas of the next decades. It isn't through-sung but otherwise it has all the marks: the melodramatic dumbing-down of a literary classic, the complete lack of humour especially when trying to be funny, and the earnest clumsiness of Joe Darion's lyrics.

Actually "The Impossible Dream" isn't as unbearable in its theatrical context as it is outside it, at least not until it starts getting shamelessly reprised. Rooney sings it superbly, quietly at first, steadily building to an impeccably held big finish. We knew that he could sing, but not that he could do it this well. He's pitch-perfect, tonally and emotionally, as he also is in his acting. As Cervantes he's excellent; as Don Quixote he's sublime: gaunt and courteous and pitiful and inspiring. In fact he's the Quixote of the book, reinstated in the teeth of the adaptation; he's the only reason for seeing the show, but he's a wonderful one.

As his squire Sancho Panza, who should be one half of the greatest double act in fiction, Steve Ross is criminally wasted, both in terms of his own talents and of his character's potential. If his role is diminished in the musical, that of Aldonza, the maiden of the hero's dreams, is greatly expanded, her character reconceived as a tavern slut; "I am only Aldonza the whore" she tells us in song, the last word coming on a long high note calculated to impress us with the authors' outspokenness, and also to capitalize on the assets of the role's creator Joan Diener, a performer who boasted many octaves and wasn't shy about showing them off. Robin Hutton has more tact as an actress but less range as a singer; besides her role, alternating between cut-rate Carmen and sacrificial victim, represents the show at its absolute worst. Mc-Queen's production (choreographed by Marc Kimelman) goes shamelessly along with it; otherwise his direction is smooth enough, if hardly on a level with the far more distinguished shows he has directed in Toronto. That's a fact. It's also the truth.
