David Mamet is famous, or notorious, for writing plays with either no roles for women or bad ones. The second part of the charge isn't altogether fair, but in 1999 he seems to have taken it seriously enough to have written a play with an all-female cast. The play, Boston Marriage, was a double departure for him, since it abandoned the edgy contemporary settings and dialogue of his other work, and settled instead in the genteel late 19thcentury world of Henry James and Edith Wharton.

Boston Marriage shows us two women trying desperately to keep their fragile footing in that world and a third without a hope of getting into it. Anna and Claire are somewhat estranged lesbian lovers. Anna has a rich male protector who has given her a diamond necklace and the means to maintain a fashionable house; against her own aesthetic judgment, she has re-decorated the place in chintz, thinking that Claire would like it. Claire has become attracted to a much younger woman and asks Anna's permission to use the house as a place of assignation. Anna is understandably not keen but, after a lot of fencing, agrees: on conditions. Meanwhile she takes her bad temper out on her maid, who is Scottish but whom Anna keeps insisting is Irish, with many a merry reference to the Great Potato Famine. Claire, when her own temper is frayed, which is often, is happy to join in the taunting.

In this respect the play is a female version of one of Mamet's best allmale pieces, American Buffalo: we have two comparatively advantaged, older characters and a third apparently helpless younger one whom they alternately patronize and victimize, though here the pattern's broken when, for plot reasons both complicated and implausible, they find they need her.

American Buffalo, of course, is written in Mamet's best manner: street speech as profane poetry. Boston Marriage uses ersatz Oscar Wilde spiked with intentionally jarring anachronisms. A line like "this fellow fancies you dead rotten" is followed a moment later by an entreaty to "only conceive, I pray you." He hasn't exactly lost his touch; lines that are intended to be funny quite frequently are, but the wit still feels strained. What he does demonstrate, devastatingly well, is a knack for running up great, non-chintzy curtain-lines. He remains committed to a two-act structure, and his Act One ends with a smashing instant reversal, a cliff from which the subsequent action can hang. Act Two doesn't fulfil that promise - in fact much of it feels like padding - but it too has a great ending, incorporating a splendidly suggestive pun and a slow-burn revelation that neatly and ironically ties up what has gone before.

Kelli Fox, for Headstrong Collective, directs a site-specific production, in a drawing-room at Campbell House. Scenically, it takes us right into the characters' world. Verbally, it halts on the threshold. The words drip, or should, with poise and poison; as an actress Fox, with her Shaw Festival background, could probably handle them perfectly. But there's little sense of phrasing or timing from Catherine McNally (Anna) or Deborah Drakeford (Claire); they mostly blunderbuss their way through their lines. They're better in their moments of vulnerability, especially Drakeford, who has more of them. As Catherine the maid Charlotte Dennis is delightful, dignified even when humiliated; she spends a lot of time standing patiently around, waiting to deliver messages that her betters, to their own detriment, refuse to hear. She may be the most positive character Mamet has ever created. Otherwise he depicts women here as manipulative or pitiable or both, which is just how he has generally depicted men.

Ubu and the Truth Commission, part of CanadianStage's Spotlight on South Africa festival, mixes together the puppeteering skills of the Handspring Company (who wrought the magic of War Horse), the historical subject of the post-apartheid judicial process, and the borrowed protagonist of Alfred Jarry's Ubu Roi, which was the original absurdist play and, like many of its successors, far more fun to read about than to watch. Jarry's Pere Ubu was the prototypical bourgeois, a greedy tyrant in his own home, a nonentity outside it. In this version he has a past as a secret policeman; Mere Ubu suspects of him sneaking out at nights to see other women, and is mightily relieved to find that he's only been killing and torturing people. Pa and Ma are given lifesize performances by David Minnaar, who's spectacular, and Busi Zokufa; other roles are played by three Handspring puppeteers, sometimes through their puppets, sometimes not.

The ingredients don't all add up. Mere's jealousy, in particular, seems like a gigantic red herring; and Pere's twists and turns, as his past threatens to catch up with him, are not always easy to follow. The actual trial testimonies, mainly from parents called to identify their butchered children, remain devastating, the more so for being understated and anonymous. Jane Taylor wrote the script; the director is William Kentridge, who's also credited with "animation"; this probably means the grim cartoon projections are a major part of the production, and in fact more visually striking than the puppetry which, however expert, is, by War Horse standards, merely serviceable. But then the show isn't trying to be War Horse. I think it may have bitten off more than it can reasonably chew. I have always thought South Africa's Truth and Reconciliation Commission to be one of civilization's more encouraging moments. This production, in which Ubu gets away with it, is a lot more skeptical.
