Anton Piatigorsky's Eternal Hydra may not actually be immortal but it's certainly proving to be durable. It was first seen as a one-act play in Stratford in 2002; it resurfaced in 2009, much enriched and expanded, in a production by Crow's Theatre at Buddies in Bad Times; and that revival is now itself revived at the Factory. The Hydra of Greek mythology had heads, some say a hundred of them; the play inspired by it has legs, legs that grow in style and strength with each excursion.

The play, actual, is named after a book, imaginary. Piatigorsky's fictitious novelist Gordias Carbuncle was, like the James Joyce of whom he cannot help but remind us, an Irishman who lived in Paris; he was also Jewish, like Joyce's most famous creation, Leopold Bloom, an Irishman who stayed in Dublin. ( "Why," asks Gordias, rhetorically and hilariously, "do you think I left?") His magnum opus was Eternal Hydra, a novel of a hundred chapters, each written in a different narrative voice. When he died, though, he had only completed 99 of them, which might be considered fortunate, since the mythical Hydra needed to keep just one of its heads unsevered in order to survive. The manuscript, long thought lost, was at length retrieved by Vivian Ezra, an English-born American-based academic, whose special subject is Carbuncle; indeed she's so obsessed with him that she believes him to have taken up residence not just in her head but in her apartment. They talk together and pay calls together.

One of their destinations is the New York office of Gordias' old publisher, whose son now runs the business and is more skeptical of the rediscovered masterwork than his dad might have been. ( "I know it's a masterpiece," says junior, "but is it any good?") He makes a deal with Vivian; he'll publish Eternal Hydra but only in tandem with a novel by one of his protegees, a young woman named Pauline Newberry, that's set in interwar literary Paris with Carbuncle as one of the major characters. Vivian protests that Pauline's portrait is a travesty, but she has no choice but to go along; Gordias, or his ghost, is most insistent that she should. Through the pages of Carbuncle's rather discreditable diary, also in Vivian's possession, we travel back to his expatriate life. We see him deflecting his publisher's erotic, though not his financial, advances; we see him in turn making advances to women whom he wants to use physically and/ or imaginatively. And, finally, we hear a chapter of his book. It's the story of a young black woman in post-Civil War Louisiana, a gifted shoemaker but illiterate, taught to read by her white employer, caught up in the struggle for black (male) suffrage and in the riots that accompanied it. She lives to hear the state's new progressive governor make a speech in which she can hear her own voice. He may have appropriated it. But then, by making him and other whites characters in her narrative, she may have appropriated them. And Carbuncle, who has put their story in his own book with who knows what changes, has appropriated the lot of them. It's not a question of this being good or bad. It's that in literature, as in any other form -- and most especially in drama--it's inevitable.

I confess to having previously underestimated the grace, fun and emotional power of this play. "Appropriation of voice" may sound like a dry dramatic subject, but its deployment here is dynamic, ever-changing: Hydra-headed in fact. It's also witty and light-handed, with a cunning use of recurring verbal motifs -- that of Hercules, for example, whose second labour, in training for the really hard stuff, was to decapitate the monster for good and all. Some of my new-found enlightenment may be attributed to the maturing of Chris Abraham's production; good before, it's superb now. Physically, it sits far better on the Factory stage than it did at Buddies. John Thompson's set, a starkly elegant arrangement of chairs and table on a sloping floor, seemed oppressive before; now, with more height to play with and imaginatively lit by its designer, it's liberating. Each of the actors plays two or more roles, which may be Hydratic in itself. The major theme is sounded from the start in Liisa Repo-Martell's magnificent Vivien, the very archetype of the star-struck academic, whose voice and mannerisms, whether or not the actress knows it, bear a close resemblance to those of a current and prominent English biographer and critic. The alternation of personal diffidence and professional possessiveness is perfectly hit off. She has one particularly fine speech, delineating the limits of the scholar's world, that combines both qualities. Sam Malkin repeats his genial triple of father, son and southern gentleman, and David Ferry is again an irresistibly grizzled and sozzled Carbuncle. Cara Ricketts, the one newcomer, starts with a magnetically cool outline of the rising star Pauline and finishes by digging deep into the Louisiana story, a solo turn that's all the more moving for being so stoic, all the more vibrant for being so restrained. It's still a shock to find that her story ends the play, without returning us to the people we came in with; but a) it would be an impossible act to follow, and b) incompleteness is built into the subject. Unless you're Hercules, the Hydra's heads are innumerable. Two years ago, Eternal Hydra won the Dora for best play, independent theatre division. This probably disqualifies it from winning this year in the general theatre category to which it's been promoted. That could be fortunate for the competition, which so far this season has been non-existent.
