The wedding in Blood Wedding is cursed before it happens, and the play makes no secret of it. The bride still has passionate feelings for an old flame, and he for her despite his having married somebody else. Even before all this, the families of the groom and his hovering rival were engaged in a bloodfeud, and the grooms mother cannot let go of the fact. Even as she strives to be an accepting mother-in-law, her thoughts and her words keep reverting to the knives that took the lives of most of her male relatives.

It all sounds very Latin, and Blood Wedding is indeed the work - maybe the best-known work though the all-female House of Bernarda Alba seems to get done more often - of the Spanish poet and playwright Federico Garcia Lorca, killed by Francos men in 1936, three years after this play was written.

Soulpepper's revival, though, re-locates it in Ontario farm-country. At least, I think it does; the publicity promised it, though the program-notes go on, rightly, about how Spanish the play is. There are no specific Canadian references in Guillermo Verdecchia's script, but the costumes have a local cut and so does the onstage band that comments on the action and sometimes seems to be substituting for it. At the wedding party itself the brides father (Oliver Dennis), hitherto a rather dour individual, surprises us all by getting up on the stand and exhorting everyone in song to Raise a Ruckus. You cant get more down-home than that.

There's something patronizing, to both parties, in the idea that audiences can only respond to a play if it's set in their own backyard. Still, the change of locale is a symptom, rather than the cause, of the failings in Erin Brandenburgs production. In fact, Verdecchias translation, stark in syntax but flowery in image, would probably work perfectly well if the play was still set in Spain. But there seems to be a conspiracy, probably unconscious, to lower the temperature. Diane D'Aquila's vengeful mother has the most commanding performance, the vengeance festering in her heart and on her lips in a society that gives her no scope for action; but she keeps having to switch tones, from Spanish fury to Canadian gossip.

Few of the others even try to reach her level. The prenuptial chat between the bride (Hailey Gillis) and her maid (Caroline Gillis) is just giggly. Theres no heat, indeed no connection, between the young lady and Leonardo (Colin Palangio), the lover who abducts her: they just stand around, speaking lines. Leonardo is the only character to whom Lorca gave a name, though here its been shortened to Leon, which doesn't have quite the same ring: indeed no ring at all. That's important, because the name is meant to reverberate, to send shivers through the community. Gordon Hecht starts off well as the groom, stolid and shy (his opening scene with DAquila is excellent, raising hopes for the production that are to remain unfulfilled) but when he has to turn avenger, he too seems merely to be going through the motions. When the text turns unavoidably formal, as when the wedding guests line up to offer lyrical salutations, the actors scamper dutifully through the lines, apparently hoping to be rid of them as quickly as possible.

To do Blood Wedding at all is brave; to do it in Toronto at this moment may be foolhardy. A production courts comparison with the admirable one mounted last year by the Modern Times and Aluna companies. That production, without going aggressively flamenco on us, had a style and a tone, the sense of a world different from ours but relatable. It had what this staging lacks: inevitability. The story had strength and purpose; here it seems thin and rather pointless. The fatal meeting of husband and lover is lost in the forest, literally; you cant see the actors for the trees.

In Soheil Parsa's production the hoofbeats of Leonardo's horse, a constant presence in the communitys consciousness, were a resonant recurring aural presence; in this one theyre just an isolated sound effect. The force and grace of Parsa's staging took us effortlessly through the play's trickiest transition: the appearance of two symbolic figures, the Moon and Death, to preside over the final mayhem.

In Soulpeppers production Andrew Penner, the lead musician, speaks the Moons lines; he does them decently but I doubt if anyone unfamiliar with the play would know who or what he was meant to be. Death becomes a cackling beggar-woman looking and sounding like one of those pantomime crones who, when someone is nice to them, turn out to be fairy godmothers, though this one definitely doesn't. D'Aquila plays this role too, before reverting in the last scene to bereaved mother, now more bereaved than ever. The production seems to intend her as the central figure, and there's a good case for that; the play begins and ends with her, and she embodies all the traditions of her world, good and bad. But here there's no world.
