MR. RAISIN HEAD AND KID

Theatre Passe Muraille Backspace, Toronto

According to her biography, "Erika Batdorf has created, performed, directed and choreographed original movement theatre since 1983." I am not sure what movement theatre is, but on the strength of this double bill (which I saw at a preview), I can at least offer the reassuring news that it isn't mime.

It starts out as if it might be. The first play, Mr. Raisin Head, finds our creator-performer (she has a consultant director, Paul Lampert) in male attire, preening in front of a mirror while flicking her tongue contemplatively between her lips, suggesting the character she portrays is simultaneously vain and insecure. All this is tidily done, and it soon turns out that Batdorf talks as well. In fact, once she starts, she hardly stops.

And, in the course of delivering her script -- in two voices -- she unquestionably moves around, and in a disciplined way, but no more than you would expect of most actors in most plays.

The two voices belong to the guy at the mirror and the upscale lady he eventually marries. He sounds like he comes from the Bronx; she sounds strangulated-English. He has been trying to attract the attention of various people he calls Virtues (Truth, Purity and others), but they just look right through him, as if he were Mr. Cellophane in Chicago.

Feeling his inferiority and trying to better himself, he goes searching for culture, and at a museum he meets his future bride, who seems to regard him with amused contempt. During one of his sessions at the mirror, he decides he resembles a raisin and christens himself Mr. Raisin Head. Clinging unshakably to his metaphor (verbal wit is not the authors strong point), he decides his fiancee is a Grape, and of a superior vintage at that.

Still, they get together. The fact she insists on referring to art exhibits as "installations" only seems to make her more attractive in his eyes, and she presumably likes having someone she can impress.

Batdorf tells us in the program that I dislike numbness -- it is a mould I have to continually fight. Whether she is fighting it in herself or in others, she doesnt make clear. (She's being hearteningly modest if it's the first; presumptuous if its the second.) The piece has been promoted as "a monologue about self- worth," but its difficult for an audience to value a play's characters if the play itself hasn't done so first. The Grape lady in particular is a patronizing figure, patronized.

The second play, Kid, is much better -- more piercingly performed, more compassionately written and telling a more compelling story. Batdorf comes before us this time in what looks at first blush like a straitjacket with the ties undone. She announces herself as one of a pair of sisters, about to tell us a story about one of another pair of sisters. The latter couple are called Pinky and Stinky, and she is Stinky; Pinky never arrives.

Stinky is four and she lives in a garden under the dominion of a Mr. Mean Man, who persecutes her and her three friends: a Mr. Frog Man, a Mr. Mole Man and a Bird Lady. What we seem to have here is a tale of sexual abuse recounted -- to make the trauma bearable -- at two removes. It works as a fairy tale, with the horrors as relentless and as saddening as only a childrens narrative can be, and it reverberates as the author wishes.

When one of Stinkys companions looks at her and declares that she is "a grown-up person," something clicks; this is an adult who has been dragging around a child's memories, and a child's humiliation, all her life. (The absent non-smelly sister is presumably her everyday grown-up self.)

Batdorf has this time to animate five characters -- verbally, visually or both, plus drawing some of them on a blackboard -- and she does it admirably. Sometimes she seems to surrender too enthusiastically to the whimsy of her chosen medium, but that may be necessary for the sake of stylistic consistency, and anyway she always extricates herself.
