Its been 40 years since Monty Python's Flying Circus made its debut on BBC Television, and the anniversary's being marked by a celebratory chronicle entitled Monty Python:Almost the Truth (The Lawyers Cut), the story of six men told in six parts. The first episode covers Python pre-history. The next two are devoted to the four seasons of the TV show itself. The remaining three deal, one apiece, with the movies: Holy Grail, Life of Brian, Meaning of Life. The last one also finds space for the groups subsequent activities, though it restricts itself to those with Python relevance; Spamalot is in, Fawlty Towers is out. In other words -- words to which the Pythons themselves would probably offer cheerful assent -- it shows them cashing in on their legend.

In the show proper, the five surviving, talking-to-camera Pythons -- John Cleese, Terry Gilliam, Eric Idle, Terry Jones, Michael Palin -- go in for much whimsical dissing of themselves and one another, most of it more tolerant than barbed; Cleese, very elder-statesmanlike, says that they were never especially close as people anyway. It sounds as if, whatever the admitted disagreements and resentments, they've mellowed. They've certainly had time. Its been 26 years since the last full Python collaboration, Monty Pythons The Meaning of Life. In all that time the TV shows, the films, the songs, the catchphrases have never gone away. The best Flying Circus acts are sheer joy, the most inventive comedy ever seen on TV.

They came from rich soil. Python was the creation of young men in their twenties, five of whom grew up in postwar Britain and went to Oxford or Cambridge -- which makes Gilliam, the lone American, seem rather out-of-place in the opening reminiscences. The others lived through, and were heavily influenced by, the great age of British radio comedy, culminating in the inspired surrealism of The Goon Show. The more explicit kind broke out in the early '60s, initially in the theatre with Beyond the Fringe, from the preceding Oxbridge generation. For the first time, jokes -- heavenly, devastating ones -- were being made about actual politicians, and about the whole of British society, on a public stage.

The Python battened on the satire boom, but also reacted against it. The team came together through what seems to have been a typical BBC fit of creative absent-mindedness, and indeed the whole Python story, from influences to accomplishments, is one of the glories of public broadcasting. Outrageously literate, the Pythons fit into an English tradition of structured anarchy that stretches back from the Goons to Lewis Carroll, reflected too in Gilliam's savage animations, whose habit of trampling a sketch literally underfoot contributed materially to Pythons greatest formal innovation, the abolition of the punchline. Stage revue, what remains of it, has never found a way of doing this and suffers accordingly. Above all, the Pythons upheld the principle that sustains nearly all comedy: that authority is inherently ridiculous.

They knew about authority since, as sons of the lower middle-class, they had all grown up just outside it; Cleese, in particular, looks like an Establishment candidate gone off the rails. So does Graham Chapman, whose early death from cancer labels him the ex-Python and who is represented here in clips from earlier interviews. Inevitably the show becomes in large part a tribute to him. The others all concur that he was the best actor, a natural leading man, and the clips from The Holy Grail and Life of Brian prove them right.

Later generations of British comics pay tribute to the Python influence, and agree on the absence of anything comparable since; among this bunch Steve Coogan seems especially smart and Russell Brand like something that might have been invented by Sacha Baron Cohen. As with The Beatles, transatlantic reactions are even more effusive than the homegrown ones, perhaps because the Americans could regard them as exotic, visitors from a comedic outer space. The information is this documentary is fascinating, and nicely balanced between the personal and the technical.

I would have liked to know how Cleese, whose low boredom threshold led him to bow out of the final TV series (which suffered for it) was lured back for the films. Perhaps theres a clue in his statement that telling a story in 90 minutes is the most exciting thing in show business," and his consequent criticism of The Meaning of Life as a reversion to the sketch-comedy they'd outgrown. (Of course he went on to create Fawlty Towers, the greatest of sitcoms as Flying Circus is the greatest of sketch shows.) Even Jones, who directed Meaning of Life, admits that there was "no consensus about what the movie was going to be" and it shows. But you'll end up wanting to watch the whole Python oeuvre over again, dodgy bits included. - Monty Python: Almost the Truth (The Lawyer's Cut) premieres Saturday on Bravo! at 9 p.m.
