Sky Gilbert's new play Reconciliation is the latest variation on the old theme of the town mouse and the country mouse. This time the mice are siblings. Some details are vague, but Blake lives in rural Ontario, and it's in his farmhouse that the play is set. His brother Jared is visiting from the city; I don't recall Toronto being mentioned by name, but it must be the place, since Jared wants to use their paternal inheritance to invest in a condo in the Trump Tower, something with which Blake is decidedly not cool.

They have other points of disagreement. Blake operates a steam-shovel at a gravel pit; he defends this as being constructive work, on the grounds that the dirt on the end of his shovel is in a different place at the end of his shift than it was at the beginning, but Jared is not impressed -- scornful in fact. But Blake is equally contemptuous of Jared's job, which is teaching language theory to students who will be able to do nothing with it except become language-theory teachers themselves. And so ad infinitum or, in Blake's judgment, nauseam. Again, no names are given, but my hunch is that Jared works at Ryerson. Jared disapproves of Blake's sex life, which consists of going out one or two nights a week for encounters with men he hopes never to see again. Blake mocks Jared for being pussy-whipped by a girlfriend who only allows him to see her at weekends, and for less than the full 48 hours at that. In sum, we learn more about Blake's lifestyle than we do about Jared's; he is the home team after all. We hear that Blake has only the most basic of TV services, and just one set of DVDs: Star Trek, which he watches incessantly because, he says, it deals with "eternal values," good and evil. He also has a pair of pet emus in his shed. Jared shudders at Star Trek, and initially at the emus as well, but the latter he eventually comes to find rather sweet.

Blake also arouses Jared's mockery, not unmixed with panic, by claiming to have conversations with their dead father. But it's the truth. Just after the intermission -- I don't think the play should have one, but there it is -- the Ghost of Dad (that's his billing) appears on the top floor of Andy Moro's split-level set, and listens to Blake's account of the rows he and his brother have been having. Dad's considered opinion is that they should cut it out. Apart from a few generally agreed rules of decent human conduct, there are no rights or wrongs. His sons should stop painting themselves into corners, give up on being judgmental, live and let live.

I couldn't agree more. But I'm not sure why we have to spend a whole evening finding this out. You have, not for the first time, to consult the program notes rather than the play itself to discover the intended wider significance. "City folk and country folk" says the oracle, "symbolize, through this specific case study, the diversity of Canadians." That's a stretch. We have two characters with differences; if they didn't have differences, there couldn't be any play. But the mere fact that these two Jacks-in-a-box are a Jack-in-the-country and a Jack-in-town doesn't make them emblematic of Canada, any more than the fact that one of them is gay and the other straight. If one of them were a woman, and the other a man, that wouldn't prove anything either. If they have different values, these aren't probed very deeply. All we have are a few fraternal grudges, spread very thin. Gilbert writes, as usual, fluent dialogue with the rare quality of sounding like believable conversation, but this time the exchanges are repetitious and, by the end, quite agonizingly tedious.

As far as performance goes, Jason Cadieux (Blake) and Wes Berger (Jared) bring the siblings' quibblings to three-dimensional life, though, at the preview I saw, Berger's score went down to two-and-a-half in his more vengeful and hysterical moments, which were both overdrawn and underpowered. Otherwise, the two play a convincing, shifting counterpoint of surliness and need. Dad is a ghostly presence indeed, but he only has one scene. Gilbert's direction, again as usual, is excellent in the easy way it cushions the actors and frames the dialogue: old-fashioned virtues but then Gilbert, for all his professions of outrageousness, is as traditional a theatre artist as we have. I'm repeating myself in saying this, but then so is he.
