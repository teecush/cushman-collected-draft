MIDNIGHT SUN

Tarragon Theatre, Toronto

Maja Ardal's play Midnight Sun is about hot music in a cold climate. In more scholarly terms, its about the impact of American big-band swing on the population of wartime Iceland. This is not a subject that gets explored very often in the theatre, or anywhere else; and in order to reduce its ramifications to manageable proportions, the author has zeroed in on an age-old romantic dilemma: Theres this cool chick, and she has to choose between a hepcat and a square. All three are native Icelanders, so the cool is literal as well, but the United States Army is also around, anxious to establish friendly relations with the locals, especially on the dance floor. Heard that before? There is perhaps more intellectual ambition here and less technical proficiency than in your average 1940s Hollywood soap opera with built-in cues for song, but the result is much the same.

Our heroine is Prila, who is going with an amiable young scientist named Petur. The two are just about to come to a life- long agreement, and it looks like we're all going to get home early, when, most frustratingly, a piece of Prila's past appears, an old flame whom everyone calls Kari Jazzplayer and who ran off to Denmark to play piano, though while there he switched to trumpet, which is certainly a lot more portable if youre going to keep hopping countries while theres a war on. He's also a piece of Petur's past - - his brother, in fact, and not at all pleased to find himself supplanted. So we settle in for an evening of Prila shuttling between two stupefyingly uninteresting young men.

Prila lives with her younger sister, Sissa, and their widowed mother, who seems to have a grudge against just about everything. Bodefully, she refuses to allow Sissa a party to celebrate her confirmation; the girl confides her frustrations to a race of little people beneath the floorboards, who apparently derive from Icelandic folklore but may also remind some spectators of the English childrens classic The Borrowers.

Caught between two stages of adolescence, Sissa is also caught between two cultures; Prila got a touch of the American pop bug when singing with Karis jazz group, but Sissa has been thorougly infected by it, by movies especially. When the Yanks arrive, she has what may or may not be fantasy encounters (it's very confusing) with GIs whom she addresses as Clark Gable and Jimmy Stewart, and who do indeed look like them. When she's with Jimmy, she quotes from The Philadelphia Story and she strongly resembles the interfering kid sister of that movie. That child, however, was a supporting character kept in her place; Sissa starts this play as if it were going to be about her, and though it turns out that it isnt, the damage has been done. In fact, it keeps being reinforced; the plays various plot lines, obviously meant to complement one another, instead get in one another's way.

Possible dream sequences apart, there is only one American in the play, a sergeant who is the base's radio announcer and disc jockey and also plays drums. He organizes a hop (a Big Band Bash or, as the locals have it, "Big Band Bass") to which only the Icelandic women are invited. Since this tactlessly clashes with the annual Midsummer Ball, the men have a double reason to be angry, and Petur orchestrates their resentment to get back at his jazz-obsessed brother and at the suspiciously swinging Prila. (This may not seem calculated to get her back, but Petur has obviously lost it.) When the Icelandic characters meet the sergeant and speak English to him, they do so in credibly Nordic accents, and this is effective; there is a feeling of two worlds coming into uneasy contact. Left to themselves, the natives speak just like you and me, and just about as entertainingly; the dialogue is flat, and there is no sense of anything going on below the surface. Not that the author is above inventing a few depths when it suits her; late on, the mother tells us all kinds of things we never wanted to know. (That is the curse of late-breaking exposition: Its always answering questions nobody asked.) Ardal, Icelandic herself, does make a point about the countrys women being more independent than either their own or the American men find comfortable; but in order to fully dramatize the culture clash she apparently has in mind, she would need to introduce us to more people. Just hearing about them, with no details supplied, doesn't cut it.

The production, by Andy McKim and Patricia Vanstone, does little for the play's credibility, especially on the domestic front. The set, by Vikki Anderson, is a multi-levelled affair and it doesnt look as if anybody lived there. (I have very few conversations on the stairs of my own house -- Alec Guinness on "one-foot-up, one- foot-down" acting.) Nor is it strong on visceral excitement; the staging of the first-act climax is extraordinarily half-hearted.

It does, though, boast a ruggedly charming performance, strong and deceptively still, by Kari Matchett as Prila; she sings well, too, in a casual tone thats a pleasing reminder of the many fine Swedish and Norwegian jazz singers who have arisen in the last half- century. (I've never heard any Icelandic ones.)

Matthew Edison is suitably stiff and uptight as Petur, and Jeff Madden, who smiles a lot, suitably devilish as Kari. Holly Lewis is defeated by the precocious outpourings of sister Sissa. While Nicky Guadagni has an even tougher time dealing with mom. She has to keep marching on, just when we've forgotten about her, and play stressful scenes with folded arms and eyes directed at the heavens; and having done that she is left standing around all high and dry at the end of them. Paul Braunstein copes cheerily, if none too subtly, with the burden of representing the entire American military presence; and all the actors, when required, play their own instruments, which is always impressive.

There is an additional performer, Greg Earle, who exists only to play the bass, which seems like cheating. Maybe they should have gone whole hog and made the show a full-scale musical; it does at least have more substance than either of the big swing shows that blighted the Toronto landscape the year before last. One of the better scenes here has a puzzled Prila coping with the lyrics of Chattanooga Choo-Choo ("What is this woo-woo?") and then sailing right through it without once looking at the paper. She and the combo also manage to leave out the entire middle of the song without consultation and without train wrecks, which just goes to show that jazz people can communicate at osmotic levels.

But why, when the American mentions Kansas, is he immediately asked if he knows Hoagy Carmichael, as Carmichael was an Indiana boy who at the plays's historical moment would have been in Hollywood? Anyway, Hoagy is constantly invoked, and the play ends with Prila singing his haunting Skylark. This presumably accounted for the cheers and stamps at the curtain -- either that or the world's gone mad. The Tarragon, which in the past has demonstrated a weakness for a dully pretentious new play, seems now to have opted for equally featureless banality.
