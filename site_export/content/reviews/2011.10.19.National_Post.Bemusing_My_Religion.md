THEATRE REVIEW

GHOSTS

Yonge Centre for the Performing Arts

In Ibsens Norway, weather is a metaphor for fate, and in Morris Panychs Soulpepper production of Ghosts the rain that pelts down outside the Alving home at the beginning of the play is practically a character in its own right.

Ken MacDonald has designed one of his finest sets, a wide-windowed antechamber furnished with all the regulation Ibsen trappings - chairs, tables, sofas and, most vitally, books - but here arranged to be stylish rather than cluttered. Alan Brodie's lighting is superb, especially in its summoning of the sun that rises at the end, herald and symbol of the worst catastrophe in a day and night that have been full of them.

Within this frame is a good, not great, production that holds its fire until the conclusion: the revelation that Oswald Alving, the young artist home from Paris, is sinking into the last stages of the brain-softening syphilis that he inherited from his father, the late and locally revered Captain. Beside him stands his mother, whom he has besought to end his life rather than leaving him to endure it as a vegetable. So assisted suicide is one still pressing issue the play raises, one more taboo it breaches.

Mentioning venereal disease now seems the least of these, even though it's what ostensibly (and ostentatiously) most shocked the 19thcentury critics. They were perturbed, too, by the plays apparent willingness to wink at the possibility of brotherand-sister incest. Underlying all the disquiet is the plays questioning, embodied in Mrs. Alving herself, of all the conventional moral and religious codes.

Long ago, she fled her debauched husband's home and sought counsel of her priest, Pastor Manders, whom she had loved and who was attracted to her as well. Doubly scared, he told her to go back to her duty, and the two have not met again until now, when they've come together to arrange the opening of the Captain Alving orphanage, a monument to a lie.

It used to be all too easy to play the pastor, with his provincialism and his prejudices and his consuming fear of what respectable people will think (and his horror of nonrespectable people thinking at all) as a figure of fun or just as a tiresome bore. The resurgence of the religious right has made him seem far more realistic a threat; the temptation now is to play him as a monster.

Joseph Ziegler avoids both extremes; hes conscientious, reflexively blinkered, and vulnerable, still shrinking away when Mrs. Alving lays a hand on his arm. (We arent told what his life was like after he rejected her, but it seems likely that he never married.) Nancy Palk's Mrs. Alving, surveying him from a handsome height (Dana Osborne has costumed her beautifully), seems to have seen right through him from the play's beginning, and through everything he represents. This makes for some exciting moments; when the pastor remarks on the "advanced" literature she seems to be reading (and that hes read about in the newspapers) and when he recommends Scripture as an antidote, Mrs. A replies, in Panychs own zippy new rendering, "the Bible; there's a book": a more spiritedly scornful response than I recall hearing in any previous translation. Instead of progressively shedding illusions as the play proceeds, this Mrs. Alving seems to be free of most of them from the start; interestingly, she seems vindicated rather than upset when the orphanage burns down, uninsured since the pastor felt that to do otherwise would suggest insufficient trust in God. (A creak in the plot: It all happens so quickly there wouldn't have been time to take out a policy anyway.)

The downside of the interpretation is that Palk has to spend much of her time being angry with Manders, and runs out of fresh ways to do it. Instead of being the culmination of a protracted unmasking, her last scene with Oswald becomes a new and separate drama, though its one that she plays magnificently, as does Gregory Prest, who from the start has been a charismatic figure, with a shaky bravado fuelled by the terrified awareness of his own mortality.

This scene faces its heroine with the one duty she has no wish to deny - motherhood - and with a horrifying uncertainty as to what, in these circumstances, that duty should be. Its where all the ghosts - spiritual, intellectual, physical - catch up with her and with us. It blazes; much of what has gone before is, presumably by intention, muted, sometimes to the point of inaudibility.

Especially subdued, though with differing results, are Diego Matamoros as Engstrand, the crippled and crafty carpenter, and Michelle Monteith as the maid Regina. Matamoros offers an engaging sketch, but no more; the scene in which he sanctimoniously and successfully blackmails the pastor lacks menace and, surprisingly from these actors and this director, humour. Monteith is the discreetest of domestics, obsequiously so until events enable her to speak for herself. This actress excels at presenting a calm exterior and then digging beneath it; she does it, in simpler and sunnier fashion, as one of the dolly birds in Soulpepper's restaging of The Odd Couple, and she does it here, sounding depths that are freezing. This is a production strong on tension, less on texture.
