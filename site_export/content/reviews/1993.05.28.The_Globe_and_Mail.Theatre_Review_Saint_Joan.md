THEATRE REVIEW SAINT JOAN

Friday, May 28, 1993

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont. -- WRITTEN BY GEORGE BERNARD SHAW Directed by Neil Munro Starring Mary Haney, Michael Ball, George Dawson, Richard Farrell, Peter Hutt and Simon Bradbury At the Festival theatre Rating: Neil Munro's production powerfully makes the play's final point, that the world will always reject its saints, but he seems impatient with its more temporal messages Shaw's out-of-context Saint Joan BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont. FIRST comes a bit of production. On a ravaged landscape, people - peasants, probably - are methodically and mercilessly gunned down. Ethnic cleansing, we are programmed to assume.

From here we progress to the first scene of Shaw's Saint Joan, in which there is still a war on: the First World War, by the look of it. Robert de Baudricourt, Joan's feudal lord and first convert, communicates by field- telephone whenever possible, and the stage is dominated by a gigantic uprooted cross, now horizontal, around and beneath which the actors dutifully scurry. At her first entrance Mary Haney's Maid is invisible from the waist up.

"All dress is fancy dress is it not, except our natural skins," says Dunois, the easy-going Bastard of Orleans, in Shaw's epilogue; and, though there are no nude scenes, Neil Munro's production seems to have taken this remark as its text. The epilogue, in fact, is the only scene done in medieval costume. There is a practical reason for this - Shaw brings on a gentleman in 1920s clothing and we need the contrast - but there is also a prankish one. The scene as written is a dream, out of time, so Munro shoves it back into history.

There is a perverse consistency here, since the avowedly historical scenes have all been shoved forward. Sometimes, as noted, the time-scheme is 1914-18; if the Baudricourt episode looks like the Expressionist war canvas from O'Casey's The Silver Tassie, the Earl of Warwick's tent is a dugout from Journey's End. (The references to other plays may or not be intended, but post-modernism is a game critics can play as well. Indeed, it was designed for critics to play. And since Shaw was defiantly pre- modernist, or at least the modernists thought he was, some occult justice is being dispensed. Where was I?) However, when we reach the trial-scene, we advance to now, what with the house-lights being up and Joan's judges confronting her from behind a row of microphones. She, facing them, has her back to us, but if we want to look at her we have a dozen TV monitors to choose from.

As it happens, the last thing I want to do in a theatre is look at a TV screen; and the video aids, like the ubiquitous phones and intercoms, looked to me like director's toys. But the fragmentation, right from that obscured first entrance, is plainly intentional. We never get a good look at Joan; and Haney's performance is teasingly disjointed. To begin with she is a cartoon visionary, autistically stammering when she speaks of her voices, whispering an unheard message of inspiration in the Dauphin's ear, throwing a possible epileptic fit when given command of the army. In her subsequent scenes, all these symptoms disappear. Then at the trial, despite the sparky dialogue she is given, she is broken from the beginning. Moon-faced and moon-voiced she is predictably haunting; bit by bit, she does everything well. In the epilogue, as a ghost, she is most like a person; and there, infuriatingly but inspringly, she is very moving.

In fact Munro's production makes the play's final point, that the world will always reject its saints, more powerfully than any other I have seen. With its more temporal messages he seems impatient, and he signals his contempt by periodically drowning out the debates with sound-efects or mocking them with visual gags. It might be more honest to cut them altogether but the Shaw Festival's mandate might not permit that.

Shaw, unlike Shakespeare, was too much the pedant and too little the poet to write timeless history; and it is irritating rather than stimulating to hear people talking about horses while travelling in jeeps. It also patronizes the audience, who might be able to make their own connections. But, as often happens, modern dress does jump-start the acting. Michael Ball's Cauchon and Barry MacGregor's Inquisitor would probably be as good and judicious as they are, whatever the frocks, but the Dauphin's courtiers, generally ciphers, are here individuals: Neil Barclay's Bluebeard a frivolous viper, Tony van Bridge's La Tremouille bluff, ignorant and finally villainous. The character who benefits most from updating is Richard Farrell's de Stogumber, an invincibly chauvinistic English army chaplain. But then that is how Shaw conceived him, as a blind blimp of his own time - which, I don't need telling, is the time that much of Munro's production is concerned to evoke. Disregard the rating at the head of this notice: my head dictates two stars , but my heart would offer three. (Or maybe the other way round.)
