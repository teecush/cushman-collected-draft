CONTACT

Canon Theatre, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - A lot of discontent was stirred up when Contact won the 2000 Tony Award for best Broadway musical. A musical is traditionally made of three things -- speech, song and dance, all delivered live -- and Contact is a show that has little of the first element and nothing at all of the second. It has music, of course, some of it even vocal, but none of it was written specifically for this show, and all of it comes canned, in pre-existing performances.

I think the fuss was unwarranted. Personally, I prefer shows that combine the three ingredients, but those that dont shouldn't be outlawed. For years, after all, Broadway, with a fixed and sickly smile on its face, handed out prizes to imported pop operas with no dialogue and virtually no dancing. Before that time -- 25 years ago - - the late Michael Bennett staged Ballroom, a story musical that was carried along by sensational dances and might well have done without songs. (It could certainly have done without the songs it actually had.) The form devised by Susan Stroman for Contact -- she calls it "a play with dancing" -- is perfectly valid, and was obviously waiting to happen.

How well it has happened is another matter. Contact is a thin mix. It's supposed to be about human contact, or the lack of it; but then, what play or musical isnt? Its actually three playlets with dancing, all of them concerned to some extent with erotic fantasy, though the first two are makeweights.

We begin with Swinging, in which a Fragonard painting comes to life. A high-born young 18th-century lady is decorously disporting herself on a swing, in the company of a complacent and equally well- bred gentleman who might conceivably be her husband. He alights and leaves the stage, to be replaced by a servant who had previously just been hanging around in the chiaroscuro. He takes our heroine higher and faster than she has been before, at least to judge from the delighted little yelps she emits. This morsel of choreographic double entendre is all the piece has to offer, apart from its music. The whole episode is played out to a single tune, Rodgers and Hart's My Heart Stood Still (a nod, perhaps, to traditional Broadway) as sparklingly fiddled by Stephane Grappelli.

Some spirited objections have been registered, not all of them by the Musicians' Union, to this shows borrowing its music from the record library. In theory I too would rather have my theatre music played by musicians who are actually there at the time; in practice, I concede that all the selections in Contact are meant to function as objets trouves, and no pit band would be likely to equal the performances we get here. Besides, I imagine that, at least under New York union rules, the local musicians were handsomely compensated for the rigours of not playing. (And indeed, getting ahead of myself a bit, I was seriously disappointed when the record of Beyond the Sea that gets spun in Contacts third segment turned out not to be Bobby Darin's.)

No words are spoken in Swinging, but there are some in the next item, called Did You Move? They are mainly expletives, delivered by a squat and menacing man who might conceivably be a gangster and who is visiting an Italian restaurant in Queens in 1954. (The program is more specific about this than the show is.) His bile is directed at the staff, for neglecting to bring him rolls, and at his meek and obviously long-suffering wife, for everything. He keeps wandering offstage in search of more nourishment -- this must be a buffet restaurant -- while barking at her not to move. She sits still as a mouse till he disappears, then starts indulging in increasingly vigorous danced flirtations with the waiters, to the sound of romantic music, from Grieg to Bizet. Always the music and the movement stop just in time, until the one time that they don't. This is ingenious and quite suspenseful, especially given Adam Dannheissers brutal performance as the husband. Stromans choreography is inventive, too, though its actual performance never catches fire, Meg Howrey as the wife being a lot better at being demure than at being aflame.

We don't hear any swing music in this piece, though it's conceivable that at that time and place it would still have been played. Its absence deprives the three parts of the evening of a second unifying pun, since the last and most substantial section -- which has the same title as the show -- is built on swing. It presents us with Michael Wiley, a 40-year-old New York ad-man who when he first meet him is receiving his umpteenth industry award but who nevertheless, or therefore, hates his life. He hates it so much that when he gets home he tries to hang himself. He fails; and is transported to a club of which he has previously heard. Its in the downtown meat-packing district, and its habitues pool-shoot by day and swing-dance by night.

The piece resembles the big-ballet numbers that used to wind up 50s movie musicals. The ones in which Fred Astaire or Gene Kelly would wander into some smoky dive and be captivated by some lanky seductress who was invariably unattainable and was usually Cyd Charisse. Michael's nemesis is a girl in a yellow dress who seems to him the incarnation of all he has been missing.

John Weidman's script makes no pretensions to depth, but it scores some fairly sharp points off award ceremonies and Manhattan celebrity and all that they entail. (In Michaels case this includes a barrage of phone calls from his deeply-concerned shrink.) As for the music and dancing: At first the club routines are executed a bit incongruously, to contemporary pop songs; finally, and presumably to demonstrate Michaels surrender to the swing culture, we get to hear Benny Goodman's Sing, Sing, Sing, the one tune without which any swing-related musical would apparently be incomplete. This exposes Stroman's choreography to damaging comparisons with Bob Fosses treatment of the same number; it isnt quite fair -- Stroman is trying to tell a story, while Fosse was having an exuberant abstract ball -- but it still leaves the routine here looking routine. Maybe it had more fizz on Broadway, and maybe the girl in the original yellow dress had more allure than her road-company successor (Colleen Dunn) can muster. As it is, it's difficult to see what Michael, played by Daniel McDonald with the right degree of awkward desperation, is getting so excited about.

Dannheisser reappears as an oracular bartender who bears a disconcerting, though probably intentional resemblance, to Michael Richards in Seinfeld. Thomas Lynch's sets are cavernously effective, both for the club itself which incorporates a louring Hopperish mural presumably held over from an earlier existence, and for the street outside, dominated by a gigantic Dont Walk sign that Michael, naturally, shows no inclination to obey. William Ivey Longs costumes are, as usual, excellent, glorying in cut and colour. The story exerts a modest romantic pull, and swing remains fun. Nevertheless the best part of the entertainment, as often these days, comes at the curtain-call which is a divertissement unto itself; the characters from all three instalments break loose from their moorings and cavort to the reconciling strains of Van Morrison's Moondance -- a newish tune that's good enough to be old.
