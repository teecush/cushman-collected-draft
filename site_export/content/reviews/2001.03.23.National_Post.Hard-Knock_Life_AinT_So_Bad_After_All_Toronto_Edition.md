ANNIE

Pantages Theatre, Toronto

The Great Depression is at its grimmest, and you are a little girl immured in an orphanage of semi-Dickensian oppressiveness, and the wealthiest and most influential man in America announces that he wants to adopt you. You are a sensible child; you hang in there.

Annie, the musical, has been hanging in since its premiere in 1977; and of course the comic strip from which it derives has been doing it for decades longer. The show worked 24 years ago, and it works now, infallibly. The recipe is simple: barefaced sentimentality accentuated by rampant cuteness but disinfected by humour, and with the mildest dash of social comment. It knows exactly how to moisten our eyes while sparing our blushes.

The best-known song in the score, though not the best, is the heroines incurably optimistic anthem, Tomorrow. When she first sings it, an outcast on the streets, its just one of those damnably effective Broadway things. In the second act she reprises it, in the White House, at a meeting of the Roosevelt cabinet. FDR is inspired by it and insists his advisors stand up and join in, even takes a chorus himself. By the end of the scene he has invented the New Deal. Of course we're not really asked to believe that an indomitable 10-year-old redhead was responsible for the salvation of America. But then again, perhaps we are.

Thomas Meehan's book is something of a masterpiece in the genre of having it both ways. Daddy Warbucks, Little Orphan Annies imposingly hairless saviour (shiny pate to her carrot top), tells her that his riches havent brought him love or happiness, though he cannot forbear boasting that he was still young when he made his first billion and in those days that was a lot of money." His mansion, full of fanatically loyal retainers genuflecting in rhythm, is both parodied and celebrated. So, at the other end of the scale, is the orphanage from which Annie escapes, administered by a heavy- drinking man-chasing harridan who forces her charges to chant "we love you, Miss Hannigan, and who has the disposition of a full- blown tyrant but not the efficiency. The script is not long on plot, but it does manage to throw in a certain amount of suspense centring on a dastardly plot by Miss Hannigan's jailbird brother and his floozie to pose as Annie's parents and capture a promised reward. They are, needless to say, foiled by Mr. Warbucks; when you can call in the president and the FBI all things are possible.

The book is better than the music. Charles Strouse wrote some of the best scores of the 60s, but Annie isnt on their level. Perhaps the most striking thing is the way in which it gets consistently worse as it goes on. It starts with Maybe, Annie's beautifully wistful dream of her lost parents; then comes It's the Hard-Knock Life, a spirited complaint for the massed orphans, and then Tomorrow. Next is Wed Like to Thank You, a mildly vitriolic chorale aimed by the New York homeless at the departing back of ex- President Hoover.

After that its downhill all the way, the decline only interrupted by You're Never Fully Dressed Without a Smile, a pastiche cheer-up song introduced as part of a wonderfully syrupy radio show, involving a smiley host, a limp-harmony girl trio, a ventriloquist and radio's only masked announcer." There are two back-to-back songs, NYC and Easy Street, that try embarrassingly hard to be show-stoppers, rousing and comic respectively, and are somewhat helped by their staging, but only somewhat. The last section of the show dissolves into a set of indistinguishable celebratory numbers, including a title song that seems to be promoting Annie as a mini-Mame or maybe -- when she comes down the staircase in her trademark red dress -- a baby Dolly.

Martin Charnins lyrics follow the same downward trajectory except that they arent as good to begin with. However, on this show Charnin wears two, or maybe two-and-a-half, hats. His was the idea for the show and he was its original, and very smart, director. He has also staged this touring revival, which is reduced in scale (I certainly missed David Mitchell's original spacious sets) but still spiffy.

Peter Gennaro's choreography remains expert fun, especially in the orphanage. Annies left-behind friends seem remarkably good- natured, expressing no jealousy whatever at her good fortune; they are also troupers. They are a riot in themselves, and riotously deployed, especially one called Molly (Danielle Freid), who is about five inches tall.

"Your days are numbered, says Miss Hannigan when she comes upon this tot being particularly rambunctious. She also swoops in after a production number yelling Do I hear happiness in here?" and, in a rare moment of philosophical calm, mutters, "Why any kid would want to be an orphan, I dont know. These are all, I assure you, very funny lines, at least they were when Dorothy Loudon delivered them on Broadway. As spoken here by Victoria Oscar they might as well be inaudible; and her dumpy, frumpy characterization lacks the sharp, manic outline Loudon supplied (as did Carol Burnett in the movie) and that is essential to both the part and the show.

Indeed none of the grown-ups here have the comic flair of the kids. Both the radio and the White House scenes go for about a quarter of their proper value; and was FDR's speaking voice really this effete? David Reynolds is no good at all at the cartoon-tycoon side of Warbucks though he is affecting when he shows his soft side, which is probably more important and certainly takes up more of his time. Marie Barlow is fine as his adoring secretary, whose adoration now seems more upfront than I'd remembered it. It may be a mark of the production -- an earnest of its earnestness -- that the most effectively sustained acting comes from Edward C. Stallsworth and Heather B. Stuart as Annies fake parents.

The two crooks play their assumed destitution so intensely -- he especially -- that we quite forget their crookedness. Instead we start thinking about life must be like outside the Warbucks stockade. This may be good for our souls but its bad for the show, and it prompts a thought about the timing of this revival; at the dawn of a recession, if recessions have dawns, it may not prove the most comfortable choice.

There are, after all, reminders all around us of what happens to Annies when they defy the cartoon rules and grow up. The role's creator, Andrea McArdle, is about to reappear among us as the dissolute Sally Bowles of Cabaret; one of her successors was Sarah Jessica Parker, whose stomping ground in Sex and the City is the same NYC so innocently and strenuously hymned here. Not that the current incumbent need worry about that yet; she is (or was, on the first night) Addison Timlin, who is all of nine. A mere swing -- that's less than an official understudy -- she nevertheless carried all blithely and confidently before her, as Annies should and almost invariably do. The cartoon-strip prototype had a dog, Sandy, so the musical does, too; he barely makes it into the story but of course he wins what's left of our hearts. This show will stop at nothing. But somehow you don't resent it.
