A Gentlemans Guide to Love and Murder is the musical adaptation of KindHearts and Coronets, greatest of all British film comedies. It only confesses, though - in very small print - to being "based on a novel by Roy Horniman," that being the 1909 book that inspired the 1949 movie. So, is it just a coincidence thatboth film and show are built upon the same conceit?

In each of them a single actor plays all of the eight aristocrats whom the hero, a poor relation, has to eliminate in order to succeed to the family earldom. And in the similarity lies the crucial difference between the movie and the musical.

Alec Guinness multiple performances in the film were a triumph of subtlety and tongue-incheek restraint with each role, including a couple of silent ones, filled so delicately to the brim the audience purred with delight the moment he appeared in a new disguise.

Delicacy is the last quality to be attributed to John Rapson, who in the show's Toronto production struts or minces or bellows through his various incarnations in what somebody apparently believes to be English high-comedy style. It isn't even high-farcical.

It also isnt altogether the actors fault. He's reproducing the Broadway performance of Jefferson Mays, who won a Tony for his pains and was equally obnoxious, though he did at least manage to make the songs comprehensible. The score - music by Steven Lutvak, lyrics by Lutvak and Robert L. Freedman - is not the greatest, but it does have its quaint and merry moments, and even a couple of good punch-lines.

In Rapson's performance, the words are just an amplified mumble.

Like his predecessor, hes most bearable as a couple of comparatively quiet (and non-singing) members of the ill-fated DYsquith clan. He's least bearable, again like his predecessor, in an agonizingly prolonged musical sequence in which he plays a philanthropic noblewoman who travels to a succession of tropical plague-spots and emerges infuriatingly unscathed from each of them. Compare, if you can bear it, Guinness' sublime silent cameo as a smiling suffragette whose balloon gets burst: Literally. Also as in NewYork, the real star turn is given in the role of Monty, the penniless son of a mother cast out by the insufferable DYsquiths, who on hearing of the connection devotes himself to getting rich by getting even. This performance also differs from its cinematic counterpart, but in this case the departure is creative.

In place of Dennis Prices suave heartlessness, Kevin Massey offers an initially bashful, increasingly confident young man who can hardly believe the depths to which he can sink or the heights that he can reach.

These heights are romantic as well as financial; once prosperous, he has to juggle the affections of a mercenary flirt (Kristen Beth Williams, campy but amusing) and a deadly serious female relative (Adrienne Eller, a self-satirizing coloratura).

A patter trio in which both dear charmers descend on Monty's chamber and have to be kept forcibly and athletically apart is the high point of Darko Tresnjak's production, which otherwise varies from the neat to the frantic, with the latter taking grisly precedence as the evening lengthens. An exchange of insults between the last of the DYsquiths and his lady is the crassest and least necessary scene Ive beheld in years.

But it gets worse. The film's last sublimely ironic moment has been replacedby a device whose feebleness beggars belief.

It's just as well that critics aren't allowed to give away endings.

A Gentleman's Guide to Love and Murder runs through June 18 at the Princess of Wales Theatre in Toronto.
