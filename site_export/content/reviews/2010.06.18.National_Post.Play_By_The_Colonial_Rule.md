THE AFRICA TRILOGY

Fleck Dance Theatre, Toronto

---

Everybody wants a piece of Africa. The old colonial theme is also, more subtly diffused, the unifying theme of The Africa Trilogy, staged at this year's Luminato festival by Toronto's Volcano Theatre, which has gathered an impressively multinational creative team. All three of the evening's plays centre around an African girl or young woman whom the other characters need for their own validation. Some of those laying claim are other Africans.

In the middle play, though, which is also the best, all the people we actually meet are European. Peggy Pickit Sees the Face of God, written by Roland Schimmelpfennig, who as you might have guessed is German, and directed by Liesl Tommy, who is South African-American, is set in what might well, for present purposes, be Toronto. There are four characters, married couples, all of them doctors. Carol and Martin have returned from four years of working in an unnamed African country; their old friends Frank and Liz have invited them over for dinner. Frank and Liz would like to have gone to Africa too, but feel that, having a young daughter, they really couldn't. This prompts their guests to point out, acidly, that there are children in Africa too. It's the first drop of acid in a play that ends up suffused in it. There are scars and chasms in both marriages, and the reunion lays them bare. The domestic bourgeois battlefield isn't exactly new territory for drama, but this one explores it sharply, economically and without patronizing anybody.

It seems for a time that the African element is almost incidental, and that the play might work better in isolation than as one third of a whole. I'm actually not convinced that that isn't the case, but the suspicion that Carol and Martin could have gone anywhere with the same dramatic results does gradually melt away. While in Africa, the two expats virtually adopted a sick girl; now, for reasons that are hinted at but never fully explained, they've returned in a hurry, without her. Frank is jovially unconcerned by this, as by most things, but Liz is appalled, for an inextricable variety of reasons. She's a concerned, if absentee, humanitarian; but she's also immediately concerned with the effect on her own daughter, who has lost a pen pal, and on whose behalf she has bought a doll, for a girl who is now probably dead. That's what the title refers to.

The play uses a stop-time device; periodically, the scene freezes, and is then replayed, with additional information providing a different perspective. (Perspectives are very much what this play's about, reflected -- the perfect word -- on a screen doubling as a mirror. The entire trilogy is very video-conscious.)

The actors, in a superbly rigorous production, are constantly required to switch tone, from polite conviviality to undercover bitterness, until the barriers drop and the moods converge. This is particularly true of the women, with Jane Spidell's Liz moving inexorably towards hysteria and Maeve Beaty's Carol turning brilliantly on a whole march of dimes. Tony Nappo plays Frank, and Trey Lyford, an American actor, plays Martin, balding and unsettlingly amiable, who halfway through steps outside the frame to narrate a fable about the sources and expectations of aid. At the time it seems gratuitous; later it reverberates, as the centre of the whole trilogy.

In the preceding play, Close Your Eye, written by Binyavanga Wainaina, a Kenyan, but set in Nigeria, the pivotal figure is Beka, daughter of a revered, and murdered, activist. Shiningly played by Dienye Waboso, she is courted, professionally speaking, by a Lagos computer entrepreneur, and more intimately and from a distance by Doreen, a compassion merchant (no slight intended) who lives in Toronto and with whom Beka maintains a lively electronic correspondence (if the Doras were to introduce a category for best Skyped performance, Karen Robinson's Doreen would be a worthy recipient). Ross Manson, Volcano's artistic director, handles this play himself, and delivers a production stronger on musical and technological bravura than on narrative development.

After one play with an all-black cast and one with an all-white one, integration arrives with Glo, unhappily the weakest of the three. Written by Christina Anderson (American) and directed by Josette Bushell-Mingo (British-Swedish) this, like Close Your Eye, has a heroine bemused by the attention she's attracting. She's Lydia, a Kenyan attending a diversity conference in New York on the strength of having written a book. She's besieged by her British sponsor; by a couple of start-up business partners, one black, one white and trying to impress with what Dame Edna Everage called "that meaningless word, infrastructure;" and, via phone calls that never connect, by her troubled brother back home. The situation keeps being restated, and the staging is dour and portentous. Dorothy A. Atabong is a sensible, sympathetic Lydia, and two of the Peggy Pickit cast reappear: Lyford, unrecognizable, as a would-be smooth operator, and Beaty, recognisable, though with an English accent that could fool Professor Higgins.
