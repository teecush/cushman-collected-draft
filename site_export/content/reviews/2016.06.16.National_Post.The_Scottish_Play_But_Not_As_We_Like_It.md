Macbeth

As You Like It

Stratford Festival

Antoni Cimolino's Stratford production of Macbeth might be called traditional, though it's in a tradition that has recently fallen into disuse. The play is set firmly in its historical pre-medieval period. Julie Fox's set splits the difference between blasted mosscovered heath and craggy castle, complete with steps on which the embattled characters can rush vigorously up and down. The witches, and the apparitions they summon, are staged and acted with good old-fashioned shrieking abandon. It looks as if Scotland's destiny is to be ruled by a race of reindeer.

In all, the production provides a fitting barbaric background for a central performance both unkempt and verklempt. Ian Lake offers us Macbeth as neurotic thug, a blood-soaked soldier transformed into a bloodbolstered psychopath. He's good at registering Macbeth's disquiet when Malcolm is proclaimed as the kingdom's heir, and at his best in the total misanthropy that precedes his final battle: the lily-livered boy who brings him news of the advancing English can never have been more witheringly dismissed. But we never get inside Macbeth's head, which is where the play's real action resides.

We do, from her very first words, get inside Lady Macbeth's. To quote her own advice to her husband: Krystin Pellerin looks, and sounds like the innocent flower, there is a serpent underneath. Her apparent girlishness makes her selfpropelled journey to the dark side all the more chilling. Her impatience with Macbeth-asmurderer is very persuasive. Her sleepwalking scene is not only haunting but moving.

In her one scene as Lady Macduff, Sarah Afful is similarly able to make sense of the words and to make them her own: the most basic requirements of Shakespearean acting. She's impatient with her own husband's absence, but then she settles into nervous affectionate banter with her young son (Oliver Neudorf, aged nine and excellent). The ensuing massacre is brutal but brief, the stage blacking out on her cry of "Murder" which echoes in our minds through the next scene, affording an extra poignance and an extra force to the anguish and anger of Michael Blake's Macduff. "He has no children," he cries out when Antoine Yared's admirably cool Malcolm promises him revenge. There's nothing he could do to Macbeth that would equal what Macbeth has done to him.

Such reliable actors as Scott Wentworth (Banquo) and Joseph Ziegler (Duncan) are, well, reliable. They both make ghostly appearances at Macbeth's banquet which is usually Banquo's solo haunt: an interesting idea but one that weakens a well-staged scene. As the drunken Porter, Cyrus Lane stopped the show on the first night, which really shouldn't happen. John Kirkpatrick is unprecedentedly present as the Doctor: guessing only too well at what's wrong with his patient.

If Stratford's Macbeth is straightforward, its As You Like It is anything but. The director, Jillian Keiley, has honoured her heritage by setting the play in Newfoundland in the 1980s, home, according to her program note, to "a cultural revolution that changed our perception of time and place."

She also aims to re-create the atmosphere of the province's kitchen-parties, with loads of music and audience participation. Spectators are given goodie-bags containing, among other things, branches to wave about and boxes that produce sound effects. Presiding over all this is Hymen, god or in this case goddess of marriage who does in fact appear at the end of Shakespeare's play when everybody pairs off. Despite the buildup, that textual appearance goes for almost nothing here.

But then the whole play seems cut off from the production's chosen time and place. To bridge the gap, much of it is played for giggles. It's a bad sign in a highly verbal comedy when none of the laughs come from the lines. The shame of it is that when Keiley does get down to directing a scene, she can do it very well.

The opening quarrel between the brothers Orlando and Oliver is strong and clear and physical, and so too, with humour added, is the subsequent appearance of the wrestler Charles (Cory O'Brien). The big wooing scene between the crossdressed Rosalind and her Orlando (played by Cyrus Lane as not too bright but engagingly sincere) is mined for much of its wit, pain and paradox. Not enough, though, because the ground hasn't been sufficiently prepared.

Petrina Bromley has barely explored Rosalind's good sense or the depths of her love, and her male disguise. Seana McKenna gives a fine disdainful reading of Jaques' seven ages of man speech but making this character a woman means excising references to his libertine past, impoverishing the role and the play. The banished duke likewise becomes a banished duchess. Brigit Wilson is touching when reunited with her daughter, but we lose the rivalry of the ducal brothers, and - most damagingly - the moment when the usurping Duke Frederick turns on the similarly fratricidal Oliver with "more villain thou." It's a moment of either hypocrisy or guilty displacement which has been known to turn the whole play around.

Oliver himself is vigorously played by John Kirkpatrick who doubles as the play's other Oliver, the hedge-priest Martext, endowed with a Maritime accent so thick that the other actors have to translate his words. It's a funny idea that actually works. Sanjay Talwar's Touchstone isn't funny at all, but there's nice work from Jamie Mac as the lovestruck shepherd Silvius,.

The words As You Like It appear in the program with the "you" heavily emphasized. So, it's all our fault. Macbeth is in repertory until November 5, As You Like It until October 22, both at the Festival Theatre, Stratford.
