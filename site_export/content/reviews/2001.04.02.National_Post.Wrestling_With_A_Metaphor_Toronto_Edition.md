PILEDRIVER!

Buddies in Bad Times Theatre, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - There is something very comforting about the sight of a stage laid out as a fight ring. It brings shape and focus and the promise of spectacle.

The last century is full of plays that use boxing or wrestling as a metaphor for the class struggle or gender wars or whatever. Piledriver!, more aggressively upfront, uses wrestling as a metaphor for wrestling. It does get in to related issues but the writing is too thin and the performance too raucous for them to matter much. What count here are the fights.

There are about a dozen of them, very well staged in Bradley Moss's production, and intrepidly performed by four actors, including one woman, out of a cast of five; the fifth is the referee.

Inevitably, unless youre enough of a fan to appreciate the niceties, all that locking of hands round necks and bashing of bodies against ropes and canvas becomes monotonous -- unpleasantly monotonous, at that -- but there is a dramatic arc.

The characters are a small-time troupe, working the circuit from Edmonton to Toronto with a brief detour into the northern United States, in the late 1970s which apparently was just before wrestling lost its soul. The fights of course are faked, performed according to carefully worked-out scenarios, up until the last one, when latent tensions between the performers come to the surface and they start hurting one another in earnest.

So the theatre audience, after an evening of watching simulated simulated violence, gets to watch simulated real violence. That, it seems, is what a gig in Toronto will do to you.

Here life seems to be imitating art, because Piledriver! itself originated in Edmonton. It's a joint presentation by two companies: Three Dead Trolls in a Baggie, which does comedy, and Guys in Disguise, which does drag. Wes Borg of the first group and Darrin Hagen of the second collaborated on the writing and both of them appear in it.

The play grew out of their discovery that much of professional wrestling is gay. As one of the characters says, what else would you expect in a sport that consists of sweaty leather-clad male bodies groping one another?

The authors were especially fascinated by the career of a Western wrestler called Harvey Jones, who fought under the name of Killer Karl Kramer. Characters bearing both these names appear in the play, but this seems to be strictly a homage rather than an attempt to present opposing sides of one personality; the play doesn't go that deep.

The plays Harvey and Karl are long-time lovers. Harvey is the promoter who dreams up the storylines and refs the matches. Karl drives the bus and, though quite the nicest guy around, he is also the designated heavy: the wrestler whom the crowds are primed to hate and who must therefore lose every bout. (Karl says Harvey proudly, "loses better than anyone else in wrestling.")

Karl functions in the act like a warm-up comic. The real stars are Randy Rage, a long-haired stud cast as barbarian hero, and Rock the Cock, who affects a punk Cockney accent and is presented as the chipper challenger to Randys crown.

Randy is newly married; his wife, Angie, travels with the gang and gradually becomes an integral part of the show. Rock, a single thrown into close proximity with a couple of couples, is promiscuously gay with a preference for straight men. He is also ambitious to undermine Randy and take his place in the real world as well as the fantasy one.

In an older play, he would have gone about this by hitting on Angie (who does seem interested). In this one, he does it by successfully coming on to Randy. If you think of Randy as Othello, then Rock is a Iago who seduces him physically rather than intellectually. Indeed, at different times he seems to be Iago and Cassio and Desdemona. Randy, personally as well as professionally macho, gets to feeling very disoriented, and chaos comes again.

The play gets the atmosphere of the tour bus and small-town bars convincingly enough, and the sexual moves (an especially appropriate term in this setting) are neatly laid out. We hear again about homophobia being a mask for insecurity, and we learn something about wrestling as show-business, the small-timers itching for the big- time -- which in this time-frame, with the World Wrestling Federation looming, seems bigger and itchier than ever.

The characters, though, are not only mean but petty, the writing neither passionate nor funny enough to stir more than surface interest; and the acting doesn't do much to lift things.

As usual, the best performance, out of the ring at least, is the quietest: Wes Borg, co-author, as Karl, an ex-cop and ex-straight whose sexual orientation changed when he met the flamboyant Rocky, to whom he has been faithful ever since. Hagen, the other author, plays Randy, and the two of them have written themselves the most thoughtful scene, one in which they compare notes on the feasibility of changing lives and leaving wives.

The rest of Hagen's performance doesn't go beyond troubled swagger, while Joe Bird as Rock sticks in a groove of untroubled swagger. April Babigan has mostly to stand around being either annoyed or concerned, and cannot find much nuance in either. Jeff Page plays Rocky as the standard fussy queen in theatrical management, who italicizes every fourth or fifth word. It's entertaining for the first five minutes.

Buddies in Bad Times has done worse shows than this, but the company still seems to be taking its name with depressing literalness. Producing a good play would not necessarily be a sell- out.
