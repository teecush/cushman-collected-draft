There are three Canadian plays in Stratford's studio season: one old, one new, one newish.

The new one is The Trespassers, written and directed by Morris Panych. The title possibly has all sorts of metaphorical significance, but on the literal level it refers to the regular excursions of Lowell, aged 15, and his grandfather Hardy into a neighbouring orchard, where they climb trees and steal peaches from the absentee owner. Gluttony apart, they do this because Hardy believes that property is theft, and is passing on his convictions to his grandson. Lowell is believed, though not by Hardy, to be suffering from some kind of emotional disorder; he certainly has a very short and angry fuse. Hardy is also teaching him how to bluff, at poker and other things. He tells the boy, "there's something in between lying and not lying; it's called a story," which is one of those nice-sounding lines with less to it than meets the ear.

The first act is mostly charming and, this being Panych, funny. The second occasionally verges on being moving, turning as it does on Hardy's imminent death from cancer. This adds assisted suicide to the play's concerns, with the characters lining up pretty much as you'd expect. But the various plot strands don't fertilize one another. The central issue is presumably Lowell's coming of age, but all we know of him is that he shouts a lot. That isn't a criticism of Noah Reid's performance; it's what's required and he does it well. He's surrounded by three of the best; none of them is much extended but Joseph Ziegler is a cunning delight; Lucy Peacock overflows with hardscrabble warmth, maybe more than the play can hold; and Kelli Fox conveys love by way of censoriousness.

Like The Trespassers, Rice Boy features a pubescent protagonist, tree-climbing, ladders and an unfulfilled title. The boy is Tommy, 12-years-old and of Indian parentage, living in the Kitchener-Waterloo area. Most of the play, though, takes place in India where, six months before the Canadian action, Tommy and his father had been visiting the family. Sunil Kuruvilla has, by his own account, much tightened his play since its 2001 production at Can-Stage. But he's also weakened it, since the Indian scenes now

so outweigh the Canadian that the play's fulcrum -- Tommy's sense of not belonging in either place -- has disintegrated. All that seems clear is that he has the Worst. Dad. Ever, whose self-pity, neglect and occasional downright cruelty can only partly be excused by his wife having recently drowned. Tommy's grandfather suffered a similar loss; his auntie is cuckolding his uncle with the local doctor; the family's servant girl has an off-and-on-thing with her husband the fish seller; and Tommy gets badly smitten with his imperious paraplegic cousin Tina, destined for an arranged marriage. Here too, a lot goes on but nothing truly happens. Sanjay Talwar and Deena Aziz reprise their original roles, and a good cast also includes Araya Mengesha (as Tommy), Raoul Bhaneja, Anita Majumdar, Sam Moses, Asha Vijayasingham and Anand Rajaram, who has a split-second opportunity to show he's an inspired clown and takes it.

Zastrozzi, directed by Jennifer Tarver, is the best time I've ever had at a George F. Walker play, dislodging Panych's Shaw Festival production of Nothing Sacred. Both these works, jokey literary extravaganzas in half-imaginary landscapes, sidestep the credibility problems that afflict Walker's plays set inpresentday Toronto. Zastrozzi is a jaundiced 20th-century lampoon of the romantic 19th-century vision of sanguinary 17th-century Italy: a Revenger's Comedy in which philosophy rules, and kills. Tarver's production impresses from its first moment: a monologue, scared and scary for Oliver Becker as a befuddled Renaissance thug who looks and sounds like Tony Soprano in reduced circumstances. Zastrozzi, "master of discipline," is the ultimate practical critic, pursuing a homicidal vendetta against bad artists; in Rick Roberts's performance, he's a sinner as impregnable as any saint. His particular prey, brilliantly twittered by Andrew Shaver, is Verezzi, a weak-minded painter who also stands accused of killing Zastrozzi's mother. How and why are never quite revealed; and strangely, when the two antagonists come finally face to face (over a heap of corpses), the matter is never mentioned. The play goes down a lot of blind alleys, narrative and intellectual. You can't, in the end, take it seriously; but you can relish it as a young man's high-style high-wire act. The best performance of all, and much his best at Stratford, is John Vickery's as Verezzi's unshakably conscientious guardian, a cropped black wig surmounting a face that's a white mask of determination and despair. - The Trespassers is in repertory through Oct. 4; Rice Boy and Zastrozzi through Oct. 3.
