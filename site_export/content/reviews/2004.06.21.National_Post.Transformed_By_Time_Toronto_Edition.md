WAITING FOR GODOT

Premiere Dance Theatre, Toronto

- - -

The Soulpepper production of Waiting for Godot, with William Hutt as Vladimir and directed by Albert Schultz, is good but not great. This is a slight disappointment after the same partnership's work on No Mans Land, which was great. But let us count our blessings.

Chief among them is Hutts performance as the senior of Samuel Beckett's two tramps, spending their days by a barren roadside awaiting, more or less patiently, the non-arrival of their presumed patron Mr. Godot. Hutt putters around the stage with his usual superb physical grace and also with his usual superb physical wit. As Spooner, the minor poet in No Man's Land he used these attributes to create a man in a social relationship, a man at once cunning and obsequious. Here, where the characters have no biographies and few visible means of support, he puts the same methods to different ends. An actor in Beckett can hardly create a character from the outside; he has to fill out the role with his own personality, his own experience, and his own technique; and the richer these are, the richer his performance is likely to be. Hutts are of course exceptionally rich; his being 84 has something to do with that, but not everything. This Vladimir moves daintily, partly because he is feeling his way with an old mans caution, but also because he is putting tentative toes into a metaphysical void. He has been burned, or maybe frozen, before.

As far as attitude goes, he is an instinctive but timorous humanitarian. He is genuinely outraged by the brute Pozzo's treatment of his tethered slave Lucky, but he can be intimidated into dropping the subject. Besides, he has ignoble instincts of his own; he, too, likes to have someone to despise. Just about every character in Beckett loves to subvert his own or the world's pretensions, and Hutt, as you would expect, flicks off these deflationary remarks with superb finesse. Vocally and emotionally, though, his finest moment -- the productions one real revelation -- comes when he faces the Boy who, for the second time, brings Mr. Godots excuses for his non-appearance. Desperately, angrily, though knowing the answer in advance, he begs the messenger to acknowledge that they have played this scene before; that, if this is a nightmare, it is at least a real one. He wants that much validation, that much dignity. Of course he doesn't get it.

This is the darkest patch in what is otherwise a fairly light- textured production. Eric Bentley once wrote that if you enter a theatre where Godot is playing you can feel the author's despondency cutting through you like an icy wind. I can see how that could be true, but I confess that I have yet to see a performance at which it seemed to be happening. Maybe, 50 years after the plays premiere, we are just too accustomed to Becketts inferno; we know about it even if we haven't seen the play. We know that Vladimir and his partner Estragon are filling in time and calling it living, and that that's what we all do, but -- just to prove the authors point for him -- we can live with that. We are inclined less to deplore the futility of their cross-talk than to enjoy its bravura; they are after all, among the theatres great clowns.

Schultz's production is lucid and almost continuously entertaining; and it pushes neither a tragic nor a comic interpretation; it seems to take the play pretty much as it comes. It lacks an overriding identity; its first image, which is deceptively witty, has little to do with its last, which is disconcertingly cute. The latter description would also do for Andrea Lundy's lighting, which allows the sun to set and the moon to rise with amusing abruptness, and for John Thompsons set which is conventional to the point of being reppy. All this has perhaps to do with an insistence on the plays overt theatricality; the funniest bit may be when Jordan Pettle's Estragon, desperate for an escape route, nonetheless recoils from the direction of the audience, and Hutt, casting his own glance out front, mutters, more or less, I don't blame you. Theres a moment like it in every Beckett play. Theyre all in part about actors taking their time on stage.

The relation between Hutt and the much younger Pettle is less rich than one had hoped; it seemed like inspired casting, considering the telepathic closeness with which they once played Lear and his Fool, and the age-difference should have added a whole new contour to the play. There is a fresh, tender dimension when this Vladimir comforts his companion, who has fewer intellectual resources and very sore feet, but it's intermittent. Pettle has less flavour and definition than his partner; as in other of his recent performances, he short-changes his own considerable charm with an excessively casual delivery. It isn't until the second act that they begin truly to complement one another, in their mournful duet on the subject of "the leaves." Then, when Hutt makes Vladimirs last stand -- we have kept our appointment, how many can say as much? -- Pettle cuts him wonderfully down to size with his simple "millions." (You can do few crueller things to a person than to answer his rhetorical question.) Then, too, its Vladimir who encourages Estragon's more vicious impulses, but it's Estragon who has them. For all the difference in their ages I did begin to consider them as two halves of one person, not an interpretation with I usually have any truck: soul and body, air and Earth, Don Quixote and Sancho Panza.

As of course, in a bitter variation, are Pozzo and Lucky. Oliver Dennis tortured servant, wispy-haired and wispy-bodied, is movingly mimed and, in his one long speech, superbly spoken. The scrambled message of doom is taken more deliberately than usual and is, by being almost comprehensible, so much the more disturbing. Joseph Zieglers Pozzo is superb at his first appearance, as much dandy as bully, taking a real if patronizing interest in the tramps' predicament, and desperately eager for their good opinion. My apologies to the director: This is another revelation, that the play is not just about two pairs of mutual dependents, but that the lines cross. But when this Pozzo returns, blind, the performance dwindles, handicapped by surprisingly clumsy staging that places his collapsed body far upstage and half out of sight. This obscures the tramps' vital, shifting reaction to his helplessness, while his famous epiphany about Time -- they give birth astride of a grave -- emerges devoid of sting and devoid of agony. This in turn lessens the impact of Vladimir's even more celebrated reprise ("astride of a grave and a difficult birth"), which should in turn lead up to his encounter with the Boy. But then Hutt, as noted, does that magnificently anyway. If this production has not been born great it stands a good chance, as the run progresses, of achieving greatness.
