Next Stage, based at the Factory Theatre, is an extension of Torontos Fringe Festival: an opportunity for Fringe companies to re-mount favoured productions in slightly less frenzied circumstances. For at least one of this years shows, the promotion (in both senses) is thoroughly justified.

LoveSexMoney, written and directed by Kit Sandler, is presented by Theatre Brouhaha, an outfit whose mission statement makes the usual inflated claims about treading boldly where no company has dared to go before. In fact, it's a play constructed on a model patented by that most commercial of dramatists, Neil Simon. Like his Plaza Suite, and its less successful successor California Suite, it's a sequence of three episodes all set in the same hotel room. (Which never gets re-decorated; theres scenic economy for you.) Theres nothing wrong with using a popular model; what's right is that Sandler has invigorated the formula with wit, invention and feeling. It's a heartening debut.

Her play differs from Simons in providing a narrative link between its scenes - characters from one reappear in the next. The jumping-off point is the famous Nevada news item about the 22-year-old student who auctioned off her virginity via radio and the Internet. The first scene here asks how the buyer and the seller, whos also the sold, might feel and behave when such a transaction came to fruition. Olivia, who's in college like her prototype (though not, apparently, in women's studies) seems to be going for thousands rather than millions - this is Canada - and has even turned down the highest bidder in favour of Simon, a middle-aged lawyer who must have seemed nonscary and in fact is. He books a room in what we subsequently discover is the Four Seasons and arrives bearing gifts. This disconcerts the waiting Olivia who had arranged herself in what she took to be an acquiescent position on the bed, and now has to get up to answer a stubbornly uncooperative door.

That door is a gag that keeps running through the evening, and earns its keep. Theres also a hilarious sequence, not repeated, about your typical hotel rooms baffling system of lamps and switches. Much of the play functions as sex farce with serious underpinnings. Olivia doesn't seem to be trying to make a political point; her motives are more personal. She wants the money, sure; she also seems to feel that, since defloration is bound to be a painful process, she may as well get something out of it. Simon wants her to get some pleasure out of it, even though this hardly falls within the terms of the contract, since a) he's a nice enough guy and b) his masculine pride is involved. Olivia wants to keep her sex life separate from her love life. As soon as they start talking, human complications are bound to arise. And of course they do talk, a process propelled by Simons placing a courtly order with room service and by a subsequent knock on the door.

The second scene, six years later, revolves around Jim, an ex-boyfriend of Olivias who can't stop reproaching himself for his reaction to her earlier behaviour, though I can't see why he should have to. Anyway, now rich and successful himself, he goes in for his own form of extravagant sex shopping. He has delivered, to the very same room, a life-size, completely obedient robotic sex toy who looks exactly like Olivia and may well be Olivia; the ambiguity is cunningly sustained, though in the end, its more tantalizing than helpful. Finally, we have a perverse tryst fuelled by alcohol and understandably mutual distaste, between two of Jims co-workers: Sandra, another ex, who left him in a strident if understandable huff, and Marcus, a self-anointed stud. Their joint unlikeability works against the scene; but they too get humanized, in part by the introduction of another toy, a mechanical arm that arouses all kinds of lubricious thoughts in Marcus and the audience, but that Sandra uses as a chaste source of comfort and consolation.

The linking conceit, that commerce and technology have made relationships more mechanical than ever before, is strained and unproven. If anything, the play shows the opposite: emotions breaking through. You might say that the first scene is the Money scene, the second gives us the Love and the third is Sex; but it's overlaps that count. The performances are excellent, with Gwenlyn Cumyn finding the robot in the woman and vice versa, and Scott Clarkson and Daniel Pagett as the two self-doubting men in her lives. Len Silvini and Brooke Morgan have tougher rows to hoe, but emerge from them with credit. The laughter, if not continuous, is frequent, sometimes uproarious, and never demeaning.

Tomasso's Party, by Jules Lewis, starts almost like a continuation of the above, with a woman in bed and a man admiring a stray part of her body. Its downhill from there, though. We have Hugo and Madeleine, who may be married but probably arent. They've been invited to Tomasso's party, Tomasso being Madeleines boss of whom Hugo is jealous despite insisting, repeatedly, that he must be gay. Most of the play consists of Hugo, who has a self-pitying knack for rendering compliments as insults, haranguing Madeleine, whose mouth reminds him of a Mexican porn star; her ankles, she tells us in one of her own contrastingly brief speeches, remind Tomasso of Jennifer Lopez. The inherent monotony is compounded in Nigel Shawn Williams production (for Rooftop Creations) by having Simon Bracken deliver Hugo's outpourings in a clenched and spiteful whine. Leah Doz's Madeleine, lying with her back to us throughout, benefits from the contrast. The most discernible influence is Pinter - the chronically insecure man, the sphinx-like woman, the offstage amorous threat - with some psychological grounding without the wit or the danger. Both plays are in the Factory Studio.
