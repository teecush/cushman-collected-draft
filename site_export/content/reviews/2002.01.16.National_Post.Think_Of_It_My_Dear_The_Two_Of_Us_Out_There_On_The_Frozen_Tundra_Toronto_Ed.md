WALKING TO RUSSIA

Theatre Passe Muraille Backspace, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - As I was saying a couple of days ago, plays connect, or at least they cluster. Here, cold on the heels of The Dragonfly of Chicoutimi and The Dance of the Dead, is the third show in a week about deceased people walking. Coming up next: a piece called Mortality.

The young lady who goes Walking to Russia is doing it, or trying to do it, in a kind of hallucination. At least she did once; now she is dead and haunts the dreams of her parents. In fact, they are possessed by her; the family is Jewish, and she seems to be a new form of the traditional demon known as a dybbuk.

The characters live in Winnipeg, but both mother and father originally came from Russia. Father is a scientist and a Marxist, the kind of cranky, obstinate Jewish atheist who looks paradoxically good in a prayer shawl. He is obsessed with global warming, a natural preoccupation perhaps in a man who has moved from one cold climate to another.

One thing he has brought with him from the old country is a family recipe for an especially potent condiment, hot enough to revive a frozen child. The play is directed by Jennifer H. Capraru, who a few years ago -- for the same company, Theatre Asylum -- staged the admirable My Mother's Courage. This new one might be called My Mother's Mustard.

It is the first play of a new writer, Jonathan Garfinkel, and it does not hang together well. The daughter left home because of her vocational clashes with her father: He wanted her to become a scientist like himself, and she chose to be an artist -- worse yet, a religious artist. Her fatal, fevered walk was a metaphorical attempt to get back to her roots, spiritual as well as ethnic. The play is sprinkled with her lyrical monologues, but they make no connection with the rest of the drama; the actress, Ruth Madoc- Jones, goes devotedly through them but really she could be talking about anything.

The more mundane parts of the play are better value. Papa, on the verge of retirement, receives a visit from a former pupil, who offers him a job researching his favourite topic. It sounds like a great deal: a new lease on life, loads of intellectual stimulation, a shot at saving the world and the firm promise of a Nobel Prize. (It seems remarkably firm; I had no idea these things were rigged.)

The catch is that the work has to be done in Churchill, in northern Manitoba, a place even colder than Winnipeg, while our professor's wife has her heart set on moving to California. For years she has slaved as his unpaid secretary, sacrificing her own talents and her own ambitions; she also carries scars from his treatment of their daughter.

He rides roughshod over her -- as, obviously, he always has -- but gets his comeuppance in the course of a CBC interview. He is humiliated before the whole country, or at least that part of it that tunes in to Peter Gzowski. (or that used to; the play is set in 1999.)

The evening comes to life when Aron Tager, as the old man, receives the offer he cannot refuse; he plays with enjoyably grumpy feistiness throughout. Ann Tager-Page, his wife in real life, seems inhibited about recreating the relationship on stage; supposed to stand up to him, she is remarkably limp and toneless. Tenderness seems to become her better; she is far more at ease when the play flashes to show her relationship with her daughter, to whom she has passed on her own suppressed yearnings.

Jim Jones is fine as the former student. He also plays the iconic Gzowski, whose appearance is tactfully staged so that we see him only from behind. He looks, from this vantage point, amusingly convincing -- but his voice is way off.
