THEATRE REVIEW

All But Gone Berkeley Street Theatre Master Harold ... and The Boys Toronto Centre for the Arts

Jennifer Tarver directs Samuel Becketts plays with iron control. A prize example is All But Gone, a kind of Beckett anthology that's a joint production of Canadian Stage and Necessary Angel.

As a playwright, Beckett was quite the control freak: his stage directions practically dictate the staging for all of his plays. All that stands between the scripts and uniformity is the stubborn individuality of actors. The Beckett play that gives its actors most scope is of course the first and most famous, Waiting for Godot. Tarver directed a fine production of Godot at Stratford a few years ago, though to call it definitive, as the CanStage press release does, is ridiculous; the same is true for her Stratford production of the briefer Krapp's Last Tape. Tarver has continually returned to what are known as Beckett Shorts, which Ive always thought should be worn with Tom Stoppards Jumpers. The new program offers four of them, with musical interludes.

The music starts the evening, performed by two opera singers, Shannon Mercer and Kristina Szabo, who are costumed as Irish peasant women and whose singing also has a keening Hibernian quality, even though the lyrics (set by an actual Irish composer, Garrett Shouldice) are in Latin. It sets the proper note, haunting and foreboding, for Act Without Words I, a solo mime that's a gloss on the myth of Tantalus. A man (Paul Fauteux) down-at-heel and probably down-and-out, rushes back and forth at the command of offstage whistles, one coming from each side. A bottle of water is dangled before him, perpetually out of his reach. He does have access to a rope but when he tries to hang himself with it, it breaks. He faces similar humiliations in Act Without Words II, which finds him supine in a sack from which he emerges at the prodding of a long hooked pole, again issuing from the wings. He engages in a series of physical jerks, looking as dejected as ever. Beside him lies another sacked man (Jonathon Young) who's subjected to the same stimulus and performs the same exercises. He, though, is dapper and cheerful about it. Both men get to eat a carrot; Fauteux finds his carrot disgusting and spits it out, Young seems to relish his and, a neat and provident soul, tucks some of it away for later consumption. Optimist and pessimist, both are condemned to reenact the same routines at the whim of a merciless universe; theyre Godots tramps deprived of the words that gave them sustenance and an audience pleasure.

Fauteux's character gets revenge when he wields a spotlight to extract jerky confessions from the other three performers in the Beckett play called Play which seems to be the Play of the moment; it also currently features in The Unending, presented by the enterprising site-specific company Convergence Theatre. Its characters are a husband, wife and mistress recounting their relationship, possibly from purgatory, at warp speed and twice over.

"Repeat play exactly," says the stage direction and they do; Beckett is very much about obedience. The two singing ladies speak their lines resonantly but without much variation; Young, the actor between them, is good at ferreting out the humour in what is one of Beckett's wittiest inventions. The same cannot be said of Ohio Impromptu, the final selection, in which one man reads while the other hears the story of a lost love. Its like Krapps Last Tape rewound and has you wishing, for once, that its author had used fewer words. Tarver's perfect-on-its-terms production leaves you feeling that there must be more to life than this while fearing that there very likely isn't.

Philip Akins production of Athol Fugards Master Harold ... and the Boys, imported from the Shaw Festival and presented by Obsidian, is perfect on anybody's terms.

It's a play rooted in autobiography, set in 1950 South Africa. Like Fugard himself in that time and place, Master Harold is a white teenage schoolboy, whose mother keeps a tea shop while his alcoholic and disabled father languishes in hospital; both parents are unseen. The "boys" are of course men, black men, Sam and Willie, who work in the shop and have been Harolds companions all his life. Sam in particular has been a surrogate father, playing with him, helping him in his studies though Harold can hardly acknowledge it. Apartheid is beginning to bite, exacerbating prejudices that were always there. In the course of a late afternoon, the relationship crumbles.

James Daly gives a wonderfully unsparing performance of Harold: defensive, mean and torn. His accent alone deserves a citation. Allan Louis, last seen by me as Julius Caesar, is now Willie, whos anything but a leader. He spends much of the play silently reacting, generally in trepidation; the amount the actor conveys through facial and bodily movement is extraordinary. Andre Sills is passionate and eloquent as Sam, though there are moments when he is too obviously his author's mouthpiece, speaking for him now while Harold represents him then. The play has other flaws; Sam is coaching Willie for a ballroom dancing contest, and the dance as an embodiment of harmony between people is a metaphor of which the characters seem too volubly aware. But this hardly hampers the emotional effect of a show in which everything looks right (set design by Peter Hartwell), sounds right, feels overwhelmingly right.

For the first time in a theatre in a long time, I wept.
