Robert Cushman is the National Post's theatre critic and the author of Fifty Seasons at Stratford (McClelland and Stewart)

For a Toronto-based theatre critic, summer used to be time off. Not any more. There is the Fringe and then, treading uncomfortably close on its heels its junior cousin Summerworks: each so stuffed with short-run events that some of us retire in confusion, not knowing where to begin, we never begin at all. There are open-air Shakespeares, more of them than ever, in parks in and around. There is Soulpepper, which has transformed the summer landscape.

Thats just in town. But for decades now the twin theatrical hubs of Southern Ontario have been the classical festivals, Stratford and Shaw, one named after a place, the other after a playwright. We take that for granted now but its rather odd. Of course, if the Shaw billed itself as the Niagara Festival, people might get confused. Would that be Niagara-on-the-Lake (as it is) or Niagara Falls? They're both tourist towns anyway, even without the theatre. If you tell somebody that you're taking a trip to Stratford, they will generally assume that youre going to see a play. With Niagara, theres always the possibility that you're going for the surrounding wineries or for the golf. Then again the name Stratford, worldwide, implies Shakespeare; and even though the festival dispensed long ago with the Bardic monopoly, you will nearly always find more Shakespeare at Stratford than you will find Shaw at Shaw.

This is a historic year for both festivals; that is a factual statement, devoid of value judgments. Stratford's season is its 50th. The Shaws marks the retirement of Christopher Newton, after an unprecedentedly long and successful reign as artistic director. Newtons achievement has been to establish a strong but flexible artistic identity in a town that often looks and feels as if it were built out of fudge. (People do live in Niagara-on-the-Lake, but it's always a puzzle, when one walks down the main street, to imagine where they can shop.)

As is well known, the Shaw Festival's policy -- not a mandate, since a mandate by definition is something youre handed by somebody else, and the Shaws choice was all its own -- has been to present plays written by Shaw himself or by other playwrights during Shaw's lifetime; as he lived for nearly a century, this is a fertile, and still accessible, field. A couple of years ago, this was amended to include plays set during that period but written after it. This year sees the first notable beneficiary of the extension: Chaplin, a play about the comedian written and performed by a long-standing member of the company, Simon Bradbury.

In fact, the Shaw has always been fairly liberal with its chronology. A case in point is its production this year of the Stephen Sondheim musical Merrily We Roll Along. This was written in 1981, and its action covers the period from the '50s to the 70s. (Or rather from the 70s to the '50s; it is, notoriously, told backwards.) So, even under the new rules, it doesn't qualify. But its based on a 1930s play by the classic Broadway team of George Kaufman and Moss Hart, so it squeezes in under the wire. The same is true of the Shaws previous two musicals: The Mystery of Edwin Drood and She Loves Me: By their sources shall ye admit them.

The Shaw Festival has a unity that does seem to radiate out from its titular figure. It explores a defined area of the repertoire, from the popular to the obscure, though avoiding the extremes of both. The masterpieces and the pot-boilers are given equal consideration -- they seem to be cut from the same cloth -- and there is an overall quality of inquiring urbanity that seems to derive from Newton himself. Three of this season's productions are his. Noel Coward' s Hay Fever, Newtons valedictory show, brings back Fiona Reid, an accomplished Coward comedian and a long- standing festival favourite. St. John Hankins The Return of the Prodigal, a deserved hold-over from last year, shows Newton and his team at their best. It's an Edwardian social drama with a satiric edge, and the practiced Shaw ensemble has all the skills it takes to play it; Ben Carlson and Blair Williams, two of the finest of the festival's younger actors, dig especially deep into the fractious but curiously understanding relationship between black-and-white- sheep brothers. The Shaw has just completed a largely triumphant cycle of the plays of Harley Granville Barker, culminating this year with the taxing but rewarding His Majesty. It is possible Newton will return as a guest to direct a similar series of Hankin. Heres hoping.

Newtons last production of Shaw at the Shaw is Caesar and Cleopatra, given a modern-dress treatment that somehow seems more persuasive in the program notes than it does on the stage. Otherwise, the patron-saint is represented by Candida, a play as compact as Caesar is sprawling. The production -- by Jackie Maxwell, Newton's designated successor -- is a good augury, since it makes an absorbing job of a play that can often be dull. It ranks at the top of this year's Niagara openings, along with the Prodigal and two productions by the long-serving Neil Munro: His Majesty and Detective Story, the latter an American genre piece that looks and sounds, like much of this directors best work, like film noir made flesh. Its also crammed to overflowing with vivid performances; sometimes one takes the excellence of the Shaw's regular players for granted, but this production is a reminder of what a priceless collection they are.

The Shaw, at least under Newton, has accumulated actors incrementally and held on to them greedily. It is rare there (except sometimes in the musicals, which make special demands) for a player to come in from outside to assume a leading role. It's likelier to happen at Stratford, where the continuity is real but more diffuse. This 50th year, in particular, is an occasion for continuity, retrospect and innovation.

One of the seasons two hottest tickets is King Lear, which brings Christopher Plummer, the Festivals first home-grown leading man, back to Stratford Shakespeare after an absence of 25 years, in a Canadian production by that most affable of British cultural legends, Jonathan Miller. The other is My Fair Lady, with Cynthia Dale as Eliza, and Professor Higgins played in succession by three more old Stratford heroes; Colm Feore played it at the beginning of the season, Geraint Wyn-Davies is currently in possession; he, in turn, will be succeeded by Richard Monette, director of the festival and of this very handsome production. Monette's acting appearances are even rarer than those of his Niagara opposite number Chris Newton (the two were Stratford juveniles together, decades ago) and this one is awaited with especial eagerness.

Monette is also responsible for an uncharacteristically austere staging of All's Well that Ends Well, with William Hutt, Stratfords undisputed doyen, as the King of France and Lucy Peacock as the medically gifted heroine Helena. Alls Well was one of the two plays presented at the first Stratford Festival; the other, Richard III, also gets a commemorative revival this year, directed by Martha Henry and with a chillingly funny lead performance by Tom McCamus. Those who wish to brush up on this history play's pre-history (always a problem in a piece where people keep referring to previously slaughtered princes, every last one of them called Edward) should brush up on the three Henry VI plays, which this year have been re-divided into two, subtitled Revenge in France and Revolt in England. In fact, they should be seen anyway since they offer a chance to see rare and exciting plays, vigorously and imaginatively done. An even rarer Shakespearean opportunity is The Two Noble Kinsmen, long regarded as apocryphal and delightfully done by young graduates of the festival's conservatory program. Contrastingly un-rare, but straightforwardly exciting, is this years Romeo and Juliet, directed by Miles Potter and with Graham Abbey and Claire Jullien as the lovers; Abbey, a past Henry V and presumed future Hamlet, does an especially winning job in a traditionally treacherous role.

Shakespeare dominates Stratford -- this year especially -- but does not set the tone in the way that Shaw does at the other place. Stratford can theoretically do anything, though it doesnt have the resources of a year-round national theatre that could systematically explore the world repertoire. (The best national theatres are, in practice, international theatres.) The line-up this year is more eclectic than ever before with the addition, to Stratford's established three theatres (Festival, Avon and Tom Patterson) of a new Studio space, long wished for and now opened -- to everybody's amazed delight -- exactly on schedule. Its stage exactly mirrors the Festival Theatres wonderful platform, but its auditorium seats only 250. It was planned as a showcase for new plays and rare classics. Retrenchment in the wake of Sept. 11 has removed the second plank of that program from this years bill, but the new plays are there in force: seven of them, of which six -- including a piece by the late Timothy Findley -- are presented two at a time. As I write, only one of these double-bills has opened; and its first half, High-Gravel Blind, an inventive riff by actor-playwright Paul Dunn on themes noticed when he was acting in The Merchant of Venice, is an auspicious start.

Stratford looks a more liveable place than Niagara. It is also a more edible place, the site of Ontario's greatest culinary explosion. The pioneering Church Restaurant, which transformed a previously dismal gastronomic landscape in the 1970s, how has an array of competition: the beloved Old Prune, the world-class Rundles, Pazzo, which offers excellent pizzas in the basement and haute cuisine appropriately above, and -- hidden coyly away in a shoppingstrip -- the sparky new Bijou. Elevated food is less easy to come by in Niagara (though elevated prices aren't) but either town, judiciously sampled, offers all the elements of a good time.
