KARMA; POP SONG; VICIOUS LITTLE BOYZ IN THE RAIN

Theatre Passe Muraille Backspace, Toronto

Theatre Direct's Buncha Young Artists Having Their Say are continuing to have it, in varying formats and with varying results.

One play, Karma, by Ivana Shein, seems something of a makeweight. Its a duologue between a respectable managerial gentleman and the bike-courier with whom he claims to be in love. The play actually begins in the lobby with the two actors (Murray Farrow and Joris Jarsky) trying to pretend that they are members of the audience, the younger one not used to going to the theatre and the older one trying to reassure him. It doesn't work. Placed among us like that, they are very plainly actors, their voices too well projected, their sentences too well formed. Not even a graphic throwing-up sequence convinced us they were real.

Once in their natural habitat -- on stage, playing scenes -- they were far more convincing, but by then it was too late. Nor did their author give them, even then, anything very interesting to say or do; it was as if putting two people on a stage and having them spend a certain amount of time discussing an unestablished relationship was enough in itself. The school parties in the audience were not, as far as I could tell, buying it.

They reacted with warm recognition, though, to Pop Song, which immediately followed it.

Their elders, especially if they were parents, probably watched in frozen horror. Sean Reycraft's play is another duologue, far more solidly constructed and based on the well-publicized news story of a year or so back about the Toronto girl who gave birth at a school dance, disposed of the baby in the garbage, went back to the floor, and kept on rocking.

The play imagines a relationship that might have led up to this incident; it isnt brutal but it is, to anyone outside the culture, unnervingly casual. We follow the characters through a school year, from one half-hearted institutional hop to another. (Now we know whats wrong with the educational system. There are too many dances.) Each scene, as per the title, is played out to the accompaniment of a different song, and they probably have more thematic relevance than I was able to distinguish.

Between scenes, we hear each upcoming function announced, and the come-ons, as increasingly desperate organizers try to appeal to an increasingly apathetic student body, can be moderately hilarious. The main dialogue (apart from an unfortunate matched pair of flowery voice-overs) is blankly functional, but that presumably is the point. These are traditional teenage rituals -- the girl who wants to dance, the boy who would rather eat chips, the criss-crossing patterns of physical and emotional need -- played out in a new and more dangerous context; and it's the context that creates the effect. Kimwun Perehinec and Matthew MacFadzean make a very convincing couple; he in particular is so real that one wonders if he is just playing himself.

The remaining play, Vicious Little Boyz in the Rain, proves that he isn't; or at least that if he is, he can do very different things as well. He appears in this as a teenage gang-leader called Freak: a preening, swaggering, homoerotic and extremely articulate mixture of Peter Pan, Charles Manson, and the choirboy bully of Lord of the Flies.

The play begins with him and his three followers swarming and presumably killing a middle-aged man with a briefcase (Murray Farrow reprising his character from Karma in extreme straits). In Andrey Tarasiuks production, this comes over as the archetypal scene of urban menace. Tarasiuk runs Theatre Direct and has established standards of presentation as high as any in the city. This though is the first play I have seen that he has directed himself, and its a tour de force.

Marshalling five actors on a sand-strewn stage he confronts us with one bold image after another, while securing a flawless level of commitment from his cast. If they believe this strongly in Gil Garratt's play, then there must be something to it. So, as it turns out, there is; but it can be tough sledding getting there. Much of the writing is in a vein of heavily abstruse lyricism; you often yearn for a few declarative sentences that would explain just what is going on. (You do eventually get them, but rather late).

MacFadzean's Freak presides over a trio of Lost Boys -- there is an explicit reference to never wanting to grow up -- who are either riding a wave of destruction or, more probably, have unleashed it themselves; it is, on a frightening scale, the revenge of the neglected and abused. The gang are trying to fight their way to a magical city; one of their number, Buzz (played by Paul Dunn, whose solo play Boys -- yes, with an s, -- kicked off this season in high gear) was told about the place by his mother; he expects to find his lost dog and dead brother there.

When disillusion inevitably sets in, Stupid (Joris Jarsky) challenges Freak's leadership, with distressing results; either Stupid is living up to his name or he's transcending it. The remaining lost boy, Rain (Kimwun Perehinec), so named because of an affinity to wet weather of which there seems to be a lot, may actually be a Lost Girl; I blush to admit it but Im not sure whether we have here a female performer playing a male role or a female performer playing a female whos playing a male role, like the tomboyish Anybodys in West Side Story. (This is the second play in the season to recall that musical, especially its kaleidoscopic Officer Krupke number, and it does so more strongly than the first.) Rain is sent on a mission in which he/she finds either straight or lesbian love, which has to be abandoned on account of Freak's stern injunction: No Wendies. (I don't think its just that he prefers Big Macs.)

Anyway, when everything explodes, the threads come satisfyingly together, although Stupids repeated admonition -- "Look what we've become" -- seems, in the blood-soaked circumstances, somewhat obvious. The underlying truth seems to be that if you demonize people they may indeed become the demons you fear, but they won't necessarily be happy about it. The cast ride the lyric-heavy dialogue with impressive ease; MacFadzean who seems to be playing with a British accent, perhaps in homage to Barrie, exerts a scary command on eye and ear. Its a demanding play, and Im not sure that all its demands are justified, but like all Theatre Direct's work that I have seen, it displays a heartening trust in both itself and its audience.
