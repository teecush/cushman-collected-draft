Frog and Toad are great friends, but the amount of time they can spend together in a given year is limited by the fact that they spend nearly half of it hibernating. A Year with Frog and Toad, the charming American musical imported to the Lorraine Kimsa Theatre for Young People, takes their relationship from its spring re-awakening (to adapt a title already taken) to its winter hiatus.

I don't know the Arnold Lobel books on which the show is based, but I did grow up on The Wind in the Willows, with which these stories seem to have a considerable affinity. Theres no frog that I recall in the Kenneth Grahame book, but his Mr. Toad does have some points of contact with this one, mainly a touching self-consciousness; they both care what the world thinks about them, though this manifested itself in the older Toad as vanity and bravado, and in this one as shyness. The Frog-Toad relationship here seems to owe something to Grahames Rat and Mole, one of them protective, the other vulnerable; though Grahame's riparian rodents switched roles more adventurously than Lobel's woodland amphibians do.

Anyway, Frog and Toad live next door to one another, and the show begins with Frog preparing to give Toad his annual wake-up call. He knows his friends limitations ( hes not so good at sports / and of course, he has those warts") but loves him anyway. When he calls round chez Toad with the news that it's April, the reply he gets is not too encouraging ( "Come back when it's May), though if ever I saw audience empathy, I got it from the massed five-to-10-year-olds watching this production. It went double as Toad re-snuggled himself beneath the covers, and triple when, in a further fit of defiance, he smashed his alarm clock. This particular plot development enables Toad to be late for every subsequent date he makes in the show, on the grounds that he has no means of telling the time. Frogs reaction, mostly, is a tolerant smile. Though, when the shows second winter comes around, it does look as if he's about to spend it camping out at Toad's place rather than back in his own bed. Seems hes not about to take any more chances.

Louise Pitre plays Toad, and she is enchanting. The further she gets from the belting persona with which she first established herself, the better she becomes. This Toad is a worrier, who needs constant re-assurance. He gets scared when he finds himself all alone on a downward-hurtling sled. (Well, who wouldnt?) He gets embarrassed when he goes swimming, because he fears that he looks silly in a bathing suit -- and the supporting characters, mainly birds and turtles, agree that he does, in chorus. (I must say I was expecting a more encouraging pay-off to this number.) He also, in a moment that Pitre makes especially touching, gets sad at 10 o'clock every morning, because that's the time when the mail gets delivered. Or, in his case, doesnt: a revelation that goes straight to Frogs heart. The role of Frog was originally intended for Denis Simpson, who died just before rehearsals were about to start. Allen MacInnis, director of the show (and of the theatre) took over, and gives a very judicious and sympathetic performance. He sings well, too.

The show ran on Broadway and got a Tony nomination. This is surprising, since in scale and style it's very off-Broadway. The music by Robert Reale and the lyrics (by Willie Reale, who also wrote the book) are in the deceptively simple, actually sophisticated, style, jolly with melancholy undercurrents, that's been standard in those parts at least since The Fantasticks. Todays kids are unlikely to have heard much like it at home, but maybe it will be a civilizing influence. Some numbers do go on too long, as do some passages of script. The structure doesnt allow for much narrative development. It does, though, make intelligent use of repetition, in the shape of gags that don't so much run as amble. One of these involves snail mail, literally; Kevin Dennis gets to sing, proudly and delightfully, a recurring country song ( I'm the snail with the mail") about how incredibly, conscientiously fast hes going, a joke thats kept going through the show and even, in a cleverly understated touch, beyond. Jennifer Villaverde and Cara Hunter complete the cast as a variety of creatures, some of them with a mean streak that proves refreshing in a show that might otherwise overdose on sweetness and light. Diane Leah's musical direction, with herself at the piano and Tom Jestadt on drums, is first-rate. Robin Fisher's set and costumes, lit by Lesley Wilkinson, are beguilingly vernal. It may or may not be easy being green, but the results are certainly good to watch. A show that hinges on, and illustrates, the turn of the seasons connects to the roots of storytelling.
