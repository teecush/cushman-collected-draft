BOOK REVIEW But what about the music, Mel?

Thursday, December 29, 1988

ROBERT CUSHMAN

BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail MEL TORME is on his fourth marriage and his umpteenth recording contract. His life has been successful but, as you would guess from those statistics, checkered. His autobiography tells more about his marriages than about his music, which is a mistake.

Maybe that's how he wanted to write it. Maybe that's how he believes books are sold. Maybe that's what his publisher told him - in which case he has been manipulated by the book trade much as he claims to have been by the record business. Virtually the only recording executive for whom he has a good word is Carl Jefferson, who runs the modest and tasteful label known as Concord Jazz and who also happens to be Torme's current employer. Similarly, Torme's only good wife, by his account, is his present one.

IT WASN'T ALL VELVET

BY MEL TORME , Viking, 400 pages, $24.95 I'm being unfair. He dismisses his first two marriages (to a starlet and a fan) as immature mistakes, but shoulders much of the blame himself. His third venture was with British actress Janette Scott, a lightning love-match that ended for him in tears, scars and a losing battle for custody of their two children. That is his side of the story, and he tells it at length. Presumably Scott has her own version, and it would have rounded things out if Torme could have brought himself to summarize it. Maybe he really is the worst wronged husband in the civilized world, but he sure protests a lot.

Why? As he himself says, if you want showbiz scandals, there are far juicier ones available. His rationale is that he is a popular singer, pop songs are mainly about love, and so his feelings and experiences are relevant to his work. That might hold, if he told us more about his work: not just where he has done it and how much he has earned from it, but how he approaches it: what songs he sings, and how, and why.

To begin with, he does. He writes about growing up in Depression Chicago with his ears glued to the radio, so fixated on the big bands that by the age of 4 he could sing with one of them. Thus established as a prodigy he progressed to child acting on the air, and to singing and songwriting in high school. He almost went on the road with Harry James, and finally made it - as singer, composer and vocal arranger - with a band ostensibly fronted by Chico Marx but actually run by swing drummer Ben Pollack. (Which is appropriate, because Torme himself is a nifty drummer and only an average pianist.) Here he lost his professional and - about which he is quite funny - personal virginity. Going solo, he had a couple of years of postwar Hit Parade successes as a singer of moonstruck ballads, rendered with fanatical delicacy and sureness of pitch. He was all-American boyish in looks and voice, an image he maintained through half a dozen mostly forgotten films. Movie stardom eluded him, but he survived; he devotes an angry, compassionate couple of pages - in some ways, the best in the book - to those who did not: the Hollywood kids who became teen-age stars and were then unceremoniously beached.

He himself progressed by outgrowing the bobby sox and turning - or returning - to jazz. And this is where the story really starts; or should. In fact, it's where the music stops and the life and loves (not to mention the gun and car collections) take over. Some of the best vocal albums ever made are dispatched in a sentence or ignored altogether.

This is howlingly frustrating, not just because Torme can write (though he has an unfortunate addiction to the reconstructed conversations, outrageously fake and jaunty, that are the curse of U.S. biography) or even because he is intelligent; as he says, a large number of singers are. Many of them keep it under wraps, but with Torme, intelligence is what he has to sell as a performer; you can hear his brow furrowing in keen appreciation of the wit or the emotion he is transmitting. Musically he is an endless explorer, a consummate enthusiast. As an incorrigible singer- along-with-records I have found his instant key-changes and on-the-note gymnastics a bracing challenge.

He has lasted better than almost any singer of a generation that was reared on jazz and Broadway standards and dethroned by rock. He has cut his small share of sell-out albums (covers of pop numbers better done by others) and he rails now at the record bosses who forced him into it: the CBS rep, for example, who looked forward to a Mel Torme album you didn't have to listen to, an Andy Williams or Johnny Mathis sort of album.

Norman Granz of Verve may, as he says, be no gentleman, but he still presided over two of Torme's consummate collaborations with arranger- conductor Marty Paich, who brought the cool, spare Gerry Mulligan sound to vocal accompaniment and rejuvenated the genre. The Torme-Paich partnership has just been renewed, after 25 needlessly dormant years, with a reunion album on Concord, and it exhilarates. Joy and precision spill off the record, the singer unmistakably back where he belongs.

I want to know more about Torme and Paich and the nuts and bolts of how they operated. I want to know more of Torme's sense of his own position in musical and showbiz history, subjects of which he plainly has an excellent grasp. I want to know how Torme feels about his peers and predecessors, who influenced him and how. He says he gave up criticizing other singers after getting a murder threat from an Elvis Presley fan, but he is obviously bursting with opinions. I want to know about Mountain Greenery, a song he has featured since having a surprise British hit with it in 1956. He has now recorded it four times, all live, all different. That must mean something. I want, in short, another book. Maybe he'll write it.
