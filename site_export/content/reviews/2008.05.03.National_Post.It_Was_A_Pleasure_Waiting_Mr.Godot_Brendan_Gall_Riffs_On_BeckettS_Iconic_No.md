And now the guy youve all been waiting for. In Brendan Gall's play Alias Godot, Alon Nashman plays the mystery man himself, the emblematic figure of non-appearance now made manifest. As befits a refugee from the universe of Samuel Beckett, he wears a bowler hat, though one in a better state of repair than you would expect to find on a Beckett tramp; the same applies to the white shirt and dark suit that complete his ensemble. He is, after all, Mr. Godot, a figure, or so we have always been led to believe, of some authority. He also wears handcuffs, since he's being detained in a dilapidated New York cop shop. This is far from any territory you might expect him to inhabit, a fact of which he is uncomfortably aware. There are people far away who expect his presence and, as he tells the two officers whove pulled him in, If I dont arrive quickly, they're likely to endow my absence with undue significance."

He has a mysterious package to deliver, which these days sounds sinister. So think the detectives, uncommonly jittery guys whose names, Vincent and Edward, are at least within shouting distance of Beckett's Vladimir and Estragon. They are joined, or rather invaded (twice, once in each act) by their own Pozzo and Lucky --here called Rocko and Linus -- whose powers outrank those of the police since theyre members of a Terrorism Unit, though as Godot, who has an extremely meticulous mind, points out, that should really be an Anti-Terrorism Unit. They seem grateful for the information.

Gall is divided into two parts, or at least his play is. The part that works is not so much a pastiche of the Beckettian model as a series of riffs on it, and it is hilarious. The other half is an examination of American, and by extension global, paranoia post-9/11, a subject on which it lacks anything new to say. In practice, the dislocation isnt that severe; the two modes overlap and the goodwill generated by the first carries over at least partway into the second. Also, Richard Rose has delivered a superb production; the director and performers paper over all the cracks except for the intentional ones in Teresa Przbylski's brilliantly bleak set, a severe frame on which everything looks good, including the actors' shadows.

Nashman plays the incarcerated Godot with a modest insistence that adds up to a peculiar kind of authority: prissiness so sure of itself, if not of its immediate circumstances, that it approaches the numinous. No wonder he makes his captors feel nervous. The very first thing he says, wonderfully linking the world hes come from to the world hes in, is "I was told I have the right to remain silent." He may have been read his rights, but that doesn't mean he's going to get them. Hes at the mercy of Bad Cop and Dumb Cop. As Victor, the bad one, David Ferry, his hair standing up on his head in a permanent state of greasy alert, gives what may be the performance of his life: The aggression that has seemed forced in some of his recent George Walker performances, here flows freely, set off as it is by the nervousness of a man whos his own ticking bomb scenario. He's permanently at the end of a very frayed tether.

Paul Braunstein's Edward is a good-natured soul adrift who, like his distant cousin Estragon, keeps getting beaten up by unseen gangs: in his case, youthful marauders feasting on what appears to be the corpse of a formerly great metropolis. Among the many other jazzed-up echoes from the source are a coat rack that sprouts leaves and Mr. Godots self-furnished job description as a farmer specializing in sheep and goats. Also, when Rocko and Linus appear, they keep falling over.

If the aggressiveness of Tony Nappos Rocco sometimes seems overdone, consider that the same criticism has been levelled at many Pozzos before him. Geoffrey Pounsett's Linus inherits from his role-model a virtuoso first-act diatribe crammed with verbal garbage -- in this case, it's a montage of police-state protocols, and the one point at which the old and new plays do achieve a kind of symbiosis -- and a second act spent in total silence: also in drag, which is a step beyond Europes Uncle Sam, if not Americas. The interaction between the five characters -- partly carried over, partly new -- has great theatrical impact, certainly in a production as charged with the right kind of energy as this one is.

Actually, the play has yet a third level, as police-procedural, real or parodied, but here it speedily runs out of gas. There's an offstage figure referred to as Jimmy Nicknames, a sort of Godot's Godot; but if Godot was obscure, Jimmy is simply confusing. The play moves towards an apocalyptic finish that it simply hasnt earned. Like Becketts first postwar, Cold War audiences, we bring our own existential dread into the theatre with us; but where his play distilled it into something new and personal, this one just plays with what we already know. People said the same thing 40 years ago about Tom Stoppard's Rosencrantz and Guildenstern Are Dead, another self-confessed Beckett knock-off, but one that also had the thickly populated landscape of Hamlet to draw from. It kept strictly and fruitfully to the rules of the Shakespearean plot. Gall has to invent his own, which proves paradoxically constricting. Also, he isn't as funny as Stoppard. But hes plenty funny, and the play, his first at full-length, is the most scintillating debut weve had this season. - Alias Godot runs through June 1 at the Tarragon.
