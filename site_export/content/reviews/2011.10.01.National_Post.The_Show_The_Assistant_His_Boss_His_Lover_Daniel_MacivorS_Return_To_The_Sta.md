The most enjoyable Daniel MacIvor plays have usually been those in which he has appeared himself. This, in recent memory, has meant that they have been monologues. However, his new play, entitled His Greatness, gives us not just the acting but the interacting MacIvor, functioning - excellently - as one equal member of a cast of three. It's very refreshing.

As is the play itself. It's a more or less realistic piece whose structure and development are almost defiantly traditional. So is its subject; it belongs to the comparatively recent but still proliferating genre of the artist-and-amanuensis drama. Unlike most of its kind, however, it isn't just a biographical retrospective thinly disguised as a play. It tells a present-tense story, and it has that all-important third wheel.

The play is billed as "a potentially true story about the playwright Tennessee Williams" though he's never given that or any other name in the text. He's simply The Playwright, ailing and alcoholic and with his greatest successes far behind him. He's in a hotel in Vancouver, awaiting the premiere of his latest play (actually the revision of an old one) on the heels of its failure in London. The play's name isn't mentioned either but it has to be The Red Devil Battery Sign, which was done in just those circumstances at the Vancouver Playhouse in 1980. That production featured a young actor named Richard Donat who, 30 years later, is now appearing as the dramatist. Mac Ivor plays The Assistant, once the playwright's lover, now a combination of secretary, banker and nurse. Or Head Cook and BottomWiper. The play begins with the assistant, in what is obviously a familiar late-afternoon routine, awakening his patron from the effects of a drunken night spent insulting and/or trying to seduce the members of his Canadian production team.

Reminiscences and recriminations ensue, as does the playwright's decree that they cannot possibly attend that evening's first night together and that the assistant will have to find him a suitable escort. The assistant, not too plausibly given that discretion is supposed to be the object, picks out a young man who's actually a rent boy from Newfoundland. The young man, who claims optimistically to be 21, knows almost nothing about plays and absolutely nothing about this particular playwright, but the assistant briefs him on what to say (i.e. as little as possible) and how to behave. The instant protege, who had assumed he was there for just one thing or maybe two, coke being the second, sets about playing his new acquaintances off against one another, with some theoretical success.

That's pretty much the first act and it's very neat and racy if a touch hackneyed. The playwright, lolling around in his dressing gown while flicking a forked tongue at everyone within reach, comes over as The Man Who Came to Dinner with a Southern accent. In its first half, the play skates merrily over the themes of failure and humiliation; in its second, while keeping its lightness of touch, it highlights and explores them. First night applause had persuaded the playwright that he had a hit, his first in decades; the next morning's papers tell him different. The young man, still very much around, is cunningly used as a lightning rod. "Is that bad?" he asks of one critique that has used the word "fiasco"; he's informed that, compared to the only other review to hand, it's a rave.

The young man, played by Greg Gale with a blend of aggression and naivete, has some dogs of his own in this fight. He'd been thrilled by the play, it being only the second one he'd ever seen and him being the playwright's date; what's more, his previous dream of becoming a porn star has now been trumped by the playwright's promise to write him a vehicle (a word with which the young man has some trouble) of his own. The assistant, of course, has heard that one before.

The rich achievement of MacIvor's script, and of Ed Roy's production, is to put three fleshand-blood people onstage and to make us feel the situation, simultaneously, as it affects each one of them. The phrase "his greatness," a mocking honorific, gets applied to each of them in turn, in descending registers of sarcasm. Donat, playing, after all, a man he once knew, gives us a wounded, thrashing sea lion, equally desperate for pity and praise and nasal spray. The play cleverly echoes the reminiscing structure of The Glass Menagerie, and there are moments when the playwright, meeting a potentially kind stranger, comes on very much like Blanche DuBois in Streetcar. It's been a long time since either of those two triumphs, or even since The Night of the Iguana, but this is still the man who wrote them and who can never forget it. Neither, for better and worse, can anyone else. In the first act, the playwright gives a phone interview and insults the interviewer; in the second, we hear her radio review, which concentrates not on how bad the play is but on how wonderful its author used to be. That must hurt a lot. When the young man puts the familiar question, "Who are they to judge you?" any critic in the audience is likely to blench.

"You think it's easy to write a play?" laments the unbuttoned Tennessee fella (from St. Louis), earning from his very buttoned assistant the disdainful comeback, "As if you'd remember." But then the assistant, we learn, had once wanted to be an actor; which is all very meta, given that his creator and impersonator is actor and writer both. The assistant is seemingly trapped in his own endgame, always about to leave his employer who's confident that he'll always return. The play's last speech closes the theatrical gap still further; like a Shakespearean epilogue it places itself, gently but firmly, at the audience's mercy. Kimberly Purtell has designed and lit what must be the solidest and handsomest set ever erected at the Factory Studio, and the production is the debut offering of the Independent Artists Repertory Theatre, a group composed of this play's author, director, production manager and publicists.
