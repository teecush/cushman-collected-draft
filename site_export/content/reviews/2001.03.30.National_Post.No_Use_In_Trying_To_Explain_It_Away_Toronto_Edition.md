ICARIA

The Great Hall, Toronto

The title Icaria suggests a re-telling of the Greek myth about the boy who defied his more careful father by flying too close to the sun and plunging to his death -- and that in fact is what it is. That at least is part of what it is. The boy's fate is mixed into a whole stew of family conflicts that are presented to us in scrambled and foreshortened form, not always comprehensibly. Icaria is devised theatre with a vengeance: technically skilled, sometimes evocative, often impenetrable.

It is the work of Number Eleven Theatre, a new group that grew out of a long-standing Winnipeg experimental troupe called Primus Theatre. Number Eleven is based in Toronto, but this show was prepared in Nova Scotia -- in Halifax, where it premiered, and in the small town of Granville Ferry, which seems to have left its mark. It has a very Maritime or just very Celtic flavour. Its four characters -- father, mother, son and daughter -- will tell one another folk tales with the slightest provocation and sing folk songs with no provocation whatever.

The creation of the show is credited jointly to its director, Ker Wells, and its four actors. The program lists 13 separate literary sources, but since the play lasts only 65 minutes, they can only be present in very compressed form. Their coherence, and that of the play itself as one scene melts into the next, is difficult to make out. The trouble with a dream play is that when anything goes, nothing counts.

The action is presented to us through the memory of Daphne (Elizabeth Rucker), the daughter, and it culminates in the suicide of Thomas (Alex McLean), the son. No motive is given, but we are at least invited to trace it back to a fight that Thomas once had with Pat (Varrick Grimes), his fisherman father.

Thomas says he wants to fly, citing historical precedents of people who almost made it. His father, home from the sea and wanting his tea, is unsympathetic, pointing out that "humans dont have the same muscles as birds." (This line has stuck in my mind as the simplest and most persuasive declarative sentence uttered in the course of the play.) While conducting this argument, they are performing aggressive acrobatics -- dad is a whiz at the old forward roll -- and stalking one another round the stage as if in a duel, only brandishing chairs instead of swords.

The correspondence to the Icarus myth is not precise. Icarus father, Daedalus, didn't try to stop his son from flying; in fact, he taught him how. He merely wanted him to pursue a sensible flight path. Here, though, it seems to have been elevated into a generalized image of paternal repression -- a symptom of everything that's wrong between the generations.

Later on, Pat appears on stilts to chase his son around the stage. There was a similar image of towering paternal terror in Ronnie Burketts puppet play Street of Blood, but it was far more effective there, partly because it was more imaginatively and economically done and partly because we knew enough about the characters to care.

The women have their tensions, too. Mother (Jane Wells), the only character given a function but not a name, tells her daughter a baleful tale and then hands her an outsize pair of scissors. We assume she wants her hair cut, but it looks more as though Daphne will stick the instrument into the back of the maternal neck.

Furniture and props in fact are a constant source of friction in this household. The family dinner table is an especially ominous place, though it also yields one moment of authentic magic: The tablecloth is whirled in the air, then unfolded and set on the ground -- suddenly we are at a picnic. At one point, we flash back to the parents wedding: the happy couple on stilts while their unborn children sing folk songs. At another, the two ladies do the keening while the men fight. The singing is fine, but the scenes have "experimental theatre," of a rather traditional kind, written all over them.

I was irreverently reminded of Coming Soon, the hilarious British comedy series shown recently on the Women's Television Network. In it, an intense London theatre group, in thrall to various fashionable prophets including our own Robert Lepage, go off to Scotland to prepare a new piece so they can qualify for a Scottish grant. (They failed the test for a lesbian grant.) Once there, they have to come up with something Scottish. Industriously researching the available folklore, and not being inhibited by any desire to communicate with their audience, they come up with what seems to be a play about the wind.

Icaria is a lot better than that; the people involved in it are accomplished (though Grimes doesn't have the weight to be a frightening father, even in this shows abstract terms), their motives are surely above reproach, and they obviously want to communicate. They dont much, though -- at least not with me. I can see that they know and mean what they're doing, and I liked their occasional lyricism when I wasn't feeling ground down by their portentousness. But for all the industrious application of local colour, there seems no particular reason for it to be going on where it does except a vague belief that these characters, being close to the water, must also be close to the earth.

It's no news, even in the theatre, that suicides are ultimately inexplicable. Square old Arthur Miller taught us that years ago in Death of a Salesman, but he rooted his play firmly so that at least we wanted to know. Icaria bequeathes us no more than a vague feeling that terrible things happen even in the best-intentioned families, leaving the mourners to mourn. It isn't enough.
