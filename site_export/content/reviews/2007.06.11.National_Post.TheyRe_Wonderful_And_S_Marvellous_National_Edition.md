In 1927, there was a Broadway musical called Funny Face that was in terrible trouble on the road but, after drastic surgery, came in as a hit. In 1983, there was a Broadway musical called My One and Only that was in terrible trouble on the road but, after drastic surgery, came in as a hit. Both these shows had scores by George and Ira Gershwin. In fact, My One and Only started out as something between a revival and an adaptation of Funny Face.

Something else: Each of these shows starred the top Broadway dancer of its day. Funny Face had Fred Astaire, playing not so much opposite as alongside his sister Adele. My One and Only had the towering Tommy Tune, piquantly matched with the diminutive Twiggy. Tune, a superb razzle-dazzler of a choreographer, also took over the direction, and his staging, plus his ruthless overhaul of the show's book, are what made it work. All the same, what arrived in New York was a rewrite of a rewrite of a rewrite of something that, even in 1927, had been regarded as awful. Its new success was a triumph of personality. It could hardly be duplicated. When Stratford announced a revival, I thought Stratford was mad.

As Ira Gershwin himself might have put it, I couldn't have been wronger. From its opening sequence -- three debonair male performers, hoofing and scatting through I Can't Be Bothered Now -- the show is a knockout, a Stratford musical that delights as a musical rather than as a classical company having fun. Indeed the joy starts even earlier, with Berthold Carriere's big festival band sounding jazzier, or at least brighter and more syncopated, than it ever has before, as it bounds through the Gershwin selection that constitutes the overture. Gershwin and dancing, the overwhelming bulk of it tap, make this show go.

How much Michael Lichtefeld's direction and choreography owe to Tune's, I couldn't say, but they're exhilarating just the same. It isn't just the steps, which are wonderfully executed by everyone. It's the way the steppers look in the extravagant hues of David Boechler's extravagant costumes. And it's the way both clothes and people move against the clean geometric lines of Douglas Paraschuk's Art Deco sets which, taking their inspiration from Mondrian, pay an abstractionist's homage to pre-Depression New York. For, despite everything, the show retains the original's time and place, or at least the idea of them.

It also has a story; not much of one, but enough. Billy Buck Chandler from Texas falls in love with Edythe Herbert from England. She's famous for having swum the Channel; he wants to be famous for flying the Atlantic but is thwarted by the kind of mechanical complications known in the trade as Second Act Problems.

The lovers get to wander as far afield as Morocco and a desert island that turns out to be Staten. That's all there is to the plot, really, barring a bit of blackmail that isn't too interesting. The score retains five songs from the original Funny Face but is otherwise Gershwin from all over. It's chary, though, of becoming a parade of greatest hits --making it superior to the later, similar and better-known Crazy for You, which had an equally inconsequential book, but far more of it. There may not be much substance in My One and Only, but there is drama. It's in the dancing and the music that summon up excitement, urbanity and loopy romance, and songs and choreography that are always right for the moment and the people.

The leads don't have the vertical tension of Twiggy and Tune, but they're excellent. They also, like the whole show, demonstrate the versatility of tap. Cynthia Dale, honouring her predecessor with traces of Cockney, vocalizes a bittersweet ballad, while seemingly unaware that her feet are continuing to do their own unpreventable thing. It's nice work if you can get it; which happens to be the song she's singing.

Laird Mackintosh is all impeccable impetuosity. His finest hour, maybe the show's, is the title number. It comes as a challenge-duet with Mark Cassius as a certain Mr. Magic who's teaching him to be a smooth yet ardent wooer and has finally to get out of his chair (something he very rarely does) to demonstrate. What follows is bliss, with a narrative charge. David W. Keeley, harmlessly sinister, and Dayna Tekatch, perkily persistent, are a secondary couple who's dialogue doesn't help them much, but who finally get to do a number (the original Funny Face) that's worth waiting for. The only number that disappoints, here as on Broadway, is the finale Kickin' the Clouds Away, simply because the tune itself falls short. If they'd asked me for a Gershwin mock-spiritual, I'd have suggested Clap Yo' Hands. But they didn't, and it's 24 years too late now.

There's a difference between the dancing here and that in Oklahoma! that tells as much about the shows themselves and the times that gave birth to them as about these productions. The rambunctious production numbers in Oklahoma! look like hard work, and win our applause for it. The performers (largely the same ones) in My One and Only bewitch us by making it look easy. They look, too, as if they could keep it up forever. I can't recall having as much fun at a Stratford musical. - My One and Only is in repertoire until Oct. 28.
