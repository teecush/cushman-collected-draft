THEATRE REVIEW

Zadie's Shoes Factory Theatre, Toronto

Adam Pettle's Zadie's Shoes was first seen at the Factory in 2001, and was successful enough to be given a commercial run, in essentially the same production the following year. Two major stagings in the same city make it, by common definition, a Canadian classic. It's now received a third, wholly new production back at the theatre where it started. That should make it a Canadian masterpiece.

It isn't quite that. Despite some trims and rewrites, it remains what it always was: a richly enjoyable play with some holes in it. The current revival is co-directed by the author and his brother, Jordan Pettle, who appeared in the original in the leading role of Benjamin. Benjamin is TorontoJewish and a compulsive gambler, like his father and grandfather (or "zadie") before him; the latter also bequeathed the play its title by losing his shoes in a bet while en route to Canada from the old country. Benjamin is currently in deep hock, and is trying to get out of it by the traditional gambler's route of gambling some more. He snaffles some money from his gentile girlfriend Ruth (Patricia Fagan), a cancer patient. Benjamin genuinely loves Ruth, and of course he means to pay her back with enormous interest. She, naturally, doesn't see it that way.

Ready to try anything, Benjamin, for the first time since his bar mitzvah, wanders into a synagogue where he meets Eli, an old man who seems to know all about him. This may be because he has prophetic powers; it may also be that he's just wise in the ways of the world and especially in those of gambling addicts, since he's one himself.

I'd forgotten just how deep and funny the scenes between Ben and Eli actually are. Joe Cobden as Ben and Harry Neiken, a wonderful Eli, by turns encouraging and disparaging, make a classic double act. Pettle (Adam, not Jordan) writes the best Jewish dialogue of any Canadian playwright.

He doesn't, for better or worse, write it just for his Jewish characters. Ruth has two sisters, and the three of them indulge in the same kind of barbed, quick-fire banter. Ruth's sisters were the weakest link in the two earlier productions and, though they're better played now than on either previous occasion, they still are. Beth (Lisa Ryder) is a curling champion, fanatically preparing for her next contest; Lily (Shannon Perreault) is an allpurpose flake, swinging from one counterculture to the next. But because they come into the play a little late, they seem like distractions from the main issue, rather than enrichments of it.

The two central roles are very well taken. Cobden's Benjamin has as much charm as Pettle (Jordan, not Adam) and more stature and technical command. But it swings and roundabouts; his slitherings into temptation are less credible than his predecessor's, and the play's climax that much less powerful.

Jackie Chau's set is less atmospheric than the original's arrangement of bleachers, but more practical. Several of the cast are more familiar in Winnipeg, Calgary and Vancouver than they are here. There often seems to be an impenetrable wall between the theatres of East and West. We're all the richer for its being broken down.
