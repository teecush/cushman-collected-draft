The Disaster -- sorry, Discovery -- Channel has a new season of Mayday, devoted to notable airplane crashes. It starts tonight with what is, from a Canadian perspective, the most notable of all: the 1986 destruction of Air India Flight 182 with 329 people, mainly Canadian citizens, on board.

This isn't exactly an under-told story, and there is something catchpenny about using it to launch a season, as there is about the overall title of the series itself. But the program itself is serious and informative, though it's a strange combination of dramatized reconstruction and investigative documentary.

The dramatized parts are inevitably the least successful. Since this was a catastrophe with no survivors, we can only have brief generic impressions (and the show, to its credit, does keep them brief) of what went on among the passengers. Conversation in the cockpit is reproduced, presumably in a literal reconstruction of the last recorded moments from the planes black box, retrieved from 6,000 feet below the surface of the ocean.

More questionable is the reenactment of the airport check-in scene in which a belligerent customer, known to history only as M. Singh, browbeats a harried clerk into sending his baggage through, despite his not possessing the appropriate ticket. The bag of course contains the bomb, and he isnt going with it. Maybe the scene had to be shown, but is it necessary for the angry non-passenger to be quite such an obvious caricature of a terrorist? And did the unfortunate agent have to be not only portrayed but named? We are told that she's had to live ever since with "her eternal sadness and heartbreak." Isn't that enough?

The Toronto-Bombay flight was approaching Ireland when it went down. We get scenes with the Shannon Airport aircraft controllers as the plane disappears from their radar, and a present-day interview with one of them. With all allowances made for the passage of time, the real and the acted personae hardly match up. But this ushers us into the interesting part of the film, which is an unusually intense, complex and meticulous police procedural. Canadian, British and Indian officials are all involved. Air Indias chief investigator, a Mr. H.S. Khola, spells out with a true sleuths delight the reasons why the black box was crucial to determining whether the crash was due to sabotage or mechanical malfunction: If there's any mechanical problem, the crew will be talking about the problem, they will not be silent about the problem. We hear this for ourselves in a twice-played scene. One moment the captain and his co-pilot are exchanging humdrum information about insurance seals, the next -- well, there isn't a next moment. Thats when theres silence. The computers went out, along with everything else on board.

It had to be "a devastating decompression or a bomb." The film rather gives the impression that the possibility of sabotage was only entertained after days, maybe weeks, of weighing the alternatives. In fact, it was already being alleged on the day of the disaster, with two organizations -- one of them, the Sikh extremist group that was eventually found guilty -- claiming responsibility. But it still had to be proved, with evidence from wreckage reclaimed from the bottom of the sea. A British salvage veteran, used to dealing with smaller craft, talks about the challenging of retrieving the shattered, scattered fragments of a jumbo jet.

Luckily -- if that's a permissible word in this context -- he found the right ones. Against comparably daunting odds, the RCMP tracked down -- via an electronics store in the small town of Duncan, B.C. -- the inoffensive stereo tuner that held the bomb. (It could hold just four sticks of dynamite, enough, in the right circumstances, to bring down an airliner: You don't need much.)

After the device that held the bomb came the suitcase that held the device. The pro-gam actually starts with the information, chilling at the best of times, that "every day, millions of pieces of luggage arrive at airports across the world," going on to advise us that "one bag led to an investigation unique in the history of aviation" -- rather a reductive view of a story that, on the films own showing, was a lot more complicated. Anyway, the resultant 20-year criminal investigation isnt really the concern here. It's about mechanics, including the information that the bombing led to the invention of new system for firewalling cargo holds that proved too costly to implement. The human cost can, in this context, be taken for granted and, for most of the film's length, it sensibly is.

The narration does pause to tell us, in dramatic tones, that the destruction of the passenger jet was a crime. But I think we knew that. - Mayday:Air India: Explosive Evidence airs tonight at 10 p.m. on the Discovery Channel.
