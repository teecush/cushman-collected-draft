Theatre Review

Good Bones

Tarragon Extra Space, Toronto

I have admired Clare Coulter in plays that were one-woman shows and in plays that, to judge from the performances of the other actors, would have been better as one-woman shows. Good Bones is not a play, and that's a drawback, but it is a formidably accomplished solo performance, one that takes a collection of literary texts, and gets a character out of them.

The texts are taken from two collections of prose vignettes by Margaret Atwood: Good Bones and Murder in the Dark. They are, as the title indicates, moderately chilling.

The sequence begins with a gleeful reminiscence of a childs experiments in mixing poison: something, we are told, thats dear to the hearts of all the young. By the end, the focus has widened into a genteel vision of apocalypse.

Coulter delivers the material quietly but with the utmost clarity. She is fastidious and bemused: somebody who might quizzically raise her eyebrows at the absurdities and cruelties of life but would never do anything so crude as to burst out laughing. She begins the evening perched on a long kitchen table, one leg over the edge, the other drawn up on the surface. You might say she was hugging herself, but she's too fine-drawn for that; it's more as if shes marshalling her forces. Later, though her movements are still contained, she begins to roam the stage, taking possession of her world.

Tone, stance, and setting are quintessentially middle-class: urban with memories of rural. Ken Garnhum has designed a cottage set with visual reverberations; its comfort is pierced when panels open up in the walls to reveal wilderness landscapes that the script seems to regard with a mixture of yearning and terror. Bonnie Beechers lighting makes a major contribution; it starts out magically dappled, and in its darker aspects it's downright frightening.

Coulter's collaborators, Urjo Kareda directing, have given her every support, and she triumphantly makes the words flesh. Or, if I may take a hint from the piece that gives the show its title and that concerns the eviscerating effects of age, she makes them bone.

It is, all the same, not a very invigorating evening. Part of the reason, for me, is that Atwoods style of genteel lyricism drives me up the wall. Another is that the material is stubbornly undramatic. Coulter has no overarching story to tell, as she did in Wallace Shawns The Fever, and she never seems to meet anyone, even in her imagination.We are stuck throughout with the same omniscient mandarin voice.

We seize with relief on a couple of passages that do involve dialogue and that allow us to laugh, perhaps more vigorously than the material really merits, but you know what audiences are. One -- part of a sequence on The Female Body -- is an argument between two parents on the advisability of buying their daughter a Barbie doll. (She's for. He's against.)

The other, taking us further into the thickets of political correctness, imagines Cinderellas author up against an implacably right-on editor. No, you cant say she was a poor girl, how could she be poor when she had a real fireplace to clear the ashes out of? (I leave you to imagine what they do with the word girl. Unhappily, the interrogation doesn't last long enough to take in the Fairy Godmother, let alone Prince Charming.)

It's not exactly a new joke, but Atwood and Coulter pursue it with engaging implacability. Indeed, they dig it to its roots. How dare the author begin her story with "once when all that's really important is now"?

Seen in this perspective, the evening's pervasive nostalgia takes on the air of a ferocious rearguard action. Its serenity is doggedness in disguise.
