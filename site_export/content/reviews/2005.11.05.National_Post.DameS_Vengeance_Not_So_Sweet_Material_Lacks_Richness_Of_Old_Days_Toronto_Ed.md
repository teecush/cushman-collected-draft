Dame Edna Everage is a star, and you are not. That is her message, now and forever. All the same, now that she's Back With a Vengeance, I fear she's slackened off. When I first saw the Dame, she was far from the megastar she has since become. I know people whose memories go back even further than that, to when she made her London debut as plain Mrs. Everage from Melbourne, determined to make it in show business and to be accepted as an idol among idols, and to do it by the simplest means of all: by standing on a stage and telling us all about herself. Hers were the dreams of an everyday housewife: pure stardom, celebrity in its most concentrated form. These days we take her surname for granted. We forget that there was once a time when, given the appropriate Australian twang, it actually meant something.

She had of course to make compromises on the way up. She had to sing and dance, like any run-of-the-mill trouper. She also had to tell jokes, but that can never have been a hardship, since her humour depends on her certain knowledge that we, her public, will be fascinated by whatever details of her life she deigns to share with us. She in turn has taken a kindly and increasingly persistent interest in the details of ours, especially of those of us who are married and female. She remains in essence a housewife talking solicitously to other housewives; it's probably the secret of her success.

Over the years, like any artist, she has kept refining her craft, but she may now have refined it too much. She has a new and cozy rapport with her pianist, but her musical routines are beyond perfunctory and beyond tacky. I realize that that is supposed to be the point, but they are also beyond functioning as parody. The spoken parts of the show consist almost entirely of Dame Edna interrogating selected members of her audience or, as she puts it, "empowering" them. Edna prophesied what Oprah wrought, and Edna can still out-wreak her.

Now, her victims have asked for it; at least they've purchased tickets near the front of the theatre, which is nearly, though not quite, as rash as arriving late for an Everage show, something that gives you a 100% chance of being singled out. (The first night's statutory latecomer claimed that he had come from the City Hall parking lot. The Dame pointed out that she'd come all the way from Australia, and she'd made it on time.) Most of them are happy to play along.

As ever, she retains total recall of those she has selected, of their names and what they have confessed. And she keeps returning to them throughout the evening, never letting go. This is impressive, but it was more dazzling in the days when she interwove these interjections with more elaborate dissertations on the follies and foibles of her near-peers among the world's famous (always so humble in asking for her advice) or indeed among her own family: "homeopathic" Kenny or prostate-stricken Norm or perpetual bridesmaid Madge. She more or less confesses that she has nothing left to say about this latter group, which is understandable, but she has nothing to take their place. Gone, too, are the rich ruminations with which she favoured us last time she was here, on modish words like "infrastructure" and "arugula." Her show, in fact, lacks texture. Even the ceremonial waving of the gladioli is an anti- climax. It doesn't have anything to top.

She can still be hilarious, in ways that are hers alone: a creation apparently independent of her creator. (It becomes increasingly hard, in reviewing Dame Edna, to mention the name "Barry Humphries" without feeling stupid.) She retains her magnificent improvisatory command of the English language: "Is your soap taupe?" she asked a lady who had admitted to having a bathroom decorated in that colour, which can't often happen. And, to the customary waves to the paupers in the balconies, she now adds a judicious comment on the few empty seats. They were purchased in advance by subscribers, who are now dead. What other explanation could there be?

Visiting Dame Edna and the new Second City show in the same week, I became aware of a new theatrical rule. When a married couple is inveigled onstage, as happened at both entertainments the nights I was present, the husband will always be called Michael. Also, audience participants have become increasingly talented. The Second City's Mike and his wife, roped into a scene about someone else's marriage, showed themselves to be expert mimes. A lady cast as Dame Edna's mother in her final skit burst into song, and tunefully, to the Dame's noticeable displeasure.

Second City: Reloaded is the troupe's first revue in their new and cozier space opposite the old one. It's also one of their best shows, with a manic quality I have never seen from them before. One number finds the cast running round the auditorium like slavering dogs. Anand Rajaram, who is especially good at it, returns at unexpected intervals to slaver some more. He also gets the show off to a terrific start as a Sikh taxi driver who discomfits his Toronto passenger by revealing a Scots accent, and helps with a terrific close-to-finish as an Arab pizza-maker suspected by his customer (Matt Baram, a walking nervous breakdown) of having mingled parts of the previous owner in with the pepperoni. This is the show's take on terrorism, fear of.

Another thing: Improv, previously confined to a separate slot at the end of the evening, now crops up within the show itself. Paul Bates pounds out heartfelt (by him and us) letters of protest to Telus Mobility and Rogers Cable, and then takes suggestions for further indignant missives; one to Highway 407 really inspired him.

There are flaws. If there are ways of being funny about either Karla Homolka or breast cancer, which I doubt, this show hasn't found them. Nor has Second City ever been much good at musical numbers. There are two here, placed back to back, as if to get them over with. Back on the credit side place a number where the cast go orgiastic and anti-environmental at the sight of a Hummer, and a bit set at an Argos game whose changes of mood require real acting, and get it.
