MY FAIR LADY

Festival Theatre, Stratford, Ont.

To quote, unfairly, from the current musical at the other festival, the one at Niagara-on-the-Lake: "I saw My Fair Lady/I kind of enjoyed it." Actually, I thoroughly enjoyed it. I always do, once I'm there. I can never, though, get excited in advance about seeing it, and its accepted status as the Great American Musical makes me uneasy.

It isn't the greatness I question, it's the American-ness. Alan Jay Lerner and Frederick Loewe who wrote it were so un-American in style and subject matter (their other big titles were Brigadoon, Camelot and Gigi) they were lucky not to have been investigated by a committee. That is not a criticism of them; they were entitled to write how, and of what, they liked; and God knows they did it well. But it did cut them off from the vernacular energy, the slang and the jazz, that made many of us fall in love with the American musical in the first place; especially, perhaps, if we're not Americans ourselves.

My Fair Lady is based on a great English comedy, Shaw's Pygmalion, that is not just steeped in the class system (like most English comedies) but is explicitly about it. Its original Broadway production starred four English actors. You could make a case for it being the Great British Musical, transplanted at birth by some mischievous fairy.

Except, of course, that no Britons could have written or produced it, then or later. It may not have the American accent, but it does have the American know-how, the confidence of craftsmen at the height of their powers working in a form that was similarly situated. What it does is brilliantly brazen. You are supposed, if you want to turn a play into a musical, to reinvent it wholly; otherwise, why bother? My Fair Lady, however, retains extensive quantities of Shaw's dialogue, and sticks songs into it. This ought to be redundant: certainly irritating, probably boring. In fact, it's perfect. Lerner edited Shaw's text superbly, and seemed to know instinctively how and when to interrupt it. To early audiences, it must have been astonishing; to us, it just seems inevitable.

The show has a secret weapon. It's the best romantic musical ever written, but its romance is against the grain. Shaw himself denied that the tale of the professor of phonetics and the flower girl whom he teaches to speak like a lady was really a love story; but no audience has ever believed him. My Fair Lady finds its own place in the world by making real everybody's secret suspicions about Pygmalion. (One side effect of this is that the two pieces have been able to co-exist. Most musicals that attain this degree of success wipe out their source material.) Lerner makes stringent, and beneficent, cuts in Shaw's last two acts, and of course brings Higgins and Eliza together at the end. But his show has asserted its identity before that: in the scene where the transformed Eliza wanders disconsolate through Covent Garden while her former friends, not recognizing her, hum a reprise of Wouldn't It Be Loverly, which was once her song. It has no equivalent in the play, and it feels absolutely right.

In a program note, Richard Monette, director of the Stratford production, writes that "the musical ending is often seen as ambiguous." I don't think it is (why would Eliza come back to Higgins if she doesn't intend to stay? To have another go at him with the slippers?) and he hasn't staged it that way, but it might have been interesting if he had; it's about the only chance a director has to put an individual stamp on this show. Short of sheer ineptitude, all productions of it seem to come out the same. That's its glory and its frustration; both the humour and the elegance are immovably nailed down.

Monette's production gives full measure of both, especially the elegance, and makes one of the definitive proscenium shows look perfectly at home on the platform stage. Debra Hanson's costumes are glorious; they're most dazzling, of course, in the Ascot scene, where black-and-white gowns are set off against a black-and-white floor, but they're spiffy throughout, from Higgins' green suit for social occasions to the multi-coloured gear that enhances the costermongers' choreography.

On which subject: This show has done much to propagate some American myths about Britain with which I do not hold. One is that a mere academic would live in a mansion, attended by a full-time staff of nine capable of appearing impeccably uniformed even when roused from their beds at three in the morning. Another is that all cockneys are lovable wastrels who clutter up the streets with their knees bent and their thumbs tucked into their waistbands. Given this convention, the choreographer, Donna Feore, makes inventive and exuberant use of it, and is very well served by her dancers. She builds Get Me to the Church on Time, Doolittle senior's song before marriage, into a tour (probably inspired by the film) of the city's watering holes. It's jolly, but it goes on a very long time, and it refuses to wind down at the end when Doolittle allows himself to be taken off to execution, which is rather missing the point.

Colm Feore gives Higgins a touch of whimsy that suggests he knows how outrageous he's being; this softens him unnecessarily, but he gets the pedant's fervour, and he handles his songs brilliantly. Unlike previous Higginses, he insists on actually singing them; notes appear in A Hymn to Him and I've Grown Accustomed to Her Face that one never knew existed. Cynthia Dale's Eliza comes and goes: a well-timed line here, especially in the Ascot scene, a bright top note there, but overall cute rather than conquering. (It's a fearsome role, musically and dramatically, especially if you're not born to at least one of the accents.) The spark between the two of them is fitful, though her reaction when he first touches her during The Rain in Spain lights a torch. That number retains its effect as one of the great climactic moments in musical comedy. It's all in the placing, since the song itself isn't all that good; it has nowhere to go once it's started. James Blendick is amusing in Doolittle's dialogue, but has to coast through the songs, which is a downer. Barry MacGregor is splendid as Colonel Pickering: a better role in the show, where he's allowed to be a gentleman buffoon, than in the play, where he's the straightest of straight men. Otherwise, it's My Fair Lady: same as ever was.
