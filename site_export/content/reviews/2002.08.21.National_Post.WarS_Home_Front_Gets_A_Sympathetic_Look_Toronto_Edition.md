THE OLD LADY SHOWS HER MEDALS

Royal George Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

Even now, nearly a century after the event, we are soaked in the front line imagery of the First World War: the mud, the blood, the trenches. We hear less of what life was like for those waiting at home. Certainly there are few plays about it.

Its a strange omission, since this was the great recruiting poster war, the one that mustered men by threatening them with the contempt of their womenfolk if they failed to join up. So one would like to know how those women felt while they kept the home fires burning, especially when the casualty figures started mounting up.

J. M. Barries The Old Lady Shows Her Medals, this year's lunchtime show at the Shaw Festival, does something to tell us. It's a one-act piece that was first staged in London in 1916, dead- centre of the conflict. It is a play of acceptance, not of protest, though there is nothing to stop us projecting onto it our own feelings about the needlessness of that war and the reckless incompetence with which it was pursued. Soldiers die and their mothers grieve and are proud. That remains true, whatever side youre on.

Barrie, the author of Peter Pan, has become a byword for sentimentality. But the definition of sentimentality is emotion in excess of the object, and its hard to see how that could be the case here. (If you want a sentimental play on this theme, try Bertolt Brecht's The Mother.) Typically, though, Barrie has given his situation a sweet-and-sour twist.

His heroine is Mrs. Dowey, a Scotswoman working as a charlady in London. Her friends and co-workers all have sons at the front, and boast about them ceaselessly. They trace the progress of the troops by moving condiments about on the kitchen table, and try to trump one another with the proper pronunciation of the various battlefields. Mrs. Dowey has no son -- she isn't even really a Mrs. - - so she pretends. She sends letters and packages to a Private Dowey (of the Black Watch) whose name she once saw in the papers, and produces replies that she mailed herself and that are actually composed of blank paper.

The inevitable happens: Pte. Dowey, home on leave, arrives to call her bluff. He is angry. He is also, it turns out, as lonely beneath his swagger as she is: no family, no girl. So the fabricated mother-son relationship becomes real, until the soldiers time is up.

Todd Hammonds production is uneven. He uses the theatrical equivalent of jump-cutting to speed up the story toward the end, and that is nifty. He is less successful in trying to turn Mrs. Dowey's trio of friends into a silent chorus, who apparently have the run of her basement apartment at the unlikeliest of hours. The three actresses concerned -- Maria Vacratsis, Wendy Thatcher, Donna Belleville -- are, amusingly enough, the same who make up the cast of the similarly titled The Old Ladies in the Royal George's evening bill. They are fine -- Vacratsis especially -- in the later and more sympathetic of their scripted interventions, but are oddly muffled at the start.

I acknowledge that Canadian actors have more important things to worry about than getting their British accents right, but the general floundering here is most unusual at the Shaw. When Pete Treadwell's Pte. Dowey (a performance more stolid and unyielding than the script requires) claims that a Scots soldier can do anything, one mentally adds "except get the voice right." He could also make more of the character's sudden burst of song, later taken up by Mrs. Dowey. It should be a Moment. (It would go to the tune of Yankee Doodle.)

Even Jennifer Phipps, who is English, tends to begin her sentences Cockney, though she has always arrived properly north of the border by the end of each one. There, though, criticism stops. I know of no actor who inhabits every role, to the smallest detail, more completely and less fussily than Phipps does, and here she is magnificent.

Her character is shy but decisive.When her friends gather and bicker, she stands quietly at the side, nervously aware of the falseness of her position, but she has plenty of spirit and, when roused, can discuss the disciplines of war more authoritatively than any of them. The look of horror that suffuses her face at the suggestion that she might be a pacifist -- part defensiveness, part genuine moral shock -- is tremendous. She has been living her fantasy to the full, and her scenes with her surrogate son are suffused with the sense of a dream finally realized. (The production, probably wisely, skirts the more troubling sexual undertones of this relationship. All the same, there are moments when she addresses him like a belated Wendy faced with a collective Lost Boy.) She also exudes a very Scots sense of pent-up mischief.

Her last scene is completely silent, and an honest heartbreaker. The emotion here is all about the character; our joy at seeing this actress in a commanding role again has to wait for its expression till the curtain call, which at the performance I attended gained a standing ovation. Both ladies -- the performer and the performed -- have earned their medals.
