THEATRE REVIEW

Kindertransport

Al Green Theatre, Miles Nadal JCC, Toronto

If there's anything more infallibly wrenching than a play that touches on the Holocaust, it's a play that touches on the Holocaust and involves children. Kindertransport falls into that second category, and there are scenes in it that duly make the heart pound and the eyes ache. For all that, it's a simplistic and half-baked piece.

This production, by the Harold Green Jewish Theatre Company, is the Canadian premiere of a British play by Diane Samuels that was first seen in London in 1993. The kindertransport was a scheme that evacuated Jewish children from Germany in the brief window between Kristallnacht and the outbreak of war, placing them with households in Britain and America. Eva, later Evelyn, is sent from Hamburg to live with a family in Manchester. We see Eva's birth mother sending her away; we see her meeting her adoptive mother and the development of their deep if sometimes fractious relationship; and we see the adult and anglicized Evelyn in later life, coping -- or failing to -- with her own college-aged daughter, who has Just Found Out. Scenes from the three time frames are played out contrapuntally.

Eva brings with her from Germany a storybook about the Rat-Catcher of Hamelin, who seems to be related to the Pied Piper, but is even more sinister; certainly he's a figure designed to scare children, and Eva/ Evelyn grows up mentally linking him to the Nazis. She herself might be suitable prey for a pack rat catcher. She can never throw anything away, and it's in her attic in London (where she's moved from up north) that her daughter Faith eventually comes upon the book. We hear it being read aloud, simultaneously, in the late 1930s and in the early 1980s, when the pfennig drops. This trite device is the kind of thing you might expect to find in an earnest instructional play for children. In fact something similar was used, only more imaginatively and effectively, in the stage version of Hana's Suitcase a couple of years ago at the Young People's Theatre in Toronto.

The author, according to her program note, based her script on three actual sets of memories, and it does have a composite feel about it, as if it were trying to take disparate stories, of survivors and their children, and make them fit together. The play gives us three overlapping sets of mothers and daughters, and it's at its best when tracing the interaction of Eva and her substitute parent Lil. This is a relation coloured by historical circumstances but not determined by them, meaning that the relevant scenes have a life of their own that doesn't necessarily cohere with the rest of the play. They are bridged to some extent by the performance of Jennifer Dzialoszynski who plays Eva, with complete physical and emotional authenticity, right up to the brink of adulthood. She shows us a girl by turns frightened, joyful, bewildered and stubborn, always loving or at least seeking love, and rather scary when circumstances lead her to rebuff it when it comes in unpalatable form. You can see why Lil, played by Patricia Hamilton with an admirable blend of sternness and forbearance, should be more drawn to Eva than she seems to be to her own flesh-and-blood daughters (whom we never meet): This one needs her more, and needs her more demandingly. And when history does intrude, notably when the two of them blunder into watching a newsreel

on the liberation of the camps, it does so economically and believably.

Other relationships in Christopher Newton's production are less convincing. Corinne Koslo as the grown-up Evelyn is fine (no Canadian actress can muster a more authentic North of England accent) until we realize who she's meant to be, at which point we -- or anyway I -- stop believing. The younger Eva may have been touchy at times; her older self is nothing but prickles, which may be acceptable in theory but not in practice. Some blame attaches to the thinness of the writing, which reduces the characters to attitudes, and more to the third-generation performance of Jenny Young, which is pitched on a single belligerent note; you might say, given her character's name, that she's going through a permanent crisis of Faith. Nancy Beatty has difficulty conveying the torment of a German-Jewish mother sending her child away, likely forever, and is thoroughly defeated by a later scene in which the two of them flatly refuse to listen to one another. Again, there's a valid idea there but it's worked out so crudely that credibility crumbles.

Fathers and husbands are kept strictly off stage; the one male actor, Anthony Bekenn, is billed as the symbolic Rat-Catcher but actually appears as a series of officials -- German and English, terrifying or benevolent or in-between -- whom he renders as a series of neat music-hall sketches. These include a wartime postman, mentally lumping Jews with Germans as undesirable aliens and refusing to hear the difference. (This does make a point.) Cameron Porteous, perhaps in reference to the triple time-scheme, has designed a three-arched set that works well for the several scenes that are set in railway stations (with announcements recorded, I suspect, by the director himself ), less well for the more numerous scenes that are supposed to go on in confined spaces like the all-important attic. There are also projections, which are helpful in letting us know where we are but are not especially atmospheric; at the preview I attended, they vanished temporarily due to a computer breakdown, and I don't think that the play suffered much from their absence. I suspect that this play would work best in an intimate theatre with a minimum of design. - Kindertransport is at the Harold Green Theatre until Nov. 23.
