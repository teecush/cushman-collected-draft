- - -

Kurt Weill and Bertolt Brecht crammed half-a-dozen of their best songs into the score for Happy End, which is a gangster musical set in a weird European idea of 1920s Chicago. There are the sinuous cautionary tales known as The Sailors' Tango; Dont Be Afraid; the Song of the Big Shot, which has also been translated as the Song of the Tough Nut; and the defiant Ballad of the Lily of Hell. There are also two nostalgic odes to improbably exotic hangouts, whose names, if nothing else, reflect Brechts deviationist affection for Rudyard Kipling: the Bilbao Song, about a rundown bar, and the Mandalay Song, about a short- order knocking-shop. Above all there is Surabaya Johnny, the most haunting of all torch songs, in part because it tells a story.

Despite these riches, the show -- planned as a follow-up to the runaway triumph of The Threepenny Opera -- was a resounding flop at its Berlin premiere in 1929. The songs, though, and the burgeoning Brecht-Weill mythology, have guaranteed it an afterlife; and it's now reached the Shaw Festival, as the second and presumably more serious musical of the season.

Nobody knows who wrote the book of Happy End. Brecht himself only took credit for the lyrics, and the script is ascribed to a mythical "Dorothy Lane." It seems it was mostly the work of Brecht's amanuensis Elisabeth Hauptmann. Brecht had a habit of screwing his female collaborators (in every sense) but this time even Hauptmann was reluctant to take responsibility. Thats understandable. Its a laboriously drawn-out piece in which long sections about a gang of bank robbers headed by a feared female mastermind called The Fly are alternated with shorter ones about the travails of a Salvation Army lieutenant popularly known as Hallelujah Lil. While on a safari for souls, Lil meets and falls for Bill Cracker, the toughest guy in the gang. The romance of Bill and Lil is bedevilled by the usual on-off misunderstandings, presented with a careless contempt that falls someway short of being satire. Guys and Dolls has nothing to fear from this one.

Michael Feingold's confessedly free adaptation seems very pleased with itself; it obviously knows more about North American pop culture than Brecht and company ever did, but in exhibiting this knowledge it pads the show out to even greater lengths. The characters include a Charlie Chan knock-off who is a pain from beginning to end, and the gang are given the impossible task of seeming dangerous and incompetent at the same time.

The Niagara staging also begins on an odious note of self- satisfaction. Happy End at the Shaw Festival? say the cast, as if congratulating themselves, "Impossible!" However, once this obstacle has passed, and after we've got through an interminable mock interrogation scene that leads precisely nowhere, Tadeusz Bradeckis production, much abetted by Jane Johansons choreography, takes on a hard-edged identity that is very satisfying. The handling of the big mission scene, revolving around an anthem called The Liquor Dealer's Dream, is a model of pace, tension and surprise.

I wish I could say as much for Blythe Wilson's performance as Lil; her acting is stilted but worse -- she doesnt seem to know how to build her songs. She unleashes all the anguish and bitterness of Surabaya Johnny in the first chorus, leaving herself with no further options, and the song, for once in its life, fails to be moving.

Benedict Campbell presents Bill as the kind of middle-aged businessman-crook that Brecht always said he wanted Mack the Knife to be. Its a stylish performance, with plenty of relaxed presence, but it too has nowhere to go. Guy Bannerman and Neil Barclay are good among the crooks, and Jeff Lillico, as an apprentice punk called Baby Face, even better. His amusing counterpart on the side of light is Mike Nadajewski as a Salvationist who faints a lot. As The Fly, Glynis Ranney reasserts her supremacy among Ontario's singing actresses, though she cannot explain the character's ascendancy over the gang; nobody could.

Theres a political message at the end, though Id call it hasty rather than happy. The main attraction remains the music, very well served up by its director Paul Sportelli, though the virtuous lack of amplification prevents all of the syllable-clotted lyrics from coming through. But the names of Brecht and Weill do not in themselves make a good musical; On the Twentieth Century, the season's other musical, though more of a strain on the festival's human and technical resources, is a far better show

- - -

I dont know if it influenced the programming but The Royal Family, the last opening on the Festivals main stage, has something in common with Twentieth Century. The leading male character in both is a thinly disguised portrait of John Barrymore. In The Royal Family, he's played by Peter Hutt, who is raffish, roguish and right. It isn't just, or even principally, his play; we get the whole Barrymore clan, their surname changed to Cavendish. First staged in 1927, its one of the bustling Broadway comedies, traditionally lucky for the Shaw, that were churned out over the decades by George S. Kaufman and Another. In this case, though, the other is not Moss Hart, who brought inspired craziness to the collaboration, but Edna Ferber, who brought mainly sentiment; she wrote Show Boat, and theatrical dynasties seem to have been her obsession. In Martha Henrys production, we do grow to love this one, though we might love it more if Patricia Hamilton had brought more wit, and certainly more savour, to the Cavendish matriarch, determined to live and die in harness. Goldie Semple, though, is in terrific form, in fact the best I've ever seen her, as the star of the next generation, the Ethel Barrymore figure. She gives brilliant expression, physical and vocal, to the character's -- and the plays -- main joke, which is that the more she professes indifference to the theatre, the more theatrical she gets.

Norman Browning offers a bluff variation on this theme, as an ageing in-law in desperate pursuit of a vehicle, and Caroline Cave in the ingenue role is all the authors could have hoped and more than they deserved. Jillian Cook has a high old time as the maid, whom she plays Irish but whom you can bet your life was written black.

- - -

Afterplay, the seasons lunchtime show, has closed but deserves a memorial. It's actually after two plays, and if that too sounds Irish -- well, so is the author, Brian Friel. He brings together Andrey from Chekhov's Three Sisters, and Sonya from his Uncle Vanya, some 20 years after the end of their respective plays; they meet in a shabby cafe in Moscow. So Andrey got to Moscow, even if his sisters didnt, and they even further down on their luck than when we last saw them. Friels speculations logically have no more value than anyone else's, but we accept his version because we are seeing it on a stage. Ah, we say, that's what happened to them; thank you, Id always wondered. Friel isnt Chekhov; his characters, after some delay, make explicit confessions of their shameful secrets, rather than letting them seep out between the lines. But his idea tickles us, and is plausibly carried out.

Simon Bradbury and Helen Taylor had some trouble locating the right register, but were admirable once they'd found it; Daryl Cloran directed.
