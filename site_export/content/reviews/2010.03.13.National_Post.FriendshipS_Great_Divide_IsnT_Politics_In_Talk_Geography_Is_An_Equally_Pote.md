I owe the Harold Green Jewish Theatre an apology. A few weeks ago I admonished them for neglecting Jewish Canadian plays like Yichud, a show out of which they pulled, in favour of pre-tested Jewish American ones. They've now removed the rug from under me, and in very short order, too. Their new production, Talk, is unquestionably a Jewish Canadian play. It isnt an especially good one, but you cant have everything.

It isn't especially bad either: just humdrum, respectably intelligent, and made palatable by its actors. Michael Nathanson's play suffers from a self-defeating format. Its title is an accurate reflection of its theme, but also of its plot and, for want of a better word, its action. Two old friends re-unite in a bar in Winnipeg, which is where the play was first staged. Josh is Jewish, Gordon isnt; though for some reason, I assumed at the start that he was, and I still think that the play might be more interesting if he had been. Gordon has been living in Europe, mainly in London: the good London, as Josh puts it, as opposed to the one in Ontario. Gordon has got engaged to a French girl, Clothilde, and he has brought her along so that Josh can meet her; she has, however, left the stage before we arrive. But while she was there, she seriously raised Joshs hackles by announcing that the scarf she was wearing was bought in Palestine. Josh counts himself a liberal, but as far as he's concerned "Palestine" is a place that never existed and doesn't now, and any reference to it is tantamount to anti-Semitism. Gordon, from what we can gather, has been apolitical most of his life, but lately he has taken to going on anti-Zionist protests. This is why I think there might have been more at stake if he, too, were a Jew. Anyway, from that one moment of dissension -- that one word, in fact -- the friendship begins to unravel as if it too were a piece of fabric.

Nobody expects a playwright to solve the problems of the Middle East, or even to have anything new to say about them; and Josh and Gordon do little more here than restate standard positions. (Its mournfully true that, though the play was first produced in 2007, the author probably hasnt had to change a word.) But that isn't what's principally on the plays mind. Its about the course of friendship and the dynamics of doubt. Josh and Gordon don't just talk to one another; they talk to us, or at least they talk to themselves with us overhearing. Each of them is continually second-guessing what he has just said, or seeking for subtext in what the other has just said. In Ted Dykstra's production -- and, I assume, in the play as written--each of these mini-monologues is accompanied by a spotlight: a device that irritated me at first but that I accepted as the play proceeded.

This may not be primarily a political play, but the politics get in the authors and the audiences way as surely as they do in Josh's and Gordon's. The pretext lacks context: If Josh, as he says, believes in a two-state solution, then why should the mere mention of the word "Palestine" rile him so much? Perhaps it depends on the exact locality in which Gordons fiancee made her purchase. If she was referring to Gaza, I think Josh might have lived with it; if it was Tel Aviv, then, yes, we have a problem. Its exacerbated when Gordon lets slip that his new activism is due to the influence of Clothilde. She looms so large in the conversation that we want to meet her. But of course we can't.

As the boys' night out collapses in what appears to be terminal rupture, Josh starts constructing what, if this were history, would be called a counter-factual: a counter-fictional, perhaps. Suppose Clothilde hadnt dropped that bait, or suppose he hadnt risen to it? He imagines himself going to France, to be Gordon's best man, and feeling out of place. Apparently, the end result would be the same, only it would take longer. We are left to mourn a relationship that we never really believed in anyway. Gordon, who gets to talk more as the play goes on, starts recapping their shared youthful experiences, but none of them are especially compelling. You can't establish that two people are friends just by having them assert it over and over: It may even be counterproductive, in art as in life.

Dykstra delivers a good yeasty production, though the rostra at either side of Steve Lucass set seem unnecessary, used too little and too late. Both Michael Rubenfeld and Kevin Bundy give excellent performances, with Rubenfelds Josh benefiting from having the more volatile, not to say voluble, role. The character conforms to at least one Jewish stereotype; he has all the funniest lines. Bundy has to play straight man, a task he undertakes with grace and some subtlety; a polite stick to begin with, he gradually reveals that insecurities and resentments seethe within him as well. The author may be saying, though I doubt it, that any friendship between Jew and Gentile is foredoomed; he may, more mournfully, be acknowledging that most friends grow apart. In which case, Josh and Gordon don't need the Middle East to separate them; time and the Atlantic Ocean will take care of it. They will probably send emails.
