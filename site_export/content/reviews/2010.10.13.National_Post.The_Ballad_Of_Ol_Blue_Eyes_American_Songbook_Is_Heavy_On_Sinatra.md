There were three stories being told by turns in last Wednesdays premiere instalment of Michael Feinsteins American Songbook and they kept treading on one another's toes. One was a celebration of the career of Feinstein himself: The New York cabaret performer made spectacularly good, singing songs from the Golden Age of popular song, a.k.a. the pre-rock era, to sold-out concert audiences all over the U.S. and beyond. The second was a history of that music as it was performed in the 1950s, when most of the canonical songs had been written but when, thanks to them new-fangled long-playing records, more of them were being more widely heard than ever before. Finally, and most interestingly, there was an account of Feinstein's own activities as a collector and curator of this music. This last subject, combined with either of the other two, might have made a coherent program. The three together were too much and not enough.

The shows most powerful moment was a shot of Feinstein looking down on a Los Angeles freeway beneath which, he told us, MGM had buried troves of musical material, turning irreplaceable history into landfill. He looked and sounded genuinely angry: bracingly so, for a performer whose usual public persona is all smiles. Id like to think that it was PBS, rather than Feinstein himself, who was responsible for so much of the rest of the show being devoted to a worshipful portrait of the star on the road, seen largely through the eyes of his companions and manager. His travels and travails (jetlag, malfunctioning microphones) didn't seem much different from those of any other performer. (Though, did my eyes deceive me, or does he have a private plane?) I suspect that it's the same conventional TV demand for instant accessibility that has led Feinstein to tell the historical part of his story backwards; this episode is being followed by others concentrating on the 1940s and 1930s. So he had, for example, to spend time bringing us up to speed on who Cole Porter was, rather than letting that information emerge naturally, in chronological sequence.

The initial episode was called "Putting on the Tailfins." This, it was explained, being a metaphor for how Frank Sinatra updated the wartime and pre-war songs to the jet age: He burrowed into the lyrics while supercharging the rhythms. There was a lot about Sinatra, rather at the expense of other singers. Feinstein did pay passing tribute to his old friend Rosemary Clooney, whom he described as the greatest female singer of the 20th century. I happen to agree, but its an unorthodox opinion and one not really supported by the cheery 1950s footage of her that was on display; Clooney didnt become a great singer until the 1970s (when she was long past being a pop star).There was, amazingly, no mention of Ella Fitzgerald, whose succession of Songbook albums probably did even more to codify the repertoire than Sinatra's more personalized collections. What Feinstein didn't have time for was to explain in any detail what makes the songs good, or how Porter differs from Gershwin. I would guess that everybody was nervous of this turning into a music appreciation class--though thats exactly what public television should be presenting.

The Sinatra emphasis, however, dovetailed--or tailfinned -- with the current focus of Feinsteins career; he has a recent album called The Sinatra Project. He applies the Sinatra-Nelson Riddle sound to songs like Begin the Beguine that the collaborators might have done themselves but didn't. He also does songs from the actual Sinatra repertoire; he disclaims imitation, but it's hard to hear anything else when he sings Come Fly with Me, complete with original arrangement. Its ironic, since some 12 years ago Feinstein wrote a good semi-autobiographical book called Nice Work if You Can Get It in which he professed himself anything but a Sinatra devotee; he admired the voice and the technique but deplored the persona and the musical and lyrical liberties. It would be interesting to know how and when the conversion happened. Whatever: Feinstein makes a more convincing, less callow Sinatra stylist than, say, Michael Buble (the film even shoots him at Sinatra angles) and is altogether a more polished singer and showman than when he started out as a singer-pianist with a little-boy-lost approach to ballads. What does seem to have disappeared is the sly joy that this former archivist to Ira Gershwin used to take in introducing his audiences to unfamiliar lyrics to familiar songs. FromS Wonderful: "But say, what's all this dizziness?/ I thought that I came here on busi-ness."

That's as a performer. In his other life, he retains all his zeal for collecting and preserving, with greatly enhanced resources for pursuing it. We see him, in mask and gloves, sorting through the garbage of a deceased composer/arranger, or carting away boxes of sheet-music from a big-band singers bemused widow. Thats probably his most valuable work and it's where the program scores. He seems to have grown more eclectic with the years; the boy who grew up fixated on Gershwin when his friends were listening to The Beatles now celebrates not only Frank but Elvis -- to whom no disrespect, but he doesn't belong to the same tradition. Saluting him makes the program even more diffuse. Maybe, as it retreats further into the past in its remaining episodes, it will become more focused, less self-indulgent. I'll hold my breath.
