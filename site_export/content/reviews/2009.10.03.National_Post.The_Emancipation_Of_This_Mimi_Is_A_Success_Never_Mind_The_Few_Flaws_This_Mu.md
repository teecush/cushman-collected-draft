The delight of Mimi, or A Poisoners Comedy, an elegant musical about group sex and mass murder in 18th-century Paris, is that its written by people who know how. That makes it a phenomenon among new musicals, Canadian or other. I feel this especially keenly, since I saw this show the night after seeing The Boys in the Photograph, whose achievements don't include the successful use of song to advance plot or explore character. Every number in Mimi does one or the other of those things, and many of them do both. What's more, they all do it with wit, occasionally of the laugh-out-loud variety. I cant remember a Canadian musical whose verbal standards were as consistently high.

What makes this all the more amazing is that, of the three credited lyricists, only one has any previous track record in musical writing. This is Allen Cole, who is also the composer, one of the most expert and prolific we have, and here doing some of his most inventive and melodious work. His partners, who also wrote the shows book, are Melody A. Johnson and Rick Roberts, both best known as actors and especially as stylish comic actors; Roberts is also a playwright, though none of his plays has been as good as this. Johnson is a former Second City performer and therefore writer, but I wouldn't be able to pick any of her stuff there out of a lineup. The writing in Mimi, though, has a determined zaniness of a kind that I associate with her work as an actress, and that is obviously infectious.

The evening starts with four young, or youngish, people frolicking on a bed: Mimi herself, more formally the Marquise de Brinvilliers; her somewhat ineffectual husband, who's Brinny for short; her lover, a promising fellow from the provinces, name of St. Croix; and Francoise, the obligatory and obliging maid. Their fun is interrupted by Mimis father, who disapproves and who holds the purse-strings. He imposes a monastic role on the household, and dispatches St. Croix to prison where he meets, and takes instruction from, an accomplished Italian poisoner. On his release, he imparts his new knowledge to his beloved who, after practising successfully on a blind and limbless beggar, uses it to dispose of her inconvenient parent. She serves him pigeon pie, her late mothers favourite recipe but with an added ingredient. She finds this very satisfying. Husband and maid are in on the plot as well, and the four of them embark on a killing spree.

The show is based on real events, unlike Stephen Sondheim's musical murderfest, Sweeney Todd, which is based on urban myth. The ironic jauntiness that's an occasional colour in that scores palette is here spread over most of the piece, mixed in with a kind of cankered innocence; its as if Sondheim had elected to write Sweeney in the style of A Funny Thing Happened on the Way to the Forum. In both Sweeney and Mimi, pies figure prominently, though at opposite ends of the homicidal process; in Mimi they're the cause of death, in Sweeney they're the result. Unless, of course, youre a pigeon. Dead pigeons in this show are constant causes of compassion, expressed in some of Coles most daintily unsettling music. Mimi herself, when forced into it, finds the role of good and charitable woman almost as erotically exciting as that of murderess. How lucky for her that she can be both at once.

I've never seen a show on the main Tarragon stage that gave me more pleasure than this one. Still, it does have flaws, and one is that, though it keeps signalling that it's going to dig deep into its heroines psychology, it never actually does. Even the statistical extent of her villainy is obscured. Though some of the shows characters (including Louis XIV, here seen riding a literal hobby-horse) talk as if there were indiscriminate killers on the loose, as historically there were, the verifiable murder count stops at four. Mimi's mother makes ghostly appearances, and the show seems to be building to a resolution of this relationship, but it doesn't arrive. This does prevent Trish Lindstrom from delivering a progressively impressive central performance; flirtatious, petulant, demonic and terrorized by turns, shes a musical-comic Lady Macbeth.

Alisa Palmers production makes fine use of Camellia Koo's magical mirrored set and of Matjash Mrozewski's choreography, which starts with an orgy and then gets sexier. The tone sometimes gets too giggly, especially with Martin Juliens Brinny, though he redeems himself with funny and touching dignity when fate catches up with him. Paul Braunstein is an entertaining master poisoner and Ron Pedersen a very apt pupil, especially when he makes a sung confession that, for undisclosable reasons, is one of the neatest things in the show. Victor A. Young underplays the puritanical dad, but is delightfully ponderous as one of the authors happiest inventions, a softhearted detective who hates being good at his work. Tamara Bernier Evans does a resounding double as mother and maid, the latter of whom gets to sing that her "salad days are almost tossed," a verbal twist I didn't see coming. The rhymes are similarly surprising, both in their accuracy, which is almost 100%, and in their inventiveness. You could kick yourself at the memory of ever having settled for anything less. Or you could kick other lyricists, which might be more satisfying. I bet Mimi would approve. - Mimi runs through Oct. 25 at the Tarragon Theatre. For ticket information, visit tarragontheatre.com.
