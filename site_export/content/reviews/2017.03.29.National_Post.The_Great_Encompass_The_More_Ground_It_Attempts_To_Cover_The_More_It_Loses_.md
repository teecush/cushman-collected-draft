Theatre Review Sousatzka Elgin Theatre

Sousatzka is a musical about a teenage piano prodigy and his teacher. Or it's a musical about the prodigy, the teacher and the prodigy's mother. Or it's about apartheid and the Holocaust.

It's trying to be about of all them. And the more ground it attempts to cover, the more it loses touch with the specifics that would keep it alive.

It's also the return to producing of Garth Drabinsky, who gave us such masterful shows as Ragtime and Kiss of the Spider Woman. At first glance, it seems to be in their socially conscious tradition. But there are significant differences. Ragtime was a wide-angle show, a historical panorama for which the story-lines, diverse but linked, were kept in an ironic, somewhat distanced perspective. Kiss had a narrower lens: it was the story of two men whose situation grew out of a particular political time and place, carefully delineated. Sousatzka invokes two historical catastrophes, but its main action takes place decades apart from one and oceans from the other. Unlike its predecessors, it doesn't have a sense of proportion.

It's based, with large alterations, on Madame Sousatzka, a novel by Bernice Rubens. Madame is the piano teacher, a survivor of the Warsaw ghetto whom we find living in London in the 1980s. Her genius student is Themba who, in the show's major departure from its source, is a black boy who escaped South Africa with his mother, following his father's arrest and imprisonment after the Soweto protests.

Father was a jazz musician, which explains his otherwise puzzling admonition when his son is torn from his arms: "Stand up for yourself. And always practice your piano." Themba prefers classical and has taught himself to play Bach, by ear. Sousatzka nevertheless tells him, in her charismatic Central European way, to forget everything he's learned and to stop being so flashy: "What" she asks, after hearing him play a scale, "did C major ever do to you?" But of course she only does this because she can tell how good he is. "Music Is In You," she sings, more than once.

Madame lives in an unfashionable part of town, in a house filled with literal or metaphorical refugees; there's the Polish countess who spirited her out of Warsaw during the war, and a somewhat transvestite music hall performer (male) turned osteopath, and a punkette rescued, more or less, from a life on the streets. They all have their little moments, making a crowded show even more crowded.

Then there's the matter of Themba's first girlfriend, a sub-plot that is quickly and mysteriously dropped. Also hovering but never really landing is the possessive rivalry of Sousatzka and Themba's mother Xholiswa, which keeps promising to be the show's main motor but never sparks. But then, who is the show really about? Sousatzka is the title character, but Themba is unavoidably the protagonist, though not a very satisfactory one. He's supposedly the victim of culture clashes that the show periodically mentions but hardly gets around to dramatizing.

The best line in Craig Lucas'script belongs to the impresario who presents Themba's debut concert: "His father is in prison for fighting apartheid, which will be an invaluable boost for sales." He seems to be describing the show that he himself is in, one that makes periodic appeals to our sense of historical outrage. Structurally, it seems to belong in the early 1960s, laboriously assembling narrative building-blocks with no particular style or concept: a literalminded approach that's pre-Hamilton, pre-Sondheim, even pre-Cabaret.

Adrian Noble directs efficiently but with no particular stamp. The score is by the fine team of Richard Maltby Jr. (lyrics) and David Shire (music), specialists in tart or tender vignettes of urban - meaning New York - neurosis. Away from home, and on a larger canvas, their work feels anonymous and surprisingly short on humour.

Standard bases get covered: there's a My Fair Ladyish upper-class snob routine, a dance-hall disco number, even a Christmas song; also, of course, breast-beating ballads about finding oneself.

And there are, both in South Africa and South London, Zulu celebrations for which Lebo M, of The Lion King, has supplied extra words and music. They can be stirring, especially in the show's Soweto prologue, or banal as in something called Rainbow Nation.

The performances are fine. Victoria Clark as Sousatzka acts incisively and sings magnificently. So do Judy Kaye as the Countess, though the role wastes her as an actress, and Montego Glover as Xholiswa.

The three of them share the one really moving song Let Go, which appears twice in a kind of knock-on effect. In Act 1 the Countess sings it to her friend the Madame, urging her not to cling on to Themba as she has done in the past to other prodigy proteges; in Act 2 the teacher shares similar sentiments with the mother.

Themba himself is Jordan Barrow, who is lively and personable and mimes his piano-playing very persuasively.

We have, as usual in such cases, to take Themba's genius on trust. In a musical his being an instrumentalist rather than a singer only compounds the problem.

His dad, seen and heard in flashbacks, is Ryan Allen, another imposing singer. All these performers are American.

In a cast of 42, huge by today's or even yesterday's standards, Canadians - some of them with impressive resumes - only make it into the ensemble. In a show that's supposedly Broadwaybound, this seems a missed opportunity to show off our own wares.

The show's best sequence is Themba's concert: a platform raises him aloft as he plays Bach, while below his friends and relations sing ingeniously-woven fragments of their hopes, fears and triumphs. The worst is its last scene, an extraordinarily clumsy revelation of Madame Sousatzka's secret.

National Post Sousatzka runs until April 9.
