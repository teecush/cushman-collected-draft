THEATRE REVIEW ANNE OF GREEN GABLES

Saturday, March 23, 1991

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Toronto ONT -- Written by Donald Harron and Norman Campbell Directed by Alan Lund Starring Barbara Hamilton and Gordon Pinsent At the Elgin Theatre in Toronto Most childrens classics from the last century and the early years of this one have in common a certain tartness that most modern adaptations are sworn to eliminate. This production of Anne of Green Gables follows that vapid tradition A musical monument to innocuousness BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Toronto THE show starts with the entire company discovered behind a scrim singing "Anne of Green Gables, never change" and a few other profundities. They get a round of applause for being so outstandingly good at turning up in the same place at the same time. They then disappear, as pointlessly as they came, and after a brief musical pause the action begins.

That prologue, of course, is stale Broadway, and it is at least an honest forecast of coming events. There are worse musicals than Anne of Green Gables, some of them American, but it is depressing to find that the most popular show in the history of the Canadian theatre, based on a book that's a national classic, is actually a catalogue of cliches from south of the border.

Most of the children's books that have survived from the last century and the early years of this one, have in common a certain tartness, but that is the one element that most modern adaptations are sworn to eliminate. (cf. the collected works of Walt Disney.) The show at the Elgin is in the tradition, a monument to innocuousness.

Lucy Maud Montgomerys book is rooted in a specific rural community in Prince Edward Island. Here it is Main Street, Anywhere, with fields attached. Nobody looks or sounds much pressed for money or anything else, though they occasionally say they are. The mainspring of the story, come to think of it, is that Matthew and Marilla Cuthbert adopt young Anne because they desperately need some cheap help on the farm. They wanted a boy but an inefficient orphanage misplaced their order.

Everybody looks effortlessly scrubbed and spruce; the general ambience is that of a day spent cruising the fudge stores at Niagara-on-the-Lake. The biddies of the town start the show proper by singing about their charitable pursuits; these have little to do with the subsequent action, or even the subsequent atmosphere, but everybody knows you have to set the scene with a chorus.

Things get even more generalized when Anne goes to school. The kids, especially the boys, never walk anywhere; they cartwheel or leapfrog, sometimes for minutes on end. When Anne arrives, she is 11 or 12; by the end she has won a scholarship to college while one of her contemporaries is hilariously pregnant. (Her teacher, in the shows one ill-judged concession to ruthless realism, has spent much of the first act energetically groping her. I think this is meant to be funny.) But there is no sense whatever of the passage of time; they told Anne never to change and she didn't. At the end they tell her again; perhaps they are anticipating sequels. Somewhere, somehow - offstage perhaps and between the acts - she has become terribly popular; it's as if everybody suddenly woke up to the fact that she has the title role. A boy called Gilbert has put in a lot of time pining for her and being rejected. How long has this been going on?

As if Annes scorn wasnt enough to live with, Gilbert is saddled with a ballad of generic banality, the kind of song that has nothing to do with being a person in a situation but everything with being a character in a musical. This kind of thing happens, I hasten to add, in far more pretentious circumstances; Les Miserables is lousy with it.

It can also happen up-tempo. In act two, Anne and company get a new unstuffy, female schoolteacher, a cross between Jean Brodie and Auntie Mame. Naturally, she gets to sing a song about opening windows. To be fair, this number may originally have been ahead of its time (Anne of Green Gables is an older musical than Mame), but the brassy orchestration, with its hapless genuflections toward swing, could hardly have less to do with P.E.I. c. 1906. Author Donald Harron and composer Norman Campbell, perhaps without even thinking about it, were imitating the imitators of Rodgers and Hammerstein; the present production, touted as the most elaborate the show has ever had, compounds the offence. It cannot get by on homespun charm.

One song works: Anne's melodramatic apology to a neighbour she has offended. Her inflated self-abasement ("I have sinned, Mrs. Lind") has a mock solemn identity of its own that is right for her, and hence for the show. It is also the only occasion on which Harron's skill at rhyme catches up with his enthusiasm for it. As a librettist, he strings anecdotes together, marking time till the final curtain. He remembers the second half of the title at the very last minute; Anne gets passionate about the fate of her adoptive home when the audience never knew she cared.

The point about Anne is that she has derived her imagination and her vocabulary from years of solitary romantic reading. Her exaggerations seem natural to her, which is why people come to love her. Jessica Wilson plays her cute, as a conscious put-on artist. Solace comes from Barbara Hamiltons crusty Marilla and Gordon Pinsents twinkling codger Matthew, though they are more like man and wife than brother and sister. In musical-comedy terms, they derive (are you listening, Garth Drabinsky, Hal Prince?) from Cap'n Andy and Parthy in Show Boat. Pinsent, in particular, seems far removed from the helplessly shy old bachelor of the book, but you feel that he could play the real man if his authors would let him and that, in the meantime, no one could possibly do this sweet substitute better.
