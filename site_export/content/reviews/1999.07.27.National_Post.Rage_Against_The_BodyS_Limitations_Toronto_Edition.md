ENDGAME

Premiere Dance Theatre

Once upon a time, it was considered the height of theatrical daring for the curtain to be already up when the audience arrived. These days, of course, everybody does it; and, besides, most of our new makeshift playhouses don't even have curtains. To install one is a radical gesture: one that the Soulpepper company have performed for their production of Endgame. This is appropriate since, 42 years after its debut, Samuel Beckett's voyage into the void remains mental shorthand for the avant-garde, an act that nobody -- including its own author -- has ever successfully followed.

The curtain rises in slow, jerky increments, emitting hilarious creaks of protest: a fitting overture for a play that is a searing vision of decay and an elegant theatrical joke. We behold a grey, bare fortress of a room (another splendid design by Julie Fox) pierced by a couple of unreachably high windows. It looks like the end of the world, and may be just that. We seem, anyway, to be in at the aftermath of some kind of cataclysm, possibly nuclear. (Endgame was first staged at the height of the Cold War.) Here live Hamm, his servant Clov, and Hamm's parents, Nagg and Nell, whom he keeps, famously, in garbage cans.

Beckett is notorious for his parsimony with plot and character, but he gets enormous mileage out of what he does provide. He superimposes on his disaster scenario a study of the dynamics of power and of parenthood.

Hamm is blind, crippled and confined to his chair. He relies on Clov for everything and gets away with this because, presumably, he always has; his exercise of authority is unquestioning, if not quite unquestioned. Clov grumbles -- a lot -- and even threatens to leave; at the end, he figuratively has his hat and coat on, which Hamm seems to half resent, half welcome. It will increase his desolation, but he likes desolation. A monster of self-pity (and of other things), he starts the play by asking "Can there be misery loftier than mine?"

Hamm reserves his especial fury for his father ("old fornicator") who has committed the unforgivable sin of begetting him: a charge to which Nagg replies that, after all, he didn't know at the time who it was going to be. Saddled with his own existence, Hamm can at least make sure that the thing doesn't go any further, and whenever some new form of life rears its head he calls for its elimination.

On a third level (or is it a fourth? -- one loses count in Beckett's universe) the play is a cry of rage against the human body, or the humiliations to which it is exposed by age. And, beyond this -- and brilliantly realized in Daniel Brooks' pellucid and perfectly-controlled production -- it is, to use the most un- Beckettian of words, a celebration: of the theatre. Beckett's jokes, which are excellent if dark, all depend on our awareness that this is a play. ("Not an underplot?" says Hamm irritably, when Clov spots signs of life outside.)

The play's title, and its first and last lines, derive from chess: a metaphor that I don't find especially fruitful. The constant allusions to Shakespeare -- to King Lear and especially to The Tempest -- are, however, immensely enriching. Hamm is a grand parody of a Shakespearean monarch (or actor of same -- just dig the name) and Peter Donat, fine player though he is, cannot find the grandiloquence that is built into the role.

Clov is magnificently played by Diego Matamoros, body arched, legs bent, voice somewhat Irish, feet performing a constant and spectacular double-shuffle. In the clarity of its outline and the quicksilver wit of its detail, this is an exhilarating performance. Coming on the heels of his cameo in Our Town and his star turn in The Play's the Thing, it made me seriously wonder whether Matamoros might not be the best actor in Canada. Jim Warren and Karen Robinson play sweetly as the two bin-ends.

The play is an ineradicable image of a desparing vision that most of us share from time to time. Only the terminally depressed live with it always and -- as has often been noted -- Beckett cannot have been living it while writing it. Work as finely organized as this is an affirmation of life in itself; and honest bleakness is far less depressing than phoney uplift.
