INVISIBLE ATOM

Theatre Passe Muraille Backspace

---

The Halifax theatre company known as 2b came to Toronto last year with Revisited, a beautiful reduction of Our Town performed by two actors on a tabletop. They are now revisiting us themselves with a piece called Invisible Atom that, despite its title, is as visually striking as its predecessor and even more minimalist, the size of the cast being slashed by 50%.

That sole actor is also the author. His name is Anthony Black, and Atom is his character's name. That, he stressfully stresses, is Atom "as in bomb," not Adam "as in apple," and indeed he carries on as if he were the last man on Earth rather than the first. Atom is a bastard -- literally -- who rose from unpromising beginnings to become, in his twenties, a high-flying financial trader. He now has the house, the car and other trappings that befit his earnings, though he does admit that the accompanying debt makes him feel as broke now that he's rich as he felt when he was poor. A former physics student, engaged to another, they have a son, Abraham("Abe the babe") whom Atom greets with delight as being the first person he's ever met who looks like him.

When we meet Atom, he's poised between life and death: most arrestingly so in Ann-Marie Kerr's moody and ingenious production that has him picked out by Leigh Ann Vardy's pinpoint lighting on a pitch-black stage, Video Cabaret style. He has hurled himself off a bridge and we catch him in free fall. "I have no time," he says, adding in a phrase that hauntingly collapses his background in science with his career in money, "who can own time?"

So what has brought him to this pass? Well, it all goes back to his birth, but in more recent times he has suffered: first, a loss of faith in both the morality and the efficacy of his business; second, a terrorist attack on his place of work on a day he happened not to be in it. He has, in the wake of these, embarked on a search for his biological parents that has taken him across the ocean and back and which resulted in severe disillusionment. Persisting in his genealogical research, he finds himself to be the latest in a very long line of bastards, a progression whose details the performer-playwright reels off in a continuous sentence virtuosic in its speed as in its wit. It seems, as far back as he cares to take us, that Atom is the multiply illegitimate descendant of Adam Smith, who was himself the unacknowledged issue of Isaac Newton. This is historically improbable on several counts, but its symbolic fitness is undeniable; the founders of modern physics and modern economics bequeath us this all too splittable Atom.

Pushing him finally over the edge, our hero has been offered the chance of renewed riches at high moral cost: a dilemma that I can only describe as the Last Temptation of Atom. (I rather hoped he'd succumb, but that's me.) His overall trajectory, a hectic downward spiral, is depicted and enacted with great narrative flair. Along the way, of course, he encounters many minor characters, who are impersonated with energy if no great depth: I certainly had my doubts about the sweet-natured taxi driver, a refugee from some devastated former Soviet state, who talks about the importance of "family" with the accent on the second syllable. In a class of his own is the insanely rich English businessman who turns out to be the kind of brutal Cockney wisecracker patented by the early Harold Pinter and most recently incarnated by Ralph Fiennes in Martin McDonagh's In Bruges. As in that film, so in Black's play and performance, I enjoyed him but I didn't believe in him.

Black does, however, keep us with his hero; in an appropriately cool manner, we sympathize with him, and we share in his suspense. I myself felt, as he unravelled, an increasing dread that he would mislay the cherished child he was supposed to be guarding; and I think the author meant me to. He gives us a haunted mind, desperately seeking exorcism; the detail is in the devils. - Invisible Atom runs until March 30. Call 416-504-7529 for information.
