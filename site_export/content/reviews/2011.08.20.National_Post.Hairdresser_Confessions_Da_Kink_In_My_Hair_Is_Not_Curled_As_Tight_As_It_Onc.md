When Trey Anthony's da Kink in My Hair was seen at the Princess of Wales Theatre six years ago it had a few musical numbers, and I wished that there might be more. I should have been more careful. The shows new staging at Harbourfront, produced by the author herself, now has 11 musical sequences by the program's count. It doesn't seem like a musical, more like a play with a lot of interruptions. The new production has its strengths, which include some of the music, but what was once a rough, uneven and invigorating piece now seems bloated in form and shrunken in content. da Kink has already had a spinoff, in the form of a mediocre TV series; in this version, it looks like a spinoff of itself.

The director is Michele Shay, who first staged the play at the NYU Tisch School for the Arts. Several recent Tisch graduates are in the current cast, along with a couple of comparably young Canadians. The setting, as before, is Novelettes hairdressing salon in Toronto. As well as being an expert stylist, Novelette functions as counsellor, confidante and mother confessor to her clientele, who are all female and nearly all black. Novelette believes the key to a woman's character lies in her hair, though the show states and restates the point rather than proving it, and she gives each customer brief advice or consolation, after each has divulged the details of her life. Some are happy, most aren't; even though the general tenor of the show is upbeat. Each vignette reaches us in the form of a monologue; or, in this production, a monologue followed by a song.

Whether through enforced economy or artistic choice, the actual life of the salon, its day-to-day bustle that was a strength of the play as originally staged by Weyni Mengesha, has virtually disappeared from this production. Theres a chair in the middle of the stage, and thats about it. Novelette herself (Alana BarrettAdkins) is reduced to the role of mistress of ceremonies, though a spirited one. The sense of group life is further diminished by the inexperience of the cast. The first thing to go in a nonprofessional or semi-professional production is usually - not always - timing: not necessarily the timing of individual lines or speeches but the rhythm, the ebb-and-flow, between actors. Which means the second thing to go is overall pace. Which is what happens here.

The monologues themselves vary greatly in effect, with the best moments involving the most pain. We have the evangelical mother (Naja S. Selby) whose boy was killed in a gang shoot-out in a parking lot, and who wonders where the police were; and the white woman from the American South (Jennifer Neales) whose family disowned her when she married a black man, and whose heart was broken afresh when, after a supposed reconciliation, they turned on her young son and she pretended not to notice. Both these performances pierce the heart; so, eventually, does the star TV actress (Teryl Brouillette) who found out who her friends weren't when she came out as a lesbian. Her story is weakened by some awkward audience participation involving her mother; on the other hand it does have the most effective musical sequel of any, composed by Michael McElroy, who's credited with half the score. (The other half is by Carol Maillard.)

The most piercing speech of all, as it was in the original, is that of the young Trinidadian girl who was overjoyed to be brought to Canada to join her mother, and who then found herself being abused by her stepfather. I never expected to find a performance in this role that rivalled that of dbi.young, but Thais Francis is equally moving, both in her initial joy and in her later shame and terror. Unfortunately shes subjected, like everyone in the cast only more so, to amplification that makes her sound as if her voice were coming from some place else. Presumably the face mics are there for the musical sections, but it's shameful to keep them on for the dialogue; the Enwave is not exactly a big theatre. It adds to the feeling that the songs and dances aren't earning their keep. (Theres already a musical about hair, and several about kinks.) Giving each character both a speech and a song makes the show feel repetitive; this, on top of the pacing problems already noted, makes it feel long. ? da Kink in My Hair runs through Aug. 21 at the Enwave Theatre, Harbourfront.
