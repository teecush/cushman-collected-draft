THE UNDERPANTS

Berkeley Street Theatre, Toronto

- - -

YOUNG WIFE: Where have you been?

WIFE'S FRIEND: I've been to the theatre. A new comedy. Sternheim.

WIFE: Should I see it?

FRIEND: Wait for the adaptation.

I quote -- well, paraphrase -- from memory, but I think I have the gist of the exchange, and especially of the punchline. It's Steve Martin being self-referential, and it appears in The Underpants, his adaptation of a German comedy, pre-First World War, by that very same Carl Sternheim, that the Shaw Festival once presented under the title The Unmentionables and that has been billed in England as The Knickers. (Truly, as an earlier and more faithful translator than Martin once noted, an inexhaustible subject.) My memories of the Shaw production, which was excellent, suggest that we didn't actually have to wait for the Martin version, but now that we have it we might as well enjoy it.

The underpants, or whatever they are, belong to Louise Maske, the stay-at-home wife of Theo, a junior civil servant with rigorous ideas about practically everything. She is out in the street one day, watching a royal parade, and the sight of the king so excites her that her undergarment falls to the ground. She rapidly picks it up, but not before the sight has excited at least two men, both of whom make their way to her husband's house and ask to rent a room. He, scenting profit, divides the available living space between them; they, scenting Louisa, agree to the arrangement; and Louisa's friend and neighbour, scenting vicarious fulfillment, eggs her on to accept the advances of at least one of them.

Exhibitionism, fetishism, voyeur-ism and, in Theo's case, avarice and possessiveness, which could just be two names for the same condition. Sternheim was writing a satiric account of a society of uptight obsessives, what the 17th century would have called a comedy of humours, something that resembles farce but is more concerned with depicting a society than with pursuing individual manias. (Has anyone ever seen a real German farce? Does one exist? The idea chills the blood.)

Martin makes it a farce, pushing the characters over the line that separates mere obsessives from demented automata. Certainly that's how it appears in Daryl Cloran's extremely fast, furious and meticulously timed production. Sternheim depicts civilians who are fanatically nationalistic and have a military sense of order and obedience; it's easy for a modern audience to see them as fascists in embryo and to congratulate the author on his foresight and themselves on recognizing it. This version, however, forecloses on any sense of prophetic subtlety; as the actors career around the stage, their arms begin forming Nazi salutes and their legs start executing goose-steps.

It's quite funny the first time. Martin, whose ambition seems to be to cross Tom Stoppard with Woody Allen, also crams his text with double entendres (in the original, the situations are sexual but the dialogue is decorous, which has its own humour) and he also works in some genuine verbal wit. "You're here under false pretences," says one suitor, to which the other replies, "What other kind are there?" I'd never thought of that.

This, unusually, is a one-door farce; no entrance is laggard, every one of them is a surprise, and the crucial aperture takes pride of place on Lorenzo Savoini's cunning set, which is a few skewed notches away from being realistic. Dana Osborne has designed clever costumes, the prize exhibit being the candy-pink number worn by Holly Lewis' Louisa. It not only decorates her, it deliciously defines her, as she pouts and minces about the stage, weighing up the pluses and minuses of erotic surrender and never really surprised by anything. In herself, she makes the show worth seeing.

Her spouse, though, almost makes it worth missing; Brian Marler plays Theo as a roaring, stomping bully, and has to work uncomfortably hard at it. It seems wrong for him, and it's wrong for the character; Theo, even in this coarsened version, is a complacent tyrant who takes his own authority for granted. He's the linchpin in what Sternheim planned as the first instalment of a comic epic about the German bourgeoisie, and the end of the play finds him comfortable enough to contemplate (and he's very contemplative, in a smug way) starting a family. I don't want to give anything away, but the sequel shows Theo's and Louisa's son, Christian, as an ace social climber, watched from the sidelines by his doting parents. That play is called The Snob.

Louisa finds herself the victor of her wooers' hang-ups. One, a self-styled poet, would rather write odes about his passion than consummate it; Evan Buliung makes him a farouche artist in narcissism, in what amounts to an amusing reprise of his performance of On the Twentieth Century. By contrast, Dylan Trowbridge, also on leave from the Shaw, breaks new ground as his rival, a timorous barber (Jewish, which allows the adaptation further leeway in the matter of mordant comic prophecy), so jealous of the poet that any passion for the lady melts into second place.

The actor shows an unsuspected talent for physical comedy and gives a splendidly uninhibited account of a deeply inhibited man; he's always been good at internalized neuroses, but now he shows he can externalize them as well. Kate Lynch gives an aptly gloating account of the confidante, and William Webster makes a welcome if belated appearance as Theo's ideal lodger, a puritanical monarchist. There is also a kind of deus ex machina (by definition, an ever tardier arrival), an invention of the adapter's, and one that shows him striving his hardest to be funny. The role is played by yet another good actor, Christopher Morris, who seems singularly ill- suited to it.

Cloran, one of our best young directors, apparently chose the piece to test himself in farce. He passes -- after the sloppiness of just about everything else we've seen recently, the discipline of this show comes as a tonic. All the same, just to clinch the case, I'd like to see him attempt a whole-hearted example of the genre -- a Feydeau or a Ben Travers or something American -- rather than this hybrid. By all means see this adaptation, unless you're a real purist, either about farce or (somebody must be) about Sternheim, in which case -- wait for the real thing.
