DEFENDING THE CAVEMAN

Elgin Theatre, Toronto

The first thing we hear is a disembodied female voice saying men are assholes. Then there's a brief video segment during which the camera pans in on a series of books whose titles suggest a gulf between the sexes that is a) unbridgeable and b) entirely mens fault. Knowing that we are here for a one-man show called Defending the Caveman, we brace ourselves for a biliously partisan male rebuttal. And on comes Rob Becker, author and performer, sporting an appropriately anthropoidal lope.

Becker is an American stand-up comic, but this show comes to us publicized as "the longest running solo play in Broadway history." Its claims to dramatic independence are reinforced by its having opened in London with another actor. I dont know what that performer made of it, but in its creator's hands it remains a comedy routine with elephantiasis.

The opening turns out to be a false trail. Becker is not taking on radical feminism and he is not peddling misogyny; the evening might be more fun if he was. He is out to build bridges. Despite his title, he does not advocate clubbing anyone. His caveman is an amiable fellow who, he says, has appeared to him in a vision to vouchsafe a piece of ancient wisdom: Men are hunters; women are gatherers. The business of the vision is, I think, the author's gesture toward a unifying dramatic structure, but it merely clutters the place up.

Clutching this philosophy, Becker surveys relations between the sexes and finds that everything slots in. Women like to shop; men dont. Women like to exchange confidences at great length; men prefer to commune in silence while fishing or watching TV. Men have, shall we say, a more narrowly concentrated attitude toward sexual pleasure than women. None of these insights, except perhaps the last, is going to be news to anyone who remembers the old-style comic monologues of, say, Alan King. Indeed, much of it, as far as it goes, is confirmable from real life.

Occasionally, Becker strikes some imaginative sparks from these age-old conceptual glitches. (She, bright-eyed: "Wouldnt that look good in our bedroom?" He, deeply perplexed: But it doesn't belong to us. No, I guess that doesn't look too funny. You had to be there.) All the same, even on shopping, which is an easy one, he manages nothing as authoritative, or as funny, as Germaine Greers summation in The Whole Woman: "Most exchanges between men and women shopping together are more or less bad-tempered."

But its a limited vision, and grossly unfair to men, who in reality are a more interestingly varied bunch of selfish bastards than this show lets on. There are heterosexual males -- I wouldn't be surprised if the numbers stretched into double figures -- who are bored by sports, not glued to the television, and enjoy the company even of women with whom they don't intend to get in bed. We arent all characters out of Home Improvement. Indeed, this show seems to be presenting the world view of Tim Allen through the stand-up techniques of Jerry Seinfeld.

Part of the appeal of Seinfeld, whom I think a superb comedian, is that he varies his subject matter and filters his aghast urbanity through silken virtuoso timing. He takes you by surprise.

Becker ploughs the same field for an hour-and-three-quarters -- the penalty, I suppose, of doing "a play" rather than an act -- and attacks all of it bullheaded. I dont mean he's aggressive -- though his miking makes him sound metallically inhuman -- merely that he's technically limited.

He ends by asking each of the sexes to enter the others imaginative space from time to time. That is an admirable prescription, and I dont think anyone would quarrel with it. I'm all for sweetness and light; I just don't want it running amok in our theatres and corrupting the middle-aged. I laughed about half a dozen times, never very loud. As grounds for defending Defending the Caveman, that is not enough.
