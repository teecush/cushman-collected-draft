A mixed first week at the Stratford Festival threw up at least one extraordinary contrast in productions. The Avon Theatre, Stratford's proscenium house, offered The Scarlet Pimpernel, which is escapism run rampant, and The Threepenny Opera, which tries very hard to be anything but.

The Threepenny Opera is early Bertolt Brecht, written before he had become a full-blown Marxist, and before he had developed aesthetic theories to match his politics. It's tempting, though, to read the theories back into the piece; and indeed Brecht himself did so, on a lavish scale.

The word "Brecht" immediately summons, as in a verbal- association test, the word "alienation," though a better translation from the German is probably "estrangement" -- making the familiar strange. Spectators are encouraged to consider the broader social implications of the funny or tearful things that are happening in front of them; actors and directors are instructed to make it happen. This does not, as I understand it, mean that audiences are not supposed to feel, only think; it means that they should feel, and then think about their feelings.

Shakespeare may have invented alienation. Look at how subversively he calls attention to style and convention in, of all harmless things, As You Like It:

ORLANDO: Good day and happiness, dear Rosalind.

JAQUES: Nay then, God be wi' you, an you talk in blank verse.

Musicals are pure alienation. What could be less real, more disorienting, than the transition from speech to song? In theory.

In practice, audiences -- whether at an Elizabethan pastoral, or My Fair Lady -- have an incorrigible habit of picking themselves up and just going on believing. They'll swallow anything.

It's tempting to say The Scarlet Pimpernel, a dedicatedly tongue- in-cheek entertainment that doesn't even take its own melodrama seriously, is as estranging as you can get; camp, after all, is one of the basic alienation effects. But even my perversity has its limits; I am not going to claim that this show will jolt anybody into a more searchingly objective view of the French Revolution. The Stratford Threepenny Opera, though, takes itself very seriously.

A supposedly drunken actor wrecks Mack the Knife, the show's celebrated opening number, making it impossible for us to enjoy the tune or to follow the words. We are not here, the message plainly goes, to be charmed by Kurt Weill's greatest hit.

At later moments, Brecht's anti-capitalist, anti-Vice Squad lyrics -- "when we've had lunch, your preaching can begin" -- are belted out by the entire company, all lined up with their committed faces on. (Brecht's irony tended to stop short at his own pronouncements; he didn't want them undercut.)

The word is that Stephen Ouimette, who directed, and Tom McCamus, who plays Macheath, originally wanted to do the show in small- scale, rough-theatre style, maybe in a garage somewhere; and that their fierce intentions were defused when it turned into a big- budget Avon production. I wonder.

I've seen three previous productions of The Threepenny Opera, which is an appallingly difficult piece. The worst of them, thin- blooded and badly sung, was by Brecht's own Berliner Ensemble (though staged many years after his death). The best was a few years ago at the Donmar Warehouse, a small, though plush, theatre in London. Its Macheath was Tom Hollander, a truculent actor about five feet tall; as far as possible from a standard leading man, but charismatic as all-get-out.

This version updated the action, which Brecht set at the coronation of Queen Victoria, to an actual current Royal celebration; making it a lot more relevant, if that's your thing, than the original could ever have been to the author's 1928 Berlin. It, too, played -- and more creatively -- with the audience's expectations of Mack the Knife by withholding the song until halfway through the evening. It had style. It had coherence (most unusual for this piece). It had impact.

But it was a strictly theatrical impact. (The director, by the way, was Phyllida Lloyd, whose next musical was that great revolutionary statement, Mamma Mia!) It didn't change anybody's view of the world, though it may have reinforced it.

I doubt things would have been any different for the Canadian team if they had put the show on in a meaner space. They would have attracted the same liberal-bourgeois audience that comes to such a play at the Avon, though presumably fewer of them. It might conceivably have made them feel uncomfortable from time to time; but feelings, as Brecht taught us, are not thoughts, and neither necessarily leads to action.

Even at the Avon, Mr. Peachum's beggars can remind us of our own homeless. But it's only titillation.

It's hard to shock a contemporary audience. It can happen (when it does, it usually seems to happen in London) but I'm not sure how useful it is. It's impossible, now, to shock them with Brecht, however heavy the obligation to do so may seem. I also feel -- and, having examined my feelings, think -- that it serves Brecht right.
