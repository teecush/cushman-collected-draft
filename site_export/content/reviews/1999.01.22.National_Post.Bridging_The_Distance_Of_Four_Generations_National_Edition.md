MY MOTHER SAID I NEVER SHOULD

Alumnae Theatre, Toronto

According to the press release, Charlotte Keatley's My Mother Said I Never Should "is the most widely performed play by a female British playwright." It has taken 12 years to reach Canada, which is par for the course, though our theatres seem willing to import just about anything from America.

The play was worth waiting for. It began its life in Manchester and then moved to London, a progress that mirrors that of its characters. There are four of these, all women: four generations of the same family, all -- thanks to a string of early pregnancies -- alive and on stage at the same time.

Doris, who married in the 1920s and relinquished a teaching job at the behest of her husband, gives birth to Margaret, who comes of age at the end of the Second World War, becomes a G.I. bride, and goes south to start a long and efficient career as a secretary -- the kind, we gather, who does most of the work while her bosses take the credit.

Margaret's daughter, Jackie, has a moderately rebellious adolescence that culminates in her having a child by a married man; she agrees -- partly from fear, partly from pressure -- to let her mother adopt the baby.

Rosie, the fourth generation, grows into her mid-teens believing her grandmother is actually her mother and her mother is big sister Jackie who descends periodically from a glamourous career in the art- world to take her on treats.

The action, abetted by jump-cuts and flashbacks, spans the years from 1923 to 1987. It also includes some interludes in which the four actresses appear -- with varying degrees of comfort -- as little girls, tentatively exploring the sexual world through games and rhymes. Among the latter is the one that gives the play its title: "My mother said I never should/Play with the young boys in the wood . . . ."

They all disobey this rule of course, thus occasioning all the play's heartbreak, and all of its happiness.

The grown women talk about their husbands and lovers with some frequency but the male-female relationship remains a subsidiary theme, and an unfocused one.

By the end, the only marriage I felt I knew about was that of Doris: long and faithful and -- to hear her talk about it -- gnawingly unsatisfactory.

The last speech in the play is the earliest chronologically. A shy, happy Doris tells her (unseen) mother that she is engaged. This movingly extends the timeline back beyond the boundaries of the main action, just as the scenes with Doris' great-granddaughter have implicitly pushed it forward.

It has echoes of the mother's great last speech in Long Day's Journey into Night ("I married James Tyrone and was so happy for a time").

But where O'Neill's finale projected despair, Keatley's sends out beams of North-country stoicism.

Jennifer Phipps does it beautifully, as she does nearly everything else. (The childhood scenes tax her somewhat.) She is tough, and delicate, and humorous, and subtle: as she ages, and her daughter and granddaughter go off to the metropolis, her own accent gets increasingly Lancashire; parents can rebel against their children as well as vice versa.

She is also the one performer with enough style and authority to override lapses in Jordan Merkur's production. The others -- in order of seniority, Claire Crawford, Alison Smiley, Maryke Hendrikse -- are none of them bad, but they flash on and off.

The network of mother-daughter relationships is very powerful, and the play's central situation -- the secret of Rosie's parentage with its accumulated freight of agony and anger -- is highly combustible.

The actresses explode as called for, sometimes when not called for, but there are no lines of force, nothing going on between the cracks.

Conceivably they need more physical support; in the published script the author calls for a bare, abstract staging, but even a minimalist staging can be suggestive; few of the scenes here feel lived-in.

One of the recurring furnishings is a piano, and it gives rise to one of the best exchanges in the play.

"At school," says Rosie, "we've got a synth."

"Ah," says Doris, "I had a friend called Cynth."

It's a nice little pun that lights up the distance between the oldest and youngest characters, and also unites them.

The closing scenes finds the two of them together, and back in Manchester -- actually in Oldham, which is Manchester's down-market neighbour. They seem, for the moment, happy.

The class-and-location theme is one that the author tends to pick up and put down as it suits her, and it carries some sentimentality with it. But it also has strength.
