This is the year of the dragon, or so the flyers tell me, and Toronto theatre is getting with the program by becoming a dragons' den. Hot - actually, rather cool - on the tail of Robert Lepages blue one comes The Golden Dragon, by Canadas and the world's new favourite German playwright Roland Schimmelpfennig.

The title of the play is also the name of what is described - many times - as "a Chinese-Vietnamese-Thai restaurant," in, above and around which the action takes place. The printed cast list comprises A Man Over Sixty (David Fox), A Woman Over Sixty (Lili Francks), A Man (Tony Nappo), A Young Woman (Anusree Roy) and A Young Man (David Yee). It rapidly becomes clear that these labels refer not to the characters but to the actors, each of whom plays many roles of many kinds. Ross Manson's production tips its hand from the beginning when, looking us straight in the eye and with a twinkle in its own, it introduces them as the Asian members of the restaurants kitchen staff. Yee is the only one of the five who looks even remotely like the relevant kind of Asian; and so the rest of the show goes. Occasionally, to keep us on our toes, a performer will appear as a character of his or her own age, race or gender, but those are rare exceptions. You may never, for example, have expected to see Fox apply his mature, weighty presence and silvery technique to the role of a blond flight attendant whose boyfriend calls her his Barbie, but he does, and its not just a hoot but a touching and persuasive hoot.

The stewardess, whose more formal name is Eva and whose accent is the only hint that the action may be taking place in the author's homeland, is dining at the Golden Dragon with a colleague who discovers something nasty in her lemongrass soup. Rather mysteriously, she fails to send it back, though the mystery is somewhat mitigated later on. Meanwhile, in the kitchen, a more desperate drama is unfolding. The youngest employee, recently arrived from China in search of work and a vanished sister, has an agonizing toothache. Since he's an illegal, professional help is out of the question, so a co-worker, calling himself the boys uncle, performs some emergency dentistry, with grim and far-reaching results. The rest of the staff helps out, mainly by holding the patients mouth open and pouring vodka into it, while still managing to take and fill a succession of complicated orders, showing that fusion can beat confusion.

On other floors of the same building, relationships are fraying or falling apart. A grandfather hears from his granddaughter that he's about to become a great-grandfather. A wife walks out on her husband, who does not take it well. He takes alcoholic refuge in the ultra-convenient convenience store next door, and later in its owner's apartment. Threaded through all the other storylines is the fable of an unfortunate Cricket who throws herself on the mercy of a more provident Ant and finds that the price of sustenance is becoming a sex slave. Here, the actors take a further step by crossing species lines, although ... well, I wont give anything away.

The kaleidoscopic method is its own reward, especially in terms of virtuosity of performance; the occasional wig or apron helps out the changes but theyre mostly done by sheer skill and imagination. Teresa Przybylski's set works the same way; it's a long flat platform, with the audience on both sides, that can function either as the kitchen counter or just as a floor. But the method also invites its own penalty; once the surprise has worn off, it can seem glib and superficial. In a way the play does for, or to, Asia what Schimmelpfennigs Peggy Pickit Sees the Face of God did for Africa; it juxtaposes us with them, comfort with misery, except that this time its about them coming among us, rather than vice versa. It doesn't have the close psychological warfare of Peggy Pickit or the black humour. On the other hand, it does build as the other play doesn't. In the middle, I was losing patience with the constant changes of focus, with the implied but unspecified guilt trip, and with some flights of fancy that seemed merely whimsical. Id count among those the mock-ceremonial use of the restaurants carpet, emblazoned with the titular image. But then, startlingly, the play pulls some - not all - of its threads together, to make some shattering connections, some of them spelled out, others not. There are also, which there weren't in Peggy Pickit, traces of compassion.

If I found myself blowing hot and cold about this dragon's text, I have no reservations about the performances or Mansons orchestration of them. Yee has a bewitching hurt delicacy as, among others, the grandfather and the cricket. Roy brings to both the agonized kitchen boy and the swaggering Barbie-fancier (he uses a more forceful word) the same mercurial authority with which she has acted in her own plays. Francks is gnarled and mean as the ant and the storeman, and all clucking concern as a senior cook. Fox doubles up on his Teutonic air hostess by acting his age as the impromptu tooth surgeon. Nappo, very affable, is the nearest thing to a master of ceremonies, or maybe mistress, since his principal role is that of Evas fellow diner who, swallowing her suspicions along with her soup, emerges, rather arbitrarily, as the nearest thing to a philosophical voice. The play's achievement doesn't quite match its audacity, but it's close.
