I trust this isn't too much of a spoiler, but Episode 5 of Luck ends with a horse leaning its head out of its stable door and nuzzling a sleepy Dustin Hoffman, who's keeping watch outside. It's a lovely moment and it sets the seal on the show's development thus far. It's progressed, that is, from a show I wanted to like to one that I see some possibility of loving: a rich, dark human comedy. That episode doesn't reach the screen for another couple of weeks, and earlier instalments have tested many people's patience, including mine, but the wait is worth it.

Luck began with Hoffman, as millionairebusinessman-gambler-crook Ace Bernstein, getting out of jail after - he said - taking a three-year fall for somebody else's misdemeanors. He now intends, like a latter-day Count of Monte Cristo, to use his wealth and skills in a labyrinthine plot to get back at the guys who put him there, in particular one called Mike.

He also bought a racehorse; or at least, since being a felon apparently debars you from owning horses, he had his driver and confidant Gus (Dennis Farina) buy one on his behalf. It still isn't quite clear whether these two plans are connected. I started out assuming they were, and that Hoffman's horse was a Trojan one, but that end-of-episode image, along with a few things that happened earlier in the hour, suggested that he genuinely loves his horse. He could certainly stand some love in his life.

His more sinister game plan involves buying the Santa Anita racetrack in California, on and around which nearly all the action takes place, and expanding its activities to include a casino. The rationale (and I really can't follow this) is that horse racing is legal in the state, but gambling - other than presumably betting on horses - isn't: in which case, why would a casino be any more kosher for being situated on a track? Still, Ace seems to know what he's doing and Hoffman, playing a dapper man with a short fuse, certainly does. He's been a star now for nearly half a century, and he's still at the top of his game.

If his motivations, and even his dialogue, seemed obscure to begin with, they were crystalline compared to those of the rest of the cast. We have Nick Nolte, who hasn't been around as long as Hoffman but who

looks and sounds much older, playing the proud but mumbling owner of a prize horse with a noble but troubled ancestry (the horse's ancestry, that is). We have a quartet of self-styled "degenerates," each with a mental and/or physical disability of some kind; hanging around the track seems to be their life and, after winning big on an impenetrable system called a Pick Six, they've bought a horse of their own. It's hard to believe that this will end well.

There's an assortment of jockeys, male and female, most of them nervous most of the time. There's Joey (Richard Kind), a sweaty agent who represents at least two of the jockeys and has a bad stammer, which actually makes him easier to understand than some of his fellows. And there is Escalante (John Ortiz), a Peruvian trainer who may be wearing his extremely thick accent as a badge of defiance; he doesn't believe in making polite allowances for anyone. They all have to put up with him, though, because he's the best at what he does, and has nearly all the show's horses in his stable. This makes him, in structural terms, a unifying factor even though in most other ways he's a divisive one.

Asked by Ace how his horse is getting on, Gus replies, "What would I know? All four of his legs touched the ground." Apart from its admirable stylistic qualities, I warmed to this line because it suggested that Gus knows as little about horses as I do. In fact, I have difficulty telling the noble animals apart, and it's only around Episode 4 that the show has begun to make it easier for me.

David Milch, who created the show, writes - or he and the other writers in his, well, stable, write - the fruitiest dialogue on television, which makes you wonder why he has made so much of it so hard to hear. However, either they've adjusted the mix, or the actors are speaking up and slowing down, or my ears are getting attuned. Milch's Deadwood was no aural or narrative picnic either, to begin with, but I got addicted to it (and am still feeling the withdrawal symptoms). Deadwood, too, was a horse opera, i.e. a Western, though looking back I don't remember horses playing as crucial a role as they do here. And the horses are what bring the show together because they're what bring the characters together.

I've given up waiting for the various storylines to converge; instead they play off one another. The characters are figures in a pattern rather than spokes in a wheel. The track is not as compelling or interdependent a community as the town was in Deadwood, but it will serve. There's a desperation to it, both good and bad; we're made aware that it's a dwindling business, with the same few people turning up for every race. Chief among these obsessives, even after making their big score, are the four degenerates, who are becoming a fine set of comedians, crisscrossing egos trying to make common cause.

"I got three friends," says their leader, Marcus, "who I can hardly stand most of the time." Marcus (Kevin Dunn, terrific) is a paraplegic with heart and respiratory problems; his anger, even greater than Ace's, is alternately tolerated and resented by his buddies, one of whom has been distinctly heard to complain that "my mental adroitness is dulled by this constant negativity." A crooked security guard has told Marcus, less ornately, "Don't hold your breath - oh, I forgot, you can't." Karma being a bitch, this guy will also find himself in dire need of friends. While I'm quoting I can't resist Ace's advice to Gus, who has a habit, handy for exposition if for nothing else, of telling people what they already know: "If you can't advance the conversation, you're not required to talk."

Each episode naturally includes a race. One of the most exciting is in Episode 4, where the jockey we've been rooting for triumphs, despite covering most of the course in slow motion. This kind of trickery, like the constant use of extraneous music, is contrary to the best canons of cable drama, but the show gets away with it. In this episode we also get to meet Mike, who turns out to be English; maybe every Milch show needs its limey "c--ksucker."

In fact, he's Michael Gambon, who's massive, though he does seem to have come out of another show, maybe a Guy Ritchie movie or a Harold Pinter play which, come to think of it, is a thought. His meeting with Ace takes place on a boat, and is shot at appropriately queasy angles. In other circumstances Hoffman can wear a face of pure heartbreak. "As far as sexual activity and so forth," he tells Gus, "that ship sailed a long time ago." Nonetheless, he seems interested in a lady (Joan Allen) who's requesting his charitable contribution to a scheme for prison inmates to work with horses. This would connect with Ace on two levels, but that may just be coincidence.

Episode 5 lets us in on touching things concerning stuttering Joey's home life. It also finds Marcus having a checkup. His doctor asks, "Do you have anyone you can talk to?" Marcus thinks and says, "a horse." "That's not bad" says the doctor, thoughtfully. In this show's universe, that's an understatement. I think we're in luck.
