The new Second City revue, We Can Be Heroes, contains the funniest beheading number I have ever seen. I'll admit that the competition in this category is limited but, still, this number is gloriously executed. I think thats the right word.

A medieval headsman, dour and as played by Connor Thompson increasingly aggravated, announces from his parapet the imminent death of a traitor. The malefactor, an entrancingly guileless Kevin Vidal confesses his guilt and proclaims his eagerness ("do with me what you will!) to take the consequences. His taking them, however, is only the beginning. youve heard the expression heads will roll"; this head rolls and rolls like Old man river, the result ofwhatmust be supernatural contortions by the parts of the victim's body that remain out of sight. meanwhile the onlookers, strategically positioned around the auditorium, provide mountingly hilarious commentary, whose best bits it costs me a supreme effort of will not to quote. If all this sounds very monty Python, it is, but homage worthy of its lineage. There's rarely been a Second City number that builds and builds and keeps topping itself as this one does.

But then there have been few Second City shows as consistently good as this one. Physically, under the direction of Kerry Griffin, it reaches new levels of agility and inventiveness, and it has a thoroughbred cast. Thompson, a newcomer with the square jaw and square voice of everybodys straight man, exploits and explodes that persona in a virtuoso solo spot as a vainglorious lifeguard who happens to be legally blind. (but he can hear you cry for help and, failing that, he can smell your fear.) After barking out answers to the audiences questions - I've seen the show twice, and he coped imperturbably with two different sets of them - he leaves us with the frightening twin thoughts that water is our enemy and that we're 80% composed of the stuff. The sketch is indeed a high-water mark, but its closely followed - actually its immediately followed - by another winner in which Vidal, another new Second Citizen, plays a black senior citizen, whose only friend and visitor is a plump and disconsolate schoolgirl, disappointed to discover that he can't solve all her problems for her like the nice old black men she sees on TV. (bullied by her peers, she says she would run away to Japan except that the people there are so small, the clothes wouldn't fit her.) Jan Caruana, also new, is the moppet who cant keep her knees or her frock still. both she and Vidal are, apart from anything else, excellent actors; Caruana indeed is unnervingly touching playing the same girl in one of Second Citys trademark sequel scenes, one whose tearful impact is only increased by the certain knowledge that its emotional balloon is going to be punctured by a punchline. I must admit though that the line itself turns out when uttered to be no great shakes. It seems that every time I review a Second City show, I complain that they rarely know how to end a routine. I hereby register that complaint again.

I must however suspend it for the item in which Stacey mcGunnigle, a comparative and consistently invaluable SC veteran, plays the curlywigged blunt-speaking voter of every progressive pollster's nightmares. (She doesn't know, and many of us will sympathize with her, what Nuit blanche is meant to be about. That isnt the punchline.) Craig brown is an unassuming but protean performer, whose turns range from a bumptious fellow shamed into undergoing a prostate exam to a tall small boy, persuaded that the bearded man beside him on the subway is Santa Claus en route to the North Pole via Finch. (Hes persuaded because he can't think of any other reason for anyone to be going to Finch.) Allison Price, a returning artist of delightful beanpole proportions, pairs off with biden in a dialogue for two figure-skaters wondering whether to boycott the russian Olympics, punctuating their discourse by describing light-fantastic patterns across invisible ice. This is the comic-choreographic highlight of a show in which everybody is a lovely mover, from Thompson as a provocatively terpsichorean bat first observed hanging coyly from the ceiling of an unwisely rented cottage to the volunteers, one per show, cajoled on to the stage to match limbs and poses with the professionals and, on each of my visits, more than holding their own. There's also a honey of a scene in which a husband feels his virility challenged by a pair of super-competent removal men who impress his wife, a delightful mcGunnigle, with words like "stud.

The weakest parts of the entertainment are, as usual, the musical ones. Nobody in the cast is a singer, though Price is nice in a song about the difficulty of distinguishing a hipster from a homeless person: a funny idea that isnt developed. Theres a title number that opens and closes the proceedings and, again as usual, has nothing to do with the rest of the show. In its first iteration it has one neat line, about the odds against being a Superman in a society that has abolished phone booths; the reprise endlessly and pointlessly repeats a joke from a preceding sketch, thereby exposing both of Second City's Achilles' heels - musicality and finality - at the same time. mostly though this is an uncommonly stylish and witty show, for which the invariable Camellia Koo has designed an unconventional, in this context, brick-walled set that comes in very handy for scenes that are set in houses and also for scenes that arent.

Stefan Dzeparsokis Birdland Theatre production of Fortune and Men's Eyes bears a family resemblance to his Gruesome Playground Injuries for the same company. Once again he has a wide open set, minimally furnished but hung and strewn with motifs. This time, though, neither the cast nor the text are strong enough to justify the treatment. John Herbert's 40-year-old Canadian classic" concerns four male prisoners, all homosexual by either nature or viciously enforced necessity, crammed into the same cell; the spaciousness could hardly be less appropriate or, once the first image has been absorbed, less helpful. Two of the actors, Cyrus Faird as the rooster rocky and Alex Fiddes as the queeny Queenie, give caricatured, vocally indistinct performances, rendered more obscure by having the theatres airconditioning running full blast almost throughout. Its turned off to allow us to listen to the big scene between david Coomber's mona and Julian deZotti's Smitty, both of whom are indeed worth listening to. deZotti is an intelligent and commanding actor, but he presses too hard to make some artificial dialogue sound natural; his journey from innocent to victim to new boss same as the old boss is a series of snapshots rather than a logical progression. Coomber plays a victim who has embraced victimhood as survival, almost as identity, and hes truthful and touching. The two jointly recite the Shakespeare sonnet that gives the play its title, and on which it relies to summon the emotion that it cant produce on its own. The situation is disturbing and persuasive; the writing is sentimental posing as tough. dzeparoski's last production was impressive; this one is just pretentious.
