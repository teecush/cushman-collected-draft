PINATA FULL OF BEES

Second City, Toronto

The second Second City is paying an anniversary tribute to the first Second City. The Toronto comedy troupe, now in its 26th year, is performing a show originally created by the parent Chicago organization, which is 40. The piece itself is two years old, and by all the rules of topical humour it should already be thoroughly dated.

It isn't, largely because it is not in fact very topical. Its comedy of attitude rather than of event, and the attitude it presents is a hardy perennial, neatly embodied in its opening number. A member of the cast is planted in the audience and then dragged from it by his five colleagues, who are dressed as terrorists. They dump an Uncle Sam hat on his head and arraign him for all the sins committed by America against the world and against itself. He, however, is empowered by the hat, and proceeds to kick every one of their revolutionary asses.

The moral, which manages to be triumphal and guilt-ridden at the same time, is that one American bully is worth five from anywhere else. The flavour of the satire naturally changes somewhat on the journey north. Performed by Americans it would seem sportingly if somewhat toothlessly self-critical. Done by Canadians it becomes a stimulating mixture of disapproval and envy: Theyre awful and we're awed.

In the best revues, all the performers are magnetic and all the jokes are funny; I saw a couple of these when I was young and impressionable. Below this top tier come the shows in which all the performers are likeable and enough of the jokes are funny for us to forgive the ones that aren't. This is the category in which Pinata Full of Bees belongs. The title, by the way, refers to a nasty device employed at parties by an evil-minded childrens entertainer; tempting on the outside and stinging underneath, its plainly a metaphor for how the show would like to see itself.

It has a Monty Python kind of structure, unlike most revues, which have no structure at all. Its sketches are not autonomous things with punchlines; they go off on tangents and beget other sketches, only to return when least expected, like deadbeat dads with nagging consciences. The guy in the Yankee Doodle headgear keeps popping up, very pleased with himself. Some of these running gags grow up and become running storylines.

There's one that parodies the social and sexual mores of the 1950s, and there's another that almost qualifies as a narrative spine. It takes place in a grade-school classroom, presided over by a subversive substitute teacher whose name happens to be Noam Chomsky. The kids call him Mr. Noam and are appalled by the things he tells them -- about the destruction of the American Indian, about global exploitation ("and the story ends with Little Bo Peep being forced to work in a Nike factory") and about the need to question authority whenever it raises its head.

A climactic flash-forward shows how his teachings have borne fruit; theres one backward boy -- well, he sits at the back of the class -- in whom it takes time, but one day in early middle age he wakes up to the lies society has told him (about 2% milk, among other things) and breaks out. He leads a march on and through Chicago, eventually returning to confront the brooding horrors that tyrannize his home suburb: ersatz Italian restaurant chains and monopolistic video stores that chop bits out of movies. He persuades some of the audience -- were talking flesh-and-blood paying customers -- to throw their Blockbuster cards on stage, and makes them promise not to renew them. It's like the '60s, only more practical.

Lee Smart, a performer massively delicate and delicately massive, plays this instant insurrectionist, as well as many other characters of various degrees of derangement; he is squishy, bland and scarifying by turns, and also simultaneously. Lisa Brooke does the evenings only sustained improvisational turn and does it brilliantly. Mary Pat Farrell has a strong line in beauteous hysterics. Paul Bates, Pat Kelly and Doug Morency are the rest of the gang.

All of them tread this alien turf as if it were their own; to an extent it is -- theyve done some rewriting. There is, besides, a comic language here that's spoken across North America and beyond, a dialect of hip despair intelligible to all of us. The show knows that Mr. Noam is right and it also knows that he's silly; it sets up a boycott of Blockbuster as a ludicrous image of revolt, but it cant think of anything better. And, hey, it would be uncool to try. The upshot, despite the tough facade, is a surprisingly gentle show that keeps suggesting that savage satire is just around the corner but wouldnt know how to turn that corner and doesn't really want to. It's a pinata full of butterflies, with maybe the occasional spider.

Maybe in two years time well see the new Chicago Second City show. I love its title. It's called The Psychopath Not Taken.
