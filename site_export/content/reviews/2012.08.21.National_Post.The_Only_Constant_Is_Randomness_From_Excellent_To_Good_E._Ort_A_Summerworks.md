The 2b Theatre Company from Halifax has an excellent track record that's further enhanced by When It Rains, the show they've brought to this year's SummerWorks (at the Factory Mainspace). It begins as a slightly whimsical cartoon, and never really abandons that mode. But as it goes on, it cuts surprisingly deep.

Anthony Black, who's both author and director, gives us a brother and sister, and their respective spouses. We first glimpse the quartet as disembodied heads, poking through the holes of a stage-size black-and-white drawing. A voice-over tells us that the sibling's father -- who is never mentioned again--was a murderer, but that since they never knew him this is unlikely to have affected them much. Of course, we don't have to believe that. Both couples originally met through coincidence, but then who didn't? A recurrent theme is the statistical improbability of absolutely everything.

This sense of randomness both raises, and discounts, the old conundrum about bad things happening to good, or at least moderately well-intentioned people. Of the two partnerships presented, one is visibly falling apart while the other is doing the same thing in slower motion. A financial high-flyer makes a little mistake that costs millions; a woman who's longed to get pregnant has her hopes and her health dashed; a betrayed wife seeks a dubious salvation in mysticism; the faithless francophone she's cast out takes to sleeping on park benches and to singing Ne Me Quitte Pas, all four and a half minutes of it, beneath the window of his unresponsive ex; he also, as per the title, gets wet. The narrative voice of the opening is replaced by captions, at least one of them quite cruelly misleading. Projections, designed by Nick Bottomley, do service for sets, and they intersect brilliantly with the live bodies of the actors. One especially striking sequence shows a couple vertically in bed; the initial image is witty enough, but the ensuing scene doubles down on it. Visually one of the funniest scenes in the show, it's also emotionally one of the most painful. Never losing its lightness of touch, the play becomes a haunting depiction of the physical and psychological spaces between, theoretically, the closest of people.

Form and content feed off, and illuminate, one another in a way that hardly anything else I've seen at this year's festival manages to achieve. Terre Haute (Lower Ossington Theatre) comes closest. It's based on the correspondence between Gore Vidal and the Oklahoma City bomber Timothy McVeigh. Vidal wrote some sympathetic articles about McVeigh, which didn't win him many friends; Edmund White's play imagines them actually meeting, for a series of death row interviews. The names have been changed, presumably to protect the fiction -- Gore is now called James, and McVeigh Harrison -- but the play still depends on our fascination with the real people. Its problem is that not even his recent death can make Vidal seem, in this situation, like the more interesting of the two. Much of the play hinges on James's growing sexual fascination with Harrison; but it's the latter's mind that we want to get into, even as we know that it's probably impossible. The play does at least let us know his unknowableness. Both characters speak with improbably elaborate fluency. Terrence Bryant's James copes the better with the words -- playing a writer, he can perhaps afford to sound literary--while Todd Michael Sandomirsky's Harrison compensates with a remarkable physical presence, mostly calm and obviously dangerous.

Anton Piatigorsky's Breath in Between (Passe Muraille) concerns another killer, an invented one this time, though he might be considered the offspring of two real-life phenomena: assisted suicide and Internet dating. Roger, our hero, places an online ad offering his services to people who want to die. He has two takers, one of each gender, and duly shoots them. All this has happened before the play starts, but Roger is still naturally haunted by the memory of his clients/victims. So is Amy, the young woman whom he manages to attract, bed and impregnate before going to jail. (The play's timeline is by no means clear.) Brendan Healy ( Blasted, The Maids) is a director who seems to thrive on extreme scenarios, and can get his actors to thrive there as well. Amy Rutherford, playing her namesake, sometimes has to try too hard, though her monologue about unwilling motherhood is very powerful. Paul Fauteux as Roger is extraordinary throughout, quiet and centred whether playing himself or, from behind masks, each of the deceased. Design, credited to audio wizard Richard Feren, is otherwise, as you'd expect, exclusively sonic, and scary. Scary, too, is much of the writing. But I felt, not for the first time with this dramatist, that a literary conceit was being stretched beyond its useful limits.

Much the same goes, if less portentously, for Pieta (Passe Muraille Backspace), by Danish playwright Astrid Saalbach. A middle-aged woman wakes up in a hotel room, next to a strange man who -- I'm trying not to give too much away -- is in no position to tell her how either of them got there. So she talks, largely to him but really to us, about herself. Since her first action on waking with a hangover at 5:30 a.m. is to pour herself a drink, we can guess straight off that she's an alcoholic, and her life turns out to have fallen apart in other ways as well. It all adds up, sort of, but the problem, endemic to monologues, of justifying the character's volubility is never solved, and Tamsin Kelsey's performance, though praise-worthily sustained, never breaks free of the banal near-naturalism with which she is surrounded.
