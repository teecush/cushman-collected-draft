THEATRE REVIEW

DRAMA AT INISH & CANDIDA

Court House Theatre; Royal George Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

Heres what happens in Drama at Inish (subtitled A Comedy, so nobody gets the wrong idea). In the early 1930s, a touring theatrical company arrives to play a date in a small Irish town. The local idea of stage entertainment has usually been on the lighter side, circuses and such, but these strolling players offer sterner stuff: "serious plays -Russian plays and that sort of thing." Their Russian repertoire seems to lean more to tragic Tolstoy than, as wed nowadays expect, Chekhov; and they also administer a hefty dose of Scandinavian. They're positively bursting with Ibsen.

Lennox Robinson, who wrote the play and was a major force in Ireland's native dramatic flowering at the Abbey Theatre, is here paying affectionate and slightly self-mocking tribute to his own youthful awakening when an Ibsen troupe came to Dublin and changed his life. Their successors, in his own plays, change the lives of the citizens of Inish in surprisingly direct ways. They play to packed houses: a surprise in itself. The play is set in a hotel where the married couple, who are the companys managers and stars, are billeted. The proprietor is a jovial heavy-drinking fella; his sister, who does much of the work, is a spinster mourning her great lost romantic opportunity. Seeing the plays brings it all back to her. Others are even more drastically affected. The local MP, after seeing An Enemy of the People, goes so far as to flout party lines and vote his conscience. That, decides the community, is the last straw.

The play (Robinsons, not Ibsen's) has a great idea, which it develops agreeably but tamely. The structure and much of the tone are conventional domestic comedy. Too many of the exciting events happen offstage, and the correspondences between the townsfolk and the plays that influence them seem too cut-and-dried, too oneon-one, to be credible. However, in production, Drama at Inish acquires a joyous dimension beyond its plot. It comes from actors playing actors, and from other actors playing the supposedly ordinary people affected by them, especially when those actors are as good as the leads in Jackie Maxwell's Shaw Festival production. You never know which is the more theatrical. Ric Reids hotelier is a genial, peppery old party whose only drawback is his lengthy absence from the stage in the second half. Mary Haney, as in Harvey last year, grounds frustrated excitability in hard reality and then takes it to delirious heights that are a kind of greatness.

And then there are the visitors, wonderfully named Hector de la Mare and Constance Constantia. Thom Marriotts Hector is a grandiose, grandiloquent figure who always seems to be swishing an invisible cloak; I never quite did myself justice in melodrama, he says, melodramatically. But when he speaks about the new drama that has inspired him, he is both moved and moving. Corrine Koslo's Constance, looking daggers at the suggestion that she might like "a nice, creamy cup of tea," is flighty but also motherly. And their domestic concerns, as they confront unaccustomed success and customary penury, are at least as real as those on which they intrude.

The proceedings get positively Pirandellian (to invoke the one modern master who doesn't seem to figure in the de la Mare repertoire) when the couple are prevailed upon to audition the shy and humble hotel boots. To their amazement, he turns out to be good; "the spirit," says Hector, wonderingly and hauntingly, "bloweth where it listeth." Indeed, it bloweth all through Andrew Bunkers performance as the aspirant, both in and out of his day job. Hector warns him of the horrors and uncertainties of the actors life, and you agree with him; and then you remember that he and the boy he's talking to and everyone around them are actually living it. Less complex in its implications but equally delightful is Craig Pike's turn as the gauche son of the house, tempted to tragic extremities by the plays hes seen and the practically minded woman hes courting.

An exceptional opening week in Niagara is completed by Candida, usually one of my least favourite Shaw plays because it seems, in its sane, and economical and well-constructed way, like something somebody else might have written. Tadeusz Bradecki's production, however, gives it a hard comic edge and, even more rewardingly, a hard passionate edge.

Nigel Shawn Williams as Morell, the Christian Socialist East End preacher, is the most muscular of Christians. He probably doesn't have to speak quite as loudly to persuade us that Morell is over-fond of the sound of his own voice, but he makes the point, and when his intensity is funnelled into anguish over the possibility that he might lose his wife to another man, he is more real in the role than any actor I have known.

Its telling that Shaw, who must have agreed with Morell politically, was so merciless at pointing out his flaws. Hes comparably, though not quite equally, ruthless with his other alter ego and Morell's rival Marchbanks, the 19-year-old poet of preRaphaelite tendencies, whom Wade Bogert-O'Brien plays with a coltish enthusiasm thats touching rather then embarrassing. As Candida herself, who has significantly less stage time than either of her admirers, Claire Jullien is a sensible, capable sphinx.

This well-made plays cast list is symmetrically divided between three principals and three supports. Most aggressive of the latter is Krista Colosimo who, as Morell's unrequitedly adoring typist, shouts even louder and far more continuously than he does, either in self-defence or because it's catching. Graeme Somerville is contrastingly sly and winning as his equally worshipful curate. Somewhere between the two comes Norman Browning as Candidas boorish but funny dad, forever on the make. Another unresolved question around this plays heroine is how the apple fell so very far from the tree, or the chip from the old blockhead. There's certainly no similarity between their accents. Maybe, along with his sermonizing talents, Reverand Morell possessed some of the skills of Professor Henry Higgins, currently in practice just down the road.
