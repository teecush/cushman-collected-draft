THEATRE REVIEW

Summerworks Performance Festival Toronto's annual Summerworks Festival is a cornucopia. It's also a crapshoot. This year the dice have mostly rolled against me.

Not altogether, though. One play, Reality Theatre (Factory Theatre) has some wit going for it. Unfortunately, much of it is muffled in performance. Julia Lederer's play is actually a succession of alternating playlets about - I think - dehumanization in the age of the Internet.

It begins promisingly enough.

In front of a backdrop of steeply receding perspectives - designed by Christine Urquhart - sits a woman eating popcorn, for all the world as if she were in the audience. A man appears, sweeping the floor. "Don't break the fourth wall" she tells him. He must take that as a challenge because pretty soon he breaks out his own popcorn. And there they both are, munching and glowering at one another.

So maybe they're both spectators, treating one another as spectacle. And maybe they provide a frame for what follows. But it's difficult to tell because the two actors disappear from the stage, at least in their initial guises. They have to return in others since they make up two-thirds of the cast. One scene has the entire company of three, banging away at their computers, trying to get a life. This is the show's most direct attack on the paradoxically isolating effects of social media, but that's hardly a new subject for satire, and the scene adds nothing.

Other narrative strands are more elaborate. In one, we meet Arthur who, some centuries ago, made a deal with the devil by which he would remain young while a portrait of himself grew steadily older. (And yes, you have heard something like this before.) Now, sick of eternal life, he's escorted by a friend named Bud - with whom he's bonded over their shared devotion to Medieval Times - to the head office of Starbucks in Seattle, otherwise known as the Gates of Hell. Here, Arthur hopes to be released from his contract. Bud is enslaved to his smartphone, and Arthur complains that "like a helpless fly I'm caught up in the world wide web." It's a smart line and one with some tenuous relevance to the presumptive overall theme.

Smarter but even fainter in its relevance is a prolonged in-joke about an actress with two small roles in Beauty and the Beast. She tells us about the multiple shades of meaning that her expensive bicontinental training allowed her to infuse into "Do you call this bacon?" - her only words in the opening Villagers' chorus. She then tells us how she made her second-act role of the Spoon so much her own that she became the go-to performer for cutlery.

Krista Morin, playing her, hints at style without actually achieving it; she tosses off her lines but doesn't shape them. She also plays Arthur/Dorian/Faust, to much the same effect, or lack of it. She was some way into her first scene before I realized that her character was meant to be a man. Akosua Amo-Adem is similarly blurry as the receptionist from Hell, though she comes on strong enough as the corn-popper of the prologue. The acoustics of the Factory are notoriously unfriendly, but that's something that the director, Rebecca Applebaum, should have taken into account. As it is, the only actor who consistently conquers them, while remaining consistently casual, is Andy Trithardt. He's also the sound designer, so he must know about such things.

Murkier still is These Violent Delights (also Factory) in which most of the cast are arrayed in what are best described as masks but actually resemble conical hats. These, apart from prompting unhelpful thoughts of the Ku Klux Klan, add so much reverberation to the voices that emanate from within them as to drown out practically everything their wearers say. The show takes its title from a line in Romeo and Juliet and its theme from that play's conclusion. Bonding over the deaths of their children, old Montague and Capulet proposed to erect golden statues in their honour. This piece echoes, especially through the voice of Juliet's Nurse. Or rather through the voices, since the Nurse is played in turn by eight members of the 10-person cast.

"Some shall be pardoned and some punished," said the Prince at the end of the source play, and it seems that the Nurse was one of the unlucky ones. She's been banished from Verona, but is now summoned back to bear witness to the unveiling of the golden monument. She doesn't want to go, complaining that Juliet when alive never once called her by her name (true) and that everybody blames her for the tragedy, including Shakespeare (false).

She also has broader, political and philosophical objections, questions that the author-director, Cole Lewis, poses more clearly in her program-note than she ever does in her text and production: "Are monuments capable of bearing witness to history? What role does a monument play in its current history? Perhaps we should take more time to consider the actions that bring a monument into being and the concrete actions we can take in light of our memorialized past." Perhaps we should, but this production does it only its own imagination, never on the stage.

Maybe Romeo and Juliet wasn't the best place to start; it isn't all that political a play. One of the Nurses - one of two to make any kind of impression - asks, "Who will remember in 400 years?" Well, if we're talking about R and J, we all remember; this show wouldn't exist if we didn't. The production's dismal aural qualities are matched by the gracelessness of its choral movement, which even manages to make a mess of the curtain-call.
