FIVE FINGERS

Theatre Passe Muraille, Backspace, Toronto

The Five Fingers of Robin Fulford's play belong on the hand that delivered a slap to the face of a woman who lived with the man who struck the blow that ended the marriage that once filled the house that somebody-or-other must have built, sometime. It's an inexorable progression, and in its inexorability resides the greatest power of the play. Also its greatest limitation.

Two years ago, Five Fingers was actually performed in a house, at Bathurst and Adelaide. We followed the characters and the story around from one room to another. Fulford now believes that the audience on that occasion got caught up as much in the manner of presentation as in what was being presented, and he is probably right. But that doesn't mean they didn't respond at all to the subject, and in fact the story was more engrossing for being presented in a super-realistic context. It got, as I remember, very scary and very involving. It retains those qualities in this new production, but to a lesser degree. Directed by Wendy White on a stylized set of her own design, principally composed of three hanging drapes, it seems short, both literally and figuratively, of places to go.

There are Tuck and Anna, who were dating until she got pregnant, at which point they got married. Now that they're alone with their baby son, Tuck feels trapped and Anna feels exploited. One morning, finding no toothpaste in the bathroom, Tuck hits Anna. He immediately regrets it, or says he does. She leaves, and refuses to return. And that is all.

The show is produced by Fulford's own company, Platform 9, whose "mandate is to do original, issue-oriented plays." Those are the author's own words, and they raise a couple of questions. One: Who gave the company this mandate? If, as is probable, nobody did and it's their own idea, then it isn't a mandate, it's a policy. Two: Who, apart from a few benighted schoolteachers, wants "issue- oriented plays," anyway? A play may be inspired by an issue, it may engage with it, but that cannot be the sole point of its existence. Why do talented writers persist in talking like social workers? If Fulford as playwright came up with a play that could not be labelled and captioned as if it were an op-ed piece, would Fulford as producer feel obliged to reject it?

On this particular issue, most of us have our minds made up before we enter the theatre. We are not in favour of men hitting women or, come to that, of women hitting men. A play is hardly likely to contest that point. All it can do is take us inside the minds of the people involved, help us understand, maybe unsettle us a bit. This play, in its current form, does that admirably with the character of Tuck, and hardly at all with that of Anna. In sociological terms, she declines to get mired in the victim role. In dramatic terms, she's stuck with it. We get very little sense of what she may have contributed to this marriage, good or bad. She has very little to say for herself, or even against herself, and Suzanne Hersh, playing her, spends much of the play in recitation mode. She is too monotonously resentful, even granted that she has a lot to resent.

Tuck is more fully explored. (Well, the author is a man.) He is a boy-man who resents the erosion of his bachelor freedoms and tries to hang on to as many of them as possible. He also has a fierce distaste for bodily functions, at least other than sexual.

The two motifs come together at the moment of violence. He wants to get out of the house but cannot bear to do so without cleaning his teeth. Mark Cassidy, who has been given some excellent speeches, gets the character's rage across from the start, and does reasonably well by what passes for his remorse. I'd say that the play is emotionally on his side and intellectually on his partner's, but this seems to denote not so much complexity as incompleteness. It does, though, cast an interesting pall of guilt over the evening.

Fulford's original title for the play was Swahili Beckett, and in its present stripped staging it has a sense of unlocalized enclosure that fits into the Beckett tradition. It's impressive as well as oppressive, but it seems to deny its characters options. As an abstract issue, spousal abuse is simple -- it's the specific cases that get complicated. This play implicitly acknowledges that, but denies itself the tools for exploration.

Maybe, if I may contradict myself, it needs to be more of an issue play, to situate itself more fully in the wider everyday world. It's offered to us here as an elemental inferno, school of Strindberg, and in those terms it can only go half the distance. What it does, it does with style, constantly reworking its central image. We hear, for example, of fingers thrust into a vagina in the dark, and of the different meanings this has for both participants. Fulford is a writer, and it shows.
