robertcushman@lycos.com; Born Ready and Pusha Man are in repertory at Theatre Passe Muraille through Oct, 19; Better Parts is at Passe Muraille Backspace through Oct. 29, and Fish Eyes is on through Nov. 5. 416-504-7529.

Theatre Passe Muraille, in a vigorous protest against the Toronto stages customary fixed-run routine, is devoting its fall season to nine new plays, performed in repertory. The program is called Stage 3, on account of its triple-barrelled marketing slogan word.sound.power." You have to love those dots.

First off are two double bills, one on each of the theatres stages. Passe Muraille's co-producer is the Obsidian Theatre Company, the city's leading black group, but the overall program might be called ecumenically ethnic. The opening main-stage show, though, is very black; two plays by a new writer, Joseph Jomo Pierre, that sound to my sheltered ears like authentic ghetto- blasters. (Pardon my archaic expression.) That goes especially for the first one, Born Ready, in which a young man known as B-Side announces, "Im from the hood, and adds, You know what a hood is? A hood is something that you pull over your head to conceal yourself." If you want word power, there it is: language that travels, sardonic and anxious and menacing and defeated all at once.

Born Ready (whose original title was Born Ready To Die) is an extended flashback; it starts with a shooting, and then has killer, victim and witness telling us how they got there. The killing has less impact than the talking. The one woman of the three announces herself right off as "the official ride-or-die Bitch, the iconic Peggy Sue, subservient to the fullest. This may be meant to be ironic -- Cara Rickettss smouldering performance points it that way -- but dramatically she gets the subsidiary role, one that embraces some stark memories of childhood in an orphanage, but also contains the cloudiest lines in a script that's best when it's most specific.

B-Side (whose every line, as spoken by Mike G-yohannes, rings like a challenge) talks about the pecking order of poverty, and of how he rose right up it when he got himself a gun. And of how, as a gang leader, he felt both elated and uneasy. (You doing dirt with your fam.... You sipping that ghetto-fabulous juice. To 200 or so people you are actually the shit.... But that all changes.... And a nigga got to live up to the hype." I quote directly.) Blackman, a little shyer (at least thats how his author plays him), grew up even more deprived ("my aunt raised me ... if moms had hard times, my aunt had it worst), with a nervous, fascinated awareness of women. B-Side seems, by contrast, to have marched right into his sexuality, along with everything else.

There is a limit to how much you can do in a play with monologues, even when theyre as strong as these. Eventually Pierre brings his characters together, but he still has them talking past one another; the knot doesn't tie, and in that regard Philip Akin's production doesnt help any. Atmospherically, though, its fine, played on flights of fire-escape steps backed by Passe Muraille's resident catwalk. The inner city has seldom come this powerfully to a Toronto stage.

The companion piece, Pusha Man, is more of an ensemble number, though it, too, gives the impression that it would be happier as a set of solos. Ricketts and G-yohannes reappear, this time as a young couple -- well, two young people who've had sex -- named Sarah and Benjamin. It starts with Sarah all but giving birth in an alley and Benjamin nervously promising that help is on the way. It turns out that he doesnt mean the doctor. He means the local dope dealer, Pusha Man himself, a.k.a. Absolute. David Collins is monstrously cool in this role; he makes a singing entrance and seems like an updating of Porgy and Besss diabolic Sportin' Life. He turns out, though, to have less of the Devil about him than of God, and a disgruntled God at that. (Your poor asses dropping your last five dollars into a plate that I ain't never going to see, that aint funny. Goddammit, buy some food!") He does make Sarah feel better, though the womans role in this play surprisingly in the circumstances, is even more passive than in the other. Absolute's main concern is to make Benjamin, who wants to run away from fatherhood and just about everything else, face up to life. The script I read was short and rather snappy, especially in the Pusha Man's exasperation with his own lingo. ("No mellow, no yellow, no fellow, I dont even wanna hear nothing that sounds like it.) But it seems to have got elongated in rehearsal, with passages both mystical and mystifying; I can just about understand why Benjamin, in worm-turning mode, puts on Absolutes trousers, but it beats me why Absolute has Benjamin suck his breasts. I would guess that Pierre, having given us the facts in the first play, is aiming for myth in this one, though he's more poetic when he's being real. But man, he can write.

- - -

Both these plays, Born Ready especially, are aiming, as far as I can tell, for theatrical rap. Metaphorically, that is; the words arent in rhyme or even in verse, and any music is strictly incidental. But the worlds the same, as are the attitudes, though they're critiqued. Better Parts, first on at the Backspace, is theatrical rap; Nicole Stamp -- author, performer and director -- tells her own story, in couplets, to musical backing, though both her rhythms and those of her accompanist (Paul Clifford on bass) have a variety and sophistication way beyond hip hop.

This show is, in fact, a total joy. It's Stamps story of her walk home from a temping job, fantasizing about a life of comparatively rich and famous, being taken home to meet the families of exotic men from villages Ill never see/called Clinton, Ontario, or Sault Ste. Marie." Finally, in one of the few successful examples of meta-theatre I have ever encountered, she envisions herself performing this very show on perhaps this very stage, watched by "actors and actors' families/as Toronto audiences always seem to be, the acting contingent observing well, she's funny but she isnt any better than me." (Between Stamp and Pierre, I havent had so much fun quoting in weeks.) She is funny, enchantingly light and sure of touch. And, considering she's doing triple duty, remarkably the opposite of self-indulgent. Her show lasts less than half an hour.

It's also, of course, a hard act to follow: and Fish Eyes -- written and performed, but not directed, by Anita Majumdar -- is much, much, much too long. Not specifically autobiographical (or so Id guess), it tells of Meena, a Canadian teenager who is being groomed for a dancing contest in her familys native India, and would much rather not go. Meanwhile she's hopelessly infatuated with the most popular boy in school, who is, of course, thoroughly taken up with the most popular girl.

This is pretty tired territory, and the ethnic component doesn't make it any fresher. It might work better if it zipped along, but Majumdar and her director, Gregory Prest, insist on lingering over every step of the story. Counterpointed with our heroine is her fanatical dance instructor, known as Aunty, who is still locked in mental struggle with the British Raj, one member of which once broke her heart in what she insists are parallel circumstances. The tone is mostly humorous without actually being funny, though there is the occasional sharp joke, as when Meenas white-bread ideal is overheard referring to his equally pale girlfriend as "my bitch," thereby casting an interestingly envious light on the plays next door. Majumdars acting dexterity, as she slips in one role and out the other, can't be faulted; neither can her skill in the dance, which she manages to satirize and celebrate at the same time.
