THE COAST OF UTOPIA

Olivier Theatre, London

About halfway along The Coast of Utopia, which is Tom Stoppard's umbrella title for a trilogy of plays about the Russian revolutionaries of the 19th century, somebody tells Alexander Herzen, you could be Minister of Paradox, with special responsibility for Irony. Its a position Stoppard himself could easily fill. If there were an election for it, he would win by a landslide.

Each of the three plays lasts some three hours. The total has been greeted by many British critics as a fascinating failure, though I would call it a flawed success. The plays opened in London last May, on the largest stage of the National Theatre in London and in a spectacular production by that theatres outgoing director, Trevor Nunn. The prudent and/or intrepid could see them all on one day, as I did just over a week ago. (It was the penultimate performance.)

It's a panoramic piece, but Herzen is the nearest thing it has to a hero. He's a crucial but somewhat forgotten figure: excoriated by Marx, favourably mentioned by Lenin (probably for the wrong reasons), more recently celebrated by Isaiah Berlin (Stoppards principal source) and, in the current issue of Atlantic Monthly, Christopher Hitchens. In the cast list to Stoppards part one, Voyage, he is described as "a would-be revolutionary." In part two, Shipwreck, he is a radical writer. In part three, Salvage, he's "a Russian exile." That neatly summarizes not only his trajectory, but those of the plays themselves.

Chronologically, they stretch from 1833 to 1865. So the halfway point, roughly, is Europe's year of revolutions, 1848. And the plays also stretch across the continent, moving westward. Part One is set entirely in Russia, where a generation of idealists is stirring, challenging czarist authority. Part Two shows them in flux, mostly banished from their own country, but deeply involved in revolts from Paris to Poland. In Part Three, most of them have fetched up in London, centre of one of the most reactionary governments but allowing refugees to live, plot and dream in peace. "Do poverty and liberty go together," asks one of them, or is it just the English sense of humour?

Stoppard is an emigre himself (from Czechoslovakia via India), and in part the play is the most elaborate, and the most ambiguous, tribute he has ever paid to his adopted country -- or, more accurately, to the country that adopted him. England is described, with a mixture of enthusiasm and exasperation, as "the rock against which revolution breaks." It tolerates you to death. There are worse ways to die.

Herzen certainly thinks so. We are told that in 1825, at the age of 17, he and a lifelong friend, Nicholas Ogarev, vowed, in memory of the failed December plot against the Czar, to devote their lives to perpetuating the struggle. The Herzen we meet is a socialist, but one increasingly distrustful of dogma, and especially of any doctrine that calls on its adherents to sacrifice their own or other peoples lives for the possible good of future generations: If we cant arrange our own happiness, it's a conceit beyond vulgarity to arrange the happiness of those who come after us.

I find that statement extremely sympathetic, not only in its own right but because it coincides with my own long-held opinion about Stoppard's moral qualities as a writer. We all know hes superbly witty. His jokes, and the texture they give his plays, are in themselves an affirmation that life might be worth living. Most political playwrights, certainly those of Stoppards own British generation, seem to want you to join them in their misery.

Herzen's mature position might be summed up as "free the serfs -- then we'll see," and its hard to quarrel with that. Hard for the audience, anyway; many of the other characters have no trouble doing it. His unfailing decency (the Paris barricades cured him of any taste for bloodshed) carries an inevitable theatrical penalty. Its probably no accident that the first play, in which he plays only a minor role, is by general consent the most enjoyable.

Its dominating figure is Michael Bakunin, who will end his days as an anarchist but who is essentially a serial enthusiast, embracing one fashionable philosophy after another. When we first meet him he's a military cadet, deeply into German idealism. Resigning his commission (study is difficult in the Artillery, owing to the loud explosions which are a regular feature of Artillery life), he then decides that physical reality has its points after all, and devotes himself to increasingly radical politics. Eventually, he gets himself sent to Siberia, but escapes. In the meantime, he deeply irritates his rich land-and-serf-owning father, sponges off his friends ("is there nobody who sees that the future of philosophy in Russia hangs on lending me a few miserable roubles?"), and exploits his four adoring sisters, who want to go to Moscow. Unlike their Chekhovian equivalents (and the resemblance is of course intentional to the point of pastiche), they actually make it there, quite often.

In fact, the play is bisected. Its first act takes place on the Bakunin family estate, and its second goes over the same period of time in Moscow, with several characters shuttling between. This yields a few nice ironies (and paradoxes), but it's needlessly confusing. When it comes to these kinds of structural games, Stoppard is no Ayckbourn, and there is more than enough to take in as things chronologically are. But the intellectual ferment of the time is wonderfully evoked, its passion and absurdity (literature is taken so seriously that the censor can close a paper over a hostile theatre review) exhilaratingly fused.

The second play is the most diffuse, though held together by the impact on the Russian emigres of the Europe whose libertarian ideals they, or most of them, had worshipped from afar. Karl Marx makes a brief appearance (hearing that Bakunin has been spending time with the working class, he asks what are they like?) and many critics seem to have found this disrespectful. They miss the point. Marx is the one character most of us already know something about, and besides, he wasnt Russian.

The last instalment is the soberest. Its history's fault rather than the author's than many of the liveliest characters have by now died off, but the play seems to share its exiled characters own sense that the real action is going on elsewhere. They would like to be back in Russia and so would we. (In another sense, they are perfectly at home. Like every character Stoppard has ever created, they all sound English.)

The earlier Stoppard play this one most resembles is Travesties, which showed us writers and revolutionaries becalmed in neutral Zurich during the First World War. But all those characters, including Lenin, were treated as historys clowns, and the narrating figure was the greatest clown of all. By contrast, Herzen, at the centre of this one, spends much of the last part making the same humane response to a series of off-stage cataclysms. Stephen Dillane, who plays him at the National, is intelligent but excessively dour: surprising, after his sparkling performance in Stoppard's The Real Thing, in a production that transformed my view of the play.

There is no lack of wit in the play. Sometimes it strikes very deep. A serf-owners' human holdings used to be described as so many hundreds (or thousands) of souls, and Stoppard can do a lot with that. But the humour proves hard to harness in performance. The main difficulty in Stoppards plays -- increasingly as time has gone on - - is their allusiveness. Almost every line is simultaneously a joke, a stage in an argument, and a chunk of exposition, and each needs to work on all three levels.

Nunns production is superbly organized, and visually stunning, with William Dudley's sets relying mainly on computerized projections on an Imax-sized screen; the camerawork scoops you into the action. It's thrilling, and a wonderful way of taming the vast Olivier stage, but it isnt what youd call intimate or laughter- friendly. The ideal Stoppard performers would perhaps be stand-up comics with university degrees, and not all this cast have the necessary skills. The best is Will Keen as Belinsky, the penurious and tubercular scribe whose road to revolution is literary criticism; he describes wonderfully funny and wonderfully touching verbal and physical parabolas.

Douglas Henshall is engagingly impossible as Bakunin, whom Herzen disowns but can never dislike. Guy Henry is imposingly languid as Turgenev, the greatest writer on display, who has Herzen's humanity without his idealism (he laments inter alia that "the word 'liberal has now entered the scatalogical vocabulary, like halfwit' or 'hypocrite ") and who, in one of the best late scenes, is brought up against a nihilist far more sinister and unyielding than Bakunin. His response is to put him into his magnum opus, Fathers and Sons. Earlier, life has imitated art imitating life by giving us an opening scene straight out of his A Month in the Country.

The much-praised actress Eve Best -- cast as Herzens wife, a Bakunin sister, and a Teutonic Mary Poppins -- was off at my visit, so a large hole in the plays' scheme gaped even wider. The characters' romantic and marital entanglements are obviously meant to parallel their intellectual commitments. Initially, this is fun. Young ladies fall for unresponsive idealists and wail I cant understand why they call it romanticism. Later, things get darker. Utopian sex proves as elusive as Utopian politics. A comrade seduces Mrs. Herzen; he in turn unites with the spouse of his oldest friend, Ogarev. But all this seems more a diversion from the main theme rather than an enrichment of it. Except, that is, for the scenes involving Herzens deaf son Kolya, whose death by drowning prompts one of the author's finest speeches: "Because children grow up, we think a child's purpose is to grow up. But a child's purpose is to be a child." Nobody (compare the TV play Professional Foul) writes more movingly and more acutely about children than Stoppard.

The response at the end was, by the standards of these marathons, muted. I can understand why. All the same, I want to see the plays again. There are rumours of this show visiting New York, and I can also imagine it being done at least as well by Canadian actors, though I doubt if we could muster such a physical production. I think, by the way, that the plays are too short.
