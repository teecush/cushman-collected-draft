Honest Ed -- The Bargain Musical

The Poor Alex, Toronto

Honest Ed may be short, but his show has legs.

Honest Ed -- The Bargain Musical is not Ed Mirvish's show in any legal sense. He has not produced it, and it is not -- not yet, anyway -- gracing the stage of his stately Royal Alexandra. You will find it at the shabby, independent Poor Alex, which, given the circumstances, is a pretty cool choice of venue.

Ed has given the show something far more important than money or a stage; Ed has given it his life. The shows creators have taken this life, torn it into flamboyant little strips, and danced joyously over the wreckage.

Surveying the career of Torontos one and only retail king turned theatre magnate, the musical comes to a momentous conclusion: Ed was in show business before he was in show business. This, given his addiction to neon and slogans, is not news, but the show takes the proposition to mythic lengths.

Ed Mirvish: man or myth? is a question that scholars will be debating for centuries. This production leaves no doubt where it stands on the matter. The first thing we are told about Ed is that he was circumcised by Al Jolson's father, who materializes, shears flashing. (As far as I know, this is the first musical ever to begin with a circumcision number. Did anyone tell them to cut it?)

Brock Simpson, co-author of the music and lyrics, plays the title role, and in flat cap and smock shirt he makes a very persuasive young Ed of the Toronto street markets.

Later, he graduates to a nattier hat and jacket, this being his sole concession to the passage of time, seventy-odd years of it. And in his performing style, which is perky but contained, he does suggest the paradox, the oxymoron, the paradoxymoron, that is Mirvish, the low-key extrovert.

Early numbers include Dundas, which blends description, defiance, and daredevil wordplay (on Dundas . . . there's no shopping on Sundas . . . well be the boy wondas") and Subtle Sells, in which Papa Mirvish advises young Ed on the detail of retail: personal service, lots of credit, and no vulgar display. Having thus spectacularly misread the future, the old man, who knows how a symbol should behave, drops obligingly dead.

Lisa Lambert, the co-composer, appears as Anne Mirvish, here a dizzy beanpole with zero resemblance to the real Mrs. M but lots of charm. On their first date, Ed takes her -- or maybe she takes him - - to the Royal Ontario Museum, a magical place whose initials inspire him to acrostic rhapsodies ("a River Of Music flies from my Round Open Mouth) that she reciprocates (Ill Reject Other Men, like Ramon Or Moishe"). Now that's a love song. Rely On Me.

By intermission the big store has opened, and the foundations of the empire have been firmly laid. Now, a sad rule about biographies, even biographies as cheerfully cavalier with mere facts as this one, is that the early struggles play more interestingly than the years of success. If Ed has had any great tragedies in his life, he has kept them hidden, and this show is not about to go to the trouble of inventing any.

And there's another thing. The first half of the production uses theatrical pastiche to spoof non-theatrical subjects; its good double-layered fun when Anne and two wartime customers at Honest Eds turn into the Andrews Sisters, or when director-choreographer Scott Morel spikes a celebration of Ed's discount emporium with a dash of Bob Fosse's Big Spender.

But the second half gets us to Eds own theatrical career, and here showbiz parody becomes an incestuous end in itself. Theres a long audition number that is less a take-off of A Chorus Line than a warped copy of it. There's an endless sequence about Andrew Lloyd Webber, on the grounds that he was once Ed's unsuccessful rival for the acquisition of the Old Vic. (Maybe its because Im a Londoner, but I thought the show lost all conviction when it crossed the pond, exchanging loving caricature for bloodless stereotype.)

The second act also boasts the only unsuccessful Jolson impersonation I have ever seen; the same actor, scoring something of a grand slam, also turns in an even less convincing Robert Goulet.

Even after the break there are good things: a twangy, down-home song called Wilderness Girl ("a friend of the fox and the red- tailed squirl"), and a glimpse of David Mirvish in what we are told were his beatnik days, when he apparently had a lot more hair than he has now.

The cast is a winning bunch of quick-change artists. The production, with no designer credited, lays out its wares in orange- boxes, just as Ed did when he was getting started, and just as inventively.

And besides, what, at the dismal end of the 20th century, do you say to a musical full of funny, inventive lyrics with hardly a bum rhyme, and wedded to music that's merry and supportive? I'll tell you what you say: Thank you.
