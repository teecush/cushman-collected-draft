MISS JULIE

Premiere Dance Theatre, Toronto

Man, according to August Strindberg, is the lord of creation, the creator of culture, while woman is "this stunted form of human being." He offers these cheerful definitions in the preface to his Miss Julie, probably the most famous of all one-act plays; and the most remarkable thing about them, apart from the casual assurance with which he tosses them off before proceeding to more important matters, is their lack of connection to the drama they are supposed to introduce. Strindberg wrote his share of misogynist plays. Miss Julie, whatever his intentions, isn't one of them.

Miss Julie, the daughter of an unseen and impoverished Swedish count, has the dignity that drama always bestows on the death- marked. Jean, the valet with whom she copulates in the course of a single midsummer night (it would be impossible to say who seduces whom) is a cold-blooded opportunist with ambitions, that will probably be fulfilled, in the hotel trade. Neither is a sympathetic character, though both are drawn from within, but theres no doubt which of them commands the greater respect. Its social rather than sexual equality that takes a beating in this play.

The play was written in 1888. Strindberg anticipated -- and with hugely superior economy -- the achievement for which John Osborne's Look Back in Anger was to be acclaimed 70 years later: that of putting the sex war and the class war on the same stage. He also managed to throw in religion. Miss Julie is oppressed by the Biblical injunction that "the last shall be first," which suggests that she, as an aristocrat, can never achieve salvation. Jean points out that, having submitted to him, she is now herself the last. Maybe Strindberg agreed. But in letting her accept her fate, he puts her right back at the head of the line.

To say Miss Julie is a power struggle is merely to say it's a good play; but it gives us that dynamic in a purer form than most. Its also one of the first avowedly naturalistic plays. Strindberg devotes much of his preface to saying so, and to proposing theatrical reforms that are now every theatres stock-in-trade. So, to restore the original impact, it's tempting to ignore the author's instructions and turn the play into a ritual.

This seems to be the intention of Herbert Olschoks new production for Soulpepper. Its extraordinarily uneven, swinging back-and-forth between brilliant and hopeless. It begins with Jean and his fiancee, the cook Christine, moving around the stage to music, like figures on a clock. It transpires that this is more than just a jazzy way of getting the show on the road; when the Count's bell rings from off stage, the two servants -- Jean, especially, with all his professions of independence -- start behaving like obedient automata. But it takes the whole length of the play for this to click into place. Last year, Olschok directed Soulpepper's La Ronde, with its 10 serial couplings, and at times he seems to be offering Miss Julie as an 11th. As in the previous show, a sax signifies sex; when Julie and Jean are off together, a lady saxophonist crosses the stage, soulfully blowing. Meanwhile, cupboard doors fly open and crockery tumbles from the shelves, to symbolize just how furiously theyre going at it. (The play takes place in a kitchen, though Peter Hartwells set, in greens and browns, looks more like a schoolroom. Maybe that's Scandinavia for you.)

When the characters are on stage there is plenty of pawing, with hands moving enthusiastically up and down legs. This is becoming a Soulpepper trademark, familiar from their productions of Chekhov. It's exactly what you expect in Strindberg: Desire is a given, and to keep illustrating it is underlining the obvious. In La Ronde the stylized techniques set off detailed realistic acting, with the heat coming from inside as well as out. In Miss Julie, with two of the same actors, this doesnt happen, or only sporadically.

Patricia Fagan and Tony Nardi are maybe the two outstanding actors of Soulpeppers second wave. (It's a tribute to the company that, at only five years old, it already has a second wave). In outline they have the characters of Julie and Jean down pat, but they have problems filling them in. Nardi conveys all of Jean's resentfulness, resolution and sullen magnetism, but his vocal limitations are nearly as troublesome here as they were in The Winters Tale. When he has a long speech -- like his reminiscence of spying on the young lady of the house when they were both children - - I found myself tuning out. Fagan -- tall, slim, wire-drawn intense and looking great in jodhpurs -- has the opposite problem; her arias, especially the self-hating ones at the end are riveting, but in dialogue she often falls back on all-purpose emoting. (The play feels under-rehearsed; some of it like a late run-through, with the actors still testing the weight of their lines.) She could become the best Canadian actress of her generation, but though shes the right chronological age for Julie, who's supposed to be 25, she doesn't yet carry the technical weight for the role.

Jane Spidell, who is certainly a comely Christine, gives the play an anchor in acquiescent normality, even when required to fall asleep, not slumped over the table as youd expect, but bolt upright in her chair. She only falters when she passes from piously lecturing her fallen mistress to angrily screeching at her, as if shed caught some kind of theatrical bug. And the three of them make the last scene, in which Jean, with Christine's silent approval hypnotizes Miss Julie to her doom, more persuasive than I have ever known it. Usually, I have lost patience with the characters by this point and would like to meet somebody else. At the end the production finds its rhythm, and comes through.
