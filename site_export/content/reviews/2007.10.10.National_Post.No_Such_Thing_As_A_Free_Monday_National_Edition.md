Monday, Monday. So good to me. Or it used to be. When I was just a theatre critic, my Mondays were free. No plays open on a Monday night and few even play then. Television, of course, is relentless. So, by way of getting my feet a little wetter, I settled down on Monday for a night of regular viewing.

8 p.m. Global. Prison Break. Sara is definitively dead, her head delivered in a box to Lincoln, who -- for reasons both selfish and un -- can't bring himself to break the news to her loving, incarcerated Michael. Informed sources (in the real world) suggest that this development is the result of a complex series of negotiations and ultimata between the producers, the network and the actress concerned who is off -- in Canada -- having a baby. I think it's regrettable since: a) both the actress and the character were attractive; b) there was real chemistry between her and Michael, actually helped by the psychological and often physical distance between them; and, frankly, it hurts; c) Michael is left devoutly planning his latest escape in ignorance of what's gone down, which makes him look rather foolish and thereby reduces the stakes. Doubtless there will be a fearsome scene when he finds out; Wentworth Miller, pent-up in his penitentiary, can certainly do intensity and he can do anger. But he's no more a tragic hero than Jack Bauer on 24 (well, maybe slightly more) and the writers of both shows are getting above themselves if they think otherwise. These thriller soaps aren't Shakespeare. They aren't The Sopranos. They aren't even Deadwood.

And though I can't give up on Prison Break, it is marking time. The titular premise was maybe good for only one, so to speak, outing. It got a miraculous second-season second wind with the ex- cons and their pursuers interacting on the outside. Now that they're back inside again, in a Panama jail where the prisoners have been left to their own devices, it's repetitive and the fact that the inmates have taken over the asylum makes it, oddly, less urgent. There are no compelling new characters this season, and the old ones are in a rut. Bellick, grandly played by Wade Williams, has surely suffered enough, even for a sadistic weasel of a corrupt corrections officer. At least T-Bag got to murder somebody this episode; I mean it's been weeks.

9 p.m. CBC. Intelligence. I've been with Prison Break since the beginning. Intelligence was new to me (stop nodding your head) and I wondered what it would be like to come in on the middle of a densely- plotted thriller. It is, of course, hopelessly confusing, though some things seemed strangely familiar. "Previously on Intelligence" (this usage seems to have become standard, thanks to 24), somebody's brother got framed for something; indeed, once the episode got started, "tell us where your brother is right now" seemed to be the key line, uttered by bullying and -- as far as I could tell -- crooked cops, most of them with shaven scalps. They were going bald- headed at the job of nailing the wrong man. A nightclub got raided by a SWAT team whose leader, not getting the attention he felt he deserved, was reduced to yelling "turn the f---ing music off." By the end of the hour things were a little less murky, and I could tell that Intelligence was intelligent. This Canadian series has been bought by the U.S.; they shouldn't need to change it much.

10 p.m. Stayed with CBC for The National. Top story of the day seemed to be that "Peter is away tonight." We were told that a visiting dignitary to our troops in Afghanistan "would be treated like any other Canadian" and the point was driven home by having posed for an interview outside a Tim Hortons. A piece about refugee Somali students at Canadian universities (title: The Lucky Ones) was very well done, genuinely heart-warming. All CBC programs were regularly interrupted by last-minute Ontario election ads, all of them desperate. Put all their messages together and you come up with there being thousands more cops on Ontario's streets, none of whom can find a family doctor.

11 p.m. Comedy Network. Jon Stewart, in fine quizzical form, mused on President Musharraf 's triumph at the Pakistani polls ("I find him charming, but 98%?") and getting positively metaphysical when interviewing John Oliver on President Bush's mixed messages on the word "torture." "I'd expect" said Oliver, wonderfully indignant, "you at least to support our words: our brave fighting words, many of them making the ultimate sacrifice: losing their definition." Orwell would have approved. Stewart's interview of Vicente Fox was, as his interviews often are, an anticlimax, prompting only the thought that the former Mexican president looks rather like John Cleese, had he elected to play Manuel rather than Basil. Stephen Colbert, on the other hand, did a super interview; he's usually better talking to people he pretends to agree with (right) rather than those he pretends to oppose (left) but this time he had a fellow liberal -- George Saunders, author of The Braindead Megaphone, attacking talk-show hosts -- whose ironic sense matched his own. Colbert is also in two minds about Columbus Day, though not for the fashionable reasons: "You don't think an American could have discovered America? We live here. How hard could it be?"
