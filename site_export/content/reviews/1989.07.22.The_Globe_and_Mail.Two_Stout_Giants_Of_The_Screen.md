ZERO MOSTEL: A BIOGRAPHY BY JARED BROWN Collier-Macmillan, 334 pages, $31.50

Two stout giants of the screen

ZERO MOSTEL: A BIOGRAPHY BY JARED BROWN Collier-Macmillan, 334 pages, $31.50

Saturday, July 22, 1989

ROBERT CUSHMAN

CITIZEN WELLES: A BIOGRAPHY OF ORSON WELLES BY FRANK BRADY Collier-Macmillan, 655 pages, $34.95 REVIEW BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Two fat volumes about two fat actors: one man who played Falstaff and another who should have. The Zero Mostel biography, to be truthful, is no more than becomingly stout; the Orson Welles is positively obese.

Both actors possessed deep, rolling voices that were among the most distinctive of the century. Welles, marinated in oil and honey, courted us from his early radio triumphs down (in every sense) to his efficacious TV commercials for sherry. He remains the only major movie director who can claim the recital of his closing credits as part of his legend. His habitual sign-off since radio days ("Obediently yours, Orson Welles") could only be matched for bland, patrician irony by Duke Ellington's repeated declarations that he did indeed love us madly.

Mostel's irony was more of the harsh, blistering kind. He was a teddy- bear with teeth. His biographer claims that the impact of his clowning was primarily physical while admitting that it was an impact too overpowering for film. On the evidence of The Producers, Mostels one totemic movie, he is right; Mostel is funny, yes, but leeringly, disconcertingly grotesque. From the one time I saw him on stage - as Leopold Bloom in an overblown Broadway revival of Ulysses In Nighttown, the Joyce adaptation that had established him off-Broadway in the fifties - I recall his inspired mime of martyred disgust when required to enact the line Bloom kissed the oozing, suppurating wounds of the veterans.

Otherwise my Mostel memories are vocal ones. I recall the introductory voice on the Broadway cast album of A Funny Thing Happened On The Way To The Forum, promising us Comedy Tonight: "We shall employ every device we know in our efforts to divert you." The promise held more than a hint of threat: we were to be steamrollered into hilarity and would very possibly die laughing. He struck the same note in Fiddler On The Roof when he told his wife, to whom he was about to relate a bad dream, Dont get frightened - it could bring on a heart attack, such solicitude. He spoke slowly, massively, in capital letters, as befitted his bulk; he sounded like the archetypal wise fool but genuinely wise, magisterially soothing. His Tevye the dairyman was plainly a great performance.

But he didn't give it for very long, at least not in its pristine form. There is debate about this, but the concensus seems clear: Mostel, blooded as a nightclub comic, quickly got bored in a stage show and began improvising lines and business, to the frequent delight of the audience and the consternation of his authors and colleagues. Jack Gilford, his co- star on stage and screen, once feelingly described to me how he suffered from Mostel's more aggressive antics: "I used to think, hes doing this to me and Im his friend; what would he do if I were an enemy?" (I suggested that if he had been an enemy, Mostel would probably have left him alone. Gilford seemed to think there was something in this.) Gilford, way back when, recommended Mostel for his first club engagement. Much later Mostel reciprocated by backing Gilford for his role in Forum. Besides being life-long friends both men were victims of the Hollywood blacklist. It skewered Mostel's career, probably accounting for much of his insecurity, though he sometimes claimed that it was a blessing in disguise; it allowed him to get on with what he really cared about, which was painting. It is also something of a boon to his biographer; it gives him something to get his teeth into, and Jared Brown (himself the son of a blacklisted actor - one who did not bounce back) duly supplies two incisive chapters.

Many of the Commie-hunting congressmen were plainly concerned with their own advancement, but the corruption of the proceedings is actually less disturbing than the absurdity. As Mostel himself inquired, What sabotage could actors be accused of - giving acting secrets to the enemy? And then the writers - they were accused of peppering their scripts with subliminal subversion that escaped the studio bosses but could somehow influence the great American public. Just what was going on in the mind of Laurence Johnson, the Syracuse supermarket king who blackmailed the TV sponsors? In Harris' words, he "would remove their products from the shelves of his supermarkets unless they agreed to fire the artists he named."

Even more terrifying than Johnsons blacklist was his whitelist, the roll of actors he approved of. No one worked without Laurence Johnsons consent. It must have done wonders for his ego, but plainly more than vainglory was at stake. He, and the thousands who thought like him, were simultaneously fuelled by a blind hatred of "Communism" - the word, rather than the thing - and a prurient, very American obsession with show business. Everyone wants to be a casting director.

Mostel managed to reconstruct his career but played it cautiously, lambasting the world for its philistinism but sticking himself to commercial products. There is no need to lament his involvement with musicals (both Forum and Fiddler are classics), but Brown plainly wishes that he had risked some of the classics he kept talking about; they might have bolstered a career that seems now to boil down to five shows and two movies. Falstaff he might or might not have encompassed, but he would surely have been superb in Moliere, as a comic obsessive, and defintive as Azdak, the holy vulgarian of The Caucasian Chalk Circle.

He and his wife fought constantly but were totally interdependent. On stage and off he felt compelled to be the centre of attention. Brown makes you love him and hate him on alternate pages. In the end, though, with Mostel dying on the brink of a probable artistic breakthrough as Shylock in Arnold Wesker's The Merchant (typical Wesker luck, by the way), you give in and are moved.

Mostel's career intersected once with that of Orson Welles, who was to have directed him opposite Laurence Olivier in the London production of Eugene Ionescos Rhinoceros. An awful accident - he almost lost a leg to a rampaging bus - kept Mostel out of it, but he triumphed in the play in New York, transforming himself from man to rhino in full fascinated view. Brown judiciously criticizes the play - its allegory is too pat - while Frank Brady, Welles new Boswell, tosses off the word "masterpiece."

Brady is not to be trusted on plays; at one extreme he thinks King Lear's howl, howl, howl, howl comes in the storm scene, and at the other he doesn't know what a curtain call is. He teaches film at an American university but hes no fount of enlightenment on that medium either; he lists Howard Hawks as a producer rather than a director, and discusses The Magnificent Ambersons with what seems like mild distaste. He may love it, though; with a style like his, its difficult to be sure. My favorite sentence among many: "Welles had long been interested in Bertolt Brecht's brilliant but thus-yet-to-be-produced The Life Of Galileo, upon which the latter had collaborated with Charles Laughton." That Galileo, he couldn't do a thing for himself.

That a man who writes so badly should be teaching anything anywhere is depressing. I suppose, though, that he has to be congratulated on pulling Welles overlapping projects into some kind of order, though in a career that defies chronology, more dates would have been helpful. And toward the end he gets us not only sad but angry at the moneys refusal to bankroll Welles for projects that could hardly have lost more than the commercial dodos that they finance all the time. He reveals that at the memorial ceremony Welles' long-time Polish mistress succeeded in making a few sycophantic skinflints thoroughly ashamed of themselves.

Welles escaped the blacklist, though his progressive views and his involvement with Roosevelt's Federal Theatre Project would certainly have qualified him for it. Perhaps it was felt that he had already blacklisted himself, or that the Hearst press, incensed by Citizen Kane, had done it for him. Louella Parsons, who worked for Hearst, and Hedda Hopper, who didnt but liked to be helpful, were McCarthyists before the fact (and during and after it as well). They couldnt get Kane banned, but they virtually ensured its box-office failure, thereby dooming Welles in Hollywood. Studios expected him to lose money, and made certain that he did. So they stopped employing him altogether.

He connived a bit at his own downfall but only on one film (It's All True) does he seem to have been truly irresponsible. Otherwise his cinematic career appears to me blameless, quite apart from the fact that it encompasses three masterpieces - Kane, Ambersons and the Falstaff picture Chimes At Midnight - plus the immensely enjoyable Touch Of Evil and his acting contribution to The Third Man. Nor can I condemn him for his nomadic, epicurean lifestyle; when he could not enliven the world with his work, he did so with his personality. Brady, obviously a very nice man himself, makes his hero appear curiously innocent; and he probably was.

It's difficult to see, though, why he didnt return more often to the theatre, which would surely have been glad to have him. His Mercury Theatre productions in the thirties sound mouth-watering, though Brady hardly conveys the excitement of his British Moby Dick in 1955, which Kenneth Tynan said dwarfs everything London has seen since, perhaps, the Great Fire. Tynan, a founding Welles cultist, is curiously ignored in this book which dismisses him as "the caustic and stuttering young drama critic." We do, in compensation, get the gorgeous prose of Welles first theatrical employer, Irish actor Michael Mac Liammoir, who wrote of his protege: He was precisely what he himself would have chosen to be had God consulted him on the subject.

This youthful self-confidence suggests nobody so much as Charles Foster Kane ("I think it would be fun to run a newspaper"); and it is amazing, considering his choice of title, how little Brady makes of the connection. Citizen Kane is of course as much Welles as Hearst, some of him prophetically so; that accounts for some of the film's power and much of its gaiety.

There are a few people with whom all the world is on first-name terms. Blossom Dearie used to sing, courtesy of the late Johnny Mercer, "When I mention Orson, I refer to Bean and not Welles." But she was kidding. Orson Bean must fend for himself; the name is bespoke. And how many other Zeroes are there? The answer is in the question. Robert Cushman is former drama critic of The Observer.
