robertcushman@lycos.com; Ain't Misbehavin' runs through June 25 at the Bluma Appel, 27 Front St. E., 416-368-3110.

This week's column is about singer-pianists. One of the greatest of them all, from the mid-1920s to the early 1940s, was Thomas Fats Waller, whose songs and personality are celebrated in Ain't Misbehavin', a show that was a Tony-winning hit In New York in 1978 and has been a staple ever since. In its CanStage revival it remains what it always was, a nightclub entertainment, magnified to a higher power. It sags in a few places but it's a welcome chance to encounter real singing, real dancing and real music in a Toronto musical.

The show is not a biography of Waller, and nobody gets to play him. Or rather, everybody gets to play him. His repertoire is shared among the entire cast, split, in this instance, six ways. It was only five ways in the original production -- two male, three female - - and the addition of a fourth woman is unwarranted. There is simply not enough material to go around. This may be the first time ever that Fats has been stretched thin.

This is an ensemble show; even so, Jackie Richardson, following her success in Cookin' at the Cookery, has been given a star entrance and star position in the curtain call. Between times, though, she is a shining member of the group, her face, a perpetually smiling moon, lighting up the stage and her voice warming it. Her buoyant I've Got a Feeling I'm Falling far surpasses that of Broadway's Nell Carter, whose voice sounded as if it were being squeezed out of a tube of toothpaste.

Divine Brown is efficient and enticing (she sings Squeeze Me), Starr Domingue energetic but arch (she sings Yacht Club Swing, one of several period pieces that get mugged into incomprehensibility) and Kim Richardson game but extra (she doesn't get a solo). The men, on the other hand, are fully extended: literally so in the case of David Lopez, whose limbs are uncannily loose and whose persona is that of mocking Harlem devil. He makes 'T Ain't Nobody's Biz-ness If I Do a wonderfully joyous hedonistic hymn: more effective, in fact, than his more showily depraved Viper's Drag, an ode to the reefer that is, in every sense, long drawn out. His horizontal contortions here demand to be described as "slithy," the portmanteau word Lewis Carroll assembled from "lithe" and "slimy." Doug Eskew is the most physically Walleresque of the gang. He is indeed mountainous, being about as long as he is broad. His is that robust complaint (to himself or a friend? -- I'm never sure) Your Feet's Too Big. He also has a rich voice and a winning grin that is more optimist than cynic, more Satch than Fats.

That's something Ain't Misbehavin' has never got right. It knocks itself out transmitting its hero's ebullience, but that was something of a mask. Waller, a compulsive eater and drinker, was a troubled man. The show hardly lets us see his demons, and we seldom get to hear his one-eyebrow-raised one-liners. The nearest we get to Waller the satirist is Lounging at the Waldorf, a spiky instrumental to which Richard Maltby, Jr., the show's first director, has set some sardonic lyrics. As Fats himself famously remarked, though not in this show, "I wonder what the poor people are doing tonight."

The mask does drop for the climactic company number (What Did I Do To Be So) Black and Blue, chillingly and beautifully sung. A hit at its birth in 1929, the song was found embarrassing by later generations, but has lately been reclaimed as a song of both protest and pride. According to Bobby Short, of whom more in a moment, it is not about black and white at all, but about tensions within the black community between the light-skinned against the dark. However you hear it, certain lines ("I'm white inside / It don't help my case") always sound shocking. Here it's a stark statement, beyond anger.

Andy Razaf wrote the words for that, as he did for Honeysuckle Rose, the title song and several others. I tell you since the program doesn't. Waller composed much of the show's music, but by no means all, and he never wrote lyrics, though, as always with these anthologies, it's what the words say that guides the choice of songs.

I also think some people would be interested to know that the raucous and jivey That Ain't Right, one of the production's highlights, was the work, words and music of a young and decidedly unsmooth Nat King Cole.

The show seems to have acquired more spoken lines than I had remembered, most of them unfortunate. Otherwise Marion J. Caffey's direction and choreography are whirlwind. There is neat design by Phillip Silver, a generic cabaret across which balls of light chase one another like soap bubbles. The band, under William Foster McDaniel, is terrific. You will have a good time.

- - -

Bobby Short once said that he loved Waller's music but always felt inadequate performing it, perhaps because, though his own urbane cabaret style could encompass the blues, it stopped short at vocal barrelhouse. (Short's style depended on not worrying what the poor people were doing tonight.) Nor could he hope to emulate Waller's ferocious skills as a stride pianist. All the same, in later life he recorded an excellent album of Razaf lyrics, many of them to Waller's music.

Short, who died a couple of weeks ago at the age of 80, was the premier New York supper-club performer of his generation, really, of all generations. Indeed, I sometimes suspect he not only epitomized the style he perfected -- a mixture of cut-glass elegance and down- home energy -- he actually invented it. Born into a large, poor black family in Danville, Ill., he helped support his family as a child performer on the vaudeville circuit. Then he went back to school. He had found himself attracted to songs he was too young to sing, and he set out to learn about them and the life they represented. As a young man he established himself in the nightclubs of Hollywood, then of Paris, then of New York. When the thriving cabaret world of the '50s collapsed, under the double onslaught of TV and rock, he scuffled, but at the end of the '60s he secured a short gig at the Carlyle, a dowager among Manhattan hotels, an engagement that was extended and in fact ended only with his death. His status as a New York landmark was cemented by his appearance in Woody Allen's Hannah and Her Sisters. Woody takes his coke-snorting young girlfriend to see Short at the Carlyle and, when she proves unappreciative, tells her "you don't deserve Cole Porter."

Short's recording career, nearly all of it available on CD, falls into three periods. There are the youthful albums of the '50s on Atlantic (my favourites are Sing Me a Swing Song and The Mad Twenties), on which he explores the show-tune repertoire. Then, still for Atlantic, there are the sets he made near the beginning of his Carlyle comeback. These include a couple of legendary concerts with his friend and inspiration Mabel Mercer, which more or less set off the New York cabaret revival, and a series of double-length songbooks (he described them as "public services") exploring Coward, the Gershwins, Rodgers and Hart and -- definitively and self- definingly -- Porter. At their best they capture him at his best, as florid as before, both vocally and instrumentally, but with an increasing depth both of timbre and feeling. His vibrato-laden voice really did improve with age. Finally, for Telarc, there were a series of old-master albums that became increasingly jazz- flavoured. After the death of his long-time bassist, Beverly Peer, he abandoned the trio format and fronted a crisp small band. To hear how his style evolved, listen to his versions, successively less flippant but no less witty, of the Vernon Duke-Ira Gershwin number I Can't Get Started. He must have sung this a thousand times but, at every live performance I heard, he kept it fresh.

He started out, and I suppose remained, one of high society's pet exotics. His affectations must, at first hearing, have been as alarming to Sinatra buffs as to rock fans -- perhaps more to the former -- but once you listened beneath the mannerisms, you found an extraordinary attention to the twists and nuances of lyrics, an infallible nose for jokes and surprising tenderness. I would rank him among the half-dozen best male interpreters of classic popular song, beside Sinatra, Mel Torme, Fred Astaire, Johnny Mercer and a floating sixth who might be Dick Haymes.

He said last year that he was retiring from the Carlyle. Then he changed his mind. Then he died, of a leukemia diagnosed only a few days before. He was a courteous and, I think, a kind man. I thought he would go on forever. In an important way he probably will.
