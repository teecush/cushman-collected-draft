robertcushman@lycos.com; Care runs through May 28 at the Tarragon Theatre, 416- 531-1827. Sparta runs through May 8 at Theatre Passe Muraille, 416-504-7529.

Diane Flacks plays all the women and Richard Greenblatt all the men in Care, a play they have cooked up together after the fashion of their delightful Sibs a few seasons ago. The characters they create this time -- the principal ones, anyway -- are easy to identify with; to believe in, as Jon Stewart might put it, not so much.

Steve and Ellie are a married couple, young-middle-aged, who have had their first baby. They cope, or fail to, with disturbed and sleep- less nights, the unequal division of labour (pun not intended, but Ill go with it anyway), the precipitous decline of their sex life and the fact that Ellie's career is on hold while Steve's seems poised to take off, thus provoking further resentment. These may not quite be universal themes, but theyre common enough to keep most audiences interested. More specialized, but still eminently recognizable, are the cultural tensions. Both Steve and Ellie are Jewish, but they differ in how they want their son to be brought up, he opting for secular and she, rather to his surprise, for religious.

Both have outside interests. Steve may be having an affair with a colleague; its never quite clear how far they've gone, and we don't see much of the relationship anyway. We do, on the other hand, see a great deal of Ellies father, who is dying of cancer, tended to at home by a succession of cartoonish cameo caregivers, and who, in his pain and his grouchiness and his craving for a Montreal bagel with all the cholesterol, is the most compelling character in the piece. We also get to meet Steves personal trainer and Ellie's masseur; and we are vouchsafed a couple of visions of God, seen here as the greatest Cartoon Cameo Caregiver -- or Withholder -- of all.

There is a geometrical imbalance in the show, and in the strengths and weaknesses of both the material and the performers. The most important emotional lines converge on Ellie in her triple capacity as daughter, wife and mother. But Flacks is not a very resourceful emotional performer, and with her at its centre, the play is sketchy. More precisely, it's revue-sketchy. She is fine as the various agency crones hovering at the bedside (especially funny as the short-tempered one whom her patient calls a Polish Nazi), but as Ellie herself, shes stuck on a single resentful note of grievance that never rises to grief.

So Greenblatt becomes the fulcrum. He doesnt have the widest range of any actor in the world, but he has a laconic strength that encompasses wit and that serves him very well as the old man. As Steve, though, he's less a person than a predicament, which in fact is the play in a nutshell. The issues are real enough, but they're journalistically familiar and they havent been made fresh. Theyre a series of jokes, surprisingly few of which get laughs. Eda Holmes directs sharply and speedily, with the baby cleverly represented as something between a projection and a hologram, but the show is as clear an example as I can remember of the theatrical gulf between mere accuracy and truth.

Sparta, a new play by Sarah Martyn, is self-referential on a couple of levels. The author apparently grew up on a farm, not unlike the one that occupies the stage here, in a small Ontario town similar to the one that gives her play its name. What's more, one of her characters, a young woman who's returned to the farm and to her highly dysfunctional family to celebrate her 25th birthday, is an aspiring writer. She decides to write a story based on the life around her, and reads some of it to her parents. They are aghast, as was I, though for slightly different reasons. As far as I could tell, what she reads out is a direct transcript of a conversation that we heard a couple of scenes before. This suggests that Christina, which is the young ladys name, has either an impressive memory or impressive shorthand skills, coupled with a staggeringly literal approach to the concept of "writing about what you know."

Whatever it is, it gets totally and unexpectedly dropped in the second act, which is all melodrama. Its melodrama that's partly rooted in researched reality. The farm, like many small farms, is going under, and father Marvin's only way out is to sell it, which he refuses to do. (If nothing else, the play supplies a grim counter- narrative to our theatres most familiar depictions of Ontario farming, the Wingfield series, of which the latest, Wingfields Inferno, opened this week at the Royal Alex, after its success at Stratford last year.) Marvin's wife, Carol, is a schoolteacher, and there is little sympathy between them; she does suggest that, if he ever read King Lear, it would strike a chord, but he's obviously not a reading man. He certainly has little sympathy with Christinas literary ambitions, especially as shes eking out a living as a waitress in order to realize them. Carol gives her family advice, which they resent, even when it's good. Christina's siblings, much older than she, are Alden, who worked in a bank but has quit to offer what his father regards as dubious help on the farm, and Mary, who is a high-flying Toronto lawyer. There is a rule, which I cannot explain, that the ostentatiously smart member of any theatrical family will be called Mary.

Martyn writes well about family tensions in the abstract, so to speak, but she is less good at tying them to a specific story. The action founders on a couple of revelations, the first highly predictable, and the second a total and unbelievable surprise, though certainly not unprecedented either in literature or in life.

The actors are left clinging. Hilary Fennell makes an impressive professional debut as Christina, hugging her own misfortunes without wallowing in them, and Ryan Hollyman is agreeable as Alden, who doesnt say much and keeps physically in the background, for neither of which one can blame him. Laura Wilson is good, too, in a rather conventional hausfrau way, as Carol. But Bob Collins Marvin is a very low-key tyrant, and having him take a long tour around the stage taking his feelings out on the furniture does nothing to enhance his credibility. Aviva Chernick's Mary carries no conviction, even (I suspect) to herself. She, too, spends much time lurking in corners, but with far less motivation, unless you count a possible fear of the big scene that lies in wait for her near the close of the play.

As you may have gathered, Layne Coleman's direction does little to help the play or the actors; surprising since, as hes currently demonstrating in Escape from Happiness a few blocks away, hes an excellent actor himself. But he puts his current cast in impossible positions on Steve Lucas's equally unhelpful set. Meant to represent the farmhouse kitchen, it has lost most of its walls and narrows to a point at the front, like the prow of a ship. Characters stand there, with their backs to everyone else, and make speeches, leaving you wondering where in any conceivable room they could be. There is also the least convincing dinner-table scene I have ever witnessed. I did, though, believe in the coffee inside the mugs that the actors kept brandishing. I'm sure it was instant. I'd bet the farm on it.
