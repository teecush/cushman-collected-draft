ROBIN HOOD

Elgin Theatre, Toronto

They all come round again. Robin Hood was the first, and remains the best, of Ross Petty's attempts to kidnap the English pantomime, disguise it as a family musical, and demand ransom from a Canadian audience. (It robbed the comparatively poor to blackmail the relatively rich.) It first came among us six years ago, and I reviewed it then for some other newspaper, one that employs a star- rating system for plays. As I recall, I gave it 500 (out of four). Thats how good I and my own family felt.

Euphoria is wonderful but it does wear off, and though my kids have continued to escort me to the later Petty pantos, I have noticed their demeanour on these occasions becoming increasingly surly. This year they flatly refused to accompany me. I blame the schools. Their desertion has thrown me back on my own memories, which are unreliable. My impression is, though, that the musical component of the show is stronger than it was six years ago, but the comedy -- with one notable exception -- has gone off.

First, some basics. One reason Robin Hood makes a better Petty protagonist than, say, Cinderella or Peter Pan, is he doesnt come with a story, or at least not with one that has to be adhered to. He is a creature of folklore, commemorated in various tales and ballads, and really any one of them will do. At the Elgin we get the barest essentials. There is Robin the chivalrous outlaw, camping out in the greenwood with Little John and the guys. There is Robin's beloved Maid Marian: rich and beautiful and an orphan. There is the villainous Sheriff of Nottingham, who in this version cuts a few legendary corners by abducting the Maid and scheming, in the most dastardly fashion, to marry her for her money. (There should be a song about Carryin' Marian Home, but there isnt). There is England, depicted as one big village green, waiting for King Richard the Lionheart to hurry back from his crusade and liberate everyone. There is Marians chaperone Nurse Tickle, and the Sheriff's henchman Pinch. You will not find these last two in any of the books, but they are among the immovable pillars of the pantomime tradition: She's the Dame, hes -- Im not sure how to finish this sentence. He's the low-comic, indeed the lowest of the low, but in panto these usually come in pairs. In Cinderella, for example, they're the Brokers Men, though in my long experience of the genre I cant remember ever meeting the Broker. This time perhaps we do. The Sheriff could be the broker, since he's called upon to supply the other half of the act while simultaneously functioning as its outraged employer. "You're next door to an idiot," the Sheriff snarls at Pinch, and Pinch immediately moves a couple of steps away. I love that joke, always have, but I remember it working much better last time than it does now.

Im not sure why. It may be that Petty himself, playing the Sheriff, is resting too comfortably on whatever villains wear in place of laurels. He comes on stage and demands to be hissed, before hes done so much as evict a widow. Simon Bradbury, as Pinch, is a natural for this kind of downtrodden knockabout, but he seems out of sorts -- he's certainly starved for good material. So is Nora McLellan, whose Nurse Tickle is excessively broad even for a pantomime dame. In the old country these roles are always played by men. I don't know on what altar of political correctness this custom has been been sacrificed, but a man-hungry harridan is not funny when played by a woman. McLellan only comes into her own when theres music. Handed by a comic song that sounds like it dropped out of an especially uninspired ancient British musical, she takes it by the scruff of its neck and makes it work. She also presides, with genuine generosity of spirit, over the inevitable song-sheet sequence.

Both Bradbury and McLellan are veterans of the Shaw Festival. Bradbury is even required to revert on occasion to his posh voice, and I suspect this double-duty has put a crimp in his style. The sight of classical performers making exhibitions of themselves has almost been one of the prime attractions of these shows. It was inaugurated in the finest style by Pettys wife, Karen Kain, who, unless I have my memories crossed, was a delicious Good Fairy in Robin Hood Mark One. The series has never quite recovered from her retirement, but this year the management has mustered her opposite number from the National Ballet, Rex Harrington, to play what would have been her opposite number in the show, a black-clad Wizard of the Wood. Speaking, or at least pouting, for the first time on any stage, he proves less adept than Kain at delivering a comic line, but he does sing, which is more than she ever did. He also, of course, glides on and off and around the trees in the most gracefully sinister manner imaginable. His antagonist -- who functions surprisingly often as his partner -- is Sara Topham, Stratford Festival ingenue and a pert pirouettist on her own account. Both suffer, though, from the fact the story has no need of them; they can hover over the narrative, but they can take very little part in it. Pantomime characters, of course, thrive on irrelevance; but they need some function to be irrelevant to.

Stratford, past and present, also contributes Thom Marriott who, hulking and sulking, looks the very image of Little John. Steve Ross who, not being rotund, is by no means the image of Friar Tuck, but who has a snappy way with a one-liner suggests he's on the way to being hilarious, given the chance; and -- most blessedly -- Graham Abbey in the title role. Abbey cut his teeth in this environment last year as Snow White's Prince, in effect burlesquing his own Romeo before hed even played it. As Robin he makes similar hay of his recent Henry V, or -- going further back -- of his DArtagnan in The Three Musketeers, which admittedly was something of a spoof to begin with. He has now got past the good-sport stage, and functions as a comic presence in his own right.

Romantic leads in pantomime were born to suffer, to be endured by the audience and mercilessly mocked by the full-time clowns. Abbey does it to himself before anyone else can do it to him, and is the funniest person in the show. He can also recover himself and act the heroics for all they're worth, or almost: We were told that on the first night he had to go easy on the Fairbanks stuff because of a leg injury.

And he sings, well enough to hold his own against the more practised warbling of his Marian, Amy Walsh. The musical choices this year are more eclectic than ever, though the score evinces a special fondness for the lyrics of the late Alan Jay Lerner. Three of his songs turn up, two with Loewe and one without: Robin sings I Talk to the Trees, which makes a lot of sense for man in his position (even the line about how he met the King of France seems to fit); the citizenry perform an unblushing makeover on Brigadoon's Down on MacConnachy Square, which here becomes Down in Nottingham Square without another word being changed; and hero and heroine carry on a long-distance duet of Come Back to Me, though with her being held in durance vile it seems an unreasonable request.

The opening number is rather less sophisticated in tone; it is in fact the title-song from the old Robin Hood TV series starring Richard Greene. (Now, there was a good show.) It needs, obviously, no lyric-laundering, but the merry men have to sing it three times while waiting for their leader, whose unpunctuality was apparently notorious, to make his expected tree-swinging entrance. The delay goads them on to increasingly desperate choreography; by the third chorus, they are executing dainty twirls with their quarterstaffs. It all sets an agreeably jokey tone which, whatever my carpings, is never subsequently imperilled. Some first-night mishaps prompted prime ad-libbing from Robin and the Sheriff; I think we can assume there will be similarly fruitful disasters for the remainder of the run. As always, the program coyly refuses to name an author but it does for once list a director other than Petty himself: Indeed rather a distinguished one, Jim Warren. He may have made a difference, but I can't say I noticed.
