Charlie Gallant, a rising young actor at the Shaw Festival, gives an intense and compelling performance as Johnny Boyle in Juno and the Paycock. Johnny is far from being the principal character of Sean O'Casey's most notable play, but in some ways hes the central one, even though physically hes mostly on its periphery, shaking in fear either at the back of the stage or in the wings. He's afraid because this is Dublin in 1922 and he's a diehard Republican, twice wounded, whose former comrades suspect him of backsliding, even of treason. Every time theres a knock on the Boyle familys tenement door - and there are a lot of knocks - the terror mounts, for Johnny and for us.

The Boyle family have plenty of other things to worry about. Titular head of the impoverished house is Jack Boyle, who likes to be called Captain and who boasts of a rip-roaring seafaring past, though the consensus among those who know him is that he never set foot on anything more dangerous than a pleasure-boat and was seasick even then. Permanently unemployed, and a martyr to terrible leg pains whenever there's a threat of a job in the offing, he spends most of his time in local snugs in the company of his pal and sycophant Joxer Daly. The family is held together, barely, by Jack's wife, the loving, sharptongued, indomitable Juno.

The tide seems to turn when a deceased relative is found to have bequeathed the Boyles a goodly sum of money; they go seriously into debt on the strength of it. But the will turns out to be a frost; the smoothtalking lawyer who brought the news disappears from town, after getting Mary, the daughter of the house, pregnant. Juno is left, toward the plays end, facing down unimaginable sorrow; the Captain is left, at the actual end, drunkenly repeating to Joxer his unshakable, and unarguable, conviction that "the whole worlds in a terrible state o' chassis."

The chassis is reflected, creatively, in the play's own construction. OCasey (like another playwright on this years Niagara bill, Edward Bond) was a self-taught, left-wing dramatist, who steeped himself in Shakespeare and the ancients. From the latter comes the play's cock-eyed classical title (the Captain is described as "struttin' like a paycock"); from the former an unselfconscious mixing, not just of tragedy and comedy, but of tears and knockabout. Captain Jack spins yarns of Falstaffian richness, shameless fictions that he may half-believe. A celebration, financed by the Boyles illusory wealth, is interrupted by a funeral procession: that of the boy Johnny is believed to have betrayed (though we dont know that yet). Jennifer Phipps, as the victim's mother, changes the temperature in the theatre as, supported by friends and neighbours, she makes her way on to the stage, sits down, and, devastated and devastating, delivers the equivalent of a Greek tragic lament couched in passionately Christian terms. (O'Casey was an atheist, but he respected all his characters, at least in his early plays like this one.) Actually she changes the temperature even before she comes on, when we glimpse her through the aperture of the door in Peter Hartwells set. This door proves one of the major strengths of Jackie Maxwells production; we never know, and neither does the stressed-out Johnny, whether the ominous shadows behind it have come to remove the furniture or to remove him.

The production overall is good, not great, feeding on the play's unmissable, and sadly universal, topicality without trading on it. The community feelings are all in place, not so much the family ones. Both Jim Mezon's Captain and Mary Haneys Juno are expectedly expert, but he seems to exist in his own acting cocoon, setting a tone and never varying it, while she lets no hint of residual affection, or even of ironic amusement, leaven her long-suffering abrasiveness. This couple have been together a long time; they know the game theyre playing, or are locked into. When Juno tells her expectant daughter that her baby will have something better than a father, "it will have two mothers" Haney makes it into a feminist clarion-call, almost inviting cheers from the auditorium. But Juno is concerned, always, with immediate problems, not general ones; the nearest she comes to a manifesto is to repeat, verbatim, the earlier lament of the bereaved Mrs Tancred, and she only does that because she's now in an identical situation.

Benedict Campbell is a delightfully furtive, low-key Joxer, a Beckett tramp before his time, his scraps of literary erudition suggesting that he might even have read the books. Supporting performances vary. Marla McLean's Mary makes little impression when happy, but is very strong in her last meeting - first hopeful, then bitter - with her rejected suitor Jerry, who is so well played by Andrew Bunker that it hurts when he, like all the men in the play, turns out to have feet of clay. Gord Rand gives an elaborately dandyish portrayal of Charlie the vanishing seducer, though its possibly more of a star turn than the role warrants; as Maisie Madigan, the voluble neighbour who contrastingly comes late to the party and stays, Corinne Koslo vanishes beneath her accent. Jay Turvey and David Ball are notably good as the furniture men, walk-ons who do as much as anyone to convey the ongoing terror that is the plays climate and the dayto-day necessity of living with it and through it.

Juno and the Paycock is in repertory through October 12 at the Royal George Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake.
