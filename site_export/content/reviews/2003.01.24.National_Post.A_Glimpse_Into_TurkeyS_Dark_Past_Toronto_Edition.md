CRIMES

Berkeley Street Theatre, Upstairs

The Crimes with which Adam Nashmans play are principally concerned are those committed in 1915, on a genocidal scale, by the Turks against the Christian Armenians. It also considers comparable atrocities, most crucially the Nazi slaughter of Europes Jews, and manages to glance en route at the current situation in the Middle East. All this, along with a slice of early-20th-century Canadian history, is piled on top of an approximation of a spy story. That's a lot, especially considering the play only lasts 90 minutes. In fact it's too much, while the play itself is much, much too little. Its scrappily written and staged, and its bad performances outnumber its good. All the same, it raises, and even answers, some interesting questions about the equivalence of evil.

Its long been an article of faith to many people, including myself, that nothing compares to the Holocaust: Not even Stalin's massacres, which were just as brutal and claimed millions more victims. But even such a dedicated anti-Stalinist as Robert Conquest has been quoted -- by Martin Amis, in Koba the Dread -- as saying he found the Holocaust worse than the Gulag, even though when pressed for a reason, he could only answer, I feel so." Amis himself suggests "the distinction may be that Nazi terror strove for precision, while Stalinist terror was deliberately random. The corollary of that may be that a potential victim had a better chance of being spared in Russia, where the terror was aimed at classes, than in Germany, where it was aimed at races.

As an English playwright once put it, perhaps a trifle optimistically, you could stop being a middle peasant; you couldn't stop being a Jew." There is, after all, no word equivalent to "genocide denoting the elimination of political opponents. Even the fact that the Russian midnight lasted so much longer than the German seems to make it less horrific. A terror that prolonged seems more porous -- its certainly less easy to pin down. And of course we know, thanks to the outcome of the Second World War, what the Nazi death-machine looked like. We found the evidence, and weve been looking at it ever since.

But the Armenians. Hitler famously invoked their fate as a kind of insurance cover for his own racial program: Who," he asked "remembers the Armenians? He had a point, although it didn't have the effect he would have wanted. The world has forgotten the Armenians, in a way that it hasn't forgotten the Jews. Holocaust denial is, in a sense, its own admission of failure. Nobody bothers to deny something that isnt still on everybodys mind.

The most effective scene in Crimes is a confrontation between our hero Tir, a Canadian of Armenian descent, and a sophisticated modern Turk who seems genuinely grieved to find his antagonist -- an anthropologist by trade -- digging up all that old stuff. One by one the Turk produces the same arguments (it was a long time ago, it was wartime, there were faults on both sides, anyway it's all a myth) that one can imagine being trotted out by an articulate neo-Nazi. The difference is that if such a man were a German it would be -- not an impossibility, certainly, but an anomaly. Germany has acknowledged its past in a way that Turkey hasn't.

To that extent, Nashmans proposed equation doesnt quite fit. But it has an emotional resonance, and there is an earlier scene in which the match is frighteningly precise. The play is full of flashbacks, and in one of them we see an Armenian woman killed during a death march that looks identical to those that followed the closing of the concentration camps. The Turks didn't have the technology for gas chambers -- no more than the more recent genocidal killers in Rwanda (an analogy Nashman doesn't get round to making, though surely an obvious one). There is a cold-bloodedness, an impersonality, to the Nazi assembly line that sets it apart. But not, I am forced to admit, as far apart as all that. Intellectually, Nashman (who is Jewish himself) has made his case.

Dramatically, though, he hardly begins. The play manages to be both thin and chaotic. Tir is part Jewish -- his father married a German-Jewish refugee whom he met on wartime service in London. (He had joined up to show solidarity with another persecuted race. It would be nice to believe that soldiers volunteered for the Allied forces in order to help the Jews, but I have my doubts.) Now, a mining conglomerate employs Tir to go to Turkey and verify the authenticity of a relic that hangs in a church whose land the company owns. This exhibit is a spear, supposedly plunged into the body of Christ on the cross. Tir ends up brandishing it against his Turkish opponent. (Is this meant to be a reversion to Christian warfare against Islam? Any deeper significance escaped me.) Before this happens, Tir -- whos shaping up to be anthropologys answer to Indiana Jones -- has been directed, by a series of mildly sinister strangers, from Amsterdam to Istanbul to Jerusalem, the last of which he wisely suspects of being a perilous place to visit. He has also taken a lot of drugs, mostly on the house, and has had erotic encounters that may or may not be hallucinations. It's certain that at least one of his trips has induced visions of his murdered Armenian ancestors.

Other glimpses into history are more prosaically presented. We keep zig-zagging between the present and various bits of the past, between one country and another, with captions unsteadily projected to tell us where and when we are. We see: Tir's grandfather as a child escaping from the death-march; the same boy taken into an Ontario refugee home specially established for Armenian orphans (an implicit rebuke to the later policy of none is too many," but dramatically a mere pious gesture to Canadian content); the boy as a man, running a fruit-store and squabbling with his two sons, who later squabble with one another. We also, as previously mentioned, see Tirs father in the army, and follow him to Berlin where he captures a German soldier who taunts him for being a Jew (which, if you can keep up with me, he isnt, though he feels a spiritual kinship). Dad gets violent, and we can't help mentally cheering him on, just as, later, we can't help cheering his son when, goaded, he turns on the Turk. Nashman probably wants us to feel this, and then feel ashamed of it; and he drives home the message that all this hatred must stop by bringing Tir to Israel. Which is morally admirable, but has no real impact because the action and the characters are so skeletal.

The dramaturgy is primitive, even leaving aside the chronological leap-frog. Most of the dialogue is naked exposition. Hrant Alianaks production -- designed, amazingly, by the usually excellent Steve Lucas -- is shabby. The supposedly atmospheric music, which often drowns out the actors, is especially painful. It seems to think the play is a movie -- probably of the made-for-TV variety -- and only draws attention to how much slicker it would be if it were.

Alon Nashman (no relation to the author, though theyve worked together before) plays both Tir and his father. Neither role has much use for his characteristic sense of mischief, but he works devoutly, and is charming when he rocks on his feet, simultaneously jet-lagged and stoned. Oliver Becker is powerfully smooth as the Turk, who trades in opium, and Michael Waller twitchily so as a German, who deals in diamonds, but isn't above handing out coke to total strangers in airport washrooms. He also plays the other German, the one who's anti-Semitic to the bitterest end. The other performances, other than that of the boy Garo Britsian, are miserable. Is it my imagination, or has the overall standard of acting in Toronto taken, this cold new year, a sharp and dispiriting turn for the worse?
