Theatre critics are resigned, given the ubiquity of open-air theatres in and around Toronto, to being exposed to A Midsummer Night's Dream on an annual basis. What other play takes you so far into the woods? Nor is it any great surprise to find regular alfresco productions of As You Like It, set chiefly in a forest, or even of Twelfth Night, which generally looks good in a garden. And they're all love plays, which go well on a summer evening.

The Comedy of Errors would seem a less likely proposition, being a predominantly unromantic farce that happens entirely in built-up areas. All the same, it has turned up here three times in as many years. There was a straightforward production by Miles Potter in Oakville, a spookily imaginative one by Richard Rose in Barrie, and now, as this year's Dream in High Park, we have a lively but uneven version directed by Kelly Thornton.

This is the play about two sets of identical twins, masters and servants, separated since infancy, each being continually mistaken for his double in the course of a single day in Ephesus, the town in which one pair dwells and to which the other has journeyed from Syracuse in hopes of effecting a grand reunion. (Mysteriously, it never occurs to the visiting pair that they have stumbled on exactly what they were looking for. But then, most farce depends on people failing to ask obvious questions.)

The new production comes heralded by directorial declarations about the play being, underneath all the knockabout, a search for identity, both individual and familial. But this doesn't emerge much from what we actually see on stage and, to the extent that it does, it adds nothing to what we have seen in earlier productions.

The final reunions of the Antipholus brothers, their father and mother (also long-lost) and the long-serving and long-suffering Dromio siblings are touching and even healing, but then they always are. And stress is laid on the nervousness of the boys from Syracuse when faced with inexplicable happenings in a town with a reputation for witchcraft (the residents, under similar provocation, merely get angry), but as that's written into the text in letters practically of neon, it hardly counts as a revelation.

Another of the production's announced angles simply doesn't fly. It's about the cities of Ephesus and Syracuse being in a state of what the director has called "a trade war," though it's really more of a reciprocal hostage-taking situation. But that's just a device to get the play started.

Indeed, the Ephesus the production actually shows us seems to be rather a liberal society; at least, it allows an alien under sentence of death to wander around, alone and unescorted, playing Shenandoah on the mouth-organ. By the end -- this is hardly a spoiler -- he will have been reprieved, by Ducal dispensation. This makes Errors the first of several Shakespearean comedies in which a ruler, invariably a Duke, starts the play by threatening the letter of the law and ends by abrogating it; others are Dream, Measure for Measure and The Tempest. But in this play at least, the gesture has no wider charitable significance; there's no suggestion that future Syracusan trespassers in Ephesus, or vagrant Ephesians in Syracuse, will from now on be let off with a caution.

It's difficult anyway to think of this production's Ephesus as a society; its inhabitants sport gaudy coiffures and multi-coloured costumes that are apparently meant to suggest the day after tomorrow, but look more like a fancy-dress parade. The set, meanwhile (I question whether open-air productions should have sets, but the High Park ones always do), is all-purpose exotic, with beaded-curtain entrances that make even supposedly respectable homes look like low-profile bordellos; Antipholus of Ephesus appears to have set up house in the pink-light district, and the female population is notable for its high proportion of masked temptresses.

Farce and fantasy are not the same thing; indeed, you could make a good case for their being polar opposites. And, clinging to the old-fashioned idea that though farce may be nonsense it should never be nonsensical, I'd question the idea of casting the Antipholi with one black actor and one white, as the plot depends on their being indistinguishable. (That's without even getting into the biology of it.) It isn't as if the production doesn't want us to notice how everybody else looks, not with that design scheme. And since the colour-blindness doesn't extend to the Dromios, both of whom are white, it doesn't even count as a convention.

Still, all four twins are admirably played. This production may be hit-and-miss, but its hits are palpable. Kevin Hanchard, apart from being the clearest and supplest speaker, makes an amusingly supercilious Antipholus of Syracuse, as much offended as alarmed by the sorcery into which he thinks he's stumbled. Alex Poch-Goldin is a contrastingly cool opposite number, volatile but still able to see the sophisticated joke in being barred from his own door.

That door, the only solid one in town, is both free-standing and delightfully mobile. It gives, all things considered, the wittiest performance of the evening, though the two Dromios run a close second. They are played by Anthony Malarky, who's especially excellent, and Bradley Brackenridge (they even have appropriate names for clowns), and have their finest moments unknowingly aping one another from either side of that door, like Groucho and Harpo in Duck Soup.

Christine Brubaker spins out a spectacular comic-melodramatic cadenza as the jealous Adriana, unleashing on the brother to whom she isn't married all the reproaches she has been saving up for the one to whom she is. She ends it squirming on the floor, though I think she throws in just one squirm too many. Niki Landau plays her conciliatory sister with a pink parasol (goes with the decor) and an unmelted-butter demureness; her performance doe-sn't get much beyond that point, but then neither does the writing. Both actresses suffer, individually and as a team, from having to play most of their scenes on a balcony, high up and far back.

Other performances are patchy, and the production's comic invention never really snowballs; we never find ourselves laughing helplessly. But we do laugh, respectably often; while the ending, as it should, makes us smile. The two Dromios have spent most of the evening cracking jokes while being cuffed by their masters; finally, they are left alone to take pleasure in one another's company -- "I see by you I am a sweet-faced youth" -- and to exit, "hand in hand, not one before another." That's nice.
