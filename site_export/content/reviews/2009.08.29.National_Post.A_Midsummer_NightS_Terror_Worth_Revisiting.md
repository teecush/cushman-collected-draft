THEATRE

Kenneth Tynan once described Jean Racine's Phedre as a disappointing sequel to A Midsummer Nights Dream." I wouldnt necessarily endorse the adjective (and he himself went on to withdraw it) but the presence of both plays at this year's Stratford Festival gives us a unique opportunity to follow the story through and to reflect on the unreliability of happy endings. Shakespeare's Dream concludes with the king and queen of the fairies blessing the bridal bed of Theseus, Duke of Athens and Hippolyta, Queen of the Amazons: "And the issue there create/Ever shall be fortunate. Boy, were they wrong.

By the time Racine takes over, Hippolyta is dead; Theseus, promoted to King by tragic convention, has married again; and Hippolytus, the issue they created, has grown to a young manhood that is to prove spectacularly unfortunate. Phedre, his stepmother, falls violently in lust with him; devastated by an unrequited passion that counts as incestuous, she confides only in her nurse, Oenone, who tries to protect her by going to Theseus and accusing Hippolytus of trying to rape his wife. Theseus, seeking vengeance, calls in a favour from Neptune, god of the ocean, who obliges by sending in a sea monster. (This is one supernatural being who delivers.) Hippolytus meets a grisly end. Phedre, crushed by layer upon layer of shame, kills herself. Theseus survives, stricken.

If the problem with the Dream is that we know it too well, the trouble with Phedre, at least in English, is that we dont know it at all. Even if weve seen it a few times, we don't know it. It looms, perceptibly great, but locked within a baroque fortress composed of structure and style. In classic French tragedy, this one above all, everybody is trapped. Racine doesn't deny free will but he shows it helpless against circumstance. Theres a situation; theres an initial action, invariably misguided; and what follows is unstoppable. The famous unities of time and place allow no opportunities of escape, and the spare, swift couplets of the text create their own prison. Every rhyme draws another bolt. Rhyme in English only works for comedy (at least that's the consensus; I'm not entirely convinced), and Timberlake Wertenbakers new translation eschews it; it also shortens the French twelve-beat line, which certainly wouldnt work in English, into good old English pentameters. Her version is clear and alive, but it lets too much air in.

The Stratford production is the culmination of two years of workshops, here and at the American Conservatory Theater in San Francisco, under the leadership of Carey Perloff, the ACT's artistic director. Her production, like its text, is intelligent and economical, but it reaches its end leaving its audience unscathed; whatever we were meant to feel of pity or terror -- and one crushing moment would be enough -- has somehow leaked away. The costumes come from the time of Louis XIV, when the play was written; this makes a kind of sense, suggesting a monumental propriety just waiting to be outraged, but the heavy clothes weigh the actors down, and it leaves the audience at two removes from the story. It would probably work in French. In English, we'd settle for tunics.

I was looking keenly forward to Seana McKennas Phedre; she holds the Canadian patent on Greek tragedy, and no actress in the land can suffer more powerfully. She starts off all right with the traditional gaunt Phedre entrance, clutching at walls. But shes naturally a fighter, as witness her Medea, a role she took to the brink of comedy. She's at her best as Phedre when acridly taunting the goddess who's destroying her ( well, Venus, are you happy with my shame?"), or when threateningly shaking her Medusa locks, or when envisioning her father, Minos, god of the dead, consigning her to eternal damnation; Racine had a pitiless eye for the right myth. But these are isolated flashes. There isnt much fight left in Phedre, and there isnt really any laughter. She may be the only tragic protagonist who starts devastated: well, Hamlet perhaps, but he climbs out of his pit. Phedre sinks deeper into hers, and I'm not sure it's a congenial place for McKenna to be.

Tom McCamus makes an unconventionally reasonable Theseus, one who has to be goaded into fury: a defensible choice but contributing to the general lack of voltage. Sean Arbuckle, too, makes surprisingly little of his horrific messenger speech, generally a showstopper in any language.

The best performances come from the characters whose temperaments, if not their fates, are the least extreme: Jonathan Goads buffeted, well-meaning Hippolytus, Roberta Maxwells Oenone, and Claire Lautier's Aricie, Phedre's rival in both love and politics. Shes Racines invention; he draws his nets very tight. David Lang has composed discreetly haunting cello music that might, for once, be used less sparingly; having it underscore Theseus' call to the god might help move us to another level. It's a level that the production and its central performance may yet reach. I'd see it again.
