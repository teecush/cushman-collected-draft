DREAM A LITTLE DREAM

Isabel Bader Theatre, Toronto

Apart from the Lovin' Spoonful, whose vocal sound was less accomplished but whose songs were better, the Mamas and the Papas were the best musical excuse for the 1960s. They offered warm and propulsive melodies, delightful harmonies and sweet, clever lyrics. I have never understood why, with all this craft and talent on your own doorstep, you North Americans got so excited about the Beatles.

Dream a Little Dream is the story of the Californian quartet, none of whom were actually Californian, narrated by the surviving Papa, Denny Doherty, who is now living back where he started, in Halifax. He does all the talking, with vocal and instrumental support from a six-person outfit calling themselves the Dream Band.

The press night started half an hour late: The musicians guitars had been stolen, and the producer had to send out for new ones. (Guitars, not musicians.) But even without this delay the evening would have seemed long: The songs remain lovely; what happens between them feels endless.

The choice of title is strange. Dream a Little Dream of Me is not a Mamas and Papas song. They didnt write it; it's an enchantingly relaxed tune from the 1930s, with lyrics by the fine and prolific Gus Kahn and music credited to a mysterious couple of surnames, Schwandt and Andree, that seem never to have appeared on anything else. Nor did the Mamas and the Papas sing it; Cass Elliot, the best- known and best-loved of the group and apparently something of a song sleuth, revived it as her first solo record and had a hit with it. It's performed here to warm us up for an account of her early death from a heart attack. After which, astonishingly, the show is over, without a word about the much later death of John Phillips, the groups mastermind and chief composer, or of the subsequent careers of his wife Michelle or of Doherty himself. We thought we were getting the story of the Mamas and the Papas, but it turns out we were only getting the story of Mama Cass.

Using her death as the climax provides an opportunity for cheap sentiment. That may sound cruel, but its what comes across. There is Doherty on stage. He has told us how he and Cass had been friends in New York in the early '60s, long before the group was formed, and that he had always loved her, though they had never been lovers. She had even asked him to marry her, but, he says, she was 300 pounds, and I wasn't man enough to deal with that." Thats a noble admission that loses some of its nobility when you think of it as being made every night to a paying audience. By the same token, his grief, though presumably genuine, registers as maudlin. Doherty, acclaimed in the publicity as a born raconteur, sounds here like a man who has told the same stories too often.

The script he and his director-collaborator, Paul Ledoux, have provided is not exactly a marvel of dramatic coherence. Misgivings set in early, as when Doherty recounts the familiar Grace Slick line about people who can remember the 60s never having been there as if we had never heard it before. There is a horribly overworked central image about his life being like crossing a stream, hopping from one stone to another, some flat and secure, some slippery and treacherous. At each turn of the story he gives a little leap, then tells us which kind of rock he has landed on. The slippery ones predominate, but that's life or, as some people call it, show business. Especially, of course, rock 'n roll show business. Theres another running gag in which Doherty tries to say the word "lawyers, finds it coming out as liars," and settles for "attorneys."

He goes on at length about his affair with Michelle -- which eventually broke up both their marriages and the group -- and about their improvidence and self-indulgence. If you like the music, you will have a fan's interest in the people behind it, but it is likely to be exhausted long before the show is.

There is some visual appeal: The band plays behind a scrim on to which are projected photographs (Michelle looks incredibly beautiful) and album covers and the like, and the juxtaposition is often effective.

Doherty still sings well, and he gets excellent musical help; Doris Mason, who does Cass, sounds at least as good as she did. If Phillips was one of the master songwriters of the era, second only to John Sebastian of the Spoonful, then Doherty was obviously a worthy collaborator, and we hear a couple of his later solo efforts that have some of the same toughness and invention; they are self- revealing without being weepily confessional.

The hits go by -- Monday Monday and Dedicated to the One I Love - - plus a few that turn out, in narrative context, to be uncomfortably apposite. They exist, though, as songs should, independent of any bleeding-heart relevance.

Anyway, for musical autobiography nothing beats that cheerful masterpiece Creeque Alley, which depicts the members of the group and their peers, scrabbling in and around Greenwich Village, just before they -- in various combinations -- made it. Maybe the show should have been built around it. It's sung here slower than on the record so, for the first time, you understand all the references. It's difficult to think of a pop songwriter since who has come up with rhymes and alliterations that are so great and sit so well on the music.

We probably have Cass to thank for the appearance of Rodgers and Hart songs on the group's albums, but it fits. Whether or not they knew it, Phillips and Sebastian, like Leiber and Stoller before them and Randy Newman coming up on their tail, were in the tradition.
