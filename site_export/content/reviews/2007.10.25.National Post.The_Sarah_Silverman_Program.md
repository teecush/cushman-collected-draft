As a stand-up comic, Sarah Silverman plays wide-eyed and brash. As the protagonist of her own TV show she plays wide-eyed and naive; brash, too, but with a brashness that seems to take its owner by surprise. The sitcom Sarah has been known to apologize, something the stand-up Sarah would never do. (The talk show Sarah has done it, sort of, but that's a third persona, combining elements of the other two.)

The most celebrated line from Silverman's club act --and probably the best --is "I was raped by my doctor; that's kind of bitter- sweet for a Jewish girl." It ranks with, and may well have been influenced by, Lenny Bruce's acceptance of Jewish guilt for killing Jesus: "Yes, we killed him. My family killed him. We killed him because he didn't want to be a doctor."

Silverman has echoed that more directly, too: "I'd kill him again, if he came down here in his Birkenstocks." There is a difference, though. I wouldn't want to push the idea of Bruce-as- moralist too heavily -- he obviously got a personal charge out of shocking people -- but it's plain that he was making savage fun of anti-Semitism. He ridiculed the Christ-killer libel by yoking it to a more benign stereotype, the upwardly-mobile doctor-lover, fostered by Jews themselves. (It's not a joke that would occur to Gentiles to make.) But that was 40 years ago. Silverman operates in an entertainment world in which it's OK to be Jewish. She's early- Barbra Streisand for the 21st-century.

Her demeanour -- demure and mentally if not physically gawky, sets off the forthrightness of her sex and bodily-function jokes. (She's disinclined to separate the two.)

She gets closer to actual outrageousness when she takes on other races: "The best time to have a baby is when you're a black teenager." "[My black boyfriend] has to start loving himself before I can stop hating his people."

A talk-show reference to "Chinks" famously got her into trouble; she said sorry, but also said that it made her wonder about her own people: "Are we losing our control of the media?" But she is also, in effect, making common cause with black comedians in their observation of how the culture, their own included, depicts African- Americans.

Each episode of her TV show concludes with her telling her dog (played by her dog) what she has learned from this week's adventures. In one, "I learned that older black women are wise beyond their years and younger black women are prostitutes." In her solo act, she has the perfect response to her critics: "I don't base my material on stereotypes -- I base it on fact" -- a dazzling piece of double-bluff that first rebuffs the imputation of bias, and then reinstates it, twice as hard.

It's the real Sarah commenting on the imaginary Sarah. (Not that I'm sure which is which. Or that she is.) This sets her apart from the boring majority of stand-ups, of any race or either gender. They rant; she characterizes. She's in the same self-ironizing game as Stephen Colbert.

She has a regrettable weakness for atrocious puns. The first episode of her current TV series was called "Bored of the Rings", and it wasn't even a Tolkien parody. It started with her invading a church whose bells had been keeping her awake, and demanding "to speak to the manager." (She must be super-sensitive. When I was an undergraduate I lived next door to King's College Chapel, Cambridge, which has the most clangorous chimes west of Notre Dame. It took me just two Sundays to learn to sleep through them.) "He's called Jesus of Nazareth," she said, "not Jesus of Noisareth."

I felt like switching off right there, but she promptly redeemed herself by looking around the building, which was absolutely packed, and muttering "no wonder no one goes to church anymore -- Jesus." The absurdity of the Sarah character --a self-proclaimed "mixture of ignorance and arrogance" -- comes into sharper relief when there are others present, and is more endearing too. The action progressed to find her recruited by two nice WASP ladies who were against killing babies, an uncontroversial cause if ever there was one; it took her an improbably long but very funny time to discover that they were militant anti-abortionists. Shock-horror from Sarah, who had had three abortions, looked forward to more and seemed to regard the whole procedure as rather cuddly.

Actually, tweaked and recut a bit, this show might make good pro- life propaganda; the ladies, despite their stern appearance ("were you,' asked Sarah, "styled by angry lesbians?"), were acted with perfect seriousness. In the way it distributed its sympathies, the show perfectly reflected most people's mixed feelings on the issue. It took place, like all the other episodes, in Valley Village, Calif., a community that seems able, like The Simpsons' Springfield, to expand and contract at will.

It feels like a village but supports not only a crowded church but a thriving full-time abortion clinic, saved from destruction at the last minute by Sarah's two best friends, a pair of overweight gays, roused from all-night session of Dungeons and Dragons (they haven't progressed to video games) to ride to the rescue in full medieval fig.

One of them took an arrow in his posterior which Sarah removed using the clinic's suction-pump. This is what you call elegant construction. The sitcom shows a capacity for affection that the stand-up Sarah would never have countenanced. That's what happens when you let other people into your world, and have to make up stories for them: tolerance.
