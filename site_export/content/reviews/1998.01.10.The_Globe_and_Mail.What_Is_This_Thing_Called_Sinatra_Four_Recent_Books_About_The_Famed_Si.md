What is this thing called Sinatra?

Four recent books about the famed singer are plagued by errors, inconsistencies and misapprehensions. Only one of them gets it almost right. ALL THE WAY: A Biography of Frank Sinatra

Saturday, January 10, 1998

By Michael Freedland


  


Weidenfeld & Nicholson, 438 pages, $35

SINATRA: The Artist and the Man

By John Lahr


  


Random House, 168 pages, $45

THE WAY YOU WEAR YOUR HAT: Frank Sinatra and the Lost Art of Livin'

By Bill Zehme


  


HarperCollins, 246 pages, $32.50

ALL OR NOTHING AT ALL: A Life of Frank Sinatra

By Donald Clarke


  


Macmillan, 324 pages, $32.95

IF they asked him, he could write a book. The result -- to judge by the few pieces of prose, genuine or ghosted, that have appeared under his byline -- would be somewhat awkwardly phrased, over-earnest with occasional shafts of brash frivolity, but it would be compulsively interesting. They must, of course, have asked him countless times, and even though he could, he wouldnt.

Frank Sinatra has consistently refused either to author or to authorize his own biography. So others rush in. The present batch (not exhaustive) is ostensibly timed to coincide with his 82nd birthday. Now theres a nice round number for a celebration. It can't have escaped the publishers' notice that Sinatras health is precarious, though it may be less so than the doomwatching tabloids would have us believe; so, at least, I was told by someone who knows someone.

Michael Freedland describes Sinatra as conducting the orchestra with his voice. He is referring to the impact the young singer made on musicians at the start of his first solo recording contract -- with Columbia in the early 1940s -- but its a nice phrase that also evokes the authority Sinatra has always seemed to exercise over his material and his accompanists, live or on television as well as in the studio. It's about the only shaft of light in a murky and practically worthless volume.

Three of the four books on the table are named after Sinatra songs -- two after titles, one after a first line -- but Freedland's All the Way takes its label to heart and raids the canon for chapter headings as well. The young singers formative periods with the Harry James and Tommy Dorsey bands are Nice Work if You Can Get It ;Mia Farrow, his third wife, is The Girl Next Door .(Some neighbourhood.) Two chapters, not very far apart, are called Dont Worry 'Bout Me ,a duplication that suggests amazing carelessness on the author's part and quite astounding indifference on his publishers. Actually, it appears the first time, inaccurately, as Dont Worry About Me ;maybe Freedland thinks they're two different songs with only an apostrophe between them. Song titles give him trouble throughout; he invents songs called Those Little White Lies , That Old Devil Moon and If It's the Last Thing I Do. He thinks that Its All Right With Me ,a Cole Porter classic, is called Its the Wrong Time .Give him his due: When he quotes a politician who referred to the appalling My Way as I Did it My Way ,he appends a "sic."Chutzpah.

Freedland, an English journalist, is a prolific writer of showbiz biographies. Maybe he is cranking them out too fast. He commends Sinatra's recordings with the Dorsey band of What Is This Thing Called Love , South of the Border , Mandy Make Up Your Mind and Little White Lies , though Sinatra recorded none of these songs with Dorsey and never recorded the last two at all. He tells us that Lorenz Hart disliked the way Sinatra tampered with his lyrics to The Lady is a Tramp ,which he first sang in 1957, 14 years after Hart's death. Its doubtful that Hart ever heard Sinatra sing any of his songs which, considering the unrivalled way Sinatra was to draw out the gentle pain of Glad To Be Unhappy or the laconic wit of I Wish I Were in Love Again ,may be considered one of Harts greatest misfortunes.

Discussing Sinatra's first major film, Higher and Higher ,Freedland writes of a legal action involving the singer Jimmy McHugh, who apparently claimed that a number in the movie, The Music Stopped , plagiarized a song of his own. In fact, McHugh was not a singer but a composer, and he wrote and was credited with the entire original score of the picture, including the song The Music Stopped ;if there were any plagiarism suit he would have been on the receiving end of it. Freedland seems to have glanced at his sources a little hastily. Is all this just song-buff's trivia? Not really. McHugh was one of the most successful songwriters of his day, and an author who doesnt know who he was or what he wrote is unlikely to have anything valuable to tell us about Sinatra. And so it proves. He gets the content and chronology of the albums hopelessly confused; his approving comments on the fine September of My Years LP are vitiated by his citing three songs that werent actually on it.

It was also news to me that in 1962 Sinatra was burned in effigy by the Ku Klux Klan with a note attached, reading "Death to Nigger Lover Number One." That is one story I would like to believe. Otherwise, Freedland seems happier chronicling his subject's more boorish behaviour, which he does with the morose relish of a tired columnist. Presumably he has listened to the records and he tells us he has seen the concerts, but enthusiasm eludes him.

He wants to please his mother audience, was Shirley MacLaine's diagnosis of her famous friends overpowering ambition. At once spoiled and neglected by his real mother -- a backstreet abortionist and backstairs politician who ruled her patch of New Jersey with a rod of iron -- Sinatra grew up insecure, arrogant, lonely and gregarious. It was a winning combination for a pop singer, allowing him to touch all the genres important emotional bases with a depth and authority previously unattempted and still unmatched.

John Lahr quotes MacLaine (from her autobiography) in his monograph, originallya New Yorker profile -- he's their theatre critic -- with a lavish pictorial appendix. The photographs make an elegant mosaic, and so does the text. In contrast to Freedland, who is bursting with proudly proclaimed exclusives, Lahr never specifically claims to have interviewed anyone himself (he never says he hasn't, either), but he orchestrates a variety of voices to build up a sketch of the career and an impression of the talent.

His book, though incomparably better written (and incomparably briefer) than Freedlands, still ends up with an odd resemblance to it; it even sports a few howlers. Lahr deplores what everybody else has deplored, praises what everybody else has praised. He notes how "we preppy white boys" identified with the Sinatra who brought grace and sexuality into the pop music of the 1950s, but thats about it. This does not come across as one of his more personal projects.

Bill Zehme's book also derives from a magazine assignment, and a bizarre one. Totally unconcerned with the music, except as a source of myth, Zehme sent Sinatra a questionnaire about his philosophy and lifestyle and, surprisingly, got a reply. This became a profile in Esquire magazine:a tribute to the lost art of livin'  (the dropped "g" says it all), which seems to reduce to boozin, wenchin and stayin' up 'til 4, not letting any of your companions leave the bar before you do. (As one of them says in Lahrs book, Its Sinatra's world, we just live in it.)

In fact, the book is a nostalgic tribute to the Las Vegas '60s, the days -- and more specifically the nights -- of the Clan. As usual, Dean Martin emerges as the funniest of the bunch and Sammy Davis Jr. as the most interestingly articulate. There was also Peter Lawford, whose greatest achievement was to marry into the Kennedys, and whom Sinatra apparently used to call "Brother-in-Lawford," one of the few good jokes with which he is credited. The leader comes off as the straight man, even as strait-laced. (His behaviour around women is described as prudish, which is strangely credible.)

There is no logical reason why a great singer should exert Sinatras kind of personal power, but the charisma obviously carried over from professional into personal life, and the process, once started, must have been impossible to stop. You treat a man like a demigod, thats how he'll behave, his own beneficiary and his own victim. The book is written in the long, tense sentences that used to be regarded as the essence of hip. It is -- however queasy it makes you feel -- more fun than a biography.

Still, Zehme leaves out too much, good and bad, and one falls back on Donald Clarke's book, the best of the bunch. Clarke is an American who lives in England, and writes about U.S. pop culture with British breadth. A musical fanatic, he edited the Penguin Encyclopedia of Popular Music ,the best book of its kind, written in a brisk kind of synoptic shorthand. (The competing tomes are either academically stodgy or poisoned by rock snobbery.) He seems less assured here, writing proper sentences; his prose rambles, although not so much when writing about the music, especially the early stuff. He knows jazz, he knows pop, he knows the big bands, and he knows how they all intersected in Sinatras singing. His analyses are less lovingly detailed than those in Will Friedwalds book The Song is You .He doesn't share Friedwald's compulsion to find nearly every record a masterpiece, and he can be unjustly dismissive, but when he loves something he lets you know why.

He grapples with the dark side, too, especially in a long chapter called The American Aristocracy. This examines Sinatras gangster connections -- never legally proved but impossible to dismiss -- and the bullying that led Sammy Davis to declare "there are many things [Sinatra] does that there is no excuse for." (He had to grovel before being re-accepted into the Clan.) Clarkes conclusion, reached after much circling and repetition, is that Sinatra's life can be seen as a paradigm of the American experiment his "emotionally deprived childhood" parallels the continuous stumblings of a nation that has had to make up its own rules as it goes along. It craves style, it worships toughness, and it gets confused.

If you admire someone's work, you cannot help wanting to like them personally; performers, whom we have the illusion of having met, get to us with particular power. There is, after all, plenty to admire about Sinatra even outside the music. He used to have decent politics. (Its difficult to imagine Bing Crosby or Elvis Presley, the two idols who flank him chronologically, having any convictions at all.) His fabled generosity, to causes and individuals, may have had all kinds of dark psychological motives, but what doesnt? The important thing about good deeds is that they get done. Accepting a humanitarian award, he paid tribute to all the caring people who don't get prizes because they weren't famous to begin with, and this streak of self-deprecation may be the most endearing thing about him.

Sinatra knew he was in a false position (Ive been over-rewarded in my life, and thats the truth) and he resisted it when he wasn't exploiting it. He summed it all up in a phrase that's quoted by everyone whos ever written about him: Whatever else had been said about him, "when I sing, I believe Im honest." Some people put a comma after "believe,"turning it from a steady hope to a ringing affirmation, but it's better without.
