Ethan Mordden is a New York author of both fact and fiction who, over the past eight years, has published a decade-by-decade history of what he terms "the Golden Age of the Broadway Musical, from 1920 to 1980. He has given us Make Believe (treating the musical in the '20s), Beautiful Mornin' (the 40s), Coming Up Roses (the 50s), Open a New Window (the '60s) and One More Kiss (the '70s). There has, obviously, been a decade missing, but he has now accounted for it with Sing for Your Supper: The Broadway Musical in the 1930s (Palgrave, $38.95). The completed six-volume chronicle, which mentions nearly every show and has something crisp and analytical to say about most of them, is a unique achievement. Mordden seems to have read every script (and every source of every script), studied every score and -- but of course -- listened to every album. He is also a very entertaining writer, and at times an infuriating one.

Right at the start of Sing for Your Supper -- up in the acknowledgements, in fact -- he describes himself as someone who believe[s] the art has been locked in a spiral of degradation ever since Babes in Toyland." Babes in Toyland, music by Victor Herbert, appeared in 1903, long before Morddens Golden Age even got started. So obviously hes joking. But there's maybe a part of him that wishes he wasn't, that would like to move the decline of the American musical as far back as possible and get it over with.

The other side of that coin is visible in yet another of Morddens books, one that appeared last year and, though not officially included in the Golden Age series, is written in the same style and format. Its called The Happiest Corpse I've Ever Seen: The Last 25 Years of the Broadway Musical (Palgrave, $38.95), and it announces itself as a jeremiad on the way things are now. You will note that though, like all the Golden Age books, it takes its title from a song lyric, this time the song in question does not belong to the period being discussed; the phrase comes, instead, from Cabaret (1966). It's as if Mordden couldnt bear to defile his title page with anything from a present he so thoroughly despises.

His introduction confirms the feeling: The musical has become the province of "inarticulate idiots specializing in worthless forms based on exhausted old song catalogues and the staging of movies. Present-day America has summoned up a new kind of musical, coarse and uneducated. Thus prepared, and feeling an instinctive sympathy with the proposition advanced, one settles down for an enjoyably destructive read.

It doesnt materialize. The generalized denunciations keep coming, it's true, and the book's last sentence expresses a repugnance that is itself repugnant. But when he comes to examine the shows, Mordden finds an amazing amount to like. The post-Golden Age musicals over which he rhapsodizes include La Cage aux Folles, The Rink, Rags, the superflop Carrie (unpredictable, and that is the best theatre possible"), Nine, My One and Only, Dreamgirls, Crazy for You, The Mystery of Edwin Drood, City of Angels, Aida (!), Urinetown, The Producers (so that doesnt count as a staged movie?), Grand Hotel, Titanic, Sweet Smell of Success, Kiss of the Spider Woman, Side Show, Ragtime (though he finds its politics too left wing), both versions of The Wild Party and the five most recent New York Sondheims. He lists some "absolutely humourless shows, which sounds both accurate and damning, but most of them turn out to be among his favourites. When he devotes a chapter to the European pop- operas," one sniffs blood, but -- though his tone is rather evasive - - he finds nice things to say about them, too. (Les Miserables, that compendium of cliches, is stupefyingly described as "the one with the best lyrics.) In fact, Mordden, after all his attacks on the dumb and the ignorant, here turns around and starts castigating the snobs" who pounce on isolated flaws in these works to denounce the whole genre. Not that that stops him from casting Tim Rice into outer darkness for one notorious non-rhyme in Joseph and the Amazing Technicolor Dreamcoat.

So what, other than the obvious monstrosities from which no period is free, doesnt he like? He doesn't like Mamma Mia!, praise the Lord, and he calls Hairspray "stupid junk, though that term would be better applied to something like Side Show, a portentous show about Siamese twins, without the brains to match its ambitions. Mordden has always been soft on solemn musicals, though he's also capable of conducting a joyous examination of The Producers, which, as he points out, is the old baggy-pants comic show reinvented.

I agree with him about some shows, not about others. I share his pessimism, though not its violence. The reasons for the decline of the musical are clear enough: rising costs leading to rising ticket prices leading to fewer shows and no middle ground between massive hit and instant flop; a falling-off not so much of talent as of avenues for talent; most important, the gap, first apparent around 1960 and widening ever since, between music thats pop and music thats dramatic -- able, that is, to explore story and character. Once upon a time the best musicals -- a Guys and Dolls or a My Fair Lady -- were also the most popular. Now, that's the exception. Maybe the best new writers, the Adam Guettels and Michael John LaChiusas, should resign themselves to being minority entertainers, though that must be hard when you're working in a form that in the past has made so much money. Mordden says that the only people who really understand musicals are gay men and Frank Rich. Maybe thats the problem: too small a demographic.

He seems inclined to blame todays audience for not being as intelligent and literate as its ancestors, who got all the references in Cole Porter. Maybe, but a writer taking this attitude should not misspell Occam (of Occam's razor) on his own second page. Mordden's great virtue is that he knows a lot; his great vice is that he cant bear to appear ignorant about anything. He also hammers bad jokes into the ground. One actress, notorious for getting cast in flops, he chases down the decades, announcing that at her third-grade Easter pageant, some parents demanded their money back, though admission had been free." Hes a very witty writer, except when he's trying to be funny.

He finds the '20s a thrusting era in which an inconsequential form became consequential partly because people like Gershwin were writing the music, partly because of Show Boat, which revealed huge new possibilities of musical storytelling. These remained mostly dormant until the Rodgers and Hammerstein shows of the 40s, while in the 50s everybody joined in. Innovation became the norm. He thinks this persisted into the '60s, though I don't; I think thats when the form began to calcify. Witness Mame, one of his real faves, which tells us (in the song that gives his 60s volume its title) "the fellow you ought to be is unconventional while remaining unshakably conventional itself. And then the 1970s, which yielded the last major show, A Chorus Line, that was also a huge hit.

The '30s, oddly, were a period of stasis. At the outset of Sing for Your Supper, Mordden makes a couple of statements about the decade's shows that are both surprising and true. One is that they werent very adventurous. The other is that they werent very political. Brother, Can You Spare a Dime, the depression anthem, was a theatre song, but it was atypical. (Even its very politicized lyricist, E. Y. Harburg, never wrote anything else like it.) There was Porgy and Bess, but that was an opera; it didn't influence anything, though it's still ahead of everything. There were also Gershwins comic operas about presidents, which were smart but harmless, and Marc Blitzsteins labour opera The Cradle Will Rock. Mordden, whose conservatism has been growing increasingly peevish (perhaps because the musical is a traditionally liberal form), does manage here to be fair to Blitzstein, whom he described in another book as a braying stooge." Otherwise, it's an era of great songs, so many that when discussing Jerome Kern's Music in the Air, Mordden doesnt even mention that it contains The Song Is You, one of the most-performed standards of all. And its a time of stars. It saw the arrival, bang in 1930, of Ethel Merman, whose Cole Porter vehicle Anything Goes is quite rightly the decade's most-revived show. Sing for Your Supper is the name of a Rodgers and Hart song, and is less celebratory than most of Mordden's titles. Its about tuneful survival, and thats just about right for the '30s.
