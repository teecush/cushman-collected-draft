I never thought that Brian Bedford could move me to tears until I saw him play King Lear. But then I had never before seen him in a role in which he was required to.

Bedford, who died this month at the age of 80, played Lear at the Stratford Festival in 2007. It was the last great Shakespearean role he attempted, and he directed it himself: an impossible idea that didn't turn too badly. He wasnt known as a heavyweight tragedian, and his first few speeches as Lear were delivered at an alarmingly low sound level. In fact they were nearly inaudible. Then suddenly, when his authority was challenged, it reasserted itself. The voice, I wrote at the time "crackles, swells, and even roars, while the tottering old man turns into a tyrant fully capable of lifting a sword and, if not prevented, doing serious damage with it." His familiar ironic and comedic skills came into play in Lears madness, which he presented as a kind of baffled logic. He reached true greatness at the end when Lear thinks he sees the dead Cordelia's breath stirring; he was looking not at her but straight ahead, as if at some phantom in his own imagination. That's when we cried.

It might be said that, in his many seasons at the Canadian Stratford, Bedford reclaimed his British classical birthright. Bedford came from Bradford, in Yorkshire, and his childhood was exceptionally bleak. Acting seems to have saved him; he won admission to the Royal Academy of Dramatic Art, where his classmates included Albert Finney and Peter OToole, fellow-members of the new wave of working-class actors who emerged in Britain in the 1950s. Bedfords subsequent career as a master of drawing-room comedy may make that hard to credit, but he seems to have delighted in the occasional chance to return to his roots; at Stratford, his Bottom in A Midsummer Night's Dream was pure Bradford, and a delight because of it, especially when he confidentially corrected the Duke of Athens on the exact plot-line of Pyramus and Thisbe.

After RADA Bedford went into rep in Liverpool, where at the age of 21 he played Hamlet, prompting a local critic to salute him as one of the British theatre's rising stars. The prophecy proved true. Within a couple of months he was in London, making a reputation as an actor of sensitive young men. He also appeared at the English Stratford as Ariel in The Tempest, directed by Peter Brook; it was a controversial performance in a controversial production though I, a schoolboy at the time, remember liking both well enough. It was the only one of his English performances I saw; somehow I missed his breakthrough role in a breakthrough play, playing the tormented son in Peter Shaffers Five Finger Exercise. The play, and John Gielguds production, took him to New York, and there he stayed. He felt, he said, that he had found his home.

He was to find a second one in Canada. He had a decent American career, including some movies, some returns to London and - decisively - his emergence as a classical comedian, notably in Moliere. Then, in 1975, Robin Phillips, Stratford's newly installed artistic director, invited him to join the company there. He must have sensed in Bedford a wasted resource, waiting to be reawakened; Bedford may have felt the same. His first Stratford role was one of his neurotics, Angelo in Measure for Measure; it was, by common consent, magnificent, and I wish I'd seen it. I did catch his Richard III a couple of years later, and I still think it the best Ive seen: not especially funny, perhaps surprisingly, but electric with malice: one resentful, ambitious man against the world.

It was for being funny, though, that Bedford became most celebrated at Stratford: especially when Phillips persuaded Maggie Smith to come to Stratford; she and Bedford, high-wire artists in touch with the earth, performed a series of scintillating duets, most famously as the squabbling couple of Noel Cowards Private Lives. Bedford was a Coward specialist and a Coward friend.

To some people's surprise, Bedford's connection to Stratford outlasted both Smiths and Phillips; lasted in fact up till his death. He and the festival were good for one another. His technical command in comedy became so consummate that people were apt to take it for granted, but there was always more to it. As Sir Peter Teazle in The School for Scandal, he made me laugh every time he opened his mouth; he also made me believe. When he returned to Private Lives, this time opposite Seana McKenna, there was an unbroken current of thought connecting the epigrams.

He directed that Private Lives himself, and directing became an important part of his career. He started with tragedies (Richard Monette once told him that they were all he could direct): a Titus Andronicus that was a triumph and a Coriolanus that was pretty good. But he moved on to comedy, frequently directing himself in roles he'd previously played for other people. His masterpiece production was The Importance of Being Earnest, in which every line and every twist of plot made delirious sense, and in which every performance was impeccable, including his own Lady Bracknell: a dragon too sure of herself, or too afraid of being contradicted, ever to need to raise her voice. The production went to Broadway, though alas without most of its Canadian cast.

Bedford was a star, and he knew it. He was also, in my experience, charming and self-deprecating. I once asked him whether there'd been any difference between his two Stratford Malvolios; he smiled and said he shouldnt think so. (The one I remember had him roused from his bed, clutching a teddy-bear.) Hes the only actor I've interviewed to suggest, half way through, that we adjourn from the office to the nearest pub.

Last week also robbed of us another Stratford treasure. William Needles was a real veteran, part of the very first festival company in 1953. The following season gave him a star role: a paradoxically shy Petruchio in a Wild West Taming of the Shrew. He never again had a part that big, but he gave great authority to supporting roles, right up to the Henry VI history cycle of 2002. Jerry Franken, a third loss, was rarely thought of as a Stratford actor, though he was a good Polonius to Paul Gross' Hamlet, and once said, wisely, "The emotion is built into the text. You can't put anything on top of it .... Using tricks with Shakespeare is dangerous." But he will surely be best remembered for his wiry, vinegary performance as the farmer in Michael Healey's The Drawer Boy.
