Theatre Passe Muraille begins its new season by reviving its greatest of the decade. This new production of Michael Healey's The Drawer Boy is, in fact, a multiply commemorative occasion. It marks the 40th anniversary of TPM itself. It's the first offering under the theatre's new artistic director, Andy McKim, and, by the same token, a valedictory tribute to his predecessor, Layne Coleman, under whose aegis the play was first staged. It's also a tribute to TPM's own origins, since The Drawer Boy is partly inspired by The Farm Show, the collective production of 1972 that first put this theatre on the map. And, if I may, it's a personal milestone for me. The Drawer Boy was the first substantial new play I reviewed for the National Post, and I still remember the delight I felt at discovering it. Its initial impact and subsequent reverberations, across the country and outside it, remain unsurpassed, and very nearly unrivalled; among later plays only Kristen Thomson's I Claudia and John Mighton's Half Life come close.

Healey himself has written subsequent plays, Plan B and Generous, that are more adventurous technically and wider-ranging intellectually. None, though, has characters with the autonomous life of those in The Drawer Boy or a story that unfolds with the same folkloric ease. It feels, still, like the one play its author had to write; one that welled up within him and may have taken even him by surprise.

It's a simple enough tale. In the '70s, a young actor working on a project not unlike The Farm Show billets himself for research purposes on an Ontario farm run by two middle-aged men, lifelong friends, named Morgan and Angus. The actor's name is Miles, after Miles Potter, who was both a member of the actual Farm Show troupe and, nearly 30 years later, the first director of The Drawer Boy.

Angus suffered a brain injury in the Second World War that robbed him of all but the shortest-term memory; he is, however, a genius at figures. He loves to hear Morgan tell him the story of his accident and of the two Englishwomen -- "the tall girl" and "the taller girl" -- whom the two friends loved and lost. Angus, who was once a gifted draughts-man, figures in this narrative as "the drawer boy." Miles overhears the story, and puts it in the show. (He hasn't been getting very far in rehearsals with his impressions of the inner life of cows destined for slaughter, maybe because Morgan, who has a cruel streak, has been putting him on.) He invites his hosts to a rehearsal; Morgan is incensed at hearing his words onstage, but Angus is enchanted. He begins to remember things. And Morgan has to reveal some hidden truths.

The play is a fable, maybe a simplistic one, about the power of performance, both for spectators and participants. Like Hamlet, though less calculatingly, Miles uses a play to catch his auditors' conscience; and The Drawer Boy in fact includes a hilarious riff on the Danish play, though without any direct reference to the play scene. What it isn't, whatever Healey's intentions may have been, is much of a tribute to The Farm Show. Admittedly, that production was long before my time in Canada and it probably wouldn't have been my kind of theatre anyway, but it does seem that what moves Miles about his hosts' history, and what moves us, has very little to do with farming or indeed with Ontario. It could have happened to anybody.

It's impossible to avoid comparing this new production with the original, and I'm not going to try. Ruth Madoc-Jones's direction doesn't have the richness and detail

of Potter's, but it's very lucid and in clarifying the last scenes, which seem rather hurried in the writing, it may even have the edge.

It does have a younger pair of farmers, so the chains that bind them, both of affection and resentment, weigh less heavy. Randy Hughson's Angus doesn't have the unique massive presence of David Fox but that ends up working to his advantage. Fox was so much himself that one couldn't imagine his ever being any different; with Hughson, glimpses of the sensitive and intelligent man he once was keep peeping through, and the effect is immensely moving. When he cried, I did, too, as I never have at this play before. And earlier on, with Hughson using his quick-fire timing to convey Angus's instant memory lapses, I laughed, too. It's a joy to see this actor in a substantial role again.

John Jarvis's Morgan lacks the agrarian authenticity brought to the role by Jerry Franken, but in all other respects he's fine. The dialogue in this play remains wonderfully sure and resonant, and Jarvis gets the two best lines. One is Morgan's sardonic response to Miles's query about the farmer's alternating round of tending and killing: "Miles, it's an emotional roller coaster." (I've been Healey's willing slave ever since first hearing it.) The other is his memory of the words spoken to him by the woman who was leaving him: "This is the worst thing I could possibly do to you. Don't you dare help me."

Miles is the least flashy role and Tom Barnett, who created it, got less attention than his colleagues. In retrospect, this was unfair; his eager solidity is much missed in Frank Cox-O'Connell's performance, which starts out as a flapping cartoon, though it acquires some stature when Miles himself becomes part of the story instead of just listening to it. It's a little easier, too, to understand why he agrees to perform all the ludicrous chores that Morgan sets him. As a docile actor, he's just taking direction.
