THEATRE REVIEW

Mary Poppins Princess of Wales Theatre, Toronto

It's a shock, if you come to the original Mary Poppins stories after seeing the movie, to realize just how stern and unyielding the central character is.

She hardly says a nice word to anybody. If she's more palatable on film, it's not just because of the sugar that the Disney people applied by the now proverbial spoonful; that could have turned out even more distasteful. It's because the central performance managed to preserve some of the tartness along with the sweetness, and to combine absolute assurance with a self-awareness that stopped just short of self-mockery. To bring that off it's probably necessary to be Julie Andrews. Failing that, it helps to be English.

In the stage version that has come to Toronto after great success in London and New York (in both of which it's still running) Mary Poppins is played by Rachel Wallace, an American performer who sings nicely but most of whose acting energies seem to be focused on getting the accent right, with special emphasis on exaggeratedly rounded vowels.

Admittedly, some of the textual and musical additions make things hard for her. When the nanny in the movie serenely informs her two young charges that she's practically perfect in every way, it's unanswerable; when, as here, she does a whole selfcongratulatory number about it, it's insufferable. That isn't the actress's fault, but there isn't much charm or humour in her performance; and what with two children (Camden Angelis and Dakota Ruiz) who are having their own enunciation problems, plus an abrasive sound system, the show - which I enjoyed when I saw it in London - turns into one of the shrillest musicals I have heard.

One minor piece of collateral damage is the character of Miss Andrew, the anti-nanny who appears in the second act when the heroine has flown off somewhere, and threatens to blight the lives of the Banks children as she blighted that of their father before them. Miss Andrew's chief weapon, as brandished by a powerful performer called Q. Smith, is her ability to hold high, loud closing notes for a long time; but as we've already been vocally belaboured for half an evening, this doesn't constitute quite the climax that it might. More damagingly, playing her as a cartoon Wicked Witch of the West works against what's best in the show, the serious treatment of the parents.

In this revisionist version (book by Julian Fellowes), Mrs. Banks is no longer a suffragette neglectful of her offspring - that would now seem politically incorrect, and I'd probably find it offensive myself - but a loving, harassed mother who misses her former life as an actress. He's a banker who, because of his upbringing, doesn't know how to relate to his children but, because of Mary Poppins, learns. He also gets into professional trouble by giving a loan to a client who wants to invest in people while turning down one who wants to invest in what sound suspiciously like unsecured derivatives. (This show, which opened in 2004, was ahead of its time.)

It's pleasing to report that all the best performances in this touring production are given by Canadians; and none are better than Laird Mackintosh and Blythe Wilson who play the Bankses. She sings like a dream, with brains as well as pipes; he's lump-in-throat moving as he reaches out, especially to his son, and even gets a chance to caper a bit. Other legitimate sources of local pride are Valerie Boyle, ferociously funny as the family cook; Janet MacEwen, who sings a beautiful Feed the Birds; and, most prominently, Nicolas Dromard as Bert, the show's master of ceremonies, the virginal Mary's unrequited and understated love interest and - at one point - everybody's human fly. That is, he climbs right up one side of the proscenium, crawls upsidedown along the top, and climbs back down again, remarking as he goes "this is harder than it looks." Wires or no wires, it's a feat worthy of a sustained ovation, which the production mysteriously denies him by carrying right along.

Richard Eyre's direction is best when most dramatically or comedically human. At showbusiness level it's an efficient reordering of familiar ingredients. Like all musicals set in Edwardian London it resembles My Fair Lady, but at various points it also recalls Peter Pan, Wicked (or would that be The Wiz?) and even the Nutcracker Suite.

The choreography, by the famous Matthew Bourne, is surprisingly run-of-the-mill, though the chimney-sweeps' chorus, a.k.a. Step in Time, breaks down defences by sheer energy and insistence, and also bleeds entertainingly into the scene that follows.

That brings us to the score, which is half from the movie, and half new. It comes hard to admit that songs with titles like Chim Chim Cher-ee and Supercal - well, you know the rest - are good songs, but in fact they are: very infectious and celebratory. They do not, however, advance plot or character, or pretend to; so the grunt work has to be done by the new stuff, supplied by George Stiles and Anthony Drewe. They are conscientious, not much more, except perhaps for Being Mrs. Banks, sung by Mrs. Banks.

Bob Crowley's sets are enjoyably mobile, solidly atmospheric whether domestic or arboreal and - when we get to Mr. Banks' bank - positively Gorey. There is of course flying, with an umbrella, and a jolly good thing, too. And if you're going to reach for the roof, then the mountainous Princess of Wales is the ideal theatre in which to do it.
