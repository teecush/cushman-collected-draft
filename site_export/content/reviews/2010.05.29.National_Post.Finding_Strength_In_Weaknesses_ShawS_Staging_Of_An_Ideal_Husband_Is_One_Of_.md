Oscar Wilde's An Ideal Husband opened in London in 1895, months before his last and most lasting play The Importance of Being Earnest. So it might be categorized as "late transitional" -- Wilde's final attempt to graft traditional society melodrama onto his own unique creation, the farce of manners, before letting the latter genre take over completely, thereby creating a masterpiece. You can see it coming in An Ideal Husband in the scenes in which Viscount Goring, young man-about-town, presses his suit on Miss Mabel Chiltern, who has no intention of refusing him but wants him to work at it. Then they have to convince their relatives, who aren't monsters. It's as if the courtship of Algy Moncrieff and Cecily Cardew were to happen in a world with repercussions

It can be quite a relief to meet Wilde characters who, while funny, are more than walking epigrams. There were even moments, during the Shaw Festival's production, when I found myself heretically thinking that An Ideal Husband wears better then The Importance, though that may well be because we don't have to see it as often. Not that it's that much of a rarity. This is the play's third major Ontario staging in a decade.

It's also the best. Jackie Maxwell's production has both wit and passion, though it delivers them in imperfect quantities. The plot is ignited by a Mrs. Cheveley, an archetypal late-Victorian adventuress whose flamboyantly dubious past entails our never learning anything about Mr. Cheveley. She returns to London, from profitable exile in Vienna, to reveal herself as Europe's most glamorous lobbyist. She wants Sir Robert Chiltern, a notoriously honourable British MP, to come out in favour of a canal-building scheme that they both know to be deeply corrupt. Sir Robert's one weak spot is that, at the very start of his career, he engaged in a not dissimilar spot of insider trading, and Mrs. C. has the goods on him. Besides, it's personal: She and Lady Chiltern were at school together; they despised one another then and they do so now. Lady Chiltern has an even higher moral code than her husband; they place one another on pedestals, and Mrs. Cheveley would like nothing more than to knock them off.

The play's wild (or Wilde) card, the one character to travel freely between plot lines and dialogue styles and moral codes, is the debonair Lord Goring, Robert's best friend and putative brother-in-law, and -- for about five minutes, long ago -- Mrs. Cheveley's fiance. He's in a good position to defeat her blackmailing schemes. He's also, and more interestingly, ideally equipped to dissuade each of the married Chilterns from being their own, and one another's, best-intentioned worst enemies. The last act is a minor triumph of interlocking ironies as the characters are brought to realize not just that in their union there is strength, but that part of that strength lies in acknowledging and accepting weaknesses.

The play's continued political and moral pertinence hardly needs underlining, but it gets some anyway. The avowed aim of Maxwell and her designers is to respect the play's period setting but "twist it ever so slightly." This just about works, especially in the nerve-jangling sound score that John Gzowski has cooked up. Judith Bowden turns the Chilterns' drawing-room, where most of the action takes place, into a vast balustraded whispering gallery, which seems apt in a play that largely hinges on gossip and the dread thereof. Her costumes are, well, eclectically creative. Moya O'Connell's Mrs. Cheveley, a classic scarlet woman at her first appearance, is even greater, later, in green. Steven Sutcliffe's Lord Goring seems to be sporting some kind of apron underneath his frock coat. The two of them play their great confrontation superbly; there is, unusually, a feeling that there was once a strong sexual connection between them, and that it still holds. Both performances can stray into the wrong kind of anachronism. Sutcliffe in particular sometimes indulges in the kind of special pleading that's the bane of all but our best actors: Especially perplexing since he is one of our best actors. Mostly though, he personifies the play's meaning and its attraction: dandyism as a moral force.

Patrick Galligan seemed, in prospect, almost too well cast as the upright Robert. His recent role in Courageous, though, gave him a welcome break from being noble, and the iron injection obviously took; his performance here is equally powerful in self-assertion, self-defence and self-flagellation. He finds the passion in priggishness. As his lady, Catherine McGregor unfortunately finds only the priggishness. When she berates or exhorts her husband, she seems to be striking attitudes rather than making her own kind of love. You wonder whether their marriage is really worth saving.

The minor aristos, presumably meant to set the social tone, are mostly dowdy, with Ali Momen's Vicomte de Nanjac a briefly sparkling exception. Marla McLean makes a pleasing bobbing-cork impression as the future Viscountess Goring, while as her putative papa-in-law, Lorne Kennedy mines every laugh from a role that's studded with them, though without gluing them together into a person. He's also the voice of the political establishment; when Sir Robert unexpectedly gains political capital from doing the right thing, he welcomes him as a new rising star who'll help keep the radicals out. One hopes that, at moments like this, Wilde's tongue was in his cheek, especially as 1895 was to be the year of his own downfall. There was no Lord Goring to rescue him, making it especially ironic that the character is generally taken to be a self-portrait. I recant my heresy: An Ideal Husband is wish fulfilment; it offers hostages to morality. It's the escapist Importance that takes no prisoners.
