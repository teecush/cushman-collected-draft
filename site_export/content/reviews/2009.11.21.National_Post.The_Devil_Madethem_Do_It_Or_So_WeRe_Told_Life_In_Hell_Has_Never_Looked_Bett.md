Jean-Paul Sartre's Huis Clos is a great image masquerading as a great play. It's been that way for 60 years now. It shows us three dead people condemned to spend eternity in one another's company, an inferno that has no need of fire, or of whips and thumbscrews. Its world-famous conclusion is "hell is other people." (Well, if you put it like that, yes.) The untranslatable title -- it refers to the vacation period when all the French law-courts are closed -- is most commonly rendered in English as No Exit, and that's the label on the version brought to Toronto by Nightwood Theatre as part of its 4x4 female directors' season. Other adaptations have used other, equally apposite names: Vicious Circle is one, and another is In Camera, which if it were adopted by Kim Collier's production, would count as a wonderful adventitious pun. As Collier has staged it, the play's a movie.

Her "live-cinematic interpretation of Sartre's play" comes to us from The Virtual Stage & Electric Company in Vancouver, to whom welcome. Visually, it's stunning. The bulk of the play is projected on a wide cinema screen, split three ways. Within each, the camera angles keep changing. In front of the screen, on what is customarily known as the stage, is a set representing the lobby of an ancient hotel, piled with the debris of former glamour. This is the domain of the play's fourth character, the hotel valet who escorts the deceased to their new permanent residence. We see him at the start, pushing and pulling the first arrival to a place he has no desire to go. He's finally deposited, as later are his companions, in a side room, where they're left to get on with their acting. All we, the audience, can see of the room is what the cameras choose to show us (though we are allowed to inspect it afterwards, occasioning much impressed head-shaking at the ingenuity of it all). What we see has the look of film, but it's being created as we watch by people in the same building. The movie's a play.

There are two principal devices at work in this production, one technical, the other textual; and the technical is far the stronger. The textual concerns the valet, who's now present throughout, keeping mainly in his own domain, and who has new lines that the actor, Jonathon Young, has written himself. We gather that he doesn't much like his job; he makes constant phone calls to his uncle (who may be God or the Devil or whoever) asking when this damnation business is going to end. Meanwhile, he amuses himself by climbing on furniture, mocking the gestures and attitudes of his captives, holding up placards to involve the audience in the game, and generally carrying on like the master of ceremonies in Cabaret. This is diverting to begin with, but it gets monotonous very fast.

The three lost souls are far more compelling. They are Cradeau, a supposedly fearless crusading journalist whose past as a Nazi collaborator isn't even the most damnable thing about him; Inez, a lesbian secretary; and Estelle, rapacious high-society blond. They have, they come to realize, been specially selected to get on one another's nerves. Inez, an expert at detecting weak spots, taunts Cradeau incessantly for his cowardice, the one thing he can't bear to admit. (He cares extravagantly what other people think about him, and the management has thoughtfully provided him with a video link to the conversations of his surviving colleagues.) Estelle, desperate for admiration, tries to seduce Cradeau, who would be happy to oblige but can't with Inez watching. And she can't be disposed of. The damned in Sartre still have bodies and bodily desires, but they cannot be killed. That's been done already.

Probably, given enough time, any three people could torment each other as effectively, but the devils at the end of the phone seem to want quick results. It does mean though that, dramatically speaking, there is something arbitrary about the whole set-up, reinforced by Sartre's inability to bring his dead characters to life. There's nothing especially compelling about their earthly biographies, or about how they're revealed to us, and the ways in which they torture one another are surprisingly limited. But if the play can't develop its conception, except to a pre-ordained philosophic conclusion, the conception itself is genius. And it's in illustrating it, drilling into it, that Collier's production is at its strongest. We are watching the actors, most of the time, in giant unforgiving close-up, and they respond with performances that are ingrained and unflinching. Andy Thompson's vulpine Cradeau tends to generalize, but the women are fiercely specific, Laara Sadiq's bruised incisiveness as Inez balancing the elegant meltdown of Lucia Frangione's Estelle. The triple screen isolates them, and accentuates the moments when one crosses into another's territory. Just occasionally an aerial surveillance shot shows us the whole room, an oppressively ornate Second Empire chamber, with heavy ornaments that can't be lifted and a door that mostly won't open (and when it does, doesn't lead anywhere you'd dare to go). Hell is also other furniture; and the sight of the three of them marooned there is far more powerful than in the sustained long-shot of a conventional staging.
