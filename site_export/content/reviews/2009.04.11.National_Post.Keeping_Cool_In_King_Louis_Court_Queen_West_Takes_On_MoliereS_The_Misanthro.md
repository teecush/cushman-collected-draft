Acompany called the Red Light District has taken up residence, perhaps appropriately, in the basement of the Drake Hotel, self-proclaimed Hot Bed for Culture. The play is Moliere's The Misanthrope, a French classic, but the atmosphere certainly isnt classic and, though it might be described as enthusiastically louche, it isnt French. It's Weimar refracted through Queen West. The actors, greeting us in slithery poses and insinuating tones, do everything but sing Wilkommen. It's the kind of divine decadence thats fascinated audiences ever since The Threepenny Opera, which is fitting since the production is directed by Johanna Schall, who is the granddaughter of Bertolt Brecht. And though the show doesnt have much to do with the chillier dogmas of alienation, it does realize the Brechtian ideal of making us view an old play through new eyes. And what we find is what was always there, made fresh.

As quite often happens with aggressive takes on classics, I started out somewhere between suspicious and hostile and was progressively converted. Or perhaps the word is seduced. Alceste, the title character of The Misanthrope, is an idealist who demands impossibly high standards of the world around him and -- mostly but not always -- of himself. He refuses to fib or flatter and is shocked, though not really surprised, by those who do those things. There are two chinks in his armour. One is a lawsuit over some disputed property, in which his amour-propre seems as much at stake as his legal rights. (He calls his unseen opponent every name under the sun without deigning to get into specifics.) The other is his love for the beautiful flirt Celimene, who's simultaneously besieged by an army of admiring sycophants, none of whom she seems exactly to discourage. If he were truly true to his principles, Alceste would just walk away. But he doesn't.

An exceptionally informative program note clues us in to the aimless, airless lives of the thousands of enforced hangers-on at the court of Louis XIV: the people of whom Moliere actually wrote. The production tries to enforce an equivalence between them and the young fashionistas of our own time: the people, in fact, whom you might see hanging out at the Drake. You cant truly equate an existence imposed by royal fiat (Louis way of de-fanging the fractious French nobility) with one freely chosen by a section of, as this production's update of Tony Harrison's already updated translation puts it, "mankind circa 2009." I mean, theres king pressure and theres peer pressure. This production has to do some forcing. But that granted, it makes good tense sense of a play that can easily come off as an inert conversation piece.

It's very much in your face. The space is confined, the music and the visuals are from clubland, and the actors, costumed flamboyantly a la mode, move around and among us, getting up close and sometimes personal. So they do with one another; sex keeps breaking out where in the original it's kept under decorous wraps. In many productions this kind of thing just seems like showing off, but here it works. Insofar as you can separate the two, this show is more concerned with love than with politics. Alcestes erotic torments trap him like a vise; the play, performed without an intermission, allows him no escape.

The Harrison translation, a classic in its own right, was first performed by the British National Theatre in 1973, in a production that substituted de Gaulles Elysee for Louis' Versailles. Its effects range from the self-flagellating fury of Alceste's Just how degrading can a passion get? to Celimenes sublime refusal of a rural retreat with her lover: "Im only 20; I'd be terrified / Just you and me all that countryside." There are times at the Drake when I wished the actors, most of them recent ex-students, would stop crawling and scampering about and give the couplets a chance, but they confront the combined challenges of text and staging with winning commitment. Ted Witzel's Alceste is sketchy on personality but dead-on in projecting his predicament; Eve Wyldens Celimene loses some vocal points in the wash, but is still the credible queen of a shallow society with tantalizing inklings of something better. Marcel Dragonieri is toweringly good as Oronte, the amateur poet who begs Alceste for an honest opinion and is arrogantly unforgiving when he gets it. (Jesus wept, explodes the misanthrope-critic, "its bloody rubbish.") Philinte, the friend who keeps urging the hero to temporize, can be interpreted, according to your preconceptions, either as the honest voice of reason or as a contemptible time-server; Reid Linforth, in the show's most original performance, makes him both, mercilessly teasing Alceste with the name of Celimene but coming eloquently through in his defence. In a world in which bisexuality is a given, two characters have their genders reversed. This doesn't really square with Celimenes attempt to excuse an incriminating letter by saying its from a woman, but it does prompt fresh performances from Simon Rainville as the gentle Eliante and from Kat Letwin as a marquise whose reading, from an iPhone, of an even pricklier communique, distinguishing every pronoun at top speed, is a riot. Spencer Evoy rounds off the beau monde as a still-male marquis, and Mairin Smit makes the prudish Arsinoe something more dangerous than the usual frump. Moliere's original subtitle was The Atrabilious Lover; this version settles for Why Contempt is Sexy. It makes its point. I wonder how Schall approaches her granddad's plays. - The Misanthrope plays through April 26 at the Drake Hotel. Visit thedrakehotel.cafor details.
