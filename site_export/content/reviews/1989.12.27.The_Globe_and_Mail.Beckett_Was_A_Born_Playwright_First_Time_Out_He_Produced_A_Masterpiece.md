Beckett was a born playwright First time out, he produced a masterpiece

Wednesday, December 27, 1989

ROBERT CUSHMAN

BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Not all authors come to resemble their work, but Samuel Beckett , living on rice and giving no interviews, nearly brought it off. He approached the end of his life as ascetic and unreachable as one of his own plays. Only death itself - last Friday in Paris - has by a typically black Beckettian paradox, spoiled the likeness, imposing a finality that the work itself could never achieve. Beckett's characters court insensibility but never achieve it. He rages, not against the dying of the light, but against its stubborn refusal to go out altogether. He liked low-key lighting effects, but he steered clear of blackouts.

The flaw in Beckett's despair was pointed out long ago. His work appears to emanate from a depression whose logical outcome would be catatonia. But, of course, if you're catatonic you can't write; and if you could, you wouldn't produce anything as elaborately controlled as Watt, his most challenging novel, or Waiting for Godot, his most famous play. Plainly, Beckett used his writing to escape his demons as much as to express them; and the outward shape of his career bears witness to this. He moved from the hermetic form of the novel - hermetic at least as he practiced it - to the inescapably sociable mode of drama. Nor did he cloister himself from the process of production. He attended rehearsals, worked closely with actors, and wound up directing his own plays. The thought of Beckett supervising a technical is a piquant one.

He seems to have been a born playwright. First time out, he produced a masterpiece. What is more, it - Waiting for Godot - was an entirely theatrical masterpiece. It was called anti-theatrical, but only because at the time (the early fifties) that was a fashionable term. There was no plot machinery in Godot, and the characters had no visible means of support. Indeed, their pants kept threatening to fall down. But there we are; they were clowns, and Beckett wrote them two acts of cross-talk, interspersed with slapstick. It is difficult to think of anything more theatrical - anything less calculated to work in any other medium - than that.

Because Vladimir and Estragon await the mysterious and perpetually unpunctual Mr. Godot by the side of a dusty road, it is customary to present them as tramps. Beckett's text, though, makes no mention of this. They might be agricultural laborers. They might be novelists or playwrights; the worst insult in their vocabulary is "crrritic," and they converse with an oddball erudition that Beckett must have encountered, or even practiced, in Dublin pubs and Paris cafs. (In at least one respect he was the last of a line: that of Bohemian expatriates.) Anyway, they are among the few great characters of postwar drama: those who have entered the mythology of actors and audiences. Owing something, surely, to Laurel and Hardy they have in turn influenced such later masters as Tom Stoppard (Rosencrantz and Guildenstern) and Jim Henson (Bert and Ernie). Stoppard has insistently proclaimed his own debt: the reassurance Beckett gave to playwrights lacking in conventional narrative and psychological skills that they could keep a stage alive, largely through making jokes.

Stoppard's jokes are profligate and sunny, Beckett's measured and funereal - which doesn't stop them being funny. But he makes it clear that the joking and the waiting are strategies of escape. Beckett's bums talk to keep from thinking; or perhaps they think to keep from feeling. "That passed the time." "It would have passed in any case." "Yes, but not so quickly." Life is small-talk, improvised to camouflage a void. We will do anything to keep from confronting it, even write plays. The only victory is suicide, which the characters contemplate but never perform. It would, they decide, be a shame to miss out on Godot, who may, like love, be just around the corner.

This terrifying but curiously consol ing view of life is not really capable of development, but it can be refined and explored. Beckett went on prodding it in progressively shorter plays as well as in prose vignettes, such as the pungently dismissive (and very Irish) First Love, that have become favorite party-pieces for his favorite actors. Of the plays, Endgame projects the Godot vision into science-fiction; it seems to take place one week after the end of the world. In reducing human relations to those of master and slave it suggests that Beckett is not as apolitical a writer as some would have us believe.

Subsequent plays are more realistic. True, Happy Days shows us a lady buried first up to her waist and then to her neck in sand, which is not a situation one encounters every day, but the immersion plainly symbolizes an entombment in routine ("Winnie, begin your day") that is all too recognizable. Winnie is even more scared and evasive than the tramps, and a long way further from salvation; even cross-talk has contracted into monologue. The piece called Play scrambles the elements of a sexual triangle into a series of intercut fragmentary confessions from three figures encased in funerary urns. The piece gives us a clear, cruel view of their relationship while also functioning as a study in the psychology of interrogation; each jabbers frantically in response to a harsh beam of light. Not I refines this still further: one breathless outpouring from a disembodied Mouth, addressed to a silent, barely visible Auditor. Footfalls is the most recognizable of all: a self-sacrificing daughter paces to and fro while her mother upbraids her. This is Beckett writing domestic drama while providing us, for the first time in years with, literally, a full-length character. And not only did she have legs, she used them. She seemed pretty miserable doing it, and this may have been the author's joke on his critics. There, he says, that is what movement gets you; don't ask me for it again.

Philosophically, Beckett's plays have had little influence. Or rather, what they say seems so unanswerable that there has been nothing for subsequent writers to do but move on to other areas. Technically, his influence has been immense. He cleared away a lot of debris, and even writers who have backslid into naturalism have economized somewhat on the clutter. In the writing of charged dramatic English, only Harold Pinter and David Mamet have approached him. This is not to deny that his plays can be trying. Their constriction may be wholly justified on their own terms but it can easily be monotonous. Still, even when Beckett is boring, there is something creative about the boredom, nothing soulless or mediocre. And narrow though his range of effects may have been, he employed them with consummate assurance: the greater the economy, the bigger the punch. "Mr. Beckett," I once wrote, "is a gentleman and a showman." I expected some pious demurs to that, but all I got was a warm, approving letter from the late Patrick Magee, who with his creaking gate- hinge of a voice and louring sepulchral presence may have been the quintessential Beckett actor, just as the warm and loving Billie Whitelaw has - because she trusted him - become, in another paradox, the definitive Beckett actress. Let the final farewell be Stoppard's, paying homage at the end of his supremely effervescent Jumpers to his melancholy master: Wham, bam, thank you Sam.
