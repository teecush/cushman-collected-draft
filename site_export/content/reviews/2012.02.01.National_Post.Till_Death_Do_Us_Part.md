THEATRE REVIEW

Cruel and Tender

Bluma Appel Theatre, Toronto

Martin Crimp is a reasonably hot British playwright. Atom Egoyan is an extremely hot Canadian filmmaker who has also directed at least one fine opera production, the COC's Die Walkure. Arsinee Khanjian's reputation as an actress is practically on fire. Canadian Stage has brought them all together, with considerable fanfare, and the results are atrocious.

Cruel and Tender, which is more the former than the latter and inspires similar feelings in return, is based on the Greek tragedy The Women of Trachis, possibly the least familiar of Sophocles' surviving seven. The original concerns Deianira, the stay-athome wife of Heracles who, having completed his 12 famous labours, has been off having other adventures. One of these has been the sacking of a city, whose princess he covets; he has sent her on ahead, hoping to pass her off as one of a convoy of slaves. Deianira finds out and, to regain her husband's affections, sends him a robe dipped in what she believes to be a love potion but is actually a poison. On discovering what she's done, she kills herself. Heracles, unable to remove the robe, comes home to die in agony.

Crimp has adapted, or appropriated, the story as part of the backlash against the backlash against 9/11. His contemporary Deianira is called Amelia; her husband, known simply as the General, is away in Africa pursuing the war on terror. Like his mythical prototype he has laid waste to a town in order to gain access to one of its young women, here named Laela, and he, too, has sent her on ahead, claiming that she and her little brother are refugees, as in a way they are.

Now, massacring for love may be standard behaviour for a mythical superhero but it isn't credible in a 21st-century commander; we have our own abuses but they aren't as narrowly personal as that. The play is trying to tell two incompatible stories and the strain shows, mainly in the form of padding. Bizarrely, Crimp has omitted the most compelling part of his source: the steps by which Deianira, maybe the earliest detective in world drama, realizes what she has done and that lead her, first to try and undo it, and then to suicide.

In this version, even the manner of the heroine's death remains obscure. Instead of logical emotional processes we get a lot of fancy talk about war that bears no relation to Amelia's specific circumstances. She seems to be trying to bond with her rival, a character whom Sophocles sensibly kept silent. It's like one of those French plays of the 1950s, also often based on the Greeks, in which abstractions trump feelings.

The wife has the first twothirds of the play and the husband the remainder; they never meet. Here the play is being true to its original in which, given the parsimonious conventions of ancient Athenian theatre, they would likely have been played by the same actor. But it feels frustrating even in Sophocles, and more so in Crimp. The General, when he finally gets his own turn to strut and fret, is a little more believable than his spouse, but not much. His dying delirium is full of scrambled imagery from the legends of his progenitor that seem like a last-minute attempt, on both his part and the author's, to grab some grandeur.

Speaking of which: Debra Hanson has designed the kind of set that looks impressive when the curtain rises (there is a curtain and it does rise) but then proceeds to eat the play. It's a vast white structure that seems to be half kitchen and half garage. It's also, literally, full of holes, with a lot of empty space on top, all allowing the sound to leak out. It accentuates the difficulties both of an acoustically tricky theatre and of a leading actress with clarity and projection problems. It does allow Egoyan to frame the occasional striking picture, notably the sight of a young boy alone on a promontory in the far corner of the stage, but that remoteness itself turns out to be symptomatic of a crabbed aloof production in which nobody connects with anyone else.

There are intermittent odd-ities such as a chandelier that goes on and off for no apparent reason and a microphone that gets passed from hand to hand like some kind of totem. I wished it might take longterm residence with Khanjian, whose Amelia is a vocal and emotional blur.

Daniel Kash's General has more presence but remains a stereotype of a bully. His death throes are shown to us in video close-up, which mainly suggests a director playing with toys.

A lot of supporting talent gets wasted. Jeff Lillico comes off best as the couple's son whom they keep using as a go-between; he in return treats them with dismay or disdain, thereby giving the audience somebody to root for. Brenda Robins gets off some tart zingers as Amelia's housekeeper, leader of a mini-chorus of domestics. Its other members, Sarah Wilson and Cara Ricketts, put that mic to use for some karaoke; they specialize in hopeful Billie Holiday impersonations but are decisively upstaged by Laela (Abena Malika), who can really do Billie and even sports the trademark gardenia. Nigel Shawn Williams as a shifty diplomat seems constantly on the verge of delivering a smooth performance but never gets beyond fits and starts.

The production may seem to fit in with Matthew Jocelyn's generally stimulating policy of promoting a "European" aesthetic at Canadian Stage. It doesn't. Misplaced grandiosity can happen anywhere.

Cruel and Tender runs until Feb. 12. For more information, visit canadianstage.com.
