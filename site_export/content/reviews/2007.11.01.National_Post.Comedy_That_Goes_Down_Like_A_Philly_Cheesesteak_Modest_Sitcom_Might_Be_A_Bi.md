There's a promo going around for the new season of It's Always Sunny in Philadelphia that's a parody of Friends. It starts, as Friends used to, with its principal characters crowding one another on a sofa in front of a fountain. Then they fall in. And just to make the point clear, the screen flashes a title proclaiming that this show is UNFRIENDLY.

I'm all in favour of Friends being taken down a peg, but I have to admit that it managed to maintain its impetus for most of its lifespan. The Philly show seems to have exhausted its energies at the start of its third season.

Like Friends, it rotates around an urban watering-hole; only instead of a yuppie coffee shop where a couple of the characters work, we get a run-down Irish pub where all of them do. (So it's also, in socio-economic-televisual terms, a poor man's Cheers.) The five leads, Mac, Charlie, Dennis, Dee and Frank, are all very similar to one another. Frank is Dee and Dennis' adoptive father -- they're twins --and probably Charlie's biological one, though this has yet to be fully acknowledged. Frank is played by Danny De-Vito, who only joined the team in the second season and who looks as if he's slumming.

The characteristics they all share are greed, sloth, lust (usually frustrated) and ignorance. This makes them pretty representative of the human condition, but it's just about all there is to them; the quintet have congealed into a mass, so that who does what in any given story-line looks arbitrary. Selfishness and stupidity may not on their own be enough to power a comedy, at least not over the long haul.

Sunday's kick-off episode starts out with Frank decrying the idea of what he calls "global heating." That turns out to be the best line in the show; the writers, Rob McElhenny (who

plays Mac) and Glenn Howerton (who plays Dennis) are too laid- back to go in much for verbal wit. What they rely on is people doing outrageous things with straight faces, and after a while this becomes self-defeating.

The gang find a baby abandoned in a dumpster. Dee and Mac decide to take care of it; at least Mac decides that Dee will take care of it while he basks in the glory of instant fatherhood. It gets worse. Told that the kid would look great in commercials, they set about blacking him up, since the market is already flooded with white mites. I hear the sound of writers' desperation.

Meanwhile, in related stories, Frank and Charlie go trawling through the trash for other valuable objects, which goes absolutely nowhere, while Dennis gets embroiled with a hippie tree-hugger (literally) who's cast aspersions on his own born-again environmentalism. That's the best part of the show.

Next week's show is better. Mac and the twins try out for the NFL's Philadelphia Eagles. The guys pour scorn on Dee's efforts because she's a girl with scoliosis, but mainly because she's a girl. Meanwhile, Frank has an acid trip which is deeply unamusing. More entertainingly, the regulars confront a rival clan, the McPoyles, who boast their own sports champion, Doyle McPoyle. Some of their interchanges almost count as character comedy.

It's Always Sunny started with modest ambitions and an even more modest budget and, but it may have been spoiled by success. At the moment, though, it's (almost) never funny in Philadelphia.
