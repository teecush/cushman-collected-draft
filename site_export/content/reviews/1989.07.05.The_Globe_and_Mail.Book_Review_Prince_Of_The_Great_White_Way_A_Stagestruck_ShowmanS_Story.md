BOOK REVIEW Prince of the Great White Way: a stagestruck showmans story

HAROLD PRINCE AND THE AMERICAN MUSICAL THEATRE BY FOSTER HIRSCH Cambridge University Press 187 pages, $29.95

Wednesday, July 05, 1989

ROBERT CUSHMAN

REVIEW BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Cambridge University Press kicked off its Directors In Perspective series a few years ago with studies of Adolphe Appia, Gordon Craig, Meyerhold and Peter Stein. Appia and Craig were basically design theorists, and Stein, though greatly talented, has an unfortunate effect on the theatre academics who write this kind of book: they use him as a stick to beat the Anglo-American theatre, its audiences and its reviewers, who have been known to treat the visiting giants work with less than the obligatory reverence.

There were no English names on the list except Peter Brook, whose volume has yet to appear, and who commends himself by working mainly in Paris and having ideas. Gordon Craig doesn't count, for he hardly directed a thing in his life. Why, I asked, with what I hoped was a disarming blend of philistinism and xenophobia, could we not have books on the directors who actually did the work - Trevor Nunn, say, or Hal Prince?

There is no sign of a book on Nunn, but Prince has now got his and it's excellent. Cole Porter and Rodgers and Hart scores, than Prince himself would be, who dismisses them as antiques that are only revivable as curios." Hirsch is more temperate: "If it no longer speaks out of a current sensibility, a well-performed Rodgers and Hart book can still entertain . . . the frothy book has the courage of its unimportance. Hes dead right.

The musicals that Prince has direced - stretching from A Family Affair (1962) to Roza (1987), with some rather more famous and successful names en route, have by contrast the courage of their importance. More exactly, they have the intelligence of their ambitions - which, in the case of Cabaret and Evita and the six shows with music and lyrics by Stephen Sondheim, are considerable. They are not pretentious, and they are not self-consciously experimental. They all give the impression that their creators, having unusual stories to tell, simply lit on the most appropriate theatrical means. The Sondheim-Prince Pacific Overtures, to take the furthest-out example, blends the forms of Japanese Kabuki and the American musical because its subject is the rape of the old culture by the new.

Most people who write about musicals are silly; others are human computers who know the date of everything and the value of nothing. Hirsch is one of the few who never denies either his brain or his appetite for fun. He sees that musicals are to be judged by different standards from plays or operas but not by lower ones. This makes him the ideal commentator on Prince, who is a mixture of explorer (not visionary) and showman. Abbott, who today is an active centenarian, was Broadways grand master of farce and melodrama as well as of musicals; Prince, who began his career in Abbott's office, learned from him pace, discipline and clarity.

When Prince turned producer, with The Pajama Game in 1954 (he was 26), he became his master's employer, and Abbott worked for him too on such other super-formula shows as Damn Yankees and Fiorello! Abbott also directed A Funny Thing Happened On The Way To The Forum, but had to call in the greatest of the choreographers (and another Abbott disciple), Jerome Robbins. It was Robbins who directed the two most ambitious of Princes early productions, West Side Story and Fiddler On The Roof, both of them fluid, impressionistic social canvases. Princes greatest achievements as a director, from Cabaret to The Phantom Of The Opera, combine Abbott's stagecraft with Robbins' scope.

With Cabaret and Phantom, Hirsch classes Princes direction of Follies, Pacific Overtures, Evita and Sweeney Todd. He excludes A Little Night Music for its linear conventionality and, though hes not quite forthright about this, Company, for its brittle script. He had doubts about Sweeney Todd, too; he seems to doubt that Prince's overlay of social combat on the industrial age was really relevant to the show's Grand Guignol story (hes right) and he questions the universality of Sondheims stated theme of revenge (he's wrong). The musical does not quite add up. Maybe it has worked better in its later chamber stagings. And yet, in memory, it seems an extraordinarily big, bold achievement.

There is also a chapter on Prince's recent acknowledged failures: Hirsch pays encouraging tribute to the underrated Larry Grossman, who composed two of them. (He was also the musical director of The Muppet Show, which makes him a hero in my house.) And, rounding out his general study of the musicals to include the post-Prince generations, Hirsch is fair to the Lloyd Webber shows that Prince didnt direct: Cats and Starlight Express are entertaining high-tech vaudeville."

Any book about Prince must in part be a book about Sondheim, and Hirsch, in limited space, offers the shrewdest Sondheim criticism yet to appear in book form. He also holds out a tantalizing promise of a renewed collaboration, after a lapse of eight years. This is how he sums the partners up: "Both are stagestruck showmen weaned on a romance of the Broadway big time that they have never outgrown and that helps to explain their drive for continued achievement, and for critical as well as popular acceptance. In terms of their record they qualify as theatrical elder statesmen, but both cloak their hefty egos in an appealing modesty.

One of Hals favorite words in rehearsal," Sondheim once said, "is energy - by which he doesn't just mean energeticness." Prince's energy - and his energeticness - pervade this book. Robert Cushman is former drama critic of the Observer, London.
