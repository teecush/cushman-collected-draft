1002 NIGHTS

Video Cabaret Cameron House, Toronto

In 1002 Nights, the delectable Tara Rosling plays a stripper. Take note: She appears before us in a dazzling set of golden undies, but that's as far as she goes. Such restraint may seem odd in todays theatrical climate, with actors shedding their clothes while playing people in all manner of professions, but there is a logic to it. For a stripper to strip would be theatrically redundant.

Anyway, Stella, the character Rosling plays in this one-woman piece, is more intent on baring her soul than her body. She is going through a personal crisis whose precise nature, in Simon Heaths script, remains somewhat muddy. She wants a change of career: She would like to give up lap-dancing for belly-dancing, a far more refined and subtle form of erotic art. Quite possibly, though, that's just a metaphor (though it's one that Rosling acts out very well). Stellas monologue has a number of different threads, which it alternates but never knits together.

First there is the theme of Scheherezade, who kept her head through a thousand and one nights in the royal Arabian bed, by telling the Caliph stories so beguiling as to leave him with no option but to tune in tomorrow. (Stella, who used some of her earnings to put herself through college, has read every one of them, which, as she correctly assumes, puts her ahead of most of us.) She never quite spells out the relevance of Scheherezades situation to her own, but you could say that they have both learned to survive by keeping men coming back for more: Stella, if we are to take her title literally, for one night longer than her predecessor.

Her other favourite literary reference is A Streetcar Named Desire, a play she says could only have been written by someone whose parents had named him Tennessee. (Actually, Mr. and Mrs. Williams named their child Tom.) She identifies with Blanche DuBois, striving to preserve some beauty in a fallen world: This despite the fact that her own parents has named after her Blanche's more practical sister. Her mother chose the name because she thought that hearing Brando howl Stella was just the most romantic thing, something indeed that she would wish for her own daughter; and she made the family watch the movie every holiday.

That must have been some mother. Still, it would have made a change from Miracle on 34th Street.

Moving from art to life, we have our Stella's experiences with an abusive boyfriend whom she met at the club: Stanley to her Blanche, perhaps, though she was smart enough to get away. And, Blanche-like again, we have her falling in love with a gay young man whom she also met at the club. This, unfortunately, is wrapped up in a coy, recurring fable in which he figures as a prince and she has dreams of flying. Indeed, we are never definitively told that this story is her own, though there seems little point in having it there if it isnt.

In short the piece, though never quite boring, is rambling and second-hand, though Ill admit that for a man to have written it at all is some kind of achievement; it doesn't sound ventriloquized. It is subtitled "A Search for Truth and Beauty," which is enough to put anybody off, but in Rosling's performance it finds what its looking for. She incarnates beauty, and eventually she embodies truth.

The latter part takes a bit of time. There is a vein of truculence in the script that she mines with excessive enthusiasm. You know how in life and in drama and on TV talk shows, which fall somewhere in between, women (somehow its always women, usually young ones) are always saying well excuse me! and "Hello?!" and "duh!" to denote sarcastic amazement. It's a tic that has long outlived its usefulness -- in fact it makes me wince. The tone, if not always the actual words, invades Rosling's performance when shes pouring scorn on the men behind the Arabian nights or the other men who have misinterpreted them ever since. (This includes the guys at Disney: tough really, considering the lengths they went to make Aladdin look feminist, but probably fair.) She blunts her points by underlining them. But she settles down, and eventually takes full bodily possession of her role; you cant see where the character ends and the actress begins. Her face is wonderfully mobile; it can warm you with a smile or it can seem to hold the grief of ages. Best of all, though, is her dancing. Shimmying, and shimmering, round the strip-club pole, she is entrancing. At the end of the play she goes into a celebratory step -- a sort of tap-and- grind -- that seems to be all Stella's own, nobody else's. Garnet Jones, her costume designer, and Claudia Moore, her choreographer, have done splendidly by her. Tanja Jacobs is the director who put it all together.
