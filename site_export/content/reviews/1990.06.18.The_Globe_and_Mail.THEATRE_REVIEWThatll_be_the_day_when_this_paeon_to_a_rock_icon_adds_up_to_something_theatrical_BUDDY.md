THEATRE REVIEW"That'll be the day when this paeon to a rock icon adds up to something theatrical BUDDY

Monday, June 18, 1990

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Written by Alan Janes Directed by Rob Bettinson Starring Paul Hipp Royal Alexandra Theatre, Toronto Buddy more concert than musical BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail THE program includes a couple of articles on Buddy Holly, the man and the myth, that are well worth a look. In fact they are essential reading, since the show itself has virtually nothing to tell us on either subject. Buddy is a fairly rousing pop concert, thinly if laboriously disguised as a musical.

I don't deny that it got to my feet and my nostalgia-glands. I'm fairly sure I never cried when I read about his widowed bride - truthfully, I don't remember his death in the l959 airplane crash registering at all - but in the years that followed Holly loomed larger in my life than Elvis ever did. His twangy, unthreatening music was the soundtrack to every teen-age party I ever attended, at least until supplanted by early Beatles. Those were innocent years. Holly, in the nicest sense, was kid's stuff.

And he was nice. Geoff Pevere and Peter Goddard tell us so in the program and the show backs them up. That is to put it a bit too positively; let us just say that it never contradicts them. It has little truck with characterization, and not much with narrative. But then there isn't much to narrate. "All that happens in the play" say its creators "is based on fact, even those events which seem beyond belief." But what they show us is that Buddy and the Crickets started out playing country-and- western in their native Texas, graduated to rock'n'roll, encountered early resistance, but soon became very successful. That's Act One. After the interval Buddy gets married, splits from his record producer and his sidemen, and accidentally dies. Only the most fearful skeptic could have trouble with any of that.

They do try and pad it out. There is a running gag, repeated roughly 18 times, about Buddy's mother phoning him to make sure he's eating right. The hero is given heroic lines ("I don't want to play country-and-western, I wanna play my music") and there are awfully knowing ironies; he is told that he has about as much chance of recording in Nashville "as that fella Ray Charles has of playing in the White House." There is suspense. When the group is booked into the Harlem Apollo, they are told - endlessly - that the black audience will eat them alive. But we know all along that they are going to be a smash.

Actually if you are looking for improbabilities, it seems odd that at a time when Buddy and the Crickets have had two number-one hits, nobody at the Apollo knows enough about them to realize they are white. Going a bit further back I was surprised to see the three guys making out with three local girls under the local stars (I don't mean that the girls are under the stars - well, maybe I do) when they are already successful enough to afford motel rooms. And further back still, the arrival, unheralded and unexplained, of a fourth Cricket halfway through the first recording session could do with some fleshing out. But strange things happen in studios; when trying desperately to explain themselves to the good ol' boys at Nashville Decca, the trio go into an impromptu rendition of That'll Be the Day, exactly as it sounds on the record.

The story swiftly becomes a matter of success and more success, linked by the triumphant pronouncements of disc-jockeys (though the one in Philadelphia sounds really mean) perched in eyries all over the colorfully ugly set, which is meant to be redolent of the Fifties and is. The sequence with most theatrical potential is the build-up to the fatal flight after a snowbound concert in Iowa. History has laid on the ironies thick. Holly was actually scheduled to go by bus, and only flew on a whim; his co-star and fellow-victim Ritchie Valens had to plead and wheedle to get on the plane. (As played here, like a gyrating Liberace, he was no great loss.) The script gives us the facts but doesn't know how to dramatize them.

Even musically Holly begins to seem an odd subject for a show. The numbers that tear the place up have nothing to do with him. A female trio at the Apollo sing an R&B number, indistinguishable from hundreds of others, but enough to put paid to the already dubious claim that Holly had a black sound. (The rednecks think him uncommercial because of it; this at a time when Presley had already broken.) The Big Bopper, his other doomed fellow-passenger, precedes him in Iowa with a number that anyone would have trouble following: Chantilly Lace, the best song of its era, performed here by David Mucci panting and lolloping all over the stage with a grin full of certifiable fangs. And for Buddy's closing number (a last-minute panic measure to judge from the song-listing in the program) the producers abandon Holly's own catalogue and have him do Chuck Berry's incendiary Johnny B. Goode.

Paul Hipp's Buddy is gawkily good-natured as prescribed, and his re- creation of the songs is note-and-letter perfect. Amazing that a mere ten songs and horn-rims should make a legend, but so it is; and you don't tamper with a legend. It means that the performer has no chance to personalize the material; the audience would hardly stand for that. It has more to do with necrophilia than with theatre, but it is a burgeoning genre; and this is one of the better examples. It comes to us from London, where it is a big success. So what do I know?
