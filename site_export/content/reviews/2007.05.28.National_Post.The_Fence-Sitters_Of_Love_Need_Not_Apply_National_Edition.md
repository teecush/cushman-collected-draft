THE PHILANDERER

Royal George Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake at the Shaw Festival

- - -

George Bernard Shaw's The Philanderer, his second play, would have us believe that, in London in the 1890s, there was an establishment known as the Ibsen Club, named for the great Norwegian playwright and providing a comfortable environment for all the well- heeled advanced thinkers of their day.

Like Shaw himself, his fictitious clubmen and clubwomen seem to have had a limited idea of what their idol actually stood for. Ibsen was never a great one for causes; he even denied, despite A Dolls House, that he was a feminist. The "Ibsenism" that the characters in The Philanderer go on about seems to be a vague label for anything progressive but boils down in practice to relations between the sexes.

Any enlightened young thing applying for membership has to be certified, to the horror of the older generation, as either an unmanly man or an unwomanly woman. This has nothing to do with homosexuality. It doesnt even ban relationships between members, which in fact, seem to be encouraged. But partisans of what the title character calls the advanced view -- our view are supposed to move easily from one partner to the next, to take rejection in their stride, and to abstain from jealousy, possessiveness and all the other appurtenances of romantic love.

Somewhat thin and protracted as a story, the play, like most of Shaw, is an astonishingly confident outpouring of language. The characters are mainly stock figures brought to new and vivacious life by sheer force of dialogue. The chat, as usual with Shaw and especially with Shaw on sex, is witty but chilly; but it's one of his most fascinating plays, because it's one of Shaws most personal. The philanderer himself, Leonard Charteris -- confident, articulate, superficially solicitous and invincibly self-centred-- is an engaging but merciless self-portrait. He may not be the sexual late bloomer that his author was, but for all his presumed conquests, theres still something virginal about him -- as there is, admittedly, about most of Shaw's characters; they may be constrained by the theatrical conventions of their time but one suspects that, even when safely offstage, they would be more talk than action.

We see Leonard getting on with a new love before being off with the old, or at least before the old is off with him. Both ladies are fellow club members. Grace Tranfield, to whom he's currently paying court, is a widow, the most sensible and truly liberated person in the play. Julia Craven, his unwilling ex, stalks, threatens, cajoles and throws tantrums, both in and out of the club, which she has obviously joined under thoroughly false pretenses; she is, at least if you happen to be in her line of fire, the womanly woman from hell. If Charteris has too little heart, Julia has too much.

He finally palms her off on a more pliable admirer, a Dr. Paramore, who happens to be treating her father for a fatal but, as it turns out, non-existent disease. You might say -- and Shaw would surely want you to -- that the father has Paramores disease, while the daughter has paramours disease.

Alisa Palmer's production, yet another bloom in the Shaw Festival's opening bouquet, is both handsomely and humorously designed by Judith Bowden. It has Ben Carlson in scintillating form as Charteris, Deborah Hay as a Grace more than worthy of the name and Nicole Underhay as a Julia whos almost totally wrong. Julia is one of natures dark ladies, but this one is light of hair and, greatly compounding the damage, of temperament. She's a rare case in Shaw where wit is inappropriate.

Also too lightweight, though for other reasons, is Peter Krantz's Dr. Paramore, whose great discovery is disproved by foreign researchers who have been granted more animals to torture. He seems less a medical star in danger of falling than a student worried about his grades.

The heroines fathers, the twin voices of outraged convention, are splendidly played. They are both members of notoriously reactionary professions -- Julias is a soldier, Grace's is a theatre critic-- and they are given buffo performances by Norman Browning and Peter Hutt, the one rumbling, the other apoplectic.

Shaw wrote a "lost" last act that is to be given at some performances later in the season. It should be worth catching, since it has more weight and more poise than anything in the rest of the play. The official final curtain is here an anti-climax. When Julia tells Charteris at the end of the play that she could kill him, we ought to believe her; the stage direction tells us that the other characters sober up, realizing that they are in the presence of real grief.

Grace, having herself effectively dumped Charteris, takes Julia's side, telling her "never make a hero of a philanderer." He laughs, but the laughs on him. Its a difficult gear change to pull off, but it's what the play's about. The man who laughed at love and the woman who staked everything on it -- the Shavian and the anti- Shavian -- have both had their ideals shattered. Ibsen himself, the perennial questioner and non-joiner, would have approved. He would never have joined a club that would have him as a mentor. - George Bernard Shaws The Philanderer is in repertoire through October 7 at the Shaw Festival in Niagara-on-the-Lake.
