THEATRE REVIEW

Puppet Up! Uncensored Panasonic Theatre, Toronto

Puppetry is an enviable skill. So is improvisational comedy. The performers of Puppet Up! Uncensored are adept at both, which puts them in a special league of accomplishment. All the same, the mixture of talents can result in a kind of split focus. And that characterizes the whole show. Some of it is fun, but the total effect is aimless.

First, there's the question of where, and who, it's coming from. The show's originating company is called Henson Alternative, and BrianHenson himself is credited as co-creator and producer. Brian is of course the son of the late genius (and I mean that) Jim Henson, creator of the Muppets. Onstage at the Panasonic, there's a multi-tiered display case of puppets, all ready to be taken out and manipulated by the live ladies and gentlemen of the cast, and all carved and dressed in Muppet style. There are a few whom I thought I recognized from Sesame Street or The Muppet Show, but I can't be sure; it's been a long time. I did think I caught a glimpse of Kermit during the opening number, but he was in the back of the group and didn't reappear thereafter.

The "alternative" part is that we are seeing Muppet types in the kind of material they wouldn't be allowed to perform on family TV. Actually, as Brian Henson himself pointed out in an interview with this paper, the Muppets have always had an irreverent and even satirical edge, certainly on their own show and even on Sesame Street, so the only departure here is in having them being more explicit, sexually and anatomically and verbally, than we might expect them to be. But since they aren't individually recognizable Muppets, even the shock effect of that is blunted. (A member of the first-night house, in a show dependent on audience involvement, inquired as to the whereabouts of a favourite character, and was told that he's owned by a different company. That's telling.) And if you want to see the Muppets satirized, that's already been done by Avenue Q.

There are half a dozen puppeteerperformers, plus co-creator, director and host Patrick Bristow, whose manner is poised somewhere between cute and oily and who is there to tell the others what to do and when to stop doing it. In other words, he's the puppeteers' puppeteer. At the start of each number he commands them to Puppet Up, suggesting analogous concerns for their morale (as it might be "Man Up!"), their efficiency ("Hurry Up!") or even their physical safety ("Buckle Up!"). His more specific instructions are mostly dependent on suggestions from the audience, prompting among other things a sketch about Rob Ford ballet dancing in a Gay Pride parade. Our Host seemed hazy as to who Mayor Ford might be, but some of his team were able to put him right; they also seem to have been in town long enough to drop the name of the Brass Rail, the strip club up the street. Anyway, it's nice of these Americans to come up to Toronto and make our local jokes for us.

And they're good at what they do, both at making up stuff and blaming it on the dolls they're holding. To appreciate their prowess fully, however, you have to devote much of your attention to the onstage video monitors that allow you to watch the puppets in close-up: another, literal, example of split focus. There's always something dispiriting about attending a live event and having to spend your time looking at a screen; and it's worse when the screens aren't commenting on the stage action but just tacitly admitting that without them you wouldn't be able see anything properly anyway.

Most striking of the cast is a long red-haired drink of strong waters identified onstage as Colleen; I can't give you her full name or those of her colleagues since the program, presumably to allow the performing help to be economically rotated, doesn't supply them. Anyway, Colleen, apart from looking good, has a wry and ready wit, exemplified by her extemporization of a song for a James Bond sketch with the suggested title of The Spy Who Bought Me Groceries. The Bond idea was a given, as presumably was the music that came with it, but the groceries were ordered on the night, and she really ran with them. The second greatest personal impression was made by an engaged couple from the audience named Chris and Barbara. Chris turned out to be an employee of Mirvish Productions, the second time I've known this to happen in a Mirvish show; maybe the staff should be demanding danger money. They watched while puppets enacted their first date, and were asked to press buttons when anything struck them as true or false. Barbara, when her surrogate declared that she was an agnostic, hesitated forever over which button to push, which was outstandingly witty.

You will guess that the comedy finally counted for more than the puppetry. A reasonable number of hits were scored, but two hours is a long time to spend watching improv, a form in which every item is arbitrarily determined and can be arbitrarily ended. You end up with an evening devoid of forward drive. And there's another thing; I haven't seen the I Love Lucy show at the Royal Alex, but it's ominous to have two Toronto stages occupied by rip-offs, I mean spin-offs, of TV shows. Still, it could be worse. Many people, I think, approach puppet shows with a certain apprehension. The cast of Puppet Up address this in their closing number. They sing (I quote from memory, but I hope not too inaccurately), "We hope that you enjoyed yourselves and had a good time/If you didn't, just be thankful that we're not a bunch of mimes". My sentiments exactly.
