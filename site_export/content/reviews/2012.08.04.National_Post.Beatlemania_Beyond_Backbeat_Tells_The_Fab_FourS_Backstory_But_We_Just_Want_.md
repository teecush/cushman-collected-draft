There's a very good scene in the second act of Backbeat in which John Lennon and Paul McCartney write a song together. Paul is singing and strumming something closely resembling the ditty later known as Love Me Do, and John happens by with some ideas on how to sex it up, both musically and lyrically. It's not unlike those scenes in old Hollywood composer biopics, where we watch a future hit being born, though the script here has rather more bite and the process does seem a bit more like hard work. Anyway, were in at the birth, not just of a hit song, but of an epochal songwriting partnership. Or are we?

Backbeat is the Beatles backstory. Most of it is set in Hamburg, where the Liverpool lads learned their trades playing punishing six-hour gigs, seven nights a week, in clubs with - we gather here - dodgy owners and dodgier clientele. The group's name and most of its personnel were already in place, but their ultimate identity, the hairstyles and clothes and the playing of their own material, had yet to emerge; they were still an R&B grunt band, grinding out sweaty, energetic covers of Chuck Berry songs. Up until the Love Me Do scene, there's been no indication - other than a few bars of I Saw Her Standing There that have shown up unexpected and unexplained - that Lennon and McCartney have had any interest in writing songs at all, though Paul now refers back wistfully to when they used to play around with the idea back in school. And after the scene, the subject of songwriting is never mentioned again. Maybe it would be dishonest to present us with an epiphanic moment at which the boys realized this was their destiny - after all, things rarely happen that neatly in real life - but in passing it up, the show may be the victim of its own integrity. That, indeed, is its overall problem.

To backtrack on Backbeat: It starts with an excessively smarmy Brian Epstein c. 1962, telling us - in a show that uses direct address intermittently and inconsistently - of this local group hes heard about and whom he has tracked down to their Cavern. He tells the foursome he wants to manage them; they can take care of their sound, hell look after their look. Oh, they say, you mean like Stu used to do? We go into flashback mode, and stay there for most of the evening.

The show isn't about John and Paul, or even about John and the other Beatles. Most of the time it's about Stuart Sutcliffe, the aspiring painter with the James Dean looks and the minimal musical talent whom Lennon persuaded to join the band as bass guitarist, apparently because he was personally obsessed with the guy and conceivably because, as a former art student himself, he both envied and resented his talent. Once theyre in Germany, Stuart does yeoman service as a chick magnet and he particularly magnetizes Astrid Kirchner, an ice-cool blond and talented photographer, taking her away from her boyfriend Klaus, self-styled original Beatles fan. Klaus takes it like a man, prophesying with as much dignity as is possible while exiting climbing a staircase that, though the first victim of Beatlemania, he will not be the last. The new relationship survives the groups temporary deportation, when the authorities discover that George Harrison is underage. Astrid, both before and after the hiatus, sets about recalling Stuart to his proper calling, and much of the show becomes an emotional tug-of-war between her and John, with Stu stewing, or smouldering, in the middle.

Sutcliffe is a moderately interesting figure. But he isn't that interesting and he wouldn't exert any fascination at all if he hadnt been a Beatle. The writing here (by Iain Softley and Stephen Jeffreys) is too generic to fill the gap; the Stuart-Astrid scenes seem to go on forever repeating the same themes, with performers (Nick Blood and Isabella Calthorpe) who are competent but hardly compelling. A long tall Lennon (Andrew Knott) makes more of an impression, though his doubtless devastating one-liners are largely drowned by his thick Scouse accent and a muddy sound system.

The show suffers from an identity crisis. Its subtitled The Birth of the Beatles, rather than The Death of Stuart Sutcliffe, and it devotes enough time to the band in performance to qualify as a musical, of the jukebox variety. However, David Leveaux' production seems unhappy about this, cutting numbers short and doing its best to deny audiences the chance to applaud. They do anyway, because they feel it's expected of them, but the upshot is frustration. Meanwhile, hanging over the entire evening is everyones foreknowledge that Stuart will die of a brain hemorrhage and that Pete Best, the bands drummer in their Hamburg days, is fated to be Ringoed. That's a double downer.

It isn't even given its full dramatic due. "Petes the drummer," says John to Astrid, "thats all you need to know about him," and the show apparently agrees. Pete as a character hardly exists until the second act when, to set him up as the sacrificial lamb, he's allowed a monologue. Back in Liverpool, and back in the show's present tense, hes fired by Epstein, his former pals too cowardly to turn up. This shabby behaviour is, I believe, historically accurate, but there must have been some discussion back at base; it could be dramatically interesting to be told why Epstein wanted him gone and shown how the others took it. Like Klaus before him, Pete (Oliver Bennett) takes his dismissal, as he says, with dignity. Of the others, George (Daniel Westwick) seems as anonymous as George in such circumstances always does; Ringo (Adam Sopp) appears, of necessity, only at the last moment but looks and sounds exactly right; and Paul (Daniel Healy) is the most interesting person onstage, partly because hes playing, not just the most talented character, but the most dedicated, the one who pushes forward the part of the story that we actually care about. He's the one who gets them their first recording gig by impressing Bert Kaempfert, who here seems as unpleasant as his middle-of-the-autobahn music always made him sound.

Staging mainly consists of characters prowling along a catwalk or climbing up and down those stairs. Within the alcove thus created, the boys play, the young Hamburgers disporting themselves in the foreground like refugees from the Berlin nightlife of 30 years earlier as depicted in revivals of Cabaret. It seems that everyone's nostalgic for Weimar Germany, even the Germans. After the curtaincall, the show jettisons its sterner principles, and gives us a mini-rock concert, with Stuart and Pete back on the bandstand. Whether this qualifies as a happy ending is anyone's guess.
