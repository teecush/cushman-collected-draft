Current crop of musicals proves lights still bright on Broadway

Wednesday, April 04, 1990

ROBERT CUSHMAN

New York NY -- BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail NEW YORK RECENTLY, COMPLETING a report on Andrew Lloyd Webber in concert, I remarked that the American musical had done itself in. This was a bit sweeping, and the current Broadway season has seen a possible resurgence. There is still the question of whether the economics can be bucked, but at least there is talent on show, both among the young and the ageing, and a satisfying number of bodies turning out to bear witness.

The surprise hit is City of Angels, which opened with a minimal advance but was turned around by a rave review in the New York Times. The Times hailed it as the funniest musical in years, which it may be. (There has hardly been much competition.) The script is by Larry Gelbart, who co- authored A Funny Thing Happened on the Way to the Forum, back when there really was such a thing as musical comedy, and was more recently responsible for the TV version of MASH.

His subject here is Hollywood: the myth and, if there is such a thing, the reality. Its the late forties, and a writer called Stine sits in L.A. working on a screenplay about a private eye called Stone, who also sits in L.A. Stine sees Stone as a lonely holdout for integrity in a corrupt world, rather like Philip Marlowe. Raymond Chandler's Marlowe, that is, not Dennis Potter's. The show might quite reasonably be called The Singing Detective, though, as it resembles the TV serial quite a lot, what with its film noir pastiches, its appearance-reality games and its pair of attendant goons threatening to beat up Stone. Come to think of it, both the games and the goons were also forecast in Kiss Me Kate.

Chandleresque narration has always been a fertile ground for parody, and Gelbart mines it richly. His wisecracking style serves him well when Stine gets into his own integrity battles. His opponents are movie moguls, after all, and we expect them to be cartoons. But when we are asked to take a serious interest in Stines love life, the writing cannot take the strain.

The entire supporting cast doubles in parallel roles, though Stone and Stine are played by separate actors. They have a duet, of course, called Youre Nothing Without Me, rather than - as might be feared - Don't Alter My Ego.

Surprisingly, this show has a British director with no track record in musicals. For once, this does not spell disaster. Michael Blakemore was probably hired for his immaculate work on Michael Frayn's equally convoluted backstage piece, Noises Off. Beyond that, he has a real flair for hard-nosed Broadway comedy (his National Theatre production of The Front Page is legendary) and an apparently equal relish for text, acting and theatrical mechanics. His work here is witty and level-headed, involving the best use I have ever seen of a live performer against mobile projections. Robin Wagners sets build up a shadowy, sinister film city before colorfully blowing their top in a climactic studio scene.

What the production has been unable to do is to integrate the script with the score. The musical parts of the story were hard to follow since David Zippels lyrics were drowned out by Billy Byers' joyful big-band orchestrations. Cy Coleman is almost my favorite Broadway composer, and I shall probably love the album, but his tunes here inhabit a world of their own, short on menace and late in date. They hail from the fifties rather than the forties, and one song might have been scored by Nelson Riddle for Frank Sinatra: a gorgeous sound but as self-contained and resistant to drama as anything in rock. Performances are disappointing as well - only James Naughton's stylish Stine has much impact. I applaud the elements if not the totality, and am glad the Times loved it, otherwise it would not have survived and I could not have seen it.

There is at least a healthy low-down Americanness about the show. The seasons other big contender, Grand Hotel, also invokes Hollywood - being a musical adaptation of a novel that everybody remembers as a movie - but is also one of Broadways recurring obeisances to the old world. Staged by Tommy Tune, in an opulent setting by Tony Walton (two tiers of old gold), it looks stunning. But you grow tired of being stunned and, in terms of narrative, nothing much happens. Or quite a lot happens but none of it seems to matter.

A couple of nice songs by the old Kismet team of Robert Wright and George Forrest prevent me from saying that the show has only one good Tune. Tommy Tune may be the last great director-choreographer on Broadway, with Bob Fosse, Michael Bennett and Gower Champion dead and Jerome Robbins retired from the fray.

City of Angels eschews dance, as do the recent Sondheim shows and the various successes of the Lloyd Webber school, Cats excepted. Story and song are back, in various combinations. Even the compilation Jerome Robbins' Broadway (now in its second year) reminds us in fractured form what a superb storyteller he was. This season has brought a full-blown revival of one of his triumphs, and one of the most dramatic musicals ever. Gypsy, directed by librettist Arthur Laurents, reinstates Robbins' choreography which boasts, in the young hoofers All I Need is the Girl, my favorite of all musical numbers: half brash, half shy. Tyne Daly, in the linchpin role of Mama Rose, lacks the bizarre grandeur of Angela Lansbury in the last revival, and she has an uphill battle with some of the songs. But her gritty determination pays off; she is a very believable theatrical mother. When Lansburys Rose said of her own performing ambitions, "If I could have been I would have been, and that's show business," it was with a resigned, healing acceptance. Daly says it with bitterness, still fighting. The end of the show, when she allows herself to be led off by the daughter who is now Gypsy Rose Lee, is not only moving but disturbing.

When at the outset I mentioned young writing talent, I spoke comparatively. The team of Richard Maltby Jr. and David Shire has been at it since the early sixties, but only a few seasons ago did they make it to Broadway together with Baby, a refreshingly humane musical about parenthood. A few off-cuts from that show, together with a raft of new songs, make up Closer Than Ever, an off-Broadway four-performer revue about life and love among the early middle-aged. They have a distinctive sound of their own, soaring and percussive, recognizable through a variety of musical styles. Maltby's lyrics are inquisitive and genuinely observant; Shires best melodies re-invent the Broadway show tune, maintaining the lift and the drive and purging the banality.

Another composer of new grown-up songs is Craig Carnelia, who, like Maltby and Shire, is a post-angst writer. (As the maestro would probably not put it, theyve been through Stephen Sondheim and they're here.) His music and lyrics were the best things in two unsuccessful Broadway shows, Working and Is There Life After High School. His songs generally sound like pieces of his own or someone else's autobiography, and this retrospective mode may limit him, but within it he demonstrates a rare combination of delicacy and strength. He showed his wares, under the title Pictures in the Hall, at a Greenwich Village cabaret called Eighty Eight's in company with his wife, an actress-singer named Maureen Silliman. She made one of his confessionals, Just a Housewife, an eye-piercing mixture of pride and heartbreak. Carnelia's taste - as lyricist, composer and husband - is impeccable. Remember his name.
