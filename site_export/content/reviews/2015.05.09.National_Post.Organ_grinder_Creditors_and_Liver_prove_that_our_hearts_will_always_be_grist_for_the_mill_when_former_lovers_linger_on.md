Two independent theatre spaces with impressive track records - the Coal Mine on the Danforth and Storefront Theatre on Bloor West - are currently adding to them.

I do have a pedantic bone to pick with the Coal Mine's production of August Strindberg's Creditors, or at least with its publicity. It has been announced as "Strindberg's least produced play." You could have fooled me. It may not turn up on our stages as often as Miss Julie (thank God) and it may not have the textbook prestigeof The Father or The Dance of Death, though I've yet to see either of those in Toronto. But I have seen Creditors here before, and it's also had a strong presence in London and New York. Meanwhile, there are dozens of Strindberg plays that never get done anywhere, except presumably in Sweden.

Creditors is a short, intense and devilishly assembled piece that administers its author's obsessions in a concentrated dose. There are three characters: Tekla, a novelist; Adolph, who is her husband; and Gustav, who used to be. The play, set in a hotel, consists of three duologues. In the first, Gustav systematically poisons Adolph's mind against Tekla, who has been off on a junket somewhere, surrounded - as Gustav is at pains to point out - by adoring young men. Adolph does not know who Gustav is, and neither at this point do we, at least in theory. But it's easy for us to guess, and dramatically more effective as well. When Tekla returns, Gustav makes himself scarce, but he can hear from offstage the destruction he has wrought. Exhausted from quarrelling with his wife Adolph, who wasn't too well to begin with, leaves the room while Gustav re-enters it. Tekla, after the initial shock, is very pleased to see him; a rapprochement seems imminent. Adolph, too, has overheard; and for a brief, drastic moment all three are onstage together.

The adaptation, by the Scottish playwright David Greig, plays well, as does Rae Ellen Bodie's tense and intense production. It may, by its author's own feverish lights, go too easy on Tekla and too hard on Gustav. Gustav voices Strindbergian misogyny at full alarming strength. The possibility of infidelity is the least of the charges that he, in her absence, levels at Tekla. He persuades Adolph that she has emasculated him. He is an artist, and she's responsible for his doing sissy stuff like painting, instead of masculine forms like sculpture (though Gustav denounces that as old-fashioned as well); she has drained his talent to feed her own feeble efforts; she may even have brought him to epilepsy. Her pet name for him - "little brother" - says everything.

The scary and powerful and, yes, funny thing about all this is that Strindberg actually believed it, as a general truth about men and women; scarier and funnier still is that he wrote the role of Tekla for his own wife and that she played it. It wasn't all he believed; he did have the genius to recognize his own self-pity, but that never stopped him indulging it.

Gustav's assault on Adolph is comparable to Iago's on Othello, with Tekla cast as a combination of Desdemona and Emilia; jealous revenge is both the motive and the weapon. Like Iago, Gustav works by insinuation, but Hardee T. Lineham delivers his attack as a series of hammerblows; it certainly has impact but it dulls with repetition.

He is more effective with Tekla, with whom he has to be subtler and to whom, it seems, he nearly succumbs. She is, at least in part, the manipulator whom he accuses her of being, but Liisa Repo-Martell downplays that aspect of her. She has a hypnotic presence, though, and the moment at which she catches on to what Gustav is up to is a genuine game-changer.

Noah Reid has maybe the hardest task, Adolph being more acted-upon than active; but there can be passion in passivity and he thrillingly finds it.

Tekla and Gustav, though probably not Adolph, might agree with a proposition advanced in Kat Sandler's play Liver: that "hell is just your ex being immortal". (It beats Sartre.) Sandler's title, like much else in her play, is doubleedged; the liver in question has been surgically removed, with no replacement, from a man who nonetheless lives. So, has he come back from the dead and, perhaps more importantly, does he deserve to? He is, as those around him never tire of reminding him, an unlikely messiah: a drunken, philandering, sentimental slob, who never apologizes; he's also apparently rich, though this is only credible if the money were inherited rather than earned. As played by Shaun Benson, he's hard to stomach and impossible to forget.

The author directs her own play, with immaculate comic and dramatic timing. Sean Sullivan plays a surgeon with a devotion to research, and his own ego, far in excess of what would generally be regarded as ethics; this in a play that makes explicit reference to Josef Mengele and even lists some of his Auschwitz experiments. Then there's Katelyn McCulloch in the generic role of the surgeon's assistant's girlfriend (well, he thinks she's his girlfriend) who is curious to see a dead body and even more curious when she finds it isn't.

All these performances are fine but better still are the two who seem to speak for the author. Michael Musi is the surgical apprentice, an elongated Woody Allen figure in scrubs or a Hazmat suit, standing awkwardly up for what is good and right. Cautioning him from a slightly older perspective is the non-deceased's happily separated spouse, played by Claire Armstrong whom I have now seen give three first-rate performances at the Storefront, all different.

The play has no dull moments, and though it doesn't fully engage with all the issues it raises, it doesn't demean them either. It's also full of firecracker dialogue; I forget which character says "you don't get special treatment just because you're dead" but in context it's a great line.

Creditors runs through May 17 at the Coal Mine Theatre; Liver runs through May 16 at the Storefront Theatre.
