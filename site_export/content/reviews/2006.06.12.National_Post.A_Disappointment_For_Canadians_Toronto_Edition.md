Well, The Drowsy Chaperone won a lot of things -- more than any other musical this season -- but it didnt win the Best Musical Tony. That went to Jersey Boys, the jukebox musical about Frankie Valli and the Four Seasons. Jersey Boys, as brash as The Drowsy Chaperone is wide-eyed, arrived at the Tonys as joint favourite for Best Musical Award. Both, though, came in as underdogs, or at least as outsiders.

Jersey Boys ends with its four heroes being inducted into the Rock 'n' Roll Hall of Fame and affirming that because it comes from the people, this award counts for more than anything at the Oscars or the Emmys. They dont mention the Tonys. That could have been tact on the writers part. It could have been superstition. Either way, it seems to have paid off.

It, too, had a Canadian component in the shape of its director, Des McAnuff (well, Canadian-raised; he works out of California). He didn't win, but John Lloyd Young as Frankie Valli won for Best Actor in a Musical and Christian Hoff as his cohort Tony de Vito for Best Supporting Actor.

Those last two prizes were warning signs that the Chaperone, unlike in the show itself, was going to end up as the bridesmaid and not the bride. It was a disappointment for reasons that go beyond local pride and national history. It has to do with Bob Martin's starring role as the droll narrator identified in the program as Man in Chair, a rrole for which he had received a best actor nomination. "Theres a certain heart to the show -- people identify with the Man in Chair," said Martin, after picking up the Tony that he did win for Best Book of a Musical, at least partially on behalf of "that group of people who create musical-parodies in Toronto." Don McKellar, his co-author and co-winner, reinforced the point that the show, in effect, makes Canadians of the entire theatre audience. That is to say, all the people who sit at musicals, gawping at the glamour and wishing that life were more like them (the musicals, that is, not the people), find a surrogate in the onstage musical- theatre nerd who watches a show coming to life in front of him, and ends by becoming part of it.

The Chaperone did win for its writing, not just the book but for the score by Lisa Lambert and Greg Morrison. Casey Nicholaw, the shows U.S. director and choreographer, surprisingly failed to win in either category, but the piece's stylish look did get celebrated in prizes for sets (David Gallo) and costumes (Gregg Barnes). So both the show's Toronto origins and its New York makeover got recognition.

The national group that really cleaned up was the British, largely on account of one superb show. Alan Bennett's play The History Boys, in a National Theatre production, won its expected prizes for best play, best direction (Nicholas Hytner), best actor (Richard Griffiths), best supporting actress (Frances de la Tour) and best designer (Bob Crowley) while John Doyle won for best director of a musical for his barebones actors-as-musicians Sweeney Todd and Ian McDiarmid for best supporting performance in Faith Healer.

Otherwise, a richly deserved Lifetime Achievement Award went to Hal Prince, though no one thought to mention that two of this year's three Best Musical Revival nominees were Tony-award winning shows that he helped originate: The Pajama Game and Sweeney Todd.
