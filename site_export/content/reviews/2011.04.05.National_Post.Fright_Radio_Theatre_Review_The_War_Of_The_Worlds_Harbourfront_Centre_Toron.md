The Art of Time Ensemble starts with music and doesn't stop. Its contribution to this year's World Stage season was a doubleheader of music and drama: the first half a concert, the second a play.

It was first planned, as the ensemble's director and conductor Andrew Burashko informed us, as a centenary tribute to the American composer Bernard Herrmann, best known for his classic movie scores. Herrmann worked with Orson Welles in his infant prodigy days; he was the musical director for Welles' New York radio shows before taking off with him to Hollywood. It seemed a reasonable assumption, then, that Herrmann had composed the music for Welles' most notorious radio project: the 1938 adaptation of H.G. Wells' The War of the Worlds that sent New Yorkers and New Jerseyites screaming out of their homes, convinced that the Martians were coming for them. Or so the legend goes.

Unfortunately, Herrmann's participation as composer proved on examination to be another legend. He did indeed conduct the CBS orchestra on the show, but it was playing other people's tunes. The Art of Time musicians did the same in the evening's second half, which was a re-enactment of the original script, read and performed by three actors in a mock-up of a radio studio.

It was preceded by a more formal composer tribute. The fearsome number of consonants in Herrmann's name seems to have been exceeded only by the quantity of famous films for which he provided scary scores; he was Hitchcock's favourite composer as well as Welles'. Dan Parr assembled some 20 of these themes into a composition entitled Herrmannthology, which the orchestra played while we watched a seamlessly synchronized montage of corresponding clips.

Back from our drinks for the main event, we discovered Nicholas Campbell as a grizzled seen-it-all actor, Marc Bendavid as a young eager-toplease actor and Don McKellar, whose name was Orson Welles. McKellar, Canadian drama's go-to guy for playing egomaniacal directors, presented this one as a self-satisfied prankster who devoted as much energy to strolling around the set exchanging gleeful winks with his collaborators as he did to his official duties of acting, announcing and seat-ofthe-pants directing -which, in this context, bore a marked resemblance to conducting.

In 1938, radio drama was done live, and McKellar left us in no doubt that Welles really dug the power involved. As is well known, Welles' War of the Worlds told the story as a succession of news bulletins, as if the invasion by aliens were happening then and (if you lived in New Jersey) there. To preserve the illusion, the announcer kept returning the audience to "our scheduled programming," which consisted of ballroom music, performed by "Ramon Raquello and his orchestra," who were impersonated by the Herrmann herd (herrd?), who were impersonated by the Burashko bunch.

Question: Was the original audience really fooled, accustomed as they must have been to tuning in every week at this time to hear Welles' Mercury Theatre of the Air? Didn't they find it suspiciously coincidental that the tunes selected by Senor Raquello -Stardust, I'm Always Chasing Rainbows -all had extraterrestrial connotations? Even taking the legend at face value, one obvious thing missing from the meticulous re-enactment onstage was a counterpoint narrative about the audience response.

Another thing was the efforts of the CBS brass to get Welles to come clean while the show was on the air and his refusal to oblige. These after all, rather than the show itself, are what have fascinated people down the decades and why we turned up for it now. We did get some inkling of it from Campbell's restrained, wearied rendition of the scientist, and narrating survivor, Professor Pierson, making us suspend two distinct levels of disbelief.

Welles apparently played this role himself, but McKellar principally confined himself to looking devilishly pleased at wreaking all that havoc. Or maybe he just enjoyed playing with all those toys. He certainly had a marvellous collaborator in the show's fourth performer, and real star, John Gzowski. Usually just an invisible name in the program, affixed to a sound-design credit, Gzowski obviously relished his time in full view, surrounded by a battery of homemade devices for answering every challenge the script could throw at him and delighting in employing them. When I was at the BBC, eons ago, the official description for this job was "spot effects." Gzowski has the grander title of "foley artist." But he deserves it.
