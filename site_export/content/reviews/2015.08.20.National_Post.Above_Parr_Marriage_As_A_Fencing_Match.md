THEATRE REVIEW

The Last Wife Stratford Festival

'Six wives said Charles Laughton in the final frame of The Private Life of Henry VIII, and the last one is the worst of the lot." In that funny and fanciful movie, Katherine Parr, the queen in question, figures rather briefly as a combination of nursemaid and nag. Kate Hennigs play The Last Wife, which is also funny and fanciful as well as entertaining and absorbing, is devoted to proving that there was much more to her in addition to showing her perform those same two functions.

Katherine - known to her husband as Parr and to the program as Kate - wins some of the kings confidence by regularly dressing a suppurating legulcer that he is otherwise too proud, or too image-conscious, to acknowledge. As for the other thing: Hennig's script is not so much in modern English as in modern American; "nag is not a word that anyone in the play actually uses, but it's easy to tell that its what some of them, the male contingent, are thinking.

Kate is a woman who knows what she wants and sets about getting it, sometimes by direct confrontation, sometimes by clever strategy. When we first meet her, shes a young widow-to-be on whom the king has already made a move. She also has a lover-in-waiting, the courtier Thom Seymour, brother of the Jane Seymour who died in childbirth as opposed to being divorced or beheaded like Henry's other exes. Both suitors are waiting impatiently for Kate's husband to croak (they say hes 70; she counters that hes 49); when he does she makes the smart, indeed the inescapable, royal choice.

She offers to be the king's mistress, but he says that he can no longer perform out of wedlock. So she insists on a pre-nup: there shall be no sex unless she initiates it, something that later turns out to be a powerful political arrow in her quiver; and she shall supervise the education of the king's two daughters and one son, each the child of a different mother and each destined in turn to assume the throne.

The boy the play calls Eddie was always going to succeed his father as Edward VI, but it took an Act of Succession to extend the same dynastic privilege to his older half-sisters Mary and Bess, later to be famous as Bloody and Good Queen respectively. Its their stepmother Kate who pushes through this legislation, or persuades the king to. She also persuades him to appoint her regent of England while hes on his last campaign in France which, in the face of an allmale Privy Council, must have taken some doing.

What she cannot do is have him decree that this position shall endure after his death. The political reasons for this are left unclear; we have to put it down to Henry's mercurial feelings about this most capable of wives. He both respects and suspects her, both loves and resents.

And she, it seems, both fears and cares for him. There's a moment in their first interview when Maev Beatys Kate stands with her eyebrows raised, her chin drawn tight, wondering what she has got herself into and how she can survive it. We can see her mind work, and it works very fast. A second later, she is verbally fencing, volatile on the surface, steely underneath. The wittiest actress of her (under-forty) generation is playing a woman who has to live by her wits, and her humane good sense, and her sexuality. The results are superb, not a word or a move or a pause out of place. A star is born. Or, more accurately, confirmed.

Joseph Ziegler must be the only Henry VIII in theatrical history not to knock himself out trying to look like a bloated Holbein portrait, and not just because the production is in modern dress. Nor is he only a ruthless sexist pig. Hes other things as well: intelligent and artistic and with a special, dangerous kind of vulnerability, the kind that does monstrous things for fear of losing face or being taken advantage of. This king and queen make great fencing partners.

So, what's not to like? That's actually a line from the play, and it sort of answers its own question. Nobody wants a history play to sound archaic, but this script makes a point of being aggressively anachronistic. Hey, that was fun," says Kate, making a rapid post-coital exit from Thoms bed. Henry refers to the likelihood of the fat lady singing. The line that really crosses the line is "Im a conformist - that makes me a fascist, not a liberal." The audience apparently can't be trusted to make its own connections. The play comes to seem less like the author's idea of what really happened than her fantasy of what ought to have happened.

The writing is certainly racy, in the sense of both pungency and speed, and Alan Dilworths production puts it across with admirable clarity and economy. Yannik Larivees set sneaks in a couple of cheeky homages to the historical period: a miniature palace, probably Hampton Court, hangs upside down over the scene, and there's an actual throne, sparingly but powerfully used. Other furnishings are purposefully nondescript.

The scenes around the Tudor family dinner-table are a hoot. The princesses are arrestingly written and played. Sara Farb's Mary is all scarcely bottled resentment, Bahia Watsons Bess is a cheeky teen in the process of growing up. Eddie, the precocious boy-king who never got the chance to grow up, is played Jonas Q. Gribble, an exceptionally palatable and intelligent child actor. As his uncle Thom, Gareth Potter does well though he gets left behind by the plays secondact gallop to keep up with history.

Religious tensions are remarkably scanted, considering that this is a play about the Reformation and its aftermath. But Hennig, a fine actress as well as a promising playwright, is planning two further plays on the reigns of Mary and Elizabeth, so chances are she'll get to it.

In repertory as part of the Stratford Festival until Oct. 7.
