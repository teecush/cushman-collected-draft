So I was eating a bowl of pasta in a downtown restaurant, pretty much blotting out the seasonal music that was oozing out of the speakers like aural spaghetti sauce, when a song that clearly wasn't Winter Wonderland or the one about chestnuts roasting on an open fire made its way through the miasma and pinned my attention. It was, I realized, a song I dimly recalled, titled The Man with the Bag-- Santa Claus, of course -- and sung, I was fairly sure, by Kay Starr. I hate not being sure of these things.

And then, half an hour later, I was sitting in the auditorium of the smallest space of the Harbourfront Centre, waiting for the start of The Stronger Variations presented by the movement-based group Theatre Rusticle, and expecting something stern and experimental. As the lights went down, the first thing we heard was another selection of pretty holiday music, starting with that very same Man with the Bag, and this time I was certain it was Kay Starr. Which was not only serendipitous but proved what turned out to be one of the production's central points; which is that, for better or worse, at this time of the year you cannot get away from commercialized Christmas spirit. You can't even escape it in an extended, and brilliantly executed, piece of movement-theatre based on a play by August Strindberg.

It's actually inherent in what Strindberg wrote, just begging to be used and embroidered. His one-act play The Stronger is a two- hander for a wife and a mistress. You can't call it a duologue because the wife does all the talking. It's Christmas Eve, she's been shopping and she drops into a cafe where she spies the woman whom she suspects, probably rightly, of sleeping with her husband. The song Guess Who I Saw Today? is not on this show's soundtrack, but you can imagine the wife returning home, greeting her spouse and giving that number's punchline a new twist by saying or singing "guess who I saw today? I saw her."

You might also say that the wife is following her shopping expedition with a fishing expedition. She talks about her home and family, commiserates with the other woman (or the Other Woman) on her singleness. And it's left to the audience to decide which of the two characters, the talker or the silent one, is actually "the stronger." It's notable, though, that star actresses have traditionally preferred to play the mistress, maybe not just because they didn't want to be bothered with learning lines.

Theatre Rusticle's adaptation, directed by Allyson McKinnon, started out on the Toronto fringe with three women -- Liza Balkan, Viv Moore, Lucy Rupert -- sharing and alternating the roles of wife and mistress. For this new and elongated version they are joined by three men -- Patrick Conner, Misha Albert, Matthew Romantini --playing husbands and lovers. The portions of text we hear (they're too substantial to be called fragments) seem to work equally well within and across gender lines.

The show begins with the sexy sextet, in a line as if on a moving platform, sidling and twisting on, in time with the happy music. They're dressed in time to it as well; in 1950s suburban happy gear, the men in casual black and white and the women sporting a lot of pink. They seem to have stepped out of the same scrubbed suburbia as the corps in the Radio City Christmas Spectacular, and the two shows in fact present rather similar world views with overlapping musical scores, except that the show at the Hummingbird offers it in all seriousness while this one up-ends it.

The same words, carefully lifted from the original, recur in duologue after duologue. Each gets started with a cheery reminiscence of an ill-omened wedding party at which bride and groom were plainly bored with one another before the marriage had even started. There are barbed condolences about loneliness

that cut both ways. Each shopper flourishes the gifts he or she has bought for the family; always it's a doll for the girl of the family ("It's for my daughter," says Wife No. 1, as if taking up position in a territorial dispute), a knife for the boy and a pair of slippers decorated with tulips for one's partner. Each of these props takes on a significance farcical or sinister or both. (One of the men screams that he's gone right off tulips.) The pattern doesn't stand quite as many variations as it gets, but it does stand up to most of them.

The performers move superbly, speak at least acceptably and even sing a bit, though most of the music, as noted, comes pre- performed. It isn't there, by the way, just to be mocked; it has its own presence, and even its own dignity. When the voice of Bing Crosby chimes

authoritatively in Count Your Blessings Instead of Sheep, it's impossible not to feel that he has a point, even if -- especially if -- it's one that the characters on stage would find it hard to appreciate. When the three guys glide to the Christmas Waltz, there is a kind of impossible grace to it, however mordant the context. And What Are You Doing New Year's Eve? -- which works cleverly in the Radio City show as a solicitous duet for Mr. and Mrs. Santa Claus -- works even better here as a bittersweet sword to be flourished over the head of someone who'll be spending it alone. (The record they've chosen, by Nancy Wilson, is especially good.) Altogether this is a rich show, it may be worth repeat visits. It could become an annual anti-Christmas staple.

Tony Nardi is an actor best known to Toronto theatre audiences for his work with Soulpepper, for whom he gave notable performances in A Flea in Her Ear, The Winter's Tale, Miss Julie (Strindberg again) and The Lesson. He is also in great demand on television, often for playing Italians, or anyway Italian- Canadians. His dissatisfaction with the ethnic stereotyping to which he has found himself subject, occasioned the first of his Two Letters, extended solo rants (that's a description, not a judgment) that he has been performing in various spots around town over the past month or two. I saw and heard the second Letter, which is focused more on theatre than on TV, and which arose from a long letter he sent some Toronto

theatre critics after a production -- in which he did not appear - - of a Goldoni play he felt both they and the director had misunderstood, largely because of their shared ignorance of the Italian commedia tradition from which it sprung.

It --Nardi's performance, not the Goldoni production -- was quite the firework display. There was plenty in it with which to quibble. It went, in its earlier stages, much too fast, as if the actor were nervous of holding the audience's attention with a script he'd written himself. It kept referring back to friends and advisors -- "ghosts" he called them -- who had obviously figured prominently in the first letter, but who remained a mystery to me. It kept going off on tangents; some of these were explosively enjoyable but at others I found myself putting my attention on hold, trusting it would turn itself back on when Tony returned to his main point. (It did.)

The point itself seemed to amount to a fairly standard actors' complaint to the effect that most directors are not to be trusted, and should not be allowed to come between the actors and the play. This is true, and some of Nardi's recreations of the despairing anecdotes with which actors regale one another are hilarious, but it remained unclear exactly what he intended to do about it. Nor am I sure that he did wisely to pin his whole argument on one production, or that he was correct to speak consistently of "Goldoni" and " commedia dell'arte" as if they were synonymous.

I raised this last point at a question- and-answer section after the show. He continued to disagree, and I confess that he convinced me he knew more about both subjects than I did. In fact, he'd already proved it, not by telling but by showing. The final section of his Letter was a kind of dream sequence in which a teacher of commedia went on trial. Nardi jumped between judge, defendant and other participants, bringing stock characters to dizzying life at a pace that now seemed the product of inspiration rather than panic. It was planned and written, but it had the manic flavour of improvisation. I think it was commedia; it was certainly virtuoso. If he wants to help our theatre and himself -- well, as that other commedia descendant Mr. Punch used to say -- that's the way to do it.

- The Stronger Variations ends tonight at the Studio Theatre, Harbourfront Centre, 416-973- 4000. Two Letters is being performed for the benefit of the Actors' Fund on Dec. 19 and 20 at the Factory Theatre Rehearsal Hall (3rd floor), 416-504-9971.
