GRACE

Theatre Centre, Toronto

The first scene of Grace is set on a bus. Two male passengers sit side by side, in uncomfortable proximity.

One is a florid middle-aged businessman, suited and briefcased. The other is in his early twenties and has the spikiest of hairstyles. As you would expect in a play, one intimidates the other. As you might not expect, it's the apparently straight guy who puts on the pressure. Its the young punk whos quiet and embarrassed, and wants to be left alone. The term "straight" proves (and remains) ambiguous, since the older man tries to pick up the younger; he even seems to be having some success, but it doesn't work out.

The tension in this apparently self-contained episode is remarkable, and it goes way beyond the weary conventions of theatrical menace. When the scene ends we still know few facts about the participants, but their encounter has had extraordinary resonance. Later we will learn a lot more, and will be able to see just how much the actors have had to draw on. For the moment all we can tell is that these characters have roots, and that they go deep.

Written by Michael Lewis MacLennan and directed by Franco Boni, Grace is a superb feat of dramatic orchestration. It unfolds through a further series of duologues. There are half-a-dozen characters; and they turn up, over a dramatic time of 24 hours, in almost every possible combination. There is only one re-match, included because the action would be frustratingly incomplete without it; otherwise we can follow the fortunes of the offstage characters from glancing references that accumulate as the day wears on.

The play has already been seen in Victoria, Vancouver and Calgary (not to mention London) and MacLennan has apparently rewritten and re-located it for each gig. This sounds opportunistic, but in Toronto at least it's been subtly and effectively done.

With only one mention of a specific location, the play still reeks of this city, especially of the run-down sections a short streetcar ride from downtown: the park thats both a haven and a threat, the draughty and deserted churches, the bars and coffee- houses where aspiring poets audition their wares, the rooms where people die. (Seeing the play out on Queen West helps.)

Compare this to the previous nights opening, The Lady Smith, up to here with local references but remaining obstinately in limbo.

I don't want to put anybody off, but the publicity claims that the re-writing allows the play to focus on brand-name Toronto problems: homelessness, spousal abuse, mental health and gay- bashing.

Why anybody would want to market the play as an identity kit issue-piece is beyond me, especially as it isn't about any of these things. They do all crop up, and none of them feels dragged in; they are things that happen to these characters, but they dont define these characters.

What does define them is desperation, past or present or both, experienced with varying degrees of intensity on a scale whose apex is self-destruction. Of the two men on the bus, Hugh the businessman has just been fired (he says, repeatedly and probably truthfully, that public transit is a novelty for him) and is also divorced. Jared, the punk, mourns the death of a caring lover whom he fears he didnt help enough; in the course of the day he will try prostitution as a means of survival (and will flatten his hair). Subsequently we encounter Lonnie, a radio professional who has just now left her husband and is at a loss where to turn, either physically or emotionally; Thomas, young and mentally ill, who carries a snake around with him in a box and attends a day-centre where he distrusts all his fellow-inmates; Ruth, a widow who looks homeless but isn't; and Paula, an aspiring poet and fully paid-up fantasist.

At least one of the six is a candidate for suicide, and one plot strand involves somebody who has recently committed it. Altogether it's a hot topic; we hear about a woman who spent three hours talking a man out of throwing himself off a bridge; she is now a local celebrity, known as "Our Lady of the Bridge" and with her own Good Samaritan phone-in show. Most of the sextet are skeptical about her, but the play moves to show its people helping, even saving, one another, and in so doing saving themselves.

For the most part, they spare our blushes by doing it tentatively. They can also, though none of them is malevolent, hurt one another; a frightened Paula does it violently to Hugh, Lonnie does it more subtly to Paula.

The most active agent of salvation, therefore the trickiest role, is Ruth, whose solo addresses to her unlamented but unforgotten husband not only infringe the convention but constitute the only soggy writing in the piece. Anne Anglin plays her much of the time as a standard mumbling bag-lady, but she rallies at the end with unsentimental benevolence; when Thomas (an innocent in Anthony McLeans performance) complains of being labelled a retard, she says crisply "thats why you're there."

Ralph Small's Hugh has the weariness and the turbulence of a full- blown tragic hero; Caroline Gillis, a ubiquitous actress, does her best work yet as Lonnie, her tartness and her terror all of a piece; Marjory Chan is piercingly accurate as Paula; and as Jared, Dylan Trowbridge is, in both feeling and technique, the plays title made flesh.

The script is frequently funny, and so pliant and unforced that one soon gives it mental permission to go where it likes for as long as it likes. In fact, this indulgence turns out to be unnecessary; as the permutations unfold, the structure is revealed to be extremely tight, themes and motifs echoing from one encounter to another. We are watching a connected world, where coincidence becomes a symbol of community. (Its like Six Degrees of Separation, laid out as a map.)

Boni's production taps, lightly but firmly, all the strengths of both the writing and the performers. In a season typified, with a few shining exceptions, by clumsy direction of half-formed texts, Grace is a blessing.
