THE CORONATION VOYAGE

Festival Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

A kind of history was made last Saturday. The Shaw Festival's main stage was given over, for the first time, to a play by a living playwright. Not only living but Canadian; and French-Canadian at that.

It wasn't, strictly speaking, a new play. Michel Marc Bouchard's The Coronation Voyage has been around since 1995, during which time it has been widely produced in both French and English. It sits well on the festival stage; it has the size and the assurance. It's a big play. It's even, some of the time, a very good play; but only some of the time.

It's set in 1953, on board a liner travelling from Montreal to Liverpool, with a complement of passengers bound for the crowning of Elizabeth II. We focus on two families. One consists of a Montreal mafioso known to us only as the Chief and his two sons, Etienne and Sandro, each born of a different mother. The Chief is escaping the country, with official connivance; and he and his boys will assume new identities when they get to the other side.

The second group contains Monsieur Gendron, a government minister, his wife, Alice, and their daughter Marguerite, all holding good seats for Westminster Abbey. There is a link between the two parties. Marguerite is a pianist, and will be playing Chopin at a gala concert in London. She has inherited this gig from Etienne, a musical prodigy whose career was ended when two men from a rival mob, by way of putting the frighteners on his old man, slammed the piano lid on the boy's fingers. The Chief now faces a threat to his other, younger, son; the equally unnamed Diplomat who holds the family's new passports, and hence their fates, in his hands -- or, more likely, in his stateroom safe -- refuses to surrender them until the Chief grants him a night in bed with Sandro, who is 13.

When I first saw the play, in Vancouver in 2000, the audience seemed to take this plot development in stride. In Niagara, it produced shock waves more palpable than any I have felt in a theatre since the night the world first heard about the blinding of the horses in Equus.

So what, theatrically speaking, are all these people doing in the same boat? Bouchard is obviously too serious to have thrown them together just for kicks, Grand Hotel style. There must be some symbolic intent in presenting us with a collection of Quebecers, en route to the ultimate Anglophile celebration. The ministerial party makes this stick. Gendron himself, in thrall to a government he more or less despises, is torn between loyalty (or, of course, careerism) and self-loathing. His wife has already made her choice; she lost three sons on the Dieppe beaches in 1942: All of them, she insists, sacrificed to the ambitions of the English. She has a lacerating speech in which she puts her view of the case while her husband, rather feebly, pushes the official line about standing by the allies.

Also travelling first class (this is not an economy play) are three over-the-moon young ladies on this trip of a lifetime after winning a competition for girls named Elizabeth. They and their attendant protocol officer (amusingly played by Glynis Ranney) provide comic relief that is genuinely funny, but they also have an obvious relevance to the play's treatment of what might be called patriotism-once-removed. The themes come together when the three tin Lizzies are taken painfully through the etiquette of the royal curtsey, with the minister's daughter standing in as queen.

Apart from being very entertaining in itself, this rehearsal proves a catalyst in the relationship of Marguerite's parents. It's a fine scene, finely acted. Donna Belleville is stylishly sozzled as the angry Alice; and though it seems odd to cast a so emphatically Anglo actor as David Schurmann in the role of the minister, he plays it very well; nobody, indeed, does world-weariness better.

But that leaves the other half of the play unaccounted for; and what the Chief and his sons have to do with the state of Quebec or of Canada, or anyway with their relation to the mother country, is beyond me. They do provide a link to another of the play's concerns, but it isn't a happy one. "Historians," intones the blackmailing Diplomat (who, as played by Peter Krantz, intones practically everything), "always disagree on one point: history." He's such a wit. We've seen a sort of illustration of his thesis in the discussion of the disaster at Dieppe, but it isn't really all that much of a disagreement. Nobody gives Alice an argument as to what actually happened; they merely differ over whether it can be justified.

All right; now get this. The Chief has brought along a tame Biographer to write his memoirs (i.e.: to tell his story to the world in the way he would like it to be believed). When the Chief finds himself entrapped by the Diplomat, the Biographer recites, in counterpoint, the ways in which the Chief might deal with the situation if he were still the Chief. The world probably divides between those who find this kind of thing profound, and those who just find it profoundly tiresome. It isn't an effective dramatization of two levels of reality, because only one of them gets dramatized. It's a single conventional action that keeps getting interrupted so that we may be told, over and over, that one of the characters would like things to be different.

It's probably not the actors' fault, but whenever I've seen this piece the Biographer has established himself, very quickly, as the character I would most like to see dropped overboard. Apart from being irritating in itself, his running commentary has the inevitable effect of knocking all the dramatic stuffing out of the Chief. I have a horrible feeling that this may be intentional, but I'd still rather watch The Sopranos. The Chief is a hollow man, striking attitudes. He still has to present an iron facade; and for that Jim Mezon, who plays him, is certainly your man. But he could do this role in his sleep.

Jackie Maxwell's production has an imposing air. Ken MacDonald has designed the kind of big-boat setting on which you keep expecting the characters to break into a number (and the way they're posed at the beginning, they look as though they very well might). The actors' delivery could have been stamped by the Department of Weights and Measures: It's very weighty and extremely measured. It's quite a surprise, on reading the published text, to discover that though Linda Gaboriau's translation may sometimes be stiff, it still bears a marked resemblance to normal conversation. There is a theoretical point in jacking up the style of the performance to match the heartlessness of the action, but it isn't sustained. The production, like Marti Maraden's three years ago, goes soft on us, mainly in the scenes between the embittered Etienne and the embarrassed Marguerite.

Dylan Trowbridge and Susie Burnett act as if they can't believe the awful things that are coming out of their mouths; instead of inhabiting their lines, they apologize for them. She is better in her scenes with her family, but he is equally strained when confronting his father, maybe because his antagonist is less a person than an idea.

Jeff Lillico's Sandro, however, is very good, and the more heartbreaking for being the one character who is permitted, from time to time, to be cheerful. And the play's ending is superb: a reverberating denouement with an innocently implacable smile on its lips. The sins of the fathers are visited upon the fathers, or at least on those who don't know how to atone. One in the play does, the other doesn't. It's a semi-fearful symmetry.
