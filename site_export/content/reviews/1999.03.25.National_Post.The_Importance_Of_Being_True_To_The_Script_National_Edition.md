The Importance of Being Earnest

Grand Theatre, London, Ont.

You know you're in trouble when, a few minutes after the rise of the curtain, the manservant perches confidentially on the arm of the sofa on which his employer happens to be sitting. The flunky in question is called Lane and he works for Algernon Moncrieff, one of the two young men-about-town who drive the action of Oscar Wilde's The Importance of Being Earnest. For him to take such a liberty is not only unlikely in the social context of late-Victorian London, it is totally at odds with the way the scene is written. Algy, with no malice whatsoever, regards Lane -- and the working classes generally -- as a remote species, created to minister to his needs and to furnish material for philosophic speculation: "Really," he says, "if the lower orders don't set us a good example, what on Earth is the use of them?" This may not be democratically acceptable, but it is funny. It also sets up the whole world of the play -- a hermetically enclosed one, whose rules go unquestioned by all its inhabitants.

At this early stage, one suspects the director, Michael Shamata, of not knowing what he is doing. By the end of the first act -- in which he shows us master and man taking a glass together in even more flagrant contradiction of the text -- it is plain that he does know but that what he is doing is almost totally wrong-headed. His Algy, hob-nobbing with the help, is symptomatic of a desire to make all the characters -- with one important exception -- terribly nice. This -- I'm cheating by paraphrasing the program-notes -- is because they are in love: Algy loves Cecily, the niece and ward of his friend Jack Worthing, while Jack loves Algy's cousin Gwendolen. And the girls love them, but principally because each has got hold of the idea that her young man's name is Ernest, and the men are prepared to go to desperate lengths to maintain this illusion. In fact, each of the characters is a monster of egotism, and the fact of their being in love only intensifies each one's monomania. (This is psychologically very sound.) That doesn't make them unsympathetic; they are in love, and that in itself will have any audience rooting for them. We don't have to soften them as well.

The generic name for a play in which the characters' drives propel them into absurdity is farce. The Importance is the greatest English farce, but it's an unusual one in which the pratfalls are not physical but verbal. It is, in fact, a farce of manners -- a form that Wilde seems to have invented and in which only Shaw and Stoppard have to some extent followed him.

Let's forget about interpretation; the task of any production is to work out where the laughs are and then get them. In practical terms, this means wrapping your tongue around the sentences. There are great schemes afoot, on both sides of the Atlantic, for helping actors to speak verse. It strikes me that it may be just as urgent to teach them how to speak prose -- perhaps more urgent given that it's the medium in which most good plays have been written for the last 200 years.

In this production, a sentimental conception is compounded by inexpert execution. What are we to make of a Gwendolen (Lisa Waines) who, when she speaks of her own sweetness of manner and gentleness of disposition, seems to want us to take the description at face value? Cecily (Deborah Drakeford) is equally callow but, she being the youngest character, somewhat more acceptable.

Jack (John Ullyatt) has some of the determination proper to this country squire -- especially when it comes to his anguished complaint about Algy having "stayed to tea and devoured every single muffin" -- but none of the weight; he actually seems younger than his friend, which destroys both the logic of the plot and the balance of the dialogue. Patrick Galligan's Algy, a silvery aesthete, is by far the best of the quartet and the only one with a sense of pace, though it often goes to his head and turns into mere rush.

Fiona Reid's Lady Bracknell -- arch-gorgon of the British drawing- room, a "monster without being a myth" -- is, by contrast, impeccably phrased. In keeping with recent tradition, she is less battle-axe than sabre, though in her delivery of the play's most famous line she is the first I have seen to go toe to toe with the legendary Edith Evans; her "a handbag" is an anguished croak, crowning a long pause in which unthinkable things appear to assault her consciousness before being swept irrevocably away. This gets to the heart of the character -- a refusal to admit the existence of anything that might contradict her. She seems, though, to be acting in a vacuum, in terms of both values and vowel sounds. In fact, Lady Bracknell's values (the same, as has often been remarked, that were about to destroy Wilde himself) are shared by everyone else in the play; the only difference is that she's had them longer.

All of the other senior characters are disappointing. David Hughes' Canon Chasuble is competent but flavourless. Hazel Desbarats' Miss Prism stands up splendidly in her last scene but goes in for lachrymose, supposedly meaningful, pauses earlier on. (I'm all for subtext, but there's a time and place.) James Macdonald doubles as the play's two servants and, despite my opening strictures, earns excellent references in both.

John Pennoyer's sets have real disinction: a crowded bachelor- pad for Algernon, all Beardsley and bric-a-brac, and a luscious garden-come-conservatory backed by a giant projection of flowers. They outclass most of the acting, though this may be a matter of practice. Life may demonstrate the triumph of hope over experience; the theatre, especially the classic theatre, has a habit of proving the reverse.
