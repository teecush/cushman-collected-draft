THEATRE REVIEW

TOPDOG/UNDERDOG

Studio Theatre, Niagara-on-the Lake, Ont.

The two characters in Topdog /Underdog, a prizewinning American play by Suzan-Lori Parks, are brothers, black, whose father named them Lincoln and Booth. This, according to Lincoln, was daddy's idea of a joke, though as the boys arent twins - Linc in fact is the elder by five years - he must have had a long-range sense of humour. A twisted one too: Short of calling them Cain and Abel, its hard to see how he could have done a better job of blighting their lives.

When we meet them, they're in their thirties and living in Booth's squalid one-room apartment. Booth has no vocation beyond shoplifting, at which hes an expert. Lincoln brings home what legitimate bacon there is, from a job impersonating his presidential namesake. He sits in an arcade, in top hat, frock coat, stuck-on beard and whiteface; and people pay money to shoot him. Once, though, he was a champion dealer in a three-card monte scam; he quit after a day when life on the street went violently wrong and one of his associates got shot. Booth itches to take his brothers former place in the game but, as Lincoln keeps telling him, he hasn't got the stuff; he lacks both the sleight-of-hand and the sleight-of-tongue. Lincoln was married, but his wife left him in the wake of his other troubles. Booth harbours a long-standing passion for a woman called Grace - amazing Grace - and claims to be making great progress with her, but it's pretty clear that most of his boasts are fantasy. He consoles himself with girlie mags, which he stacks under the one bed. Lincoln sleeps in a reclining chair. Despite this, and despite constant fluctuations in mood and fortune, he is, as he has always been, the top dog. Booth reacts with a smouldering blend of love and resentment.

The play is brilliantly written, in probably the best American dialogue for urban no-hopers since the David Mamet of American Buffalo. A program note makes even grander poetic claims for it, but in this Shaw Festival production it comes over as a naturalistic piece with a few bits left out. The missing parts include any clear statements about time and place. We dont know if its happening in New York or, say, Atlanta, which could make a difference, especially given the presidentialassassination motif. And though the idiom sounds very up-to-theminute, Booth's insistence that in order to get a woman Lincoln needs to get a phone - "the world's most revolutionary communication apparatus" - would seem to put us back in pre-Internet days.

Lincoln, defending the integrity of his day job, says that people like their history to be served up "in a certain way. They like it to unfold the way they folded it up." Both brothers are keenly aware - at least they mention it from time to time - of the bitter irony of a black man making a living by pretending to be a white one. (And not, though they dont explore it, not just any white man but a liberating president who was killed by a white supremacist, to whose spiritual descendants Linc may now be affording targetpractice.) Theres some amazing writing, too, some of it bitterly moving, in the brothers' reminiscences of their parents who left them, one at a time, when they were both still young, bequeathing them $500 apiece. Lincoln spent his inheritance, Booth has hoarded his, and who's to say which was the more provident? But its hard to draw the personal and political threads together, unless were meant to think that a black father has taken a crazy delight in dooming his sons to be even more disadvantaged than he was himself, playing a trick on both races at once. What we have is a drama of shifting domination and dependency, in the Harold Pinter tradition: as if the two brothers from The Caretaker were to be thrown onstage together, with no tramp to act as buffer. The play moves towards a pre-ordained ending, whose precise moment the author cunningly delays and diverts, giving us the shock of the expected when we least expect it.

It's very well acted. Kevin Hanchard's Booth is electric as an eel; Nigel Shawn Williams Lincoln is almost too weighty for the situation in which he finds himself, but the interplay between the two is flawless. Philip Akins direction is less funny than it could be, but I suspect that's due to the Shaw studio's logistics; the audience surrounds a surprisingly ample playing space on three sides, which somehow makes it seem diffuse rather than confined.

The festival has brought back Blair Williams lunchtime production of Ferenc Molnars The President, in which a New York tycoon turns a Communist taxi driver into a plutocrat in 60 minutes, real time; and it remains an intoxicating treat. Many roles have been recast but all fit perfectly into the play's and its protagonist's immaculately oiled machine. Julie Martell now plays the pregnant bride who makes it all necessary, and is lubriciously entertaining, if less subtly slutty than her predecessor Chilina Kennedy. (This role seems reserved for leading ladies from festival musicals.) Jeff Meadows repeats his wonderful turn as the made-over lunk; and Lorne Kennedy is again worldclass incomparable as the smoothtalking fast-thinking manipulator who makes it all happen. There is no better hour to be spent or time to be had, anywhere.

- Topdog/Underdog is in repertory until August 27, The President until Oct. 9.
