Tonight, CTV unwraps two new comedy shows that, while not exactly spinoffs of Corner Gas, trade in the same kind of gentle urban-folksy humour. An American sitcom, even one as sophisticated as Seinfeld, virtually screams at you "aren't these people crazy?" A Canadian sitcom is likelier to roll its eyes and ask you to identify with its people while suggesting that they are, really, rather odd. Both Hiccups and Dan for Mayor look, on the strength of their first episodes, mildly promising, with an emphasis on the adverb.

Both are set in larger communities than that of Corner Gas, though the size, nature and even the region of the towns in question has so far been left vague. Both, as is typical of a current sitcom as it scrambles for new subject matter, have unlikely premises that leave

you sceptical of their capacity for development. Or rather perhaps of their capacity for repetition, as the plague of episodic television, comic or dramatic, is that it has to tell the same story every week.

Hiccups is the show with the stronger links to Corner Gas; even the title suggests a kind of word association. More importantly, the man behind it, as writer and originator, and in front of it, as a principal performer, is Brent Butt. He plays Stan, a self-appointed "life coach" who, when we meet him, has spent six poverty-stricken months waiting for someone to drop by his office and ask to have their existence straightened out. Finally, someone does. This person is Millie, who writes children's books and does very well at it; her most famous creations are called the Grumpaloos, and she's something of a grumpaloo herself. She's a diminutive lady with a big heart and a short fuse, or as Stan professionally puts it, she's an over-reactionist. Nancy Robertson, also of Corner Gas, portrays her.

I've probably strayed into spoiler territory, as Millie's profession and her success at it are only revealed at the end of the half-hour. But I can't see any way of discussing the show without revealing it. It may even be that we were meant to twig early on that the bossy lady (Laura Soltis), in whose office Millie seems to occupy some barely tolerated menial position, is actually her publisher, who has every reason to put up with her. We find too that that staple of workplace comedy, the smooth and unsquashable office Lothario (David Ingram), doesn't belong on the premises at all; he's Millie's agent.

These people, plus a couple of others, are obviously going to be shaken up into various formations in the weeks ahead. I'd like to say I can't wait, but I can. The real problem is that, though Butt is charmingly frazzled and understated as Stan, he isn't the lead. Millie is, and she belongs to that large class of fictional characters whose eccentricities aren't nearly as endearing as their creators think they are. It's fun when she's kept waiting in a coffee-shop line-up, as we all have been, and makes a scene about it, as we all haven't but would like to do. It's merely irritating when a carton of milk explodes all over her, and she hops in a taxi to complain to the manufacturers in person.

Nor, though it tries hard, does the show ever make credible that someone as prosperous as she should seek the services of a small-time operator like Stan. Robertson brings gumption to the proceedings, but to win us over she'd need the comic genius of Nancy Walker -- whom she resembles and who could well have done it if the show were transported back 40 years and set in New York. Presumably, given Millie's success level, the setting is meant to be metropolitan but nothing, inside or outside Joyce's office, suggests it.

Dan for Mayor is, on the face of it, even farther-fetched. Dan (Fred Ewanuick), a barman at a fairly scruffy tavern, decides to run for mayor of his hometown of Wessex (impersonated by Kitchener, Ont.), even though the incumbent has never had to face a rival candidate in living memory. Fred's most prized possessiom is a Pac-Man video game. His ex-girlfriend (Mary Ashton) is about to marry someone else. Dan's ambitions are nearly derailed at the start, when he can't come up with the $1,000 registration fee, but stuff duly happens. His best buddy Jeff (Paul Bates) runs a print shop, which qualifies him to be Dan's campaign manager. Dan also gets ambiguous support from his employer (a more than usually hirsute David Ferry); some real-life mayors, including David Miller, are slated to make cameo appearances, though not necessarily as themselves. An unexpected plot development, shot with gleefully brutal economy, leaves Dan suddenly in with a chance. This, at the 29th minute of the half hour, potentially raises the show's entire game. Could we be about to see a sitcom with suspense? Even with a plot? The general air of harmless whimsy could well put paid to that but, like Dan himself, we can dream.

- Hiccups premieres tonight at 8 p.m., followed by Dan for Mayor at 8:30 p.m., on CTV.
