THE TWO SIDES OF KURT WEILL

Gravenhurst Opera House and Arts Centre

FRIENDS, LOVERS, HUSBANDS

Berkeley Street Theatre Upstairs, Toronto

- - -

Kurt Weills well-documented pilgrimage from the theatres of 1920s Berlin to those of 1940s Broadway has made him one of the most contested composers of the 20th century. Fifty-two years after his death, the skirmishing continues. Here are a few of the principal positions:

1. Weill was an artistic renegade who showed real genius when writing dissonant, pugnacious scores for satirical operas with texts by Bertolt Brecht, and then diluted his talent by writing polite scores for polite American musicals;

2. Weill was a born melodist and popular theatre composer who was bushwhacked by Brecht but really found himself, and did his best work, when writing with the likes of Ogden Nash and Ira Gershwin;

3. When Weill went to Broadway he beat the Americans at their own game and, by virtue of his classical training and avant-garde roots, wrote more distinguished theatre music than did any of his adoptive compatriots;

4. Weill's European reputation has led to his American works being accorded a pre-eminence they don't actually deserve; i.e., his Broadway songs are taken seriously by music snobs who wouldnt give Richard Rodgers house-room;

5. Weill remained essentially the same composer throughout his career, and had a perfect right to write with whom he wanted, about what he wanted.

The last is the one I would now subscribe to, though at various times in the past I have flirted with all of them except number 3. (I like to think I invented 4.) Anyway, the thesis that Weill was, in all senses, a singular composer underlay the concert presented at Gravenhurst on Saturday, in the last of the seasons Pamela Wallin Cultural Weekends at Muskoka Sands.

Two singers, Catherine Malfitano and Joan Morris, performed, individually and in tandem, songs from both ends of Weill, with Morris's husband, composer William Bolcom, acting as accompanist and affable host. Early on, Malfitano sang Denn wie, am sich bettet ("as you make your bed, you must lie there") from Mahagonny; much later she performed One Life to Live from Lady in the Dark; and one sensed a direct line between the two of them that was due to more than the coincidence in lyric.

They're both fatalistic songs -- one grim, one cheerful -- though, oddly enough, the darker one is the one with more joy in it. There are good dramatic reasons for this -- the German lady believes in what shes singing about, the American is trying to convince herself -- but there is more to it than that. Heres my own modest contribution to Weill scholarship: Weill's Berlin songs have more rhythmic drive than his Broadway ones. They are, in fact, jazzier.

I put the word in quotes because the so-called "jazz" that Weill wrote with Brecht was actually an academic, mythologized approximation of American music. And yet these apparently ersatz up- tempo tunes are closer to the real American thing than the ones Weill wrote when he got geographically closer to the source. The Weill songs that swing, or at least can be made to swing, are Mack the Knife, Alabama Song, Song of the Tough Nut. One Life to Live and its equivalents are forced by comparison.

Where the American Weill excelled was in his ballads: Speak Low, My Ship, September Song, It Never Was You, the almost unknown If Love Remains. Alan Jay Lerner, who wrote a show with Weill, once told me he had to keep begging him to keep two thousand years of Jewish misery out of his tunes, but in fact connecting with the Broadway tradition of Hebraic melancholia (not misery) brought out much of the best in him; he was allowed to be emotional without putting his feelings between inverted commas.

Some of Malfitano's performances were marred by an aggressive (and, in a small house, excessive) determination to demonstrate that opera singers can be actresses, too. But she did give a properly scarifying Pirate Jenny, and a very fine rendition of the great bitter torch-song Surabaya Johnny, in which the resigned, anguished set of her head was as expressive as her voice.

Morris was another thing. In a Sunday morning discussion at the Muskoka Sands hotel, Bolcom described Weill -- with some reluctance, I think -- as the original crossover composer. Morris might, by the same token, be described as the paragon of crossover singers, a classically trained mezzo who has devoted most of her career to singing American popular songs. (She crossed over and stayed.)

So she and Weill, though new to one another, make a good match, and her radiantly sensible personality -- and voice -- make a welcome change from the line of doomy diseuses. She paid her respects to the German canon with a wittily vulnerable Barbara Song, and then moved on to the Broadway songs, making the yearning of My Ship and the resigned tenderness of September Song fresh and touching all over again.

Weills total output remains hard to judge. He was primarily a theatre composer, but though we often see his German shows (The Threepenny Opera is on at Stratford this season) we never see the American ones. In the meantime, I think of Weill as the great added extra in the Broadway pantheon: there were Gershwin, Rodgers, Porter, the whole amazing list, and then to make the mixture even richer we get him, too -- not quite of the tradition, but wonderful to have on its edges.

- - -

And what have we now? Well, in Toronto there is Friends, Lovers, Husbands, an unbearable new musical billed in the program as "a most civilized comedy!" Civilized, according to the lyricist and librettist, Nancy Phillips, means its not rock 'n' roll. Well, thats certainly a step in the right direction, but you dont arrive at civilization simply by going back to the cabaret/off-Broadway tradition of the early 1960s. It was limited even then.

The songs here are perfectly craftsmanlike. The rhymes rhyme, and a couple of Bob Ashley's tunes have original twists. (He also plays very good cocktail piano.) But they are smug, assuming the airs of sophistication and worldly wisdom, without ever earning them.

Blame, mainly, the -- for want of a better phrase -- dramatic situation. There is a sixtyish woman, a poet apparently, wondering whether to re-marry the louse she divorced 40 years ago. There is her younger self, who hovers outside the window and then comes in and makes herself at home. Home is rather crowded, owing to the presence of a large fairground horse, apparently there so that we can have one of those songs about life being like a carousel. Anyway, the two of them proceed to spend the whole evening discussing their life, and especially the men in it.

Their primary ex materializes. Not only is he outnumbered two to one but he has to spread himself even thinner by impersonating the male sex generally. We learn nothing solid about any of these people, and so can take no interest in any of the random reminiscences they insist on subjecting us to. All there is to ponder is why sexually explicit lyrics, when set to blue-rinsed show tunes, invariably sound so excruciatingly self-conscious.

Mary McCandless is Older Woman and Lisa Horner is Younger Woman. Cliff LeJeune plays Man -- valiantly, I thought, in a pixieish sort of way -- and I was touched that a man with so little hair on top should be depicted as still irresistible to women. Touched, but not convinced.
