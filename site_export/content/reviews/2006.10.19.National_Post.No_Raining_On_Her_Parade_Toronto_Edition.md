CONCERT REVIEW

Barbra Streisand

Air Canada Centre, Toronto

I was a Barbra Streisand fan in 1963, the year of her first album. I stopped being one, while remaining something of an admirer, circa 1967, by which time she had half a dozen LPs behind her, all filled with original takes on terrific songs, and was in between playing Funny Girl onstage and onscreen. Your attitude to Streisand may well be defined by which of those performances you prefer. In the show she was an endearing singing actress in a musical; in the movie, where practically nobody else gets to sing a note, she is already on her way to being -- in a word she professes to hate, but which there is really no avoiding -- a diva. In the decades since, she has increasingly devoted herself to the production of technically perfect performances of mediocre material. So the wonder of her Tuesday night performance in Toronto -- part of what's being promoted as her farewell tour, though divas can always change their minds -- was how much of the old Barbra was back.

The repertoire, for a start, was almost unimpeachable; the worst it got was The Way We Were and her own Evergreen, which are at least passable. Otherwise, nearly everything was reclaimed from those early records, with a special emphasis on the songs of Harold Arlen: Not, alas, A Sleepin' Bee but Come Rain or Come Shine, which was buoyant; When the Sun Comes Out, which was all-purpose dramatic but excitingly so; My Shining Hour, which was new for her and the closest she came to touching as opposed to just impressing me; and a sizzling and sardonic Down with Love. This last was the nearest she came all night to singing a comedy song. The irreverent Brooklyn inflections that used to light up her performances are apparently gone beyond recall. She isn't a funny girl any longer, at least not in song, and that's a loss.

She still sings Funny Girl, lots of it. Her show starts, thrillingly, with the overture to the theatre version, and she's meticulous about telling us which songs were dropped and which interpolated. Thus she segues from The Music that Makes Me Dance, with its long and lovely lines, to My Man, the Fanny Brice staple that replaced it in the film. This must count as one of the great bad songs, and Streisand sang it with surprising restraint, which is the wisest course; unless you actually are Fanny Brice, giving yourself over to the song's melodrama can be embarrassing. And, of course, she sang People. This is one of those occasions on which a score's best-known song is one of its worst, but she took some of the banality out of it by setting it in its dramatic context and including the verse.

People may or may not need people, but they do seem to need platitudes. That song aroused some of the most frenzied cheers of the night, along with Somewhere, announced as a hymn to peace. She might even have got away with that one had the preening quasi- operatic quartet known as Il Divo not administered the coup de grace by joining in. Left to their own devices, they performed My Way as a joint tribute to Frank Sinatra, who should have had more tact than to sing it, and to the present star, who does have the tact but can tolerate having it sung at her. This amounted to more varieties of cheese than you could cut with a single knife.

Since we seem to be asked to pass judgment on the woman as well as the singer, I must say that, a few pious moments aside, I liked the Streisand who talked to us on Tuesday. In her black dresses and trademark long blonde hair, she was a superb sight, and vocally she was in immaculate, unfaltering control; a slight rasp only adds texture. She doesn't have the ability of a Sinatra, Garland or Astaire -- or of herself when young -- to cut to the heart of a song, or of the listener, with a single well-turned

phrase, but that doesn't stop her from delivering fine versions of Rodgers and Hart's Where or When or of I Stayed Too Long at the Fair.

She finished -- well, barring the encores -- with the other famous song from Funny Girl and by far the better one, the defiant take-no-prisoners number. It sounds especially defiant here because she sings the "stiff upper nose, kid" triumph-over-adversity reprise. This sounds a bit strange, coming from someone who's just been cheered to the echo and beyond, but there's no gainsaying it. Don't rain on her parade.

- Barbra Streisand plays her final Canadian show at the Air Canada Centre tomorrow.
