Edward Albee, who died last week at the age of 88, arrived in the American theatre at just the right moment. When he came along, with The Zoo Story in 1960, Arthur Miller and Tennessee Williams - the great postwar duo - had their best work behind them, and there were no apparent contenders. There was certainly no-one of international stature. It had been a while since an American dramatist had produced a play of interest to any theatre outside America.

Albee changed that; he was international from the get-go. He was also seen, initially, as an American writer in a fashionable European mode. The Zoo Story was assigned to that catch-all category, the Theatre of the Absurd, and Albee was hailed as the American heir of Samuel Beckett and Eugene Ionesco. In fact the play, a conversation piece for two men on a park bench ending in a killing, presents a situation more naturalistically credible than anything from Ionesco and its characters come with more back-story than anyone from Beckett. Albee himself reinforced the point toward the end of his life by writing a new first act to his original one-act play, presenting the domestic life of one of the duo immediately prior to his showing up on that bench. He went so far as to insist that the original Zoo Story could no longer be produced without its prequel.

What really commanded attention about the play when it first appeared was the fizz and eloquence of its language. It restored the theatre to its place in the great crackling line of American wit, even of the American wisecrack: a humour employed not, as in the plays of Neil Simon, to reassure but to unsettle.

The same quality, writ large - very large - pervaded the play that in 1963 marked Albee's breakthrough and is still his best-known work: Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf.

A wise British director once offered a persuasive diagnosis of the blight that afflicts most contemporary drama. There are no plays, he said, with characters one can imagine future actors passionately wanting to play. He was talking about British plays, but its true of American ones, too. Virginia Woolf is an exception; ever since Arthur Hill and Uta Hagen created the roles of George and Martha, the bogged-down history professor and his flamboyantly embittered wife, major actors have been lining up to play them.

Martha may be the more legendary role of the two. In the last decade or so I have seen three great Canadian actresses - Fiona Reid, Martha Henry, Nancy Palk - triumphantly take it on. But George, in his less demonstrative counterpunching way, carries an equal share of the plays weight, maybe more, which, as his wife belatedly acknowledges, is also true of their marriage. The play has been credited with all kinds of political and metaphysical overtones, but what carries it in the theatre is its linguistic virtuosity, embedded in two compelling characters who are a master and mistress of the rhythmic put-down, directed first against one another and then - because the couple who joke together stick together - against the outsiders who have strayed into their territory. It's a high-wire act, maintained over three long acts. The length was another quality that gained Albee instant respect; it placed him in the marathon tradition of Eugene O'Neill.

His subsequent plays have been more compact, though they havent always seemed so. Tiny Alice (1965) and All Over (1971) were soporific. They seemed weighed down with significance, while giving us no human reason to be interested. Its perfectly possible that future productions will reclaim them, but I don't see any theatres lining up to try. Still, such late Miller plays as The Price and Incident at Vichy have proved unexpectedly resilient, and so has Albee's A Delicate Balance (1966), a play that re-orchestrates the domestic dynamics of Virginia Woolf for a larger ensemble, overlaying them with a quasisymbolic menace that doesnt seem meretricious.

If Albee seemed to rule the American drama of the 1960s, in the following decades his fortunes, and indeed his plays, went into a prolonged slump. But in his last decades he rallied.

The Play About the Baby (1998) is a notably deft reworking of some of the themes of Virginia Woolf, light-handed though not lighthearted; at the end it reverts to its authors characteristic biliousness. Soulpepper once gave it an enjoyable workshop presentation, with a fine performance by Juan Chioran, but it still awaits a full Toronto production; come to that, it's never had a full Broadway production. The Goat or Who Is Sylvia? (2002) was outrageous - it is indeed about a man unabashedly in love with a goat - and exhilarating.

There cannot be many writers like Albee, who commenced operations halfway through the 20th century and were still going strong at the dawn of the 21st.
