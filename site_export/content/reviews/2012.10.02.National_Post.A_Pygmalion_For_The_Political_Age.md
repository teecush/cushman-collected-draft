Michael Healey's Proud is notorious as the play the Tarragon Theatre wouldnt do, despite it being the last part of a trilogy with whose first two instalments the theatre had had considerable success. It was widely speculated that the Tarragon got cold feet on account of Proud being a play whose central character is the current Prime Minister. Although, as no official statement was ever made, we dont know if the cancellation was for that reason or if the Tarragon simply didn't think the play was good enough. If the latter's the case, I can only say that the Tarragon must have unrealistically high standards. Or, considering some of the plays that it has produced, that its fooling itself.

Proud, which has now reached the Toronto stage in a production essentially promoted by its author with himself in the leading role, is in fact a smaller, less formally adventurous play than its well-named predecessors Generous and Courageous, though on a minute-by-minute basis, its also a funnier one. (This last quality also has something to do with Healey's own presence on stage.) It's a counter-factual piece, in which Stephen Harper still has a majority, but a larger and more volatile one. In this alternate universe, hes won as large in Quebec as the NDP did in the real one and, like the NDP, he has a caucusful of rookie MPs as a result. Much of the play turns on the relationship between the PM and one of the most volatile of these recruits, a woman with the not-quite-persuasive name of Jisbella Lyth. (Ive never met a Jisbella. Have you? Has Healey?)

We start, in superior revue-sketch style, with the premier addressing his newly elected troops, promising huge but unspecified changes, warning of opposition from the opposition, and insisting on their unqualified loyalty. He finishes by directing them toward an open bar, which he promised will remain open for a full three quarters of an hour.

We then shift to his office, where he is directing Cary, his chief of staff, on house seating arrangements, stipulating that he does want to have to meet the gaze of any party member who has ever disagreed with him on any matter whatsoever. Cary says this will be difficult, and has a chart to prove it, but promises to do his best. And then Lyth bursts in, asking if either of them has a condom. It seems that Evan Solomon is interviewing her in her office, and she wants to have sex with him on her desk.

This development is fairly unlikely on several counts. (Spoiler: Solomon does not appear in the play.) It moves the tone into farce: not necessarily a bad move, but one it takes time to adjust to. Identifying Jisbella as a loose woman, which they don't much care about, and a loose cannon, which they do, her leaders set about firing her. When this doesn't work, she being smarter than they think, they decide to use her instead. She will sponsor an anti-abortion bill that has no chance of passing; shes an unlikely fit for this, being a single mother who has had a couple of terminations herself, but shell do as a piece of red meat thrown to the base while proving that her leader, who will not of course support her, isn't the fundamentalist fanatic of popular belief.

A double distraction in fact; and even more topical now than Healey must have thought when he wrote it. The scheme doesn't quite work out; the bill brings Jisbella attention, and even a certain amount of power, and she revels in it. It seems shes a Marxist,

of the Groucho persuasion: She has her principles, and if you dont like them, she has others. She also begins to fancy the Prime Minister something rotten, perhaps because she finds that seeming rigidity a challenge and a turn-on.

Healey has said that Shaw was a great influence on the writing of this play. People have pointed out the resemblances to Pygmalion (though to me the transformation of Jisbella rather suggests Frankenstein) while just last year at the Shaw Festival Healey adapted On the Rocks, another talkfest about a prime minister with transformational ambitions. Healey though adds an element that Shaw, at least at the explicit level, left out: sex. The mix isn't too comfortable. The plot requires us to accept the PM as a sex symbol, and I don't buy it. Still, Philip Larkin thought Mrs. Thatcher was sexy; it takes all sorts. But the play works best as crackling debate.

The stand-out scene has the PM passionately listing all the things that he doesnt feel passionate about, but that everybody thinks he does. ( "I dont care about Quebec. At all. I don't care about hospital wait times. I don't care about where the salmon went or why they came back." Im being very selective.) All he cares about is "an appropriately

sized government" and he wont even say what size that is. He makes all this sound, if not reasonable, then very appealing. After all, most of us don't care about the things we're supposed to care about.

Personally, I find it hard to believe that the real Prime Minister Harper doesnt believe in at least some of the things on his anti-hit-list. I also doubt, in my naive way, that the real Harper would swear as freely and engagingly as this one does, or that he could or would give his protegees young son such friendly and practical telephonic advice on how to turn potato latkes into hash browns. Healey's Harper is in fact a lot more likeable than his namesake, This is partly because of the way Healey writes him, and even more because of the way Healey plays him. It is, of course, impossible to write a decent play without falling in love with your central character, which is not the same as approving of him. Even reading the script, it's plain that Healey has given his protagonist his own sense of comic timing, which in performance is overwhelming. Healeys acting persona has generally been that of a bumbler with, increasingly, a backbone of steel. This is its definitive incarnation.

As Jis, a name the PM flatly refuses to use, Maev Beaty is sensationally good: switching from ditziness to determination and back again, with a quick flash timing to match Healeys slow burn. She's always had presence; she now has control. Tom Barnett plays fine third fiddle as Cary, and Miles Potter's direction doesnt let a moment slip.

There is a fourth character, a younger one, who moves the play into yet another dimension, and about whom I cant give anything away except to say that he also has an Evan Solomon connection and that he's played by Jeff Lillico, who's always welcome. The play needs him in order to transmit its darker implications, but its an awkward fit. Proud is not a conventionally well-made play, but it also isnt as radically and constructively ill-made as was Generous. But it's always sharp and sometimes hilarious, and though you may hope for more than that, you don't often get it. You don't usually get as much.
