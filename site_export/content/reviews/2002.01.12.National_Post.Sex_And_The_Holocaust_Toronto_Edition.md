SLIP KNOT

Factory Theatre

- - -

In 1995 Vancouver, there live two old Germans, Second World War vintage, one of whom has lost his accent.

Fritz, the still-guttural one, is poor and part-Jewish and apparently had to do dreadful things in the death camps to maintain his cover. William, who is rich and successful, did far more dreadful things -- in both quality and quantity -- without the same excuse.

In the first scene of Ken Gass's Slip Knot, Fritz, who is mortally ill, pays a call on William and announces that he is about to go public with their past records. William kills him.

Judith, Fritz's daughter, knows about the murder, and at her father's funeral she makes eyes at William's son, Jonathan, who asks her back to the house. There is much saucy dialogue about the aphrodisiac effects of bereavement, and a fair amount of saucy action as well. Judith keeps referring to her Old Testament namesake, who gained access to the tent and bed of the tyrant Holofernes and killed him at the moment of climax.

This should count as fair warning, but Jonathan, lost to lust like a typical man, ends up sated and dead like Holofernes. (He, by the way, is pronounced throughout with the accent on the second syllable. I, having always heard it on the third, found this very grating but I acknowledge that there are two schools of speech.) So ends Act One.

Act Two brings together William and Judith, the surviving half of the cast, for a more elaborate cat-and-mouse, or sex-and-death, game. The play is billed as a thriller and it has a couple of twists that are genuinely surprising, as well as a couple that aren't. Much of the time, though, it seems to be nervously reveling in its own implausibility.

It also pauses from time to time to reflect on the Holocaust, and these moments, pinned as they are on to pasteboard characters in gimcrack situations, are intolerable. Together with the sexy overlay, they turn the play into concentration-camp chic.

In which connection: George Steiner once remarked on how sinister it was that "camp" should have become a vogue word for a flippant semi-style. I thought at the time that he was being over-ingenious, but a succession of plays and movies has changed my mind. This play may well have serious intentions as a sardonic fable of guilt and responsibility, but it could only realize them if it were written and performed with the icy high-comic authority of a parable by Friedrich Durrenmatt. Sarah Stanley's production is giggly and -- except in its enthusiastic sound and lighting effects -- insecure.

William, the eternal and invincibly amoral Nazi and a medical experimenter at Auschwitz and Dachau, is played by John Gilbert, who hovers, physically and vocally, between smooth and crinkly in the English manner, neither of them quite adequate either to the character's monstrosity or to his occasional qualms. On the first night he seemed unsure of his words, or at least of his phrasing.

Philippa Domville, by contrast, throws herself at the role of Judith with reckless abandon; a perplexingly uneven actress (wonderful in The Chet Baker Project, far from it in everything else), she tries to unite her two roles of temptress and avenger by adopting the winsome lisp of a Marilyn Monroe impersonator. She plays, somewhat too literally, the blonde bombshell.

Dmitri Chepotevsky starts well as Jonathan, who is both callow and callous, but his confidence eventually turns into coarseness. Les Carlson comes off best as Fritz, very trembly and ashen.

Not much is made of the play's being set in Canada. Nothing is said about this country's tolerance, or otherwise, of possible mass murderers, and one wonders if the characters' journeys were really necessary. Ex-Nazis have been known to thrive without ever leaving home.
