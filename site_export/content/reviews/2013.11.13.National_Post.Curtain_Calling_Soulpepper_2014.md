The standout productions at Toronto's Soulpepper theatre this year have been the marathons: the two-part Angels in America and the three-part The Norman Conquests. Both, its announced yesterday to nobodys surprise, are being revived in the company's 2014 season. Their success did leave some people wondering what Soulpepper intended to do for an encore. The Oresteia? Schiller's Wallenstein trilogy? Ibsens reputedly unstageable double-header Emperor and Galilean? Tom Stoppards The Coast of Utopia? That last triple-decker might actually make sense, since according to Soulpepper's artistic director Albert Schultz and to others as well, Stoppard is one of the surest box-office bets a prestige theatre can make.

As it turns out, all the other plays in next year's battalion are single spies. Arthur Millers The Crucible is being brought back from two years ago. Among the new entries are two other American plays: Reginald Roses familiar Twelve Angry Men and the decidedly unfamiliar Idiot's Delight by Robert E. Sherwood. Otherwise there'll be plays from Ireland (The Gigli Concert by Tom Murphy, whose A Whistle in the Dark rocked Toronto a few years back), South Africa (Athol Fugards The Road to Mecca) and of course Canada (David Youngs Glenn, which splits Glenn Gould four ways).

Canada may also claim at least a partial share in Of Human Bondage, which is an adaptation by Canadian playwright Vern Thiessen of the famous novel by Somerset Maugham: the first ever stage version apparently. What might be called canonical classics have one-and-a-half representatives. The half is A Tender Thing, described as a modern, moving reworking of Romeo and Juliet. The one is Moliere's Tartuffe, in the Richard Wilbur translation that's already a near-classic on its own account.

Two things to note about that list. First, in a departure from previous Soulpepper practice, it only takes us up to next September; the lineup for the remaining three months wont be revealed until next April. Schultz, when I spoke to him on the phone, said that "were too big an organization to do it all at once," and that he wants to leave the space to deal with unforeseen events from markets crashing to new plays arriving - or of course the desirability of extending the run of a successful show. That sounds sensible.

The second feature is that there have been no announcements of actors or directors. It seems that final phone calls have yet to be made and contracts signed. One directorial assignment can be revealed: Schultz told me that he'd "offered Albert Schultz the job of directing Idiot's Delight and that Schultz had accepted."

That play is the most intriguing choice on the list, for several reasons. One is that it will be the first play to open. Another is its rarity; some of us may have seen the MGM movie version but few will have seen it onstage; I certainly never have. A third is the nature of the play itself; first staged in 1936 its about dancing on a literal and metaphorical precipice; its setting, in Schultzs own description, is a resort hotel in the Italian Alps where English, German, Italian and American characters engage in a celebration of life - a recognition of it as an idiot's delight. And of course they're only three years away from apocalypse.

Schultz describes it, referring to two other American plays of the same era that have been successes for Soulpepper as "The Time of Your Life meets You Cant Take It with You," all, directly or indirectly about the imminence of war and the end of a society. This, apparently, will be a key theme of Soulpeppers programming in the next few years: a commemoration and examination - involving poetry, music and dance as well as plays - of two world wars and the space between and beyond them. There are hints that some of this will make itself felt in those blank three months of next year. It's reasonable to guess that at some point we'll see some kind of production of Journeys End, the classic First World War that was once given a notable production by the Stratford Festivals Young Company, some of whose members, including Schultz himself, went on to form the nucleus of Soulpepper.

About that word "classic"; in what might be called its vulgar sense - plays written before 1900 - it's an area that seemed a vital part of Soulpepper's identity when it started 15 years ago but has become steadily less important since. In that sense, Soulpepper has grown less adventurous. Schultz disagrees; he says that the 12 actors who started the company all had "a similar aesthetic", a similar classical background and training. They were people who wanted to do what we do in our own city, and for them nothing could have been less adventurous than doing Schiller and Moliere. Well, maybe, but theres a time when being reactionary is the most revolutionary thing you can do. Nobody else was doing those plays in Toronto at the time; more to the point, nobody is now either. Even toprank Shakespeare is hard to come by in Canadas largest city and supposed cultural capital.

Schultz's answer would be that, since moving into its own premises in the Distillery District, Soulpepper has been more adventurous, establishing what he calls "a creative village" and bringing on new generations of artists. Our core artists don't just do a play; they teach the next generation; they contribute curatorially. Were training actors, directors, designers; were even training producers - no one else is doing that. They're also doing it, as he continually points out, with very little subsidy: "We invest a million dollars every year. 92% of it we raise ourselves, from box office fundraising. We have a company of 150 artists, including poets and choreographers and composers. We employ more people than any four other theatres."

I wouldn't argue with any of that, though I can't claim to love all of the results. I'd still like them to do more old, preferably rare plays. It would be nice to have both. And there are hints that we yet might.
