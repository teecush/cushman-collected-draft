CHAPLIN

Court House Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

Mixed media is a fancy phrase that usually means getting the worst of several worlds, but Simon Bradbury, as author and performer of Chaplin, does the mixing as well as, if not better than, anyone I have seen.

Onstage, he is Charlie Chaplin, alone in his office-cum- screening-room, keeping colleagues and intruders at bay while running the rushes of The Great Dictator. Onscreen he is Chaplin again, trying to get his lines and business right, and spending a great deal of time, energy and exasperation saying "cut". When the canned Charlie and the live Charlie begin to interact, the one practically stepping off the screen to shake hands with the other, Bradbury's timing, in Neil Munro's production, is brilliant.

What, however, is it all for? The play is subtitled, mock- portentously (at least I hope it's mock), The Trial of Charles Spencer Chaplin, Esq. It presents its subject at a moment of crisis. It is 1940 and Chaplin, having held out against spoken dialogue for more than a decade, is making his first full talkie. He has always been his own director, producer, writer and composer; now even his acting involves him in a dual role.

The Great Dictator is of course the movie in which Chaplin exploited his own phenomenal resemblance to Hitler, or rather -- since the clown was famous first -- Hitler's phenomenal resemblance to him. Chaplin plays the dictator Hinkel, while doubling as his own familiar Tramp. In the latter role he gets to end the picture with an impassioned six-minute speech. It seems many of the people around Chaplin doubted the wisdom of this; and in Bradbury's scheme Chaplin himself comes to share their doubts.

Some of the points raised are commercial or political: Chaplin risks alienating the American public by making an anti-fascist, and hence pro-interventionist, picture, especially if he tops it up with a sermon. Others are personal or artistic: Chaplin's beloved little man has no place in so heavy a project, and Charlie risks killing off his own greatest creation. (This in fact happened; after this film, the Tramp never twirled his cane again.)

Bradbury's Chaplin brushes off most of these objections, but they lead him to confront a deeper problem. Alarmingly, Hinkel -- Chaplin's own invention played by himself -- starts haranguing him from the screen. How dare he presume to criticize dictators? Isn't he a dictator himself? And hasn't he built his whole career on the destruction of another human being? Chaplin got his start as a five- year-old in England, singing a comic song in the family music-hall act when his unstable mother went down with stage-fright. She never recovered, and ended her life in a mental hospital. Incidentally, the song he sang was about one Jack Jones, a snob who cut all his friends and relations when he came into money. "Being on your Jack Jones" is Cockney slang for going it alone. Chaplin has been, selfishly, on his Jack Jones ever since.

That's a neat verbal ribbon in which to tie up a theme: Bradbury is a writer. But the overall structure is strained. Chaplin surely had his guilts and insecurities but it is hard to believe that he would have embodied them in the figure of Hitler. (We can surely, without weaving elaborate psychological fantasies, give Chaplin credit for being ahead of his time and recognizing monstrosity when he saw it, even when it came wearing his own moustache.)

The Hitler-Hinkel whom Bradbury conjures up has to be, for the sake of providing an articulate opposition, improbably suave and coherent. Martin Amis, in his excellent and underrated new book Koba's Dread, remarks that it's possible to laugh at the big moustache, Stalin, but not at the little one, Hitler. He's right, now. But in 1940, when people knew less about Hitler, even his enemies could find him funny. Chaplin, who knew more than many, certainly could: He could hardly have taken the dictator as a subject on any other terms. And to be a worthy antagonist to his double, Hitler-Hinkel needs to be comic; a strutting, screaming buffoon. A short sequence in which Bradbury, live, gets to play him that way is the highlight of the evening.

Themes apart, the play staggers under the built-in curse of biographical drama, especially when it has a satirical or deflationary bent. The author has to keep pumping information as fast as he undercuts it. And even then a lot of interest is taken for granted: Bradbury has probably over-estimated the amount that most of us know, or care, about Chaplin. (I found myself leaning rather heavily on my memories of the Robert Downey Jr. film).

Add to this the similar curse on one-person plays, to which the technological trappings here add a special intensity. To keep faith with its own convention the play has to keep all other characters off stage. But it's one thing when the actor is cleverly conversing with his own image on a screen, and quite another when he's exchanging dialogue with the recorded voices of other actors playing the people Chaplin is trying to keep out of his room and his life. Some of these one-on-one dialogues go on a long time, and the effect is not only deadening but arbitrary: There seems no reason for the production not to cut its losses and get the interlopers off the phone and onto the set.

All these unseen folks, upbraiding Chaplin while feeding us the facts, make the play seem like a rogue edition of This Is Your Life, devoted to bringing its subject down instead of puffing him up. The method only works when Chaplin is persuaded to give an interview to a trio of journalists. We don't see them either, but at least the three voices can act among themselves. It's good radio. One of them asks Chaplin whether his anti-Nazism means that he's Jewish. (He wasn't.) The question goes unanswered, and the fact that it does is a tribute to Bradbury's dramatic tact.

You like Bradbury himself throughout. He is a champion actor of scrawny underdogs who here resists all temptation to play for sympathy but gets it anyway. He comes off, in fact, nicer than Chaplin is ever likely to have been. His technique is as winning as his personality; and when, on the first night, technology betrayed him -- a voice-tape kept repeating itself and he had to step out of character and call a halt -- we warmed to him all the more. On one level it was poetic justice; on another a heartening example of man asserting himself over the machine. (I suppose they can't keep it in.) The play is the first visible fruit of the Shaw Festival's augmented dramaturgical policy: adding plays set in Shaw's lifetime but written since. When this was first announced, some of us thought it would mean an instant diet of Tom Stoppard. We did not expect plays to emerge from within the company, or at least not this quickly. It's a hopeful start: The last production of the current regime pointing directly to the next.
