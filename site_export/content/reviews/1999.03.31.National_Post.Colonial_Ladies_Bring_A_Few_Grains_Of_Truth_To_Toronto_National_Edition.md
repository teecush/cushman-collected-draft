THE BUSH-LADIES

Tarragon Extra Space, Toronto

I can't remember the last time I saw so many bonnets on one stage. The Bush-Ladies is a splicing together of the memoirs of four Englishwomen who emigrated to Canada in the 1830s. Molly Thom, the adaptor-director, began to assemble this material 30 years ago and the show that has finally arrived at the Tarragon (which has something of a tradition of literary theatre) is transparently a labour of love. It is certainly an informative evening, and it is sometimes an amusing one. It is not what anyone would call thrilling.

Each of our heroines was responsible for one magnum opus. Anne Langton (played by Elva Mai Hoover), miniature-painter and musician, wrote A Gentlewoman in Upper Canada. Anna Jameson (Meg Hogarth), touted in the program as "an intellectual, a social critic, and an ardent feminist" married the Attorney General of Upper Canada, speedily left him, travelled through the wilds for a few months, and then went back home where she published Winter Studies and Summer Rambles.

Catherine Parr Traill (Wendy Springate), officers wife and botanist, wrote The Backwoods of Canada. Susanna Moodie (Mary Ann McDonald), Catherines sister and also an officer's wife, wrote about her experiences for magazines and called the collected result Roughing It in the Bush.

Set apart from her colleagues by this forthright choice of title (and also by the fact that her book has endured as something of a classic -- at least I've heard of it) Susanna emerges as the most individual, and as the one whose story is easiest to follow. She projects delicacy that gradually reveals its strength: The transformation from a sheltered existence to a life where shelter is the goal.

Its a shared trajectory, and the show alternates personal with communal experience. The observation that woman is a bit of a slave in this country -- an insight hardly won and somewhat reluctantly expressed -- takes shape as the outcome of backbreaking litany of chores. (These are rhythmically listed, and then made the foundation of a small dance routine, rather too redolent of Higher Amateur Dramatics. A burst of singing from McDonald, on the other hand, is delightful).

Admiring the characters abilities to put in such days, and then write at the end of them, you also reflect, somewhat ruefully, that this was an age in which literate people did write, and not just for publication. Intentionally or not, the show makes a very good case for Anglo-Christian gentility, presenting it as a tradition that expands under pressure but never breaks.

Much of the show is about homesickness, complicated by an increasing ambiguity about just what or where "home" is. The longer you remain in Canada, the less you will like it is what we hear at the outset; by the end, there has been a journey from enthusiasm for the new country to resentment to surprised acceptance: the universal immigrant experience, it seems.

All the deprecating lines about Canada, by the way, get laughs, as does a more localized observation about the remote possibility of bringing "a few grains of truth to Toronto." The ability to laugh at one's own culture -- or at least at one's own place-names -- is one of the things that British and Canadians have in common: an overlooked portion, perhaps, of the colonial legacy.

This is not a show that I could ever be wild about but it does what it wants to do, and the performance-level is unobtrusively high. Everybody resists temptation: Which is to say that nobody over- colours, and nobody recites.
