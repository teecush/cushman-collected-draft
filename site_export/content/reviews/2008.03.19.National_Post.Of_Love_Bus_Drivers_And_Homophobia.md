A fondly-remembered exchange from an old radio show: the scene, a fashionable London soiree in the 1890s. "And who are you?"

"Oh, I'm merely the wittiest, most fascinating man in West End society."

"Are you sure?" "No, of course I'm not Shaw; he's that awful fellow in a red beard and knickerbockers. I'm Oscar Fingal O'Flaherty Wills."

"Oh, you're Wilde!" "So would you be if you were called Oscar Fingal O'Flaherty Wills."

(Ah, the days when comedy was literate.) Anyway, Oscar Fingal what-have-you figures prominently in A Man of No Importance, a winning-if-uneven 2002 off-Broadway musical now receiving its Canadian premiere, and he still isn't happy about his full name.

As a man who lives for fame, he frets that it's too long to fit on billboards. Wilde, I should make clear, isn't really a character in this show; he's a ghostly presence in the mind of the hero Alfie Byrne, a closeted gay bus driver in 1964 Dublin for whom fame is the least of his problems.

Alfie is prone to recite Wilde poetry to his passengers (they seem to like it), and he also runs a church drama group that specializes in Wilde plays; they've already done The Importance of Being Earnest, though not, as far as we're told, A Woman of No Importance. This year Alfie has set his sights on the lush tragedy Salome, which he thinks is a masterpiece. The church establishment -- the "sodality" they call it -- disagrees; it thinks the play pornographic, and it bans both the production and the group. Which is where we come in.

Alfie is packing up his props and thinking about his amateur-theatrical friends and the show they'll never give. He imagines them acting out the story of his recent life, playing all the people in it --including themselves. This elaborate device never really takes hold; it merely looks as if a small cast is doing a confusing amount of doubling.

This isn't just, or even principally, aputting-on-a-show-show --much of it is concerned with Alfie's emotional life, or lack of it. He's a middle-aged man who lives with his sister. Two attractive young people unwittingly force him to confront his own desires. One is Robbie, his driver, whom he secretly and unrequitedly loves. The other is Adele, an out-of-towner who steps onto Alfie's bus one day and whom he instantly casts as Salome, the virginal seductress.

Both show him that love, of any variety, is a messier business than he would like to think. His idolized Oscar makes spectral appearances, urging him to step out and come out. (Wilde plays a very similar role in Tom Stoppard's The Invention of Love, tempting and taunting the historically repressed A. E. Housman.) This, in a homophobic town,

doesn't work out too well either. Robbie has to work out his own salvation which, with a little unexpected help from his friends, he finally seems capable of doing.

Based on a little-known movie, the show is the work of Terrence McNally (book), Stephen Flaherty (music) and Lynn Ahrens (lyrics), the team that wrote Ragtime. This is a smaller work, in every way, that -- at least in this production -- never takes off as a musical. The songs vary in quality, but even when good they don't lift the story.

Lezlie Wade's direction doesn't turn the economy of scale to advantage; much of the staging consists of moving chairs around, and one important scene comes bewilderingly out of nowhere (and returns there). A vigorous pub band plays reduced orchestrations. An ambitious scene-setting title-song founders under the weight of its title phrase.

There are a couple of comic numbers, one titled Books and the other Art, that aren't bad (Ahrens can be a witty and perceptive lyricist) but seem to have come out of some other show, or at least to be sung by characters who need more development; one of them is the show's villain, a butcher who deals in ham offstage as well as on, and whose destructive bigotry is hardly established, let alone explored.

Flaherty's showbizzier tunes come off as derivative. Far more creative in its pastiche is Streets of Dublin, a rousingly atmospheric piece of Poguery, resoundingly sung by Kyle Blair as Robbie; he's the best singer in the cast, and one of the best actors. But his role too is underwritten, and even that song is some distance from the show's emotional centre.

That resides with Alfie himself whose Love Who You Love (it sings better than it reads) is a song both halting and lilting, and who is beautifully played by Douglas E. Hughes, a Shaw Festival regular who, on this evidence, has been underused at his home base. He is quiet, kind and contagiously fervent (and very Irish) in his worship of Art, lost and fumbling in everything else. His anguish could probably break the surface more powerfully than it does, but this is still a moving performance that knows what Alfie, and the show, are all about.

There is good support from Patty Jamieson as the sister (a loving shrew more vigorously desperate), Bethany Jillard -- who isn't the securest singer but makes a touchingly bruised Adele -- and Gary Krawford as a theatre-group veteran who tells Alfie that he's a terrible director and makes it sound the most affectionate of tributes.

There is a generous amount of wishful thinking, mixed in with shameless theatrical sentiment, in the way the show ends, but the generosity conquers. Oscar Fingal would probably drop all poses and agree. - A Man of No Importance is at the Berkeley Street Theatre until March 22. For more information call 416-368-3110.
