You've heard of milestone productions. And Up They Flew is a two-milestone production. It's the 25th anniversary show for Theatre Columbus, the Toronto company that combines a physical aesthetic with a literary bent. And it's the last play to be presented under the joint artistic leadership of the company's founders, Leah Cherniak and Martha Ross, who function on this show as director and author respectively.

They're going out on a high note of style and a low one of substance. To take the best first: And Up They Flew has a cast of seven actors who by the end of the evening stand revealed as a comic ensemble of intoxicating skill and cohesion. They also fall revealed, since the play, which is in part a bedroom farce that never gets to the bedroom, requires them to bump repeatedly into swinging doors, recalcitrant furnishings and -- especially -- one another. There are possibly more collisions here per minute than I have ever seen on a stage. To begin with, it just seems untidy but as it goes on it becomes its own medium (which reminds me, the plot includes a seance) and, indeed, message. The cast become so intoxicated by it that at one point they take off into a dance number, as if the characters' desperation has become the actors' joie de vivre. It's infectious.

Impressive though it is, it isn't all that funny. But then clown shows seldom are, certainly not when they go on for two-and-a-half-hours, and Theatre Columbus has its roots in clowning. Slapstick for its own sake rather quickly outstays its welcome; if we're to keep laughing we have to know just why the characters are behaving like accident-prone automata and, even more important, to care. And this is where And Up They Flew falls to Earth. It's billed as "Gosford Park Meets the Marx Brothers," which is both presumptuous and inaccurate. The show does include a human-mirror sequence like the one in Duck Soup but, good as they are, no one onstage amounts to one-third of a comic genius (i. e. a Marx brother). And any resemblance to Gosford Park is confined to setting the play in

a 1930s English drawing room and throwing in references to the class-system, at least insofar as one character is a servant while the others are all masters and mistresses.

It's also subtitled "a comedy about the 20th century," and it is, quite self-consciously, one of those plays set between the world wars in which people keep harking back to the carnage behind them and, if the author is feeling prophetic or is the beneficiary of hindsight, forward to what worse may come. There are many such plays in existence, some of them with the added attraction of having been written at the time; the Shaw Festival keeps reviving them. This new one has nothing to add beyond the stubbornly unhelpful conceit embodied in its title. Jane Ashbury wants to be the first woman to fly the Atlantic in the opposite direction to Amelia Earhart; in the meantime she and her husband are hosting a house-party. Jane enlists the help of her childhood friend Nora Duckworth whom she hires as a maid, keeping their old acquaintance secret so that her husband will not know of her own lowly origins. (He's big in toilet goods, and self-conscious about it). Another married couple is present, plus a bachelor with a Casanova complex and somebody's elderly aunt. There is a lot of lusting after others' partners, but though they go through the motions of not wanting to be found out, none of them seems really to mind very much. They also go through the motions of being worried over Jane's aviatrix ambitions, but again without much conviction. Her desire to fly away and rise above it all seems equally thin and whimsical. These people barely exist, so the farce doesn't function. Neither do the ideas. That British drawing-room society of the '30s was shallow and headed for a rude awakening has been said before, usually by the British. Why say it again, now, in Canada?

In fact, there is some Canadian content here. John Jarvis plays, deliriously well, a fellow who goes humorously mad and starts speaking with a Canadian accent. I'm not suggesting these conditions are coequal; his character is a traumatised war vet who starts channelling a slain Newfie comrade. He even talks of moving to Canada, though neither he nor the play gets far in this direction. He also has apocalyptic visions in which he foresees the atomic bomb, the oil race and Monty Python. Others delightful are Paul Braunstein as a dogged but not quite dashing seducer, and as the oracular aunt, the still-youngish Michelle Fisk taking subtle advantage of age's privilege to stay comparatively still and quiet. Julie Stewart puts on a bold comic face as a semi-willing adulteress (the spirit is willing, etc.) and John Cleland blusters effectively as the bathroom magnate. Of the two putative heroines, Lisa Norton has an intriguingly baleful air as Martha the reluctant, not to say incompetent, maid; Kerry Ann Doherty smiles prettily as Jane, but the character's a hole in the play. As a text, And Up They Flew is earthbound, but the production flaps wings. - And Up They Flew runs until April 4 at the Berkeley Street Theatre Upstairs.
