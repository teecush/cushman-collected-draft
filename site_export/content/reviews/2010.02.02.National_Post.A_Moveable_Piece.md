The Thistle Project is a company avowedly dedicated to adapting "non-theatrical sources to the stage." Peer Gynt, Ibsen's sprawling comic epic, may just about qualify; Ibsen didn't intend his poem -- a sprawling comic epic -- to be staged, at least not initially. All the same, it's long since become part of the theatrical repertoire; it's the work of a master playwright and is in five-act dramatic form, with dialogue and characters and a story and stuff, so it can hardly help being a play. In fact, it's a great play: the greatest, I think, outside of Shakespeare.

It does present practical problems. Performed as written it would take at least four hours and a cast of thousands. The Thistle's adaptation lasts just over 90 minutes and has a cast of two. Susan Coyne plays Peer, the 19th-century Norwegian dreamer whose turbulent peasant youth gives way to a prosperous expatriate middle age and who eventually returns home to face death and -- the one thing he has always evaded -- disillusion. Matthew Romantini plays everyone else, from Peer's exasperated but fiercely protective mother to the Button-Moulder, sent to melt down Peer's soul in his casting ladle and then recycle it, on the grounds that he has never been whole-hearted enough to qualify for either heaven or hell.

The Thistle's other advertised principle is the use of "space and the body." The space around which Erika Batdorf, the director, moves her two actors' bodies is that of Holy Trinity Church, just behind Toronto's Eaton Centre. During Peer's youth, when he's climbing up mountains and invading the kingdom of the trolls, we follow him around, promenade-style. When he advances into his more sedentary years, we too are allowed to sit down (on benches) and watch him. Much of Batdorf's staging is brilliant; she makes imposing use of the site's built-in resources of lighting and sound (Richard Feren composed the music, Tom Kerr plays it) and inventive use of a few simple recurring props and masks. She also continually finds new and surprising ways to introduce the characters. Romantini, whose background is in dance, is as likely to appear beneath our feet as before our eyes, and his multiple performance -- switching roles and sexes by the mere donning or doffing of a shawl -- is unfussily spectacular; he can raise or chill the temperature without raising his voice. He seldom has to play more than one role at a time, but when he does, he's fully up to it; one scene has him faultlessly alternating poses and accents as a trio of gentleman tourists. His Button-Moulder, courteous and implacable, could well be definitive.

Coyne's Peer is unlikely to be, and not just for reasons of gender, but it has a lot going for it. Her emergence as a writer (notably with Slings and Arrows) has coincided with her new maturity as a character actress (notably in Slings and Arrows). She has great shy charm, and charm is part of what makes Peer, especially in his youth, tick; he gets away with everything short of murder. She is fine, too, in Peer's most moving scenes. Even better is the biting satire she brings to the middle-aged Peer's gospel of success. "Being oneself " is Peer's obsession and the play's; as a middle-aged slave trader who's salved his conscience by simultaneously exporting missionaries, he preaches a entrepreneurial creed of self-sufficiency that denies all social bonds and all responsibilities. It's very topical and practically prophetic. It's also what leads him to the casting ladle.

The performance falls short in Peer's final despair and, throughout, in his swagger. What first strikes anyone in reading the play is its humour, which hardly stops; in this production -- as, to be fair, in almost every one I've seen -- it largely evaporates. The spartan staging contributes to this; so does the unfriendly church acoustic and the text itself, credited to Batdorf "with the company" and probably patched together from existing translations. The original isn't just in verse, it's in rhyme; and I'm convinced, for it to work in English, someone needs to do for Ibsen what Richard Wilbur and Tony Harrison have done for Moliere: restore the clinching force and the sheer fun, of couplets. Without it, the piece seems pinched. It also, what with all the cutting and doubling, can't help being confusing; for all the virtuosity in display, I doubt if anyone without prior knowledge of the text could always figure out what was going on or between whom. I couldn't tell at the end whether Romantini was playing the Button Moulder or Solveig or both at once. Here I suspect the production lost its nerve. It couldn't face up to the possibility of Peer being saved by the love of a good woman or countenance the idea of her doing such a thing. But it wouldn't be sentimental; Peer still has to face the Button-Moulder --and it brings back the mother-motif full force. The play's a comedy, and it ends with the hero getting the girl, albeit in peculiar circumstances. This isn't, then, the ideal way to do Peer Gynt; but we seldom see it done in any way at all, and on its own terms this attempt is remarkable.
