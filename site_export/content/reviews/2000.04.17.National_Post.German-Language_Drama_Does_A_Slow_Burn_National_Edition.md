FIRE IN THE HEAD

Imperial Oil Opera Theatre

If Fire in the Head were an English play, one would take the title to be a conflation of Shakespeare's fire in the blood and D. H. Lawrences "sex in the head." As it happens, the play is German, brought to Toronto last week by the Berlin Schaubuhne Theatre as the first foreign-language offering in the duMaurier World Stage season.

The allusions, though, still seem pertinent. Both those quoted phrases are disparaging: Shakespeares a puritanical warning against premature experience; Lawrence's a furiously distasteful summary of whatever he disapproved of. They both concern sex gone wrong. In Marius von Mayenberg's play sex never seems to stand a chance of going right.

A bourgeois family -- father, mother, elder daughter Olga, younger son Kurt -- is paraded before us; and you can tell from their fixed smiles, dads especially, that theyre living a lie, or maybe a pack of them. (German theatre, even more than most, is still fighting the battles of the '60s.)

Kurt is on the brink of adolescence; Olga is in its throes. Both feel an acute discomfort with the human body, especially those of their parents. In Thomas Ostermeier's production, the family home -- and by extension the family itself -- is laid out for us as if in a diagram. There, side by side on a wide and shallow white platform, are the dining room, denoted by a table constantly laid; the bedroom, reduced to one frame and one mattress; and the bathroom, represented by a wash basin.

Mother and father, who seem in theory to be engagingly permissive, give their children brief, embarrassed and somewhat contradictory talks on sex. Olga gives Kurt, who is watchful and withdrawn, a more practical initiation by reaching inside his pants and manipulating him. He seems to like it enough to become obsessed with her. She, however, has found herself a boyfriend, a contrastingly sporty type named Paul. Her family approves of him; father even treats him to a jovial drink and handshake after Paul has deflowered his daughter.

The tone of the plays early scenes is comic, if not really funny, and its performed with a delicacy and fullness of detail that puts most of our local product to shame. (Of course, German subsidized theatres have resources we can only dream of.) All the actors have mobile faces that are just as expressive when they shut down -- as Karl's, in particular, often does.

The mixture of shyness and boldness in the sex scenes is absolutely truthful; and the end of the first encounter between Olga and Paul has maybe the most creative use of audience participation I have encountered. Paul, shaking off his underpants, has tossed them into the front row; now he has to ask for them back -- very politely and in English -- and in doing so he involves us in his embarrassment without actually embarrassing us.

Judith Engle, Robert Beyer and Mark Waschke give remarkably free and full performances as the juniors, while Wolf Aniol and Gundi Ellert give them solid but flexible support as the increasingly strained elders.

The strain sets in when Karl, who has perched impassively on the wash basin, either seeing or imagining his sister and Paul making love, begins to act out the play's title and turns into a full- blown pyromaniac, setting fire first to himself and then to everything else. Olga becomes his enthusiastic accomplice.

What was an acridly sympathetic comedy of puberty turns into an update of the Jacobean incest tragedy Tis Pity Shes a Whore, brother and sister against the world -- and whore is precisely the word that Paul throws at Olga when he finds out Kurt is revolting against his family, against society, apparently against human biology. He reprises his opening lament about the degrading process of birth, and one wants to shout back "get over it," or perhaps -- since the proceedings are now becoming exceedingly drawn out -- "get it over."

The problem is not that the couples excesses are incredible; it's that the characters don't have enough life in them for us to care either way. They are puppets for their author to manipulate as he pleases, and any obsession on display seems his rather than theirs.

Staging and acting are as good as they ever were, but their virtues now begin to seem like vices. They are taking enormous care over a text that doesn't deserve it.

I have two questions to ask concerning the program. One: Why is it so very difficult to discover the name of the playwright? The author of the simultaneous translation, relayed over rented headphones, gets better billing. Two: Why, given that most of the audience will be reliant on that translation to understand the play at all, is there no synopsis?
