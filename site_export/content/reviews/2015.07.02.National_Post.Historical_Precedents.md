THEATRE REVIEW

Top Girls Court House Theatre, Niagara-on-the Lake

The last scene of Caryl Churchill's Top Girls shows the playwright at her very best. There are two sisters, Marlene and Joyce, having a rare reunion at the house in the English countryside where they both grew up and where Joyce, at the dawn of the 1980s, still lives. I use the term "countryside" rather loosely to mean "not in London." London is where Marlene lives and thrives; she's a high-flyer at the Top Girls Employment Agency, with a clear shot at becoming managing director. Joyce resents Marlene's success and everything it stands for; Marlene, Thatcherism personified, has no patience with what she takes to be Joyce's lack of ambition. Opposites they may be, bitter ones, but there's still a bond between them, sometimes even a fondness. Also present is Angie, who hates her mother, meaning Joyce, but adores, and is dazzled by, her "auntie Marlene." Churchill, with superb economy, manages to tell us a lot, and to let us infer a lot more, about the lives, past and present, of this trio, and of their offstage relatives and acquaintances. In Vikki Anderson's Shaw Festival production Fiona Byrne as Marlene and Tara Rosling as Joyce bring the sisterly confrontation to blazing life, though they're almost too hot when they start yelling at one another simultaneously; important points get lost, including the revealing one that Joyce's idea of political protest is scratching rich people's cars. Julia Course is both touching and disturbing as Angie, brought to bliss trying on the blue dress that Marlene has brought her for her birthday.

The earlier parts of the evening haven't been as great. Top Girls is famous for its one-of-a-kind structure. Its last scene is, chronologically, its first. Its actual first scene is fantasy: Marlene, on the eve of her great promotion, hosts a restaurant dinner for five famous ladies - historical, fictitious or legendary - from previous centuries: each of them in her way a high achiever, though their circumstances prevented them from reaching their hostess' level of success. (And when they protested, their protests were as furtive as Joyce's). Between the first scene and the last, we see Marlene at work, in her new position. She is not what you would call nice.

In this production, the incoherence that briefly disfigures the last scene all but destroys the first. It looks as if it's going to be great; Sue Le-Page's set gives a sumptuous round dinner-table instead of the long one that, in previous stagings, has made this posh party look like the Last Supper. Of the distinguished guests, Claire Jullien is delightfully blunt and unruffled as the apocryphal Pope Joan, stoned to death after giving birth during a pontifical procession; and Laurie Paton steals a lot of thunder as Dull Gret (after Breugel), stuffing both her face and her shopping bag in anticipation of hungrier days ahead. Rosling, in stark contrast to her modern role to come, is magically gentle as Chaucer's Patient Griselda; she arrives late but when she does her patience is monumental. But the others are vocally indistinct and temperamentally undefined, and the overall orchestration is clumsy. Shorn of its wit and bravura, the scene seems merely gimmicky; and the directorial notion of showing us the actresses at dressing tables, helping one another out in preparing for their roles, doesn't add much.

Byrne, the only performer who doesn't double, plays the brittle hardness of Marlene, both here and in the succeeding office episodes, to the limit; it may make her partial softening in the last scene more effective, but it's tough going on the way. The scenes involving her colleagues and clients stumble at the same fence as the Soulpepper production of a few years back; they're written in a particular chatty idiom that non-Britishers find it hard to get into. Jullien has a good crack at the job-applicant who seems to be imitating a male stereotype, boasting about burning (i.e. hurtling) down the motorway and ordering gin-and-tonics in the best hotels; it's written like a revue sketch but she doesn't get the androgynous quality that makes it real. Paton is excellent as the woman who pleads with Marlene to give up the top job in favour of her husband who can't face working for a woman; again a whole relationship, indeed a whole political structure, is summoned in one brief scene. Equally suggestive, and disturbing on a more acutely personal level, is Angie's appearance at her auntie's workplace; Course's earlier portrayal of the angry Angie playing with a younger friend (Tess Benger, very good) has been unconvincing, a generalized idea of a torn teen, but she's very touching as the girl whose dreams are destined to be ashes. She'll never be a Top Girl - as her beloved Marlene is, brutally, the first to say though not, mercifully, to her face.

Much of the force of the final flashback scene comes from the ways in which it picks up and fleshes out the hints, both narrative and psychological, that have been dropped earlier on. It also drops a narrative bombshell of its own, one that remains effective even if you've seen the play before. The play is, incidentally, quite kind to men: perhaps because we never meet any of them in person. But then we have Marlene. However much her dream dinner-guests may congratulate her, she doesn't beat the patriarchy. She joins it.
