DOUBT

Factory Studio Theatre, Toronto

There is a venerable comic-romantic tradition that's known in Hollywood as "meeting cute." It consists of throwing the principal couple together for the first time in some mildly ridiculous or potentially embarrassing situation that will get the audience rooting for the characters and for their future happiness. Think Grant and Hepburn, or Gable and Colbert. The phenomenon isn't confined to the movies; there are two more-or-less likeable couples among the five characters of Michael Redhill's play Doubt, and each of them meets, or has met, cute.

There is Henry, who is a writer, mainly a novelist with poetry somewhat shamefacedly on the side. He met his wife, Helena, when she was out by the lakeside gazing at the stars; he was bathing, and emerged naked to find that his clothes had been stolen. He shyly begged Helena to lend him a towel, and true love was born.

Now Henry is at work on a thriller, which is giving him trouble; in fact, he has all but given up on it, and when the-story-so-far takes shape before us we can understand why. It concerns Keith, who has stolen something and, with the cops on his tail, has taken refuge in a hospital elevator; here he meets Karen, who becomes, in quick succession, his hostage and his lover. "We're in this together," says Keith, "like peanut butter and jam." His opinion of the dialogue Harry has written him is not high, and he complains about it. It's one of those plays.

The fifth character is Melanie, who seems to be living with Henry now; in fact, though the domestic arrangements are left vague, she looks as if she might be his second wife.

It's impossible to imagine Melanie meeting cute with anybody; she hasn't the humour. She appears for most of the evening to be a bitch, and not even on wheels; she stomps through the play, ordering people about and trampling on their feelings. There turn out to be good if totally unexpected reasons for her behaviour, but that isn't much consolation while she's actually behaving, and it leaves the actor (Deborah Drakeford) giving the one charmless performance in a show that otherwise stakes everything on charm.

It's subtitled "a suspended comedy," a phrase that has any number of implications. One is that the play could be funny, constantly seems about to be funny, but never quite makes it; that is indeed the impression that it gives, and it's a more agreeable feeling than you might imagine. On a more literal level, Keith and Karen are of course suspended in their elevator and in their author's stalled imagination. And there is Henry himself, his life and talent -- at the very least -- in a state of suspended animation.

The play begins with Henry rising from his bed in the middle of the night to grapple again with that long neglected cops-and- robbers epic. Melanie, finding him at it, is not amused and drags him off to confront his ex. When they arrive, Melanie tells Helena, roughly speaking, that Henry is her problem and she had better solve him; she still, though, seems reluctant to relinquish him. Henry, meanwhile, stands around in his bathrobe, looking half-tickled, half- pained.

Odd though these carryings-on may seem, we accept them, perhaps more literally than we should. But then, on the other side of the stage, Keith and Karen are playing at love in a lift or are asking Henry for better material; so anything could happen.

This play about writer's block seems to have been created by a whole block of writers. It's described as "written by Michael Redhill, with inspiration from the company." At least three of the cast have authored or co-authored plays in their time, as has the director, so the sweet aroma of deeply-felt communal anguish wafts off the stage.

Henry, fittingly enough, is played by Michael Healey, who has the most distinguished literary track record of any of them; he may not have the widest range of any actor in Canada, but he is good at being the ineffectual intellectual, and he gives the play the understated centre it needs. Martha Ross' Helena is a tough waif, while Chris Earle and Waneta Storms are the two characters who, unlike Pirandello's celebrated sextet, actually find their author.

The fact that they do makes this a very different, and much lighter, play. The Pirandello bunch are real people (no metaphysical arguments, please) who have burst the bounds of fiction; Keith and Karen are stock characters who glory in their stockness. They even boast of the past novelists whose work they have adorned: Dostoevsky and Stendhal and two scribes they are pleased to call Tony Trollope and Larry Sterne.

The play's real affinity is with Tom Stoppard (though Stoppard's jokes are much better); the teasing Tom of The Real Inspector Hound, mixed in with the authorial agonizer of The Real Thing (whose maritally challenged hero is also called Henry). Keith the thief also shares a vital quality with the Players of Rosencrantz and Guildenstern Are Dead; he knows the value of a good exit, and beseeches Henry to give him one. In other words, he wants to die cute. (Not to mention fast; he's heard there may be a vacancy with that Milan Kundera.)

In granting his wish Henry ushers us into the presence of the play's underlying theme; which, since it is tied up with climactic plot and character revelations, I feel unable to discuss. Let me just say that the matter turns out to be one of life and death. All the pieces come satisfyingly together, jigsaw-puzzle-like.

Leah Cherniak's production is very smooth, though I wish the transitions could be faster (as, probably, does she); Glenn Davidson's sets are otherwise useful, and, as lit by Andrea Lundy, they shimmer. There are no great depths here, but the surface holds.
