The American TV networks, made nervous by the writers' strike, are said to be looking greedily at new Canadian shows; and the CBC, which has a raft of new shows to offer, has been patting itself on the back at the prospect. One of the shows in which the Americans are said to be most interested is the comedy series Sophie They must really be desperate. I can't imagine Sophie being anyone else's choice.

Remember the play, and later film, A Taste of Honey? It concerns a white teenage girl, pregnant by a departed black lover, counselled and comforted by a gay male friend, and periodically invaded by a dissolute mother who has man-problems of her own.

Sophie, probably by accident rather than design, reassembles many of these elements. There are, of course, some differences. A Taste of Honey was firmly set in 1950s working-class England; Sophie emphatically positions itself in the middle-class here and now. (Though the "here" part, at least, is very vague. Canadian the show may be, but it has no sense of place. Its spiritual home seems to be a fantasy version of the fantasy New York of Friends. This rootlessness may, of course, be what recommends it to the Americans.)

Sophie herself is no teenager but the glossy owner of a glossy talent agency. Her pregnancy was the result of a one-night stand with someone unknown; she assumed, up until delivery, that the father was her regular boyfriend Rick. Rick is now her ex, having taken up with Sophie's best friend Melissa; he is also, and viciously, her professional rival.

Matt, the gay confidant, is a doctor. There are two mother figures, and it would be hard to say which is the more destructive. The better-intentioned one is Estelle, longtime client of Sophie's and longtime lover of Sophie's late father; Estelle has an adopted Chinese daughter whom she, being an actress who once played Stratford, has named Ophelia and who has grown up judgmental and impatient with Estelle's fantasies. ( Shades, but sadly faint ones, of Absolutely Fabulous, a much-imitated show that is, of course, inimitable.)

Sophie's embittered birth-mother is called Judith; she's as focused on her own grievances as Estelle is on her stalled career, but she can sometimes be a force for good. As played by Mimi Kuzyk, she's the best thing on the show. But most of the actors are good; or at least blameless. It's the writing. And the producing.

The character of Melissa brings back our old friend the dumb blond, all pout and wiggle and benevolent intentions. Otherwise -- black, gay and Asian elements notwithstanding -- the characters aren't stereotypes. They aren't anything. They seem to have been assembled by a computer, collectively and -- in the case of Sophie herself -- individually. She has something for everyone; she is, to quote the press-release, " hot-tempered, loving, generous, practical and filled with a sense of wonder."

That is, she's a blur. What's supposed to be funny about her? Her personality? Her situation? If it's the latter, does the humour arise from her being a single mother, or from her being an embattled professional? The show has as much trouble juggling her two roles as she does; it tends to focus on each one, on alternate weeks.

Tonight's episode is marginally better than its predecessors, despite being another round between Sophie and Rick and having no role for Judith. Both agents are after "Miriam Cambridge, the hottest young star in town." ("I discovered her, I nurtured her," says Sophie late in the half-hour, which doesn't make sense. We've been led to believe that Miriam's already a star, and that neither has previous history with her.) But along the way we do get to hear of actors being malevolently sent off to the wrong auditions.

Middle-aged Estelle shows up for a hip-hop musical, and tries fearlessly, or just shamelessly, to ingratiate herself; she pronounces herself "rad" and says "I'm down with that." But even this goes sour.

Sophie tries to titillate us with a glimpse of a supposedly glossy world and to reassure us by showing us its inhabitants coming croppers. It then reassures us further by telling us that some of its people are really nice and the rest are harmless. Comedy without danger: It's a familiar formula. It's also a dead zone.

The show doesn't have a laugh track, which I guess is an advance for civilization, but it also doesn't have laughs. I mean that literally. What it has is the presumption of comedy. Featureless people make weightless remarks, while plucked strings on the soundtrack try to make us believe that, if this isn't funny, then at least it's cute.

There's a lot of it around. But if CBC is going to imitate American television then surely it should be setting its sights on the best of the cable shows rather than the worst of the networks. It's been known to aim high in the past. Just think: They cancelled This is Wonderland and they gave us this.

-Sophie airs tonight at 8:30 p.m. on CBC.
