One of the rousing lines in Hillary Clinton's Philadelphia victory speech was her promise of a return to the family home: "It's a long road to 1600 Pennsylvania Avenue and it runs right through the heart of Pennsylvania." She then asked her listeners to log onto her website and donate the fare to get her there. It's been a long time away, but she did get two terms out of her previous stay. Compare that to the tenure of the first inhabitants, John and Abigail Adams, who hardly had a chance to enjoy the place at all.

That at least is the impression given in John Adams, the HBO series that wrapped up last Sunday having got finer with each successive week. When the Adams moved into the White House it was still under construction, and they had to keep getting out of the way of the decorators. But then there weren't many places they did enjoy.

We had already seen them ill at ease in Paris, out of sorts in London, and -- in John's case -- foaming with frustration in Philly, trying to persuade his fellow delegates to the Continental Congress to act like men and make their defiance of Britain official. (John's view of the hearts of Pennsylvanians seemed notably less rosy than Hillary's.)

It was only on their farm in Massachusetts that they felt at home; and happiness to them meant being in one another's company. If you want a First Couple who could function both as political allies and devoted spouses, here they were. Their marriage lasted 54 years, and one of the signal achievements of the series and of its stars, Paul Giamatti and Laura Linney, was to make their bond believable and, especially at the end, moving.

By many people's accounts -- most definitely including his own -- John Adams was the forgotten man of the American Revolution. So making him the hero of a TV miniseries -- with George Washington, Thomas Jefferson, Benjamin Franklin and Alexander Hamilton reduced to supporting roles -- was a gesture worthy of the man himself.

Adams, who spent the last and crabbiest years of a long and crabby life justifying himself in memoirs and in criticisms of others' memoirs, would have been pleased. Or maybe he wouldn't. Probably he would have dwelt on mistakes and inaccuracies (it's a safe bet that there were many), refusing to hear any defence on the grounds of artistic licence.

The scattershot nature of history is one thing that the miniseries got right. I wrote of the earlier episodes that all the battles happened off-screen; and this remained true of the series as a whole. The only things that happened were deaths. First there was John and Abigail's oldest daughter, also called Abigail, succumbing to breast cancer, but not before undergoing a mastectomy -- without anesthetic, of course. We all but saw the knife go in, and most viewers must have looked away from the scene, praying for the camera to do likewise. (Sarah Polley played the younger Abigail, a role that in previous episodes had hardly tested her; one suspects she signed up mainly for the death scene.)

Then Mrs. Adams went, graciously and sensibly as she had lived, leaving John as piteously bereft as an actor on television has ever seemed. He himself went at age 90, on the very same day -- the Fourth of July, of all amazing dates -- as his old friend and enemy Thomas Jefferson. This phenomenal double coincidence was given a long build-up but, typically, no great pay-off.

The two men were afforded no in-person reconciliation. We heard their letters to one another in voice-over, with all the affecting reticence that period prose, quietly delivered, could afford. It wasn't completely satisfying; the production never seemed quite sure how central it wanted Jefferson to be; he wasn't in the title, he didn't get star billing, but sometimes his relationship to Adams seemed the point. Other times, it disappeared. History's fault, I guess.

The approach had the defects of its virtues, like Adams himself, who emerged as a man whose dignity was all on the inside. As Giamatti played him, he knew he was right but could never clinch the deal in public. We tend to praise great performances for their variety but their secret may actually be a kind of monotony; the actor finds a rhythm and holds to it.

Giamatti's keynote here was arrogance expressed as diffidence, and so bound to offend his hearers on both accounts. He perfected a delivery I'd describe as vocally hunched, as if some truths were too self-evident to need explaining. This made some of the politics hard going; one wanted him to explain himself more.

All the show's faults, though, were on the right side. We've become used to HBO taking the high road with contemporary drama; they've shown here that it's possible in costume as well. Excerpts from contemporary political journalism shows that, in terms of character assassination, our own age has hardly begun.

As for the actual dialogue, I don't know how much of it was reproduced from the record and how much was pastiche, but it sounded wonderful: "He did me and my reputation great insult," said Adams of Jefferson. Imagine the White House crowd talking with such simple force on The West Wing. Imagine them doing it in real life.
