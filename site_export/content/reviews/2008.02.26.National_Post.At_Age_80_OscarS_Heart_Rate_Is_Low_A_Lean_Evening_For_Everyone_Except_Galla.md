If there can be a low-key Oscars show, this year's was it. For a start, or rather for a finish, it wrapped up before midnight. That's short --probably the shortest it's been in decades. Maybe this had something to do with the writers' strike; four writers were listed on the credits, plus another 10 including Jon Stewart for "special material," but they may well have been rounded up too late for anything elaborate. Mind you, this was hardly the first Oscar year that has left one wondering why it took so many people to come up with so little.

Anyway: no bloated and embarrassing musical numbers, other than the performances of the five nominated songs. And those adjectives could really only apply to three of the nominees, the three from Enchanted, each more vacuous than its predecessor. To think that these were composed by the Alan Menken who once gave us Little Shop of Horrors. To this has he come. To this have we all come. Can anyone imagine, in the golden age of Hollywood song, any picture having more than one song nominated, though many were the scores that deserved it. Anyway, the prize, to everyone's relief, went to a nice modest Irish lad for something folksy. "That guy is so arrogant," said Stewart, deftly puncturing the pretensions of everyone else. (Few Oscar recipients actually boast, unless they're James Cameron waxing Titanic, but most of the Americans have an overweening way of playing humble.) Shortly afterwards, Stewart topped this by acknowledging an error and bringing back the song's female co-author, who had been crowded out of the acceptance speech. Great to have a host who can think on his feet. He got better and better as the evening went on: our own representative in the land of cleavage and tuxedoes. He wore a tux too, of course, but we persuaded ourselves that it was camouflage.

It was not a night of surprises. The greatest suspense came from wondering who would make a first entrance by being discovered inside the set's central column (Stewart), who would manoeuvre their way round the back of it (Nicole Kidman) and who would make a regal entrance down the middle of the stage with the architecture cleared away (Helen Mirren). Queen Helen proceeded to present the best actor award to Daniel Day-Lewis without, as far as I could see, even bothering to look at the contents of the envelope. There had been the same acceptance of the inevitable in the earlier bestowal of the supporting actor prize on Javier Bardem. Everyone refrained from making the obvious jokes about what Bardem might have done to them with his cattle gun if he'd lost or what Day-Lewis might have done with anything available. The latter's award was to me the occasion of what might be called negative suspense. I'd hoped, very much against hope, that somebody else would get it, as there is effectively no one else in There Will Be Blood except Day-Lewis, and by the time the picture clocked up its second hour I felt I had had quite enough of him. Incidentally, was I imagining it, or does no woman get to utter a word in the whole long course of that movie? Even a bride about to say "I do" has to say it in sign-language.

Bardem thanked his mother in their native tongue, leaving one to ponder whether from now on all acceptance speeches shouldn't be made in Spanish; it seems a much faster language. Penelope Cruz, announcing the foreign film competitors, astonishingly said (in English) "congratulations to all our nominated countries." Not the films or their creators, but the countries. Sounds paternalistic to me. The off-screen announcer later reinforced the head-patting effect by informing us "this is the first Academy Award and the second nomination for Austria." Gallant little Austria. Still, the non-American part of the world got its sweet revenge later when the French Marion Cotillard surprised everyone -- including, very obviously and delightfully, herself -- by nabbing the best actress prize with her Edith Piaf. I was glad for her though, not having caught her at it, I was expecting a win for either Julie Christie or Ellen Page while forlornly rooting for Laura Linney.

You see the insidious things the Oscars do to you? They get you feeling sorry for your favourite losers; though, since everybody who gets nominated in a major category is either a millionaire already or will be very shortly, they can probably do without our pity. I discovered, what everybody else may already have known, that Joel Coen is the talkative brother; they were called to the podium three times, but you could hardly accuse No Country for Old Men of sweeping any boards: another cause of the low-keyness. Was Cormac McCarthy actually there? I was shocked; Hollywood is surely no country for him. Diablo Cody, by contrast, seems to have picked up Hollywoodspeak fast; "I worship you guys," she assured her unvictorious co-nominees in the original screenplay stakes. Jack Nicholson now personifies Old Hollywood; and we could wonder why he wore dark glasses when in the audience but no glasses when on stage presenting. Tilda Swinton, best supporting actress, maintained the good British (and Canadian, had we won anything) tradition of being pleased but incredulous: not just at her victory but at the whole parade.

Funniest line of the evening was Bob Hope's on the Oscar-judging process: "A secretary types out the final list, and is then taken out and shot." He said it, of course, a long time ago and in black and white. Most fatuous was wished on Renee Zellweger, who informed us that "some of the most exciting moments of your life have won Oscars." I told you they should have let the strike go on. I always enjoy the obituary section (and, not I promise you, in any ghoulish sense); Kitty Carlisle's name got respectful applause, but Betty Hutton's didn't. The fickleness of fame. But then, who is famous? Any dead person who wasn't an actor is routinely identified by profession. Well all right, "Bud Ekins, stunt artist" is information previously unknown to most of us, but do we really need to be reminded that Michelangelo Antonioni was a director? And who was Ingmar Bergman?

Them out there got to party on (though, be still my schadenfreude, it was raining in sunny Cal) while us out here got, by Oscar standards, an early night. I approve of Oscar losing weight, but I confess that his leaner look left me feeling shortchanged. Perhaps they are making the event less of a blockbuster because the movies that get to be contenders aren't busting many blocks either. Whoever thought the Academy Awards would live up to their name and become an art-house event?
