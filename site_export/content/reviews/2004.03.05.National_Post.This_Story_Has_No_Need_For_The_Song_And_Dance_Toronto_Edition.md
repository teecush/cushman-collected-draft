THE TRIALS OF JOHN DEMJANJUK: A HOLOCAUST CABARET

Harbourfront Studio Theatre

The musical Cabaret, in its most recent incarnation, ended with a flash-forward to the death camps. The nightclub master of ceremonies, who had guided us through the rise to power of the Nazis in Berlin, was suddenly transformed into a pyjama-clad victim of the Holocaust. Many people seemed to think this terribly brave, but it struck me as an awful, and awfully cheap, idea: The musical genre straying into territory to which it had neither the vocabulary nor, in this case, the time to do justice. Now along comes The Trials of John Demjanjuk, subtitled A Holocaust Cabaret, which takes up, almost literally, where Broadway's Cabaret left off and which might be described as an atrocity about atrocities.

John Demjanjuk is the Ukrainian-born Cleveland auto worker who was put on trial in Israel in the 1980s, accused of having been the man known as Ivan the Terrible, a sadist remarkable even among the guards of Treblinka. He was convicted, but was acquitted on appeal. He returned to the United States, where, two years ago, his citizenship was revoked; he now awaits further judgment.

The show tells his story, or at least dances around it, in what we all fondly imagine to have been Weimar nightclub style. A wailing- voiced announcer, who might be Cabarets emcee reincarnate and who expounds the same mordantly escapist philosophy, teams up with a rouged, red-wigged chanteuse who doubles on accordion and suggests Sally Bowles run hopelessly to seed. It made sense for Cabaret to use cabaret, since it was a style that flourished in Germany in the early 30s, which is when and where the show is set. It had been stamped out by the time of the Second World War, which is when this play's events begin. In adopting it, the show is trying to cash in on the legend of the style's eternal hard-hitting relevance (Brecht and Weill and all that) and also, of course, to shock us. Its saying: You never thought such material could be handled in so flippant a manner -- guess again.

Behind this lies the assumption that we have never considered the issues for ourselves and need to be jolted into doing so; its undoubtedly sincere -- solemn theatre people have an unlimited belief in their own commitment and everybody else's apathy -- but it's mistaken. And besides, it isnt enough to be politically incorrect; you also have to be good at it.

The author, Jonathan Garfinkel, originally wrote the piece as a poem, though one hopes his poetry isnt fairly represented by the song-words he's served up here. He doesn't seem to know what a rhyme is. I know poetry doesnt have to rhyme, but if its going to try, then it had better do better than "Porsche" and of course. The "of course," by the way, signifies shameless padding, of which there is plenty; Garfinkel is also a great one for inverting the natural order of words so as to push the one he needs to the end of the line. If we are to consider these as lyrics, then they are the worst I have ever heard -- and I've heard some doozies.

The nadir comes in the material for the prosecution and defence lawyers in the Jerusalem trial, each concerned with his own glory and his own agenda, each treating the courtroom as his stage. (Defence counsel is the one who drives the Porsche, of course.) This conceit -- the law as showbiz, the lawyers as prima donnas -- is not as outrageously novel as the production seems to think it is. It was given a thorough workout in Chicago, another Broadway musical and from the same composers as Cabaret. There is no reason why the poor theatre shouldn't borrow from the rich; the latter has a lot to teach. But I wish the experimentalists wouldnt give themselves such airs while doing it.

When the real-life Demjanjuk trial began, I rejoiced that the real Ivan had been found and looked forward to his conviction. I then began, grudgingly, to admit that there seemed to be serious doubts about his identity. I imagine many people felt the same. Its to Israel's credit that his appeal could be both made and upheld, though the show doesn't seem inclined to get into that. It does, though, maintain a scrupulous ambiguity about the man accused. He might be a decent family man, helplessly railroaded (vignettes of his life in Cleveland are among the shows better moments), or he might be the Devil incarnate. He is given a haunting, mocking double (psychic, not physical) who might be the Ivan inside him or might be the real Ivan, somebody else altogether who really did those things. Thats in theory; in dramatic practice, he's your standard sinister Nazi, a sneering psychopath strongly resembling Ralph Fiennes' performance in Schindlers List. There are also scenes of Demjanjuks service in the Russian army, from which he was captured and appropriated by the SS. He is seen with a fellow conscript, who is a Jew, and the two of them are played as a bad cross-talk act. It's an intentionally bad cross-talk act, but that doesn't help. The bold questions the play claims to be asking -- chiefly, would we behave any better under comparable pressures? -- have been asked a hundred times before and a lot more clearly, and the answer must always be the same: No, very likely we wouldnt. Now tell me something about this man.

Frank Moore as Demjanjuk does seem to be trying and, as far as possible, succeeding. He is, by turns, dignified, sinister and frightening. He also, it turns out late in the proceedings, can sing, while most of his colleagues seem to have been selected for their inability to carry a tune. Their acting varies: At the low end are Dmitry Chepotevskys inner Ivan and the duelling lawyers of Dov Mickelson and Michael Rubenfeld, each of them a platitude on legs; at the high is Clinton Walker as a Treblinka survivor, recounting unspeakable memories that chill the soul, just as the show's creators knew they would. Whether they have earned the right to use them is another matter; on the evidence of the rest of the evening, I would say not. Christine Brubaker, whom I have greatly admired in other circumstances, is here pointlessly abrasive as both performer and composer. Jennifer Capraru's portentous production includes such high-school drama cliches as a whispering chorus echoing significant words from the main dialogue.

We may no longer be able to regard the Holocaust as something uniquely horrific -- not after Cambodia, Rwanda and what we know of the Gulag. None of them should be treated as pretexts for showing how clever and daring you are. Its immoral; its also self- defeating, since nothing can be more shocking than the facts. Treat them soberly, or don't treat them at all. Where mass human suffering is concerned, there are worse sins than being boring.
