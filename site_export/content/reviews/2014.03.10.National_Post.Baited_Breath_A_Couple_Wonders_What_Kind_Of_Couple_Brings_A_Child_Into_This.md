THEATRE REVIEW

Tarragon Extra Space, Toronto

I'm not sure why Lungs is called Lungs. It could be because the plays two characters talk a lot about the state of the environment, and hence of course with the quality of the air they and everyone else breathe. Or it could just be because they talk a lot.

That is not meant to be disparaging. Lungs, a 2012 British play by Duncan Macmillan, is a terrific evening: a script crammed with wit and surprise, delivered here by performers whose own lungs, not to mention their hearts and brains, are up to the fearsome demands made upon them. They play a couple - ill-assorted like most but plainly devoted to a degree that would make separation dangerous - unmarried, in their thirties and consumed by the question of when or whether or to have children. We never learn their names, but the program labels them, for obvious gender-related reasons, as W and M.

It seems at first that the matter of whether or not to propagate is all the play is going to be about. In fact it goes much further, in more senses than it would be fair to reveal. It covers a lot of ground, speedily and without strain, its fluency enabled by the simple artfulness of its construction; its a continuous duologue made up of many smaller ones, their dates and subjects and locations changing, with no alteration in set or costume, as we watch.

When we first meet M and W, they're in the check-out line at IKEA (this is a play of international relevance), an awkward place, as they both agree, to raise the subject of impending parenthood, but then where wouldn't be? Well put it back in the box" they say, leaving me uncertain as to whether they were talking metaphorically about the lifechanging decision on their minds or literally about the purchase they were about to make. Before we know it theyre driving home, conversation apparently uninterrupted, and then they are home, still talking.

They're always a second ahead of us, but no more than that; the only time I felt thoroughly left behind was during what I had taken to be a later car scene but one in which neither character appeared to have a hand on the wheel. This seemed very irresponsible behaviour for people with such highly developed social consciences.

W, at the play's beginning, is a PhD student. M is a musician, though when W actually does get pregnant (I have to give something away), the responsibility goes to his previously irresponsible head and he becomes, in his own words, "a cog in the corporate machine; quite a dedicated one, too, which leads W to accuse him of neglecting her.

The two are never short of things to quarrel about, or of feelings and beliefs to agree about. They veer between blame and self-blame, doubt and self-doubt, notably on their pet preoccupation of whether its right to bring new life into an increasingly poisoned and fractious world. The question of who, if everybody refused to give birth for these reasons, we would be saving the planet for is left unexplored, but then it always is. Besides, the play makes clear that M and Ws ecological concerns, however genuine, are just another layer on the doubts and uncertainties that have beset impending parents of every generation. If it laughs at their desire to think of themselves as good" people, it does so by letting them intermittently laugh at themselves, or at least question themselves; after which they carry on exactly as before. We are watching a love story, a joyous one all told, but one in which the joy encompasses the dark or painful parts.

Weyni Mengesha directs, on a quadrilateral stage in one corner of the auditorium, neutrally set (designer, Ken Mackenzie). She gets immaculate, razor-sharp performances from her two actors. W is Lesley Faulkner, who knocked me out when she played the Marilyn Monroe equivalent (biographical, not physical) in an Equity Showcase production of Arthur Miller's After the Fall. That must have been a decade ago, and I haven't seen too much of her work since, but her intensity, her magnetism and above all her vocal control are undiminished (and she hardly looks a day older). Her role requires her to describe great parabolas of anger and guilt and blame and yearning, one thought or feeling free associating flowing into the next, seemingly without benefit of punctuation.

She is the one who sets the pace and the tone of nearly every scene; Brendan Gall's M, a self-confessed goofball who towers or at least sways over her, is content most of the time to act as her punching-bag, deferring to the unchallengeable fact that she will be the one carrying the baby. Aroused, though, he can give as good as he gets, with the same rhetorical force, though usually in shorter sentences.

The two of them are locked, like any couple, in recurring psychological patterns, and there are times when the repetitiveness of this jars on the patience, but the play always rescues itself. There are twists in the action, expected ones that make you nod your head in agreement, unexpected ones that make you gasp. The end is very moving; I was in tears. In part, though, they were tears of joy: delight in a play and production that got so much right, about both the big things that can weld people together and tear them apart, and the small ones - the shared jokes, the memories - that make both processes possible.
