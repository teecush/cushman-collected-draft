TIME AFTER TIME: THE CHET BAKER PROJECT

Buddies in Bad Times Theatre, Toronto

The good things in Time After Time: The Chet Baker Project remain as they were: the music, the moody and smoky staging and the playing of the female roles, all of them by the same actress.

The major change since the show's Toronto debut two years ago at Theatre Passe Muraille is in the casting of the central character. That character is not Chet Baker himself; he is still played by the trumpeter Danny DePoe, who continues to offer superb simulacra of Baker's piping, pleading way with a musical phrase and of his affectingly affectless singing. He also has the sulky looks for Baker the babe magnet, and is reasonably convincing when speaking the tactfully few lines he has been allotted. If he seems out of it dramatically, that's hardly inappropriate for Chet, who lived much of his surprisingly long life strung out on heroin and who drifted through marriages and other relationships without, apparently, giving a damn -- indeed, without giving anything.

Chet was a star -- a pop star, even, which is rare for a jazz man -- but he isn't the lead here. That distinction goes to Ted, his would-be biographer, a role previously played by the play's author, James O'Reilly, but now entrusted to a real actor, Randy Hughson. The change is of less benefit than I had hoped; Hughson's anxious, amiable presence -- part bear, part cherub -- is welcome on any stage, but the pleasures of having him around are diluted by the fact that it is not until the show's end that this fine actor is given a chance to act. At that point he is allowed to contemplate, through pained and narrowing eyes, the approaching shipwreck of his character's marriage. Up until then he has functioned as a link man and raconteur. It isn't enough.

One assumed when O'Reilly himself played Ted the role was at least partly autobiographical. Even now one needs to believe it for the piece to have any dramatic interest at all. We first meet Ted looking back to his first date with his future wife 10 years earlier; they hear the Baker recording of I Fall in Love Too Easily. The couple are smitten with Chet and with one another. It doesn't occur to them to give any credit to Jule Styne who composed it, though it's possibly his second-best tune, the best being the same Time After Time that gives the play its title and which we also get to hear.

Ted embarks on a quest to find the man behind the sound and soon enough, like many before him, finds himself asking how such a creep could have given voice to such beauty: or, more specifically, "where did the sadness in those songs come from?" It's an outstandingly dumb question that presumes a one-on-one correspondence between artists and their art; and a long, long line of creative bastards has given it the finger. As it happens the only clues that can be found in Baker's work are morally discouraging; its beauty, though real, is almost entirely narcissistic.

Not for nothing was Chet's theme tune My Funny Valentine.

Ted's own drama, contemplating the fascinating question of whether he's fallen in love too easily, is relayed to us in fractured form; Hughson stands and watches while another actor plays his only-ten-years-younger self. The narrator may be the protagonist, but heaven forbid that we should get too close to him. You can call this hall-of-mirrors stuff post-modern if it makes you feel better, but it's dramatically useless; Ted isn't nearly as interesting as Chet, but it's Ted who hogs the stage. We end up with a pretentious frame surrounding a scissors-and-paste bio-drama: the kind of thing in which Benito Mussolini's musician son has to be given a scene, just to give Chet an excuse to let off his most famous stoned one-liner. ("Yeah, man, it was a drag about your dad.") Figures more central to Baker's life make more reasonable appearances, but the men at least are seldom convincing; Russ Freeman, pianist and arranger on the crucial sessions of the 1950s, is given lines that sound as if they came from a magazine interview. That isn't how people actually talk, and Shaun Smyth, who plays all the supporting male roles, cannot convince us otherwise.

The women are another matter; they seem to have been more imaginatively written, and Philippa Domville plays them in dazzling succession. They range from a buxom groupie at an early recording session to a line of justifiably aggrieved ex-wives, culminating in Ruth, an aspiring singer and fully-achieved lush who is the most hard-bitten (by Chet, principally) and hard-biting of them all.

One thing you could say for Baker; he had excellent taste in tunes. They are terrifically played here by DePoe and a backing trio, in a style that some might call retro or neo-classic but that many of us think of as just the natural way to play this music. It may not be better than what you might hear in a club most nights of the week, but there's a special thrill in hearing it in a theatre: just as a song, in any play that isn't actually a musical, always comes as a treat; it's like playing hooky. Jim Millan's production is highly atmospheric; and though Steve Lucas' set doesn't help much, dominated as it is by a bed slung awkwardly and precipitously in space, his lighting certainly does.
