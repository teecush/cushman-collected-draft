IN GOOD KING CHARLES'S GOLDEN DAYS

Royal George Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

---

Bernard Shaw's In Good King Charles's Golden Days was the last of his plays to appear before the Second World War and the last to have remained in something approaching general currency. Like most of his late works, it's an unabashed discussion piece, with what might be called a relaxed attitude to character and even to theme. Relaxed but still active: The characters go where their author's interests take them, but they do so with a surprising degree of warmth. This is possibly Shaw's mellowest play, tormented though some of its characters are, and its current production is in the Shaw Festival's best tradition of articulate ensemble. There are no weak performances and a few outstanding ones.

The golden days are those of Charles II of England, the fabled "merry monarch" restored to the throne after the execution of his father and the brief republican rule of Oliver Cromwell. Charles may be most celebrated for the number of his mistresses, and he is here escorted, or pursued, by a trio of them:Nell Gwyn and a couple of duchesses. It was a time of scientific revolutions as well; Charles established the Royal Society and much of the play is set in the Cambridge home of that fellowship's leading light, Isaac Newton. An amiable but driven and tormented man, Newton is not pleased to have his house invaded and his work interrupted by a gaggle of celebrities; his highly respectable housekeeper Mrs. Basham is downright scandalized. She still buckles down and provides dinner for nine (with borrowed furniture), the list of uninvited guests being completed by George Fox, the first Quaker; the king's younger brother James, a fanatically unreconstructed Papist; and Godfrey Kneller, a portrait painter. They all parade their personal obsessions, a circus of ideas with the king as ringmaster.

This makes it, to juggle historical order in a way that both playwrights might approve, Shaw's most Stoppardian play, but it differs from Travesties (say) in having no central conflict, not even one as capacious as art vs. politics. And then there's the sex. The director, Eda Holmes, tries to unify the play by beginning with Newton's maid having an erotic daydream about the king; and later, when Charles goes home for an epilogue, having his sleeping couch surrounded by the phantasmal figures of his three lady friends before he wakes up to converse with the true chaste love of his life, his wife. But the real strength of the production, apart from graceful sets by Camellia Koo, lies in the wit, lucidity and occasional passion of the acting, and the way in which these qualities all flow together.

If there is an overall theme, it's in the struggle of Charles himself to maintain a precarious point of rest in a chronically violent world. It's splendidly captured by Benedict Campbell, who makes a deceptive start as a mere bluff and genial master of ceremonies, but who can unleash fury and scorn in an instant. The one thing this above-average sensual man cannot tolerate is intolerance, which means that he prizes good manners and that his one real enemy is his brother (a pugnacious Andrew Bunker).

Shaw's Newton is half genius, half virginal eccentric; Graeme Somerville, crowning a series of fine festival performances, imbues him with the priceless gift of making ideas sexy. In a time-travelling acknowledgment of Shaw's own acquaintance with Albert Einstein, Newton is provoked by Kneller (Ken James Stewart, prickly and promising) into a vision of a curvilinear universe, something that brings him both to exaltation and despair, since it would mean tearing up much of his own work (including a world chronology that Shaw himself pronounced mad). Also very fine is Ric Reid's proudly uncouth Fox, a variant on a familiar Shavian theme: the puritan seduced, if only intellectually, by art and female company. This comes principally in the form of Nicola Correia-Damude's Nell, who can declaim a tragic speech with no reference to its context and who charmingly drops a spoon when Newton expounds on gravity. The two haughtier wenches are done by a commanding Claire Jullien and, almost comprehensible through her French accent, Lisa Codrington. Mary Haney is briskly and expectedly superb as the housekeeper who will stand no nonsense (and is therefore much provoked); Laurie Paton is gravely enchanting as, in effect, the housekeeper's royal equivalent, the queen who came from Portugal and knows that, when these golden days are over, she'll have to return there. The royal domesticity, beautifully captured by both actors, was probably Shaw's idealized view of his own marriage. He seems to have lit on a historical period, taken what appealed (or appalled), and bound it together with sympathy rather than strict logic. So with this production: the Shaw's best Shaw in a number of years. - In Good King Charles's Golden Days continues to Oct. 9. For tickets and information call 1-800-511-SHAW or visit shawfest.com.
