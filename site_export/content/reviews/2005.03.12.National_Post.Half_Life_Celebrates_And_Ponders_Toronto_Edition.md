robertcushman@lycos.com; Half Life plays at the Tarragon Theatre, 30 Bridgman St., 416-531-1827, through April 3.; A Suicide-Site Guide to the City plays at Buddies in Bad Times, 12 Alexander St., 416- 975-8555, until March 20.

John Mightons Half Life, the dramatist-mathematicians first new play in eight years and a beauty in every department, runs at the Tarragon for 90 packed minutes. Packed but not rushed: The play, which is set in an old people's home, has an enchanting air of relaxation. If it is advising us to make the most of the moments we have, then it is practising what it preaches. Except that it doesn't preach; it celebrates, and mourns, and ponders.

Its opening scene is exemplary, setting up characters, action and themes. Two middle-aged people, Donald and Anna, he with a mother in the facility and she with a father, make conversation in the waiting room. In his first speech, Donald talks about a party trick hes developed -- he will start telling a story and when, as invariably happens, he is interrupted, he will check on whether his hearers remember what he was saying. Sixty per cent of the time, theyve forgotten. Later he and Anna get to talking about dogs. Donald, a neurologist, says reassuringly that they "don't have a very keen sense of time." Anna, a designer, muses that that must account for their capacity for devotion: Every moment is a new moment for them. Their hurts are forgotten.

Both Donald and Anna are currently single, and we are primed to expect something to develop between them. So are they. But their attraction turns out to be a stalking horse for the play's real romance, which is between their octogenarian parents. Annas father, Patrick, was a code-breaker during the war (this is important), and - - though a heavy smoker, drinker and grump -- he still has his faculties.

Donalds mother, Clara, is beginning, sweetly, to lose hers. ("Is your mother happy here?" Anna asks. My mother is always happy, Donald replies, "but she's not often here." He may not be quite right on the second point.) Clara becomes convinced that she and Patrick knew and loved one another 60 years ago. Patrick neither confirms nor denies it. It may be fantasy, but the more I think about the play the less inclined I am to think so. The play is about forgetting or remembering things, not about making them up. The point is made near the end when a scene that we might well have taken for a dream is shown, in the deftest manner imaginable, to have been real.

If Mighton's writing is both luminous and tactful, then so, too, are Daniel Brookss direction and all of the performances; the various excellences are inextricably knit. Carolyn Hetheringtons Clara, still deeply concerned for her son if vague about some of his details, has extraordinary grace, a sensible and sensitive woman with her edges blurred but her essence unimpaired. Eric Peterson has a mischievous gleam, and a lurking sense of hurt, as Patrick. Neither actor strains after senility (Peterson in fact seems younger than he does on Corner Gas) but both convey the sad, not bitter, comedy of a Romeo and Juliet in reverse -- they would like to marry, but their children disapprove.

We wouldn't survive, Donald says, "if we remembered everything." Even computers, he points out, have limited memories, and he seems to think it's a point in their favour. But he can still be saddened by his mothers lapses, and grieve in advance his own daughters forgetting of that intensity of happiness only available to 10-year-olds. The role, a flawed raisonneur, finds Diego Matamoros at his incomparable best. He plays a fine mind, a gentle soul, a wearied and maybe despairing man. His spiritual and intellectual opposite is the local clergyman, dismayed by Donald's tendency to prefer machines to people but otherwise finding good in everything. His beaming optimism can seem fatuous but, when you get beyond the veneer, a surprising amount of what he says makes sense. Randy Hughson's performance takes the Reverend repeatedly to the edge of farce then, just as regularly and in the nick of time, rears back.

I still havent done justice to the economy of the plays story- telling, or to its feeling for the quotidian reality of life within an institution and also beyond it. This is encapsulated in Maggie Huculak's nursing assistant, who cares about her charges, and may possibly be exploiting them, and who is loved by them and then, when a much-needed better job comes along, is gone from their lives. It is there, too, in Barbara Gordon's embittered resident who cries out, in the plays bleakest and funniest moment, I want to be treated like a senior citizen. (She is. Thats the trouble.) If Laura de Carteret's Anna has less definition than the others, that may be because she has been written mainly as a foil, the artist to Donald's scientist, and because she, unlike him, is denied scenes with her parent. There is also a last dying fall that, as staged here, registers more as an inconclusive thud. Otherwise, no complaints. Springing some great tricks but playing no games, this is on occasion both subtle and satisfying: a permanent play about evanescence.

- - -

Its rough luck on Darren ODonnell to have opened A Suicide- Site Guide to the City in the same week as Half Life. It sort of spikes his guns. A first-person stand-up act, O'Donnell's piece is offered, both implicitly and explicitly, as an antidote to conventional plays with plots and characters.

He has a point. When it doesnt work, all that dramatic machinery can get very oppressive. Suicide-Site, which is just him and us (and him a commanding actor, though not quite as good at this kind of thing as Daniel MacIvor), is a better night out than Unless or No Great Mischief or Poochwater or Bat Boy: The Musical or Omnium Gatherum or ODonnell's own last show, pppeeeaaaccceee, which he wrote but didn't act in. But it isnt as good as Half Life or A Whistle in the Dark or Trout Stanley, all of which opened this year. (And its only March. We grumble, but at this rate we'll be having a golden age.)

O'Donnell, who greets patrons in the lobby both before and after the show, claims to be playing himself, since hes tired of doing impersonations. Having met him (though not in the lobby), I would say hes doing a good job. But of course, this kind of acting is just as artificial as any other, and he isn't even making it up as he goes along. Indeed, he draws attention to this fact, telling us when he wrote the various parts of the script, and commenting on the differences between himself then and now, which don't seem that great. He also introduces us to his sound man, his lighting designer and an onion he uses to make himself cry. If hes telling us that all theatre is fake, his own included, then we know already. And nobody, apart from a few guilt-ridden professionals, cares. (To be fair, Daniel Brooks was sounding off just as self-indulgently a few years ago about how no show is ever ready, and nothing deserves good reviews. I hope hell forgive me my rave for Half Life.)

That leaves us with what O'Donnell actually says. Sometimes he's ruefully funny, especially when genially presuming on the entire audiences feelings of middle-class guilt. Other times, hes imaginatively chilling, as when he talks about his own suicidal impulses. (Though the tour promised in the title unhappily never materializes.) When he gets to the reasons for his angst, he's not so hot. He admits to not feeling as upset as he might over 9/11, on the grounds "the enemy of my enemy is my friend." He doesn't seem to realize that this cuts both ways. Osama bin Laden would be an implacable enemy of anyone and anything presented at Buddies in Bad Times, which would make ODonnell the buddy (in bad times) of George W. Bush. Do you ever get the feeling that political debate is polarized between the jerks and the knee-jerks?

He also riffs on Richard Floridas theory on the rise of the urban creative class (i.e. us and him) without really taking it anywhere. But he has fine timing, and he clearly wants to say something. Like I said, there are worse ways.
