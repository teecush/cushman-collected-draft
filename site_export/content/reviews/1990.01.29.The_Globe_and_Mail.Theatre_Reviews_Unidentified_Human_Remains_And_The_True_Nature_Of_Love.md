THEATRE REVIEWS UNIDENTIFIED HUMAN REMAINS AND THE TRUE NATURE OF LOVE

Monday, January 29, 1990

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Written by Brad Fraser Directed by Jim Millan Starring Brent Carver and Lenore Zann Crow's Remains glows with confidence BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Crow's is flying. Correction: Crows are flying. That doesnt look right either. Anyway, the theatre group known as Crow's and based at Toronto's Poor Alex swoops brilliantly into the nineties with Unidentified Human Remains and the True Nature of Love. As titles go, thats a beakful.

It provides a good overview of Brad Frasers play, which is half horror story and half romance. Interestingly, and all too credibly, Fraser's characters are self-conscious about their love affairs, to the point of emotional suicide, while taking the horror pretty much in their stride. They are not monsters. They are, for the most part, well-intentioned people genuinely shocked by what is going on around them. Shocked, but not surprised.

The play is set in Edmonton, Fraser's home town. By the time he is through with it, it has taken on the aura of Dostoevskis St Petersburg. Both are cities made up of back streets. The inhabitants, spiritually isolated, still cling together in unexpectedly close-knit communities. People know one another. In Dostoevskis The Devils, an escaped convict terrorizes the town. In Fraser's play, a psychotic is on the loose, killing and dismembering women.

David (Brent Carver) is a local boy made bad. After fleeing success as an actor in Toronto, he is back and working as a waiter. Or, as he puts it on entering his apartment, Hello, I'm homo. That is cheekily effective exposition, or sexposition. It is the plays first conversational line, and its first big laugh. Later on somebody asks David if he can stop playing the professional faggot. It turns out that he can, which is probably wonderful for his personal growth. But while the act lasts, it gives the audience a lot of fun. David is terrific at it.

His sex life is a series of anonymous, dangerous encounters, given an extra spice by the unremitting threat of AIDS: cruising plus condoms. Scared of commitment, he consistently attracts it. Two theoretically straight men are spooked by him. The 18-year old Kane (Duncan Ollerenshaw) , busboy at Davids restaurant, is dazzled by him, and plays out an agonized, puppyish scenario of advance and withdrawal. Bernie (Daniel Kash), David's long-time buddy, is more ominously fixated. Married and a self-proclaimed womanizer, he turns regularly and bloodily up at his old friend's door, asking for shelter from jealous wife or jealous husbands.

Nor are women immune. Benita (Kristina Nicoll), a chains-and-leather hooker, has at least a sisterly affection for David. Besides being his confidante she is the plays chorus; sex and violence are her hobby as well as her livelihood and she has a telling collection of atmospheric anecdotes.

Candy (Lenore Zann) is Davids flat-mate, and may have been his one heterosexual entanglement. At one admittedly pressured moment she cites him as the love of her life. But she is his female counterpart, besieged by the two remaining characters - an adoring lesbian (Arlene Mazerolle) whom she hurts, and a bartending pick-up (Joe-Norman Shaw), who winds up hurting her.

So far we have a landscape with figures, the most impressive theatrical descent into the urban inferno since David Mamet's Chicago nightmare Edmond. (Recent Canadian shockers have been of documentary interest at most.) But this picture is really animated. It grips as a thriller. Brian de Palma might relish the scene in which Candy finds herself locked in with the fiend of Edmonton. (There are Elizabethan plays, set in what is now a North London suburb, called The Merry Devil of Edmonton and The Witch of Edmonton . Fraser could have used both titles.) It scintillates as a comedy of manners, almost invariably bad, and at times as a bedroom farce, the funnier for being underpinned with real embarrassment and real pain.

Its more conventional bedroom scenes actually have the surprise and abandon of real sex about them. And all these levels are there to support a single theme: the search for love in all the wrong places. If the statement of this theme is more persuasive than its hopeful resolution, that may be the nature of the beast. Or of three related beasts - modern drama, modern audiences, modern life. All take naturally to cynicism and reject uplift.

Jim Millan's production is a feat of verbal and visual orchestration. One bed and one futon - referred to by Robert the bartender as a tofu - do unembarrassing duty for all. Brent Carvers David levitates the play with a wit and insouciance that he never relinquishes. Asked to leave when both Candys lovers turn up at once, he replies, "are you crazy?" and settles in to enjoy the scene.

But as the play peels layers from the character so does he, and he seems to grow older by the second. Lenore Zann partners him with comparable crackle, nerves more obviously frayed but the instincts, however she may kid herself, of a survivor. And the others are unfaultable, from the disparate earnestness of Mazerolle and Ollerenshaw, through the doom-laden relish of Kristina Nicoll and the emotional and social clumsiness of Joe-Norman Shaw to the true desperation of Daniel Kash.

There is some glibness in the writing, some portentousness in the direction, but the overall effect is one of triumphant fluency. The show glows with confidence, and it's justified. Today Toronto; tomorrow Edmonton, where the playwright with some audacity will direct it himself. The day after tomorrow, maybe the world.
