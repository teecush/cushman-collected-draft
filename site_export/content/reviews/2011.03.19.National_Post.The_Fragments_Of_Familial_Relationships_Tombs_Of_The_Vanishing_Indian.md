For the second time in one week we have a new play that revolves around three sisters, and that invokes Chekhov in doing so. Though in Marie Clements' Tombs of the Vanishing Indian (a Native Earth Performing Arts production) the invoking is done in the program rather than, as cheekily in More Fine Girls, in the script itself. Unlike the Fine sisters, or indeed the Chekhov sisters, the Native American sisters in Clements' play haven't grown up together. They were separated in childhood and, as in a folk tale, all turn up in the same place years later. Unlike in a folk tale, their proximity doesn't result in their paths actually crossing; not at least on any realistic level. Spiritually, they may still be family; practically, not a chance.

They inhabit a political-psychological-symbolical drama whose powerful qualities are sidelined by diffuse construction and flat performance. Line by line, even scene by scene, it's often very well written. One of the most powerful scenes, and also one of the briefest, is the first, in which the girls' mother is gunned down by state troopers (at least I think that's what they are) who she fears have come to take her children from her: an anxiety well-rooted in both personal and tribal history. After her death the girls were placed in foster homes, and now (which for the play's purposes is circa 1975) we find them all in Los Angeles, in widely differing circumstances. Jessie is a doctor, Miranda is an aspiring actress and Janey is troubled, apparently vagrant, and convinced that she's killed a baby she may never actually have had. (The names aren't quite as insistently alliterative as those of the Fine girls, but close.) Each of the three is linked with a white man who has, for reasons affectionate and/or voyeuristic, a thing about Indians. For Janey, it's a friendly detective with anthropological interests. For Jessie, it's her husband, also a doctor, with whom she runs an inner-city clinic. Miranda's sparring partner is a washed-up movie director, at work on what promises to be a very minor Western -as, by the 1970s, most Westerns were. Miranda has been unadventurously cast as an Indian maiden, required to utter a line that she reads as "I'm dying; it is a good day to die, as usual," words of which she proceeds to make fine ironic hay. This arouses both the ire and the interest of the director who, we subsequently learn, has never got over playing cowboys and Indians as a kid. Their scenes together are the sparkiest and the most entertaining in the play, at least until they turn selfconsciously nasty.

There's fine writing in another vein, in the sisters' memory of their journey with their mother in a bus "with a dog on the side," along the traditional hopeful road from Oklahoma to California. Each of them in turn recounts the same incident, the words changing only slightly according to perspective. It does more than anything else to give the play some kind of poetic unity. Far less successful are the periodic appearances, in a recessed compartment high up on the stage, of a woman who offers obscurely encouraging exhortations and who might, at any given moment, be the ghost either of the mother or of a semi-legendary Indian woman who lost a child more than a century earlier. The program, which calls her The Lone Woman, provides more information about her than the play ever does. To add to the confusion, the same actress (Michelle St. John) also plays a succession of patients at Jessie's clinic whose reality status is also in question. Jessie's story, like Miranda's and also like Janey's, starts strong and ends up misty and overwrought ...

The play's constant shifts between levels, both psychic and physical, are more or less ignored in Yvette Nolan's production, which on an unfriendly set, takes everything pretty much as it comes. Performers, male ones especially, are left stranded on the edges of scenes in which they don't belong. The overall impression is of actors wandering through fog, guided only by their own survival skills. Falen Johnson's Miranda comes off best among the sisters, the feistiest performer handed the feistiest material; David Storch partners her wittily in their earlier scenes and rather desperately in their later ones. (Storch is an actor frequently attacked for extravagance. I've never understood this; he's usually seemed to me both funny and centred: indeed, funny because of being centred. But he may have met his match here.) PJ Prudat's Janey presses hard on the panic button, which seems to be what's required of her; Martin Julien, as a rumpled representative of L.A. Law, provides her with a sympathetic restraining influence. As Jessie, Nicole Joy-Fraser starts composed but goes out on an emotional limb that's increasingly unsupported: by the writing, by her own resources or by the stiff performance of her spouse (Keith Barker). It turns out that he, to her horror, has been running what's basically an Indian-sterilization operation: as mandated by a now-discredited official policy. This is at the heart of the play, and if I'm late in mentioning it, then so is the author. It links Jessie's story with Janey's (I'm not so sure about Miranda's) but dramaturgically it's stuck in its own little corner. As are too many parts of the play. The evening may aspire to be a universal lament but it comes off as more of a lopsided case study.

Tombs of the Vanishing Indian runs through March 27 at Buddies in Bad Times Theatre. Visit buddiesinbadtimes.com or call 416-975-8555 for ticket information.
