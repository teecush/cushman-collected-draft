THEATRE REVIEW

Noises Off Soulpepper

Michael Frayn's Noises Off is, famously, a farce about the rehearsal and performance of another farce, whose ingeniously matching title is Nothing On. Nothing On is an oldschool British caper revolving, at a furious rate, around doors and underwear and sardines. The sardines being a tasty new addition to the traditional recipe. We only get to see the first act of Nothing On, though we see or at least hear it three times. This is because Noises Off presents three episodes in the life of its inset show, each more desperate than the last.

Nothing On is, we gather, a touring production, underwritten by its leading lady, an ex-sitcom star called Dotty Otley, hoping to put something by for her retirement. She is also romantically involved with at least one of her fellow actors, with much jealousy resulting. The director has been busy as well, seducing both the ingenue and the assistant stage manager. Act One of Noises Off shows us the company at a combined technical and dress rehearsal, a doubly ghastly occurrence rendered even ghastlier by stretching into the early hours of the actual opening day. In Act Two the show is on the road and we are backstage, watching the company acting out their personal feuds with whatever props come to hand.

Theyre all professionals, though, so whenever their cues come they drop everything, literally, and rush on - or, from our perspective, off - to perform their roles. Part of the genius of this act is that we always know where theyve got to, even though we can't actually see it, so well has the ground been prepared. In Act Three, the tour is on its last leg - or, as the stage manager all too accurately puts it, its last legs - and chaos has come.

Frayn once told me that he had been besieged by offers from theatre managers, begging him to write the rest of Nothing On, since it was far better than the farces they were actually producing. The first act of Soulpepper's production bears them out; most of its laughs come from the play within the play. Nothing On seems funnier than Noises Off.

This is partly due to the physical adroitness of the company, under Ted Dykstras direction, a quality that sustains and multiplies through the rest of the evening. In Act Two, especially, the performers throw themselves into hilarious heaps, even to excess. Its sometimes hard to follow who is trying to do what to whom, and why.

They aren't as limber with the words. The British comic idiom defeats several of the players, surprisingly, considering Dykstra's mastery of it as both actor and director in Soulpeppers Alan Ayckbourn productions. Brenda Robins cant find the right tone for Dotty's chosen role of the housekeeper continually robbed of her sardines. Christopher Morris goes for low camp though he's supposed to be a heterosexual threat on both the plays levels. David Storch has the proper weariness for the director, a self-proclaimed intellectual selfhatingly slumming it, but not the proper sarcasm; without that, and the actors responses to it, much of the play's verbal wit goes missing.

Completely on top of the style is Oliver Dennis as the veteran actor whose drinking problem is everyone's problem. Matthew Edison is excellent as a lascivious leading

man, and spectacularly acrobatic when falling down a flight of stairs craftily shaped to inflict maximum damage. Raquel Duffy is bang-on as the relentlessly upbeat trouper who knows all the companys secrets except for those she doesnt, and Myrthin Stagg is endearingly accident-prone as the theatrical debutante who battles on regardless, except when her contact lenses fall out: a running gag of which the production might make more than it does. Anand Rajaram, in an intriguing world of his own, has some hysterical moments as the overworked stage manager. Oyin Oladejo some appealingly earnest moments as the equally exploited assistant stage manager, though the narrative bombshell she explodes at the end of Act Two is something else that's curiously muted.

Its lack of effect rebounds onto Act Three. The end of Noises Off has always been a problem. Every production seems to try something different and nothing works. The cause, I've come to realize, is that the play doesnt have a central character. Kenneth Tynan described tragedy as "the piling of agony on a human head until it splits" and the same is true of farce, except that the fatal split is always averted at the last moment.

In Noises Off, the agony is spread democratically around so the play, despite enormous hilarity on the way, finally lacks shape and even purpose. Theres no principal sufferer for us to fix on, no personto-whom-all-this-could-not-happened-at-a-worse-time. The director comes closest, but he's too often absent: admittedly a complaint that actors frequently make about directors. His final appearance is something that nobody has ever been able to explain. This production seems to be suggesting that the finale is fantasy but farce, of all genres, cannot afford a change in convention. It needs to be mercilessly consistent.
