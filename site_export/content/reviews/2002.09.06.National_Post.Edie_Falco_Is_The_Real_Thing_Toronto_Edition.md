FRANKIE AND JOHNNY IN THE CLAIR DE LUNE

Belasco Theatre, New York

For a discerning minority, Edie Falco is the hottest actress in America, currently enjoying simultaneous triumphs in three media. It seems safe to assume that she is as good in the imminent fourth series of The Sopranos as she has been in its predecessors. She gives the best of the many excellent performances in the John Sayles movie Sunshine State. And she is now appearing on Broadway in a revival of the Terrence McNally play Frankie and Johnny in the Clair de Lune.

These are three disparate characters, united -- at least as she plays them -- by a common quality: a crinkle-eyed world-weary toughness, especially when dealing with the opposite sex. (In the play, that's all she's dealing with. It is, and I trust you not to misunderstand the expression, a post-coital two-hander.)

All three are poised between hope and battered resignation. Carmela Soprano, being rich and married, is in a different situation from Marly in the movie and Frankie in the play, who are emphatically neither; but -- living on the edge of the law, and with a multiply unfaithful husband -- she has her own insecurities and her own strategies for coping. Dealing with Tony, she has consistently fought, winning battles if losing the war. When faced with the occasional tentative admirers the scripts have thrown in her path, she has let her face relax into a broad, wide smile, as she sends another illusion speeding on its way. The mixture of grit and grace is extremely attractive.

Carmela has an expensive wardrobe. As the reluctant motel manager of Sunshine State, a rumpled Falco goes through the whole film looking great in a tank top. She has inner style, born of wit and self-awareness. She doesn't expect much; her summing-up moment comes when she surveys herself in the mirror after what will likely be a one-night stand and lightly chides herself for her foolishness. And that note, which comes toward the end of the picture, is pretty much the starting point for her performance as Frankie in the Clair de Lune.

Frankie, too, is in the hospitality business -- she's a New York waitress. (The two characters share faded showbiz ambitions: Frankie wanted to be an actress, Marly's happiest days were spent as a kind of professional mermaid in an aquashow.) Frankie's Johnny -- Stanley Tucci, another Italianate actor with gangster connections; he is Capone's front man in Road to Perdition -- works in the same joint as a cook. They have been eyeing one another for some time, have finally gone out together, and have ended up at her apartment and in her bed. The play begins in darkness, to the accompaniment of noises that sound so exactly like two people having sex that one expects it to turn out to be something else. But no, it wasn't a joke, and the lights go up on the sight of two reassuringly middle-aged people, naked. Frankie, soon but not immediately, puts on a robe. Johnny is eventually persuaded to don his underpants, and struts around in them for most of the rest of the play.

The thing is, he is convinced this may be love, and possibly the last chance either of them may have at it, and he is reluctant to jeopardize it by vacating the premises. This is despite the fact that she, with no malice intended, would like him to go, or says she would, repeatedly. Now, where I live, if someone asks you to leave their home, you leave. If you don't, your behaviour is regarded as boorish rather than (as Johnny and his author would have us believe) charming. It's their first date, and already he's talking about marrying and having children. When Frankie says she is unable to have them, Johnny has an instant comeback: They'll adopt. Boy, he's cute.

The play (first seen in 1987) is the kind of purposefully bitter- sweet confection that is as close to serious as today's Broadway is prepared to get. Neil Simon writes the same kind of thing. So does Wendy Wasserstein. They never aspire to full-throated laughter or to deeply felt pain, and you know in advance that the ending will be neither happy nor the reverse. Indeed, suspense largely consists in wondering what compromise the author will manage to work out.

We all identify with broken dreams, and with the hopes of mending them, and McNally's play duly reels us in. It has also been cunningly scored for atmospherics. Frankie has left her radio tuned to the classical station (she doesn't seem the type, but apparently it was an accident) and Johnny calls the DJ and asks him to play the most beautiful music he knows. He obliges with the Debussy piece mentioned in the title, though he never names it; the characters reach the end of the play knowing less than we do. In a similar vein of cultural flattery, Johnny repeatedly quotes from Shakespeare, whom he finds difficult but rewarding, but he could as easily have been reading a Dictionary of Quotations as the Complete Works, since the lines he comes up with are invariably the most famous ones.

McNally indulges his characters but he does respect them, certainly enough to give the actors legs. If Tucci's Johnny becomes irritating in his unstoppableness, that is probably inherent in the role; and Falco, a woman coaxed out of a shell of exasperation and always liable to retreat back in again, crackles with life, wit and vulnerability. Her casual nude scene is incomparably more erotic than Kathleen Turner's celebrated turn in The Graduate, simply because you believe there's a real woman up there. If one thing unites Falco's three current performances -- disparate as they are in background, social status and even accent -- it's the sense they all convey of resilient women with reserves of intelligence that even they haven't fully tapped yet. You want to know what each of them is going to do next.
