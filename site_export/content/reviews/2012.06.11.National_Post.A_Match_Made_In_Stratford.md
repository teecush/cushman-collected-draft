THEATRE REVIEW

The Matchmaker Festival Theatre, Stratford

Unless I'm forgetting something, Thornton Wilders The Matchmaker, at the Stratford Festival, is the richest and funniest production of a farce that Ive ever seen in a Canadian theatre.

It's other things as well, as befits a play with a tangled but illustrious history, one that goes back to 1835 when an English playwright, John Oxenford, came up with a one-act trifle called A Day Well Spent.

A Day Well Spent has proved to be a play well spent. Seven years later an Austrian dialect dramatist, Johannes Nestroy, adapted and elongated it under the title Einen Jux will er sich machen, which is roughly translatable as he's going to have a good time if it kills him.

A classic in his own country, Nestroy is virtually unknown outside it, but the omnivorous and multilingual Wilder knew about him, and in 1938 came up with his own North American adaptation, The Merchant of Yonkers. It was a flop, but Wilder persevered and in 1955 he turned it into The Matchmaker, which was, and has remained, a hit. It became an even bigger hit in 1964, when it was musicalized as Hello, Dolly!

Meanwhile, or rather some time later, in another part of the forest, Tom Stoppard, apparently unaware that hed had competition, wrote his own version of Einen Jux, On the Razzle, which took the setting back to Vienna and contains more good jokes than any play since The Importance of Being Earnest. In a preface, Stoppard summed up the storys age-old appeal: it's the tale of the country mouse - or mice - out on the town.

In The Matchmaker, though, that's only half the story. We have Cornelius Hackl, head clerk, 33, and Barnaby Tucker, assistant clerk, 17, who escape from dry-goods servitude in Yonkers, N.Y., for a day and night of adventure in the big gas-lit city. Their employer, the tight-fisted demi-millionaire Horace Vandergelder, is bound there himself, in search of a bride. To help in the search hes engaged Dolly Levi, a bustling widow who, in need of both security and companionship, is determined to have Horace for herself. She is Wilders own addition to the mix, one that gives the play its own peculiarly complex, satisfying rhythm.

It works at two speeds. We have the helter-skelter excursion of the clerks, and we have the slow burn of Dolly's entrapment of Vandergelder for, as he comes reluctantly to realize, his good as well as hers. Dolly may be the play's title figure but its only in the last of its four elaborate scenes that she takes full control.

That at least is the pattern is Chris Abrahams masterful production, in which each location - store, shop, restaurant - is solidly and delectably conjured by Santo Loquasto's settings. There are also, this being an open stage, some delightfully inventive transitions, one of them evoking a train.

In both scale and style this is a departure for Abraham, previously best known for staging intense new plays in small spaces.

Cornelius and Barnaby agree that, when they know they're having an adventure, theyll shout out "pudding"; this production comes over as Abrahams own adventure, his own pudding, and it's a wonderfully satisfying one.

Farcically, Mike Shara's Cornelius makes a lot of the running, especially when he finds himself in the millinery establishment whose proprietress, Irene Molloy, is his own bosss temporary intended. He spends half the scene diving into closets to escape detection, the other half falling irretrievably in love. Its a treat merely to watch Shara rock, perplexed, on his heels when crisis approaches, as it continually does; an even greater one to hear him step forward and tell us how he feels about Molloy.

Laura Condolln is a totally charming Molloy, and Andrea Runge an unexpected treat as her shy bespectacled beanpole assistant. As Barnaby, equally shy but volcanically so, Josh Epstein is a sweet riot.

Wilder was the most selfconscious of playwrights, and part of his program was to break down the fourth wall by periodically having his characters confide in us. These soliloquies are beautifully presented here; farce meets sentiment and, though the combination shouldn't work, it does: perhaps because each element has so much tradition and so much conviction behind it.

Tom McCamus, muttonchopped and lobster-voiced, makes a crackling Vandergelder, whose crackle gets more desperately effective the more hemmed-in he becomes.

Seana McKenna, an unusually svelte Dolly, moves as stealthily in on us as she does on him, until, suddenly, with her speech about spreading his money judiciously around like manure, it's her play.

Then theres Geraint Wyn Davies as Horaces hastily hired help, who stops the show when he explains to us his philosophical reasons for not stealing a purse. This comes at the height of the mayhem in the Harmonia Gardens Restaurant, which brings all the characters together, most of them unwillingly, with a comic climax involving screens and disguises that, in intensity and elaboration, is beyond any production of this play that I've encountered.

There's yet another couple involved, too: Vandergelders tearful niece (Cara Ricketts deals deliciously with the tears, but has problems with her Victorian demureness when dry-eyed) and the young artist (Skye Brandon), whom her uncle has, naturally, forbidden her to marry. Chick Reid inhabits two worlds of her own as a pair of selectively deaf domestics, one at each end of the play; and the restaurant, at which I want a reservation, is superbly staffed at every level.

A critic once wrote that Wilder "arranges flowers beautifully but he doesnt grow them." Right, but this is his most creative arrangement: a tribute to comedy, in its many forms, that's also the thing itself. As done here, The Matchmaker is a match - many matches - made in heaven. Or, to put it another way, Pudding.
