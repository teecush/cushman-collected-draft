It would be stretching the truth to say that Wal-Town is a film about Wal-Mart. Rather, it's a film about a journalist who writes a story about a half-dozen Quebec activists who cross the country, twice, distributing leaflets about and mounting pieces of street theatre outside its stores. That's more degrees of separation than anyone needs.

It also suffers from a sense of deja vu. As an attack on Wal- Marts hiring, firing, pricing and outsourcing practices, it makes no points that werent made in Wal-Mart Nation a couple of years back, and it's not nearly as effective. Wal- Mart Nation was a CBC News-world production, so it makes little sense to blame the new show for being too Canadian. It's tempting, though, because Wal- Town, a co-production of Productions Grand-Nord Quebec Inc. and the National Film Board of Canada, is selfdefeatingly nice, whimsical and polite. It takes its name from the protesters themselves, who have adopted it as a parody of their target.

We should start with Jason, a freelance writer who in 2004 boarded the protesters van to chronicle their cross-continental magical mystery tour. He is very earnest and has the general air of a nineteenth-century Mormon. He had a commission from Corporate Knights, the corporate social responsibility magazine, and his professed aim was to be objective. Or, as he puts it, I think Im going to have a multi-angled, multi-dimensional viewpoint." Too multi-dimensional, it turns out, for the publication, which prides itself on being provocative. Too multi-dimensional as well for Jason himself, who has a personal epiphany. While sitting in his bath he tells us that on his second trip with the group he was determined to take an active part in their protests. And indeed we see him taking part in a play-demo, urging customers to "step up and see the Beast of Bentonville, a fellow-performer with a golden-painted Happy Face. He's cornered and contained" (their word) as bemused passersby are instructed "this is only a short-term solution. They can say that again.

It's rather sweet to be thrown back into the 1960s ambience of the Wal-Town six, who are like Merry Pranksters without the acid, either in their veins or in their thoughts. The film devotes a lot of time to showing the activists sitting around the campfire questioning their own motives and the efficacy of their methods. Of course, everything has to start small, as Wal-Mart itself did, but by 2004, anti-globalization was not exactly a newborn babe. When youre opposing an organization that constitutes the 22nd biggest economy in the world (bigger, to repeat the obvious, than most countries) youre unlikely to achieve much by dressing up in clown suits and handing out pamphlets to customers who drive away without reading them, usually making rude remarks as they go.

Wal-Mart's customers are, as the Wal-Towners themselves admit, the hardest moral nut they have to crack. It's hard to argue with people who want, and need, to buy stuff at rock-bottom prices. That does not, however, explain why Wal-Mart cant devote some of their billions of profits to paying their employees a decent wage, or why they come down so hard on any attempts to secure one. In 2004, we pass through Jonquiere, Que., site of the only Wal-Mart with a unionized workforce; in 2005, its been closed down. Management's claim that it was unprofitable seems a little too convenient. A former mayor of Guelph complains that Wal-Mart, after knocking on the town's door for ten years, has finally bought its way in. A company spokesman asks rhetorically to be shown a town where businesses have been decimated since Wal-Marts arrival; we cut to a row of boarded-up storefronts in Sault Ste.-Marie. In Stratford, Ont., which has held out, a shop owner, in business since 1938," says that "theyve doughnut-ed every town they've gone into. There certainly seem to be no holes on Stratford's main street, though the town, which I know rather well, may have gone too far the other way; there isnt even a supermarket within walking distance of the town centre. There are merry tales of a Quebec Wal-Mart where the staff had to pay for the Christmas party, and even then, the manager of the store won the prize." Monty Burns lives. And thats without getting into the business of child labour abroad, as Wal-Mart has, with the lawsuits to prove it.

It's all stirring stuff, even if we've heard it before, but in this program we only get it in little bits. It makes the cardinal error of believing that protesters are more interesting than what they're protesting against, and that those who chronicle the protests are more interesting still.

"Is that what it comes down to? asks one of our crusaders. Everybody making a lot of money?"

"It came down to that years ago," says a jaundiced but satisfied shareholder. And there the conversation ends, just when it was getting somewhere. This is a red-hot subject, but the treatment is milky white.

-Wal-Town premieres tonight at 10 p.m. on TVO.
