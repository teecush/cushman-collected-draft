Bring back The Wrong Son, and can someone explain Hosanna?

One of the best things to have happened recently in the Toronto theatre was also one of the least trumpeted. It was, in effect, a two-night stand, though I hope it will return in some more permanent form. The Wrong Son, a new musical by Allen Cole that premiered last month in Ottawa as the first production of the new regime at the National Arts Centre, was given two concert performances at the Harbourfront Arts Centre.

These featured the original Ottawa cast and musicians, and traces of the staging, in the form of projections of Peter Hinton's production. These suggested that the show must have looked stunning. All the same, Hintons most tangible contribution to the Harbourfront presentation (for which he was listed as "consulting director") was in the quality of the acting. We had the near-best of both worlds: the simplicity and immediacy of the concert format combined with the depth of performance obtainable from actors who have rehearsed their way into their roles.

And the piece itself is fascinating, the prolific Coles best that I have heard. Inspired by, rather than based on, a 1940s novel, it tells of Ryle, a Halifax piano player, returned with issues from the Second World War, whose new romantic and professional relationship with his female singer is threatened by the reappearance after many years of his vengeful wife. There is a shooting, and our musician finds himself on the run. He hitches a ride with another troubled young man, Cornell Tremble III, on his way to confront the father whom he hasn't seen since he was given up for adoption as a child and whom he has reason to believe is hoarding a considerable stash of cash. But it's Ryle who turns up at Cornell IIs remote house by the Bay of Fundy, and whom the old man embraces as his son.

There is a strong Maritime wind blowing through Coles treatment of the story, but the score has more to do with time than with place. It isn't, for the most part, folksy. It's more jazzy and cinematic. The first act, in fact, is film noir pastiche, and though its expertly done, its a seam that has been pretty thoroughly explored elsewhere. The second act, though, changes into something altogether more rich and strange, as old Cornell's love for his new- found son overrides any consideration that Ryle may not actually be that son, and as feelings that Ryle had thought long buried begin to re-emerge. The plot, too, revs up as characters reappear in unexpected combinations and circumstances, with a couple of cops in dogged chase. (They also form a chorus, one of whose functions is to act as the voice of Ryle's conscience, which is fairly dogged on its own account.)

The last section of the show is seriously tense, with a narrative and emotional power that invite comparison with Sweeney Todd. Like Sweeney, this is almost a through-composed piece, with occasional dialogue scenes whose brevity makes them stand out all the more starkly. Coles lyrics have a strong sawn-off quality; his music includes at least three independently beautiful melodies and much close-knit dramatic music that verges on chamber opera. It was tautly and lusciously played, in Toronto as in Ottawa, by the Arraymusic Ensemble, the shows co-producers, with Bob Stevenson conducting.

The cast boasted two stellar

actress-singers in Tamara Bernier, an ambiguous ingenue as Ryle's pet thrush, and Corinne Koslo, tough and vibrant as his ex. Frank Moore was both moving and disturbing

as the old man. (The projections

revealed that in the full staging he was dressed in yellow, with his house done up to match. Cool.) Ryle himself is largely a passive character, certainly an enigmatic one, but David Keeley, just sitting on a stool in a black sweater, established him as the piece's magnetic centre. Martin Julien gave the real Cornell a seedy swagger, and Randi Helmers and John Millard were an unimpeachable pair of avenging angels. The total effect was of yearning melancholy with the kick of a mule. Bring it back.

Maybe its because Im an Anglo -- and a British Anglo at that -- but I've never been able to get very interested in Michel Tremblay's Hosanna as an allegory of the divided soul of Quebec, or even to detect how that could be the case. I certainly dont believe that thats what has kept the play alive for 30 years. It survives on the wit, compassion and scabrous detail with which Tremblay depicts the relationship between Hosanna, the drag-queen hairdresser humiliated when impersonating Elizabeth Taylor impersonating Cleopatra (his favourite role, though probably not Taylor's), and Cuirette, the biker-lover who jealously helped engineer his/her downfall. Tremblay's ability to show this menage on its own terms -- no sensationalism, no special pleading -- seems daring even now.

That shines through, along with the plays evocation of Montreal at a certain time, even in the new production by John Van Burek (the original translator, with Bill Glassco), which is rather dull. Jean- Stephane Roys lumbering Cuirette is vocally and physically indistinct; and though Salvatore Antonio's Hosanna looks a peach, he misses out on the verbal glitter that keeps the first act afloat.

He and the play only begin to come together in the long agonized monologue that comprises much of the second half; and they finally close (with Roy, too, beginning to pull his bearish weight) when the play itself does. The two movingly reconcile, or something like it, and Hosanna, self-stripped of finery, admits that he is "a man." Forgive my innocence, but I'm not sure whether this means he intends to give up drag, and whether, if it does, Tremblay is not more of a puritan than we might have suspected. And though the very best people have tried to explain it to me, I don't see how this would equate with Quebec turning its back on strange gods and embracing its own identity. You can, of course, compare anything to anything but this is ridiculous.
