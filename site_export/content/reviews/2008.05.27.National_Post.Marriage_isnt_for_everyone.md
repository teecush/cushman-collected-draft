GETTING MARRIED

Royal George Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

---

Stephen Sondheim said it faster in a patter song from Company (A wedding, what's a wedding / It's a prehistoric ritual / Where everybody promises fidelity for ever / ...Thank you all. But I'm not getting married today), but Shaw said it first, in 1908 to be precise. And in both cases, the betrothed couple go ahead and do it anyway.

That apart, Getting Married is the most relentless of the author's talkfests. The last time it played at Shaw, the production imposed some kind of mood and shape on it by ignoring Shaw's instructions and setting it in a gazebo, a sort of pre-nuptial tent.

This new production, by Joseph Ziegler, puts the play back where Shaw said it belonged, in the Bishop of Chelsea's kitchen. It's handsomely designed by Sue LePage, but it doesn't have the same kind incipient air of carnal carnival. And it only comes to life at the start of the second half when a new character, who has been given a considerable build-up, justifies it by lighting up the show, which is the impact Shaw intended her to have but doesn't always happen.

We're in the Bishop's kitchen because his daughter is about to get hitched. She and her intended though, are less in evidence than the rest of the episcopal brood. Marriage, or the lack of it, is on all their minds.

We find that the young lovers want to call it off; they've also taken a look at the marriage contract and are shocked at the responsibilities he is expected to take on and the independence she is expected to give up. So, says the Bishop cheerily (he says everything cheerily), why don't all present sit down and try to work out a new rational contract of their own?

So they try. And of course they can't agree on anything. While their elders are arguing, the young people sneak off and tie the knot anyway, before a registrar. The world must be peopled.

This supposedly pivotal pair hardly exist as people: A fault in the play is made worse in this production, which gives us the groom (Gray Powell) as a ninny in knickerbockers and the bride (Krista Colosimo) as a pretty nothing. Of course it's tough to make an impression in a Shaw play when you aren't given much to say, but even the talkers here give us stray sparks rather than a cumulative verbal bonfire.

The ladies do best, by and large. Fiona Byrne's Lesbia is all businesslike sparkle, Nicola Correia-Damude has a throaty if anachronistic attractiveness as the free-talking, ultra-conventional Leo, and Sharry Flett is winningly selfless as Alice.

Peter Krantz has the General's boyish hurt but not his mannish bluster, while Peter Millard, initially a bracingly brisk Reginald, eventually runs out of changes. David Schurmann as the Bishop played the first night with viral laryngitis, which actually worked to his advantage; the scratches gave his customary golden voice some welcome new contours.

Michael Ball is familiarly cast as a genial greengrocer; Norman Browning is most unfamiliarly but delightfully cast as the Bishop's chaplain whose puritanical punchlines -- he's an adherent of the primitive church, and fiercely celibate -- are here delivered with unfailing pungency through the actor's disapproving nose.

The play breaks down, not before time, into a death-against-life contest between him and the production's late great arrival Mrs. George, mayoress of the town and the greengrocer's sister-in-law, given a transforming performance by Laurie Paton. She is the first person to mention sex though, typically, she does it in a cloud of metaphor while in a clairvoyant trance.

As she describes it, it seems to be a one-way process -- the woman giving everything to the man--but the actress casts a spell with it. When awake she's no less enchanting, a down-to-earth siren, bewitching every man in town except the Bishop, to whom she herself is in thrall -- platonically, of course. Her special prey is the dashing Hotchkiss, one of Shaw's philosopher kings who proves to be a philosopher fool. Here it's not even that fair a fight, since Martin Happer's performance, all right on looks, is frustratingly short on intellectual muscle.

So, having blazed, the play begins again to fizzle and, Shaw fan though I usually am, I ended feeling that, like the play's less regenerate characters, I still draw the line at Getting Married. - Getting Married runs until Nov. 1.
