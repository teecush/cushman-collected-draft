LOVE AMONG THE RUSSIANS AND THE MAGIC FIRE

Court House Theatre, Shaw Festival, Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont.

THEATRE REVIEW

This year's lunchtime show at the Shaw Festival brings together two of Chekhovs early one-act farces, or "vaudevilles," as he called them. The Bear, which leads off, was the first he wrote and is probably the best-known. This is its third production in these parts in the last five years. Its popularity stems from it being the most endearing kind of love story: the kind about two people who are obviously destined to get together but who, on first encounter, cannot stand one another.

The bear of the title is a landowner called Smirnov, a man bereft of social graces, who comes to collect a much-needed debt repayment from Popova, who is also a landowner. (Who isnt in Chekhov?) Popova refuses to hear him, as she is much too busy mourning her late husband. He was apparently an all-out cad, but now that he's dead she intends to devote herself to his memory in the hope that this will give her a moral leg up in the afterlife. Smirnov, hopping mad at her intransigence, goes so far as to challenge her to a duel. She accepts, on condition that he show her how to fire the pistols. By now, of course, he is hopelessly smitten and so, equally of course, is she. He proposes. Widowhood is forgotten. There is another primal theme at work here: the triumph of life over death. Or, to put it in traditional comic terms, the folly of making idealistic vows -- of chastity or of eternal fidelity -- that will inevitably be mugged by reality

Eda Holmes' production is a high-voltage affair, with songs thrown in, though it isnt a musical. (There is, however, a musical of The Bear. Its called A Night in the Ukraine, and it's the play as it might have been done by the Marx Brothers. Smirnov is transformed into a shyster lawyer, played, of course, by Groucho: My father named me Serge; he really wanted a suit. Popova is Margaret Dumont, and her servant is Chico, who says of his bereaved employer, "she's-a more than unhappy, shes-a housebroken." I recommend it.) Morwyn Brebner has provided a lively translation, up- to-date to a fault. Smirnov, pondering the ethics of shooting at a woman, justifies it by saying that after all women can vote; they can join the army. In Tsarist Russia? I dont think so.

Blair Williams, known in this vicinity as an impeccable high comic, drops his customary suave facade while retaining all his technical skill; his Smirnov goes from unassuageable anger to complete erotic capitulation, with no visible break, in about 20 minutes. Diana Donnelly's widow-with-all-the-trimmings -- she looks great in black -- navigates emotional hairpin bends with similar insouciance; this is the best thing she has done at Shaw (or anywhere else, for all I know). Physically as well as temperamentally, they make a towering team; William Vickers, as a servant in a constant state of intimidation, gives a general impression of scurrying around between their legs.

The second half of Love Among the Russians is another farce, called The Proposal. This comes from Russia with rather less love. A shy landowner called Lomov attempts to pop the question to Natalya Stepanovna, another landowner's daughter. And she might very well have him -- truth be told, shed probably have anybody -- only he never gets to ask, since they start quarrelling, first about the precise boundaries of their respective territories and then about the precise qualities of their respective dogs. This pair, too, may well be a predestined match, but it isnt because of their attractive characteristics; their squabbles and misunderstandings get monotonous. This is less the fault of the play than of Holmes' production; the vocal and physical energy of the previous play here deteriorates into mere shouting and gesticulation. Donnelly and Vickers reappear, as Natalya and the father who will do almost anything to get rid of her. An additional actor, Martin Happer, play the suitor; this spoils the symmetry of the double-bill but permits a series of impressive comical contortions; the performance is, in fact, a continuous pratfall. This half of the show has its due quota of laughs, but they're compromised by a general sense that its all very hard work.

Most autobiographical plays are as much fun as leafing through somebody elses souvenir album. The exceptions are those in which the author's memories have been rigorously edited and fitted into a frame. The classic examples are Long Day's Journey into Night and The Glass Menagerie, the first of which compresses the anguish of years into 24 hours and both of which confine their attentions to a mere four characters. And even in those cases the least vital person on stage is the surrogate writer.

Lillian Groag, the American author of The Magic Fire, takes the opposite tack. She does fit her story into a limited time-span, but she fills her stage with characters, most of them from the same family. She actually chooses to sprawl. She tried, she tells us, to write "a novel directly for the stage," which considering the difficulties of putting a novel on stage in any circumstances, seems foolhardy. I share her preference for large-cast plays over small (they are, for obvious reasons, generally less boring) but when the cast consists of the authors own relatives, the form is an open invitation to self-indulgence.

Groag, in her program note, is coy about the extent to which her play actually is autobiographical, but there seems little reason for it to exist if it isnt. We are told that Groag was born and grew up in Argentina and then moved to the United States. This is also the trajectory described by her narrating character, who has the not dissimilar name of Lise Berg and who spends the evening looking back on a two-month period in Buenos Aires in 1952, in the course of which she turned seven and Eva Peron died. Lise has an Austrian immigrant father and an Italian immigrant mother, plus a stageful of aunts, uncles and grandparents, who in turn spend a lot of time reminiscing and squabbling over their extremely cultural roots; they are all heavily into opera. The audience in its turn spends a lot of time trying to work out which of the characters is related to whom, and how. Meanwhile the play lacks a central character or, if that's too old-fashioned a concept or a focus. The grown-up Lise talks as if the point of it all is the effect it had on her and on her subsequent development, but we learn virtually nothing of what that development was. We're told, in passing and with no details, that she became an actress and was twice unhappily married; thats all. Tara Rosling, who plays her, is reduced to patrollling the edges of the stage, trying not to sound literary and occasionally succeeding. Sometimes she gets to harangue her younger self, who is peppily played by Lila Bata-Walsh but seems no different from the precocious kid of a hundred other plays. There are hints that her recollections are mere nostalgia and not to be trusted, but unreliable memory is only an interesting subject when you care about the person doing the misremembering.

It ties in, though, with the conversation of Lises elders, who, as the play hardly ever stops informing us, are nostalgics to a person. How many times -- it feels like hundreds -- do we need to be told that their devotion to high European culture is a defence mechanism that encourages them to shut out the world around them? (The title refers to the ring of fire that surrounds Wagner's Brunnhilde, and there are hints that Lise is in some way similarly encircled, but that's just something the characters say; its never dramatized.)

We are in the last stretch of a three-hour evening before it occurs to Lises father to compare the dictatorship in his adopted country to the one that he fled Austria to escape. The Bergs refer to Eva as "that woman," and there is an unintentionally hilarious moment when, without so much as coming in on the middle of a sentence, they turn on the radio at the precise moment her death is announced. It's like a Tom Stoppard parody. Lise, in her own emigre's epilogue, speaks of the U.S. as a country where Fascist dictatorships are the stuff of Broadway musicals. The implicit condescension seems doubly unwarranted when you consider a) that the play tells us less about Peronism than Evita does; and b) that when the author has one of her characters say "its all just politics, nothing to do with us", shes quoting that more celebrated escapist, Sally Bowles from Cabaret, practically verbatim. It's a dim, self- important play; and though Jackie Maxwell stages it well, especially a long dinner-table scene that's something of a tour de force, the acting is all over the place. A bevy of the Shaw Festivals top character ladies (Sharry Flett, Goldie Semple, Patricia Hamilton, Donna Belleville) do their stuff in self-contained little bursts, going on and off like light-bulbs. Each seems to be in a play of her own. Jennifer Phipps is in a world of her own, which is something quite different; shes also in a class of her own, which is something else again. She follows her saintly Rebecca Nurse, an unsentimental jewel of a performance that got squeezed out of my review of The Crucible, by playing an irascible old lady from the Italian side of the family who seems to have selective dementia and is still furious with Mussolini; she can exude wit, just by thrusting out her chin. She shares an accent with Michael Ball who, as her son, alternates entertainingly between long-suffering and explosive. Both these actors are flesh and blood, as, more quietly, is Waneta Storms as a maid whose brother is a political fugitive; she only falters when her professional competence overcomes her sisterly concern too efficiently to be credible.

Other compensations include Ric Reid, a quiet tower of strength as Berg pere, and Dan Chameroy who, as a high-up fascist moonlighting as family friend, continues his impressive progress from musical-comedy hunk to subtle and authoritative dramatic actor. As his opponent, an outspoken liberal journalist, Jay Turvey is cruelly miscast but puts up a good fight. The play violates the Shaw's supposed policy of producing plays written either during or about their patron's lifetime. So of course, and more spectacularly, does The Crucible. But thats worth breaking the rules for. This isnt.

- Love Among the Russians is in repertory until Sept. 24. The Magic Fire is in repertory until Oct. 8. 1-800-511-SHAW.
