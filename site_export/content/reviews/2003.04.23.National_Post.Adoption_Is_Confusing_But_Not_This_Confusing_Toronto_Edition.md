HOME IS MY ROAD

Factory Theatre, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - Florence Gibson's new play, Home Is My Road, has a couple of exceptionally powerful speeches lined up in its closing stretch. Both are delivered by the same performer, Brenda Robins, though as two different characters. In the earlier sections of the play the doubling is confusing since Robins' admirable qualities do not include a facility for vocal or physical disguise, but by the end we have our bearings where she is concerned. Unfortunately there are other confusions, in both script and production, that are not so easily sorted out.

Most of the play is set in Romania, 12 years ago, though both of the women Robins plays are Canadian. One is an adoptive mother and the other desperately wants to be. The latter, whose name is Grace, comes to Romania in the meltdown period after the dethroning of Ceausecu to buy a baby from an orphanage. Eventually she acquires one only to find herself up against its mother.

Meanwhile, back in Canada, a woman who went through a similar adoption process some 20 years before, is writing letters to her daughter: a daughter who has quarrelled with her, as daughters will, and has gone off to Europe to try to locate her birth mother. The speeches both women deliver are great self-lacerating odes to the power and the pain of motherhood. Yes, says one, I know it will make me an impossible person, possessive and unreasonable, but it's still what I want. Yes, says the other, before entrusting her sentiments to the mail, adopting a child has made a loving monster of me, but I still wouldn't change a thing.

I have made it sound as if Robins were the star of the show. She isn't, but -- with no disrespect to her colleagues -- the play might be better off if she were. She carries the theme. But it's only very late on that we realize that it is the theme; there have been many detours on the way. She's offstage for much of the play, and when she is on most of her scenes are played out behind a symbolic slatted rope curtain that runs the width of the stage; it doesn't make her invisible or inaudible but it does make her remote.

Gibson, as she showed in her previous play Belle, can write dramatic poetry unmatched by any other playwright in Canada. There are flashes of that quality in this play, but they are rare, maybe because the story doesn't spur the style. Belle, you may remember, dealt with the experiences of American blacks and whites, post- Civil War, post-reconstruction. Its five fictitious characters were living creations, but their actions and reactions reflected and were governed by the broad swathe of history that they inhabited. Times changed, and so did they. Home Is My Road also starts from a historical, post-cataclysmic situation, but doesn't advance from it. The characters are left to their author's devices, and these turn out to be scattered.

Much of the play is devoted to Esme, the girl in search of her mother. (She does once mention a father, but fathers in this play are marginal figures.) She falls in with a family of gypsies (or Roma), one of whom, a young man named Stefan, first tries to rob her and then becomes her lover. Esme becomes convinced she is of gypsy birth and at one angry moment goes so far as to tear up her Canadian passport to prove the point. Stefan thinks she's being self- indulgent, a conclusion the audience is likely to have reached before him. From an unsympathetic point of view, which is the obvious one to take, she's having an unusually elaborate teenage tantrum, rejecting the parent who loves her in favour of one she is highly unlikely to find. She is also making a shallow and romantic identification with a people she knows almost nothing about.

The gypsies she meets are more than usually displaced, constantly pushed from one side of the border to the other and at the mercy of an exploitative if not quite conscienceless local bureaucrat. The family also includes Stefan's sister, Trinquet, and their brother, Fedora. Trinquet's husband is an imprisoned activist; she also has a baby. Fedora is mentally handicapped. They are a play in themselves, a play on which Gibson has expended a lot of stage time and energy but that doesn't co-exist very well with the drama of motherhood and adoption that finally takes precedence. Plainly the author has chosen Romania as her flashpoint, since it offers a graphic example of Western women indulging their thwarted maternal urges at the expense of actual mothers too oppressed or impoverished to resist. (At least, that's one way of looking at it. It doesn't hold up when you consider the children in those fetid state orphanages who actually were orphans.) By that token, young Esme, the indulged child, is playing out the maternal drama in reverse. But making the exploited family gypsies only adds extra baggage. It's Trinquet's child that's in dispute at the end; Stefan's fecklessness and Fedora's slow-wittedness help bring this situation about. But it all seems a needlessly cumbersome way of setting up a fine final scene.

The characters all present hefty acting problems, and in Ken Gass's production each of the actors seems stuck in his or her own corner trying to work them out. Patricia Fagan's forthright Esme is the only one who ever seems to be making contact with anyone else, and even she becomes increasingly rigid as her behaviour becomes increasingly impossible. Arsinee Khanjian does her best stage work yet as Trinquet, but she is stuck with being a nobly suffering symbol for both her gender and her race. Belle, in Gibson's previous play, had to shoulder similar burdens but was given far feistier material to work with.

Even Gibson's ear deserts her when it comes to working out a dramatic language for the Roma characters, who are given a kind of pidgin English, denuded of articles and spiced, in Stefan's case, with the occasional saucy pun. The role reduces Brandon McGibbon to a state of permanent, and not always comprehensible, sing-song. Sean Dixon does a lot of enthusiastic grunting as Fedora. There is also a character billed as Dumptruck, played by Dragoslav Tanaskovic, the production's music and sound designer, who stands around playing authentic-sounding accordion music; it's never clear whether he's meant to be there or is just a piece of atmosphere made flesh.
