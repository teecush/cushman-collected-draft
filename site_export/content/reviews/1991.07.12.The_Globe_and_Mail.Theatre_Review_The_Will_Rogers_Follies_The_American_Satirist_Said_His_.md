THEATRE REVIEW THE WILL ROGERS FOLLIES The American satirist said his life story would never work as a Ziegfeld spectacular, but this award-winning Broadway musical takes the plunge, synchronized high-kicks and all An act of folly? Will was right, as usual

Friday, July 12, 1991

ROBERT CUSHMAN

New York NY -- WRITTEN BY PETER STONE, BETTY COMDEN, ADOLPH GREEN and CY COLEMAN Directed and choreographed by Tommy Tune Starring Keith Carradine and Dee Hoty At the Palace Theatre, New York BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail New York IENTERED the theatre knowing little about Will Rogers, but well disposed toward him and mildly curious, and I left in much the same state. Rogers was a homespun American satirist who in the early decades of the century told the people the truths they wanted to hear. He wrote newspaper columns and he appeared on the stage, notably as a continuing star of The Ziegfeld Follies, providing interludes of comic sanity while all around him were getting on with the job of Glorifying The American Girl. The Will Rogers Follies, this years Tony Award-winning musical on Broadway, presents Rogers' own life story in the terms of a Ziegfeld spectacular.

There is a problem here, and Will himself, played with all-conquering charm by Keith Carradine, is allowed to state it in the show with his customary candour. He says that he told Ziegfeld his biography would never make a Follies but Ziegfeld wouldn't listen. Will was right, as usual, but the creators of The Will Rogers Follies, having put those words in their heros mouth, have gone ahead and done it anyway.

Tommy Tune, New Yorks reigning director-choreographer, has given the show a lavish staging, one that provides more dancing-girl employment than any New York show has done in years. The dancers cavort with tireless precision up and down a flight of steps worthy of Grand Central Station, but then synchronized high-kicks are the least you expect on Broadway and after a time you yearn to see some dancing on a flat surface.

From all the staging, one moment lingers. The Wall Street Crash brings an especially spendthrift routine to an abrupt close; chorines who a moment before were strutting their stuff in exotic plumage huddle shivering in grey blankets. The Follies has self-destructed before our eyes.

This, obviously, is what it was meant to do all along. Next thing we see, Rogers, at the behest of President Hoover, broadcasting to the nation about his bewilderment at what has happened and the shameful contrasts of riches and poverty that it has revealed. The text is authentic, and the house-lights are brought up (as apparently they were for Rogers' stage routines in his own day) so the exposed audience may ponder the relevance of his words now.

The double focus is integral to the show. It is understood that we are watching a 1991 Follies - an item that the show seems to think is a contradiction in terms but kinda fun anyway. Ziegfeld is long since dead, though he is still likely to boom down instructions from wherever. His prerecorded voice actually belongs to Gregory Peck, suggesting that the Great Glorifier has become a lot more benign since going to glory himself.

Will, though thoroughly and enchantingly present, is also dead. He was killed in a plane crash, a pleasure jaunt over Alaska, and the pilot he went with (a great flier, says Will, "except for that one time") is on hand in a side-box, looking darkly sinister and periodically luring him to his doom in an ominous monotone. (The part is played by Carradine's understudy. Ive heard of motivation but this is ridiculous.) The show begins with a squealing song-and-dance about the joys of aviation, but as we have yet to meet Will or learn anything about him the ironic point is rather muffled. Frankly, it was done with more finesse in Buddy.

This number is followed by another flag-waver called Will-a-mania, which is not an easy word to sing. Who, one wonders, is this Wilhemina? It gets the evening off on the wrong daintily tapping feet, since it is ages before we get to see Carradine, who in every sense is what the show is about. The real Rogers was probably slyer but this one can spin ropes, jokes and homely wisdom with an open-faced gaucherie that conceals enormous professional skill. He can even sing, which is more than his prototype could, or anyway did.

The problem of what he should sing, though, has hardly been addressed, and his numbers melt into the parade. So does his relationship with his loyal and loving wife, played with almost equal appeal by Dee Hoty. Their courtship is rendered as an intentionally bad Follies skit, which is an example of a concept eating itself. It leaves us nothing, either style or substance, to believe in.

And yet the shows total gesture is not meant to be cynical. Unlikely though it seems, the best number is an expansion of Rogers' famous catch- phrase, I Never Met a Man I Didn't Like, a closing chorale stirringly set by Cy Coleman to believably benevolent lyrics by Betty Comden and Adolph Green. (Elsewhere their distinctive talents - his for personalizing traditional forms, theirs for satirizing or celebrating show-biz monsters - are muted.) Finally, it seems, we are meant to take heart in our own troubles, spurred by the example of an Oklahoma boy who made good and did good and whose jokes are still genuinely funny. In which case, debunking the Follies seems a red herring. Tune's staging seems uncertain, whether it is supporting Peter Stone's script or compensating for it, veering between the styles of Gypsy, Barnum and Chicago and finally settling - right down to a scene-stealing dog act - for Sugar Babies. In fact, the ground was burned up 20 years ago by the Hal Prince"Stephen Sondheim Follies, which deconstructed the form before most of us had even heard of deconstruction.
