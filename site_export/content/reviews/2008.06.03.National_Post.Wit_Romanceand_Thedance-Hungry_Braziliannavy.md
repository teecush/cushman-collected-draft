Wonderful Town

Festival Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake

The Shaw Festival's production of Wonderful Town is a lot of fun with one major flaw. The spirit lifts when Paul Sportelli's pit band strikes up the overture, which kicks off with Swing!, the most invigorating of the encyclopedic collection of 1930s pop pastiches that make up Leonard Bernstein's terrific score. It then sinks a bit at the realization that this chamber outfit, very light on the brass, is too genteel to swing. Worse, it's muffled and slow. A similar lack of punch and speed dogs the evening, in the book scenes as well as the musical ones.

Wonderful Town needs pace because it doesn't have much substance. What it has lots of are flavour and pedigree. It's based on a play that was based on a book that was based on real life. Ruth and Eileen, the show's two heroines, are sisters who come to Greenwich Village seeking their fortunes as writer and actress respectively. (Ruth wrote the book -- the original one, I mean.) Ruth, by her own acerbic account, has trouble attracting men; bubbly Eileen has trouble holding them off. The wit and fizz of Bernstein's music are matched by those of the lyrics, the best and funniest ever to be penned by the team of Betty Comden and Adolph Green. It's a pity that these quintessentially urbane Manhattan madcaps, who wrote the deathless screenplay for Singin' in the Rain, didn't get to do the musical's book as well. They might have given it the smart 1930s zaniness for which its characters and its time-frame cry out.

The actual script (by Jerome Chodorov and Joseph Fields) is rather aimless. Since the show was written in 1953, it has the appearance of a well-made musical in which the plot sparks off the songs and the songs illuminate the characters, but in fact it's a series of sketches. The bond between the sisters is the evening's emotional core.

There's a love story too: Ruth, trying to sell her work to magazines, meets an editor at The (thinly-disguised) New Yorker: They fall for, and after the usual complications, get one another. Meanwhile, Eileen attracts the attentions of a gauche drugstore clerk (specialty: banana splits), a sleazy newspaper editor and the entire New York police force, every man-Seamus of whom is Irish. Arrested for taking her sister's place at the head of a conga-line otherwise composed of Brazilian naval cadets (it's perfectly logical) she becomes the toast of the station-house, its officers flatly refusing to believe that she isn't from the Auld Country herself.

In this scene the book, the score and Roger Hodgman's production reach simultaneous peaks. The coppers have been serenading their heroine with an ardent ballad, My Darlin' Eileen, which by a mere change of tempo reveals itself as a jig. New York's finest, headed by the diminutive and invaluable William Vickers, proceed to throw themselves around the stage at impossible angles. Jane Johanson's choreography here is a joy, as it is for most of the evening. Lisa Horner's Ruth climbing the rigging in the naval yard to escape the attentions of the dance-hungry Brazilians is an especially fragrant memory.

Ruth, who contemplates writing a book entitled One Hundred Easy Ways To Lose a Man and who is given a song of the same name, is the show's presiding spirit, one that Horner captures with near-perfect style, timing and delivery. All she lacks is the final spark of personality that would make her not only the show's main attraction, but its reason for being. Chilina Kennedy is as delectable and tuneful an Eileen as one could dare imagine. If the girls are great, the guys vary. Jay Turvey sings very nicely but is implausible as a hard-bitten journalist and unbelievable as a romantic lead. Thom Marriott has a rollicking time as a star football player out to grass for the football season, who gets one very funny song and a mother-in-law problem, which occasions a particularly inconsequential subplot.

Among the other Village archetypes, Speedy Valenti, jazz clarinetist and nightclub proprietor, seems in Lorne Kennedy's performance to have arrived on the scene 10 years too early; he's more hipster than hepcat. We get the lowdown on the lingo and the rhythm in the above-mentioned Swing!, a real musical and lyrical treasure of a number. William Schmuck's sets and Judith Bowden's costumes do the stage and the characters proud.

I've stated my reservations. but they shouldn't stop you from making yours. Praise be to the Shaw Festival for reviving a musical that isn't one of the usual suspects but is definitely in the great tradition. - Wonderful Town runs until Oct. 5.
