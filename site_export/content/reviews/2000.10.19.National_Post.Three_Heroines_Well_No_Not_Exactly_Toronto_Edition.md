SHEROES

Theatre Passe Muraille Backspace

It starts like a celebration. Three chicks are dancing and drugging at a New Year's party. The conversations difficult to follow, what with the music and the general haze, but for the moment they seem pleased with themselves and with the world in general. They proclaim themselves "sheroes." Then one of them gets violently sick and goes home. The other two move on to look for further fun without her.

The rest of Sarah Martyns play, her first on a central stage, shows us in alternate bursts what came next and what had gone before. The three women turn out to be roommates. Rachel, the one who left alone, is a PhD mathematics student described (by the others) as a Jewish princess. We find her, three days into the year, still awaiting the return of Ester, an unemployed actress who survives by scamming the system; i.e., drawing multiple welfare cheques.

Rachel and Ester are lovers, and when Ester finally returns, Rachel is furious, especially when she learns that Ester's three- day adventure included having sex with a male drug dealer.

That's nothing to the fury of the third party, who is called Cat. The street-smartest of the three, Cat is Asian, a former waitress turned lap dancer; she decided, or so she tells us in the plays best speech, that if she was going to be humiliated in a service industry, then she was going to make serious money at it. She reveals that Ester also stole a large quantity of the guys stock- in-trade, thereby putting all of their lives in danger.

So that's the central situation: They wait, terrified, and wonder what to do. There's moderate suspense; were not actually on edge to discover what happens next, but since were there anyway we would quite like to find out.

There is also moderate humour; they spend a lot of time insulting one another. All three seem sexually insecure; the fact or the idea of being with a man can be used as either a boast or an accusation.

But the characterization is generally thin, and the flashbacks don't help much. Indeed, they hinder; they are built on the assumption that we are interested enough to want to know how the characters got where they are, and we aren't.

We see Ester meeting Cat for the first time and inviting her to move in; but there is nothing to make us believe that Cat has ever occupied the same space as the other two; theres no sense of the intimacy (not necessarily friendly and certainly not sexual) that arises in any shared domestic situation. Cats hostility is always that of the outsider. We see Rachel and Ester having their first lesbian encounter, which moves from declaration to consummation with amazing speed. (But what would I know?)

I don't know how ironically Martyn meant her title, but I doubt that she intended us to see her sheroes as zeroes. But it's impossible to take much interest in them, because theyre dumb. Their reaction to the stash on their premises is to start snorting it. In itself this isnt implausible, but it makes not only the characters listless but the action; it's as hard to deal with dramatically as a stageful of boozers would be. At one point, somebody invokes Trainspotting, but that (I only know the movie, not the book or the play) had some narrative strength, deriving from the protagonist's attempt to stay clean.

Much of the acting is listless, too, though it tries to boost excitement by shrill displays of forced rage. Cheri Maracle as Rachel shines briefly when she talks about her ambition to transform the study of math by teaching it with passion. Ester, played by the author, never seems either clever enough to have devised her welfare- fraud scheme or stupid enough to have put it into practice. Keira Loughran as Cat is not much more resourceful than the others at close verbal combat, but she has more opportunities to strike out on her own, and she plays them with a hard-as-nails relish thats the only really gripping thing in the evening.

The play proceeds to a half-hearted climax. Theres a blackout, and on the night I attended, as the lights came up and the actresses moved forward for their calls, I heard, from two seats away, the most damning question that can be asked at the finale of any show: "Is that all, then?"
