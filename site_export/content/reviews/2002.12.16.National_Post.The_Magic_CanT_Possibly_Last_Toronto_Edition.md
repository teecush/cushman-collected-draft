THE CONJUROR

Isabel Bader Theatre, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - I never yet saw a stage magician whose tricks I could begin to decode. To me theyre all wonder workers, and probably in league with the Devil. Still, even by my credulous standards, David Ben ranks very high among the elect or, alternatively, very low among the damned. Some of his feats unfold within the space of his own hand; others need the width of a whole stage. He is juggler, illusionist, apparent mind reader, and occasional escapologist. He is also a historian of his own craft. What he isnt is much of an actor.

This deficiency matters since in The Conjuror (which he co-wrote with Patrick Watson, who also directs) Ben appears before us, in sober black though with a lock of hair hanging flirtatiously down over one eye, as an Edwardian globetrotter, bearing news of all that's best in the world of magic circa 1909. This quasi-historical framework has earned the show a past gig at the Shaw Festival, but it hangs heavy on the evening as entertainment.

The visual fustiness, which extends to the use as an assistant of a pigtailed junior mandarin, is obviously intentional. Less so, perhaps, is the irritating coyness of the Conjuror's patter, full of references to obscure young pioneers he has met on his travels: a promising doctor called Sigmund Freud, a certain T.E. Lawrence (who will become famous, or so it is predicted, as Lawrence of Egypt") and "a young painter named Pablo. It seems the Conjuror was able to give Pablo and his pal Braque invaluable help with a new style they were some way to stumbling upon and to suggest they call it Cubism."

Ben does not have the panache that might turn this laborious stuff into humour; he just sloughs it off, leaving it stranded somewhere between old-fashioned formality and contemporary casualness. Understatement is only a virtue when it is cunning and confident enough to count as statement. Anyway, the veracity of the act is shattered whenever Ben, like any other trickster, invites members of the audience up on stage as helpers or victims. They of course couldn't look less Edwardian, not even the one who turns out to be a plant.

All magicians need patter -- partly to distract our attention and partly to hold it. Nothing could be more monotonous than simply being dazzled for an evening. (This show lasts a full two hours, with intermission, which is pushing it for a magic act.) Most of what Ben does could function just as well within a simpler, speedier and -- it is to be hoped -- funnier framework. He starts by producing an endless succession of oranges from about his person and tossing them to the audience, which is ingratiating as well as dazzling.

He makes a spectator levitate, and later coaxes even more fearsome physical contortions from his own son. He saws a man, rather than the traditional woman, in half -- and does it with unrepentant relish. Most charmingly, he endows a handkerchief not only with the ability to dance but with a shy personality of its own; when he is brusque with it, you may want to use your own eyes to wipe the hankie. Half the show is, in the strict sense of the word, marvellous; the other half is dull. Choose.
