Therac 25 runs until May 26. Box office: 416-504-9971.

THERAC 25

Factory Theatre Studio

Adam Pettle, the rising young playwright of Zadie's Shoes and Misha, originally intended to be an actor. That career was derailed when he was struck by cancer; he survived, but the treatment left him with a depleted vocal cord, making full-time acting impractical. He has returned to the boards, though, in his own Therac 25, an early (1995) play, based on his own experiences in the cancer ward. His character, Alan, has suffered similar side-effects: "Hence, he says, the Tom Waits impersonation."

As a description this seems approximate -- Pettle's voice is at least an octave higher than the Waits growl -- but one sees what he means. Vocal range apart, there is a perceptible lack of professional finish in his performance; but -- given the subject matter and his own involvement -- this may be a positive gain.

The only other character, a fellow patient named Moira, is played by Patricia Fagan, a young actress who has given a string of notable supporting performances for Soulpepper over the last few years, and who here comes superbly into her own.

Moira, unlike Alan, is undergoing chemotherapy, and she has a far lower chance of survival. She has a set of caustic defence- mechanisms; among other things she puts an unconditional ban on the question "how are you feeling?" (Alans unthinking contravention of this rule, in a telephone conversation after their first meeting in the waiting room of the Princess Margaret Hospital, occasions one of the plays best sequences.)

Fagan, a fighting-spirit in a head scarf, is in unwavering control of her role; she times her author's zippy comebacks more smartly than he does himself. And this, perverse as may seem, is a problem.

Pettle, as his subsequent plays have confirmed, is a master of the wisecrack; his jokes vary in quality but the best are very good. They are, though, a double-edged weapon. They can speed a play appealingly along; they can also make it seem glib. Pettle's own character here is an aspiring writer, and the one-liners fall more naturally from his lips, rendered more credible, if anything, by his own somewhat unpolished delivery of them.

Fagan plays an aspiring social worker, and there seems no reason for her to be speaking the same language he does, and to be speaking it more adeptly. (Not to mention that he is Jewish and she isnt.)

The play is, of course, a love story, and it sometimes seems to be taking the Hollywood convention of meeting cute to grotesque extremes. Its as if Neil Simon had decided to write a cancer play: A Simon born a couple of generations later, into a climate that was by that time much more outspoken, but still cloaking his characters in a laughter that belongs to him rather than to them.

These considerations hobble the play in its early scenes but fall away as it progresses. (This is a short play and it rides to its climax in double-quick time.) It is an unconsummated, indeed almost undeclared, love story; and this fact, apparent from the outset, gives it more than pathos. The fact the characters are out-patients rules out the sentimentality endemic to hospital romances. We get to see the characters in the outside world: In their respective apartments (they never meet there but they talk on the phone), on a park-bench, in a church, where Moira throws up into a bowl of holy water.

The fact the play is born of grim personal experience predisposes you to respect the author's authority (and makes me feel slightly shamefaced about my own doubts). You also come to respect his skill, the way in which he plays off one kind of defensiveness against another. Moira's thin skin is matched by Alan's resistance to being physically touched; neither can get through to the other as they would wish. The tension builds to a stark, unsentimentalized ending; and one mourns the loss of a relationship; which -- in the theatre - - is maybe the most moving kind of loss there is.

The play is directed by Jordan Pettle, the author's brother, who is a successful actor, making the role-reversal satisfyingly complete.
