Undiscovered Country and After the Dance play in repertory at the Leonor & Alvin Segal Theatre (Saidye Bronfman Centre for the Arts) until March 25. Box office: (514) 739-7944

The Montreal Young Company, composed mainly of actors in their twenties, began operating last year with productions of Measure for Measure, Shakespeare's old parable play, and The Possibilities, Howard Barkers new one. These are plays in which raw actors can play ripe roles without the spectators minding too much; Shakespeare is protected by verbal style and chronological remoteness, and Barker never writes real people anyway. But the closer a play comes to contemporary naturalism, the more exacting our standards of belief are likely to be. This year, the young Montrealers have given themselves a tougher break.

Their chosen plays -- both rare and rewarding -- are Arthur Schnitzlers Undiscovered Country, from the Austrian 1910s, and Terence Rattigans After the Dance, from the English 1930s. These are plays with recognizable, if sometimes exotic, domestic set-ups. Their characters have homes to go to, including some that we actually get to visit. Both works, too, present delicate generational problems of casting and interpretation; they each concern the conflict between genuine youth and early middle ageing. So the audience has to make adjustments and allowances. But they are worth making -- these are two very stimulating evenings.

Schnitzler is a superb playwright of whom we still know far too little (though Toronto, at least, is about to get a basinful of him with two separate adaptations of his once-notorious La Ronde). Writing appropriately from Vienna, the cradle of 20th-century angst, he chronicled the sexual and social caprices and self-delusions of a war-doomed epoch.

Undiscovered Country, adapted by Tom Stoppard, though not that you would notice (apart from the occasional line like one about a man who wouldn't be caught dead with a suicide note), concerns a prosperous light-bulb manufacturer and serial philanderer named Heinrich Hofreiter. After ploughing more or less contentedly through his friends wives, he comes up against a much younger woman who makes demands on him that he cannot meet, though part of him would like to, and that circumstances finally allow him to evade.

He has a similarly ambiguous attitude toward his own wife. He loves her, and would like her to be happy; and if that would mean her taking a lover, he would be easy. That, at least, is what he thinks. When it actually happens, he is outraged, not so much out of jealousy as resentment. The lover, too, is guilty of the unpardonable sin of being young, and Hofreiter has had quite enough of youth.

Herr Hofreiter is not perhaps an admirable man, but he has the virtue of being clear-sighted: He can see through everybody, including, uncomfortably, himself. In this he resembles Chekhovs Platonov, another helplessly rakish hero whom we have recently got to know and to recognize as still one of our own. Indeed it's something of a shock that Hofreiter, unlike Platonov, finishes his play alive. But then Schnitzler's play was written a few decades later and a few thousand miles west of Chekhovs, and is by so much the more cynical.

If there is meant to be something heroic in Hofreiter, Shawn Campbell, the actor here, has not found it; he is rather snaky and oily. From the moment he comes on, though, he strikes a note of commanding intelligence that sets him apart from those around him, and he maintains it, barring a few lapses, throughout the play. By the end, he has imposed himself on his own terms. Amy Price- Francis, too, grows steadily as his wife, gracious and troubled, especially when she comes to measure out, with perfect candour, the depths of her erotic surrender. Both rise finely to the last act, in which the play does reach Chekhovian heights.

Hofreiter, returning from a duel, encounters his dead opponents unknowing mother (Kate Hemblen, also admirable) in the presence of his own wife. Schnitzler is a master of irony, and this scene is the tragic-comic variety made flesh. Stoppard made a cunning choice of English title. The undiscovered country," according to the Montreal program, signifies human behaviour. But when Hamlet coined the phrase he was talking about death, and that is as much on these characters' minds as love is. In fact, they go together.

There is plenty of encircling social comedy as well, especially in the middle act, which finds most of the characters holidaying in the Alps. Here, Hofreiter meets a hotelier (Robin Wilcock, excellently iced) whose heartless skill in personal relations leaves our Heinrich looking and feeling like an amateur. Other attractions hereabouts include a frazzle-haired mother-ridden poet (Eric Davis) and an explosively indignant guest (Shawn Riedle) whose anti- establishment tirades when his laundry goes missing prophetically approach Hitlerian intensity. There are also other characters whom one simply gets mixed up. Here we really miss the weight and experience that can create an identity simply by coming onstage. Even those playing their own young age come short of the essential worldliness.

Abundant doubling, though unavoidable, compounds the problem; and Bill Glassco's shoestring production does not provide much atmospheric support. His spare approach worked better in last years Measure (also, oddly enough, a sex comedy set in Vienna) than it does here.

In this regard Chris Abrahams production of the Rattigan has a distinct advantage. For one thing, it only requires one set, a Mayfair flat, which the designer, Vincent Lefevre, has managed with what seems in this context like stunning opulence. One scene is given over to a party that Abraham, also known hitherto as a distinctly puritanical director, has staged with remarkable mordant verve; it's the kind of thing you expect to see at the Shaw Festival (which should be noting his name). Then too, though Rattigan can hardly match Schnitzler for satiric intensity or philosophic breadth, he does have the edge as a story-teller. His play proceeds in a straight line, while Schnitzler's keeps moving into eddies and piling up parallels. With Schnitzler you would quite like to know what happens next. With Rattigan you cant wait to find out.

After the Dance was Rattigans second produced play, and his first -- almost his only -- commercial flop. He virtually disowned it, and it has only recently been reinstated. In fact it might be billed as Rediscovered Country, and it pairs excellently with the Schnitzler.

Rattigan gives us a society on the brink of the next war: the left-over bright young things of the 1920s versus the tough new generation who can see what's coming to them. It's like the continuing war between the Baby Boomers still stuck in the 60s and their younger successors who cant see what all the fuss is about. The differences are that a) in this play it's the younger ones who are in the majority and b) Rattigan's nostalgists make no pretense of being idealists. What they preach, and continue to practise is hedonism. This is a play about people who, when they drank, drank.

Presiding lush is Peter Scott-Fowler, who is rich and a historian, as far as hes anything. Like Hofreiter, he is amiably unfaithful to a wife who minds a lot more than she lets on. Like Hofreiter, he encounters a young woman who wants to reform him; the difference is that this one looks like succeeding. She gets him on the wagon; she gets him divorcing his wife; she damn near gets him writing that book hes supposed to have in him. It is, needless to say, a strain, and we wait to see whether she will succeed, our suspense diversified by our never quite knowing whether or not we want her to.

This, apart from narrative flair, is the play's great strength: It divides its sympathies very fairly. The intrusive Helen is both a pain and a paragon, and Michelle Monteith (who in the other play is overstretched in a middle-aged role) does full justice to both her aspects. So, conversely, does Robin Wilcock, who last year made a fine job of Shakespeare's lapsed puritan Angelo; he does equally well as Rattigans lapsed hedonist, for whom he finds the right quizzical weight to compel both our amusement and our sympathy -- even our respect. Kate Hemblen plays his wife, who doesnt take the pledge; and though the Mayfair drawl doesn't seem to sit on her lips with absolute naturalness, she moves easily within it, from drunk and happy to drunk and desperate. If she sometimes seems to be trying too hard, then so, it turns out, is the character.

The inner circle is completed by Joe Cobden, a new actor of remarkable ease and insistent promise, who contributes a delightful boyish cameo to the Schnitzler and here ripens, beautifully and almost effortlessly, into a permanent, and permanently sozzled, house guest. He is the play's commentator, and his commentary is, as he admits, outrageously self-interested. It is also, most of the time, absolutely accurate.

The others fit in with varying degrees of accuracy, but the frame sustains them. The protagonist has a habit of siting down at the piano and playing Noel Cowards World Weary, thereby acknowledging both a mood and a generational allegiance; his contemporaries are Cowards little ladies still dancing, his poor little rich girls with all their stitches dropped but still locked into their mad jazz pattern. Rattigan disapproves but he does not condemn, and he proves them still worthy of attention. The play probes less deeply into human nature than its Viennese companion, and moralizes more, but it is both honest and attractive.

Matching new talents to old plays, the Montreal Young Company is in some ways a paradoxical enterprise but it delivers a lot of what it promises, and its importance to the Canadian theatre, in terms of both actors and repertoire, extends beyond its home city.
