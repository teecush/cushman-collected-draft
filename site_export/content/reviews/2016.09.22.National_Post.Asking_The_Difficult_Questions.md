THEATREREVIEW

Aunt Dan and Lemon Theatre Passe Muraille Backspace

Lemon, short for Leonora, is a solitary insomniac woman of indeterminate age who subsists on fruit and vegetable juices. She doesn't watch TV or listen to the radio, and the play in which she appears predates the Internet, but she probably wouldn't have had much use for it even if it had existed. She likes to lie in bed and read about the Holocaust, though the word itself is unlikely to have passed her lips. The formative influence on her childhood was Dan, short for Daniella, a bisexual Oxford don with a passionate admiration for Henry Kissinger.

Aunt Dan and Lemon, first seen in 1985, is the work of Wallace Shawn, known apart from his plays for being a droll cameo performer in movies and for being the son of William Shawn, for many years the editor of The New Yorker.

Aunt Dan is Lemon's aunt only by courtesy; she's a close friend of Lemon's parents and a constant presence in their house. She's an American expat, as is Lemon's father who married an Englishwoman and stayed; he professes to love nearly everything about England, especially its crumpets. He's an executive in an auto-parts company; it's a job despised by his old friends from university but he likes to point out that it comes with a load of hiring-and-firing responsibility that they couldn't possibly handle. He also has regular rows with his wife over their daughter's eating disorders.

All this is recalled for us by Lemon as the play's narrator. But what she mainly remembers are her conversations with Aunt Dan, especially the ones that took place when she was 11. Dan's main topics are sex and Kissinger, with an occasional overlap between the two. She passionately defends Kissinger's aggressive foreign policies (in her admiring mind they always seem to be his, not any president's); she argues - or rather declares - that such actions are necessary to protect the lifestyles of people like themselves. She's also sure that Kissinger, like Lemon's dad, agonizes over every painful decision. She once caught a glimpse of Kissinger in a restaurant and could see how compassionate he was. Lemon's mother disagrees, but all she can really say is that it seems wrong. She's expressing the audience's instinctive feelings, and she isn't doing a very eloquent job of it.

At the beginning and end of this play, Lemon ruminates on the care the Nazis took to conceal from the Jews what was about to happen to them and on the brutality with which they drove them, women and children included, into the gas chambers when concealment was no longer possible. She then wonders whether we condemn the Nazis only because they were so unashamed about what they did, when in fact any society will kill when it thinks it has to. And any individual will do the same. And this "compassion" that people (like her mother?) go on about: she's felt it watching movies or reading novels, but never in real life.

This is purposely awkward stuff, the most unsettling piece of devil's advocacy since the heyday of George Bernard Shaw. If the play no longer feels as shocking as it reportedly did 30 years ago, that may be partly because the name Kissinger no longer presses as many buttons. It's still a thoroughly compelling piece of theatrical writing, despite being nearly all talk, no action. Indeed, when it does get physical is when the interest slackens. Lemon, as far as we can tell, is sexless; Aunt Dan certainly isn't, or wasn't. She loves telling Lemon about Mindy, a callgirl friend whom she fancied and who would do anything for money, including kill a client. Dan has no problem with this, but it's a problem for the play; it shifts her character from being a principled if misguided advocate of ruthless realpolitik to being turned on by violence for its own sake. The play proposes a continuum from Aunt Dan's apparent amorality to Lemon's theoretical rejection of any morality at all, and dares us to prove either of them wrong. Dragging in crime-fiction lets them, and us, off the hook.

The play gets an excellent revival from a new outfit, Shadowtime Productions, directed by Dan Spurgeon on a set, by Elizabeth Traicus, that elegantly combines elements from the drama's shifting times and locales. Joanne Latimer, a flamboyantly handsome Dan, makes every line count; Helen Juvonen does the same for Lemon, and has maybe the harder task since she has to be both nagging, at our consciences, and naive.

Both are idiomatically exact, as are the rest of the cast, including Breton Lalama as Mindy and Philip Cairns who plays a variety of roles and is a dead ringer for Seinfeld's Newman. In an essay quoted in the program, Shawn wrote "we might still be able to prevent the complete triumph of evil if we recognize that the complete triumph of evil really is a possibility and that the partial triumph of evil has already occurred." He wrote that in 1986.

Until September 25.
