THE ADVENTURES OF ALI & ALI AND THE AXES OF EVIL

Theatre Passe Muraille, Toronto

There are Ali Hakim, named after the peddler in Oklahoma!, and Ali Ababwa, named after the genie in Aladdin. They come from the unmapped country of Agraba, and right now they are in Canada, hell- bent to entertain us. They are also out to earn a buck by any other means possible, including hawking anything that isn't nailed down, and to get asylum.

That is the premise behind The Adventures of Ali & Ali and the Axes of Evil, a funny, scattershot show that has come to us from Vancouver, having existed before that as a radio comedy item. The Axes of Evil is a terrorist group from whom the two Alis are pledged to protect us. We in the theatre are particularly at risk because, we are told, terrorists are likeliest to strike at groups of people enfeebled by boredom.

Camyar Chai (Ali Hakim) and Marcus Youssef (Ali Ababwa) are respectively of Iranian and Egyptian extraction, though their characters seem to stand for the entire Middle East. (Well, almost. They dont stand for Israel. Not in any sense.) Their humour, though, is double-edged. They are likely to strike at the whole multiculturalism industry or at least at what it has actually become. Broadly but gleefully, they present mini-dramas in which immigrant sons who wish to assimilate clash with immigrant fathers who dont. One of the unregenerate dads is a butcher, who seems to be engaged in chopping up beavers; I'm not sure whether this would make him an exceptionally bad Canadian or an exceptionally good one. To be honest, I'm not even sure if Ive seen any plays that resemble the ones theyre skewering, but I'm sure I would hate them if I did. Anyway, I get the idea.

Ali and Ali also tell us about Agraba's native religious cultists, the Sammites, who worship Sammy Davis Jr. It seems that moderate Sammites, who might settle for a chick and a martini, have been overcome by frenzied hard-liners who insist on singing themselves hoarse and tap dancing until their feet are worn into stumps. I was hoping this notion would turn into a running gag -- maybe they could impersonate themselves into an identity crisis -- but in fact it makes just the one appearance. (Possibly just as well. The show isnt always that prudent.)

The Alis are more imaginative in their self-satire than when they are taking on the opposition. Their George Bush jokes are weak, and their Tony Blairs are worse. On the other hand, it would be hard to resist the earnest anti-terrorism operative (Pakistani, with a maple leaf) who turns up in a series of nifty video bits to lecture us on the degree of danger we are in. The bottom rungs of this very Canadian ladder are Decaf (which I think means "you can go out and play") and Double Double (which might mean go out and play but wrap up warm.) Near the top, and frightening, is Boot Cut, a level of terrorism alert brought to you by the Gap.

This Dr. Mohanides Panir (his name as it appears on the screen is much longer, but I can only pass on the edited version that theyve squeezed into the program) appears three times and may, like this sentence, outstay his welcome. There is, to be sure, a classic law of comedy known as the Rule of Three, but nobody said it was immutable. In a similar way, though I enjoyed the way the twosome kept buttering up Ian Hanomansing ("celebrated handsome brown fellow"), they exhausted my patience when they made him the supposed hero of some future movie epic about the Iraq War, one that might be entitled Saving Private Ryan's Football Jersey. There's an idea here, but it goes on forever and is carried entirely by narration. Here we could really use some film clips.

There is a third member of the cast, a white actor named Tom Butler. He plays two roles. One is the Alis long-suffering stooge (they dress him up like some houri out of Kismet and address him, in full knowledge, as Tim) who gets to reflect bitterly on the fine career he had in movies before coming to this pass; the other is a kilted theatre manager who marches periodically on whenever the act threatens to overstep some kind of mark. This last extra layer strikes me as a rather craven safety net ("You dont believe these guys are outrageous; well he does"), but the character sort of justifies himself when he delves into his own historic past to recount the last battle of William Wallace. He moves between the chilling and the absurd, from the grisly details of Wallace's execution by the English to the claim that his severed head was placed upon a pole -- and then they killed him. This shows that even Dead White European Males have their historic grievances, and that they can be just as sincere and just as self-indulgent about them as anybody else.

And so it is with the Alis. One of them has lost his fiancee; the other wants to send money home to his imprisoned wife. It's laid on thick but it isnt dismissible. The pair, like most double acts since 1955, have a whiff of Waiting for Godot about them, and their battered-but-hopeful act allows them to get away with an ending that is probably soppy and definitely touching. Chai and Youssef are wily, charming performers who know how to work an audience and one another. I was in their pockets from the moment one of them told a rude story about a leprechaun (great accent, by the way) and I remained there, despite some restive moments, until the end, which features a surprise appearance by their sponsor Osama bin Laden, who isnt really much into the arts but figures he might as well give it a try. (They suggest that he improve his outfit's image by re- branding it al-Qaeda 2.0.)

Guillermo Verdecchia collaborated with the two stars on the script and he is also the director. His production is not what you would call polished, but chances are the show wouldn't work if it were.
