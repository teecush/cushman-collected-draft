THE CELLO SUITES

By Eric Siblin, Anansi, 304 pp., $29.95

- - -

At the beginning of the 21st century, Eric Siblin had an epiphany. He was staying at a hotel in Toronto, close by the Royal Conservatory of Music and, having just quit a job in Montreal as pop critic for The Gazette, was searching for some other music that might, he says, "occupy a central place in my life."

He saw a listing for a performance at the RCM of Bach's Cello Suites, went along, and had his life changed. Eventually he wrote a book: one that's partly about the composer, partly about Pablo Casals, who rediscovered the six Suites and made the talismanic recordings of them, and partly -- a large part -- about Siblin himself.

Bach does that to people. Of all classical composers, he seems the one most likely to reach out to those immersed in what academics used to call "secondary music." Siblin himself points to Disney's Fantasia (Stokowksi shaking hands with Mickey Mouse) and to the realization, in the 1930s and since, that Bach swings, which inspired, among others, Benny Goodman (Bach Goes to Town) and Django Reinhardt and, of course, the Swingle Singers, who made Glenn Gould feel "like lying on the floor and kicking my heels."

Contemplating "Bach's street cred," Siblin notes that "his peers in the classical pantheon, Mozart and Beethoven, aren't tampered with nearly as much. . . . Perhaps it has something to do with the steady beat of baroque music . . . or the possibility that Bach's reportedly great skill at improvisation is somehow embedded in the music itself."

Bach was a great rearranger, of his own compositions and others'. The Cello Suites, a lengthy series of unsupported solos for what was then a new-fangled instrument unaccustomed to such exposure, may seem an ascetic, rather forbidding introduction; more people probably get hooked on Bach via the Brandenburgs or the St. Matthew Passion. But each suite is in fact a set of dance pieces, rooted in popular rhythms.

Siblin constructs his book as something between a homage and a seance. His opus, too, is divided into six sections, each sub-divided, like each suite, into six sections: Prelude, Allemande, Courante, Sarabande, Gigue and one variable movement that might be Minuet, Boure or Gavotte. It's difficult, though, to see any particular correspondence between the sub-headings and their content. Except, that is, for the way in which Siblin begins each of his Preludes with an impressionistic account of what Bach was up to in each of his. These are evocative and helpful; he's especially good on the ominous fifth prelude and the explosive sixth. (Meaning, I think I can hear what he hears.) Otherwise there is, for better or worse -- I think for worse -- very little musical description and no musical analysis.

The three narrative threads unfold alternately, in what's presumably meant to be counterpoint, and they get in one another's way, as in a destructive kind of Siblin rivalry. The sections on the composer himself are the least satisfying. Siblin chafes at the stuffy image of Bach inspired by his best-known portrait (Shakespeare, his literary equivalent, has had the same image problem), pointing out that Bach fought a duel -- with a bassoonist -- and was sent to prison for defying a patron. But these were early, passing excitements.

Most of the time he seems to have led an industrious life, chiefly remarkable for the quantity as well as quality of the music he produced and the number of children he fathered, 20 of them. ("He was very prolific, Bach," said Bernard Miles, the British character actor and comedian, in a mock lecture; "used to practice on an old spinster up in the attic.")

Casals is easier, fierier meat: the prodigy from Catalonia who in 1890, at the age of 13, came across the sheet music for the Suites in a second-hand store in Barcelona at a time when hardly anyone knew they existed, and would devote a sizeable portion of his life to studying, practising and performing them. He and the Suites conquered the world in tandem. There was, besides, an exciting sub-plot to his life: a Catalan patriotism that found hope and then disillusion in the Spanish Civil War. "He was," writes Siblin, "a leftist in conservative eyes, but extreme leftists saw him as a well-connected monarchist."

Casals made his first recordings of the Suites in London and Paris, while desperately scanning the news from Spain. After Barcelona fell, and Catalan autonomy with it, he took up residence in the French Pyrenean village of Prades, as close to home as he could safely get. He remained an implacable enemy of Franco's regime for the rest of his life, refusing to play in any country that recognized it. When in 1958 he was begged to play in New York, for the United Nations, he was able to persuade himself that U.N. headquarters were extra-territorial; but he still refused to play the Suites, what Siblin calls "his spiritual signature."

Casals' decades-long stand against fascism is worth remembering any time you hear scorn poured on performing artists for getting involved in politics.

In the personal sections, we follow Siblin as he travels in America and Europe, seeking traces of both Bach and Casals; declines to explain to a Montreal waitress that he's going to a Bach recital when everyone else in town is off to hear U2; interviews world-class cellists; and meets Walter Joachim, a shuffling 80-year-old veteran of the Montreal Symphony who encourages him to learn the cello: "Not that I would ever be any good . . . I was too old for that ever to happen. But it would give me some insight."

And so, apparently, it does. He also, it seems, embarks on a quixotic quest to discover Bach's lost original manuscript, which fizzles out. He reintegrates his own split personality with a familiar but vivid case for classical music as the pop music of its day, pointing out that Bach premiered his own works in coffeehouses as well as churches, and that we could do with some of the same informality: "Why shouldn't the audience be able to unleash its bravos or reward a smoking instrumental solo in real time?"

And conversely, while acknowledging the fine and varied performances that have succeeded and in some ways improved on him, he writes that Casals "played the suites the only correct way -- by breathing life into them."

This is an untidy, sometimes frustrating book, but it's smart and passionate and it, too, breathes life.
