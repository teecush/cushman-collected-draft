O pening title of the pilot episode of The Good Guys: "Dallas. 11.27 pm" Opening scene: A burglar, plying his trade at that near-midnight hour, answers his cellphone and tells the caller "I'm at work." Disgusted by the low calibre of the TV set he was about to appropriate, he settles instead for a humidifier. This minor misdemeanour sets off a chain of events that will obsess the show's title characters right up until the rolling of the end credits.

The Good Guys may be the first TV show to be set in Dallas since Dallas, and the place has certainly come down in the televised world. The good guys themselves are Dan (played by Bradley Whitford and a moustache) a constantly inebriated middle-aged cop who's been coasting for 25 years on the memory of one heroic exploit, and his partner Frank (Colin Hanks), a conscientious and ambitious young man whose progress in the department has been impeded by a lack of interpersonal skills. "You corrected the captain's grammar in front of the chief " his immediate superior points out, to which Frank replies, "It's just that there's no such thing as a statue of limitations." This of course means that the matter Frank was correcting was not one of grammar but of vocabulary, so maybe he isn't as smart as he thinks he is.

Or maybe the show isn't. It's still, at least on this initial outing, pretty funny. Dan and Frank have, for their respective sins, been consigned to the Petty Crimes unit. Pursuing Number 4312 on the docket (The Case of the Stolen Humidifier) they naturally go first to the scene of the petty crime. The victim proves to be a comely divorcee whose susceptibility to Dan's heroic past prompts her to yield what he claims is valuable information. It also prompts Frank to promise her, as they take their leave, that "we'll send out another minor celebrity for you to sleep with." Dan, who has no time for new-fangled police stuff like computers, likes to address Frank as "college boy." The two find their horizons broadening when the Case of the Stolen Humidifier morphs into the Case of the Embezzled Golf Bag, due to a complex series of coincidences, rendered even more convoluted by the script's cheerfully shameless recourse to explanatory flashbacks.

The complications involve a pawn-shop, a weak-willed but bloody-minded plastic surgeon, and a drug king from way south of the border, who hires a man named Pedro, officially ranked as the Second Best Assassin in the World. "You can do the thing with the gun" says his employer, and Pedro can. Just to make sure, however, the boss brings in the First Best Assassin in the World, which suggests that he's the kind of criminal mastermind who spares no expense. Not that this affects his treatment of lesser employees, for whom business travel is a dreary round of cheap cars and cheaper motels. One of these mules kicks, absconding with the loaded company bag, and thereby precipitating the entire imbroglio. Its finale with the First and Second Best Assassins silently daring one another to do the thing with the gun, a face-off that Frank, obviously a student of early Clint Eastwood movies, says could go on for days. (It doesn't.) Second Best Assassin seems to share Officer Frank's passion for linguistic correctness; as the wounded pawn-shop guy relates, after Pedro has paid him a visit, "He corrected my Spanish; then he shot me."

You've got it, I'm sure. This is a detective show, combining tongue-in-cheek spoof with blunderers making good: Think Get Smart, if you're old enough. Some very loud songs on the soundtrack diminish the appeal by underlining it, but some endearingly precise place-setting in the captions ( "DA's office: conviction rate 73%") does a lot to restore it.

A problem, not so much with the episode as from, is that the Latino crooks, obviously guest artists, are more entertaining than the starring Gringos. Dan and Frank have two female foils, both of them as yet shadowy. One is the station lieutenant (Diana Maria Riva), depicted as simultaneously long-suffering and short-tempered; the other is an Assistant DA (Jenny Wade) whose success rate, at least in relationships, may actually be higher than 73% since, before the show even began, she dumped Frank. I've made a vow never to invoke The Wire in connection with any other cop show, but I have to break it already, and ask: Do statistics support TV's repeated claims for the attraction of high-flying feminine lawyers to low-lying masculine police? Whitford and Hanks themselves are unobjectionable (Hanks is especially good when he's being sarcastic, for which Whitford gives him much cause), but it's hard to see where their relationship can go beyond endless re-statement. This show could get real old, real fast. But I recommend the pilot.

- The Good Guys premiers tonight at 8 p.m. on Global and Fox.
