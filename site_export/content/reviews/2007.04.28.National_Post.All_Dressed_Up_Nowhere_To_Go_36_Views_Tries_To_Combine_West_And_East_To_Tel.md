Once on The Simpsons, when Homer expressed reluctance to visit Japan, Marge tried to cane his mind by saying "you liked Rashomon." "That's not how I remember it," said Homer.

Ha ha. Still, our abiding sense of Japanese art as being about shifting perceptions, epitomized in the very title of that movie, is doubly reinforced in 36 Views. It deals with the contrasting and overlapping reactions to Japanese culture of Asians and Westerners and it is itself the work of a Japanese-American dramatist, Naomi Iizuka.

The title derives from a series of prints by Hokusai, a 19th- century Japanese artist, and the play unfolds in 36 scenes, each numbered off at the side of the stage and accorded delicate pictorial accompaniment. (Hokusai actually bequeathed 46 views, but the author obviously felt enough was enough.)The action concerns the discovery of a "pillow book," the work of an 11th-century Japanese noblewoman. Only it isn't; it was run up, initially for laughs, by a young 20thor 21st-century forger, American, and an artist- associate, Asian. Their work dazzles both scholars and buyers. So all kinds of questions emerge. If the work is beautiful anyway, what does it matter whether it's "authentic"? Can you put a cash price on imagination? In Shakespeare's Troilus and Cressida, Troilus asks rhetorically, "What's aught but as 'tis valued?" To which his big brother Hector says "value lies not in particular will." So, are there absolutes?

The matter is complicated in the play, as it always is, by personal emotions. If the play has a central figure, it's an American named Wheeler. Wheeler's a dealer. (No, honestly.) But with his keen appreciation of the market value of Japanese artifacts, there seems to co-exist a genuine infatuation with Japanese art and with at least one Japanese person, a beautiful scholar. She doesn't reciprocate. So the play in its 36 episodes, some of which are mere fragments, tantalizes us with facts and fancies, seen from different angles, though they hardly amount to three dozen completely different ones. And to show how much these angles are the products of conflicting or intersecting cultures, it offers a mixture of theatrical styles. At dialogue-level, this is a Western work, but its staging partakes of Japanese kabuki techniques, with visible stagehands.

At least, that's the idea. What actually comes across is a commonplace story of love and fraud, told in a confusing order, with a couple of extra actors lurking under tables and occasionally helping the other performers out with costume changes that they could perfectly well execute themselves.

Yasmina Reza's play Art was a far more scintillating examination of the ways in which different people may react to the same picture, while Pacific Overtures, the Stephen Sondheim-John Weidman- Harold Prince musical, fuses American and Japanese stagecraft to far more dazzling effect, reflecting the clash of cultures and the variables of human perception, while throwing in a solid history lesson at the same time.

36 Views, according to the Toronto program, has received a truly terrifying number of workshop productions in the United States, reinforcing my own perception that "workshopping" has become an end in itself.

David Ferry's production, for the Actors Repertory Company, is lovely in its sets, costumes, lighting and especially in Jamie Nesbitt's projections. But in other respects, it's heavy-handed, with performances that border on lifeless. The actors are as likely to intone their lines as to speak them. This is especially noticeable in a choric sequence that has them all putting on their best prose-poem voices in what is presumably meant to suggest the wisdom of the ages. But it affects the chattier sections as well. John Fitzgerald Jay makes Wheeler's fumbling attempts at erotic advances seem like the best scene in the play, or at least the most humanly credible, but the character's other drives are stuck in neutral. As the object of his affections, Ginger Ruriko Busch has a bewitching presence, all the mystery of old Japan in a svelte new dress, but not the authority to back it up, so she becomes less mysterious than anonymous. As the principal faker, Gordon Bolan renders gifted naivete as simple blandness, while Marjorie Chan turns the colleague who supposedly spurs him on with her charm and energy into a grinding bossyboots. Gary Reineke has some amiable moments as a senior expert ("We used to call ourselves Orientalists - - that's how old I am") but is at sea when his character is required to expound on weightier matters. As a prying journalist, Kyra Harper acts as if she were in a thriller, which is pretty much what her scenes require but puts her out of sync with everybody else. The play seems to call for a synthesis of styles; the actors have trouble deciding which they're in at any given moment.

If the show had a bigger budget, one would be tempted to use one of the script's better lines against it: "All it is is money, jazzed up to look like art." As it is, it does look like art. But it doesn't feel like it.
