They decapitated Deadwood and they've foreshortened Boardwalk Empire. HBO seems to have it in for ambitious drama series with recent historical settings.

The death of Deadwood was a cultural crime. That new look at the old West had a rhythm and flavour all its own; it belonged in the same bracket of achievement as The Sopranos and The Wire. Boardwalk Empire has never reached the level of those shows; indeed it has dwelt in their shadow, sharing a principal author (Terence Winter) and a principal subject (gangsters) with The Sopranos and inheriting actors from both. It hasn't had a protagonist as magnetic as Tony Soprano, and it hasnt made the steady progress through different levels of society that distinguished the five seasons of The Wire (as far as I know the only one of these "TV novels" to have covered all of its intended territory and then stopped). But it has been a consistently gripping and entertaining show and its fifth and last season, now winding down to its final episode this Sunday, has a sombre grandeur all its own.

Its had to do it quicker than was intended. Boardwalk Empire is a chronicle of Prohibition, meaning it had to cover the whole of the 1920s and the first few years of the 1930s. It proceeded through them in a fairly leisurely fashion. The fourth season let us off at 1924, after having ruthlessly disposed of the show's two most beloved characters: Eddie, Nucky Thompson's faithful German attendant, offed himself rather than betray his employer, while Richard Harrow, the hitherto invincible half-faced sharpshooter, took a bullet after having, for the first time in his life, hit the wrong target. That seasons last image was of Richard, collapsed on the Atlantic City boardwalk, hallucinating the happy domestic ending that we had all been wishing for him and that had seemed to be within his grasp. We may have seen him kill more people than any other character on the show, but we never held it against him.

At that rate, it would have taken another six seasons to reach the end of the decade. But, as in the ruthless world of the show itself, word came down ordering a hasty execution, and the fifth season has taken us straight to 1931. In the interim we lost another favourite character, his end decreed not by sadistic scriptwriters but by history; this was Arnold Rothstein, the New York gangster played with enchantingly chill soft-spokenness by Michael Stuhlbarg. We were sorry to see him go - he deserved a proper death scene.

Nucky and Margaret, meanwhile, are no longer estranged, their reconciliation occasioned by the necessity of making common cause against the vengeful Mrs. Rothstein. One of the pleasures of this season has been seeing Steve Buscemi and Kelly Macdonald return to striking wary sparks off one another, he obviously still wanting and respecting her, she reciprocating some of the want but holding off on the respect.

Its been a teasing, leisurely process. In fact it's remarkable that a season that one might expect to be hurtling to a finish - and in some respects is - should also be taking its time exploring byways. One obvious example of this is the flashbacks that have been filling us in on Nucky's childhood and early career, his gradual acceptance of venality, leading up to his real deal with the devil: his delivery of the 13-year-old Gillian to the all-powerful Commodore. These scenes arent telling us things we didnt know or couldn't guess, but they're bringing them home. Theyve also managed to find, for the emerging Nucky and his brother Eli, young actors who actually look and sound as if they could grow up to be Buscemi and Shea Whigham.

Slow build-ups to explosions of violence: that has been the pattern this season, and a very effective one. Weve lost two major characters. Chalky White (Michael Kenneth Williams) went down in dignity to a Harlem firing squad after a protracted game of catand-mouse with his long-time nemesis, Dr. Narcisse (a character whose carefully cultivated cadences have been far more tolerable this season than in the previous one when he unfolded them at much greater length). Chalky thrived in Atlantic City, but only under Nucky's patronage; Narcisse, who seemed all-powerful, is paying protection to Lucky Luciano; even in crime, it's a white mans world. Not that white men cant be humiliated, too.

We've been witness to the unlikely partnership of Eli, former sheriff now on the run, and Nelson Van Alden (the marvellously straight-backed and wild-eyed Michael Shannon), former Prohibition enforcer ditto, both of them in simultaneous bondage to Al Capone and to the feds who tracked them down. I'm not convinced that the writers had quite this character arc in mind for the fanatical Van Alden when they first conceived him, but theyve certainly capitalized on it. He and Eli have consistently had the best lines. (Van Alden, cuckolded: Ive been having problems at home. Eli, who did it to him: "I can attest to that.") Van Alden's death, struck down while hurling himself at Capone in a furious re-statement of his previous identity, was abrupt but strangely fitting.

I've left Buscemi till last, because his performance exemplifies both the achievement of Boardwalk Empire and its limitations. I love the way he plays Nucky, and relish his wit, his bite and the way he can switch from charming self-deprecation to chilling self-assertion. The problem is, the boardwalk may be his, but the empire isnt. When the show started we were led to believe that it would all be about Atlantic City, but soon it started taking in the organized crime scene in New York and Chicago as well. This was an irresistible choice, given the richness of the terrain; we must have met, or at least heard about, every famous gangster of the period. They have all had dealings with, or against, Nucky, but there has never been the sense, as there was with Tony Soprano, that all the threads somehow led to him and that he epitomized his era. (The Wire didnt have an overwhelming protagonist either, but then it never pretended to.)

You can't be half a gangster, Nucky was told, in what is probably the show's most famous line. It seems though that his historical prototype, Nucky Johnson, actually could be, and was. He was a corrupt all-powerful municipal pol, who waxed rich through bootlegging, and eventually went to jail for tax evasion (like Capone), but he never killed anybody. Nucky has taken that final step, several times over, but its made him less interesting, morally indistinguishable from the other hoods, and very much at the mercy of events. Ive lost count of the number of times he's risen and fallen and risen again.

So now we await his final fall. Or not. Nucky Thompson isn't Nucky Johnson, who lived to a ripe and even respected old age. Our Nucky could still go down; and we know from history that he cant dispose of his chief remaining enemy Luciano, who proved himself truly Lucky by also dying old.

I hope Nucky survives: not that I think he deserves to but that he doesnt deserve not to; unlike Chalky or Van Alden or even Eli, he's never reached tragic despair so shouldn't have a tragic end. Besides, I like him. Its hard to dislike a man who in his very first episode, and with all the scorn and disgust at Buscemis elegant disposal, could tell Mickey Doyle, that inexplicable survivor, to "read a f-king book," or who, more recently, could drunkenly recite the whole of Hiawatha to a couple of tarts, before being mugged by one of them.

That the focus has narrowed to Nucky vs. Lucky is evidence of how neatly the show has winnowed its field. It may have done so under duress, but now, in its final stages, it feels inexorable.

Boardwalk Empire's series finale airs Sunday at 9 p.m. on HBO Canada.
