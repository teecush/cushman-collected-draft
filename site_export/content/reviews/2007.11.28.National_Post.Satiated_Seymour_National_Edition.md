LITTLE SHOP OF HORRORS

Bluma Appel Theatre, Toronto

---

It's business time again at the (bop-she-bop) Little Shop of Horrors. The place has never looked or sounded better.

Little Shop of Horrors is something unique: a rock musical that's unpretentious, unsentimental, witty, lean as well as mean. It derives, as is well known, from a 1960 movie -- not so much Grade B as Grade Z-- that Roger Corman shot in two days on a leftover set: It became an off-Broadway musical in 1982, with Howard Ashman, who also directed, writing the book and lyrics and Alan Menken the music. Their score is rooted in 1960s Brill Building pop, so you might say the show represents the perfect melding of two schlock genres, each containing at least some seeds of sophistication. These seeds, to adopt the show's own warped horticultural imagery, were lovingly watered and tended by the authors until they blossomed into musical comedy numbers in the sharpest American tradition. The story's setting and ethos are established in an opening number called Skid Row (Downtown), and if you remember Uptown ("where people don't pay much rent") -- well, this song is pretty much its mirror image, but accentuating the negative. (It also sounds a bit like Up on the Roof might have done, had it been reworked as Down in the Gutter. The Gutter, in this show, is an S &Mnightclub.) Soon after this, Seymour, the unawares botanical genius who is our protagonist, sings of his existence as an overworked flower-shop assistant and underloved orphan, his voice pounding in time with his heart: "I keep asking God, what am I for / He says, gee, I'm not sure." Ashman's early loss -- first to Disney, then to death -- was a grievous one; Menken's music has never been the same without him-- he subsequently took to writing with Tim Rice.

Perversely but triumphantly, the show makes us care about its characters and situations by exaggerating them; the music blows them up, the words bring them down to something resembling Earth. Seymour is the nerd of all nerds transformed into a cross between Faust and Frankenstein. Audrey, his blond beloved and co-worker, is the waif of all waifs, oppressed by a dentist boyfriend who enjoys his work. (As she sings, "I know Seymour's the greatest / But I'm dating a semi-sadist." In her accent, the rhyme works.) And the plant that Seymour discovers during an eclipse of the sun and on which he bestows the loving name of Audrey II, turns out to be the monster of all monsters. At first, for all his expert care, it refuses to grow: "What," he sings at it rhetorically but unwisely, "do you want from me, blood?" Well, yes. It wants to eat everyone. And does. But first, it makes the flower shop's fortune and also the guilt-ridden Seymour's, its strangeness and its awesomely increasing dimensions bringing in customers where none were before. Michael Gianfrancesco's flock-wallpapered set for the Canadian Stage Company's revival suggests that this may have had something to do with its looking less like a retail outlet than like someone's dilapidated living room.

Ted Dykstra's direction is immensely gleeful and polished. He and his choreographer, Jody Ripplinger, have staged each number with maximum punch and point. Every song lands, musically and lyrically; I've seldom heard more laughs at a musical. Note, for example, the mental and physical struggle between Orin the dentist, asphyxiating on his own laughing-gas, and Seymour whose "ethical dilemma" leads him briefly into rapid-fire Gilbert and Sullivan. Little Shop may not be as distinguished a musical as Sweeney Todd, but the two have some surprising similarities of theme and structure And, a scene in Sweeney reminds us, barbers once doubled as dentists.

There are some flaws. The all-purpose girl group who act as chorus -- their iconic names are Crystal, Chiffon and Ronette -- are too pushy for their own good -- and the show's. Sheldon Davis, as the little shop's little owner, is likewise effortful, especially at being Jewish. Rejean Cournoyer is a fine Elvis-clone of an Orin (whose mother, observing his proclivities, told him, "Son, be a de- en-tist") but he's ineffectual as a succession of later characters, all of them emissaries of the establishment, tempting the hero with riches. So the conscientious torments of Ron Pederson's Seymour, which should drive the second half, go for less than they should; he's also the victim of a harsh sound system which makes it hard for him to come off as shy.

Otherwise, he's the singing and dancing Seymour of your dreams, incisive even when dithering. Patricia Zentilli's Audrey I, a gentle vision whom nature intended to be pre-Raphaelite but whom fate has painted post-Pollock, may even surpass your dreams, especially when she sings Somewhere That's Green, a yearning hymn to plastic- covered suburbia. Best of all is Audrey II, voiced by Jeff Jones as a soul singer who starts all wheedle and ends all gravelly gloat, andmanipulatedby actor-puppeteer Eric Woolfe to take over the stage, and potentially the world, with grasping tendrils and yawning mouth.

This plant deserves an award: a Flora Dora. - Little Shop of Horrors runs until Dec. 15. For tickets, call 416-368-3110.
