ELLA FITZGERALD

FACTS & ARGUMENTS LIVES LIVED

ELLA FITZGERALD

Friday, June 21, 1996

THIS is meant to be about Ella Fitzgerald's life, not mine, but I cannot escape the feeling that the two are intertwined. Not that I ever impinged on her life -- she had never heard of me -- but she changed mine.

I must have been 18 when I bought Ella Fitzgerald Sings the Cole Porter Songbook, Vol. 1. I was aware, through movies and the radio, of many of the great songs of Broadway and Hollywood. I knew the names of the major composers, and even had some rudimentary idea of who had written what. But it took the Songbook series -- 19 LPs recorded between 1956 and 1965, and continuously available ever since -- to codify the repertoire for me and to change a mild interest into an obsession. I devoted the next few years of my life to collecting the discs (I was in time to catch the last couple as they came out), listening to them, singing along with them, memorizing the music and lyrics. I must have been one of thousands.

When she recorded that first Porter album, Ella was in her 30s and had already been singing 20 years. A Harlem orphan who wanted to dance but got nervous at a talent contest and sang instead, she was rescued from poverty (she was actually sleeping in the streets) by a job with the fine Chick Webb band. She became their biggest attraction, adored for her sweet, husky voice and innate, invincible sense of swing. Most band-singers were hired for their looks; Ella was hardly a sex symbol, but the combination of personal shyness with professional accomplishment proved immensely attractive, and it remained constant throughout her life.

The voice itself was the best-loved of the century. Her range turned out to be the equal of any opera singer's but her actual tone was warm, honeyed and unthreatening. She could sound like a little girl, but one who sang her own lullabies. No matter what she was singing, she made the loveliest noise in popular music: maybe in all of music. She could have rested there. Wonderfully, she didn't. Many of the best popular singers triumph over their vocal limitations. Ella triumphed over her lack of them.

Eventually, her voice did give in to time. An Ella with imperfect pitch was an Ella inconceivable; nevertheless, it happened and the reality of it could be painful. But she was still worth listening to, for the joy she took in music and the further joy she took in communicating that joy. And that is the best answer to those who claim that Ella Fitzgerald was incapable of communicating emotion.
