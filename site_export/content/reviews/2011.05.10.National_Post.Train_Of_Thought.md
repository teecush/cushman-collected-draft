THE RAILWAY CHILDREN

Roundhouse Theatre, Toronto

The star of The Railway Children is a genuine vintage locomotive, both engine and carriages, imported, like the show itself, from England. Knowing like all stars -and indeed like many other trains -how to make its audience wait, it husbands its appearances. Indeed it only makes two of them, if you don't count its personal curtain call. These come at the end of each act. The first one is thrilling, coming at a moment of potential disaster. The second is nice, but not quite as celebratory as both the storyline and the production mean it to be. We're travelling on a diminishing return ticket.

The Railway Children is a charming show with a built-in flaw. It's based on a classic children's novel, by the Edwardian writer E. Nesbit, which has, like many of its kind, an episodic structure. Three children and their mother are up-rooted from their cosy metropolitan existence when father is arrested and sent to prison on what, because he's such an obviously nice man, has to be a trumpedup charge. The children, two girls and a boy, aren't told exactly what's happened, but the audience is fed heavy hints. The family moves to a Yorkshire village; their house is near the railroad tracks, with which they become happily obsessed. They wave at all the trains and befriend the station master.

The story's most congenial medium for adaptation is the TV serial, which allows every episode its own episode, but this stage version has a lot going for it. It originated, fittingly enough, at the Theatre Royal, York; and had a successful London run at, even more fittingly, Waterloo Station. Its Toronto home is a specially constructed theatre, actually a large tent, on Bremner Boulevard between Skydome and the CN Tower, where retired Canadian steam-engines can make their English cousin feel at home. The audience sits on two sides of a platform, beneath which run rails to enable the visitor to make its spectacular appearances. Most of the action takes place around and between the big holes necessary to allow the train its space. This looks at first as if it is going to be confining, but in fact Damian Cruden's production uses the space resourcefully and at times beautifully. Joanna Scotcher's set and costume designs make major contributions, and Richard G. Jones' lighting is wonderfully atmospheric. So, up to a point, is Christopher Madin's music, the point being reached somewhere in the second act where it turns syrupy and manipulative.

Here, though, it may only be keeping pace with Mike Kenny's script, whose own limitations probably come with the territory. It has a good framing device; the three heroes, at some unspecified later date, recall how they became, and then ceased being, railway children. This gives a nice sense of reminiscence and regret, and it also permits the hiring of adult actors to play the central roles. In this production, that's certainly a blessing. As Roberta the burgeoning eldest, Natasha Greenblatt, thoughtful and concerned, puts a new gloss on the battered word "sensitive." As Phyllis, the youngest, Kate Besworth is unquenchable even when terrified. As Peter, squeezed between his sisters while trying precociously to lead, Harry Judge tends, in his own words, "to lay it on thick," but so does the character.

The adaptation tries conscientiously to give them, Phyllis in particular, a through-line, but it can't disguise the fact that, basically, the kids just do one good thing after another. They prevent a train wreck. They rescue a trapped boy from a tunnel, which scenically is exceptionally well evoked. They give succour to a nonEnglish-speaking passenger who turns out to be a dissident writer, escaped from Siberia, and whose initial reception by the local authorities makes you feel grateful that tasers hadn't yet been invented. Their mother, who in an autobiographical touch is supporting her family by writing herself, is very appreciative, but the man passes on out of their life, leaving no dramatic mark. They also overstep the bounds of middle-class manners by throwing their station master friend a birthday party, making him feel patronized. He does though forgive them later; Craig Warnock plays him warmly enough but without ever quite locating the character's centre. Marilla Wex, however, is right on target as the family's newly recruited housekeeper.

As the incidents mount up so, in the second half, do the coincidences, a character known as the Old Gentleman being pressed into quadruple service as a fairy godfather. Which reminds me: In a show that makes constant use of direct address, the audience gets told, more or less, to clap hands if it believes in returning fathers. This one, naturally, arrives by train: its second full-dress appearance. But the moment isn't quite the coup that must have been hoped. In the first place, father hasn't been much of a presence; he's been too busy playing other characters. In the second, we've already had the thrill of seeing the train, if only once. Not that there haven't been many other arrivals and departures -the show practically consists of them. But they've been represented by simple trucks, the theatrical rather than the locomotive kind. And they work so well that you begin to wonder whether we really need to see the real thing at all, which I admit is an ungrateful thought, and commercially counterproductive.

Anyway, there's a decent, benevolent feel to the production, as well as a lot of imagination, and the combination takes us way above the usual familyentertainment level. Make tracks for the Roundhouse. After all, the producers have.
