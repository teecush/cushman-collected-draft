CROSS CURRENT Miss Saigon controversy illustrates hypocrisy in racial casting

Friday, August 24, 1990

ROBERT CUSHMAN

BY ROBERT CUSHMAN THE Heat Is On In Saigon. That is the title, and the pounding refrain, of the opening song in Miss Saigon, a musical that's very big on pounding refrains. For the moment, as far as this show is concerned, the heat is off in New York. Or rather, it is on hold.

The show, a huge hit in London, was scheduled to open next April on Broadway. It was stopped in its tracks when American Equity objected to the importation of British actor Jonathan Pryce to play the pivotal role of the Engineer, a Eurasian pimp. Pryce created the role in London and has played it for a year, to great applause. Cameron Mackintosh, the show's producer, threatened to pull the plug on the Broadway version if Equity persisted; they did persist and he duly pulled it.

This was a shock. The cancellation of a guaranteed money-maker, which had already raked up a record advance sale, felt like a breach in nature. Hits travel, especially to Broadway; it's a rule. Perhaps in recognition of this Equity retreated, acknowledging that Pryce's star status (he won a Tony some 15 years ago for his performance in Comedians) was an automatic passport to Broadway. Somewhat surprisingly Mackintosh refused this olive- branch. In a statement, Mckintosh said in effect that instead of grudging assent, he wanted enthusiastic co-operation from the union, especially from its Asian members.

My first thought was that he was being unreasonable, even petulant; that he should quit, or rather restart, while he was ahead. Re-reading his statement, I changed my mind. The show, if and when it opens, is bound to attract some flak. But attacks from fringe elements are one thing; official union-endorsed hostility is another. It would make the position of the show and its cast untenable.

In fact, I am not sure Mackintosh goes far enough. Before Pryce was cast for Broadway, there was the regulation search for an American - and preferably an Asian-American - actor to play the part instead. This should not have to happen. A successful production should be able to transfer from one part of the world to another with its original cast intact. If the producer or director want to tamper with it, that's their prerogative, but it is only theirs.

In practical terms, we are talking about transfers from London to New York, and vice versa. If we have to appease the protectionists, who are rampant in both places, we can point out that over the years the proportion of success in both centres evens out. Further, a hit show now runs for years and needs many re-castings. If only for convenience' sake, there will mostly be locally recruited.

Miss Saigon is a retelling of the Madam Butterfly story, updated to the Vietnam war and its aftermath. Some characters are American, mainly GI's; most are Vietnamese, and in London they have been cast ethnically, or approximately so. (The leading lady is actually from the Philippines.) Mackintosh and his colleagues claim that the Pryce role, being a half- caste, is a special case: mother French, father Asian. He would, a spokesman told me, be played by anyone - white, black, Asian, Hispanic - providing he were good enough.

This, I think, is disingenuous. If the show had originated on Broadway 25 years ago the role would probably have been played by Joel Grey. But I am sure that, if it were starting on Broadway today the role, if only for the sake of a quiet life, would go to an Asian. However it started in London, and the part went to Pryce, and he is now identified with it. The audience - the forgotten factor in all these calculations - want to see him do it. And his star status, as I think he himself would admit, is irrelevant.

An enormous amount of hypocrisy and inconsistency surrounds the question of racial casting. Directors who expect you to respond to every visual nuance in their productions also expect you to go color blind when they cast a black actor in what was plainly written as a white role, without stylizing anything else to match. "If you notice an actor's color," says Joe Papp of his multi-cultural Shakespeare shows, "you're not seeing Shakespeare." And as a Jew, should I object or rejoice if Shylock is played by a Gentile? It's very perplexing. If blacks or Asians can freely be cast as Caucasians, then the reverse must apply. And in either case - I think I'm brave enough to type this - they should try to look the part. That's a component of acting. The only yardstick can be talent, and that is not objectively measurable, certainly not by a committee. The only people who can make casting decisions are the people putting on the show. If I had to (and I know I won't) I would defend Mackintosh's right to cast non-Asian actors in every Vietnamese role in Miss Saigon, and to bring over Brits to play the Americans. If he got it wrong the critics and - far more importantly - the public would soon let him know. Give my regards to Broadway. The heat is on.
