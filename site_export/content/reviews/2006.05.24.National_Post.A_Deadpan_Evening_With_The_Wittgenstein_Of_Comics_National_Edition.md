STEVEN WRIGHT

Elgin Theatre, Toronto

- - -

Somewhere in the course of his two-hour show at the Elgin last Thursday, Steven Wright admitted to being 50. This puts him closer to my age than to my children's. All the same, they were the ones who introduced him to me, mainly by playing me tapes in the car. This is their favourite method of broadening my horizons. It seems to work better with comedy than with music.

To Wright, though, I had some resistance. I had been far more susceptible to his more manic disciple, Mitch Hedberg, who died unexpectedly a couple of years ago. Hedberg is the guy you still see in trailers for stand-up shows on Comedy Central, easily identifiable because hes the only one who suggests that stand-up might actually be funny. Hes the one with the juiced-up weirdly italicized delivery who says "when I was a boy, I used to lie in my twin bed and wonder where my brother was." In some ways he recalls the incredulous observational style of Richard Pryor (when I first heard him I assumed he was black, which he wasn't) minus the political content. I surrendered the moment I heard him.

Like Hedberg, Wright dispenses one-liners and two-liners in oceanic quantities, in an order over which he presumably has some control -- he sounds too assured to be shuffling them on the spot -- but which seems quite arbitrary to the rest of us. He redefines deadpan, to the point of vocal somnambulism. The sentences emerge at a level pace from a throat lined equally with gravel and molasses. I found him, in sound only, rather pointless.

Still, I agreed to take my two sons to his appearance in Toronto, or to let them take me. (Apparently, he likes it here. We were his audience of choice for his DVD.) I promised the Post that I would file a piece on the unbridgeable generation gap.

Sorry. My boys fulfilled their part of the deal; they were gone from the moment Wright strolled on stage and unleashed his first joke. Trouble is, so was I. I don't see how Im expected to resist a man who says -- without preamble, as if we had just boarded his train of thought -- that he wished the first word he ever uttered had been quote so that, just before he died, he could round it off by saying "unquote."

Hes been known to say, quite audibly, that if we can't hear him, it's because hes in parentheses. Hes fascinated, first, by words, and, secondly, by behaviour as it's dictated by words. He's the Wittgenstein of stand-up comics, and he looks as the philosopher might if hed been dragooned into appearing in a production of Waiting for Godot. (Alternatively, he looks -- and sounds -- like an undertaker who lives, as opposed to loves, his work.) The visual element makes all the difference. He doesnt actually do much. He has three chairs on stage, and he divides his time between them with scrupulous fairness. Occasionally he picks up a guitar, and even plays it, while he sort of sings. That's all. But his presence, which is as powerful as downbeat gets, automatically gives his act a structure.

I used, he says, "to be a narrator for bad mimes," and though it takes a moment for the concept to sink in, it sounds reasonable. Like Lewis Carroll, he pays words the compliment of taking them literally, and then observes the chaos. You can't have everything. Where would you put it? He rubs concepts together. "I stayed up all night playing poker with tarot cards. I got a full house, and four people died."

Unlike most comics, he casts few aspersions on his own personal life. If hes been a failure with girls, he doesnt let on. Well, he does say on one of his tapes my girl has a queen-size bed and I have a court-jester size bed, but it isn't one of his best. Hedberg took the same idea to far wilder heights, when he fantasized about being visited by a king and saying "Hey, your majesty, we have a bed just your size", adding How come you kings are all the same size? and adding to that "We got one for your lady too." Hedberg, a comedian with exclamation-points, got excited by absurdity; Wright takes it as it comes. He doesn't do cadenzas that well. He had a surprisingly long and coherent routine last week about picking up a hitchhiker, but it never really got better than his chilling initial question to his passenger, How far do you think youre going?

Ask Wright a silly question ("did you sleep good?") and you get a disconcertingly sensible answer (no, I made a few mistakes). At the supermarket checkout he asks to buy the divider that separates his purchases from the next customers; asked why, he replies "you don't know the people I live with." I think this is the closest he comes to making a social statement. All the same, a view of human relations lurks darkly in everything he says.

I don't know why the kids have been keeping him from us; hes very grown-up. Maybe its just that the young have more time and energy to visit comedy clubs, so stand-up becomes part of their culture. (Of course, most children are comedians by nature. It's only when they start doing their routines for an audience beyond their own family that we should get nervous.) It's also true that most stand-up is awful; people spewing out fragments of supposed autobiography in no particular order. Wrights genius is to embrace the random as a principle while keeping his distance. He doesnt bore us with his personal problems or invent imaginary ones. Indeed, he doesn't seem to invent things at all; he just finds them. No wonder he's an idol.
