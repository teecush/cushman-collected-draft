Ross Petty and his gang should immediately be put in charge of all TV commercials. Twice in Robin Hood, this year's Petty "family musical," as its creators describe it -- or pantomime, as the rest of us describe it -- a screen is lowered, and the show treats us to a few words from or about its sponsors. Each has its product lauded in a brief film clip, performed by the stage cast in their stage characters, and each so funny and so well-done that one quite forgets that it's an advertisement. I especially enjoyed the plug for Tim Hortons, which involves Jessica Holmes as the Queen of the Forest and Dan Chameroy as someone called Nurse Plumbum, stirring up viciously competitive toil and trouble over their double-doubles.

Now, of course not every theatre can give thanks for corporate help in this unbuttoned manner, though it might be fun if one or two of them did. The far-from-unspoken rule of a panto is that anything goes, and the ads are just the most blatant example of the ways in which Robin Hood pokes holes in the story it is theoretically telling.

They are also, I am inclined to think, the most enjoyable; their outrageousness is thoroughbred. Some of the rest comes off a bit apologetic. This Robin Hood is a reasonably entertaining entertainment, but it isn't one of the team's best. (As far as I remember -- and they do tend to blur together in the mind -- it's less good than the last one with this title.) Unlike, say, Cinderella, Robin Hood doesn't have a single recognizable story for a comedy writer to bounce off. He's just a cluster of legends, with a few permanent signposts. One of these, of course, is Maid Marian, of whom Chris Earle's script takes a revisionist view. She's a bossy environmentalist, bent on preserving Sherwood Forest from despoilers, while misguidedly respectful of her guardian, the Sheriff of Nottingham, who is of course the despoiler-in-chief, and suspicious of her outlaw-admirer whom she initially finds to be frivolous and far too full of himself. (She has a point.)

Neither the eco-friendly theme nor the love story develops much impetus. One doesn't expect a family musical (it's actually billed as The EnvironMENTAL Family Musical) to be as integrated as West Side Story, but if you're going to deconstruct something, it helps to have a structure there first. This one lurches from one scene to another, mostly helped by its gags, mostly hindered by its songs. These are a mix of rock, rap, contempo pop and Michael Jackson: all of them genres with a built-in disinclination to tell anybody's story but their own.

They're dragged in on the thinnest of pretexts; Marian, having been smitten by Robin, wanders about the stage singing Desperado, which should give you an idea. Admittedly, pantos have always interpolated the songs of whatever day it was, but I don't remember them doing it quite like this. Desperadoes indeed: the show's creators seem desperate to reassure the kids, not to mention themselves, that they're up to date. It feels, to be frank, like pandering.

All right, I'll stop being solemn. Ted Dykstra's production boasts three first-rate comic turns. Best of them is Holmes, who starts the show doing a riotously irrelevant lampoon of Celine Dion (it doesn't technically interrupt the story because the story hasn't even started) and then switches to her Fairy Queen role, which she discharges with a devastatingly snappish lisp. Next is Petty, whose Sheriff is the mixture as before, and more richly self-referential than ever; producers may sometimes be villains, but this one actually gets to tell his employees "if I leave the theatre, none of you lot will work again." Finally there's Chameroy whose man-hungry nurse is a most delicate creation, even if it does consist solely of jokes about her being a man-hungry nurse.

On the subject of repeated jokes: no show needs two references to Tiger Woods, and the one Chameroy gets is a lot better than the one Petty gets. Of the romantic supports: Eva Avila, former Canadian Idol, shows as Maid Marian that she knows her way about a stage, and Jeff Irving is a cheerful Robin. And then there are the Musically Merry Men: in the words of a song unfortunately not from the show but that I've just discovered and would like to share: "They laughed, they loved, they fought, they drank/They jumped a lot of fences/They robbed the rich, gave to the poor/Except what they kept for expenses."

The current representative selection includes Jaz Sealey whose Li'l Sean John is a hulking and magnetic rapper, and Yvan Pedneault whose Jacques le Rock (try finding him in the source material) is a full-throated chansonnier, though he has less good material than he had in We Will Rock You. That's a phrase I never thought I'd write.
