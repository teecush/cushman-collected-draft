Samuel Beckett, I once wrote, is a gentleman and a showman. (He was alive at the time. Hence the tense.) It may seem odd to speak of Becketts showmanship. He was, after all, the low priest of theatrical minimalism. But the fewer effects you use, the more powerful they can be, if you know how to use them. Beckett always knew. And he made no secret of it. His stage directions are as detailed as, if less loquacious than, those of that other great Irish expatriate Bernard Shaw. And, unlike Shaw, he expected them to be carried out to the letter.

As they mostly are in The Beckett Trilogy, an hour's worth of his latest and shortest, performed by Lisa Dwan, Irish herself, who learned the trick of them from Billie Whitelaw, the English actress who first played them and who was Beckett's latelife theatrical muse. We start with Not I, the one in which a woman pours forth an unstoppable stream of high-velocity reminiscence. The text calls her the Mouth, and a pair of lips is all we see of her. Indeed, in this production, its all we see of anyone. But here the director, Walter Asmus, does depart from his authors stated intentions. Beckett's directions dictate the presence of a figure - shrouded, hooded and silent - referred to as the Auditor, the almost-impassive recipient of the Mouth's confessions. Paring down Beckett might seem impossible, but in eliminating the Auditor this production achieves it. The change, though, is for the worse. Every so often the Auditor is supposed to incline slightly, as if he or she might actually be listening. Without this both the Mouths speech and her plight become less tantalizing, less rich.

Her words suffer, too, from the record speed at which Dwan delivers them, too fast and too low for comprehension. When Whitelaw played the role, a little slower, you couldnt understand everything, but you could understand something. All that really registers in this performance of Not I are the reverse title-words: every so often the Mouth, who's been speaking entirely in the third person, pulls herself out and says -screams - what? ... who? ... no! ... she!, as if dissociating herself. (It's at these points that the Auditor is supposed to audit.) The cries, as Dwan emits them, are electrifying. Plainly she is denying something, probably the experience of pleasure. The script is full of clues, about her birth 70 years ago to unknown parents, about her growing up in a hellish and probably religious institution, about guilty moments of human contact in a pastoral setting. They would be worth hearing. But you cant.

Two things seem to have happened over the course of Becketts playwriting: he grew more interested in women and less interested in everything else, especially in what is commonly called action. The plays get progressively shorter. Waiting for Godot gives us four men and a boy, all capable of moving around. Endgame has four characters, three of them immobile, one of those three female and also the least voluble. Happy Days is a nearmonologue for a woman buried in sand, with a husband of whom we see little and hear less. The play called Play has three people - one male, two female - encased neck-down in funerary urns. Which brings us to Not I and its successors.

Footfalls, the first of these, is something of a reversal. Again a woman is on stage but this time she is glimpsed fulllength and - wonder of wonders - she moves. In fact, she paces, back and forth on an uncarpeted floor, patiently responding to the offstage questionings of her mother. (Dwan does both voices, brilliantly.) Mother, we learn, is 90. "So much?" she marvels. Daughter is in her forties. So little? she complains. Each is solicitous of the other, daughter offering to change her mother's sheets, mother entreating daughter to stop pacing and, above all, to stop remembering. The amount of naturalistic detail is, for Beckett, astonishing, not to say haunting. The play, short as it is, doesn't quite sustain; an uncertainty over whether the daughters name is May or Amy is cheap mystification, unworthy of its author.

And that, you can hear Beckett saying, is what movement gets you: dont ask me for it again. Which brings us to Rockaby, in which motion is reduced to a rocking chair going backwards and forwards, with a probably dying woman inside it, her recorded voice (Dwan haunting again) piped in as counterpoint. Hypnotic here, and what I mean by Beckett's showmanship, are the chair and its occupant rocking away from the light, what there is of it, and back in again. The stygian precision of James Farncombe's lighting here reaches its apogee. The message remains the same: life is something best ended but with occasional moments, or their echoes, that may make it worth living. Or at any rate enduring. The trilogy is a bracing experience, not fun exactly, but hard to shake off. Dwan, having taken us through it all, took her curtain calls with a radiant smile. She even blew us kisses. So shes a showperson too. She says in her bio that she "is also open to the possibility of comedy." I bet shed be great at it.

Matt Baram and Naomi Snieckus are more than open to such possibilities. It's what they do, usually very well. They are a married couple who are also alumni of Second City; and You and Me Both, their sketch comedy revue, is like an hour's worth of Second City marriage skits laid end to end. They're good skits, starting with a number in which, fully clothed, they play themselves in the shower, trying to be playfully nice with each other and not always succeeding. They also do nifty theatrical satire, as in a bit of updated Chekhov, in which they lament how hard it is to get to Moscow when you live in Liberty Village. "You should have given me a child," she upbraids him, "so we could use the HOV lane." They are appealingly retro, though I think their 1950s-style title song is something they could do without; likewise, their overly ingratiating chats to the audience. But they're a pleasure, and the more so the more you get to know them.
