THEATRE REVIEW

Royal Alexandra Theatre, Toronto

Here we have the Canadian premiere, in a visiting British production, of a U.S. musical, based on a very English novel by a writer, Frances Hodgson Burnett, who was indeed English but who wrote the book in Tennessee, where she lived most of her life. Just to make things more confusing, the story is mostly set in a house on the Yorkshire moors but has a prologue in India, a locality that keeps reasserting itself in the characters' nightmares and the shows creators wildest dreams.

The heroine, Mary Lennox, is a 10-year-old child of the British raj, whose entire family has perished in a cholera epidemic. She's adopted, or at least taken in, by her uncle Archibald, who has also suffered a loss: the death of his beloved wife, Lily, sister of Mary's mother. This, and his self-consciousness about his humped back, have made him very remote; he spends a lot of time travelling and when at home, though he means Mary no harm, he wishes to see as little of her as possible; she reminds him too much of the late Lily, especially around the eyes. He shares this ocular fixation, and even a song on the subject, with his brother, a doctor, who also loved Lily and is a semi-scoundrel; that is, he wants possession of Misslethwaite Manor, Archies country seat, and is willing to sacrifice everyone, including Mary, to get it. But mindful perhaps of his professional oath, he tries to persuade himself that he is doing the right physicianly thing. In other words, hes a Hippocratic hypocrite.

Archie has a bedridden son, Colin, whom he can hardly bear to look on (care of memories of dead mother) and whom his brother tries to keep out of sight as well. But Mary, who's both nosey and bossy, wanders into Colin's room and, after an initial round of mutual youthful insults, sets about making him well, or rather proving he was never ill in the first place. She does this by getting him out into the open air, and especially by introducing him to his mothers garden.

Growing things from the ground is Marys chief pleasure, and she gains access to Lily's own beloved orchard, long locked up by her grieving husband, but still faithfully tended by her old gardener. Mary's own horticultural mentors are Martha the housemaid, and especially Marthas green-fingered brother Dickon. Together they represent life, as opposed to the creeping deadliness of the grown-up world indoors. Pauline Kael once wrote that its almost impossible not to be moved by the sight of a sick child, and by the same token it's even harder not

to respond to a child's restoration to health. The end of this show leaves the throat duly lumped. But it takes a long and anguished time to get there.

First, theres the Gothic overload of Marsha Normans script, which seems to draw on the Brontes as much as on Burnett. This can be unwittingly funny; the Misslethwaite housekeeper's description of the sound the Yorkshire wind makes -- "we call it wuthering"-- is a rare moment of high literary camp. Then there's the Asian overload of Anna Linstrums production; on the slightest provocation, the stage is filled with ghostly Indian figures from Marys past. It illustrates very little and explains less. It also clutters up a stage that is already in an unnerving state of permanent revolve; you can see why Archie would want to get away from a house whose walls refuse to stand still. Well, that and the fact that his late wife keeps haunting it in flashbacks. There's Lucy Simon's score, which is appealing and original in its folksier moments but deadening in its more frequent moods of neo-Broadway bombast; as usual, the worst song -- the brothers duet of competitive agonizing -- gets the loudest applause, perhaps because its so loud itself. And there's the acting.

Mary is asked at one point whether she's always so rude and so loud, and the answer must be: usually, yes. Of course shes been through a lot, and must have been terribly spoiled in India, but some modulation might be in order; and its probably the director, rather than the perfectly competent young actress (Ellie Coldicutt at the performance I saw) who's to blame for its absence.

Similarly with Gene Goodman's Colin, whom one certainly wants to get well and who can be touching. Martha (Lauren Hood) and Dickon (Jos Slovick) are pleasant if indistinct. Caspar Phillipson works himself up to a fearful pitch as Archibald, and is very coy about letting us get a look at his hump; Graham Bickley ignores any hints of ambiguity in the doctor in favour of cardboard mutton-chop villainy. Bickley was last seen on this street in The Pajama Game, in which he was very good, but of course thats a much livelier show.

The performer with the most impressive resume is Siobhan Redmond, best known in these parts from the fine TV cop series Between the Line, and here wastefully cast as the housekeeper; she pounces when the occasional good line comes her way, but its very occasional. I realized how accustomed we've become in Canada to seeing musicals that are well acted; I found myself thinking that our local talent might even have handled the accents better.
