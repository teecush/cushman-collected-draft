THEATRE REVIEW

Les Miserables Princess of Wales Theatre, Toronto

Jean Valjean, no evil-doer, Stole some bread though he was pu-er.

A detective chased him through a sewer.

The End

The above lyric is not from Les Miserables. It's from a 1940s revue number called Reader's Digest, written by Betty Comden and Adolph Green, and a refreshing example of wit and of the brevity that is its soul. It certainly refreshes me just to type it after spending an evening at Les Mis, a show to which both brevity and wit are strangers. The restaged version that has arrived in Toronto has some individual merits, but the musical itself is as miserable as ever.

Chief of the redeeming features is Ramin Karimloo's performance as the redeemed redeemer himself: Jean Valjean, saviour and faker and candlestick-taker.He has exquisite vocal control and an intensity that, in his vengeful pre-reformation state, suggests that he might turn into Sweeney Todd. Even when a compassionate bishop inspires him to choose the more tedious, and largely unexamined, path of sanctity, he pursues it with a passion whose climactic expression is his singing of the show's best song, Bring Him Home, where his fists clench as his voice rings. Even he, though, cannot do much for the finale, where the hero dies for no clear reason except that it's getting late and time to send everybody, onstage and in the auditorium, home feeling noble.

Apart from being the one moment in Claude-Michel Schonberg's score where simplicity doesn't tumble over into pounding banality, Bring Him Home works because it shows Valjean, praying for the survival of his adopted daughter's beloved, discovering the depth of his feelings. It explores a character and develops a situation; it does in fact what songs in musicals are supposed to do.

At the opposite extreme, and far more typical, is the appalling dirge I Dreamed a Dream, which, starting with its tautological title, repeats things we already know in the loudest and most shopworn of terms. Nothing in it is specific to the woman who sings it, Fantine, a prostitute who's had to abandon her child. It could be sung by any deserted heroine in any musical.

The same goes for the later On My Own. The titles here are real giveaways. Do you hear the people sing? It's hard not to at this volume, but harder still to discern what they're singing about. The number in question is a call to a French revolution, though not the French Revolution; that's already happened, 40 years earlier. What the singers are revolting about or against is never mentioned, even though the revolt itself takes up most of the second act. There is an irony here - the revolutionaries are mostly students whom "the people" fail to support-but it's barely stated, never explored.

To some extent, it's the form that's to blame. Les Miserables is a throughsung musical: a species that seems, mercifully, to be dying out but that survives in revivals and in endless original runs. It delegates the tiresome business of storytelling to intermediate musical passages that aren't dialogue, aren't recitative, are in fact miniature songs, subject to song rules. They place an enormous burden on the lyricist who, in this English version of the French stage version of the Victor Hugo novel, is Herbert Kretzmer. In a recent interview in The New Yorker, Kretzmer, a veteran pop song and theatre man, said that he avoided cliches "like the plague." I think, hope, that he was joking, but his work on Les Mis is one giant plague spot.

Hugo's novel is vast, partly owing to his compulsion to provide a back-story for everybody and everything, from the bishop to the sewers. The adaptation does cut a clear path through the jungle, though it leaves out such useful information as how the hero transformed himself from hunted convict to factory owner and mayor.

It also flattens his inner conflicts, and those of his hunter Inspector Javert,whose suicide, prefigured in the book, here seems arbitrary. The most powerful sustained passage is the prologue, Valjean's release from the galleys and subsequent turnaround. In this new staging (by Laurence Connor and James Powell) it might be called the pre-credits sequence as, once it's over, the show's title is flashed up and we're plunged into the murk which, in a design inspired by the thunderclap style of Hugo's own paintings, is often literal.

The previous production had a scale and spaciousness this one has lost; but it also had a ceaselessly turning stage that took revolution rather too literally. One definite improvement is the flight through the Parisian drains, which projections make far more exciting. A definite deterioration is in the quality of the sound; many of the words are muddied and, apart from Karimloo, only Earl Carpenter (Javert) and Melissa O'Neil (the on-her-own Eponine) pierce through, clear and unforced. Fantine (Genevieve Leclerc) is a real screecher.

Bizarrely unchanged, in a French story with a mostly Canadian cast, is the convention that the lower orders, especially the sex-workers, all have cockney accents. This doesn't quite apply to the dastardly Thenardiers (Cliff Saunders and Lisa Horner), the designated comic relief, who are in a bizarrely capering world of their own. They turn up at the end in presumably filched finery at the marriage of Marius, the revolutionary hero who seems to have joined the class he was rebelling against. Maybe there's an irony here, too; the choreography, half-heartedly grotesque, doesn't seem to have made up its mind.

And there you have it: a long-haul show dedicated to short-term effects.
