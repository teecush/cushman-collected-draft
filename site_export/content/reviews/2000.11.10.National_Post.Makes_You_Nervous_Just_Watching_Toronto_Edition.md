JITTERS

Studio Theatre, Toronto Centre for the Performing Arts

David French's play Jitters was written in 1979, and it still stands up today. This is an achievement, especially in the current revival where it has a lot to stand up to.

It is a backstage piece, portraying the first preview, first night, and first morning-after travails of an imaginary play that appears to be of the family-fighting, hands-across-the-kitchen- table persuasion. Those who were around at the time have hinted that French based his own piece on real-life goings-on in the early days of Torontos Tarragon Theatre. There is a director -- of both the theatre and the play -- said to be based on the Tarragons founder (and French's own mentor), Bill Glassco; though here he more closely resembles a professor in one of our leading university drama departments.

French's method is to take a few strongly drawn types, throw them together in a situation of crisis, and let them slug it out.

There is the long-absent Canadian star who has had a couple of flops in New York and who is counting on this play to restore her credibility at home and also to carry her straight back to the Great White Way. There is the self-destructive hard-drinking Irish-born actor who ceaselessly rails at her for selling out, while secretly harbouring a great respect for her talent. There is the prim, mother- dominated actor, of Greek extraction, whose worst neurotic fears (hes a walking Greek tragedy) are confirmed when he learns the production doesnt have a prompter. There is the young actor fresh out of drama school. There is the director whose skills, at this stage of the proceedings, seem to be more diplomatic than creative. There is the promising playwright, whose second play this is. There is the stage manager, a hectoring martinet, obsessed with having the actors clock in. Somewhere offstage there is the Broadway producer they all hope will make their fortunes.

Jitters has a few resemblances to Michael Frayn's immortal Noises Off, which originated in London at about the same time. That, too, has a leading lady whose desire for a comeback makes her the power behind the production, and an actor whose colleagues have to keep confiscating his booze; and of course it has the same kind of ego battles driving it along.

Frayn's play, though, is a thoroughgoing farce while Frenchs only makes passing use of farcical devices; it turns out in fact to be an affectionate show-must-go-on kind of piece in which everybody ends up pulling together; in fact its sentimental, though not objectionably so. It also makes far less use than Frayn's of the play-within-the-play; we only see its opening five minutes, and they seem to be terrible: Canadian domestic drama at its turgid worst. Maybe, though, that's just the way this cast -- either by accident or design -- is playing it. Certainly the participants -- unlike those of Noises Off -- seem to believe they have a potentially good show on their hands; and if they dont, then theres not much reason for us to take an interest.

What happens is that final rehearsals before first preview get scuppered, largely through the feuding of the two leading players. Then there is mayhem just before curtain-rise when the male juvenile arrives very late and dead drunk, leaving the author -- paranoid at the best of times -- uncertain from moment to moment whether to don an unbecoming pair of pyjamas and go on in his own show. Anyway, it all works out -- how, we never quite discover -- and the last scene finds the company reacting to reviews in the local press that don't seem too bad, considering.

This, remember, is 1979, long before the existence of the National Post, for which they should probably thank their lucky stars, not to mention their lucky globes. Strangely though, the newspapers the characters flourish feature brightly coloured artwork, so either the production is trying to update the play or it's being careless.

Its certainly slapdash in other respects. The tone of the writing is primarily comic -- that much gets across -- but on the night I saw it the admittedly sparse audience failed to muster a single unanimous laugh. The only scenes that approach competence are the occasional conciliatory ones, which just about play themselves. I spent much of the evening replaying the dialogue in my own mind, and speculating on how amusing it might have been, given competent timing and intelligent inflection.

The best I can say for Eli Lukawitzs production is that nobody in it overacts. You feel that some of them couldn't even recognize a dramatic climax, let alone play one; it's all unbelievably listless.

The actor I liked best was Sarah Robinson, whose character -- some kind of wardrobe mistress -- barely appears. Samuel Owen, in black horn-rims and matching sweater, at least looks right as the dramatist; so does Allan Price as the drunken Celt, and he also seems to have the right temperament. But he never engages with Jane Moffat, who plays the homecoming queen as an amiable trouper without an ounce of glamour; their stormy relationship never actually happens, though its supposed to spark the play. Price plays the director as a middle-aged, ineffectual fusspot. (Mr Glassco, if its truly meant to be him, has ripened with the years into gentle sagehood, but at the time of the events presented here he was, by all accounts, a young pioneer.)

Other performances range from null to unspeakable. And who knows if the director could have done more with the cast, or they could have done more without him? One of the characters jovially remarks that "half of the audience come here expecting the set to fall down," which suggests the Tarragon has come a long way in the intervening 20 years, technically if not dramaturgically. But it must be possible to suggest poverty with more wit than is shown on this occasion. The physical design is not so much economical as grudging.
