There was an episode of 24 in which Jack Bauer extracted a suspects confession by threatening to do terrible things to his wife and family, driving the point home with a video link to the possible victims. It turned out that Jack was bluffing, though not before his flame of the season had told him that he had become as bad as the terrorists he was pursuing. There's a similar situation in Nicolas Billon's play Butcher, except that this time it isnt a bluff.

Or then again, maybe it is. Butcher is a devilishly clever piece of work, and one of the most diabolical things about it, for a reviewer, is that its almost impossible to describe without giving too much away. Butcher springs one surprise after another; and though a good play should of course be able to stand up to a second viewing, that hardly invalidates the first-time pleasures of suspense, which in this case are extreme. Billon keeps you on the edge of your seat throughout; his script would make a fabulous 24 episode. It digs deeper, though, than that show usually did; or at least it conscientiously tries to. There is, though, such a thing as being too clever for your own good, or at least for your own good intentions.

Anyway, here goes. We appear to be in a Toronto police station. It's Christmas Eve, and the inspector wants to get home to his children, the younger of whom needs reassurance about the existence of Santa. In the copshop itself, though, Santa has arrived; at least a man in a Santa hat has been dropped off, dazed and speaking no English, the only clue to his identity being a business card on which has been scrawled "please arrest me" and that bears the name of a local lawyer. The lawyer is summoned, but all he can contribute is that the man comes from a recently war-torn country called Lavinia, a theatrical stand-in for all the European hellholes of the post-Soviet era.

Around the stranger's neck is a butchers hook. Before the play is over, we will learn that just such a hook was a favourite implement of the commandant of a Lavinian concentration camp. He was in fact known as the Butcher, and is on Interpols wanted-list, as well as that of a group of victim-survivors who call themselves the Furies.

Atrocities beget atrocities, and the play is cunning about just how explicitly to present them: about when to let us off its own hook and when to impale us on it. Both its tact and its cruelty are precisely mirrored in Weyni Mengesha's excruciatingly excellent production. Tony Nappo makes a persuasive hardbitten copper; John Koensgen expresses fear and anger while speaking entirely in Lavinian

(a language invented for the occasion); and Michelle Monteith brings her uncanny practicality, plus a talent for lightning changes of front, to a nurse unexpectedly fluent in the same language. As the lawyer (specialty, copyright), raised in Britain and ostentatiously civilized, Andrew Musselman adjudicates, admirably and agonizingly, between the claims of the wild justice called revenge and the tamer and more dilatory justice called justice.

Which is the question the play finally poses.

It gets there ingeniously; initially incredible coincidences slide into place with perfect logic. A character whom we had taken, if not for a moral compass then at least for a reasonably decent Everyman, turns out to be no such thing. This, however demanded by the plot, is one twist too many. It makes you doubt - or made me doubt - the credibility, not just the narrative plausibility, of the play as a whole. The political thriller is a slippery theatrical genre; Friedrich Durrenmatt's The Physicists is one of the few to keep action and texture in balance to the end. The end of this one suggests that the authors head is in the right place. But it still feels like an exercise, though a riveting one.

Butcher is the first offering in a three-play season presented by Why Not Theatre (head honcho, Ravi Jain). Meanwhile the Factory has begun its own new Naked season, presenting remounts of local plays from the recent past - or, as theyre rather too promiscuously known, Canadian classics. The initial production, of Linda Griffiths' Age of Arousal, tends to confirm suspicions that the Naked imperative - no sets, off-the-peg costumes - might be economic rather than artistic. The Factory's normally dodgy acoustics are much worsened by the echoing void, particularly ruinous to this play, a whimsically panoramic study of late 19th-century English feminism, heavily dependent on its language.

The characters excursions into "thoughtspeak" - Griffiths term for the traditional "aside" - are here indistinguishable from their more conventional speakspeak, much of. which is indistinguishable on its own account. In Jennifer Brewin's production, it's every woman (theres only one man) for herself. They mostly muddle through; Juno Rinaldi is especially good at flicking off sarcastic commentary, while Julie Stewart excels at bestowing looks of worried concern. She plays the senior character, a former suffragette who teaches her juniors to type and thus acquire economic independence while leaving them to acquire the sexual and emotional variety on their own. The production has been twice trumped by the Shaw Festival: by a superior staging of the same play some seasons ago, and by this years lunchtime rediscovery of J. M. Barrie's The Twelve Pound Look, an earlier and far more trenchant and witty demonstration of the emancipating power of the Remington.
