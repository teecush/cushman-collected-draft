Red One Theatre Collective's production of Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf ? is the first I have seen to make me believe in the imaginary son. Or rather, it's made me believe in why George and Martha believe it. For those who may not know: they, the couple at the black heart of Edward Albee's play, have no children but have indulged in the shared fantasy of having one. This is one of the ways in which they try to shore up their 20-year marriage, which needs all the shoring-up it can get. Their other principal resources are alcohol and savage jokes, mostly at one another's expense though other targets are welcome when available.

The time is the early 1960s: the Kennedy era. The place is a university town in New England. George is a history professor, though not - as Martha never ceases to remind him and everybody else - the head of it: this despite the fact that he should have had the inside track, what with Martha's father being the university president. When we meet them, the happy couple have just returned home from a faculty party. Martha, to George's initially mild displeasure, has invited two of the other attendees to drop in for a few extra drinks.

These guests are another. much younger married couple, also childless: Nick, a newly appointed biology prof, and his wife Honey. George and Martha, compulsive grudgeholders and character assassins, now have an audience to play to. Nick and Honey are both fascinated and horrified, though she leans more toward the fascination and he toward the horror. It becomes clear that their marriage has as shaky a foundation as that of their hosts, and fewer builtin defences.

Red One's production is a family affair. George is played by Booth Savage, Martha by his wife Janet-Laine Green, and the play has been directed, excellently, by their un-imaginary son Tyrone Savage. I don't know whether these intimate connections have contributed to the immediacy of the experience, but another kind of intimacy certainly does. Being an acknowledged modern classic, Who's Afraid usually gets produced in medium-to-large institutional spaces. Red One operates out of the tiny Storefront Theatre; the same venue in which the same company gave us last year's Strindberg adaptation After Miss Julie. That was another memorable Walpurgisnacht, Walpurgisnacht being the sub-title that Albee gave to one of the three acts of his marathon play. Being so close to the action certainly drives home the brutality of both pieces. But it also, perhaps less expectedly, emphasizes the compassion. Or to put it more prosaically, I felt sorrier for Albee's characters, as for Strindberg's, than I ever have before. Booth Savage's George is, in his scruffy cardigan and red shirt, the very image of a disappointed academic, taking it out on everybody else. He's wonderfully sly and waspish in his intellectual dismemberment of Nick, the scientist who Martha claims will deposit old humanists like him in the dustbin of history. Trouble is, George more than suspects she may be right; in the meantime he takes enormous pleasure in purposely misunderstanding everything Nick says about his work and pouncing on everything he inadvertently reveals about his life and wife. He's better at the one-liners than he is at George's longer prose arias, but he's splendidly sacerdotal when he comes to the Exorcism, Albee's sub-title for his last act. This is when George announces, in public, the death of their fictitious son. Martha is horrified; the boy, every detail of his life carefully fabricated, was meant to be their secret. George points out that she already had told Honey about it; she broke their agreement.

So his destroying the fantasy is partly judgment and partly revenge, for her breach of faith and for her at least attempting to cuckold him with Nick. (Nick had had too much to drink. That's the trouble with the younger generation. They can't hold their liquor.)

But it's also a kind of mercykilling. Martha really had come to believe in "the little bugger" and she'll be better off without it. The thing is this couple, unlike Nick and Honey, really do love one another. At the very least, they need one another, and this production makes that manifest. Green's Martha is commonplace in her earlier wisecracks and put-downs. But she flares into life in her seduction scene; hilariously self-aware in her slinkiness, she's putting Nick down as much as she's leading him on. Then, in the last act, hysterically fighting off disillusion before succumbing to it, she is piteous, beyond anything I have known in this play before. The play's title denotes fear of suicide. For once, it doesn't seem forced or sentimental.

Benjamin Blais perfectly captures the square-shouldered all-Americanism of Nick. while Claire Armstrong's Honey is extraordinary. Honey is the real hysteric here; it was a hysterical pregnancy that occasioned her marriage, and she spends a large part of the play, having drunk too much brandy and heard too many truths, offstage, face down on the bathroom tiles: a favourite position, according to her husband. When she's on, she's mostly on the sofa, registering barometrically every change in the dramatic weather. She's shocked, she's delighted, she's intrigued, she's appalled, over and over again. Armstrong communicates - in face, posture, voice giggle and continually rearranged costume - every transition, every facet. It's a totally truthful performance: always changing, always the same.

Some doubts about the play remain. George and Martha still seem too cut-and-dried: always announcing their own strategies, always knowing what they're doing. This time, though, there's a saving ambiguity. I had always thought the imaginary child to be a crutch for the playwright rather than for the characters. This production has changed my mind. Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf ? runs through Dec. 21 at the Storefront Theatre.
