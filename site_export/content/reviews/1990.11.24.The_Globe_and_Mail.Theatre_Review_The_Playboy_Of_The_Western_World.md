THEATRE REVIEW THE PLAYBOY OF THE WESTERN WORLD

Saturday, November 24, 1990

ROBERT CUSHMAN

By J. M. Synge Directed by Vincent Dowling Starring Roma Downey Macdara O Fatharta At the Elgin Theatre, Toronto VIOLENCE EMPHASIZED"Caustic comment on a culture that venerates violence, gets a sexual charge out of it, lives on fantasy and alcohol, and is governed by counsel of unseen priests Playboy a scathing comment on the soul of Ireland BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail SURE, and it must have been a glorious thing to be alive in Dublin in 1907, when the Abbey Theatre was giving The Playboy of the Western World its world premiere, the way the people would be takin' to the streets and riotin' and they red-faced and slaverin' mad over the defamation of Irish womanhood and the utterin' of filthy words.

Today's realism is tomorrow's escape, and the problem with The Playboy -maybe inside Ireland and certainly outside - is how to recapture that delicious feeling of shock. Synge's characters live in what one assumes to be direst poverty in the remotest part of the country, and they seem to have little on their minds besides sex, drink and violence, but you have to analyze long and hard before these facts come into relief.

The piece plays, stubbornly, like a charming fantasy; it may be hard work for the heroine to get up early in the morning to fetch goat's milk for the hero's breakfast, but to us it seems kind of cute. We expect her to meet a leprechaun on the way.

The same young lady gets to speak the word that struck the original audience as the ultimate obscenity. The word is "shift." As well as discussing her undergarments so frankly this girl, Pegeen Mike, bridles at being left alone for twleve hours while her dad goes off and gets stinking at a wake, and demands a man on the premises. This forwardness shocks her intended, a shy one called Shawn, and he rushes off to church for counsel. (The apparent concensus of priest, betrothed, and original spectators is that it would be preferable for her to be murdered in her bed.) Meanwhile Pegeen finds her protector in Christy Mahon, who wanders in from nowhere claiming to have murdered his father. This impresses the men, and dazzles the women. Pegeen, known throughout Mayo for her scoldin' tongue but actually a deep romantic, sees in him the warrior of her dreams. The local widow, and a quartet of young girls, are more starightforwardly predatory.

All this does wonders for Christy's confidence, which is dashed to the ground when his dad arises from it.

A second, more delibetare, attempt at bumping the old man off proves disastrous; the villagers - witnessing the deed instead of just hearing about it - are horrified, and Pegeen most of all. So we have a caustic comment on a culture that venerates violence, actually gets a sexual charge out of it, lives on fantasy and alcohol, and is governed by the counsel of an unseen priest.

If they got beneath their surface outrage the rioters should have seen a scathing comment on the soul of Ireland. Very likely they did.

But it doesn't play that way. Sean O'Casey's Abbey plays (among which The Shadow of a Gunman is alsmost an urban rewrite of The Playboy) say much the same thing, and say it less eloquently, but they carry a greater theatrical charge, simply because they are set amidst actual historical carnage that is still with us, even if its address has changed from Dublin to Belfast.

Synge has bequeathed us a wry fable - tart but charming.

The new Abbey production, on tour in North America, is a conscientious attempt to preserve the flavor while capitalizing on the charm. The cottage-cum-pub in which the play is set - the Pegeen shebeen - is sparely furnished but seductively lit. Some of its outside walls are down, so we get a glimpse of countryside and of characters approaching through it: ingratiating in itself. Characters, especially female ones, flounce around musical-comedy style but the violence is carefully emphasized. It culminates in Pegeen applying a firebrand to the leg of the bound and helpless Christy. This is a savage moment, but its impact quickly passes.

This is odd because it seems to be happening in slow motion. The action is preceded, accompanied and followed by acres of chat. That is the way in this play. All the characters talk, talk, talk; or - with acknowledgements both to Benny Goodman and the play's celebrasted folk- poetic quality - they Synge, Synge, Synge.

It is not so much the dialogue that is difficult to animate - it is lyrical, pungent and sometimes very funny, if incurably hung up on its own metaphors - as the reactions to it. Anyone not talking in this play is on his own. That at least is the impression derived from Vincent Dowling's production which is mosytly good at driving a straight dramatic line through the speeches while relishhing their style, but leaves those listening to them much to their own devices. There is a lot of standing around, registering by numbers.

Some of the most enjoyable performances are the most ancestrally shameless: John Cowley waddling and slithering as Pegeen's pa, drinking himself asleep at a wake, or Peadar Lambe, a real Abbey veteran, as one of his two choric cronies: the one with the better lines, or at least the more accommodating timing. (You know when he is pausing for a laugh, but one reason is that you are actually laughing.) Frank McCusker is a febrile, wire-drawn Christy, definitely a person if not quite a prototype. (The play does have religious overtones beyond the obvious satiric ones; it teems with scourgings and resurrections, and its protagonist is not Chrysty-an for nothing.) Roma Downey's Pegeen, physically and temperamentally, is pretty rather than handsome, nothing for anyone to be afraid of, which diminishes Christy's conquest. There is a buxom widow - would there be any other kind? - and among the four girls, whose names I can never get straight, there is a very striking ringleader.

People are always adapting this play. How about a slicked-up homogenised version called The Penthouse of the Western World?
