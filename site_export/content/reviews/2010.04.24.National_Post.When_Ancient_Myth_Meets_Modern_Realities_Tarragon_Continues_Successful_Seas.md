This is the story of Philomela, as told by the Roman poet Ovid. Philomela was the daughter of Pandion, King of Athens. Her father married off her sister Procne to his ally and vassal Tereus, King of Thrace. At Procne's request Tereus sailed to Athens to bring Philomela on a visit; on the voyage, he lusted after her and, on their arrival, took her to a cabin where he raped her. To prevent her telling, he cut her tongue out. She still, however, had the use of her hands, and with them she wove a tapestry that informed Procne of her fate. The sisters took a grisly revenge on Tereus, one involving his and Procne's young son. Not to give too much away but when Shakespeare's horror-comic protagonist Titus Andronicus proclaimed "Worse than Philomel you used my daughter, And worse than Procne will I be revenged" he was setting the bar very high. At retribution's climax, Procne, Philomela and Tereus were all changed by the gods into birds, Philomela most famously into a nightingale, thus qualifying for a triple in Ovid's magnum opus The Metamorphoses.

Their transformation also provides the title and governing image for Erin Shields' startlingly fine play If We Were Birds. Much of it is a straightforward retelling of the myth in barely noticeable verse: easy conversational exchanges that are alternately stark and playful. But it also opens the story up to wider, later vistas. As befits a Greek tragedy it has a chorus: six women, enslaved by one king or the other. They tell their own stories, of capture and violation; and as one talks of soldiers gang-raping her in her home ( "My husband refuses to see me. It was a lot for him, poor thing"), and another mentions internment-camps, it becomes clear that they also represent the victims of contemporary atrocities, from Bosnia to Rwanda and beyond. This is remarkably unforced, ancient myth and modern reality illuminating one another, as they're always supposed to but very rarely do. A powerful link is established between military and sexual enforcement. The legendary women are able to strike back in ways unavailable to those of the present day, but then they do have the advantage of being princesses.

The parallels strike less hard in Alan Dilworth's current production than when he first directed the play, with an almost identical cast, at Summerworks two years ago. I can't claim total recall of the original, but my impression is that rewriting, elongation and the move to a more formal venue have diluted the impact a little. But only a little. It's no longer the surprise that it was to find a newish playwright writing so extraordinarily well, but at least two of her scenes seem to me to touch greatness. Both are domestic episodes that forecast or reflect the central horrors, and both are superbly staged. One finds the two sisters sharing a bath, comparing their respective records for staying underwater and comparing what they're discovering about their own bodies and what they've heard about men's: specifically about the respective "fleshy utensils" flopping between each gender's legs. It's both more charming and more ominous than I've made it sound. The other finds old king Pandion, sick and senile but possessive as ever, berating his slave-women as if each of them were the daughter whom he simultaneously laments as having drowned. Author, actors and director combine, here and elsewhere, to freeze characters in the moment while keeping them flesh and blood and troubling: in fact, to make them mythic. David Fox's Pandion, pettish, gloating and obsessed with the grisly technicalities of far-off conflicts, suggests his Donald Rumsfeld (in Stuff Happens) translated to a grander sphere. Tara Rosling, in an astonishing elemental performance, captures Philomela as in a set of snapshots. First comes girlish awareness, rendered completely without self-consciousness; then, imperious naivete, demanding her attendants' experiences ( "speak or I won't give you any lunch"); then, half-aware young womanhood; then horrified resistance and recrimination; then mute, vindictive fury (in both senses of the word). The ineradicable memory of the girl makes what happens to the woman all the more appalling. Philippa Domville's Procne goes, in her best performance to date, a more sophisticated version of the same route. Geoffrey Pounsett's Tereus doesn't have the same iconic effect, but his role doesn't allow it; he's sweatily, uncomfortably human, a decent enough chap, in his own estimation at least, when not terrorizing people and blaming it on his "blood." This, one of the play's key words, informs the spasms of red that periodically splash across Jung-Hye Kim's mostly white set, and which Dilworth's staging brilliantly orchestrates. The chorus now includes a couple of older women, Karen Robinson and Barbara Gordon, the latter peering and squinting at the action as one who has seen the worst and only expects more of it.

The least satisfying part of the text, oddly, is the stuff about the birds. It doesn't fly. The Greeks, never too obsessed with moralizing, seem to have regarded such last-minute transmutations as a kind of escape: a celestial washing of the hands. Shields wants it to be the opposite; however much they flap their wings, these women can never forget their experiences, nor indeed can their oppressors. But I don't see that she needs the image to make the point. There's a touch of preciousness about it, the kind that, magnified, made her Epic of Gilgamesh (last year's Summerworks) a fable thrashing about for a meaning and her contribution to Red Machine (last year's Fringe) almost unbearable. This play, though, surmounts it. It tells a story -- cleanly, hauntingly, thrillingly, wittily -- and makes it reverberate. Its presence at the Tarragon crowns what must be this theatre's best season ever.
