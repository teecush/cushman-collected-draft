THEATRE REVIEW Young cast struggles to develop Album's snapshots of the sixties Album

Friday, March 09, 1990

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Written by David Rimmer Directed by Bernadette Jones Starring Laura Partland, Kirk Dunn, Shelley Owens and Bryan Elliot At the Tarragon Extra Space BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail THE PRESS release says: "Modern Times of Theatreworks Productions seeks to reach out to young adults in an effort to reflect their needs and to give young artists a unique opportunity to work in a professional and challenging setting."

These are noble aims, and only a curmudgeon would fault them. But they do entail pitfalls, and into those pits MT of TP has fallen. The cast it has assembled for David Rimmer's play, Album, at the Tarragon Extra Space, is only just professional, and the setting may be a little too challenging.

Also, I am unsure who the young adults are whose needs are being reflected. According to the PR, the magic age range is from 15 to 25, and Album does indeed deal with four kids growing together, apart, and together again from 14 to 18. But the years in which they do it are the mid-l960s, and the play's heavy reliance on the sounds and icons of that era may make it of greatest appeal to today's young middle-aged.

Peggy and Trish and Billy and Boo - it sounds like a pop song already - transfer their adulation from the Beach Boys to the Beatles and from the Beatles to Bob Dylan. Progressive degeneration, I'd call it, but then I just missed being a baby boomer. Anyway, the songs, quoted in the dialogue and heard between the scenes, exemplify my personal definition of nostalgia: music one thought negligible at the time but that casts an irresistible spell years later. What today's young make of it, only they know.

Still, let us assume that youth is always youth and that one generation's rites of passage will seem poignant and funny to another. Album starts with an abortive game of strip poker, the girls running for cover and the boys stomping about in self-consciously masculine frustration. Cutting across the gender lines are one confident pair, Peggy and Billy; Trish and Boo are separately despondent over whether they will ever make out. It is reassuring, considering that they are only 14 and this is only 1963, to discover that for them "making out" means at most heavy petting. Of course, it turns out that Peggy and Billy have their problems, too, and that Trish and Boo have less to worry about than they might think. At least Trish does; Boo seems well on the way to being a fully paid-up weirdo. He worships Dylan, despite Billy's impeccably mathematical put-down: "How can he be greater than the Beatles? They're four guys." Boo's discourse becomes a full-time Dylan impression: the snarl, the whine and the more aggressive sections of the lyrics. One of the problems with Bernadette Jones' production is that, though Kirk Dunn does a very good Dylan, he cannot place the impersonation within the characterization of Boo. He is too belligerent to be good company, but the audience has to spend a lot of time with him.

This is just an extreme example of a general malaise. The actors may be slightly older than the characters but they are still too young, or too raw, for the play. The U.S. theatre turns out such pubescent memoirs in large quantities, and they need speed and flash and wit and charm. They need to be framed, not by production tricks but by the actors' own skills. You cannot act vulnerability just by being vulnerable. The characters' tentativeness here is often indistinguishable from the actors'. Missing the pain, they also miss the joy. The play is meant to end in a precarious afterglow, the foursome - fresh out of high school and finally into sex - joyfully hurling appropriate song titles at one another. It is plainly intended to be a great scene. Here, it is just dogged.

The set, even given the constrictions of the space, is baldly unevocative. A scene meant to be done in near-darkness is staged in a half-hearted murk. The play seems truthful but dull.

Bryan Elliott is the most advanced actor, too slight for Billy but with the right dubious swagger. Shelley Owens as Peggy - "the girl most likely to be the girl most likely" - is a great provocative gum-chewer. Laura Partland as sensitive Trish is, well, sensitive. They have their individual moments of rightness, but they never fizz together.

According to his program bio, Album was David Rimmer's "first Broadway hit." Come on; Album was staged in 1980 at the delightful but diminutive Cherry Lane, a prototypical off-Broadway house, and it lasted there four months. As to what his subsequent Broadway hits may have been, the program is oddly coy. On this evidence, we have still to mark him down as a promising playwright, in need of more than promising performance.
