The "Palestinian Chicken" episode of Curb Your Enthusiasm, shown two Sundays ago, ended in a classic standoff. On one side of the screen, a group of Los Angeles Jews, protesting the opening of a Palestinian restaurant next door to a deli, this apparently being the Californian equivalent of a mosque at Ground Zero. On the other side, a group of Palestinians protesting the protest. And in the middle, Larry David, his allegiances hopelessly split, partly by his lust for Palestinian chicken, but more by his lust for a Palestinian chick, who had given him the best sex of his life and called him a dirty Jew while giving it. He had first attracted her attention by getting into a fight with a coreligionist who had tried to enter a similar restaurant while wearing a yarmulke. Larry, it seems, likes a little bit of trangressiveness but draws the line at outright provocation.

The appeal of Curb Your Enthusiasm is that it displays everybody in the worst possible light; it's the most misanthropic American comedy since, I should think, W.C. Fields. My own enthusiasm for it was severely curbed by the last season, which engineered a Seinfeld reunion while still insisting that it was a very bad idea (and proving it). The new series, though, is more than a return to form; it's the best ever. The "Palestinian Chicken" finale illustrates why. It's something of a political metaphor; it scales wonderful heights of bad taste; and it brilliantly pulls together all the themes and plot-threads of the preceding half-hour. Construction, despite the apparent casualness, is where the show is strongest. All the elements of each instalment pay off. There's a prize example coming up this Sunday. The ingredients include the putting-down and last rites of Larry's best friend's wife's dog; the greed of both Larry and friend for a particular brand of ice cream; Larry's own outrage at motorists who straddle two

parking spaces; the earnings of Lexus driving vets; and a vow of silence taken, for spiritual reasons, by a guy who's goaded to break it at the worst possible moment. Feydeau, the master of French farce, would have approved that last device.

It's tempting to call Larry the ultimate self-hating Jew, but I'm not sure that sticks. His social circle and his frame of reference are thoroughly Jewish, or Jewish-American (I doubt if Gentiles have any trouble understanding the show but I do wonder if they find it funny), but Larry doesn't have enough passion to hate, or love, anyone. What he's very good at is criticizing or despising. Here, of course, we often cheer him on. His attacks range from the petty (parking) to the devastatingly logical: As he says, a vow of silence is pretty meaningless when you still expect people to read your lips. It's common knowledge that David, when writing Seinfeld, based the character of George on himself, but George has the sympathetic safety net of being an all-around loser. Larry is rich and successful and he gets laid with implausible frequency, though of course the last quality may be a consequence of the first two. George sometimes insulted people on purpose, but mostly he did it by accident. Larry makes a point of it, is even (sometimes) applauded for it. It's his contribution to the social mix: speaking truth to powerlessness. As a performer, David is limited, but he does what he does -- or is what he is -- very well. And he retains his gift for inventing the phrases we never knew we needed. To George's "double dipping" (and Elaine's "sponge-worthy" and the whole crew's "master of your own domain") we can now add Larry's "chat and cut," meaning jumping the queue in a buffet line by latching on to an acquaintance. In Larry's world, such things are vital.

I especially recommend tuning in to HBO Canada on Sept. 4, when the scene is New York (Larry has fled there to avoid a charity event in L.A.) and the narrative involves a lost softball game, a scorned baseball player, a psychiatrist who doesn't mention names but one of his clients directed Star Wars, an aphrodisiac carseat, and Larry's boyhood encounter, socially and sexually traumatic, with an ice-cream van: the episode is called "Mister Softee." This one has a redemptive finale. Larry is not the one redeemed.
