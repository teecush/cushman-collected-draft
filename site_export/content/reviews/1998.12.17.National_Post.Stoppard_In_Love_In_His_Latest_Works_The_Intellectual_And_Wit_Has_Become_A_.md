Both of Tom Stoppard's most recent works have "love" in the title. He has only a joint screenplay credit on the new film Shakespeare in Love, but the picture has his erudite and hilarious fingerprints all over it. Meanwhile, his latest play, The Invention of Love, is running in London's West End. It too is vintage Stoppard. It too portrays an actual writer -- in this case the late Victorian poet and classical scholar A.E. Housman -- in the grip of erotic passion. Stoppard is now the foremost romantic dramatist writing in English.

This is not what he's been famous for. He's been known, quite rightly, as an intellectual and a wit. This, mysteriously, has caused him to be labelled cold and frivolous. In the two new pieces, romantic love co-exists with the love of learning, or of art, or of show business, and is the richer for it.

Shakespeare in Love is partly Stoppard in love -- in love with the theatre, of all ages. The Elizabethan playhouse manager Philip Henslowe -- historically a notorious loan shark -- is softened into a tradesman who has gone into the business for profit and has been caught up in the economic irrationality of it all. (A Renaissance Ed Mirvish perhaps, though not as well dressed.) Shakespeare (Joseph Fiennes) is the Bankside's flavour of the month, assailing his friend and rival Christopher Marlowe with the deadliest of compliments: "I loved your early stuff." John Webster, future author of such horror comic tragedies as The Duchess of Malfi, is an unappetizing youth, playing with rats and relishing the more sadistic death scenes.

In Shakespeare's time female roles were played by boys. Like at least two novels and one play before it, the movie offers a stage- struck young woman disguising her gender in order to become the first (uncredited) English actress. In most versions she gets to play Juliet; the present treatment is more sophisticated, and Gwyneth Paltrow's Lady Viola spends a lot of time playing Romeo behind an endearingly false little beard. And, of course, she bequeaths Shakespeare the name of a heroine for Twelfth Night. And, of course, they fall wildly for each other, despite him having a wife back in Stratford, and her being betrothed. And we -- buoyed up on the film's mixture of anachronistic satire, learned fantasy and radiant goodwill -- believe in and root for them. The action of the movie plays a brilliant and very Stoppardian cat-and-mouse game with that of Romeo and Juliet itself. Romeo is the play that first brought love believably to the stage -- or so we are told by Queen Elizabeth herself, magisterially played by the great Judi Dench in a performance that leaves her Queen Victoria in the dust.

So the film might actually have been called The Invention of Love if the play hadn't got there first. According to its hero, Housman, (best known as the author of The Shropshire Lad) love was invented by the ancient Roman poets. Horace and Catullus, in writing the first formal love lyrics, gave the rest of us something to live up to.

Certainly Housman himself takes them to heart. However, his passion, given his time and temperament, can hardly be declared, let alone consummated. He loves Jackson, a good-natured and thoroughly heterosexual athlete whom he met when they were both undergraduates.

Jackson's realization ("it's terrible but it's not your fault") and Housman's suffering are conveyed with an agony and delicacy unprecedented in Stoppard.

"You're half my life," says Housman. The other half is textual criticism; he's just as passionate about that, and a lot more articulate. Stoppard takes as his subject the scholar's lust to establish an accurate classical text and he actually makes it register theatrically as passion. It's difficult to imagine another playwright doing that.

It's also impossible to think of another actor who could embody that passion as thrillingly as John Wood, for years a player of flawless technical and intellectual prowess. In 1974, Stoppard wrote the central role in Travesties for him; and that play -- a kaleidoscopic political-literary-intellectual vaudeville -- in many ways prefigured The Invention of Love. Its onstage characters included Lenin and James Joyce, but it was haunted by the offstage presence of Oscar Wilde; a large part of the dialogue was pastiche of The Importance of Being Earnest.

The new play is presented as Housman's deathbed dream. We first meet him, as befits a deceased classicist, being ferried across to Hades. ("I don't suppose I"ll have time to meet everybody," he says to Charon, the mythological boatman. "Yes, you will," says Charon.) Later, he gets to meet his younger self, encounters to which Wood brings a pathos and vulnerability that I have never seen in him before. This play too is haunted by Oscar Wilde, an offstage personality in everyone's lives, first as the Oxford aesthete, then as the homosexual martyr. Finally, Wilde makes a personal appearance, to confront Housman -- the peacock recklessness of the artist against the lifelong timidity of the scholar. As usual in Stoppard, everything dovetails.

In fact, he's been a romantic -- an intelligent romantic -- for most of his career. Not quite all: His first success, Rosencrantz and Guildenstern are Dead, had no discernible love interest. It was an investigation of the off-stage life of two notoriously indistinguishable minor characters from Hamlet -- part metaphysical game, part literary joke, and hugely funny.

But in his next major play, Jumpers, he was already moving on.

Its hero is a professor of ethics, pondering the differences between good as in "a good man" and good as in "a good bacon sandwich."

Helplessly anachronistic, he believes in God. His opponents, very modern, believe mainly in success. His wife, a disoriented former musical comedy star, no longer believes in anything much. One endearing feature of the play is that husband and wife seem genuinely fond of one another, however incapable of doing anything constructive about it.

Personally, I cared more about them than about the couple whose relationship -- first adulterious, then marital -- was exhaustively examined in Stoppard's later play, The Real Thing. This was a naturalistic piece by his standards, and a considerable commercial success; but it seems to me the Stoppard play for people who don't like Stoppard. It had the trappings of seriousness. But with Stoppard, the lighter the touch, the deeper the feeling. Arcadia was a triumphant demonstration of this. It juggled historical periods.

It took in literature, landscape gardening, and chaos theory. It also took in sex. And it was outrageously persuasive about all of them.

Shakespeare in Love is full of in-jokes, but they're incidental; if you miss them you won't miss them. The Invention of Love is more demanding: allusive, not to say elusive, even by Stoppard's standards. But the film and the play, a light comedy and a dark, go well together.

There has always been a pervasive joyousness in the way Stoppard structures his plays. The delight he takes in his own craft and his own wit is contagious; it suggests that life might be worth living and people worth loving -- unusual ideas to encounter in the modern theatre. As in all the best comedy -- screwball or Shakespeare, even sometimes in life -- laughter sets you up for romance.
