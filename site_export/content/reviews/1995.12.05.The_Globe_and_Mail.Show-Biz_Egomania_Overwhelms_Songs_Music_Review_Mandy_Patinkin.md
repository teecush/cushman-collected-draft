Show-biz egomania overwhelms songs MUSIC REVIEW MANDY PATINKIN

Tuesday, December 05, 1995

Robert Cushman

At OKeefe Centre in Toronto, Saturday Reviewed by Robert Cushman BATTLESHIP Patinkin steamed into the OKeefe Centre on the weekend and fired himself a 12-gun salute. He began with a trick I have never seen before: He stood, his face eerily lit in the surrounding darkness, while the Canadian national anthem played. Before the show had even started, he had got the audience to its feet.

Later on, he joked about this, as part of a fast resume of the act so far for the benefit of late-comers. Mandy Patinkin's solo performances (Solo apart from his excellent pianist, Paul Ford) are a three-way tussle between his highly wrought, not to say overwrought, emoting in song; the deflationary humour that sometimes escapes between numbers; and his audience's determination - greatly reinforced by his TV successes - to love him no matter what.

Patinkin performs on a bare stage but still manages to chew the scenery. His repertoire, of pop and especially theatre songs from all over the century, is extraordinary, and he pays an actors attention to their texts; he had me hanging on his every word. But the suspense was not often pleasurable. His range extends to some sonorous low notes (he uses them to haunting effect on Once Upon a Time, a very tricky song) but he works most of the time in an intense falsetto. Its beautiful on its own terms, and he pitches it perfectly, but the ethereal tone suits very oddly with the earthy material. It resounds to the singer's glory rather than the songs' it sounds self reverential.

Patinkin combines the black-clad accoutrements of art-song cabaret with the uninhibited egomania of the great show-biz hams: Al Jolson or the forgotten Ted Lewis. (He even sings some of their songs.) But their exhibitionism is forgivable, even endearing, because, to put it bluntly, they didnt know any better. Patinkin, a talented and intelligent actor, does, and there is something terribly self-conscious about his carryings- on. Its also self-defeating; marathon numbers such as Ya Got Trouble (from The Music Man) and The Soliloquy from Carousel are certainly tours- de-force, but they are supposed to look easy. The Soliloquy carries all its drama on its musical line; to talk or shout is to diminish it.

Patinkin shouts, or screams, a lot. He does it to show off pathos (in Me and My Shadow), to show off political correctness (in South Pacific's Carefully Taught), or just to show off (in The Atchison, Topeka and the Santa Fe). Always, it destroys the song, which often was going along quite nicely up to that point. I did enjoy his transformation of Ella Fitzgerald's A Tisket, A Tasket, switching between the voices of bratty little girl and policeman with bull horn; maybe his bravura works best with bad songs. And I like his brisk, unfussy treatment of Cohen Owes Me $97, a very old and very funny Irving Berlin song that Berlin once offered to pay Groucho Marx not to sing. (Hed decided it was anti-Semitic).

He also sang a sweet and soothing Jitter Bug Waltz, until he blew the lyrics, which wouldnt have mattered in the least except that he made such a performance of doing it over and over until he got it right. I suppose it was spontaneous, but it came off as part of the machinery; even his self-deprecation seemed self-serving. It makes no sense to arraign any performer for narcissism - why else would they be up there? - but something is supposed to transcend that. Patinkin has his audience's adoration, no quarter asked. But he gives the impression that the songs he sings are there to love him, too, and I thought I heard some of them, above the roar, squeaking in protest.
