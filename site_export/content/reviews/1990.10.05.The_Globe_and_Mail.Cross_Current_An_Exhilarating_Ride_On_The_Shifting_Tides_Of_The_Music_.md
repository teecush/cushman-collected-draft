CROSS CURRENT An exhilarating ride on the shifting tides of the music business

Friday, October 05, 1990

ROBERT CUSHMAN

ROBERT CUSHMAN IBLAME Ella Fitzgerald. She marked me for life. My first clear pop-record memory, I have to admit, is of Music, Music, Music, with Teresa Brewer beseeching everybody within earshot to put another nickel in, in the nickelodeon, because all she wanted was loving us and the title phrase. But then nostalgia can exact blushes from us all. The reference books tell me that song came out in 1950; I would have been 6. The radio was constantly on in my home, but it was Teresa who first made me aware of the shifting tides of the music business. For a few weeks her hit number was inescapable and then gradually it disappeared, to be replaced by something else that followed the same course. I accepted this as a system, benevolently ordained. I was right about the system part.

For some years after this it seemed that every song I heard was a bouncy shanty about girls who wore red feathers with hulie-hulie skirts (I blush again but that's how they pronounced it) or were called - clap, clap - pretty little black-eyed Susie. Everything seemed to have a Naval angle. I didn't know it at the time but I was listening to Korean war pop. All the songs were sung by the same man, so omnipresent that it would have seemed sacrilegious to inquire his name. (He did have one. It was Guy Mitchell.) This was the fag-end of the Tin Pan Alley tradition that had been dominant since 1900; my generation was being softened up for rock and roll.

I escaped. My mother, like everybody's, was a Crosby fan. Bing was singing novelties too, but they were wittier than other people's; Busy Doing Nothing struck my playmates and me as admirable philosophy, and Sunshine Cake at least sounded edible. I like to think, too, that his musicianship got to me although it still puzzles me that so avuncular a figure could ever have been a sex symbol.

I was also taken to movies. Even before my Brewer experience I saw Easter Parade. Fred Astaire and Judy Garland doing A Couple of Swells introduced me to show business at its most delightful. I didn't know that Irving Berlin had written it or that such a person existed, but in retrospect I see that it was typical of him. He had spent most of the century doing the right thing at the right time. Called upon now for a show-stopper, or movie-capper, he simply and unostentatiously provided it. I was lucky. A generation later I might have had to make do with Hello Dolly! It could have stunted my growth.

I got hooked on the theatre. Guys and Dolls and My Fair Lady became part of my mythology. The airwaves were still filled with songs from the shows, and I learned some names, Gershwin, Cole Porter, Rodgers and Hammerstein. In 1960 I bought Ella Fitzgerald Sings the Cole Porter Song Book Volume One and sealed my fate as a song-freak.

Ella was working her way methodically through the Broadway repertoire and I followed her, generally a record or three in arrears. Her Rodgers and Hart set especially got to me: vulnerable as well as sophisticated, intoxicatingly witty and melodic, and the essence, I was sure, of being young, broke and in love. (Hart's characters neaerly always sound like perpetual students.) The albums were also my gateway to jazz. I know now that there were emotional depths in the songs she never sounded; Sinatra taught me that. I also know that there were many songs she didn't get around to; the Manhattan cabaret specialists, Mabel Mercer and Bobby Short, taught me that, beginning with my first trip to New York in 1967, just before a new Dark Age removed their records from circulation.

Whitney Balliett, The New Yorker's jazz critic and champion list-maker, once spent two pages cataloguing the great American singers of great American songs, and he still left plenty out. It's an accommodating repertoire that has thrived on cross-fertilization; theatre, movies, jazz and pop have continually swapped idioms, in writing and performance, and even some classicists and rockers have contributed. Much of the foundation was laid by blacks and immigrant Jews, running creatively riot among European harmonies and English words. They fused them into an American language; even the debased stuff, kiddie-pop for adults, that I was exposed to in the early fifties, preserved some of its disciplined slanginess. It exhilarated me then, and it still does.
