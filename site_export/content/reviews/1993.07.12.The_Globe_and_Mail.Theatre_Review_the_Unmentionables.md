THEATRE REVIEW THE UNMENTIONABLES

Monday, July 12, 1993

ROBERT CUSHMAN

BY CARL STERNHEIM Translated and adapted by Paul Lampert and Kate Sullivan Directed by Paul Lampert Starring Norman Browning, Tracey Ferencz and Wendy Thatcher At the Court House Theatre, Niagara-on-the Lake Rating: A German comedy about underwear transplanted to the Shaw Festival offers fun, social comment and large helpings of nostalgia for the 1950s Consumerism exposed in translation BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail THE title chosen by the Shaw Festival for a German comedy previously billed in English as The Underpants or The Knickers, is The Unmentionables . ("Truly," remarked Eric Bentley, an earlier translator, "an inexhaustible subject.") Anyway, whatever they are, they are inadvertently dropped - offstage, I hasten to add - by a young married woman in a public place. And that is where the story really starts.

It was told originally in 1911 by Carl Sternheim, a satirical playwright who devoted much of his oeuvre to anatomizing the rising middle class of his own time and place. At the Shaw, these have been transposed to the United States of the Eisenhower years. Theodor Maske, minor civil servant and husband in the original play, becomes Ted Mask, ditto, in the adaptation. His fury at his wife Louise's bloomer, or bloomers, begins the play. What will the neighbours think? What might the government think? What were the bystanders' thoughts?

The last question is quickly answered: unclean, unclean. Two male spectators follow the Masks to their suburban bungalow (Flushing, N.Y.), hoping for more. They rent rooms, to Ted's avaricious satisfaction. One is a preening Frenchman, who enters through the window and spouts philosophy. The other is an extremely nervous barber. Louise, who feels both oppressed and neglected by her husband, prepares after a short preliminary stage of virtuous indignation to succumb to the more romantic of the two. She is much encouraged in this by a female neighbour who has resigned herself to living vicariously, and who sets about embroidering her friend some new and frillier underwear.

I guess you could call Sternheim's original a play about fetishism. (The word I heard bandied about on the Niagara streets was "risque." The implication was that the metropolitan sophisticates who run the Shaw, and we decadents who write about it, have lost touch with the people.) The current adapters, Paul Lampert and Kate Sullivan, give us, by fairly logical extension, a play about consumerism. Between scenes, and even during them, the relentlessly cheerful sound of 1950s TV commercials fills the blacked-out air, providing fun and broad social comment and some hefty helpings of nostalgia.

Ted, the play's monster-in-chief, is Mr. Materialist, pleased with his place in the world and terrified of any threat to it. The set, designed by Yvonne Sauriol and representing the kitchen-and-living-room plus a few doors, is a luscious nightmare of pinks and yellows; and Louise is dressed to match.

The German bourgeoisie of Sternheim's plan were possibly more given to conceptual thought than their North American successors. "What," asks Ted, contemplating a newspaper report of a fabulous creature discovered in the Indian Ocean, "do I have in common with this sea-serpent?" The choice of words, and even of thoughts, sounds strangely formal. But mostly the transfer works amazingly well. It even fills a gap. As greed, lust, hypocrisy and prejudice continue their spiral, this becomes the hard U.S. comedy about the 1950s that nobody would have thought to write at the time or have bothered to write since. It even avoids being patronizing. Sternheim's original generic title was From the Heroic Life of the Middle Class and he was only half joking.

Certainly there are some heroic performances here, in stature if not in ethics. Norman Browning's Ted is a mentality come to life: chunky, crew- cut, blue-suited. Only his red tie seems odd, considering he's a zealous McCarthyite, but his gleaming tie-pin - when did you last see one of those? - makes amends; it's positively artifactual. He is not inhuman; he even acquires a stolid, blinkered dignity when he defends the instincts of the little guy against the Nietzschean posturing of his Gallic lodger. William Vickers as the barber, a short and manic hypochondriac with a passionate reverence for Rodgers and Hammerstein, seems to cover all the native bases his landlord leaves untouched. Wendy Thatcher is the heroine's dowdy best friend from any sitcom with all her defences down. In this menagerie, Tracy Ferencz as Louise manages a few moments of vulnerability without insisting on them; though - I may as well rise to this bait - it seemed to me unlikely that someone capable of singing Que Sera Sera right through would need to be informed that South Pacific is a love story.
