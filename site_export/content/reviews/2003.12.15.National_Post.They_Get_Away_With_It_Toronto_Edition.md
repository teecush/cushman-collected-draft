THE PRODUCERS

Canon Theatre, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - The film The Producers was a satire; the stage show is a celebration. Partly it celebrates the movie, and the myth that it has turned into in the 35 years since its creation. But most of all it celebrates the great gleaming entertainment machine that used to be the American musical, and in so doing gets it up and running and funny again. That makes it something for us, the public, to celebrate as well. Also something for us, the local public, to be proud of, as the performance by this Canadian cast is top-notch. They may not want our help in rebuilding Iraq, but theyd be dumb not to let us help them rebuild Broadway.

Let me, as if we were two characters in an exposition, inform you of what you undoubtedly know: Mel Brooks conceived and wrote The Producers, and it concerns two impresarios -- the veteran Max Bialystock and the neophyte Leo Bloom -- who raise millions more than necessary to produce a musical called Springtime for Hitler in the expectation that it will close on opening night and they can disappear with the balance. (The show wont run; they will.)

Sean Cullen plays the overbearing Max ("the man who invented Theatre in the Square -- nobody had a good seat"), a role for which neither his voice nor his personality are large enough. He rolls his eyes very expressively, though, and a spot of ad-libbing late in the evening gave hope that he will loosen up as the run proceeds. Michael Therriault is magical as Leo, the inhibited accountant who first attracts Max's attention by spotting a lacuna of $2,000 in a Broadway show capitalized at $100,000. (Oh yes, the time is 1959.) Therriault has done musicals at Stratford, but we know him best as a classical actor. He establishes the character immediately, as an adorable tussle-headed bundle of neuroses. But it's in song and dance that this Leo blooms -- first venting his fantasies, with soft shoe and ringing tenor, in I Wanna Be a Producer, just one guy and a chorus line; then succumbing to the attentions of a pneumatic, Amazonian Swedish showgirl in a number euphemistically titled That Face, which involves a lot of bobbing up and down behind sofas. This is a twist the movie never gave us: Leopold Bloom Gets Laid.

Apart from taking to the medium like one born to it, Therriault appears to be creating his own character while his colleagues look as if theyre faithfully following tracks that were laid down three years ago in New York. Many of them, though, do it with a skill and panache that might just as well be original: Paul OSullivan as Franz Leibkind, chicken-raising Nazi author of the worst play ever written; Juan Chioran imperiously swish as director Roger De Bris, also the certified worst, whose personal and professional credo is Keep It Gay; Sarah Cornell, six foot four of Scandinavian delight (That Face, That Body, That Accent); and the burly Steve Ross, who appears every five minutes in a different costume. And the ensemble singing and dancing are marvellous.

Susan Stroman directed and choreographed the show; Robin Wagner designed the sets, and William Ivey Long the costumes. They are Broadway's current A-team, the people who get called for everything. Stroman's direction (her first) keeps the show moving and also knows when, briefly, to let it breathe. She also delivers her best comic choreography, whose wonderful mad touches include a chorus of chickens shaking a wing to the music or, in one extreme case, extending it into a Nazi salute. She gets nuns dancing the hora, which must be a first, and repeats her trademark device of bringing on the girls from unlikely places -- in Crazy for You they poured out of a car; here they emerge from a row of filing cabinets. She even finds an equivalent for the climactic overhead shot in the movie that showed us the Springtime for Hitler chorus line in swastika formation; I wont give away details except to say that it involves Wagner (the designer, not the composer) paying homage to his own famous tilted-mirror set for an earlier Chorus Line.

Brooks book, in collaboration with Thomas Meehan, follows his screenplay very closely (all the funniest jokes derive verbatim from the film), except in the closing sections, which are the weakest. He also wrote the score, which of course incorporates the two songs that were in the picture. Springtime for Hitler itself remains the best; apart from the insouciant outrageousness of the words (winter for Poland and France) the tune has an insane Hollywood-Viennese lilt that's absolutely right. Brooks is not an outstandingly original composer (Prisoners of Love, the other movie holdover, slithers into Bill Bailey Won't You Please Come Home within seconds, and I would like to set a tune detective on most of the newer numbers), but there is an unassumingness about his pastiches that gets them by; this score, unlike most that try to recreate the Golden Age, is not soulless. It benefits greatly from Doug Bestermans orchestrations, which sound as if he had a ball. And the 1940s vocal harmonies in Springtime are killers. My favourite new song, partly because of OSullivan's dynamite performance, is Fritz's Haben Sie Gehort Das Deutsche Band?, which is in a mode that might be called Swingkraut and sounds like something a very young George and Ira Gershwin might have knocked off while no one was listening. Brooks music is, surprisingly, more interesting than his lyrics, which race along all right but are good-humoured rather than actually funny. In a song like Where Did We Go Right? (the producers are ruined because Springtimes a hit) the punchline is given away in the title, though there is one couplet folded in there that made me laugh: "Oh, we knew we couldn't lose/Half the audience were Jews."

The show The Producers is far gentler than the film. This is partly because the movie Max was Zero Mostel, who could be threatening even when benevolent -- especially when benevolent. It's partly because the show is a full-blown musical; Max and Leo have numbers and are thus endearing. But mainly it's because the movie itself exhausted all the shock value there was in the material. The show is nostalgia for the film. Camp is a loaded word in connection with Nazis, but having had its harmless fun with Hitler, the show is permitted to divide women into young nymphomaniacs and old nymphomaniacs, and -- while being itself the most heterosexual of musicals -- to bring the rest of the genre out of the closet in Keep It Gay. (This, by the way, was once the presumably innocent title of a song by Rodgers and Hammerstein.) None of this is especially incendiary, but if there had been no movie and The Producers had appeared in 2001 as a totally new show, I doubt that they would have been allowed to get away with it. But they have, as Max and Leo did, and we can feel happy for it. "If you've got it," sings Ulla the sexpot Swede, "flaunt it." They have, and they do, and we love it.
