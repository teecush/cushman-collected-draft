PHEDRE

Du Maurier Theatre Centre, Toronto

- - -

Inexorable, says Theseus, king of Athens, in Ted Hughes translation of Racine's Phedre, "that is the word for the gods." It's also the word for the play in which he is -- I use the word advisedly --caught: the world of Racine is one of omnipresent trap. Its characters, drawn from Greek tragedy, sometimes see themselves as the pawns of destiny, but destiny only means forces within themselves. When they take action, whether its to escape these forces or to help them along, the results are invariably appalling. Just like life, really. When he makes his pronouncement, Theseus thinks things are as bad as they can possibly get. He is wrong.

Racine, writing in supposedly rational 17th-century France, never mocks his myths. Almost alone among the modern playwrights who have plundered the Greeks, he seems to believe passionately in the stories he is telling. He alters them, of course. Phedre, which depicts the consuming desire of Theseus wife for her husband's virginal stepson, is based on Euripides' Hippolytus, and the difference in titles tells us much about the plays. Euripides was more interested in the young man, and Racine, celebrated for writing "she-tragedies," in the woman. Then, Racines Hippolytus is not Euripides untouchable ascetic; he is in love, with the captive princess Aricia. This looks suspiciously as if Racine were throwing in the neo-classic equivalent of a Hollywood romantic interest, and this may indeed have been the case, but he turns it to characteristic account.

Phedre only learns of Hippolytus' love half-way through the play. Until now, passion and shame have been combusting inside her. Now jealousy is added to the mix, pushing her into the abyss on whose edge she has been trembling from her first appearance. She becomes, for the first time, consciously malevolent. And since she is a high- principled woman, with a strong sense of family honour (daddy is a god, with an important position in hell) the guilt helps destroy her.

Racine offers no naturalistic crutches to either his actors or his audience; his concentration makes Shakespeare look slap-happy. Soulpepper's Phedre is brave and ambitious. Im not sure how successful it is because, frankly, I dont know what I want from Racine, certainly not from Racine in English. Even in France, the play's exalted reputation weighs it down; outside it, we have to add to the legend of the stupendously moving Phedres of the past (all of them French) the general consensus that Racine's fanatically economical poetry is untranslatable. We approach the play asking for the moon, knowing we wont get it, and wondering how much to settle for instead.

There is something very lunar about Daniel Brooks production, which Dany Lyne has set in a wasted chamber backed by a series of sternly receding arches, each framing a door harder than its predecessor to squeeze through. Andrea Lundy's lighting punctuates the action with uncommon boldness, switching abruptly from one state to the next, and sometimes settling with cruel insistence on the face of a single actor. Phedre and Theseus, in particular, look under the white beams as if their souls have been stripped.

There is a matching soundscape (by Richard Feren), which hums behind much of the dialogue, quietly but distractingly; between scenes, and far more helpfully, it blows up a storm. This may be meant to suggest the approach of the sea-monster who plays a crucial role in the play's catastrophe; it also sounds like the roar of an express-train, hurtling toward disaster, which is by no means a bad metaphor for the action itself.

The staging pushes the play to a higher pitch than does Hughes translation, which is gratifyingly speakable but is not on the same level as his Agamemnon, currently at Stratford. His blood-red poetic talent may not have been in tune with Racines. Being English, he can't resist bringing the characters down to Earth; Tanja Jacobs as Phedre's meddlesome confidante (a role she may be too young for) is allowed more or less to tell her mistress that being unlucky in love runs in her family and that she really should pull herself together. Racine certainly wasnt humourless but his jokes, usually sadistic ones, are in his texture rather than his text. Hughes script is in verse, but it doesn't rhyme; the couplets, the strict form that strains the passions to breaking-point, are what make Racine terrible, just as they're what make Moliere funny.

The production seems split between Racines demands and Hughes concessions, and Brooks' grip on the acting is far less rigorous than it is on the effects. It's a famous moment in theatrical history when Phedre asks for a chair; it shows how far gone she is, since people in French tragedy seldom do anything so undignified as to sit down. Here they sit all the time, admittedly on very unwelcoming high-backed seats that seem to have come from the demanding workshop of Charles Rennie Mackintosh. (At a moment of high passion, one of the chairs gets knocked over, and its thrilling; then another one is, apparently for the sake of symmetry, and its an anti-climax.)

Once seated, the actors are tempted further into chattiness, with attendant gestures, and each gesticulation lowers the temperature; I wanted to tie their hands to their sides, especially in the opening expository scene where we're more aware of the characters trying to explain what they mean than of what it is they're explaining.

As Phedre, Nancy Palk makes a thrilling first entrance, swathed in veils, keeping her back to us and hugging the walls, the very picture of libido at the end of its tether. One wondered if she could keep it up; in fact she does, but with not enough variation. The power remains but its indiscriminately channeled, with crucial changes in the characters mental state going unregistered. She is the most sensual of Anglophone Phedres (of those I've seen, Diana Rigg was nun-like and Glenda Jackson downright spinsterish) and she has the technical and mental resources for the role; but not, perhaps, the temperament. She is a generous actress trying to feed an insatiably ravenous role; and though that may sound an ideal arrangement, it doesn't quite balance.

Phedre is not, despite the legend, a one-woman show; and one great virtue of this production is that it emerges as the tragedy of a whole family, and indeed of a kingdom. (Three people die; the rest are devastated.)

The play has been cast up to the hilt, with actresses of the standing of Kate Hennig and Patricia Fagan drafted to deliver brief bulletins of bad news, though their chief contribution is to stand around paying mutely eloquent tributes to doom. Jonathan Watton has the looks and the air -- not insensitive but callow -- for Hippolytus, though his verse-speaking is in urgent need of attention. Diego Matamoros, though not quite one's idea of a man with a thousand ravishments on his resume, is a fierce, uncompromising Theseus; uncompromising because I, at least, had never realized before just how mean and vindictive this character is. William Webster makes amends for his earlier fidgets with a finely focussed account of the great messenger speech, and Yanna McIntosh -- whom Soulpepper should start pelting with major roles -- is a vibrant Aricia; the most sensible character in the play but as incapable as the others of stopping tragedy in its tracks. To make us feel that may be achievement enough.
