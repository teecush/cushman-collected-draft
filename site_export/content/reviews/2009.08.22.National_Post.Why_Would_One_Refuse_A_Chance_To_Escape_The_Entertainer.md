A solid production despite dying art form The Shaw Festival now has a fourth stage, bringing it level with Stratford. Jackie Maxwell, director of both the festival and the inaugural studio production, says in the program that the new space, in back of the main house, is to be devoted to post-Shavian work that's part of a continuum of provocative, subversive playwrighting by "writers [who] continue to question the status quo." That would include all serious, and many non-serious, plays, since, without a status quo to challenge, dramatists would have nothing to be dramatic about.

Maxwell appends a shopping list of contemporary candidates that oddly omits Tom Stoppard, whose devotion to wit and ideas makes him the most obviously Shavian of contemporary playwrights, and whose The Invention of Love, which has been begging for production for 10 years now, already sits bang in the middle of the Shaws previous mandate. The studio has started with The Entertainer by John Osborne who, as playfully admitted in the program, was a vociferous anti-Shavian, denouncing the sages plays as fraudulent, inept ...posturing wind and rubbish. Imagine an Osborne Festival at which his plays were endlessly recycled. It would be hell.

In a better world, Osborne's career would have ended with Inadmissible Evidence in the mid 1960s. In a perfect one, it would probably have stopped a decade earlier with Look Back in Anger, the bombshell debut that thrust him into a position as generational spokesman that he'd never asked for and that he spent the rest of his life trying either to live up to or to live down. The Entertainer was his follow-up play and it shows him imposing a schematic pattern on an English music-hall family at the time of Suez. Music hall, as demonstrated this year at the Shaw, took a long time dying; its already in the throes in Cowards Red Peppers, written 20 years earlier. Archie Rice, protagonist and archetype, is a cankered and self-hating philanderer and comic, fronting a seaside nudie show. His father, once a top-liner, hankers for Edwardian glories when Britannia ruled. Archie's son and daughter represent Youth, disaffected by definition. Another son, offstage, is killed in the war.

The play alternates between Archie's on-stage routines and his fractious home life. You expect the usual Osborne mix: compelling solos, tedious ensembles. In Maxwells production, a good one, these preconceptions are up-ended. The scenes in the Rice family digs are at least as charged as those in the theatre; more important, the two worlds progressively merge. Peter Hartwells set design has much to do with this. The audience sits in two opposing banks looking down on the actors. There are two music hall stages, one at each end of the room, and the domestic scenes happen in the middle. We never quite know where we are; there's a nightmare element that's very helpful. The space doesnt permit an orchestra pit, so Archies accompaniment comes from an on-stage pianist. This makes his act seem more cabaret than music hall, and indeed Benedict Campbell's Archie, square, brusque and insulting, has a strong whiff of German nightclub. This is disconcerting at first, but it becomes its own creation. His life becomes a comment on his act rather than, as you might expect, the other way around.

Besides, Osborne's music hall pastiche isnt very good. The last-ditch attitudes (racist, sexist, you name it) may be authentic enough, but theyd never have been as in your face and devoid of lilt as they are here. The songs ( Thank God I'm Normal) are especially atrocious. Nor do we ever find out why Archie became a comedian; he has no love for it, and no need: He had an expensive education, one that permitted his brother (shades of Death of a Salesman) to become a successful lawyer. What did his dad make of it? There's no trace of the halls in David Schurmanns pained gentlemanly performance, but he, too, comes to fiery life when the family starts looking in all possible directions in anger. Real demons are let loose, at racking but very believable length. As Phoebe, Archies sozzled, nervous and incorrigibly loving wife, Corinne Koslo is sublime; when she sings The Boy in the Gallery it's like the stoned Mary Tyrone in Long Day's Journey recalling when she was happy for a time. Krista Colosimo and Ken James Stewart are respectively clenched and quirky as the juniors, faltering only when they force emotion: an endemic disease among young actors that Maxwell, like many directors, seems disinclined to stamp out. In minor roles, Graeme Somerville and especially Ric Reid strike virtuous blows for the ensemble spirit.

Ive never believed in Archies channelling of a black gospel singer when he hears of his son's death. It's too set up. I dont even know if were meant to believe it. Hounded by the taxman, Archie refuses the chance of escape to Canada because "you can't get draught Bass in Toronto." Some things change.
