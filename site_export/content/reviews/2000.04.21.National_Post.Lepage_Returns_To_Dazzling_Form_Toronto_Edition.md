Far Side of the Moon runs till Sunday, but is fully sold out.

THE FAR SIDE OF THE MOON

Premiere Dance Theatre, Toronto

I had forgotten how immensely charming Robert Lepage can be. The Far Side of the Moon, his new show, is a solo performance as dazzling as his previous Needles and Opium and even more enjoyable. It is also longer, at least approaching the scope of such ensemble pieces as Tectonic Plates.

After his more recent productions -- the clever but pointless one- man Hamlet and the dismally ragged Geometry of Necessity (the Frank Lloyd Wright one) -- we were praying for Lepage's return to form. Our prayers have been answered, and then some.

The marvel of Lepage at his best is he can mount the highest of high-tech productions and still place humanity lovingly and touchingly at its centre. He can achieve this with a large company or, more often these days, with just himself.

As it happens, the relationship between humanity and technology is much of what this show is about. Lepage plays, principally, a former philosophy teacher (cultural philosophy, he says his game is) who finds speaking in public almost impossibly difficult. He has in fact done the opposite of talking himself out of a job. He is obsessed with man's conquest of outer space, and especially with his journeyings to the moon.

He draws a stern contrast between astronauts (in it for the glory, and usually American) and cosmonauts (in it for the wonder, and usually Russian). Most space travel, he says, is undertaken for reasons of narcissism. His ideal observation post would be mounted on the far side of the moon, the side from which the Earth is invisible.

Our guy, who lives in Quebec City and whose name is Philippe, spends his weeks in study and his weekends working as a tele- marketer, in which capacity he at one point finds himself poignantly addressing a long-lost, now-married girlfriend. (The play is full of telephone conversations, of which we hear only one side, and not the least of its accomplishments is that nearly all of them are thoroughly plausible. We never feel one speaker is repeating the other, just for our benefit.) He was diagnosed in boyhood with an eye defect that still necessitates regular brain scans. He is also coping with the recent death of his mother, and with the care of her pet goldfish. He wanders around his tiny apartment, and also around the surrounding countryside, discoursing on the state of both locales and of the world in general for the benefit, it seems, of an audience of unseen extra-terrestrials. In these musing, misanthropic monologues -- hilariously written and perfectly timed -- he comes over as part Woody Allen, part Glenn Gould.

In the end, Philippe, who has never even flown before, gets to go to Moscow to give a talk. It misfires, for reasons it would be cruel to reveal. The event prompts Philippe's younger brother to describe him, also on the phone, as "a loser, but a nice loser."

The brother is a winner, and seems for much of the time to be not nice; certainly Philippe resents him, though both he and we may learn better. He is a TV weatherman, who seems to regard the North American climate as a conspiracy against French Canada. ("Yes, they're sending us their bad weather again.")

Rich and successful, also gay, he advises Philippe to consult a "wallet-psychic" who apparently can tell from the state of your purse whether it's ever likely to attract any money. (He drops this idea after opening Philippe's closet and declaring "if I were money, I'd never come in here.") He does, though, display a more vulnerable side when trapped in an apartment-building elevator -- and this, we are presumably meant to reflect, in an age when men can reach the moon.

Lepage also appears as a Russian spaceman and part-time painter; as his homier American counterpart televised live from his capsule to answer awkward questions from kids; and -- magically but mysteriously -- as a green-clad woman who reaches into a washing machine and fishes out a space-child.

Washing machines bulk large in this production; one of them apparently figured in a traumatic incident in the youth of the two brothers. Early on, we find ourselves taken from outside the wash to inside, an optical effect I commend to future stagers of Alice Through the Looking-Glass.

The laundromat is the basic location of a set (Marie-Claude Pelletier is listed as assistant designer, but it's not clear whom she's assisting) that displaces that for The Overcoat as the most innovative and effective in recent Canadian memory. Basically a long grey sliding wall, it reveals, among other things, Philippe's apartment, a large and lonely hotel bar (spectacular), the inside of a plane and an accusingly bare lecture hall. Within it are a few simple props (the different things Lepage as director can do with an ironing board are enough in themselves to guarantee this show a place in theatrical history).

Around everything there is music, unobtrusively spooky, by Laurie Anderson, and much film footage of the history of space travel. I'm not sure that this counterpoints the main action as meaningfully as Lepage intended, but then I had doubts about the equation of lunar conquest and declining human morality in Tom Stoppard's somewhat similar play Jumpers (which also had a vulnerable goldfish in its cast, along with a more celebrated hare and tortoise). It was still the best comedy of the last 50 years. This show has the wittiest script in ages (by Lepage, with Adam Nashman), perfect performance (in, for good measure, the performer's second language), and sly and superlative staging. It's an incomparably joyous occasion.
