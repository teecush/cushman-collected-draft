There is a powerful, chilling scene at the beginning of Wild Dogs. Six of the shows seven characters stand at the edge of a wood, each calling out the name of a favourite hound who's disappeared into it. Their individual tones vary from wistful to hopeful to hopeless to desperate. The gradations, in Kelly Thornton's production, are haunting and, in at least one case, frightening. The effect is truly theatrical, something you couldnt get from a book. But its the only sequence in the evening of which that is true.

Wild Dogs is in fact based on a book--a novel by Helen Humphreys -- that I haven't read, but have read about -- enough, anyway -- to have learned that it's a story narrated in turn by each of the people in it. In other words, its a sequence of monologues. In this stage version from Nightwood Theatre, the monologues are retained, but are chopped up and intercut; Anne Hardcastle is credited not with having adapted the original, but with having "arranged it.

Plays based on novels dont have to be lifeless, though there's a better-than-average chance that they will be. Neither do plays composed of monologues; one of the most compelling plays I ever saw, Kennedy's Children (a 70s view of the 60s) was made up entirely of half a dozen people in a barroom talking to themselves. Once you realized that was all you were going to get, you adjusted and let yourself be lured in by language that was immediately witty and eventually moving, and that had innate stage presence. None of these things are true of the speeches in Wild Dogs, which has adopted a convention that seems increasingly arbitrary. The people in Kennedy's Children never broke out of their respective cocoons; that was the point. Those in Wild Dogs are figures in a shared story. On occasions, presumably when there's no help for it, they even interact. Only they havent been given the theatrical tools to do it.

They are all members of a depressed community; depressed economically and, for the most part, psychologically as well. The dogs have escaped, or been let go, largely because their owners can no longer feed them. The central character, or at least the narrator-in-chief, is Alice, who recently broke up with her boyfriend and who strikes up a new relationship with Rachel, a conservationist academic on a field-trip. This affair, like much of the rest of the play, falls into the category of things that are presumed to have happened but, in dramatic terms, never do. Tamara Podemski, playing Alice, soliloquizes well enough but seems still to be doing it when supposedly talking to the person she loves; Raven Dauda as Rachel responds in kind and, when left to herself, is something of a grouch.

Taylor Trowbridge gives a wrenching if inevitably unvaried account of a brain-damaged young woman who actively identifies with the wild things. The mens roles are more recognizably grounded, with Les Carlson affecting as a neglected ancient, Steve Cumyn touchingly creepy as a frustrated artist with an advanced degree in voyeurism and Stephen Joffe arresting, alternately pitiful and aggressive, as a boy peer-pressured into petty crime. His are the shouts that ring out most agonizingly in that initial sequence, and his later encounters with Alice, who sees in him an all-too-familiar reflection of her own youth, bring the proceedings briefly to life. But the attempt to render all the characters through their relations to their lost dogs simply misfires. It merely leaves the impression that all the exciting stuff is happening offstage, among a collection of mutts we naturally never meet. It's no accident that the most complete performance comes from Tony Nappo as the one figure outside the charmed circle, a hunter who is nobody's dog lover. He is given feelings to portray (from anger to guilt, but thats enough) and actions through which to convey them. And when he talks to us, he does seem to be telling a personal story rather than reciting a prose poem.

The production keeps all the performances to a rigorous line; there is no emotional or oratorical overflow, though the temptations that way must have been considerable. But this is another play enslaved to its own metaphor: literary theatre" in a sense beyond merely being taken from a book. Its the kind of thing that often gets rave reviews and even awards, so don't let me put you off when I say that I think it's deadly. - Wild Dogs continues until Nov. 8 at the Berkeley Street Theatre.
