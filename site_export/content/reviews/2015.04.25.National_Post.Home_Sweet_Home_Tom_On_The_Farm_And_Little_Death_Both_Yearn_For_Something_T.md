The least interesting character in Tom on the Farm is Tom himself. That is no reflection on Jeff Lillico's hyper-active and hyperintelligent performance. But he's playing someone who is both protagonist and reflective surface, and the two functions get in one another's way.

In Michel-Marc Bouchard's play, receiving a delayed English-language premiere in Linda Gabouriau's translation, Tom is a young advertising man whose male colleague and lover has died in an accident. The dead man grew up on a farm, miles from anywhere, and Tom makes the trek there for the funeral. Here he meets his partner's mother, Agathe, who didn't know her son was gay, and his brother Francis, who knew only too well. On Tom's first night at the farm, Francis pins him down on his bed (they share a room - like brothers) and threatens him with dire consequences should he even hint at the truth; he even insists that Tom invent a girlfriend for his boyfriend. Tom agrees, partly because he's terrified, but maybe also in part because, whatever the emotional cost to himself, he does want to spare Agathe's feelings.

The maybes pile up. Tom keeps up a continuous interior monologue, letting us know about the pain he's in and the lies he's forced to tell. At first, when it's in counterpoint to what the others are saying out loud, this is both touching and amusing. When it extends into long soliloquies, it defeats itself. However much Tom talks, he leaves questions unanswered, including the obvious one of why he sticks around, given that he has a job to go back to, and that Francis treats him appallingly; he ends up very bloodied.

One possible answer is that he finds life on the farm unexpectedly fulfilling, especially when it involves the delivery of a calf; he bonds with it, perhaps identifies with it, or finds the newborn to be a substitute for the life that was lost. Another is that he develops a sado-masochistic attraction to Francis, one that may very well be reciprocated. "Anything but normal round here is multiplied by twenty" says Francis, and he could be guiltily referring to himself as well as to his late brother. This could explain, on two levels, why he ratchets up the violence. This includes suspending Tom, upside down, over a giant cow-pat; Tom gamely continues to soliloquize.

The melodrama in the play involves several physical and psychological improbabilities, but it's brought to full atmospheric life in Eda Holmes' unimpeachable production. The slatted walls of Camellia Koo's set carry appropriate suggestions of prison bars; Rebecca Picherack's lighting is eerie and John Gzowski's sound design scary. There's humour too; about the least plausible thing Tom does is to summon a co-worker to impersonate the imaginary and supposedly francophone girlfriend, but it justifies itself in Christine Horne's bold comic turn, forced into speaking a language she barely knows.

The mother and brother, characters who, unlike Tom, develop before our eyes, are superbly played. Jeff Irving finds all the torment, of himself and others, in Francis, notably in his filial conflict between resentment and affection. As Agathe, Rosemary Dunsmore says the least (though she does have a funny aria about pasta salad) but conveys the most. She is religiously obsessed but also very practical. The play's most powerful irony is our gradual realization that she could have dealt with her son's sexuality had anyone told her.

The play rises to a violent climax that we wish for in advance but find hard to credit when it happens. But its brief aftermath, filled with a terrible tenderness, makes some sense of it. Two characters are left with what, it seems, they wanted.

Daniel Karasik's Little Death explicitly invokes the old meaning of its title phrase as a synonym for orgasm. (It casts a new and probably accurate light on a song in "A Little Night Music.") It also refers to an imminent bigger death; its hero Alex (the characters have names in the program, though they're never spoken in the play) having received the diagnosis, asks permission of the wife with whom he has been monogamous since they were both 16 to experiment while there's still time; she agrees but begins to wish she hadn't.

The play alternates his encounters with other women, or their beginnings in a hotel bar, with his returns to his increasingly angered and anguished spouse. This play, too, gets a beautifully tuned production, directed by Zachary Florence.

The text is in short-lined verse that plays as heightened prose, taut and full of echoes. Sometimes it's too brittle, as in the fencing of Alex (an ambiguously ingenuous Christopher Stanton) with a magazine writer (Shauna Black) and an actress (Elizabeth Tanner, a promising newcomer in an A-list cast). Sometimes it's conversationally perfect, as in a consultation with a doctor (Sarah Dodd, perfectly grounded in a brief role.) And sometimes it strikes ruthlessly and/or compassionately home, in the scenes of actual intimacy, physical or otherwise.

Kate Hennig is wonderfully warm and sensible as the maturest of Alex's pick-ups, though it seems she did the picking. Nicole Underhay is even better as the wife, nailing every loving or resentful word, thought or feeling with extraordinary quicksilver precision. The scenes seem to be chronologically ordered, though the last one cheats by diverting into fantasy; it's affecting, though, and the feelings behind it truthful.

Tom at the Farm runs through May 10 at Buddies in Bad Times Theatre; Little Death runs through May 3 at the Theatre Centre.
