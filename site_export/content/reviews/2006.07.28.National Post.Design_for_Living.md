DESIGN FOR LIVING

Royal George Theatre, Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ont.

- - -

Gilda, Otto and Leo have Central European names, though they themselves seem to be English. It's hard to be sure because they spend their time hopping in and out of countries, as well as in and out of one another's lives and beds. They are the trio of Destiny's Tots whose gay goings-on, and sad goings-off, are the subject of Noel Coward's Design for Living. Coward wrote the play in the 1930s as a vehicle for himself and his close friends -- and America's favourite married acting team -- the Lunts. It bears the marks of its origin in the demands its characters make on our attention, time and tolerance. The implication is that we have to care, because they're stars. However, one of the best things about Morris Panych's fine production at the Shaw Festival is that it doesn't indulge them. Another, stemming paradoxically from the first, is that it wins our sympathy for them.

At the beginning of Act One, Gilda, an interior decorator, is living with Otto, an artist, in his studio in Paris , but has just spent the night with Leo, a playwright. Otto is very upset when he finds out, and storms off, though it's an open question as to which of the two he is more jealous of. Probably he doesn't know himself; the three seem to have been together, in one formation or another, for years. Act Two finds Gilda shacked up in London with Leo, now the much-courted author of a hit play. Otto arrives and history repeats -- or, if you prefer, reverses -- itself. Gilda, appalled at the havoc the three of them are wreaking, walks out on the arm of Ernest, art-dealer and long-time friend and admirer, leaving the two boys (that really does seem to be the right word) to be furiously angry at one another, and then to reach a mutual accommodation by way of getting elaborately and hilariously drunk. Act Three shows us Gilda in New York, a great professional success in her own right. She's also married to Ernest, an arrangement that Otto and Leo flatly refuse to take seriously. They descend like a pair of not-so- heavenly twins (they spend much of the act in matching pyjamas), stake a joint claim to Gilda, and waltz off with her. Or, more accurately, Ernest waltzes off without her, even though it's his apartment.

In a way the play is a reprise-with-variations of Coward's Private Lives, although instead of two people who can't live with or without one another, we have three. This dilutes the humour (the drunk duet is the only really funny scene in the play) but expands the pain. There are other gains and losses. Both sets of characters share their author's preference for living anywhere but England, but while we never find out how Private Lives' Elyot and Amanda finance their cosmopolitan lifestyles, the later trio are at least shown to have jobs. We don't, however, learn much else about them; we have to take a lot on trust, including the vital assumption that they are made for one another. This may well be a legacy of the original casting. The roles are empty vessels for the stars to fill up, which in turn may account for the play's uncommon length. Each of them had to be given sufficient room to shine. Or rather to talk; because while Private Lives shows us its squabbling protagonists in action, some of it quite violent; their descendants in Design for Living seem to have one of those relationships that consists mostly of discussing the relationship. They don't, however, talk about how it affects them as individuals so much as how their bisexual menages a trois affects them in relation to the rest of the world. They end up with the declaration that they've found something that works for them, if not for anyone else. Their isolation makes them seem defensive, and also strangely innocent.

That, at least, is how it comes out here; and it's much preferable to presenting the threesome as cast-iron sophisticates, forever scoring points over the unworldly and unworthy squares. Ken MacDonald gives us a succession of skewed and skeletal sets, outsize windows through which we glimpse the Eiffel Tower, Big Ben and the Empire State Building, each one bent at an angle suggesting that if it had its druthers it would be in Pisa. But when it comes to the acting, Panych's production forswears brittleness, and with it any attempt to persuade us that the characters are wittier or more glamorous than they actually are. This does have its drawbacks in some of the stock comedy scenes (a sequence involving a Cockney maid's doomed attempts to answer the phone falls noticeably flat) but it works wonders for the emotions. Nicole Underhay is Gilda ungilded: Not one of her lines comes out as a wisecrack. She is instead a handsome, generous woman who for much of the play is scared of whom she may be hurting. By avoiding obvious star tactics, she emerges as a star.

Her two partners are more mischievous, David Jansen's Leo the more insistently (sometimes over-insistently) so, Graeme Somerville's Otto graver and more easily injured, the play's nearest approach to a thoughtful commentator. A key factor in keeping the leads human is the presence of a worthy antagonist, in the shape of Lorne Kennedy's Ernest, a wonderfully unselfish performance. He isn't a buffoon; he isn't a prig; if he were either we'd suspect the play of loading the dice, and take against it. He's sensible and he's dignified; he's also humourless and, as the boys fatally detect, sexless. So he has to go, which he does after finally losing his cool and unleashing a moralistic tirade -- though I think the production loses its own cool, not to say its magnanimity, in having him make his exit underneath a funny hat.

This being a 1930s boulevard comedy, the homosexuality is kept under wraps, at least at dialogue level; but they're thin wraps, easy for a modern production (and audience) to pierce; this one does so with enthusiasm, with the drunk scene spiralling into erotic knockabout. That's's how it ends; it starts as a display of professional as well as personal jealousy. The subject of success, its penalties and rewards, is much on the play's mind -- autobiographically so when Leo-the-lionized gets to reading and commenting on his first-night reviews and when he meets the press (Harry Judge as a young reporter who conducts an interview as if it were a police interrogation). That raises the question: How well does this secondary theme fit in with the main one? And it prompts the answer: Hardly at all. Coward had various things on his mind and, in airing them, he wrote a frankly self-indulgent play about frankly self-indulgent people. But he knew what he was doing, and did it with wit, and he made a vital moral point: Those who indulge themselves by indulging themselves do less harm than those who indulge themselves by suppressing others. Its time may have come again.
