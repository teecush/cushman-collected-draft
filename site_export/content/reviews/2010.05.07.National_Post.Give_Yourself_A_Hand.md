FAITH HEALER

Young Centre for the Performing Arts

---

The only faith healer I ever met did his laying-on of hands in the privacy of his own living room and, as far as I could make out, never charged for the service. Frank, the title character of Brian Friel's Faith Healer, fronts a different kind of operation. He goes out on tour, puts up posters, seeks a paying audience. This means that he's in show business, pretty much in its armpit. He even has a manager, Teddy, whose previous clients seem mainly to have been animal acts. Travelling with them is Grace, whom Frank introduces as his mistress though both she and Teddy insist that she's his wife.

There are other discrepancies in their stories, which come to us as memories. The play is a sequence of soliloquies; Teddy and Grace get one each, Frank gets two, one at each end of the play. Despite this exposure, he remains the most mysterious of the three. We gather that he's both exhilarated and tormented by his gift, which only works in a minority of cases anyway. Probably because of this, he's self-destructive. He's also destructive of other people, especially of Grace, a judge's daughter and herself a former solicitor, who left home and career to be with him. She also, after a couple of miscarriages, bore him a stillborn child, in a van stuck in the mud in a very remote Scottish village, where the baby was subsequently buried. These, too, are details that Teddy and Grace mostly agree on, but on which Frank differs.

Their speeches are detailed, atmospheric, well-written, and they read like short stories. And they do for the most part read better than they play. Something is missing in all Friel's plays, a refusal to bring things to a boil. Given the play's structure, it may seem naive to complain about a lack of explicit conflict, but the denial reaches further, into a deadening of the audience's curiosity. The Rashomon element doesn't really count for much; it's two against one, and we believe Teddy and Grace, who may often lie to themselves about feelings but not to us about facts.

Frank doesn't like to work in England because -- he says -- it's where Teddy and Grace come from. He plays really shabby dates in Wales and Scotland -- Celts are more credulous? -- but avoids Ireland, because it's his own homeland (and his author's). But, with the act on its uppers, they make the trip anyway. And there, at a pub in Ballybeg, confronted by a paraplegic whom no amount of faith is likely to heal, Frank comes face to face with his fate. It happens offstage of course. All three characters hint at what took place, but as for the final revelation, we're kept hanging: too long and by too slim a thread.

Friel has claimed that Frank's doubt-wracked relationship to his own talent is a metaphor for the fears and misgivings that he himself feels as a writer. That, though, can't really be of much concern to anyone except him. It's easier to see Frank as a figure for all performers; easier to understand Grace's and Teddy's devotion on that basis too: he's their rock star. The image struck especially close on the first night of the Soulpepper production when Stuart Hughes, playing Frank, very noticeably dried. This must have thrown him, but it allowed the actor's vulnerability to merge with, and illuminate, the character's. For all that, and for all the intelligence of his performance, he seems too chipper for the role. Frank is a riven heavyweight, a desperate, drunken charismatic. Hughes is a bantam, a scrapper, and always seemingly young.

Brenda Robins gives a strong bitter account of Grace, but the evening belongs to Diego Matamoros' Teddy. He starts, admittedly, with several advantages. Gina Wilkinson's production takes its cue from where the text is strongest, in its evocation of the unrelenting shabbiness of the trio's existence.

Ken MacDonald's set is a collage of all the tatty draughty village halls they ever played, with chairs stacked picturesquely high at one side of the stage and another, single, chair hanging in solitary glory on the back wall. Teddy, in his red smoking jacket, with bow tie to match, provides the only patch of colour in the show. He also provides the only humour, in his comparisons of Frank to other top-liners from Houdini to Olivier ( "not one of them has two brains to rub together") and his reminiscences of Rob Roy, The Piping Dog (great talent, no sex drive) and Miss Mulatto and Her Pigeons. Matamoros takes these breaks, and adds an idiosyncratically impeccable Cockney accent, to create a towering image of a little man: a wheedling small-timer who belies his own showbiz platitudes as fast as he dishes them out. He's that tragic oxymoron, a lonely, gregarious man. He counsels never getting attached to clients, though his love for Frank, and for Grace, is manifest even before he lets it pour out. The moral: the star is always a faith healer; to everyone -- Teddy, Grace, his "patients" -- Frank furnishes something, however tarnished, to believe in.

The only person he can't do it for is himself, at least until the end, and that we have to take on trust. It's a great idea. I don't think it's been dramatized.
