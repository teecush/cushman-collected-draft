I AM YOURS

Equity Showcase Theatre

- - -

Judith Thompson's I Am Yours, first staged in 1987, is concerned with emotional need and emotional greed. They could well be the same thing. It's set in a bleak urban landscape that's willed rather than observed or created.

There is Toilane, a young man working as superintendent in an apartment block, who conceives an instant passion for one of the residents, an artist and teacher named Deirdre: Dee for short. He's especially crazy about her feet.

Dee, as far as we can tell, lets Toilane into her bed, or on to her floor, once and once only, and then tells him to get out and stay out. Well, almost. What she actually says is, "I know you're the super, but other than those kinds of things I never want to see you again." This might be described as not burning your bridges before you come to them.

Dee does, however, get pregnant. She has recently split with her husband, Mack, a bookseller, but she now lets him back into her life rather than acknowledge Toilane as the father. (The time-frame here seems somewhat murky.) Dee draws back at the last minute from having an abortion, decides to have the child and then immediately gives it up for adoption. She also enlists the help of her sister, Mercy, who has gone through life acutely conscious of being the less pretty of the two; it doesn't help when Dee tells her that she's better off that way. Mercy has been married herself, but the great love of her life, constantly remembered, is an older man who drove her to school when she was a girl and touched her in the car.

Completing the cast is Toilane's mother, Pegs; she's a single parent, he's an only child. Pegs has cleaned floors for a living, and she talks eloquently about the hatred she feels for her employers, and about the ingratitude of children once they get past the stage when, as she says, "they think you're Christmas." That line has stayed with me over the 15 years since the first production; in fact, it wasn't until I heard those words in the Equity Showcase revival that I realized that I had seen the play before. Everything else -- characters, action -- had escaped from me. There is a sort of justice in this. What Thompson's people say is more touching and believable, in this play at least, than what they do.

This is accentuated in the current production. Christopher Morris, a smouldering young actor, here establishes himself as a sensitive young director. (It's his first time out.) He may even be too sensitive for the play, since its earlier, more intimate scenes work better here than the full-scale battles that break out later.

The audience sits above and around a cockpit stage; for solos and duets the performers hug the sides of the acting area, very effectively. In ensemble scenes, though, they seem oddly reluctant, both physically and psychologically, to take over the arena.

The play talks a lot about love, but what it actually feeds on is anger. Dee, presumably, has scars, but we're not told much about them. What we actually see is a spoiled and breathtakingly manipulative woman who also happens to be an expert in blackmail, of both the actual and emotional varieties. She battens on her sister's insecurities; she threatens Toilane with a rape charge; she throws her husband out, begs him back, then banishes him again, all within the space of three minutes.

I thought for a moment that we were watching a cunningly collapsed version of the events of several months; but no, it's meant to be happening in real time.

Toilane falls for Dee at the same feverish speed, and the production is too gentle to get us into this emotional climate. Probably no actress could make Dee sympathetic; she emerges as someone who deserves what she gets, and also deserves to lose what she loses. But if we can't like her, we might at least be scared of her, or of what she describes as her "inner animal." Tracey Ferencz (who came late into the show) is altogether too careful and thoughtful for the role; she may be aiming for torment but she ends up playing spite.

The balance is further upset by the characterization of Toilane. In theory, his neediness is as sinister as hers. He is obsessively territorial; he has been a gay-basher; he has also done time. But when the story gets going we forget all of this; we merely see a not very bright young man being taken advantage of; especially since Benjamin Clost's performance is alive and sympathetic, down to his fingertips. Diane Hollingsworth lacks the panache for his mom, either in her semi-comic tirades or in her metamorphosis into the role of full-throated avenger. This is the play's set-piece scene, and it requires her almost to hypnotize her antagonists into surrender. But here, as earlier with Dee, it's difficult to see why they should sit quietly and listen to her. There's an element of personal feud here, plus a touch of class war, but nothing that's developed.

The remaining performances are fine. Adrian Churchill's Mack, very solid, takes full advantage of two of the play' s best speeches: one about a plague of bees behind his mother's pantry wall, and another recalling the humiliation of high-school dances.

Caroline Gillis as Mercy captures the still-lost girl within the grown woman; she's the one character who not only looks for love in all the wrong places but also, surprisingly, finds it. (One wouldn't put bets on it lasting.)

William Webster, the one survivor from the cast of '87, brings great tact and presence to three characters, plus the voice and silhouette of a fourth: the sisters' late father, who apparently is German.

That, at any rate, is the language in which, from off-stage, and from the depths of memory, he intones the phrase that gives the play its title. Its relevance remains mysterious.

"I am yours" is doubtless the kind of ecstatically surrendering avowal that most of the characters yearn to make; some of them even do, generally with dire results. Dee, the woman at the centre, never does and probably never could. Maybe that's the point.
