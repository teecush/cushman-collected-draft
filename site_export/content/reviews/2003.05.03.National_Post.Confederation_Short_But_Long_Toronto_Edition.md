CONFEDERATION

Cameron House

- - -

Here we go again. Confederation, which is about what its title says it's about, is the fourth installment of Video Cabaret's comic- strip history of Canada. This project has always been noted for the boldness of its staging, the reductiveness of its text and an acting- style that has partaken of both qualities. Nothing much has changed.

Except in one respect. The History of the Village of the Small Huts, which is the mischievous overall title of the project, is now on its second complete go-round; and Confederation is, fittingly enough, a reconstitution of elements from the first cycle.

As I understand it (I wasn't around for the previous try), five plays, of which Confederation is the first, have been distilled from what used to be two, dealing with the fortunes of Canada during the later 19th century -- what Michael Hollingsworth, the author and director, calls, with surprising anglocentricity, "the Victorian age."

Though material has apparently been added, simple arithmetic suggests that each of the five new plays will be briefer than each of the two old ones, and Confederation in fact clocks in at an unprecedented 75 minutes without interval. It feels too short but I didn't want it to be longer, if you follow me.

The stage is crammed with people itching to inscribe their names on their country's destiny, or at the very least on its future highways and hotels.

Here is John A. Macdonald, with a bibulous nose and before his knighthood (the latter being a difficult concept to accommodate), scheming to turn Canada into a Dominion, and then to have dominion over it.

In his Ontario corner is George Brown, scribbler, and in his coin- de-Quebec George-Etienne Cartier, distinguished by a castellated hairstyle, heaped-up in the middle and with a protruding wisp on either side, that's extraordinary even by this company's standards of baroque wiggery-pokery. After stringing them along and having his way with them, Macdonald hangs them both out to dry, leaving each to salve his wounds as best he may. Cartier resorts to guttural splutters, Brown starts The Globe.

Meanwhile in Montreal, Wilfrid Laurier, very insistent on the proper pronunciation of his Christian name, is preening in opposition. And under his influence comes the student Louis Riel, fresh out of the seminary, where he has soaked up the free-thinking texts of Voltaire while enduring the hell-fire denunciations of a thunderously bigoted Bishop Laurier.

The bishop stands out among the play's characters by virtue of being a full-fledged monster rather than just a fool or a knave. The clinching touch of grotesquerie is added by having the role played by a woman; Aviva Armour-Ostroff, in a performance as fearsome as her name, wields a very mean crozier. There is a similar cross- gendered enthusiasm in Elizabeth Brown's George Coles, "a portly man": his own complacent description and a riotous understatement, since his padding could fit out a Falstaff and still leave a bit over.

The two ladies also play ladies, though to less effect than is usual in this series; there is the regulaton amount of philandering but it has little effect on the body politic. It seems to have been included in the play out of habit. The men, as is customary, correspond to everybody's private opinion of politicians: They're venal and incompetent, even when these qualities would seem to cancel each other out.

Patrick Conner's Macdonald, who gets what he wants, is as much the clown as Brown and Cartier, who don't. (The show takes it for granted that what he wants is a bad thing, though the case is unproven, and anyway Video Cabaret thinks that about everybody.)

The cast acts with great forcefulness and enthusiasm; as Laurier, Bruce Vavrina, a veteran of the style, adds a touch of delicacy. Dylan Roberts' Riel is almost sympathetic but then his character is still a young thing, and very oppressed. At the end he takes himself back to Manitoba to foment resistance.

It's a "watch this space" kind of moment; obviously in the next play he will become important, thus giving the production licence to Get Riel.

I usually begin my Small Hut reviews by paying tribute to the precision and ingenuity of the black-box staging. I'm beginning to take it for granted, but it's as fine as ever, seven actors crammed into a tiny space, with a huge number of lighting areas, and always managing to hit the right spot. (And the right spot, conversely, always managing to find them.)

The speed of the transitions continues to take the breath, as does the speed and the complexity of the costume changes; Armour- Ostroff, at the operation's sharpest end, plays seven roles, most of them recurring. Astrid Janson and Julie Kenton designed the costumes, which are grotesquely splendid, and Brad Harley the props, which are splendidly grotesque.

The pace and the trappings give the proceedings an aura of wit that the script may not actually support. Full-throated laughs are few, though there's one terrific exchange about safeguarding the rights of minorities, especially those of that notorious minority, the rich. VideoCabaret gives very good cartoon. But it does get monotonous.
