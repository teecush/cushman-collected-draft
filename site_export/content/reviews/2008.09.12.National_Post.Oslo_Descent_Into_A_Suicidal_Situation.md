If the title Norway. Today sounds ominously like an email address, there's a reason. The play comes from deep within the world of dot-commery. Its first scene is a conversation held online. Somewhere in Norway (today) a 20-year-old woman has entered a chat room, looking for someone to join her in a suicide pact. Her call is answered by a man of similar age in another country. What that country might be is never revealed, and probably doesn't matter, but one can't help wondering. The author, Igor Bauersima, comes from Switzerland, so maybe his hero does, too. I know the internet has broken down all kinds of barriers, but it still seems a handy coincidence that boy and girl are able to speak, or text, the same language. How's their German in Norway?

The boy's name is August, the girl is July, a. k. a. Julie, but don't let that put you off. Their contact switches, along with the play's action, from virtual to real; August flies to Norway -- with Julie, as we subsequently learn, paying the fare. She looks able to afford it; he certainly doesn't. We join them high on a mountainside overlooking a fjord, into which they propose to fling themselves. Only not just yet. Julie's brought a camera on which to record her last thoughts, August has brought a portable music player to assist whatever mood may arise, and they also have a tent, in which they propose to spend their last night. Guess what happens.

So it's Love and Death, or Love or Death, and it's cuteness and pain. (And the whole damn thing, to invoke an ancient movie title.) The pain comes through most clearly in Julie's stark opening statement in the chat room, the point at which this self-styled "existential comedy" comes closest to justifying the description. She wants to kill herself because life seems pointless. He wants to kill himself because his seems pointless, which is a bit different. He doesn't have a girlfriend. She isn't surprised.

Ieva Lucs' Julie is, at the beginning, impressively stark. Later on, I suspect, she suffers in translation. Speaking in English with a North American accent she sounds, inevitably, like a spoiled rich American girl, not so much implacable as bossy. She's better later, when she gets to soften up and even laugh at herself. August, by contrast, laughs at himself almost continually, as a defence mechanism. He's a great roller with the punches as, with his insecurities, he needs to be. This makes his the more rewarding role; and Steven McCarthy, a definite up-and-comer with electricity in his veins (he was great last year in Revisited), gives a bold performance of shyness: a neat feat.

We never believe he'll go through with it; we think she just might. Only she's set on doing it in her own time, and doesn't take kindly to having it accidentally anticipated. As they both perch precariously on the literal edge of doom, there are a fair number of laughs of the "I might have got killed!" variety. There are touching moments, too, including a sex scene that starts as fantasy because that's the most comfortable way in which these awkwardly-matched partners can get revved up. Besides, it's virtually (ha!) the way they met. Their love-making is seen live in silhouette, and also in projections, tasteful both ways; Sarah Baumann's production (for Theatre Smash) takes, and runs with, the play's idea of life as something that exists in order to be recorded on video. Or at least its characters' idea; audiences above a certain age may feel like voyeurs, not so much of activities as of a mindset. Like those characters, the play keeps approaching the brink -- of sentimentality or facile philosophizing -- and pulling back just in time. If love conquers all (and I'm not saying it does, go and find out for yourselves) that could be because all doesn't put up enough of a fight. The play's an odd blend of romantic comedy and fashionable angst that just about holds.

To give the most basic recommendation: It's a 100-minute play with only two characters and no intermission that isn't boring. - Norway. Today runs until Sept. 21 at Tarragon Extra Space. Call 416-531-1827 for information.
