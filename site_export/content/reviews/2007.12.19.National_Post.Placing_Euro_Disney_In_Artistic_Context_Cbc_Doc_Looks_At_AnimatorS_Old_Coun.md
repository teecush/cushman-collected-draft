Walt Disney spent four years of his boyhood on a Missouri farm. The experience doubtless contributed to his sense of the natural world as a backdrop, to his anthropomorphic approach to animals and to his general good ol' folksiness. Just as important, according to Walt Disney: Once Upon a Time (in CBC Newsworld's Wild Docs slot), was his mother's habit of telling him fairy tales at dinnertime. The traditional, and fairly horrific, European stories ate deep into his imagination. Add this to his innate technical, pictorial and entrepreneurial talents and you have what this film's commentary calls "a worldwide empire," built, in a memorably jaw-breaking phrase, on "turning the phantasmagorical into the cinematographical."

"It all started," as you may have heard before, "with a mouse." We hear again the story of how the struggling Disney operation, after having a couple of its best ideas stolen right out from under it, came up with the fabulously squeaky and rubbery Mickey (voiced by Walt himself ), whose debut picture Steamboat Willie, the first animated feature with synchronized sound, conquered the world. What is new in this telling is the way that it juxtaposes Disney's creations with those of earlier authors and illustrators, starting with the British ones: Richard Dadd, Arthur Rackham and most notably Beatrix Potter. He then moved beyond and behind them. The director of Once Upon a Time, Samuel Doux, places Mickey and his friends within a literary and pictorial tradition. Emulating his subject's bent for intrusive cuteness, he even has a couple of cartoon rodents of his own sitting at the side of the frame and saying, in marvelling tones, "we are distant cousins of Mickey Mouse."

The device is only used once and momentarily, making you wonder why it's there at all. For the most part, the film is a conventional mixture of clips, montage and talking heads. Doux is French, and his main point -- a fascinating one -- is to show Disney as a very American artist paying consistent homage to European art. Partly this is a matter of source material; we are told that of the 17 feature-length films in which Disney himself had a hand, 14 derive from European sources. He was self-taught, with "a suspicion of culture in general," but, in a familiar paradox, "he had a fascination with more learned culture." He was shy

of it, but he also wanted to co-opt, and perhaps to be coopted by it. (In that sense he might be said to represent the essence of studio-era Hollywood, though, among those using flesh-and-blood performers, MGM came close.)

He came back from his own first trip to Europe loaded down with art books. He also, again like the other studios but more so, assembled a creative team drawn from the European diaspora. The result was a fruitful clash of traditions. The stern engravings of Gustave Dore inspired the fearsome forests of Sleeping Beauty and Snow White and the Seven Dwarfs, where the trees come to terrifying life. Meanwhile, the Dwarfs are sheer cuddly Americana (nobody before Disney had thought to give them distinct personalities, let alone names), bluebirds start warbling at any hint of romance and the musical score is liable to turn to syrup, though less so than in later Disney pictures. Snow White herself, we learn -- "the first time the studio had to animate a human character" -- was consciously designed as a combination of Shirley Temple and Janet Gaynor, with a singing voice even more irritating than Temple's, which at least came naturally. But she also has a touch of Fay Wray menaced by King Kong. Most intriguing nugget: The mature and still beautiful actress singing the mirror-gazing Queen removed her false teeth to provide the voice for a Witch, whose visual inspiration was Honore Daumier.

Pinocchio gives us an Italian story set in a village that's pure Bavaria. Salvador Dali said that there were three great surrealists in Hollywood: the Marx Brothers (that's three already, but never mind), Cecil B. DeMille and Disney. He especially loved Disney's Pink Elephants Parade. Walt proposed a collaboration, something called Destino that was meant as a supplement to Fantasia. We are shown the 18 seconds of animated test for a picture that remained unmade: a "great American-European cultural partnership" left unrealized.

Disney's favourite book apparently was Alice in Wonderland, and he consciously planned Disneyland as a trip through the looking- glass. The Alice movie took him 15 years to make, and it's tempting to ask why, if he loved the book so much, he had to desecrate it. But the clips shown here suggests that he didn't, or not as much as one remembers. His Alice is a very fair descendant of the original as drawn by John Tenniel, and it's irresistible to watch Jerry Colonna, complete with shriek and moustache, laying down his vocal for the March Hare. Anyway the film -- which, it's surprising to learn, lost money -- is integrity itself compared to what the Disney corporation later did to the Winnie The Pooh stories, text, visuals and all. The latest picture we're shown here is The Jungle Book. The Bare Necessities number is shown in a French-dubbed print, which is charming but does rather destroy the pun in the title. Disney was never at his happiest with English Lit. What turned him on were the old French, Italian and especially Germanic titles, wild woods and bare mountains, given his personal once-over. Nature sweet of tooth and red of claw.
