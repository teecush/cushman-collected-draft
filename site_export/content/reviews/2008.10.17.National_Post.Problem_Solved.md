THEATRE REVIEW

THE SOUND OF MUSIC

The Princess of Wales Theatre

---

The new production of The Sound of Music, by David Mirvish via Andrew Lloyd Webber, begins with singing nuns filling not only the stage but the auditorium. They come at us down the aisles, carrying candles. It's truly frightening, and makes you wonder what the show can possibly have in store when it gets to the Nazis.

This Austrian convent has been staffed with some of the best singing actresses in Canada, and they make a beautiful introductory noise. Then we suddenly burst through a cloudscape in the middle of the Princess of Wales' wide and wonderful stage, and there is Elicia MacKenzie's Maria, carolling and cavorting through an undulating Alpine seting that, as designed by Robert Jones, would take most people's breath away but seems to leave her with plenty. The hills are alive, and it's very impressive.

So is she. I still believe that turning theatrical casting into a branch of the reality TV industry is an abomination; democracy is a wonderful thing, but producers and directors should make their own artistic decisions. Still, judging pragmatically, this solution of the problem called Maria (to misquote a song that's one of my own least favourite things) is a very happy one. Apart from a voice that's gorgeous, clear and natural, she has a quality that may be unprecedented in this role: youth. Mary Martin, the first Maria, was middle-aged when she played it; Julie Andrews, the legendary one, may have been fresh and scrubbed, but she was born mature; the stage Marias I've personally witnessed, Petula Clark and Cynthia Dale, weren't spring chickens either. Maria, if you pay any attention to lyrics, is supposed to be eighteen going on nineteen. Nor is she a natural-born governess; she learns on the job. MacKenzie, tomboyish and impetuous, outwardly insecure but inwardly tough as old lederhosen, brings the show remarkably close to making sense.

She has the benefit of a production by Jeremy Sams that, especially in its early stages, is as intelligent as The Sound of Music is ever going to get. It's sometimes weighed down by memories of the movie; Mac-Kenzie, in her opening song, does the Andrews twirl as if it's in her contract and she can't wait to get it over. But note how the direction sets up My Favourite Things to make it credible that Maria and the Abbess (Noella Hurt, who acts stiffly but sings resoundingly) should be sharing such thoughts. The show also goes on looking sumptuous but it falters in the second half, where it falls foul of those two operetta perennials: love and war.

As a romantic musical, The Sound of Music, despite its chocolate-box reputation, really isn't up to much. It's largely a rerun of Rodgers and Hammerstein's earlier, and much superior, The King and I, with that show's love that could not speak or sing its name replaced by one that would like to but doesn't have the material. Maria and Captain von Trapp, the melting martinet, have never on stage or screen been granted a decent duet, while the script simply takes their mutual attraction for granted. (This is the one R&H score for which Hammerstein didn't also write the book, and it shows. Everything feels as if it's written in capital letters, though that may be partly due to the amplification, which makes singing sound great and acting sound awful.) There is no chemistry between MacKenzie and Burke Moses's untender Captain von Trapp, a block in a double-breasted suit. He does, to be sure, have the show's one truly heartwarming moment when he embraces his previously neglected children; that's because they are children, hence irresistible, especially when hurt or healed.

These seven junior Trapps are especially well-acted, despite the extremes of cuteness to which they have driven. The eldest (Megan Nuttall) forms part of one of the show's other couples, one that starts out with a good-old fashioned charm song that never gets followed up; their relationship's doomed by history (he's in the Party), a development that could be interesting but is hung out to dry. That's not the production's fault, nor is the fact that Maria's older highborn rival (Blythe Wilson) retires from the scene without a fight. But it could make more than it does of her cynical duets with the time-serving Max, whom Keith Dinicol plays with a twinkle that obliterates all his ambiguities.

What he's collaborating with is the Anschluss, which the production does its level best to make terrifying without much help from the text. (What's there is real enough; it's just that so much is left out.) The theatre itself becomes the Salzburg concert-hall in which the Trapp Family Singers' effect their trompe l'oeil transformation into the Trapp Family Refugees, and there are indeed storm troopers in the aisles, though they're still not as scary as the nuns. But no staging has the technical resources to compete with the film here, or in what follows. The production does add a new dark touch: Max, left behind, gets arrested. But we're given no time to ponder what might happen to him (or to thousands of others); we're whisked on to an uplifting finale. The Sisters reprise that meaningless mountain-climbing song, which is You'll Never Walk Alone minus the soul, in the bottom half of the stage, while in the top half the Trapps on their Alp are seen obeying the injunction, while fixing the audience with a collective grin that's pure postcard kitsch. It's certainly in keeping with the spirit of the show, if not with the general handsomeness of the production. It's just that after Cabaret and, in its way, The Producers, a (literally) escapist musical about Nazism seems -- I was going to say dim-witted but I'll settle for myopic. - The Sound of Music runs until Jan. 11.
