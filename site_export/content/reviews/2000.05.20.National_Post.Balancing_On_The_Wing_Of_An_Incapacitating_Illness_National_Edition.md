Wings runs until May 28 at the George Ignatieff Theatre, 27 Devonshire Place. Call (416) 504-7529.

The American playwright Arthur Kopit wrote Wings after his father suffered a stroke and was smitten with aphasia: the loss of the ability to communicate through words. Kopit met other victims with the same condition, one of them a woman who had been a stunt pilot and wing-walker; that is, she flew old-fashioned biplanes, and even performed gymnastic feats on their wings.

Wings presents a similar character, giving a spare account -- part personal, part clinical -- of her journey through an appalling physical and mental crisis. It also uses her previous accomplishments -- summed up in the recurring phrase I walked out on wings" -- to provide an image of her present free-floating agony. It is remarkably successful on both counts.

We begin with our elderly heroine, Emily Stilson, sitting quietly, reading a book. Suddenly a lamp goes off, then on; a clock stops, then starts. Life has ceased to make sense. Actors in white coats shift screens rapidly about the stage, signalling both the short-circuiting in Emilys brain and her physical removal to the hospital. We hear her talking, or perhaps thinking, and much of what she says makes perfect sense, though sometimes -- and with increasing frequency -- she seems to have hit on the wrong word. The doctors and nurses around her, though, dont seem to understand her, and when one of them asks her something, her answer, though it's certainly in English, seems to bear no relation to the question. The agony of a mind still lucid within itself but unable to convey that lucidity to anyone else is given about as powerful a dramatic expression as it is ever likely to receive.

Emily does make progress; the turning point comes when, after a false start, she picks up a toothbrush and demonstrates that she knows what it is and how to use it. She even seems to be cured.

In the meantime, she has devised her own secret theories to explain what is happening to her, and why everyone around her is behaving so strangely; this, she decides, is no hospital but a farmhouse in disguise, probably in Romania (Radio Bucharest, jamming her intercom, figures at one point in her memories of life as an aviatrix) and she is being subtly interrogated.

Emily is established as a character in her own right -- a sharp- tongued, sharp-minded and self-sufficient woman -- and this is what keeps the play from seeming sentimental or exploitative. We have all seen plays in which the author has had a character diagnosed with cancer or AIDS: to assume a spurious air of tragic importance, to tug at the audience's heartstrings or simply to tie up loose ends. (AIDS, with its built-in political agenda, has proved especially useful here, and, in most cases, especially meretricious.)

There is no way not to be affected by a play about terminal or incapacitating illness; truthfully, it hits us far harder than Hamlet, or King Lear, or even some highly persuasive piece about nuclear apocalypse is ever likely to do. Medical melodramas tap into our solitary fears. Most of them, though, are pointless; they seem to be pointing a finger at an audience that is in no position to do anything. (At best, the position seems to be "these people are suffering and you should be sorry" -- but most of us were, even before we went into the theatre.) Also, they rarely tell us anything about the condition. Wings, being both brisk and evocative, does accomplish this, or something of it.

One aspect of the situation, and perhaps the most intolerable, it cannot convey: the sheer unforgiving ongoingness of it. The play happens to be short; it started some 20 years ago as a radio piece, starring the fine Anglo-American actress Constance Cummings, and directed by John Madden; their subsequent stagings of it -- at Yale, on Broadway and in London -- proved the climax of Cummings career and set Madden on the theatrical and cinematic path that led to Shakespeare in Love. But even if it were of Eugene ONeill length, it would still let the audience off lightly. There are some things that the theatre, perhaps mercifully, cannot do.

Ken Albanese's production, a belated Canadian premiere, is lighter in tone than the original (which I remember as oppressively portentous) and the more effective for it; it takes its emotional colouring from its clinical crystalline set, designed by Joanne Thompson and Graeme S. Thomson. Nonnie Griffin is excellent in the virtuoso role of Emily, meticulously accurate in her charting of confusion, and fastidiously avoiding both the plaintive and the excessively crotchety. In the only other substantial role, that of an inspiringly practical doctor and therapist, Claire Crawford is finely sympathetic, and Brian Otto has a bright, troubling little scene as the loose cannon at a rehab group. At the end, the performance takes off on its own winged metaphor; transcending what might be a cruel conclusion, it also mufffles it. That, though, is in the writing; and in this context, I'm not sure that it's a fault.
