JAZZ REVIEWS Jazz divas serve up musical feast

Tuesday, July 04, 1989

ROBERT CUSHMAN

BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Due mainly but not entirely to the du Maurier Jazz festival, last week in Toronto was a continuous feast of singing.

At either end of the week in Roy Thomson Hall were the accredited jazz divas Sarah Vaughan and Cleo Laine. In between, delighting a smaller group for one night at Harbourfront was the cult vocalist of them all, Sheila Jordan. And just next door, independent of the festival, Harbourfront kicked off its summer season of offbeat stars with a week of recitals by Barbara Cook, Broadway ingenue turned cabaret madonna.

All of these ladies, now in their sixties, are formidable technicians. A lot of notes got polished, a lot of octaves spanned.

They share a basic repertoire of jazz standards and show tunes, though none of them drives through the centre of it in the manner of a Sinatra, Bennett or Fitzgerald. Rather, they chip away at its edges.

I missed Sarah Vaughans concert but what I read about it suggested that she is still, as always, subordinating sense to sound. Like Ella Fitzgerald, she can move you if the arc of her musicianship happens to coincide with that of the lyric, but this is likely to be more chance than design.

Ella, whom I find more congenial because less overwhelming (she could never sing opera as Vaughan conceivably could), seems a more sensitive interpreter because she ornaments less; you could trust her with a joke. Vaughan may be a riot in private life but her singing rarely betrays a hint of a sense of humour.

Ella is really two singers: unbridled scat-artist and simple balladeer. Sarah is always herself, compulsively (or helplessly) inventive, which may be why diehard jazz fans prefer her. Of course, the grandeur of her tone can be overwhelming; there are at least two oft-thumbed ballads, The Man I Love and My Old Flame, on which I have never heard her surpassed.

Cleo Laine is all kinds of singer. In forty years, almost, as Britain's top vocalist she has done dance bands, musicals, poetic song-cycles, a spot of lieder and a lot of pop. On Saturday night, though, accompanied by husband John Dankworth and his quartet, she was back to her roots, beginning with a salvo of Gershwin and ending with a medley of Ellington.

Her Gershwin arrangements must go back 20 years, and she seems to be singing them note-for-note the same; she is not an improviser. Rather than exploring a song she decorates it with frilly preludes and codas apparently designed to prove that she is more than just another vocalist riding a familiar war-horse.

Her spouse's penchant for chamber jazz, sturdy but polite, seems to have rubbed off on her. She can sound affected, which is odd since in the important things she is unaffected. She can handle comedy; as it happens, her repertoire on this date didnt give her humour much scope but at least her links between songs were delightfully down-to-earth. It is another shared Dankworth trait, and, I chauvinistically believe, a British one. And she can handle passion; for so studied a performer, she delivers a majestic blues. She irradiates Ellingtons Sophisticated Lady with stabbing single notes of pain and anger. She selects her dark tones with the care of an actress.

That sonority, like Sarah Vaughan's, is the source of widespread popularity. Sheila Jordan, another singer of phenomenal range and control, is never going to challenge them in the lullaby stakes. She sings unyielding bop lines, and her preferred accompaniment is a solitary bass, generally manned by a virtuoso named Harvie Swartz. These spartan tastes make her records easier to respect than enjoy, but in person she proved a surprisingly warm performer. She was apt to bend in low towards the bass, as if about to lock horns with it, and she carried on a genially flirtatious relationship with Mr Swartz.

Sometimes she seemed unnecessarily stark, prefacing a Billie Holiday selection with impromptu blues about the Lady's persecution by the New York cops. Its her job to move us with the song, not the legend. But then she proved she could, and overall she showed that she was into songs, not just sounds. Her high and hauntinng Baltimore Oriole was memorable, and she made Oscar Brown Jr.s Dat Dere a persuasive ode to her own daughter.

Barbara Cook is not jazz, and probably doesn't intend to be, though her buoyant rendition of Sooner or Later (a rhythm ballad rescued from Song of the South, Disney's cartoon of the Uncle Remus stories, which was withdrawn from circulation through racial sensitivity) made you wonder how she would fare with backing less formal than she gets from Wally Harper, a fine man in the Broadway-pit and supper-club mold. Their showboating Sweet Georgia Brown, replete with a few needless bars of scat, does everything but swing.

What Cook does best is to distill the essence of musical theatre: that is, to be musical and theatrical at the same time. She can do this with an isolated standard (Arthur Schwartz's I See Your Face Before Me was lovely) but her strongest suits are drawn from her own Broadway wardrobe: Dear Friend, a complicated song from She Loves Me that she makes crystal clear, and the enchanting Mister Snow from Carousel.

Harry Connick Jr. is a ringer in this company, being young, male, and mostly a pianist. At the Diamond last Monday night he played an intoxicating couple of sets, in every keyboard style from Bourbon Street to Birdland, with a lot of stomping boogie-woogie on the way. (He also brought on Branford Marsalis in the kind of last-minute surprise that makes a festival festive.) From time to time he sang. He mooned Satchmodically through I've Got the World on a String; he orated Come Fly with Me, with only a bass and his own snapping fingers, which seemed fated not to work bbut took off through sheer ebullient insistence; and he hunched over the keys to croon There But For You Go I, an elusive song from Brigadoon in a subfusc rendition that lingers as potently as any of the full-throated singing that filled the rest of the week.
