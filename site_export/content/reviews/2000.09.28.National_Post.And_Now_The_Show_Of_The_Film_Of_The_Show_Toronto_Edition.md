GREASE

Elgin Theatre, Toronto

And here it is, the show of the film of the show, Canadian style. It is, as you would expect, the worst of all possible worlds. It also lends a new edge to the old saw about nostalgia not being what it used to be.

There are many nostalgias at work here. There is the fondness of the show Grease itself, first staged in 1972, for the musical and social styles of the post-Elvis 1950s: the days when high school really was high school and rock 'n roll ruled. This obviously tapped in to something, since the original Broadway production ran for a phenomenally long time. I saw it when it came to London (with an unknown Richard Gere in the cast) and I have fond, if misty, memories of it. It was tuneful, it was funny, and it was terrifically danced. One number, Born To Hand Jive, was a legitimate riot.

So thats my nostalgia -- for the show, rather than for the period it depicts, even though I lived through it. It's more fun to remember than it was to experience; the pastiche is better than the real thing.

I never got round to seeing the 1978 movie, but it did very well without me. It was, in fact, the last movie musical with live actors to make any money. Its impact, of course, dwarfed that of the show - - a fact not lost on the originators of the current production, which started out in Montreal.

It sets out to honour (i.e. cash in on) its audience's fond memories of the film. Indeed, if we take the background article in the program literally, and I dont see why we shouldnt, then the producers were unaware Grease had ever been a stage show in the first place. This kind of ignorance (as opposed to innocence, of which this production knows nothing) makes me want to tear its owners into small pieces and scatter the sections around the stage - - which in this case could hardly make it look more of a mess than it does at present.

In practical terms, staging the movie means shoving into the stage script the two hit songs that were added for the screen: Hopelessly Devoted to You and You're the One That I Want. These numbers, credited to J. Farrar, have stronger tunes -- or, anyway, stronger hooks -- than anything in the theatre score, but their lyrics barely exist. The second one, in particular, seems ludicrously thin to function as the climax of what is still -- barely -- a narrative musical, however much noise it makes. They are also out of period: Seventies disco rather than Fifties pop.

This built-in anachronism seems to have been taken by the director, Denis Bouchard, as licence for chaos. The show still takes place in and around Rydell High (named after Bobby?), but the school seems to have been relocated in Canada -- or at least in some milieu where people automatically fall about at the word "Winnipeg." (In the States, they'd simply say Canada and expect the same response.) The clothes and musical are still just about those of the Fifties but the manners have changed and definitely for the worse. Shy louts and virginal flirts have become sociopaths, incipient or actual. I must be a hypocrite: Here I am, eagerly lapping up the violence of The Sopranos every night, but jibbing at the sight, apparently meant to be funny, of a leathered youth shoving a blind man. Even when the blind man was allowed, a full act later, to shove him back, I still didnt find it hilarious, just mechanical. The characters include the school principal, who seems to constitute the entire teaching faculty, and a gangling nerd with the giveaway name of Eugene; both are there only to be mocked, and in the crudest possible way. (The show sometimes seems like the late lamented Freaks and Geeks, shorn of its truth and its humour and seen entirely from the viewpoint of the freaks.)

The conceit seems to be that we are at a reunion in the school gym, interrupted by the more unruly alumni of the Fifties, who proceed to whisk us back to a simpler time. (Or is it just that their ghosts descend? Either way.) The gym, with a couple of shallow steps and a false proscenium, remains our unit set. But there must have been productions of Grease that actually were staged in high- school gyms and looked better than this one.

The actors, meanwhile, all wear those determinedly brutalist face- mikes. This makes them look ugly -- and not only ugly but wrong -- and it does nothing for clarity. (The sound-quality is appalling; its all blare). It also destroys the show's most appealing ingredient: the wit of the songs. Even now, the score is the one thing that saves the evening from being absolute hell.

Jim Jacobs and Warren Casey, the uncelebrated author-composers (this was not only their one hit, but their one show) heightened what was already Fifties rock's most endearing characteristic, its awareness of its own absurdity -- something that disappeared when the Beatles arrived and spoiled everything.

So we get Beauty Queen Dropout, with a full-dress Teen Angel descending in a burger bar to counsel and console the singer, and the heart-rending Alone at a Drive-In Movie ("its no fun watching werewolves without you." ) We even get, or should get, a certain amount of character development. I remember being pleased when the soundtrack recording of Summer Days, Summer Nights, a song that hadnt made much impression in the show, went to No. 1. That, I felt atavistically, was how hit records ought to sound. More than that, though, I enjoyed the neat wit with which the song laid out two teenage perspectives on the same relationship: Danny the jock, Sandy the demure, having their own little Rashomon. Here, though, you can't make out the words: Bang goes your only chance of getting to know the protagonists, who proceed to go through the most unmotivated sequence of partings and reconciliations in the history of either musical theatre or teenage romance. The original script must have been pared to the bone: I remember it as simple but not stupid. Rick Miller, who plays Danny, is credited -- rather far down the list -- with "script adaptation," so maybe it's his fault.

As an actor, hes pleasant when he gets the chance, but when hes with his buddies -- which is most of the time -- he simply merges into the crowd. As Sandy, Ma-Anne Dionisio is rather more plaintive than she was as the doomed heroines of Miss Saigon and West Side Story: this despite her obvious resilience. (Her biography informs us that in London she played star roles in Miss Saigon and Martin Guerre at the same time. Quite a gal.) Her Oriental looks may or may not be supposed to connect with her family's apparent strictness: something else the production hasn't thought through.

In what should be the plum female role of Rizzo, the One Who Puts Out, Katie Emmie McInich displays a powerful set of lungs, even when just talking. She sings her big number There Are Worse Things I Could Do (a not unthoughtful song, when she thinks shes pregnant) straight into the audiences face, rather than that of the person she's supposedly addressing. This, yet again, is the musical theatre as wannabe rock concert. Why bother? The director seems to have imposed a unified acting style -- i.e., all the performances are alike. They go on and off like light switches. It's probably not their fault, but the entire cast comes off energetically rotten.
