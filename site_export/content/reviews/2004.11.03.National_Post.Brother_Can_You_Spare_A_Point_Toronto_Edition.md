COUPLE OF BLAGUARDS

Winter Garden Theatre, Toronto

- - -

Once upon a time, Frank McCourt, who was later to write Angelas Ashes, and his brother Malachy, a sometime actor, pooled their talents to create an autobiographical entertainment. It dealt with their poverty-stricken childhood on the worst street in the Irish city of Limerick and with their subsequent emigration, not both at the same time, to New York where, on the show's evidence, they had a very good time. They performed the piece, often making it up as they went along, in the pubs and barrooms of their adopted city. Gradually it solidified into a play, and a successful one. Eventually the brothers tired of performing it and it passed into the hands of other, professional actors, two of whom have now brought it to Toronto.

I don't know what the McCourts are like as performers, but I suspect the show worked better when they were doing it themselves, primarily because the authors have supplied no characterizations for other actors to seize upon. There is nothing in the writing to distinguish Frank from Malachy or Malachy from Frank. The first act is entirely devoted to Franks boyhood -- grim at the time, fairly funny in the telling -- but what happens to him could equally well have happened to his brother, and there is nothing distinctive about the way he reacts. When Frank takes a ship across the Atlantic just before the intermission, there seems no particular reason for him rather than Malachy to be doing it. Indeed, when its revealed in the second act that Malachy went on the same journey just a little later, one feels distinctly had.

Worse than their anonymity as individuals is the lack of any dynamic between them. There is no reason beyond the accident of birth for them both to be in the same play. This matters less in the first act, where some unity is bestowed by the fact of their belonging to an identifiable community. They grew up in the poorest part of a depressed town. There was a lavatory outside their house: not theirs, the whole street's. "Worse than the ordinary miserable childhood, says Frank in Angela's Ashes, is the miserable Irish childhood, and worse yet is the miserable Irish Catholic childhood."

In the book, thats a source of both anger and laughter, with the anger predominating. In this earlier hack at the same material, everything is played for laughs. Its true that we hear of a drunken and often absentee father, and of the infant deaths of too many McCourt siblings; the loss of one young brother and the account of the father's taking him for burial provide the play with its only touching moments. But for the most part, we hear about bigoted priests instructing impressionable schoolboys on the Seven Deadly Sins, with special emphasis on the horrors of "the Lust. The implications of all this are horrific, as the McCourts themselves in other contexts have testified, but here it's presented, if not as endearing, then certainly as amusing. Which it is, though its effectiveness is lessened by the fact that weve heard it all before. Irish Catholicism at its most parochial is rather an easy comic target, certainly a much-battered one.

Frank is played -- man, boy and casual acquaintances -- by Jarlath Conroy, an actor in the sly-voiced, sharp-featured Irish mould that traditionally inspires adjectives like ferrety" and "weaselling; by way of variation, I would like to describe this latest exemplar as stoatlike." He is more competent than compelling. Howard Platt, an actor of greater bulk and greater resource who is also the director, plays all other roles, before settling down in the second act to being Malachy.

Here, with both brothers arrived in New York, the show splits its focus and becomes thoroughly arbitrary. We get alternating pages from their stateside careers, assembled with no shape and no logic. Frank gets a job looking after canaries, and glues them to their perches to conceal their deaths on his watch. On military service, he meets a girl who introduces him to the glories of his own countrys literature. Malachy, after reading a speech from The Playboy of the Western World, gets an acting job from a director who seems easily pleased. The father, a shadowy figure in the first act, turns up in the second to make emotional demands on the brothers and us; so, with even less preparation, does their mother, the Angela of the ashes. A third brother, never so much as mentioned before, also puts in an appearance, under the apparent impression that we've known him all along.

The show remains mildly entertaining, and seems to be making very few claims for itself -- which, considering its overall pointlessness, is a blessed mercy.
