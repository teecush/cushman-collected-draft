Critics cool to play but warm to star

Friday, January 27, 1984

ROBERT CUSHMAN

London ENGLAND -- BY ROBERT CUSHMAN LONDON THIS WEEK Larry Finebergs Eve was added to the list of Canadian plays which have opened in London to a frosty response from British critics. It may, however, stand a better chance with British audiences.

One thing that could recommend the play to them is that - unlike such previous imports as Balconville, Waiting for the Parade and Billy Bishop Goes to War - its drama is not dependent on either a Canadian setting or peculiarly Canadian characters. The play, which was first seen at Stratford, Ont., in 1976, happens to be set in Montreal (where Fineberg grew up) but its story would ring true anywhere. The title almost shrieks universality; the heroine's name is actually Eva, but there she is on the marquee simply as Eve.

Fineberg's Eve may not quite qualify as Everywoman but she is a lively lady in a painfully recognizable situation. In her sixties, she finds her marriage has worn out. She leaves her invalid husband and goes to live alone in a seedy rooming-house. Conventional society, represented by her lawyer son, assumes she will soon be back, but the end of the play finds her reasserting her precarious independence. The play almost becomes propaganda for the liberation of senior citizens, but the writing is real and rooted enough to transcend this.

That, at least, was my reaction. The London daily reviewers were generally less enthusiastic. The Daily Telegraph called it a slight, monochrome study of a lonely woman with "little but its quiet integrity to recommend it." The Financial Times said there was little fabric to Mr. Finebergs text and little weight - little wit, too, for that matter. The Times was heavily critical of the technique: "The play runs into the dramatic quicksands of solitude, and attempts to extricate itself with non-stop monologue - part free association, part explanatory narrative - plugging the silence with garrulous telegraphese." The Guardian was the most positive: "one or two shrewd points . . . accurate and optimistic." All were united, however, in their enthusiasm for the central performance. At Stratford the role was created by Jessica Tandy, a British actress who has spent most of her life in America. In London, neatly enough, it is a triumph for Constance Cummings, American-born but British- based. Her career reached a new plateau when she played opposite Laurence Olivier in Long Days Journey Into Night at the National Theatre. More recently she won a Tony as the stroke-victim heroine of Wings. Her forte is playing women in extreme states while keeping us constantly in touch with their normal selves.

Miss Cummings, who is herself a stunningly youthful 74, makes the audience feel the astonishing ease with which a middle-class woman can turn into a bag-lady rummaging in trash-cans. She is very fine in her scenes with a heavy-drinking Hungarian refugee who breaks into her room to throw up in the sink, and stays to help her rediscover her womanhood. She has an admirably light touch in a play whose preponderance of monologues could easily drag it down. Fineberg accounts her performance the best he has seen in the role.

Ronnie Letham, a Scots actor-director, has staged the piece in the studio space of the Lyric Theatre, Hammersmith, one of London's outlying theatres but also one of its most prestigious; it has originated several successful plays, including the current West End and Broadway smash Noises Off. The Hungarian is admirably played by Glyn Owen; Canadian actor Blain Fairman is the son.

Robert Cushman is drama critic for The London Observer.
