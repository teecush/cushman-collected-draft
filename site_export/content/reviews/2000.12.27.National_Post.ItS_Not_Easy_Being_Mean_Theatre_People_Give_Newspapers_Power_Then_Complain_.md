The weeks leading up to Christmas were a terrible time for people in my racket, both practising and dormant. That at least is what I gather from reading them. Martin Knelman, an experienced critic of theatre and other things currently writing an entertainment column in The Toronto Star, used his space to examine an issue often pondered by people in the reviewing dodge, though probably not by many others: the use in ads of extracts from our immortal prose to make it appear that we endorsed the product more enthusiastically than was actually the case. He's against it.

Although Martin was complaining he seemed to find the whole matter fairly amusing, which I think is the right attitude. (I call him Martin because he's a friend of mine; also because this is John Frasers space and Im keeping it warm for him. John's a friend, too.) We critics spend a lot of our time misrepresenting the -- shall we call them works of art? -- that are placed before us, so we should not complain when we too get misrepresented. If we really feel passionately about it, then we should watch our language, so as to make life as hard for the advertising people as possible. This can be difficult. In a former existence I wrote that a disastrous play at London's famous Royal Court Theatre contained one very funny line. The play was then described in the listings as Very funny -- Robert Cushman, Observer". The PR told me that this expressed the managements genial contempt for critics. I couldnt help feeling that this contempt would have been more effectively expressed if they'd refrained from quoting us altogether. I also suspected that if they'd received any genuinely enthusiastic reviews, they would have used them. But the Royal Court was and is a very high-minded organization, and Im probably just being cynical.

Being misquoted is one thing. Being prevented from furnishing the quotes in the fist place is another. That, though, is what happened to Kate Taylor, my opposite number at The Globe and Mail, when she attempted to review New France, the two-part show currently being presented by the VideoCabaret company as the first instalment of their revived comic-strip history of Canada. She had to pay for her own tickets for the first part and was refused admission altogether to the second. These essential facts are not in dispute, though some of the details seem to be. Kate claims to have been intimidated by an anonymous bouncer; VideoCabaret say that, actually, they didnt buy in any heavies for the occasion and the guy in question was their own thoroughly unthreatening director, Michael Hollingsworth. Presumably there wasn't time for formal introductions.

The grounds for VideoCab's displeasure were that six years ago, Kate, seeing their work for the first time, had based an unflattering review partly on what her predecessors had written about previous shows, and that nothing she had come up with since had atoned for this. This surprises me a bit, since her review of the parts of the current show she did see seemed to contain about the same proportions of praise and blame that my own did; and theyve always been very nice to me. Kates subsequent complaints about this infringement on the freedom of the press were subsequently seconded by a Globe columnist, Russell Smith; and more surprisingly questioned by another Globe columnist, Rick Salutin.

Salutin -- we aren't really friends, though I admire his writing very much and agree with him 90% of the time -- has complained before about critics, not because of the harm they do to practitioners (a word I prefer to artists) but because of the harm they do to themselves: sitting in judgment all the time, we imperil our souls, or at least our senses of humour. I think there's something in this, though I guess Ill live with it. In the present instance he queried the custom of having a regular reviewer for each branch of the arts. Why not spread the load around a bit, allow for alternative voices? After all, even right-wing papers allow the occasional left-wing voice on their op-ed pages. (He should know.)

Well, for a start, arts writers dont divide quite as rigidly and predictably as political ones. Kate Taylor, and the Star's Richard Ouzounian, and I are very different people, of different ages and backgrounds, but our opinions diverge and overlap in ways that I certainly can't predict. (Well, sometimes.) Obviously I have a turf and a job to defend here, but I dont see how parcelling out reviews would work -- not at least unless we are talking about having two or more reviews per paper of each show, and I dont see any editor springing for that. If reviews were assigned on an ad hoc basis, then the situation would be even more arbitrary than it is at present. If it's a matter of fitting individual reviewers to individual plays, well, do either reviewers or practitioners want to be pre-judged on past records, which would be the only way of doing it? Isn't that, in fact, what VideoCabaret was complaining about?

I think they were within their rights to deny a reviewer free tickets. I doubt that they had the right to deny her entrance altogether. (Would they discriminate against non-publishing spectators in the same way?) I am sure that they were wrong to ask the Globe to send another critic; if they were going to take a stand they should have done it properly and gone voluntarily Globeless.

Obviously they werent about to do this because -- this is hardly a secret, nor is it shameful -- they wanted the free publicity that reviews bring. They dont give us free tickets because they love us. They have in fact, like all theatres at all levels of both economy and pretension, made a Faustian deal. (So have we; somehow the road to hell manages to be a two-way street.) And it's ratified every time they quote us, even when they do it accurately. Practitioners complain about the power of the critics, but it's largely they who have given it to us. People would forget what wed said if the ads didnt keep reminding them.

These days there's a new wrinkle in that situation because papers, with the blessed exception of this one, now accord plays star ratings, and the theatres seem much to prefer quoting them; they take up less space. They also look more judicial, but that's the harm of them. (They are also much less amenable to misappropriation -- unless, of course, you hire a press agent who can't count.)

They are cloaking a purely personal opinion in the dignity of statistics and perpetuating it, week after week.
