Near the beginning of the year, Factory Theatre introduced us to Florence Gibson and her play Belle, a richly written and richly imagined piece about black-white relationships in the industrial North, in the immediate wake of the American Civil War. It painted a broad but detailed canvas, was determinedly un-parochial, and used language more creatively and dramatically than any new Canadian play in ages.

Near the end of the year the same theatre presented David Widdicombe's Science Fiction, a hugely enjoyable take on the paranormal, the paramilitary, and the just plain paranoid. (It was also an auspicious directorial debut for Ted Dykstra.)

The Factory, like its blood brothers, the Tarragon and Passe Muraille, offered plenty of the regulation terrible stuff in between, and its actual production standards are very hit-and miss, but it deserves credit for giving us the year's two best new plays. It also brought us One Yellow Rabbit's Thunderstruck, which was elemental, and revived Michael Mackenzie's Geometry in Venice, which was a good idea.

Two achieved home-grown plays in a year may not seem much, but history (not to mention those revivals) suggests that you would have to be an incurable sap to expect more. The runners-up were at Tarragon: The delightfully funny Sibs, from Richard Greenblatt and Diane Flacks, may have been more of a revue than a play, but so much the worse for plays; Judith Thomson's Perfect Pie (with Nancy Palk at her best) was a solid slice of the rural.

The remaining members of Ontario's approved holy trinity of playwrights turned up in care of the Canadian Stage Company: George F. Walker's Heaven played like self-parody; Jason Sherman's Patience went on, and on, and on. It was technically a revival and, like a similar re-staging of David Young's Glenn, a disillusioning one.

Canadian Stage, as in previous years, took up some of the slack by not worrying too much about being Canadian. They did give us Brad Fraser's Outrageous, the year's best musical and, under its author's direction, the best performed. They also brought in The Overcoat, a magical spectacle from Vancouver, and enterprisingly followed that up by turning its director (Morris Panych) and designer (Ken MacDonald) loose on the fine British play Hysteria.

It wasn't a perfect match, and the actors, with the notable exception of Kristen Thomson, tended to get lost; but it struck any number of sparks. Soon after this came The Weir which was very moving, as productions of this uncanny Irish conversation-piece tend to be. Another worthwhile import (at Tarragon) was the American Tony Kushner's Slavs!, an ambitious survey of Russia post-perestroika, which started terrible and ended superb. It was acted by some of Ontario's best, though only Randy Hughson and Maria Vacratsis were actually at their best.

Nationalists take heart: In a crowded World Stage season -- which included Peter Brook, the Russian Gaudeamus, and the British National Theatre's intermittently magnificent Oresteia, we did as well as anyone, what with John Neville excelling himself in Krapp's Last Tape, and Robert Lepage, in The Far Side of the Moon, at his most beguiling, not least as a performer. He's underrated as an actor, perhaps because he makes it look easy. And, technical wonders apart, his stuff can be genuinely funny.

The Mirvishes locked up the commercial scene with The Lion King, Mamma Mia! (probably a very nice musical if you can stand the songs), and Dame Edna Everage.

They also brought Donald Sutherland impressively home in Enigma Variations; but they have yet to originate anything substantial.

Others' attempts at turning a profit were, as usual, woeful, based on un-bright ideas (a revue about ageing, a new musical of The Taming of the Shrew): apart, of course, from The Vagina Monologues, which happens to be an extremely well performed show.

There remains a yawning gap between the coterie and the marketplace, one that can only be filled by the classics. In 1999 it was triumphantly filled by Soulpepper, who perhaps aroused unfair expectations. Some of their work this year was very fine: a searching School for Wives and a re-mount of their heart-lifting Platonov (both directed by the invaluable Laszlo Marton) and a good account of Harold Pinter's Betrayal. But The Mill on the Floss wasn't worth their time (it didn't even feel like a Soulpepper production) and their first attempts at Shakespeare misfired: One production was cancelled, dampening the whole season, and the other, Twelfth Night, was only as good as its most experienced actors (very, in the case of Steven Sutcliffe's Sir Andrew) and rather self- consciously directed.

So Stratford retained its Shakespearean hegemony, expressed in three perfectly decent productions: a record that should be better but could easily be worse. Nearly everything else there was splendid. The house playwright appeared as a character, and a fascinating reticent one, in Timothy Findley's Elizabeth Rex which, as directed by Martha Henry, was a great experience but not necessarily a great play. The program told us it was about gender roles; I'm not sure we'd have known otherwise.

Even better was Euripides' Medea with Seana McKenna (this was a great year for the Greeks); and we also got Uta Hagen, another who makes it look easy, in Collected Stories, Brian Bedford, ditto, in Tartuffe, the best kind of romp in The Three Musketeers, and a musical Fiddler on the Roof that actually justified its place in the bill. The Shaw Festival had, for once, a lower profile; but The Doctor's Dilemma, Six Characters in Search of an Author and A Room of One's Own (with the Australian actress Pamela Rabe) were all first-rate, and She Loves Me a perfect delight.

A final word for three of our very best actors who this year scaled personal peaks: Diego Matamoros, poisonously bantering in Betrayal; David Fox, immaculately terrifying in Science Fiction; and Brent Carver, who was perfect in Elizabeth Rex and who proved in Fiddler that, given the material and the talent, there is no such thing as miscasting.
