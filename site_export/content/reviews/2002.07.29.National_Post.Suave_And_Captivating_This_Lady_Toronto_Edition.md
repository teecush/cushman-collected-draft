MY FAIR LADY

Festival Theatre, Stratford, Ont.

- - -

The Stratford Festival's continuing cycle of American musicals effectively began in 1986, with a production of The Boys from Syracuse, Rodgers and Harts vaudevillean adaptation of The Comedy of Errors. The leading roles of the twin Antipholi were entrusted to two actors who might have been cast in the same parts had Stratford elected to do the original Shakespeare play: the companys two reigning juveniles of the day, Colm Feore and Geraint Wyn-Davies. Neither was known as a singer, but this score gave them testing romantic ballads to get through. The critics at the time expressed pleased surprise at how well Feore sang and sympathetic disappointment at how well Wyn-Davies, despite the triple Welshness of his name, didn't.

Sixteen years on, the two actors, both now valued commodities in the world outside and hence largely unavailable to Stratford, have returned to the Festival and to musicals. This season's production of My Fair Lady (also with classic lineage and a show that, unlike Syracuse, treats its source as an object to be venerated rather than gleefully plundered) opened with Feore playing Professor Henry Higgins. Wyn-Davies has now succeeded him in the role, and in the vocal department things seem much as they were. Feore may have been the most full-throatedly musical enry iggins in 'istory.

Wyn-Davies returns to the tradition, inaugurated by Rex Harrison and honoured by nearly every actor since, of pattering his way through the numbers. He is also less impish and acerbic than Feore, and far less impulsively energetic, but he brings far more lyrical fervour to the phoneticist's passion for the English language. Cuddly in his cardigan, where Feore was angular, he is surprisingly reminiscent of Kelsey Grammer in Frasier. Now, there would be a Higgins, pride just galloping for a fall; and he can sing. So, too, I believe, can his Daphne, Jane Leeves, who of course is English, and working-class English at that. (Northern, not Cockney, but I assume she could do both.) Of course shes too old for Eliza, but so is nearly everybody whos ever played her, with music or without, all the way back to Mrs. Patrick Campbell in the first Pygmalion.

Stratford's Eliza, Cynthia Dale, is of course not English, and certainly not Cockney. You feel the professor should be objecting, not to the ugliness of her vowels, but to their strenuous inaccuracy.

Admirably unfazed by the change in leading men, she has sharpened up Eliza's assertive and rebellious side to great comic and dramatic effect, in speech and especially in indignant mime, but she cant translate it into the more formal medium of song: Just You Wait, her impotent dream of revenge, is near to excruciating. (Not entirely her fault; the middle section of this song has some of the awkwardest lyrics the gifted but uneven Alan Jay Lerner ever penned.) In the gentler fantasy of Wouldnt It Be Loverly, though, where accent is a lot less important than mood, she is enchanting, as is the whole show, with special credit to Donna Feore's wistful choreography. At this point, Richard Monette's suave and captivating production lets us know exactly why we are watching the musical rather than the play.

Barry MacGregors Colonel Pickering remains a robust and amusing tower of strength. As Mrs Higgins, the musicals other guardian of decency, Joyce Campion deposits each of her pearls of wit and wisdom daintily and infallibly in the audience's laps.
