Daniel MacIvor's retreat into conventional narrative play- writing continues apace. Though "apace" may not be the right word for his new play, How It Works, a slight piece in a slow production.

It's a mildly interesting, mildly entertaining play about relationships ruptured and possibly repaired. It starts well, with Al, a cop, and Christine, a pharmacy cashier, having a beer together at a Halifax pub called the Thirsty Duck. (I think. They just refer to it as The Duck. Im all in favour of plays set in Halifax, but most of the time this one could be going on anywhere. Call it universal. Or just vague.) This is their first flesh-and-blood encounter after striking up an acquaintance online, and author and actors capture the promise and discomfort of a first date that isnt quite a first date. Al, the first to arrive, sits there, happily lip- synching to a record of Trouble in Mind, until Christine arrives to find him covered in the mild embarrassment that's almost obligatory in a white man caught singing the blues. They get on. We learn that both are ex-marrieds, though it subsequently transpires that Christine is referring to a youthful two years spent in a convent. So she's a bride of Christ; though if she never took her vows, wouldnt that make her more a fiancee of Christ? And is her name significant? Anyway, the idea later yields a couple of fairly funny lines but doesnt contribute as much to her strength of character as seems to be hoped.

Tom Barnett gives a well-grounded performance of Al; he's the only member of the cast who never forces a note. Caroline Gillis's Christine is strong and likeable, though shes never as solid again as she is in that first scene. But then neither is the play. Tom has insisted that he cant be involved with anyone who does drugs, and we find when we follow him home that this isn't just professional. His 19-year-old daughter, Brooke, is hooked on crystal meth (though the program gets more specific about this than the play does) and is lying and stealing to support her habit. From here on, the play declines into a routine family-problem drama, decked out with a few tricks and aphorisms. We see Brooke with her mother who, when we first meet her, is getting dressed for a date of her own. This turns out to be the reddest of herrings, and Fiona Highet cannot make the woman anything but brittle. Her relationship with Al is never credible, least of all when it gets shown to us in flashbacks; these include a satire on old-fashioned experimental theatre that is briefly amusing but seems to belong in some other show.

Christine manages to convince both of Brooke's parents that shes the person who can, if not cure Brooke, make her face up to her situation. And, yes, its a credit to both MacIvor and Gillis that this is believable, in terms of who Christine is, if not of what she actually does. This connects with the play's prologue, a short credo that Gillis delivers very well, and with its title, How It Works, in that we all have stories inside us that we need to let out. Brooke's is of a youthful trauma that triggered her addiction. What the incident was is signalled many scenes before its actually revealed. I appreciate that the play probably isnt aiming at suspense, but it creeps in anyway, the issue being not what happened to Brooke but when she will put us all out of our misery by telling. Bethany Jillard has Brooke's teenage rhythms down perfectly; so has the writing, but neither is able to develop them. The final note is one of carefully qualified optimism: an honourable posture but, with the characters faint and the action fragmentary, no more than a posture.

MacIvor's direction proceeds laboriously from one episode to another and -- on a set dourly designed and lit by Kimberly Purtell - - from one anonymous rostrum to another. Recorded songs between scenes, mostly with a country sound, depress the mood and the pace still further. Most of the world, the theatre world anyway, is a Daniel MacIvor fan, and I was able to join the club myself when I finally caught up with his one-man shows. But after the flash of those solo plays (and, to be fair, of his twohander A Beautiful View, in which he didn't act but which he directed superbly), the drabness of this one is a shock, and an unwelcome one Instead of attitude, platitude.
