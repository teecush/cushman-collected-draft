THEATRE REVIEW

SMALL METAL OBJECTS

Eaton Centre, Toronto

---

So there we were, sitting on benches (hell on the back, but the show's only an hour long), in a makeshift open booth just south of Sears, gazing out at three heavily populated levels of shopping mall, and at the catwalks and escalators that connect them. We had headphones clamped to our ears, or earphones clamped to our heads. We must have looked odd.

We were there to witness small metal objects, brought to us by the Back to Back Theatre of Australia, and the first offering in this year's World-Stage season. (The production moves next week from Toronto Vancouver.)

In this piece four actors are placed within a large and crowded public space; the premiere was at a railway station in Melbourne. They act out a drama that we may be able to see, if we can find the right direction in which to look, and that we can hear, courtesy of those headsets.

It concerns two modest-sounding men, Gary and Steve, who have a mutual-support system going. Gary has a wife and children -- he will also shortly be a grandfather -- and boasts, quietly, of all the things, including his life, that he would be willing to sacrifice for their sakes.

Steve has never even had a girlfriend and is emotionally dependent on Gary; he's terrified when Gary reveals that he is about to have a knee operation, even though Gary reassures him that this is a procedure that has seldom, if ever, proved fatal.

The two turn out, rather surprisingly, to be drug dealers; and they are contacted, first by cellphone, then in person, by a far smoother operator named Alan who needs their product for a function he's hosting that evening. The problem: Gary has to reclaim his stash from a nearby locker, Steve refuses to move from the spot to which he is presently rooted and Gary will not abandon him.

Alan has to call in heavy reinforcements, in the shape of a very upscale motivational psychiatrist. If she can't succeed in uprooting Steve, Jim will have to find an alternative source of supply: in Scarborough. (Just why these four Aussies are transacting their purely domestic business right here in Toronto is a question, obviously, not to be asked.)

According to Bruce Glad-win, director of both the company and the production, the show operates on two levels. One of them is situational: the depositing of actors and audience among a crowd of people who aren't in on the act. It doesn't work too well.

Maybe the Eaton Centre is too big and self-absorbed a space; anyway, the actors are hard to pick out (of course, if they'd done the show inside Sears, they would have been visible for miles), and the hoped-for spontaneous interaction between them and perplexed members of the general public mostly failed to materialize.

If there were any spirited verbal dust-ups going on, they failed to communicate themselves through my cans. Nor did we weirdos in the bleachers attract more than passing curiosity. There is a mildly hypnotic fascination about watching the metropolitan shoppers, heedlessly thronging; all human life was probably there, but we never got to find out about it. And really, why is this stuff about mingling with the public and making the spectators part of the spectacle regarded as such a big philosophical deal? We know already.

The other aspect of small metal objects is the play itself, and that is far more satisfying. (I don't understand the title, and I've yet to read an article or review that attempts to explain it.) The Back to Back company is partly composed of actors who, in the group's own words, "are perceived to have an intellectual disability." These include Sonia Teuben, whose idea the show was and who plays Gary, and Simon Laherty who plays Steve.

Teuben's voice and delivery are pinched, concerned and urgent; Laherty's is blockish and deliberate. Their rhythm, a mental connection riddled with pauses but essentially unbroken, may remind you of early Pinter. The counterpoint of question and reassurance may also remind those with long memories of the television duologues of Peter Cook and Dudley Moore.

Their would-be clients, by contrast, are played by for-want-of-a- better-word normal actors (Jim Russell and Caroline Lee); and the kick comes in seeing the worldly, successful couple failing to impose their will on the two in the margins. They can't break Steve's stubbornness or Gary's loyalty: not with money, not even with sex.

When the lady, who's like a walking, talking copy of Cosmopolitan, offers as a last resort to go down on Steve and is refused, her fury at the man she's conditioned to despise knows no bounds. For once, the humble and meek get exalted; and if the mighty aren't exactly put down from their seats, they are at least obliged to shuffle a little. The actors may be physically hard to discern, but aurally they're very present. This theatre production is terrific radio. - In Toronto until Saturday.
