INTO THE WOODS

Avon Theatre, Stratford

- - -

As usual with Into the Woods, I was in tears when the curtain came down. This composite fairy tale, with music and lyrics by Stephen Sondheim and book by James Lapine, has the most moving finale of any musical since Carousel. And in that respect it may well be better than Carousel: That shows Youll Never Walk Alone is an infallible throat-lumper, but it would probably work just as well at the end of any sad story with an uplifting finish. Children Will Listen, the choral number that concludes Into the Woods, makes a general statement about parents and how they habitually both love and fail their children. Its import is universal, but the impact stems from its direct connection to the action we have been watching. And it isn't heavy; it moves easily into a reprise of the title tune, which is a cheerful cautionary tale.

You could apply that description to the whole show, with the rider that the cheer has a darkness to it and that that combination, increasingly complex as the evening proceeds, here makes a masterpiece. It helps that the material is mythic to begin with, and so slides naturally into song. Here are the familiar stories of Cinderella, Jack and the Beanstalk and Little Red Ridinghood, told simultaneously with their destinies intertwined (a little later, Rapunzel gets added to the mix). Here, too, are the newly invented figures of a Baker and his Wife, a childless couple promised offspring -- by the Witch next door" -- if they can beg, borrow or steal a set of ingredients that happen to be the property of the other characters. Into the woods they all go, as in some new Midsummer Night's Dream, to find their fortunes and even their identities. Act One ends with all the nice peoples ambitions achieved, all the wishes granted; as the final song is to say, tying everything yet more firmly together, "wishes are children. The second act shows how those children grow up.

The giant whose partner was slain by Jack on his last trip up the beanstalk comes down to exact revenge. But if theres guilt, there are plenty of people whom Jack can share it with -- as is demonstrated in a breathless cat's cradle of a number called Your Fault, a sort of musical Gordian knot that gets savagely cut by the Witch, with a song of her own. The Witch, who in another part of the show is a helpless grieving mother, represents the way things are -- which doesn't mean that its how they should be, or that they cant be altered. The last part of the play finds the characters, or some of them, joining forces to destroy the Giant, knowing both that it's the only thing to do and that the Giant also has a case. Even the Wolf who (temporarily) devoured Red Ridinghood may not have deserved to die. As the Witch says, unanswerably, ask a wolf's mother." Such even-handedness as this is rare in the theatre. Its rarer still outside it.

The most immediate joy of the show, now as at its premiere 18 years ago, is the way in which it juggles and combines its sources. You never know who is round the next corner, or what the ramifications will be. The Bakers Wife, needing a slipper for the Witch's potion, finds it on the foot of Cinderella, in flight from the Prince's ball. She also reveals how dazzled she herself is by the Princes image ("they say that hes charming), an infatuation that will have an ironic sequel much later in the evening. The links are in the script, but also in the score. Sondheim has written shows that are richer in free-standing melody, but none in which the themes are so tightly and scintillatingly interwoven. The last batch of songs tear equally at the mind and the heart. If there is in any musical a more poignant line, set to more healing music, than sometimes people leave you/halfway through the wood," I haven't heard it.

At Stratford the show gets a production from Peter Hinton that's visually imaginative and textually lucid; it makes some connections, such as Prince Charmings farcical fear of blood, that Id never noticed before. I still prefer the pastoral setting of the first New York production; it seems to me that if you first charm an audience with traditional greenery, you can more effectively shock them when things start to go wrong. Dany Lyne's setting, in which the trees are spare and blood red, is as grim as ... well, as Grimm. (Most of us never learned as kids that Cinderella's sisters, who werent ugly, cut off bits of their feet in order to fit into the slipper. Its in the story and in the show.) Some things here, like the Giant's descent, are actually understaged. Others are extraordinary, notably Rapunzel's tower, which is co-terminous with her long, long dress, and the Wolfs last two-course meal.

Talking of swallowing, some of the costumes do just that to the actors. Susan Gilmours Witch is fatally lacking in wit, but she's hardly helped by being costumed first as some sort of green-swathed fertility symbol ("you should see my nectarines is not a funny line when she shakes her ass to show them growing on her) and later as a dominatrix. (She's supposed to be beautiful. Each to their taste, I suppose.) The Baker and his Wife are flour-faced Gothic figurines, but here the actors do break free; Mary Ellen Mahoney develops intelligence and humour, while Bruce Dow simply and breathtakingly develops. His is the character who learns and suffers most; the emotional core of this production is his last encounter with his father to whom Peter Donaldson, classical casting paying off in spades, brings unprecedented power.

Kyle Blair is a fine fresh-faced Jack, and his exultant singing of Giants in the Sky is the musical thrill of the evening. Then there are Jennifer Waisers bratty Little Red, Dayna Tekatchs Cinderella, and the princes, Thom Allison and Laird Mackintosh, whose Agony, a contest in romantic narcissism, is as always the comic peak. (I can't pause to explain why Mackintosh's theres a dwarf standing guard" is the shows most hilarious line, but trust me.) Allison also plays an excellent Wolf, producing a ringing voice from within a very literal-minded animal suit. As Cinders' late mama -- who is usually left unseen -- Barbara Dunn-Prosser stalks the stage majestically and sings to match. Bethold Carriere's musical direction is customarily impeccable, except that Your Fault isn't fast enough. Hello, Dolly!, Stratford's other musical, is a great show as musicals go; Into the Woods is a great show as anything goes.
