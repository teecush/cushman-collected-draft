Heres a typical moment from tonight's instalment of The Naked Archaeologist. Our narrator and star, Simcha Jacobovici, fully clothed, says that there is evidence all over the Middle East of the truth of the story of Sodom and Gomorrah. Cut to a scene from an old movie: a newspaper editor saying, presumably to some reporter, "I thought I told you to kill that story." Funny.

Well, no, not really.

Actually, it's bloody irritating. The use of these commentative clips is Jacobovicis trademark: He, or his researchers, must spend as much time digging in the film archives as they do digging in the desert. As comic relief, its pathetic. As a means of reassuring the viewers that they don't have to feel intimidated by all that scholarship, it's close to insulting.

Its even more so because the tale Jacobovici has to tell is interesting. Its the story behind the story of Lot in the Book of Genesis. Two stories really. Part 1 is the familiar one of Abraham's nephew taking God's advice to evacuate the Twin Cities of the Plain (nomad that he was, he must have lived in one and made side-trips to the other) and of his wife, perhaps anxious about whether or not shed packed everything, looking back and being turned into a pillar of salt. Part 2, the bit they were more reticent about teaching you in school (at least in my day -- things might well have moved on since then), tells how Lots two daughters, under the impression that the two vanished towns accounted for the whole of humanity, seduced their father so that the world might be re-populated. Backward -- or at least sheltered -- in some ways, they were definitely forward in others. It may be the earliest known argument for providing women with a balanced education.

I had never realized before that the biblical story is the only written testimony we have to the existence of either Sodom or Gomorrah. This being the case, it's hardly surprising that scholars have regarded the whole tale as a fable. The favoured interpretation, apparently, is that the incest story was the Israelites' method of dissociating themselves from the rival tribes of Ammonites and Moabites, Lots progeny (as opposed to Abrahams). And since there was no archeological evidence, either, for the cities of the plain--five in all, of which one was supposedly spared -- there the matter rested.

Until recently. Treading, one assumes, in the footsteps of other explorers, Jacobovici takes us to an old Jordanian church (cue old-time chase footage as he drives through Iraq) on whose floor is an old mosaic -- as opposed to Mosaic -- map suggesting there really were such towns, and they really had been consumed by fire, divinely ordained or otherwise. And from there to another church where an inscription has been discovered honouring Saint Lot (this must be a translation unless the early Christians had prophetic and linguistic powers), suggesting that somewhere underneath is where Lot and his relations had relations. Jacobovici even finds some pillars of salt, though, as he says, these are unlikely to include the saline remains of Mrs. Lot unless she was a midget.

Indiana Jones taught us that archeologists can be swashbucklers. Jacobovici shows that they can also be cut-ups. It pays off: When he accosts people in the street, they say, "Hey, you're the naked archeologist" -- this despite the bright orange T-shirt he wears everywhere. Even his more sober colleagues and consultants like a bit of back-chat: We're not going to look too good ourselves, 3,000 years from now, says a respected emissary from the British Museum while meditating on a mummy.

Underneath it all, Jacobovici can get quite awestruck. "To be in a Bronze Age gate, at the Dead Sea, in a candidate for Sodom and Gomorrah is amazing, just amazing," he gleams. It is. But it doesn't compensate for the relentless jokiness of a show torn between smartening up and dumbing down. - The Naked Archaeologist airs Fridays at 10 p. m. on VisionTV.
