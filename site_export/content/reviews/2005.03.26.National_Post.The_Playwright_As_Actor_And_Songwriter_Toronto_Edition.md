Gypsy opens at the Shaw Festival on May 7; Into the Woods opens at the Stratford Festival on June 3.

Sondheim makes his characters sing as if they were talking

Stephen Sondheim, composer and lyricist, celebrated his 75th birthday on Tuesday, and much of theatrical New York, both practitioners and fans, celebrated it with him. There was a 12-hour marathon concert of his songs the previous Saturday, admission free, and a formal gala on Monday, proceeds to the encouragement of new playwrights. The honours and the affection are deserved. For 35 years now -- the breakthrough show in this regard was Company in 1970 -- Sondheim has been Americas pre-eminent musical dramatist. He may well be her best living dramatist of any kind.

In a book of interviews that appeared a couple of years ago (Sondheim on Music by Mark Eden Horowitz) he himself offers the clearest description I have encountered of what he does, and the best clue as to why it works so powerfully: "I've discovered over a period of years that essentially I'm a playwright who writes with song, and that playwrights are actors.... Ill go upstairs, and Ill get back into the character of Wilson Mizner [in the show that was then called Wise Guys but eventually reached the stage as Bounce], and I'll start singing to myself. It'll take me a while to make that transition, because its been a couple of days since Ive been Wilson, but I'll get upstairs, and I'll be Wilson."

I would add that he gets to be Wilson -- or Sweeney Todd or the Baker in Into the Woods or Desiree in A Little Night Music -- in a concentrated form. Songs, even the intricate musical scenes that Sondheim often writes, are comparatively brief things. They can crystallize speech patterns -- and, through them, thought patterns and feeling patterns -- in ways unavailable to most spoken drama, whose methods are longer and messier.

Richard Rodgers (with whom Sondheim collaborated, not too happily, on the 60s musical Do I Hear a Waltz?) got close to the heart of it when he said, Steve has a curious way of making people sing as if they were talking.

People in real life dont usually express themselves in rhyme, to musical accompaniment, but the unlikeliness of the feat -- making an outrageously artificial form sound colloquial -- is a large part of its attraction.

It also, when done well, gives a feeling of inevitability. The same is true, of course, of Shakespeare -- people don't talk in blank verse but he and his contemporaries persuade you that they do, and that the beat you hear is the pulse of common speech or, in soliloquy, of a mind inventing itself. In theory, musicals should do for us what verse drama did for the Elizabethans. It doesn't often work out that way in practice, but Sondheim has come closer, more often, to making it work than anyone else.

Colloquial lyrics were nothing new in American song when Sondheim began his Broadway career in the 1950s. They had been perfected in the 30s by Cole Porter and Lorenz Hart and Oscar Hammerstein and all the other writers of the songs we now call standards. Mostly, though, they had been stand-alone things, expressing nobodys sensibilities but their authors'. Hammerstein, who was Sondheim's mentor, lacked the verbal wit of some of the others but he went beyond them in putting song at the service of character and narrative; his people, for the most part, sing what they mean. Sondheim took the next step, and nobody has yet got beyond it. He restored the wit, and not just as decoration: He uses it to point up the gap between what people say and what actually drives them. Sometimes theyre conscious deceivers, more often theyre in the grey area between self-assertion and self-doubt, giving themselves away without knowing it. Wilson Mizner in Bounce (which has yet to reach New York but is full of good songs) is both -- he's a con-man from America's Gilded Age, but his greatest victim may be himself.

Sondheims own term for this, borrowed from Methodspeak, is "subtext," which hes defined as giving the actors something to act. There isn't much of it in his lyricist-only debut in West Side Story (some way to start a career) -- there's so much action in that show that there just isnt time. But its there in Gypsy, the next one, with its whirlwind theatrical mother blinded by her own ambition. It works wonderfully, of course, for comedy, including farce.

There's a recurring character in Sondheim's work, the complacent self-glorifying male, who gives himself away with every word he utters. He is given classic status (literally) in Miles Gloriosus, the braggart soldier of A Funny Thing Happened on the Way to the Forum, whose establishing number is an impatient command: Bring Me My Bride. He reappears as the peacock Count of A Little Night Music, pluming himself on the devotion of his wife and his mistress: The song is titled In Praise of Women. He splits amoebically into the two Princes of Into the Woods, commiserating on the agony of falling sequentially in love with every fairy-tale princess in the book. "Ah well," they conclude in unison, back to my wife.

They sing, most of these guys, rhapsodic tunes. Being his own composer has expanded Sondheims dramatic resources enormously. He writes beautiful music; his melodies are astringent, which may explain why theyre so often undervalued in a world that equates bloatedness with emotion, but they soar; he also writes ironic, searching music. Often the two modes co-exist: Sweeney Todd eulogizes Pretty Women, harmonizing with the Judge who raped his wife and whose throat he plans to cut; a discontented wife in Follies uses a suave and sweeping waltz to ask her rich husband Could I Leave You?, a question less rhetorical than it may seem, considering the offhand bitterness of the subsidiary questions that go with it. ("Not to fetch your pills again / Every day at five, / Not to give those dinners for ten / Elderly men / From the UN-- / How could I survive?")

Or in Company, a trio of husbands sing Sorry-Grateful, spelling out their conflicted feelings concerning the married state. They are upfront about their doubts, and about the impossibility of resolving them, and the tune, which is sweet and plangent, blesses and absolves them. But its unsettling harmonies suggest there may be doubts even about their doubts. Everybody identifies with this; it exemplifies Alexander Pope's description of poetry as what oft was thought but ne'er so well expressed.

Company, usually regarded as quintessential Sondheim, is in fact an anomaly. People (including me) have described him as the bard of modern Manhattan, but its his only show to be set entirely in the time and place at which it was written. Follies, Assassins, Bounce are all, in different ways, American panorama. Pacific Overtures, one of my two or three favourites, is a complex conceit, deeply funny and deeply sad, about the Western powers "opening up" of Japan during the 19th century. One number, Please Hello, a multiple pastiche for a succession of diplomat-admirals making blandly outrageous demands, is so faithful to the history that it's been adopted as a teaching aid in schools.

He hasn't often originated his own projects. Hes been drawn in by collaborators: by Hal Prince, who has produced and/or directed about half his shows; or by his librettists: Arthur Laurents, James Lapine or (specializing in the political shows) John Weidman. But he has immersed himself in each subject and emerged, I imagine, slightly different from when he went in. I suspect, if I can pursue this extravagant analogy a little further, that Shakespeare worked the same way: Burbage or someone said remember that old play about King Leir [yes, that was the spelling] and his daughters? and -- after protesting "oh not that old thing again" -- he threw himself into it. I imagine, too, that if youd had a drink with Shakespeare at the Mermaid Tavern, the conversation, though interesting, would not have been on the philosophical level of Hamlet's soliloquies or Lear on Dover cliff. Sondheim in conversation is unflaggingly humorous and penetrating. His eyes light up when he pounces -- the only word -- on a new idea.

But the wisdom -- again the only word -- that comes at the end of Into the Woods is something else. It's distilled from the characters experience, and presumably the authors, but it transcends both.

And then it's on to the next. Perhaps the best birthday tribute he can be paid is that everybody who cares wants to know what that will be.
