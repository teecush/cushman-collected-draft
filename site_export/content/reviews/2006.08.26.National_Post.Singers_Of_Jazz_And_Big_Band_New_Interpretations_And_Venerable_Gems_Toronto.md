Some half-dozen years ago there was a Broadway musical titled Swing! which was, according to your level of cynicism, an authentic tribute to the jazz and big-band music of the 1930s and 40s or an attempt to cash in on what was, at the turn of the millennium, the latest retro-craze. Either way, one of its highlights was a performance of Blues in the Night by Ann Hampton Callaway, a performer previously known as a cabaret singer-pianist, one of the few in modern Manhattan to make a steady living. She has now released a CD (on Telarc) of which that song is the title number and the centrepiece.

Technically, Callaway is an awesomely proficient singer, with range and control in the Sarah Vaughan league. (She looks awesome, too; a towering brunette.) She's a songwriter as well as a singer, and one of the staples of her club act has been the construction on the premises of a brand-new song from a series of ideas submitted by the audience. Impressive the first time you hear it, this rapidly loses its appeal at subsequent encounters; once you know she can do it, you know she can do it.

A more formal number of her own, that she must have been doing at least since the '70s, when I first heard her, is The Im-Too-White- To-Sing-the-Blues Blues, in which she laments that, despite her double-barrelled surname, she is related neither to Lionel nor to Cab. The song is on the new CD, and its self-consciousness is multiplied tenfold in a setting apparently designed to present her as a big-band blues singer in the Chicago or Basie tradition; shes either protesting too much or admitting too much.

Backed by the Diva Jazz Orchestra, who are what used to be known as an all-girl band, she storms her way through a bunch of Harold Arlen tunes: Stormy Weather, When the Sun Comes Out and the title track. She can do them all right, but it all sounds like an imitation, based on a prefabricated idea of what a jazz singer should sound like, and with no particular relation to the songs themselves. She's best when she turns down both the pace and the volume and delivers a breathtaking Willow Weep for Me that touches tragedy (not just "bluesy" but certifiably blue) and, as a closer, The Glory of Love, a corny tune with a surprising habit of turning up trumps. Here she goes back to her roots as a cabaret singer, one who has brushed up against jazz and found it productive.

- - -

Callaway ranks, by current standards, as a veteran. Barbara Lea, who has been at it, mainly in New York, since the '50s is an institution. At least she should be. Her career, which got off to an excellent start, stalled when this music fell out of fashion in the 60s, and she has become a cult performer, though with none of the preciousness or extravagance that phrase usually implies. Shes nobody's diva; she is instead a singer puritanically devoted to the shape and meaning of the songs: not an obviously subtle singer like Peggy Lee or Blossom Dearie, whose quietness signals that something combustible is going on under the surface, but a subtly subtle singer.

She seems to be singing no more than the plain sense of a lyric, but then will alert you, with an unexpected emphasis, of a tiny shift in rhythm. Her new CD (available from barbaralea.com), Black Butterfly -- named after an obscure Duke Ellington song that's a strange choice of title cut for a decidedly WASP lady -- teams her, too, with a crisp and sometimes sonorous big band, led by an old collaborator, pianist and saxophonist Loren Schoenberg.

Lea, in her 70s, sometimes wavers on the long notes, but she can transfix you with a punched-out syllable, especially when the subject is loss. The albums principal fault is its almost unwavering solemnity; Lea has a delicious sense of humour, but its not much called-on here, though she can put herself into a delightful period frame, as on Ellington's Just Squeeze Me and Irving Berlin's All by Myself, both duets with Schoenberg. The triple highlights are When They Ask about You, an old song new to me (and an apparent precursor of Dont Get Around Much Anymore), the best-ever vocal of Thelonious Monks Round Midnight, recreated as a tone poem, and a barebones reclamation of an even more overdone song, Come Rain or Come Shine, which here becomes all about need. Since the death of Rosemary Clooney, Lea sings this repertoire better than anyone.

- - -

Small labels keep the voices of lesser-known singers in this area available, and also cast new light on the famous ones. Harbinger Records is one of them. They offer two volumes of Ethel Merman singing her famous songs and others, like My Funny Valentine, that you would never expect her to do in a million years. (She does it very nicely.)

The performances are drawn from private recordings, demos (including the original Gypsy) and nightclub performances, and in this altered ambience, even the warhorses come up fresh.

Susan Johnson, a lesser-known Broadway belter (The Most Happy Fella) with a more sophisticated sense of humour, has a disc in the same series, and it's a delight, especially the discovery of an unknown (to me, anyway) Cole Porter song, I've Got Somebody Waiting. And there is another devoted to the cabaret doyenne Mabel Mercer, including a few songs she never recorded commercially.

My only disappointment in the Harbinger catalogue is the disc I anticipated most, The Land Where the Good Songs Go, dedicated to the Broadway lyrics of P.G. Wodehouse. Too many of these wonderfully conversational songs are weighed down with portentousness and stripped of sparkle. There is some nice singing, though, by classical singer turned nightclub thrush Sylvia McNair, and the point is conclusively made that Wodehouse, God's Englishman, was also America's first great lyricist.
