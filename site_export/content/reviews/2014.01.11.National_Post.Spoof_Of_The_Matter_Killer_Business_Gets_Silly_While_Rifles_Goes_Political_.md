The Next Stage Theatre Festival, which began as a way of giving a longer life to shows that had been successful at the Fringe, has grown into a showcase of its own: one with a more rigorous selection process, or at least with fewer shows. I caught two of the more logistically ambitious productions at Fringe Mark Two on opening day this week.

Rifles is an adaptation by Nicolas Billon of Senora Carrars Rifles, a lesser-known play by Bertolt Brecht set in the Spanish Civil War. Its central figure is a widow in a fishing village whose sympathies are with the Republicans but who is determined, having lost her husband to the fighting, to keep her two sons out of it. Not having intimate knowledge of the original, I cannot say how much of the present script is Billon and how much is Brecht; it's a safe assumption, though, that in Brecht's text the soldier whose wounds Mrs. Carrar, as shes called here, solicitously dresses was not an English member of the International Brigade by the name of Eric Blair, later to be better known as George Orwell. He doesnt say much but he observes; and at one point, when we're told about a female teacher who fought against Franco for the freedom to continue saying that two and two make four, we see him noting it down for when 1984 rolls around. Other than that, it's hard to see what his presence adds to anything.

The plays political standpoint is unwavering: The Republicans are the good guys (and gals). Its moral compass is, at least until the end, harder to read and hence more interesting. The late Mr. Carrar left behind him a set of rifles; his firebrand brother Pedro has come to reclaim them for the cause, and also to recruit his two nephews: Juan, whos out in his boat, and Jose, who very much wants to join up. The senora is determined to stick to her guns, and to her sons. We sympathize with her, even while acknowledging the futility of her belief that war has nothing to do with the poor; as Pedro observes, the poor end up paying for it. Their opposing arguments are fairly but passionately thrashed out; the village priest is allowed to make a decent if doomed case for non-violence. This being Brecht, it's a foregone conclusion that Mrs. Carrar will donate her rifles in the end; as to how and why it will happen, we're kept guessing.

Michael Wheelers production (for Praxis Theatre) has the right hard practical aspect, perhaps best symbolized on Erin Gerofskys set by the peasant fireplace in which Senora Carrar bakes her bread. She herself is given a performance of matching granite, streaked with unshed tears, by a close-cropped Kate Hennig. Even finer perhaps is Barbara Gordon as one of this kind of play's indispensable neighbours, judicious but not judgmental, mourning but not regretting the death of her daughter the teacher. Not all the acting manages to keep the proper Brechtian cool (my vulgar translation of the alienation effect): as Pedro Cyrus Lane, though certainly strong, is inclined to have his foot on the gas when it should be on the brake, while some others are simply shrill. What should be the play's most shocking moment is shuffled through, in both staging and performance. Araya Mengesha is suitably fresh and eager as young Jose; Wade Bogert-O'Brien, recent ornament of the Shaw Festival, is his older brother, a role whose main requirement is stillness.

Killer Business (a possible alternate title for Rifles) comes with the all-too-familiar sub-head The Musical. Itsalso supposedly a thriller and it shows, like some glossier shows before it, that the two genres don't mix. It's hard to get caught up in the quest for the killer when the suspects keep holding up the investigation with song. Admittedly, the show doesnt seem to be asking us to be involved, but it isnt giving us much else to hold on to either. It's all-purpose, sprayedon spoof. It supposedly happens during the actual performance of a musical; suspicions are first alerted when a stage-weight falls from above, narrowly missing one of the cast. The identical plot device was used in Rodgers and Hammerstein's backstage musical Me and Juliet; whether its recurrence here counts as homage, plagiarism or accident I couldnt say.

Talking of musical echoes: The producer of the musical-within-the-musical is a short squat Italian-American named Tony, who addresses his late mama in song like his namesake in The Most Happy Fella. It turns out, though, that hes thinking more of Barbra Streisand in Yentl, because what he's always wanted to be, because you need it for success in showbiz, is Jewish. He sings the word with the accent on the second syllable, and compounds the offence by giving the same treatment at the very end of the song to the word Hebrew; though the line would make as much sense, and much better sound, if the punchwords were simply "a Jew." Even by current lyric-writing standards this is unforgivable. That's apart from the fact that the joke, such as it is, has already been made in Spamalot.

Tony is a poverty-row impresario who uses cardboard cut-out chorus girls because he cant afford real ones. The show hes presenting sounds like it was written in the 1920s, though there's no suggestion that it's either a parody or a revival and the outer setting seems to be contemporary. After that one number, Tonys would-be Jewishness is never mentioned again; which may be a blessing, considering the relentlessness of the shows other jests about being fat, being gay or being a dick. The last category is occupied by the evening's narrator-figure, a Marlovian private eye played by David Keeley, conspicuously the most experienced performer on stage, in an echo of his role in Urinetown.

For better or worse, most of the songs are drowned out by the piano accompaniment; one singer, Matthew G. Brown, does get to sing a few bars a cappella and he sounds good. He plays a lighting technician of a philosophical bent. There a few other nice ideas, including a couple of gabby, educated flesh-and-blood chorines, an overworked but everwilling allpurpose assistant (Julia Juhas) whose fate includes not getting a solo number, and a bit of funny choreography. Overall, though the show might best be described, in keeping with Tony's ethnic aspirations, as a fleet of ships that sailed already.
