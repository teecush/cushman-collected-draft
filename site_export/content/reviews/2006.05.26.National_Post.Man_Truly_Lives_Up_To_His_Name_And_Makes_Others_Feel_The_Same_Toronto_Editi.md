CRINGEWORTHY

Theatre Passe Muraille

- - -

Things certainly seem to be looking up at the end of the season. Cringeworthy apparently came into existence because Alex Poch- Goldin, playwright and actor, had thought up a wonderful name for a character and needed a play to go with it. This isn't the generally approved method of writing plays, but it's probably as good as any other. The result certainly justi-fies it. Cringeworthy is more than praiseworthy. It is very nearly raveworthy.

Mr. Cringeworthy lives, at least during his onstage hours, in a basement office in London in 1912. (An exceptionally cold winter, if the play's to be trusted.) He is a serious man of business, as is demonstrated by the presence, on Joanne Dente's evocative set, of two separate desks: A large flat one, to be worked at, and a roll- top, to make the back wall look good. He is in the moneylending dodge, with opium-trafficking and a little procuring on the side. He also subscribes to charities, and has testimonials on his walls to prove it. Scrooge might have been like this if he'd done some backsliding and discovered how to make his conversion pay.

Into Cringeworthy's web stumbles a hapless Italian named Luigi Tarantella. His brother in the old country has run up a debt he has no means or intention of repaying, offering Luigi as security. After offering a brisk run-down of all the nasty things that can happen to defaulting debtors, Mr. C. takes pity on Luigi, who is starving, and offers to ease his pain by bringing his wife, Angelina, over from Naples, all expenses paid.

We can guess that Cringeworthy intends to put Signora Tarantella on the game, and so he does. But he proceeds by easy stages, first sending her out to have innocent suppers with rich gentlemen, then employing her on drug drop-offs.

There are hints that Cringeworthy gets at least half his kicks from the power he wields over the couple, though personally I think he's mainly in it for the money. (He calculates interest by the quarter-hour, if not the minute.) But his crooked benevolence undoubtedly has an effect on their married life. Luigi claims to have found a job, though more likely he survives by stealing food. Angelina finds him out and comes to despise him and, very likely, hate herself.

Cringeworthy, meanwhile, has his own problems, chiefly venereal. He is writing an epic poem about his own disintegration, composed entirely in limerick form. It's very picturesque, though some of its rhymes are suspect. I'm prepared, though, to accept that as a reflection on the character rather than on his author's skills.

In fact, Poch-Goldin's ear, both as author and as actor of his own protagonist, is almost, though not quite, flawless. He relishes both the character and his background, and they take shape together. A mass of Victorian and Edwardian details, gleaned from other plays and other books, fuse into an individual creation. Literature becomes life, a shared fantasy relished on both sides of the house that first feels like history, and then like a premonitory mirror of our own time.

We believe in Cringeworthy's rise from pauperized slavery in a northern pencil factory, in his fanatically exact exactions, in his taste for sweet baked goods with his tea, in his ambiguous musings on a freer future, in his obsessive twitting of his victims' eating habits and their inability to indulge them (the word "linguini" really gets him off), and in the havoc he obsessively wreaks on their personalities and on his own.

The only flaw in the play, and in Chris Abraham's otherwise impeccable production, is that after a riveting half-hour they start to seem drawn out.

Short though the play is (85 minutes, going on 90), it begins to feel long. In its second half the scene keeps shifting from Cringeworthy's den to the Tarantellas' hotel room and back again, and a metronomic predictability sets in.

Both apartments, incidentally, are visible throughout, with no discernible gap; leaving me to imagine during the first part of the play that Cringeworthy was so conscientious, or just so cheap, that he slept in the shop.

Paul Fauteux and Tara Nicodemo are brilliant as the Tarantellas forced to dance to Cringeworthy's tune, he endearingly pitiable -- before selfishness and resentment set in and leave him merely pitiful -- and she increasing her stature as she loses her innocence.

They also, for extended periods, speak Italian like natives, which neither of them are.

This may be the best three-hander to hit one of our stages since The Drawer Boy; it's certainly (with the possible exception of Poch- Goldin's ear- lier This Hotel) the first time since then that this particular space has been so alive with the sounds of discovery and surprise.
