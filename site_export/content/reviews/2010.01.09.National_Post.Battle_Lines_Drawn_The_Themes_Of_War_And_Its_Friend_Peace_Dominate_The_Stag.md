War and peace. Theres nothing novel about those as dramatic subjects, nor is there much new about the fact that, of the two, war provides the better copy. The first theatrical quarter of 2010 certainly looks set to confirm that. War is topical, of course. But then it always is.

Soulpepper is in an especially bellicose mood, with two productions -- both musicals of a kind -- set in that recurring theatrical reference-point for all armed conflicts, the catastrophe of 1914-18. (Think, for a start, of all the Shakespeare productions that have been set there.) Eric Peterson returns as the Canadian air-ace in Billy Bishop Goes to War (Jan. 22 to Feb. 27), a showpiece that he's been performing for upwards of 30 years now, and that seemed funnier and more affecting than ever when he revived it last year.

This solo-focused piece is paired with the panorama of Oh What a Lovely War (Mar. 11 to April 10), in which the history of the First World War is relayed through skits, statistics and, most memorably, music-hall songs. The show was the creation, in 1963, of Theatre Workshop, the aesthetically and politically radical company that operated mainly out of the Theatre Royal in Stratford, East London -- itself a house with the atmosphere of a converted music hall. It was, to choose my words carefully, the most powerful emotional experience I have ever had in a theatre. The juxtapostion of the cheery, increasingly sardonic music with the brute facts of the carnage, enumerated in the neon equivalent of tickertape, was overwhelming. The comic effect was slightly diluted when the show moved to the West End, but the sadness and the anger remained. (The movie version diluted everything.)

An acknowledged if peculiar classic, Lovely War has had few major revivals, perhaps because it was so much the creation of its original director, Joan Littlewood, and her cherished band of off-beat performers. Littlewood is said to have denied Britain's National Theatre permission to do the show on the grounds that they hadnt yet earned the right to do it. Albert Schultzs revival, with Marek Norman as musical director, should at least have the ensemble basics; two Soulpepper stalwarts, Oliver Dennis and Michael Hanrahan, appear in a cast otherwise recruited from the company's academy, whose members have a disconcerting habit of being gifted musicians as well as actors.

The war play of all 20th-century war plays, though, must be Bertolt Brecht's Mother Courage, another play associated with an ensemble (Brechts own Berliner one, which mounted it after the Second World War though it had been written during) and one that certainly influenced Theatre Workshop, which had staged it with Littlewood herself playing the leading role -- not, by all accounts, very well. Brechts plays, which used to be inescapable, have fallen out of fashion; the only ones that get done in Canada are -- for obvious reasons -- the musicals, The Threepenny Opera and Happy End. But even if the play has been seldom seen, Mother Courage herself, the camp-follower blindsided by her devotion to war and its profits, has remained an archetypal figure. The English company at the Canadian Arts Centre gives Ottawa audiences a chance to renew her acquaintance (Jan. 12 to 30) in, appetizingly, a new adaptation and production by Peter Hinton, the company's artistic director, that seems unlikely to be tethered to Brechtian tradition or theory. For one thing, it features music by Kurt Weill, which Brecht's own productions certainly never did. It features the NACs newly recruited 40th Anniversary English Theatre Acting Company, which one hopes will be able to stay together. Tanja Jacobs plays the lead, and her colleagues include at least two actors too long absent from our stages, Geordie Johnson and Waneta Storms. Also noteworthy: The assistant director is Stephen Ouimette (Scrooge in the NACs Christmas Carol), who, apart from being one of our best actors, directed Stratford's fine Timon of Athens a few years ago.

As for peace, or at least virtue: Michael Healey's new play (at the Tarragon till Feb. 7, then at the Citadel, Edmonton, Feb. 20 to March 16) is called Courageous, and -- following on his generous Generous -- its the second in what promises to be a cycle of plays named after hopeful adjectives. And in fact, though no one would call Healey a Pollyanna, his plays do tend to be more hopeful than most; the phenomenal success of The Drawer Boy is due in part at least to the human bond it celebrates. The new play deals, says the Toronto press release, with gay marriage, religious freedom, and liberal [it seems to be a small l"] immigration policy" all stemming from a chance meeting in City Hall. The Edmonton release, more broadly philosophical, says it deals with the gulf between "perfect ideals and imperfect humans," which is a good working definition of comedy. Its always about frailty. Healey breaks with his previous custom by acting in the play; he continues his custom of entrusting each of his plays to a different director. This time, it's Richard Rose.

Courageous exemplifies another, possibly less momentous trend: plays with oracular one-word titles. The Tarragon's other upcoming shows are Hush (in which, apparently, a thrilling mystery takes us to the core of our consciousness) by Rosa Laborde, author of Leo (Extra Space, Feb. 9 to March 21); and Communion, written and directed by Daniel MacIvor (Feb. 23 to April 4). The two have starting points that sound oddly similar, or at least female characters who do; in Hush, "Lily wakes up screaming, again," while in Communion, Lida has a secret; when her psychotherapist does the unthinkable and offers concrete advice (good one) "Lida acts on it."

Opera, of course, has always gone in for one-word titles though, since its probably the ultimate star-vehicle form, the words generally denote people rather than abstractions. The Canadian Opera Company is offering two favourites: Carmen (Jan. 27 to Feb. 27) and Otello (Feb. 3 to Feb. 28). Less familiar titles ( Maria Stuarda, Idomeneo) are held in reserve till later in the season.

One other much-anticipated opening is Cloud 9 (Panasonic, Jan. 19 to Feb. 21), Mirvish Productions annual venture into comparatively high culture. Like Oh What a Lovely War, this is a British play, honoured as a modern classic more in the breach than the observance. (It probably made more of an initial impact in New York than it did in London.) It's the Caryl Churchill play that came before Top Girls, and it anticipates that play in its fracturing of time and in its gender bending; Act 1 is set in Victoria's England, or in its African colonies, Act 2 in Margaret Thatcher's, and the more things change ... well, you get the idea. Alisa Palmer directs, following her successful Top Girls. Her cast bears witness to the fact that the winter, in between festivals, is the best time to cast a top-level Toronto play; everyone's available. Those assembled here include Evan Buliung, Ben Carlson, Juan Chioran and Blair Williams. One of them, if memory serves, gets to play a little girl, complete with skipping rope. The actor who did it originally, Antony Sher, got a knighthood.
