THEATRE REVIEW GUYS AND DOLLS

Saturday, June 05, 1993

ROBERT CUSHMAN

Toronto ONT -- WRITTEN BY FRANK LOESSER, JO SWERLING AND ABE BURROWS Directed by Jerry Zaks Starring Lorna Luft, Lewis J. Stadlen, Richard Muenz and Patricia Ben Peterson At the Royal Alexandra Theatre Rating: Damon Runyon it aint. But Guys and Dolls is worth a look, especially if you never see the show before Sit down, sit down, sit down... BY ROBERT CUSHMAN Special to The Globe and Mail Toronto THERE are many citizens who believe that Guys and Dolls is the best musical comedy that is ever written, and personally I do not disagree. In fact, I hold this opinion since long before I ever see the show, as I grow up listening to the original cast recording, and in fact I play this disc so often that I wear it out and have to buy a replacement, although such extravagance is by no means my custom.

It is common knowledge that the guys and dolls in question are first dreamed up in the 1920s and 1930s by Damon Runyon, who is such a scribe as hates and despises the use of the past tense. This Runyon is in his day the second highest paid reporter in the United States of America, while his short stories are widely held to be the essence of Broadway. Indeed it is a wonder that they are not turned into a musical until 1950, as they are already the source of many movies. On the other hand this procrastination is maybe a blessing, as if the show is done sooner it may be done by guys other than those who actually do it, and the chances of them doing it anything like as well are very slim indeed.

The book of Guys and Dolls is credited to Jo Swerling, who does not actually write a word of it but has a good lawyer, and to Abe Burrows who is called in at the last minute and winds up writing a masterpiece. He tells two love stories. One concerns Sky Masterson, who is known across the country as a most intrepid gambler, and Miss Sarah Brown, who is a sergeant in what is plainly the Salvation Army but is here called the Save-A-Soul Mission.

The other is between Nathan Detroit, who operates a floating crap game - which is to say a game that changes its location every night so as to baffle the gendarmes - and Miss Adelaide, a nightclub chanteuse who is Nathan's fiancee of many years' standing, although to hear her tell it standing is by no means her usual position. She gets to sing a song about this situation, in the course of which she says that she is getting a kind of a name for herself and the name aint his, which is maybe the funniest line that any lyricist ever dreams up for a musical.

Anyway, to make a long story short - which of course is the precise opposite of what the musical does, though this is by no means meant as a criticism - the whole action turns on the result of a crap game, and the construction is nothing less than classical. It is also very hilarious in its dialogue, and though there are many who attribute this to Runyon, it is really down to Burrows who here does nothing less than create his own style.

What he does take from Runyon is an atmosphere and characters, and especially their names. The guys of Guys and Dolls are called things like Benny Southstreet and Liver Lips Louie and Society Max and Honest Ed, though the last-named is not on the stage but in the auditorium. Ed makes a fortune in the retail dodge and subsequently in the restaurant business, and he is a well-loved figure and a cultural force, and on the first night his pin-striped suit and natty demeanour will certainly earn him a part in the show in the unlikely event that he should want one.

The revival he brings in originates two years ago in New York where it wins every award, except for one or two that are left over to prevent the competition from killing themselves. It is a colourful affair, to say the least, and the red decor that Tony Walton dreams up for the nightclub - which is called the Hot Box - is as deep as Hell or maybe deeper. Nor will you ever see a more spectacular sewer. It is possible, though, that the emphatic style amounts to overkill, and that the striking stripes affected by the chorus might look better in a zoo. Lieutenant Brannigan of the local police force wears a yellow raincoat like Dick Tracy, but he is meant to be an overworked cop, not a dashing private eye. Some guy in the audience is heard muttering about a production mounted by the National Theatre in London, Europe, that he much prefers, and though he himself is nothing but an Englishman he may have a point. As regards dancing and orchestrations the limey version cannot compare, but it radiates love of the material, and also trust, and every character in it is truly and thoroughly acted.

In this one, many lines are overstressed or mistimed, others are murdered by having the actors laugh themselves, which is unforgivable, and the minor people tend to be either caricatures or nonentities. Nicely- Nicely Johnson, for example, is deprived of his greed and his girth and other endearing qualities, so when he comes to sing Sit Down Youre Rockin' the Boat, he has no special claim on our affections; and the number, which usually raises the roof, will leave Honest Ed with not a cent to pay in ceiling repairs.

Nathan Detroit is played by Lewis J. Stadlen, who spends much of his previous career impersonating Groucho Marx, which turns out to be a useful accomplishment. He sings Sue Me better than I ever hear it done before. Miss Lorna Luft, his Adelaide, is willing but loud. I like the best Patricia Ben Peterson's Sarah, who acts as good as she looks, and sings even more so.

I prefer Jerry Zakss direction of Anything Goes to his work here, but I do not expect anyone to agree with me. Nor would I dissuade anyone from buying tickets, especially if they never see the show before. After all, the score is intact, with Frank Loessers comedy lyrics the best anyone ever writes, while to hear his music travel from the wistful romance of I'll Know or More I Cannot Wish You to the stage-grabbing panache of the title-song or Luck Be a Lady is to experience a perfect marriage of style and substance. It is certainly true that they do not write them like that any more. It is also true that except in this one instance - and for the verb that I am about to employ may Damon Runyon forgive me - they never did.

Guys and Dolls continues at the Royal Alexandra Theatre until July 17.
