ASHES TO ASHES

Gramercy Theater, New York

Forty years after his debut, Harold Pinter remains the best living English playwright. I don't mean that he necessarily writes the best plays -- canon for canon, I get more out of Tom Stoppard -- but he has the most natural talent. He is in perfect command of his skills. He can write words and he can write scenes: His sentences, silky and precise, resonate individually and they set up instant tensions between the people speaking them. He is famous for telling us less about his characters than we want to know, but even when the surroundings are obscure, the moment-to-moment relationships -- the balances of power -- are needle-sharp.

After a few years of silence, Pinter has recently returned to the theatre with a series of miniatures. The latest, Ashes to Ashes, clocking in at 48 minutes, has just opened off-Broadway two years after its premiere in London. It brings together many of his previous preoccupations. He started out as the pre-eminent playwright of bullying, depicting struggles between men intent on gaining control of their space. Later on, he added women to the mix, and began to concentrate on the dynamics of sex. Most recently he has dealt with political oppression, though with hindsight it seems obvious that this Jewish playwright, growing up in the East End of London during the Second World War, must have had this in mind all along.

Ashes to Ashes is set in a living room, reduced by its designer Tony Walton to the elegant essentials of two chairs, a picture window and a drinks trolley. The chairs are occupied by an academic and his wife. As is usually the case with Pinter, the woman, Rebecca, seems to have a richer interior life than the man, Devlin, and he is badgering her about it. She recalls a man with his hands around her throat; this could be love-play or an interrogation or both.

Later she conjures up a persistent nightmare image: women lined up on a railway platform, soldiers tearing their babies from their arms. She speaks about this from the viewpoint first of a spectator, then of a victim. But she herself is childless. On another level she talks about her sister, who does have a family, with a kind of chilly concern.

Rebecca seems to have internalized some of the worst traumas of the century, and used them to fill, to bursting, the cracks in her existence. Her husband, meanwhile, is jealously concerned with bullying her back into his own frame of reference. Pinter's ambiguities dont guarantee profundity, in fact they can be irritating, but here he has compressed a rich amount of feeling into a small space, echoing his previous plays but not slavishly recycling them. (For those in the know, this piece strikes me as Landscape meets The Lover, watched over by Old Times.) Karel Reisz, celebrated movie director now working mainly and very effectively in the theatre, keeps the text taut and the performers free.

Pinter himself started out as an actor and he has probably occasioned more great performances than any other current dramatist. To a list that stretches back to Donald Pleasence in The Caretaker, through Dorothy Tutin in Old Times, John Gielgud and Ralph Richardson in No Mans Land, and Judi Dench in A Kind of Alaska, we may now add Lindsay Duncan, the English actress best known here for her TV performances in The Rector's Wife and GBH.

A slim but commanding blond in a permanent state of beleaguered repose (in shorthand: bleached and beached), strategically crossing and uncrossing her legs in the immemorial style of the Pinter woman, she strikes calmly and unerringly at the centre of the role: She carries her pain with pride, and uses it as a weapon. Her partner is David Strathairn, an actor whose energies seem to go into creating the exterior of the English professor, smooth even to blandness. I would love to see the author play this role. These days he gives forceful and exhilarating performances that couldn't be further from what is known as "the Pinter style;" he barrelhouses through.

- Ashes to Ashes runs until May 9.
