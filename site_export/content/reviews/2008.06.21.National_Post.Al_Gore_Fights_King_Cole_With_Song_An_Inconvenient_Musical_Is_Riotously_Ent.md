So what do you do with all those suddenly outdated Toronto recycling bins and boxes? You recycle them: as stage furniture. They make up, in various configurations, the set for An Inconvenient Musical. Good luck doing anything as constructive with the new wheeled monsters that have taken their place.

An Inconvenient Musical is a riotously entertaining show that achieves the remarkable feat of mercilessly lampooning Al Gore's famous documentary while reinforcing its message. Gore himself appears as the narrator-figure, impersonated by Doug Morency with a perfectly judged air of self-satisfied self-deprecation. Having tried, he tells us, to get his point across by every other available means, he has finally settled on "the most powerful weapon on Earth:musical theatre."

The musical he presents is, like so many these days, largely a spoof of other musicals, but it avoids being irritatingly parasitic, perhaps because it actually has something on its mind. Here, as well as in some plot points, it resembles Urinetown -- less musically sophisticated, perhaps, but funnier. It isn't the kind of show that you would expect to have an intermission (it started out last year on the Toronto fringe where small is necessary), but it justifies the indulgence with a first-act closer that's terrific and a second-half opener that's sublime. The former is a bombastically prophetic number in Les Mis style celebrating The Day After Tomorrow ("the day after the day after today"). The latter is a very close parody of the Seasons of Love song that kicks off the second half of Rent, renamed Seasons We Love and performed by the cast -- all eight of them -- in mufflers and mittens: odd garb in which to complain about global warming but this is Toronto, a climate in which, as they complain with some bitterness, it can be hard to keep the faith.

The Rummoli Brothers wrote the script and collaborated with Waylen Miki on the score. The story's hero, played by Cyrus Lane with an impressive combination of square jaw and vibrant voice, is Gordon Green, a Middle American lad, whose dad has a job with an energy company and dies when working on a top-secret device called the Samoflange. (Gord loses his mom too. And his dog. And his guitar.) Following his nose and connecting the dots, Gordon arrives at the HQ of his father's former employer, mega-tycoon Cole Powers who wants to re-convert the world to, guess what, coal power, and has developed the Samoflange to bring this about, never mind that it kills everyone who comes within a few feet of it. (So some environmentalists complain about overpopulation. This'll show 'em.) Mr. Powers has also bought the state of Wyoming for his testing ground (on Craigslist for $640, no pets).

He has a slavering supporting trio of lesser-mega tycoons with military uniforms and fluctuating Dr. Strangelove accents. General Motors, General Electric and General Store, which I think runs Wal-Mart. Others of the same rank are mentioned but do not appear, including General Public "who never goes to the theatre unless he knows someone in the cast." In his fight against this dastardly conglomerate, Gordon falls in with a vegan cafe owner and cabaret artiste and her friends, most of whom have been drastically affected by pollutants. One of them claims to have mutated into C3P0 from Star Wars, though he's at least as reminiscent of the Tin Man from The Wizard of Oz. Whatever, he's played by Matt Baram with divine gentleness and tick-tock timing. The gang spearhead an anti-Cole resistance movement whose acronym is so crowded with consonants as to be unpronounceable by all but the exceptionally gifted, most of whom seem to be in the show. Gordon tries to write a protest song, but finds that neither folk, heavy metal, nor that freedom-fighters' favourite, doowop ("that's all the existing musical genres"), will serve his turn. Finally, though, he does come up with a lucky number. And that is where the plot really thickens.

One of the Rummolis, under what I take to be his real name of Kurt Firla, designed the production's numerous slide-shows and videos, which make great use of pie-charts and are highly accomplished, both as satire and in their own right. One of their figures looks like Ned Flanders, and the show as a whole recalls The Simpsons in the unflagging and unpredictable way in which it keeps its jokes coming -- and in its combination of comic licence and civic responsibility.

There are a couple of minor acting disappointments: Lee Smart's King Cole has a highly twirlable villain's moustache, but not much vocal clarity behind it. Since his role consists largely of malapropisms, that's a handicap; it's hard to understand what he isn't saying. Ayumi Iizuki vacillates uncertainly between heroine and villainess, though admittedly so does her role. She does get to sing a song about being Vice President, of Powers' Enward company, which Morency's Gore indignantly interrupts and then takes over, on the grounds that VP is his job, or at least his identity. David Suzuki also sings, a rousing anthem about non-passive resistance, in which he's unexpectedly and irresistibly joined by two bushes-full of small furry animals. He's played by Chris Gibbs. Aurora Browne and Sandy Jobin-Bevans complete the cast and are both very good. Nearly all the performers are Second City alumni and, much as I like revue, this musical shows them off better than almost any show I can remember at their home base. This show will burn up a lot of fossil-fuel before it's done. It's going a long way.

- An Inconvenient Musical runs through June 29 at the Factory Studio Theatre; 416-504-9971.
