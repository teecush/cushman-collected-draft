OUR CLASS

Berkeley Street Theatre, Toronto

There are 10 actors in the cast of Studio 180's Our Class, and the play begins with them all sitting in rows, as if in a schoolroom, making grotesque faces and a lot of noise. The year is 1925, and the characters are young children, growing up together in a village in Poland. Five of them are Polish; the other five are Polish, too, but as history progresses -and the play takes us practically up to the present day -their national identity, along in many cases with their lives, gets stripped from them.

This exceptionally powerful and also remarkably thoughtful play has already been seen in England but not in Poland, though its the work of a Polish playwright, Tadeusz Slobodzianek. (The translation is by Ryan Craig.) Its based on an atrocity that took place in the village of Jedwabne in 1941; maybe 1,000 Jews were herded into a barn by their Christian compatriots and burned to death. The precise number of victims is in dispute, a point the play acknowledges in passing, but even if it were only" a few hundred, and "only some local Poles participated (as claimed last week in a stomach-turning press release by the Canadian Polish Congress) it would still be a major crime. And though it wouldn't have happened if there hadn't been a Nazi occupation, we are still faced with the recurring spectacle of neighbours turning on neighbours or merely turning away.

The villages name isnt actually mentioned, which normally would prejudice me against the play (repeat after me: You can only reach the universal through the specific") but here seems justified. The national and historical colouring is detailed enough, and the characters, though thoroughly individualized, aren't documented people. They are figures in a pattern, or maybe ingredients in a crucible: boys and girls, later men and women who, whatever they do to one another or suffer from one another, remain linked throughout their lives and even beyond.

The play's first act shows childhood unity fractured by social and sexual rivalries and jealousies, by the toxic blend of religion and nationalism, and most of all by the succeeding traumas of Russian and German invasion. The march to terror, through resistance and collaboration and betrayal, seems relentlessly logical. The second act takes us past the conflagration. Two survivors get protected by, and even married to, ex-classmates, who in later years are honoured as Righteous Gentiles: One of them likes the idea, the other resents it. The marriages have turned out mixed in more senses than one. One escapee gets to return, and to investigate the massacre, proving himself as enthusiastic an interrogator as his former tormentors.

Technically, the play is a remarkable achievement. It manages to tell its entire story through its 10 representative characters, with no recourse to small-part doubling or to lengthy expository monologues. Joel Greenbergs production shows Studio 180 at its semi-documentary best: speedy, rigorous, humane. Chairs are again the only furnishings; the actors line up at the back of the stage as each passes out of life, though not out of their fellows consciousness.

Fine individual performances include: Michael Rubenfeld's expatriate rabbi, with his virtuoso delivery of two long roll calls of his relatives' names, one a shattering memorial, the other a joyous celebration; Alex Poch-Goldin, whose progression from fugitive to torturer echoes the role he played in Scorched; Mark McGrinder, awkwardly blundering in and out of a kind of heroism; Ryan Hollyman and David Beazely, symbiotically linked as killer and victim; and Jonathan Goad, whose face in the opening tableau is the most hideously contorted of all, prefiguring the mental contortions he will later execute in order to feel good about himself.

Apart from Amy Rutherfords touching sacrificial offering, the women in Our Class are mostly outclassed by the men; Kimwun Perehinec is shrill and Jessica Greenberg shriller. Not everybody in this play dies a violent death, but disappointment, disengagement or just the cruel infirmities of old age await the others. The plays ultimate theme, whether or not it knows it, may simply be mortality. It's sweeping enough and sympathetic enough to be up to the task.
