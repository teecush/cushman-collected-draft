Danny Boyle leaped in the air a couple of times when receiving his best director Oscar for Slumdog Millionaire. He then explained, nice Anglo-Irish boy that he is, that he'd once promised his children that, if he ever got an Oscar, he'd respond like Tigger. I thought that was endearing, especially when he added that his kids were now too old to remember that he'd said it.

Less endearing was his comment that he didn't know how the show looked to the folks at home, but from where he sat in the Kodak Theater, it was wonderful. Oh Danny Boyle, the pipes, the pipes are calling; and what they're saying, in their mournful Celtic way, is that for all us underprivileged gawpers, the show looked terrible.

Some of its awfulness was common to all awards shows. Some seemed to have been dreamed up specially, especially the weird, supposedly arty camera angles from which they shot the on-set TV monitors on which the customary montages were screened. These were mostly shot from high up and lopsided, so that it was impossible to see just what was being celebrated.

Even the annual roll-call of the recently deceased wasn't spared. I always look forward to the morgue montage -- it's genuinely informative -- but this year they really managed to cheapen it.

What kind of a crazy idea was it to intersperse the montage of scenes from the five best picture nominees with scenes from movies past to which they bore some faint resemblance? These all flashed by so fast, that only when something turned up in black-and-white could we get any purchase on which era it belonged to. Not to mention that most of the supposed connections were laughable. "Mad as hell and not gonna take it any more" doesn't bear much resemblance to the struggles of Harvey Milk. Hollywood's compulsive referencing of its own past may indicate a proper piety; it's more likely to be born of desperation.

If it isn't montages, it's medleys. We had one of the nominated songs (with dances) and another of the nominated incidental music (orchestra on stage); and it was impossible to tell the individual items apart. Though maybe it would have been impossible anyway.

Hugh Jackman, hosting, told us that musicals are back, and proceeded, with assistance from Beyonce and numerous others more anonymous, to celebrate them in a shapeless parade of intertwined snippets that began with Top Hat, White Tie and Tails, and then tried to reflect its glory onto a trip through the Dark Ages. If musicals being back means Mamma Mia!, better they should stay away.

Why not just let Jackman do a number? It's presumably what he was there for. I spent the latter part of the evening eating the kind words I wrote about him yesterday on the strength of the first hour. He isn't a comic, and he isn't an emcee. He looked like a fall guy.

One nice innovation: the acting awards were each announced by a team of five former winners saluting the current nominees, one apiece and face virtually to face. Some of them sounded as if they might actually have written their own speeches: Whoopi Goldberg, for example, commiserated with Amy Adams ( Doubt) on the difficulties of playing a nun (those constricting habits, plus "your love interest is always off-screen"), or Shirley MacLaine making Anne Hathaway cry by congratulating her for everything, including the singing voice that she'd earlier shown off by duetting with Jackman in the opening medley's Frost/Nixon bit.

All right, it's ridiculous to be touched by the sight of very rich people paying tribute to other very rich people; but Hathaway had a right to smile through tears, since she deserved to win for Rachel Getting Married and she must have known she hadn't a prayer. And, oh God, there was Sophia Loren, just beginning to look her age but only just.

Among the gents, Cuba Gooding gave a shout-out to Robert Downey, perhaps on behalf of all actors called Junior. Robert DeNiro solemnly inquired, "How did he do it? How for so many years id [Sean Penn] get all those jobs playing straight men?" Penn's victory was a palpable upset. Everyone expected best actor to go to Mickey Rourke, to whom I bear no malice; but the voters made the right decision, relieving us all of the necessity of crying over spilt Milk. (Other surprise: best supporting actress to Penelope Cruz. I think everyone expected it to go to Viola Davis. I certainly did. How could you miss with a single scene in which you get to upstage Meryl Streep?)

Slumdog Millionaire cleaned up and nobody, I imagine, was surprised or even displeased. It's a feel-good picture that knows, indeed revels in, its own unreality. (Of course it's contrived. That's the point.) And a lot of Mumbai kids got a trip to California. Heath Ledger's family collected his award with dignity; they didn't cry, but everybody else did. (Talk about a foregone conclusion; his living competitors must have wondered why they bothered to show up.)

Steve Martin, who must definitely have written his own stuff or at least had it tailored for him, hit the evening's high point early on by quoting something about writers living forever and then commenting "the man who wrote that is now dead." Best timing of the evening. Bring him back.
