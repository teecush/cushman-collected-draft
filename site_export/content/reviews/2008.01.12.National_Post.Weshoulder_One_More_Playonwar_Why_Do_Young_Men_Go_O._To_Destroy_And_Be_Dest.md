Canada, we are frequently told, found its national identity in the First World War. Whether or not that is true, it often seems to have found its theatrical identity there: not at the time, but in retrospect. Play after play takes us back to the years in which Canadians, with horrifying cheerfulness, shouldered the military burden of being citizens of the British Empire. Maureen Hunters Wild Mouth is the latest to arrive bearing the revelation that war is hell and that that war was especially so.

In some ways, the play takes the historical issue back to its roots. Its set on a Saskatchewan farm, on which both the farmer and his wife are English immigrants. They have had one son killed in the Somme; another is over there now; and the youngest is raring to go. They are visited by the farmer's sister, who has also lost a son to the war and is furiously embittered about it. Her stated aim is to understand what drives young men to destroy themselves and others. She seeks enlightenment in particular from a Lithuanian farm labourer, himself a decorated soldier who's been invalided out. After considerable goading, he obliges with a practical demonstration of what carnage is like by plunging his hand into a bucket of intestines and showering her with the blood. This is the showiest scene in the play but its too forced to be genuinely dramatic. Its the author, rather than Bohdan, the ex-soldier, who is straining for effect.

She finds a lot of them down on the farm. There is apparently a buffalo jump nearby, and the air is full of thundering hooves, along with the more immediate complaints of slaughtered livestock and refractory tractors. (Though, as often happens at the Tarragon, the atmospherics are upstaged by the sound of a train on the tracks outside the theatre, a real live noise blessedly innocent of being a metaphor for anything.) Some of the rural touches seem to be there for their own sake, a rival focus of interest fighting the play's ostensible theme. Is this a play about the prairies or a play about the trenches? Most of the characters may belong to the same family but there seems to be no urgent reason for them to be occupying the same stage.

Possible connections are left lying about unused. Take the farmer, Logan (Ian D. Clark, appropriately stalwart), and his wife, Roberta (Brenda Robins, branching fiercely out with a North Country accent and a grey wig, but too undisguisably agreeable for the suspicious termagant the play seems to have in mind). They're from the old country and have lost one child, and hazarded another, in its service, despite living thousands of miles away. Are their allegiances in any way split? Do they have unpatriotic doubts? They dont give sister Anna much of an argument or much of an agreement either. Their daughter Claire (Sarah Allen) has no links to the war theme, beyond fancying Bohdan and getting jealous when he starts taking an interest in her aunt. Her bellicose young brother, Jamie (a spirited debut performance by Simon Rainville), starts out by branding Annas pacifism as treason; later on, either he or his author grows tired of this and he settles for other forms of attention-getting: loudly reciting advertising slogans, for example. (These do have a certain period charm.) I suppose you could claim that both he and Anna are trying to make a dent in their relatives' unshakeable stoicism, which they read as complacency, but you'd have to be quite desperate to do it.

As Anna, Sarah Orenstein occasionally falls back on stonewalling histrionics but most of the time she blazes; Oliver Beckers Bohdan is rather conventionally broody. Theres one other character, an older farmhand called Aloysius, a veteran of earlier wars; he doesn't say much, and most of what he does say he mutters, but since he's acted by David Fox his mutterings are quite compelling, while his physical presence is completely so. He also plays the violin, an accomplishment that gets him the final curtain, its good for a dying fall. But the play as a whole is so low-key, despite everything thats presumed to happen in it, that a melancholic ending merely seems like more of the same.

Theplay does lookgood, or nearly: Yannik Larivee has encased it in a slatted box -- floor, walls and ceiling -- receding in deep focus and marred only by a small screen at the back, carrying miniature views of the outside world. It's a nice thought, but it makes the place resemble a primitive movie theatre in a converted barn. R.H. Thomson's production starts off well with most of the cast at dinner --an image that recurs several times --but it doesn't manage to inject much energy. Also, though we accept that actors in small theatres have to move their own furniture, they should maybe not try too hard to do it in character.
