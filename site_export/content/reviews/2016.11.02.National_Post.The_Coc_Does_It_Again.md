THEATRE REVIEW

Ariodante

The Canadian Opera Company continues to provide some of the richest theatre in Toronto.

Its production of Bellini's Norma offers glorious singing; Handels Ariodante offers equally glorious singing plus inspired and meticulous staging by the British director

Richard Jones. Its full of Shakespearean echoes.

A villain seduces a waiting-woman into impersonating her mistress on the eve of that mistress' wedding, causing the bride's father and fiance to revile her as an premarital adulteress, just as in Much Ado About Nothing. A mysterious martial champion arrives at the last moment, just as in King Lear. The wronged heroine revolts against the happy ending and walks off in protest. just as in Measure for Measure; or rather as in modern productions of Measure for Measure. But here the departure, in both senses, is fully

thought out and stimulatingly presented.

The sung text, or the surtitles, give us the traditional story, the staging gives us the new one, in bracing counterpoint. The setting has been transposed from a Macbeth-like mythical royal Scotland to a tribal, more or less modern Hebridean island, where the local customs include much ceremonial shifting of furniture and a puppetry fetish; handmanoeuvred dolls enact tabletop routines of celebration or condemnation, the last one making a brilliant contribution to the ironic ending. Meanwhile the human action has been seamless, never a slack moment in performance or design.

The stage becomes one great hypnotic moving picture.

Ariodante is in repertory through November 4 at the Four Seasons Centre
