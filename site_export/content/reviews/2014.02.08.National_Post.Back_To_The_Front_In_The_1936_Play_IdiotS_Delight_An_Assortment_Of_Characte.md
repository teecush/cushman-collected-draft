Albert Schultz, director of Idiot's Delight, has described this 1936 American play as The Time of Your Life meets You Can't Take It with You. To me it feels more like the meeting of The Time of Your Life and Casablanca. Like that famous film, it presents a cosmopolitan assortment of characters in an international watering-hole, though here the location is cold rather than hot: not a North African nightspot but an Italian hotel with ski-lodge ambitions, perched high in the Alps and commanding tantalizing views of Austria and Switzerland.

Also l ike Casablanca, Idiots Delight has a rootless American hero, surprised into a kind of commitment by the reappearance in his life of a woman he had loved and lost. Most crucially, both play and movie unfold against the background of the Second World War, though with a significant difference. Casablanca is set, historically if not geographically, in the thick of it; in Idiots Delight, we're on the brink, awaiting - nervously or fatalistically, according to temperament - a conflict that finally erupts. Robert E. Sherwood, one of the period's most successful if less remembered playwrights was writing, remember, three years before this actually happened. He got the mood prophetically right, even if he got the eventual historical details wrong. In his vision, the war starts when Italy bombs France and France retaliates. No suggestion of Germany invading Poland: indeed no mention of Poland at all, and not much of Germany: just one reference to Jews to hint at what National Socialism was actually like. Sherwood, whose extra-theatrical activities included speech writing for FDR, was a pacifist who turned when he found out about the Nazis; this play seems to have been written when that conversion was, at most, a work in progress. The Italian Fascists are here the heavies, and even they seem reluctant about it. Sherwood is mournfully arraigning self-destructive humanity in general and, if he exists, God: "Poor, dear God. Playing Idiots Delight. The game that never means anything, and never ends."

Im quoting Irene, the play's - for want of a better word -heroine: a self-described Russian adventuress whose story keeps changing and who may in fact be American; Harry, our hero, claims to have spent a night with her in Omaha when he was touring with a mindreading act, though right now he's touring Europe as frontman for a quintet of singing, dancing and (in this production) squealing blonds. Like most of their fellow guests, theyve arrived at the previously deserted hotel because their train was derailed, and want to get out before its too late. The play begins as a mobile genre-painting from those halcyon days when American plays could afford teeming casts, Depression or no Depression; their comings and goings and philosophical musings are where the Time of Your Life likeness makes itself felt, and Schultz orchestrates them here as masterfully as he did in his production of the Saroyan play. He has, again, a fine set by Lorenzo Savoini, high, wide, suggestively furnished, and mountain-viewed.

Gradually, through the bustle, the focus narrows down to Harry and Irene, roles in which Dan Chameroy and Raquel Duffy exceed themselves. He projects a kind of alert worldweariness, she a sophistication that's ambiguous, vulnerable and only slightly impaired by the tendency of her words to get lost in her accent. Their last scene reaches a high level of both mood and contact.

Harry's transformation is as decisive as that of Bogarts Rick in Casablanca, though it goes in the opposite emotional direction.

You must remember this: Ricks cafe derives much of its mystique from the playing of one song. Harry and his troupe provide the occasion for many more from American song's golden age, some of them specified by the author, some not, but all apposite. Especially haunting is Let's Face the Music and Dance, a number originally performed by Fred and Ginger on the edge of a precipice, and here employed to summon a whole world in the same situation. The production credits Sherwood with additional prophetic powers when Evan Buliung as a gentle Austrian waiter sings Ill Never Smile Again (which really does date from 1939) while clearing tables; its a charming moment in an altogether lovely performance by a man out of several of his elements.

Some of the play's actual writing is stuffy, but the actors humanize it, none more than Gregory Prest who, as an agonized French Communist, finds pain and passion in a role composed mainly of slogans. Gordon Hecht and Michaela Davies are delightful as a pair of young English honeymooners and William Webster powerfully impatient as a German doctor with an excruciating case of divided loyalties, plus a possible cure for cancer and a convoy of lab-rats. Diego Matamoros is all iron and irony as Irene's ambiguous protector, a munitions-king on whom, next to God, the play seems inclined to blame everything. Hes like Shaws Undershaft (from Major Barbara) without the intellectual muscle, and the play's ending, defying air-raids, definitely recalls that of Heartbreak House. Instead of Keep the Home Fires Burning, we have, authorially mandated, Onward Christian Soldiers: a mordant, bitter finale that Schultz's production, atoning for the sentimentality with which he spoiled Oh What a Lovely War, plays to the hilt. In all, a great rediscovery and a Soulpepper success.

Everybody knows the opening sentence of Franz Kafkas Metamorphosis, about Gregor Samsa waking up one morning to find himself changed into an insect. What follows is less known than Kafkas The Trial, but actually hits harder. Certainly it does in the stage version brought to Toronto by David Mirvish, adapted and directed by David Farr (of London) and Gisli Orn Gardarsson (of Reykjavik) and performed by a four-fifths Icelandic cast whose accents, not to mention their talents, give the text the right sense of jolting familiarity.

Josef K was betrayed by bureaucracy; Gregor is betrayed by his family. That hurts a lot more, especially when the prime rejector is his sister, the only one who initially showed sympathy. Even more poignant than his physical transformation is the fact that they can't understand a word he says, though he keeps trying. Gregor is the victim, among other things, of his inability to stop loving, and Bjorn Thors gives him a wonderfully sympathetic performance. Wonderfully acrobatic, too; he still looks like a man but he moves like an insect, hanging, gliding, clutching.

The upper half of the set, representing his bedroom, has been turned at an angle of 90 degrees, prompting physical feats and scenic images that recall Robert Lepage's Needles and Opium and, less expectedly, the opening number of Hairspray. Some of the family scenes go on too long, and analogies to Nazism are pushed too hard (Idiot's Delight in reverse), but this is a superb - well, a transformative - production. Weekend Post robert.cushman@hotmail.com

Idiot's Delight runs at the Young Centre in the Distillery District to March 14; Metamorphosis runs through March 9 at the Royal Alexandra Theatre.
