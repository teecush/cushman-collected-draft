There are five musicals contained within The Musical of Musicals: The Musical!, and they all have the same plot. Heroine, whose name is June or Juny or Jeune or Junita or Junie Faye, cannot pay her rent. Sinister landlord, variously known as Jidder or Jitter or Mr. Jitter or Phantom Jitters or Jutter, tells her she can avoid eviction by succumbing to his whims, which are mostly marital. Hero, who might be called Bill or Billy or Villy or William or Big Willy depending on ethnicity or (he would have us believe) endowment, comes to heroines rescue, sometimes. Older woman, identified as Abby or Aunt Abby or Mother Abby or Fraulein Abby or Abigail Von Starr, dispenses advice, of a highly vocal nature, from the sidelines, secure in the knowledge that the outcome will not affect her in any way whatever.

Each of these musicals of musicals (the musicals!) is written in the style of a recognizable Broadway master or team of masters. Just to put the recognition beyond doubt, each portion of pastiche is preceded by a caption that tells us not just the title of the segment but just who it is "in the style of ": a concession to ignorance that strikes me as distinctly uncool and also as useless, since anybody who can't identify the models instantaneously will get nothing out of the show at all, and probably wouldn't be attending in the first place. The piece has music by Eric Rockwell, lyrics by Joanne Bogart, and book, what there is of it, by the two of them together. Their parodies are verbally clever and musically acute, sometimes even subtle. Theyve taken a long time to reach us; the show was done in New York in 2003, and had its Toronto premiere this year in a small production at SummerWorks. I thought it was great. Now that its been promoted to the glossier or at least more commercial precincts of the Panasonic, I have, in the curmudgeonly way so typical of critics, to revise that opinion. I now think it's good.

The one reservation that I expressed, back when the living was easy and the seats cheaper, now bulks larger than before. It is that the show too often confuses satire with mere quotation. This is especially the case in the first and longest segment, the one that lays down the ground rules for all the others. It's called Corn!, and its principal target is Rodgers and Hammersteins Oklahoma!, with side glances at the rest of their canon, starting with the characters names. Heroine June gets hers repeated in triplicate, like the month that was bustin' out all over in Carousel; villainous Jidder is but a double consonant away from the no-good Jigger in the same show; Mother Abby is the Mother Abbess of The Sound of Music, though her maternity seems purely secular. Big Willy doesn't have any R&H namesakes that I can call to mind, though he compensates by combining the avocations of cowboy and fairground barker. The state of Oklahoma itself has been relocated to Kansas where, you will recall, it is very corny, especially in August. The field, Big Willy sings on the beautiful mornin of his first appearance, is all ears, which is a line to die for. Rockwells pastiche of Rodgers' tunes is very sharp, as close to the originals as anyone could come without being sued, and he and Bogart do a delightful job in re-casting Carousel's Real Nice Clambake as a serenade to clam-dip, following the idea through in precise, affectionate detail. The problem, though, with this whole R&H section is that its too affectionate. It doesnt have an attitude.

The better parts of the evening have some poisoned darts to aim at their targets. Dear Abby! takes on the world, meaning the heroines, of Jerry Herman; the diva Dollys and managing Mames and their choruses of sycophants, on and off the stage. "The audience applauds her," says the production's pianist doubling as narrator even though she hasn't done anything yet, and the ensemble proclaim their own worthlessness and hopelessness when deprived of her presence, even though she still hasnt done anything. Even Mr. Jitters succumbs to her; sent away to put a little more mascara on, he returns sounding just like Carol Channing, thereby proving that Hermans earlier big ladies were really the drag-queens of La Cage Aux Folles waiting to happen. Aunt Abby's obligatory self-doubt number is splendidly titled Did I Put Out Enough? Aspects of Juanita, dedicated to aspects of Andrew Lloyd Webber, gets even more personal, giving us Sir Phantom Jitter, a masked operatic impresario who tells his protegee that she has the voice of a whiny self-absorbed angel but nevertheless wants her to sing for him. The authors fail, though, in the impossible task of satirizing Cats.

A Little Complex, located in the Sondheim part of town, presents Rockwell with his toughest compositional challenge, one to which he impressively rises. Bogart's lyrics, too, are aptly tongue-twisting. The setting is an alienated and alienating apartment building reminiscent of Company but called The Woods; indeed the writers reference most of the Sondheim oeuvre with the notable exception of Follies, perhaps because so much of that score is already pastiche. They are very perceptive about the resemblances between Sondheims two great obsessives: Sweeney Todd, artist in murder, and Georges Seurat, artist in art.

Sondheim of course uses pastiche, not just for its own entertaining sake, but to make dramatic points. This distinguishes him not just from Rockwell and Bogart but from John Kander and Fred Ebb, who are the objects of Speakeasy, the last and most narrowly focused of the evenings travesties. (I use the word in its original non-pejorative sense.) It summons the cynicism and the funny accents of Cabaret and Chicago, which it seems to regard, probably rightly, as the only two entries in Kander and Ebb's extensive catalogue that would be readily recognizable; though they do throw in a bit of Kiss of the Spider Woman as well. By this fifth go-round, the rent plot (which of course was also the Rent plot) has begun to seem tired, indeed perfunctory. But the real subject here is Bob Fosse, the directorchoreographer whose stage and screen visions of these dedicatedly decadent musicals have proved indelible.

The choreography in this Toronto production is, as it happens, excellent. It's the work of Vinetta Strombergs, whos also the director; her miniature takeoffs of Fosse, and of Gower Champion in his Hello Dolly mode, are very witty, but her tour-de-force, and that of her corps of four, is her take on the high-horse stepping of Agnes de Milles Oklahoma! dream ballet. The performers are unglamorous but expert; to judge from the New York cast album, they're better than their American predecessors, who included the composer and lyricist themselves. This production has a discrete (and discreet) narrator-pianist, Michael Mulrooney, who carries out both his duties with schoolmasterly aplomb Mark Cassius gets the Jitters and Adrian Marchuk the Willies, while Dana Jean Phoenix does the flaming Junes and Paula Wolfson takes us on a tour of the Abby's who consistently get the best material. In Corn!, her fortissimo admonition to Follow Your Dream is, like the Climb Evry Mountain that inspired it, magnificently irrelevant to the situation at hand; in Dear Abby she makes the conflicting exhortation to Take My Advice and Live sound divinely patronizing; and in Speakeasy, as old Berlins throatiest gift to Chicago nightlife, she's Lenya and Dietrich combined as she urges the permanently impoverished Juny to Sell Your Body ("Who are you? Joan of Arc? Find yourself an easy mark.")

The best is last. To a tune reminiscent of a familiar song from A Chorus Line and to the steps of the familiar hat-tipping dance that goes with it the entire cast, pianist this time enchantingly included, bow and twirl, and tell us that they are done ("So why are you still sitting there?") It is, in many ways, the wittiest and most refreshing thing in the evening. It sent me away happy before, and it sent me away happy again.
