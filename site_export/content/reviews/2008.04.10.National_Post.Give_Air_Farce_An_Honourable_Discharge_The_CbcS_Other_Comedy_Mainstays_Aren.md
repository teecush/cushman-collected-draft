For the last 10 years I have missed The Royal Canadian Air Farce -- missed it, in fact, as often as I could. But now that I will really have to miss it, with the announcement that the shows next season will be its last, I find myself becoming nostalgic. Watching the next-to-last season's finale, which went out last Friday, reminded me that the Air Farce regulars are real comic talents, however familiar their format and bland their material.

Luba Goy, for example, may not give very good Hillary Clinton (though she can put up a passable resemblance), but she gives excellent Luba Goy. Last week allowed her a turn as herself, stiff-upper-lipping over the show's imminent demise while drowning the sorrows she affected not to feel. (Ive been unemployed before -- and this isnt a very expensive wine.) The lines may not have been all that great ("rent-a-Luba, play-my-tuba" as she contemplated hiring herself out for future gigs at lower rates), but she was. Goy, unlike many people on TV comedy shows, is an actor, which is not the same thing as an impersonator. It means that she can give herself over to the situations she's involved in.

Friday found her as a Tim Hortons customer, incensed to discover that her rim had already been rolled. It wasn't the brightest of comic ideas, but it was rooted in and played for a certain kind of reality -- a rarity in sketch-comedy shows that have increasingly become a matter of dropping names into limbo. And when Goy took another moment or few to lash out at the creepiest TV ads, she had her audience enthusiastically with her, especially when she took aim at the Pepto Bismol diarrhea spokesman (if anyone should get whacked, its him) and at that Alexander Keith talking statue that confronts me whenever I turn on my set ("still, unmovable, isolated and incapable of performing any meaningful action"). At times like these, Air Farce is performing a public service: comedy as safety valve.

Talking of taking aim, Don Fergusons Colonel Stacy is still firing off that Chicken Cannon, with a relish that seems only slightly diluted by time (and with impeccably poker-faced support from his female assistant). There is a certain atavistic attraction to the routine after all these years, and I guess it's too late to wonder whether such a bizarrely cozy form of satire is very Canadian or just very Air Farce. My favourite of last week's targets: Jim Flaherty, for telling Dalton McGuinty his provinces finances have been screwed up ever since Flaherty himself was Ontarios finance minister.

There's an obvious logic to having the colonel do what he does. I'm still not clear about Sister Bessie giving out her Bessia Awards at the start of the show. Is a nun automatically funny or -- gasp--daring? Blessings on the sister, though, for her gloss on Stephen Harpers citing of Rick Mercer as a credible commentator: "If you want credibility, watch Rick Mercer; if you want comedy, watch Question Period."

That s about as bitchy as Air Farce gets; and though it's possible that the laugh was meant to be on Parliament rather than on Mercer, I know

which I prefer to believe. The Comedy Network, which specializes in trailers calculated to scare you off the actual product, has been running -- for what seems like an eternity -- a clip of Mercer saying Today is flag day. Settle down, Stephen Harper, I said flag day. Well, you cant accuse them of false advertising. In its combination of lame joke and unctuous delivery, that line is perfect Mercer.

Settling down on Tuesday for an evening of CBC comedy (I earn my pay), I was a little dashed to find that the night's Rick Mercer Report was a repeat from February. But if they can rerun it, I can revile it.

Mercer's idea of humour seems to be to attend an event and have himself filmed there, grinning. On this show, he paid a call on Defence Research and Development Canada, got strapped into some whirling device, and mystifyingly remarked, "Its like my brothers wedding all over again." Later he went to the Canadian Auto Show, where he conducted interviews on the probing level of Entertainment Tonight.

For topicality, we had The Front Page, with snarky comments at which

theaudience laughed before the punchlines, which sounded suspicious to me, and The Rant, in which Rick talks and walks very fast while the camera shoots him at 45-degree angles. He gave us A Message from Elections Canada: Lower Your Expectations. Well, that might be worth a smile; the audience squealed.

This show turns up the laugh track, and the music, very loud. Like it has something to conceal. To be fair, Mercer can do better than this. He was an impressively crisp host on Canada's Next Great Prime Minister a few weeks ago, though Paul Martin on the judging panel had slyer comic timing.

After Mercer's frenzy, the more laid-back This Hour Has 22 Minutes came as a relief. It wasnt much funnier though. I think the best bit featured a thin girl on an exercise bike ("Ellen OHanlon, fitness enthusiast"), lamenting the privileges afforded to fat people (They get 12 square meals a day). But I'm stretching even to find that. This Hour fancies itself as more cutting-edge than Air Farce, but it seems like the same show done by younger people. (For years, I thought it was the same show.)

Air Farce has recently said that it never set out to be satire, just to be funny, but in practice it adheres to the same lame philosophy as the other shows: the belief that satire consists of mentioning a politician by name. And if you do it on CBC? Wow, you're brave.

Actually, the funniest moments on Tuesday night came during the commercial breaks. Peter Mansbridge, trailing The National, buttonholed us through the screen Mercer-style and promised to tell us "why broadcasters want more money --meaning you might pay more." But he is a broadcaster (isnt he?). An ad for the corps new Bold channel promised us Intelligent

Drama. Audacious Comedy.

So that's where they're putting it.
