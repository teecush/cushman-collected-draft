Theatrefront is a Toronto-based company that travels abroad, does workshops and brings the goods back home. Its previous production, Return, was subtitled The Sarajevo Project. Now comes Ubuntu (The Cape Town Project). Seven people are listed as creating the show, of whom one is the director and five -- two Africans, three Canadians -- make up the cast. (The seventh person, Deborah Hay, is also an actor but doesn't appear.) The impression it leaves is that the resident team thought it would be cool to collaborate with South African actors but, as so often happens with group creations, left the matter of what story they might want to tell to a prolonged afterthought.

There is an idea mooted, and it's a valid and provocative one. It's partly about the clash of cultures but more about their unity. One of the characters, a scientific academic, points out to his class -- and, more importantly, to us -- the implications of humanity having originated in Africa and of some of it having migrated, all over the globe, changing pigmentation through the millennia. So, despite passing tensions, the unprecedented array of faces on contemporary streets really represents a return to basics.

In more immediate theatrical terms, Theatrefront's hunch that black African actors would expose them to a vigorously physical style of playing that might rub beneficially, works a treat. Mbulelo Grootboom and Andile Nebulane start the play with some startlingly acrobatic vignettes of life under, and escaping from, apartheid. They have transmitted at least some of these skills to their white collaborators, who sometimes look a bit self-conscious in the mime sequences but nonetheless do very well. Early in the play, a plane from Cape Town makes a bumpy landing at Pearson. The bumpiness has more to do with spectacle than plot, but the doing is very good. After touchdown, plot and dialogue take over, and we're in dramaturgical trouble.

This is despite Grootboom and Nebulane being fine naturalistic actors, as well. They play father and son, named Philani and Jabba. The play unfolds in two time frames. Philani came to Toronto 20 years ago as a student, found work as a lab assistant and began sending money home to Jabba, then one year old. He also found love with a shy Canadian student with a consuming interest in the mating-calls of auks. The grown-up Jabba comes to Canada to find his father, his only clue a photograph showing Philani with Michael, the aforementioned academic. Michael used to wear glasses and now has apparently switched to contacts; we know where we are time-wise from how David Jansen, who plays him, puts on and takes off his specs.

The scenes of the past work better, especially a sweet love-in-the-library scene between Grootboom and, as the bird-fancier, the remarkable Michelle Monteith, who can infuse shades of meaning into the simplest lines without overloading them with "significance." Philani has a limp, explained by his having received surgical treatment back home from a revered doctor-counsellor, the Sangoma. Michael can't believe his ears when he hears this, nor can he credit Philani's insistence on the importance of tradition. As quite often happens in dramatic debating-scenes, at least one obvious argument goes unstated: That Western medicine has its traditions, too, many of which it has discarded in the light of subsequent knowledge. Anyway, this part of the story includes an inexplicable, or at least unexplained, death.

Jabba arrives to almost literally pick up the pieces; a fair amount of his story turns on intercontinental differences in burial practices. Of course, it's meant to signify more than that, but it doesn't have the backup. If you believe the publicity, the show is really about the impact on a second generation of interactions with the first. In other words, it's about the young (who also, for symmetrical reasons, meet in a library). The plan just about holds for Jabba, whom we meet early and whom Nebulane invests with an involving edge; though he, too, seems reluctant to ask obvious, if dramatically inconvenient, questions. (Odd how many conversations in plays end for that reason.) It doesn't work for his opposite number (Holly Lewis), who arrives late and is asked to shoulder a lot of unrealized angst. The plot is slender, implausible and time-consuming, climaxing in a revelation that may break new ground for half-hearted melodrama.

Which is a shame, because Daryl Cloran's production is technically excellent (it's a far more polished piece of work than Return, though with less to bite on). Lorenzo Savoini has designed a bold set, a revolving wall made out of suitcases, and even bolder lighting, and Christian Barry provides a cross-cultural sound score that starts with drums and builds from there. The show's ending, a funeral ceremony back in Africa, seems similarly planned to draw on the best of both worlds. But it requires a certain suspension of disbelief, not so much in the action as in the actors, the Canadian ones anyway.
