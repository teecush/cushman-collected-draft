Welcome to Gracepoint, Americas Last Hometown. That's how it announces itself to any visitors who care to read its billboards. Its main industry is whale-watching. Or rather, it's catering to the tourists who come to watch the whales. However, when we visit the place, its being roiled, and its reputation soiled, by what we are told is its first-ever murder case.

Gracepoint is the title, and the setting, of a new 10-part thriller serial on Fox. It has has a British twin. Or, to be less fancy about it, the show is an American re-make of the British series Broadchurch; Gracepoint has even imported Broadchurchs star, David Tennant, who repeats his role as a wild-eyed, control-freakish cop parachuted into the top detective job in this sheltered community after having mysteriously fouled up in a larger one. His English character name was Alec Hardy, which sounds almost too British detective-story to be true. His U.S. equivalent is called Emmett Carver; Emmett doesn't like his name, but then he doesn't like many things. Or many people, especially the ones hes thrown among in Gracepoint. Tennant, with his hair spilling defiantly over his eyes, plays him very well, and his accent is impeccable, at least as far as I could tell through the angry dismissive growl in which he delivers most of his lines.

Even better is Anna Gunn as Detective Annie Miller. Shes Emmett's professional partner, and neither of them enjoys the partnership. In fact, when the show starts, Anna has just come back from vacation to find that she's expected to work with the man who has taken job shed been promised. Professional resentment, however, has to go on the back-burner when, mere minutes after the opening credits, the body of a dead 12-year-old boy is discovered on the beach. The boy is the best friend of Ellies own son, and she herself is on close terms with his parents.

In fact, it seems that everybody in Gracepoint knows everybody else. And yet it's also big enough to support its own newspaper, the Gracepoint Journal, whose mission statement, dripping with retrospective irony, is "Celebrating the Everyday." It's like the Simpsons Springfield, which can transform from village to metropolis according to the needs of a particular episode.

Its also like Springfield in that everybody in it goes to church. The minister, though, is more like the priest in The Sopranos, being fairly young and twitchy, and with an obvious crush on a married parishioner, in this case the bereaved mother. He may have some more sinister secrets as well; the show certainly works overtime to make us think so. But then, of course, it has the whodunnit's vested interest in making everybody a suspect. The first episode begins with the still-unknowing father of the dead boy walking through town, greeting and being greeted by what turns out to be most of the cast.

At its primary level, the show works. I've viewed seven of the episodes and I really want to know what happens in the remaining three. (Never having seen Broadchurch, Im unspoiled.) But, given how susceptible we all are to suspense, that may not be setting the bar very high. Gracepoint hardly misses a manipulative trick. The background music is incessant, and there are more slow-motion sequences than I have seen in any other show. (Maybe the slow-mo is intended to drive home the point that Gracepoint is a sleepy town. No, probably not.).

Once again the gulf yawns wide between drama on cable and drama on the networks, even in police procedurals. Or especially in police procedurals. I wasnt a great fan of True Detective, which seemed far too enamoured of its own obscurity; still, one recognized, even while trying to make out what Matthew McConaughey was talking about, the presence of a certain depth and texture. Gracepoint seems to have ambitions in that direction - with the death of a child as its starting point, it can hardly afford not to have - and it has Tennant as the obligatory corroded cop. But the writing is very run-of-the-mill.

Maybe the British version was better; at eight episodes instead of 10 it was presumably tighter. Still, Chris Chibnall, who wrote Broadchurch, is also an executive producer on Gracepoint (and wrote the pilot), so it's presumably much the same animal. Nick Nolte turns impressively up as the town loner, the grizzled runner of an informal wildlife observation society for the town's youth; there should be sign over his shoreline shack reading Acting Done Here. He also has the shows best single line; interrogated by Tennant as to his whereabouts on the night of the crime (hes all too natural a suspect) he says that he was home reading: "Infinite Jest - you might not like it; hardly any pictures."

The acting in general is unspectacularly good, in the baseline way of most TV. Virginia Kull and Michael Pena build a believable agonized relationship as the bereaved parents, though pseudo-profound lines like "you learn to live with pain" don't do Kull any favours. I also lost count of the number of occasions on which people threatened to punish other people in unspecified ways that they would never forget. One who escapes unthreatened, though she certainly doesn't deserve to, is a lady journalist, who descends on Gracepoint from the big city, seduces (physically and otherwise) an impressive young local reporter and twists everything anybody tells her, with dire results; she also seems to have the goods on Tennant though, even seven hours in, I still dont know what they are. The smug unpleasantness of her character is obviously intended - theres an echo, given the show's origins, of the scandals over Britain's tabloids - but it's surprisingly dull to watch. Here, as in other ways, the show is in over its head.
