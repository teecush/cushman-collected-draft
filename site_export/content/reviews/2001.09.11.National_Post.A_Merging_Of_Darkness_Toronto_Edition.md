WOZZECK/WOYZECK

Bathurst Street Theatre, Toronto

So what fresh hells are these?

Georg Buchner's play Woyzeck, written in the 1830s, was the basis of Alban Berg's opera Wozzeck, first staged in 1925. Wozzeck/ Woyzeck, presented by the new and grandiosely named Theatre of Ideas, is a collage of the two works.

The disparity in the names of the two originals is due to a scribal error in reproducing one of Buchner's manuscripts, a mistake inherited by Berg. This production splits the difference by calling the principal character Woyzeck, while giving Wozzeck top billing in the title.

Buchner's play, discovered just in the early 20th century, was the last of its extraordinary author's four extant works. The others include Danton's Death, the greatest of all modern history plays, an especially impressive achievement when you consider that, at his death, Buchner was 23.

In his brief lifetime, he had passed at lightning speed from revolutionary enthusiasm to revolutionary disillusion. (A strictly radical progression, by the way. Nothing in his work suggests he would have become a conservative.)

The darkest of all his works is Woyzeck, whose protagonist is a tormented and, by all previous dramatic standards, inarticulate German soldier, at the mercy of his superiors.

The play's nightmarish quality is partly responsible for its magnetic appeal to modern directors, especially those of the Gothic twilight persuasion.

Even more attractive is the fact that it exists in four separate drafts, all of them different and none of them complete. We don't even know how the play was meant to end. For directors with delusions of grandeur, this degree of carte blanche has proved irresistible.

Berg's opera necessarily imposes a shape on the piece, though it comes at a price. Wozzeck/Woyzeck pursues an insight expressed 40 years ago by George Steiner in The Death of Tragedy. Steiner wrote that, though Berg's opera was superb as both music and drama, it distorted the play's central device by giving the hero an eloquent singing voice.

In the play, he just blushes and stammers, the impoverished victim of three cruelly fluent tormentors who between them drive him mad.

These are the Captain, to whom Woyzeck is batman and barber and who, at his most genial, addresses him as if he were an animal; the Doctor, who uses him as a guinea pig for bizarre dietary experiments; and the Drum-Major, who destroys the one meaningful corner of Woyzeck's life by seducing the mother of his child.

In this production, we get something that plays as a cut-down version of the opera -- the music rarely stops -- but in which some of the text is sung in German, some in English, and some, including all of the title role, is spoken.

In theory, it should give us the best of both worlds. In practice, it doesn't.

The show's director and originator is Misha Aster, a Canadian with extensive theatrical and operatic credits in the United States and Europe, and himself a figure of Buchnerian precocity. (He, too, is 23. We must wish him good health.)

This production is mounted with the co-operation of the Austrian Embassy, and its 21-piece orchestra is more or less evenly composed of Austrians and Canadians. Its five-person cast is similarly international.

This is a big deal. But the director's skills seem to lag some distance behind his aspirations. The production is staged on a large tilted disc with a hole in the middle that turns out to represent a pool. The design (uncredited, so presumably the director's) would probably remind me of Bayreuth, if I had been to Bayreuth.

So much empty space needs to be brought carefully and rigorously to life, and most of the staging here is ungainly and lethargic. Indeed, about the only scene with any physical energy is an elaborate beating-up episode. This is a giveaway in itself, especially when you consider that its violence is far in advance of anything specified in either play or opera.

Experienced actors would have their work cut out in these circumstances, but most of these players, naturally enough, are singers, with no particular physical presence. As Marie, Woyzeck's beloved whom he eventually murders, Renee Salewski sings beautifully and expressively, but she droops in speech and wilts in silence.

Nor does it help that, whether for reasons of economy or interpretation, the list of characters has been drastically reduced. Two of Woyzeck's oppressors, the Doctor and the Captain, have been combined into one, though Doug MacNaughton sings the lines written for the first in German (so he sounds more than ever like a proto- Nazi) and those for the second in English.

The Captain's is the first voice we hear, and the fact that I could actually understand him gave me high hopes for the whole enterprise. Later, I decided what I really wanted was to hear the whole opera sung in English, in a production as good as that mounted a few years ago by the Canadian Opera Company.

Anyway, though the bilingualism certainly has an effect, compressing these two characters into one reduces Woyzeck's predicament.

So, far more damagingly, does the merging of his friend, Andres, with the lecherous Drum-Major, who wins Marie principally through his confidence in the power of his uniform. (He wants to beget on her "a whole regiment of drum-majors.") Here, he is a stocky, ordinary young man in spectacles, a short beard, and a checked shirt. We exchange the tragedy of a baffled man -- alternately starved, bullied and cuckolded by those placed above him -- for the melodrama of the guy whose best buddy done him wrong. A lot of the piece ceases to make even dreamlike sense.

Jonno Roberts, the New Zealand actor who plays Woyzeck, looks authentically haggard, but the confused circumstances give him little chance to affect us. Paul Andres Mahr conducts John Rea's "chamber orchestration" of Berg's score. To me, it sounded scrawny rather than rich or stabbing -- but then the Bathurst Street Theatre is hardly Toronto's longed-for opera house.
