There's a dull green chair, turned over on its side, and a low stool of the same colour, doing duty as a table, with a bottle on top of it. Theres also a dirty cloth on the floor. Thats what we see before the lights go down. When they do, we hear the voice of the one and only actor, sounding muffled. The reason for this becomes clear when we get a sight of him. He has a plastic bag over his head. He's playing a suicide game, how seriously we cannot decide. Neither, it turns out, can he.

The actor, who is also the playwright, is Cliff Cardinal, and the play is Huff, presented by Native Earth Performing Arts in its own fairly new black-box space, the Aki Studio at Daniels Spectrum. The play has been in various stages of development for several years, but this should probably be taken as its definitive version. It comes before us in two simultaneous guises: as a stark, sometimes darkly funny portrayal of neglect and abuse - substance and otherwise - among First Nations; and as a display piece for its authorperformer, one man playing several parts. Both aspects have their virtues but they get in one another's way.

The narrating protagonist, the suicide-tease at the plays start, is named Wind. Hes the middle one of three brothers, whose father left and whose mother subsequently committed a real suicide. Father then returned with a new woman in tow, and set about raising his sons with a mixture of violence and affection, the former predominating. Some protection was provided by the boys' stepmother, and rather more by their maternal grandmother, whose dislike of her son-in-law was heartily returned. Older brother was a sadistic bully. His two siblings, mutually protective, took refuge in a rich fantasy life that includes imagining that their dog can talk and that each of them is possessed of a sacred gift from the Creator." Wind's gift is shoplifting.

Experimenting with fluids, bodily and other, the boys accidentally start a forest fire. (Its complicated.) They subsequently hear that a local firefighter suffered a fatal heart attack while trying to put it out. This could have been coincidence but it supports Winds

belief that his and his brother's lives are at the mercy of a conscienceless practical joker, roughly equivalent to what the Greeks called Fate and Christians the Devil. Wind's name for it, or for him, is Trickster. Trickster is the spirit of things going wrong.

Tricksters tricks plague Wind and his family at home, at play and at school. Rather late on in the proceedings Wind says, with heavy but justified sarcasm, that we will have guessed by now that he and his younger brother have had all the benefits of a reservation education. We see them in class, making life hell for a teacher who more than returns the compliment. In fact, soon after making a sympathetically helpless first appearance, she transmutes into a clone of the monster schoolmistress in Roald Dahls Matilda, the hit musical coming soon to a theatre near us. (The fact that both ladies are played by a man may have something to do with this.) Some earlier parts of the play have whimsically dawdled but the schoolroom episode is the real deal, sad and savage. It is also, in Karin Randoja's production, inventively staged with the various students represented by a line of bottles, the one white pupil alone and aloof at the end of the row. This doesn't make him a victim, the reverse in fact.

School gives the play a booster shot, a momentum that it retains from there on in, as we move forwards to where we came in - to what, if I were more culturally sensitive, I might be able to resist describing as Winds game of Indian roulette. On the way we encounter some more-than-usually pointless gestures toward audience participation. These underline a pervasive cuteness in Cardinals performance, perhaps inseparable from the device of having a single actor play all the roles. This can work excellently when a play is constructed as a series of monologues (think I Claudia or The Blonde, the Brunette and the Vengeful Redhead). But when it goes in for quickfire dialogue, we get what I think of as ventriloquist's timing: the breathless rushing from one voice to the next with a consequent loss of flavour and conviction. Cardinal is a limber performer, but he isn't in Rick Millers virtuoso class as a vocal chameleon. On the other hand, he has more to say than Miller has usually had and when he allows his people room to breathe, he brings them to life. Wind himself, in the way of narrators, is a bit of a blur but his kid brother is a heartbreaker, his drunken dad and take-charge grandma are vivid lightning sketches, and his stepmom a more elaborate one, movingly out of her depth. Shes the most conflicted character in the piece, and therefore the most interesting.
