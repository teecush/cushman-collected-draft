If Stephen Sondheim had written nothing else, he would still deserve canonization for three lines in Comedy Tonight, the scene-setting and tone-setting number of A Funny Thing Happened on the Way to the Forum:

Goodness and badness, Man in his madness, This time it all turns out all right!

That's as conciseandhumane a definition of classic comedy as you could find. It not only stimulates the smile-muscles; it lifts the heart. So does the whole song, a catalogue of farcical types ( "tumblers! grumblers! fumblers! bumblers!") and farcical action, all of which we get to see in furious action in the show that follows. The song is reprised, of course, at the finale, when everything's been sorted out, with the combination of improbability and strict logic that's the hallmark of farce, and a golden glow suffuses the theatre. It's a glow that doesn't need to be disturbed by the threat of an earthquake, however momentary. But that's what happens here. It's an emblem of the way in which Des McAnuff's generally enjoyable Stratford production goes periodically off the rails.

It works when it trusts the actors and, through them, the text. It's a text worth trusting, assembled with diabolical craftsmanship by Burt Shevelove and Larry Gelbart from a handful of the plays written in the second century BCE by the Roman dramatist Plautus, whom Sondheim has described as "the guy who invented comedy." (More exactly, he was the guy who invented situation comedy.) Then add in Sondheim's music and lyrics, which are unfailingly delightful. Take, for example, Everybody Ought To Have a Maid, the trippingly lascivious soft-shoe performed by a gradually accumulating quartet made up of Senex, a gamely lecherous old man; his slave Pesudolus, whose desire to win his freedom helps drive the plot (Plautus may not have been a liberal, but his adaptors are); Hysterium, another slave, with a low panic-threshold; and Lycus, the brothel-keeper

next door. This goes right and wrong. I thought at first that McAnuff and Wayne Cilento (choreographer and co-stager) were killing it by having the actors exchange lengthy conspiratorial whispers between stanzas. My mistake: It makes it all the funnier when the music starts up again. But they do kill or at least wound it when Lycus' emporium revolves during the last chorus, and the courtesans arrayed within grab a piece of the lyric. It's the wrong kind of incongruous; this is a song with a strictly male point of view. It's also meant to be a showcase for those four comedians. It's between them and us. Nobody should get in the way.

Now, Pseudolus as prologue does promise to use "every device we know in our efforts to divert you" (Zero Mostel, the first Pseudolus, made it sound more like a threat) and probably Plautus himself would have used modern scenic technology if he'd had it. But whenever this production throws in a scenic gag -- when, for example, the breasts light up on the statues of Muses that flank John Arnone's set -- well, it doesn't always hinder but it never actually helps. Admittedly, there's a casting problem with this show. Its authors described it as "a scenario for vaudevillians," and it's a tribute not just to old Roman comedy but to old American. (My favourite exchange: "You want a soothsayer?" "How did you know that?" "I'd be a fine

soothsayer if I didn't." They should interpolate that in Julius Caesar.) This crosstalk clowning tradition was coming to an end in 1962, when the show premiered, and it's died out now. These days, we have to make do with mere actors.

Some actors, of course, can leap the gap. Stephen Ouimette is one; his Hysterium is comic genius. Scarcely less hilarious are the young lovers, Mike Nadajewski and Chilina Kennedy, he endearingly immature, she provokingly virginal. (Yes, she's a courtesan. But she's on special order.) Brian Tree is pleasingly befuddled as Erronius, checking in periodically while running seven times around the seven hills of Rome, and if Randy Hughson is somewhat junior for Senex, whose name means "old man," he compensates with beaming crispness.

Hughson and Ouimette get their laughs cleanly on the lines. Bruce Dow, in the star role of Pseudolus, acts fussily around his; he only comes into focus when he duets with Ouimette on the reprise of Lovely, the comic love-duet (already a hoot when the young 'uns do it). Most spectacularly miscalculated is Dan Chameroy's Miles Gloriosus ( "braggart soldier") who should be impregnably self-satisfied and terrifying, but is here made of jelly. But the actor hardly stands a chance in a production that has a single chariot wheel careening across the stage before his entrance, thus undercutting his immortal first line, "Stand aside, everyone! I take large steps!" Lycus is an underwritten role but Cliff Saunders' decision to play him as an epicene eccentric rather than a mercenary man of business is not a happy one. His non-speaking merchandise includes regular Stratford adornment Sara Topham, who may not be playing Medea later this week, as the song says, but was once a memorable Cassandra, and is obviously having a ball here. They've cut Pretty Little Picture, which is unforgivable, but they're not the first. The show remains irresistible. It all turns out all right.
