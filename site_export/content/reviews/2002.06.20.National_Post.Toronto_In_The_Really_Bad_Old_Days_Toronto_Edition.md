TORONTO THE GOOD

Artword Theatre, Toronto

Something has gone calamitously wrong with Toronto the Good. (I'm speaking theatrically, not historically). The idea is intriguing enough: A scrapbook of life in the city in the 1890s, drawing on songs, pamphlets and journalism of the time. The songs seemed especially promising. The topical tunes of this period were often musically tame, but their lyrics, even when not very funny, teem with sociological interest. What appears on the stage, though, is almost totally inert.

There are some excuses. There are only five in the cast: Two men, three women, all of them constantly on the go. One of the quintet had to be replaced a week before opening; her substitute was unready to face an audience, and her uncertainty seems to have infected her colleagues. I have never known so many lines to be fluffed or mistimed in a single show, or heard so many songs so haltingly and illogically phrased. I have rarely beheld a cast so palpably uneasy about what they were doing. Two of the performers (Ann Bisch and Michelle Piller) display occasional verve. It would be cruel even to mention the names of the others. In the circumstances, it was irresponsible as well as foolhardy not to postpone the premiere.

I doubt, though, that all of the problems can be attributed to last-minute panic. The show, assembled and directed by Ronald Weihs, lacks focus. It sets up a contrast between a puritanically reforming Mayor of Toronto, William P. Howland, and the human impulses that go on cheerfully defying him: A bit like Measure for Measure, really.

But this conflict never takes off; after his initial proclamation, Mayor Howland -- here implausibly young and weightless -- disappears both from the stage and from our consciousness.

On the other side, there is the inescapable fact that few of the new freedoms -- of women to ride bicycles, for example, or to go out in boats with young men -- strike us now as remotely shocking. We need to be taken far deeper into the contemporary mindset if we are ever to understand why they should have been.

When it comes to depicting more radical vice -- the flourishing turn-of-the-century brothel-trade, for example -- the show becomes unhelpfully dainty. A police-court scene, in which a beaten wife pleads for her husband's acquittal because without him her children will starve, does strike an uncomfortable -- and uncomfortably modern -- note. But though the show gives a lot of time to the burgeoning of feminism, the whole issue seems cut adrift from the vice-virtue contest that is supposed to be governing the show. As sung here, some supposedly stirring hymns to emancipation are notable only for their feebleness. Nothing in this production ever seems to lead smoothly or pointedly to anything else.

Obviously it is meant to, or at least it was once. We of the press were handed a synopsis that reads like the fossil of some abandoned concept. It speaks of a pungent contemporary chronicler named C.S. Clark, and hints that he will be our narrator and tour guide. Well, one actor does do the bulk of the scene-setting, but he is never identified as C.S. Clark or anyone else, and he never gives the show any kind of tone: Certainly not a satirical one.

The first half is interminable. The second begins more promisingly, in a music hall. A comedian does an all-purpose introductory act that is devilishly hard to bring to life but does at least suggest, in its stream-of-consciousness versatility, a performing line that was to stretch in the next century at least as far as Jimmy Durante and Danny Kaye. Subsequent items, though, turn out as formless and arbitrary as the rest of the show, right down to a closer about coming to the end of the day -- a day that we never knew had started.

There is some amusement, as there generally is, in the recital of patent-medicine ads, many of which seem to have originated in the United States. And that's another thing: Most of the trends depicted in the show, and most of the music, could equally well be American or British. (One song, in and about a streetcar, strikingly anticipates the Trolley Song from Meet Me in St. Louis, though it's nothing like as good.)

There is little here that is specifically Canadian, let alone Torontonian. That could have been a point -- maybe Mayor Howland should really have been fighting for cultural protectionism -- but nothing is made of it. Indeed the only local interest -- sometimes the only interest of any kind -- lies in hearing the names of Toronto streets, trying to picture how they must have looked then, and comparing that with how they look now. I suppose it was a slip of the tongue that led somebody to give the address of one house of ill-fame as Sheppard and Adelaide; it must have been a hell of a place to get to, even then.
