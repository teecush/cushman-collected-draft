YICHUD (SECLUSION)

Theatre Passe Muraille, Toronto

---

The main space at Theatre Passe Muraille has been transformed into an orthodox synagogue. There's even a Hebrew inscription over the door whose English translation is "without walls," this being the equivalent of the theatre's own name. (I confess: I had to have this pointed out and explained to me.)

Within those non-existent walls, there's a wedding going on. Arrive in good time, and you can watch, even join in, the pre-ceremonial dancing and singing. We are here to celebrate, boisterously, the marriage of Rachel and Chaim.

Well, we aren't really. Yichud (Seclusion) is a play. There is a meta-dramatic kick to it, though, since the actors playing the couple, Julie Tepperman and Aaron Willis, are husband and wife in real life. And just to make art's imitation of life imitating art even more intense, Tepperman wrote the play and Willis directs it. Mazel tov to both of them, as they've done spirited and intelligent jobs, though the writing at least is fragmented -- which, up to a point, was probably the intention.

There is a large -- and by today's straitened standards, a phenomenal -- ensemble, plus a klezmer band, but only six speaking characters. They come in pairs. Apart from Rachel and Chaim, we have her parents and his brothers. Relations within each of the latter couples are strained, and this puts extra pressure on the newlyweds. Theirs in an arranged marriage, and their few dates have been chaperoned. So when, immediately after being officially pronounced man and wife, they are sequestered in the yichud room it's the first time they've been alone together. They've grown up in a North Toronto enclave that seems rather less liberal than the viilage in Fiddler on the Roof, and indeed it's often hard not to be reminded of that show, especially in the staging of the post-nuptial celebrations (though there is, alas, no Bottle Dance). The play is savvy enough to acknowledge the resemblance; bride and groom break into a gently self-mocking rendition of Miracle of Miracles, that proves more touching out of context than it has ever been inside it.

Outside the isolation booth, the in-laws conduct their own duologues, bringing to the boil tensions that have been simmering throughout. Rachel's mother, Malka, has been waiting until the wedding is over before walking out on her own marriage, and she resents her daughter for delaying the moment by insisting on an education and a career. Her husband Mordechai acknowledges that he's been a selfish lover, and has contacted a Torah sex-therapy site for advice. He presents Malka with a numbered list of proposals; she is, smoulderingly, not impressed. Diane Flacks plays Malka and Richard Greenblatt plays Mordechai. He is especially funny when telling the guests a long Talmudic joke whose sexist implications come back, in

the audience's minds at least, to haunt him. She turns on a dime to lacerating effect.

Chaim's elder brothers, Menachem and Ephraim, are both yeshiva teachers. Ephraim (Jordan Pettle) has done better at it; he has a job in New York and Menachem (Michael Rubenfeld) resents it. ( "How's Brooklyn?" Menachem asks. "How's Thornhill?" Ephraim replies.) We first meet them, in the only substantial scene that isn't a duologue, giving their shy and sheltered sibling some highly suspect marital advice. Their gloves come off later, when Ephraim tells how he's incurred the displeasure of his employing rabbi, who's also his father-in-law, by standing up to him over the threatened dismissal of a gay student. Menachem is shocked that Ephraim should have jeopardized the advantages of which he himself is jealous, and tells him he should have toed the line. Ephraim wants to "cure" the student, which seems naive of him; still, there's no doubt that he wins this round, though Jordan Pettle's being an exceptionally sympathetic actor may have something to do with this.

Meanwhile, back in the seclusion zone. Chaim, under the impression that this is where the marriage actually gets consummated, wrestles with Menachem's supposedly Biblical instructions about doing it through a hole in a sheet. I laughed, I admit, like a drain over the uncertainties about how big the hole should be. It gives Chaim and Rachel something to laugh at, too, and it could be the start of a good marriage; we want it to be, since Tepperman is attractively sparky and Willis delightfully earnest. (I'm talking, of course, about their onstage personae, though I'm sure they're very nice off as well.)

I guess most courtship rituals are bizarre, and none come with guarantees of happy endings; still, to these secular Jewish eyes, many of the rules and prohibitions related here seem ridiculous, and some of them barbaric. Rachel seems to endorse this when she suggests that maybe some customs should have been left in the ghettoes of the Middle Ages, and she isn't just talking about holes in sheets. So the play comes over as an attack on ultra-orthodox Judaism, even though it was obviously aiming at something more nuanced; which is why I call it fragmented. Moment by moment, though, it's well written and excellently acted. It's also beautifully staged, on both levels and in all corners of the Passe Muraille auditorium: much credit to Beth Kates, the newly ubiquitous designer also responsible for the modestly environmental set for Assassins and the striking abstract background for Such Creatures. Credit, too, to Passe Muraille for saving the day: Yichud was originally to be co-produced by the Harold Green Jewish Theatre, who pulled out; though a show like this one -- joyous and critical and indigenous -- is surely just what they should be doing. Though if they prefer to mount pre-tested American plays whose authors just happen to be Jews, that I suppose is their business.
