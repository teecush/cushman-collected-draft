THEATRE REVIEW

This Must Be the Place: The CN Tower Show Theatre Passe Muraille, Toronto

The problem with This Must Be the Place is that this doesn't have to be the place. Let me explain.

A few years ago a group called the Architect Theatre Collective came to Toronto with a play entitled Highway 63: The Fort Mac Show. It was set, as you might guess, in Fort McMurray, Alta., and though much of what it showed us, in terms of characters and relationships, didn't seem to be peculiar to that locale, it didn't matter too much: They were characters and they were relationships and it was a play, well-written and well-acted.

The same group now offers Toronto a show about Toronto, or professing to be; its contents turn out to be no more peculiar to Toronto than those of The Fort Mac Show were to Fort Mac. And it matters more because the new offering doesn't have its predecessor's traditional virtues. It isn't a play and doesn't pretend to be. It's more of a revue; or, to be franker, a grab-bag.

It does make regular references to Toronto areas and landmarks, including the one that provides if not a focus, then at least a subtitle. Indeed, it starts there, with a reasonable evocation of what it's like to have an evening out in an establishment more notable for the view than the cuisine. A much later scene suggests what a blessing it is that the CN Tower didn't become the focus for redevelopment that was initially intended. That's probably the most interesting point of the evening. We also hear testimony from people who have worked there, and who don't appear very enthusiastic about the experience.

Much of the show seems to have been assembled from interviews. This kind of theatre verite can produce exciting results when there's a story to be told or a thesis to be advanced. Here it just feels tepid and aimless. The overall impression is that Toronto is a big city that can be scary when you first arrive but can later turn out to be reasonably friendly. That's true enough. It would also be true of almost any big city you care to name.

The show's refusal to make big statements, about the CN Tower or anything else, does have an upside. It suggests honesty. Certainly the four performers - Georgina Beaty, Greg Gale, Ingrid Hansen, Thomas Olajide - are pleasant enough, though both Beaty and Gale showed in Highway 63 that they were capable of more. Gale and Olajide are the most aggressively ingratiating here, sometimes to the point of overplaying their hands.

Near the end they all inveigle members of the audience to come onstage and dance with them, which takes you back a bit, though not necessarily in a Torontonian direction. Ditto for some earlier audience-involvement games: There's nothing threatening about them, but there's nothing very relevant either. In fact they feel like padding, as if the company had taken on an assignment that it couldn't quite fulfill but couldn't very well walk away from.

I called the show a revue, but it doesn't seem to fulfill a revue's usual obligation to at least try to make every number funny. The only item that could be described as a sketch is a recreation of a City Hall debate about the plastic-bag levy. Real names are used and real people impersonated, with councillors Adam Vaughan and Giorgio Mammoliti especially prominent. It's quite lively, but gets nowhere; which may of course be the point.

The show's named creators, other than the actors, are JonathanSeinen(director)and Charlotte Corbeil-Coleman (fairly experienced playwright) "with Layne Coleman" (very experienced actor, author and director). The senior Coleman is a former artistic director of Theatre Passe Muraille and must know the city very well. Beaty, the first performer to identify herself, confesses to not being a resident, or at least not on any consistent basis. But it's never very clear whether we're getting an outsider's view, an insider's view or something in between.

The show is part of TPM's ongoing celebration/examination of Toronto: a town that Andy McKim, current artistic director, describes in his welcoming program note as "a vibrant, intercultural, progressive and worldly city that is home to the most extraordinary people possible." He's right. Making that description theatrically real is another thing. Soulpepper's Window on Toronto managed it, partly by concentrating on a single specific image that was also, literally, a viewpoint.

The present show is well-intentioned but scattershot. This, you feel, can't be the place.

This Must Be the Place: The CN Tower Show runs until Oct. 27 at Theatre Passe Muraille in Toronto. For tickets, call 416-504-7429 or visit passemuraille.on.ca.
