FIGHTING WORDS

Factory Theatre, Toronto

- - -

TORONTO - The first image we see is a photograph: a gloved young man with fists raised and cauliflower ears. The first live person we see is a young woman, skipping rope. For a moment she could be any girl playing. Then we realize through the intensity of her exercise that she is an aspiring boxer, maybe fixated on the boy in the picture. A snatch of dialogue with another woman, slightly older and carrying a laundry basket, confirms this is indeed the case. It's a neat opening.

We are in Wales. In 1980, Johnny Owen, a bantamweight from Merthyr Tydfil, challenged a Mexican, Lupe Pintor, for the world championship. They fought in Los Angeles; Owen was knocked out, went into a coma and died a few days later.

Sunil Kuruvilla, a Canadian playwright whose plays up to now have only been seen in the United States, has taken this event, kept its factual antagonists offstage and told us the story through the eyes of three fictitious characters. Kuruvilla has had to invent people whose significance lies in their relation to somebody else real and unseen and make them live as more than representative figures. This cannot be easy, but he has managed it impressively well. We know these women didn't exist, but we believe they could have. Keeping them in action for the duration of a play is another matter, and here there are problems.

All the men in Merthyr have flown off to L.A. to attend on their hero and witness the fight. The women they have left behind are all as fixated on him as they are; the whole village seems to centre on boxing in general and Johnny in particular.

We meet Peg, obviously in love with Johnny, who used to let her box with him; her married sister Nia, more cerebrally attached to Johnny and with ambitions of becoming a BBC announcer; and Mrs. Davies, with whom the sisters apparently live.

Nia calls her husband George; Mrs. Davies, from an older generation, calls hers Mr. Davies. There are, we gather, problems with both marriages; and as nerves become frayed, the two wives begin dragging them to the surface. This, though, is an inconclusive sideshow from the main event: the struggle between the sisters over Johnny and the different things they each see in him.

The play is performed on a square platform, surrounded by hanging laundry. The laundry lines also get to double as the ropes of a boxing ring, the domestic turning into the epic; we are watching a metaphorical prizefight between Peg and Nia. That at least seems to be the idea, but the dramatic structure never really supports it; we are stuck at the level of two women naturalistically quarrelling, and the words don't fight fiercely enough.

Rosemary Dunsmore's production is sensitive, if sometimes slow, but it lacks emotional size. This becomes especially troublesome after the intermission. (A surprise -- I thought I had this pegged as a no-intermission play.) At the end of Act One, Peg breaks away and catches a plane to L.A. to see the real fight for herself. In Act Two she returns and tells what she has seen. It's a graphic speech. It's also a formal piece of classic storytelling, and it leaves her two hearers stranded between styles.

They are also somewhat marooned by their accents. The Welsh lilt, on the lips of non-natives, is apt to come out sing-song; and Linda Goranson as Mrs. Davies seems in particular to be operating within a limited expressive range.

Irene Poole is beguilingly gentle, but almost too consistently so, as Nia; a quirk of the play is that we are more concerned to learn the result of her radio audition than we are to learn the result of the fight. Jody Stevens is engagingly tough on the outside, fragile within, as Peg, but can neither maintain nor quite suspend her characterization during her long narration.

Both sisters turn out to be surprisingly good mimics; they can each put on the voice of Johnny's Mexican nemesis and communicate the hunger for success that he obviously possesses and that Johnny as obviously - and fatally - lacks. Indeed, we learn more about Lupe Pintor in those few moments than we learn about Johnny Owen in the entire play.

We also get a sharper sense of what Johnny meant to his community at large than we do of what he meant to the women we actually see, however much they talk about him.

With its fate-battered women and offstage violence, the play could be a distant descendant of Greek tragedy. What it doesn't have -- except conceivably for dedicated boxing fans who enter the theatre knowing all about the Owen-Pintor contest -- is an audience who carry the myth in their bones and can invest the proceedings with an overarching sense of doom.
