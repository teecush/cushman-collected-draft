Fyodor Dostoevskys short novel The Gambler is a highly dramatic work composed under highly dramatic circumstances. Dostoevesky was under the gun when he wrote it, committed to writing two books more or less simultaneously (the other was Crime and Punishment) with the knowledge that if this one was not delivered on time, he would forfeit to his publisher the rights to his complete works. Unable to write fast enough, he engaged a secretary, a young woman with an understanding nature and excellent shorthand skills. He dictated the story to her, and together they had it ready for delivery at the last moment of the 11th hour. Soon after this was accomplished, the young woman, Anna Grigoryevna, became Dostoevskys second wife.

Ronald Weihs's dramatization of The Gambler (the last production at the Artword, at least in its current location) splits the playing time and the stage space between the story itself and the manner of its composition. This is an interesting idea, or so it seems to begin with, and practical, too; it enables David Ferry, a good actor who is at least 20 years too old for the title role, to put quotation marks around the incongruity by jumping back and forth between playing the character and playing his creator.

The fictional protagonist is a 22-year-old tutor called Alexei, whom we meet dancing attendance on his employer, an impoverished Russian general, and all his family. The clan has fetched up at a German town unequivocally named Roulettenberg. They send Alexei to the casino to try and recoup their fortunes. He succeeds often enough for his employers to get dangerously hooked, and finally, he succumbs himself. Dostoevsky, a serious gambling addict, knew what he was writing about.

He also put into the story another real-life addiction -- his hopeless love for a woman named Polina, a name he here bestows on the General's stepdaughter, who has Alexei on a string and is pretty highly strung herself. Fyodor (thats the program billing for the author-as-character; the name Dostoevesky is sprung on us later, as if it were some kind of surprise) makes the connection himself, and we wait for the two Polinas to come together in a dazzling coup de theatre. If the outer and inner stories have so much in common, they ought to collide at some point. It never happens; Ferry merely keeps crossing from the side of the stage, where hes Fyodor, to the centre, where's he's Alexei, and back again.

Fyodors courtship of his adoring stenographer (Claire Jenkins, doing her best) is both prosaic and insipid. Theres no indication that his gambling continued to plague the earlier years of their marriage, though that's what happened and it would seem to be relevant. Instead we get some stuff about Fyodor gambling on getting his book finished, or maybe on proposing, and the gamble paying off. He proposes; she accepts; happy ever after.

The dramatized story itself is a little livelier. It's a doggedly literal adaptation, until it runs up against scenes it cant stage, at which point it throws its hands in the air and hopes for the best. Molly Thoms production suffers about equally from lack of imagination and lack of resources. In the book, the casino scenes are made feverishly thrilling by the presence of a mob of spectators, egging the principals on, giving them good and bad advice, sometimes making off with their winnings. Here all we get are generic recorded crowd noises plus a roulette wheel that doesn't spin. (Economy? Symbolism?) There goes the atmosphere, there goes the whole thrust and flavour of the story.

What actors there are suffer from double-trouble. Irene Poole plays the stepdaughter, icily and effectively, but has nothing left over for the very different role of the General's gold- digging mistress. Brett Christopher does well as a decent English gentleman, not so well as a dubious French one. The machinations of these people, murky enough in the book, become impenetrable in the play. Karl Pruner gets his one role of the General completely wrong, as a colourless martinet instead of the virtuoso of self-abasement that hes meant to be. Jennifer Phipps plays a grandmother who arrives to lose her fortune at the tables, when her family were counting on her to die quietly in Russia and leave it to them. She brightens things up, but shes an eccentric English lady rather than a possessed Russian one. Ferry and Poole do get some erotic tension out of their later scenes, but he's too leathery to encompass the tutor's frenzied vulnerability, either to desire or to the game. A line in both play and novel says that the Russians have never understood roulette. Maybe thats why they had to invent their own brand.

And talking of inventors: Andrew Moodies The Real McCoy is another case of writing-by-numbers, but one that doesn't even have Dostoevsky to fall back on. Its source, far less amenable, is Life. It's a biography of Elijah McCoy (1844-1929), born in Colchester, Ont.

That about exhausts the Canadian content, since McCoy was educated at Edinburgh University and spent his adult life in and around Detroit. Its an astonishing story; McCoy, the son of escaped slaves, had a remarkable career as an engineer and inventor, his signal achievement being the invention of a lubricating device for industrial machinery. Customers wary of being fobbed off with imitations would make a point of asking for "the real McCoy." So a man of whom few people have heard has nonetheless become a proverb. This is mentioned in the publicity but, strangely enough, not featured in the play.

As a black genius in a prejudiced society, McCoy faced huge pressures, and the play mentions them from time to time. But it doesnt find any consistent way of dramatizing them. In fact it lacks any central conflict, personal or political. Elijah is disowned by his fundamentalist father and, tragically and prematurely, loses two wives. But these things seem to happen just because they happened. They carry no weight, and the characters are either stereotypes or ciphers. McCoy's scientific career is more intrinsically interesting, and there's an amusing sequence in which - - apparently to stop his second wife from nagging him -- he creates an ironing board, a lawn sprinkler and a pair of waterproof boots, one after another, just like that. But the comedy of this has no connection to the rest of the play, which also moves fast but only because there is nothing it wants to linger on. And the play is full of loose ends: Elijahs parents cant afford to send him to college, but next thing we know he's there, and across the Atlantic at that; there's a line suggesting that one of his employers is going blind, but thats the first and last we hear of it.

Moodie is his own director, and may have set himself an impossible task. At any rate the production is scrappy and much of the acting feeble. A major exception is Maurice Dean Wint who endows the adult McCoy with a courteous nature that expresses itself chiefly by speaking (and writing) in fluent polysyllables. A minor one is Kevin Hanchard, who starts as the boy McCoy and then moves on to the heros best friend, who is unable to live up to Elijah's gospel of self-help and self-reliance: a noble and necessary doctrine in science but limiting in terms of personal relations. There's the glimmer of a theme there, but it never burns.
