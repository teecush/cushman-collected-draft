HOW THE GRINCH STOLE CHRISTMAS! THE MUSICAL

Sony Centre, Toronto

---

Literature's doughtiest Warriors Against Christmas are out in force on Toronto's stages this year. Charles Dickens' Scrooge is making his biannual return to the Young Centre, while over at that other Centre currently known as Sony, Dr. Seuss' Grinch is meeting his pre-destined fate as the subject of a holiday musical.

A Christmas Carol is a great show, How the Grinch Stole Christmas! The Musical is a terrible one; and in both cases, the causes lie about equally with the original material and with how it's been put on the stage. The two stories are of course identical: misanthrope hates Christmas, does his best to spoil it for everyone, is made to see the error of his ways and ends up as a kind of super-Santa. The difference, apart from the fact that Dickens got there first, is that one provides a social and psychological context, while the other exists in a self-created void. That is its right as a fantasy, but it doesn't give theatrical adaptors, or indeed audiences, much to work with. So they have to pad. The original Seuss poem takes about five minutes to read; double that, perhaps, if you're doing it aloud, and add in a few more to savour the illustrations. It's already been blown up into a TV cartoon special. It now has to be further inflated to 90 minutes of stage time, which is a problem.

The solution adopted by Timothy Mason (book and lyrics) and Mel Marvin (music) is to take what was already sugary and sweeten it. The Grinch's dog Max, barely mentioned in the original, is promoted to a mixture of sidekick and conscience: a sort of canine Jiminy Cricket. Max's later self narrates the story, in a suitably hangdog fashion, and there are times when both are onstage simultaneously, castigating the Grinch for his Grinchiness. But then this is a musical whose last seven numbers are all reprises; it has no qualms about repeating itself. The Grinch, looking down from his Mount Crumpit fastness, sings a song about how much he detests the Whos and their incorrigible Christmas cheeriness; the scene then shifts to Whoville itself, whose inhabitants prove the Grinch's point by going through exactly the same number, with nothing changed except the tone of voice and the pronouns.

As may be evident, I'm no great fan of Seuss in general, or of this poem in particular. What the original does have going for it -- the swing of the verse and some of the rhymes -- gets lost amid the clumsier couplets added here, both spoken and sung. The interpolation, from the TV version, of You're a Mean One, Mr. Grinch, with Seuss's own lyrics, turns out not to be the expected bright spot; it's an awful, laboured song. Musically it's inferior to the new stuff, though I might be influenced here by memories of a lovely show called Tintypes, which has a score assembled, though not written, by this musical's composer.

Talking of pedigree here, How the Grinch ... was originally directed by Jack O'Brien, who's American theatre royalty, though how long it's been since he saw it, I couldn't say. I doubt, though, that it ever ranked among his most distinguished work. The Grinch himself has quite the resume, too; he's Stefan Karl, whose record in Reykjavik, from Cyrano to Singin' in the Rain, suggests Iceland's answer to Colm Feore. Good place for the Grinch to come from, really, and Karl plays him with enthusiastic grittiness, cresting in a shameless audience-baiting solo called One of a Kind. It makes no sense of course: when Mr. Burns, on The Simpsons, does a comparable hat-and-cane routine, it's because he's a social monster who wants applause; the Grinch is anti-social and only wants to be left alone. Anyway Karl looks the part, or at least he has a good green costume. His best moment on the first night came near the end, when he traded jibes with small members of the audience who were stubbornly refusing to say what was expected of them. For one blissful minute of a pre-packaged production, spontaneity reigned.
