HONK IF YOU LOVE CHEESES

Tim Sims Playhouse, Toronto

TORONTO - The latest Second City revue has something in common with The Lion King: The best thing about it is its opening. And that opening is itself like that of The Lion King, and a very funny parody of it.

It appears to begin with a joke we've heard before, the one in which members of the cast sing settings of the regulations governing attendance at theatres. We are musically enjoined to turn off our cellphones, bleepers and digital watches, and forbidden to take pictures of anything.

This time, though, the subject matter expands, and we are told where we can buy our T-shirts and other memorabilia. Indeed some samples are brought on, and paraded around the stage, on the ends of sticks. It begins to seem like an amusing comment on commercial, or at least on commercialized, theatre. (The Second City, a very profitable franchise, is in no position to take the high ground here; but then, it isn't trying to.)

And we begin to notice that the sounds that are coming from the actors' mouths somewhat resemble African chants, and the aisles as well as the stage are filling up with performers -- which, with a cast of six, is quite a trick. A couple of guys in animal masks wander on -- an elephant and would that be a giraffe? -- and look somewhat surprised to be there. Anyway, they are gathering at the water-hole and there we are, in the dinner-theatre audience, taking the waters.

Also like The Lion King, Honk if You Love Cheeses (these titles get more and more mysterious) never returns to its initial level; and it doesn't have the same resources for taking up the slack.

It does make two departures from recent Second City tradition. One of these is an attempt at an overall shape; we are introduced early on to characters and situations that recur throughout the evening. This can be confusing, since the same actors appear in other and quite disconnected sketches, but there is something reassuring about it; it makes you more inclined to laugh at the material, which, considering some of the material, is just as well.

The second innovation is that this supposedly improvisatory troupe has gone, to some extent, back to improvisation: not just in the coda that they customarily tack on to the end of every show, but in the main body of the performance.

Twice they build numbers around suggestions from the house, and they are quite artful about the way they collect these building blocks. A couple in the audience are quizzed about where they first met. "At camp" said the respondents the night I attended, and two of the performers went into a long wailing love-duet with as many references to cabins, counsellors and enforced exercise as they could manage to cram in.

A later item began as a harangue about Hooters, delivered by a recruitment officer to a bunch of aspirant waitresses, they being us, or some of us. We were asked, among other things, to name rooms and pieces of furniture that might be found at our place of employment. The answers were solemnly chalked on a blackboard; and suddenly we were in the midst of an Agatha Christie spoof in which, as in some elaborate game of Clue, the words inscribed had to be worked into the solution of the crime.

It's probable that audience responses don't vary much from night to night, and that the cast have a firm structure that they can hold on to no matter what. But it's still pleasant. The Hooters sketch is good in itself (Sandy Jobin-Bevans, who seems rather tall to be a comedian, has exactly the right creased enthusiasm to put it over) and the Christie spoof is great fun, topping its resident sleuth with a visiting super-sleuth, who boldly tells us, "I know who did it and so do you", then backs off a bit and asks shyly, "So, what do you think?"

Making it up as they go along entails a strain of hesitant bravado that is rather appealing; unfortunately, the same quality distinguishes this company even in the bits that are supposed to be rehearsed, where it doesn't work nearly as well.

There is quite a good number about two women chatting endlessly in a mall to the despair of an accompanying child (Doug Morency performs prodigies of infant petulance in an attempt to capture their attention) and a very good, even imaginative, one about Blockbuster where, a once- idealistic employee despairingly notes, they now file Canadian movies under "foreign." That's all.

Especially surprising and, in the end, depressing, is the fact that spoofing The Lion King and The Mousetrap are the nearest the show gets to topical satire. There's no law; it's just that with an election brewing and health-care in crisis and the schools in disarray and newspapers shooting it out in the streets, they do seem to be passing up an awful lot of promising material. There is one number that looks like it's going to be about teachers having sex with students, but it stops short. (It does have a good punchline: a commodity in short supply elsewhere.)

There's even a sketch about cancer, which might count as being brave; it even has an idea -- that the medical profession, by definition, is in the business of profiting from human suffering -- that seems like it might be neat. But it would need more rigorous working-out than it gets here to escape seeming merely distasteful. But it does illustrate, on a large scale, a comic technique that's far too prevalent both in and out of this revue: take a dodgy notion and pummel it whimsically to death, hoping that if you go on long enough you'll hit something funny. Or at least that your audience will give in.

As for the show's recurring plotline: it concerns a couple of guys who go into partnership in what they call the human-fulfilment business, plus some of their clients and employees. The more vulnerable and exploitable of the partners is played by Lee Smart, a Second City veteran whose large frame and butterball countenance allow him to ring some fairly ingenious changes on the themes of gullibility and desperation, and who can sometimes get angry by way of contrast. (He has a freezingly genial smile.) Lisa Brooke is sharp, if too consistently abrasive.

I wasn't crazy about the two remaining cast members: Paul Bates being oily to the point of deliquescence; and Carolyn Taylor confusing comedy with exaggerated pouting and squealing. The director is Sandra Balcovske who, just to clinch the parallels with The Lion King, is a woman.
