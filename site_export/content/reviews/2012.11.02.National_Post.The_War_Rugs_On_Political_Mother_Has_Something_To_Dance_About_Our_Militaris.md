THEATRE REVIEW

Political Mother Bluma Appel Theatre, Toronto

For the second time straight, Canadian-Stage has housed the most spectacular show in Toronto. Well, maybe not counting War Horse. Right after Tear the Curtain, play and movie in one, came Political Mother, a dance-rock-theatre piece that's visually and aurally blistering but with beatific interludes. Its too-short run at the St. Lawrence Centre is already over, but it was only the first leg of a Canadian tour.

Its the creation of Hofesh Shechter, a composer and choreographer (a portmanteau of talents that may be unprecedented but is certainly useful and even logical), born and trained in Israel but now based in London. It starts, on a smoky stage, with a lone figure in medieval armour. I thought for a moment that it might be Joan of Arc; maybe I was misled by the gender implications of a show title that otherwise remains inexplicable. Although, if you take the Mother part to be an abbreviation, it does make a certain sense.

Anyway, the figure, which on later consideration is probably male, draws its sword and commits hara-kiri. And, with that image of martial self-destruction, the show is on.

Its about war: how people are incited to it, how enthusiastically they sign up for it, how quickly and bitterly they come to regret it and how they keep coming back for more. These are hardly new themes, but Shechter has found powerful ways of illustrating them. On the top level of a double-tiered set, a dictator-demagogue rants incomprehensibly. He fades in and out, vocally and also visually - Lee Curran's marvellous lighting is an integral part of the experience - to be replaced by a line of pounding guitarists. They alternate in turn with a line of equally pounding drummers, placed at ground level. And in front of them, there are Shechter's magnificent dancers, a dozen of them, responding enthusiastically to the beat. (There are times when the choreography suggests West Side Story gone nuts.) The intoxication is part-way understandable; the show, in addition to its other virtues, is a foot-tapper.

Sometimes the music changes. Bach is piped in, and the dancers enjoy lyrical passages of peace, the Four Minute Hate giving way to the Ten Minute Love. (My timings are approximate.) Other scenes depict the underside of war; the intoxicated warriors of earlier episodes become the haggard, stumbling inhabitants of death camps. Then theyre back in the ranks, the latest turn in an endless cycle. Its the message of Buffy Sainte-Marie's Universal Soldier but without the words, which is probably an improvement.

Regarding the lack of words, in a piece that's billed as theatre as well as "dance": it confirms that rock works best in the theatre when its nonverbal. Rock musicals are an oxymoron, since you cant tell stories or develop characters through monotonous music wedded to inaudible (or, where audible, woolly and pretentious) lyrics.

Political Mother, whose story is an endlessly recurring loop and whose characters are robots or archetypes, doesn't have that problem. It uses its music for visceral effect, which is tremendous. It may even constitute an unwitting indictment of rock. Its dictator figure, ranting gibberish, is a rock star twice-removed, and the drums and guitars are his method of inciting his subjects to frenzy. When the show wants to evoke tenderness it goes, as previously noted, for baroque.

The proceedings aren't entirely non-verbal. There are two outbreaks of distinguishable words, one written, one sung. Near the end, an electrical sign is displayed, reading "where there is pressure there is ..." Were kept waiting for the punch word for the longest time; when it arrives it turns out to be "folkdance." The anti-climax gets a laugh, but the purpose is unclear. It seems to be mocking the gentler steps that have punctuated the evening, or at best to be treating them as a naive delusion.

Even more perplexing is the recorded singing, at the last moment, of Both Sides Now: a vocal folk dance perhaps (though the selected performance is grittier than that). Well, Joni Mitchell did claim to have looked at love and life from both sides, and the show may possibly be claiming to have done the same, though more at life than at love, despite some nice pas de deux. (It doesnt much look at clouds, except perhaps mushroom ones.)

The use of the song has an ironic air about it, without much clue as to what it's being ironic about. I'm not convinced that Shechter knew how to finish his creation, but that could be the penalty of telling a never-ending story.

Meanwhile, both the way he tells it, and the way his dancers and musicians execute his telling, are conceptually and technically superb.

Political Mother plays in Montreal until Nov. 3, Quebec City (Nov. 5) and Ottawa (Nov. 9-10) For more information, visit canadianstage.com.
